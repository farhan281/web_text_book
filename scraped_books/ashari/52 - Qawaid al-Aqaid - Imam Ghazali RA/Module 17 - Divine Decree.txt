Debates on Divine Decree with Hasan and Ali
Imam Ghazali recounts debates with Qadaris, where Hasan al-Basri and Ali refuted predestination objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how correct belief in divine decree (qadr) does not negate human responsibility and reward, as shown in two early debates.

Core Idea
Belief in predestination does not imply coercion; reward and punishment remain meaningful.

This passage presents two encounters from early Islamic history where scholars refuted misconceptions about divine decree. First, Hasan al-Basri convinces a Qadari to recant. Then, a Syrian questions Ali about the implications of predestination. As you read, notice how Ali affirms decree while denying coercion.

This is a standard kalam argument.

Truths Harmful to Disclose: The Second Category

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Some truths are like sunlight for bats: harmful not because false, but because the listener cannot bear them.

Ghazali continues his classification of hidden matters. Having covered matters beyond understanding, he now turns to a more subtle category: things the mind can grasp but that should still be guarded. This passage explains why even understandable truths can be dangerous if disclosed to the wrong audience, using vivid analogies and the example of qadr.

Commentary
This passage defines the second category of hidden matters in Ghazali's classification: things that are intellectually understandable—the mind can grasp them—but whose disclosure is harmful to most people, though not harmful to the prophets and the righteous (siddiqin) because of their deep-rooted knowledge. Ghazali gives the example of the secret of qadr (divine decree), which scholars were commanded not to divulge. He uses two analogies: sunlight harms the eyes of bats, and the scent of roses harms dung beetles—indicating that what is true to some can be harmful to others. He then says it is not far-fetched when we affirm that disbelief, adultery, sins, and all evils are by God's decree, will, and intention—this truth itself has harmed some (who misunderstand it, leading to fatalism or denial of moral responsibility). The footnotes: (1) extends the category to saints and deep scholars; (2) explains why prophets and siddiqin are not harmed; (3) reports that the Prophet or a scholar rebuked those who spoke about qadr; (4) and (5) define bats and the dung beetle; (6) notes a textual variant. The passage teaches that wisdom in teaching requires considering the recipient's capacity, not just the truth of the content.

The First Principle: All Events Are Created by Allah
Imam Ghazali establishes the first principle that every event is created by Allah alone.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic doctrine that every event, including human actions, is created and brought into existence by Allah alone, as established by Imam Ghazali.

Core Idea
From a hair's movement to human choices—everything is created directly by Allah.

Imam Ghazali begins his foundational principles of creed by establishing the absolute sovereignty of Allah over all occurrences. This lesson examines his first principle, which affirms that every event in existence is Allah's creation, directly countering the Mu'tazilite view of human self-agency. Through Quranic proof and logical reasoning, Ghazali sets the bedrock of Sunni Aqidah.

Guided Path
1
Read the source carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation once to get the overall meaning. Notice how Imam Ghazali states his principle clearly and then elaborates.
The passage includes footnotes that expand on the types of events and the opposing view.
2
Define 'every event'
Identify what Ghazali includes under 'every event': substances, accidents, voluntary and involuntary actions. This shows the comprehensive scope of Allah's creation.
Even subtle movements like a hair's motion are included.
3
Understand the Quranic proof
Reflect on how the verse 'Allah is the Creator of all things' (39:62) is used. Ghazali argues that since it is a verse of praise, it must encompass everything; otherwise the praise would be incomplete.
This logical point refutes the Mu'tazilite claim that humans create their own actions.

Quranic Foundation: Allah Creates All Actions
Ghazali cites Quranic verses to prove Allah creates human actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Ghazali uses Quranic verses to establish that Allah is the creator of human actions and that His creation is evidence of His knowledge.

Core Idea
The Quran declares that Allah creates both you and your actions.

The debate over human free will and divine creation has deep roots in Islamic theology. Imam al-Ghazali begins his argument by turning directly to the Quran, citing two powerful verses. These verses not only affirm that Allah creates human beings but also that He creates what they do. Additionally, they link Allah's knowledge of all secrets to His act of creation, providing a foundation for the broader kalam argument.

Commentary
This passage presents Quranic proofs and a logical argument for the creation of human actions by Allah. It begins by quoting two verses: 'And Allah created you and what you do' (37:96) and the final part of Surah Al-Mulk (67:13-14) which highlights Allah's knowledge of all secrets and His creation. The passage then explains that these verses command caution in words and actions because Allah knows all, and He deduces His knowledge from His creation. The core logical argument follows: Allah's power (qudra) is complete and related to the movements of human bodies. Since all movements are similar in essence (mithmāthila), and divine power is attached to them equally, if Allah created only some movements, others would not exist, which is impossible; thus, He must create all. This leads to the conclusion that all creation belongs exclusively to Allah. The passage refutes any notion that humans independently create their actions.

The Second Principle: That God alone creates the movements of servants does not remove them from being within the capability of servants by way of acquisition. (The plural of 'servant'; here it means every event that occurs within one's power, a voluntary act from humans, jinn, or angels.) As for the movement, it is a creation of the Lord, a description of the servant, and an acquisition for him. How could it be pure compulsion when man necessarily perceives the difference between a voluntary movement and an involuntary tremor? Or how could it be a creation of the servant when he does not encompass knowledge of the details of the parts of acquired movements and their preparation? And when both extremes are invalid, nothing remains but moderation in belief: that it is an object of power by God's power as invention, and by the servant's power in another mode of relation expressed as acquisition. And it is not necessary for the relation of power to the object of power to be only invention, because God's power in pre-eternity was related to the world without invention occurring through it, and it is by acquisition.

Commentary
This passage is a statement and explanation of the second principle of Ash'ari theology concerning human action and divine creation. Imam al-Ghazali begins by asserting that God's unique creation of human movements does not negate their being within human capability by way of acquisition (kash). Rather, God creates both the power and its object, both choice and the chosen. Then he distinguishes: power is an attribute of the servant but created by God, it is not the servant's acquisition. Movement, however, is created by God, is an attribute of the servant, and is acquired by him. He argues that if actions were pure compulsion, man would not perceive a difference between voluntary movement and involuntary tremor (like a shaking hand); thus there is genuine human capability. If actions were created by the servant, he would have to know all details of the act's parts, which he does not. So both extremes (jabr and qadr) fail. The moderate position is that actions are created by God (invention) and also related to human power in another mode called acquisition. This is based on the principle that non-existence is nothing, so only God can specify actions. The footnotes elaborate on these points, including the famous saying "more subtle than Ash'ari's kash."

Commentary
This passage presents an objection and its answer. The objection is: If all actions are created and willed by God, how can He forbid what He wills (sin) and command what He does not will (faith for those who disbelieve)? The answer: The command (amr) is distinct from the will (irada). Ghazali provides an analogy: a master strikes his slave, the sultan rebukes him, so the master claims the slave rebelled. To prove it, he commands the slave in front of the sultan to do something, but he actually wills that the slave disobeys, so that the disobedience shows the slave's rebelliousness, thus proving the master's excuse. This shows that commanding what one does not will is possible.

Fifth Principle: It Is Permissible for God to Burden Beyond Capacity
Ghazali states the Ash'ari principle that God may burden beyond ability, against the Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari position that God may burden creatures beyond their capacity and the proof from the Qur'anic verse.

Core Idea
God's sovereignty means He can command what He wills, even beyond human capacity.

This lesson introduces a key Ash'ari principle: God is not limited by human notions of fairness when imposing obligations. Ghazali uses a powerful argument from the Qur'an—the very prayer asking for relief proves that such burdening is possible. Let's explore this reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its literal translation to get the gist of Ghazali's argument.
Notice the Qur'anic citation at the end.
2
Identify the two positions
Identify the Ash'ari claim ('it is permissible') and the Mu'tazila opposition. Ghazali is defending the Ash'ari view.
The Mu'tazila argue it would be unjust.
3
Understand the proof from the Qur'an
Ghazali argues that if burdening beyond capacity were impossible, then asking for relief in the Qur'an would be meaningless. The very act of asking implies the possibility.
This is a logical argument based on the verse's implication.
4
Reflect on theological implications
Consider how this principle upholds God's absolute sovereignty and the Ash'ari emphasis on divine will over human notions of justice.
This distinguishes Ash'ari from Mu'tazila theology.

Commentary
This passage introduces the Ash'ari position that God can impose obligations beyond human capacity. The Mu'tazila deny this, arguing it is unjust. Ghazali gives a proof: the Qur'anic verse where believers pray 'Do not burden us beyond our capacity' (Q 2:286) would be meaningless if such burdening were impossible. The fact that they asked for relief implies it is possible to be burdened beyond capacity. The argument relies on the logical contradiction of asking for something that is already impossible.
