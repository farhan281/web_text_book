The Parable of Valleys and Hearts (1)
Ghazali explains the Quranic parable of valleys and hearts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Quranic parable of valleys and hearts and how it illustrates the relationship between the Quran, knowledge, and the purification of hearts from disbelief and hypocrisy.

Core Idea
The Quran purifies hearts like water washes away foam.

In this lesson, Imam al-Ghazali unpacks a profound Quranic analogy, showing how the Quran and knowledge act like a torrent that cleanses hearts of falsehood. He explains the different capacities of hearts and how guidance settles while doubts are cast away, illustrating this with two vivid examples from nature and metallurgy.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation to get the flow of the parable.
Notice how Ghazali uses the imagery of valleys and water.
2
Identify the three types of hearts
Look for the three categories of hearts (valleys) that Ghazali describes: those that bear much, little, or nothing. Understand what each represents.
The wide valley is the receptive heart; the small valley is limited; the valley with 'qaba'an' is completely closed.
3
Understand the symbolism of foam
Foam represents disbelief, hypocrisy, and false doubts. Notice how the Quran extracts this foam and how it does not settle in the heart.
The foam floats and is cast away, like falsehood that cannot remain in the presence of truth.
4
Contemplate the second parable (fire and metals)
Read how fire purifies metals from dross. Reflect on how the Quran burns away the heart's impurities, leaving pure guidance.
Both parables reinforce the same point: the Quran distinguishes truth from falsehood.
5
Reflect on the closing Quranic verse
End by reflecting on the verse: '…none understand them except the knowledgeable.' This invites the learner to seek deeper understanding.
Consider what it means to be among 'the knowledgeable'.

This is the Quran(1) which He sent down to His Messenger, so the comparison is for what is obtained from each of them of life and the interests of servants in their livelihood and afterlife, and the meaning of 'valleys' is the hearts. Commentary
This passage is an explanation of a Quranic parable and a warning against innovation. Ghazali begins by identifying the Quran itself as the subject of the parable, noting that the comparison is about the life and benefits it brings. He then explains that 'valleys' are the hearts, and there are three types: some hearts bear much knowledge (like a wide valley), some bear little (like a small valley), and some bear nothing (like a valley that cannot hold water). The 'foam' represents disbelief and hypocrisy and false doubts that float on the surface. The Quran and knowledge extract this foam, just as a torrent brings out foam. The foam does not settle; similarly, falsehood does not last in the heart once knowledge removes it. The 'guidance that benefits people' (clear water) remains and settles in the heart. Ghazali then extends the parable to the second example of fire purifying metals—gold, silver, etc.—where fire extracts dross and leaves pure substance. Similarly, Quranic verses burn away the heart's impurities. After expounding the parable, Ghazali warns against those who delve into figurative interpretation (ta'wil) of eschatological matters like the Scale and Path, declaring it an innovation (bid'a) not transmitted from reliable authorities. He insists on literal application, thus closing the door to such interpretations.

The Warning Against Figurative Interpretation of Eschatological Matters (2)
Ghazali condemns figurative interpretation of eschatological matters as innovation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali considers figurative interpretation (ta'wil) of eschatological matters a reprehensible innovation and why literal interpretation is necessary.

Core Idea
Innovation in religion begins with abandoning the literal meaning of revelation.

After explaining the parable, Ghazali turns to a critical methodological warning. He condemns a group of innovators who overstepped bounds by interpreting eschatological descriptions—like the Scale and Path—figuratively. He declares this a forbidden innovation not transmitted from reliable scholars and insists on taking them literally.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to grasp the warning.
Pay attention to the strong language: 'بدعة قبيحة' (reprehensible innovation).
2
Identify what is being interpreted figuratively
The innovators interpreted matters of the Hereafter such as the Scale (ميزان) and the Path (صراط) figuratively. Note these examples.
These are specific eschatological objects mentioned in Quran and hadith.
3
Understand Ghazali's reasoning
Ghazali gives two reasons for his condemnation: (1) such interpretations are not transmitted from reliable authorities, and (2) literal interpretation is not impossible (so there is no need for ta'wil).
The second reason is crucial: if literal meaning is possible, we must stick to it.
4
Grasp the conclusion
Therefore, it is obligatory (يجب) to apply these texts literally, and this closes the door to such figurative interpretations.
This is a methodological rule in dealing with eschatological passages.

This is the Quran(1) which He sent down to His Messenger, so the comparison is for what is obtained from each of them of life and the interests of servants in their livelihood and afterlife, and the meaning of 'valleys' is the hearts. And that some of them bear much (2) because of its spaciousness like a great valley that holds much water, and some of them little (3) like a small valley that holds only a little water, and some of them do not bear (4) anything like a valley that has [؟]—this is a parable that Allah Almighty struck for the Quran and knowledge when their sweetness intermixes with the hearts. And the foam is like disbelief and hypocrisy (5) and false doubts, so they float on the surface of the heart, for the Quran or knowledge extracts that foam just as the torrent extracts from the valley foam that rises above the water, and He informed that it rises and floats on the water. And the guidance that benefits people remains (7)—it remains in the heart and settles, just as pure water settles in the valley and the foam goes away as scum. Then Allah Almighty struck for that another parable, saying: 'And from that which they kindle in fire, seeking adornment or utility, there is foam like it'—meaning that what the sons of Adam kindle of gold, silver, copper, and iron, their dross emerges—that is the foam which fire casts out from that substance due to its mixing, for it is cast and thrown away, and the pure substance alone settles. And He struck a parable for what is in it of life, cooling, and benefit, and a parable with fire for what is in it of illumination, radiance, and burning. So the verses of the Quran give life to hearts as water gives life to the earth, and burn their dross, doubts, desires, and grudges as fire burns what is thrown into it and distinguishes its foam from its [؟] as fire distinguishes dross from gold, silver, copper, and the like. This is some of the lesson and knowledge in this great parable. Allah Almighty said: 'And such parables We set forth for people, and none understand them except the knowledgeable.' And in this section a group (8) of innovators delved deep and transgressed the limits, so they interpreted what has come about the Hereafter—the Scale, the Path, and others—and that is an innovation (9). And this interpretation which they made regarding such matters is a reprehensible innovation, not held by any of the reliable authorities, since that was not transmitted by way of narration, and applying it literally is not impossible. So it is necessary to apply it literally (1), and thereby the door of interpretations in such matters is closed.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of a Quranic parable and a warning against innovation. Ghazali begins by identifying the Quran itself as the subject of the parable, noting that the comparison is about the life and benefits it brings. He then explains that 'valleys' are the hearts, and there are three types: some hearts bear much knowledge (like a wide valley), some bear little (like a small valley), and some bear nothing (like a valley that cannot hold water). The 'foam' represents disbelief and hypocrisy and false doubts that float on the surface. The Quran and knowledge extract this foam, just as a torrent brings out foam. The foam does not settle; similarly, falsehood does not last in the heart once knowledge removes it. The 'guidance that benefits people' (clear water) remains and settles in the heart. Ghazali then extends the parable to the second example of fire purifying metals—gold, silver, etc.—where fire extracts dross and leaves pure substance. Similarly, Quranic verses burn away the heart's impurities. After expounding the parable, Ghazali warns against those who delve into figurative interpretation (ta'wil) of eschatological matters like the Scale and Path, declaring it an innovation (bid'a) not transmitted from reliable authorities. He insists on literal application, thus closing the door to such interpretations.

The Analogy of Written Names: Understanding Signs and Realities
Ghazali explains scriptural presence through the analogy of names and essences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali uses the analogy of written names to clarify that the presence of the Prophet in earlier scriptures is through indication, not physical inclusion.

Core Idea
Written names are signs, not the things themselves.

When we read that the Prophet is 'written' in the Torah and Gospel, we might mistakenly think he is physically present in those books. Ghazali clarifies this by drawing a simple yet profound analogy: just as the name 'fire' on paper does not burn, so too does scriptural mention only indicate, not contain. Let's explore this analogy step by step.

Pay attention to the logical flow from example to conclusion.

The passage says it means 'indication' (دلالة), not literal presence.

Commentary
This passage is an analogical explanation to refute a potential misunderstanding about the Quran being created in its physical form. Ghazali argues that just as the name of fire written on paper does not cause the paper to burn, the names of things in a book do not carry the actual properties of those things. Similarly, Paradise and Hell are mentioned in the Quran, but no one believes they are literally inside the book. The same applies to the Prophet being mentioned in the Torah and Gospel: he is not physically present in those scriptures, but they contain indications of him. The point is to distinguish between the created physical script and the eternal meaning it signifies.
