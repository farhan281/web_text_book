Tawhid al-Uluhiyya is the Reality of Islam
Ghazali defines tawhid al-uluhiyya as the reality of Islam, linking it to the five pillars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how tawhid al-uluhiyya (the oneness of divinity and worship) is the reality of Islam, entailing the first two types of tawhid and being manifested in the five pillars.

Core Idea
Tawhid al-uluhiyya is the heart of Islam; the five pillars are its visible expression.

This lesson presents Ghazali’s definition of tawhid al-uluhiyya and his argument that it is the essence of acceptable Islam. He ties it directly to the famous hadith of the five pillars, showing that the religion is built on acts of worship. As you read, notice how Ghazali moves from the concept of tawhid to the practical foundation of Islamic practice.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow, then read the literal translation. Pay attention to key terms like 'توحيد الإلهية والعبادة' and 'الأركان الخمسة'.
The passage begins with 'فهذا هو توحيد الإلهية والعبادة'.
2
Identify Ghazali's key claim
Notice that Ghazali states this tawhid entails the first two types and is the reality of the acceptable religion of Islam. This is his central thesis.
3
Examine the hadith proof
Read how Ghazali uses the hadith of the five pillars to support his claim. The pillars are actions, so Islam is essentially worship and sincerity.
The hadith is cited from Bukhari and Muslim.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Ghazali concludes that Islam is worship of God alone by doing commanded actions and avoiding prohibitions, with sincerity exclusively for God.
Think about how this connects belief with practice.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and exposition of tawhid al-uluhiyya (the oneness of divinity/worship) and its centrality to Islam. The author first states that this type of tawhid entails and includes the other two types (presumably tawhid al-rububiyya and tawhid al-asma' wa al-sifat). Then he explains that the good news of reward for the people of tawhid and the warning of punishment for the people of shirk are both recompense related to tawhid. Next, he declares that this tawhid is the very reality of the religion of Islam that God accepts. He supports this with the famous hadith of the five pillars, which shows that the entire structure of Islam is based on these acts, which are worship. From this, Ghazali concludes that Islam is purely worshiping God alone, doing what He commands and avoiding what He forbids, with sincerity exclusively for God. The verse 'Ala Allah al-din al-khalis' (Quran 39:3) is alluded to. The argument is that the five pillars are actions of worship, so Islam is essentially worship and sincerity.

The Shahada: The Key to Islam and Tawhid
Ghazali explains the function of the shahada and the implication of its first part for tawhid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the shahada is the key to Islam and how its first part, the testimony of God's oneness, encompasses perfect belief in tawhid from the aspects of lordship and divinity.

Core Idea
The shahada is the entry point to Islam and contains the entire belief in God within its first part.

Imam al-Ghazali explains that the shahada is more than a verbal formula—it is the title that signifies the realization of beliefs and the key to entering Islam. He then shows how the first part, 'la ilaha illa Allah,' implicitly includes the two fundamental aspects of tawhid: rububiyya (lordship) and uluhiyya (divinity). This lesson unpacks that profound connection.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the role of the shahada
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how Ghazali presents the shahada as the 'title' and 'key' to Islam.
Pay attention to the phrase 'عنوان تحقق هذه العقائد'.
2
Identify the two aspects of tawhid
Ghazali mentions 'جهتى الربوبية والالوهية'. Understand what each term means: rububiyya is lordship (creation, sustenance), uluhiyya is divinity (worship).
These are the two main categories of tawhid.
3
Reflect on the implication
Ask yourself: How does simply saying 'La ilaha illa Allah' imply belief in both God's lordship and His exclusive right to worship? Consider the logical connection.
This shows the depth of the shahada beyond mere utterance.

The Muqarraboon: Those Brought Near
Description of the muqarraboon rank in tawheed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the stage of the muqarraboon (those brought near) in tawheed, how they perceive God's signs and then witness Him with the heart.

Core Idea
The muqarraboon see God through the heart after seeing His signs in creation.

Imam Ghazali classifies the higher ranks of tawheed, beginning with the muqarraboon. These are people who have reached the third station, where they clearly see the contingency of creation and the need for God, and then go on to behold God with the eye of faith. Their nearness has degrees depending on certainty and purity of heart.

Guided Path
1
Read the description of the muqarraboon

The passage emphasizes that they see with the 'faith of their hearts' and 'unseen of their souls'.

Look for three stages: seeing the mark of contingency, witnessing neediness, and then seeing God. Write them down.
These stages show a movement from creation to the Creator.
3
Understand the graded nature of nearness

This shows that even within a high station, there is variation.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the higher ranks of tawheed (divine unity) and a ruling on what should be taught to the common people. Ghazali divides those who achieve realization of tawheed into four categories: the muqarraboon (those brought near), the siddiqoon (the truthful), and then he discusses the muridoon (seekers) and muradoon (the sought). He begins with the muqarraboon, who occupy the third station. These individuals perceive the mark of contingency in created things clearly, witness the states of neediness towards God, and see all things as indicating tawheed. Then they see God with the 'eye' of faith in their hearts and behold Him with the 'unseen' of their souls, observing His majesty and beauty. Yet they still have degrees of nearness depending on their certainty and heart purity. Next, the siddiqoon occupy the fourth rank. They first see God, then they see things after that, but they no longer see anything other than God in the two abodes (this world and the hereafter) or in existence; everything is seen as Him or from His perspective—a state of annihilation in tawheed. Ghazali then explains that the muridoon (seekers of God) usually must first settle in the third rank (the tawheed of the muqarraboon) before crossing to the fourth. In contrast, the muradoon (those chosen by God) generally begin directly at the fourth rank and are established in it. Finally, Ghazali gives practical guidance: the common people (al-`awamm) should not be taught more than what was mentioned in the concise creed (likely earlier in the book), as that suffices and deters them from harmful confusion. He defines 'common people' broadly: anyone who is not characterized by the attributes of sincere seekers—i.e., those who do not devote themselves to acquiring deep knowledge, are not free from worldly desires and fanaticism, and do not avoid boasting about knowledge before others.

It then quotes Ibn al-Humam's explication: knowledge of His existence is easy, and the divine matters discussed about God are of three types – what is necessary (wajib), what is impossible (mustahil), and what is possible (ja'iz) for Him. Focusing on the necessary, it notes that God has twenty attributes. The question arises: are His attributes limited to these twenty? The correct view is that they are dependent on His perfections, and His perfections are infinite. However, we are not accountable for what no evidence is set for us. The meaning of 'infinite perfections' is then analyzed: it could be from our perspective (due to limited knowledge), from God's perspective (He knows them as infinite), a linguistic expression for great multitude, or a consideration of the negative and relational attributes which are infinite, while the positive (ma'ani) are finite. Thus, all attributes are considered infinite when combining the finite positive with the infinite negative. This passage guards against limiting God's perfections to a finite number.

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'tasleem' (submission) and clarifies its relationship to 'iman' (faith). He explains that two concepts can be mutually exclusive in definition yet mutually entailed in existence, and he refines the expression to 'every iman entails Islam, but not vice versa.' He also addresses the condition of verbal utterance for the validity of iman.
