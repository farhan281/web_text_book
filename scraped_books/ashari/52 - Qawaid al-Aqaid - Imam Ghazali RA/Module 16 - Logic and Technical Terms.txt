Imam al-Shafi'i (d. 204 AH) is known for his strong stance against speculative theology (kalam). Metaphor or Literal? Decoding the Hadith of the Donkey's Head
Ghazali explains a hadith metaphorically using form-meaning distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Ghazali uses the distinction between form (surah) and meaning (ma'na) to explain a prophetic hadith that might seem to imply physical transformation.

Core Idea
Hadith threats are often metaphorical — look for the intended meaning, not the form.

This passage from Imam al-Ghazali's Qawa'id al-'Aqa'id tackles a challenging hadith: the Prophet (peace be upon him) said that one who raises his head before the imam will have his head turned into a donkey's head. Taken literally, this seems impossible. Ghazali explains that the hadith is metaphorical, pointing to the foolishness of such an act. He introduces a key principle: understanding when scriptural texts are to be interpreted figuratively, determined by rational and transmitted proofs. Let's see how he unpacks it.

Guided Path
1

Notice the concise style of Imam al-Ghazali.
2
Identify surah vs ma'na
The author contrasts 'by form' and 'by meaning'. Understand that the hadith is not about physical change but about the underlying characteristic.
This distinction is central to metaphorical interpretation.
3
Understand the characteristic
The donkey's head is associated with the characteristic (khasisah) of stupidity and foolishness. Relate this to the act of raising one's head before the imam.
4
Grasp the contradiction
The author points out the contradiction between following the imam and preceding him. This shows why the act is foolish.
This logical point supports the metaphorical reading.
5
Recognize the proofs
The final sentence states that such inner meanings are known by rational or transmitted proofs. This sets up the broader discussion in the text.
Think about how you would use reason or scripture to confirm this interpretation.

The reason is that it is foolish to follow the imam and yet precede him, as these are contradictory. Then the author states that this inner meaning being contrary to the literal wording is known either by rational proof or transmitted proof, setting up the subsequent discussion.

Movement: Created by Allah, Acquired by the Servant
Imam Ghazali proves that human movement is created by Allah and acquired by the servant, refuting jabr and qadariyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari doctrine of kasb and how Imam Ghazali refutes both compulsion (jabr) and human creation (qadariyya).

Core Idea
Human action is Allah's creation and the servant's acquisition—an act of moderation between two extremes.

Imam Ghazali addresses one of the most debated questions in kalam: the nature of human acts. He affirms both divine creation and human responsibility through the concept of kasb. In this passage, he systematically refutes two erroneous extremes and establishes the middle path.

Guided Path
1
Understand the three aspects of movement
Notice that Ghazali attributes movement to Allah as creation (khalq), to the servant as description (wasf) and acquisition (kasb). Grasp that movement is created by Allah but related to the servant's power through kasb.
Kasb does not mean the servant creates the movement.
2
Refutation of pure compulsion (jabr)
Ghazali argues that if movement were pure compulsion, the servant would not perceive the difference between voluntary movement and involuntary tremor. Since he does perceive it, jabr is false.
3
Refutation of human creation (qadariyya)
If the servant created his own movement, he would need comprehensive knowledge of its details. He lacks that, so creation by the servant is impossible.
4
Establishment of kasb and two modes of power
The only remaining position is that movement is created by Allah's power (ikhtiraa') and related to the servant's power in another mode, called kasb. Allah's power relates without invention (as in pre-eternity) and with invention (when creating).
This shows power relation is not limited to invention.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
وأما الحركة فخلق للرب تعالى ووصف للعبد وكسب له، فإنها خلقت مقدورة بقدرة هي وصفه وكانت للحركة نسبة إلى صفة أخرى تسمى قدرة، بإعتبارها تلك النسبة كسباً. وكيف تكون جبراً محضاً وهو بالضرورة يدرك التفرقة بين الحركة المقدورة والرعدة الضرورية؟ أو كيف يكون خلقاً للعبد وهو لا يحيط علمًا بتفاصيل اجزاء الحركات المكتسبة وإعدادها، وإذا بطل الطرفان لم يبق إلا الإقتصاد في الإعتقاد، وهو أنها مقدورة بقدرة الله تعالى اختراعاً، وبقدرة العبد على وجه آخر من التعلق يعبر عنه. وليس من ضرورة تعلق القدرة بالمقدور أن يكون بالإختراع فقط، إذ قدرة الله تعالى في الأزل قد كانت متعلقة بالعالم ولم يكن الإختراع حاصلاً بها، وهي عند الإختراع متعلقة به نوعاً آخر من التعلق، فيه يظهر أن تعلق القدرة ليس مخصوصاً بحصول المقدور بها.
As for movement, it is creation (khalq) by the Lord, description (wasf) for the servant, and acquisition (kasb) for him. For it is created as being capable by a power which is His description, and for the movement there is a relation to another attribute called power; by virtue of that relation it is deemed acquisition. How can it be pure compulsion (jabr) when he perceives by necessity the difference between the capable movement and the involuntary tremor? Or how can it be creation by the servant when he does not comprehensively know the details of the parts of acquired movements and their preparations? And when both extremes are nullified, nothing remains but moderation in belief, which is that it is capable by the power of Allah Almighty by invention (ikhtiraa'), and by the power of the servant in another mode of relation which is expressed (by kasb). And it is not a necessity of the relation of power to the capable that it be solely by invention, for the power of Allah in pre-eternity had been related to the world while invention was not actualized by it, and it (the power) at the time of invention is related to it in another mode of relation, in which it appears that the relation of power is not restricted to the actual occurrence of the capable by it.
Source pages p. 196

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the Ash'ari doctrine of kasb (acquisition). The author begins by stating that movement (al-haraka) has three aspects: it is creation (khalq) by Allah, a description (wasf) for the servant, and an acquisition (kasb) for the servant. He then explains that movement is created as an object of power by Allah's attribute of power. However, movement also has a relation to another attribute called power—that is, the servant's power—and by virtue of that relation, it is called acquisition (kasb). The author then refutes two extreme views. First, if movement were pure compulsion (jabr), then the servant would not perceive by necessity the difference between a voluntary capable movement and an involuntary tremor (like a shivering hand). This self-perception disproves pure compulsion. Second, if movement were created by the servant himself, then the servant would have to have comprehensive knowledge of all the details and preparations of his movements, which he does not—so creation by the servant is impossible. Having refuted both extremes, the only remaining position is moderation (iqtisad): the movement is capable by Allah's power through invention (ikhtiraa'), and by the servant's power through another mode of relation that is called kasb. The author then argues that it is not necessary for the relation (ta'alluq) of power to the capable to be only through invention. He gives the example of Allah's power in pre-eternity: it was related to the world even though invention had not yet occurred. Similarly, even when invention occurs, Allah's power is related to the created thing in another mode of relation. This proves that the relation of power is not restricted to the actual production of the thing (i.e., invention); there can be a relation without direct creation, which is the kasb relation.

The Two Meanings of Obligatory and the Refutation of the Mu'tazila
Ghazali defines 'obligatory' and refutes the Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two distinct meanings of 'obligatory' (al-wajib) in Islamic theology and how Ghazali uses this distinction to refute the Mu'tazila argument that creation is obligatory upon God.

Core Idea
Obligatory has two meanings: avoiding harm or logical necessity. Only the latter applies to God.

In this passage, Imam al-Ghazali tackles a critical theological dispute with the Mu'tazila by first clarifying the term 'obligatory'. He shows that the term has two distinct meanings—one related to avoiding harm, the other to logical necessity—and then demonstrates that the Mu'tazila claim about creation being obligatory on God fails unless they intend the second meaning, which actually supports the Ash'ari position. This careful semantic analysis is a hallmark of kalam reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the two definitions
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice that Ghazali presents two (or three) meanings of 'obligatory'. Identify them.
2

The first meaning involves harm (deferred or immediate), while the second involves impossibility of non-existence. Grasp the difference.
3
See how Ghazali applies the definitions to the Mu'tazila claim
Ghazali takes the opponent's claim that creation is obligatory on God and tests it against each meaning. Observe the logic.
4
Reflect on why this distinction matters
Consider how this argument protects God from being subject to harm and affirms the necessity of creation given God's prior knowledge.
This connects to the Ash'ari position on divine attributes and volition.

والمراد بالواجب أحد أمرين: أما الفعل الذي في تركه ضرر، أما أجل كما يقال: يجب على العبد أن يطيع لا يعذبه في الآخرة بالنار. أو ضرر عاجل كما يقال: يجب على العطشان أن يشرب حتى لا يموت. وأما أن يراد به الذي يؤدي عدمه إلى محال، كما يقال: وجود المعلوم واجب، إذ عدمه يؤدي إلى محال وهو أن يصير العلم جهلا. فإن أراد الخصم بأن الخلق واجب على الله بالمعنى الأول فقد عرضه للضرر، وإن أراد به المعنى الثاني فهو مسلم، إذ بعد سبق العلم لا بد من وجود المعلوم، وإن أراد به معنى ثالثا فهو غير مفهوم.
And what is meant by 'obligatory' is one of two things: either the action whose abandonment involves harm, either deferred as it is said: The servant must obey so that Allah does not punish him in the hereafter with fire. Or immediate harm as it is said: The thirsty person must drink so that he does not die. Or it is meant by it that whose non-existence leads to an impossibility, as it is said: The existence of the known is obligatory, for its non-existence leads to an impossibility which is that knowledge would become ignorance. If the opponent intends that creation is obligatory upon God in the first sense, then he has exposed Him to harm; and if he intends the second sense, then it is accepted, for after the precedence of knowledge, the existence of the known is inevitable; and if he intends a third meaning, then it is unintelligible.
Source pages p. 202

Commentary
This passage provides a precise definition of the term 'obligatory' (واجب) to clarify the dispute with the Mu'tazila. Ghazali explains that 'obligatory' has two primary meanings (though he mentions three): (1) an action whose omission entails harm, either deferred (like punishment in the afterlife) or immediate (like death from thirst); (2) something whose non-existence would be impossible (such as the existence of something known if knowledge of it has already been decreed). He then applies these definitions to the claim that creation is obligatory upon God. If the opponent means the first sense (harm-related), that would imply God is subject to harm, which is impossible. If the opponent means the second sense (logical necessity from prior knowledge), then it is accepted: since God's knowledge precedes creation, creation must occur. If the opponent means any other sense, it is unintelligible and thus invalid. Thus, the Mu'tazila argument fails.

Refuting Obligatory Benefit: Ghazali's Argument
Ghazali rejects obligatory benefit with logic and reductio.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali argues that God cannot be obligated to act for the benefit of His servants.

Core Idea
Obligation only makes sense where harm or benefit can be affected—God is beyond both.

Ghazali begins his refutation by exposing the logical flaw in the claim that God must consider His servants' benefit. He points out that 'obligation' implies potential harm if unmet, which is impossible for God. Then he presents a reductio: if God were obligated to benefit, He would have created everyone in Paradise immediately, not in a world of trial—so why didn't He?

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the passage once to get the overall argument. Notice the two distinct parts: the logical argument and the reductio.
Pay attention to the phrase 'كلام فاسد' (corrupt speech) as a strong opening.
2
Identify the two premises
First, Ghazali states that obligation implies potential harm (if not fulfilled). Second, he argues that if God were obligated to benefit, He would create all in Paradise. Find these in the text.

Ghazali states and defends the fifth principle with two proofs and answers an objection.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This principle tackles a major debate: can God require what humans cannot do? Ghazali defends the Sunni view with two arguments. Ghazali clarifies a common linguistic usage: when someone says 'إن شاء الله' in response to a statement about death or recovery, it often signals a wish or hope, not uncertainty. This lesson explores two examples to show how the phrase shifts from doubting to desiring.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow, then read the literal translation. Notice the key terms رغبة (desire) and تشكيك (doubt).
2
Identify the two examples
Find the two scenarios: someone's quick death and someone's recovery. Observe how Ghazali contrasts doubt vs. desire in each response.
3
Understand the shift in meaning
Note the word 'معدولة' (diverted) – the phrase has been redirected from its original sense of doubt to indicate desire. Also note the added notion of politeness (تأدب).
4
Reflect on the broader lesson
Consider how this affects your understanding of 'إن شاء الله' in daily speech. Ask yourself: what does a speaker intend when saying it in different contexts?

And this wording indicates it, so that the usage became known as an expression of desire and wishing. So if it is said to you: 'So-and-so will die quickly,' we say: 'If Allah wills,' and from it is understood your desire, not your doubt. And if it is said to you: 'So-and-so will recover from his illness and get well,' you say: 'If Allah wills,' meaning desire. So the word has been diverted from the meaning of doubt to the meaning of desire, and likewise the diversion to the meaning of politeness in mentioning Allah, exalted, in whatever manner the matter is.

Commentary
This passage explains the second usage: 'إن شاء الله' can be used to express one's wish or desire. For example, when told someone will die soon, saying 'إن شاء الله' conveys that you desire it (perhaps out of mercy) not that you doubt the inevitability. Similarly, when told someone will recover, saying 'إن شاء الله' expresses your hope. The author states that the word has been shifted (معدولة) from its original meaning of doubt to the meaning of desire, and also to the meaning of politeness.

Hearts Four: Faith and Hypocrisy
Ghazali relates a hadith classifying hearts, focusing on the mixed heart.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the classification of hearts according to a hadith of Abu Sa'id al-Khudri, particularly the state of a heart that contains both faith and hypocrisy.

Core Idea
A heart can contain both faith and hypocrisy, like a plant and a wound competing for dominance.

In this lesson, we examine a hadith that classifies hearts into four types, but focuses on the mixed heart—one that contains both faith and hypocrisy. Through a powerful metaphor, the Prophet ﷺ illustrates how these two forces coexist and compete within a single heart.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith
Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation carefully. Notice the fourfold classification and the description of the first two types.
The hadith mentions four types but details only two; the other two are implied.
2
Understand the metaphor
Focus on the second heart: faith is likened to a plant watered by sweet water, hypocrisy to a wound fed by pus. Grasp how these images describe a spiritual struggle.
The variant wording emphasizes that whichever dominates 'carries him away'.
3
Reflect on dominance
Think about what it means for faith or hypocrisy to dominate. How does this apply to a person's spiritual state and actions?
This hadith shows that a person is not simply good or bad; there can be an internal conflict.

وفي حديث ابي سعيد الخدري القلوب أربعة : قلب أجرد وفيه سراج يزهر ، فذلك قلب المؤمن ، وقلب مصفح فيه إيمان ونفاق فمثل الإيمان فيه كمثل البقلة يمدها الماء العذب ، ومثل النفاق فيه كمثل القرحة يمدها القيح والصديد، فأي المادتين غلب عليه حكم له بها . وفي لفظ آخر ( غلبت عليه ذهبت به)
And in the hadith of Abu Sa'id al-Khudri: 'Hearts are four: a heart that is bare and in it is a lamp that shines—that is the heart of the believer; and a heart that is coated in which there is faith and hypocrisy—the likeness of faith in it is like a plant that sweet water irrigates, and the likeness of hypocrisy in it is like a sore that pus and matter irrigate; whichever of the two substances predominates, judgment goes with it.' And in another wording: 'whichever predominates, it carries him away.'
Source pages p. 276

Commentary
This passage is a classification of hearts into four types based on a hadith from Abu Sa'id al-Khudri. The first type is a bare heart (qalb ajrad) with a shining lamp—symbolizing pure faith and enlightenment. The second is a coated heart (qalb muṣaffaḥ) that contains both faith and hypocrisy. This mixed state is illustrated with a metaphor: faith is like a plant nourished by sweet water, and hypocrisy is like a wound fed by pus and blood. Whichever substance dominates determines the person's state. The variant adds that whichever dominates 'carries him away', indicating that the person is overtaken by that quality. This classification shows that faith and hypocrisy can coexist in a heart, but they are in constant conflict, and the outcome depends on which is stronger.
