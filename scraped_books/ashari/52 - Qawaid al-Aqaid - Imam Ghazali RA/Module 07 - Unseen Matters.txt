Eschatological Certainties: From Death to the Vision of God
Enumeration of certain eschatological events from death to the vision of God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the series of eschatological events that are certain to occur, from death and the grave to the vision of God in the Hereafter.

Core Idea
The afterlife is certain: death, resurrection, judgment, and seeing Allah.

Imam al-Ghazali now enumerates the beliefs whose occurrence is certain (yatawaqqanu wurūduhu). These are not speculative but are essential articles of faith. This passage covers the transition from this world to the next, starting with the inevitability of death and the trial of the grave, leading up to the believers' vision of Allah in Paradise.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the translation. Let the author's rhythm come through before interpreting.
The passage uses a repetitive structure: 'أن...' (that...).
2
Identify the sequence of beliefs
List each belief mentioned: fana' of world, death, grave trial, resurrection, reckoning, scale, bridge, pool, paradise/hell, vision of God. Notice the chronological order.
3
Focus on the grave trial
Notice that 'fitna' in the grave includes both trial and reward/punishment. This is a reality that occurs immediately after death.
This is debated among theological schools; Ghazali affirms it as certain.
4
Understand the vision of God
The passage affirms that believers will see Allah with their eyes in the Hereafter. This is a key Sunni belief, grounded in Quran and hadith.
The phrase 'بأبصارهم' emphasizes physical sight.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
أما ما يتيقن وروده فهو : أن تعتقد أن الدنيا فانية ، وكل من عليها فان ، وأن الخلق يفتنون في قبورهم ، وينعمون ويعذبون ، وأن الله تعالى يحشرهم يوم القيامة كما بدأهم يعودون ، وأن الحساب حق ، والميزان حق ، وأن الصراط حق ، وأن الحوض حق ، وأن الأبرار في الجنة في نعيم ، والكفار في النار في جحيم ، وأن المؤمنين يرون الله عز وجل بأبصارهم في الآخرة ، وأن الله تعالى يعذب بالنار من يشاء من أهل الكبائر من المؤمنين ، ويغفر لمن يشاء ، ويخرجهم من النار إلى الجنة بفضل رحمته وشفاعة الأنبياء والصالحين من عباده ، حتى لا يتبقى في جهنم إلا الكافرين . إنَّ اللهَ لا يَغْفِرُ أَن يُشْرَكَ بِهِ وَيَغْفِرُ مَا دُونَ ذَلِكَ لِمَنْ يَشَاءُ
As for what is certainly known to occur, it is that you believe that the world is perishing, and everyone on it is perishing. That the creation will be tried in their graves, and will be given bliss or punishment. That Allah the Exalted will gather them on the Day of Resurrection, as He began them they will return. That the Reckoning is true, the Scale is true, the Bridge is true, the Pool is true. That the righteous are in Paradise in bliss, and the disbelievers are in Hell in a blazing fire. That the believers will see Allah Almighty with their eyes in the Hereafter. That Allah the Exalted will punish with fire whom He wills of the major sinners among the believers, and forgive whom He wills, and will bring them out of the Fire to Paradise by virtue of His mercy and the intercession of the prophets and righteous among His servants, until none remain in Hell except the disbelievers. 'Indeed, Allah does not forgive association with Him, but He forgives what is less than that for whom He wills.'

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration of beliefs whose occurrence is certain (yatawaqqinu wurūduhu). It begins with the temporality of this world and the inevitability of death. It then affirms the trial of the grave (fitnat al-qabr) and its reward or punishment. Next, it affirms bodily resurrection as God will bring creation back as He began it. It then lists the eschatological events: the Reckoning (ḥisāb), the Scale (mīzān), the Bridge (ṣirāṭ), and the Pool (ḥawḍ) as true realities. It affirms that the righteous are in Paradise and the disbelievers in Hell. A key point is the vision of God (ruʾyat Allāh) by the believers in the Hereafter. The passage then addresses the fate of sinful believers (ahl al-kabāʾir): God may punish them in Hell temporarily, but then by His mercy and the intercession of prophets and righteous people, He will bring them out to Paradise. The passage ends by quoting the Quranic verse (4:48) that God does not forgive shirk but forgives lesser sins for whom He wills, indicating the possibility of forgiveness for major sins short of shirk.

Divine Mercy and Intercession for Sinful Believers
Explanation of the fate of major sinners among believers and the role of mercy and intercession.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fate of believers who commit major sins and the role of divine mercy and intercession in their eventual salvation.

Core Idea
Believers' sins may be punished, but mercy and intercession lead to Paradise.

Having affirmed the general states of the righteous and disbelievers, al-Ghazali now addresses the delicate question of believers who die without repenting from major sins. Does their sin exclude them from Paradise? The passage clarifies that such believers may be punished temporarily, but ultimately they will be saved through Allah's mercy and the intercession of prophets and righteous people.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow. Notice the structure: punishment, forgiveness, intercession, then the Quranic verse.
The verse is from Surah an-Nisa 4:48.
2
Identify the key actors
Allah punishes whom He wills, forgives whom He wills, and brings them out by His mercy and the intercession of prophets and righteous people.
Note that intercession is only for believers, not disbelievers.
3
Understand the role of shirk
The Quranic verse establishes that shirk (associating partners with Allah) is the only unforgivable sin if one dies without repentance.
This implies that all sins other than shirk may be forgiven, whether through direct mercy or after punishment.

This world is passing, and the grave is the first stop of the afterlife.

Allah will resurrect everyone, hold them accountable, and weigh their deeds.

Grasp the significance of 'hisab' and 'mizan'
Reckoning is the accounting of all deeds, and the Balance is the just weighing of good and bad deeds.

The Path, the Pond, and the Final Abode
Lists beliefs about the Sirat, Hawd, and final abodes.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
After judgment, the righteous cross the bridge to Paradise, while the wicked fall into Hell.

Ghazali continues the sequence of afterlife events with three more certainties: the Sirat (a bridge over Hell), the Hawd (the Prophet's pond), and the final abodes of Paradise for the righteous and Hell for the disbelievers.

A pool (pond) of the Prophet Muhammad from which believers will drink before entering Paradise.
4

الوجه الثاني التأديب بذكر الله تعالى في كل حال ، وإحالة الأمور كلها إلى مشيئة الله سبحانه ، فقد أدب الله سبحانه نبيه فقال تعالى : وَلَا تَقُولَنَّ لِشَيْءٍ إِنِّي فَاعِلٌ ذَلِكَ غَداً إِلَّا أَن يَشَاءَ اللَّهُ ثم لم يقتصر على ذلك فيما لا يشك فيه ، بل قال تعالى : لَتَدْخُلُنُ الْمَسْجِدَ الْحَرَامَ إِن شَاءَ اللهُ آمِنِينَ مُحَلِّقِينَ رُءُوسَكُمْ وَمُقَصِّرِينَ وكان الله سبحانه عالماً بأنهم يدخلون لا محالة ، وإنه شاءه ، ولكن المقصود تعليمه ذلك ، فتأدب رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم في كل ما كان يخبر عنه معلوماً كان أو مشكوكاً ، حتى قال لما دخل المقابر السلام علیکم دار قوم مؤمنين و إنا إن شاء الله تعالى بكم لاحقون واللحوق بهم غير مشكوك فيه ، ولكن مقتضى الأدب ذكر الله تعالى ، وربط الأمور به، وهذه الصيغة دالة عليه حتى صار يعرف [؟] الإستعمال عبارة عن إظهار الرغبة والتمني ، فإذا قيل لك : إن فلاناً سريعاً [؟] ، فنقول : إن شاء الله ، فيفهم منه رغبتك لا تشكك . وإذا قيل لك : فلان سيزول مرضه ويصح فتقول : إن شاء الله ، بمعنى الرغبة ، فقد صارت الكلمة معدولة عن معنى التشكيك إلى معنى الرغبة ، وكذلك العدول إلى معنى
The second aspect: discipline (adab) by mentioning Allah, may He be exalted, in every situation, and referring all matters to the will of Allah, glorified be He. For Allah, glorified be He, disciplined His Prophet, saying: 'And never say of anything, "Indeed, I will do that tomorrow," except [when adding], "If Allah wills."' Then He did not limit that to what is not doubted, but rather He said: 'You will surely enter the Sacred Mosque, if Allah wills, safe, heads shaved and hair shortened.' And Allah knew that they would enter without doubt, and that He had willed it, but the purpose was to teach him that. So the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, observed adab in everything he reported, whether known or doubtful, until he said when he entered the graves: 'Peace be upon you, abode of believing people, and we, if Allah wills, will join you.' And joining them is not doubtful, but the requirement of adab is to mention Allah and connect matters to Him. And this formula indicates it, until it became known [?] usage is expression of desire and wish. So if it is said to you: 'Indeed so-and-so [will die?] quickly,' we say: 'If Allah wills,' and from it is understood your desire, not doubt. And if it is said to you: 'So-and-so will recover from his illness and be well,' you say: 'If Allah wills,' meaning desire. So the word has been turned from the meaning of doubt to the meaning of desire. And likewise turning to the meaning of...

Commentary
This passage presents the second face (al-wajh al-thani) for the permissibility of istithna. It is about discipline (adab) by mentioning Allah in every situation and referring all matters to His will. The author proves this from the Quran: Allah disciplined His Prophet by commanding him to say 'if Allah wills' when speaking about future actions (Surah al-Kahf 18:23-24). Then Allah did not limit this to doubtful matters; He even used istithna in a certain event (Surah al-Fath 48:27) where He knew they would enter the mosque without doubt. The purpose was to teach this adab. The Prophet followed this adab in all reports, whether certain or doubtful. An example is when he entered the graveyard and said: 'Peace be upon you... and we, if Allah wills, will join you,' even though joining them is certain. The adab requires mentioning Allah and connecting matters to Him. The usage of 'in sha Allah' also expresses desire (raghba) rather than doubt. For instance, if told about someone's speedy death, saying 'if Allah wills' indicates your desire (or hope) not doubt. Similarly, if told someone will recover, saying 'if Allah wills' expresses desire. Thus the word has been turned from doubt to desire. The text ends with 'and likewise turning to the meaning of...' (possibly continuing to another meaning).

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think 'in sha Allah' is only for doubtful future events, and using it for certain events is inappropriate.
Correction: The passage shows that 'in sha Allah' is used even for certain events as an act of adab, and it can also express desire or hope, not just doubt.

Checkpoint

Key Takeaway
The second face of istithna teaches us to mention Allah in all situations, even when certain, as an expression of adab and hope.
