The Seven Eternal Attributes and Clarification of Sight
Ghazali enumerates seven eternal attributes and clarifies sight.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the seven attributes that Allah possesses and why the attribute of sight is specifically defined to avoid anthropomorphism.

Core Idea
Allah's seven attributes are real, eternal, and distinct from His essence.

Imam Ghazali opens the tenth principle by listing the seven essential attributes of Allah. He then immediately clarifies the attribute of sight to prevent any misunderstanding of a physical eye. This foundational list is the basis for all further discussions on divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage listing the seven attributes
Read the Arabic passage carefully. Notice the order: knowledge, life, power, will, speech, hearing, and sight. Each is stated as 'by X' (e.g., knowing by knowledge).
2
Note the repetition in hearing
The text says 'hearing by hearing and hearing by hearing'—a possible scribal duplication. Focus on the list itself.
The duplication does not affect the doctrine.
3
Understand the clarification on sight
The footnote explains that 'sight' is an attribute called sight, not a physical eye. It prevents the misimpression that Allah has a physical eye.
Hearing and sight mean perception, not sense organs.
4
Reflect on the importance of this clarification
Why is it necessary to specify that sight is not a physical eye? Consider the danger of anthropomorphism (tashbih).

The Tenth Principle: That God Most High is knowing by knowledge, living by life, powerful by power, willing by will, speaking by speech, hearing by hearing, and seeing by sight (2), and He has these descriptions from these eternal attributes (3). (2) That is, by an attribute called sight (basar). And one expresses this in sight specifically to prevent the prior misimpression of the eye from the term 'basar'. Therefore more than one of them stated that by hearing and sight is meant the perception itself, not the sense organ. So they affirm an existing essence and existing attributes, which are knowledge, power, will, and states (ahwal) established for the essence by virtue of these attributes subsisting in it—and this is rationally conceivable as attribution. And they refer to that state as 'being knowing' (al-alamiyya) and 'being powerful' (al-qadiriyya), and they do not consider this state as existent but merely as established (thubut). (3) And whoever denies states (ahwal) says: 'knowing with knowledge, powerful with power,' and likewise the rest of the attributes, and that His being knowing is precisely by His being described with knowledge. There is nothing existent or established externally except the essence and attributes themselves. They deny states. For if one speaks of the described, one says 'essence'; if one speaks of the meaning, one says 'knowledge' and 'power'; if one speaks of the essence with consideration of the meaning, one says 'knowing' and 'powerful'. So the statements are two, and the expressions are three. And the Mu'tazila and the Shi'a denied the attributes additional to the essence. They ascribed the effects of these attributes to the essence, and also denied the meanings themselves, saying that the Creator is living, knowing, powerful by Himself. So they affirmed the derived term without the derivative source. And some of them say 'by Himself'.

Commentary
This passage presents the tenth principle: the affirmation of seven eternal attributes for God. It begins by stating that God is knowing by knowledge, living by life, powerful by power, willing by will, speaking by speech, hearing by hearing, and seeing by sight. These attributes are distinct from the essence and are eternal. Footnote (2) clarifies that 'sight' is an attribute called sight, not a physical eye, to avoid misunderstanding. It explains that hearing and sight mean perception itself, not sense organs. The footnote then discusses the concept of 'states' (ahwal): there are some (the Ash'aris following Abu Hashim) who hold that in addition to essence and attributes, there are states that are not existent but established (thubut), such as 'being knowing' (alamiyya) and 'being powerful' (qadiriyya). These states are rationally conceivable. Footnote (3) discusses the position of those who deny states (like Imam al-Ash'ari and the majority). They say that God is knowing with knowledge, powerful with power, etc., and that there is nothing existent or established besides the essence and attributes. They express this with two statements (essence and attribute) and three expressions (essence, attribute, and the description like 'knowing'). The passage then refutes the Mu'tazila and Shi'a, who denied attributes additional to the essence, attributing the effects of these attributes to the essence itself and denying the meanings (like knowledge, power). They claimed that God is living, knowing, powerful by Himself (li-nafsihi), thereby affirming the derived term (e.g., 'knowing') without the derivative source (knowledge). Some of them say 'by Himself.' This is a clear refutation of the Mu'tazili and Shi'i position, upholding the Sunni doctrine of real, eternal attributes.

The Concept of States (Ahwal) in Divine Attributes
Ghazali explains the concept of states and the debate over their reality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the theological concept of 'states' (ahwal) and the two positions: affirming states (as per Abu Hashim) and denying them (as per majority Ash'aris).

Core Idea
Some theologians affirm states as non-existent realities; others deny them, explaining attributes through essence and attributes alone.

The footnote continues by explaining a subtle point: in addition to essence and attributes, some Ash'aris (following Abu Hashim al-Jubba'i) affirm 'states' (ahwal) that are not existent but established. Others, like Imam al-Ash'ari, deny states and express the relationship between essence and attributes through three expressions. This lesson unpacks that nuanced discussion.

Guided Path
1
Read the portion on those who affirm states
Read from 'fayuthbituna' to the end of the footnote (2) content. Understand that they affirm a state (hal) such as 'being knowing' (alamiyya) which is not existent but established (thubut).
2
Understand the terminology for states
They call the state of being knowing 'alamiyya' and being powerful 'qadiriyya'. Note that these are not separate entities but relational properties.
These states are rationally conceivable as attribution.
3
Read the position of those who deny states
Read footnote (3). They say 'knowing with knowledge' and deny any third thing besides essence and attributes.
4
Compare the two expressions
Those who deny states use two statements (essence, attribute) and three expressions (essence, attribute, description). Those who affirm states have an additional term for the state.
5
Reflect on the debate's significance
Why does this matter? It shows the care theologians took to avoid implying multiplicity in God while preserving the reality of attributes.

Refutation of Mu'tazila and Shi'a on Divine Attributes
Ghazali refutes Mu'tazila and Shi'a by exposing the incoherence of their position.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazili and Shi'i denial of real attributes and how Ghazali refutes them by showing they affirm the derived term without the source.

Core Idea
Mu'tazila and Shi'a reject attributes as real entities, but this leads to affirming 'knowing' without 'knowledge'—an illogical position.

After discussing the internal Ash'ari debate on states, Ghazali turns to refute external sects: the Mu'tazila and Shi'a. These groups denied that God has attributes additional to His essence, claiming instead that God is knowing, powerful, etc. by Himself. Ghazali points out that this is like affirming a derived term without its source—an untenable position.

Guided Path
1
Read the final paragraph of the passage
Read the Arabic: 'Wa nafati al-Mu'tazila...' Understand that they deny attributes additional to the essence.
2
Identify their claim
They say that God is living, knowing, powerful 'by Himself' (li-nafsihi). They deny the meanings (knowledge, power, etc.) as real entities.
3
Notice Ghazali's refutation
Ghazali says: 'So they affirmed the derived term without the derivative source.' This is a linguistic and logical argument.
4
Understand the linguistic point
In Arabic, a derived term (mushtaqq) like 'knowing' must come from a source (mushtaqq minhu) like 'knowledge'. Denying the source while using the term is contradictory.
5
Reflect on the implication
If they say 'knowing' without knowledge, they are making an incoherent statement. This shows the necessity of affirming real attributes.

Proof that a Created Will Requires Infinite Regress
Ghazali proves that a will not in the essence leads to infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's argument that a will not identical to the essence leads to an infinite regress, and why this is impossible.

Core Idea
A will not in the essence requires an endless chain of wills.

Ghazali presents a critical argument against the idea that God's will is something other than His essence. He shows that if the will is created, it must be specified by another will, leading to an infinite regress. This passage employs a powerful reductio that exposes the incoherence of the opponent's position.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation to understand the flow of the argument.
Notice the use of analogy and reductio.
2
Identify the key claim
Ghazali starts by stating that if the will is not the essence, then the essence cannot be willing. Understand this conditional.
3
Follow the regress
Trace the argument: each will requires another will to specify it, leading to an infinite chain. Consider why this is problematic.
4
Examine the reductio
Ghazali argues that if a will could occur without a will, then anything could occur without intention. This is absurd, so the premise must be false.
5
Conclude
The opponent's claim that the will is created leads to an impossible infinite regress. Therefore, the will must be identical to the essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari concept of 'states' (أحوال) as fixed attributes that are neither existing nor non-existing, and the alternative view of those who deny states.

Core Idea
States are real but not existent – a key distinction in Sunni theology.

Having affirmed the attributes, Ghazali now addresses the more subtle issue of 'states' (أحوال) that arise from attributes, such as 'knowingness' (العالمية). He explains that these states are fixed (ثبوت) but not existent (وجود). Then he presents the view of those who deny states, who use a tripartite expression: essence, attribute, and description. This lesson unpacks this nuanced doctrine.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and translation together. Notice the shift from attributes to states (أحوال).
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the three categories affirmed
The Ash'ari position affirms: 1) an existing essence, 2) existing attributes (knowledge, power, will), and 3) states that are fixed for the essence due to the attributes.
States are expressed as 'knowingness' (العالمية) and 'powerfulness' (القادرية).
3
Understand the nature of states
States are not existent (وجود) but have fixedness (ثبوت). They are rationally conceivable as descriptions of the essence in relation to attributes.
This distinguishes Ash'ari from Mu'tazila and others.
4
Understand the opposing view
Those who deny states only affirm essence and attributes. They express: 'He is knowing and has knowledge', etc. Their statements are two: essence and attribute; their expressions are three: essence, attribute, and description (عالم, قادر).
The three expressions correspond to: الموصوف (ذات), المعنى (علم وقدرة), and الذات باعتبار المعنى (عالم قادر).
5
Summarize the difference
Contrast: Ash'ari affirm states as fixed but non-existent; deniers of states say the description is merely the attribute's relation to the essence, not a separate entity.
Both views aim to avoid multiplicity of eternals.

Wajib has two valid meanings: harm-related obligation and logical necessity.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal statement and a dialectical refutation of the Mu'tazilite position on God's obligation to do what is most beneficial (al-aslah). Ghazali begins by stating the seventh principle: God does with His servants whatever He wills, and He is not obligated to observe what is most beneficial for them. The reason is that nothing is obligatory upon God; indeed, obligation (wujub) is not even conceivable in His case, because He is not questioned about His actions—they are the ones who are questioned. The footnote reinforces this by contrasting God's true ownership and lordship with human servitude: a slave cannot impose obligations on his master. Then Ghazali reports the Mu'tazilite views: the majority hold that al-aslah is obligatory upon God; Bishr ibn al-Mu'tamir held that only what is beneficial (al-maslaha) is obligatory, not necessarily the most beneficial, and that God cannot do what is harmful. Ghazali then poses a reductio ad absurdum: imagine a debate in the afterlife between a child and an adult who both died as Muslims. God gives the adult a higher rank because he strove in obedience after puberty—this, per the Mu'tazila, is obligatory. The child objects: 'You are just—why did You raise his rank over mine?' God replies that the adult strove. The child then argues: 'You caused me to die in childhood; it was incumbent on You to prolong my life so that I could also strive. By giving him a long life and not me, You have favored him unjustly.' This shows that if al-aslah were obligatory, God would have to give everyone an equal chance, but that is impossible. Thus, the Mu'tazilite position leads to an absurdity. The passage moves from principle statement, to reported views, to a concrete argument.

The Fear of the End: A Rational Doubt
Ghazali explains the fourth aspect of doubt: fear of the final end.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why fear of the end (khawf al-khatimah) is a rational source of doubt in faith and how Ghazali uses the analogy of fasting to show that faith's validity depends on its completion at death.

Core Idea
Faith's validity depends on its final state; no one can be certain of their end.

In this passage, Imam Ghazali explores one of the most unsettling sources of doubt for believers: the fear of how one's life will end. He argues that just as a fast is only valid if completed until sunset, faith is only valid if it persists until death. This introduces deep questions about predestination, certainty, and the hidden decree.

Guided Path

Read the Arabic text once, then read the English translation. Notice the numbered footnotes that refer to key terms.
Pay attention to the analogy of fasting; it is the core of the argument.
2
Understand the Fasting Analogy
Ghazali compares faith to a fast: a fast is only valid if completed until sunset; similarly, faith is only valid if it lasts until death. This shows that present certainty does not guarantee future validity.
3
Grasp the Concept of Istishab
The term 'istishab' refers to assuming continuity of a previous state. Ghazali says describing faith as valid before death is based on istishab, which is uncertain because the outcome is unknown.
Istishab is a legal principle, but here it is used to highlight uncertainty.
4
Reflect on the Prior Decree and Eternal Will
The passage connects fear of the end to the hidden prior decree and eternal will. No one knows what has been decreed for them, and the final outcome may contradict current expectations.
This leads to the conclusion that fear of the end is like fear of the initial decree.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth aspect of doubt, which stems from fear of the final outcome (khawf al-khatimah). The author argues that no one knows whether his faith will endure until death. He compares faith to a fast: a fasting person may be certain of his fast, but if he breaks it, his certainty was false because validity required completion. Similarly, faith's validity depends on persisting until death; describing it as valid before death is based on istishab (legal continuity), which is uncertain. The outcome is feared because of the hidden prior decree (qadiyya sābiqa) and eternal will (mashī'a azaliyya) that no human can know. Therefore, fear of the end is like fear of the initial decree. The passage cites the Quranic verse 'the stupor of death came with the Truth' interpreted as the prior decree, and a Salaf saying that only final ends of deeds are weighed. Footnotes explain key terms: the definition of daytime and lifespan, the meaning of istishab, the nature of the prior care, textual variants, and the identification of Abu Darda.
