Juwayni's biographer describes the political decay of the 5th century.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the political factors that led to the decline of the Abbasid caliphate in the 5th century and how this context prompted scholars like Juwayni to defend the truth.

Core Idea
Political decay created the conditions for scholarly duty.

Before diving into Juwayni's kalam, we must understand the turbulent times he lived in. This lesson examines how the Abbasid caliphate's weakness spurred provincial independence and corruption, ultimately motivating scholars to defend their beliefs.

The spirit of the age - the author - his view on studying kalam - the book. Imam al-Haramayn Abu al-Ma'ali al-Juwayni, author of Al-Irshad, which we present today to readers, lived his entire life in the fifth century under the Abbasid caliphate. Therefore, before we speak in this introduction about the writer and the book, we must briefly portray the image of this age in which he lived from both the political and intellectual aspects, so that we may know the possible effect of the environment in which he moved on his scholarly output, and among this output is the book Al-Irshad. The reliance of the Abbasid caliph al-Mu'tasim Billah (218-227 AH) on the Turks, hoping through them for strength and glory, heralded the beginning of the emergence of weakness and the loss of the caliphs' prestige, when these slave-soldiers sensed that they were the caliphs' strength and power; hence they began to monopolize authority, until they controlled the caliphate, its affairs, and their lives. And this weakness in the caliphs, and this degraded status to which the caliphate had fallen, had the inevitable natural consequence: the independence of many provincial governors, resulting in not a few small states such as the Hamdanids in the Jazira, and the Samanids in Transoxiana; and great corruption in administration caused by the weakness of legitimate authorities and the greed of usurping rulers. It was natural, as we have mentioned, that injustice would increase, seditions would spread, that proponents of misguided opinions would find no impediment to broadcasting and defending them, that people would feel a clear sense of distress and constriction because of all this, and that scholars of particular competence would feel a strong sense of their duty to debate the people of falsehood and defend the truth they believed. 2- But if we see that the weakness of the caliphs' authority, the corruption of the administration, and other such factors led to moral decay and social disorder in that age, due to the connection of this with that, it is not correct to understand the matter similarly regarding intellectual life; rather the opposite was the case. For the weakness of the caliphs, the loss of their authority in Baghdad and its environs, and the emergence of many small states within the body of the Islamic state - we say, this was a powerful factor in the flourishing of science and the patronage of scholars by rulers in those days. In truth, we can affirm that that age was the most brilliant of Islamic ages in the East in terms of knowledge, and that it abounded in geniuses in every science and art. This was after Muslims - and those whom Islam sheltered under its banner among non-Muslims - had begun to digest Greek sciences and philosophy, Indian and Persian thought, and after they had blended all this with religion and Islamic culture, resulting in what is known in the history of thought as Islamic philosophy. It suffices for anyone who wishes to look into this to consult the 'Fihrist' of Ibn al-Nadim and the 'Ta'rikh al-Hukama' (History of the Philosophers).

Commentary
This passage is a historical introduction by the editor or biographer, setting the stage for Juwayni's work. It explains the political and intellectual context of the 5th century AH. First, it describes the political decay: the Abbasid caliph al-Mu'tasim's reliance on Turkish slave-soldiers led to the caliphs' weakness, as these soldiers seized power. This caused provincial governors to become independent, forming small states like the Hamdanids and Samanids, and resulting in administrative corruption and injustice. Consequently, scholars felt a duty to defend the truth against falsehood. The author then makes a key observation: while political and social conditions deteriorated, intellectual life flourished paradoxically. The weakness of central authority and the rise of multiple dynasties actually promoted science, as local rulers patronized scholars. This era, the author claims, was the most brilliant in Eastern Islam for knowledge, producing geniuses in all fields. This was due to the synthesis of Greek, Indian, and Persian thought with Islamic culture, giving rise to Islamic philosophy. He references Ibn al-Nadim's 'Fihrist' and the 'Ta'rikh al-Hukama' as evidence. The passage thus provides the backdrop for Juwayni's kalam work.

Commentary
This passage is a historical explanation of why scholarship flourished in that era. It begins by stating that the reason is readily understandable. It points to the Abbasid caliphs' initial encouragement of knowledge, including free thought and philosophy. When al-Mutawakkil reversed this policy, it coincided with the weakening of caliphal authority and the breakup of the Islamic empire into smaller states. This fragmentation led to competition among local rulers to patronize scholars and thus enhance their own prestige. Moreover, scholars could move between courts if they felt threatened, ensuring their safety and continued work. The examples of al-Farabi with Sayf al-Dawla and Ibn Sina illustrate this mobility.

Al-Juwayni's Directive on Tafwid and Tanzih
Al-Juwayni instructs believers to avoid ta'wil and consign ambiguous attributes to God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the correct approach to ambiguous divine attributes according to Imam al-Juwayni: affirming transcendence, avoiding interpretation, and consigning meaning to Allah.

Core Idea
For ambiguous attributes, affirm the wording, believe in transcendence, and entrust the modality to Allah.

How should a believer approach Quranic verses and hadiths that describe Allah in human-like terms? Al-Juwayni provides a clear directive: affirm Allah's transcendence, avoid delving into interpretive speculation, and consign the true meaning to Him. This lesson unpacks that directive and its application to several key examples.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context
Recognize that Islamic scripture contains verses and hadiths with ambiguous meanings (mutashabihat). Learn the danger of ta'wil (figurative interpretation) and the alternative of tafwid (consignment).
This is a classical Sunni position; the salaf practiced tafwid.
2
Read the passage and identify key instructions
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Identify the three main commands: 1) believe in tanzih, 2) do not delve into ta'wil, 3) consign the meaning to Allah.
The passage begins with a decisive assertion that this is the correct way.
3
Examine the listed examples
Al-Juwayni cites specific instances: istiwā', the 'coming', 'as I created with My hands', 'the Face of your Lord will remain', 'running under Our eyes', and the hadith of descent. Understand each as an ambiguous attribute.
These are all attributes that seem to imply anthropomorphism if taken literally.
4
Distinguish tafwid from ta'wil and tashbih
Contrast the correct approach (tafwid) with two errors: ta'wil (human interpretation) and tashbih (anthropomorphism). Tafwid means acknowledging the literal meaning without knowing how.
Tafwid does not mean ignorance; it means reverently affirming what Allah has said.
5
Apply the methodology
Reflect on how this directive guides your own engagement with similar verses. Practice by stating for each example: 'I affirm the wording, believe Allah is beyond likeness, and consign the reality to Him.'
This is the way of the salaf.

Commentary
This passage is a directive from Imam al-Juwayni on how to handle the ambiguous attributes (mutashabihat) of God. He begins by stating that the correct way is decisive. He then lays out the duty of the believer: to affirm that God is utterly transcendent (tanzih) above any resemblance to created things, and therefore not to delve into figurative interpretation (ta'wil) of difficult verses. He then lists specific examples of such verses and hadiths: God's istiwa' (ascending), the 'coming' (presumably in a hadith), the verse 'as I created with My hands', 'the Face of your Lord will remain', 'running under Our eyes' (from the story of Noah), and the hadith of God's descent to the lowest heaven. All these are to be accepted as they have come, without explaining how, as per the method of the early Muslims (salaf).
