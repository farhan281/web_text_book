Refuting the Claim that Anything is Obligatory Upon God
Ghazali refutes the claim that obligation applies to God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali considers it impossible for obligation to apply to God and how he decisively refutes any attempt to define a new meaning of obligation.

Core Idea
Obligation cannot apply to God; any proposed meaning is either impossible or unintelligible.

In this passage, Ghazali confronts the claim that God is obligated to bring about resurrection and reward. He cuts off the argument by referring to his earlier analysis of the meanings of obligation (wājib), showing that none of them can apply to the divine. Even if the opponent suggests a fourth meaning, Ghazali dismisses it as meaningless. This lesson explores his decisive refutation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the opponent's claim
Read the quoted sentence: the opponent says that something obligates God to resurrect and reward. Note that this is a direct statement of the view Ghazali is refuting.
The opponent might be a rationalist theologian arguing for divine justice.
2
Recall Ghazali's earlier analysis
Ghazali refers back to the meanings of wājib. In previous passages, he has analyzed the concept and shown that none of the coherent meanings—such as necessary existence or moral duty—can apply to God.
3
See how Ghazali blocks any escape
Ghazali says that if the opponent proposes a fourth meaning, it is unintelligible. This closes the door on any attempt to salvage the argument by redefining terms.
Think about why a 'fourth meaning' cannot be accepted: it would be ad hoc and undefined.

The True Meaning of Divine Wisdom (al-Ḥikmah)
Ghazali refutes the claim that God's actions contradict wisdom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali defines divine wisdom exclusively as knowledge of order and ability to arrange, and how this refutes the objection that God’s actions could contradict wisdom.

Core Idea
Divine wisdom is not about motives; it is perfect knowledge and power.

In this passage, Ghazali counters a common objection: if God is wise, how can He leave some things undone? The answer lies in what 'wisdom' truly means for God. Ghazali restricts the term to its revealed definition, dismissing any other meaning as empty.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the literal translation. Notice the conditional structure: 'if by it is meant... then...'.
The ellipses are original and indicate a pause or omitted repetition.
2
Identify the two definitions of wisdom
Ghazali presents two possibilities: (1) wisdom as knowledge of order and ability to arrange, and (2) any other meaning. Note how he handles each.
The first definition is what Ghazali has already established; the second is dismissed.
3
Understand the refutation strategy
Ask yourself: why does this refute the objection? Because if wisdom means only what Ghazali says, then God's actions (or inactions) cannot contradict it—they are expressions of His perfect knowledge and power.
The objection assumes a broader definition of wisdom, which Ghazali rejects.

Commentary

If, however, the opponent intends some other meaning of wisdom, then we do not attribute any other wisdom to God except what we have already mentioned; any other definition is just a word without real meaning. Thus the objection falls.

God is Not Obligated to Do What is Most Beneficial
Fourth claim: refuting the obligation of al-aslah with logical and observational proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that God is not obligated to do what is most beneficial for His servants, contrary to the Mu'tazilite view, and how the three children example demonstrates this.

Core Idea
God's actions are not bound by human notions of what is most beneficial.

In this passage, Imam al-Ghazali takes on a key Mu'tazilite claim: that God must always do what is most beneficial (al-aslah) for His creatures. He counters this by presenting two lines of evidence: a logical proof from the negation of obligation, and an observational proof from the differing fates of three children. Let's examine his argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the claim and the general proof
Read the opening lines where al-Ghazali states the fourth claim and presents the first proof: that obligation is negated from God.
Notice the phrase 'ما دل على نفي الوجوب عن الله تعالى'.
2
Study the three children example
The second proof is empirical: the example of three children with different outcomes. Consider how this illustrates that not all divine acts appear beneficial.
The example serves as a powerful counterexample to the Mu'tazilite position.
3
Draw the conclusion
Conclude that the obligation of al-aslah is invalid because God's acts are not constrained by human notions of benefit.
This refutes the Mu'tazilite claim.

The fourth claim: negation of observing benefit. We claim: He is not obligated to observe what is most beneficial for His servants, but He may do whatever He wills, and the most beneficial is from God, and He judges whatever He wills, contrary to the Mu'tazila; for they restricted God Almighty in His actions and made it obligatory for Him to observe what is most beneficial. Explaining the necessity of evil as a shadow and the two worlds of justice and grace.

Reflect on the Quranic support

Ghazali presents his fourth claim: God is not obligated to do what is most beneficial for His servants. Commentary
This passage defines the fifth claim in Ghazali's discussion of divine justice: that God is under no obligation to reward obedience or punish disobedience. He begins by stating the claim clearly: reward is not obligatory; God may reward, punish, annihilate, or forgive all disbelievers and punish all believers without contradiction to divinity. The reason is that taklīf (religious obligation) is God's disposal of His own slaves, and reward is a separate free act. The three senses of obligation (wājib) mentioned earlier – reward, punishment, etc. – are not applicable to God's actions. The concepts of good (ḥusn) and bad (qubḥ) have no meaning here, except that breaking a promise would be impossible (since God's promise is true). Thus Ghazali defends the Ash'arī position that God's actions are not subject to objective moral obligations.

Core Idea
Hearing and sight are distinct perfections, not reducible to knowledge or flawed by temporal limitations.

After establishing that God has knowledge, will, and power, Ghazali turns to the attributes of hearing and sight. These were controversial because they seem to entail that God is affected by temporal events or that He perceives non-existent things. In this passage, Ghazali presents a series of challenges from his opponents and, through brief responses, defends the reality and perfection of these attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify each challenge
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Note that each 'تحريجة' introduces a new objection. Count how many challenges are presented.
There are six distinct challenges in this passage.
2
Examine the first two challenges
The first attempts to reduce hearing/sight to knowledge; the second argues they would make God a locus for temporal events. Understand why these are serious objections and how Ghazali's brief 'فكذلك' hints at a response.
Ghazali denies reduction and implies that hearing/sight do not entail temporality.
3
Consider the pre-eternity objection
How could God see the world before it existed? The response: just as knowledge and will relate to non-existent things, so do hearing and sight. This draws an analogy with already established attributes.
This shows consistency in Ghazali's attribute theology.
4
Analyze the perfection-related challenges
The remaining challenges question whether hearing/sight are truly perfections, why God must be more perfect, and why not extend to smelling, tasting, or pleasure. These test the criterion of perfection.
Ghazali's school would argue that these are not genuine perfections or not fitting for God.
5
Reflect on Ghazali's method
Notice that Ghazali does not fully elaborate responses here. He presents objections to show they are answerable within a sound theological framework. The brevity assumes prior knowledge of kalam arguments.
This passage is a summary of debates; full responses are in other works.

Commentary
This passage covers the fifth and sixth attributes: hearing (السمع) and sight (البصر). It begins with a challenge: why not interpret these as merely knowledge? The answer is 'likewise' but not elaborated. Next challenge: If God had hearing and sight, He would become a locus for temporal events (since sounds and sights are accidents). Also, how could He see the world in pre-eternity when it was non-existent? The response: the attachment of knowledge to the non-existent is like the attachment of will to it, implying that hearing and sight can relate to non-existent objects. Then a challenge: why must the Creator be more perfect than creation? Challenge: we do not concede that hearing and sight are perfections. Challenge: why not attribute smelling and tasting to God? Challenge: if perfection is your proof, why not attribute pleasure to God? These objections reflect debates about divine attributes: which ones are literally applicable and which are not.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Hearing and sight are merely metaphorical expressions for God's knowledge, or that attributing them implies God is temporal or limited.
Correction: Ghazali affirms that hearing and sight are real, distinct attributes that do not entail temporality or imperfection. They relate to objects even before their existence, as do knowledge and will.

Key Takeaway
Hearing and sight are real, distinct divine attributes, defended against objections of temporality and non-existence by analogy with knowledge and will.
