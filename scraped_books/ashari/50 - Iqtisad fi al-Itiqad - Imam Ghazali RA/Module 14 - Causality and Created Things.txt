Ghazali's Refutation of Latency in Accidents
Ghazali rejects the objection that accidents merely appear rather than being created, giving a succinct refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's concise dialectical argument that even if accidents were latent, their latency itself proves they are originated.

Core Idea
Latency or manifestation—both states are accidents, proving substances are never free from originated things.

In this passage, Ghazali responds to an objection against his proof that accidents are originated. The objector suggests accidents might merely be latent rather than created. Ghazali shows that even if true, the objection fails because latency itself is an originated accident. Let's examine his sharp dialectical move.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and Ghazali's initial remark
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the objection: perhaps accidents were latent and then became manifest. Note Ghazali's reluctance to engage in a full refutation.
This sets up the dialectical context.
2
Identify Ghazali's key move
Ghazali points out that both latency and manifestation are states of the substance. He argues that both states are accidents and are originated.
This is the crux of his argument.
3
Understand the implication
Since the substance cannot be free from either latency or manifestation, it is always accompanied by an originated accident. Therefore, the substance is never free from originated things.
This establishes the substance's contingency.
4
Reflect on the dialectical strategy
Consider why Ghazali does not fully refute the theory of latency. He uses a minimal argument that suffices for his purpose, showing his economy of reasoning.

If it is said: 'How do you know that they are originated? Perhaps they were [merely] latent and then became manifest.' We say: If we were to occupy ourselves in this book with superfluous digressions beyond the purpose, we would invalidate the theory of latency (kumūn) and manifestation (ẓuhūr) in accidents completely. Rather, we say: the substance does not free itself from either the latency of motion within it or its manifestation, and both are originated. Commentary
This passage is a response to an objection. The opponent asks: 'How do you know that accidents are originated (ḥāditha)? Perhaps they were latent (kāmina) within the substance and then became manifest.' Ghazali replies that he will not engage in a full refutation of the theory of latency (kumūn) and manifestation (ẓuhūr) because it is beyond the scope of the book (which is the 'Iqtisad' or 'moderation'). However, he gives a succinct argument: the substance is never free from either the latency or the manifestation of motion—both are accidents and both are originated. Therefore, the substance is always accompanied by originated accidents. Whether the accident is latent or manifest, the state of latency or manifestation is itself an accident and thus originated, so the substance cannot be free of originated things.

The False Analogy Behind Accident Transference
Ghazali dismantles the false analogy between substance-place and accident-substrate, showing it leads to infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali argues that the transference of accidents leads to an infinite regress and thus is rationally impossible.

Core Idea
Transference of accidents is a false analogy that collapses into infinite regress.

In this passage, Ghazali responds to an objection that accidents might transfer between substances. He first sets aside weak arguments and then presents the core rational proof: the very concept of transference, taken from substances, cannot apply to accidents because their relation to a substrate is fundamentally different. This lesson walks through that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Read the opening lines where an opponent suggests accidents transfer, and Ghazali dismisses weak proofs.
2
Analyze the analogy
Notice how the term 'transference' is taken from substances and applied to accidents by analogy. Identify the similarity and the key mistake.
The specification (ikhtiṣāṣ) of a substance to place is additional; if applied to accidents, it leads to infinite regress.
3
Trace the infinite regress
Follow the logical steps: if the analogy held, an accident would need another accident to inhere in the substrate, and so on ad infinitum. This shows the impossibility.
The conclusion: without an infinite chain, no accident could exist at all.

If it is said: 'Perhaps it [the accident] transferred to it from another place. How is the falsehood of the theory of transference of accidents known?' We say: Weak proofs have been mentioned for its refutation, and we will not lengthen the book by reporting and criticizing them. But the correct way to unveil its falsehood is to show that the intellect cannot accommodate this possibility unless it is heedless of understanding the reality of accident and the reality of transference. Whoever understands the reality of accident realizes the impossibility of transference in it. Its explanation: transference is a term derived from the transference of a substance from place (ḥayyiz) to place. That is established in the intellect by understanding the substance, understanding the place, and understanding that the specification (ikhtiṣāṣ) of the substance to the place is additional to the essence of the substance. Then it is known that an accident must have a substrate (maḥall) just as a substance must have a place. So one imagines that the relation of the accident to its substrate is like the relation of the substance to its place, and thus it occurs to the imagination that transference is possible for it as it is for the substance. If this analogy were correct, then the specification of the accident to the substrate would be a mode (kawn) additional to the essences of both accident and substrate, just as the specification of the substance to the place is a mode additional to the essences of substance and place. Then an accident would inhere in an accident, and then the inherence of an accident in an accident would require another specification additional to the inhere and the substrate, and so on ad infinitum, leading to the non-existence of any single accident unless an infinite number of accidents exist! Let us then investigate the reason why a distinction is made between the specification of the accident to the substrate and the specification of the substance to the place, such that one of the two specifications is additional to the essence of the specified thing and the other is not. From this, the error in imagining transference becomes clear. The secret in this is: the substrate, although necessary for the accident as the place is necessary for the substance, there is a difference between the two necessities. For some necessities are essential (dhātī) to a thing, and some are not essential. By 'essential' I mean that whose abolition necessitates the abolition of the thing. If it is abolished in existence, the existence of the thing is abolished; if it is abolished in the intellect, the knowledge of it in the intellect is abolished. But the place is not essential to the substance. For we know the body and the substance first, then after that we consider the place: is it a fixed thing or an imaginary thing? And we arrive at its verification by proof, but we perceive the body by sense and observation without proof. Therefore, a specific place, for example the body of Zayd, is not essential to Zayd, so the loss or change of that place does not necessitate the abolition of Zayd's body.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed refutation of the theory that accidents can transfer (intiqāl) from one substance to another. An opponent suggests that perhaps an accident simply moves from one location to another. Ghazali first dismisses weak arguments and then gives the correct refutation. He explains that the idea of transference arises from a false analogy between a substance's relation to its place (ḥayyiz) and an accident's relation to its substrate (maḥall). For a substance, its existence is independent of any specific place; place is an extrinsic attribute. But for an accident, its existence depends entirely on its substrate. If you try to conceive of an accident transferring, you would have to imagine that the accident's connection to its substrate is like the substance's connection to its place—an additional attribute. This would lead to an infinite regress: because the accident's inherence in the substrate would itself require another accident (a mode of inherence), and that accident would require another, and so on. The key distinction is that the necessity of the substrate for the accident is essential (dhātī): if the substrate is annihilated, the accident is annihilated. But the necessity of a specific place for a substance is not essential: the substance can change place without being destroyed. Ghazali illustrates this with the example of Zayd's body: if Zayd moves from one place to another, his body remains. Therefore, transference applies to substances, but not to accidents.

The Essential vs. Non-Essential Necessity
Ghazali distinguishes essential necessity (substrate) from non-essential necessity (place) to explain why transference is impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction Ghazali makes between essential necessity (substrate for accidents) and non-essential necessity (place for substances), and why this distinction explains the impossibility of transference.

Core Idea
A substrate is essential for an accident; a place is not essential for a substance.

After showing the infinite regress, Ghazali delves into the deeper reason: the necessity of a substrate for an accident is essential (dhātī), while the necessity of a place for a substance is not. This key distinction explains why accidents cannot transfer while substances can. This lesson unpacks that distinction and the example of Zayd's body.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key question
Read the opening where Ghazali announces he will investigate why the two specifications differ in being additional or not.
2
Understand 'essential' (dhātī)
Learn Ghazali's definition: something is essential if its abolition abolishes the thing itself, both in existence and in the mind.
3
Apply to substrate vs. place
Notice that substrate is essential for an accident (without it, the accident ceases), but a specific place is not essential for a substance (Zayd's body remains even if he moves).
The example of Zayd moving from one place to another illustrates that place is not intrinsic to his body.

The False Analogy Between Accident and Substance
Ghazali refutes the possibility of accident transference by showing it entails an infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the analogy between an accident's relation to its substrate and a substance's relation to its location leads to an infinite regress, thereby refuting the possibility of accident transference.

Core Idea
If accidents could transfer, then accidents would need accidents, leading to an infinite chain.

Ghazali begins by exposing the false analogy that leads some to think accidents can transfer between substrates. He shows that if we treat an accident's specification to its substrate as an extra attribute, we end up requiring an infinite number of accidents—an impossible consequence. This lesson examines that argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice how Ghazali sets up the analogy between accident-substrate and substance-location.
The passage uses a conditional 'if...then...' structure.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the possibility that accidents (a‘rād) can transfer from one substrate to another. Ghazali begins by stating the problem: an accident inheres in a substrate (maḥall) as a substance (jawhar) occupies a location (ḥayyiz), but the substance's relation to its location is additional to its essence, whereas the accident's relation to its substrate is not. He first shows the false analogy: if accidents could transfer like substances, then the specification (ikhtiṣāṣ) of an accident to its substrate would be an extra attribute, leading to an infinite regress of accidents inhering in accidents. Then he investigates the reason for the difference. The key distinction is between essential (dhātī) and non-essential necessary concomitants (lāzim). An essential concomitant is one whose non-existence entails the non-existence of the thing itself. For a substance, location is non-essential: we can conceive a substance without its specific location, as location is known through proof, not primary intuition. But for an accident like length, it is inconceivable apart from its subject; its specification to the subject is essential—it has no being without that subject. Therefore, transference, which would change that specification, would annihilate the accident's essence. Ghazali concludes that the specification of an accident to its substrate is not an additional attribute but is identical to its essence; thus transference is impossible. The argument relies on the epistemological point that substances are known independently, while accidents are only known through their substrates.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof against the Mu'tazilite theory of generation (tawallud) by analyzing the necessity of conditions for physical motion. Thus, what the opponent calls 'generation' is actually of two kinds: if it is a condition, only conjunction is possible; if it is not a condition, separation is possible when miracles occur.

Ghazali's Definition of Generation and Its Conditions
Ghazali defines generation and distinguishes necessary from non-necessary concomitants.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's definition of generation (tawallud) and the crucial distinction between conditional and non-conditional necessary concomitants.

Core Idea
Generation is not mere succession but a necessary relationship.

This passage from Ghazali's Iqtiṣād tackles a fundamental question: what does it mean for one thing to 'generate' another? It begins with a challenge—why not say movements generate each other?—and then clarifies the correct definition by distinguishing between necessary concomitants that are conditional and those that are not. This distinction is key to understanding how kalam thinkers approach causation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that it contains two 'challenges' (tarīja) and a definition in between.
The term 'tarīja' means a critical question or objection.
2
Identify the two challenges
The first challenge asks why we don't say movements generate each other. The second asks why generation is not simply existence after another. What is Ghazali's response?
His response is the definition that separates conditional from non-conditional concomitants.
3
Understand the distinction
Ghazali states that conditional necessary concomitants (al-lāzimāt al-sharṭiyya) are inseparable, while non-conditional ones may separate. This implies that true generation requires a conditional necessity.
Think of fire and burning: under normal conditions, fire necessarily produces heat—that's a conditional necessary concomitant.
4
Connect to the broader debate
Consider why Ghazali must make this distinction. It prevents reducing generation to mere temporal sequence and affirms that true causality involves a logical or ontological connection.
This aligns with the Ashʿarī view that habitual causation is not necessary but depends on God's will.

Commentary
This passage presents a discussion about the concept of generation (تولد), i.e., the idea that actions or movements are produced by other actions. It begins with a challenge: why not say that movements are generated from one another (as in causation?) Then Ghazali gives the correct definition: generation is when a thing comes into existence consequent upon another, but he adds that necessary concomitants (اللازمات الشرطية) that are conditional are inseparable, while those that are not conditional may separate. There is then another challenge: why is generation not simply the existence of an existent after another? The passage seems to be arguing that generation requires a specific relationship, not mere temporal succession.
