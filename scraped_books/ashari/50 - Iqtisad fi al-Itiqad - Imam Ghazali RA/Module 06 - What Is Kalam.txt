Ghazali on the Role of Kalam and Fiqh
Editor presents Ghazali's quote on kalam and fiqh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's nuanced position on the role of kalam relative to fiqh and the sufficiency of taqlid for correct belief.

Core Idea
Ghazali: Kalam is not the sole foundation; taqlid suffices for correct belief.

This passage from the editor of al-Iqtisad presents Ghazali's own words on the status of kalam. Ghazali warns against those who overstate the importance of kalam as the foundation of religion, pointing out that true foundation is correct belief, which can be attained through simple acceptance. He acknowledges the truth of the claim but limits its applicability, giving us a balanced perspective on the role of theological argument.

The true foundation is correct belief (اعتقاد صحيح) and decisive assent (تصديق جزم).

This shows Ghazali's pragmatism: kalam is valuable for specialists but not required for everyone.

Core Idea
Consensus is not mere opinion; it is certain knowledge through tawatur.

In this passage, Imam al-Ghazali establishes the foundational authority of consensus as a definitive proof. He defines consensus as the agreement of the community and links its certainty to the concept of tawatur, which compels knowledge. Despite textual corruption, the key assertion remains clear: consensus yields necessary knowledge, not probability.

Ghazali classifies speech and dissects the report to show its components.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali classifies speech and identifies the minimal components needed for a report.

Core Idea
A report requires only knowledge, power, and will—no extra entity.

Ghazali begins by dividing speech into four categories. He then takes the report as an example to show that uttering a statement like 'beating' does not require anything beyond known mental faculties. This sets the stage for arguing against a distinct internal speech.

Commentary
This passage provides the detailed clarification (إيضاح) of the earlier objection. Ghazali explains that speech can be classified into four categories: command (amr), prohibition (nahy), report (khabar), and question (istikhbar). He analyzes the report as an example: when someone says 'beating' (darab), this act of speech requires only three things: knowledge of the meaning, knowledge of the word, the power to utter it, and the intention to indicate and to produce the word. Nothing else is needed. Therefore, if one claims that there is an additional internal speech entity beyond these, it can be denied and the act of speech still occurs. The same analysis applies to questions, commands, and prohibitions—they all reduce to the same components: knowledge, will, and power. Some of these components (like sounds) cannot be attributed to God (they are impossible for Him), but others (will, knowledge, power) are attributes of God. Anything beyond these components is unintelligible (ghayr mafhum). Thus, the passage concludes that there is no need to posit an independent internal speech as a distinct entity; what people call internal speech is sufficiently explained by these cognitive and volitional factors.

Commentary
This passage provides the detailed clarification (إيضاح) of the earlier objection. Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof of internal speech (kalam nafsi). Ghazali begins by stating that the speech they intend is an additional meaning beyond mere expressions. He limits the discussion to the command as an example. He is definitely commanding but not desiring compliance. Thus, the demand in his soul is speech, distinct from will. An objection arises: such a command is not real but merely apparent. After these refutations, Ghazali concludes that internal speech is a distinct entity, not reducible to knowledge, will, or belief. It is a perfection, so it must be attributed to God as eternal speech. External letters and sounds are mere indicators, originated and conventional, indicating the eternal attribute.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof of internal speech (kalam nafsi). Ghazali begins by stating that the speech they intend is an additional meaning beyond mere expressions. He limits the discussion to the command as an example. He gives the example of a master telling his slave 'Stand' – the expression indicates a meaning, and that meaning (the internal speech) is itself speech. He refutes the idea that this meaning is reducible to the will of the commanded or the will of indicating. The will of indicating is impossible because it requires a distinct indicated. The will of the commanded is impossible because one can command while not desiring compliance, as in the scenario where a master, to avoid punishment, commands his slave to stand before the king, knowing compliance would cause his death. He is definitely commanding but not desiring compliance. Thus, the demand in his soul is speech, distinct from will. An objection arises: such a command is not real but merely apparent. Ghazali gives two refutations: 1) If it were not a real command, the master's excuse would not hold; people would say he is incapable of commanding. But common sense says his excuse is valid. 2) If he swore an oath, jurisconsults would uphold that his command was real; they would not say he didn't command because he didn't want obedience. After these refutations, Ghazali concludes that internal speech is a distinct entity, not reducible to knowledge, will, or belief. It is a perfection, so it must be attributed to God as eternal speech. External letters and sounds are mere indicators, originated and conventional, indicating the eternal attribute.

Proof of Kalam Nafsi as an Eternal Attribute

Ghazali tackles a verbal dispute about whether faith increases or decreases. He argues that the confusion arises because the word 'iman' is shared among three distinct referents. In this passage, he identifies each meaning and provides evidence from scripture and reason.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation to get the overall flow. Notice that Ghazali starts by stating the dispute and its cause.
2
Identify the three meanings
Find where Ghazali enumerates the three meanings of 'iman'. List them: intellectual certainty, traditional acceptance, and acceptance with action.
The Arabic terms are: التصديق اليقيني البرهاني, التصديق الاعتقادي التقليدي, and تصديق معه العمل.
3
Examine the evidence for each
For each meaning, look at the proof Ghazali provides. Note the scriptural and rational arguments he uses.
The first meaning is supported by the case of someone who knows God through proof and dies immediately. The second by the early Arabs' belief in the Prophet. The third by hadiths linking faith and action.
4
Understand how the dispute is resolved
Reflect on how differentiating these meanings removes the disagreement. The dispute about increase or decrease only arises when 'iman' is understood in a single sense. Once the multiple referents are clear, each can be analyzed separately.
This is the core of Ghazali's verbal analysis.

المسألة الثانية: وهي اللفظية: هل يزيد الإيمان أو ينقص؟ وهذا الاختلاف منشأه الجهل بكون الاسم مشتركاً؛ أعني: اسم الإيمان، وإذا فُصِّلَ مسميات هذا اللفظ ارتفع الخلاف. وهو مشترك بين ثلاثة معانٍ؛ إذ قد يعبر به عن التصديق اليقيني البرهاني، وقد يعبر به عن التصديق الاعتقادي التقليدي إذا كان جزماً، وقد يعبر به عن تصديق معه العمل بموجب التصديق. ودليل إطلاقه على الأول: أن مَنْ عرف الله تعالى بالدليل ومات عَقِيبَ معرفته فإنا نحكم بأنه مات مؤمناً. ودليل إطلاقه على التصديق التقليدي: أن جماهير العرب كانوا يصدقون رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم بمجرد إحسانه إليهم وتلطفه بهم ونظرهم في قرائن أحواله، من غير نظر في أدلة الوحدانية ووجه دلالة المعجزة، وقد كان يحكم رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم بإيمانهم وقد قال الله عز وجل: {وَمَا أَنتَ بِمُؤْمِنٍ لَّنَا} أي: بمصدق، ولم يفرق رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم بين تصديق وتصديق. ودليل إطلاقه على الفعل: قوله عليه السلام: «لا يزني الزاني حين يزني وهو مؤمن»، وقوله عليه السلام: «الإيمان بضع وسبعون باباً أدناها إماطة الأذى عن الطريق».
The second issue: and it is the verbal issue: does faith increase or decrease? And this disagreement originates from ignorance that the name is shared; I mean: the name 'faith' (iman). And if the referents of this term are differentiated, the disagreement is removed. And it is shared among three meanings: for sometimes it is expressed about conclusive intellectual certainty (al-tasdiq al-yaqini al-burhani), and sometimes it is expressed about traditional doctrinal acceptance (al-tasdiq al-i'tiqadi al-taqlidi) when it is decisive, and sometimes it is expressed about acceptance along with action according to the requirement of acceptance. And the proof for its application to the first: that whoever knows God Almighty through proof and dies immediately after his knowledge, then we judge that he died a believer. And the proof for its application to traditional acceptance: that the masses of the Arabs used to believe the Messenger of God, may God bless him and grant him peace, merely due to his kindness to them, his gentleness with them, and their observation of the indications of his state, without consideration of the proofs of oneness and the aspect of the miracle's indication, and the Messenger of God, may God bless him and grant him peace, used to judge their faith, and God Almighty said: {And you are not a believer in us} that is: a confirmer, and the Messenger of God, may God bless him and grant him peace, did not distinguish between one acceptance and another. And the proof for its application to action: his saying, peace be upon him: «The adulterer does not commit adultery while he is a believer», and his saying, peace be upon him: «Faith has seventy-some branches, the lowest of which is removing harm from the road.»

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification. Ghazali introduces the second issue of the verbal disputes (al-lafziyya): whether faith (iman) increases or decreases. He states that the disagreement arises from ignorance that the name 'iman' is shared (mushtarak) among multiple meanings. Once the referents are differentiated, the dispute disappears. He then enumerates three meanings: (1) conclusive intellectual certainty (al-tasdiq al-yaqini al-burhani), (2) traditional doctrinal acceptance (al-tasdiq al-i'tiqadi al-taqlidi) when it is fixed and decisive (jasm), and (3) acceptance accompanied by action in accordance with that acceptance. For each, he provides evidence. For the first meaning, he argues that if someone knows God through proof and dies immediately afterward, we judge him to have died a believer, indicating that this meaning is sufficient for salvation. For the second meaning, he points to the early Arabs who believed the Prophet based on his character and signs without formal proofs, and the Prophet judged them as believers; likewise, the Qur'anic verse 'you are not a believer in us' is interpreted as 'you are not a confirmer', showing that tasdiq alone suffices. For the third meaning, he cites the hadith that the adulterer is not a believer at the time of adultery (implying loss of faith by losing action) and the hadith that faith has seventy-some branches including removing harm, showing that action is part of faith. Thus the three meanings have scriptural support.

Key Takeaway
Faith (iman) has three distinct referents: intellectual certainty, traditional acceptance, and acceptance with action; recognizing this resolves the verbal dispute about its increase or decrease.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
You may consider yourself a believer, but which of the three meanings of 'iman' do you truly possess? Have you ever settled for mere traditional acceptance without ever seeking intellectual certainty? What would it take for you to move toward the first meaning?
?
If your faith were tested by the loss of all external actions—if you could no longer pray or give charity—would you still be a believer according to the third meaning? What does your answer reveal about your understanding of faith?
?
Ghazali shows that the dispute about faith's increase is verbal. But deep down, is there a part of you that resists this resolution because you want to claim a higher level of faith than others? Confront that pride today.
