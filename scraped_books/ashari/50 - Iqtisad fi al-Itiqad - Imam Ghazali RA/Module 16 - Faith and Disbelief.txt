Learning Objective

Core Idea
Uttering kufr under the sword is not ugly; insistence has roots in reward and praise.

In this passage, Ghazali addresses a critical question: is it blameworthy to utter words of disbelief when under threat of death? He argues that a rational person does not deem it ugly under duress, and then analyzes why someone might still choose to refuse—either for a greater reward or for the sake of praise. This discussion reveals Ghazali's psychological approach to moral actions.

Resolving the Dispute Over Iman's Increase: Three Meanings
Ghazali enumerates three meanings of iman and resolves the increase dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the dispute over whether iman increases or decreases is a verbal one, and how distinguishing three meanings of iman dissolves the apparent contradiction.

Core Idea
Iman is a shared term; its three meanings resolve the increase debate.

A long-standing debate among Muslims is whether iman (faith) can increase or decrease. Imam al-Ghazali cuts through this dispute by showing it is merely verbal—rooted in assuming that 'iman' has only one meaning. In this passage, he identifies three distinct referents of the word and demonstrates that the dispute disappears once each is properly understood. Let's explore his clear and practical resolution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute and its cause
Read the opening lines where Ghazali states the question and the source of disagreement: ignorance that 'iman' is a shared name (mushtarak).
Notice the key phrase: 'If the referents of this word are detailed, the disagreement disappears.'
2
Learn the three meanings of iman
Examine the three definitions: demonstrative certainty, traditional conviction, and conviction with action. Each is supported by evidence from revelation or reason.
Look for the evidence given for each meaning—these show why each usage is valid.
3
Understand the conclusion on increase
Read the final part: if iman means demonstrative certainty, it cannot increase or decrease because certainty is either complete or absent.
Compare with the other two meanings—do they allow increase? The passage focuses on the first meaning.
4
Reflect on the verbal nature of the dispute
Consider how the dispute arose from ambiguity. Explain in your own words why the dispute is verbal and not real.
This step connects the lesson to broader methodology in kalam.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and refutation regarding the verbal dispute (mas'ala lafziyya) on whether iman increases or decreases. The author identifies the source of disagreement as ignorance that 'iman' is a shared name (mushtarak) with multiple referents. He then enumerates three meanings: (1) demonstrative certainty (tasdiq yaqini burhani) – rational conviction from proof; (2) traditional conviction (tasdiq i'tiqadi taqlidi) – firm acceptance based on authority or habit; (3) conviction with action (tasdiq ma'a al-‘amal) – belief accompanied by deeds. For each meaning, he provides evidence. For the first, he argues that one who knows God through proof and dies immediately is judged a believer, showing that iman applies even without actions. For the second, he cites the early Arabs who believed the Prophet due to his character and signs without formal proofs, and the Prophet accepted their iman; Qur'anic verse (Yusuf 12:17) uses 'mu'min' simply as 'confirmer'. For the third, he cites hadith linking iman to abstinence from sin (adultery) and to branches including actions like removing harm. The author concludes that if iman is taken as demonstrative certainty, it cannot increase or decrease because certainty is either complete (thus no increase) or incomplete (thus not certainty). This meaning is one (wahida) in its essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Ghazali evaluates the possibility of increase and decrease for each of the three distinct meanings of faith: intellectual certainty, traditional acceptance, and action combined with acceptance.

Core Idea
Faith's increase is not uniform; it depends on which meaning of faith you adopt.

Not all discussions about faith's increase and decrease are about the same thing. Ghazali clarifies that the word 'faith' can mean three different things, and each meaning allows or disallows increase differently. Without distinguishing these, debates become confused. This passage carefully separates the three and explains what kind of variation is possible in each.

Guided Path
1
First meaning: Intellectual certainty
Read the part about 'tasdiq burhani'. Note how Ghazali says certainty itself cannot increase, but its accompanying tranquility can.

Read the part about 'tasdiq taqlidi'. Notice Ghazali's empirical observation of Jews, Christians, and Muslims. He uses the 'knot' analogy.
Variation is undeniable to anyone who observes inner states.

Third meaning: Action with acceptance
Read the final part about 'amal ma'a al-tasdiq'. Ghazali notes action obviously varies, and then asks if repeated action affects the acceptance itself. He concludes that perseverance reinforces tranquility.
The effect is on tranquility, not on the essence of tasdiq.
4
Synthesize the three
Compare the three meanings. Notice that only the second meaning allows increase in the acceptance itself; the first and third restrict increase to tranquility or action.

Commentary
This passage is an analytical examination of increase and decrease (ziyada wa nuqsan) for each of the three meanings of faith. Ghazali begins by stating the conclusion for the first meaning (intellectual certainty): increase and decrease cannot be conceived in the certainty itself. If certainty is perfect, no increase is possible; if imperfect, it is not genuine certainty. However, he allows increase in the sense of clarity or tranquility of soul (itmi'nan). The soul's tranquility toward theoretical certainties varies based on the abundance of evidences. Using empirical observation, he notes that even necessary truths (like two>one) and theological conclusions (like the world's temporality) evoke different levels of tranquility depending on repeated proofs. So if one interprets 'increase of faith' as increase in tranquility, this is permissible. For the second meaning (traditional acceptance), variation is undeniable. Ghazali describes how individuals differ in the firmness of their beliefs: a Jew may be unshakable in his creed, while a Christian or Muslim may be more amenable to change. He likens belief to a knot in the heart, which varies in strength. Only those who lack inner experience deny this variation. For the third meaning (action combined with acceptance), variation in action is obvious, but Ghazali raises a question: does repeated action cause variation in the acceptance itself? He says this requires consideration (nazar), and then gives his own view: perseverance in obedience reinforces the tranquility of the soul toward the belief. Thus, he cautiously affirms a secondary effect of action on the certainty component, but focuses on tranquility rather than the essence of tasdiq.

Error in interpretation is not denial; the shahada's immunity requires certain evidence to be removed.

Commentary
This passage is a proof (dalil) for the prohibition of declaring the fourth rank groups as unbelievers. Ghazali argues from established principles: the only textual basis for takfir is outright denial of the Messenger. Those who err in interpretation do not engage in such denial. Therefore, takfir requires a separate proof, which is lacking. Moreover, the default immunity granted by the shahada (testimony of faith) can only be removed by a certain proof (qati'). Since error in interpretation is not certain proof of disbelief, the immunity remains. Ghazali then criticizes those who exaggerate in takfir as lacking proof, because all evidence is either a direct source (asl) or analogy (qiyas) on a source. The only source for takfir is clear denial, and the interpreters are not in that category. Thus they remain under the general protection of the shahada.

Commentary
This passage defines the fifth rank of people who may be subject to takfir. Ghazali describes someone who does not explicitly deny the Prophet but denies a foundational religious principle known by tawatur (mass transmission), such as the obligation of the five daily prayers or the location of Mecca. Such a person is considered a denier in effect, even if they feign ignorance. However, Ghazali makes an exception: if the person is new to Islam and genuinely lacks exposure to the tawatur, they are given time to learn. He distinguishes between denying a mutawatir matter of practical obligation (like prayer) and denying historical facts (like a battle or marriage of the Prophet), the latter not necessarily warranting takfir because it is not a matter of belief in a foundational principle. The passage then discusses the role of consensus (ijma'), arguing that consensus on theoretical matters does not produce certain knowledge like tawatur does for sensory matters, so rejecting consensus alone does not automatically make one a kafir. He uses the example of al-Nazzam, who denied the authority of consensus, as a case where takfir is not straightforward.

Core Idea
Kufr is rooted in belief, not mere action; all takfir cases fall under six principles.

He emphasizes that takfir must be based on clear, foundational rules, not arbitrary judgments. The key is that every instance of potential kufr can be traced back to one of these six ranks. He then addresses a common objection: is prostration to an idol itself a separate principle? Ghazali explains that the action is a sign of an underlying belief, and that belief is the real cause of kufr.
