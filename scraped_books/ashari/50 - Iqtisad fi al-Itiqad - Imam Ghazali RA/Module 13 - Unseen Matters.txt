Sam‘iyyat: Scriptural Premises in Theological Argumentation
Ghazali defines sam‘iyyat using the example that sins are by God's will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what sam‘iyyat are and how Imam al-Ghazali uses the consensus of the community (ijma‘) to establish a scriptural premise when the opponent denies it.

Core Idea
Scriptural premises rely on revealed authority, and when denied, consensus becomes the proof.

Ghazali has discussed four types of premises; now he introduces the fifth: sam‘iyyat, premises based on scripture. These are essential in arguments about matters like divine will and sin, where reason alone may not be accepted by the opponent. In this lesson, we'll see how he uses an example and demonstrates the role of consensus in shoring up scriptural authority.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the English translation. Notice how Ghazali defines sam‘iyyat and gives a concrete example.
Focus on the flow from definition to example to proof.
2
Identify the structure
Break down the passage: definition, example, two premises, and the response to denial. List the two premises and how each is known.
One premise is known by sense and law, the other is scriptural.
3
Understand the role of consensus
When the opponent denies the second premise, Ghazali resorts to consensus (ijma‘). Why can consensus serve as a scriptural proof? Reflect on the authority of the community in preserving revelation.
This shows that sam‘iyyat are not limited to direct texts; consensus is also a scriptural source.
4
Reflect on the lesson's significance
Consider how this method protects theological arguments from being dismissed by those who reject scripture. Sam‘iyyat are a key tool for engaging with opponents who accept the Law.

The fifth: the scriptural [premises]. Its example: we claim, for instance, that sins are by the will of God. Commentary
This passage presents the fifth type of premise: sam‘iyyat, i.e., premises based on revealed scripture. The example is proving that sins occur by God's will. The argument uses two premises: (1) sins are occurrences (known by sense and their sinfulness by law), (2) everything that occurs is by God's will. If the opponent denies the second premise, Ghazali says that if the opponent accepts the authority of the Law (or if the Law has been proven to him), then we establish this premise by the consensus of the Muslim community (ijma') on the truth of the statement: 'What God wills happens, and what He does not will does not happen.' Thus, the scriptural evidence (sam‘) prevents denial.

The Slave Girl's Pointing: A Clarification of Muteness
Ghazali clarifies the slave girl's gesture was due to muteness.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The slave girl's gesture was a physical necessity, not a theological statement.

A famous hadith tells of a slave girl who pointed to the sky when asked about Allah, and the Prophet declared her a believer. Some have used this as evidence that Allah is spatially above. Ghazali clarifies the real reason she pointed: she was mute. This lesson unpacks that clarification and its importance for preserving divine transcendence.

This connects to the broader Islamic creed that Allah is not confined to space.

Testing faith with theological questions feeds doubt, not faith.

Handling Apparent Texts with Commoners
Ghazali answers an objection about 'istawa' and 'nuzul' with a method for commoners.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's method for handling apparent texts about God's attributes with commoners: removing anthropomorphism and establishing transcendence without engaging in detailed allegorical interpretations.

Core Idea
For commoners, remove tashbih and establish tanzih—no need for detailed ta'wil.

In this passage, Ghazali responds to a question about two well-known scriptural texts that seem to attribute location and movement to God. He outlines a concise method for dealing with such texts, especially when addressing common believers. We will see how he distinguishes between scholars and commoners and what approach he prescribes for the latter.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and Ghazali's initial response
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Note the two texts mentioned and Ghazali's acknowledgment that the full discussion is long.
The objection cites two specific texts: the Quranic verse on 'istawa' and the hadith on 'nuzul'.
2
Identify the two groups of people
Ghazali divides people into commoners and scholars. Notice that his method focuses on the former.
This division is key to his pedagogical approach.
3

Commentary
This passage addresses an objection regarding the distinction between the 'returned' being (al-mu'ad) and a 'like' or similar being (al-mithl) that never existed before. The objector asks: if the original person is annihilated, how can the returned be the very same person (the first) rather than a similar but new being? Ghazali answers by explaining that the nonexistent (al-ma'dum) is divided in God's eternal knowledge into two categories: that which previously existed and that which never existed. Similarly, in pre-eternity, the nonexistent was divided into what will exist and what will never exist. This division in God's knowledge is undeniable. The meaning of 'return' (i'adah) is to bestow existence upon the nonexistent that previously had existence, whereas the 'like' (al-mithl) is to invent existence for a nonexistent that never existed. Thus, the identity is grounded in God's knowledge of the prior existence. Ghazali then refers to his work 'Tahafut al-Falasifah' where he refuted the philosophers by arguing on their own grounds, positing that if the soul (which they consider non-spatial) persists, then it could be reunited with the same body or a different body. However, he notes that this argument is a polemical device (ilzam) that does not align with the actual Ash'ari belief (which does not posit a non-spatial soul as separate in that way). The purpose of that book was to refute the philosophers' doctrine, not to present Ash'ari doctrine.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal exposition of the Balance (Mizan) on the Day of Judgment, addressing two objections. However, since many Quraysh may possess these qualities, a further distinction is needed, which is the appointment (tawliya) or delegation (tafwīḍ) from someone else. The imam is only specified when such appointment occurs specifically for him. Ghazali then discusses the quality of the one who appoints (al-muwallī), stating that not everyone can appoint; it must come from one of three sources: (1) explicit designation (naṣṣ) from the Prophet, (2) explicit designation from the current imam appointing a successor (wālī al-‘ahd) from his children or other Quraysh, or (3) delegation from a man of power (dhū shawka) whose obedience and delegation compel others to follow. This outlines the legitimate methods of imam appointment according to Sunni political theory.

The Order of the Caliphs: Consensus and Revelation
Ghazali explains the ranking of caliphs via consensus and revelation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the ranking of the four Rightly Guided Caliphs is established through the consensus of the Companions and revealed law, as argued by Imam al-Ghazali.

Core Idea
The Companions' consensus on caliphal precedence is the most reliable guide to virtue.

In this passage, Imam al-Ghazali concludes his discussion on the imamate by addressing how we know the order of virtue among the four caliphs. He argues that since inner piety is hidden, we must rely on the consensus of the Companions, who were intimately familiar with the Prophet's guidance. Their agreement on the sequence of Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali is itself a form of revelation-based evidence.

Commentary
This passage concludes the discussion on the imamate by establishing the order of the caliphs (Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, Ali) based on the consensus (ijma') of the Companions and the revealed texts. Ghazali begins with a general principle: outward piety does not guarantee inner purity; only Allah knows the secrets. This sets up the need for revealed knowledge to determine virtue. He then argues that since virtue is only known through revelation and the Prophet's guidance, and the Companions were the closest to the Prophet, their consensus on the order of the caliphs is the most reliable source. He emphasizes that it is inconceivable that the Companions acted out of worldly interests in their consensus. Therefore, the Ahl al-Sunnah adopt this order of precedence in virtue, and after accepting this, they looked for textual evidence (reports) that support this consensus. The passage concludes the section on imamate.
