Introduction to Al-Iqtisad: A Balanced Fortress of Creed
Editor praises the book and describes its place among Ghazali's works

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the significance and position of 'Al-Iqtisad fi al-I'tiqad' among Imam al-Ghazali's works, and what the book aims to cover.

Core Idea
'Al-Iqtisad' is a balanced fortress of creed—detailed but not exhaustive, concise but not shallow.

This introduction to the book 'Al-Iqtisad fi al-I'tiqad' sets the stage for why this work is so valued. It describes the book as a fortified shield for Sunni creed, authored by Imam al-Ghazali at his scholarly peak. The editor explains where it fits among Ghazali's other writings and corrects a common misunderstanding that it is merely a summary. As you read, notice how the editor highlights the book's unique balance between conciseness and thoroughness.

Guided Path
1

2

The editor says 'Al-Iqtisad' is above the conciseness of 'al-Risala al-Qudsiyya' and below the exhaustiveness of his lengthy kalam books. This tells you the book's middle path.
This balance is what 'iqtisad' (economy/moderation) refers to.
3
Note the correction of the misconception
The editor explicitly states that some think the book is limited to mere summaries, but it actually covers principles with proofs. This is an important warning for readers.
Guard against underestimating the depth of this work.

Commentary
This passage is an editorial introduction to the printed edition of 'Al-Iqtisad fi al-I'tiqad'. It begins with the basmala and praise of the Prophet. The editor then declares the book to be a strong fortress for Sunni creed, authored by Imam al-Ghazali. He mentions that Ghazali wrote it at a mature stage of his knowledge, using a poetic phrase that unfortunately is partially unclear in the manuscript. The editor then describes the book's position relative to Ghazali's other works: it is more detailed than the concise 'al-Risala al-Qudsiyya' or 'Qawa'id al-'Aqa'id', but less exhaustive than his lengthy kalam treatises. This means it covers the fundamental principles and proofs of creed, contrary to some who mistakenly think it is merely a summary. The editor notes the scholarly reliance on the book despite complaints about previous editions, and highlights the value of the manuscript used for this edition, which is very close to Ghazali's death. The introduction concludes with a hope that this edition will be beneficial and a cure for people's ailments.

Nizam al-Mulk and political-social reform:
The Seljuqs had a most wholesome effect in the land of Khurasan by appointing Nizam al-Mulk as vizier, about whom the books of history concur in praise. And Nishapur is a piece of the age of Ghazali that gathered the scattered fragments of the images of this age, so that some researchers were pleased to call the fifth century the age of Khurasan. Nizam al-Mulk (408-485 AH) was born, lived, ruled, and died in this century, and he was more than a vizier. Whoever reads his biography and knows him… the station and love of Qiwam al-Din settle in his heart. He was a skillful politician who knew how to repel opponents and fortify the pillars of the state, and a scholar possessing hadith, literature, and language; scholars, reciters, and jurists frequented his assembly. He had a clear inclination toward the Sufis and the people of hearts, and great courtesy toward the Law and its rulings. Among those who attended his assembly were Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni and Abu al-Qasim al-Qushayri, author of al-Risala. And we will see for Imam al-Ghazali a close connection with this unique personality. But… why speak about Nizam al-Mulk?
We do not exaggerate if we say: Nizam al-Mulk had an almost complete awareness of the currents of his time and the details of its events, and this awareness enabled him to treat its difficulties and rid [the state] of its most prominent seditions. His bringing close of scholars – especially the heads of Ahl al-Sunna – was not mere coincidence or a habitual custom, nor was his building of the Nizamiyya schools named after him in the most prominent cities of the Caliphate, such as Baghdad, Nishapur, Tus, and Isfahan, random. These specific cities had their problems and disturbances.

Nizam al-Mulk believed that intellectual and creedal reform is the foundation of all reform, and he knew that suppressing sedition is one thing and uprooting it from its roots is entirely another. There is no way to uproot ideas except by confronting ideas and making the truth clear, not by confronting swords and spears. And we see that Imam al-Ghazali, his teacher al-Juwayni before him, al-Qushayri, al-Shirazi, Ibn al-Sabbagh, and others were on the scene at that time, and they were supporters of this idea; indeed they were the knights of its field after Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari, may Allah be pleased with them all.

So ‘Abd al-Jabbar al-Hamadhani, the two Jubba'is, al-Nazzam, and al-‘Allaf before him, and Ibn Sina and al-Farabi after him, and the secret creeds hiding behind the name of Shi'ism and their ancestors such as al-Muqanna‘ the sorcerer, al-Rawandi, and al-Janabi!! Commentary
This passage is a historical contextualization that sets the stage for Imam al-Ghazali's work. It begins by citing a statement about the spread of various heretical sects (Qadariyya, Jahmiyya, Mu'tazila, etc.) and their common engagement with philosophy, which only deepened their deviation. The author then focuses on the reformer Nizam al-Mulk, the Seljuk vizier. He describes Nizam al-Mulk as a skilled politician and a patron of scholars, especially the Ash'ari Sunni scholars like al-Juwayni and al-Qushayri. The author emphasizes that Nizam al-Mulk understood that intellectual and creedal reform is the foundation of all reform, and that eradicating ideas requires confronting them with arguments, not violence. This principle aligns with the approach of Ghazali and his teacher al-Juwayni. The passage then narrates the assassination of Nizam al-Mulk by the Batiniyya (Qaramita), showing their cowardly and terrorist methods. Finally, it summarizes the complexities of Ghazali's age: rationalist excess (Mu'tazila), deviant Batiniyya, and deceptive philosophy, represented by figures like Ibn Sina and al-Farabi. The author poses the rhetorical question whether a figure like Ghazali was necessary to address these challenges.

Book of Iqtisad fi al-I'tiqad. The books of al-Ghazali, may God have mercy on him, represent a coherent intellectual unity, not [such that] one abrogates another as some claim, but the stages through which the Imam's life passed caused some of them to be colored by the hues of the stage in which they were written, manifesting in seeing the truth from another aspect, and all aspects are true. The Imam's references in books he wrote in the later part of his life to his older books written before his travels... a great proof of that. When was the writing of al-Iqtisad? Without doubt it was after his book al-Tahafut and before his famous travels and seclusion. For in al-Tahafut he intended [with it] a work that establishes the doctrine of the people of truth after demolishing the doctrine of the people of falsehood. And although he stated that this book [i.e., al-Iqtisad] is [the same as] Qawa'id al-'Aqa'id... that does no harm, because of the strong connection between al-Iqtisad and Qawa'id al-'Aqa'id. As for its being before the travels, it is because he relies on it and refers to it in the books of this stage, like al-Ihya' and al-Arba'in. Thus the age of the Imam at the time of composing al-Iqtisad was close to forty. Qawa'id al-'Aqa'id and the book al-Iqtisad: We do not exaggerate if we say that Imam al-Ghazali explained Qawa'id al-'Aqa'id or al-Risala al-Qudsiyya in his book al-Iqtisad, but how could that be, when Qawa'id al-'Aqa'id had not yet been written? Let us listen to Imam al-Ghazali as he defines for us the status and rank of al-Iqtisad: 'As for the first rank of the two ranks—i.e., after knowledge of the creed—which is knowledge of the proofs of the creed... we have entrusted it to al-Risala al-Qudsiyya, about twenty folios in length, which is one of the chapters of the book Qawa'id al-'Aqa'id from the book Ihya' 'Ulum al-Din. And as for its proofs with additional verification and additional elegance in presenting questions and difficulties... we have entrusted them with the book al-Iqtisad fi al-I'tiqad, about one hundred folios in length, and it is a standalone book, containing the core (lubab) of the science of the mutakallimun, but it is more thorough in verification and closer to knocking at the doors of knowledge than the formal discourse found in the books of the mutakallimun.' Qawa'id al-'Aqa'id was present in the Imam's mind at the time of composing al-Iqtisad, then he wrote it down to be close to the hands of the common people during his journey to Jerusalem. This meaning is confirmed when we see Kamal ibn Abi al-Sharif in al-Musamara fi Sharh al-Musayara frequently quoting from al-Iqtisad—and without doubt it was before him when composing al-Musamara, proceeding chapter by chapter—and al-Musayara by Ibn al-Humam was named thus because of its alignment with the book Qawa'id al-'Aqa'id, as is known. The status of al-Iqtisad among the books of kalam: Al-Iqtisad, as its author said of it—'it contains the core of kalam scholars'—took care to be midway between deficiency and tedium. In it he presented the proofs of the people of truth in the manner of the mutakallimun. Thus it is for the level above the level of al-Risala al-Qudsiyya, and a completion of Tahafut al-Falasifa, as he himself established. Relationship with Qawa'id and Ibn al-Subki's Objection
Editorial discussion of the relationship between Iqtisad and Qawa'id and response to Ibn al-Subki.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the relationship between Ghazali's Iqtisad fi al-I'tiqad and his earlier work Qawa'id al-'Aqa'id, and how Ibn al-Subki's critique is addressed.

Core Idea
Iqtisad is the detailed proof version of the creed, while Qawa'id contains the basic creed.

This lesson explores Ghazali's own explanation of the two works and why some scholars like Ibn al-Subki did not consider Iqtisad a proper kalam work. Understanding this clarifies the purpose and audience of Iqtisad.

Guided Path
1
Read Ghazali's classification from al-Arba'in
The editor quotes Ghazali directly to show the distinction: the basic creed (al-Risala al-Qudsiyya within Qawa'id) is for beginners, while Iqtisad contains the detailed proofs with more rigor.
Note that Ghazali himself distinguishes the two works.
2
Understand the purpose of Iqtisad
Iqtisad is described as a standalone book containing the core of kalam, more thorough and closer to knocking at the doors of knowledge than typical kalam books. It is positioned between al-Risala and Tahafut.
3
Examine Ibn al-Subki's objection and the editor's response
Ibn al-Subki claimed he did not find a kalam work by Ghazali. The editor questions this, suggesting that Iqtisad is indeed such a work, and speculates on what Ibn al-Subki might have meant by 'method of the mutakallimun'.
This shows the reception history and debates about Ghazali's works.

Book of Iqtisad fi al-I'tiqad. The books of al-Ghazali, may God have mercy on him, represent a coherent intellectual unity, not [such that] one abrogates another as some claim, but the stages through which the Imam's life passed caused some of them to be colored by the hues of the stage in which they were written, manifesting in seeing the truth from another aspect, and all aspects are true.
