God's Actions: Seven Foundational Ash'ari Claims
Ghazali enumerates seven Ash'ari claims about God's actions and mentions a preface on ethical terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the seven Ash'ari claims about God's actions and the ethical terms that preface them.

Core Idea
God is free from all obligation; His actions are not bound by human notions of duty.

This lesson introduces the third pole of Ghazali's creed, focusing on the actions of God. Ghazali begins by listing seven key claims that Ash'aris affirm, all centered on the idea that nothing is incumbent upon God. He also signals that before diving into these claims, he will clarify the meaning of three ethical terms: obligatory, good, and evil. This orientation prepares you for the detailed arguments that follow.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the seven claims
Read the Arabic and translation, then list each of the seven claims Ghazali makes. Notice they are all negations of obligation or impossibility.
2
Understand the Ash'ari principle behind these claims
Reflect on why Ghazali emphasizes that nothing is obligatory upon God. This is a cornerstone of Ash'ari theology, asserting God's absolute sovereignty.
3
Note the ethical preface
The passage ends by mentioning a preface explaining 'obligatory', 'good', and 'evil'. This will be crucial for understanding the arguments.

Commentary
This passage outlines the third pole, dealing with God's actions. Ghazali lists seven claims that will be proven: (1) God is not obligated to impose religious duties (taklif) upon humans; (2) He is not obligated to create; (3) He is not obligated to reward for obedience; (4) He is not obligated to consider the welfare of servants; (5) It is not impossible for Him to impose a duty beyond one's capacity (taklif ma la yutaq); (6) He is not obligated to punish for sins; (7) It is not impossible for Him to send prophets; rather, it is permissible. Additionally, the preface to this pole will explain the meaning of 'obligatory' (wajib), 'good' (hasan), and 'evil' (qabih). These are foundational Ash'ari positions denying any necessity upon God from external sources.

Objection: Acts of Living Beings and Divine Power
Ghazali presents an objection about whether acts of creatures are under divine power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the dilemma posed by the objector against the doctrine of universal divine power.

Core Idea
The objection forces a choice: either divine power is not universal, or creatures have no real power.

This passage presents a serious objection to the Ash'ari doctrine of universal divine power. The objector argues that if all acts are under God's power, then either acts are between two agents (impossible) or creatures are not truly agents (contradicting religious obligation). Ghazali includes this objection to set up his response.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once and then the translation. Notice the structure: a question, then two possible answers each leading to a problem.
2
Identify the dilemma
The objector presents two options if we say acts are under God's power. Identify each and the consequence that follows.
The two horns: either a single act has two agents, or creatures are not agents.
3
Reflect on why this matters
Ask yourself why this objection is a challenge to the doctrine of universal divine power. How does it relate to human responsibility and religious obligation?
This connects to the problem of free will and divine causality.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection (al-far' al-thani) against the doctrine of universal divine power. The objector argues: you claim divine power extends to all possibles. Are the acts of living beings (animals, humans) among God's power or not? If you say no, you contradict your claim of universality. If you say yes, then either (a) there is a single act between two powerful agents (God and the creature), which is impossible, or (b) you must deny that creatures have power at all, which contradicts the clear evidence of human agency and makes religious obligations meaningless. The objection thus forces a dilemma: either divine power is not universal, or creatures have no real power.

The Two Factions: Jabriyya and Mu'tazila
Ghazali contrasts Jabriyya and Mu'tazila and their consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the positions of the Jabriyya and Mu'tazila on human power and the immediate logical consequences of each extreme.

Core Idea
The middle path between denying human power and giving it independence from Allah.

Imam Ghazali begins by identifying two extreme factions that have divided over the issue of human free will. The Jabriyya deny any human power, while the Mu'tazila affirm human power so strongly that they deny Allah's power over human actions. Understanding their errors sets the stage for the correct Sunni position.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the two factions
Read the Arabic passage and translation carefully. Notice how Ghazali names the two groups: the Jabriyya (or Mujabbira) and the Mu'tazila.
Pay attention to the different consequences he draws for each.
2
Understand the consequence for the Jabriyya
The Jabriyya deny any created power in humans, leading them to deny the distinction between voluntary and involuntary movements, which makes religious obligations impossible.
3
Understand the consequence for the Mu'tazila
The Mu'tazila deny that Allah's power attaches to creatures' actions, claiming that all such acts come from the servant's power alone. This leads them to two great errors.
These two errors are the focus of the next lesson.
4
Notice the introduction of the two errors
Ghazali states that the Mu'tazila are forced into two 'enormities' (شناعتان). Read what they are: denial of the Salaf's consensus and attributing creation to an ignorant power.
The second error will be elaborated with examples in the next lesson.

We say in response: People have differed over this into factions: The Jabriyya said by denial; the Mujabbira went to denial of the human's contingent power, so they were forced to deny the necessity of distinguishing between the movement of tremor and voluntary movement, and they were forced to the impossibility of the legal obligations. And the Mu'tazila went to denial of the attachment of the power of Allah, Most High, to the actions of servants, animals, angels, jinn, and devils, and they claimed that all that issues from them is from the servant's power opposing the creation and invention of the servants, not by the power of Allah, Most High, in either negation or bringing into existence; so they were forced to two great enormities: One of them: denial of what the Salaf, may Allah be pleased with them, unanimously agreed upon, that there is no creator but Allah and no inventor other than Him. And the second: their attribution of invention and creation to a power that does not know what it creates; for the creator of a thing is knowledgeable of it. The movements that issue from humans and other animals: if he were asked about their number, details, and measures, he would have no knowledge of them. And the bee forms its houses in a hexagonal shape, so it does not have a square, nor a circle, nor a heptagon, nor any other shape; that is because the hexagonal shape is distinguished by a property which geometrical proofs indicate, not found in others, and it is based on principles: first: that the most encompassing and capacious of shapes is the circular shape, free of angles and deviating from straightness. Second: that circular shapes, when placed compactly, leave gaps inevitably unused. Third: that the closest of the few-sided shapes to the circular and to encompassing is the hexagonal shape. Fourth: that all shapes close to the circular, such as the heptagon, octagon, and pentagon, when placed compactly adjacent, leave unused gaps and are not contiguous.
Source pages p. 154

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of two extreme sects regarding human free will and divine power. The author, Imam Ghazali, first identifies the two factions: the Jabriyya (or Mujabbira) who deny any human power, and the Mu'tazila who affirm human power so strongly that they deny Allah's power over human actions. He explains the consequences of each. For the Jabriyya, they deny the distinction between involuntary movements (like tremors) and voluntary ones, and thus make legal obligation impossible. For the Mu'tazila, they attribute all actions of living beings to their own created power, leaving no room for Allah's power in those acts. This leads them to two great errors: first, they contradict the consensus of the Salaf that there is no creator but Allah; second, they attribute creation to a being that does not know what it creates, since a creature cannot possibly know the details and quantities of his own movements. Ghazali provides examples: an infant crawling to the breast, a kitten seeking its mother's milk with eyes closed, a spider weaving intricate webs that engineers marvel at—all these actions occur without the agent's knowledge of the complexity involved. He then focuses on bees building hexagonal honeycombs. He explains that the hexagonal shape is optimal for space and efficiency, proven by geometrical principles: the circle is the most capacious but leaves gaps when packed; shapes close to the circle in packing efficiency are those with many sides, but among the few-sided polygons, the hexagon is closest to the circle and allows seamless tiling without gaps. This shows that the bee's construction is a result of divine guidance, not its own knowledge, refuting the Mu'tazila claim that the agent must know its act. Thus, the passage argues that since animals perform complex, purposeful actions without knowledge, the Mu'tazila's attribution of creation to them is absurd, and all such acts must be attributed to Allah.

The Second Great Error: Attributing Creation to an Ignorant Agent
Ghazali refutes the second error of Mu'tazila using animal examples that act without knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali uses examples from animal behavior to prove that creatures act without knowledge, thereby refuting the Mu'tazila claim that creatures create their own actions.

Core Idea
Animals perform complex acts without knowledge, proving creation is from Allah alone.

Having introduced the second error of the Mu'tazila, Ghazali now provides a detailed proof using examples from nature. He shows that infants, kittens, spiders, and especially bees perform intricate actions without any knowledge of their complexity, demonstrating that their creator must be Allah, not themselves.

Analyze the bee's geometric construction
Focus on the four principles Ghazali lists about why bees use hexagons. Understand that this shows divine guidance, not bee knowledge.
The hexagon is the optimal shape for packing without gaps.
4
Connect to the second error
Ghazali's conclusion is that since these creatures lack knowledge of their own complex acts, attributing creation to them is absurd. Thus, Allah is the true creator of all actions.
This refutes the second error of the Mu'tazila.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of two extreme sects regarding human free will and divine power. The author, Imam Ghazali, first identifies the two factions: the Jabriyya (or Mujabbira) who deny any human power, and the Mu'tazila who affirm human power so strongly that they deny Allah's power over human actions. He explains the consequences of each. For the Jabriyya, they deny the distinction between involuntary movements (like tremors) and voluntary ones, and thus make legal obligation impossible. For the Mu'tazila, they attribute all actions of living beings to their own created power, leaving no room for Allah's power in those acts. This leads them to two great errors: first, they contradict the consensus of the Salaf that there is no creator but Allah; second, they attribute creation to a being that does not know what it creates, since a creature cannot possibly know the details and quantities of his own movements. Ghazali provides examples: an infant crawling to the breast, a kitten seeking its mother's milk with eyes closed, a spider weaving intricate webs that engineers marvel at—all these actions occur without the agent's knowledge of the complexity involved. He then focuses on bees building hexagonal honeycombs. He explains that the hexagonal shape is optimal for space and efficiency, proven by geometrical principles: the circle is the most capacious but leaves gaps when packed; shapes close to the circle in packing efficiency are those with many sides, but among the few-sided polygons, the hexagon is closest to the circle and allows seamless tiling without gaps. This shows that the bee's construction is a result of divine guidance, not its own knowledge, refuting the Mu'tazila claim that the agent must know its act. Thus, the passage argues that since animals perform complex, purposeful actions without knowledge, the Mu'tazila's attribution of creation to them is absurd, and all such acts must be attributed to Allah.

The author answers by explaining that God's creation of the movement and the power over it in the servant is conceivable. The servant's power is created by God, so God is the sole creator of both power and act. The servant is powerful (qādir) but not a creator; his role is acquisition (kasb). This clarifies how two powers relate to the same act without conflict.

The Scope of Divine Will
Ghazali argues that all events, including evil, are willed by God, refuting the Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sunni theologians hold that all events, including evil and sin, are willed by God, and how this responds to the Mu'tazilite objection.

Core Idea
All events are willed by God because every created thing needs both power and will.

Ghazali takes us through a logical chain: everything that happens is created by God's power, and every act of power requires a will to direct it. The conclusion is that everything that occurs — even evil and sin — is willed by God. This is the orthodox Sunni position, and Ghazali shows why the Mu'tazilite alternative leads to absurdity.

Al-Isfara'ini distinguishes between what belongs to the servant and what belongs to God: if guidance were the servant's right, then withholding would be injustice; but guidance is God's possession, so He may grant it to whom He wills. This argument silences the opponent. The second illustration from Ibn 'Abbas makes the same point: a man asks whether God wrongs him by withholding guidance. Ibn 'Abbas applies the same logic: if guidance were the man's property, withholding would be wrong; but since it is God's, He may give or withhold it without injustice. Ibn 'Abbas's sharp dismissal ('do not sit with me') indicates the seriousness of the theological error.

The Obligation of the Impossible: A Rational Possibility
Ghazali argues for the intellectual possibility of taklif al-muhal and compares muhal lidhatihi and lighayrihi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali argues that obligating the impossible is intellectually possible, and how he distinguishes between muhal lidhatihi and muhal lighayrihi.

Core Idea
Obliging the impossible is rationally conceivable even if the act cannot be performed.

In this passage from al-Iqtisad, Ghazali addresses a key theological puzzle: can Allah command something that no human can do? He defends the Ash'ari position that such obligation is intellectually possible, paving the way for discussing divine omnipotence and human accountability. Let's examine his argument step by step.

Commentary
This passage presents an argument about the obligation of the impossible (taklif al-muhal). It begins by stating that denying the possibility of obliging something impossible would be a denial of reason itself, because every faction is forced to admit that a command can be conceived even if compliance is inconceivable, as in the case of the disbeliever who is commanded to believe while having only the power for disbelief. The author then compares the Ash'ari and Mu'tazili views on power. According to the Ash'aris, power is only present simultaneously with the act, so before the act the servant has no power. According to the Mu'tazila, power can exist but is insufficient without conditions such as will; one condition is that the act must not entail a change in Allah's knowledge, which is impossible. The conclusion is that obligation concerning what is impossible due to an external factor (muhal lighayrihi) is analogous to obligation concerning what is impossible in itself (muhal lidhatihi); there is no difference regarding the possibility of verbally stating the command, conceptualizing the requirement, or moral judgment. The argument supports the Ash'ari position that taklif al-muhal is intellectually possible, even if the act is not possible from the servant's side.
