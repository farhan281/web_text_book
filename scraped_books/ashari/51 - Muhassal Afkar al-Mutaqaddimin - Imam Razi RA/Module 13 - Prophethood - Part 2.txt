Refuting Claims of Angelic Superiority
The author refutes three Qur'anic arguments for angelic superiority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Razi refutes three Qur'anic arguments for angelic superiority over humans.

Core Idea
Verses cited for angelic superiority are misinterpreted.

Imam al-Razi engages in a critical examination of verses that some claim prove angels are superior to humans. He carefully shows that each verse, when properly understood, does not support such superiority. This lesson walks through his three refutations.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and translation. Notice the author quotes three verses and gives a reason why each does not prove superiority.
The passage is brief but packed with references.
2
Understand the first refutation
The first verse (about Iblis) might show superiority at the time of addressing Iblis, but not after Adam was chosen (ijtiba'). Understand the distinction.
The key term 'الاجتباء' (election) is important.
3
Understand the second refutation
The second verse (angels refusing to prostrate) does not indicate superiority over the Messiah because it is contextualized by polytheists' claim about angels being daughters of God.
The author argues the verse is a response to polytheists, not a statement of superiority.
4
Understand the third refutation
The third verse (about beauty) only shows people's imagination that angels are more beautiful, not that they are superior.
This refutes a common anthropomorphic argument.
5
Reflect on the overall method
Notice how Imam al-Razi refutes each proof by contextualizing the verse and clarifying its actual meaning.
This is a model of tafsir-based theological argument.

(1) أقول: لو دلت الآية الأولى على تفضيل الملك على آدم وقت مخاطبة إبليس لكنها ما دلت على تفضيله عليهما بعد الاجتباء، وفي الآية الثانية في الاستنكاف عن الملائكة لا يدل على تفضيلهم على المسيح بل إنما ذكرهم بعد المسيح الذي قتل النصارى إنه ابن الله لدلالة المشركين أنهم بنات الرحمن، والآية الثالثة تدل على تخيل النساء أن جمال الملك يكون أكثر من جمال البشر لا على تفضيل الملك على البشر.
(1) I say: If the first verse indicated the superiority of the angel over Adam at the time of addressing Iblis, it does not indicate his superiority over them after election (i.e., after Adam was chosen). And the second verse, concerning the angels' refusal to prostrate, does not indicate their superiority over the Messiah; rather, it mentions them after the Messiah whom the Christians killed claiming he is the son of God, indicating that the polytheists said they (angels) are daughters of the Merciful. And the third verse indicates the imagination of people that an angel's beauty is greater than human beauty, not the superiority of angels over humans.

Commentary
This passage is a critical note by the author responding to an argument that certain Qur'anic verses prove angels are superior to humans. He examines three verses. The first verse, if it shows angelic superiority over Adam at the time of Iblis's rebuke, does not prove superiority after Adam was chosen (اجتباء). The second verse is about angels refusing to prostrate to Adam, but it does not imply their superiority over Jesus; rather it is mentioned in the context of polytheists claiming angels as daughters of God. The third verse only suggests that people imagine angels to be more beautiful, not that they are superior. Thus the author refutes the claim that these verses establish angelic superiority.

Refutation of Spirits' Knowledge of Future
Refutation of spirits' knowledge of future

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the philosophical claim that spirits know future events contradicts their own doctrines.

Core Idea
Universal knowledge cannot yield particular futures.

Philosophers often attribute knowledge of future events to spiritual beings. Sanusi shows that this claim is internally inconsistent because spirits possess universal, not particular, knowledge. Let's examine his refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic sentence and its translation carefully. Note the key contradiction the author points out.
The passage uses 'علومهم كلية' and 'علومهم فعلية'.
2
Identify the claim
The opponent claims spirits know our future states. The author counters that their knowledge is universal, not particular.
3
Understand the contradiction
If their knowledge is actual (فعلية), it can only be of what they cause. But future states are not caused by spirits. Hence they cannot know them.
This also implies that lower spirits cannot know higher ones.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
اطلاعهم على مستقبل أحوالنا يناقض قوله، لأن علومهم كلية وقوله وعلومهم فعلية يقتضى أنها لا تعلم إلا له لأنها ليست بفاعلة إياه ولا يعلم السافل منها ما هو أعلى درجة منه. وأما عكوفهم على العبادة فمن شأن النفوس السامرية عندهم التي تحرك أجسامها تقربا إلى مباديها. وقوله: الروحانيات تقوى على تصريف السحاب والزلازل فهذا أخرج العقول عن الروحانيات لأنها لا تباشر الأجسام والرياح والأبخرة التي تصرف الرياح وتعمل الزلازل ليست بعقول ولا نفوس. وفي قوله الجسمانيات اختياراتها غير جازمة أخرج النفوس البشرية عن الروحانيات، وفي قوله الروحانيات مختصة بالهياكل العلوية والجسمانيات بالهياثل الفاسدة أخرج المقول من الروحانيات وجعل النفوس البشرية جسمانية. وقوله الأرواح الفلكية هي المدرات أمر خاص بالفرس السماوية وخرج العقول من الروحانيات. وقوله: هي المبدأ والمعاد لا يقول به أحد فإن الفلاسفة يقولون إن المبدأ من الله والمداد إليه لا من النفوس وإليها، أما الأول فظهر، وأما الثاني فإن كمال النفوس الإنسانية وغاية سعيها معرفة الله والتوجه بالكلية إليه وهو المراد من العود إليه.
Their knowledge of our future states contradicts his saying, because their knowledge is universal and his saying that their knowledge is actual requires that they do not know except Him (?) because they are not the doer of it, and the lower of them does not know what is higher in degree. As for their devotion to worship, it is the nature of the Samarian souls in their view, which move their bodies in proximity to their principles. And his saying: 'The spiritual beings have power over the disposal of clouds and earthquakes' – this removes intellects from the spiritual beings because they do not directly handle bodies, and winds and vapors which direct winds and cause earthquakes are not intellects or souls. And in his saying 'the corporeal beings have non-definitive choices' – he excluded human souls from spiritual beings, and in his saying 'the spiritual beings are specific to the celestial structures and the corporeal beings to the corrupt structures' – he excluded the intellect from spiritual beings and made human souls corporeal. And his saying 'the celestial souls are the causes' – a matter specific to the celestial souls, and he excluded intellects from spiritual beings. And his saying 'they are the principle and the return' – no one says that, for the philosophers say that the principle is from God and the return is to Him, not from souls and to them. As for the first, it is clear; as for the second, the perfection of human souls and the goal of their striving is knowledge of God and complete orientation towards Him, and that is what is meant by the return to Him.

Commentary
This passage is a series of refutations against various philosophical claims about spirits. The author attacks several statements made by an opponent (likely a philosopher). The first refutation concerns the claim that spirits have knowledge of future events; the author argues that their universal knowledge does not entail knowledge of future particulars. The second refutation addresses the claim that spiritual beings can control clouds and earthquakes; the author notes that such actions involve direct contact with bodies, which spirits do not do. The third refutation deals with the classification of beings into spiritual (associated with celestial structures) and corporeal (associated with corrupt structures); the author argues this excludes human souls from spiritual beings. The fourth refutation addresses the idea that celestial souls are the causes; this is rejected. Finally, the author rejects the notion that spirits are the principle and return, affirming that the principle is God and the return is to Him, with human perfection being knowledge of God.

Correction: The author shows that spirits' knowledge is universal and actual, precluding knowledge of particulars they do not cause.

Their knowledge of our future states contradicts his saying, because their knowledge is universal and his saying that their knowledge is actual requires that they do not know except Him (?) because they are not the doer of it, and the lower of them does not know what is higher in degree. As for their devotion to worship, it is the nature of the Samarian souls in their view, which move their bodies in proximity to their principles. And his saying: 'The spiritual beings have power over the disposal of clouds and earthquakes' – this removes intellects from the spiritual beings because they do not directly handle bodies, and winds and vapors which direct winds and cause earthquakes are not intellects or souls. And in his saying 'the corporeal beings have non-definitive choices' – he excluded human souls from spiritual beings, and in his saying 'the spiritual beings are specific to the celestial structures and the corporeal beings to the corrupt structures' – he excluded the intellect from spiritual beings and made human souls corporeal. And his saying 'the celestial souls are the causes' – a matter specific to the celestial souls, and he excluded intellects from spiritual beings. And his saying 'they are the principle and the return' – no one says that, for the philosophers say that the principle is from God and the return is to Him, not from souls and to them. As for the first, it is clear; as for the second, the perfection of human souls and the goal of their striving is knowledge of God and complete orientation towards Him, and that is what is meant by the return to Him.

The first refutation concerns the claim that spirits have knowledge of future events; the author argues that their universal knowledge does not entail knowledge of future particulars. The second refutation addresses the claim that spiritual beings can control clouds and earthquakes; the author notes that such actions involve direct contact with bodies, which spirits do not do. The third refutation deals with the classification of beings into spiritual (associated with celestial structures) and corporeal (associated with corrupt structures); the author argues this excludes human souls from spiritual beings. The fourth refutation addresses the idea that celestial souls are the causes; this is rejected. Finally, the author rejects the notion that spirits are the principle and return, affirming that the principle is God and the return is to Him, with human perfection being knowledge of God.

Refutation of Spirits as Principle and Return
Refutation of spirits as principle and return

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why philosophers do not actually believe that spirits are the principle and final return.

Core Idea
The principle is God, and the return is to God.

Some attribute to philosophers the idea that spirits are the ultimate source and destination. Sanusi corrects this: philosophers themselves affirm that the principle is from God and the return is to Him, not to spirits.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic sentence. Note the author's assertion that no philosopher says this.
2
Understand the philosophical position
Philosophers say the principle is from God and the return is to God. This is the correct view.
3
Understand the second part
The return to God means the perfection of human souls through knowledge of God and orientation towards Him.
