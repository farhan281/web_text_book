First Proof: The Necessity of a Preponderant Cause for Choice
Razi gives a rational proof for the Ash'arite position on divine determination of acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational argument that human acts are ultimately determined by a preponderant cause not from the servant.

Core Idea
Every choice requires a preponderant cause, which ultimately comes from Allah.

Imam Razi begins his case for divine determination with a logical proof. This argument challenges the Mu'tazilite notion of independent human agency by examining the relationship between the ability to refrain and the occurrence of an act. Through a series of conditional statements, he demonstrates that either the act is necessitated or an infinite regress results, leading to the conclusion that the servant is not independent.

Guided Path
1
Understand the starting point
Read the first sentence: the servant either can or cannot refrain from the act. This sets up a dichotomy.
If he cannot refrain, Mu'tazilite free will is immediately refuted.
2
If he can refrain, why does he act?
The argument then asks whether the act over refraining requires a preponderant cause (murajjih). Recognize that without a cause, it would be arbitrary preference, which is impossible.
This is a key move: a preference without cause is irrational.
3
Trace the regression of causes
If the preponderant is from the servant's own act, the same question recurs, leading to infinite regress. So it must end in an external preponderant.
This external cause is ultimately from Allah.
4
Analyze the two possibilities after the preponderant
Once the external preponderant is obtained, either the act may not occur (leading to arbitrary preference) or it must occur (necessitation). Both lead to determinism.
Thus, the servant is not independent in choice.

لنا وجوه . الأول : أن العبد حال الفعل إما أن يمكنه الترك أولا يمكنه فإن لم يمكنه الترك فقد بطل قول المعتزلة وإن أمكنه ، فإما أن لا يفتقر ترجيح الفعل على الترك إلى مرجح وهو باطل ، لأنه تجويز لأحد طرفي الممكن على الآخر لا لمرجع ، أو يفتقر ذلك المرجح إن كان من فعله عاد التقسيم وإلا يتسلل، بل ينتهى لا محالة إلى مرجح لا يكون من فعله ، ثم عند حصول ذلك المرجح إن أمكن أن لا يحصل ذلك الفعل فلنفرض ذلك وحينئذ يحصل الفعل تارة ولا يحصل أخرى ، مع أن نسبة ذلك المرجح إلى الوقتين على السواء ، فاختصاص أحد الوقتين بالحصول ووقت الآخر بعدم الحصول يكون ترجيحا لأحد طرفي الممكن المتساوى على الآخر من غير مرجح وهو محال ، وإن امتنع أن لا يحصل فقد بطال قول المعتزلة بالكلية ، لأنه متى حصل المرجح وجب الفعل ومتى لم يحصل امتنع فلم يكن العبد مستقلا بالاختيار فهذا كلام قاطع .
We have proofs. The first: That the servant at the time of the act either can refrain (tark) or cannot. If he cannot refrain, then the Mu'tazilite doctrine is invalidated. If he can, then either the preponderance (tarjih) of the act over refraining does not need a preponderant cause (murajjih) – and this is false, because it is allowing one side of a possible over the other without a preponderant – or it needs a preponderant. If that preponderant is from his own act, the division recurs; otherwise it goes on, but inevitably ends at a preponderant not from his act. Then, when that preponderant is obtained, if it is possible that the act does not occur, let us suppose that. Then the act sometimes occurs and sometimes does not, even though the relation of that preponderant to both times is equal. So the specification of one time for occurrence and the other for non-occurrence would be a preponderance of one side of an equally possible over the other without a preponderant, which is impossible. And if it is impossible that it not occur, then the Mu'tazilite doctrine is completely invalidated, because whenever the preponderant occurs the act is necessitated, and when it does not occur the act is impossible, so the servant is not independent in choice. This is a decisive argument.

Commentary
This passage is a rational proof (wajh) for the Ash'arite view that human acts are ultimately determined by God. The argument proceeds by examining whether the servant can refrain from an act. If he cannot, Mu'tazilite free will is false. If he can, then a preponderant cause (murajjih) is needed to explain why he acts rather than refrains. That preponderant cannot be the servant's own act without infinite regress, so it must be external. Then whether the act is contingent upon this preponderant or not, either choice leads to determinism: either the act may not follow even with the preponderant (leading to arbitrary preference without cause) or it must follow, so the act is necessitated, not free.

The Second Proof: Human Ignorance of Details
Razi presents a proof that if humans created acts, they'd know details, but they don't.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that if humans created their own actions, they would necessarily have detailed knowledge of those actions, which they lack.

Core Idea
If we create our acts, we must know their details—but we don't.

Razi presents a logical argument that if humans were the actual creators of their own actions, they would need to have detailed knowledge of every aspect of those actions. Since we observe that humans lack such detailed knowledge, especially in cases like swimming or slow movements, the premise that humans create their own acts is false. This lesson walks through the proof and its three supporting examples.

Guided Path
1
Understand the core logical structure
Read the passage to see how Razi connects creation with knowledge: if you create something, you must intend it particularly, and that requires particular knowledge.
The key link is 'particular intention requires particular knowledge'.
2
Examine each of the three examples
The passage gives three reasons why humans lack detailed knowledge of their acts: the swimmer, the slow movement, and the Mu'tazilite causality. Identify each and see how it supports the conclusion.
3
Connect back to the overall proof
After seeing the examples, confirm that the conclusion is that since humans do not know the details, they cannot be the creators of their acts.
This proof is used to argue for divine determination (qadar).

The second: If the servant were the bringer-into-existence (mujid) of his own actions, he would know their details. For if we permitted bringing-into-existence without knowledge, the proof of establishing God's omniscience would be invalidated, because the universal intention is not sufficient for the occurrence of the particular, since the universal relates to all particulars equally, so the occurrence of some rather than the rest would not be preponderant. Thus it is established that particular intention is necessary, and it is conditioned by particular knowledge. So it is established that if he were the bringer-into-existence of his own actions, he would know their details. And permitting bringing-into-existence without knowledge does not invalidate the proof of God's omniscience, because those who affirm omniscience do not infer it from the act of bringing-into-existence itself, but rather from the perfection and precision of the act. This passage is the second proof for divine determination, arguing that if humans created their own acts, they would need detailed knowledge of those acts, but they lack such knowledge. The marginal note counters that bringing-into-existence does not require knowledge, using fire as an example, and critiques the proof's premises.

Rebuttal: Creation Does Not Require Knowledge
The note rebuts Razi's proof by denying that creation implies knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the counter-argument that bringing-into-existence does not necessarily entail knowledge of the thing brought into existence, and how this undercuts Razi's proof.

Core Idea
Fire burns without knowing it; creation does not imply knowledge.

A critical note counters Razi's proof by arguing that the act of bringing-into-existence itself does not require the agent to have knowledge of the product. Examples like fire burning wood show that effects can occur without awareness. Moreover, the proof's reliance on particular intention is questioned, and the inference of God's omniscience from the perfection of acts is distinguished from creation itself.

Guided Path
1
Read the rebuttal passage carefully
The note begins with 'I say:' and presents a direct critique. Understand its main claim: bringing-into-existence does not necessitate knowledge.
This is a common objection to arguments from ignorance.
2
Analyze the counterexample of fire
Fire burns a piece of wood without any knowledge of the wood. This shows that an agent can cause an effect without having knowledge of the effect.
This attacks the core premise that creation implies knowledge.
3
Examine the second objection: how God's omniscience is proven
The note argues that those who affirm God's omniscience do not infer it from the act of creation itself, but from the wisdom and perfection in creation.
This preserves the proof of God's omniscience while rejecting Razi's argument.

Third Proof: Conflict of Divine and Human Power
Razi presents a proof and its refutation regarding conflicting divine and human wills.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's third proof regarding the conflict of divine and human power over a single object, and why the marginal note refutes it by asserting the superiority of divine power.

Core Idea
Divine power is not equal to human power; God's will always prevails.

In this passage, Imam Razi presents a philosophical argument about what happens when God and a human have conflicting wills for the same object. He then provides a marginal note that criticizes the argument, showing that divine power is superior. This lesson will guide you through the argument and its critique.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the literal translation. Notice the structure: a main proof followed by a marginal note starting with 'I say'.
2
Identify the main argument
The third proof argues that if two powers are equal, preponderance (tarjih) is impossible, so either both fail (impossible) or one yields (false). Trace the reasoning.
3
Analyze the marginal note's refutation
The note counters that powers are not equal; God's power is greater. It provides examples and connects to the proof of mutual prevention (tamanu').
4
Understand the connection to the tamanu' argument
The proof is taken from the argument against multiple gods, where equality is assumed. Here, equality does not hold, so the proof fails.
5
Synthesize the lesson
Ask yourself: Why does the marginal note reject the proof? What does this teach about divine power?

The Impossibility of Two Independent Causes on One Effect
Razi presents a counterexample to the Mu'tazilite claim of generated actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that tawallud (generated actions) is invalid because it would imply that two independent agents can combine on a single effect, which is rationally impossible.

Core Idea
Two causes cannot produce one effect independently.

In this passage, Imam al-Razi takes on the Mu'tazilite theory of generated actions (tawallud) by presenting a thought experiment. If two people simultaneously pull and push the same object, the resulting movement cannot be attributed to either one alone. This leads to a rational impossibility, disproving the theory.

Guided Path
1
Understand the scenario
Read the scenario of Zayd and 'Amr pulling and pushing the same object. Notice that the object's movement cannot be more likely caused by one than the other.
This sets up a symmetrical situation.
2
Identify the two possibilities
The movement must be caused by both agents together or by neither. There is no third option.
These are the only logical possibilities.
3
Examine the impossibility of 'both'
If both caused it, two independent causes would combine on one effect. This is impossible because each independent cause alone would be sufficient, leading to contradiction.
Razi refers to a previously established principle.
4
Conclude 'by neither'
Since both together is impossible, the movement must be caused by neither agent. This shows that the direct agent's action does not necessarily generate the effect.
This refutes the Mu'tazilite claim.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal question (mas'ala) refuting the Mu'tazilite theory of tawallud (generated actions), according to which an agent's act directly causes a subsequent effect that is generated from it. Razi provides a proof by counterexample: if a body part is simultaneously pulled and pushed by two different persons, then the resulting movement cannot be attributed exclusively to either one – it is either caused by both (impossible on the principle of two independent causes for one effect) or by neither. The latter shows that the movement is not necessarily generated by the direct agent. The Mu'tazila appeal to moral accountability: we command and prohibit killing and breaking, which implies the agent causes these effects. Razi answers that God's custom (jariyah) creates the effects subsequent to the direct act, so command and prohibition are still sound. This suffices for the validity of moral address without requiring genuine causal generation by the human agent.

Answering the Moral Accountability Objection
Razi answers the moral objection by appealing to God's custom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi answers the Mu'tazilite objection that without generated actions, commands and prohibitions regarding killing and breaking would be meaningless.

Core Idea
God's custom (ʿādah) creates effects, not human causation.

The Mu'tazila argued that if human acts don't generate effects, then commanding and prohibiting killing or breaking would be futile. Razi responds by invoking God's established custom: He creates the effects subsequent to the human act, so commands remain meaningful.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Mu'tazilite objection
The Mu'tazila appeal to the goodness of commanding and prohibiting acts like killing and breaking. This implies that the human agent causes the effect, otherwise the command would be pointless.
They assume causation is needed for moral address.
2
Razi's answer about God's custom
Razi says God has established a custom (ʿādah) of creating these effects in the direct agent after the agent's act occurs. So the effect is not caused by the agent but created by God following a consistent pattern.
This preserves the validity of command and prohibition.
3
Why this suffices
Because the command and prohibition are directed at the act that is followed by the effect according to God's custom. The agent is still responsible for initiating the act, and the effect reliably follows, so moral address makes sense.
No need for genuine causal generation.

Refuting the Mutazila Theory of Generated Acts
Razi presents a counterexample to refute the Mutazila claim of generated acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's logical argument against the Mutazila claim that human actions can generate effects in other objects.

Core Idea
Two opposing forces cannot both cause the same effect—so generated acts are impossible.

The Mutazila believed that when we move our hand, the motion of an object our hand touches is 'generated' (tawallud) from our action. Razi challenges this with a clever example: if a particle is pulled by one hand and pushed by another, its motion cannot be attributed to either alone. In this lesson, we will examine Razi's proof and see why it undermines the doctrine of generated acts.

The example involves two hands acting on the same particle.
2
Analyze the logical structure

The impossibility of two independent agents causing one effect is key.

Consider the implications

This argument supports Ash'ari occasionalism.

Distinguishing Divine Will and Command
Razi distinguishes two types of divine will to answer the command objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between God's will for His own act and His will for another's act, and how this addresses the objection that commanding belief implies God wills belief.

Core Idea
Command indicates the second will (for another's act), not the first will (for His own act).

In this marginal note, Razi refines the argument from divine command. The opponent argues that God commands the disbeliever to believe, and command indicates will, so God must will disbelief? Razi responds by distinguishing two types of divine will: His will for His own act (creating faith?) and His will for another's act. Command indicates the latter, not the former. Similarly, obedience is conformity to the second will. He also addresses the issue of contentment with divine decree, clarifying that contentment with disbelief as God's decree is obedience, not assent to disbelief itself.

Guided Path
1
Read the marginal note carefully
This note contains several intertwined arguments. Read the Arabic and translation slowly, noting the distinctions.
Look for the phrases 'الإرادة الأولى' and 'الإرادة الثانية'.
2
Identify the two wills distinction
Razi divides God's will into: will for His own act (first) and will for another's act (second). Command indicates the second will.
This is a key Ashʿarī distinction to avoid implying that God wills disbelief.
3
Understand the contentment objection and answer
The opponent says 'I am content with God's decree' which would imply contentment with disbelief. Razi clarifies: contentment is with the decree as God's act, not with the disbelief itself.
The phrase 'رضيت بقضاء الله' does not mean approval of the content (disbelief) but of God's attribute of decree.

Critique of the Pulling/Pushing Example and the Mutazila Theory of Generation
Razi critiques the pulling/pushing example and explains Mutazila view on generation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Razi rejects the example of two agents pulling and pushing, and how the Mutazila theory of generation (tawallud) attempts to ground commanding and forbidding.

Core Idea
The example of pulling/pushing fails because force is divisible, not because two agents cannot act.

In this second marginal note, Razi critiques an example previously used (perhaps by the Mutazila) to illustrate the impossibility of generated acts. The example involves two agents pulling and pushing the same object. Razi argues it is not applicable because the body's force is divisible, so each agent can contribute a part. He then explains the Mutazila theory of generation (tawallud), where the agent directly performs i'timad (leaning) which generates motion, and their argument that if generated acts were not necessitating, then commanding would not be good. This note also clarifies the concept of 'i'timad' and 'generation'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the example and its critique
Read the first part where Razi says the example of al-jadhb wa al-raf' (pulling and pushing) is not applicable. Understand that the objection is about two agents acting on one object.
Razi says 'because the body's force is divisible' — meaning the forces can be distributed.
2
Understand the Mutazila theory of generation
The passage then describes the Mutazila view: the agent does i'timad (leaning) which generates motion. The agent necessitates the motion by generation in a separate thing.
I'timad is a direct action, motion is generated.
3
Grasp their argument for commanding and forbidding
The Mutazila argue that if the generated act (the motion) were not necessitated by the agent, then it would not be good to command the act obtained through generation.
This shows the importance of causation for their ethical framework.

The Necessity of Multiplicity in the Cause
Razi argues against the principle 'from one only one' and shows why multiplicity requires a multiple cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's critique of the emanationist principle 'from one only one' and his argument that multiplicity in effects requires multiplicity in the cause.

Core Idea
If only one effect comes from one cause, all things would be a single causal chain—which is false.

Razi begins by testing the philosophers' emanation theory. If each cause produces only one effect, then every pair of things would be directly cause and effect—contradicting the observed diversity of independent existences. To avoid this, the philosophers must admit that some causes produce multiple effects, which requires an internal multiplicity in the cause. This leads them to propose a composite nature in the first intellect.

2
Examine the philosophers' solution
Read the part where the philosophers assign possibility and existence as two aspects of the first intellect, making it a composite cause.
Notice that they attribute possibility to the intellect itself and existence from God.
3
Reflect on the implications
Ask yourself: if the first intellect has a composite nature, does that not violate the simplicity of the First Cause? This sets up Razi's later critique.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the emanation theory. First, Razi argues that if the principle 'from one only one' holds, then the world would be a chain of single causes, which is false because we observe multiplicity. So there must be a cause with multiple effects, requiring multiplicity in the cause. The philosophers attempt to explain multiplicity by saying the first intellect has something from itself (possibility) and something from God (existence). They then assign possibility as the cause of the sphere and existence as the cause of the next intellect, leading to a hierarchy. Razi declares this false because it relies on two invalid premises: (1) the principle that from one only one proceeds, already refuted, and (2) that possibility is an efficient cause. He then proves that possibility is not an existential thing but a relative non-existence, so it cannot cause existence. He also shows that if possibility were a cause, it would lead to contradictions like making a possible thing necessary. Finally, he points out that even in a single sphere there are multiple realities (matter, form, etc.), contradicting the claim of a single effect.

Impossibility of Possibility as a Cause
Razi refutes the notion that possibility is an efficient cause and proves it is non-existential.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's critique of the claim that possibility can be an efficient cause, and why he argues possibility is non-existential.

Core Idea
Possibility is non-existential, so it cannot cause anything existential.

After exposing the philosophers' solution to the multiplicity problem, Razi now attacks its foundations. He declares the entire emanationist scheme invalid because it rests on two false premises: the already-refuted 'from one only one' principle, and the assumption that possibility is an efficient cause. Razi then presents a rigorous argument demonstrating that possibility cannot be an existential entity, making it impossible to be a cause of existence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two invalid premises
Read the passage from 'And know that this is false' to '...so it is impossible that it be a cause of an existential thing.' Notice that Razi lists two bases: the 'from one only one' principle and possibility as a cause.
The first premise was already discussed; the second is the focus here.
2
Follow the argument against possibility
Razi argues that if possibility were existential, then it would be either necessary or dependent, both leading to contradictions. Understand each step.
He uses proof by contradiction: assuming possibility is existential, then it must be necessary (impossible) or have a cause, which leads to infinite regress or requiring the Necessary Existence to cause both possibility and existence.
3
Grasp the conclusion
Razi concludes that possibility is a non-existential thing, so it cannot be a cause of something existential.
This is a key point in Islamic theology: possibility is a relative notion, not a positive entity.

Equality of Possibilities and Multiplicity in the Sphere
Razi refutes emanation by equality of possibilities and the internal multiplicity of spheres.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's additional arguments against emanation: the equality of possibilities leads to self-causation contradictions, and even a single sphere contains multiple realities.

Core Idea
If possibility causes existence, then every possible thing would cause itself, and spheres are not simple.

Razi continues his refutation with two further points. First, he argues that if possibility were a cause, then since all possibilities are equal, each possible thing's own possibility could cause its own existence—leading to making the possible necessary. Second, he points out that even a single sphere is not a simple entity but contains multiple components like matter and form, contradicting the assumption that a sphere is a single effect of a simple cause.

Guided Path
1
Read the equality argument
Read from 'And because possibilities are equal' to '...this is a contradiction.' Understand how Razi uses symmetry to show absurdity.
If the possibility of the first intellect can cause the sphere, then the sphere's own possibility should cause its own existence, making it necessary.
2
Notice the self-causation problem
Razi says: if existence is necessary from possibility, then the possible becomes necessary in essence—a contradiction in terms.
This is a classic rebuttal against any attempt to derive existence from essence.
3
Consider the sphere's complexity
Read the final part about the sphere having matter, collective form, and logical form. This shows multiplicity within a single sphere.
Even if we accept emanation, the sphere itself is not a single effect but a composite.

(1) I say: They (the philosophers) say that the one does not produce except from one, not absolutely, but from one respect. As for two different respects, it may be permissible. The First Principle has no two respects, so it cannot be a principle of two things. As for its effect, it may have respects: one from it alone, a second from the First alone, a third from both together. Also, they do not say that possibility is a cause for the existence of something; rather they say that the First Principle can, by the condition of its possibility, be an effect of something, and by the condition of its effect's existence, be a cause of something else, and by both conditions together, be a cause of a third. The conditions may be nonexistent as previously. As for his saying that possibilities are equal, it is not true because they differ according to essences, and are distant and near. In summary, they apply to possibles by analogy, and the significates, as they become distant from the First Principle, increase in conceptual multiplicity. In summary, what the author (al-Razi) objected to them is not valid. The refutation of these principles is only by proving the origination of everything other than the First Principle.

Commentary
This is a gloss by a commentator defending the philosophers against al-Razi's objection regarding the principle 'from one only one proceeds'. The commentator explains that the principle is not absolute; it allows for exceptions when different respects are involved. The First Principle has no multiplicity, but its effect can have multiple respects. The commentator also addresses the role of possibility and conditions, arguing that possibilities differ and that al-Razi's criticism misconstrues the philosophers' position. The proper refutation, according to the commentator, is to prove that all contingent beings are originated (hadith), not to attack the causal principle itself.

Argument on Impossibility of Miracles and Response
Objection against miracles and Al-Razi's response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Razi presents and refutes the argument that miracles are impossible.

Core Idea
Miracles are possible because God has free will and pure souls have power.

Al-Razi presents a common objection: if miracles were possible, absurdities like mountains turning to gold would follow, making them logically impossible. He then responds by showing that both theologians (mutakallimun) and philosophers (hukama') accept the breaking of custom, either through divine free will or the extraordinary power of pure souls.

The objection uses a reductio ad absurdum argument.
2
Understand the response

3
Compare the schools
Understand why each school accepts the breaking of custom: theologians due to God's free agency, philosophers due to the power of pure souls.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Consider how this response refutes the original objection and affirms the possibility of miracles.
The objection was that miracles lead to absurdities, but the response shows they are not absurd because they have a valid cause.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection against the possibility of miracles. The objector argues that if miracles (breaking of custom) were possible, then absurd consequences would follow, like mountains turning to gold or utensils becoming people, which contradicts self-evident truths. Thus miracles are impossible. However, a marginal comment responds by clarifying that the breaking of custom is not denied by the mutakallimun (theologians), because they believe in a free agent (God) who can do whatever He wills. Nor is it denied by the hukama' (philosophers), because they hold that pure souls have extraordinary powers that can affect physical bodies. So the objection is answered by showing that the possibility of miracles is accepted by both schools.
