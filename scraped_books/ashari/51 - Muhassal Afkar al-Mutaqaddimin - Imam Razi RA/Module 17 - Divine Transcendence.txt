The Necessary is Not Composite
Imam Razi proves the second property: the Necessary is not composite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Necessary Being cannot be composed of parts, based on Imam Razi's concise proof.

Core Idea
Composition implies need, and need contradicts necessity.

Imam Razi now turns to a second essential property of the Necessary Being: its incompositeness. Using a brief but powerful argument, he shows that if the Necessary were made of parts, it would depend on those parts, which would make it contingent—contradicting its very definition. This passage establishes divine simplicity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the concise structure: claim, then 'لأن' (because) introducing the proof.
2
Identify the key terms
Locate the terms 'مركب' (composite), 'جزء' (part), 'محتاج' (in need), 'ممكن لذاته' (contingent in itself), and 'واجب لذاته' (necessary in itself). Understand their meanings.
3
Trace the logical chain
Follow the argument step-by-step: composite → need for part → need for other → contingency → not necessary. Write each step in your own words.
This proof is a syllogism: every composite needs its part; what needs something else is contingent; nothing contingent is necessary; therefore the necessary is not composite.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider why this matters for theology. If the Necessary were composite, it would not be truly self-sufficient, undermining the concept of absolute independence.
This connects to the broader theme of tawhid and divine simplicity.

Commentary
This passage is a proof of a property of the Necessary Being. Imam Razi argues that if the Necessary were composed of parts (yutrakkabu 'an ghayrihī), then it would need those parts to exist, and each part would be distinct from the whole. Anything that needs something else is contingent (mumkin) by itself. Since the Necessary is by definition not contingent, it cannot be composed.

Correction: Composition by definition implies the existence of parts, each of which is other than the whole. The whole depends on its parts for its existence; this dependence makes the composite contingent, not necessary.

Key Takeaway

The Necessary is Not a Part of Anything
Imam Razi proves the Necessary is not a constituent of any composite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Necessary in itself cannot be a part of any composite, based on its absolute self-sufficiency and lack of relation to others.

Core Idea
The Necessary cannot be a piece of anything larger.

Having established that the Necessary is not composed of parts, Imam Razi now proves the opposite: nothing else is composed from the Necessary as a part. This property further underscores divine transcendence and independence. The argument is concise but powerful: composition entails relation, and the Necessary has no relation to anything other than itself.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how Imam Razi presents the claim and then gives a brief proof using a conditional (otherwise...) and an unconditional statement about the Necessary.
The proof is only two sentences, but each clause carries weight.
2
Identify the claim and the proof
First, identify the claim: the Necessary is not a constituent of any composite. Then, identify the two premises of the proof: (1) if it were a part, there would be a relation between it and the other part; (2) the Necessary has no relation to others.
3
Reflect on the meaning of 'relation'
Consider what 'relation' (علاقة) means here. In kalam, a relation implies a kind of dependence or correlation. Why would the Necessary being a part require a relation? Think about how parts in a composite are related to each other.
The Necessary is absolutely independent; any relation would compromise that.
4
Connect to divine transcendence
Understand how this property reinforces tanzih (transcendence). The Necessary is not only beyond composition, but also beyond being used as a building block for anything else.
This protects tawhid from any notion of God being part of creation.

The Impossibility of a Partner in the Mind and the Metaphysics of Nonexistence
Razi presents an argument against a partner being mentally present and then a commentary on the metaphysics of nonexistent entities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a partner of God cannot be mentally present and how the kalam distinction between mental and external existence resolves the apparent contradiction.

Core Idea
A partner's necessary existence cannot be contained in the mind, but its concept can be known through mental existence.

Razi raises a subtle problem: if we think of a partner of God, is that partner somehow present in the mind? He argues it is impossible because a partner must have necessary self-existence, while mental objects lack that. But an objector says we only have the concept. Razi then deepens the discussion into the nature of nonexistent entities, showing how something can be mentally established without external existence. This lesson unpacks that argument and the commentator's resolution using the crucial distinction of aspects (جهات).

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the main claim and objection
Read the Arabic and translation. Focus on the first part: the claim that a partner cannot be mentally present, and the objection that we only have a concept.
Notice the definition of partner: that whose existence is necessary by itself.
2
Understand Razi's response to the objection
Razi responds that even if it's a concept, we must inquire about its object: if it's a pure negation, how can it have extension? If affirmative, its existence must be mental or external, repeating the problem.
3
Follow the commentary on nonexistent entities
The commentary explains that something nonexistent externally can be established in the mind from the perspective of being described with nonexistence, and there is no contradiction.
Key: mental existence is judged from one perspective, not another.
4
Apply the distinction to the partner case
The concept of partner involves similarity and distinctness. From the angle of similarity, it is judged necessary; from the angle of distinctness from God, it is judged impossible. These are not contradictory because the subject is different.
This resolves the confusion.

Commentary
This passage is a kalam argument about the impossibility of a partner (شريك) of God being present in the mind, followed by a commentary that explains the underlying metaphysics of nonexistent entities (معدوم) and the concept of partnership. The author begins by asserting that the presence of a partner in the mind is impossible because a partner is defined as that whose existence is necessary by itself, whereas anything present in the mind does not have such necessary existence. An objector suggests that what is present in the mind is only the concept (تصور) of the partner, not the partner itself. The author responds that this does not solve the problem, because the inquiry concerns the object of that concept: if it is a pure negation (i.e., nothing), how can it have extension (تحيز)? If it is something affirmative, then its existence must be either mental or external, and the same difficulty recurs. The commentator (أقول) then provides a more detailed resolution. He explains that something nonexistent in the external world can be 'established' in the mind insofar as it is described with the attribute of nonexistence; it is judged to have mental existence from that perspective, but from another perspective it is not so judged, and there is no contradiction because the subject of the two judgments is different. He applies this to the partner: the concept of partner involves similarity between two distinct things, which necessitates sharing (اشتراك) in respect of similarity, and impossibility of existence in respect of distinctness from God. The entity described with impossibility is judged to have nonexistence externally only when considered with that descriptive attribute in the mind; without that attribute, it is judged with necessity in terms of similarity. The key is to distinguish between the different aspects (جهات) under which something is considered, and this resolves the many confusions that arise in such discussions.

God's Precedence Without Time
Imam al-Razi answers the objection about divine precedence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that God's precedence over creation is ontological, not temporal, and that non-temporal precedence is rationally conceivable.

Core Idea
Precedence does not always mean time—God is prior in essence, not in time.

In this passage, Imam al-Razi addresses a subtle objection: if God precedes the world, does that imply time exists before the world? He uses an elegant analogy from the nature of time itself to show that precedence can be non-temporal. Understanding this distinction protects divine transcendence from anthropomorphic assumptions about time.

Guided Path
1

Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the concise structure: it begins with 'قلنا' signaling a direct answer.
2
Identify the objection being answered
Consider what objection the author is responding to. The objection likely claimed that any precedence must be temporal, implying time is eternal.
The commentary in the source chunk explains this context.
3
Understand the analogy
The author draws an analogy: parts of time (moments) precede each other, but not by a separate time. Similarly, God's precedence over the world is not by time.
Reflect on how this analogy undermines the objection.
4
Reflect on the implication for divine attributes
Consider why this distinction matters for Islamic theology: it preserves God's transcendence and avoids making time eternal.
This connects to the broader topic of divine attributes.

The Divine Essence Is Simple, Not Composite
Imam Razi proves that the divine essence is simple, not composite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the divine essence cannot be composite based on the argument from dependence and contingency.

Core Idea
Composition implies need; need implies contingency; Allah is necessary.

This short but powerful argument from Imam Razi uses a simple logical chain to establish that Allah's essence is simple. The reasoning is foundational in Islamic theology: anything composed of parts is dependent on those parts, and dependence entails possibility (contingency). Since Allah is the Necessary Being, His essence cannot be composite.

The reference to 'as previously established' connects to the earlier lesson on contingency.
3
Reflect on the implication
Ask yourself: why does dependence on parts lead to possibility? Because anything that depends on something else is not self-sufficient, hence contingent. Contradiction with Allah's necessary existence proves the impossibility of composition.
This shows that divine simplicity is a necessary consequence of tawhid.

The Third Proof: Divisibility Argument
Third proof: divisibility dichotomy leading to composition or absurdity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third proof that Allah is not a body, which uses the dichotomy of divisibility: either divisible (leading to composition) or indivisible (problematic on both views of atoms).

Core Idea
If Allah were a body, He would be either divisible or indivisible; both options lead to absurdity.

Imam Razi presents a stronger argument against corporealism. If Allah were a body, He must be either divisible or indivisible. If divisible, that would mean composition, which is impossible for God. If indivisible, that contradicts both those who deny the indivisible atom (jawhar fard) and those who affirm it, because the latter would imply Allah is the smallest thing. This lesson explores this decisive reasoning.

1

2
Examine the first possibility
If divisible, then composition (tarkib) follows, which is impossible.
3
Examine the second possibility
If indivisible, it is invalid according to those who deny the atom, and if atoms are affirmed, it implies Allah is the smallest thing.
4
Understand the conclusion
Both branches lead to an absurd conclusion, so the premise (Allah is a body) is false.

The Fourth Proof: Composite Parts Leading to Multiple Gods
Fourth proof: composite bodies imply multiple independent divine parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth proof that Allah is not a body, which argues that every body is composite, and its parts would each be independent divine beings, leading to a multiplicity of gods.

Core Idea
If Allah were a body, His parts would each be independent gods, which is absurd.

Moreover, this arguer also commits to the idea that a human is not a single living being but multiple, which is absurd. This lesson explores this argument.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational argument against the doctrine of incarnation (hulul) in God, including its logical consequences and the definitive definitional refutation.

Core Idea
Incarnation is only conceivable for physical entities; God is beyond space and locus.

Imam Razi engages with proponents of incarnation, granting their premises and exposing logical flaws. He then provides the decisive refutation based on the very meaning of hulul. This lesson traces his reasoning step by step, from conditional responses to the final definitional argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the English translation. Notice the dialectical structure: opponent claim, response, analogy, counter-claim, final argument.
2
Examine the initial dialectic
Identify the opponent's claim that God's essence necessitates indwelling. Note how Imam Razi grants exclusivity but questions whether indwelling is absolute or conditional.
The conditional approach avoids problems of God's temporality or locus eternity.
3
Analyze the analogy with God's knowledge
Observe how the analogy with God's knowledge of the world (conditional on the world's existence) supports the conditional indwelling argument.
This shows that conditional necessity does not imply absolute necessity.
4
Understand the definitive refutation
Focus on the final argument: the rationally conceivable meaning of hulul is spatial occurrence of an accident. Since this is impossible for God, hulul is impossible.
This is the core argument against incarnation in Islamic theology.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical refutation of the doctrine of incarnation (hulul) in God. The argument proceeds by granting certain premises from the opponent and then showing their consequences or demanding proof. First, the opponent claims that God's essence necessitates being an indwelling state in a locus. The response grants the exclusivity but questions whether the indwelling must be absolute or conditional upon the locus's temporality. An analogy is drawn with God's knowledge of the world: it is necessary only when the world exists. Similarly, indwelling might be conditional upon the locus's existence. Then the opponent asserts that independence from the locus is impossible for God. The author dismisses this as a mere claim without proof. Finally, the definitive refutation is given: incarnation is only conceivable as the occurrence of an accident in a space following its locus. Since God is not subject to spatial occurrence, incarnation is impossible.

God is Not in Any Spatial Direction
Imam Razi provides two proofs that God has no spatial direction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational arguments that God cannot be located in any spatial direction.

Core Idea
God is beyond space and direction; direction is impossible for the Creator.

Imam Razi presents two powerful rational arguments to demonstrate that God is not in any direction. This refutes the Karramiyya who claimed God is in a particular direction. The arguments show that direction implies either being a body or indwelling in a body, both impossible for the Creator.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the two separate arguments that Imam Razi presents. The first is short, the second is longer and more detailed.
The passage begins with 'مسألة' indicating a new discussion.
2
Understand the first argument
Identify the first argument: God is neither a body nor an accident in a body, thus cannot be in a direction. This is claimed to be known necessarily.
'متحيز' means a body or spatial entity.
3
Analyze the second argument
The second argument assumes God has a place and examines two possibilities: either the place is like other places (leading to an originated specification) or it differs (leading to the place being an existent that is either a body or an accident, both causing absurdities).
Follow the logic step by step: equality vs difference, each leads to contradiction.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Both arguments lead to the conclusion that God cannot be in any direction. This affirms divine transcendence and refutes anthropomorphic conceptions.
Consider why the Karramiyya held their view and how this argument counters it.

مسألة : أنه تعالى ليس في شيء من الجهات خلافا المكرامية لنا أنه ليس بمتحيز ولا حال فى المتحيز وما كان كذلك لم يمكن فى جهة أصلا وذلك معلوم بالضرورة ، ولأن مكانه تعالى إن ساوى سائر الأمكنة كان اختصاصه به دون سائر الأمكنة يستدعى مخصصا ، وذلك المخصص لابد أن يكون مختارا وكل ما كان فعلا الفاعل مختار فهو محدث فكونه في المكان محدث هذا خلف . وإن خالف سائر الأمكنة كان ذلك المكان موجودا لأن الاختلاف في النفى المحضر محال وذلك الموجود إن لم يكن مشار إليه لم يكن الموجود فيه مشار إليه فإن كان كونه كذاك بالذات كان جسما فإذا فرضنا الله تعالى موجوداً فيه كان البارى. تعالى حالا فى الجسم وهو محال ، وإن كان بالعرض كان ذلك عرضا حالا في الجسم فالبارى تعالى لما كان حالا فيه كان حالا فى الجسم فكان حالا في الجسم هذا خلف
Question: He, exalted, is not in any of the directions, contrary to the Karramiyya. For us: He is not a body nor an indwelling in a body, and whatever is not so cannot be in a direction at all, and that is known necessarily. And because His place, exalted, if it equals other places, then its specification to it without other places requires a specifier, and that specifier must be a free agent, and everything that is an act of a free agent is originated, so His being in the place is originated—this is a contradiction. And if it differs from other places, then that place exists, because difference in pure negation is impossible. And that existent, if it is not pointed to, then what is existent in it is not pointed to. So if its being as such is essential, it would be a body; if we suppose God exists in it, then the Creator would be an indwelling in a body, which is absurd. And if it is accidental, then that accident is an indwelling in a body, so the Creator, since He is an indwelling in it, would be an indwelling in a body, thus an indwelling in a body—this is a contradiction.
Source pages p. 159

Commentary
This passage is a proof that God is not in any direction. Two arguments are given. First, God is neither a body nor an accident in a body; anything that is not a body or in a body cannot be in a direction, as direction is a property of bodies or accidents in space. This is known necessarily. Second, if we assume God has a place, two possibilities: either His place is like other places, then its specification requires a cause and would be originated, contradicting God's eternity; or His place is different, then it is an existent entity. That existent either is pointed to (as a body) or not. If it is not pointed to, then God is not pointed to either, but that still requires the place to exist. If the place is a body, God becomes an accident in that body, which is absurd. If the place is an accident, then God, being in it, is in a body, also absurd. Thus, direction is impossible.

Impossibility of Colors, Tastes, and Smells for God
Imam Razi argues by consensus and reason that God cannot be described with colors, tastes, or smells.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the theological and rational arguments for why God cannot be described with colors, tastes, or smells, and the principle that God cannot be a locus of accidents.

Core Idea
God's transcendence excludes attributes that imply passivity or accident-hood.

Imam Razi presents a two-pronged argument: first by consensus, and second by a rational analysis showing that no specific color can be affirmed over another, and more fundamentally, that God cannot be a locus of accidents. This passage establishes a key principle of tanzih (transcendence).

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow, then read the English translation. Pay attention to the structure: consensus, rational argument, objection, and response.
Notice the footnote (2) which contains the author's own response.
2
Identify the two main arguments
First, note the consensus (الإجماع) that God is not described with such attributes. Second, follow the rational argument of 'the companions' about colors being a genus with species, none more perfect than another, and agency not depending on them.
The rational argument uses the principle of 'no preference without a differentiator'.
3
Understand the objection and response
The objector challenges whether the equality is in reality or in our mind. The response clarifies that the stronger proof is that God cannot be a locus of accidents (محلا للأعراض) due to the impossibility of passivity (انفعال) in His essence.
This is the core theological principle: God's essence does not admit accidents.
4
Reflect on the significance
Consider why this argument is important for tawheed. It safeguards God's transcendence from anthropomorphic or material attributes.

Issue: All have agreed that He Almighty is not described with colors, tastes, and smells. The relied-upon is consensus, and the companions said: Color is a genus with species under it, and some of them are not a perfection in relation to others, nor a deficiency in relation to others; also, agency does not depend on the realization of any of them. The first requires a proof, so why is it not possible that the essence of His self necessitates a specific color without the reason for that necessitation being known? The second is granted, but it does not necessitate negation; we know of that specific color by negation, but as for its non-existence in itself, it is not valid. (2) I say: Adherence to consensus in intellectual matters is necessary when unavoidable, but the relied-upon in this context is that He Almighty cannot be a locus of accidents due to the impossibility of passivity in His essence.

Commentary
This passage argues for the impossibility of attributing colors, tastes, and smells to God, relying primarily on consensus and rational argument. The author first states the consensus. Then the 'companions' (الأصحاب) provide a rational argument: color is a genus with many species; none of these species is inherently a perfection or deficiency relative to another, and God's agency does not depend on any of them. Therefore, affirming any particular color is arbitrary, so all must be denied. An objector raises two possibilities: either the claim of equality is about reality or about our perception. In the first case, one could argue that God's essence might necessitate a specific color even if we do not know why. In the second case (equality in our mind), it is granted but does not entail that the color does not exist in itself; we only lack knowledge of it. The footnote (أقول) responds that consensus in such intellectual matters is binding when necessary, but the stronger proof is that God cannot be a locus of accidents (محلا للأعراض) because passivity (انفعال) is impossible for His essence. This is a general principle: God does not have attributes that are accidents inhering in a subject.

Consensus and the Weak Material Argument
Razi states the consensus on pain's impossibility and presents a weak argument against intellectual pleasure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the consensus on God's immunity from pain and why the material basis for pleasure is an insufficient argument.

Core Idea
Pain for God is impossible by consensus; intellectual pleasure is disputed.

Imam Razi begins by establishing the universal agreement that God cannot experience pain. He then turns to the more contested question of intellectual pleasure, presenting an initial argument that ties pleasure to bodily temperament—but he immediately undermines it, showing the need for a stronger proof.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the consensus
Notice that all groups agree pain is impossible for God. This is a foundational point.
2
Identify the dispute
The philosophers affirm intellectual pleasure; others deny it. Razi presents a first argument against it.
3
Examine the weak argument
The argument claims pleasure depends on bodily equilibrium, so it cannot apply to God. But Razi flags it as weak.
4
Understand the objection
The absence of one cause (equilibrium) does not rule out the effect (pleasure) from another cause.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional proof and objection-response. It begins by stating the consensus on the impossibility of pain for God. Then it addresses the disputed case of intellectual pleasures. The first, weak proof uses the material nature of pleasure, but it is set aside. The stronger proof is that if such pleasure were pre-eternal, it would act as a motive (داعية) to create the object of pleasure before it exists, which is impossible; if originated, it would make God a subject of accidents. The philosophers object that their claim is not about pleasure from creation but about the necessary pleasure from self-perfection. The answer is that this is rejected by consensus of the Ummah, and similarly for pain. The argument hinges on the impossibility of a pre-eternal enticement that would require creation before creation.

The Stronger Proof: Pre-Eternal Pleasure Entails Contradiction
Razi presents the decisive argument against intellectual pleasure using the motive contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument that a pre-eternal pleasure in God would require creating its object before it exists, and that an originated pleasure would make God a locus of accidents.

Core Idea
Pre-eternal pleasure would demand creation before creation, which is impossible.

After discarding the weak material argument, Razi presents the principal proof. He shows that any intellectual pleasure in God leads to an impossibility: if pre-eternal, it would act as a motive to create the object of pleasure before it exists; if originated, it would make God subject to accidents. Thus, intellectual pleasure cannot be attributed to God.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two possibilities
The proof considers two cases: the pleasure is either pre-eternal or originated.
2
Analyze the pre-eternal case
If pre-eternal, it would be a motive (داعية) to create the pleasurable object. That motive would exist before the object, requiring creation before existence, which is impossible.
3
Analyze the originated case
If originated, it would be an accident, and God cannot be a locus of accidents.
4
Conclusion
Both possibilities lead to impossibility, so intellectual pleasure cannot exist in God.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of the Ash'ari doctrine that God will be seen by the believers in the afterlife. It begins by reaffirming the interpretation of the verse 'وجوه يومئذ ناضرة إلى ربها ناظرة' (Surah al-Qiyama 75:22-23) as referring to vision (ru'ya) rather than expectation or looking at reward. The author argues that 'nazar' (looking) is a cause for vision, and using the cause to refer to the effect is a strong metaphor. Then he refutes two alternative interpretations: (1) that 'ناظرة' means 'expecting' – this is rejected because expectation causes grief, contradicting the context of blessings; (2) that it means 'looking at reward' – this is rejected because looking at reward must mean seeing it, otherwise turning the eye without seeing is not a blessing. Moreover, if one must imply vision, implying reward would be an unnecessary addition. Next, the opponent raises three objections: (1) from the verse 'لا تدركه الأبصار' (Surah al-An'am 6:103), which is argued to be praise, and since non-comprehension is praise, comprehension would be a deficiency impossible for God; also, the verse is universal and negates any vision. (2) If God were visible, we would see Him now. (3) A visible object must be opposite the viewer or in its ruling. The answers: (1) 'Idrak' means encompassing vision from all sides, which applies only to material objects with sides; thus God cannot be 'comprehended' but can be 'seen' without encompassing. (2) The conditions for vision of creatures do not necessarily apply to God; we do not grant that the same conditions are required. (3) The requirement of being opposite is exactly the disputed point; even if established in the witnessed world, it does not necessarily apply to God (the absent). Thus the objections are refuted.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of the Ash'ari doctrine that God will be seen by the believers in the afterlife. It begins by reaffirming the interpretation of the verse 'وجوه يومئذ ناضرة إلى ربها ناظرة' (Surah al-Qiyama 75:22-23) as referring to vision (ru'ya) rather than expectation or looking at reward. The author argues that 'nazar' (looking) is a cause for vision, and using the cause to refer to the effect is a strong metaphor. Then he refutes two alternative interpretations: (1) that 'ناظرة' means 'expecting' – this is rejected because expectation causes grief, contradicting the context of blessings; (2) that it means 'looking at reward' – this is rejected because looking at reward must mean seeing it, otherwise turning the eye without seeing is not a blessing. Moreover, if one must imply vision, implying reward would be an unnecessary addition. Next, the opponent raises three objections: (1) from the verse 'لا تدركه الأبصار' (Surah al-An'am 6:103), which is argued to be praise, and since non-comprehension is praise, comprehension would be a deficiency impossible for God; also, the verse is universal and negates any vision. (2) If God were visible, we would see Him now. (3) A visible object must be opposite the viewer or in its ruling. The answers: (1) 'Idrak' means encompassing vision from all sides, which applies only to material objects with sides; thus God cannot be 'comprehended' but can be 'seen' without encompassing. (2) The conditions for vision of creatures do not necessarily apply to God; we do not grant that the same conditions are required. (3) The requirement of being opposite is exactly the disputed point; even if established in the witnessed world, it does not necessarily apply to God (the absent). Thus the objections are refuted.

The Second Proof Against Objective Moral Evil
Imam Razi presents a conditional argument against objective evil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Razi's second proof that moral evil cannot be objective, including its logical structure and reliance on divine compulsion.

Core Idea
If evil were objective, it must come from God or man — both impossible.

In this passage, Imam Razi presents a crisp logical refutation of objective moral evil. He structures his proof as a conditional with two exhaustive possibilities, then systematically eliminates each. This argument is central to the Ash'ari position on ethics and divine transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the rhetorical flow, then read the literal translation to grasp the argument's sequence.
Notice the concise, syllogistic style typical of kalam texts.
2
Identify the two possibilities
The argument states that if something were evil, it would be evil either from God or from the human servant. Understand why these are the only two possible sources.
This is a classic disjunction (qisma) dividing the problem exhaustively.
3
Understand the first rejection
The first possibility (evil from God) is rejected by unanimous agreement among Muslims. This reflects the principle of divine transcendence (tanzih).
4
Understand the second rejection
The second possibility (evil from the servant) is rejected because the servant's act is compelled: God creates the motive (dāʿī) that makes the act necessary, so the servant is under compulsion (idtirār), and the compelled cannot be evil.
This relies on the Ash'ari doctrine of kasb (acquisition) and occasionalism.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Since both branches are false, the original assumption that moral evil is objective must be false. Ask yourself how this argument fits into the larger debate on good and evil.
This proof does not deny that acts appear evil in this world; it denies an objective moral standard independent of divine will.

Commentary
This passage is an argument (proof) against the objectivity of moral evil, presented as the second of a series. The argument begins by stating a conditional: if something were evil (in an objective, rational sense), then that evil would have to be ascribed either to God or to the human servant. The first possibility—that evil comes from God—is rejected because it is unanimously agreed (among Muslims) that God does not do evil. The second possibility—that evil comes from the human servant—is rejected on the grounds that the servant's actions are compelled by God: the servant only acts when God creates in him a motive (dāʿī) that makes the act necessary for him. Since the compelled individual is not blameworthy, the act cannot be evil. Therefore, objective evil does not exist.

3

Because God can create the motive directly without an intermediary, there is no obligation to create it via a specific act. Thus luṭf is not obligatory.

Refuting the Obligation of Compensation (ʿIwaḍ)
Razi argues that if ʿiwaḍ were obligatory, then removing pain would be evil, which is absurd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ashʿarī argument against the Muʿtazilī claim that God must compensate for pains.

Core Idea
If compensation were obligatory, then alleviating pain would be evil—but it is not, so it is not obligatory.

The Muʿtazila hold that God must compensate (ʿiwaḍ) for any pain He causes. Imam Razi counters using an analogy from medicine: if preventing phlebotomy is evil because it prevents a benefit, then removing pain would be evil if compensation were obligatory. Since removing pain is not evil, compensation is not obligatory.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Muʿtazilī claim

2
Follow the hypothetical reasoning
Razi imagines what would follow if compensation were obligatory: removing pain would mean removing the compensation benefit, making pain removal evil.
3
Apply the shāhid analogy
Razi compares this to preventing phlebotomy (a medical procedure that brings benefit), which is considered evil because it prevents benefit. Since removing pain is not evil, the obligation of compensation is false.

Refuting the Obligation of Optimal Benefit (Aṣlaḥ)
Razi argues against aṣlaḥ by showing it would lead to absurdities like creating no one or creating everyone in Paradise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ashʿarī refutation of the Muʿtazilī claim that God must do what is most beneficial for creatures in this world.

Core Idea
What is truly optimal for a poor disbeliever would be non-existence—but God is not compelled to that absurdity.

The Muʿtazila assert that God must create the most beneficial (aṣlaḥ) arrangement for humans in this world. Imam Razi replies with a reductio: if aṣlaḥ were obligatory, then for a poor disbeliever, the best would be not to be created at all, to avoid torment. But God does not do that, so the principle is false. Moreover, the best would be to create all in Paradise—but God is not compelled to do so.

Guided Path

Understand the Muʿtazilī claim
They believe God must do what is most beneficial for people in this world.
2
Follow Razi's reductio for the disbeliever
Consider a poor disbeliever: the most beneficial for him would be non-existence to avoid eternal punishment. This is absurd, so aṣlaḥ cannot be required.
3
See the broader implication
Razi also notes that the most beneficial would be to create all in Paradise and give them all desires, which is not done. Thus the principle fails.

Refuting the Obligation of Punishment (ʿUqūba)
Razi argues that punishment is God's right and not obligatory because He can remit it without harm.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ashʿarī argument that punishment is not obligatory for God, but rather His right which He may remit.

Core Idea
Punishment is God's right; He may exact it or remit it without loss.

The Baghdadis among the Muʿtazila claim that God must punish sinners. Imam Razi responds that punishment is God's right: He gains no benefit from exacting it and suffers no harm from remitting it. Therefore, remitting it is good, just as in human affairs a creditor may forgive a debt. Thus, punishment is not obligatory.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument
Read the Arabic and translation. Identify the key terms: ʿuqūba (punishment), ḥaqq (right), nafʿ (benefit), ḍarar (harm).
2
Understand the concepts of right, benefit, and harm
Punishment is God's right (ḥaqq), meaning He is entitled to it. Exacting it gives no benefit to God; remitting it causes no harm.
3
Apply the shāhid analogy
In human affairs, a person with a right to compensation may forgive it without detriment. Similarly, God may remit punishment.
