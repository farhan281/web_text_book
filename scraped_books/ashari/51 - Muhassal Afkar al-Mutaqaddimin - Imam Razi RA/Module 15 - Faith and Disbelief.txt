Objection: Revelation Commands Conforming Belief, Not Knowledge
Razi reports an objection that revelation only commands conforming belief, not rational knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand an important objection against the claim that revelation commands demonstrative knowledge, and how the command is interpreted as applying to any conforming belief (including taqlid) rather than specifically rational proof.

Core Idea
Revelation commands conforming belief, not necessarily theoretical knowledge.

In this lesson, we encounter a key objection raised against the view that religious commands obligate demonstrative knowledge. The objector concedes that there is a command, but argues that it is directed at belief that corresponds to reality, whether based on authority (taqlid) or proof. This objection challenges the necessity of engaging in rational theology.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context of the objection
This objection is part of a debate about whether revelation requires knowledge (ʿilm) or just correct belief. The opponent argues that the command to believe implies a command to acquire demonstrative knowledge.
2
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once slowly, then read the translation. Notice the structure: a concession ('sallamnā') followed by a denial ('lā nusallim').
The phrase 'sallamnā' indicates the objector grants a previous point.
3
Identify the two parts: concession and counter-argument
The objector first concedes something (likely a general command), then denies that the command specifically targets knowledge. Instead, the command is for 'conforming belief' (al-iʿtiqād al-muṭābiq).
4
Grasp the key term 'al-iʿtiqād al-muṭābiq' and its implications
Conforming belief means any belief that matches reality, regardless of how it is reached. This includes taqlid (adopting belief from authority) and knowledge derived from proof. The objector uses this to argue that rational theology is not obligatory.
This term is central to the objection.
5
Reflect on the objection's significance
If true, this objection would undermine the necessity of studying kalam (rational theology) for every Muslim. It suggests that simple, correct belief is sufficient.

This passage presents a further objection against the claim that revelation commands demonstrative knowledge (al-ʿilm). The evidence given is that the Prophet (peace be upon him) did not specifically charge anyone with the detailed rational proofs (al-adilla) that theologians use. Therefore, the command is not specifically for acquiring theoretical knowledge, but for any belief that corresponds to reality.

Refuting al-Nazzam on God's Power Over Evil
Refutation of al-Nazzam's denial of God's power over evil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Razi's refutation of al-Nazzam's claim that God cannot create evil, and the distinction between impossibility in itself and impossibility due to motive.

Core Idea
Impossibility due to lack of motive does not negate divine power.

Al-Nazzam argued that if God created evil, it would imply He is ignorant or needy, which is impossible, so evil itself is impossible—and thus outside God's power. But Imam Razi dismantles this reasoning by first rejecting the premise that creating evil indicates deficiency, and then clarifying that even if it were impossible, that impossibility stems from the absence of a motive, not from any inability in God. This distinction preserves divine power while upholding God's wisdom.

Guided Path
1
Read al-Nazzam's argument
Read the first part of the passage where al-Nazzam claims evil is impossible because it would imply ignorance and need, leading to impossibility of being an object of power.
Notice he uses the principle that 'what leads to the impossible is impossible' and 'the impossible has no soundness of existence'.
2
Identify Razi's first response
Find where Razi says 'We do not grant that doing something indicates ignorance and need; rather He is the owner, so He may do whatever He wills.' This rejects the premise outright.
3
Analyze Razi's second response
Read the second part where Razi grants the impossibility for argument's sake but locates it in the motive (daa'i) rather than power (qudra). Understand the distinction between impossibility due to lack of motive and impossibility in itself.
This is the key theological nuance: an act can be impossible given a certain will, but the agent retains power to do it if the motive changes.
4
Check your understanding
Answer the checkpoint question to ensure you grasp the core distinction Razi makes.

As for al-Nazzam, he claimed that He is not able to create ignorance and other evils, and he argued that doing evil is impossible, and the impossible is not an object of power. As for it being impossible, it is because it indicates ignorance and need, both of which are impossible, and what leads to the impossible is impossible. And as for the impossible not being an object of power, it is because [the object of power] is that whose existence is actualized, and that requires the soundness of existence, and the impossible has no soundness of existence. The answer: We do not grant that doing something indicates ignorance and need; rather He is the owner, so He may do whatever He wills. Granting [that it indicates ignorance], this impossibility comes from the side of the motive (al-daa'i). So why do you say it is impossible from the side of power? For the powerful agent, when his will is determined towards refraining, the act is impossible for him with respect to this motive, but he is capable of the act with respect to [the possibility that] if he had the motive to do the act instead of the motive to refrain, he would be capable of it.
Source pages p. 181

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of al-Nazzam's claim that God cannot create evil (such as ignorance and other bad things). Al-Nazzam argues that evil acts are impossible (muhal) because they would indicate ignorance or need on God's part, which are impossible. Since evil leads to impossible, it is impossible; and the impossible cannot be an object of power. Moreover, for something to be an object of power, it must have the soundness of existence (sihhat al-wujud), which the impossible lacks. Razi's answer has two parts: First, he denies that creating evil indicates ignorance or need; God is the owner (malik) and can do as He wills. Second, even if we grant that evil is impossible, this impossibility arises from the lack of motive (daa'i) to do it, not from a deficiency in power. The powerful agent, when his will is determined to refrain (al-tark), the act is impossible for him given that motive, but if the motive had been different, he would be capable. Thus, the act is not impossible in itself (muhal li-dhatihi) but only impossible due to external factors (muhal li-ghayrihi), and such an act can still be an object of power.

The Punishment of Major Sins Is Interrupted for Believers
Reconciling Quranic promises on sin and reward.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari argument that the punishment of major sins for believers is temporary and not eternal, based on reconciling Quranic promises.

Core Idea
The Quran's promises of reward and punishment are reconciled by temporary punishment then eternal reward.

Imam al-Razi defends the Ash'ari position that believers who commit major sins will not remain in Hell forever. He uses two Quranic proofs to show that the only logical reconciliation of God's promises is that the sinner enters Hell for his sin, but then Paradise for his faith. This section sets out the argument clearly.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that al-Razi uses a very logical, deductive style.
2
Understand the three possibilities in the first proof
The first proof presents three possible fates for a believer who commits a major sin. Identify each and note which ones are rejected and which is accepted.
3
Examine the second proof about persisting merit
The second proof hinges on whether the merit of faith remains after a major sin. Al-Razi argues that if it remains, transfer from Hell to Paradise is necessary; if not, that would be irrational.
4
Reflect on the concluding footnote
The footnote declares the issue settled. Consider why al-Razi might say there is no room for discussion.

مسألة: وعيد الكبائر منقطع عندنا خلافا للمعتزلة. لنا قوله تعالى ولمن يعمل مثقال ذرة خيرا يره ومن يعمل مثقال ذرة شرا يره، ولابد للجمع بين العمومين، فإما أن يقال صاحب الكبيرة يدخل الجنة بإيمانه ثم يدخل النار وهو باطل بالاتفاق، أو لا يدخل أحدهما وهو باطل أيضا، أو يدخل النار بكبيرته ثم يدخل الجنة بإيمانه وهو الحق. وأيضا قوله (من يعمل صالحا من ذكر أو أنثى وهو مؤمن فأولئك يدخلون الجنة) دليل ثان أن الخصم معترف بأن المؤمن استحق الثواب بإيمانه فإذا فعل الكبيرة فالاستحقاق الأول إما أن يبقى أو لا يبقى، فإن بقى وجب اتصال الثواب ولا طريق إليه إلا بنقله من النار إلى الجنة، وإن لم يبق فهو محال لوجوه. أحدها: أنه ليس انتفاء الباقي لطريان الحادث أولى من اندفاع الحادث لوجود الباقي. (1) أقول: ليس في هذه المسألة موضع بحث.
Issue: The punishment of major sins is interrupted according to us, contrary to the Mu'tazila. Our proof is His statement 'And whoever does an atom's weight of good will see it, and whoever does an atom's weight of evil will see it.' It is necessary to combine the two generalities. So either it is said that the committer of a major sin enters Paradise by his faith, then enters the Fire — which is unanimously false; or he enters neither — which is also false; or he enters the Fire by his major sin, then enters Paradise by his faith — which is the truth. Also His statement 'Whoever does righteousness, male or female, while being a believer, those will enter Paradise' — a second proof. The opponent acknowledges that the believer deserves reward for his faith. So when he commits a major sin, the first deserving either remains or does not remain. If it remains, then the connection of reward is necessary, and there is no way to it except by transferring him from the Fire to Paradise. If it does not remain, it is impossible for several reasons. One of them: that the cessation of the remaining (reward) due to the occurrence of the new (sin) is not more appropriate than the elimination of the new due to the existence of the remaining. (1) I say: There is no room for discussion in this issue.
Source pages p. 237

Commentary
This passage argues for the Ash'ari position that the punishment of major sins (wa'id al-kaba'ir) is interrupted (munqati') — meaning that believers who commit major sins will eventually enter Paradise after being purified in Hell, not remain forever. It is opposed to the Mu'tazila who hold that such sinners will be eternally punished. Two proofs are given. First, the verse 'Whoever does an atom's weight of good will see it, and whoever does an atom's weight of evil will see it.' Both promises are general, so they must be reconciled. The only viable reconciliation is that the sinner enters Hell for his evil, then Paradise for his good (faith). The alternatives (entering Paradise then Hell, or neither) are impossible. Second, the verse 'Whoever does righteousness... while a believer will enter Paradise.' Since the opponent admits that faith merits reward, that merit either remains or is nullified by the major sin. If it remains, reward must be given, which requires transfer from Hell to Paradise. If it does not remain, that is impossible because it is not more rational for a later sin to cancel a prior merit than for the prior merit to prevent the effect of the sin. The footnote comments that there is no room for discussion in this issue, implying it is settled.

Three Proofs that Punishment for Major Sins Ceases
Razi enumerates three proofs for interrupted punishment of major sins.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's three scriptural and rational arguments for the Ash'ari position that punishment for major sins is interrupted, and why the Mu'tazili view of perpetual punishment is refuted.

Core Idea
Even major sins do not negate faith; Allah's forgiveness overrides punishment.

Imam Razi presents three proofs from the Quran and rational argument to show that the punishment for major sins is not eternal. The first proof reconciles two general verses, the second analyzes the logic of deserving reward and punishment, and the third appeals to verses about forgiveness. This passage is a direct refutation of the Mu'tazili doctrine that grave sinners will be eternally punished.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the dense argumentation style of Razi.
2
Study the first proof
Focus on the reconciliation of the two 'atom's weight' verses. Understand the three possibilities and why only one is valid.
3
Examine the second proof's structure
The second proof argues that the entitlement from faith remains. Note the four sub-arguments (wujuh) that show the impossibility of the entitlement being cancelled.
This part is the most detailed.
4
Consider the third proof
The verses about forgiveness indicate that forgiveness can occur even while a person is sinning, implying it can happen before repentance.
5
Synthesize the three proofs
Reflect on how these three proofs together establish the Ash'ari doctrine that punishment for major sins is interrupted.
Contrast with the Mu'tazili position.

مسألة : وعيد الكبائر منقطع عندنا خلافا للمعتدلة
لنا قوله تعالى ولمن يعمل مثقال ذرة خيرا يره ومن يعمل مثقال ذرة شرا يره ، ولابد للجمع بين العمومين، فإما أن يقال صاحب الكبيرة يدخل الجنة بإيمانه ثم يدخل النار وهو باطل بالاتفاق ، أو لا يدخل أحدهما وهو باطل أيضاً ، أو يدخل النار بكبيرته ثم يدخل الجنة بإيمانه وهو الحق ، 
وأيضا قوله من يعمل صالحا من ذكر أو أنثى وهو مؤمن فأولئك يدخلون الجنة، دليل ثان أن الخصم معترف بأن المؤمن استحق الثواب بإيمانه فإذا فعل الكبيرة فالاستحقاق الأول إما أن يبقى أو لا يبقى ، فإن بقى وجب اتصال الثواب ولا طريق إليه إلا بنقله من النار إلى الجنة ، وإن لم يبق فهو محال لوجوه .
أحدها : أنه ليس انتفاء الباقي لطريان الحادث أولى من اندفاع الحادث لوجود الباقي .
والثاني : وهو أنهما لو كانا ضدين كان طريان الاستحقاق الطارئ مشروطا بزوال الاستحقاق السابق ، فلو كان زواله لأجل طريان هذا الحادث لزم الدور .
الثالث : وهو أنه إذا استحق عشرة أجزاء من الثواب وفعل معصية استحق بها خمسة أجزاء من العقاب فليس انتفاء استحقاق إحدى الخمسين أولى من انتفاء استحقاق الخمسة الأخرى ، لأن أجزاء الثواب لما كانت متساوية كانت استحقاقاتها متساوية، أيضا فإما أن ينتفي مجموع العشرة فهو ظلم أو لا ينتفي شيء منها و هو المطلوب .
الرابع: إذا استحق عشرة أجزاء من الثواب ثم فعل ما به يستحق عشرة أجزاء من العقاب ، فالطارئ، إما أن يحبطه الأول ولا ينحبط كما هو قول أبي علي ، أو يحبط وينحبط كما هو قول أبي هاشم في الموازنة ، والأول محال لأنه يصير فعل الطاعة السالفة لغوا محضا لا يظهر له أثر في جلب نفع ولا دفع ضرر ، وهو باطل لقوله تعالى أن يعمل مثقال ذرة خيرا يره ، والثاني باطل لأن سبب زوال الاستحقاق الأول حدوث الاستحقاق الثاني ، فإذا ما لا يوجد الاستحقاق الثاني لا يزول الأول ، وإذا وجد الاستحقاق الثاني وزال به الأول استحال أن يزول هذا الاستحقاق الثاني ، لأنه ليس له مزيل فيصير هو القسم الأول الذي كان مذهبا لأبي علي وقد أبطالناه . يبقى أن يقال : كل واحد من الاستحقاقين يزول بالآخر دفعة ولكن هذا محال ، لأن علة عدم كل واحد منهما وجود الآخر فلو عدما دفعة لوجدا دفعة ، لكن العلة موجودة حال حدوث المعلول فهما موجودان حال كونهما معدومين هذا خلف ، فهذه وجوه دالة في فساده قولهم في المحابطة ، ومتى ثبت ذلك ثبت انقطاع عقاب .
دليل ثالث : قوله تعالى : إن الله لا يغفر أن يشرك به ويغفر ما دون ذلك لمن يشاء، وكذا قوله تعالى وإن ربك لذو مغفرة للناس على ظلمهم ، وكلمة على للحال ، يقال رأيت الأمير على أكله أي حال أكله ، فالآية تقتضي حصول المغفرة حال اشتغال العبد بالظلم ، وهو يدل على حصول المغفرة قبل التوبة .
Issue: The punishment of major sins is interrupted (munqati') according to us, contrary to the Mu'tazila. We have His statement: 'And whoever does an atom's weight of good will see it, and whoever does an atom's weight of evil will see it.' It is necessary to reconcile the two generalities. So either it is said that the committer of a major sin enters Paradise by his faith then enters Hell – which is false by consensus; or he enters neither – also false; or he enters Hell for his major sin then enters Paradise by his faith – and that is the truth. Also His statement: 'Whoever does righteous deeds, male or female, while being a believer – those will enter Paradise.' A second proof: The opponent admits that the believer deserves reward for his faith. So when he commits a major sin, the first entitlement either remains or does not remain. If it remains, then continuous reward is obligatory, and there is no way to it except by transferring him from Hell to Paradise. And if it does not remain, that is impossible for several reasons. First: That the non-existence of the remaining through the occurrence of the new event is not more likely than the repulsion of the new event due to the existence of the remaining. Second: That if they were two opposites, the occurrence of the new entitlement would be conditional upon the removal of the previous entitlement; so if its removal were due to the occurrence of this new event, circularity would follow. Third: That if he deserves ten parts of reward and commits a sin deserving five parts of punishment, then the non-existence of the entitlement of one of the two fives is not more likely than the non-existence of the entitlement of the other five, because since the parts of reward are equal, their entitlements are also equal; so either the total ten is negated – that is injustice – or nothing of it is negated – and that is what is sought. Fourth: If he deserves ten parts of reward, then does something deserving ten parts of punishment, then the new event either cancels the first and is not cancelled, as is the saying of Abu Ali, or cancels and is cancelled, as is the saying of Abu Hashim in al-muwāzana. The first is impossible because it makes the previous act of obedience pure vanity, with no effect in bringing benefit or repelling harm, and it is false due to His statement: 'That an atom's weight of good will be seen.' And the second is false because the cause of the removal of the first entitlement is the occurrence of the second entitlement. So if the second entitlement does not come to exist, the first does not vanish; and if the second entitlement comes to exist and the first vanishes by it, it is impossible for this second entitlement to vanish, because it has no remover, so it becomes the first type which was the doctrine of Abu Ali, and we have refuted it. The remaining possibility is to say: Each of the two entitlements vanishes by the other simultaneously, but this is impossible, because the cause of the non-existence of each one is the existence of the other; so if they both cease to exist simultaneously, they would both come to exist simultaneously – but the cause exists at the moment of the effect's occurrence, so they exist while they are non-existent – a contradiction. Thus these are proofs of the corruption of their doctrine of mutual cancellation (muḥābaṭa). And when that is established, the interruption of punishment is established. Third proof: His statement: 'Indeed, Allah does not forgive that partners be associated with Him, but forgives what is less than that for whom He wills.' Likewise His statement: 'And indeed your Lord is full of forgiveness for people despite their wrongdoing.' And the word 'upon' (ʿalā) indicates circumstance; one says 'I saw the emir while he was eating' i.e., the circumstance of his eating. So the verse necessitates the occurrence of forgiveness at the time of the servant's engagement in wrongdoing, and it indicates the occurrence of forgiveness before repentance.

Commentary
This passage presents three proofs that punishment for major sins is interrupted (munqatiʿ) at the Ashʿarī view, contrary to the Muʿtazila. The first proof uses the general verses about reward and punishment for atom's weight of good and evil. Since both are general, they must be reconciled. The only reconciliation that avoids contradiction is that the sinner enters Hell for his evil and then enters Paradise for his good. The second proof uses the verse about righteous believers entering Paradise. Since believers deserve reward for faith, if that entitlement remains after a major sin, the only way to connect reward is to transfer the sinner from Hell to Paradise. If the entitlement does not remain, the author provides four arguments (wujuh) showing impossibility: (1) it's not more likely that the remaining entitlement disappears due to a new event than that the new event is repelled by the existing entitlement; (2) if they were opposites, circularity results; (3) if reward and punishment are deserved in different amounts, it is unjust to cancel all reward; (4) analysis of the two theories of cancellation (iḥbāṭ) and mutual cancellation (muwāzana) shows both lead to absurdities, so the only coherent view is that both entitlements cannot cancel each other simultaneously, thus punishment must cease. The third proof uses verses about forgiveness: Allah forgives what is less than shirk whenever He wills, and He is forgiving despite people's wrongdoing; the phrase 'upon' indicates simultaneous circumstance, so forgiveness can occur while sin is being committed, implying it can happen before repentance. This directly contradicts the Muʿtazilī stance that punishment for major sins is eternal unless repented.
