Pre-Aristotelian Philosophers and Dualists on the Origin of the World
Enumerates the first group of materialist dualists (monists) and the second group of dualists (light/darkness).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the various pre-Aristotelian and dualist theories about the origin of the world, including monistic materialist views and the dualist theory of light and darkness.

Core Idea
Ancient thinkers offered diverse explanations for the origin of the world, from water, air, fire, earth, atoms, to light and darkness.

This passage from Imam Razi's Muhassal surveys the third school of thought regarding the origin of the universe, which includes pre-Aristotelian philosophers and dualists. It categorizes them into two groups: the materialists who held that the primordial substance is a body, and the dualists who posited two principles, light and darkness. The first group is further elaborated with the views of Thales, Anaximenes, Empedocles, Anaxagoras, Democritus, and others, each identifying a different elemental source.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the classification
Read the opening lines to understand that the third school is divided into two groups. The passage begins by naming the third school and then states 'then these are two groups'.
The first group claims matter is a body; the second group (dualists) claims light and darkness.
2
Explore the first group: material monists
List each philosopher mentioned and their proposed primary substance: Thales (water), Anaximenes (air), Empedocles (fire), others (earth, vapor), Anaxagoras (infinite mixture), Democritus (atoms). Notice the recurring ideas of transformation (condensation, rarefaction) and Anaxagoras' theory of concealment and manifestation.
The names are garbled due to OCR; refer to the footnotes for correct identification.
3
Examine Democritus' atomism
Democritus proposed spherical atoms that are indivisible in reality but divisible in imagination. They move perpetually and collide to form the world. This is a key precursor to modern atomic theory.
This is a distinct view from the others.
4
Summarize the second group: dualists
The dualists (Manichaeans, Dayṣānites, etc.) believe the world originates from two eternal principles: light and darkness. This is a fundamentally different approach from the material monists.
This group is mentioned only briefly; details are omitted.

Commentary
This passage presents the third school of thought regarding the origin of the world, which includes the pre-Aristotelian philosophers and the dualists. It then divides the dualists into two groups. The first group asserts that the original matter is a body. The author lists various philosophers and their specific claims: Thales said it is water because it is receptive to all forms and undergoes transformations; he also notes a report that Thales derived this from the Torah. Anaximenes said it is air, with fire and water/earth formed by rarefaction and density. Empedocles (likely intended by 'Abr Bliṭs') said it is fire, with things formed by condensation. Others said earth or vapor. Anaxagoras said it is an infinite mixture of particles of every kind, which when gathered become perceptible; he denied transformation and asserted 'concealment and manifestation' (al-kumūn wa al-ẓuhūr). Some added that the mixture was at rest until God moved it. Democritus said the origin is small spherical atoms that are indivisible and always moving; their collisions produced the world. Finally, the dualists claim the origin is light and darkness. The passage presents each view succinctly, setting the stage for later refutation.

First Objection: The Cause of the Soul's Attachment
First objection and its refutation by two schools

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the first objection regarding the soul's attachment to matter is refuted by both the mutakallimun and the philosophers.

Core Idea
Free choice or eternal causal chain explains the soul's attachment without external cause.

Having established that the soul attaches to matter, an objection arises: what caused this attachment? The answer differs between two schools of thought: the mutakallimun appeal to the principle of free preference, while the philosophers invoke an eternal causal chain. This lesson unpacks both responses.

Guided Path
1
Read the first objection
Read the opening lines of the passage that present the first question: why did the soul attach to matter after being detached?
Notice the assumption that attachment requires a new cause.
2
Understand the mutakallimun's response
The mutakallimun reject the question by citing their principle of free preference: a capable free agent may prefer one possible over another without a preferrer. Therefore the soul's attachment can be a direct act of choice.
This principle is central to their theology.
3
Understand the philosophers' response
The philosophers also reject the objection, but on different grounds: they allow that an eternal chain of perceptions can necessitate the attachment, so no new cause is needed.
Compare the two approaches.
4
Reflect on the implications
Consider how each response preserves the coherence of the soul's attachment without requiring an external cause.

There remain here two questions. First, that it be said: Why did the soul attach to matter (hayula) after it was not attached to it? For if that attachment occurred without a permitting cause [such as] the temporal origination of the world entirely without cause. Second, that it be said: Then why did the Creator (al-Bari), exalted, not prevent the soul from attaching to matter? They answered the first: That this question is not accepted among the mutakallimun, because they say the capable free agent (al-qadir al-mukhtar) may prefer one of his two capabilities over the other without a preferrer (murajjih), so why do they not allow that regarding the soul? And it is also not accepted from the philosophers because they allowed that a preceding thing be a cause of the succeeding thing, so why do they not allow that it be said the soul is eternal and has multiple infinite perceptions and every preceding thing is constantly a cause of the succeeding until it ended at that perception necessitating that attachment? And they answered the second question: That the Creator knew that it is most beneficial (al-aslah) for the soul to perceive its world in opposition to this attachment so that it itself abstains from that mixing, and also the soul by its mixing with matter acquires intellectual virtues that were not existing for it, so for these two purposes the Creator did not prevent the soul from attachment to matter.

Commentary
This passage presents two objections concerning the soul's attachment to matter (hayula) and provides refutations. The first objection asks why the soul attached to matter after being detached, implying a cause is needed. The answer distinguishes between the mutakallimun and the philosophers: the mutakallimun reject it because they hold that a free agent can prefer one possible over another without a reason, so the soul's attachment could be such a preference; the philosophers reject it because they accept a causal chain from eternity, so the attachment could be the result of an eternal chain of perceptions. The second objection asks why God (al-Bari) did not prevent the attachment. The answer is twofold: first, God knew it is most beneficial for the soul to perceive its world in opposition to this attachment, so that it avoids mixing; second, the soul acquires intellectual virtues through mixing with matter that it did not have before. Thus God allowed it.

Second Objection: Why God Didn't Prevent Attachment
Second objection and its two-part answer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two reasons why God allowed the soul to attach to matter despite knowing its negative consequences.

Core Idea
God permitted attachment for the soul's ultimate benefit and virtue acquisition.

The second objection questions God's wisdom: if attachment is problematic, why didn't God prevent it? The answer is twofold: first, the opposition inherent in attachment allows the soul to understand its world; second, mixing with matter enables the soul to gain intellectual virtues it otherwise lacks. This lesson explores these two purposes.

Understand the first reason

The soul learns by contrast.
3
Understand the second reason
The second reason: through mixing with matter, the soul acquires intellectual virtues it did not have before.
These virtues are acquired, not innate.
4
Reflect on divine wisdom
Consider how both reasons show that God's permission serves the soul's ultimate good.

Bodies Can Be Free of Colors and Tastes
Razi argues that bodies can lack accidents like color, taste, and smell, and refutes two analogies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's proof that bodies can lack accidents like color, taste, and smell, and how he refutes the analogies of his opponents.

Core Idea
Bodies are not required to have colors or tastes; even air has none.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi defends the Ash'arite position that accidents like color and taste are not essential to bodies. He offers a simple empirical observation — air has no color or taste — and then addresses two analogical arguments from his opponents, showing their weaknesses. This lesson unpacks his reasoning and the key concept of "common ground" (al-jāmi') in analogical argumentation.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
First, read the full Arabic passage and its English translation to get a sense of the argument's flow. Notice the concise style of kalām reasoning.
The passage begins with 'Issue: Bodies are permissible to be free...'
2
Identify Razi's main proof
Razi's proof is direct and empirical: air has no color or taste. This shows that a body can exist without these accidents. What does this imply about the necessity of accidents?
Consider how this counters the assumption that bodies must always have some color.
3
Understand the two analogies and their refutations
The opponents raise two analogies: one comparing color to the accident of rest (sukūn), and one comparing the state before attribution to after. Razi refutes each: the first lacks a common ground (jāmi'), and the second ignores the role of a contrary (ḍidd) in removing permanent accidents.
Focus on the terms 'jāmi'' and 'ḍidd' – they are critical to the argument.
4
Reflect on the overall argument
After studying the commentary and breakdown, ask yourself: Why is the distinction between 'before attribution' and 'after attribution' important? How does Razi's objection to the analogy hold even if the opponent's analogy is sound in other cases?
This connects to the broader kalām debate about the nature of accidents and their dependence on bodies.

Issue: Bodies are permissible to be free of colors, tastes, and odors, contrary to our companions. Our proof: that air has no color and no taste. They argued by analogy of color to rest (sukūn) and by analogy of what is before attribution to what is after. The first analogy lacks the common ground (jāmi‘). As for the second, we hold that it is permissible for it to be free of what does not remain after being attributed with it. As for the remaining (accident), it is not negated from the locus except by a contrary that removes it. If this is sound, the difference is clear; otherwise we deny the ruling in the original case.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof and refutation regarding whether bodies can be devoid of certain accidents like color, taste, and smell. Razi (the author) begins by stating the issue: bodies may be free of these accidents, contrary to the position of 'our companions' (likely the Ash‘arites). He provides a simple empirical proof: air has no color and no taste. The opponents raise two arguments by analogy. First, they compare color to the accident of rest (sukūn), but Razi notes the analogy lacks a common ground (jāmi‘). Second, they compare the state before a body acquires an accident to the state after it has acquired it, arguing that just as the accident cannot cease after attribution without a contrary, so it cannot be absent before attribution. Razi counters that the absence before attribution is not analogous because the ruling depends on the presence of a contrary only after attribution; before attribution there is no such condition. If this distinction is valid, the difference between the two cases is established; otherwise, he rejects the original ruling of the opponents.

The Possibility of Vacuum: Definition and Proofs
Razi defines vacuum and provides two proofs for its possibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's definition of vacuum and the two proofs he offers to demonstrate its possibility within Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Vacuum is possible: two bodies can exist without touching, proven by motion and lifting.

Imam Razi addresses a key cosmological debate: can there exist a space entirely devoid of body? Against Aristotle, he affirms that vacuum is rationally permissible. In this passage, he defines vacuum and presents two arguments from everyday physical observations to support his claim, then refutes the objection that a measurable vacuum would be a body.

Guided Path

Read the Arabic passage

Focus on the phrase: 'al-murād min al-khalā’ kawn al-jismayn bi-ḥaythu lā yatamāssān wa-lā yakūn baynahumā mā yumāssuhumā.' This defines vacuum as a state where two bodies do not touch and there is nothing touching them between.

Division of bodies
A body is either simple, which is that whose every part resembles the whole in complete quiddity, or composite, which is not so. As for the simple, it is either celestial or elemental.
( The celestial body )
As for the celestial bodies, the philosophers have claimed that they are neither heavy nor light, neither hot nor cold, neither moist nor dry, and that penetration, recomposition, generation, and corruption do not occur in them. They argued by saying that direction is the aim of the moving thing and the object of indication, so it is existent because negation has no distinction, and it is indivisible; otherwise the moving thing, when it reaches one of its two halves and remains moving, either it is said that it does not move from the direction, so the direction is that limit and nothing beyond it, or to it, then that limit is not from the direction but the direction is what is beyond it, so it is established that the direction is an indivisible limit. Then they showed that there must be a spherical determiner by which the above and below are determined by its circumference and center. Then they said: This determiner is not susceptible to rectilinear motion, otherwise the two directions, i.e., what it is from and what it is to, would be attained by it. And if it is not susceptible to rectilinear motion, it follows that it is neither heavy nor light, because the heavy is that which rises from it? (maybe 'to'?) and that is rectilinear motion. And it does not admit penetration and recomposition because that is rectilinear motion. And if it does not admit penetration, it is simple, because every composite is susceptible to dissolution. And every simple thing, every part assumed in it can attain the position that another part has attained, and everything like that is susceptible to motion, and everything like that has a tendency (mayl) that moves it, and everything like that moves circularly. So its motion is not natural, otherwise it would move naturally from what it naturally moves from, so the same nature would be both seeking a thing and fleeing from it, which is impossible. Nor is it forced, because force is contrary to nature, and there is no nature here, so no force. So that motion is voluntary. Thus the heaven is an animal moving by volition. (1) I say: The philosophers based the proof of directions and their determiners on the finitude of dimensions. They said: Since dimensions are finite, sensory indication cannot go to infinity, nor can the mover intending a direction. Direction cannot be a body because every body is divisible, and nothing of direction is divisible for the reason mentioned. So direction is a bodily entity indivisible. Every direction necessarily comprises two endpoints. The body by which direction is determined cannot be composed of different parts because they would have different directions and directions must be prior to the parts and then upon them. So the determiner is simple in itself, similar in shape, and nothing is similar in shape except a sphere, so it is a sphere. It cannot be determined by something external to it because its attachment to what is external would require a direction prior to it. The simplicity of the determiner can be shown by what we said, although showing it by the impossibility of difference on it is also possible as he mentioned. As for showing the necessity of motion in the determiner, it is only achieved by two premises: one is that a body cannot be devoid of a tendency, and the second is that a simple body cannot have two tendencies of different directions, and the determiner cannot move in non-circular motion, so it has a circular tendency. ←

Commentary
This passage classifies bodies and then focuses on celestial bodies. It begins by dividing bodies into simple and composite. Simple bodies are those where every part resembles the whole in quiddity; composite bodies are not. Simple bodies are either celestial (فلكي) or elemental (عنصرى). Then it presents the philosophers' view on celestial bodies: they lack the four primary qualities (heavy/light, hot/cold, moist/dry) and are not subject to penetration, recomposition, generation, or corruption. Their proof hinges on the concept of direction (جهة) as an existent, indivisible limit, leading to the necessity of a spherical determiner (محدد كرى) that defines up and down. This determiner cannot have rectilinear motion, hence it is neither heavy nor light, and it is simple (since it does not admit penetration). Simplicity implies that any part can take the position of another, which allows circular motion. This motion is not natural (because a single nature cannot both seek and flee the same thing) nor forced (since there is no nature to oppose), so it must be voluntary (إرادية). Thus the heaven is an animal moving by will. The footnote expands on the philosophers' argument: directions are based on the finitude of dimensions, direction as an existent must be a bodily entity (جسماني) but indivisible, and the determiner must be a simple sphere. It explains the necessity of two directions (above and below) as the endpoints of one extension. Other directions like right/left are accidental. The necessity of motion is derived from two premises: every body has a tendency (ميل), and a simple body cannot have two opposing tendencies, so the only possible motion is circular.

Proof that the Body Perceives Universals
Razi presents two arguments that the body perceives both particulars and universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's two arguments that the body perceives universals because it perceives particulars, and the rebuttal of counterarguments.

Core Idea
The body's perception of particulars necessarily includes perception of universals.

This passage presents a dense argument from the reliable scholars (al-thiqat) that the body, not just the soul, perceives universals. Razi gives two proofs: one based on predication of universals on particulars, and another based on the part-whole relationship between universals and particulars. He also addresses objections. Understanding this argument helps clarify the scope of bodily cognition in Islamic thought.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then the English translation. Let the argument flow naturally.
Notice the structure: two premises, two arguments, then rebuttals.
2
Identify the two premises
The first premise is that the body perceives particulars (obvious from sensation). The second premise is that the body perceives universals, supported by two arguments.
3
Understand the first argument (predication)
When we feel a particular heat, we can predicate universal 'heat' on it. The one who predicates perceives both the particular and the universal. Since the body perceives the particular, it must also perceive the universal.
A counter that the soul perceives both is rejected because it would lead to double perception of particulars.
4
Analyze the second argument (part-whole)
The universal 'man' is part of the particular 'this man'. Perceiving the composite (this man) includes perceiving the simple (man). So the body perceives the universal.
The objection that only the 'thisness' is perceived is refuted.
5
Examine the rebuttals to the objection
The objection says only individuation (thisness) is perceived. Razi refutes it: individuation is not an existential attribute, so it cannot be an object of perception; if only pure individuation were sensed, no differences would be perceived.
This shows the argument's strength.
