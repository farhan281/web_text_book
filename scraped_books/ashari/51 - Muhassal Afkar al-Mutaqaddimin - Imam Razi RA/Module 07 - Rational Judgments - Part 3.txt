Essential Possibility vs. Preparedness: Reframing Philosophical Arguments
Razi critiques three philosophical arguments, distinguishing essential possibility from preparedness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi critiques the philosophers' arguments by distinguishing essential possibility from possibility as preparedness, showing that the arguments only prove conditional impossibility.

Core Idea
Philosophers' arguments for impossibility often confuse essential possibility with preparedness.

Imam Razi engages deeply with the philosophers' claims that the world is impossible to annihilate. He shows that their arguments, upon analysis, only establish the world's necessity by another—not inherent impossibility. The key to his critique is a distinction between two types of possibility: essential possibility (imkān dhātī) and possibility as preparedness (imkān bi-maʿnā al-istiʿdād). This lesson unpacks that distinction and its application to three arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation without analyzing. Allow the author's critique to take shape naturally.
Notice how Razi begins by summarizing the philosophers' arguments before critiquing them.
2
Identify the three arguments being critiqued
Find where Razi refers to 'the first argument', 'the second', and 'the third'. Note the specific objection he raises for each.
The third argument is the most detailed and introduces the key distinction.
3
Focus on the third argument: understand the distinction
Razi distinguishes between essential possibility (imkān dhātī) and possibility as preparedness (imkān bi-maʿnā al-istiʿdād). Understand that essential possibility does not require a substrate, while preparedness does.
This distinction is central to refuting the philosophers' need for a pre-existing matter.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Razi concludes that the arguments only prove impossibility by another (al-imtināʿ bi-al-ghayr), which does not contradict the world's essential possibility. Consider why this matters for the debate on the world's eternity or contingency.
The critique preserves the world's ontological possibility while acknowledging conditional necessity.

I say: He argued for his claim by [stating] that the world is possible by itself, and he adduced arguments from the philosophers that essentially reduce to its being necessary by another. There is no contradiction between the two matters that would entail their opposition. As for the first argument, it is apparent that he ascribed impossibility to its being a necessary effect of the necessitator. As for the second, because the impossibility of time’s non-existence is qualified by its being after its existence, and that does not negate its intrinsic impossibility. As for the third, he did not distinguish between essential possibility (al-imkān al-dhātī) and possibility in the sense of preparedness (al-istiʿdād), as we have explained previously. The second kind of possibility entails need for matter, unlike the first. Neither of the two opponents claimed that possibility. Commentary
This passage is the author's (likely Fakhr al-Dīn al-Rāzī) critical commentary on the philosophers' arguments presented earlier. The author notes that the philosophers' arguments, which claim the world is impossible to annihilate, actually prove that the world is necessary by another (wājib bi-ghayrihī), not that it is impossible by itself. He argues that there is no contradiction between the world being possible in itself and necessary by another. He critiques each argument: the first argument (about the necessary cause) only shows that the world's non-existence is incompatible with the continued existence of its cause, not that it is intrinsically impossible. The second argument (about time) merely shows the impossibility of time's non-existence given that it is posterior to its existence, but this does not prove intrinsic impossibility. The third argument (about the need for a substrate) fails to distinguish between essential possibility (imkān dhātī) and possibility as preparedness (imkān bi-maʿnā al-istiʿdād). Essential possibility does not require a substrate; only preparedness does. The philosophers' argument for a substrate (hayūlā) relies on the latter, which the author claims neither opponent actually asserted. Thus, the philosophers' arguments only prove impossibility by another (al-imtināʿ bi-al-ghayr), which does not conflict with the world's essential possibility.

Objection and Answer on the Karamiyya's Proof
Objection and its refutation defending the Karamiyya's proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three objections raised against the Karamiyya's proof that the world cannot be annihilated by a contrary, and how each objection is systematically refuted by showing the enduring's causal dependence and the invalidity of the objector's assumptions.

Core Idea
Strength is not measured by novelty or frequency, but by causal dependence.

The Karamiyya argued that the world cannot be annihilated by the occurrence of a contrary, because the contrary is new (ḥādith) and must overcome the enduring (bāqī). An objector challenges this by claiming the new is stronger for three reasons. This passage presents the objection and its detailed refutation, defending the original proof and clarifying key concepts in causality and the impossibility of simultaneous existence and non-existence.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and its three reasons
Read the Arabic passage and translation of the objection. Notice the three distinct arguments: (1) causal relation, (2) impossibility of simultaneous existence and non-existence, (3) frequency of occurrence.
The objection starts with 'لا يقال' and presents three 'لأن' clauses.
2
Read the answer's response to each reason
After the objection, the answer begins with 'لأنا نجيب'. Follow the refutation for each of the three points. The first response reaffirms that the enduring is also causally linked; the second clarifies that the enduring prevents the new from existing; the third denies the premise of coexistence of similars.
The answer uses 'عن الأول', 'عن الثاني', 'عن الثالث'.
3
Understand the additional argument about the condition for annihilation
The passage concludes with an extra argument against annihilation by removal of a condition, showing that such a condition would lead to a vicious circle between substance and accident.
This part starts with 'وإنما قلنا'.

It should not be said: The new (al-ḥādith) is stronger than the enduring (al-bāqī), because the new, at the moment of its occurrence, is related to the cause, while the enduring is not; and because the new, at the moment of its occurrence, if it were to become non-existent, the coexistence of existence and non-existence would be necessary, unlike the enduring; and because it is possible that the non-existence of the new is more frequent, so it is stronger. [We answer:] For the first, we have explained that the enduring, in the state of its persistence, is related to the cause. For the second, we do not say that the new exists and becomes non-existent simultaneously; rather, we say that the enduring prevents the new from entering into existence. For the third, it is based on the possibility of the coexistence of similars, which is impossible. And we only say that it is not permissible for [annihilation] to occur by the removal of a condition, because that condition can only be an accident, since the condition external to the quiddity of a thing is an accident; thus the substance would need the accident, and the accident would need the substance, leading to a vicious circle, which is impossible.

Commentary
This passage contains an objection (dafʿ) raised against the Karamiyya's proof that the world cannot be annihilated by the occurrence of a contrary. The objector claims that the new (al-ḥādith) is stronger than the enduring (al-bāqī) for three reasons: (1) the new is directly linked to the cause at its moment of occurrence, while the enduring is not; (2) if the new were to be annihilated at its moment of occurrence, it would entail simultaneous existence and non-existence, unlike the enduring; (3) the new may occur more frequently, indicating greater strength. The response (jawāb) refutes each point. First, the enduring is also linked to the cause during its persistence. Second, the claim does not assert simultaneous existence and non-existence of the new; rather, the enduring prevents the new from coming into existence. Third, the argument regarding frequency is based on the assumption that two similar things can coexist, which is impossible. The answer then argues against the possibility of annihilation by removal of a condition, contending that the condition must be an accident, leading to mutual dependency between substance and accident, resulting in a vicious circle.

Arguments Against Determination as a Positive Attribute
Three arguments against determination as a positive attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three rational arguments that the mutakallimun used to deny that determination (ta'ayyun) is a positive attribute.

Core Idea
Determination cannot be a positive attribute because it leads to infinite regress, circularity, or multiplication of unity.

In this passage, al-Razi presents a classic theological debate: is the determination of a thing (its being this specific thing) an additional positive attribute or merely a negation? The mutakallimun argue it is not positive, using three distinct proofs. We will examine each proof carefully.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to understand the flow of the arguments.
Focus on the logical structure.
2
Study the first argument
Identify the first argument: if determination is positive, it would be common, leading to infinite regress.
3
Study the second argument
The second argument involves timing: determination cannot join essence after essence exists, causing circularity.
4
Study the third argument
The third argument says if determination is other than essence, it leads to one thing being two, then infinite.
5
Reflect on the overall logic
Consider how all three arguments share a common theme: any attempt to treat determination as positive leads to absurdities.

خاتمة في أحكام الموجودات والنظر من وجهين : النظر الأول في الوحدة والكثرة . مسألة : كل موجودين فلا بد وأن يكونا متباينين بنفسيهما ثم المتكلمون أنكروا كون العين أمرا ثبوتيا واحتجوا بأمور الأول الله لو كان التعين أمر ثبوتيا لمكان مساويا لسائر التعينات في الماهية المسماة بالتعين ويمتاز كل واحد منهما عن صاحبه بخصوصية فيلزم أن يكون للمتعينين تعين آخر إلى غير النهاية الثاني وهو أن التعين لو كان أمرا ثبوتيا لاستحال انضمامه إلى الماهية بعد وجود الماهية لكن الماهية لا توجد إلا بعد التعين فإن كان هذا التعين هو الأول لزم الدور وإن كان غيره كان الشيء الواحد معينا مرتين وهو محال الثالث: وهو أن التعين إذا كان أمرا مغايرا للماهية استحال أن يكون الوجود القائم بأحدهما هو الوجود القائم بالآخر لاستحالة قيام الصفة الواحدة بمحلين بل يكون وجود أحدهما غير وجود الآخر فيكون الشيء الواحد ليس بواحد بل اثنين ثم الكلام فيهما كما في الأول ولا شيء من الواحد ليس بواحد بل أمور غير متناهية . واحتج القائلون بكون التعين أمرا ثبوتيا زائدا بأن هذا الإنسان يشارك الإنسان الآخر في كونه إنسانا ويخالفه في هويته فهويته مغايرة للإنسانية وتلك الهوية صفة ثبوتية لأن هذا الإنسان موجود والمفهوم من هذا جزء المفهوم من هذا الإنسان وجزء الموجود موجود فالمفهوم هذا موجود .
Conclusion on the judgments of existents. The consideration is from two aspects: the first aspect is on unity and multiplicity. Issue: Every two existents must necessarily be distinct in themselves. Then the mutakallimun denied that determination is a positive attribute and argued with things: First: If determination were a positive attribute, it would be equal to other determinations in the essence called determination, and each one would be distinguished from its companion by a particularity, so the two determined things would need another determination ad infinitum. Second: If determination were a positive attribute, its conjunction with the essence would be impossible after the existence of the essence, but the essence only exists after determination. If this determination is the first, a circularity follows; if it is another, then one thing would be determined twice, which is impossible. Third: If determination is other than the essence, it is impossible for the existence subsisting in one of them to be the existence subsisting in the other, because it is impossible for one attribute to inhere in two loci. Rather, the existence of one is not the existence of the other, so the one thing is not one but two, then the discussion about them is as in the first, and nothing of one is not one, but infinite matters. And those who affirm that determination is an additional positive attribute argued that this human being shares with another human being in being human and differs from him in his identity, so his identity is other than humanity, and that identity is a positive attribute, because this human being exists, and the concept of 'this' is part of the concept of 'this human being', and part of what exists is existent, so the concept 'this' is existent.

Commentary
This passage is the first issue in a conclusion about judgments of existents. It states that any two existents must be distinct in themselves. The mutakallimun deny that determination (ta'ayyun) is a positive attribute (amr thubuti) and present three arguments: (1) If determination were a positive attribute, it would be common to many determinations, leading to an infinite regress. (2) If it were positive, it could only join the essence after the essence exists, but the essence only exists after determination, causing circularity or double determination. (3) If determination is distinct from essence, the existence of the determined thing would differ from the existence of the determination, making one thing two. Then a counter-argument is given: human beings share humanity but differ in identity (huwiyya); that identity is a positive attribute because it is part of the concept of the existing individual, and part of what exists is existent.

The Counter-Argument: Determination as a Positive Attribute
Counter-argument that identity is a positive attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that identity (huwiyya) is a positive attribute, supporting determination as a positive attribute.

Core Idea
Since identity distinguishes individuals, it must be a real positive attribute, part of what exists.

After presenting the arguments against determination as a positive attribute, al-Razi now presents the case of the proponents. They argue that identity (huwiyya) is not merely conceptual but a real attribute because it is part of the concept of an existing individual and part of what exists is existent.

Guided Path
1
Read the counter-argument
Read the passage and translation to grasp the argument from identity.
Notice the key terms: huwiyya, insaniyya.
2
Analyze the reasoning
The argument states that two humans share humanity but differ in identity; identity is other than humanity and is a positive attribute.
Why is it positive? Because the concept of 'this' is part of the concept of 'this human', and part of what exists is existent.
3
Consider the implication
If identity is a positive attribute, then determination is also positive, since determination is what makes a thing this specific thing.
This directly opposes the mutakallimun's view.

Commentary
This passage presents the first proof for the possibility of seeing Allah and then subjects it to a detailed dialectical critique. The proof states: existence (wujūd) in the witnessed world (shāhid) is a cause (ʿilla) for the soundness (ṣiḥḥa) of vision, so it must be the same in the unseen (ghāʾib). The author immediately notes this indication is weak from several aspects. First objection: Allah's existence is identical to His essence and unique, so it cannot be compared to ours. The opponent concedes equality but denies that soundness of vision needs a cause because soundness is not a positive attribute but a negation. Then the opponent concedes it is caused but asks why existence is the cause. The response: substance and color share in soundness of vision, so a shared cause is needed; only temporality (ḥudūth) and existence are shared; temporality is unfit because it involves non-existence, so existence remains. The author then mentions a second proof (invented by the author of the book, likely Imam Razi himself), but it is only critiqued: it relies on his theory of concepts, and it is argued that the author holds that Allah's essence (māhiyya) is other than His existence. The dialectic continues on page 192 with a series of objections and answers. The critic first denies that substance is seen (following earlier objections), then argues that the soundness of vision differs between substance and color in essence, so the shared judgment does not require a shared cause. Even if sharing is conceded, the critic argues that possibility (imkān) is an alternative shared attribute, and possibility is non-existent, so explaining a non-existent by a non-existent is allowed. Further, temporality is defended as a modality additional to non-existence. The critic then argues that even if existence is the corrector, the presence of a preventive (māniʿ) in Allah's essence or attributes could negate the judgment, analogous to life not causing ignorance/shahwa in Allah. Finally, the critic argues that vision in us depends on conditions like imprinting an image or facing, which are impossible for Allah, so we cannot see His essence. The passage ends with this conclusion.

First Proof: Imposing the Impossible
Razi argues God imposes the impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's first proof that God imposes the impossible, and why this challenges the Mu'tazilite claim that such imposition is necessarily ugly.

Core Idea
If imposing the impossible were ugly, God would not do it. He does, so it isn't.

Razi opens his first proof by taking the Mu'tazilite assumption head-on. They claim that imposing what cannot be borne (taklif ma la yutaq) is rationally ugly. Razi counters that if that were true, God—who is all-wise—would never do such a thing. Yet the Qur'an shows He does. Let's examine the evidence.

The second example is subtler.

Understand the logical structure
Razi's argument is: if it were ugly, God wouldn't do it; but God does it (by these examples); therefore it is not ugly. Trace this logic.
This is a reductio ad absurdum of the Mu'tazilite position.

Why does Abu Lahab's command involve a contradiction? Because he must believe God's report that he won't believe, which is believing a contradiction.
This shows imposing the conjunction of two opposites.

Razi doesn't say it's good; only that it's not necessarily ugly.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof against the objectivity of moral evil, focusing on the example of lying. The argument is that lying can be good if it serves to save someone from injustice. The author anticipates two objections. First objection: the good in such a case is actually dissimulation (taʿrīḍ), not outright lying. Second objection: lying inherently entails evil, but the effect (good) may differ from the cause (evil) due to an impediment (māniʿ). The author answers the first objection by pointing out that if all apparent lies are interpreted as dissimulation, then there would be no lies at all in the world—and if the speaker intends something true by his words, it becomes truth. To the second objection, he responds that then we could never be certain that any particular lie is evil, because we cannot rule out a hidden impediment. Then the opponent (presumably the Muʿtazila) presents an argument from necessary knowledge: humans know necessarily that injustice and lying are evil and beneficence good, and this knowledge is not derived from revelation, so it must be objective. The author answers by distinguishing between natural compatibility/incompatibility (which he admits as necessary knowledge) and any other claimed necessary moral knowledge (which he denies). Thus, he rejects the objectivity of moral judgments beyond natural inclinations.
