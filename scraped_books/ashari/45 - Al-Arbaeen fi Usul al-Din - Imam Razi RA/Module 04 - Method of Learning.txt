Opening Supplication and Purpose of the Book
Author opens with supplication, praise of God, and states his purpose in writing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Razi opens his theological work with praise of God, testimony of faith, and statement of purpose, establishing the foundation for the arguments to come.

Core Idea
The opening of a kalam work is itself a theological act.

Imam al-Razi begins his book with a supplication and elaborate praise of God's attributes. This is not merely a formality; it sets the theological tone and reminds the reader of the doctrines being defended. The passage concludes with a statement of purpose: that the author has compiled books containing proofs against doubts. This lesson examines this opening section.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening supplication and praise
Read the beginning supplication and the extended praise of God. Notice the specific attributes highlighted: uniqueness, pre-eternity, transcendence, omniscience, beneficence, majesty, greatness, generosity, and wisdom.
These attributes are central to Islamic theology and will be defended later.
2
Understand the Shahada and blessings
Read the testimony of faith and the blessings upon the Prophet. Recognize that this is a standard component of classical Islamic works.
3
Spot the transition 'أما بعد'
Identify the phrase 'أما بعد' (to proceed). This formula signals the end of the preamble and the start of the main content.
It is a common device in Arabic scholarly literature.
4
Recognize the author's stated purpose
Read the final sentence where Razi states that God enabled him to write books containing proofs and answers to doubts. This frames the present work as part of that larger project.
This purpose aligns with the genre of kalam: defending faith through reasoned argument.

Why Rest Must Be Proven as a Positive Attribute
Razi explains why rest must be proven as positive to argue against eternal rest.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why proving that 'rest' (sukūn) is a positive, existential attribute is a necessary preliminary step in the argument against the eternity of bodies.

Core Idea
Rest must be a real thing, not just absence, or the argument against eternal rest collapses.

Imam Razi is setting up a crucial methodological point. Before he can argue that bodies cannot be eternally at rest, he must first establish that 'rest' is a real, positive attribute—not merely the absence of motion. This may seem like a subtle distinction, but it has profound implications for the logical coherence of his proof. In this passage, he explains why defining rest as a non-entity would undermine his entire case.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions of rest
Read the passage carefully and note that Razi contrasts his own view (rest is existential) with the philosophers' claim (rest is absence of motion). Mark where each definition appears.
The philosophers' definition is the key obstacle Razi must overcome.
2
Understand the logical link
Notice Razi's reasoning: if rest were merely non-existence, then its eternal cessation would be possible by consensus—which would break his proof that eternal rest cannot cease. Work through the conditional argument step by step.
Use the example of the world's origination to see the problem clearly.
3
See the conclusion
Razi concludes that the claim 'what is eternal cannot cease' only applies to existent things, not non-existents. Therefore, to apply it to rest, rest must be proved existential.
This shows why proving rest is existential is a necessary preliminary.
4
Reflect on the method
Consider why Razi begins with a methodological introduction rather than jumping straight into proofs. This is a hallmark of systematic theology (kalām).

The third introduction: on explaining that it is impossible for bodies to be at rest in pre-eternity. Know that we need in this place to establish the proof that rest (sukūn) is an existential (wujūdī) matter. The philosophers claim that it is an expression for the absence of motion from what is capable of moving. We only needed to establish this premise because our proof that rest cannot be eternal is that we say: if rest were eternal, its cessation would not be possible, but its cessation is possible, so it must not be eternal. And if rest were a non-existential (ʿadamī) matter, this speech would not be valid, because the cessation of non-existence in pre-eternity is agreed upon as possible. For if that were not possible, the statement of the world’s temporal origination (ḥudūth) would be invalid. For the questioner may say: if the world were temporally originated, its non-existence would be eternal. And if its non-existence were eternal, it would not cease due to the advent of existence. Since the world exists, we know that its non-existence was not eternal. If its non-existence was not eternal, then its existence was eternal, so the world would be eternal. Thus it is established that we cannot say: everything that is eternal is impossible to cease; rather we must restrict this claim to existential matters. So it is said: everything that existed in pre-eternity is impossible to cease. That being the case, then we need to establish that rest is an existential matter, so that the argument is sound.
Source pages p. 23

Commentary
This passage is a methodological introduction. Razi begins by stating the third introduction of his work: proving that bodies cannot be eternally at rest. He immediately identifies a key obstacle: the philosophers define rest as merely the absence of motion (ʿadam al-ḥaraka), which is a non-entity (ʿadamī). If rest were non-existential, then his planned argument against eternal rest would fail. He explains why: if rest were merely non-existence, then its cessation in pre-eternity would be possible by consensus, because non-existence is not an entity that could be said to be eternal in a way that prevents cessation. He gives an example of a potential objection: if the world is temporally originated, its non-existence before creation would be eternal, and if that non-existence could not cease, the world could never come into existence. But since the world does exist, its non-existence cannot be eternal. Therefore, the claim that an eternal thing cannot cease must be restricted to existent things, not non-existents. Consequently, to argue that eternal rest cannot cease, he must first prove that rest is a positive, existential attribute. Only then can the argument proceed. The passage sets up the necessity of proving rest as wujūdī, establishing a foundational step for the subsequent proofs.

Necessary knowledge of place suffices; no deep definition needed.

Razi addresses a common demand for a precise definition of space. He argues that the everyday, necessary knowledge that bodies can be pointed to as 'here' or 'there' is enough to distinguish between rest and motion. This practical approach cuts through philosophical complexity and establishes the required premise for further arguments.

All Conceptions Are Intuitive: Razi's Proof and Defense
Razi enumerates the possibilities for conceptions and proves they are all intuitive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that all conceptions are intuitive, including his key dilemma and his responses to two objections.

Core Idea
Seeking a conception is impossible whether you already have it or not.

Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, in the final chapter of his work, tackles a foundational question: can we acquire new conceptions, or are all conceptions innate? He proposes a powerful argument that any attempt to acquire a conception either presupposes ignorance (impossible to seek) or prior knowledge (redundant). This leads him to conclude that all conceptions are intuitive. He then addresses clever objections to this position.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Focus on the flow of the argument: Razi states his claim, gives a proof, and then answers objections.
Notice how Razi uses technical terms like tasawwur and tasdiq.
2
Understand the classification of knowledge
Razi begins by dividing knowledge into conception (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq). This is a key distinction in logic and epistemology.
Conception is simple understanding; assent is judgment of truth or falsehood.
3
Grasp the dilemma for acquiring conceptions
Razi argues that if a conception is sought to be acquired, either it is not perceived (then the mind cannot seek it) or it is already perceived (then acquiring it is redundant). This is the tahsil al-hasil argument.

An objector suggests a conception might be perceived from one aspect but not another, so acquisition completes the deficient aspect. Razi replies that the aspects are different, so the same dilemma applies to each aspect separately.

Another objection says the same dilemma would apply to assents, implying no assents are acquired, which is false. Razi distinguishes conception from assent: assent is a composite state involving multiple conceptions, so it can be partially known. Conception is unitary, so it cannot be partially unknown.
This reveals a key difference: assents can be acquired even though conceptions are intuitive.

Then from this premise branches off another premise, namely the premise of good and evil, such as it is said: 'This act is good, so it must be done; and this act is evil, so it must be avoided.' This premise seems to be one of the types of the first premise, because perfection and deficiency are a genus under which there are three types: perfection and deficiency in essence, in attributes, and in actions. Evil and good are expressions of perfection and deficiency in actions. When you have understood this, we say: Most of the investigations of the sects of the theologians are derived from this premise.
Source pages p. 472

Commentary
This passage classifies the foundational premises of kalam. Al-Razi states that there are two major premises used by theologians and philosophers. The first is the premise of perfection (kamal) and deficiency (nuqsan): whatever attribute is a perfection must be affirmed for God, and whatever is a deficiency must be negated. Most theological schools are built on this. From it branches the premise of good and evil (husn wa qubh): acts that are good should be done, and acts that are evil should be avoided. This is a type of the first premise because perfection and deficiency are a genus with three species: in essence, in attributes, and in actions. Good and evil pertain to perfection and deficiency in actions. Thus, most theological debates derive from this single premise.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think 'good' and 'evil' are independent moral axioms independent of the concept of perfection.
Correction: Al-Razi subsumes them under the broader principle of perfection and deficiency, specifically as subtypes related to actions.
