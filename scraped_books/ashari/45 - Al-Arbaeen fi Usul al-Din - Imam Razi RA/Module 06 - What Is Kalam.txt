The Definition of the World and the Two Categories of Existence
Razi defines the world and presents the binary division of existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the technical definition of the world in kalam and the fundamental division of existence into necessary and possible being.

Core Idea
Existence is divided into that which cannot not exist and that which can.

In this first preliminary, Imam Razi establishes the foundational definitions needed to discuss the world and its relation to God. He begins by quoting the mutakallimūn’s definition of the world as everything other than God, and then offers a precise logical analysis of existence.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of the world
Read the first sentence where Razi reports the mutakallimūn's definition: 'the world is everything that exists besides God.' Notice the scope — it includes all created beings, not just physical matter.
This definition is the starting point for all discussions about the world.
2
Identify the two kinds of existent
Razi then says existence is of two kinds: one that is essentially not susceptible to non-existence, and one that is. Find these two categories in the text.
The criterion is the intrinsic capacity for non-existence, not actual occurrence.
3
Distinguish Necessary from Possible
The first kind is called the Necessary Existent (God), the second is the possible existent. Understand that only God is necessary; everything else is possible.
This distinction is crucial for later proofs of God's existence.

The First Preliminary: On the Reality of the World. The theologians (al-mutakallimūn) said: the world is everything that exists besides God Almighty. The precise statement on this topic is that we say: existence is of two kinds. This is because a thing that exists is either, by virtue of its very essence, not susceptible to non-existence at all, or, by virtue of its very essence, susceptible to non-existence. The existent whose essence, as such, is not at all susceptible to non-existence is called the Necessary Existent in Himself (wājib al-wujūd li-dhātihi), which is God—exalted and high. As for the existent whose essence, as such, is susceptible to non-existence, it is called the possible existent in itself (mumkin al-wujūd li-dhātihi), and it is, according to rational division, of three kinds: what occupies space (al-mutahayyiz), what inheres in what occupies space (al-ḥall fī al-mutahayyiz), and that which neither occupies space nor inheres in what occupies space.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification. First, Razi gives the technical definition of the world as used by the mutakallimūn: everything that exists other than God. Then he provides a thorough analysis: existence is divided into two fundamental categories based on the intrinsic capacity for non-existence. The possible existent is then rationally divided into three: (a) that which is spacial (al-mutahayyiz) - something that can be pointed to in physical space; (b) that which inheres in a spacial entity (al-ḥall fī al-mutahayyiz) - i.e., accidents; and (c) that which is neither spacial nor inhering in something spacial - which refers to non-physical substances like the soul or intellect. This classification covers all created beings.

The Three Kinds of Possible Existent
Razi divides the possible existent into three rational kinds.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational division of possible existents into three categories: spacial, inhering in spacial, and neither.

Core Idea
All possible beings are either spacial, accidents in spacial entities, or non-physical substances.

After establishing that possible existents are those that can cease to exist, Razi explains that they can be further classified rationally. This classification covers all created beings without exception.

Guided Path
1
Read the threefold division
Read the sentence that begins 'وهو بحسب القسمة العقلية على ثلاثة أقسام'. Notice the three categories listed.
The pronoun 'هو' refers back to the possible existent.
2
Understand each category
The first is al-mutahayyiz (spacial, i.e., bodies), the second is al-ḥall fī al-mutahayyiz (inhering in spacial, i.e., accidents), the third is that which neither occupies space nor inheres in space (e.g., souls, intellects).
These three exhaust all possible created beings.
3
Reflect on the completeness
Consider why Razi says this division is 'according to rational division' — it is a logical classification, not based on empirical observation.
This classification is foundational for later discussions on cosmology and substance-accident ontology.

Spatial Entities: Bodies and Atoms Defined
Razi defines al-mutahayyiz and divides it into body and indivisible atom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's definition of a spatial entity (al-mutahayyiz) and its division into body (jism) and indivisible atom (al-jawhar al-fard), and why the dispute with the Mu'tazila over the definition of body is linguistic rather than rational.

Core Idea
Every spatial being is either a divisible body or an indivisible atom.

Razi begins by establishing a fundamental category of possible existents: that which occupies space. He defines it clearly and then divides it into two types. This classification is essential for later arguments about the nature of the universe. As you read, notice how Razi handles the Mu'tazila's differing definition—he dismisses it as merely linguistic, not theological.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
First, read the Arabic passage and its translation to get a sense of Razi's concise style. Pay attention to the key terms: al-mutahayyiz, jism, and al-jawhar al-fard.
2
Understand the definition of al-mutahayyiz
Razi defines al-mutahayyiz as that which can be pointed to sensibly as being 'here' or 'there'. This is the core idea of spatial occupancy. Ensure you grasp this before moving on.
3
Identify the two subdivisions
Al-mutahayyiz is either divisible (body) or indivisible (atom). Note Razi's definition of a body: something composed of two or more parts.
4
Note the Mu'tazila's alternative definition
Razi mentions that the Mu'tazila define a body as long, wide, and deep, with a minimum of eight parts. He comments that this dispute is linguistic, not rational.
This is a key point: Razi considers the disagreement about wording, not about reality.
5
Reflect on the significance
Ask yourself: Why does Razi dismiss the dispute as linguistic? What does this tell you about his approach to theological disagreements? Think about how definitions shape arguments.

Al-Razi defines the world and divides existence into necessary and possible.

Learning Objective

Al-Razi begins by giving the theologians' definition of the world: everything existing that is not Allah. Then he immediately deepens the analysis by dividing all existing things based on their relationship to non-existence. This twofold division is the conceptual foundation for all subsequent arguments about the contingency of the world.

Razi defines qadir and defends against the objection of eternal necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's definition of qadir and why original suitability suffices to distinguish a free agent from a necessitating cause.

Core Idea
A qadir is defined by original suitability for both act and its opposite.

Razi begins this dense passage by clarifying what it means for God to be qadir (powerful agent). He then presents a powerful objection: if God's will is eternal, does that not make Him a necessitating cause? His response hinges on the concept of 'original suitability' (al-salāḥiyya al-aṣliyya), which preserves divine freedom without requiring change in God.

Answering Objections on Knowledge and Ability
Razi answers two objections on knowledge and the meaning of 'able to leave'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's responses to objections that eternal knowledge or the meaning of 'ability to not do' might negate divine freedom.

Core Idea
Knowledge follows action, and 'able to leave' means real possibility to not do.

After establishing the core distinction, Razi addresses two further objections. The first concerns God's eternal knowledge: if God knows the act will occur, can He still refrain? The second asks how 'leaving' (tark) can be an object of power. Razi's answers are subtle yet crucial for a coherent understanding of divine agency.

1
Read the answer about knowledge
Start with 'وأما الجواب عن السؤال الثالث'. Razi argues that knowledge is consequent upon the act, which depends on power and will; thus knowledge cannot prevent power.
This is a causal order argument: knowledge follows the known.

Trace the dependency chain
Identify the sequence: act → knowledge; act → power and will. Since knowledge is dependent on the act, it cannot be a prior constraint.

Examine the answer on 'ability to leave'
Read 'وأما الجواب عن السؤال الرابع'. Razi clarifies that 'being able to do' includes the real possibility of not doing, which is equivalent to 'leaving' as it was.
This does not require a separate act of leaving; it is the absence of the act.
4
Connect to the bigger picture
These answers reinforce that divine power is not undermined by prior knowledge or by the notion of leaving. The qadir remains free.

The Linguistic Distinction Between Nazar and Ru'ya
Al-Razi presents a linguistic proof distinguishing nazar from ru'ya using transitivity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi uses a linguistic argument (transitivity and Quranic usage) to prove that 'nazar' (looking) is different from 'ru'ya' (seeing).

Core Idea
Verb transitivity can reveal whether two actions are truly identical or distinct.

Here we encounter al-Razi's precise linguistic approach to theological questions. He observes that while 'nazartu ilayhi' (I looked at him) is correct, 'nazartuhu' (I looked him) is not, unlike 'ra'aytuhu' (I saw him). This grammatical difference, combined with the unanimous interpretation of a Quranic verse as metaphorical, shows that nazar and ru'ya are distinct acts.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the literal translation. Pay attention to the verbs used.
Notice the difference between 'nazartu ilayhi' and 'nazartuhu'.
2
Identify the core linguistic observation
Al-Razi points out that 'nazar' takes the preposition 'ila' (to) while 'ru'ya' is transitive without preposition. Why does this matter?
3
Understand the Quranic evidence
The verse 'Did you not see your Lord?' uses 'tara' (from ra'a) with 'ila', and exegetes unanimously interpret this as metaphorical. That supports the distinction.
If nazar were the same as ru'ya, the metaphorical interpretation would be unnecessary.
4
Grasp the logical conclusion
Since 'nazartuhu' is not valid Arabic, but 'ra'aytuhu' is, and the Quranic usage reinforces the difference, we know nazar and ru'ya are different acts.
This is a simple but powerful linguistic proof.

Commentary
This passage is the Ashab's response to the Mu'tazila's argument that the validity of exception proves the original statement was universal. Classification of Knowledge into Tasawwur and Tasdiq
Imam Razi classifies knowledge into conceptualization and affirmation and argues for self-evident concepts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Razi's classification of knowledge into conceptualization (tasawwur) and affirmation (tasdiq) and his argument that basic concepts must be self-evident to avoid circularity.

Core Idea
Basic concepts must be self-evident to avoid endless circularity or infinite regress.

In this concluding section of his work, Imam Razi establishes a foundational classification of knowledge and demonstrates that not all concepts can be learned through acquisition. His argument is crucial for avoiding logical pitfalls like circular reasoning and infinite regress. Read on to see how he proves that some concepts must be innate to the mind.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and argument about the classification of knowledge. The author, Imam Razi, begins the fortieth question by stating that knowledge is either conceptualization (tasawwur) or affirmation (tasdiq). He then considers three possibilities for conceptualizations: all are acquired, all are self-evident (badihiyya), or a mix. He argues that the first possibility (all acquired) is false because it leads to circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul). He holds the second view: all basic conceptualizations are self-evident. His proof: if a conceptualization is sought to be acquired, either it is already perceived (mash'ur bihi) or not. If not perceived, the mind is unaware of it, so it cannot be sought. If already perceived, its conceptualization is already present, so seeking to acquire it is asking for what is already had (tahsil al-hasil), which is impossible. An objection is raised: what if the concept is perceived in one respect and not in another, and the acquisition is to complete the deficient aspect? The author answers that the mind's judgment of perception and non-perception apply to different respects, so the division (into perceived and not) must be made within each respect. Then a second objection: the same argument would apply to affirmations, making all affirmations self-evident, which is false. The author answers that conceptualization and affirmation are different: affirmation is an additional mental state over the conceptualizations of subject, predicate, negation, and affirmation. It is possible to have these four conceptualizations without the affirmation, so affirmation is a distinct, non-self-evident cognition. The passage ends mid-sentence, indicating that the discussion continues, but the core argument about conceptualizations and the response to objections is complete.
