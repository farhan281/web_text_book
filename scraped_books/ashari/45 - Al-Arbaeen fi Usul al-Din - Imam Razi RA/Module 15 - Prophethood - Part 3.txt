Imam al-Razi presents the fourteenth objection and three answers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Razi defends the Prophet's infallibility by interpreting the verse on shirk through three distinct answers.

Core Idea
A conditional threat does not imply the condition is possible.

This lesson examines the fourteenth objection to prophethood: Quran 39:65 threatens the Prophet with loss of reward if he commits shirk, which seems to imply he could do so. Imam al-Razi provides three sophisticated answers that preserve the Prophet's infallibility while respecting the Quranic text. You will see how indirect address, hidden shirk, and hypothetical statements are used to counter the objection.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and verses
First, read the objection based on Quran 39:65. Notice how the threat is directed at the Prophet personally. Then read the three answers that follow.
The verse says 'If you associate others with Allah, your work would surely become worthless.'
2
Understand the first answer: indirect address
The first answer uses the principle of 'I mean you, but listen, O neighbor.' The Quran sometimes addresses the Prophet while meaning his community, as shown in the divorce verse (65:1).
This is based on a narration from Ibn Abbas.
3
Understand the second answer: hidden shirk
The second answer interprets 'shirk' as hidden shirk (al-shirk al-khafiyy), which is a subtle reliance on other than Allah, not literal idolatry. This is a spiritual imperfection but not a major sin.
Hidden shirk can be present even in prophets as a minor flaw.
4
Understand the third answer: hypothetical statement
The third answer treats the verse as a hypothetical (taqdir al-wuqu'), similar to Quran 21:22 which says if there had been multiple gods, the heavens and earth would have been ruined. The condition is impossible by divine ordainment.
This shows that the threat is a logical explanation, not a real possibility.
5
Reflect on the overall defense
Think about how these three answers together safeguard the doctrine of prophetic infallibility. Each answer offers a different angle: linguistic, spiritual, and logical.
The objection is effectively countered without denying the Quranic text.

Proof that the Harder is Superior in Reward
Proof that the harder act is superior via text and rational analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the textual and rational arguments that harder acts of obedience yield greater reward.

Core Idea
Both hadith and rational consistency confirm that difficulty increases reward.

Having shown that human obedience is harder, Imam Razi now proves that the harder act is superior in reward. He cites a hadith about the best deeds being the most difficult, and then offers a powerful rational analogy: if hard and easy acts gave equal reward, the extra hardship would be pointless and harmful, which cannot be from God. Let's analyze this proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. Note the two sources: textual (hadith) and rational (qiyas).
The text says: 'وإنما قلنا: إن الأشق أفضل للنص والقياس'.
2
Examine the textual evidence
Look at the two hadith references: 'أفضل الأعمال أحمزها' and 'أجرك على قدر نصبك'. Understand their meaning.
'أحمز' means most difficult; 'نصب' means effort.
3
Understand the rational analogy
Follow the logical argument: if easy and hard acts had equal reward, the extra hardship would be pointless and harmful, so such acts would be forbidden. Since they are not forbidden, hardship must bring more reward.
This is a qiyas (analogical argument) based on the principle that God does not command pointless harm.

Fourth Proof: The Verse of Selection
Imam Razi uses a Quranic verse to argue for prophetic superiority over angels and addresses an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi uses the verse of divine selection (Ayat al-Istifa) to argue for the superiority of prophets over angels, and how he resolves a potential objection from another Quranic verse.

Core Idea
The verse of selection proves prophetic superiority over all creation when interpreted with context.

Imam Razi presents a scriptural proof from the Quran, the verse of selection (Al-Imran 3:33). He interprets 'worlds' to include angels, establishing that the chosen prophets are superior to all creation. However, a potential contradiction arises from another verse that seems to favor the Children of Israel. Razi resolves this by clarifying the conditional meaning of 'worlds' and the specificity of divine statements.

The Conjunction Fallacy: Why 'Waw' Does Not Imply Order
Razi presents the third objection and a detailed logical and linguistic analysis.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Waw only combines; it does not rank.

In the third and most detailed objection, Imam Razi turns to the linguistic nature of the conjunction 'waw'. He argues that 'waw' simply combines items without implying any order of superiority. The opponent's example of the scholar, minister, and sultan only appears to show order because we already know the sultan's rank by reason, not from the conjunction. Applying this to the verse leads to a circular argument: we would need to already know angels are better to interpret the verse as showing that.

Priority in seeking forgiveness reveals moral status.

In this passage, Razi presents a subtle argument based on how prophets and angels seek forgiveness. He observes that prophets always begin with themselves, while angels only ask for others. This behavioral contrast, he argues, points to the sinlessness of angels and their higher rank. Let's examine the evidence and reasoning.

Since angels do not seek forgiveness for themselves, they are free from sin. This makes them superior to prophets, who do seek forgiveness for themselves.
This is a kalam argument based on observed prayer patterns.

Knowledge and Rank: Jibril vs. Muhammad
Razi argues for Jibril's greater knowledge but denies automatic superiority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi argues that Jibril has greater knowledge than Muhammad, yet does not hold a higher rank, based on the division of sciences into rational and transmitted.

Core Idea
Greater knowledge does not automatically mean higher rank.

In this passage from the thirty-third issue of his work, Razi tackles a delicate theological question: Does Jibril's role as the conveyor of revelation make him more knowledgeable than Muhammad? He distinguishes between types of knowledge and argues that while Jibril is indeed more knowledgeable in certain respects, this does not entail superiority in rank.

Guided Path
1
Understand the teacher-principle
Razi begins by stating a general principle: the teacher must be more knowledgeable than the student. This sets up the logical framework for the discussion.
2
Learn the division of sciences
Sciences are divided into two types: rational (acquired through reason) and transmitted (known only through revelation). This classification is key to the argument.
3
See why Jibril excels in rational sciences
For rational sciences, especially those about creation, Jibril has more knowledge because he is older and has witnessed more of creation firsthand.
4
Understand Jibril's role in transmitted sciences
Jibril is the intermediary for all revelation, so he knows all past and present laws, while Muhammad only knows what was revealed to him. Thus Jibril is more knowledgeable here too.
5
Grasp the distinction between knowledge and rank
Despite being more knowledgeable, Jibril is not necessarily superior in rank. Knowledge and virtue are separate; rank depends on other factors.
Razi addresses Adam's knowledge of names to reinforce that even if Adam knew all names, Jibril's knowledge of realities and laws is superior.

Commentary
This passage presents and analyzes the twelfth doubt (shubhah) of the opponents, which is the argument from the Prophetic hadith 'Man kuntu mawlahu fa 'Aliyyun mawlahu' (Whoever I am his mawla, Ali is his mawla) to prove the imamate of Ali. Imam Razi structures the discussion into two stations (maqam). The first station concerns the authenticity (tashih) of the hadith itself. He reports the strongest argument: the community (umma) is divided into two groups regarding this hadith—some use it to prove a virtue (fadila) of Ali, others to prove his imamate. This disagreement implies that both groups accept the hadith as genuine, and any report that the umma agrees to accept is necessarily authentic. The second station is the actual argument for imamate, which has two aspects. The first aspect relies on the Prophet's preparatory question: 'Am I not more entitled (awla) to you than yourselves?' When they answered yes, he said: 'So whoever I am his mawla, Ali is his mawla.' Razi then establishes three premises. First, the word mawla can carry the meaning of awla (priority/entitlement), citing Quran 57:15 where 'mawla' means 'more entitled.' Second, whether mawla has other meanings or not, the context forces the interpretation of awla because if mawla were ambiguous, the preceding phrase mentioning awla clarifies it. So mawla must be interpreted as awla. Third, from the meaning of priority (awlawiyya) in all matters, it follows that Ali has authority over the believers in the same way the Prophet did—namely, that his command takes precedence over their own judgment. This is precisely the meaning of imamate. The second aspect approaches the word mawla from its linguistic meanings. Razi lists possible meanings: manumitter, manumitted, cousin, ally, helper, and disposer. Consensus (ijma') excludes the first four. The meaning of helper (nasir) is rejected because it is too obvious and not worthy of the Prophet gathering a crowd to explain. Thus the only remaining meaning is disposer (mutasarrif), i.e., one who has authority over others. This again leads to the definition of imamate. Thus both aspects converge on proving Ali's imamate.

The twelfth doubt: clinging to his saying: "Whoever I am his mawla, Ali is his mawla." The discussion about clinging to this report is based on two stations: The first station: authenticating the origin of the report, and the strongest thing said about it is that the community (umma) regarding this report are on two opinions: some cling to it in establishing a virtue of Ali, and some cling to it in establishing his imamate, and that entails their agreement on its wording, and every report that the community has agreed to accept, definitive judgment of its authenticity is obligatory. Know that clinging to this aspect depends on premises: The first: that the word mawla can bear the meaning of awla (more entitled/priority), and the evidence for it is His saying, exalted: "Your abode is the Fire, it is your mawla" [Al-Hadid: 15]; the commentators said: its meaning is: the Fire is more entitled to you. So this indicates that the word mawla can bear the meaning of awla. The second premise: that the intended meaning of his saying: "Whoever I am his mawla, Ali is his mawla" is: whoever I am more entitled to him, Ali is more entitled to him; and the evidence for it is that the word mawla can bear the meaning of awla. If the word mawla does not bear another meaning, then it is obligatory to interpret it here as awla; and if it bears another meaning besides awla, we say: on this assumption, the word mawla would be ambiguous (mujmal), needing clarification and explanation, and the speech mentioned in its prelude—which is the mention of awla—is suitable as a clarification for it, so it is obligatory that this ambiguous word be interpreted by what we mentioned in the first premise to remove the ambiguity. So it is established that whether the word mawla can bear another meaning besides awla or not, it is obligatory that mawla be interpreted here as awla. The third premise: when it is established that his saying: "Whoever I am his mawla" means "whoever I am more entitled to him, Ali is more entitled to him," we say: this indicates the imamate; because his saying: "whoever I am more entitled to him, Ali is more entitled to him" must be interpreted as establishing priority (awlawiyya) in all things, by the evidence of the validity of the exception, by the evidence of removing ambiguity, and the meaning of priority is: that his authority over you is more entitled than your authority over yourselves, and there is no meaning for imamate except this. So by this, the indication of this report for the imamate of Ali ibn Abi Talib is established. The second aspect in explaining the indication of this report for the imamate: that the word mawla in the language came with the meanings of the manumitter, the manumitted, the cousin, the ally, the helper, and the disposer. And consensus (ijma') indicates that the manumitter, the manumitted, the cousin, and the ally are not intended. And it is not permissible that the helper be intended; because then the estimate would be: "Whoever I am a helper to him, Ali is a helper to him," and this is not permissible; because this meaning is extremely obvious, so it is not fitting for the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) to gather a great multitude to explain this meaning. When all are invalidated, nothing remains except that the intended meaning be the disposer (mutasarrif). So the meaning becomes: "Whoever I have disposal over him, Ali has disposal over him," and there is no meaning for imamate except that. So it is established that this report indicates his imamate.

Commentary
This passage presents and analyzes the twelfth doubt (shubhah) of the opponents, which is the argument from the Prophetic hadith 'Man kuntu mawlahu fa 'Aliyyun mawlahu' (Whoever I am his mawla, Ali is his mawla) to prove the imamate of Ali. Imam Razi structures the discussion into two stations (maqam). The first station concerns the authenticity (tashih) of the hadith itself. He reports the strongest argument: the community (umma) is divided into two groups regarding this hadith—some use it to prove a virtue (fadila) of Ali, others to prove his imamate. This disagreement implies that both groups accept the hadith as genuine, and any report that the umma agrees to accept is necessarily authentic. The second station is the actual argument for imamate, which has two aspects. The first aspect relies on the Prophet's preparatory question: 'Am I not more entitled (awla) to you than yourselves?' When they answered yes, he said: 'So whoever I am his mawla, Ali is his mawla.' Razi then establishes three premises. First, the word mawla can carry the meaning of awla (priority/entitlement), citing Quran 57:15 where 'mawla' means 'more entitled.' Second, whether mawla has other meanings or not, the context forces the interpretation of awla because if mawla were ambiguous, the preceding phrase mentioning awla clarifies it. So mawla must be interpreted as awla. Third, from the meaning of priority (awlawiyya) in all matters, it follows that Ali has authority over the believers in the same way the Prophet did—namely, that his command takes precedence over their own judgment. This is precisely the meaning of imamate. The second aspect approaches the word mawla from its linguistic meanings. Razi lists possible meanings: manumitter, manumitted, cousin, ally, helper, and disposer. Consensus (ijma') excludes the first four. The meaning of helper (nasir) is rejected because it is too obvious and not worthy of the Prophet gathering a crowd to explain. Thus the only remaining meaning is disposer (mutasarrif), i.e., one who has authority over others. This again leads to the definition of imamate. Thus both aspects converge on proving Ali's imamate.

Commentary
This passage introduces the thirteenth doubt (shubhah), which relies on the famous hadith 'Anta minni bi manzilati Harun min Musa' (You are to me in the station of Harun to Musa). Imam Razi notes that the authentication (tashih) of this report follows the same method used for the mawla hadith, so he does not repeat it. He then proceeds to the argument for imamate, which is built on premises. Only the first premise is given here, and it is incomplete. The premise is that Harun was not the khalifah of Musa after Musa's death (in the sense of being his successor after his lifetime), but he was the khalifah during Musa's lifetime, as proven by Quran 7:142. From this, it follows that if Harun had survived Musa, he would have been the khalifah after his death; otherwise, his khilafa would have been revoked, which is problematic. The argument implies that if Harun would have been the successor after Musa's death (had he lived), then by analogy, Ali—who holds the same position relative to the Prophet—must also be the khalifah after the Prophet's death.
