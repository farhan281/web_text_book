Allah Exposes the Slanderer al-Ahwazi
Ibn Asakir narrates an incident exposing al-Ahwazi's hypocrisy in accusing Imam al-Ash'ari.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Allah exposed the liar al-Ahwazi through a specific incident, demonstrating the principle that slanderers are disgraced and the honor of the imams is protected.

Core Idea
Allah exposes the hypocrite al-Ahwazi to protect the honor of the imams.

This passage recounts a powerful historical example of divine justice. Ibn Asakir narrates an incident where al-Ahwazi, who falsely accused Imam al-Ash'ari, was himself caught in a compromising situation. The lesson shows that Allah unveils the lies of slanderers and vindicates the righteous.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice the flow: first the author's comment, then the narrative.
Pay attention to the emotional tone – the author is clearly defending the honor of Imam al-Ash'ari.
2
Identify the key figures
Identify the main characters: al-Ahwazi (the slanderer), al-Muharrasi (his source), al-Mutarriz (the witness), and Imam al-Ash'ari (the victim). Understand their roles.
Al-Ahwazi is the one who falsely accused al-Ash'ari of something inappropriate.
3
Understand the contrast
Reflect on the irony: al-Ahwazi accused al-Ash'ari of being with a slave, but he himself was found with a black slave in a bathhouse.
This contrast shows the hypocrisy and Allah's exposure of his lie.
4
Reflect on divine justice
Consider why the author includes this story: to show that Allah disgraces liars and defends the honor of the pious imams.
The passage ends with a moral: such is the recompense for those who disparage the imams.

Criticism of al-Ahwazi's Linguistic Errors and Weakness
Ibn Asakir attacks al-Ahwazi's language and knowledge, warning against his deceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ibn 'Asakir discredits al-Ahwazi by exposing his linguistic errors and the danger of his deceptions, and why he focuses on the transmitters of his falsehoods.

Core Idea
Falsehood finds carriers; ignorance spreads through unworthy transmitters.

In this passage, Ibn 'Asakir turns from doctrinal refutation to a pointed critique of al-Ahwazi's language and knowledge. He argues that al-Ahwazi's speech is so flawed that it would be beneath notice, except that ignorant people might be deceived by it. This lesson examines how Ibn 'Asakir uses linguistic criticism to undermine his opponent's credibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic carefully, then the translation. Notice the strong language Ibn 'Asakir uses, such as 'lahn' and 'rakāka'.
Pay attention to the emotional tone.
2
Identify the two levels of criticism
Ibn 'Asakir criticizes both al-Ahwazi's wording (grammar, flimsiness) and his meaning. Understand why he disconnects the two.
He says: 'how can he be required to be correct in wording when he has erred in meaning?'
3
Analyze the proverb and its application
Reflect on the concluding proverb: 'For every fallen thing there is something that picks it up.' How does it relate to al-Ahwazi and his transmitters?
The proverb warns that falsehood will always find someone to propagate it.

Commentary
This passage is a direct polemic against an opponent named al-Ahwazi. The author (Ibn 'Asakir) criticizes al-Ahwazi's speech on multiple levels: grammatical errors (lahn), flimsiness (rakāka), and vulgar expressions that only lowly people use. He argues that al-Ahwazi's mistakes are obvious, and demanding correctness in wording from him is pointless since he already errs in meaning. The author then explains that he would have ignored such a person, except that naive people might be deceived. He expresses more amazement at those who transmitted al-Ahwazi's words—whom he derisively calls 'goats' and 'ignorant'—than at al-Ahwazi himself. The passage ends with a proverb: 'For every fallen thing there is something that picks it up,' meaning that every falsehood finds someone to propagate it.

Defense of al-Ash'ari: The Companions as Stars
Ibn Asakir defends al-Ash'ari by comparing slander against him to slander against the Sahaba.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ibn Asakir defends al-Ash'ari by demonstrating that even the most honored Companions were slandered, and thus slander does not diminish true rank.

Core Idea
Slander is no proof: the Companions were slandered yet remain guided stars.

In this passage, Ibn Asakir responds to a critic of Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari by turning the tables: the attack says more about the attacker than the target. He then invokes the highest standard—the Prophet's Companions—who were also bitterly slandered by various groups. By showing that the best of creation faced such attacks, he neutralizes the critic's argument and elevates al-Ash'ari's standing.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its English translation together. Let the argument unfold in the author's own words.
Notice the rhetorical force: the critic's attack is turned back on himself.
2
Identify the structure
Observe how Ibn Asakir moves from a direct refutation of the critic to citing the hadith about the Companions, then lists several Companions who were slandered, and ends with a general conclusion.
This is a classic argument a majore ad minus: if the best were slandered, then al-Ash'ari being slandered is no discredit.
3
Reflect on the argument's power
Ask yourself: why does comparing al-Ash'ari to the Companions strengthen the defense? What light does the hadith 'My Companions are like stars' shed on the matter?
The Companion's status as guiding stars is unaffected by slander; similarly, al-Ash'ari's status remains.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari against his critics. The author argues that the critic's attacks only harm himself and do not diminish al-Ash'ari's rank. He then draws a parallel: the Prophet's Companions were also slandered and cursed, yet they remain models. He cites the hadith that the Companions are like stars guiding the community. He addresses the Ash'aris directly, saying that if they are cursed like the Companions were, the cursers are the transgressors. He lists several major Companions—Abu Bakr, 'Umar, 'Uthman, 'Ali, 'A'isha—and notes that each was targeted by various sectarian groups (Rafida, Khawarij, Umayyads). The conclusion is that nearly every eminent figure in Islam has faced criticism or cursing, so al-Ash'ari being attacked is not surprising and does not diminish his stature.

Core Idea

Ibn Asakir responds to critics of Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari by reminding us that even the Prophet's Companions—whom we are commanded to follow—were slandered by enemies. He then extends the argument to subsequent imams, demonstrating that criticism is not evidence of fault but a trial for the community.

And I do not fear the blame of anyone who denies what I said, because I mentioned what I have fully known, and I intended to clarify the innocence of those who preceded among the Salaf from the accusation of those who fell into the evil of the later generations. And the Shaykh Abu Muhammad Hibatullah Ahmad ibn ibn ibn al-Akfani informed us, saying: Abu Muhammad Abd al-Aziz ibn Ahmad al-Kattani narrated to us, saying: Abu al-Husayn Abd al-Wahhab ibn Ja'far al-Maydani narrated to us, saying: Abu Hashim Abd al-Jabbar ibn Abd al-Samad al-Sulami narrated to us, saying: Abu Bakr al-Qasim ibn Isa al-Assar narrated to us, saying: Ibrahim ibn Ya'qub al-Juzajani narrated to us, saying: Abd al-Salam ibn Muhammad and Nu'aym ibn narrated to us, they said: Baqiyya narrated to us, saying: Bujayr ibn Sa'd narrated to me from Khalid ibn Ma'dan who said: Whoever endures blame in following the truth, God will turn that blame into praise for him; and whoever seeks praise in opposing the truth, God will turn that praise into blame against him. If someone blames me for defending this Imam and threatens me for clarifying his state out of ignorance or pretended ignorance, then I have no consideration for his objection, because what motivated me to do that is the desire for deliverance from the Fire in the Hereafter. And I said in response to him: O brothers, if my hand had found a helper, supporter, and friend, I would have explained what I attempted with a clear explanation and joined the previous of that with the later. By God, I swear the most sincere oath: none hates the scholars except a worthless person. O you who threaten me out of extreme ignorance, stop your threat, for I am not afraid. If you knew me, you would not frighten me; the filth of threat is not known to me. I have never softened to a backbiter who incites trouble, nor have I ever bent like the bending of the unjust. So I am the choke in the throat of every hypocrite, and I am the irritant in the eye of every opponent. I am the one who traveled in seeking guidance two journeys between deserts and wildernesses. I am the one who toured many cities from Isfahan to the borders of Ta'if. I have seen most of the cities of the East after Iraq and our well-known Syria. I collected in my travels every precious thing, and met every opponent and supporter. I heard the Sunnah of Ahmad after spending on it my inherited and acquired wealth. I narrated it with trustworthiness, integrity, and purity that repels the foolishness of the fornicator. I chose a doctrine that no innovation taints, but rather a successor follows a predecessor. So the just ones verify my creed, while the deniers reject it for lack of justice. So why do you blame me—may your blame be futile—for praising one whose praise exhausts the describer? This book contains the description of the One God, possessed of knowledge, and cognizant. Unique in knowledge, his other books are filled with his knowledge in subtleties. Distinguished by nobility, not denied his eminence in virtue without falsehoods. A sword against the enemies of Muhammad's religion, whether a denier, a skeptic, or a waverer. His companions are like stars, and his party are the people of knowledge and guides of the deviating. They are the security of people in their religion in the East and West, and a refuge for the fearful. So the Lord of servants, by His grace, placed them in the abode of standing—that is the wish of the cognizant—in a garden intertwined with meadows, surrounded by cushions and carpets. I authored this not to take dirhams from you for it, nor to eat small cakes, but to refute by it the speech of a lying fabricator in what a falsifier narrated. So look at its composition with contemplation upon its truth, and thank the deed of the arranger. For truth is not hidden from the contemplator, and falsehood vanishes like a passing lightning bolt. O our Lord, have mercy on our Shaykh and Imam, and reveal the truth of his worth to the revealer. And by Your power, tear the veil of whoever backbites him, whether a jealous one, a reproacher, or a slanderer. And turn their hearts to his companions, since they have declared You one, for You are the most capable of turning. And seal our speech with Your praise, O Generous, in gratitude for Your successive favors.

Commentary
This passage is a poetic defense and conclusion by the author, Ibn Asakir. It begins with a hadith (through a chain of transmission) about enduring blame for truth, then the author states his motivation: to clear the reputation of Imam al-Ash'ari from false accusations, seeking deliverance from Hellfire. He then recites a long poem in response to a critic. In the poem, he asserts that scholars are only hated by worthless people, and he is fearless because he is on the side of truth. He describes his extensive travels in pursuit of knowledge, collecting hadith and the Sunnah. He defends his creed as free from innovation, and praises the book itself as a description of God and a sword against enemies. He clarifies that his authorship is not for material gain but to rebut falsifiers. He ends with a supplication for mercy on the Imam and for protection from backbiters, asking God to turn hearts to the companions of al-Ash'ari. The poem uses vivid imagery and strong rhetorical devices to convey sincerity and authority.
