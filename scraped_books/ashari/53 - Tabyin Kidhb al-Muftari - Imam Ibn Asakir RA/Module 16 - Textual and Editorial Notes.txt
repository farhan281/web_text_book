The Principle of Acceptance: Only Islam is Accepted
Al-Zajjaj explains the principle of acceptance and the meaning of 'losers'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Allah accepts only Islam as a religion and deeds from its people, and the meaning of 'losers' in the Quranic context.

Core Idea
Allah only accepts Islam and deeds performed by its followers.

In this lesson, we explore al-Zajjaj's commentary on verse 3:85, where he establishes the fundamental principle that Allah does not accept any religion other than Islam. Al-Zajjaj explains the grammar of the verse and defines the term 'losers' as those whose deeds are lost, citing another Quranic verse as evidence.

Guided Path
1
Read the full passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice the chain of transmission and then al-Zajjaj's statement.
The chain shows the reliability of the narration.
2
Identify the principle
Find where al-Zajjaj states that Allah does not accept any religion other than Islam. Understand that this is the core claim.
3
Examine the grammatical note
Notice the explanation of 'yabtaghi' in the jussive form and the use of 'falan yuqbala' as the answer. This shows how grammar clarifies meaning.
Al-Zajjaj was a renowned grammarian.
4
Understand 'losers' with evidence
Al-Zajjaj defines 'al-khasirin' as those who lose their deeds, supporting this with verse 47:1. Reflect on the connection.
This emphasizes that deeds without correct faith are wasted.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary (tafsir) on the same Quranic verses (3:85-91) narrated through a chain of transmission from Ibrahim al-Zajjaj. The chain includes several scholars: Abu al-Fadl al-Baghdadi, Abu al-Hasan al-Asadi, al-Qadi Abu al-'Ala' al-Wasiti, Abu 'Ali al-Nahwi, and finally Abu Ishaq who heard from al-Zajjaj. Al-Zajjaj begins by asserting the principle that Allah only accepts Islam as a religion and deeds from its people. He then explains the verse 'And whoever seeks a religion other than Islam...' (3:85), noting that 'yabtaghi' is in the jussive form due to 'man', and the answer is 'falan yuqbala minhu'. He defines 'losers' as those who lose their deeds, citing 47:1 as evidence. Then he covers the verse about those who disbelieved after belief (3:86-91). He presents two interpretations of its revelation context: one about a group that apostatized and wanted to return with hidden disbelief, and another that it refers to Jews who disbelieved in Muhammad despite knowing his prophethood out of transgression and envy. He explains the curse's meaning: Allah distancing them from mercy and praising them for their disbelief, and people cursing each other on Judgment Day. He explains 'abiding eternally therein' as the punishment of the curse without reprieve. Then he addresses the exception 'except those who repent' (3:89), explaining that it means they show their error and reform what they corrupted. He emphasizes Allah's vast mercy in forgiving even such audacity. Finally, he discusses the verse denying repentance for those who increase in disbelief (3:90), explaining that it refers to hypocrites who outwardly pretend to repent while inwardly persisting in disbelief. The evidence is that they are called 'astray', indicating they are not truly repentant. The passage ends abruptly during the explanation of the next verse (3:91).

The Curse on Those Who Disbelieve After Belief
Al-Zajjaj explains the curse on those who disbelieve after belief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two interpretations of the verse about disbelieving after belief, the meaning of the curse, and the eternal punishment associated with it.

Core Idea
Disbelief after clear truth incurs the curse of Allah, angels, and all people.

In this lesson, al-Zajjaj continues his commentary on verses 3:86-88, explaining the context of those who disbelieve after having believed. He presents two interpretations: one about apostates who pretend to repent, and another about the Jews who rejected Muhammad despite knowing his prophethood. The lesson also delves into the meaning of the curse and the eternal punishment.

Exception for True Repentance and Warning Against Insincere Repentance
Al-Zajjaj explains the exception for true repentance and the rejection of insincere repentance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that sincere repentance is accepted even after great sin, but insincere repentance (with hidden disbelief) is not accepted.

Core Idea
True repentance erases the past; hidden disbelief blocks forgiveness.

This lesson covers al-Zajjaj's commentary on verses 3:89-90. He explains that the curse is lifted for those who truly repent and reform, showing their error. However, he warns that those who increase in disbelief while outwardly claiming repentance—like the hypocrites—will never have their repentance accepted. The passage ends with a partial explanation of the next verse.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic and translation. This part contains two main points: the exception for repentance and the warning about increased disbelief.
The text ends abruptly.
2
Understand the exception
Identify the exception clause 'except those who repent and reform' and note al-Zajjaj's explanation: they show their error and correct what they corrupted.
Repentance requires outward demonstration of change.
3
Grasp the warning about insincere repentance
The verse 'those who disbelieve after belief and then increase in disbelief... their repentance will not be accepted' is explained as referring to hypocrites who pretend to repent while holding disbelief. The evidence is that they are called 'astray'.
This shows that repentance must be genuine.
4
Reflect on Allah's vast mercy
Despite the severity, al-Zajjaj emphasizes that for true repentance, Allah's mercy is immense—He even forgives the one who dared to disbelieve after truth became clear.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary (tafsir) on the same Quranic verses (3:85-91) narrated through a chain of transmission from Ibrahim al-Zajjaj. Al-Zajjaj begins by asserting the principle that Allah only accepts Islam as a religion and deeds from its people. He then explains the verse 'And whoever seeks a religion other than Islam...' (3:85), noting that 'yabtaghi' is in the jussive form due to 'man', and the answer is 'falan yuqbala minhu'. He defines 'losers' as those who lose their deeds, citing 47:1 as evidence. Then he covers the verse about those who disbelieved after belief (3:86-91). He presents two interpretations of its revelation context: one about a group that apostatized and wanted to return with hidden disbelief, and another that it refers to Jews who disbelieved in Muhammad despite knowing his prophethood out of transgression and envy. He explains the curse's meaning: Allah distancing them from mercy and praising them for their disbelief, and people cursing each other on Judgment Day. He explains 'abiding eternally therein' as the punishment of the curse without reprieve. Then he addresses the exception 'except those who repent' (3:89), explaining that it means they show their error and reform what they corrupted. He emphasizes Allah's vast mercy in forgiving even such audacity. Finally, he discusses the verse denying repentance for those who increase in disbelief (3:90), explaining that it refers to hypocrites who outwardly pretend to repent while inwardly persisting in disbelief. The evidence is that they are called 'astray', indicating they are not truly repentant. The passage ends abruptly during the explanation of the next verse (3:91).

The Scribal Colophon: A Manuscript's Certificate
Colophon recording scribe, date, and transmission chain.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how manuscript colophons authenticate texts by recording the scribe, date, and transmission chain.

Core Idea
Colophons are the DNA of manuscript transmission.

The colophon is the final note a scribe adds to a manuscript, recording when and by whom it was copied, and from whom the copy was heard. This lesson unpacks the colophon of a key Ash'ari work, revealing the chain of oral and written transmission that connects us to the author.

The colophon is written in dense, formulaic Arabic typical of medieval manuscripts.

Colophons provide a chain of custody for the manuscript, ensuring it was transmitted accurately.
