Al-Ash'ari on the Nature of Faith
Al-Ash'ari distinguishes between God's eternal faith and human created faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Ash'ari's balanced position on faith—distinguishing God's eternal attribute from the created act of faith in humans.

Core Idea
God's faith is eternal; human faith is created.

The early theological schools debated whether faith was created or eternal. Al-Ash'ari resolved this by distinguishing between the divine and human aspects. In this passage, he outlines his middle path.

2
Analyze the middle path
Al-Ash'ari says faith is of two kinds. Identify the basis for each: God's faith is eternal because of His name al-Mu'min; human faith is created because it appears from them and they are accountable.
The Qur'anic proof is key.
3

This shows al-Ash'ari's commitment to both divine transcendence and human responsibility.

Faith: Two Kinds, Created and Uncreated
Ibn Asakir reports Ash'ari's distinction between God's eternal faith and creatures' created faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari resolved the debate over whether faith is created or eternal by distinguishing between God's attribute of faith and human acts of faith.

Core Idea
Faith is not all one kind; God's eternal attribute differs from our created act.

This passage from Ibn Asakir presents al-Ash'ari's balanced position on a contentious issue. The Mu'tazila and others insisted all faith is created, while the Hashwiyya insisted it is all eternal. Al-Ash'ari steered a middle course by showing that 'faith' applies to both Allah (as an eternal attribute) and to creatures (as a temporal act). Let's explore his reasoning.

Al-Ash'ari says 'Faith is two faiths.' Identify the two kinds he lists.
He distinguishes the faith of Allah from the faith of creatures.

This avoids both extremes: it preserves God's transcendence while affirming human responsibility.

The faith of created beings (humans, angels, etc.) is created because it originates from them and is subject to reward and punishment. This allows a nuanced view: God's attribute of being 'the Faithful' is eternal, but human belief is a temporal act.

The Grave Sinner Under God's Will
Ibn Asakir presents Ash'ari's middle view on the fate of major sinners.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari position on the fate of a believer who commits a major sin, and how it differs from the Murji'a and Mu'tazila views.

Core Idea
Al-Ash'ari's middle path: major sins are not punished eternally.

In this passage, Ibn Asakir presents the view of al-Ash'ari on the fate of a Muslim who commits a major sin. Al-Ash'ari distinguishes his position from two extremes: the Murji'a who say no sin harms a believer, and the Mu'tazila who say a major sinner will be eternally in Hell. Instead, al-Ash'ari teaches that such a person is under God's will—He may pardon or punish temporarily.

Guided Path

Commentary
This passage addresses the fate of a believer who commits a major sin (kabira). The Murji'a held that once a person has sincere faith, no sin, even apostasy, can harm him, and no major sin is recorded. The Mu'tazila held that a person committing a major sin will be eternally in Hell even if he had faith and good deeds for a hundred years. Al-Ash'ari took a middle position: such a person (called a fasiq, sinner) is under God's will. God may pardon him and admit him directly to Paradise, or He may punish him temporarily and then admit him to Paradise. The key point is that the punishment for a major sin is not eternal (mu'abbada) because the sin is finite and separated (munfasila munqati'a), not infinite. This is the classic Ash'ari position that a believer who dies unrepentant for a major sin is not eternally damned.

The Middle Path on Intercession
Ibn Asakir presents Ash'ari's restricted view of prophetic intercession.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari position on prophetic intercession and how it avoids the extremes of the Rafida and Mu'tazila.

Core Idea
Intercession is limited to believers by God's permission alone.

In this passage, Ibn Asakir presents Imam al-Ash'ari's balanced view on the controversial topic of intercession. The Rafida claimed absolute intercession even for disbelievers, while the Mu'tazila denied it outright. Al-Ash'ari steers a middle path, affirming intercession but only for believers who deserve punishment and only with God's command.

Commentary
This passage addresses the doctrine of intercession (shafa'a) of the Prophet Muhammad (and in the Rafida's case, also 'Ali). The Mu'tazila denied intercession entirely, holding that forgiveness is purely based on justice. Al-Ash'ari's middle position affirms intercession but restricts it: it is only for believers who deserve punishment (i.e., sinning believers), not for disbelievers. Moreover, it is only by God's command and permission, and only for those whom God approves (irtada). This preserves both divine mercy and justice.

Major Sins and the Boundaries of Faith
Ibn Asakir refutes extremists and lays foundational creedal distinctions.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni position on major sins: that only deeming them lawful (istihlal) leads to disbelief, and that Islam is broader than iman.

Major sins do not remove faith unless one deems them lawful.

Ibn Asakir begins by addressing a critical theological question: when does a major sin cause disbelief? He distinguishes between the act of sinning and the stance of legitimizing it. He then clarifies that Islam encompasses more than iman, setting the stage for a nuanced understanding of faith levels.

Commentary
This passage is a narrative with a theological conclusion. It begins with a chain of transmission (isnad) for a story about Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal. In the story, Ahmad is told that Abu Talib claimed Ahmad said 'My wording of the Quran is uncreated.' Ahmad becomes angry, summons Abu Talib, and denies ever saying that. He states that the Quran is the speech of God, uncreated, wherever it is expressed. He forbids relating this statement from him. Ibn Abi Zayd then draws a lesson: since Ahmad rejected this statement, they (the Ahl al-Sunna) reject it too. He then addresses the critic who wants to declare al-Ash'ari a kafir over this issue. He argues that such an accusation is impermissible because al-Ash'ari was known for refuting the Jahmiyya and Qadariyya, and he held the same view as Bukhari and others. Ibn Abi Zayd lays down a principle: takfir is not permissible unless the matter is clear and beyond doubt among scholars. If a scholar has a unique opinion in a branch issue, they are not to be declared misguided. He then compares the Mu'tazili critic to a Rafidi who criticizes a Sunni for disparaging Marwan while he himself curses the Rightly Guided Caliphs. The Mu'tazili critic, who holds the Quran is created, is attacking Ash'ari who holds that the utterances (alfaz) are created. Ibn Abi Zayd dismisses the criticism as based on ignorance and deception. Finally, he mentions that Ash'ari himself did not consider takfir of anyone from the Qibla, and that his final stance on this issue is represented by the story of Zahir ibn Ahmad.

Commentary
This passage presents a collection of transmitted reports (athar and hadith) illustrating the virtue of being backbitten and the reward for defending a fellow Muslim's honor. It begins with a saying of Abd al-Rahman ibn Mahdi, a prominent early scholar, who expresses a paradoxical wish: he would desire that everyone in his city backbite him, except that he dislikes that Allah be disobeyed through the sin of backbiting. The reason is that such backbiting provides him with a hidden good deed (hasana) in his record, unknown to him and others, which is especially delightful. He then argues that the one who is slandered is not a loser; rather, the slanderer and curse become the cursed ones. The slandered person is rewarded while the slanderer is sinful. This sets the stage for the three prophetic hadiths that follow, all transmitted through different chains but conveying the same core message: whoever defends the honor ('ird) of a Muslim brother in his absence (al-mughayyab) makes it incumbent upon Allah to protect him from Hellfire. The first hadith uses the phrase 'defends the flesh' (dhabba 'an lahm), metaphorically meaning defending against slander that tears the person's reputation. The second hadith uses 'wards off dishonor' (radda 'an 'ird), and the third uses the same phrase with slight variation. Together, they establish a powerful incentive for Muslims to protect the dignity of others, especially in their absence, and highlight the immense reward of such conduct.
