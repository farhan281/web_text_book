Al-Ash'ari's Reconciliation Between Extremes
Al-Ash'ari reconciles Mu'tazila and Hashwiyya by bringing them to the middle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Ash'ari reconciled opposing theological extremes by acknowledging valid elements in each position while correcting their excesses.

Core Idea
Al-Ash'ari's middle path reconciled extremes by preserving truth from each side.

In a time of deep sectarian division, Imam al-Ash'ari did not simply pick one side. Instead, he carefully distinguished between correct and excessive elements in both the Mu'tazila and Hashwiyya positions. This passage from Ibn 'Asakir's Tabyin Kadhib al-Muftari describes his conciliatory method and his success in uniting the Muslim community.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to grasp the narrative of al-Ash'ari's approach.
Notice the balanced tone from the start.
2
Note his dual address
Observe how al-Ash'ari speaks separately to each faction, acknowledging part of their stance while correcting their overreach.
He says 'you are correct if...' to both.
3
Identify the middle path
Look for specific corrections: to Mu'tazila on affirming tajalli, to Hashwiyya on not affirming direction and form, and the common ground on kalam nafsi.
4
Reflect on the outcome
Consider why this method succeeded: it did not dismiss one side entirely, but brought them to a balanced position, uniting the community.
The passage ends with his fame and students spreading his works.

In such critical circumstances, Imam Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari, may Allah be pleased with him, became zealous for what had befallen the Muslims from various calamities, and stood to support the Sunna and suppress innovation. He first sought reconciliation between the two factions of the Ummah by bringing them back from their extremism to the just middle. He said to the first (the Mu'tazila): 'You are correct if by "creation of the Qur'an" you mean the utterance, recitation, and script.' And to the others (the Hashwiyya): 'You are correct if your intention by "the eternal" is the attribute subsisting in the Essence of the Creator, not separate from Him - as Ibn al-Mubarak says - meaning the internal speech (al-kalam al-nafsi). And you have no scope to deny the temporality of the reciter's utterance and the reader's recitation, just as the former have no scope to deny the attribute subsisting in Him, Exalted He be, without utterance or sound.' He also said to the first: 'Denying facing (al-muhadha) and form (al-sura) is correct, but you must affirm the manifestation (al-tajalli) without modality (kayf).' And to the others: 'Beware of affirming form and facing and everything that implies temporality; and you are correct if you limit yourselves to affirming the vision for the believers in the Hereafter without modality.' Thus he continued until Allah granted him success in uniting the word of the Muslims and unifying their ranks, suppressing the obstinate and breaking their extremism. Questions came to him from all regions of the world and he answered them, so his fame filled the horizons and he filled the world with his books and the books of his companions on the Sunna and refutations of various innovators, heretics, and People of the Book. His companions dispersed in the lands of Iraq, Khurasan, Syria, and the Maghrib, and he went on his way.

Commentary
This passage describes the pivotal role of Imam al-Ash'ari in reconciling the extreme positions of the Mu'tazila and the Hashwiyya. The author first sets the scene: in these critical times, al-Ash'ari became concerned and took action to support the Sunna and suppress innovation. His first step was reconciliation rather than outright rejection. He addressed both groups, acknowledging the element of truth in each while correcting their errors. To the Mu'tazila, who affirmed tanzih (transcendence) and the creation of the Qur'an, he said they are correct if by 'creation' they mean the uttered words, recitation, and script. To the Hashwiyya, who insisted on the eternal attribute of speech, he said they are correct if they mean the internal speech (kalam nafsi) subsisting in God's essence, as Ibn al-Mubarak stated. But he also reminded them: you cannot deny the temporality of the recitation and utterance, just as the Mu'tazila cannot deny the eternal attribute without sound or letters. On the issue of vision and direction, he told the Mu'tazila: your denial of direction (muhadha) and form is correct, but you must affirm the manifestation (tajalli) without modality. To the Hashwiyya: beware of affirming form and direction which imply temporality; you are correct if you only affirm the vision without modality. This balanced approach succeeded in uniting the Muslim community, breaking extremism, and suppressing the obstinate. His fame spread, and his books and those of his students filled the world. His students dispersed to various regions, and he passed away. The passage highlights al-Ash'ari's method of finding a middle path (wasat 'adl).

God's Existence and the Creation of Water and the Throne
Interpretation of a hadith about God's existence and creation of water and Throne.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how classical Sunni scholars interpret the temporal creation of water and the Throne while affirming God's sole pre-existence.

Core Idea
God alone existed before all creation; water and the Throne are created.

This passage from Ibn Asakir's Tabyin Kadhib al-Muftari addresses a foundational theological point: the absolute uniqueness of God's existence before any creation. By interpreting two key phrases from a prophetic hadith, the author clarifies that water and the Throne are created entities, distinct from God. This understanding is central to the Sunni creed and to the science of kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow. Then read the literal translation. Notice the two sayings being interpreted.
2
Understand the first saying
Focus on 'كان الله ولم يكن شيء غيره'. What does it imply about the existence of water and the Throne?
It negates any other existence alongside God.
3
Understand the second saying
Now examine 'وكان عرشه على الماء'. How does the author explain the sequence of creation?
He interprets it as creation first water, then the Throne, then writing in the Remembrance.
4
Synthesize the meaning
Combine both interpretations: God existed alone, then created water and the Throne. All creation is distinct from God and came after Him.
This establishes the contingency of the world.

Commentary
This passage is a theological explanation connecting a question from the Ash'aris (likely about the creation of the world) to the inheritance of knowledge (الكلام في علم الأصول) from their forefathers. It then interprets two key divine sayings: 'كان الله ولم يكن شيء غيره' (God existed and nothing else existed with Him) to affirm God's unique existence before all creation, negating any pre-existent water or Throne. The second saying 'وكان عرشه على الماء' (And His Throne was upon the water) is interpreted as a temporal sequence: first God created water, then created the Throne upon it, and finally wrote everything in the Preserved Tablet (الذكر). This establishes that all created things are distinct from God and originated in time.

The Scope of al-Ash'ari's Writings
Ibn Asakir lists al-Ash'ari's works from al-'Umd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the range of al-Ash'ari's theological and polemical works up to 320 AH, covering refutations of various sects and topics like Imama and the creation of acts.

Core Idea
Al-Ash'ari's writings defended orthodoxy against a wide array of opponents.

In this passage, Ibn Asakir provides a rare glimpse into al-Ash'ari's own self-catalog of his works, written by the year 320 AH. By examining these titles and their descriptions, we see the breadth of his concerns—from refuting philosophers and materialists to defending the caliphate of Abu Bakr. This lesson will walk you through the major books mentioned and the groups they address.

Commentary
This passage provides a bibliography of al-Ash'ari's works up to 320 AH as recorded in his own book al-'Umd. First, it mentions al-Fusul, a large work refuting various groups: atheists, philosophers, naturalists, materialists, anthropomorphists, and those who assert the pre-eternity of time. It also refutes non-Muslim religions. The book consists of twelve books, starting with a defense of rational reflection (nazar) and the proof of the intellect against those who deny it. Al-Ash'ari also addresses the arguments for the pre-eternity of the world, specifically engaging with Ibn al-Rawandi's Kitab al-Taj. Then he mentions al-Mujaz, another twelve-book work covering various opponents, ending with a book on Imama (leadership) that defends the caliphate of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq and refutes the Shi'a doctrine of nass (explicit designation) and the necessity of an infallible imam. Finally, al-Ash'ari mentions his book on the creation of acts (khalq al-a'mal), which refutes the Mu'tazila and Qadariyya on the issue of human acts. This passage shows the breadth of al-Ash'ari's scholarly output, covering theology, philosophy, and polemics.

Commentary
This is a long panegyric poem defending the Ash'ari creed. It begins with a simile: the Ash'ari creed is like a necklace of jewels (عقود الجوهر), implying beauty, value, and complexity. Only an ignorant slanderer (جهول مفتری) would deny it. The poem then mocks those who criticize it without deep study; they are like a poor person trying to buy a pearl they cannot afford. The knowledge of al-Ash'ari is accessible only to those who strive: staying up late, traveling, consulting masters. Those lacking virtue cannot attain it. The poem warns not to listen to enemies who disparage him. It then praises al-Ash'ari himself: he is an imam, never led astray, combining the resolve of Abu Bakr, the justice of 'Umar, the forbearance of the Nuriyya (perhaps a saintly lineage), and the knowledge of Haydari (referring to 'Ali?). Finally, it asserts that his creed never deviated from scripture or sound rational proof. The poem thus serves both as a defense and an exaltation of the Ash'ari school.

It begins with an interpretive note that the term 'al-diraya' (understanding) might refer to limiting oneself to kalam at the expense of fiqh and practice. The author then reports the saying of Hatim al-Asamm, a renowned ascetic and scholar. He categorizes kalam as the root (asl) of religion, fiqh as its branch (far'), and action as its fruit (thamara). This organic simile shows that all three are interconnected. From this, he derives four types of people: 1) One who limits himself to kalam only: he becomes a zindiq (heretic), because he neglects legal knowledge and practice. 2) One who limits himself to practice only: he becomes an innovator (mubtadi'), because he lacks correct theological foundation and fiqh. 3) One who limits himself to fiqh only: he becomes a sinful transgressor (fasiq), because he neglects kalam and practice. 4) One who masters all three: he becomes saved (takhallasa). The passage then provides a variant from Abu Bakr al-Warraq with slight differences: instead of 'amal, it mentions zuhd (asceticism); instead of fiqh alone, it adds wara' (scrupulousness). The same four categories appear with the same consequences. The clear message is that all three—correct theology, proper jurisprudence, and sincere practice—are necessary for religious integrity.

بشار أستاذ ابن سريج يقول جالست المزني عشر سنين فلما كان بأخرة اجتمعنا في جنازة بعض أصحابه فقلنا يتحدثون بمذهب المزني وينسبونه إلى أنه يتكلم في القرآن، ويقول بالمخلوق فلو سألناه قال فتقدمنا إليه فقلنا يا أبا إبراهيم إنا لنسمع منك هذا العلم ونحب أن يؤخذ عنا ما نسمع منك والناس يذكرونك أنك ساكت عن القول بما يقول أهل الحديث في القرآن، ونحن نعلم أنك تقول بالسنة وعلى مذهب أهل الحديث فلو أظهرت لنا ما تعتقده، فأجابنا فقال أنا لم أعتقد قط إلا أن القرآن كلام الله غير مخلوق، ولكني كرهت الخوض في هذا مخافة أن يكثر علي وأطالب بالنظر في هذا وأشتغل عن الفقه، فلما كان من الغد بعث إليه رئيس رؤساء الجهمية يقال له ابن الأصبغ رسولا، فقال يا أبا إبراهيم بعثني إليك وهو يقول لم تزل تمسك عن الخوض في القرآن والكلام فيه، فما الذي بدا لك الآن وقد بلغني أنك أجبت بكذا وكذا فما حجتك فيما أجبت أن القرآن غير مخلوق، فنظر إلينا وقال ألم أقل لكم إني كنت أمتنع من أجل أني أطالب بمثل هذا، قال أبو القسم فقلت أنا أتولى عنك جوابه، قال شأنك فمضيت إليه فقلت له أن رسولك جاء إلى أبي إبراهيم بكذا وكذا فجئت لأتولي عنه الجواب، وأنا أحد من تحمل عنه العلم فقال ما حجتك، فقلت له أقول القرآن غير مخلوق وأدل عليه بكتاب الله وسنة رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم، وإجماع أمته، ومن حجج العقول التي ركبها الله في عباده قال فأوردت عليه ذلك فبقي متحيرا، قال البيهقي فالمزني رحمه الله كان رجلا ورعا زاهدا يتجنب السلاطين، فامتنع من الكلام مخافة أن يبتلى بالدخول عليهم مع ما شاهد من محنة البويطي وأمثاله من أهل السنة، في أيام المعتصم والواثق، وفي كل ذلك دلالة على أن استحباب من استحب من أئمتنا ترك الخوض في الكلام إنما هو للمعنى الذي أشرنا إليه، وأن الكلام المذموم إنما هو كلام أهل البدع الذي يخالف الكتاب والسنة، فأما الكلام الذي يوافق الكتاب والسنة ويبين بالعقل والعبرة فإنه محمود مرغوب فيه عند الحاجة، تكلم فيه الشافعي وغيره من أئمتنا رضي الله عنهم عند الحاجة، كما سبق ذكرنا له
Bishar, the teacher of Ibn Surayj, said: I sat with Al-Muzani for ten years. When it was near the end, we gathered at the funeral of some of his companions and said: They speak about the madhhab of Al-Muzani and attribute to him that he speaks about the Qur'an and says it is created. If only we would ask him. So we approached him and said: O Abu Ibrahim, we hear this knowledge from you and we love that what we hear from you be taken from us, and the people mention you as being silent about the statement of what the people of hadith say about the Qur'an, while we know that you follow the Sunnah and the madhhab of the people of hadith. If only you would show us what you believe. He answered us and said: I have never believed anything except that the Qur'an is the speech of Allah, uncreated, but I disliked delving into this for fear that it would be demanded of me to investigate it and I would be distracted from fiqh. When it was the next day, the chief of the Jahmiyya leaders, called Ibn al-Asbagh, sent a messenger to him, saying: O Abu Ibrahim, he sent me to you saying: You have refrained from delving into the Qur'an and speaking about it. What has now appeared to you? It has reached me that you answered such-and-such. So what is your proof for your answer that the Qur'an is uncreated? He looked at us and said: Did I not tell you that I refrained because I would be demanded to answer such as this? Abu al-Qasim said: I said: I will respond on your behalf. He said: Go ahead. So I went to him and said: Your messenger came to Abu Ibrahim with such-and-such, so I came to respond on his behalf, and I am one of those who have taken knowledge from him. He said: What is your proof? I said to him: I say the Qur'an is uncreated and I bring evidence for it from the Book of Allah, the Sunnah of the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him), the consensus of his community, and from rational proofs that Allah has placed in His servants. He said: So I presented that to him and he remained bewildered. Al-Bayhaqi said: Al-Muzani, may Allah have mercy on him, was a pious, ascetic man who avoided the rulers. He refrained from kalam for fear of being tested by entering upon them, along with what he witnessed of the ordeal of Al-Buwayti and others like him among the Ahl al-Sunnah during the days of Al-Mu'tasim and Al-Wathiq. In all of this is evidence that the preference of those among our imams who preferred to abandon delving into kalam is only for the meaning we have indicated, and that blameworthy kalam is only the speech of the people of innovation that contradicts the Book and the Sunnah. As for the speech that agrees with the Book and the Sunnah and is clarified by reason and reflection, it is praiseworthy and desirable when needed, and Al-Shafi'i and others of our imams, may Allah be pleased with them, spoke in it when needed, as we have previously mentioned.

Commentary
This passage is a narrative and ruling on the permissibility of kalam. It begins by recounting a story about Al-Muzani, a prominent Shafi'i scholar. For years he avoided discussing the createdness of the Qur'an out of fear of being drawn into lengthy debates that would distract from fiqh. However, when pressed by the chief of the Jahmiyya, a student (Abu al-Qasim) stepped in to defend the orthodox position that the Qur'an is uncreated, using proofs from the Qur'an, Sunnah, consensus, and reason. The narrator then presents Al-Bayhaqi's commentary: Al-Muzani's avoidance was due to piety and fear of rulers, not because kalam itself is forbidden. The passage then draws the general principle: the kalam that is blameworthy is the speech of innovators that contradicts the primary sources; but kalam that conforms to the Qur'an and Sunnah and uses rational proofs is praiseworthy and necessary when needed, as practiced by Al-Shafi'i and other imams. Thus the author refutes the blanket condemnation of kalam and shows its legitimate role in defending orthodoxy.

Key Takeaway
Praiseworthy kalam agrees with the Qur'an and Sunnah and uses reason to defend the truth; it is permissible and even desirable when needed.
