The Purpose of al-Ash'ari's Kalam
Ibn al-Qabisi answers a question about al-Ash'ari's kalam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Ibn al-Qabisi saw al-Ash'ari's kalam as a tool to clarify and confirm the sunna and repel doubts, not as an innovation.

Core Idea
Al-Ash'ari's kalam defended the sunna, not invented it.

Ibn al-Qabisi, a leading Maliki scholar, responds to critics who questioned al-Ash'ari's use of kalam. He explains that al-Ash'ari's engagement was purely to clarify and strengthen the sunna against doubts, and that he was one of many defenders of truth. This passage establishes al-Ash'ari's orthodoxy and the beneficial role of kalam in preserving faith.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how Ibn al-Qabisi frames his defense by first stating al-Ash'ari's intention.
The passage is a response to a question from Tunis.
2
Identify al-Ash'ari's purpose
Find the phrase that describes what al-Ash'ari aimed to achieve with kalam. The passage mentions three specific aims.
Look for 'ايضاح السنن والتثبيت عليها ودفع الشبه عنها'.
3
Understand al-Ash'ari's rank
Notice how Ibn al-Qabisi describes al-Ash'ari's position: he is one of many who stood for truth, and fair-minded people do not lower his rank.
This shows the consensus of the Ahl al-Sunna on his stature.
4
Reflect on the lesson
Ask yourself: Why is it important to distinguish between using kalam to defend the sunna versus to introduce new ideas?
This connects to the broader theme of defending orthodoxy.

The shaykh, the imam, the hafiz, may Allah be pleased with him, said: I read in the handwriting of some scholars of fiqh and hadith from the people of al-Andalus, whom I trust in what he narrates and consider truthful in what he reports, regarding an answer to a question that was asked about Abu al-Hasan 'Ali al-Faqih al-Qayrawani known as Ibn al-Qabisi, who is among the great imams of the Malikiyya in the Maghrib. Some people from Tunis in the Maghrib asked him, and in his answer to them he said: 'Know that Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari, may Allah be pleased with him, did not bring from this matter — meaning kalam — except what Allah intended by it: clarification of the sunan and confirmation upon them and repelling doubts from them. And Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari is only one of those who stood for supporting the truth. We have not heard from the fair-minded anyone who places him below his rank, nor anyone who prefers another over him in his time, and after him the people of truth followed his path in upholding the command of Allah and defending His religion according to their ijtihad.' He said: 'As for your statement: "Tawhid is only complete with the doctrine of al-Ash'ari," this indicates that you understood that al-Ash'ari said something about tawhid that departed from the people of truth. If this meaning has been attributed to al-Ash'ari, then whoever said that against him has falsified [it], because al-Ash'ari died, and on the day he died, the Ahl al-Sunna were weeping over him and the people of innovation were relieved of him. So whoever describes him with other than this does not know him.' He said, may Allah be pleased with him.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of al-Ash'ari by Ibn al-Qabisi, transmitted via a written report from Andalusian scholars. The defense addresses two main points: First, it asserts that al-Ash'ari's engagement in kalam was solely for the purpose of clarifying and confirming the sunna and repelling doubts; it was not an innovation. Second, it refutes the claim that al-Ash'ari's tawhid was heretical, arguing that the Ahl al-Sunna mourned his death while innovators were relieved, proving his orthodoxy. The passage also emphasizes al-Ash'ari's role as one of many defenders of truth, and that fair-minded people acknowledge his rank. The answer includes a logical refutation: if the questioners claim that tawhid is only complete with al-Ash'ari's doctrine, that implies they think he departed from the truth, which is false because his death showed the consensus of Ahl al-Sunna upon him.

Refuting the Claim of al-Ash'ari's Heresy
Ibn al-Qabisi refutes an accusation about al-Ash'ari's tawhid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ibn al-Qabisi refutes the accusation that al-Ash'ari's tawhid was heretical by citing the consensus of Ahl al-Sunna and the reaction to his death.

Core Idea
Ahl al-Sunna mourned al-Ash'ari; innovators rejoiced at his death.

In the same response, Ibn al-Qabisi addresses a specific criticism: that tawhid is only complete with al-Ash'ari's doctrine. He argues that this implies al-Ash'ari departed from the truth, which is false. Instead, the reaction to al-Ash'ari's death—Ahl al-Sunna weeping, innovators relieved—proves his orthodoxy. This passage uses a powerful historical argument to silence detractors.

Ahmad ibn Hanbal and the Danger of Rash Takfir
Ibn Asakir recounts an incident with Ahmad ibn Hanbal to argue against rash takfir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal forbade attributing a specific statement to him and the broader lesson about caution in takfir.

Core Idea
Takfir must be based on certainty, not doubtful statements.

This lesson takes us to a tense moment in the life of Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal. A man had reported that the Imam said 'my utterance of the Qur'an is uncreated' — a phrase that could lead to accusations of innovation. Imam Ahmad's strong reaction and subsequent instruction reveal a crucial principle about declaring someone a disbeliever. Let's read the account and uncover the lesson.

Guided Path
1
Read the story and notice the emotional tone
Observe how Imam Ahmad's anger is described and his immediate reaction. What does he demand?
Pay attention to the dialogue between the parties.
2
Identify what exactly Imam Ahmad forbade
He forbids reporting the statement 'my utterance of the Qur'an is uncreated' both from himself and from the man himself.
This is the core issue: misattribution.
3
Understand the principle he established
Imam Ahmad then states the correct position: the Qur'an is uncreated 'however it may be expressed.' The author extends this to a general rule about takfir.
The principle: takfir only on matters beyond doubt.
4
Reflect on how this applies to takfir practices
Consider why Ibn Asakir uses this story to caution against declaring someone a disbeliever or innovator based on doubtful issues.
