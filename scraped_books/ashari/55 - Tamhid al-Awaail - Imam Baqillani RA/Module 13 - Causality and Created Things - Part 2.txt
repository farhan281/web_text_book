The Quran's Miraculous Composition: The Challenge to the Arabs
Baqillani presents the first evidence: the Quran's matchless composition and the challenge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first evidence of the Quran's miraculous nature according to Imam Baqillani: its matchless literary composition and the challenge issued to the Arabs.

Core Idea
The Quran's literary perfection is proven because the Arabs, masters of eloquence, could not meet its challenge.

Imam Baqillani begins his proof of the Quran's miracle by focusing on its unique literary composition. He points out that the Prophet (peace be upon him) challenged the Arabs—known for their eloquence—to produce anything similar, even a single surah. This passage presents that challenge and the Quranic verses that record it. By examining this, we see the foundation of the i'jaz argument from composition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the challenge and qualities
Read the passage and its translation. Notice that Baqillani states we know the Prophet challenged the Arabs. Underline the description of the Quran's qualities (eloquence, fluency, etc.).
2
Examine the three verses
Find the three Quranic verses cited: 17:88, 11:13, and 2:23. Notice how each one progressively lowers the requirement from the whole Quran to ten surahs to one surah.
This shows the challenge was comprehensive and escalating.
3
Reflect on the public nature
Baqillani notes the challenge was issued during seasons and to both groups and individuals. Reflect on why this strengthens the proof.
The Arabs had every opportunity to respond but could not.

Refutation of the Condition Excluding Rational Impossibilities
Baqillani refutes a condition and applies it to Christian transmission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's refutation of the condition that mutawatir reports cannot contain rationally impossible content, and how he applies this to Christian claims about the crucifixion.

Core Idea
A mutawatir report cannot be false, so rationally impossible content proves it is not mutawatir.

Baqillani here defends the reliability of tawātur by rejecting a condition that would undermine it. He then applies his reasoning to a historical example, showing that apparent contradictions do not invalidate the method but rather reveal the need for careful analysis.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice the structure: a condition is stated, then refuted, then an objection is answered.
The passage is dense; read slowly.
2
Identify the general argument
Find Baqillani's initial argument: why the condition is false. He says that mutawatir reporters cannot lie, so reporting something rationally impossible would be a lie, and if possible, all tawātur knowledge would collapse.
This is the core logical move.
3
Analyze the Christian example
Examine how Baqillani addresses the crucifixion claim. He gives two responses: (1) the transmission is from only four individuals, not tawātur; (2) even if a crucifixion occurred, the identity of the crucified is doubtful. See how these undermine the claim of certain knowledge.
This shows the practical application.

As for their stipulation that they should not include in their report what reason deems impossible, it is false, because the people of tawātur cannot be imagined to commit falsehood, and reporting what reason deems impossible is necessarily falsehood; if that were possible for them, knowledge from their report would be invalidated. Commentary
This passage is a refutation of a condition set by opponents (likely Jews) for a mutawātir report: that it must not contain content that reason deems impossible (muḥīl). Baqillani first gives a general argument: the people of tawātur cannot possibly lie; anything reason deems impossible is necessarily a lie. If they could include such impossibilities, then all tawātur knowledge would be undermined. He then applies this to Christian claims: Christians did not transmit the Trinity literally but interpreted it allegorically. To the objection that Christians transmitted the crucifixion of Jesus, Baqillani provides two answers. First, that transmission comes from only four individuals (the evangelists), who are not mutawātir, so falsehood is possible. Second, even if the general fact of a crucifixion is true, the identification of the crucified as Jesus is mistaken; the person was transformed in appearance (a reference to substitution theory), so doubt remains. A report does not produce necessary knowledge unless the transmitters were compelled to what they report and all doubt is removed. Thus the Christian claim does not meet the conditions of certain knowledge.

First Objection to Naskh: Contradiction or Context?
Baqillani presents and refutes the rational objection that naskh implies contradictory divine will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational objection that naskh implies contradictory divine will and how Baqillani refutes it by distinguishing between different times.

Core Idea
Naskh does not imply contradiction—different times have different rulings.

In this passage, Imam Baqillani presents a key rational objection to naskh (abrogation) that might trouble the student: if God commands something, it indicates He desires it; later prohibiting it suggests He dislikes it—how can He both desire and dislike the same act? Baqillani answers by showing that desire and dislike are time-relative, not absolute, using a concrete example from daily life.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
The objector argues: command implies desire, prohibition implies dislike. Naskh would mean God both desires and dislikes the same thing, which is impossible. Identify the logical structure.
The objection relies on the assumption that desire and dislike are timeless.
2
Examine Baqillani's answer
Baqillani responds by distinguishing different times: what is intended at one time is not intended at another. He gives the example of eating when hungry vs. when full.
The answer uses a simple analogy to show that context matters.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
Baqillani asserts that the objector has no valid response. This shows that naskh is rationally possible without contradiction.

The author concludes that the objector has no valid response to this distinction.

The Objection of Badā' from Naskh and Its First Answer
Baqillani presents the objection and gives the first part of his answer using a time-distinction analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection that naskh implies badā' is answered by distinguishing between the identical act and similar acts at different times.

Core Idea
Naskh does not imply badā'; it is a change of ruling for a different time.

Baqillani faces a strong rational objection: if God commands something, then later prohibits it, it seems God has changed His mind. But God is all-knowing and does not change His knowledge. In this passage, Baqillani gives the first part of his answer, using the analogy of days of the week to show that naskh changes the ruling for a similar act at a different time, not the same act.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the objection assumes a change in God's knowledge.
2
Study Baqillani's analogy
Baqillani uses the example of Friday and Saturday work to show that a prohibition of something similar at a different time is not a contradiction. Identify how this analogy addresses the objection.
3
Reflect on the argument
Consider why the analogy refutes the charge of badā'. What does it say about the relationship between the command and the prohibition?
The key is that the command and prohibition apply to different times or similar acts, not the same act at the same time.

And if they say: The proof for the impossibility of abrogation from the rational perspective is that it necessitates badā' (change of mind), because commanding something implies that it is a benefit (maṣlaḥah) and the commander's belief necessitates it to be so, and prohibiting it after commanding it indicates that the commander has changed his mind (badā) and it became clear to him that what he commanded was a harm, not a benefit, as they imagine—and that is negated from God, exalted be His mention. And also, we permit abrogation of a thing before its time of action and before its fulfillment, and that does not necessitate badā', since the commander knows that preserving the command would lead the responsible person to abandon all obligations, and that lightening the trial by prohibiting it is a benefit and a grace in the action of the responsible person concerning what remains of the command; thus the command is a benefit, and its removal before fulfillment is a benefit, except that the prohibition applies to it in a different manner from how the command applies, because the command to act was a command to do it if the command remained, while the prohibition comes with the removal of the command and not with the persistence of the command, and the command to do it was a command to do it with its persistence without that. And we have explained this discussion in Uṣūl al-Fiqh in a way that suffices the examiner, God willing.

Commentary
This passage is a second objection-and-answer. The objection claims naskh implies badā' (change of mind) because a command indicates the act is beneficial, and then prohibiting it suggests the commander discovered it was harmful—impossible for God. Baqillani answers by distinguishing times: God prohibited something similar to the command but not the identical act at the same time, similar to how working on Friday is not prohibited on Saturday. He then adds a further argument: naskh before the action time is permissible and does not imply badā' because God knows that keeping the command might cause harm (e.g., neglecting duties), so removing it is itself beneficial. The author notes that he has explained this fully in his works on uṣūl al-fiqh.

The Second Answer: Naskh Before Action and Benefit
Baqillani gives a second argument that naskh before action is permissible and does not imply badā', based on benefit and divine wisdom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's argument that naskh before the time of action does not imply badā', because God knows that preserving the command may be harmful and removing it is beneficial.

Core Idea
Naskh before action is permissible and does not indicate badā', as it is based on divine wisdom.

Baqillani continues his defense of naskh by addressing the case of abrogation before the command is fulfilled. He argues that God may abrogate a command before its time if keeping it would lead to harm, such as neglecting other obligations. This removal itself is a benefit and shows God's grace.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on naskh before action
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Pay attention to the logic.
This argument is separate from the previous one.
2
Understand the benefit logic
Baqillani argues that preserving the command could lead the person to abandon all obligations, so abrogation is a benefit. Why would keeping a command cause harm?
3
Note the distinction between command and prohibition
The prohibition applies only when the command is removed, not while it remains. How does this distinction avoid contradiction?
The command was conditional on its persistence, and the prohibition comes after its removal.

Ability is Only at the Time of the Act
Baqillani argues that power exists only during the act with two proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's argument that human ability to act exists only at the moment of the act itself, not before, and the rational proofs he provides.

Core Idea
Ability to act is simultaneous with the act—it cannot precede, or absurdities follow.

In this passage, Baqillani responds to an objection about the timing of human ability (istita'a). He first clarifies his position: ability is concurrent with the act, not before. Then he provides two rational proofs: one from the nature of power as a non-enduring accident, and another from the implications of independence from God. Both arguments lead to the same conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the opponent's question
Read the Arabic passage once. Notice the question-and-answer format. The opponent asks whether ability precedes or accompanies the act. Baqillani answers immediately.
The answer is clear: ability is only at the time of acquisition.
2
Examine the first proof: power as a non-enduring accident
Baqillani argues that since power (qudra) is an accident, it cannot endure over time. If it existed before the act, it would vanish by the time of the act, meaning the act would be produced by a nonexistent power—an absurdity analogized to burning without heat.
This proof relies on the consensus that accidents do not remain.
3
Examine the second proof: implication of independence from God
If ability preceded the act, then at the moment of acting the person would not need God's help. But if independence from God were possible for an act, it would be even more plausible when not acting—leading to the absurd conclusion that one could be independent of God entirely.
This proof reinforces total dependence on God for all actions.

Commentary
This passage is a proof that human ability (istita'a) exists only at the moment of the act itself, not before. The opponent first asks whether ability precedes the act or coincides with it. The answer is clear: it is concurrent with acquisition (fi hal iktisabihi). The opponent then asks for the reasoning, and Baqillani gives two main arguments.

First argument (from the nature of power): The power (qudra) relevant to acquisition is an accident (arad), and accidents cannot endure (baqa') over time. Second argument (from the implication of independence): If a person could perform an act before the power to do so is present (i.e., if ability preceded the act), then at the time of actually performing the act, the person would not need God's assistance—they would be independent of their Lord. But if independence from God in a particular act were possible, then being independent from God when not acting at all (and thus needing no help at all) would be even more plausible. That is impossible by consensus, as it would imply that the creature can be self-sufficient, which contradicts the principle of total dependence on God.

Both arguments converge on the same conclusion: the ability must be simultaneous with the act (al-istita'a ma'a al-fi'l li al-fi'l).

Ability to Divorce and Free Slaves: Answering and Reversing an Objection
Baqillani answers an objection about timing of ability and turns the question back.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Baqillani responds to an objection about the timing of ability for divorce and manumission, and how he turns the question back on the objector.

Core Idea
Timing objections cut both ways: Baqillani turns the question against the objector.

This passage presents a classic dialectical move in kalam. The opponent tries to trap the author by asking when a person is able to divorce or free: if at the time, the subject is no longer valid; if before, the opponent concedes the author's position. Baqillani gives the proper answer and then flips the question, showing the opponent cannot escape their own logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's dilemma
Read the opening question and note the two alternatives: ability at the time of the act or before it. The opponent tries to force a contradiction.
The opponent thinks both alternatives lead to absurdity.
2
Examine Baqillani's initial response
Notice that Baqillani first presents the two possible replies the opponent might expect, then gives the correct answer: ability is at the time of the act, but the subject had the relevant status beforehand.
He uses the analogy of the divorcer divorcing a non-wife at the moment of divorce.
3
See the generalization
Baqillani states this applies to similar cases like throwing a stick or breaking a jug. Understand that the principle is general.
4
Observe the reversal
The author turns the question back: when does the divorce itself occur? The opponent is caught in the same dilemma.
This demonstrates the power of the dialectical method.
5
Reflect on the efficacy
Consider why this reversal is effective: it shows the opponent's assumption leads to the same problem they intended to create.
The opponent's only escape is to accept Baqillani's position.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer followed by a reversal of the question. The author presents two possible replies from the opponent: either ability is at the time (leading to the absurdity) or ability is before (which the author says the opponent thereby agrees with the author's position). The author then gives the proper answer: the ability is indeed at the time of the act, but the subject (woman, slave) had the appropriate status before that moment, and that suffices. The author then flips the question: when does the divorce itself occur? If the opponent says before the divorce, that's nonsense. If at the time of divorce, then at that moment the woman is not a wife, so by the opponent's own logic, he is divorcing a non-wife. Thus the opponent's objection is turned against them.

The Timing of Capacity for Divorce and Manumission
Baqillani responds to an objection about timing of capacity for divorce and manumission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's argument that capacity for an action exists at the moment of the action, not before, and how he turns the objection against the opponent.

Core Idea
Capacity for divorce exists only at the moment of the act, not before.

Baqillani faces an objection: if Allah creates all actions, how can humans have capacity before the act? He responds by analyzing the timing of divorce and manumission, showing that the opponent's own logic leads to absurdities. This passage is a classic example of turning an objection back on the questioner.

The Ifrit's Claim and Divine Enablement
Baqillani answers an objection about the ifrit's claim in Surah al-Naml.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani interprets the ifrit's claim of power as contingent on Allah's enablement and within normal customary limits, not as proof of independent ability.

Core Idea
All ability is by Allah's enabling, even in the speech of jinn.

In this passage, Baqillani addresses a potential objection from those who cite the Quranic verse where a jinn ifrit boasts of bringing the Queen of Sheba's throne to Solomon. Critics might argue this proves that creatures can have power independent of Allah. Baqillani explains that the ifrit's statement must be understood within the context of divine enablement and normal causation.

See how Baqillani presents two possibilities: either the ifrit meant his ability is by Allah's enabling and within usual limits, or he lied. Note the analogy with a person saying 'I can come' implying 'if Allah enables me.'
This shows that claims of power are always conditioned by divine permission.
3
Draw the theological lesson
Conclude that such claims do not prove independent power; all ability is from Allah. Reflect on how this protects tawhid by affirming that no creature acts independently of Allah's will and power.

Baqillani explains the meaning of 'istita`a' in the verse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani interprets the phrase 'ma istata'tum' in the verse and why it supports the Ash'ari view of simultaneous ability.

Core Idea
The verse's ability refers to capacity during the act, not before it.

In this brief but crucial exchange, Baqillani answers an objection about a well-known Qur'anic command. The objectors ask about the meaning of 'as much as you are able' – does it imply ability before action? Baqillani's reply clarifies the intended meaning, grounding it in the moment of action itself.

This passage is a proof (burhan) that refutes the claim that generated accidents are acquired by humans. Baqillani presents a reductio ad absurdum. The opponent asks: why do you deny that these accidents are from the acts of servants, occurring via generation from causes like movements and pressures? Baqillani answers: we deny that because if they were acquisition for the servant, then either the human agent is capable (qadir) of them or incapable. If incapable, then all his actions could occur from him while he is incapable, which is absurd because an action cannot occur without a capable agent; if it could for some, it could for all, eliminating the need for any capable agent. Thus the agent must be capable. If he is capable, then either his capability is at the time the accident exists, or at the time its cause exists. If at the time of cause, then if the cause and effect differ, problems arise: (1) capability cannot precede the act, as argued earlier; (2) one originated capability cannot attach to two different objects (cause and effect). If capability is at the time of the accident (direct conjunction), then according to the Ash'ari view this is invalid because it would make the cause unnecessary — if the agent can directly produce the accident by a concurrent capability, there is no need for a generating cause. This contradicts the opponent's own theory of generation (tawallud), which requires a cause. The final line emphasizes that there is no proof compelling such a generation. Thus, logically, generated accidents cannot be acquired; they must be directly created by God.

Baqillani begins his refutation of the accompanying power theory by showing its internal inconsistency. If a human has a power that accompanies the act itself, why would any cause be needed? Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite theory of generated acts (al-mutawalladāt). The author presents two main arguments. The Opposite Power Dilemma
Second argument: power over opposites leads to contradictions.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This leads to contradictions with everyday experience, such as the impossibility of freely ceasing to move an object.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite theory of generated acts (al-mutawalladāt). The author presents two main arguments. First, he argues by analogy: just as there is no proof that God (al-qadīm) cannot act directly without causes, so too there is no proof that the human servant cannot act directly without generated causes. This reduces generated acts to direct acts. Second, from the premise that if a person has power over an act via a concomitant power, he would also have power over its opposite, leading to absurd consequences: e.g., a person would have to be capable of stilling a stone even when not moving it, and he could never be free of both motion and stillness unless incapacitated or dead. This is contradicted by observed reality. The author then extends the argument: if power over distant objects were possible without causes, then a person in Baghdad could move a body in Khurasan, which is impossible. Thus the claim collapses. In the second part, he argues that if the generated act requires a power existing with it, this leads to an infinite regress or contradiction, and that causes become superfluous. The conclusion is that the Mu'tazilite position is invalidated as a whole.

Commentary
This passage is a hypothetical argument that exposes the flaw in the Mu'tazilite reasoning about generated acts. Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite theory of generation (tawallud), the idea that human actions necessarily produce their effects by a natural causal connection. Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite theory of generation (tawallud), the idea that human actions necessarily produce their effects by a natural causal connection. Baqillani begins by addressing the claim that events are the act of the servant, asking for proof and noting that the opponent only has a mere assertion. He then presents multiple hypothetical scenarios to show the absurd consequences of their reasoning. First, he imagines if God were to change the regular custom regarding a rope and stone: if the stone moved only when the hand separated and became still when touched, then the opponent would have to say that separation generates movement and touch generates stillness—contradicting their own theory. Second, he imagines if God made light striking cause great pain and violent striking cause little pain, then the generation would be reversed. Third, he imagines if throwing a pot caused its parts to come together and holding it gently caused it to break, then generation would be reversed for the pot. If the opponent ignores these, they abandon their own claim; if they say that in such a changed custom the causes would not be generators, then they have destroyed their own proof, because the generation they claim relies on the fixed custom. If they object that such a change is impossible because it would corrupt the proofs (i.e., make it impossible to infer causality), Baqillani retorts that this is pure ignorance: a true rational proof cannot be invalidated by any contingent act, just as the proof of the temporality of bodies from accidents cannot be invalidated by a change in custom; if a proof can be invalidated by a possible act, it was never a valid proof. Finally, he extends the argument: on their reasoning, if custom were different, then death would be generated by striking the neck, pleasure by scratching, color by striking, whiteness and hardness in molasses by lashing, and health by setting and splinting—all equally absurd. Thus, the Mu'tazilite theory of necessary generation is shown to be untenable.
