The Definition of Faith as Tasdiq and the Necessity of Verbal Confession
Juwayni defines faith as tasdiq and explains the necessity of verbal confession.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Juwayni's definition of faith as tasdiq (affirmation) and the distinction between inner conviction and verbal confession, along with the ruling on those who stubbornly refuse to confess.

Core Idea
Faith is inner conviction plus verbal confession; stubborn silence turns knowledge into disbelief.

Juwayni opens the chapter on faith with great emphasis, outlining the four pillars of the discussion. The first and foundational pillar defines the reality of faith (iman) as tasdiq (affirmation/assent). He grounds this in both linguistic usage and Quranic evidence, and then explains the conditions under which inner knowledge saves or does not save a person. This passage sets the stage for the entire doctrine of faith in the Ash'ari tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four pillars
Read the passage and list the four pillars Juwayni mentions for the chapter on faith.
The pillars are: reality of faith, fate of sinful believers, increase/decrease of faith, and the Salaf's saying.
2
Focus on the definition of faith
Juwayni defines faith as tasdiq. Note that he says this is both the linguistic and technical meaning.
Tasdiq means affirmation or assent, not just knowledge.
3
Observe the Quranic evidence
Juwayni cites Quran 12:17 to show that 'mumin' means 'musaddiq' (one who confirms).
This supports his linguistic argument.
4
Understand the role of verbal confession
Juwayni explains that inner conviction alone benefits only if accompanied by verbal confession. If one refuses out of obstinacy, he is a disbeliever.
Examples: Pharaoh and the Jewish scholars who recognized Muhammad's prophethood but denied it.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of a new chapter on faith (iman). Juwayni first states the importance of the chapter and then outlines its four pillars: the reality of faith, the fate of sinful believers, increase and decrease of faith, and the meaning of the Salaf's saying 'we are believers if Allah wills.' He then explains the first pillar: faith is tasdiq (affirmation/assent), both linguistically and in Islamic law. He supports this with Quran 12:17, where 'mumin' means 'musaddiq' (one who confirms). The true believer is one who inwardly holds a firm conviction (yaqin) regarding the truth of the message about God, His attributes, and His prophets. If this inner conviction is accompanied by verbal confession (iqrar bil-lisan), then the person is a believer both outwardly and inwardly. However, if one has inner knowledge but refuses to confess out of stubbornness (inad), that inner knowledge is of no benefit; such a person is considered a disbeliever—specifically disbelief of denial (kufr juhud) and obstinacy (inad). Juwayni gives the example of Pharaoh and the Jewish scholars who recognized Prophet Muhammad's prophethood but rejected him out of obstinacy, thus being treated as disbelievers in Allah's judgment.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Iman is inner affirmation; sin does not remove it.

This passage from Imam Juwayni establishes the foundational Sunni definition of iman as tasdiq and defends it against those who equate sin with loss of faith. By examining Quranic evidence and logical distinctions, Juwayni shows that a person can commit major sins and still be called a believer. Let's unpack his argument.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof passage. It opens by introducing a chapter on iman, its meaning, and the destiny of believers, stating that the chapter contains four pillars. It then immediately begins with the first pillar: the reality of iman. The author defines iman as tasdiq (affirmation), which he says is its linguistic meaning as well, quoting Quran 12:17 as evidence. He then elaborates on the true mu'min: one who inwardly holds a conviction (in the sense of a firm belief) of knowledge affirming the truthfulness of the one who informed about God, His attributes, and His prophets. Next, he distinguishes between outward and inward: if one acknowledges with his tongue what he knows in his heart, he is a believer both outwardly and inwardly; if he does not acknowledge due to obstinacy (mu'anada), his inner knowledge does not benefit him, and he is considered a disbeliever—specifically a kafir juhud (denier) and 'anid (obstinate). He gives examples: Pharaoh, and the Jewish scholars who recognized the prophethood of Muhammad (peace be upon him) and found his description in the Torah but denied him out of transgression and envy. Then he describes the hypocrite (munafiq) who conceals disbelief and outwardly professes iman, whose abode is the lowest depth of Hell. Crucially, he asserts that the name 'iman' does not disappear due to sin (i.e., a believer who commits major sins is still called a believer). He provides evidence: most verses of obligation (ayat al-taklif) begin with 'O you who have believed,' such as the fasting verse (2:183), indicating that the address includes sinners who are still believers. This is a foundational Sunni position against the Kharijites and Mutazilites who held that major sin expels one from iman.

Qur'an and Consensus: Faith Persists with Sin
Al-Juwayni provides Qur'anic and consensus evidence that sinners remain believers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Juwayni uses a Qur'anic address and scholarly consensus to prove that sinful believers remain within the fold of faith.

Core Idea
Sinners are still believers — the Qur'an and consensus confirm.

Al-Juwayni continues defending the position that sin does not remove faith. He brings forward two powerful proofs: a direct Qur'anic verse addressing sinners as believers, and the consensus of the Muslim community that the worship of a sinful person is valid. These two arguments together solidify the doctrine that faith persists even with major sins.

Guided Path

The passage is concise — each sentence carries weight.

Core Idea

Imam Juwayni begins the third pillar of his discussion by presenting the position of the Salaf (the early pious generations). For them, faith was not merely an internal state but comprised three elements: heart knowledge, tongue confession, and limb actions. This comprehensive definition has direct implications for whether faith can vary in intensity.

Commentary
This passage presents the classical theological dispute regarding the definition of faith (iman) and whether it increases and decreases. Juwayni begins the third pillar (al-rukn al-thalith) by stating the position of the early Salaf: faith comprises three components: knowledge in the heart (ma'rifah bi al-janan), verbal confession (iqrar bi al-lisan), and action of the limbs ('amal bi al-arkan). By including actions, they subsume all acts of obedience under the term 'faith'. Juwayni supports this by citing the Qur'anic verse 'And Allah would never let your faith be lost' (Q 2:143), which the Companions understood to refer to their prayer towards Jerusalem, thus calling prayer 'faith'. Consequently, if actions are part of faith, faith increases with obedience and decreases with disobedience. Juwayni then presents the opposing view: those who define faith exclusively as assent (tasdiq) argue that assent is a single, indivisible state; a person either believes or does not. Just as a person may know death is certain yet indulge in pleasures, similarly a believer's faith (assent) remains unchanged even if his actions vary. The analogy of the wise person (al-'aqil) and the pleasure-seeker (al-munhamik) illustrates that knowledge alone does not guarantee action; thus, actions are not constitutive of faith but are manifestations of it. Juwayni does not decisively side with either view here but records both, noting that the fourth pillar will clarify further. The lesson thus identifies the core disagreement: whether faith is merely internal assent (tasdiq) or includes external actions, and whether it is subject to increase and decrease.

After presenting the Salaf's comprehensive definition, Juwayni now turns to a significant counter-position. Commentary
This passage presents the classical theological dispute regarding the definition of faith (iman) and whether it increases and decreases. Commentary
This passage presents Juwayni's position on the fate of sinful believers who die without repentance, followed by a summary of the views of other sects (Khawarij, Hasan al-Basri, Mu‘tazila), and then lists the eight scriptural proofs used by the Khawarij to argue that a major sinner becomes a disbeliever. Juwayni begins by stating that the fate of such a person is hidden (mughayyab): Allah may forgive him directly, or intercession may be accepted, or He may punish him in the Fire proportionally, but ultimately his final outcome is salvation (al-fawz al-akbar wa al-najah). This reflects the Ahl al-Sunnah view that major sinners are subject to Allah’s will and may eventually enter Paradise after punishment if they die as believers. Then three opposing views are mentioned: (1) The Khawarij declare such a person a disbeliever (kafir) if he does not repent. (2) Hasan al-Basri calls him a hypocrite (munafiq). (3) The Mu‘tazila take an intermediate position, calling him neither a believer nor a disbeliever but a fasiq (sinner) in a state between two abodes. The remainder of the passage is dedicated to the Khawarij's evidence. They adduce eight proofs from the Quran, each verse either explicitly or implicitly labeling evildoers as disbelievers: (1) The verse about judging by other than Allah’s law (al-Ma'idah 5:44). (2) The verse 'And do we recompense except the ungrateful?' (Saba' 34:17)—applying it to all sinners. (3) The verse after the Hajj obligation 'And whoever disbelieves, then Allah is free from need' (Aal-e-Imran 3:97). (4) The verse 'Indeed, the punishment is upon whoever denies and turns away' (Ta Ha 20:48). (5) The verse threatening with the blazing Fire the 'most wretched one who denies and turns away' (al-Layl 92:14-16). (6) The verse addressed to those whose scales are light: 'Were not My verses recited to you, and you used to deny them?' (al-Mu'minun 23:105). (7) The verse about faces turning black on Judgment Day (Aal-e-Imran 3:106), and asserting that the fasiq is among those with blackened faces. (8) The verse declaring that those who disbelieve in Allah's verses are companions of the left hand (al-Balad 90:19). The Khawarij use these verses to argue that any major sinner fits these descriptions and thus is a disbeliever. Juwayni does not refute them here; he merely presents their proofs. The lesson thus contrasts the Ahl al-Sunnah view (with its hope and fear) with the extreme takfiri position. 8 proofs. Their listing is complete.

Arguments for Considering the Non-Acting Muslim a Hypocrite
Presents two arguments for judging a non-acting Muslim as a hypocrite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two arguments used by scholars who consider a Muslim who does not perform religious duties to be a hypocrite.

Core Idea
Actions are the proof of faith – their absence reveals hypocrisy.

This passage presents two arguments from a group of scholars who hold that a Muslim who fails to act on religious obligations is a hypocrite. The first is based on a prophetic hadith listing three signs of hypocrisy. The second uses a vivid analogy about a snake in a hole to argue that genuine belief must translate into action.

Commentary
This passage presents two arguments used by a group of scholars who judge the non-acting Muslim (the one who does not perform religious duties) to be a hypocrite (munafiq). The first argument is a direct quotation from a hadith in which the Prophet (peace be upon him) lists three signs of a hypocrite: breaking promises, lying when speaking, and betraying trusts. The implication is that a Muslim who consistently fails to act (e.g., does not pray, does not keep promises) exhibits these signs and thus is considered a hypocrite. The second argument is an analogy: if a person truly believed there was a dangerous snake in a hole, he would not put his hand in it. If he claims to believe there is a snake and then puts his hand in, the inconsistency proves his claim is false. Similarly, if a Muslim claims faith but does not act upon it (e.g., does not perform obligations), his actions contradict his claim, revealing hypocrisy. The argument assumes that genuine faith necessarily produces corresponding actions.
