Repentance from One Sin While Persisting in Another: Al-Juwayni's Resolution
Al-Juwayni presents the dispute on repentance and offers his resolution based on the state of the repenter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Juwayni's nuanced position on whether repentance from a single sin is valid when one persists in other sins, and how the validity depends on the spiritual state of the repenter.

Core Idea
Repentance's validity depends on the repenter's spiritual station — gnostics cannot persist, but ordinary believers may.

Al-Juwayni tackles a subtle theological question: can a person truly repent of one sin while still committing others? He reviews earlier opinions, critiques an unsatisfactory answer, and then offers his own insightful division between the gnostic and the ordinary believer. This lesson walks through his reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is a theological discussion about the validity of repentance from one sin while persisting in others. It begins by stating the differing opinions: the People of Truth (referring to the Ash'aris) hold that it is permissible, while the Mu'tazilites Abu Hashim and al-Jubba'i hold it is impossible. Al-Juwayni then mentions a response attributed to some Imams: that true repentance is about having a sense of the gravity of sin, and this general feeling does not single out a particular sin; thus, if one truly repents of a sin, that sentiment should cover all sins. However, al-Juwayni criticizes that this answer lacks persuasive merit. He then offers his own solution by dividing repenters into two categories: (1) the 'arif (gnostic) who knows Allah deeply and is secure in his knowledge – his sin arises from a moment of heedlessness, and his repentance is a return to presence of mind; once knowledge returns, he cannot remain on any sin. (2) The ordinary believer who holds by bare creed – when his desire for a particular sin weakens, his intellect overcomes and he appears to repent, but he still persists in other sins for which his desire remains. This nuanced explanation shows that the ruling depends on the state of the repenter. The passage concludes that this understanding is accessible only to a perceptive and deep thinker.

Core Idea
The appointment of an imam is obligatory by consensus, with only a rare dissent from Abu Bakr al-Asamm.

Three Opinions on How One Becomes Imam
Juwayni lists three views on becoming imam: nass, group nass, or consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three classical views on how a person becomes imam, focusing on the role of nass and consensus.

Core Idea
The imamate is established either by direct designation or by community selection.

This passage from Imam al-Juwayni presents the three main scholarly opinions on how an imam is legitimately installed. You'll see that the first two involve explicit designation (nass) by the previous imam, while the third relies on the consensus of qualified electors. Understanding these views clarifies the Sunni framework for political authority.

Binding Nature of a Single Elector and Seizure by Force
Juwayni argues that a single elector can contract imamate and that seizure by force by a qualified person is valid.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Imamate is valid when contracted by one qualified elector or seized by a qualified person.

Imam Juwayni addresses two key questions about how legitimate authority is established. First, does it require a group of electors, or is a single person sufficient? Second, what if a qualified individual seizes power by force? This passage presents the Sunni position that both can produce a binding imamate, and that obedience is required to maintain order.
