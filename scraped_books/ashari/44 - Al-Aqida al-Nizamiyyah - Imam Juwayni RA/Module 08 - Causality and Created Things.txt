Refutation of Eternal Determination: The Agent Must Be a Free Chooser
Juwayni refutes the claim that an eternal cause could necessitate the world without choice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Juwayni argues that the world's agent cannot be a mere necessitating cause (موجب) but must be a free chooser (مؤثر مختار) who specifies by will.

Core Idea
A blind cause cannot choose; only a free agent can pick one equal possibility.

In this passage, Juwayni confronts a powerful objection: could an eternal cause produce the world without any priority, like a cause-effect relationship? He first pauses to invoke divine guidance, then systematically refutes the idea. The key move is a dichotomy: the determinant is either a necessitator without choice or a free chooser. Using a vivid analogy from medicine and an argument about spatial location, Juwayni shows that a necessitator cannot specify a contingent world. Only a free agent with will can do so.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and Juwayni's call for reflection
Read the opening objection carefully. Juwayni advises the reader to pause and pray. Notice the seriousness of the issue.
The objection claims the world is eternal, caused by an eternal determinant with no priority.
2
Identify the two possible determinants
After stating that contingent things need a determinant, Juwayni presents a dichotomy: the determinant must be either a necessitator without choice or a free chooser. Note the terms: موجب and مؤثر مختار.
This is a fundamental division in Islamic theology.
3
Understand why a necessitator is impossible
Juwayni gives an example from natural medicine: a drug that attracts yellow bile cannot, as a necessitator, fail to attract equally under identical conditions. Similarly, the world in space: if a necessitator existed, it would have to specify the world's location, but all locations are equal, so specification is impossible without choice.
The argument relies on the principle that a necessitator produces identical effects under identical conditions.
4
Conclude that only a free chooser is possible
Since a necessitator cannot specify, the only remaining option is a free chooser who specifies by will and volition. This refutes the claim that the world could be eternal with a mere cause.
Juwayni's conclusion establishes divine freedom and contingency of the world.

This is impossible to imagine. And when this is established, we say: the world in its entirety is stationary in a known space, and its being supposed to be in that void is similar to it being supposed in a void to the right or to the left. This is close to the level of perception. And when the spaces and directions are similar, it is impossible to believe a necessitator that specifies the world to a region while the other regions are equal to it. For the necessitator does not specify anything among its likes. And the effective chooser is the one that specifies by His will and volition one of the likes. So the invalidity of resorting to an eternal necessitator without choice became clear.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection and its refutation. The objector claims the world could be eternal, existing with an eternal cause that is like a cause-effect relationship: no priority. Juwayni advises the reader to pause and pray, then responds. He argues that if contingent things need a determinant, that determinant must be either a necessitator (موجب) without choice or a free chooser (مؤثر مختار). A necessitator is impossible because it cannot specify one possibility over another when all are equal, illustrated by the example of a drug attracting bile: under identical conditions, a necessitator would produce the same effect always, but the world has many possible states (e.g., location). Only a free chooser can specify by will. Therefore, the agent must be a free chooser, not a mere necessitator.

Refutation of the Denial of Human Power's Effect
Juwayni refutes the denial of human power's effect on actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Juwayni's argument that denying the effect of human power leads to absurdity and the nullification of the Sharia.

Core Idea
Denying human power's effect makes God's commands impossible to fulfill.

Juwayni addresses a key theological controversy: does human power have any real effect on actions? Some claimed no, citing God's absolute sovereignty. But Juwayni shows that such a claim contradicts both reason and revelation. In this passage, he refutes this denial by exposing its logical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the false claim
Read the first part of the passage to find the claim that human power has no effect at all.
2
Examine the claimant's defense
Notice how the claimant uses the Quranic verse that God is not questioned to evade the issue.
Juwayni calls this 'a word of truth by which falsehood is intended'.
3
Understand the principle of capacity
Juwayni asserts that from reason and revelation, we know God only commands what servants are capable of doing.
This is a key principle in kalam.
4
See the absurd consequence
If human power has no effect, then demanding actions is like demanding impossible things, such as establishing colors in oneself.
5
Recognize the final verdict
Juwayni concludes that such a denial nullifies the Sharia and rejects the message of the prophets.
This shows the high stakes of the discussion.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation. It begins by stating the false claim: that human power has no effect at all. Juwayni then shows the absurd consequence: when the claimant is pressed to explain how God's commands (obligation and prohibition) relate to human acts, he falls back on saying 'God does as He wills' and cites the Quranic verse that God is not questioned. Juwayni notes that this is a true statement used to support a false conclusion. He then asserts from reason and revelation that God only charges humans with what they are capable of; this is a known principle. To deny any effect of human power would make God's commands like demanding impossible things (e.g., establishing colors in oneself), which is absurd. The conclusion is that such a denial nullifies the Sharia and rejects the message of the prophets.

Temporal Power Has Effect, Servant Is Not Creator
Juwayni concludes on human power and creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why affirming that human power has an effect does not make the human a creator, and why a single act cannot be caused by two agents.

Core Idea
A single act cannot be split between God and the servant.

Juwayni concludes a key point: human power does have an effect, but that does not make the human a creator. He also argues that a single act cannot be from two powers. This lesson explains the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the strong conclusion: temporal power has effect, but servant is not creator.
2
Identify the conclusion and its reasoning
The passage states that temporal power has effect, but it is impossible to say the servant is creator. Then it gives the reason: a single act cannot occur by two powers because it is indivisible.
The indivisibility of the act is key.
3
Reflect on the implication
Think about why this matters: if we affirm human effect without calling the human a creator, we avoid both innovation and the error of denying accountability.

Thus it follows necessarily that we must affirm that the temporal power has an effect in its object, and it is impossible to say that the servant is the creator of his actions, for in that there is a departure from the precedent of the predecessors of the community and a plunging into difficulties of error. Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal conclusion followed by a classification of false positions. It begins by stating the necessary consequence: temporal power does have an effect, but the servant is not a creator (that would be innovation). The reason given is that a single act cannot be caused by two independent powers because it is indivisible. Juwayni then digresses to clarify terms of will: universal will directs to all possibilities, particular will to a specific one. He notes that logicians' usage of universal and particular does not apply here, contrary to Maturidis. Returning to the main argument, he asserts that if the act is by God's power, the temporal power's effect is null. He then describes three false positions a person might fall into: (1) claiming independence in creation, (2) denying obligation (i.e., not being accountable), (3) claiming partnership with God in creating the act. All these are false, and the issue is a dangerous valley from which only a successful guide can escape.

Universal Will, Particular Will, and the Maturidi View
Juwayni explains universal and particular will and critiques Maturidis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Juwayni's distinction between universal and particular will, and his critique of the Maturidi position on these terms.

Core Idea
Universal will is real; particular will is derivative – not to be confused with logical universals.

Juwayni digresses to clarify two types of will: universal and particular. He explains that the universal is a real existing quality, while the particular is a conceptual derivative. He warns against equating these with the logicians' categories and critiques the Maturidi view.

Guided Path

The example 'He devoted himself entirely to knowledge' illustrates universality.
2
Distinguish the two types of will
Universal will is directed to all possibilities and is real; particular will is directed to a specific direction and is a derivative concept.
The first is a reality, the second is extracted from the relation between willer and willed.
3
Understand the critique of Maturidis
Juwayni argues that the logicians' terms of universal and particular do not apply here, contrary to the Maturidis. He insists that the universal will is existent, not missing.
He says 'let one be attentive' – a warning against confusion.

And there is no way to attribute the occurrence of the servant's act to both his temporal power and the eternal power, for a single act is impossible to occur by two powers, since the one is indivisible. So if it occurs by the power of God—glorified is He—it is independent of the servant. And if we consider its direction to all possibilities, it is called universal will, as it is said: 'He devoted himself entirely to knowledge,' meaning with all his powers. And its direction to a particular direction is called particular will, specifying the orientation towards it. The first is an existing reality, and the second is a derivative matter extracted from between the one who wills and the object of will, like other source-derived meanings. So there is no relevance to the terms 'universal' and 'particular' in the terminology of logicians here to allow quibbling that the universal is missing and the particular is the existing, contrary to the opinion of the Maturidis regarding universal and particular will. So let one be attentive. And the effect of the temporal power falls, and it is impossible for part of it to occur by the power of God, mighty and majestic, for a single act has no part. And this is a dangerous valley from whose disasters none is saved except a successful guide. For a person is between seeking to be independent in creation, and between removing himself from being obligated by the laws, which invalidates the call of the prophets, peace be upon them, and between establishing himself as a partner to God, blessed and exalted, in bringing about the single act. And all of these divisions are altogether false.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal conclusion followed by a classification of false positions. It begins by stating the necessary consequence: temporal power does have an effect, but the servant is not a creator (that would be innovation). The reason given is that a single act cannot be caused by two independent powers because it is indivisible. Juwayni then digresses to clarify terms of will: universal will directs to all possibilities, particular will to a specific one. He notes that logicians' usage of universal and particular does not apply here, contrary to Maturidis. Returning to the main argument, he asserts that if the act is by God's power, the temporal power's effect is null. He then describes three false positions a person might fall into: (1) claiming independence in creation, (2) denying obligation (i.e., not being accountable), (3) claiming partnership with God in creating the act. All these are false, and the issue is a dangerous valley from which only a successful guide can escape.

The Three False Positions on Human Action
Juwayni lists three false positions on human action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three false positions that Juwayni identifies as dangerous alternatives to his doctrine of human action.

Core Idea
Three false positions: independence, exemption from law, or partnership with God.

Juwayni describes a dangerous valley with three false positions a person might fall into when considering human action: claiming independence in creation, denying accountability, or asserting partnership with God. All are false. This lesson explains each.

Guided Path
1
Read the final part of the passage
Read from 'Wa-saqata atharu al-qudrati al-haditha' to the end. This lists three false positions.
Note the strong warning: 'a dangerous valley from whose disasters none is saved except a successful guide.'
2
Identify each false position
The three positions are: (1) seeking independence in creation, (2) removing oneself from obligation by the laws, (3) establishing partnership with God in bringing about the act.
Each is introduced with 'bayna... wa-bayna...'
3
Understand why each is false
Claiming independence contradicts Quran. Denying obligation invalidates prophets' call. Partnership is impossible because a single act cannot be shared.
The indivisibility argument from earlier applies.
