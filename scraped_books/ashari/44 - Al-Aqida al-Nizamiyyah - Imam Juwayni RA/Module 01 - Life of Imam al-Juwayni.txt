Commentary
This passage provides a biography of the author, Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni. It begins by stating his full name: 'Abd al-Malik ibn 'Abd Allah ibn Yusuf ibn Muhammad ibn 'Abd Allah ibn Hayawayh, with a note on the pronunciation of the last name. It mentions his attribution to two places: Juwayn and Naysabur, both in the region of Fars. It explains that his attribution to Juwayn came from his father, who was originally from Juwayn; when the father died and 'Abd al-Malik took over his teaching position, he inherited the nisbah. He was also called al-Naysaburi due to his long stay in Naysabur. The passage then explains the nickname 'Imam al-Haramayn', meaning 'Imam of the Two Sanctuaries' (Mecca and Medina), because he resided in Mecca for four years, engaging in debates and teaching. Finally, it addresses the controversy over his birth year: some say 419 AH, others 417 AH, but all agree he died in 478 AH at age 59. It then quotes Dr. Fawqiyyah Husayn Mahmud who, after verification, concludes the birth was on 18 Muharram 419 AH (22 Feb 1028 CE). This biography is typical of introductory sections in classical works, establishing the author's lineage and scholarly credentials.

This lesson traces his journey from teaching in Nishapur to his travels and eventual leadership under the patronage of Nizam al-Mulk. Commentary
This passage is a biographical narrative about Imam al-Juwayni's life and role. It begins by stating that he established (or was in charge of) a school in Nishapur, where he taught the Shafi'i school and defended the Ash'ari creed against opponents. It then recounts his travel to Mecca and his return to Nishapur after four years, around 451 AH. Upon his return, the political landscape had changed: Sultan Alp Arslan was in power with his vizier Nizam al-Mulk, who actively facilitated the return of Ash'ari scholars who had previously been forced to leave. The passage quotes al-Subki to highlight Nizam al-Mulk's extensive building of schools across the Islamic world to promote Sunni Islam. It then notes that al-Juwayni became one of the heads of the Nizamiyya school in Nishapur, while Abu Ishaq al-Shirazi was head of the Nizamiyya school in Baghdad. Finally, it summarizes his rise to leadership among the Shafi'i-Ash'ari community, as he assumed leadership of the sect and management of endowments, and became the khatib (preacher) of al-Manai'iyya mosque.

Al-Juwayni's Promise of a Book on Imamate
Al-Juwayni announces his plan to write a separate book on Imamate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Juwayni's decision to write a separate book on the Imamate and his critique of prior works.

Core Idea
Al-Juwayni's promised book on Imamate was a necessary corrective to past excesses.

In this passage, al-Juwayni shares his personal resolve to write a dedicated work on the Imamate. Dissatisfied with earlier treatments that either overreached or fell short, he envisions a comprehensive book that would begin with the Imamate and conclude with Sultanic Rulings. This lesson explores his motivation and the intended scope of that work.

Commentary
This passage is a personal note from al-Juwayni about his plan to write a book on the Imamate. He had earlier promised to include chapters on Imamate in the present work, but decided instead to write a separate book for the 'noble assembly' (probably the vizier Nizam al-Mulk). He laments that previous writings on the Imamate have been incomplete and that the topic is poorly handled. He names his intended book 'The Great Imamate', which would begin with the Imamate and end with Sultanic Rulings (i.e., the regulatory laws of governance). He then praises the book as if it were a bride awaiting a suitor, and expresses the hope that his patron (the 'master') will allow him to present it. The footnote (which may be an editorial addition) identifies the book as 'al-Ghiyathi' and notes that al-Juwayni also wrote another work on Imamate called 'Ghiyath al-Umam'.
