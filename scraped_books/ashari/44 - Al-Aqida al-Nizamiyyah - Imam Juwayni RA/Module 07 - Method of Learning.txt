Rational Obligations: Necessary and Theoretical
Juwayni classifies rational obligations into necessary and theoretical and states their rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Juwayni's classification of rational obligations into necessary (dharuri) and theoretical (nazari) and the foundational principle that whatever reason judges as necessarily existent cannot be negated, and whatever it judges as impossible must be avoided.

Core Idea
Rational obligations are the unshakable premises from which all religious rulings branch.

Juwayni begins his discussion of rational obligations by distinguishing two fundamental categories: those that are self-evident (necessary) and those that require reasoning (theoretical). He then lays down a decisive principle: whatever reason affirms as necessary cannot be denied, and whatever reason deems impossible must be avoided. This passage establishes the bedrock of all subsequent theological arguments.

2

Find where Juwayni divides rational obligations into necessary and theoretical. Note the example he gives for each.
The necessary example is about the maker being powerful; the theoretical example is about the inventor knowing details.
3
Understand the principle of necessary existence and impossibility
Examine the sentence: 'Whatever reason judges its existence as necessary, its non-existence is impossible; and whatever reason implies the impossibility of its existence, its avoidance is obligatory.' This is the core logical rule.
This principle underlies all subsequent proofs.
4
Reflect on why these are called premises
Juwayni calls these 'premises' and says no rational person disputes them. Consider how they serve as the foundation for religious rulings.
He promises to arrange the rulings in chapters later.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and explanation of rational obligations (wajibat aqliyya). Juwayni first distinguishes two categories: necessary (dharuri) and theoretical (nazari). The necessary rational obligation is illustrated by the knowledge that a thing's maker must be powerful over his act; this is self-evident. The theoretical rational obligation requires reasoning, such as knowing that the inventor of all things must know the details of His actions. He then states a key principle: whatever reason judges as necessarily existent cannot be negated, and whatever reason judges as necessarily non-existent must be avoided. These are foundational premises (muqaddimat) that no rational person disputes. Finally, he asserts that all rules of religion branch out from these rational judgments, and he will arrange them in chapters.

The Three Main Chapters of Kalam
Juwayni enumerates the three chapters of kalam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three main chapters that constitute the science of kalam according to Imam Juwayni.

Kalam is structured around three essential chapters: God, human obligation, and prophecy.

Imam Juwayni provides a clear methodological outline for the entire discipline of kalam. After establishing that the world is created, he explains that the subject matter is summarized in three main chapters: knowledge of God, the basis of human obligation, and prophethood. These three chapters form the backbone of theological reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological classification of the subject matter of kalam (ʿilm al-kalām). He promises to mention something about it briefly.

The Status of Imamate in Sunni Creed
Juwayni clarifies that imamate is not part of creed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Imam Juwayni states that imamate is not part of the fundamental creeds but is included by custom.

Core Idea
Imamate is not a core creedal matter; neglecting it does not harm one's faith.

After completing the main chapters of kalam, Juwayni addresses the topic of imamate (leadership). He makes a significant methodological point: imamate is not part of the genuine creeds (al-ʿaqāʾid), and one who neglects it is not harmed in their faith. However, he notes that it has become customary to include it at the end of works on tawḥīd. This lesson explores that distinction.

The Salaf affirmed attributes while avoiding ta'wil and asking 'how'.

Imam Juwayni presents a key proof for the Salaf's approach: the Companions and Successors refrained from ta'wil of ambiguous verses, and their unanimous practice is decisive. Commentary
This passage is a proof from the practice of the Salaf (the early righteous predecessors) for the correct method of dealing with ambiguous Quranic verses and hadith about Allah's attributes. The author begins by stating that the companions (al-sahaba) and the followers (al-tabi'un) did not engage in ta'wil (interpretation that departs from the apparent meaning). If ta'wil were permissible, they would have been more concerned with it than with the branches of Shari'a, but they were not. Therefore, their refraining is a decisive proof that the correct path is to refrain. Then the author affirms that it is the duty of a religious person to declare Allah transcendent above the attributes of created beings, and not to delve into ta'wil of problematic texts, but to consign their meaning to Allah. Then he mentions the verse 'And none knows its interpretation except Allah' (Al 'Imran 3:7) and says that stopping at 'except Allah' is the firm resolve, and then beginning with 'and those firmly grounded in knowledge' – this refers to the well-known recitation where one stops at 'illa Allah' and then starts 'wa al-rasikhuna fi al-ilm' (i.e., they say 'and the firmly grounded in knowledge say "we believe in it"'). This was the practice of Imam Malik. Then he quotes Imam Malik's famous response when asked about istiwa': 'The istiwa' is known, its how is unknown, and asking about it is innovation.' This encapsulates the Salaf method: affirm the attribute (al-istiwa' is known) but negate any modality (al-kayfiyya majhula) and forbid speculative questions. The author then extends this principle to other verses like 'When I created with My hands', 'the Face of your Lord', 'sail under Our eyes', and hadith like the descent (nuzul). Finally, he concludes that this is the explanation of what is obligatory regarding Allah.
