The Meaninglessness of Kasb and the Proof that Only Allah Creates
Juwayni refutes kasb as incoherent and proves sole divine creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Juwayni dismisses the concept of kasb as incoherent and how he uses consensus, scripture, and reason to establish that only Allah creates.

Core Idea
No one can be a creator without full knowledge of their creation.

Juwayni takes on the controversial theory of kasb (acquisition), which attempts to attribute human action to both servant and God. He first exposes its emptiness by demanding a clear definition, then provides overwhelming evidence from early consensus, the Quran, and rational argument that creation belongs exclusively to Allah. This passage cuts to the heart of the qadar debate.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the literal translation to get the overall flow of the argument.
Notice that Juwayni uses a challenging tone with the kasb proponent.
2
Identify the refutation of kasb
Find where Juwayni questions the meaning of 'kasb' and says the divisions are exhausted. Understand why he thinks the term is meaningless.
He asks 'What is kasb? What is its meaning?' and implies no answer can escape.
3
Observe the positive proof for divine creation
Read the part about early Muslim consensus, the Quranic verses (16:17, 6:102, 6:101, 35:3), and the rational argument. Notice how he combines authority and reason.
The rational argument: a creator must know details of creation; humans do not.
4
Reflect on the rational criterion
Consider why Juwayni insists that knowledge of a thing is nearer than its creation. How does this undermine the claim that humans are creators?

Commentary
This passage contains two arguments. First, a refutation of the claim of 'kasb' (acquisition): if someone says the servant is an 'acquisitor' and his power merely 'acquires' while God creates, he is asked for the meaning of 'kasb' but cannot provide a coherent answer; all possible divisions are exhausted without escape. Second, a positive proof: the early Muslim consensus before innovations affirmed that there is no creator but Allah, as they professed 'there is no god but Allah'. Juwayni cites four Quranic verses that attribute creation exclusively to Allah (16:17, 6:102, 6:101, 35:3). Then he gives a rational argument: a creator must have comprehensive knowledge of the details of his creation, but humans do not know the full reality of their own actions (they often err in measure and extent). Knowledge of a thing is more immediate than its creation, so the lack of knowledge precludes being a creator. Thus, the claim that humans are creators is false.

Worship as Thank-Offering: Why Grace Cannot Be Repaid
Juwayni gives a second proof: worship as thank-offering cannot obligate reward.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Juwayni's argument that acts of worship are a thank-offering for blessings received in this life, and therefore cannot serve as a basis for obligatory reward in the afterlife.

Core Idea
Worship repays nothing; it is a thank-you that cannot earn a wage.

This lesson presents a second proof from Imam al-Juwayni that reward is not obligatory upon God. The argument hinges on the nature of worship as a form of thankfulness (shukr) for blessings already given. Since the servant's deeds are already an exchange for those blessings, they cannot simultaneously be a means to earn further reward. This challenges the rationalist view that God must reward obedience.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the key claim: acts of worship are a thank-offering for blessings.
The author uses a rhetorical question to make his point.
2
Identify the key terms
Look for the words 'ibadat, ni'am, shukr, 'iwad. Understand how they relate: worship (ibadat) is a thank-offering (shukr) and exchange ('iwad) for blessings (ni'am).
These terms are central to the argument.
3
Reflect on the logic
Ask yourself: If worship is already an exchange for blessings, can it also be a basis for earning reward? Why or why not?
The answer leads to the conclusion that reward cannot be obligatory.

ومما يقطع مادة كلامهم : أن العبادات التي يقيمها العبد لا تفي بالنعم التي تتوفر عليه من ربه ، ناجزا ، وهى تقع شكرا لأنعم الله تبارك وتعالى بل لا تفى بأقلها فاذا وقعت شكرا عوضا عما تعجل من نعم الله ، فكيف يستمر في حكم العقل استحقاق الثواب على أعمال وقعت عوضا عن نعيم توفاه العبد ؟ ثم قالوا : ليس على أهل الجنان شكر النعيمها . فانه عوض أعمال العبد ، وليس للمعوض عوض . فمن أضل سبيلا ممن يوجب على الله تبارك وتعالى ثواب أعمال العبد ، وهى عوض ما ينجز من النعم ، ولم يوجب على العبد ، شكر الثواب غدا لكونه عوضا ؟ ثم من زعم أن العقل يدل على استحقاق العقل بكفر ساعة الخلود في دركات النيران ، فقد ادعى فى مقتضى العقول محالا . هيهات ، برح الخفاء ، يحكم الله ما يريد ، ويفعل ما يشاء .
And among what cuts off the substance of their speech: that the acts of worship which the servant performs do not suffice for the blessings that come to him from his Lord, immediately, and they occur as thankfulness for the blessings of Allah, Blessed and Exalted; rather they do not suffice for the least of them. So when they occur as thankfulness in exchange for what he has received quickly of Allah's blessings, how can there persist in the judgment of reason the deserving of reward for deeds that occurred as exchange for bliss that the servant received? Then they said: The people of Paradise are not obligated to thank for its bliss, for it is an exchange for the servant's deeds, and there is no exchange for the exchange. So who is more astray than one who makes obligatory upon Allah, Blessed and Exalted, reward for the servant's deeds, while they are an exchange for the blessings that are granted, and does not make obligatory upon the servant thankfulness for the reward tomorrow because it is an exchange? Then whoever claims that reason indicates the deserving of punishment for an hour of disbelief of eternity in the depths of the Fire, he has claimed what is impossible according to the requirements of intellects. Alas! the concealment has been removed; Allah judges what He wills and does what He wishes.
Source pages p. 56

Commentary
This passage is a second proof that reward is not obligatory upon God, based on the nature of worship and blessings. Juwayni argues that the servant's acts of worship cannot repay even the least of God's blessings; rather they are a thank-offering (shukr) for those blessings received in this life. Since they are already an exchange ('iwad) for the blessings, they cannot also be grounds for earning a reward. He then cites a further argument: the people of Paradise do not owe thanks for its bliss because that bliss is itself an exchange for their deeds. So it would be inconsistent to require God to reward deeds that already serve as an exchange. Finally, he dismisses the idea that reason could obligate eternal punishment for an hour of disbelief as impossible. The conclusion is that God does as He wills; the concealment of the truth is removed.

The Inconsistency of Claiming Obligatory Reward and Punishment
Juwayni extends the exchange argument to paradise and punishment, concluding with God's absolute will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Juwayni's further argument that if deeds are exchange for blessings, then paradise is also exchange, and that eternal punishment for a moment of disbelief is irrational, leading to the absolute will of God.

Core Idea
If deeds are exchange, then paradise is also exchange — and eternal punishment for finite sin is absurd.

This lesson continues Juwayni's argument by drawing out the implications of the exchange view. He cites a companion point: the people of Paradise do not thank for its bliss because it is an exchange for their deeds. Then he highlights the inconsistency: those who obligate reward on God do not obligate thankfulness for reward. Finally, he dismisses the claim that reason obligates eternal punishment for an hour of disbelief as impossible. The conclusion is that God's will is absolute.

Guided Path
1
Read the second part of the passage
Start from 'Then they said' and read to the end. Notice the logical steps: paradise as exchange, inconsistency of obligation, and rejection of rational punishment.
The passage ends with a statement of God's absolute will.
2
Understand the parity argument
The author argues that if worship is exchange for blessings, then paradise (the reward) is exchange for worship. Hence, the people of Paradise do not need to thank for it.
This undermines the idea of a separate obligation.
3
Reflect on the punishment point
The final point targets those who claim reason obligates eternal punishment for finite sin. Juwayni calls this impossible and concludes that Allah judges as He wills.
This reinforces the Ash'ari position that God's actions are not subject to rational obligation.

Reward and Punishment: By Promise, Not Obligation
Juwayni answers the objection that reward and punishment obligate God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Juwayni's argument that reward and punishment are established by God's promise and threat, not by any obligation upon Him, using the analogy of a servant and master.

Core Idea
Reward is by God's promise, not by desert.

Juwayni now addresses a key objection to the Ash'ari position that nothing is obligatory upon God. If Shari'a mentions reward and punishment as recompense, does that not imply obligation? Juwayni responds with a clear distinction: reward and punishment are real, but they are based on God's faithful promise and threat, not on any debt or necessity. To illustrate, he gives a striking analogy of a servant serving a master.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Read the Arabic passage and translation. Identify who is speaking – first the objector, then Juwayni's response.
Notice the objection uses 'فان قيل' (if it is said) – a common formula.
2
Understand the analogy
Extract the analogy of the servant and master. Compare the servant's service to the believer's worship. Note what the servant does not deserve.
The servant does not deserve emancipation, only maintenance.
3
Grasp the key distinction
Juwayni says reward is by promise, not desert. How does this preserve God's sovereignty? The servant becomes free by the master's word, not by his own work.
The analogy directly parallels reward by God's promise.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider how this relates to the first pillar – that nothing is obligatory upon God. Reward and punishment are real but stem from God's own voluntary promise.
This upholds God's absolute freedom and power.

If it is said: You have built the first pillar upon the establishment of the Shari'a and following its sources. And reward and punishment in the laws and religions are established. And Allah, Blessed and Exalted, has named them as recompense for the deeds of the servants. We say: We do not deny them, but they are established by the promise of Allah. So we say: When a servant serves his master, he does not deserve from him that he should free him and release him from the captivity of bondage and the humiliation of servitude; rather the measure established by custom is that he should provide for his maintenance, and he does not burden him with work except what he can bear. Likewise, reward is established decisively by the promise of Allah Almighty, and punishment is established by His threat.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. The objector says: You (Ash'aris) have built your first pillar (the principle that nothing is obligatory upon God) on the basis of Shari'a, yet Shari'a itself mentions reward and punishment as recompense for deeds. So how can you deny that God is obligated to reward and punish? The answer: We do not deny reward and punishment; rather they are established by God's promise and threat, not by any obligation upon Him. Then Juwayni provides an analogy: a servant who serves his master does not deserve emancipation; at most he deserves maintenance. But if the master promises freedom for a specific act, then when the servant performs it, he becomes free by the master's word, not by desert. Similarly, reward is by God's promise, not by desert. The analogy illustrates that reward is a fulfillment of a promise, not a necessary recompense.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Wa'idiyya's key Quranic proof. Juwayni presents two interpretative responses to Quran 4:93, which threatens eternal Hell for one who kills a believer intentionally. First interpretation: attributed to Ibn Abbas, the verse refers to one who considers murder lawful (mustahill), because true 'intentional' (amdan) requires the absence of a deterrent, and faith itself is a deterrent. This is supported by other verses that treat the killer as a brother and urge pardon, and by the fact that the verse of threat does not mention the law of retaliation. Second interpretation: the word 'khālidan' (abiding) can mean a long duration, not necessarily eternal, as in customary speech (e.g., 'may God immortalize the king'). Juwayni then appeals to the decisive text of Quran 4:116, which states that Allah forgives anything less than shirk for whom He wills, without condition of repentance, thus leaving the fate of the disobedient believer to Allah's will, possibly through intercession or temporary punishment. He concludes with the prophetic hadith that no one with faith in his heart will remain in Hell forever. Thus, the Wa'idiyya proof is refuted, and the Ash'ari position of potential forgiveness and intercession is affirmed.

Juwayni appeals to Quran 4:116 and a hadith to affirm forgiveness for sinful believers.

Learning Objective

Commentary

Commentary
This passage presents a series of arguments from those Muslim scholars (identified as Dirar ibn Amr, Bishr al-Marisi, and later Mu'tazila) who deny the reality of questioning, punishment, and bliss in the grave. The author, Imam Juwayni, is reporting their position as a prelude to refuting it. The passage follows a sequential logic: (1) They first cite the Qur'anic verse 'They will not taste death therein except the first death' (44:56), arguing that if the deceased were given life in the grave, they would taste a second death — which contradicts the verse. (2) They then cite the verse 'Our Lord, You have made us die twice and given us life twice' (40:11), interpreting the two deaths as pre-birth non-existence and natural death, and the two lives as life in this world and the Hereafter; adding a third life (in the grave) would violate the verse's enumeration. (3) Regarding the verse about Pharaoh's family being exposed to the Fire morning and evening (40:46), they claim this 'fire' is metaphorical, not literal, referring to worldly hardships due to their opposition to Moses. They support this by citing other verses where 'fire' is used metaphorically (e.g., 4:10), and argue that the text is ambiguous and must be referred to clear verses (muhkam) like 'Then you will surely be asked about the pleasures' (102:8) and the atom's weight verse (99:7-8), which they interpret as referring only to the Hereafter. (4) Concerning the verse about Noah's people (71:25), they offer two explanations: either there is a long interval between drowning and entering Hellfire, or the perception of time for the dead is unlike that of the living. They invoke the story of the People of the Cave (18:19) and a Qur'anic dialogue on the Day of Resurrection (23:112-113) to argue that the dead do not sense the passage of long periods, just as sleepers do. They support this with the famous hadith 'Whoever dies, his Resurrection has begun,' implying that the deceased are not in a state of conscious life. Finally, they address the hadiths that explicitly affirm punishment in the grave, claiming that even the affirmers do not take them as independent proofs but only as confirmations after interpreting Qur'anic verses. If these hadiths were self-sufficient, they would not be accompanied by such interpretations.

Commentary
This passage is a combined statement and objection-response. Al-Juwayni first affirms that the Bridge is a physical bridge over Hellfire and that the Balance is a sensory balance. He admits he did not derive these from explicit Qur’anic texts, but from the principle that nothing is impossible for God’s power. This raises an objection: can God’s power alone establish a creedal matter without textual evidence? The objector notes that creeds are only established by the Qur’an. So why did al-Juwayni interpret 'prepared' (from the verse that the Fire has been prepared) as past tense and the Bridge and Balance as sensory? The answer begins by describing a historical view that metaphor is impermissible in the Qur’an. Adherents of this view interpret all verses literally. As an example, they take the verse 'They eat fire in their bellies' to mean literal fire. In contrast, metaphorical interpreters argue that it cannot be literal because the immediately following verse mentions Hellfire, indicating that the fire in the bellies is a metaphor for the unlawful wealth they consume. This same principle extends to the Bridge and Balance: literalists affirm them as sensory based on apparent verses; metaphorical interpreters deny that, considering the Bridge a metaphor for the straight path (as in al-Fātiḥa) and the Balance a metaphor for justice (as in al-Raḥmān). They argue that other verses like al-Aʿrāf:8-9 are ambiguous and cannot constitute a decisive proof. They interpret the verse 'Guide them to the path of Hellfire' as taunting and rebuke, not a literal path. So al-Juwayni’s answer is that he follows the literalist school, which affirms the sensory reality of these eschatological items based on apparent meanings, rejecting metaphorical interpretation in the Qur’an.
