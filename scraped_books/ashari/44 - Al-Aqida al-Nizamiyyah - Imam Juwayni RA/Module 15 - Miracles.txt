The Objection to Miracles and the Division into Two Types
Juwayni presents the objection and divides miracles into two types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the skeptical objection that miracles could be due to hidden properties, and Juwayni's initial response dividing miracles into two kinds.

Core Idea
Miracles are either extraordinary acts or prevention of the customary.

Juwayni begins by presenting a strong objection: how can we be sure a miracle is not just hidden knowledge or a natural property? He then gives a crisp answer: this objection only impresses the weak, and miracles are fundamentally of two distinct types. Let's read his precise formulation.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Notice how the objector uses examples like magnetism and hidden properties of drugs to cast doubt.
This is a common skeptical argument.
2
Identify Juwayni's response
He calls it 'obfuscation' and then gives the division.
3
Understand the two divisions
Memorize the two types: unprecedented act and prevention of customary.
This sets up the rest of the lesson.

If a claimant of prophethood brings something unprecedented, one cannot be safe from the possibility that he has attained hidden knowledge and used it to manifest what he is distinguished by over people, and perhaps one may come across a body among bodies with an unknown and unfamiliar property, and there is no end to the marvels attributed to the properties of drugs. If a presenter presented a magnet in a region, and they had not heard of it, they would imagine its attraction of iron to be a breach of custom. So how can one be safe from this? And what distinguishes miracles from it? We say: This is an obfuscation upon the weak. Those with insight do not care for its likes. The way of answer for it is: The miracle is divided into two divisions. One of them is that it is an unprecedented act that breaks custom. The second is that it is the prevention of the customary. If it is from the breakers of custom, its condition is that it rises above the path of conjectures and reaches a level where the estimations contained in the question are resolved. The clarification of that by example: He who does not deem remote the distinction of some people with advantages from sciences - as previously indicated - it is not permissible to let every unprecedented custom-breaking [act] proceed from the properties of substances, nor does the matter in that end to permitting everything mentioned for it. And whoever reaches that has removed the yoke of intellect from his neck, contradicted intuition, and denied necessary truths of intellects. If a doubt were to doubt that the turning of the staff into a serpent is not something attainable by a property of a substance or the grasp of an advantage in hidden sciences, then he is afflicted in his intellect. Likewise, whoever considers that what was performed at the hands of Jesus, peace and blessings be upon him, such as reviving the dead, curing the blind and leprous, and his other signs, are from the art of tricks attained by those who acquire subtleties of sciences, then he is confused and insane. As for miracles, they are extraordinary; they are distinguished decisively from the ranks of unprecedented crafts and matters that special people are distinguished by. This is the meaning of 'breaking custom' in the conditions of miracles. What clarifies that is that whoever manifests something that the special people are distinguished by, and challenges with it the [opponents?], and calls with them to himself, then the motives become abundant to attempt opposing him and striving to bring the like of what he brought. And he will be opposed from this soon. If what the claimant of prophethood brought is something the like of which is expected, his prophethood is not established, with the objection of [the devils?] that this belongs to one of the two divisions, namely that which is extraordinary and unprecedented in itself. As for what is a prevention of the customary, such as the claimant of prophethood saying: 'My sign is that standing should be prevented today for the worlds' - then what is like that becomes impossible for the rational person to imagine it from a hidden scientific advantage or the grasp of a special property. This is clear, no need for excessive elaboration. This is the extent of what we presented concerning the second condition from the conditions of miracles.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection to the claim that miracles (mu'jizat) are distinguishable from other extraordinary phenomena, followed by Juwayni's answer. The objector argues that if someone claims prophethood and performs a marvel, we cannot be sure it is not due to hidden knowledge or the special properties of objects (e.g., magnetism). Juwayni responds that this is an obfuscation (tamwih) that impresses only the weak; people of insight disregard it. He then divides miracles into two types: (1) an unprecedented extraordinary act (fi'l badi' khariq li'l-‘ada), and (2) the prevention of the customary (man' al-mu'tad). For the first type, he stipulates that it must transcend all possibility of being due to natural properties or hidden sciences. To illustrate, he argues that no rational person could claim that Moses' staff turning into a serpent was due to some natural property, or that Jesus reviving the dead was a trick—those who do so are insane. Such true miracles are decisively distinguished from all human crafts and special abilities. For the second type (prevention of the customary), the example is a claimant saying 'my sign is that standing is prevented today'—this cannot be imagined as due to any hidden knowledge. Juwayni concludes this discussion as covering the second condition of miracles.

The Condition for Extraordinary Miracles and the Examples of Moses and Jesus
Juwayni explains the condition for the first type of miracle and gives examples from Moses and Jesus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the condition that an extraordinary miracle must transcend all possibility of being due to hidden causes, as illustrated by the miracles of Moses and Jesus.

Core Idea
True miracles are so extraordinary they cannot be explained by natural properties or hidden sciences.

Juwayni now lays down the key condition for the first type of miracle: it must rise above all plausible natural explanations. He uses two powerful examples—Moses' staff and Jesus' reviving the dead—to show that claiming these are tricks or properties is irrational. This condition defines what 'breaking custom' really means.

The Second Type: Prevention of the Customary
Juwayni explains the second type of miracle (prevention of the customary) and concludes the section on conditions.

He gives the example of a claimant saying 'standing is prevented today'—this cannot be due to hidden properties. This type is even more clearly distinct from human abilities. Commentary

However, if the same action (standing and sitting) occurred without the preceding context—the claim, the people's distress, the announcement—then the king's mere departure from habit would not indicate the servant's truthfulness. Thus, the miracle's indicative force depends on its connection to a prophetic claim and the context that makes it a divine endorsement.

Commentary
This passage is an argument about miracles and prophecies. Juwayni first states that miracles are not limited to one type of knowledge but encompass all sciences, referring to his own book on the inimitability of the Quran. Then he discusses sensory miracles (physical miracles) and notes that there is debate about them, but the Quran itself is sufficient as a miracle. He quotes the Quranic verse (29:50-51) where the disbelievers demand signs, and Allah replies that signs are with Him and that the Quran is enough. Then he turns to prophecies in the Torah that point to Prophet Muhammad. He explains that Jews and Christians interpret these prophecies differently, but Muslims hold that they indicate Muhammad because his description matches that of Moses, and the Torah states that no prophet like Moses will appear within the Israelites, and a blessing for the nations is promised through Ishmael. Therefore, the prophet must come from Ishmael's lineage. Juwayni references his own works on the distortion of the Torah and Gospel.
