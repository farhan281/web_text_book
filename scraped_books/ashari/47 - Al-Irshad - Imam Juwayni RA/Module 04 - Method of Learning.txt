Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal statement from Juwayni's al-Risala al-Nizamiyya, addressing the correct approach to ambiguous scriptural texts (al-zawahir). Juwayni begins by noting that scholars differed over these texts: some resorted to ta'wil (figurative interpretation), while the Salaf (early pious ancestors) refrained from ta'wil, instead letting the texts stand as they are while consigning their true meanings to Allah (tafwid). He provides a decisive transmitted proof: namely, the consensus (ijma') of the umma, which is a foundational source of the Shari'a. He argues that the Companions, despite their deep engagement with the Shari'a and their efforts in preserving and teaching it, did not delve into interpreting these ambiguous texts. If ta'wil were permissible or obligatory, they would have been more concerned with it than with the practical branches of the law. Since both the Companions and the Followers persisted in refraining from ta'wil, this serves as evidence for the correctness of the Salaf's approach. The passage is cut off, but the argument is clear: the historical practice of the early community constitutes a binding precedent.

He distinguishes two scholarly approaches and argues that the Salaf's practice of tafwid (delegation of meaning to God) is supported by a decisive proof from consensus. Commentary
This is a doctrinal passage in which Juwayni establishes his position on the interpretation of ambiguous scriptural texts (zawahir). He begins by noting that scholars have differed: some adopted ta'wil (allegorical interpretation), while the Salaf (early pious predecessors) refrained from ta'wil and instead practiced tafwid (delegation of meaning to God). Juwayni then declares his own stance: he follows the Salaf, choosing ittiba' (following) over ibtida' (innovation). To support this, he provides a decisive scriptural proof (dalil sam'i qati') based on consensus (ijma'). He argues that the Companions, the best of the community, did not engage in ta'wil of these texts, despite their complete dedication to preserving and teaching the religion. If ta'wil were legitimate or obligatory, they would have been the first to practice it. Since they and the Followers refrained from ta'wil, this silence is a decisive proof that tafwid is the correct approach. Consequently, Juwayni concludes that every religious person must believe in God's transcendence (tanzih) over created attributes, avoid plunging into interpretation of ambiguous texts, and delegate their meanings to God. Specific examples like istiwa', maji', God's hands, face, and the 'eyes' in the Quran, as well as hadiths about descent, are to be understood in this way without ta'wil.

This passage marks a turning point in Islamic theology. Imam al-Juwayni and his student al-Ghazali introduced a more flexible approach to reasoning, freeing scholars from the rigidity of earlier methods. Let's see how they transformed the study of kalam.
