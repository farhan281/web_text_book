The Great Fitna of 408 AH
Juwayni reports a major sectarian conflict in 408 AH.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the historical context and causes of the sectarian conflicts reported by Imam Juwayni, particularly the Great Fitna of 408 AH.

Core Idea
Weak leadership invites communal strife.

Imam Juwayni, in his historical account, draws attention to the frequent sectarian seditions that plagued Muslim society. He points to a key underlying factor: the weakness of the caliphate and administrative failure. This lesson examines his report of a major fitna in 408 AH between Sunnis and Shias, highlighting the consequences of political decay.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without pausing. Then read the translation to grasp the full meaning.
Notice how Juwayni first generalizes about fitan before giving a specific example.
2
Identify the groups involved
List the groups mentioned: Ahl al-Sunna, Shi'a, Hanabila, and others. Why are they in conflict?
The passage suggests conflicts were both theological and political.
3
Analyze the root cause
Juwayni states that these seditions frightened Muslims only because of weak caliphate and bad administration. Reflect on this causal link.
This is a key historical insight about governance and social stability.
4
Consider the outcome
Note the result of the fitna in 408 AH: deaths and police inability. What does this teach about state capacity?

As for the seditions that often occurred between the Ahl al-Sunna and the Shi'a, or between the Hanabila and those generally who opposed them in opinion, or between others and those among the people of madhhabs and creeds, and by which the Muslims were not frightened except due to the weakness of the caliphate and bad administration – as for what concerns these seditions, it suffices us to point out what Ibn al-'Imad mentioned: that in the year 408 a great sedition occurred between the Ahl al-Sunna and the Shi'a, in which a group from each party was killed, and the chief of police was unable to control the violent combatants.

Commentary
This passage discusses the historical occurrence of fitan (sectarian seditions) among various groups. It first broadly mentions that such conflicts often happened between Sunni and Shia, between Hanbali and their opponents, and among other madhhabs. The author notes that these disturbances troubled Muslims only because of the weakness of the caliphate and poor administration. Then he specifically cites Ibn al-'Imad's report of a major fitna in 408 AH between Sunnis and Shias, resulting in casualties and the inability of the police to control the fighting.

Caliph al-Qadir's Defense of Orthodoxy
Juwayni describes Caliph al-Qadir's enforcement of Sunni orthodoxy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the political and theological measures Caliph al-Qadir employed to enforce Sunni orthodoxy in the early 5th century AH.

Core Idea
Caliph al-Qadir used both state power and theological writings to defend Sunnism.

This passage from Juwayni's *Al-Irshad* offers a window into how the Abbasid caliphate actively shaped sectarian boundaries. We see Caliph al-Qadir deploying both coercive state power and his own pen to suppress what he considered deviant groups. As you read, notice the dual approach: political orders to a sultan and personal theological authorship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the caliph's main action
Read the first part of the passage where al-Qadir sends an order to Sultan Mahmud. What does he command? Note the word 'bathth al-sunna' (spreading the Sunna).
The caliph is not just a religious figure; he wields political authority.
2
List the targeted groups
The passage names five groups. Find them and recall who they are (Mu'tazila, Rafida, Isma'iliyya, Jahmiyya, Mushabbiha). Consider why these particular groups were seen as opponents of Ahl al-Sunna.
The Rafida are extreme Shia, Isma'iliyya are Sevener Shia, etc.
3
Examine the caliph's personal involvement
Read the second part about al-Qadir authoring a book. What does he do? He writes a book supporting one madhhab over another and declares takfir of the Mu'tazila over the createdness of the Qur'an.
Notice the contrast: he was pious (tahajjud) yet engaged in takfir.
4
Reflect on the significance
Ask yourself: Why would a caliph, the head of the Islamic state, personally write a theological book? What does this tell you about the relationship between political power and religious orthodoxy in this period?
This represents the state's active role in defining orthodoxy.

Commentary
This passage details the actions of Caliph al-Qadir in 408 AH. It first describes his order to Sultan Mahmud to propagate Sunnism in Khurasan, which the sultan executed excessively, involving killings and exiles of various sects (Mu'tazila, Rafida, Isma'iliyya, Jahmiyya, Mushabbiha) and ordering curses from pulpits. Then it notes that the caliph personally entered the fray by authoring a book that supported one madhhab over another. Despite his personal piety and nightly tahajjud prayers, he did not refrain from writing a book that declared takfir (disbelief) of the Mu'tazila who held the createdness of the Qur'an. This book was publicly recited every Friday for a period.

Caliph al-Qadir and the Enforcement of Sunni Orthodoxy
Al-Juwayni recounts sectarian conflicts and Caliph al-Qadir's repressive measures.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Caliph al-Qadir used political authority to enforce Sunni orthodoxy during the sectarian conflicts of the 5th century AH.

Core Idea
Caliph al-Qadir turned political power into a weapon for theological conformity.

This passage gives us a window into how early medieval Islamic rulers intervened in theological disputes. Imam al-Juwayni recounts the severe measures taken by Caliph al-Qadir to suppress what he considered heresy, from ordering the cursing of opponents from the pulpit to composing a creedal document read every Friday. This historical backdrop helps explain the intellectual context in which later Sunni theology developed.

Guided Path
1
Read the historical context
Read the passage and translation to get a sense of the sectarian strife and the caliph's response. Notice the specific groups named.
Pay attention to the year 408 AH and the groups mentioned: Sunnis, Shiites, Mu'tazila, etc.
2
Analyze the caliph's methods
Identify the actions taken by Caliph al-Qadir—spreading Sunna in Khurasan, killing/exiling opponents, cursing on minbars, writing a book declaring disbelief.
The passage shows a range of repressive measures.
3
Reflect on the author's tone
The author (al-Juwayni) notes both the piety of the caliph and his harshness. What does this tell us about the relationship between political power and religious authority?
Consider how the author's own theological stance might influence his narration.
