Proof that Capacity Cannot Precede the Act
Ash'ari argues capacity must be simultaneous with the act using absurdity proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ash'ari proves that human capacity must be simultaneous with the act, not preceding it.

Core Idea
Capacity does not endure; if it preceded the act, the act would occur without it.

In this passage, Ash'ari responds to an objection: if capacity is a distinct accident, why can't it come before the act? He uses a logical disjunction and absurdity to show that precedence leads to impossible consequences. Let's follow his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and initial disjunction
Read the Arabic passage and translation to see the objection and Ash'ari's two-part division: the act either occurs with capacity or after it.
Notice the logical structure: if after, problems arise.
2
Examine the absurdity of precedence
Focus on the argument that if the act occurs after capacity, since capacity does not endure, the act would happen with a non-existent capacity. This is declared impossible.
Ash'ari adds that this would allow an act during incapacity, which is absurd.
3
Consider the extended absurdities
Look at the analogies: fire without heat, sword without blade, etc. These illustrate the impossibility of effects without their proper causes.
This strengthens the argument by showing universal absurdity.
4
Conclude that capacity must be simultaneous
The only remaining option is that the act occurs simultaneously with capacity, which is the conclusion.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer dealing with the temporal relation between capacity and the act. After establishing that capacity is a distinct accident, the objector asks why the author holds that capacity cannot precede the act. The answer is a logical disjunction: the act must occur either simultaneously with the capacity or after it. If simultaneous, the capacity is present with the act, which is fine. If after, since Ashʿari has already proven that accidents (like capacity) do not endure (lā tubqā), the act would occur when the capacity no longer exists—i.e., with a non-existent capacity. This is impossible. The argument then shows the absurd consequences of such a possibility: it would allow an act to be performed while the agent is actually incapable, even for a hundred years, by a capacity that vanished long ago. Furthermore, it would allow effects without their proper causes, like fire burning without heat, a sword cutting without a blade, etc. Since these are impossible, the original premise that capacity can precede the act is false. Therefore, capacity must be simultaneous with the act.

Core Idea
Action cannot be performed by a non-existent power.

Ash'ari presents a rigorous proof that the capacity to act must be present at the moment of action, not before or after. He uses logical deduction and striking analogies to show the impossibility of action occurring after capacity has vanished. This lesson walks through his argument step by step.

Commentary
This passage is a proof. The author argues that if an action could occur after the existence of capacity (while capacity does not endure), then the action would be performed by a non-existent power, which leads to absurd consequences. Then he illustrates the absurdity: if that were allowed, it would also be allowed for incapacity to occur after capacity, so the act would be by non-existent power, and it would be possible to act after a hundred years with an extinct power, which is clearly false. He adds another analogy: if burning could occur by non-existent fire heat, that is impossible because God turned fire cool; likewise cutting by non-existent sword is impossible because God turned swords into reeds. So just as those are impossible, action cannot occur without concurrent power. Therefore, the act must occur together with capacity.

Why Power Cannot Persist
Al-Ash'ari gives two arguments showing that power cannot persist.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ash'ari theologians argue that power (qudra) cannot persist, and the two logical arguments they use to prove this.

Core Idea
Power cannot persist because any alleged mechanism leads to contradiction or infinite regress.

This passage from Al-Ash'ari's Al-Luma' addresses a key theological question about the nature of created power. The author presents a rigorous logical disjunction to demonstrate that power (qudra) cannot persist after its initial moment. Let's examine his reasoning carefully.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its literal English translation. Pay attention to the logical structure: the author considers two possibilities and refutes both.
The argument is a disjunction (either/or) leading to absurdity in both cases.
2
Identify the two possibilities
The passage considers two ways power could persist: by itself or by a separate attribute called persistence. Notice how each possibility is defined and what follows from it.
3
Understand the contradictions
For each possibility, Al-Ash'ari shows a logical contradiction. The first implies power must be eternal at its origin; the second requires an attribute to subsist in an attribute, which is impossible. See why both are invalid.
This argument also prevents an infinite regress of attributes.

If it persisted by itself, it would be necessary that its own essence be a persistence for it, and that it not exist except as persisting, and in this is what necessitates that it be persisting at the moment of its origination, which is absurd. Commentary
This passage is a proof by disjunction. Al-Ash'ari responds to an objection about why power (qudra) does not persist. He divides the possibilities: either power persists by itself or by a separate attribute of persistence (baqāʾ). If it persists by itself, then its essence would be identical with persistence, meaning it could never exist without being persisting—which would require it to be persisting at the very moment of its origination (ḥudūth), a contradiction. If it persists by a separate attribute, then an attribute (persistence) would subsist in another attribute (power), which means an accident (ʿaraḍ) subsists in an accident, an impossibility. He then generalizes: if this were allowed, it would permit power to subsist in power, life in life, etc., leading to an infinite regress. Thus both options fail, proving power cannot persist.

Why Human Power Cannot Cover Opposites
Al-Ash'ari refutes the claim that power can be over a thing and its opposite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why human power cannot simultaneously encompass a thing and its opposite, based on the condition that its existence requires the existence of its object.

Core Idea
Human power is tied to its object; God's power is not.

Al-Ash'ari addresses a key objection: if God can do anything, why can't human power cover opposites? He responds by clarifying the nature of originated power—it must have an existing object simultaneously. This foundational principle distinguishes human ability from divine omnipotence.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Notice how the opponent's claim is immediately challenged by a conditional argument. The passage begins with an objection and then gives a detailed answer.
2
Understand the condition of originated power
The passage establishes that for originated power, the existence of power coincides with the existence of its object. This is the key condition.
Consider what would happen if this condition were not true.
3
Contrast with divine power
Compare the different rules for human and divine power. Divine power can exist without an act, but human power cannot. This shows why only human power is limited.
This contrast highlights the distinction between Creator and creation.

For humans, since power cannot exist without an object even for a single moment, it follows that power is limited to producing its object, and cannot be over opposites because that would require both to exist simultaneously, which is impossible.

Core Idea
One power, one action: power is tied to its locus.

Al-Ash'ari addresses an objection: why can't one power cover two wills or movements? He begins by stating a foundational principle: power is only over what coexists with it in its locus (mahall). This lesson unpacks that principle and its implications.

The objector assumes no contradiction.

Locus (mahall) refers to the location of the power, e.g., the hand for movement.
3

The text says 'if one power were over two movements, it would not be free from:' — the answer is cut off, but we can infer the two possibilities: either both movements occur simultaneously or sequentially. Reflect on why both are problematic.
Simultaneous movements in one locus are impossible; sequential would require the power to persist over time, which is debated.
4
Connect to the bigger picture
Understand that this principle reinforces that each action requires its own specific power at the moment of action, preventing multiple actions from a single power.

Al-Ash'ari refutes distinct incapacities with two reductio arguments.

Core Idea
Allah alone creates; humans acquire; nature does not cause.

It distinguishes between creation (khalq), which belongs solely to Allah, and acquisition (iktisab), which belongs to humans. The text also emphatically denies that natural objects like water, bread, or fire have any inherent causal efficacy.
