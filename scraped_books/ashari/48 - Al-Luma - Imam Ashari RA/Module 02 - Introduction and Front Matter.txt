The Ash'ari Principle of Not Excommunicating Muslims
Editor's preface describing Ash'ari inclusivity and proof texts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari school's position on the inviolability of Muslims who profess the Shahada and pray toward the Qibla, and the two key proofs for this principle: the Prophetic hadith and Imam al-Ash'ari's deathbed declaration.

Core Idea
The Ash'ari school does not excommunicate any Muslim who prays toward the Qibla.

This lesson examines a key principle of the Ash'ari school as articulated in the preface to a new edition of Al-Luma. The passage emphasizes that the school protects the life, property, and honor of every Muslim who professes the Shahada and prays toward the Kaaba, grounding this stance in a well-known hadith and in Imam al-Ash'ari's own deathbed testimony. It also clarifies that this school is not an innovation but a revival of the original Prophetic way.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice the forceful emphasis on protection and the two proofs presented.
The passage is from a contemporary preface, not from a classical text, but it summarizes a core Ash'ari stance.
2
Identify the two main proofs
The passage gives two proofs: first, the hadith from Sahih al-Bukhari defining a Muslim; second, Imam al-Ash'ari's deathbed statement. Find these in the text.
These two proofs anchor the school's inclusivity in both prophetic authority and the founder's own words.
3
Understand the scope of protection
Note what is protected: blood, wealth, and honor. Also note the condition: violation of clearly stipulated divine limits with definitive textual proof.
The protection is comprehensive but not absolute; it respects the Sharia's boundaries.
4
Reflect on the broader implication
Consider how this principle distinguishes the Ash'ari school from other groups. The preface asserts it is the only school that does not excommunicate anyone from the Qibla.
This is a bold claim that sets the school apart in debates about takfir.

Commentary
This passage is from the preface to the new edition of Al-Luma, written by the Grand Imam of Al-Azhar. It first describes the Ash'ari school's inclusive and protective stance towards all Muslims who profess the shahada and pray toward the Kaaba. It emphasizes that the school does not permit violating a Muslim's life, property, or honor except for clearly established legal violations. The preface then asserts that the Ash'ari school is not an innovation but a revival of the Prophetic way. It grounds this in a hadith from Sahih al-Bukhari where the Prophet defines a Muslim by prayer, qibla, and eating slaughtered meat, and warns against violating God's protection. The author connects this to Imam al-Ash'ari's own deathbed declaration that he does not excommunicate anyone from the people of the qibla because all point to the same object of worship, differing only in expression. Finally, the preface justifies the new edition as part of a series of works on Ash'ari theology, acknowledging the difficulty and value of these texts, and notes the editor's work in teaching them at Al-Azhar.

The Author's Opening Doxology
Al-Ash'ari opens his book praising Allah and bearing witness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure and theological significance of Al-Ash'ari's opening doxology.

Core Idea
A doxology sets the theological foundation before arguments begin.

Imam al-Ash'ari begins his work with a doxology—a liturgical praise of Allah—followed by the two testimonies of faith. This opening is not just a formality; it establishes the author's Sunni commitments and the framework for the rational arguments that follow. In this lesson, you will examine the components of this opening and its role in the text.

Editor's Classification of al-Ash'ari's Works
Editor classifies al-Ash'ari's works into two functional categories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the editor's two-category classification of al-Ash'ari's books and the reasoning behind it.

Core Idea
Al-Ash'ari's corpus splits into works advocating Sunni views and works objectively reporting others' doctrines.

The editor of this modern edition faced a challenge: how to organize the vast and partially lost corpus of Imam al-Ash'ari. Instead of a simple list, he proposes a functional classification based on the content and purpose of each work. The following passage presents this classification, which is essential for understanding the scope of al-Ash'ari's contribution to Islamic theology.

Commentary
This passage is the editor's introduction to the book, explaining his methodology for classifying the works of Imam Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari. The editor begins by stating that he extracted the present work from about thirty of al-Ash'ari's books, most of which are now lost. He then notes that Ibn Furak, a later scholar, also collected al-Ash'ari's writings and even gathered the articles of Ibn Kullab (al-Ash'ari's predecessor), though those too are lost. The editor mentions that some Maghribi scholars have tried to reconstruct Ibn Kullab's works from Ibn Taymiyyah's books. The editor then announces his plan: instead of providing a simple list of al-Ash'ari's works (as earlier scholars like Ibn 'Asakir did), he will offer a preliminary classification based on the meanings of the titles and the scarce descriptive data available. He lists several scholars who have relied on these works, such as Ibn Furak, al-Baqillani, al-Juwayni, etc., and notes that his classification is a modest step toward a proper catalog. Finally, the editor presents the classification itself: (1) books in which al-Ash'ari expressed his own views (i.e., the views of Ahl al-Sunna), supported by his own proofs, composed after his reformation of kalam; and (2) books dedicated to presenting others' views fairly and objectively, with only occasional critical remarks, such as his famous work 'al-Maqalat'.

Commentary
This passage is the introductory khutba (sermon) of Ibn Khafif's creed. It begins with praise of Allah for guidance, revelation of the Book, and sending messengers. Ibn Khafif emphasizes that Allah has explained the traditions and the verses, and that the Qur'an contains warnings, commands, prohibitions, and exhortations, all as lessons and admonitions. He then sends prayers on the Prophet Muhammad and his family. After the traditional opening, he states the purpose: the intelligent person corrects his creed as provision for meeting his Lord, purifies his intention to make his deeds sincere, and worships Allah excellently. He recognizes that he was not created in vain or left without purpose, so he strives to strengthen his religion, purify his actions, and correct his worship. As a result, faith becomes perfect, pure, increases, and grows. The khutba closes with a prayer that Allah guides to the paths of right guidance. This introduction sets the tone for the creed that follows, emphasizing the necessity of sound belief as the foundation for all deeds.
