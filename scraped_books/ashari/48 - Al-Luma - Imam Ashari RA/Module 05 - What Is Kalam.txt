Two Verses Praising Kalam
Two verses praising kalam as superior to all other sciences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how these two verses present kalam as the foundational science to which all other sciences are subordinate.

Core Idea
Kalam is the master science; all other sciences serve it.

Every field of knowledge has a relative importance, but early scholars asserted that kalam holds a unique position. These two poetic lines express the relationship between kalam and other sciences with striking clarity. Let's explore how the poet elevates kalam above all else.

Guided Path
1
Read the verses and translation
Read the Arabic verses once without analysis, then read the English translation. Let the meaning settle before interpreting.
Notice the direct address 'O you who set out seeking knowledge' – it hooks the reader.
2
Understand the metaphor of 'slave'
The first line states that every science is a 'slave' (عبد) to kalam. Consider what this metaphor implies about hierarchy and dependence.
In classical Arabic, 'abd' implies complete servitude and subordination.
3
Analyze the critique of fiqh
The second line criticizes those who study fiqh to correct rulings but neglect the Revealer of rulings (Allah). How does this highlight the necessity of kalam?
The poet suggests fiqh without kalam is incomplete – it focuses on details while ignoring the ultimate source.
4
Reflect on the overall message
Consider how these two verses together argue for the primacy of kalam. What does this tell us about the purpose of knowledge?
The poet is not dismissing fiqh, but placing it in proper perspective under kalam.

Commentary
This passage presents two lines of poetry (a couplet) attributed to Ibn Mujahid (or transmitted from him) that praise the science of kalam (theology). The first line addresses the seeker of knowledge, stating that every science is a servant ('abd) to kalam, implying that kalam is the master science. The second line critiques those who study fiqh (jurisprudence) to correct a ruling (hukm) but neglect the Revealer of rulings (munzil al-ahkam), meaning God. The point is that kalam is needed to properly understand the foundations of faith and the attributes of the Lawgiver, without which fiqh is incomplete. Thus, the verses present kalam as essential and superior to other sciences because it deals with the ultimate source of rulings.

Kalam as the Highest Science: Ibn Mujahid's Couplet
Ibn Mujahid's couplet and its commentary on kalam's supremacy.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Kalam is the servant of nothing; all other sciences serve it.

This lesson presents a famous couplet by Ibn Mujahid that poetically asserts the supremacy of kalam over all other Islamic sciences. The subsequent commentary unpacks this claim, explaining that kalam investigates the foundational principles that other sciences take for granted. By studying this passage, you will see how kalam was conceptualized as the 'highest science'—the discipline that provides the metaphysical groundwork for jurisprudence and theology.

Faith as Inner Assent: The Linguistic Proof of Al-Ash'ari
Al-Ash'ari defines faith as taṣdīq based on language and Qur'an.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that according to Al-Ash'ari, faith (īmān) is defined as inner assent (taṣdīq) based on the language of the Qur'an and common Arabic usage.

Core Idea
Faith is not just words or deeds—it is inner assent.

Al-Ash'ari begins his discussion on faith by grounding its definition in the very language of the Qur'an. He argues that the word īmān in Arabic means taṣdīq (assent), and this meaning is confirmed by both a Qur'anic verse and everyday speech. In this lesson, we will examine his linguistic argument step by step.

Guided Path
1

Pay attention to the verse from Surah Yusuf.

Find where Al-Ash'ari states directly what faith is. He says it is 'al-tasdiq billah' (assent to Allah).
This is the core claim.
3
Understand the first proof: language of the Qur'an
Al-Ash'ari argues that the Qur'an was revealed in Arabic, and the Arabs used 'īmān' to mean 'taṣdīq'. He quotes verse 17 of Surah Yusuf as evidence.
The verse uses 'mā anta bi-mu'min' meaning 'you are not an asserter'.
4
Understand the second proof: common usage
He then points to common expressions like 'yu'minu bi-adhābi al-qabr' where 'yu'minu' means 'yuṣaddiq' (assents to).
This shows the word's consistent meaning in Arabic.
5

The Linguistic Argument Against the Intermediate Station
Linguistic analogy establishing naming by action.

Learning Objective

When we say someone strikes, we call them a striker; when someone disbelieves, we call them a disbeliever. Distinguishing Iman, Islam, Tawhid, Ma'rifa
Al-Ash'ari distinguishes key terms and categorizes types of knowledge and faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the clear distinctions al-Ash'ari makes between iman, Islam, tawhid, and ma'rifa, and the classification of knowledge of God into necessary, acquired, and gifted.

Core Idea
Iman is not the same as Islam; tawhid is not the same as ma'rifa; knowledge of God's existence is necessary, but details are acquired or gifted.

After defining ma'rifa and iman, Al-Ash'ari explicitly separates related terms to prevent confusion. He then explains that knowledge of God's existence is rationally necessary, while knowledge of His attributes is acquired through study and evidence. Furthermore, recognizing God's specific actions (takhṣīṣ) is a gift from Him. The origin of faith is also a gift, but its conditions must be acquired. This passage clarifies the nuances of Islamic epistemology.

Guided Path

This shows these are not synonyms but overlapping concepts.
2
Categorize knowledge of God
Identify the three types: knowledge of existence (necessary), knowledge of attributes (acquired), knowledge of specification (gift). Note how each is obtained.
Necessary means it is inherent or rationally unavoidable; acquired means learned; gift means divinely bestowed.
3
Understand origin and conditions of faith
The origin of iman is a gift from God, but its conditions (like performing acts of worship) are acquired by the individual's effort.
This balances divine grace and human responsibility.

The Outward and Reality of Faith
Al-Ash'ari explains the outward and reality of faith and the relationship between believer and Muslim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that iman, tawhid, and ma'rifa have both an outward aspect and an inner reality, and that every believer is a Muslim but not every Muslim is a believer.

Core Idea
Faith has an outward form and a deeper reality; all believers are Muslims, but not all Muslims are true believers.

The lesson ends with the famous principle that every believer is a Muslim, but not every Muslim is a believer, emphasizing that faith is the deeper state beyond mere outward submission.
