Commentary
This passage is an editorial discussion covering two main aspects of the book 'Al-Luma': its title and its attribution to Imam al-Ash'ari (Abu al-Hasan).

First, the editor discusses the name of the book. He states that 'Al-Luma' is the most famous and perhaps the most important of al-Ash'ari's works. The full title is 'Al-Luma' fi al-Radd 'ala Ahl al-Zaygh wa al-Bida'' (The Luminous [Book] in Refutation of the People of Deviation and Innovations). Among the second generation of Ash'aris, such as al-Baqillani and Ibn Furak, the full title is used. Later scholars like Ibn Taymiyya abbreviate it by omitting 'al-Zaygh' (deviation), calling it 'Al-Luma' fi al-Radd 'ala Ahl al-Bida''. The editor notes that this shortening is plausible after about four centuries. Modern editors like Dr. Gharaba have published it with the full title, and bibliographies consistently list it under the full name.

Second, the editor addresses the attribution of the book to al-Ash'ari (Abu al-Hasan). He asserts there is no doubt about its authenticity. He mentions several chains of transmission: al-Ash'ari's own list of works, his direct students (Ibn Khafif, Abu al-Hasan al-Bahili), and their students (al-Baqillani, Ibn Furak) who heard the book's explanation from the direct students. Even opponents like the Mu'tazili 'Abd al-Jabbar, who wrote a refutation ('Naqd al-Luma'), attest to its authorship. The editor then provides a decisive proof: a certificate (thabat) from the later scholar Muhammad al-Sanbawi al-Azhari, who lists 'Al-Luma' among his transmissions in his work 'Sadd al-Arb', confirming its attribution.

In summary, the passage firmly establishes both the correct full title of al-Ash'ari's work and its unbroken chain of attribution to him, supported by early sources, later scholars, and even adversaries.

Commentary
This passage continues the reasons for re-editing by mentioning the efforts of Dr. Hamuda Gharaba. Gharaba corrected many errors from McCarthy's edition and studied its defects thoroughly. The editor then describes the British Library manuscript (Or. 3091), which is a hand-copied version of the sole Beirut manuscript. The Austrian orientalist Alfred von Kremer noted that this copy was likely made by a Christian boy untrained in Arabic, leading to numerous errors, including the alteration of Quranic verses—for example, changing 'فَلَبِثَ فِي السِّجْنِ بِضْعَ سِنِينَ' (So he remained in prison several years) to 'أيام' (days). Additional mistakes came from poor reading, making this copy a distorted version of the original. This explains why a new critical edition was necessary.
