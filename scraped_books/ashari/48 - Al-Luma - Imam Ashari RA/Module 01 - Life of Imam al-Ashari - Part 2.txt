I clung to his eyelashes for the characteristics (4) of his manners, and I competed in his friendship for the precious gems of his attributes. I stayed with him for a time, benefiting from him daily a fresh knowledge, and repelling from myself the doubt of the Mu'tazila. Then I found, despite the height of his rank and the enormity of his status, he would straighten his crookedness from the earning of his hand, by adopting the trade of drugs as a livelihood (1), and being content with it as a life, avoiding doubts, preserving passions, being pleased with sufficiency, and preferring chastity. End.

Commentary
This passage is the narrator's testimonial after observing the author firsthand. The narrator, having heard about the author's reputation, now sees his character and confirms that the reality exceeds the reports. He uses metaphors of gold and brass to show the author's words are pure and others are base. He then describes the author's virtues: piety, trustworthiness, and being the one indicated by scripture to renew the religion. The narrator expresses his deep attachment and admiration. Finally, he notes a striking fact: despite the author's high rank, he engaged in the humble trade of a pharmacist (selling drugs) to earn a lawful living, avoiding doubtful matters and preferring simplicity and chastity. This illustrates the author's God-consciousness and detachment from worldly status.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Engaging in a humble trade like pharmacy diminishes a scholar's status.
Correction: The narrator presents it as a virtue, showing that true piety includes earning lawfully and avoiding doubts.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?

?

?
Are you truly content with sufficiency (kafaf) and chastity (afaf), or do you secretly yearn for more comfort and status? Ask Allah to make you content, and name one luxury you will forego this month to train your soul.
