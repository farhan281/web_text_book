The Philosophers' Argument Against Bodily Resurrection
Ghazali presents the philosophers' argument against bodily resurrection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' key objection to bodily resurrection and why Ghazali refutes it.

Core Idea
The philosophers claim bodily resurrection is impossible and morally repulsive.

In this passage from the Incoherence of the Philosophers, Ghazali presents the arguments of the falasifa who deny physical resurrection. They argue that descriptions of Paradise and Hell are merely symbolic for common people. Their main objection is that the return of the soul to the same body is impossible because the body disintegrates, and even if possible, it would lead to absurdities like resurrecting amputees with defects.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the passage is part of Ghazali's refutation, so he first presents the opposing view.
2
Identify the philosophers' claim
The philosophers claim that descriptions of the afterlife are merely examples for common people to understand spiritual realities. Find where this is stated.
3
Understand the impossibility argument
They argue that the body disintegrates into dust, water, vapor, and air, mixing irretrievably with the elements. This makes reassembling the same body impossible.
4
See the dilemma
Even if God could reassemble the body, a dilemma arises: either He gathers only the parts present at death, resulting in a defective body (e.g., amputee), which is repulsive, or He gathers different parts, which would not be the same body.
5
Reflect on Ghazali's purpose
Ask yourself why Ghazali includes this argument. It sets up his refutation, which we will study later.

Commentary
This passage presents the philosophers' argument against bodily resurrection as quoted by Ghazali. First, the title of the 20th discussion indicates that the philosophers claim that all descriptions of physical afterlife are merely examples for common people to understand spiritual realities. Then they argue that the return of the soul to the same body is impossible because the body disintegrates into dust, water, vapor, air, and mixes irretrievably with the elements. Even if one assumes it is possible by God's power, a dilemma arises: if God gathers exactly the same parts, then a person who was amputee or deficient at death would be resurrected with those defects, which is repulsive especially for paradise dwellers; if He gathers only perfect parts, it is not the same body. The argument aims to show that bodily resurrection as commonly understood is either impossible or leads to absurdities.

Ghazali's Critique of the Philosophers' Claim that the Heavens Are Animate
Ghazali examines the philosophers' claim that the heavens are animate and begins to critique their rational proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali separates the question of possibility from rational proof, and why he argues the philosophers' proof for an animate heaven yields only probability, not certainty.

Core Idea
Possibility does not imply rational proof; the heavens may be animate, but reason cannot prove it.

In this passage from the Tahafut al-Falasifa, Ghazali takes on the philosophers' claim that the heavens are living beings that worship God through their circular motion. He begins by affirming that such a claim is not impossible—after all, God can create life in any body. But his real target is not the possibility; it is whether reason can demonstrate it. Ghazali insists that only prophets can know such truths through revelation. Let's read his analysis of the philosophers' attempted rational proof.

Commentary
This passage introduces Ghazali's critique of the philosophers' claim that the heavens are animate and obedient to God. He begins by stating the philosophers' position: the heaven is an animal with a soul that moves its body by will for worship. Ghazali then acknowledges that this claim is not impossible, since God can create life in any body regardless of size or shape. However, Ghazali's main point is that the philosophers cannot prove this claim by rational means; it can only be known through prophecy. He then examines their attempted proof: they offer two premises—that the heaven is moving (sensory) and that every moving body has a mover (rational). From this they conclude the mover is either internal (nature) or external (will). The text cuts off at this point, but the intended argument is that the philosophers' proof only yields probability, not certainty, and that their proof is flawed. The passage demonstrates Ghazali's method of separating what is possible from what is rationally demonstrable.

Commentary
This passage introduces the sixteenth problem (masʾala) of the Tahāfut, in which al-Ghazālī sets out to refute the philosophers' claim concerning the souls of the heavens and the Preserved Tablet. The passage first states the philosophers' position: the souls of the heavens are aware of all particular events happening in the world, and the 'Preserved Tablet' (al-lawḥ al-maḥfūẓ) refers to these souls. The impression of the particulars in these souls is analogous to the memory faculty in the human brain; it is not a physical tablet with writing. The philosophers argue that if it were a physical tablet, the infinite number of particulars would require an infinite tablet, which is impossible for a body. Al-Ghazālī then reports that the philosophers identify the celestial angels with the souls of the heavens, and the cherubim (karūbiyyīn) with the abstract intellects (al-ʿuqūl al-mujarrada) — self-subsisting, non-spatial substances. These abstract intellects are the bestowers of the particular forms, and the celestial souls are the recipients; thus the intellects are more noble. This leads them to interpret the Quranic 'Pen' (qalam) as the bestower (the abstract intellects) and the 'Tablet' as the recipient (the celestial souls). Finally, al-Ghazālī notes that this problem is distinct from the previous one: the earlier claim that the heaven is an animate being moving for a purpose is possible, but this problem involves affirming that a creature has knowledge of infinite particulars, which is the real point of contention. The passage ends mid-sentence, suggesting that the refutation will follow.

Al-Ghazali said: The knowledge of God Most High is not related to His known things in the manner of the knowledge that belongs to creatures.
Source pages p. 357

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer argument. The philosophers claim that prophets, due to their strong souls, can see in wakefulness what others only see in dreams. Ghazali responds by asking how they can deny someone who says the prophet knows the unseen directly from God (ibtida') without mediation, and similarly the dreamer is shown by God directly. Then he poses a logical impossibility: how can a single created soul contain infinite detailed knowledge at one moment without succession? He concludes that whoever does not see the impossibility should despair of his intellect. Finally, he distinguishes God's knowledge from created knowledge, implying that infinite knowledge is exclusive to God. Thus, the philosophers' claim is refuted both by argument from direct divine action and by the impossibility of infinite content in a finite created soul.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That created beings can have infinite knowledge.
Correction: Ghazali denies this, saying it is impossible for a finite soul.

Key Takeaway
A created soul cannot simultaneously possess infinite particular knowledge; such knowledge belongs only to Allah.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Have you ever assumed that your own understanding of a person, a situation, or even a religious text is complete—that you can grasp all its details? How does Ghazali’s argument about the impossibility of infinite knowledge in a created soul humble that assumption right now? What will you do the next time you catch yourself thinking you have 'figured it all out'?
?
When you make du'a, do you genuinely believe that Allah’s knowledge is entirely different from yours—that He knows every detail simultaneously without effort, while you struggle to remember even a few things? Or do you unconsciously imagine Him as a bigger version of your own mind? Let this passage correct your heart: admit that you cannot truly conceive of His knowledge, and ask Him to increase your awe of Him.
?
Is there a matter in your life—a worry, a plan, a relationship—that you have been trying to exhaustively analyze and control, as if you could foresee every outcome? If you truly accepted that such infinite knowledge is impossible for you, would you not finally release that burden to the One whose knowledge is truly infinite? What is one concrete thing you will hand over to Allah today because you admit you cannot know it all?
