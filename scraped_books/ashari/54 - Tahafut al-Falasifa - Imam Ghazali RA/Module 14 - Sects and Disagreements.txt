Ghazali's Rejection of the Ta'limite Infallible Imam
Describes Ghazali's turn to the Ta'limites and his subsequent refutation of their doctrine.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Ghazali rejected the Ta'limite doctrine of an infallible imam despite agreeing with them on the fallibility of reason.

Core Idea
Reason is fallible, but the Ta'limite 'infallible imam' does not exist.

Al-Ghazali, after his critique of the philosophers, found himself in agreement with the Ta'limites on one point: reason cannot be trusted absolutely. Yet he could not accept their solution—an infallible imam. In this passage, we follow his search and ultimate rejection, and glimpse his turn toward Sufism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the point of agreement
Notice that Ghazali agrees with the Ta'limites that reason is not immune to error. This sets the stage for their shared starting point.
2
Examine the proposed solution
The Ta'limites claim that certain religious knowledge comes only from an infallible imam. See how Ghazali first praises this idea but then questions its reality.
3
Follow Ghazali's investigation
Ghazali searches for this imam and finds no evidence for his existence. Conclude that the Ta'limite doctrine is baseless, leading him to return to his own path.
The footnote indicates Ghazali later turned to Sufism, not that he abandoned reason entirely.

Al-Ghazali turned his face towards the Ta'limites, who say: 'Reason is not immune from error, so it is not correct to take religious truths from it.' And to this judgment al-Ghazali arrived when he examined the philosophers; so they are in this point in agreement. Through what then do they take the matters of religion in their certain garb? They take them from the infallible imam who receives from God through the prophet. How beloved is this imam and what comes through his path! But where is that imam? Al-Ghazali searched for him long and did not find him, and it became clear that they are deluded concerning him, and that this imam has no reality in external existence. So he turned back and returned, after having authored books against them, in which he hurt them with critique and refutation, as he says. (And it does not follow from this that al-Ghazali discarded reason, for perhaps he submitted it to a means that would bring him to the truth, as happened to him with the method of the Sufis.)

Commentary
This passage is a historical and argumentative account of Ghazali's encounter with the Ta'limites (Batinis). It begins by stating that the Ta'limites argue that reason is fallible, so religious truths cannot be known through it. Ghazali, after testing the philosophers, reached the same conclusion – on this point they agree. The passage then poses a question: if reason is insufficient, how do they obtain certain religious knowledge? The answer is through an infallible imam who receives guidance from God via the Prophet. The passage expresses admiration for this concept but then challenges its reality: Ghazali searched for such an imam and found none; he concluded that the Ta'limites are deluded and the imam has no external existence. Consequently, Ghazali turned away and wrote refutations against them. The footnote adds that this does not mean Ghazali abandoned reason entirely, but rather that he later subjected it to the method of the Sufis. The passage follows a dialectical pattern: point of agreement, proposed solution, empirical investigation, rejection, and return.

Ghazali's Critique of Blind Imitation
Ghazali laments those who reject Islam through blind imitation of philosophers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's argument that the disbelief of his contemporaries is rooted in unreflective imitation of prestigious philosophers, not in reasoned investigation.

Core Idea
Blind imitation of philosophers is no better than blind imitation of ancestral religion.

In the opening of the Tahafut, Ghazali sets the stage by describing a group who, considering themselves intellectually superior, have cast off Islamic duties. He argues that their rejection of religion stems not from genuine inquiry but from hearing the grand names of ancient philosophers and uncritically adopting their rejection of revealed laws. This passage establishes the psychological and social critique that underpins his entire work.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening prayer
Begin by reading the elaborate prayer. Notice how Ghazali asks for guidance and protection from error, setting a spiritual tone before the critique.
The prayer reflects Ghazali's concern for true guidance versus mere imitation.
2
Identify the group criticized
Ghazali describes a group who pride themselves on intelligence, reject Islamic worship and rites, and follow a band that turns people from God's path. Identify their key traits.
3
Understand the basis of their disbelief
Ghazali asserts that their disbelief is not based on rational proof but on habitual imitation (taqlīd) similar to Jews and Christians, or on being deceived by doubts and fantasies.
4
Analyze the role of philosophers' names
The real source is hearing names like Socrates and Aristotle, and the praise of their followers. This impresses them and matches their inclinations, leading them to adopt disbelief to appear elite.
5
Reflect on Ghazali's conclusion
Ghazali contrasts such 'cleverness' with the simplicity of common folk, who are safer because they do not seek to appear clever by imitating the misguided.
He concludes that moving from one imitation to another is folly.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of Ghazali's Tahafut al-Falasifa. It begins with an elaborate prayer beseeching God for guidance, protection from error, and the ultimate bliss of Paradise, followed by blessings on the Prophet. Then Ghazali states the purpose of his work: he addresses a group who consider themselves superior in intelligence and refinement, having rejected Islamic obligations and rites. They have cast off the yoke of religion by following a band (the philosophers) who turn people from God's path. Ghazali asserts that their disbelief is not based on rational proof but on blind imitation (taqlīd) similar to that of Jews and Christians—inherited from upbringing and ancestors—or on being deceived by specious doubts and embellished fantasies, as happened with other heretical sects. He identifies the real source of their disbelief: hearing grandiose names like Socrates, Hippocrates, Plato, and Aristotle, and the praises of their followers lauding their intellects, foundations, and sciences (geometry, logic, physics, metaphysics), and the claim that these philosophers deny all revealed laws and consider them fabricated tricks. This impressed them and matched their own inclinations, so they proudly adopted disbelief to be part of the elite, distinguishing themselves from the masses and abandoning ancestral religion. They mistakenly think that departing from imitation of truth to embrace imitation of falsehood is cleverness, but it is merely moving from one imitation to another, which is folly. Ghazali then declares that no rank is lower than someone who abandons the truth he followed by imitation and rushes to accept falsehood without verification. He adds that the simple common folk are safer from this abyss because they lack the desire to appear clever by imitating the misguided. Thus simplicity is closer to salvation than a truncated cleverness, and blindness (literal following) is safer than a distorted insight.

The Foolish Imitation of Philosophers
Ghazali describes the cause of deviation among those who imitate philosophers and reject religion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's critique of those who abandon religious beliefs by blindly imitating philosophers, and why he considers such imitation worse than simple faith.

Core Idea
Blind imitation of philosophers is no better than imitation of ancestors — both are folly.

This passage from Ghazali's Tahafut al-Falasifa diagnoses a common intellectual pitfall: people who reject religion by imitating famous philosophers, thinking this shows sophistication. Ghazali compares their behavior to a mirage and warns that it is merely exchanging one form of blind imitation for another. As you read, notice how he contrasts the cunning of the imitators with the simple ignorance of the common folk.

Think about what Ghazali is teaching about truth and imitation. He warns against abandoning truth for falsehood simply out of pride or desire to be part of an elite.

Commentary
This passage describes the cause of deviation for those who reject religion by imitating philosophers. Ghazali begins by comparing them to a mirage: those who investigate creeds can fall into error. He states that the source of their disbelief is merely hearing grand names of famous philosophers like Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle, along with exaggerated descriptions of their intellect and sciences, and reports that they denied religious laws. When this information agrees with their disposition, they embrace disbelief to align themselves with the supposed elite, to rise above common people, and to avoid following their fathers' religions. They think this shows cunning, but Ghazali points out that moving from one imitation (of ancestors) to another imitation (of philosophers) is folly. He then contrasts the common people's simplicity, which keeps them from this abyss; their stupidity is safer than a truncated intelligence or diseased insight.

Commentary
This passage introduces a new mas'ala (question) in which Ghazali aims to expose the deception of the philosophers when they claim that God is the agent and maker of the world. The Philosophers' Dilemma on God's Intellectuality
Ghazali presents a dilemma: shared intellectuality forces either a broken principle or an unacceptable consequence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali argues that the philosophers' claim that God is an intellect leads to a logical dead end.

Core Idea
If God and the first intellect share intellectuality, their own rules force a contradiction.

Ghazali here turns the philosophers' own principles against them. He shows that their claim that God is an intellect (abstracted from matter) and that the first created intellect also is an intellect leads to an unavoidable dilemma. This passage is a classic example of internal critique in Islamic philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Understand the philosophers' claim
The philosophers assert that the First (God) is an intellect of Himself and of others, being abstracted from matter. Also the first created intellect shares this quality.
This is the starting point of the dilemma.
2
Identify the shared attribute
Both the First and the first intellect are said to be intellects. Intellectuality is therefore a common attribute.
3
Grasp the dilemma
Ghazali points out that either the philosophers must break their rule that a shared attribute is accidental (since the First and the first intellect differ in species), or they must admit that intellectuality is not constitutive of the essence of the First. Both are impossible for them.
This is the core logical trap.
4
See why both options are impossible
Breaking the rule undermines their entire system; accepting intellectuality as non-constitutive contradicts their view that the First is intellect by essence.
Thus their position is internally inconsistent.

Commentary
This passage presents a dilemma that Ghazali presses against the philosophers. The philosophers claim that the First (God) is an intellect, and that He knows Himself and knows others. They also hold that the first created intellect (the first effect) is an intellect abstracted from matter, sharing in the same reality of intellectuality. Ghazali points out that if they share in intellectuality, then intellectuality is a common property. But if it is common, it must be either essential (constitutive) or accidental to each. If it is essential, then the First and the first effect would share the same essence, which is not the case (their species differ). If it is accidental, then intellectuality would be an accident in the First, which the philosophers reject because they claim the First is pure act and has no accidents. Thus they are forced either to break their own rule (that what is common is accidental) or to admit that intellectuality is not part of the essence (which contradicts their view that the First is intellect by essence). Both outcomes are impossible for them, so their position is contradictory.

The Pre-Eternity of the Genus and Ibn Taymiyya on the Throne
Al-Dawani, Ibn Taymiyya, and Muhammad Abduh on pre-eternity of genus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the concept of 'pre-eternity of the genus' and how Ibn Taymiyya applied it to the Throne to reconcile his literalist interpretation with God's eternity.

Core Idea
The Throne is eternal in kind, not in essence.

The previous passage highlighted the general difficulty. Now we turn to specific scholarly positions that attempted to resolve tension between scriptural literalism and philosophical reasoning. Al-Dawani reports that some traditionists held the pre-eternity of the genus (qidam al-jins), and Ibn Taymiyya is said to have applied this to the Throne to avoid making the Throne co-eternal with God. Muhammad Abduh explains this concept. Let's unpack it.

Guided Path
1
Read the second part of the passage
Read from 'Moreover, some scholars...' to the end. Focus on the definition of 'pre-eternity of the genus'.
2
Understand the concept
What does it mean for something to be 'eternal in kind'? Use Muhammad Abduh's explanation.
It means there is always an individual of that kind existing, but not the same individual eternally.
3
Apply to Ibn Taymiyya's position
How does Ibn Taymiyya use this concept to defend his literalist view of God sitting on the Throne?
He argues that God constantly destroys and creates thrones, so His sitting is an eternal process.

Commentary
This passage is an extended footnote discussing the profound difficulty of the issue of the world's eternity. It begins by characterizing the issue as extremely perplexing, citing Galen's honest admission of ignorance. Imam al-Razi praises Galen's fairness. Then it quotes al-Ghazali himself, who advises caution: a speculative theologian (nāẓir) should not hastily declare another a disbeliever for a perceived error in what he considers a demonstrative proof, because such matters are not easy. The note then reports that some well-grounded scholars have held a view resembling the philosophers' position, specifically the pre-eternity of the genus (qidam al-jins) – the idea that there has never been a time without some individual of the world existing. Al-Dawani is cited as saying that some later traditionists (muhaddithīn) held this, and Ibn Taymiyya applied it to the Throne. Muhammad Abduh then explains: this means the world by genus is pre-eternal – there is always some originated thing in every time. Some traditionists who take the literal outward meanings of hadith adopted this view because they saw indications in the texts. The note elaborates on Ibn Taymiyya's position: as a Hanbali literalist, he believed God literally sits on the Throne. When it was pointed out that this would make the Throne eternal (since God is eternal), which contradicts his doctrine that only God is eternal, he responded by saying the Throne is eternal in kind (qadīm bil-naw‘) – meaning God constantly destroys and replaces thrones, so His sitting is a permanent, unending state.

Ghazali's Restriction of Takfir to Three Issues
Ghazali concludes which philosophical positions constitute kufr and discusses takfir of ahl al-bid'a.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Imam Ghazali restricts declaring philosophers unbelievers to only three specific issues, and he distinguishes these from other philosophical views that are shared with some Muslim sects.

Core Idea
Ghazali limits takfir to three core issues, not all philosophical views.

In this concluding passage from his Tahafut, Ghazali clarifies his position on takfir. He had identified three issues on which the philosophers contradict the clear teachings of Islam. Here, he emphasizes that only these three constitute kufr, while other philosophical positions on divine attributes and causality have parallels in Islamic sects like the Mu'tazila. He also briefly discusses takfir of ahl al-bid'a but sets it aside.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three issues
Read the passage and note that Ghazali mentions 'these three principles' as the unique grounds for takfir. Recognize that he refers to the eternity of the world, denial of God's knowledge of particulars, and denial of bodily resurrection.
These three issues were discussed earlier in the Tahafut.
2
Understand the contrast with other views
Ghazali says the philosophers' views on divine attributes and natural causality are close to those of the Mu'tazila and have been expressed by some Muslim groups. He is showing that only the three issues are unique to the philosophers.
3
Distinguish takfir of philosophers from takfir of ahl al-bid'a
Ghazali refrains from discussing general takfir of ahl al-bid'a, saying their reasoning is different from deliberate falsification. He keeps the focus on the three issues.
This shows his caution in applying takfir.
4
Grasp the conclusion
Recognize that Ghazali's approach is cautious and restricted to core creedal violations. He does not want to expand the scope of takfir beyond what is necessary.

Commentary
This passage concludes Ghazali's discussion of the three issues for which he declares the philosophers unbelievers. He first states that the prophets spoke metaphorically for the benefit of the common people, and that the philosophers' denial of the reality of these matters (e.g., bodily resurrection) is clear unbelief, not held by any Muslim sect. He then contrasts the philosophers' positions on divine attributes and natural causality, which he says are similar to the Mu'tazila and have been expressed by some Muslim groups. Only the three specific issues (which he has previously identified as the eternity of the world, denial of God's knowledge of particulars, and denial of bodily resurrection) are unique to the philosophers and necessitate takfir. He notes that those who generally declare ahl al-bid'a as unbelievers will also declare the philosophers unbelievers on all points, while those who are cautious restrict takfir to these three issues. Ghazali then states his own reluctance to delve into general takfir of ahl al-bid'a, emphasizing that the meaning arrived at by their reasoning is distinct from deliberate falsification. He mentions that the other two issues (likely the other points of disagreement with the philosophers beyond the three) do not involve falsification or interpretation. He ends by saying he will stick to the purpose of the book.

Mu'tazila view: annihilation via self-annihilating passing-away
Ghazali presents the Mu'tazila view and three objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila doctrine of annihilation via an independently subsisting 'passing-away' and the three objections raised against it.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila invented a self-annihilating entity to avoid infinite regress, but faced serious objections.

Ghazali begins by presenting the Mu'tazila interpretation of how God annihilates the world. They propose that God creates a passing-away (fana’) not in a locus, which then annihilates the world and itself. However, this view is fraught with logical difficulties, and three specific objections are leveled against it. Let us examine this position and its critiques.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazila view in Arabic
Read the Arabic passage describing the Mu'tazila concept of fana' and how it annihilates the world and itself.
Notice the phrase 'ينعدم الفناء بنفسه' (the fana' becomes non-existent by itself).
2
Understand the concept of fana'
The Mu'tazila say fana' is created by God not in a locus (لا في محل). It annihilates the world and then annihilates itself to avoid infinite regress of fana's.
3
First objection: fana' is not a real entity
The first objection states that fana' is not a thing that exists (ليس شيئاً يوجد), so it cannot be created by God.
This challenges the very existence of fana'.
4
Second and third objections: subsistence and partial annihilation
The second objection questions where fana' subsists: if in the world, existence and non-existence coexist; if in itself, it is not opposite to existence. The third objection: if fana' has no locus, it relates equally to all parts, so God cannot annihilate only part of the world.

And the theologians went into sects in interpreting the meaning of the annihilation of the world: The Mu'tazila hold that its annihilation is by a passing-away (fana') that God creates not in a locus, by which the world becomes non-existent, and the passing-away becomes non-existent by itself, so that it does not need another passing-away, and the other passing-away [needs] another passing-away, and so on. And objection was raised against the Mu'tazila's doctrine in aspects: The first aspect of objection: That passing-away is not a thing that exists so that God creates it. The second aspect: In what does the passing-away subsist? If it subsists in the world, then existence and passing-away would coexist in the world, which is impossible by intuition; and if it subsists in itself, then it would not be an opposite of the world's existence. The third aspect: That this doctrine leads to God not being able to annihilate some of the world without some; because if passing-away does not subsist in a locus, its relation to the entire world is equal. As for the Karrāmiyya, they hold that annihilation is a term for something existent that subsists in God's essence, so the world becomes non-existent through it. It was said: This leads to God's essence being a locus for accidents. And it is also a departure from the rational; because nothing is conceivable from bringing-into-existence except something existent, then will and power that subsist in the one who brings into existence; so affirming something else beyond them has no justification. As for some Ash'aris, they say: Accidents are not lasting, but they are renewed; so God withholds their renewal, so they perish; and when the accidents perish, the substances perish.

Commentary
This passage classifies the interpretations of the mutakallimun (theologians) regarding how God annihilates the world. Three schools are presented: 1) The Mu'tazila: God creates a passing-away (فناء) not in a locus, through which the world becomes non-existent, and this passing-away annihilates itself to avoid an infinite regress. Three objections are raised: first, passing-away is not a real entity to be created; second, if it subsists in the world, existence and passing-away would coexist, which is impossible, and if it subsists in itself, it would not be opposite to the world's existence; third, if it does not subsist in a locus, it applies equally to all parts, so God could not annihilate only part of the world. 2) The Karrāmiyya: annihilation is a thing subsisting in God's essence. This is rejected because it would make God's essence a locus for accidents and is rationally unwarranted. 3) Some Ash'aris: Accidents are not permanent but are renewed; God simply stops renewing them, so they perish, and when accidents are gone, the substances (جواهر) also perish (since substances require accidents). This avoids the problems of the other two views.

Karrāmiyya view: annihilation in God's essence
Ghazali presents the Karrāmiyya view and two objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Karrāmiyya view that annihilation is a thing subsisting in God's essence and why it was rejected.

Core Idea
The Karrāmiyya placed annihilation in God's essence, but that made God a locus for accidents.

After the Mu'tazila, Ghazali presents the Karrāmiyya view, which holds that annihilation is an entity that subsists in God Himself. This view is quickly rejected on two grounds: it makes God's essence a locus for accidents, and it is rationally unjustified. Let us see why.

Guided Path
1
Read the Karrāmiyya position
Read the Arabic passage describing the Karrāmiyya view that annihilation is something existent subsisting in God's essence.

2
Understand the first objection: God as locus
The first objection states that this would make God's essence a locus for accidents (محل للحوادث), which is unacceptable.
3
Understand the second objection: rational justification
The second objection argues that rationally, bringing into existence requires only an existent, will, and power. Affirming an additional entity like annihilation has no justification.
