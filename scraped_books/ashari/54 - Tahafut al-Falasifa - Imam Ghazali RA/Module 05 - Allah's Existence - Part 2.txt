The Logical Flaw in the Possibility Proof
Ghazali exposes the logical flaw using the philosophers' own definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical contradiction in the philosophers' possibility proof when each cause is individually possible but the whole is claimed not possible.

Core Idea
If every part has a cause, the whole must also have a cause.

Ghazali begins by accepting the philosophers' own definitions of necessary and possible. He then points out a simple inconsistency: if each individual cause in the chain is possible (has a cause), then the entire chain must also be possible. Claiming the whole has no cause while each part does is a contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Read the definitions
Read the passage carefully and note how Ghazali defines 'necessary' and 'possible' based on the philosophers' own terms.
The definition of 'possible' includes having a cause additional to its essence.
2
Identify the contradiction
Ghazali says 'Each one is possible' but 'the whole is not possible'. Ask yourself: if every part has a cause, can the whole lack a cause?
This is the core logical flaw.
3
Reflect on the implication
Consider why this contradiction undermines the philosophers' attempt to prove a necessary being through the possibility argument.
