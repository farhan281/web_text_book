The Purpose of Kalam and Its Limitation
Ghazali contrasts kalam's defensive aim with his own aim of certain knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's distinction between the defensive purpose of kalam and his own quest for certainty, and why he found kalam insufficient.

Core Idea
Kalam defends faith; it does not create it.

In this passage from the Tahafut, Ghazali clarifies what kalam is meant to do—and what it cannot do. He begins by stating the accepted purpose of kalam: to protect the creed of a Muslim raised in Islam from doubts. But then he reveals his own dissatisfaction: kalam cannot produce the certainty he seeks, which is more akin to mathematical proof. This lesson unpacks that crucial distinction.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without overanalysis. Then read the translation. Notice how Ghazali first describes the purpose of kalam and then contrasts it with his own purpose.
The passage includes both editorial framing and direct quotes from Ghazali.
2
Identify the two purposes
Locate where the text explains the aim of kalam (preserving creed) and where it states Ghazali's aim (certainty like mathematics). Compare them.
The key phrase is 'هذا هو مقصود علم الكلام؛ أما مقصود الغزالي فهو...'.
3
Understand why kalam falls short
Reflect on Ghazali's claim that kalam is 'of little benefit' for someone who accepts only necessary truths. Why does he say this? Consider the method of kalam—using weak premises from opponents.
Ghazali wanted a cure for his bewilderment; kalam only preserved what he already had.

As for the purpose of the science of kalam – as al-Ghazali narrated – it is to preserve the creed for a person raised a Muslim, who took his creed from the Book and the Sunna, from the doubts that are raised against it and the attacks directed at it. As for kalam creating the creed of Islam in a person who grew up devoid of it, not believing in it, that is what the science of kalam did not attempt and was not its task. Its task required it to take its premises from those attackers and doubters in order to hold them accountable for the implications of their own assumptions, and those premises are weak and flimsy. He said: Most of their engagement – meaning the theologians – was in extracting contradictions of opponents and holding them accountable for the implications of their assumptions. This is the purpose of kalam. As for al-Ghazali's purpose, it is to attain religious truth with a certainty supported by reason, such that it reaches the rank of mathematical science in precision and clarity. How different the two purposes are! For this reason, al-Ghazali says, referring to kalam: It is of little benefit for someone who accepts nothing but necessities at all. So kalam was not sufficient for him, nor a cure for the illness I was complaining of. It did not yield what would completely erase the darkness of bewilderment regarding the differences among people, nor did it remove.

Commentary
This passage distinguishes between the purpose of kalam and Ghazali's personal objective. It begins by stating the accepted purpose of kalam: it is a defensive science aimed at preserving the faith of a person who was already raised as a Muslim and accepted the Quran and Sunna. It protects against doubts and objections by using the assumptions of opponents to refute them. However, kalam does not attempt to create faith from nothing in a person who has no prior belief. Its method is to take premises from the skeptics and show their implications, but these premises themselves are weak. The editor then contrasts Ghazali's goal: to attain religious truth with a certainty that rivals mathematics in clarity and precision. Ghazali finds kalam insufficient because it does not produce certainty for someone who, like him, doubts everything except self-evident truths. The direct quote from Ghazali states that kalam is of little benefit for such a person and does not cure his bewilderment about the differences among sects. Thus, kalam is accepted for its limited purpose but rejected as a path to Ghazali's desired certainty.

The First Meaning of Madhhab: Inherited Tradition and Fanaticism
Defines the first meaning of madhhab as inherited tradition and fanaticism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first meaning of madhhab as inherited tradition and how it leads to fanatical partisanship.

Core Idea
Madhhab as inherited tradition breeds tribal-like fanaticism.

In this passage, Ghazali identifies the first sense of 'madhhab' as the uncritical adherence to the ways of one's ancestors, teachers, and homeland. This understanding is crucial for recognizing how school loyalties often mirror tribal allegiances, setting the stage for his later distinctions.

3

This fanaticism is contrasted with the other meanings.
4
Reflect on the implication

This connects to the overall purpose of distinguishing true conviction from blind adherence.

فأما المذهب بالمعنى الأول ، فهو نمط الآباء والأجداد ، ومذهب المعلم ومذهب أهل البلد الذى فيه النشوء ، وذلك يختلف بالبلاد والأقطار ، ويختلف بالمعلمين . فمن ولد فى بلد المعتزلة ، أو الأشعرية ، أو الشفعوية ، أو الحنفية ، نفسه منذ صباه ، التعصب له ، والذب عنه ، والذم لما سواه . فيقال هو أشعرى المذهب ، أو معتزلى ، أو شفعوى ، أو حنفى ؛ ومعناه أنه له ، أى ينصر عصابة المتظاهرين بالموالاة ، و يجرى ذلك مجرى تناصر القبيلة بعضهم لبعض . يتعصب المذهب الثاني : ما ينطق به في الإرشاد والتعليم، لمن جاء مستفيداً مسترشداً . وهذا لا يتعين على وجه واحد ، بل يختلف بحسب المسترشد ؛ فيناظر كل مسترشد بما يحتمله فهمه ، فإن وقع له مسترشد تركى ، أو هندى ، أو رجل بليد جلف الطبع ، وعلم أنه لو ذكر له أن الله تعالى ليس ذاته في مكان ، وأنه ليس داخل العالم ولا خارجه ، ولا متصلا بالعالم ، ولا منفصلا عنه ؛ لم يلبث أن ينكر وجود الله تعالى ، ويكذب به ، فينبغى أن يقرر عنده أن الله تعالى على العرش وأنه يرضيه عبادة خلقه ، ويفرح بهم ، و يثيبهم عوضاً وجزاء . و إن احتمل أن يذكر له ما هو الحق المبين ، يكشف له . فالمذهب بهذا الاعتبار يتغير ويختلف ويكون مع كل واحد على ما يحتمله فهمه . المذهب الثالث : ما يعتقده الرجل سراً بينه وبين الله عز وجل ، لا يطلع عليه غير الله تعالى ، ولا يذكره إلا مع من هو شريكه في الاطلاع على ما اطلع عليه ، أو بلغ رتبة يقبل الاطلاع عليه ويفهمه ، وذلك بأن يكون مسترشداً ذكياً . إلى آخر ما ذكره من الشروط في النص السابق . ومن هذا يظهر أن الغزالى يقول عن الله مثلا للبليد ما لا يقوله للذكى يصور الحقيقة صوراً مختلفة ، على حسب تفاوت الاستعدادات والمدارك
As for the madhhab in the first sense, it is the way of the fathers and forefathers, and the school of the teacher, and the school of the country in which one grows up. That differs by countries and regions, and differs by teachers. So whoever is born in a country of the Mu'tazila, or Ash'arites, or Shafi'is, or Hanafis, his self from childhood becomes fanatical for it, and defends it, and condemns what is other than it. So it is said he is Ash'ari in madhhab, or Mu'tazili, or Shafi'i, or Hanafi; and its meaning is that he belongs to it, i.e. he supports the party of those who outwardly profess allegiance, and that runs in the manner of tribal mutual support. He becomes fanatical. The second madhhab: what is spoken in guidance and teaching to one who comes seeking benefit and guidance. This is not fixed in one way, rather it varies according to the seeker; so one debates each seeker according to what his understanding can bear. For if he encounters a Turkish seeker, or an Indian, or a dull-witted coarse-natured man, and he knows that if he were to mention to him that God Almighty is not in a place, and that He is neither inside the world nor outside it, nor connected to the world nor disconnected from it, he would not delay in denying the existence of God Almighty and disbelieving in Him, so he should establish in his mind that God Almighty is upon the Throne, and that worship of creation pleases Him, and He delights in them, and rewards them as compensation and recompense. And if it is possible that what is the clear truth is mentioned to him, he unveils it. So the madhhab in this consideration changes and differs, and is with each one according to what his understanding can bear. The third madhhab: what a man believes secretly between himself and God Almighty, no one knows it except God Almighty, and he does not mention it except to one who is his partner in having access to what he has accessed, or has reached a rank that accepts access to it and understands it, and that is by being an intelligent seeker. ...to the end of what he mentioned of conditions in the previous text. And from this it appears that Ghazali says about God, for example, to the dull-witted what he does not say to the intelligent; he depicts the truth in different pictures, according to the disparity of capacities and perceptions.
Source pages p. 62

Commentary
This passage is a classification: it defines three distinct meanings of the term 'madhhab' (school/method) as used by Ghazali (or the commentator). The author begins by explaining the first meaning: madhhab as the inherited tradition of one's ancestors, teachers, and country. This leads to fanatical partisanship (ta'assub), similar to tribal loyalty. The second meaning is adaptive teaching: a teacher or debater adjusts his presentation according to the capacity of the seeker. An example is given: for a dull person, one should not mention sophisticated theological concepts about God's location, but rather affirm that God is on the Throne in a way the seeker can accept. This meaning is flexible and varies with each individual. The third meaning is the secret personal conviction that a person holds between himself and God, which he only shares with someone of equal understanding or intelligence. After listing these three meanings, the text notes that this explains why Ghazali would say different things about God to different people, tailoring the depiction of truth to their capacities. The passage thus shows Ghazali's pedagogical principle of accommodation.

The Second Meaning of Madhhab: Adaptive Teaching
Defines the second meaning of madhhab as adaptive teaching depending on the seeker's capacity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second meaning of madhhab as pedagogical accommodation, where teaching is tailored to the listener's capacity.

Core Idea
Madhhab as teaching adapts truth to the seeker's understanding.

Ghazali now presents a radically different sense of madhhab: the method of instruction that varies with the student's intellectual level. This reveals his pedagogical principle of gradualism and protection of faith.

This meaning is about method, not fixed doctrine.

The deepest madhhab is the secret truth between the believer and God.

Ghazali presents the philosophers' thesis and their distinction between essential genus and necessary accident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' claim that God has no definition and their distinction between essential genus and universal necessary accident.

Core Idea
Definition requires genus and differentia; God shares no genus, so He has no definition.

The philosophers argue that if God shared a genus with anything, He would be composite and hence dependent. In this passage, Ghazali presents their reasoning and clarifies the logical distinction between an essential genus—which enters into the quiddity of a thing—and a universal necessary accident—which accompanies the essence but is not part of it.

Guided Path
1
Read the thesis statement
Read the opening lines from 'مسألة [۷]' up to 'فلا حد له'. This states the philosophers' position: God cannot share a genus or differ by a differentia, thus no definition.
Notice the immediate link between genus, differentia, and definition.
2
Examine the anticipated objection
Read the part starting 'وزعموا : أن قول القائل' which presents an objection: God seems to share being existent, substance, and causality with the first effect. The philosophers respond that this is not generic sharing.
3
Learn the distinction between genus and necessary accident
Focus on the sentence 'فإن الجنس الذاتي...' and the example of man. Understand that an essential genus is part of the quiddity, while a necessary accident (like being born) is not.
This distinction is crucial for the entire argument.
4
Connect to God's uniqueness
Reflect on why this distinction matters: if God's existence and attributes are not part of a genus, then He is not subject to definition.
The philosophers will conclude this in later passages.

And they have agreed upon this, and built upon it that if He does not share with another in a generic sense, He is not distinguished from it by a specific sense, and thus He has no definition, for definition is composed of genus and differentia, and that which has no composition has no definition, and this is a type of composition. And there is a difference between genus and necessary accident in reality, even if they do not differ in universality, as is known in logic. For the essential genus is the universal that is said in answer to 'what is it?' and enters into the quiddity of the limited thing and is constitutive of its essence. So man's being alive entering into the quiddity of man – I mean animality – thus it is a genus; and his being born and created is a necessary accident, never separating from him, but not entering into the quiddity, even if it is a universal necessary accident. And this is known in logic with a knowledge that is not disputed. And they claimed: that existence never enters into the quiddities of things, but rather is added to quiddity, either as a necessary accident not separating like heaven, or as supervenient after non-existence like generated things. So sharing in existence is not sharing in genus. And as for sharing in His being a cause for something else, like other causes, it is a sharing in a necessary addition, which also does not enter into quiddity. For principality and existence neither one subsists in the essence, but rather they supervene upon the essence after the essence has subsisted by the parts of its quiddity. So sharing in it is nothing but a sharing in a universal necessary accident that follows the essence in its necessary attachment, not in a genus. Therefore things are defined only by the constitutives; if they are defined by necessary accidents, that would be a description for distinction, not for depicting the reality of the thing. So it is not said in the definition of triangle: it is that whose angles equal two right angles, even if that is a universal necessary accident for every triangle; rather it is said: it is a figure surrounded by three sides. And likewise sharing in being a substance: for the meaning of its being a substance is that it is an existent not in a subject; and 'the existent' is not a genus. For by adding a negative attribute – i.e., 'not in a subject' – it does not become a constitutive genus; rather, if an affirmative were added to it and it were said 'existent in a subject', it would not become a genus in the accident. And this is because one who knows substance by its definition which is like a description of it, i.e., 'it is an existent not in a subject', does not know its being existent, let alone know whether it is in a subject or not in a subject. Rather, the meaning of our saying in the description of substance: 'the existent not in a subject' (i.e., (1)), it is a certain reality which, when it exists, exists not in a subject; and we do not mean by it (2) that it is actually existent at the time of definition. So sharing in it is not sharing in genus. Rather, the sharing in the constitutives of quiddity is the sharing in genus which necessitates differentiation afterward by differentia. And the First has no quiddity other than the Necessary Existence. For the Necessary Existence is a nature, a reality, and a quiddity in itself, belonging to Him not to another. And when the necessity of existence belongs only to Him, no other shares with Him, so He is not distinguished from another by a specific differentia, and thus He has no definition. So this is an exposition of their doctrine.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of the philosophers' argument that the First (God) cannot share a genus with anything else and therefore has no definition. The passage begins by stating their thesis: that the First cannot share a genus with another or be distinguished by a differentia, and therefore has no definition because definition requires composition of genus and differentia. They then address a potential objection: someone might claim that the First shares with the first effect in being existent, substance, and a cause. The philosophers respond that such sharing is not in genus but in a universal necessary accident (lāzim 'āmm). They distinguish between an essential genus (jins dhātī) that enters into the quiddity and is constitutive, versus a necessary accident that supervenes on the already-constituted essence. They illustrate with examples: animality is a genus for man, but being born is a necessary accident. They then argue that existence itself is not a real genus because it does not enter into quiddities but is added to them. Similarly, being a cause is a relational attribute, not part of quiddity. They also discuss the definition of substance: 'existent not in a subject' is a description (rasm) using a negative, not a true definition. Therefore, sharing in substance is not generic. The conclusion is that only sharing in the constitutive parts of quiddity is generic sharing. Since the First has no quiddity other than Necessary Existence, and that is unique to Him, He shares nothing with others, has no differentia, and thus no definition.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of the philosophers' argument that the First (God) cannot share a genus with anything else and therefore has no definition. The passage begins by stating their thesis: that the First cannot share a genus with another or be distinguished by a differentia, and therefore has no definition because definition requires composition of genus and differentia. They then address a potential objection: someone might claim that the First shares with the first effect in being existent, substance, and a cause. The philosophers respond that such sharing is not in genus but in a universal necessary accident (lāzim 'āmm). They distinguish between an essential genus (jins dhātī) that enters into the quiddity and is constitutive, versus a necessary accident that supervenes on the already-constituted essence. They illustrate with examples: animality is a genus for man, but being born is a necessary accident. They then argue that existence itself is not a real genus because it does not enter into quiddities but is added to them. Similarly, being a cause is a relational attribute, not part of quiddity. They also discuss the definition of substance: 'existent not in a subject' is a description (rasm) using a negative, not a true definition. Therefore, sharing in substance is not generic. The conclusion is that only sharing in the constitutive parts of quiddity is generic sharing. Since the First has no quiddity other than Necessary Existence, and that is unique to Him, He shares nothing with others, has no differentia, and thus no definition.

The Three Philosophical Meanings of Bodily Resurrection
Classification of three possible meanings of bodily resurrection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three ways philosophers conceived of bodily resurrection and how each relates the human being, body, and soul.

Core Idea
The philosophers classified bodily resurrection into three models based on soul-body relation.

In this passage, the philosophers present a rational classification of possible meanings of bodily resurrection. Before engaging with theological critiques, they map out the conceptual space: either the human is reducible to the body, or consists of soul and body with the soul surviving death. Each division leads to a different understanding of what 'return to the body' means. This framing sets the stage for Imam al-Ghazali's subsequent refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the classification
Read the Arabic passage carefully. It enumerates three divisions. Notice the phrase 'ثلاثة أقسام' (three divisions). Then read the translation to grasp the basic meaning.
The Arabic has some textual uncertainty in the second division, but the overall structure is clear.
2
Understand each division's definition of the human
Each division defines what the human is (body alone, or soul + body). Identify how each leads to a different meaning of resurrection. The first division equates the human with the body; the second sees the human as soul and body; the third also sees soul and body but resurrection is return to a body (any body).
3

The third division allows the soul to return to a body different from the original. This is a broader possibility than the others. Consider how this impacts the philosophical debate on resurrecting the same person.

Commentary
This passage presents a classification of three possible understandings of bodily resurrection as argued by the philosophers. First, they delineate the possible relations between the human being, the body, and the soul. The first division identifies the human with the body alone, where life is an accident that ceases at death, and resurrection would be the re-creation of that life in the same body. The second division posits both an abstract soul and a body, with the soul surviving death; resurrection then means reassembling the dispersed body parts to reconnect them with the soul. The third division also has an abstract soul and a body, but resurrection is the soul's return to a body, whether the original or a different one. The Arabic text contains some corruption in the second division, but the intended classification is clear: three ways to conceive of resurrecting the same individual.
