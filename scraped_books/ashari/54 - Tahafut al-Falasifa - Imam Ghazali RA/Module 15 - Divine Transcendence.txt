Ghazali's Critique of Relativizing Impossibility
Ghazali critiques the relativization of impossibility to a subject.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's argument that not all impossibility is relative to a subject, using the case of a partner of God.

Core Idea
Impossibility of a divine partner is absolute, not subject-relative.

The philosophers argued that all impossibility is relative to a substance's nature. Ghazali counter-argues that the impossibility of God having a partner (sharik) has no substrate--it is absolute. He then dismantles the philosophers' attempted reduction of this impossibility to God's necessary uniqueness, showing their inconsistency.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation. Notice how Ghazali structures his critique.
Pay attention to the key terms like 'امتناع' and 'انفراد'.
2
Identify Ghazali's counterexample
Find the example of 'وجود شريك لله' (existence of a partner of God). Understand why Ghazali says there is no 'matter' (مادة) for this impossibility.
3
Follow the refutation of the philosophers' response
Ghazali anticipates their attempt to reduce impossibility to necessity of uniqueness (انفراد). He argues that on their own principles, God is not absolutely unique because the world exists. Then he distinguishes uniqueness from a peer versus from creatures.
The philosophers' move is to convert 'impossibility of partner' into 'necessity of uniqueness'.
4
Understand the 'trick' argument
Ghazali concludes that the philosophers' reduction is a 'حيلة' (trick). They artificially attribute possibility to God by rephrasing impossibility as necessity and adding uniqueness.
Reflect on why Ghazali considers this artificial.
5
Synthesize the theological lesson
Consider the implication: some impossibilities are absolute and rooted in the nature of God, not in a substrate. This preserves divine transcendence.

Impossibility Without a Substrate
Ghazali refutes the claim that impossibility always attaches to a substrate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali argues that impossibility does not always require a material substrate, using the example of a partner for God.

Core Idea
Logical impossibility does not require a physical substrate.

This passage from Ghazali's Tahafut challenges the philosophers' view that impossibility is always linked to a material substrate. Ghazali presents a powerful counterexample: the impossibility of a partner for God, which has no substrate at all. Through this, he exposes a weakness in their metaphysical framework.

Two Types of Multiplicity Negated from God

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first two ways multiplicity enters beings and why they are negated from the First Principle according to the philosophers.

Core Idea
Absolute unity requires negating both division by quantity and composition into matter and form.

In this passage from the Tahafut, al-Ghazali presents the philosophers' argument for God's absolute unity by enumerating the types of multiplicity that must be negated from Him. The first two types—division by quantity and division into distinct metaphysical components—are explained and ruled out. This lays the foundation for understanding divine simplicity.

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration of the first two of five aspects by which multiplicity enters into beings, which the philosophers negate from the First Principle (God). The author, Imam al-Ghazali, presents these as part of explaining the philosophers' doctrine. The first aspect is division in quantity, either actually (by cutting) or mentally (by imagining parts). A body is one only by virtue of a continuous connection that can cease, so it is divisible in the mind by quantity. This division is impossible for the First Principle because He is not a body. The second aspect is division into two different meanings without quantity, such as body being composed of matter (hayula) and form (sura). Though neither matter nor form can subsist independently, they are distinct in definition and reality. The combination produces a single body. This composition is also negated from God. Al-Ghazali then explains why God cannot be matter, form, or the combination. God cannot be matter because matter needs form to actualize. He cannot be form because form needs matter as a substrate. He cannot be the combination of both because that combination would be divisible by quantity and by meaning. The key point is that the Necessary Existent must be absolutely one, without any composition or potential for division.

Definition of Substance and Denial of Genus for the First
Ghazali explains the definition of substance and argues the First has no genus or species.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why 'existent' cannot be a genus and why the First (God) has no genus or species.

Core Idea
Existence is not a genus; God's necessary existence is unique.

In this passage, Ghazali clarifies the logical structure of definitions and genera. He shows that 'existent' is a common notion that does not constitute a genus, and then applies this principle to argue that the First (God) has no genus or species, because He is uniquely necessary existence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the triangle example
Read the opening example about the triangle. Notice that having right angles is a necessary attribute, but the definition of triangle is 'a shape surrounded by three sides.' This illustrates the difference between essential definitions and attendant attributes.
This example sets up the distinction between what is constitutive and what is merely descriptive.
2
Analyze the definition of substance
Ghazali defines substance as 'existent not in a subject.' Examine why he says 'existent' is not a genus, and how adding a negative or positive attribute does not change this.
The key point is that the definition is a description of the mode of existence, not a specification of quiddity.
3
Apply the reasoning to the First
Consider Ghazali's conclusion: the First has no quiddity other than necessary existence. Since no other being shares necessary existence, the First cannot be distinguished by a differentia and thus has no definition.
This upholds the philosophers' claim that God is simple and not composed of genus and species.

Commentary
This passage explains the nature of definition and genus, and argues that the First (God) cannot have a genus-species composition. It begins with an example: saying that a triangle has three right angles is a necessary attribute, but the definition of a triangle is a shape surrounded by three sides. The point is that essential definitions are not based on attributes. Then it applies this to substance: the meaning of substance is 'existent not in a subject.' But 'existent' is not a genus; adding a negative attribute does not make a genus. Even if you add a positive attribute like 'existent in a subject,' it still does not become a genus. The reason is that when you define substance as 'existent not in a subject,' you are not specifying its existence, but rather describing a mode of existence. Participation in that description is not participation in a genus. True participation in a genus is participation in the constitutive elements of the quiddity, which then requires a differentia to distinguish species. The First (God) has no quiddity other than necessary existence. Necessary existence is a nature and quiddity unique to Him. Since no other shares in necessary existence, He cannot be distinguished by a differentia, so He has no definition. Thus, the philosophers' claim that the First is not composed of genus and species is upheld.

He accuses the philosophers of arbitrarily building their system: they negate number and duality on the basis of negating composition, and they negate composition on the basis of negating essence other than existence. Al-Ghazali claims to have uprooted this final foundation and shown arbitrariness.

Commentary
This passage contains a third objection and a conclusive argument. Ghazali's Second Distinction: Reason vs. Afterlife Literalism
Ghazali distinguishes rational impossibility vs. divine power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's criterion for deciding when to interpret religious texts literally and when to interpret them figuratively, based on rational possibility.

Core Idea
Reason and scripture: literal for possible, figurative for impossible.

In this passage from the Tahafut, Ghazali clarifies a crucial methodological principle. He argues that rational impossibility demands figurative interpretation for God's attributes, but the afterlife—being possible for God—must be taken literally. This distinction safeguards both divine transcendence and the truth of eschatological promises.

Guided Path
1
Read Ghazali's distinction
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to get the full argument. Notice the contrast between the two categories.
2
Identify the two categories
Find where Ghazali discusses divine attributes (place, direction, etc.) and where he discusses the afterlife. Understand why each is treated differently.
3
Understand the criterion
The key is rational possibility vs. impossibility. Reason proves impossibility for God's attributes but not for the afterlife. This determines the method of interpretation.
This criterion is the heart of Ghazali's hermeneutics.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider how this principle allows Ghazali to defend both divine transcendence and the literal reality of the afterlife against philosophers and literalists alike.

الثاني : أن أدلة العقول دلت على استحالة المكان والجهة والصورة، ويد الجارحة، وعين الجارحة، فوجب التأويل بأدلة العقول وما وعد به من أمور الآخرة، ليس محالا في قدرة الله تعالى، فيجب إجراؤه على ظاهر الكلام، بل على فحواه الذي هو صريح فيه
The second: that the proofs of reason indicate the impossibility of place, direction, form, a literal hand, and a literal eye, so interpretation is obligatory by the proofs of reason. But what has been promised of the matters of the Hereafter is not impossible in the power of God most high. So it must be taken according to the apparent meaning of the speech, rather according to its import which is explicit in it.
Source pages p. 368

Commentary
Here Ghazali gives the second distinction. Reason (aql) proves that God cannot have a physical location, direction, form, or literal body parts—so anthropomorphic verses must be interpreted figuratively. However, the promises of the afterlife (Paradise, Hell, bodily resurrection) are not impossible for God's omnipotence. Since there is no rational impossibility, these texts must be taken literally (zahir al-kalam), or more precisely, according to their plain import (fahwa) which is explicit (sarih) in them.
