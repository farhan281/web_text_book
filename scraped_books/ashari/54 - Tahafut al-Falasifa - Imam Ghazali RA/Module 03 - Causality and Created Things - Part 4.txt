The Philosophers' Three Divisions of Bodily Return
Philosophers enumerate three possible meanings of bodily return.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three possible meanings of 'returning to the body' that the philosophers propose and how they categorize them before refuting them.

Core Idea
The philosophers reduce bodily resurrection to three exhaustive possibilities — and deny all of them.

Imam al-Ghazali reports the philosophers' argument against bodily resurrection. Before refuting their view, he presents their classification of what 'returning to the body' could mean. They identify three exhaustive divisions based on the nature of the human being. Understanding this classification is crucial to following their subsequent refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once and then the translation. Notice the clear enumeration of three divisions.
The philosophers are not endorsing these views; they are listing possibilities to refute.
2
Identify the three divisions
Identify each division: (1) man is only body and life is an accident; (2) man is soul and body with regathering of parts; (3) man is soul and body returning to any body.
The second division has a slightly garbled OCR phrase, but the meaning is clear: gathering of dispersed body parts.
3
Understand the philosophers' purpose
Recognize that the philosophers are setting up these three meanings to argue that all are rationally impossible. This is the first of two approaches they take.
The second approach is not included in this passage.

قالت الفلاسفة : لقد دل الدليل العقلى على استحالة بعث الأجساد، كما دل على استحالة تلك الصفات. ولهم في بيان ذلك مسلكان : المسلك الأول : أنهم قالوا : تقدير العود إلى البدن ثلاثة أقسام : لأن الإنسان : إما أن يكون هو البدن، والحياة عرض قائم به، ومعنى الموت انقطاع الحياة . ومعنى المعاد إعادة الحياة التي انعدمت وإما أن يكون نفساً مجردة، وبدناً، والنفس تبقى بعد الموت، أجزاء البدن بعد تفرقها، ليعود إلى الاتصال ومعنى المعاد بالنفس جمع وإما أن يكون نفسا مجردة تبقى بعد الموت، وبدنا، ومعنى المعاد عودتها إلى بدن سواء كان هو الأول أو غيره
The philosophers said: Indeed, rational proof has indicated the impossibility of the resurrection of bodies, just as it indicated the impossibility of those attributes. And they have two approaches in explaining that. The first approach: they said: The estimation of returning to the body has three divisions. For man: either he is the body, and life is an accident subsisting in it, and the meaning of death is the cessation of life, and the meaning of the return is the restoration of the life that ceased; or he is a soul and a body, and the soul remains after death, the parts of the body after their dispersion to return to connection, and the meaning of the return is the gathering of the parts; or he is a soul that remains after death and a body, and the meaning of the return is its return to a body, whether it is the first or another.
Source pages p. 369

Commentary
This passage presents the philosophers' argument that rational proof demonstrates the impossibility of bodily resurrection, just as it demonstrates the impossibility of anthropomorphic attributes for God. They have two approaches (maslakan). The first approach is to analyze the possible meanings of 'returning to the body' into three exhaustive divisions: (1) Man is nothing but the body and life is an accident; death ends life, so resurrection would be recreating life in the same body. (2) Man consists of a soul and a body; the soul survives death, and resurrection means regathering the dispersed parts of the body to restore physical connection. (3) Man is a soul and a body; the soul survives death, and resurrection means the soul returning to a body, either the original or another. The philosophers will then argue all three are impossible.

Philosophers Refute First Two Divisions of Bodily Return
Philosophers argue first two divisions of bodily return are impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' arguments against the first two divisions of bodily return and why they consider them impossible.

Core Idea
The philosophers argue that resurrecting the same body is both conceptually and physically impossible.

The philosophers present a logical critique of bodily resurrection by dividing the possible scenarios. They argue that the first two divisions lead to contradictions: either we get a similar but not identical body, or we cannot physically reassemble the original matter. This passage sets up the classical theological problem of numerical identity and material continuity in resurrection.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice how the philosophers structure their objection by referring to three divisions but only refuting the first two here.
The Arabic uses 'fii al-qism al-awwal' and 'bil-nisbah lil-qism al-thaani'.
2
Identify the two divisions discussed
The passage mentions three divisions but only argues against the first two. Identify what each division claims and why the philosophers reject it.
The first division is about re-creation ex nihilo; the second about reassembling dispersed parts.
3
Understand the argument against the first division
The philosophers claim that if life and body have completely ceased, re-creating them produces 'mithl' (similar) not 'ayn' (identical). Grasp why this is a problem for numerical identity.
This is a conceptual objection: without continuity, the new entity is a replica, not the same individual.
4
Understand the argument against the second division
The philosophers argue that it is physically impossible to gather the exact original body parts because they have mixed with other matter. Even appealing to God's power, they pose a dilemma: which parts to gather?
The dilemma: parts at death or parts over a lifetime? Both options are problematic.
5
Reflect on the implications
Consider how these arguments challenge the traditional Islamic belief in bodily resurrection. Note that the philosophers do not deny God's power but question what is logically possible.
This connects to the broader discussion of God's omnipotence and the nature of bodily identity.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
قالت الفلاسفة : وهذه الأقسام الثلاثة باطلة : لأنه فى القسم الأول قد انعدمت الحياة والبدن ومهما انعدمت الحياة والبدن فاستئناف خلقهما إيجاد لمثل ما كان، لا لعين ما كان وبالنسبة للقسم الثانى محال أن يعاد البدن إذ صار تراباً واختلط بسائر أجزاء العالم فلا يتصور جمعه، وإن ادعى ذلك اتكالا على قدرة الله، فلا يخلو إما أن تجمع الأجزاء التي مات عليها فقط، أو التي لازمته طول عمره، . . . وكل ذلك محال
The philosophers said: And these three divisions are false. Because in the first division, life and the body have ceased, and whenever life and body have ceased, then re-creating them is bringing into existence something similar to what was, not the identical thing. And regarding the second division, it is impossible to bring back the body, since it has become dust and mixed with other parts of the world, so its gathering cannot be conceived. And if one claims that relying on God's power, then it is either that the parts with which he died only are gathered, or those that accompanied him throughout his life... and all that is impossible.
Source pages p. 369

Commentary
In this passage, the philosophers argue against the first two divisions. For the first division (man is just body and life), they claim that since both life and the body have completely ceased to exist, any new creation would be a similar (mithl) entity, not numerically identical (ayn). For the second division (soul plus body, soul survives, body parts regathered), they argue it is impossible to gather the exact original body parts because they have become dust and mixed with other elements of the world. Even appealing to God's power, they ask: would God gather only the parts present at death, or all parts consumed over a lifetime? Both options lead to absurdities. The refutation of the third division is not included in this text, but the argument is that no bodily resurrection is possible.
