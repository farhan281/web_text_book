Refuting the Philosophers on Accidental Knowledge in God
Ghazali refutes the philosophers' proof that accidental knowledge cannot subsist in God's essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali refutes the philosophers' argument that accidental knowledge cannot subsist in God's essence by exposing the inconsistency in their own principles.

Core Idea
Ghazali shows that the philosophers' own principles allow for the very possibility they deny.

In this passage, Ghazali tackles a key objection from the philosophers: that God cannot have originated (accidental) knowledge because it would imply change. After agreeing that change is impossible for God, Ghazali presents a clever argument that, on the philosophers' own terms, such knowledge is not impossible. He draws on the views of Jahm and the Karramiyya to illustrate that the real issue is not originatedness but whether it entails change—something the philosophers' eternal world already allows.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then the English translation. Get a sense of the flow of argument.
Notice that the passage begins with a shared premise.
2
Identify Ghazali's strategy
Ghazali agrees with the philosophers that change in God is impossible. But he questions what 'change' means and whether originated knowledge necessarily entails change. Identify how he uses the examples of Jahm and the Karramiyya.
Jahm and the Karramiyya held views that seem similar to what the philosophers reject.
3
Analyze the philosophers' argument
The philosophers argue that originated knowledge must come either from God or from another, both impossible in their view. Trace their reasoning and see why they think these are impossible.
They rely on the principle 'nothing originated originates from the Eternal'.
4
Understand Ghazali's rebuttal
Ghazali counters each of the two divisions. He claims that they have already refuted the first division in a previous discussion, and the second division is not impossible on the philosophers' own principles. Ask yourself: why does Ghazali think this undermines their objection?
Ghazali's refutation is dialectical: he uses the philosophers' own principles against them.
5
Reflect on the broader significance
Consider how this argument fits into the larger Tahafut debate. Ghazali is not just refuting a single point; he is showing that the philosophers' system is incoherent.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the philosophers' objection against accidental knowledge (علم حادث) subsisting in God. The passage begins by stating the shared goal of denying change in God. Core Idea
Infinite knowledge cannot be inscribed on a finite physical tablet.

Al-Ghazali said: “How can there come together in a single created soul, in one state, without succession, detailed particular sciences that have no end to their numbers and no limit to their individuals? … And whoever’s intellect does not witness the impossibility of that, let him despair of his intellect.” Al-Ghazali said: “The attachment of God’s knowledge to His objects of knowledge is not of the same kind as the attachment of the sciences that belong to creatures.”

Commentary
This passage contains Ghazali's second, more decisive argument. He poses a rhetorical question: how can a created soul simultaneously possess an infinite number of detailed, particular items of knowledge without succession? This is inherently impossible because the creature is finite. He then states that anyone whose intellect does not see the impossibility of this should doubt his own reason. Finally, Ghazali draws a crucial distinction: divine knowledge is not like creaturely knowledge. God's knowledge is not a 'science' that inheres in a subject in the same way. Thus, the philosophers' analogy between God's knowledge and the supposed knowledge of heavenly souls is invalid. This argument undercuts the entire foundation of their claim that a creature can have infinite knowledge.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think Ghazali is denying the possibility of any knowledge in creatures.
Correction: Ghazali is specifically denying infinite simultaneous knowledge in a creature, not all knowledge. Creatures can have finite knowledge.

Key Takeaway
Ghazali's argument shows that the philosophers' analogy between God's knowledge and creaturely knowledge fails because a finite creature cannot contain infinite knowledge at once.
