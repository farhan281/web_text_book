The Debate on the Three Brothers
Al-Ash'ari debates al-Jubba'i on the fate of three brothers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari used logical reasoning to expose a contradiction in the Mu'tazili doctrine of divine justice.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazili system collapses when asked why God spared the child but not the disbeliever.

This lesson presents one of the most famous debates in Islamic theological history. Al-Ash'ari, a former Mu'tazili student, challenges his teacher al-Jubba'i with a hypothetical scenario that exposes an inconsistency in the Mu'tazili understanding of God's justice. As you read the passage, pay close attention to each step of the argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its literal English translation. Notice the conversational style of the debate and the key terms used.
The passage includes an introduction by Ibn al-Imad, the debate itself, and a concluding remark.
2
Identify the three brothers and their fates
Note the three brothers: a believer, a disbeliever, and a child. According to al-Jubba'i's initial answer, what happens to each?
The child is in a state of safety, neither rewarded nor punished.
3
Trace the logical progression of the debate
Follow al-Ash'ari's questions: first about the child wanting to join the believer, then about the child's excuse, and finally about the elder brother's counterargument. Observe how each response from al-Jubba'i leads to a contradiction.
Al-Ash'ari's final question forces al-Jubba'i into silence.
4
Reflect on why al-Jubba'i was silenced
Consider why al-Jubba'i could not answer. What is the logical inconsistency in the Mu'tazili position that al-Ash'ari reveals?

Commentary
This passage presents the famous debate (manazara) between al-Ash'ari and his Mu'tazili teacher al-Jubba'i. Ibn al-Imad introduces it as a decisive refutation of the Mu'tazila and Jahmiyya. The debate revolves around the issue of divine justice and predestination. Al-Ash'ari poses a hypothetical scenario of three brothers: one believer who dies in obedience, one disbeliever who dies in sin, and a child who dies before reaching puberty. Al-Jubba'i, according to Mu'tazili theology, assigns the believer to Paradise, the disbeliever to Hell, and the child to a state of safety (neither rewarded nor punished). Al-Ash'ari then asks whether the child could request to join the believer in Paradise. Al-Jubba'i answers no, because the child did not earn those rewards. Al-Ash'ari points out that the child could argue that the inability to obey was due to God not granting him life and ability. Al-Jubba'i responds that God knew the child would have disobeyed if he had lived, so He protected him. Al-Ash'ari then asks why God did not similarly protect the disbeliever, who also would have disobeyed? This leads to a contradiction in the Mu'tazili position, forcing al-Jubba'i into silence. The lesson concludes with Ibn al-Imad's comment that this debate demonstrates God's absolute sovereignty and mercy, not constrained by human notions of justice.

The Beatific Vision of Allah in the Hereafter
Al-Ash'ari affirms the beatific vision for believers, veiling for disbelievers, and its impossibility in this world.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that believers will see Allah in Paradise with their eyes, that disbelievers are veiled from Him, and that no one can see Allah in this world.

Core Idea
Allah will be seen by believers in Paradise, but not in this life.

This passage from al-Ash'ari's classic creed, al-Ibānah, states the Sunni position on the vision of Allah in the afterlife. The author grounds this belief in both prophetic tradition and the Qur'an, while also clarifying that disbelievers are veiled from their Lord and that even Prophet Musa could not see Allah in this world. Let's unpack this foundational creedal statement.

Notice the use of the moon analogy—a common sunni trope.

The passage makes three distinct theological claims: (1) believers will see Allah in the afterlife like seeing the full moon, (2) disbelievers are veiled from Him, (3) no one can see Allah in this world, as shown by Musa's request. Find each one in the text.

The author quotes Qur'an 83:15 for the veiling of disbelievers and alludes to the story of Musa and the mountain (Qur'an 7:143) to show that vision in this world is impossible. Ask yourself: why does each proof support the claim?
These scriptural references are the backbone of the argument.

Refuting the Claim of Parallelism Between Two Verses
Imam Ashari refutes an objection about conjunction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Ashari uses dialectical questioning to refute an opponent's claim that grammatical conjunction forces identical scope between two verses.

Core Idea
Conjunction does not force identical meaning; context determines scope.

In this passage, Imam Ashari confronts an opponent who argued that because two verses are grammatically conjoined, they must have the same scope of generality. By getting the opponent to admit that the first verse negates all physical perception, and that the second does not imply physical perception, Ashari shows the argument's flaw. This lesson examines his logical technique.

Guided Path
1
Understand the opponent's claim
The opponent says the two verses are parallel because they are conjoined (عطف). What does that imply?
The opponent likely thought both verses refer to physical perception.
2
Imam Ashari's first question
He asks whether the eyes perceive Allah by any means. The opponent admits no. This establishes that 'La tudrikuhu' negates all perception, not just sight.
3
Second question and conclusion
He asks whether Allah perceives sights by physical means. The opponent says no. This shows that 'yudriku' does not mean physical perception. Thus, the conjunction doesn't force identical meaning.

Quranic Proof that God Does What He Wills
Uses Quranic verse to prove God's effective will and refutes deniers by showing absurdities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quranic verse 'Doer of what He wills' establishes that God's will is perfectly effective, and why denying this leads to absurd implications of heedlessness or weakness in God.

Core Idea
Denying God's effective will forces an admission of divine heedlessness or weakness.

This lesson presents a concise Quranic argument from Imam al-Ash'ari. By citing the verse 'Doer of what He wills,' he shows that God's will is always effective. He then uses a reductio ad absurdum: if God did something He did not will, then He must have been either unaware or powerless—both impossible for Allah. The same logic applies to human actions within God's dominion.

1

The passage argues that if someone claims God does what He does not will, then that act must have occurred while God was either heedless (sahw/ghaflah) or weak (da'f/taqsir). Recognize both as impossible for God.
3
Extend the same logic to human actions
The author applies the same reasoning: if any event in God's dominion occurs against His will, then again either heedlessness or weakness must be attributed to God. This shows that all events, including human actions, are under God's effective will.
This refutes the notion of independent human will.

Core Idea
Foolishness only applies to beings under law; God has no lawgiver above Him.

Al-Ash'ari presents a sophisticated argument defending God's transcendence. He clarifies that calling someone foolish requires that they have violated a superior's command. Since God has no superior, even if He wills what appears ugly, it cannot be called foolishness.

وقال تعالى: (وَاللهُ لَا يَهْدِى الْقَوْمَ الْكَفِرِينَ ﴾ [البقرة: ٢٦٤] فإذا أخبر الله عز وجل أنه لا يهدي القوم الكافرين، فكيف يجوز لقائل أن يقول إنه هدى الكافرين مع إخباره أنه لا يهديهم، ومع قوله تعالى: ﴿ إِنَّكَ لَا تَهْدِي مَنْ أَحْبَبْتَ وَلَكِنَّ اللَّهَ يَهْدِي مَن يَشَاءُ ﴾ [القصص : ٥٦] ومع قوله تعالى: (لَيْسَ عَلَيْكَ هُدَتهُمْ وَلَكِنَّ اللَّهَ يَهْدِي مَن يَشَاءُ ﴾ [البقرة : ۲۷۲] ومع قوله تعالى: ﴿ وَلَوْ شِئْنَا لا تيْنَا كُلَّ نفيس هُدَنهَا ﴾ [السجدة : ۱۳]؟ وإن جاز هذا جاز أن يقال: أضل المؤمنين، مع قوله تعالى: (وَمَن يَهْدِ اللَّهُ فَهُوَ الْمُهْتَدِ ) [الإسراء: ٩٧] ومع قوله : هُدًى يتشقينَ) [البقرة : ۲] فإن لم يكن ذلك، فما أنكرتم أنه لا يجوز أن يهدي الكافرين مع قوله تعالى: ﴿ وَاللَّهُ لَا يَهْدِى الْقَوْمَ الْكَفِرِينَ ) [البقرة: ٢٦٤] ومع سائر الآيات التي طالبناكم بها.
243 - Another issue: And it is said to them: Has not Allah, exalted, said: 'Whoever Allah guides is the guided one, and whoever He leads astray, you will not find for him any protecting guide' [Al-Kahf: 17]? And He said: 'How shall Allah guide a people who disbelieved after their belief' [Aal-e-Imran: 86]? So He mentioned that He does not guide them. And He said: 'And Allah invites to the abode of peace and guides whom He wills to a straight path' [Yunus: 25]? So He made the invitation general and the guidance specific. And He said: 'And Allah does not guide the disbelieving people' [Al-Baqarah: 264]. So when Allah, Mighty and Majestic, informed that He does not guide the disbelieving people, how can a speaker say that He guided the disbelievers while He informed that He does not guide them, and with His statement: 'Indeed, you do not guide whom you love, but Allah guides whom He wills' [Al-Qasas: 56]? And with His statement: 'Not upon you is their guidance, but Allah guides whom He wills' [Al-Baqarah: 272]? And with His statement: 'And if We had willed, We would have given every soul its guidance' [As-Sajda: 13]? And if this is permissible, then it would be permissible to say: He led the believers astray, with His statement: 'And whoever Allah guides, he is the guided one' [Al-Isra: 97] and with His statement: 'Guidance for the righteous' [Al-Baqarah: 2]. Then if that is not the case, what prevents you from [accepting] that it is not permissible that He guided the disbelievers with His statement: 'And Allah does not guide the disbelieving people' [Al-Baqarah: 264] and with the rest of the verses with which we have confronted you?
Source pages p. 150

Commentary
This passage is a scriptural argument. The Ashari cites multiple Quranic verses to prove that Allah does not guide disbelievers. He lists: (1) Al-Kahf 17: guidance and misguidance are exclusively from Allah. (2) Aal-e-Imran 86: Allah does not guide a people who disbelieved after belief. (3) Yunus 25: Allah's call is general but guidance is specific. (4) Al-Baqarah 264: Allah does not guide disbelieving people. (5) Al-Qasas 56: Guidance is exclusively from Allah. (6) Al-Baqarah 272: The Prophet is not responsible for guidance. (7) As-Sajda 13: If Allah willed, He would have guided everyone. The Ashari then uses a reductio: if it were permissible to claim that Allah guided disbelievers despite these verses, then by the same logic one could claim that Allah led the believers astray despite verses promising guidance to believers. Since the latter is clearly false, the former must also be false. Thus the opponent's claim that Allah guided the disbelievers (in the sense of granting them true guidance) is impossible. The overall point is that God's guidance is effective and selective; He does not guide those whom He has decreed will not be guided.

The Purpose of Misguidance: Not for Guidance
Ashari argues that God's misguidance is not intended to lead to guidance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ashari argues that divine misguidance is not intended to lead to guidance, using a logical symmetry argument.

Core Idea
If misguidance could aim at guidance, then guidance could aim at misguidance—absurd.

This lesson examines Imām al-Ashʿarī's argument from Al-Ibānah that God's misguidance of the disbelievers is not a means to guide them. He uses a logical reductio: if misguiding could be for guiding, then guiding could be for misguiding, which is impossible. The passage shows how ḥikmah (wisdom) in divine actions is understood through rational consistency.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage carefully, then its literal translation. Notice how the argument begins with a verse and then poses a question.
The verse is Al-Jāthiyah 23: 'Allah has sent him astray due to knowledge...'
2
Identify the logical structure
Find the three parts: (1) the opponent's claim, (2) the reductio ad absurdum, (3) the conclusion that the opponent's claim is false.
The argument uses symmetry: if A can cause not-A, then not-A can cause A.
3
Understand the reductio
Ashari says: if you claim misguidance can lead to guidance, then by the same logic guidance could lead to misguidance. Since the latter is impossible (God never guides believers to misguide them), the former must be impossible too.
4
Reflect on the theological implication
Consider why this matters for understanding divine justice. Misguidance is a punishment or decree, not a pedagogical tool.
This distinguishes Ashʿarī from Muʿtazilī views of divine purpose.

244 - Issue: And it is said to them: Has not Allah, exalted, said: 'Have you seen he who has taken as his god his [own] desire, and Allah has sent him astray [adalla] due to knowledge and has sealed over his hearing and his heart and put over his vision a veil' [Al-Jathiya: 23]? Commentary
This passage is an argument about the purpose of divine misguidance. The Ashari cites Al-Jathiya 23, where Allah says He led someone astray after knowledge. The opponent might claim that this misguidance is meant to ultimately lead to guidance (i.e., a pedagogical purpose). The Ashari responds with a logical symmetry: if you claim that God misguides in order to bring about guidance, then by the same logic one could claim that God guides in order to bring about misguidance. But the latter is impossible—God never guides believers so that they become misguided. Therefore, the former is also impossible. If there is no case where God guides to misguide, then there is no case where God misguides to guide. The purpose of misguidance is simply misguidance itself, as a just punishment or decree. The argument uses the principle that if a means is used to achieve its opposite in one case, it could be used in the reverse, but since the reverse is absurd, the original claim fails.
