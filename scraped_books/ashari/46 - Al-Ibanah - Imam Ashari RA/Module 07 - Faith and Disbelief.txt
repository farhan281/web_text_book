Rejecting Khariji Takfir for Major Sins
Al-Ash'ari refutes Khariji takfir and defines the condition for disbelief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari position that committing a major sin does not automatically make one a disbeliever, and the condition under which it does.

Core Idea
Major sin does not equal disbelief unless the sinner deems it lawful.

Imam al-Ash'ari opens this passage by distinguishing the Sunni position from the Khawarij, who declared Muslims guilty of major sins as disbelievers. This lesson clarifies that while major sins are grave, they do not remove one from the fold of Islam except when the sinner rejects the prohibition.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice how Imam al-Ash'ari presents two rulings: a general rule and an exception.
Pay attention to the phrase 'كما دانت بذلك الخوارج'.
2
Identify the two parts
The passage first states the general rule: no takfir for sins. Then it states the exception: takfir if the sin is considered lawful. Identify these two parts in the Arabic.
3
Understand the condition
The exception requires 'mustahillan laha' (considering it lawful) and 'ghayr mu'taqid li-tahrimiha' (not believing in its prohibition). This is the key condition for takfir.
The word 'tahrim' appears as 'tahri' in the text; it means prohibition.
4
Reflect on the wisdom
Why does Ash'ari make this exception? Because rejecting a clear prohibition of Sharia is itself an act of disbelief. This safeguards the religion while avoiding extreme takfir.

Faith is Statement and Action, Increases and Decreases
Al-Ash'ari defines Iman as statement and action subject to increase and decrease.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that according to Al-Ash'ari, faith (iman) comprises both statement and action, and that it can increase and decrease.

Core Idea
Faith is not static; it is both statement and action, and it fluctuates.

Al-Ash'ari presents the defining Sunni view of faith in this brief but weighty sentence. This definition directly opposes two extreme positions: those who reduce faith to mere inner belief and those who equate sin with loss of faith. Understanding this balanced view is foundational for the rest of 'Aqidah.

The original is a single phrase but packed with meaning.

Statement includes verbal profession and inner conviction; action includes deeds of heart and limbs.
3
Understand the fluctuation of faith
The phrase 'increases and decreases' shows faith is not fixed. How does it increase or decrease?
It increases with obedience and decreases with disobedience.
4
Connect to historical context
This definition refutes the Murji'ah (faith is only belief) and the Khawarij (major sin nullifies faith). Why is this balance important?
It preserves both the necessity of action and the mercy of Allah.
5
Reflect on practical implications

It encourages striving to increase faith while remaining hopeful of Allah's mercy.

Commentary
This passage is a continuous creedal statement by Imam al-Ash'ari enumerating the core beliefs of Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah. It follows the method of the Salaf in affirming what is reported without modality or figurative interpretation.

The passage begins by affirming God's absolute control: He turns hearts between two of His fingers, and He places the heavens and earths on a finger, as reported in hadith. This is an affirmation of God's attributes of action and His power, without speculating on how.

Next, al-Ash'ari addresses faith and judgement of believers: we do not definitively assign anyone to Paradise or Hell except those explicitly attested by the Prophet. For sinful believers, we maintain hope of Paradise but fear punishment, balancing hope and fear.

He then affirms intercession: God will bring out from the Fire those who have been charred (i.e., severely punished) through the intercession of the Prophet, in line with authentic hadiths.

Eschatological beliefs are listed: punishment of the grave, the Basin (of the Prophet on the Day of Judgment), the Scale (for weighing deeds), the Bridge (over Hell), resurrection, and the standing before God for reckoning of the believers.

Regarding faith itself, al-Ash'ari defines it as 'speech and action,' and it increases and decreases. This is the Sunni position, opposing the Murji'ah who say faith is only belief and does not increase or decrease.

He then emphasizes acceptance of authentic hadiths transmitted through trustworthy chains, affirming the importance of the Sunni method of hadith criticism.

The passage then turns to the Companions and the Caliphs. We must love the Salaf (the early Muslims, especially the Companions) and praise them as God praised them. The best after the Prophet is Abu Bakr, followed by 'Umar, then 'Uthman (noting his murder was unjust), then 'Ali. These four are the imams and their caliphate is the 'caliphate of prophethood.' He also testifies to Paradise for the ten Companions given glad tidings (the 'ashara mubashshara), refrains from commenting on disputes among Companions, and affirms the superiority of these four imams over all others.

Al-Ash'ari then affirms the accepted narrations about God's descent to the lowest heaven and His asking 'Is there anyone asking?' etc. He rejects the interpretations of those who deny these (the 'people of deviation and misguidance') and states that in matters of disagreement, the source of authority is the Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma'. He warns against innovation and speaking about God without knowledge.

Finally, he affirms that God will come on the Day of Resurrection in a manner befitting Him, and that He draws near to His servants as He wills, citing Quranic verses. The passage concludes with the practical ruling that we pray behind any ruler, whether pious or impious, as 'Abdullah ibn 'Umar did behind al-Hajjaj, a notoriously unjust governor. This demonstrates the Sunni principle of obedience and unity in congregational worship.

Love of the Salaf and the Rightly-Guided Caliphs
Al-Ash'ari states beliefs regarding the Companions, the four caliphs, and the ten promised Paradise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni position on loving the Companions, the priority and superiority of the four caliphs, and the practice of refraining from discussing their disputes.

Core Idea
The best after the Prophet are Abu Bakr, then Umar, then Uthman, then Ali.

Al-Ash'ari emphasizes the obligation to love the Salaf, especially the Companions, and details the order of the four caliphs. He also affirms the caliphate of prophethood for the first four and the Paradise of the ten promised Companions.

Guided Path
1
Read and distinguish the components
The passage covers: love of Salaf, praise of Companions, succession of four caliphs, testimony of Paradise for the ten, and refraining from disputes.
2
Learn the order of the caliphs

3
Understand the principle of refraining from disputes
We befriend all Companions and do not delve into their conflicts. Their caliphate is that of prophethood.

Al-Ash'ari affirms divine descent, coming, and nearness, and states the Sunni methodology for resolving differences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Ahl al-Sunnah affirms God's descension to the lowest heaven, His coming on the Day of Resurrection, and His nearness to His servants as reported, without figurative interpretation.

Core Idea
God descends, comes, and draws near, but we do not ask how.

Al-Ash'ari returns to divine attributes, affirming the narrations about God's descent to the lowest heaven, His coming on the Day of Resurrection, and His nearness to His servants. He rejects the interpretations of those who deny these, and emphasizes reliance on Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma.

3
Understand the methodology
Al-Ash'ari states the sources of authority: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and warns against innovation and speaking without knowledge.

Praying Behind All Rulers: The Sunni Practice of Unity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Ahl al-Sunnah prays behind both pious and impious rulers as a matter of unity and obedience, following the example of Ibn Umar.

We pray behind every ruler, regardless of their piety.

In this passage, we encounter a powerful statement from the early scholar Waki' ibn al-Jarrah. He leaves no room for ambiguity: the one who claims the Quran is created has left the fold of Islam. This lesson unpacks the gravity of that judgment and its implications for early Islamic orthodoxy.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and the literal English translation. Notice the concise style of the report and the key terms used.
The passage is a short athar (report) from a companion of Waki'.
2
Identify the key terms
Look for the terms 'مخلوق' (created), 'مرتد' (apostate), and 'يستتاب' (he is asked to repent). Understand their meanings and significance.
These terms are central to the ruling.
3
Understand the legal procedure
Waki' prescribes a procedure: first, the person is asked to repent; if they do, they are left; if not, they are executed. Recognize that this is the standard Islamic punishment for apostasy.
Istitaba (asking to repent) is a requirement before execution.
4
Reflect on the doctrinal context
Consider why this issue was so grave. The createdness of the Quran was a major controversy between the Mu'tazila and Ahl al-Sunnah. This statement shows the firm stance of early Sunni scholars.
The Quran's uncreatedness is a foundational Sunni belief.

If the person repents, they are left alone; if not, the punishment is execution. This reflects the strict position of the early Salaf on the issue of the createdness of the Quran.

The Created Quran as Disbelief
Al-Ash'ari quotes Sufyan al-Thawri declaring the createdness of the Quran as disbelief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the early Sunni position that declaring the Quran to be created constitutes disbelief, as expressed by Sufyan al-Thawri.

Core Idea
Calling the Quran created is not just error—it is disbelief.

This short but powerful statement from the early scholar Sufyan al-Thawri draws a clear line in the sand. Al-Ash'ari includes it to show that the doctrine of the created Quran was not a minor disagreement but a matter of fundamental creed. Let's examine what this report means and why it matters.

Guided Path
1
Read the report
Pay attention to the chain of narration and the direct statement. Notice that al-Thawri says 'whoever claims... has disbelieved.'
The chain indicates reliable transmission.
2
Identify the ruling
The word 'kafara' (disbelieved) is a strong term. Understand that this is not just a mistake but an act that removes one from the fold of faith.
3
Consider the context
Why would al-Thawri make such a strong statement? Reflect on the gravity of attributing creation to the eternal speech of Allah.
This relates to the doctrine of divine attributes.
4
Reflect on implications
What does this teach us about the seriousness of theological errors? It shows that early scholars saw creedal deviations as matters of disbelief.
This is a key principle in Sunni orthodoxy.

Commentary
This passage is a statement from the early authority Sufyan al-Thawri. Al-Ash'ari uses it to show that the doctrine of the created Quran is not just an error but tantamount to disbelief (kufr). Al-Thawri's verdict places the issue within the realm of fundamental creed. The strong language indicates that affirming the Quran as created contradicts core Islamic belief in the divine attributes. This statement serves as a precedent for anathematizing those who hold this view, underlining the seriousness of the matter in early Sunni orthodoxy.
