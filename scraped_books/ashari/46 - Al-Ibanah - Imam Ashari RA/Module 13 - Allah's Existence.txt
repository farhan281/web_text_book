God's All-Encompassing Knowledge in Islamic Creed
Declaration of praise, dependence, forgiveness, and divine omniscience.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the comprehensive nature of God's knowledge as affirmed in classical Islamic creedal declarations, and how praise, dependence, and testimony are foundational expressions of tawhid.

Core Idea
God's knowledge encompasses every detail, from hearts to seas, from falling leaves to hidden secrets.

In this passage, the author crafts a declaration that begins with praise, moves through dependence and forgiveness, and culminates in a powerful testimony of God's oneness and His all-encompassing knowledge. This is not merely a prayer; it is a theological statement that anchors the believer's relationship with Allah in both humility and certainty.

Guided Path
1
Observe the structure of the declaration
Read the passage and notice how it progresses from praise (hamd) to seeking help (isti'ana) to seeking forgiveness (istighfar) to the shahada (testimony of oneness) and finally to an extended description of God's knowledge.
This structure mirrors the classical format of khutbah and supplication.
2
Identify the attributes of God's knowledge
List all the things God knows according to the passage: hearts, inner thoughts, souls, seas, secrets, wombs, leaves, grains, fresh/dry, and the actions and returns of human beings.
Notice that the list covers hidden, natural, and cosmic phenomena.
3
Connect to tawhid and divine lordship
Reflect on how this comprehensive knowledge is a necessary implication of God's oneness and lordship (rububiyya). A true Lord must have perfect knowledge of His creation.
This passage counters any concept of a limited or partial divine knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is a declaration of praise, dependence, forgiveness, and monotheism. The author first praises God in the manner God praised Himself and as His creatures praised Him. Then he bears witness to God's oneness and His attribute of knowledge, citing Quranic verses about God's comprehensive knowledge of all things, even the smallest details. This establishes the fundamental belief in God's omniscience.

We Affirm All That Comes from Allah
The author affirms comprehensive acceptance of divine revelation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the Islamic creed begins with accepting all that is authentically revealed, without rejection.

Core Idea
The creed starts with acceptance, not rejection.

Imam Ash'ari begins the summary of the creed by establishing the foundational attitude of a believer: we affirm everything that comes from Allah and His Messenger. This sets the tone for the entire article of faith.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic text and its translation to grasp the comprehensive nature of the affirmation.
Notice the phrase 'لا نرد من ذلك شيئاً' – we reject none of that.
2
Identify the items affirmed
List all the things the passage says we affirm: Allah, angels, books, messengers, what came from Allah, and what trustworthy narrators transmitted.
3
Understand the principle
Reflect on why the author emphasizes 'we reject none of that'. This establishes that selective belief is not sufficient.
This principle guards against rejecting any part of revelation.

Quranic Proof That God Wills Fighting
Al-Ash'ari uses Quranic verses to argue that God wills the fighting that occurs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Ash'ari uses Quranic verses and conditional reasoning to prove that God wills all fighting that occurs.

Core Idea
Fighting occurs because God wills it; otherwise it would not happen.

This lesson presents a concise Quranic argument from Imam Al-Ash'ari. He cites two verses to show that the actual occurrence of fighting proves that God willed it. The logic is simple: if God had willed otherwise, fighting would not have taken place. This refutes the claim that evil events happen without God's will.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic text once to get its rhythm, then read the literal translation. Notice the conditional structures.
The passage uses 'law' (لو) conditionals.
2
Understand the main argument from the first verse
The first verse says: 'If Allah had willed, they would not have fought.' This implies a conditional necessity. Since fighting does occur, we can deduce that God did not will to prevent it—i.e., He willed it.
This is a straightforward logical inference (modus tollens).
3
Examine the confirmation from the second verse
The second verse, 'But Allah does what He wills,' reinforces that God's will is effective regarding fighting. Whatever happens, He wills it.
This verse is general, but applied specifically to fighting.
4
See the parallel with the disbelievers verse
The third verse about disbelievers: if they were sent back, they would return to disbelief. Their actual state (not sent back) shows that God did not will to send them back. Similarly, the actuality of fighting shows God willed it.
This strengthens the argument by analogy.

Source Text

ويقال لهم : قال الله عز وجل : ﴿ وَلَوْ شَاءَ اللهُ مَا أَقْتَتَلُوا فأخبر أنه لو شاء أن لا يقتتلوا ما اقتتلوا، قال: ﴿وَلَكِنَّ اللَّهَ يَفْعَلُ مَا يُرِيدُ ﴾ [البقرة: ٢٥٣] من القتال، فإذا وقع القتال فقد شاءه، كما أنه قال: ﴿وَلَوْ رُدُّوا لَعَادُوا لِمَا نُهُوا عَنْهُ ﴾ [الأنعام : ۲۸] فقد أوجب أن الرد لو كان إلى الدنيا لعادوا إلى الكفر، وأنهم إذا لم يردّهم إلى الدنيا لم يعودوا ، فكذلك لو شاء الله أن لا يقتتلوا لما اقتتلوا، وإذا اقتتلوا فقد شاء أن يقتتلوا .
And it is said to them: Allah, mighty and majestic, said: 'And if Allah had willed, they would not have fought' — so He informed that if He had willed that they not fight, they would not have fought. He said: 'But Allah does what He wills' (al-Baqarah 2:253) regarding fighting. So when fighting occurs, He has willed it. Similarly, He said: 'And if they were sent back, they would return to what they were forbidden from' (al-An'am 6:28). So He established that if they were sent back to the world, they would return to disbelief, and if He does not send them back to the world, they will not return. Likewise, if Allah had willed that they not fight, they would not have fought; and if they fought, then He has willed that they fight.
Source pages p. 129

Commentary
This argument (hujja) uses explicit Quranic texts. The verse 'if Allah had willed, they would not have fought' implies conditional necessity: if God had willed non-fighting, it would have happened. But since fighting does occur, we can infer that God willed it. Another verse 'But Allah does what He wills' affirms that God's will is effective. Al-Ash'ari then parallels with the verse about disbelievers: if they were sent back, they would return to disbelief. This conditional shows that their actual state is not being sent back. So analogously, the actual occurrence of fighting proves that God willed it. The argument refutes those who claim that evil occurs without God's will.
