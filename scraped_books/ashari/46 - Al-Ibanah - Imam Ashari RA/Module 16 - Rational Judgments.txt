Question About Obligating Disbelievers to Hear and Believe
Imam al-Ash'ari presents an objection and answers with Quranic verses showing obligation without ability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Ash'ari uses Quranic verses to demonstrate that God can obligate even those who are incapable, contrary to the claim that ability is a prerequisite for obligation.

Core Idea
God's obligation does not depend on human ability.

This lesson examines a theological objection: if God obligates disbelievers to hear and believe, they must have had the ability to do so. Imam al-Ash'ari counters with Quranic verses indicating they were unable, showing that obligation and ability are distinct. Prepare to see how scripture resolves a key debate in kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
The passage opens with a question posed to opponents. Notice the assumption that God's obligation implies ability. Read the Arabic and translation.
2
Examine the counter-evidence
Imam al-Ash'ari quotes two verses (Hud 11:20 and al-Kahf 18:101) that state the disbelievers were unable to hear. Reflect on how these verses contradict the assumption.
3
Understand the conclusion
The final line states that God obligated them to listen despite their inability. This establishes that ability is not a condition for obligation. Consider the theological implications.

Issue concerning obligation: And it is said to them: Did not Allah, Mighty and Majestic, obligate the disbelievers to listen to the truth, accept it, and believe in Allah? There is no escape from 'yes'. Then it is said to them: Indeed Allah, Mighty and Majestic, has said: 'They were not able to hear' (Hud: 20) and said: 'And they were not able to hear' (al-Kahf: 101), and He obligated them to listen to the truth.
Source pages p. 138

Commentary
This passage is an objection raised by the Ash'ari against his opponents (likely the Qadariyya or Mu'tazila). The opponents claim that God's obligation on disbelievers to believe implies that they have the ability to believe. The Ash'ari counters by citing verses that state the disbelievers were unable to hear, implying that God obligated them to something they were incapable of. This supports the Ash'ari position that God can obligate what is impossible (taklif ma la yutaq), or at least that ability is not a prerequisite for obligation.

Correction: Imam al-Ash'ari shows that God can obligate even those who are unable, as seen in the Quran.

The Refutation of Provision Entailing Ownership
Al-Ash'ari refutes the entailment of ownership from provision.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Al-Ash'ari argues that God providing something does not necessarily mean He owns it, and how the examples of infants and animals demonstrate this.

Core Idea
Provision does not require ownership; God gives without giving legal title.

In this passage, Al-Ash'ari tackles a subtle objection: if God provides haram (forbidden things), wouldn't that imply He owns them? He counters with everyday examples—infants feeding from milk and animals grazing. The logic is simple yet profound: God can provide without transferring ownership. Let's examine the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then read the English translation. Notice the back-and-forth dialectical style: the opponent states a claim, then Al-Ash'ari responds with a counterexample.
This passage is a concise theological argument.
2
Identify the opponent's claim
The opponent says: 'If God provided the haram, He would own the haram.' Understand why this is problematic: if God owned haram, that might imply imperfection or contradiction with His attributes.
3
Examine Al-Ash'ari's counterexamples
Al-Ash'ari asks about an infant nourished by mother's milk and a beast grazing grass. God provides these, but the infant and beast do not own them. This shows provision does not entail ownership.
The examples are from everyday life, making the argument accessible.
4
Grasp the logical conclusion
The conclusion: 'God provides something without giving ownership of it.' So the opponent's objection fails because it conflates provision with ownership.

مسألة أخرى في الأرزاق : ويقال لهم: لم أبيتم أن يرزق الله الحرام؟ فإن قالوا: لأنه لو رزق الحرام لملك الحرام. يقال لهم : خبرونا عن الطفل الذي يتغذى من لبن أمه، وعن البهيمة التي ترعى الحشيش، من يرزقهما ذلك؟ فإن قالوا : الله قيل لهم : فمن ملكهما؟ وهل للبهيمة ملك؟ فإن قالوا: لا قيل لهم : فلم زعمتم أنه لو رزق الحرام لملك الحرام، وقد يرزق الله لشيء ولا يُملكه إياه .
Another question about provisions: And it is said to them: Why do you refuse that God provides the haram? So if they say: Because if He provided the haram, He would own the haram. It is said to them: Inform us about the infant who is nourished from his mother's milk, and about the beast that grazes the grass, who provides them that? So if they say: God. It is said to them: Then who owns them? And does the beast have ownership? So if they say: No. It is said to them: So why did you claim that if He provided the haram He would own the haram, while God provides something without giving ownership of it?
Source pages p. 145

Commentary
This passage addresses the objection that God providing haram would mean He owns the haram. Al-Ash'ari responds by giving examples of infants and animals: God provides them with milk and grass, but they do not own those things. Thus, provision does not entail ownership. Therefore, the objection fails.
