Wiping over Leather Socks is Sunnah in Travel and Residence
Imam Ash'ari affirms wiping over khuffs in all circumstances

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Imam Ash'ari asserts wiping over khuffs is a Sunnah practiced both in residence and travel, refuting those who deny it.

Core Idea
Wiping over leather socks is a confirmed Sunnah in all situations.

This lesson presents a concise but significant ruling from Imam Ash'ari's Ibana. In a single sentence, he establishes the Sunni position on wiping over khuffs during ablution. The statement is a direct refutation of those who reject this practice, affirming its legitimacy in both settled and traveling contexts.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the original Arabic phrase and its literal translation to grasp the direct meaning.
Notice the two conditions explicitly mentioned: residence and travel.
2
Understand the term 'Sunnah'
In this context, 'Sunnah' refers to a recommended practice established by the Prophet, not obligatory but strongly encouraged.
3
Recognize the contrast
The phrase 'contrary to those who denied' highlights that this was disputed by some groups, such as the Khawarij.
Reflect on why some might deny it and how the Sunni position is grounded in hadith.
4
Consider the implication
This ruling emphasizes that the dispensation of wiping over khuffs applies universally, not limited to travel.
It counteracts the misconception that it is only allowed when traveling.

Commentary
This passage is a concise ruling on the practice of wiping over leather socks (al-masḥ 'alā al-khuffayn) during ablution. Imam Ash'ari declares that this is a confirmed Sunnah, applicable both when one is resident (ḥaḍar) and when traveling (safar). He contrasts this with the position of those who reject this practice. Historically, the Khawarij and some other sects denied the validity of wiping over khuffs, insisting on washing the feet. The Sunni position, based on widespread hadith and practice of the Companions, affirms it as a legitimate dispensation.

The Linguistic Reality of 'Basar'
Imam al-Ash'ari refutes the restriction of 'basar' to the eye using linguistic evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Ash'ari uses linguistic evidence (ahl al-lugha) to argue that 'basar' applies to both the eye and the heart, countering those who restrict it to physical sight.

Core Idea
Language itself shows that 'basar' includes both eye and heart vision.

This short passage presents a critical argument about the term 'basar'. Some claimed it refers only to physical eye sight. Imam al-Ash'ari refutes this by appealing to the actual usage of the Arabic language, showing that 'basar' is used for both the eye and the inner vision of the heart. This linguistic principle has theological implications for how we understand seeing Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic mas'ala and its translation. Notice the structure: a conditional statement (if someone says...), followed by a refutation using language.
Pay attention to the rhetorical flow.
2
Identify the linguistic argument
The author cites 'ahl al-lugha' (the people of language) as authority. Find how he uses their usage to argue for the dual meaning of 'basar'.
The phrase 'سمى أهل اللغة القلب بصراً' is key.
3
Consider the logical implication
The argument uses a reductio ad absurdum: if one can arbitrarily restrict 'basar' to the eye, another could restrict it to the heart. Since neither is permissible, the word must encompass both.
This is a classic argumentative move.

مسألة :
إذا قال قائل منهم : إن البصر في الحقيقة هو بصر العين لا بصر القلب. قيل له : ولم زعمت هذا؟ وقد سمى أهل اللغة القلب بصراً، كما سموا بصر العين بصراً؟ وإن جاز لك ما قلته جاز لغيركم أن يزعم أن البصر في الحقيقة هو بصر القلب دون العين وإذا لم يجز هذا فقد وجب أن البصر بصر العين وبصر القلب معناه؟
A question: If one of them says: The vision in reality is the vision of the eye, not the vision of the heart. It is said to him: Why do you claim this? While the people of language have called the heart "vision" just as they called the eye's vision "vision"? And if what you have said is permissible, then it is permissible for others to claim that vision in reality is the vision of the heart without the eye. And if this is not permissible, then it is necessary that vision is the vision of the eye and the vision of the heart in meaning.

Commentary
This is a separate question (mas'ala) about the definition of 'basar'. The opponent restricts the term to the physical eye. The author refutes by appealing to linguistic usage (ahl al-lugha) who use 'basar' for both the eye and the heart (e.g., in the Quran 'basar' is used for insight). The argument shows that if one can arbitrarily define 'basar' as only the eye, another could define it as only the heart. Since neither is exclusive, the term must encompass both in its meaning. This protects the possibility of seeing Allah with the heart (inner vision) and also with the eyes (in the Hereafter).

The Linguistic Scope of Basar (Sight)
Imam Ashari refutes restricting basar to physical sight via linguistic usage and reductio.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why 'basar' in classical Arabic applies to both physical sight and spiritual insight, based on linguistic evidence and logical consistency.

Core Idea
Basar in Arabic includes both eye and heart.

This passage presents a dialectical argument from Imam Ashari against those who restrict basar to physical sight. Before reading, note that the argument hinges on the authority of linguistic usage, not rational speculation. The opponent's claim is met with a linguistic counterexample and a reductio ad absurdum.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation once to get a sense of the argument's flow.
Pay attention to the question-and-response structure.
2
Identify the opponent's claim
Find the statement attributed to the opponent: that basar in reality is only the sight of the eye.
This is the starting point of the refutation.
3
Notice the linguistic evidence
Imam Ashari cites that the people of language have used basar for both the eye and the heart.
This is a direct appeal to established usage.
4
Understand the reductio argument
If the opponent's arbitrary restriction were allowed, someone else could restrict basar to the heart alone, leading to contradiction. Therefore, the only consistent position is to accept both meanings.
This shows the logical flaw in the opponent's position.
5
Apply the lesson
Reflect on how this argument supports the possibility of seeing Allah with the heart (spiritual vision) while affirming the literal meaning for the eyes.
This connects to broader theological discussions about ru'yat Allah.

Commentary
This passage is a question (مسألة) where Imam Ashari refutes an opponent who insists that 'basar' (sight) only refers to the physical eye. The argument is linguistic: the Arabs (people of language) have used 'basar' for both the eye and the heart (mental insight). The opponent's restriction is arbitrary. If they can arbitrarily restrict 'basar' to the eye, someone else could arbitrarily restrict it to the heart. Since that leads to contradiction, the correct understanding is that 'basar' encompasses both meanings. This supports the possibility of seeing Allah with the heart (spiritual vision) while also affirming the literal meaning for the eyes.
