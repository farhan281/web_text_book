Imagination's Limits for Divine Actions
Razi presents four examples showing imagination fails to grasp God's creative actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why human imagination cannot comprehend God's creative actions, as illustrated by four specific examples.

Core Idea
Divine creation defies the patterns our minds expect from worldly experience.

Razi begins the tenth point by establishing that knowledge of God's actions and attributes is closer to the intellect than knowledge of His essence, yet it still contradicts sense and imagination. He then presents four examples related to God's actions to show how our mental habits fall short of divine reality. This lesson unpacks those examples.

Guided Path
1
Set the context
Read the opening lines where Razi rejects the idea of time as imaginary and aligns with Ahl al-Tawhid. Understand that the tenth point argues for the inadequacy of imagination regarding divine acts and attributes, and note that even anthropomorphists agree on this.
This sets up the framework for the examples.
2
Examine the first two action examples
Read the first example: we only see transformation of existing matter, yet God creates from nothing. Then the second: we think of origination within time, yet time itself is created. Notice how each example contrasts common experience with divine action.
3
Examine the third and fourth action examples
Read the third: agents act only through change of state, but God creates without any change. Then the fourth: agents act for benefit or harm, but God acts without need. Reflect on how imagination cannot grasp these aspects.
4
Synthesize the principle
Consider the pattern: each example highlights a limitation of imagination. The principle is that divine actions are not bound by the patterns we observe in creation.
This prepares for the next lesson on attributes.

فثبت : أن الوهم والخيال قاصران عن معرفة أفعال الله وتعالى - وصفاته . ومع ذلك فإنا نثبت الأفعال والصفات على مخالفة
Time: an imaginary, false statement. That is the statement of our companions, the people of tawhid and tanzih, who removed the judgment of wahm (imagination) and khayal (fantasy) from the essence of Allah and His attributes. That is the upright path and the straight way.

The tenth: Knowledge of Allah's actions and attributes is closer to intellects than knowledge of His essence. Then the anthropomorphists agreed with us that knowledge of Allah's actions and attributes is contrary to the ruling of sense and imagination.

As for establishing this meaning concerning Allah's actions, that is through aspects: One: What we have witnessed is the transformation of attributes, such as the transformation of water and earth into plants, and the transformation of plants into part of an animal's body. As for the origination of animals initially, without prior matter and clay: this is something we have never witnessed, and our imagination and fantasy do not judge it possible, while we affirm that He is the originator of animals initially, without prior matter and clay.

Second: We do not conceive of the origination and becoming of something except in a specific time. Then we judged that time originated not in time at all.

Third: We do not conceive of an agent acting after having not been an agent except due to a change of state and alteration of attribute. Then we acknowledged that He created the world without any of that.

Fourth: We do not conceive of an agent performing an action except for gaining benefit or repelling harm. Then we acknowledged that He is the creator of the world without any of this.

As for establishing this meaning concerning attributes, that is through aspects:

One: We do not conceive of an essence being knower of infinite things in detail all at once. For if we test ourselves, we find that when we occupy ourselves with summoning a specific known thing, we are prevented in that state from summoning another known thing. Then despite that we believe that He is knower of infinite things in detail without any confusion or ambiguity. So His being knower of all things is contrary to what is required by imagination and fantasy.

Second: We see that everyone who does an action must have tools and instruments, and that difficult actions cause fatigue and hardship for the agent. Then we believe that He manages from the Throne to beneath the earth, while He is exalted above fatigue, weariness, and exhaustion.

Third: We believe that He hears the voices of creation from the Throne to beneath the earth, and sees the small and the large, above the layers of the high heavens and beneath the low earths. And it is known that human imagination and fantasy are incapable of acknowledging this existence. Despite that we believe that He is such.

So it is established that imagination and fantasy are incapable of knowing Allah's actions and attributes. Yet we affirm actions and attributes contrary to [what imagination dictates].

Commentary
This passage is an argument establishing the inadequacy of imagination (wahm) and fantasy (khayal) for knowing God's actions and attributes. It begins by rejecting the idea that time is imaginary, affirming the position of Ahl al-Tawhid who separate imagination from God's essence. The tenth point is introduced: knowledge of God's actions and attributes is more accessible to the intellect than knowledge of His essence, and even anthropomorphists agree that such knowledge contradicts sense and imagination. Razi then provides seven examples: four concerning actions and three concerning attributes. The first action example: we only observe transformations of existing things (like water to plants), but we affirm God creates animals from nothing, which imagination cannot grasp. Second: we think of origination only within time, yet we judge time itself created without time. Third: we expect a change of state when an agent acts, but God created without any change. Fourth: we expect benefit or harm as motives, but God acts without need. For attributes: first, our minds cannot know infinite detailed things simultaneously, but God does without confusion. Second, worldly agents need tools and tire, but God manages all without fatigue. Third, God hears and sees all things everywhere, which imagination fails to comprehend. The conclusion is clear: imagination is deficient, yet we affirm these truths about God.

Imagination's Limits for Divine Attributes
Razi presents three examples showing imagination fails to grasp God's attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how three examples concerning divine attributes show that imagination cannot grasp God's omniscience, omnipotence, and all-encompassing perception.

Core Idea
God's attributes surpass imagination—He knows infinite details, acts without tools, and perceives all without limitation.

Having shown the inadequacy of imagination for divine actions, Razi now turns to divine attributes. He provides three examples: God's knowledge of infinite particulars simultaneously, His management without tools or fatigue, and His all-encompassing hearing and sight. Each example challenges what we normally conceive. This lesson explores these profound truths.

Guided Path
1
Read the transition and first attribute example
Notice the shift from actions to attributes. Read the first example: we cannot know multiple things at once, but God knows infinite details simultaneously without confusion. Reflect on why imagination fails here.
2
Examine the second attribute example
Read the second: worldly agents need tools and get tired, but God manages all creation effortlessly, from the Throne to beneath the earth. Consider how this contradicts our experience.
3
Examine the third attribute example
Read the third: God hears all voices and sees all things, everywhere, without limitation. Imagination cannot accept this, yet we affirm it.
4
Understand the conclusion
Read the final lines where Razi asserts that imagination is indeed incapable of knowing God's actions and attributes, yet we affirm them on the basis of revelation and reason.
This reinforces the theme of tanzih (transcendence).

The Possibility of a Unique Existence Without Likeness
Razi introduces the second introduction and gives the first proof from intellectual intuition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that denying a peer does not imply denying existence, and how intuitive reason supports the possibility of a unique divine being.

Core Idea
Uniqueness does not require a peer; intuition confirms possibility.

Razi begins the second introduction by establishing a crucial principle: not every existent must have a likeness. He then presents the first proof from the intuitive grasp of reason. This argument clears the ground for discussing God's absolute uniqueness by showing that a being with exclusive attributes is rationally conceivable.

3

Commentary
This passage begins the second introduction of the book. The author states the topic: not every existent must have a likeness or peer, and denying a peer does not entail denying the thing itself. He then presents the first proof: the primary intuitions of reason do not reject the possibility of an existent that has unique attributes with nothing else sharing that specificity. Since this possibility is not ruled out by intuition, we know that the lack of a peer does not imply non-existence. This is a direct argument for the possibility of God's uniqueness.

Second Proof: Existence Does Not Depend on a Like
Al-Razi gives the second proof against requiring a like for existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the existence of a thing does not require a similar being, based on the argument against circular dependence.

Core Idea
Existence stands alone; requiring a like leads to absurd circularity.

Imam al-Razi presents the second argument against the claim that every existent must have a like. He demonstrates that if existence depended on a like, it would lead to an impossible self-dependence or circularity. Thus, the absence of a like does not imply non-existence.

وأما المعقول : فهو أن الأفعال التى كلفنا بها قسمان : منها ما نعرف الحكمة فيه على الجملة بعقولنا ، كالصلاة والزكاة والصوم . فإن الصلاة تواضع وتضرع للخالق ، والزكاة إحسان إلى المحتاجين، والصوم قهر النفس . ومنها ما لا نعرف وجه الحكمة فيه ، كأفعال الحج. فإنا لا نعرف وجه الحكمة في رمي الجمرات ، والسعى بين الصفا والمروة . ثم اتفق المحققون على أنه كما يحسن من الحكيم تعالى أن يأمر عباده بالنوع الأول ، فكذا يحسن بالنوع الثاني . لأن الطاعة من النوع الأول لا تدل على كمال الانقياد ، لاحتمال أن المأمور ، إنما أتى به لما عرفه بعقله من وجه المصلحة فيه . أما الطاعة في النوع الثاني فإنها تدل على كمال الانقياد ، ونهاية التسليم ، لأنه لما لم يعرف فيه وجه المصلحة البتة ، لم يكن إتيانه به ، إلا لمحض الانقياد والتسليم . وإذا كان الأمر كذلك في الأفعال ، فلم لا يجوز الأمر كذلك في الأقوال ؟ وهو أن الذى أنزله الله علينا ، وأمرنا بتعظيمه في قرآنه ، ينقسم إلى قسمين : منه ما يعرف معناه ونحيط بفحواه ، ومنه ما لا تعرف معناه البتة ، ويكون المقصود من إنزاله والتكليف بقراءته وتعظيمه : ظهور كمال العبودية والانقياد لأوامر الله تعالى . بل ههنا فائدة أخرى : وهى أن الإنسان إذا وقف على المعنى، وأحاط به ، سقط وقعه عن القلب ، وإذا لم يقف على المقصود مع جزمه بأن المتكلم بذلك أحكم الحاكمين ، فإنه يبقى قلبه ملتفتا إليه أبدا ، ومفتكرا فيه أبدا . ولُباب التكليف : اشتغال السر بذكر الله تعالى ، والتفكر في كلامه . فلا يبعد أن يقال : إن فى بقاء العبد ملتفت الذهن مشتغل الخاطر بذلك أبدا : مصلحة عظيمة له ، فيتعبد الله تعالى بذلك ، تحصيلا لهذه المصلحة . فهذا ما عندى من كلام الفريقين في هذا الباب ، وبالله التوفيق.
As for the rational: it is that the actions with which we are commanded are of two divisions: among them is that whose wisdom we know in general by our intellects, like prayer, zakat, and fasting. For prayer is humility and supplication to the Creator, zakat is beneficence to the needy, and fasting is subduing the self. And among them is that whose aspect of wisdom we do not know, like the actions of Hajj. For we do not know the aspect of wisdom in stoning the jamarat, and the running between Safa and Marwa. Then the verifiers agreed that just as it is fitting from the Wise, exalted is He, to command His servants with the first type, so too it is fitting with the second type. Because obedience in the first type does not indicate perfection of submission, due to the possibility that the commanded one only performed it because he knew by his intellect some aspect of benefit in it. As for obedience in the second type, it indicates perfection of submission and the utmost surrender, because since he did not know any aspect of benefit in it at all, his performance of it was only for pure submission and surrender. And if the matter is so concerning actions, then why is it not permissible that the matter be so concerning words? That is, what He revealed to us and commanded us to venerate in His Quran divides into two divisions: among it is that whose meaning we know and comprehend its purport, and among it is that whose meaning we do not know at all, and the purpose of its revelation and the obligation of its recitation and veneration is the manifestation of the perfection of servitude and submission to the commands of God Almighty. Rather, there is another benefit here: that a person, when he understands the meaning and comprehends it, its impact falls from the heart; and when he does not understand the intended meaning while being certain that the Speaker of that is the Most Just of judges, his heart remains perpetually attentive to it and perpetually reflecting on it. And the core of obligation is the occupation of the innermost self with the remembrance of God Almighty and reflection on His speech. So it is not far-fetched to say that in the servant remaining attentive of mind and occupied of thought with that perpetually, there is a great benefit for him, so God Almighty is worshipped by that, to attain this benefit. This is what I have from the discourse of the two groups on this topic, and with God is success.

Commentary
This passage presents a rational argument defending the presence of commands in revelation whose meaning is not fully understood. The author begins by classifying commanded actions into two types: those whose wisdom we can grasp rationally (like prayer, zakat, fasting) and those whose specific wisdom we do not know (like the details of Hajj). He then notes that scholars agree both types are appropriate from the Wise God. The key argument is that obedience in the first type may not demonstrate complete submission because the person might be motivated by the perceived benefit. In contrast, obedience in the second type—where no benefit is apparent—proves perfect submission and surrender, as the action is done solely out of obedience. The author then extends this reasoning from actions to words (i.e., verses of the Quran). If this principle holds for actions, it should also hold for statements: some Quranic verses are clear in meaning, others are not. The purpose of such verses is to manifest perfect servitude and submission to God's commands. Moreover, the author adds an additional benefit: when a person fully understands a meaning, its impact on the heart diminishes; but when the meaning remains unknown while one remains certain of the Speaker's perfect wisdom, the heart stays constantly attentive and engaged. Since the core of obligation is occupation with remembrance and reflection, the perpetual mental engagement with such verses serves a great benefit, and thus God is worshipped through that engagement. The passage concludes by noting this is the sum of the arguments from both sides on this issue, and success is from God.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Commands whose wisdom is unknown are arbitrary or pointless.
Correction: Razi shows they are not arbitrary; they serve to demonstrate perfect submission and are fitting from the Wise God.

Checkpoint
According to Razi's argument, what does obedience in actions whose wisdom we do not know (like Hajj rituals) primarily demonstrate?
A) The wisdom of the action is hidden but can be discovered through reason.
B) The action has no real benefit.
C) Perfect submission and complete surrender to God.
D) The action is only for the sake of tradition.

Key Takeaway
Not all divine commands need to be rationally understood; those without apparent wisdom serve to demonstrate perfect submission.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you perform an act of worship whose wisdom you do not grasp—like a specific Hajj ritual—do you feel your obedience is more pure, or do you find yourself seeking a reason? What would change if you truly performed it solely out of submission, as Razi describes?
?
How often do you try to understand the wisdom behind every divine command before obeying? Does your faith depend on rational satisfaction? Reflect on one command you find difficult to accept without understanding—what would it cost you to obey it anyway, just because God commanded?
?
Razi says that obedience without known benefit indicates 'perfection of submission' and 'utmost surrender.' In your daily life, where do you fall short of this ideal? Name one area where you hold back from full obedience because you don't see the benefit. What would it look like to surrender completely in that area?

The Wisdom Beyond Understanding: Unclear Quranic Verses
Razi extends the argument from actions to words and highlights the benefit of unclear meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi extends the same logic to Quranic verses whose meanings are unclear, and how such verses serve the purpose of keeping the heart perpetually engaged with God's speech.

Core Idea
Unclear meanings keep the heart perpetually engaged with God's speech.

Having established the principle with actions, Imam Razi now applies it to the words of the Quran. He argues that just as some commanded actions are not understood, some Quranic verses are not understood, and that this is not a deficiency but a design that manifests perfect servitude and keeps the believer's heart constantly reflecting on God's speech. This lesson explores that extension and the profound benefit of not always understanding.

Guided Path
1
See the parallel between actions and words
Razi asks: if the principle holds for actions, why not for words? Read how he divides Quranic verses into those whose meaning is known and those not known.
This shows the consistency of his reasoning.
2
Understand the additional benefit
Razi explains a further benefit: when a meaning is understood, its impact on the heart diminishes. When it is not understood but the Speaker is known to be the Wisest, the heart remains perpetually attentive and reflective.
This is a psychological and spiritual insight.
3
Realize the core of obligation
Razi concludes that the core of obligation is occupation with remembrance and reflection. Unclear verses keep the heart engaged, fulfilling this core. Thus, they are not pointless but highly beneficial.
