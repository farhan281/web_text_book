Proof: Existent Must Be Body, In Body, or Neither
Razi argues the logical necessity of a threefold division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a logical division of existents must include a third category (neither body nor in a body) for it to be exhaustive, as argued by Imam Razi.

Core Idea
A complete logical division requires three categories, and the intellect affirms this.

Imam Razi now presents a logical proof that the intellect demands a threefold division of existents. He shows that a simple twofold division into body or in a body is incomplete. Only when we add the third possibility—neither body nor in a body—does the division become exhaustive. This third category is necessary for the intellect to accept the division as sound, and it directly relates to the nature of Allah, who transcends both body and location.

The Arabic ends mid-sentence; the final phrase implies the third category.
2
Understand the twofold division's weakness
Razi states that if we only use two categories (body or in a body), the division is necessarily incomplete and not exhaustive.
The intellect immediately recognizes the need for a third category.
3
Grasp the role of the third category
The third category—neither body nor in a body—completes the division and makes it sound. This is the category for Allah.
4
Connect to theology
This logical proof establishes that Allah cannot be a body or in a body; He must belong to the third category, affirming His transcendence.
This is a rational proof for tanzih (divine transcendence).

Commentary
This passage presents Razi's third proof, which is a logical argument about exhaustive division (taqsīm). He states: if we propose a threefold division of existents—(1) a body (mutahayyiz), (2) indwelling in a body (ḥāll fī al-mutahayyiz), or (3) neither a body nor indwelling in a body—then the intellect judges this division to be sound (qāṭiʿ bi-ṣiḥḥa). However, if we propose only a twofold division—either a body or indwelling in a body—we know necessarily that this division is incomplete (ghayr tāmm) and not exhaustive (lā munḥaṣir). It can only be completed by adding the third category: an existent that is neither a body nor indwelling in a body. The Arabic text cuts off at the beginning of the third category ('or it is...'), but the argument's conclusion is clear: the twofold division is insufficient, and the third category (which applies to God) is logically necessary for a complete division.

Third Objection: Relations as Real Accidents
Razi argues that relations are real accidents, neither immanent nor separate, refuting the dichotomy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that relations are real, existent attributes that are neither immanent in the world nor separate from it, based on the distinction between essence and relation.

Core Idea
Relations are real accidents, not mere concepts, and they defy the immanence/transcendence dichotomy.

In this third objection, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi targets the claim that all accidents must be either immanent (within the world) or separate (beyond it). He argues that relations—like fatherhood—are real, existent attributes that cannot be placed in either category. By proving that being a father is an additional existent attribute beyond the essence, he sets up a refutation of the opponent's dichotomy.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage carefully
Read the Arabic text once to get the flow of the argument. Notice the structure: Razi first states his claim, then offers two proofs, then analyzes the nature of the relation, and concludes (though cut off).
Pay attention to the terms: الإضافات, الأعراض, الأعيان, الأبوة.
2
Understand the two proofs
Razi gives two reasons why the relation 'father' is distinct from the essence: (1) we can conceive of the essence while forgetting the relation, and (2) an essence can exist without any relation (e.g., Jesus). These show that the relation is an additional attribute.
This is a standard argument in kalam for the reality of relations.
3
Analyze the disjunction
Razi then says the additional factor must be either a negative attribute (salbī) or an affirmative one (thubūtī). He argues it cannot be negative because paternity removes the negation of fatherhood, and removing non-existence results in existence.
This is a logical move: if the relation were merely the absence of something, it couldn't be a real addition.
4
Connect to the overall objection
Having established that relations are existent attributes, Razi claims they can be neither immanent nor separate directionally, which would refute the opponent's dichotomy. The argument is cut off, but you can see the direction.
The lesson ends at the threshold of the conclusion; realize that the full objection would complete the refutation.
5
Reflect on the misconception
Many think relations are just mental concepts. Razi insists they are real, external attributes. Ask yourself why this matters for divine transcendence: if relations are real, how can Allah have relations without being immanent?
This anticipates later discussions about divine attributes.

Second Question: The Receptivity of Division is Non-Existent
Imam Razi argues that the capacity for division is a non-existent judgment and cannot be caused.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Imam Razi argues that the capacity for division is a non-existent judgment and thus cannot be caused, and how this argument fits into his defense of the original dichotomy.

Core Idea
The capacity for division is not a real attribute; it is a non-existent judgment requiring no cause.

Imam Razi now addresses a hypothetical objection. Even if we grant that every two existents in the witnessed world must be related by immanence or transcendence, we might still ask: what about the capacity for being divided into these two? Razi argues that this capacity is not a real positive attribute but a non-existent judgment. Since non-existence cannot be caused, no further explanation is needed. This short but decisive argument blocks a potential infinite regress.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note the concession
Begin by reading the Arabic and translation. Notice that Razi starts by granting a premise for argument's sake ('We grant...'). Identify what is being conceded.
2
Identify the key claim about receptivity
Find where Razi states that the receptivity of division is a non-existent judgment (hukm 'adami). This is the central thesis.
He uses the term 'qabul al-qisma'.
3
Follow the proof: infinite regress
Trace the reasoning that the principle of receptivity itself cannot be a positive attribute because that would lead to infinite regress. Understand why this makes the principle non-existent.
Look for the phrase 'yalzam al-tasalsul'.
4
Understand the second part: specificity
Razi then argues that the specific receptivity of division is also non-existent because if its specificity were existent, existence would subsist in non-existence, which is impossible.
This reinforces the main conclusion.
5
Conclude with the implication
Finally, note the conclusion: since it is a non-existent judgment, it cannot be caused. This blocks the need for a cause for division.

Commentary
This passage is a response to a hypothetical concession (the 'second question'). Imam Razi concedes for argument's sake that every two existents in the witnessed world are either immanent or transcendent. However, he then examines what it means for a thing to be such that it can be said 'either it is or it is not' – this indicates that the thing is capable of division (qabil li al-qisma) into the two alternatives. The key move is to argue that this 'receptivity of division' (qabul al-qisma) is not a real positive attribute; rather, it is a non-existent judgment (hukm 'adami). The proof is as follows: The principle of receptivity (asl al-qabul) itself is non-existent because if it were a real attribute, it would require another receptivity to receive it, leading to infinite regress. Therefore, the principle is non-existent. Consequently, the specific receptivity of division, being a kind of receptivity, is also non-existent. Furthermore, if the specificity of this receptivity were existent, it would require existence to subsist in non-existence, which is impossible. Thus, the receptivity of division is a non-existent judgment. Since non-existence is pure negation, it cannot be caused (ta'lil). This argument blocks any attempt to demand a cause for why a thing is capable of division, thereby preserving the original dichotomy without needing to explain it.

Receptivity to Division as a Negative Attribute
Imam Razi responds that receptivity to division is non-existent and cannot be caused.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why receptivity to division is considered a non-existent attribute and why it cannot be caused.

Core Idea
Non-existence cannot be caused; therefore receptivity to division cannot be a caused attribute.

In this passage, Imam Razi responds to an objection that assumes receptivity to division is a real attribute that must be caused. He argues, through careful logical steps, that receptivity to division is actually a non-existent attribute (حكم عدمي), and since non-existence cannot be influenced by a cause, the objection fails. This lesson walks through his reasoning.

Read the passage carefully

'حكم عدمى' means an attribute that is not real in the external world.

Razi shows that the origin of receptivity (أصل القبول) cannot be an existent attribute because that would lead to infinite regress. Therefore, it must be non-existent.
This is a classic kalam argument against infinite regress.

Since the general receptivity is non-existent, the specific receptivity to division is also non-existent. If its specificity were existent, existence would subsist in non-existence, which is impossible.
This seals the proof.
5
Conclusion: cannot be caused
Non-existence is pure negation, so a cause cannot act upon it. Therefore, the objection that receptivity to division must be caused is invalid.
This refutes the opponent's premise.

So when you know this, we say: The word nafs with respect to God Almighty is nothing but the essence (al-dhat) and reality (al-haqiqa). So His saying: 'And I have chosen you for Myself' (Quran 20:41) is like an emphasis indicating increased intensity. For when a man says: 'I made this house for myself and built it for myself,' intensity is understood from it. And His saying: 'You know what is in my nafs and I do not know what is in Your nafs' (Quran 5:116) means: You know my knowledge, and I do not know Your knowledge. And likewise is the case with the rest of the verses. As for his saying, peace be upon him, relating from the Lord of Might: 'If he remembers Me in his nafs, I remember him in My nafs,' the meaning is: if he remembers Me in such a way that none other than him is aware of it, I remember him with My favor and beneficence without any of My servants knowing it. Because remembrance in the nafs is an expression of hidden speech and recollection concealed in the nafs. And that is permissible for God Almighty. As for his saying: 'Glory be to God, the weight of His Throne, and the pleasure of His nafs,' the meaning is: what God Almighty is pleased with for Himself and His essence, i.e., glorification befitting Him. As for his saying, peace be upon him: 'He wrote a book upon His nafs,' the meaning is: He wrote a book and made it obligatory to act by it. And the meaning of His saying 'upon His nafs' is emphasis and intensity in obligation and necessity. So it is established that what is meant by 'nafs' in these places is the essence (al-dhat), and that the purpose of mentioning this word is emphasis and intensification. And with God is success.

Commentary
This passage is a lexical and theological analysis by Imam Razi of the Arabic word 'nafs'. He first enumerates five distinct meanings found in the Arabic language, each supported by Qur'anic verses or common usage: (1) the body, (2) blood, (3) the spirit, (4) the intellect, and (5) the essence or self of a thing. After establishing these meanings, he applies the fifth meaning to God, arguing that when the word 'nafs' is attributed to God in the Qur'an and hadith, it invariably refers to His essence (al-dhat) and not to any physical or created attribute. He then interprets specific Qur'anic verses and prophetic hadiths that use the word 'nafs' in the context of God, showing that they must be understood as expressions of emphasis and intensification, not as implying any literal limitation or component. For example, in the verse 'I have chosen you for Myself,' the phrase 'for Myself' underscores the special favor to Moses. Similarly, the hadith 'if he remembers Me in his nafs, I remember him in My nafs' means that God responds to hidden remembrance with hidden reward. The concluding hadith about God writing 'upon His nafs' indicates a firm obligation. Razi's overall argument is that the term 'nafs' when used of God is unambiguously a reference to His essence, and its function is to emphasize the action or attribute in question, thereby avoiding any anthropomorphic misunderstanding.

Four Quranic Proofs that the Quran is Comprehensible
Imam Razi enumerates four Quranic proofs that the Quran must be knowable.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Classification of Words: Nass, Zahir, Mujmal, Mu'awwal
Razi defines nass, zahir, mujmal, mu'awwal and links them to muhkam and mutashabih.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four categories of words (nass, zahir, mujmal, mu'awwal) and how they relate to the Qur'anic concepts of muhkam and mutashabih.

Core Idea
Words are classified by the strength of their probability for different meanings.

Imam Razi begins by establishing a key principle: words can have multiple possible meanings. The strength of a word's probability for one meaning over another determines its classification. In this lesson, you will learn the four categories that emerge from this principle and how they correspond to the firm (muhkam) and ambiguous (mutashabih) verses of the Qur'an.

Guided Path
1
Understand the basic principle
Razi states that when a word has a stronger probability for one meaning over another, it is called 'apparent' (zahir) for the stronger and 'interpreted' (mu'awwal) for the weaker. When probabilities are equal, it is 'common' (mushtarak).
2
Identify the four categories
From this principle, Razi derives four types: plain (nass), apparent (zahir), ambiguous (mujmal), and interpreted (mu'awwal). Note how nass and zahir share preference (tarjih), but nass prevents the opposite while zahir does not.
3
Connect to muhkam and mutashabih
Razi states that the quality of nass and zahir is called muhkam (firm). The ambiguous and interpreted share lack of preference; the interpreted is called mutashabih (ambiguous) because understanding is absent.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of words (alfaz) based on the strength of their probability (tarjih) between two or more possible meanings. The author, Imam Razi, begins by establishing the principle: if a word has a stronger probability for one meaning over another, it is called 'apparent' (zahir) for the stronger and 'interpreted' (mu'awwal) for the weaker; if probabilities are equal, the word is 'common' (mushtarak) between both meanings collectively and 'possible' (muhtamal) for each individually. From this, he derives four categories: plain (nass), apparent (zahir), ambiguous (mujmal), and interpreted (mu'awwal). He then pairs them: plain and apparent share the quality of having preference (tarjih), but the plain prevents the opposite meaning while the apparent does not. This level of clarity is called 'firm' (muhkam). The ambiguous and interpreted share the lack of preference in the word's indication, but the ambiguous has no preference for either side, while the interpreted has a preference for one side of the common meaning—specifically, the lack of preference for the other side. The interpreted is called 'ambiguous' (mutashabih) because understanding is absent. Next, Razi addresses a special case: when the word's two meanings are equally likely, the mind is suspended, such as the word 'qur'' meaning either menstruation or purity. The truly difficult problem arises when the literal coinage gives a preference to one meaning, but that preferred meaning is false and the less preferred is true. He provides two Qur'anic examples. The first: 'And when We intend to destroy a township, We command its affluent ones and they act wickedly therein' (17:16). The apparent meaning is that Allah commands them to act wickedly, but the muhkam verse 'Indeed, Allah does not command immorality' (7:28) refutes that, showing the true meaning is that they were commanded to obey but they chose wickedness. The second: 'They forgot Allah, so He forgot them' (9:67). The apparent meaning of 'forgetting' is the common one (forgetfulness), but the muhkam verses 'And your Lord is not forgetful' (19:64) and 'My Lord does not err nor does He forget' (20:52) show that the intended meaning here is abandonment. Thus, the ambiguous (mutashabih) verses must be interpreted in light of the firm (muhkam) verses.

Interpreting Ambiguous Verses: The Principle of Ta'wil
Razi discusses the problematic case and illustrates ta'wil with Qur'anic examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rule for interpreting ambiguous verses when the apparent meaning conflicts with the firm (muhkam) verses, using Qur'anic examples.

Core Idea
When the apparent meaning is false, the less probable meaning is intended.

In the previous lesson, you learned the categories of clear and ambiguous words. Now, Imam Razi addresses a special problem: when the literal coinage of a word gives preference to one meaning, but that meaning contradicts the firm verses of the Qur'an. In such cases, the less probable meaning must be taken, as demonstrated by two powerful examples.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the special case
Razi first notes that when two meanings are equally probable, the mind halts—like the word 'qur''. But the real problem is when the preferred meaning is false and the less preferred is true.
2
Analyze the first example
The verse 'When We intend to destroy a township, We command its affluent ones and they act wickedly' (17:16). The apparent meaning is that Allah commands wickedness. But the muhkam verse 'Allah does not command immorality' (7:28) shows this cannot be the intended meaning. Thus, the command must be interpreted as 'command to obey' which they disobeyed.
3
Analyze the second example
The verse 'They forgot Allah, so He forgot them' (9:67). The apparent meaning of forgetting is forgetfulness. But muhkam verses 'Your Lord is not forgetful' (19:64) and 'My Lord does not err nor does He forget' (20:52) show that here 'forgetting' means abandonment.
4
Understand the principle
The principle is that the ambiguous (mutashabih) verses must be interpreted in light of the firm (muhkam) verses. This is the basis of ta'wil.

If its probability is stronger for one of the two meanings over its probability for the other, then that word is, with respect to the stronger meaning, apparent (zahir), and with respect to the weaker meaning, interpreted (mu'awwal). But if its probability for them is equal, then the word is, with respect to both together, common (mushtarak), and with respect to each one individually, a possible meaning (muhtamal). From this division emerges that the word is either plain (nass), or apparent (zahir), or ambiguous (mujmal), or interpreted (mu'awwal). The plain and the apparent share in possessing preference (tarjih), except that the plain is preferred and prevents the opposite, while the apparent is preferred but does not prevent the opposite. This amount is what is called the firm (muhkam). As for the ambiguous and the interpreted, they share in that the indication of the word is not preferred, except that the ambiguous has no preference with respect to either of the two sides, and the interpreted has a preference with respect to one side of the common meaning, which is the lack of preference with respect to it, and it is called the ambiguous (mutashabih), because lack of understanding occurs in it. Then know: if the word is, with respect to two meanings, equal, then the mind halts, like 'qur'' with respect to menstruation and purity. The truly difficult problem is that the word, by its original coinage, is preferred in one of the two meanings and less preferred in the other, and then the preferred meaning is false and the less preferred is true. An example from the Qur'an: His statement, the Most High: 'And when We intend to destroy a township, We command its affluent ones and they act wickedly therein.' The apparent (zahir) of this speech is that they are commanded to act wickedly. Its firm (muhkam) is His statement, the Most High: 'Indeed, Allah does not command immorality,' as a refutation of the disbelievers regarding what is narrated about them: When they commit an immorality, they say, 'We found our fathers upon it, and Allah has commanded us to do it.' Likewise His statement, the Most High: 'They forgot Allah, so He forgot them.' The apparent (zahir) of forgetting is that which occurs with knowledge, and its less preferred meaning is abandonment in His statement, the Most High: 'So He made them forget themselves.' Its firm (muhkam) is His statement: 'And your Lord is not forgetful' and His statement: 'My Lord does not err nor does He forget.' This is a summary of the discourse on the interpretation of the firm (muhkam) and the ambiguous (mutashabih). And with Allah is success.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of words (alfaz) based on the strength of their probability (tarjih) between two or more possible meanings. The author, Imam Razi, begins by establishing the principle: if a word has a stronger probability for one meaning over another, it is called 'apparent' (zahir) for the stronger and 'interpreted' (mu'awwal) for the weaker; if probabilities are equal, the word is 'common' (mushtarak) between both meanings collectively and 'possible' (muhtamal) for each individually. From this, he derives four categories: plain (nass), apparent (zahir), ambiguous (mujmal), and interpreted (mu'awwal). He then pairs them: plain and apparent share the quality of having preference (tarjih), but the plain prevents the opposite meaning while the apparent does not. This level of clarity is called 'firm' (muhkam). The ambiguous and interpreted share the lack of preference in the word's indication, but the ambiguous has no preference for either side, while the interpreted has a preference for one side of the common meaning—specifically, the lack of preference for the other side. The interpreted is called 'ambiguous' (mutashabih) because understanding is absent. Next, Razi addresses a special case: when the word's two meanings are equally likely, the mind is suspended, such as the word 'qur'' meaning either menstruation or purity. The truly difficult problem arises when the literal coinage gives a preference to one meaning, but that preferred meaning is false and the less preferred is true. He provides two Qur'anic examples. The first: 'And when We intend to destroy a township, We command its affluent ones and they act wickedly therein' (17:16). The apparent meaning is that Allah commands them to act wickedly, but the muhkam verse 'Indeed, Allah does not command immorality' (7:28) refutes that, showing the true meaning is that they were commanded to obey but they chose wickedness. The second: 'They forgot Allah, so He forgot them' (9:67). The apparent meaning of 'forgetting' is the common one (forgetfulness), but the muhkam verses 'And your Lord is not forgetful' (19:64) and 'My Lord does not err nor does He forget' (20:52) show that the intended meaning here is abandonment. Thus, the ambiguous (mutashabih) verses must be interpreted in light of the firm (muhkam) verses.

Ibn Taymiyyah's Inconsistency on Metaphor in Quran
Author argues that Ibn Taymiyyah's acceptance of contextual metaphor contradicts his denial of majaz in Quran.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author exposes Ibn Taymiyyah's contradiction: acknowledging that context determines metaphor yet forbidding it for the Quran and divine attributes.

Core Idea
Context, not the word, makes metaphor — a principle Ibn Taymiyyah accepts but inconsistently rejects for the Quran.

This short passage from Imam Razi's work highlights a critical logical inconsistency in Ibn Taymiyyah's approach to metaphor. By agreeing with rhetoricians that a word like 'lion' is only metaphorical because of context, Ibn Taymiyyah undermines his own ban on metaphor in the Quran. Quranic Metaphor Predates Scholarly Conventions
Author argues Quranic metaphor predates later scholarly convention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the Quran contains metaphorical language that was understood before scholars formalized the terms 'literal' and 'metaphorical'.

Core Idea
Metaphor in the Quran is older than the scholarly terms for it.

This passage presents a second argument against Ibn Taymiyyah's rejection of metaphor in the Quran. The author shows that the Quran itself contains clear metaphorical expressions, like 'light running before them,' which were understood by the early Arabs long before scholars coined the terms 'ḥaqīqa' and 'majāz.' Understanding this helps us see that the literal-metaphorical distinction is a useful analytical tool, not an innovation that contradicts the Quran.

Commentary
This passage is a second argument against Ibn Taymiyyah's rejection of metaphor in the Quran. The author argues that labeling words as literal (ḥaqīqa) and metaphorical (majāz) is a later scholarly convention (iṣṭilāḥ muḥdath). The Arabic language and the Quran existed before this convention. The verse 'their light running before them' (Quran 57:12) is cited as an example: light does not literally run, so the word 'runs' is metaphorical because context indicates a comparison to a man walking. This metaphorical usage was understood before the scholars formalized the division. The author then warns that rejecting later conventions entirely would absurdly deny the scientific miracles (i'jāz) of the Quran, since those scientific terms are also later conventions. Thus, Ibn Taymiyyah's position is untenable.
