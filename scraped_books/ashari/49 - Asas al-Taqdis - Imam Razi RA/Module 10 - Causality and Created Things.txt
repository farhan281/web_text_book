The Eighth Objection: Existence as Cause of Divisibility Leads to Absurdity
Eighth objection: reductio on existence as cause of divisibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection that existence as cause leads to absurd divisibility is rebutted by showing that specific essences can block general causal claims, which then applies to God's essence as well.

Core Idea
The opponent's own admission undermines their objection.

This passage presents a clever reductio argument. The objector tries to show that if existence were the cause of the judgment of immanence or transcendence, then by analogy existence would cause divisibility into substance and accident, leading to absurdity. But the opponent's response reveals a key flaw: if specific essences can block a general cause, then God's specific essence can also block the judgment, neutralizing the objection.

Guided Path
1
Understand the reductio argument
The objector argues that if existence causes the judgment (immanent or transcendent), then by the same reasoning, existence should cause divisibility into substance and accident. Since that leads to absurdities (substance dividing into substance and accident, etc.), the original claim must be false.
Notice the parallel: just as existence is claimed to cause the immanence/transcendence judgment, it would also cause divisibility.
2
Examine the opponent's response
The opponent replies that a 'preventer' (māniʿ) — the specific essence of each thing — blocks the divisibility. While existence makes divisibility possible in general, the specific essence prevents it from actually occurring.
This is a typical response in kalām: a general cause can be blocked by a specific condition.
3
See how the author turns the tables
The author points out that admitting a preventer undermines the objection. If specific essences can block the effect of existence in one case, then God's specific essence can block the judgment of immanence or transcendence. Thus the objection fails.
The author uses the opponent's own logic to defend the original thesis.

السؤال الثامن : سلمنا أن ما ذكرتم يدل على أن الوجود هو العلة لهذا الحكم. لكن هذا دليل آخر يمنع منه . وهو إن المقتضى لقبول الانقسام في الجوهر والعرض ، لو كان هو الوجود ، لزم في الجوهر وحده أن يقبل الانقسام، إلى الجوهر والعرض. وإنه محال . ولزم أيضا في العرض وحده، أن يقبل الانقسام إلى الجوهر وإلى العرض . ومعلوم أن ذلك محال . فإن قالوا : إن كل جوهر وعرض فإنه يصبح كونه منقسماً إلى هذين القسمين نظرا إلى كونه موجوداً ، وإنما يمتنع ذلك الانقسام نظراً إلى مانع منفك وهو خصوصية ماهيته . قلنا : هذا اعتراف بأنه لا يلزم من كون الوجود علة لصحة أمر من الأمور ، أن يصح ذلك الحكم على كل ما كان موصوفا بالوجود ، لاحتمال أن تكون ماهيته المخصوصة مانعة من ذلك الحكم . وإذا كان كذلك، فلم لا يجوز أن يقال : الوجود وإن اقتضى كون الشيء اما محايثا لغيره وإما مباينا عنه ، إلا أن خصوصية ذاته تعالى كانت مانعة من هذا الحكم ، فلم يلزم من كونه تعالى موجوداً ، كونه بحيث يكون إما محايثا للعالم أو مباينا عنه بالجهة ؟
The eighth question: We concede that what you have mentioned indicates that existence is the cause of this judgment. But there is another argument that prevents it. That is: if the thing that necessitates acceptance of division in substance and accident is existence, then it would follow in substance alone that it accepts division into substance and accident, and that is impossible. And it would also follow in accident alone that it accepts division into substance and accident. And it is known that that is impossible. If they say: Every substance and accident indeed becomes something that is divided into these two divisions in view of its being existent, but that division is prevented due to a separate preventer, which is the particularity of its essence. We say: This is an admission that from existence being the cause of the validity of a matter, it does not necessitate that judgment applies to everything described by existence, because it is possible that its specific essence prevents that judgment. If so, then why is it not permitted to say: Existence, though it entails that a thing is either immanent to another or transcendent from it, yet the particularity of His essence prevents this judgment, so it does not follow from His being existent that He is such that He is either immanent to the world or transcendent from it by direction?

Commentary
This passage is the eighth objection. The objector concedes the previous point but offers a new counterexample. If existence is the cause of the judgment 'either immanent or transcendent', then by analogy, existence should cause the property of being divisible into substance and accident. But if existence causes divisibility, then every substance would need to be divisible into substance and accident (which is absurd), and similarly for accidents. The opponent might respond that the particular essence (māhiyya) of each thing prevents that division. The author's reply is that this admission undermines the general principle: if existence's causal power can be blocked by specific essences, then why not say that God's specific essence blocks the judgment of immanence or transcendence? Thus the objection fails to refute the main thesis.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: If existence causes some property, that property must apply to all existents universally without exception.
Correction: Specific essences can act as preventers (mawāniʿ) that block the effect of a general cause, as admitted by the opponent.

Checkpoint
What does the opponent's response using a 'preventer' (مانع) imply according to the author?
A) Existence does not cause anything
B) Existence's effects can be blocked by specific essences, so the general claim is undermined
C) All things are divisible
D) God's essence is like other essences

Key Takeaway
Admitting that specific essences can block a general causal claim undermines any objection based on that claim, as it allows God's essence to block the judgment.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?

How might this assumption have shaped your understanding of God in a way that this lesson challenges?
?
Recall a time when you confidently reasoned about God using analogies from creation. Did you ever stop to consider that God's essence is so unique that it can block the very causal connections you relied on? What would it mean for you to humbly admit that your logic about God may be limited by not accounting for His uniqueness?
?
This argument shows that admitting a 'preventer' can save a general causal claim from absurdity. In your own life, do you ever demand that God act according to predictable patterns, and then feel confused when He doesn't? Could it be that you are ignoring the 'preventer' of God's absolute freedom and uniqueness? How will you adjust your expectations of God after learning this?
