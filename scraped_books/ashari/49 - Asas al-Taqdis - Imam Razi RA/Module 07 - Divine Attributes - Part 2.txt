Two Schools of Ta'wil on Divine Attributes
Classification of ta'wil practitioners regarding problematic divine attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how scholars of ta'wil are classified into two groups regarding attributes like mahr (plotting) and istihya' (shyness), and the reasoning of the first group that uses mushakala.

Core Idea
God uses human-like expressions to draw near to our understanding, not because He actually possesses those traits.

In this passage, Imam al-Razi (may Allah have mercy on him) classifies those who practice ta'wil into two groups regarding divine attributes that seem to imply human limitations. The first group, which includes many theologians, argues that such expressions are used by way of rhetorical similitude (mushakala) to bring God's essence closer to human minds. This lesson unpacks their reasoning and the theological necessity behind it.

The first group uses 'mushakala' (rhetorical similitude) as a justification. This means God uses expressions that resemble human speech for approximation, not literal attribution.

Examine the reasoning against literal mahr and istihya'

This reasoning applies to all similar attributes (anger, love, etc.).

Reflect on the broader principle
The first group's approach is systematic: any attribute that implies imperfection in Allah must be explained via ta'wil, here using mushakala. This preserves tanzih (transcendence).

The Divine Attribute of Haya' as Metaphorical Abandonment
Defines haya' as psychological withholding, then applies it to God as abandonment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that when haya' (shyness) is attributed to God, it signifies abandonment (tark) of punishment or refusal to return empty hands, not a human emotion.

Core Idea
God's 'shyness' means He abandons punishment, not that He feels embarrassed.

In Islamic theology, God is described with attributes that may seem anthropomorphic. The attribute of haya' (shyness) appears in hadiths, but how can God be shy? This lesson explains the theological interpretation: it is a metaphorical expression for God's act of abandoning punishment or answering prayers favorably.
