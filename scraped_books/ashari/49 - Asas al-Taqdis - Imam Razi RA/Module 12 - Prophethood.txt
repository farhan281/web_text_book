Second Approach: Pronoun Refers to Adam
Razi presents the second approach and its first interpretation regarding preservation from disfigurement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's second interpretative approach for the hadith 'God created Adam in His image,' where the pronoun refers to Adam and the phrase is understood as divine protection from disfigurement.

Core Idea
The hadith affirms Adam's honor, not God's attributes.

Razi offers a second way to interpret the pronoun in the controversial hadith. He argues that referring the pronoun to Adam is grammatically preferable because it returns to the nearest antecedent. Under this approach, the first interpretation explains that the 'image' is Adam's own form, preserved from the disfigurement that befell other creatures after his sin. This lesson examines that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice Razi's argument structure: first establishing the approach, then presenting the interpretation.
2
Understand the grammatical rule
Razi uses the principle that a pronoun returns to the nearest antecedent. Identify why this makes referring the pronoun to Adam 'more appropriate'.
The phrase 'أقرب المذكورات' (nearest of the mentioned things) is key.
3
Analyze the interpretation of preservation
Razi explains that the hadith means God created Adam in his own form, which remained unchanged, unlike the serpent and peacock. Consider how this interpretation honors Adam.
The story of the serpent and peacock is used to illustrate preservation from disfigurement.
4
Compare with the previous answer
Read the final sentence contrasting this answer with the first one. Notice the added element: protection from disfigurement as a mercy after Adam's sin.
This distinction shows the theological nuance in Razi's exegesis.

Commentary
This passage presents the second interpretative approach (al-tariq al-thani) for the pronoun in the hadith 'God created Adam in His image'. Razi argues that the pronoun 'His' (in 'His image') refers to Adam, not to God. He justifies this by the grammatical rule that a pronoun normally refers to the nearest antecedent mentioned, which in the hadith is Adam. He then lists three interpretations (wujuh) under this approach, but only the first is given here. The first interpretation is that after God honored Adam by making the angels prostrate to him, Adam committed a sin. God did not punish him by changing his form as He did with the serpent and the peacock, which were disfigured. Instead, God preserved Adam's original form out of honor and protection from disfigurement. Thus the hadith means that God created Adam in his own (i.e., Adam's) form, which remains unchanged. Razi contrasts this answer with the previous one (from the first approach, where the pronoun referred to the listener present): the earlier answer only stated that the current form is the same as the original, while this one adds the element of protection from disfigurement as a mercy regarding Adam's sin.

Razi critiques the second hadith and provides two responses.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A hadith from Jews carries no authority; even if authentic, it figuratively describes God's power.

Imam Razi continues his refutation of anthropomorphic interpretations by addressing a hadith in which Abdullah ibn Salam reports a Jew's claim that the Prophet laughed in approval. Razi offers two rebuttals: first, the speech originates from Jews and is not authoritative; second, even if accepted, it must be interpreted figuratively. This lesson unpacks his arguments.

This response refutes the reliability and interpretation of the hadith.
4
Understand the second response
Even if the hadith is authentic, Razi interprets 'fingers' as a metaphor for God's effortless power over the cosmos, like an object held between fingers.
Razi uses an analogy: saying a task is 'in one's palm or on one's fingertip' means it is easy.
5
Reflect on the key takeaway

Commentary
This passage presents two responses to a second hadith where the Prophet laughed at a Jew's claim about God's fingers. This is analogous to the saying 'this work is in his palm or on his fingertip,' meaning it is easy for him. So the finger metaphor again points to God's perfect power, not a body part.

بمحضره ، فنادى : الله الله . ويكون تكرير الله للنداء لأنه تعالى المنادى - بفتح الدال - مثل : موسى موسى ، إبراهيم إبراهيم . وخلاصة الكلام 1 - أن مرأى النبوة مثل الحلم ففيه إدراكات عقلية لا وجود لها في الخارج. ٢ - أنه أدرك فى اليقظه مخلوقاً بحس البصر هو المجد أو السكينة أو القول. يساعد على كمال الإدراك العقلى . ٣- أو يكون الصوت هو الذي مر قدامه في اليقظة
In his presence, he called out: 'God, God.' And the repetition of 'God' is for the call because He, exalted, is the caller (with a fatha on the dal), like 'Moses, Moses,' 'Abraham, Abraham.' And the summary of the speech: 1. That the vision of prophecy is like a dream, wherein there are intellectual perceptions that have no existence in the external world. 2. That he perceived in wakefulness a created thing by sense of sight, which is the glory (al-majd) or the shekhinah (al-sakinah) or the speech (al-qawl), aiding the perfection of intellectual perception. 3. Or that the sound is what passed before him in wakefulness.

Commentary
This passage explains the nature of prophetic vision. It begins with an example of a call 'God, God' and notes that the repetition is for emphasis, similar to calling 'Moses, Moses' etc. Then it summarizes three points: First, prophetic vision is like a dream — it involves intellectual perceptions that have no external physical existence. Second, during wakefulness, the prophet perceives a created being with his eyesight, which could be the divine glory, the shekhinah, or divine speech, aiding intellectual understanding. Third, it might be that the sound is what passed before him in wakefulness. The passage thus distinguishes between intellectual and sensory perception in prophecy.

Misconception & Correction

Correction: According to Imam Razi, they are primarily intellectual perceptions akin to dreams, though sometimes accompanied by sensory elements.
