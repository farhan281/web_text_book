Analogy of Creator with Other Attributes
Maturidi defends the temporality of creation by analogy with other attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses an analogy between the attributes of Creator and other attributes like Knowing to argue that 'Creator' does not imply eternal creation.

Core Idea
The attribute 'Creator' does not imply eternal creation, just as 'Knowing' does not imply eternal objects.

This passage presents a key argument in Islamic theology defending the temporality of creation. By drawing an analogy with other divine attributes, the author shows that the relationship between attribute and object does not entail the eternal existence of the object. Let's unpack the reasoning from the original Arabic.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the literal translation to grasp the overall argument.
The Arabic is dense; note the key terms 'خالق', 'سميع', 'عليم'.
2
Identify the analogy
Notice how the author compares 'Creator' to 'Hearing, Knowing': just as these attributes do not necessitate eternal objects, similarly 'Creator' does not necessitate eternal creation.
3
Understand the conclusion
The conclusion is that the attribute 'Creator' is analogous to other attributes and does not require an eternal creation. The author also notes that definite articles and common usage support this.
Pay attention to the phrase 'ليس في قوله الخالق ذلك'.
4
Reflect on the implication
This argument refutes the idea that if God is Creator, then creation must be eternal. Ask yourself how this preserves God's transcendence while affirming temporal creation.
This connects to broader kalām discussions on God's attributes and the world's temporality.

Commentary
This passage argues by analogy that the attribute 'Creator' does not imply that the created things are eternal. Just as saying God is 'Hearing, Knowing' does not mean that all known and heard things exist eternally (since objects of knowledge are temporal), similarly saying 'Creator' does not imply that the creation is eternal. The point is that attributes like 'Knowing' and 'Creator' describe God's relation to things without eternalizing those things. The author then notes that 'Creator' and 'the Creator' are essentially the same. He adds that if the word 'Creator' were taken to imply the eternity of creation, it would be more so when used as 'the Creator' (with the definite article), but this is not the case. He concludes by citing examples like 'Master of the Day of Judgment' and 'Creator of everything' – these do not imply that the Day of Judgment or everything always existed. This refutes the idea that 'Creator' requires eternal creation.

Why Negative Descriptions Alone Cannot Describe God
Maturidi argues negative descriptions alone insufficient for knowing God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi argues that merely negating imperfections does not provide positive knowledge of God's attributes.

Core Idea
Negating imperfections does not teach us what God is, only what He is not.

Maturidi opens a crucial discussion on how we describe God. Many assume that saying 'God is not ignorant' tells us something about His knowledge, but Maturidi points out the logical gap: negation alone fails to affirm any positive attribute. This passage challenges us to think deeper about the language we use for the Divine.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation. Notice the logical flow Maturidi employs.
Pay attention to the examples: not ignorant, not incapable, not deaf.
2
Identify the key examples
Maturidi lists negative descriptions and contrasts them with positive ones. List each negation and its corresponding positive attribute.
The positive attributes are powerful, knowing, hearing, seeing.
3
Understand the logical gap
Why does Maturidi say that 'not ignorant' does not imply 'knowing'? Consider that a stone is not ignorant but also not knowing. This shows the gap.
4
Examine the alternative proposal
Maturidi suggests if one only intends to negate opposites, one could say 'He is sound, safe, healthy' – which are also negative. Why is that unacceptable?
5
Draw the conclusion
Since the alternative is not permissible, the original claim that negations alone are sufficient fails. Reflect on why.
This shows that positive attributes must be affirmed, not just negatives.

Since this is not acceptable for describing God, it shows that the method of only negating opposites fails to give a true account of God's attributes.

Orderly Acts Prove God's Knowledge and Power
Maturidi presents the proof that orderly acts directly indicate God's knowledge and power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the orderly emergence of consecutive acts in the world directly indicates the agent's knowledge and power, not merely that He lacks ignorance and weakness.

Core Idea
Orderly acts are direct evidence of knowledge and power, not just of not being ignorant.

Maturidi begins by establishing that the well-ordered, consecutive acts we observe in creation are themselves proof of the agent's knowledge and power over them. He distinguishes this from the weaker claim that they only show the agent is not ignorant or weak. This distinction is crucial for affirming positive attributes of Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the phrase 'هي العلم بها والقدرة عليها' — this is the key claim.
2
Identify the contrast
Maturidi contrasts two interpretations: (1) the acts are proof of knowledge and power, vs. (2) they are merely proof that the agent is not ignorant or weak. Which does he affirm?
He affirms the positive attribution.
3
Understand the example of accidents
He says that no one described solely by negative traits can produce any act or consistency, as with accidents. This illustrates that positive attributes are necessary for orderly action.
4
Reflect on the implication
Ask yourself: Why is it important to affirm that Allah has knowledge and power, not just that He lacks ignorance?
This connects to broader kalam discussions about divine attributes.

Names Derive from Attributes: The Case of 'Creator'
Maturidi discusses the derivation of names from attributes and refutes that 'Creator' implies pre-eternal creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that divine names derive from attributes, and that the name 'Creator' (al-Khaliq) does not imply that creation is eternal.

Core Idea
If attributes are denied, names become meaningless titles; 'Creator' does not necessitate eternal creation.

Maturidi continues by discussing the relationship between divine names and attributes. He argues that names are derived from attributes; if attributes are not affirmed, the names become mere titles, and it is meaningless to say God has always been such a title. He then applies this to the name 'Creator', showing that just as 'Knowing' does not imply that all known things are eternal, 'Creator' does not imply that creation is eternal.

Guided Path
1
Understand the principle: names derive from attributes
Maturidi states that names come from attributes. If attributes are not realized, names become mere titles (ألقاب). This is important for understanding divine names.
2
The problem with titles
If a name is just a title, saying God has always been that title is meaningless because a title cannot be eternal. This shows why we must affirm real attributes.
3
Apply to 'Creator'
Maturidi argues that just as the name 'Knowing' does not imply that all known things are eternal, 'Creator' does not imply that creation is eternal.
4
Grammatical argument
He notes that the form 'Creator' (خالق) is like 'Master' (مالك) and does not necessitate the pre-eternity of its object.

Knowledge of God Through the World's Indication
Maturidi establishes that the world indicates only God's attributes, not His essence, through three proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why knowledge of God is only possible through the world's testimony to His attributes, not His essence, and the three rational proofs Maturidi presents.

Core Idea
The world points to God's attributes, never to His essence directly.

Before we can affirm anything about God, we must first establish how we can know Him at all. Maturidi begins with a foundational principle: since our senses and direct revelation cannot access God Himself, we must rely on the world as a sign. This passage explains exactly how the world indicates God—by testifying to His attributes (power, knowledge) through observable patterns, while never revealing His essence. It also warns against false attributions like spatial or relational qualities.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once slowly, then the literal translation. Notice the flow: principle → proof → examples → conclusion.
Pay attention to the repeated phrase 'دليل' (proof) – it structures the argument.
2
Identify the three proofs from the world
Look for the three phenomena that Maturidi presents as evidence: existence after non-existence, variation and combination of opposites in one thing, and consistent administration despite multiplicity.
Each is introduced with 'هو دليل' or similar.
3
Understand the 'attribute vs essence' distinction
Why does Maturidi insist that the world only indicates attributes, not essence? Reflect on his reasoning: nothing in the sensed world directly shows the 'what' of God, only the 'how' of His actions.
This refutes those who claim direct intuitive knowledge of God's essence.
4
Apply the principle to spatial/relational attributes
Maturidi concludes by denying that God can be described by place, connection, separation, etc., even as negations. See how this follows from the earlier rule.

The Claim of Al-Aslah: God's Actions Must Benefit Others
Maturidi presents the claim that God's actions must benefit others.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the initial Mu'tazilite claim that God's actions must be for the benefit of others, and how this is based on an analogy to human wisdom.

Core Idea
The claimants argue that a wise agent cannot act without benefit, so God must always benefit others.

Maturidi begins by presenting the view of a group who claim to uphold tawhid but have strayed. They assert that since God is wise, His actions must have a purpose—benefit to others—because, as they argue, acting without benefit is frivolous. This sets the stage for a critical analysis of attributing human-like motives to God.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice the structure: the group's deviation, their premise, and their conclusion.
The passage starts with 'مسألة أفعال الله'.
2
Identify the two deviations
The group says most adherents of tawhid depart from it in two ways: ignorance of God's obligations or inability to refute dualists and atheists. Find these in the text.
The two ways are mentioned at the beginning.
3
Understand the core argument
The core argument: a wise agent does not act without benefit or cause. Therefore, God, being wise, cannot initiate harm and must always act for the most beneficial outcome for others.
This is the basis for al-aslah.

Issue: God's actions. Regarding that a group from among the people of tawhid claimed that most of its adherents departed from it in two ways: either ignorance of what is obligatory for Him, or inability to summarize the statement about it from the school of the dualists and the rest of the atheists. So a group claimed that if every rational being acts without benefit [his action falls] then he is not wise, and whoever does an action without a cause is frivolous. So they thought that it is not permissible for God to initiate a harmful action against anyone, and that would remove wisdom from Him. So they obligated upon Him in every action He does the most beneficial for others in religion and the best for others in the final outcome; since He is exalted above a saying that benefits Him or harms Him. So they saw no action for Him except that which benefits others, or repels harm from others. That also becomes the cause of His action, just as is the cause of every wise person among us who considers immediate or delayed benefit or repels an incurred harm, thereby earning good praise and abundant reward. And they set an example for estimating His action by the action of others, including that lying or injustice cannot come from Him, nor motion without displacement, nor stillness without stability. So it was established that estimating His action on the model of wise people in the witnessed world is necessary, except that they removed from Him exaltation by action and abasement by leaving an action. So they thereby obligated that by His action He does not draw benefit to Himself nor repel harm from Himself, so His action must be wisdom by benefiting others or repelling harm from others. They made that the cause of His action, so that in their view His action would exit from the meaning of frivolity. And in this respect they opposed the dualists, since the dualists rejected that an action without benefit to the agent could be from wisdom. So the dualists affirmed action with the substance of wisdom at the time of mixture, so that in his salvation from the species of the substance of folly, his action becomes wisdom according to the estimation of the witnessed world: that something cannot come from nothing in the witnessed world. So they obligated for the whole world an origin of creation and formation, because there is no distinction between the action exiting from the right of existence in the witnessed world, so it necessitates that it be from a wise [agent]. So the people of tawhid opposed them in these two points. Then a group among them obligated upon Him that in the intellect there is a cause without which intellect would not exist, because they found an action like His in the witnessed world to be frivolous. And they obligated in the case of harmful actions if it were without benefit, that it follows folly, as the dualists mentioned concerning an action from which the agent does not benefit. Then they differed. A group claimed that there is no real harm on the one acted upon, even if from it is supplication and complaint. And a group claimed that there is real harm upon him, but he must compensate him for it, so that the action becomes wisdom, like what exists in the witnessed world: one who bears great burdens and drinks unpleasant medicines with intention, and intends taxation for outcomes, and he cannot harm others except with compensation.

Commentary
This passage presents the claim of a group from among Ahl al-Tawhid (likely the Mu'tazila) who deviate from true tawhid in two ways: ignorance of what is obligatory for God, or inability to articulate the correct position against the dualists and atheists. They argue that a wise agent does not act without benefit or cause, so God, being wise, cannot initiate harm. Therefore they obligate al-aslah (the most beneficial) for others in religion and destiny, because God is self-sufficient. They limit God's actions to those that benefit or repel harm from others, making that the cause of His action, analogizing to human wise agents who act for benefit or to avoid harm. They illustrate by saying God cannot lie, be unjust, move without displacement, or be still without stability, so His actions must be estimated like those of wise people in the witnessed world, except that God is elevated above gaining or losing by action. Thus they conclude that God's actions are necessarily for the benefit or harm-repulsion of others, to avoid frivolity. They distinguish themselves from the dualists, who also require benefit to the agent, but the dualists explain wisdom through mixture (mizaj) and substance, whereas the people of tawhid oppose them on two points. Then these claimants differ among themselves: some say there is no real harm to the one acted upon (only perception of harm), while others say there is real harm but compensation is required, like bearing burdens or taking medicine for a good outcome, and one cannot harm without compensation. This sets up the position Maturidi will refute.

Analogy to Human Wisdom: The Claimants' Argument from the Witnessed World
Maturidi presents the claimants' analogy and its implications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the claimants use analogies from the witnessed world to argue that God's actions must be estimated on the model of wise humans.

Core Idea
The claimants use the witnessed world to claim that God's actions are like those of wise humans, except He gains nothing.

The claimants deepen their argument by drawing analogies from the witnessed world (al-shahid). They argue that just as a wise human cannot lie or act arbitrarily, God cannot act without benefit to others. They also exclude from God any elevation or abasement through action, concluding that His actions are purely for the benefit of others.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on analogy
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice the examples they give: lying, injustice, motion without displacement, stillness without stability.
These are impossible for God, just as they are for a wise person.
2
Understand the necessity of analogy
They say it is necessary to estimate God's actions on the model of wise people in the witnessed world, but with the exception that God does not gain or lose by action.
This exception allows them to claim that God's actions are entirely for others.
3
Grasp the conclusion
The conclusion is that God's action must be wisdom by benefiting others or repelling harm, and that is the cause of His action to avoid frivolity.
This is the core of the al-aslah theory.

زعم قوم من أهل التوحيد أن أكثر منتحليه خرج منه من وجهين: إما جهلا بواجبه، وإما عجزا عن تلخيص القول فيه من مذهب الثنوية وسائر الملحدين. فزعم قوم أنه إذ كان كل مما يعقل لغير نفع [فعله يقع] فليس بحكيم، ومن فعل فعلا لغير علة فهو عابث، فظنوا أن لا يجوز لله أن يبتدأ فعل ضرر بأحد، وأن ذلك يزيل الحكمة عنه، فألزموه في كل فعل يفعله الأصلح لغيره في الدين والأحسن لغيره في العاقبة؛ إذ هو متعال عن قول ينفعه أو يضره شيء، فلم يروا له الفعل إلا بما ينفع غيره، أو يدفع به الضرر عن غيره، فيكون ذلك أيضا علة فعله، على ما كان علة فعل كل حكيم منا، ما تأمل من نفع عاجل أو آجل أو دفع [ضرر] لزم به، فيجر بذلك حسن الثناء جزيل الثواب. وضربوا لتقدير فعله بفعل غيره مثلا بما لا يجوز أن يكون منه الكذب أو الجور، أو يكون منه الحركة [من] غير زوال، أو السكون [من] غير قرار، فثبت أن تقدير فعله على فعل الحكماء في الشاهد لازم، إلا أنهم دفعوا عنه الارتفاع بالفعل، والانحطاط بترك فعل ما، فأوجبوا بذلك أنه بفعله لا يجر إلى نفسه النفع ولا يدفع عنها الضرر، فيجب أن يكون فعله الحكمة بما ينفع غيره أو يدفع عن غيره الضرر، وجعلوا ذلك علة فعله؛ ليخرج عندهم فعله عن معنى العبث. وبهذا الوجه خالفوا الثنوية؛ إذ أبوا الفعل بغير نفع للفاعل فيه أن يكون من الحكمة، فأثبتوا الفعل بجوهر الحكمة عند المزاج، ليكون في خلاصه من جنس جوهر السفه، فيصير فعله حكمة على التقدير بالشاهد ما كون شيء لا من شيء ممتنعا في الشاهد، فأوجبوا لكلية العالم أصلا جعل وإنشاء؛ لأنه لا فصل بين خروج الفعل عن حق الوجود في الشاهد، فيلزم دفعه أن يكون ذلك من حكيم. فخالفهم أهل التوحيد في هذين، ثم ألزم فريق منهم إياه ما في العقل علة لم يكن له العقل دونها لما وجدوا فعل مثله في الشاهد عبثا، وألزموا في فعل المضار لو كان لغير نفع، يعقب سفها على ما ذكرت الثنوية في فعل لا ينتفع به الفاعل منه. ثم تفرقوا، فزعم قوم أنه لا ضرر في الحقيقة على المفعول به، وإن منه التضرع والشكوى. وزعم قوم أن عليه في الحقيقة ضررا، لكن عليه أن يعوضه عن ذلك؛ ليصير الفعل به حكمة، كالموجود في الشاهد ممن يحمل المؤن العظام وشرب الأدوية الكريهة مع القصد، وقصد الخراج لتقع العواقب، وليس له فعل الضار بغير إلا بعوض.
Issue: God's actions. Regarding that a group from among the people of tawhid claimed that most of its adherents departed from it in two ways: either ignorance of what is obligatory for Him, or inability to summarize the statement about it from the school of the dualists and the rest of the atheists. So a group claimed that if every rational being acts without benefit [his action falls] then he is not wise, and whoever does an action without a cause is frivolous. So they thought that it is not permissible for God to initiate a harmful action against anyone, and that would remove wisdom from Him. So they obligated upon Him in every action He does the most beneficial for others in religion and the best for others in the final outcome; since He is exalted above a saying that benefits Him or harms Him. So they saw no action for Him except that which benefits others, or repels harm from others. That also becomes the cause of His action, just as is the cause of every wise person among us who considers immediate or delayed benefit or repels an incurred harm, thereby earning good praise and abundant reward. And they set an example for estimating His action by the action of others, including that lying or injustice cannot come from Him, nor motion without displacement, nor stillness without stability. So it was established that estimating His action on the model of wise people in the witnessed world is necessary, except that they removed from Him exaltation by action and abasement by leaving an action. So they thereby obligated that by His action He does not draw benefit to Himself nor repel harm from Himself, so His action must be wisdom by benefiting others or repelling harm from others. They made that the cause of His action, so that in their view His action would exit from the meaning of frivolity. And in this respect they opposed the dualists, since the dualists rejected that an action without benefit to the agent could be from wisdom. So the dualists affirmed action with the substance of wisdom at the time of mixture, so that in his salvation from the species of the substance of folly, his action becomes wisdom according to the estimation of the witnessed world: that something cannot come from nothing in the witnessed world. So they obligated for the whole world an origin of creation and formation, because there is no distinction between the action exiting from the right of existence in the witnessed world, so it necessitates that it be from a wise [agent]. So the people of tawhid opposed them in these two points. Then a group among them obligated upon Him that in the intellect there is a cause without which intellect would not exist, because they found an action like His in the witnessed world to be frivolous. And they obligated in the case of harmful actions if it were without benefit, that it follows folly, as the dualists mentioned concerning an action from which the agent does not benefit. Then they differed. A group claimed that there is no real harm on the one acted upon, even if from it is supplication and complaint. And a group claimed that there is real harm upon him, but he must compensate him for it, so that the action becomes wisdom, like what exists in the witnessed world: one who bears great burdens and drinks unpleasant medicines with intention, and intends taxation for outcomes, and he cannot harm others except with compensation.

Commentary
This passage presents the claim of a group from among Ahl al-Tawhid (likely the Mu'tazila) who deviate from true tawhid in two ways: ignorance of what is obligatory for God, or inability to articulate the correct position against the dualists and atheists. They argue that a wise agent does not act without benefit or cause, so God, being wise, cannot initiate harm. Therefore they obligate al-aslah (the most beneficial) for others in religion and destiny, because God is self-sufficient. They limit God's actions to those that benefit or repel harm from others, making that the cause of His action, analogizing to human wise agents who act for benefit or to avoid harm. They illustrate by saying God cannot lie, be unjust, move without displacement, or be still without stability, so His actions must be estimated like those of wise people in the witnessed world, except that God is elevated above gaining or losing by action. Thus they conclude that God's actions are necessarily for the benefit or harm-repulsion of others, to avoid frivolity. They distinguish themselves from the dualists, who also require benefit to the agent, but the dualists explain wisdom through mixture (mizaj) and substance, whereas the people of tawhid oppose them on two points. Then these claimants differ among themselves: some say there is no real harm to the one acted upon (only perception of harm), while others say there is real harm but compensation is required, like bearing burdens or taking medicine for a good outcome, and one cannot harm without compensation. This sets up the position Maturidi will refute.

God's Wisdom Beyond Benefit
Maturidi refutes the claim that God's actions must benefit others.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi argues that God's action cannot be constrained by the principle of benefit (al-aslah) and how he bases this on God's attributes of self-sufficiency and knowledge.

Core Idea
God’s actions are wise by essence, not by benefit.

In this passage, Imam Maturidi directly challenges the theological claim that God must always act for the optimal benefit of His creatures (al-aslah). He argues that such a view misunderstands God's nature. By establishing that God is wise by essence, free from ignorance and need, Maturidi shows that God's wisdom is not dependent on external benefits. This lesson unpacks his reasoning and its implications.

The Shaykh said: Whoever knows God with true knowledge and knows His self-sufficiency and sovereignty, then His power and dominion, that to Him belongs creation and command, knows that His action cannot depart from wisdom; for He is wise by essence, self-sufficient, all-knowing. That which causes departure from wisdom in the witnessed world and drives its possessor to it is his ignorance or his need, and both are negated from God. Commentary
This passage is Maturidi's refutation of the claim that God's actions must be for the benefit of others. He begins by stating a condition: whoever truly knows God—His self-sufficiency, sovereignty, power, and dominion—knows that His action cannot depart from wisdom. The reason is that God is wise (hakim) by essence (bi-dhatihi), self-sufficient (ghani), and all-knowing ('alim). In the witnessed world (al-shahid), departure from wisdom is caused either by ignorance or by need. Both ignorance and need are decisively negated from God. Therefore, God's action is necessarily within wisdom. Maturidi then applies this to the specific analogies used by the claimants: the examples of motion and stillness. Motion and stillness in creatures arise from need (e.g., need for rest or movement), but God is free from such needs, so it is invalid to compare God's actions to those of creatures in these respects. The passage concludes with an incomplete sentence (likely due to the manuscript break) about these needs leading to self-contemplation of rest and comfort, which further emphasizes that God is above such states.

Knowing God's Attributes Leads to Knowing His Actions Are Wise
Maturidi presents the conditional proof that knowledge of God's attributes necessitates wisdom in His actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that recognizing God's self-sufficiency, power, and sovereignty necessarily leads to the conclusion that His actions are wise, because ignorance and need—the causes of unwisdom—are absent in Him.

Core Idea
God's actions are wise because He is free from ignorance and need.

Maturidi begins by establishing a fundamental link between knowing God's attributes and recognizing the wisdom in His actions. He argues that anyone who truly understands God's self-sufficiency, authority, and power will inevitably see that His actions must be wise. This lesson unpacks that connection and shows why unwise actions are impossible for God.

The Shaykh said: Whoever knows God with true knowledge and knows His self-sufficiency and authority, then His power and dominion, that to Him belong creation and command, knows that His action cannot exit from wisdom; for He is wise by His essence, self-sufficient, knowing. That by which exit from wisdom occurs in the witnessed world and impels its possessor to it is his ignorance or his need, and both are negated from God. So it is established that His action does not exit from wisdom. And according to what I have mentioned, it is invalid that His action be in motion or rest; for they are two needs that settle in their possessor, one bringing him to contemplation of himself from rest and consolation, to what aspiration and desire bring him; for there is no way to his goal except through movement and displacement, and no way to repel weariness and fatigue except through stillness and rest. As for God exalted, since His self-sufficiency and power are established, it is invalid that need or aspiration should befall Him. And likewise, when His power, authority, and knowledge are established, it is invalid to describe Him as not being able to do something initially from nothing; for that indicates need and is a sign of weakness. And the need of all that is sensed and attained by human knowledge is the indication of the ordainment of the world; and the world, with Him being powerful, self-sufficient, it is not permissible to remove that by what He knows of His self-sufficiency, power, wisdom, and knowledge. There is no power except with God. Therefore it is necessary to say, by necessity of reason, for the permissibility of the world's existence from nothing, and for His action to be upon wisdom, even if the intellects of the wise of the world are incapable of comprehending it because the aspect of wisdom exceeds the limit of their intellects' strength, according to what we explained of the existence of something from nothing, and of the permissibility of action by one who does not benefit from it, and by that the true nature of the matter becomes clear, that to Him belong creation and command, and every owner may act in his dominion according to what he possesses of it as he wills. There is no power except with God.

Commentary
This passage is a proof (burhan) that God's actions are necessarily wise. Maturidi begins with a conditional: whoever truly knows God's attributes (self-sufficiency, authority, power, dominion) will know that His actions cannot lack wisdom. The reason is that in the witnessed world, actions lacking wisdom stem from ignorance or need—both absent in God. He then argues that God's actions are not like human actions of motion and rest, which arise from need (e.g., need to move to achieve a goal, need to rest to relieve fatigue). God is free from such needs. Since God's power, authority, and knowledge are established, it is invalid to claim He cannot create from nothing, as that would imply need and weakness. The ultimate conclusion is that reason necessitates acknowledging that the world can come from nothing and that God's actions are wise, even if human intellects cannot fully grasp the wisdom. This establishes God's absolute sovereignty: He does as He wills in His dominion.
