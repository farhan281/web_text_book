Maturidi cites multiple Quranic verses to show that faith remains despite major sins.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses multiple Quranic verses to prove that faith remains despite major sins, refuting the Kharijite and Mu'tazilite positions.

Core Idea
Major sins do not remove faith; the Quran consistently calls sinners 'believers'.

Maturidi was a master of Quranic reasoning. In this passage, he deploys a series of Quranic verses that all address believers who are sinning. Each verse preserves the title 'believers' for those committing major sins, showing that faith and sin can coexist. This refutes those who claim that major sins expel one from faith.

Guided Path
1
Read the full passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation to get the overall argument. Notice the pattern: each verse addresses 'O you who have believed' followed by a prohibition or command implying sin.
This is a long passage with many proofs; read patiently.
2
Examine the first verse (61:2)
Allah rebukes the believers for saying what they do not do, yet still calls them believers. The rebuke (maqt) is not a removal of faith.
This sets the pattern: sin does not negate the name of faith.
3
Look at the fighting and retaliation verses
In Surah Al-Hujurat (49:9) and Al-Baqarah (2:178), Allah calls the parties 'believers' despite transgression and prescribes retaliation. Brotherhood among them is maintained.
If sin removed faith, such rulings would be impossible.

Consider the emigration and prohibition verses
In Surah Al-Anfal (8:72) and Al-Mumtahanah (60:1) and Al-Anfal (8:27), Allah addresses as believers those who failed to emigrate or are warned against betrayal, preserving the name of faith.
Even severe sins like betrayal do not remove the title of faith.

Reflect on the repentance verses and conclusion

This directly refutes those who say major sinners are no longer believers.

Commentary
This passage is a series of Quranic proofs demonstrating that the name of faith (īmān) remains upon a believer even when he commits major sins (kabā'ir). Maturidi begins by citing Sūrat al-Ṣaff (61:2) where Allah rebukes believers for saying what they do not do, yet still addresses them as 'believers'. The rebuke implies displeasure (maqt) but not removal of faith. Then he cites Sūrat al-Ḥujurāt (49:9) about two groups of believers fighting; Allah calls them believers while condemning one group as transgressors. If sin removed faith, the ruling would be different. Next, Sūrat al-Baqarah (2:178) on retaliation: Allah addresses believers and prescribes capital punishment for intentional killing, yet still calls them believers and brothers. Then Sūrat al-Anfāl (8:72) about those who believed but did not emigrate: Allah still calls them believers despite their failure to emigrate, and Sūrat al-Nisā' (4:97) warns those who wrong themselves but still calls them believers. Then Sūrat al-Mumtaḥanah (60:1) and al-Anfāl (8:27) forbid taking enemies as allies and betraying Allah and the Messenger, yet those addressed are called believers despite the ugliness of their actions. Finally, Sūrat al-Taḥrīm (66:8) and al-Nūr (24:31) command repentance to believers, implying they have sins that are forgiven while the name of faith remains. Maturidi concludes that the correct position is that the name of faith is not removed from sinners, refuting the Kharijites and Mu'tazilites who hold that major sins expel one from faith.

Faith Persists Despite Sin: Maturidi's Argument
Maturidi argues that obligatory acts are ascribed to faith, so faith persists in the sinner.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that the name of faith remains with a person even if they commit major sins, based on the fact that acts of worship are obligated under the name of faith.

Core Idea
Sin does not erase faith — the obligation itself proves it remains.

Imam Maturidi presents a compelling argument from the nature of religious obligations. He notes that Allah has made many acts of worship contingent upon the name of faith. If faith were removed by sin, the sinner would no longer be obligated. Yet the sinner still participates in these acts, proving faith remains. Let's examine his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the original argument
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the logical flow: obligation under faith name → sinner participates → conclusion that faith remains.
The passage is dense; read slowly.
2
Identify the key premise
The key premise is that Allah obligated acts of worship 'under the name of faith' (bism al-īmān). This means that the obligation itself is tied to the person being a believer. If faith were lost, the obligation would be lost.
3
Draw the conclusion
Since the fāsiq (grave sinner) still participates in these acts, the name of faith is not removed. Maturidi also points to previous explanations as sufficient.
This argument is part of a larger proof that faith and sin can coexist.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an argument made by opponents. The argument is: one who initially believes but then commits an act of disbelief (like apostasy) shows by his later action that his initial belief was a lie. Maturidi refutes this as invalid and corrupt. He begins by stating why: every person's intellect obligates avoiding lying, and this obligation is preserved even when one accepts belief. The existence of later disbelief does not prove that the intellect's initial belief was a lie, because the intellect itself prevents lying. If one argues that later action transcends the initial belief, then the same logic would apply to acceptance itself at the time of transgression—leading to absurd consequences. For example, if this reasoning were valid, then everything would be judged by the first act, which would corrupt the obligation of apostasy rulings distinguishing apostates from original disbelievers. Moreover, if later action indicated lying in the first belief, then a disbeliever's subsequent faith would also indicate that his first disbelief was a lie—which is absurd. Maturidi then presents two fundamental aspects (wajhan) of the refutation:

First, if later action revealed lying in the first belief, then the obligation (luzūm) to avoid lying would be removed. Once the obligation is removed, the existence of the action cannot be a cause for revealing the lie. This invalidates the opponent's claim.

Second, every person knows from himself at the time of his belief that he is not lying in that belief. Then he also knows about one who later transgresses in his religion. If later action were to reveal lying, then no certain knowledge would be possible in reality—because everyone's self-knowledge of sincerity would be undermined. The claimant of this argument is himself bound by it: he believes in his own belief that he does not lie, but every believer knows that the claimant is by this statement a liar. Likewise, Allah knows the truth of everything as it is, not by external signs, and He knows the sincerity of the original belief. If someone later apostasies, then the claimant's statement would make him a liar in Allah's sight and before witnesses, deserving the judgment of disbelief upon himself—since his statement aims to establish the disbelief of others, but instead establishes his own.

Finally, Maturidi cites the Quranic verse 'Indeed, those who believed then disbelieved then believed then disbelieved' (4:137). If the opponent's claim were true, such people could never have faith established for them after repeated disbelief. But the verse acknowledges their faith after disbelief, showing the corruption of the opponent's argument. Maturidi concludes: if this argument fails regarding disbelief, how much more so for lesser sins? And the differing states in creation do not necessitate the corruption of their opposites in other states. This comprehensive refutation upholds the sincerity of initial belief despite later apostasy.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an argument made by opponents. The argument is: one who initially believes but then commits an act of disbelief (like apostasy) shows by his later action that his initial belief was a lie. Maturidi refutes this as invalid and corrupt. He begins by stating why: every person's intellect obligates avoiding lying, and this obligation is preserved even when one accepts belief. The existence of later disbelief does not prove that the intellect's initial belief was a lie, because the intellect itself prevents lying. If one argues that later action transcends the initial belief, then the same logic would apply to acceptance itself at the time of transgression—leading to absurd consequences. For example, if this reasoning were valid, then everything would be judged by the first act, which would corrupt the obligation of apostasy rulings distinguishing apostates from original disbelievers. Moreover, if later action indicated lying in the first belief, then a disbeliever's subsequent faith would also indicate that his first disbelief was a lie—which is absurd. Maturidi then presents two fundamental aspects (wajhan) of the refutation:

First, if later action revealed lying in the first belief, then the obligation (luzūm) to avoid lying would be removed. Once the obligation is removed, the existence of the action cannot be a cause for revealing the lie. This invalidates the opponent's claim.

Second, every person knows from himself at the time of his belief that he is not lying in that belief. Then he also knows about one who later transgresses in his religion. If later action were to reveal lying, then no certain knowledge would be possible in reality—because everyone's self-knowledge of sincerity would be undermined. The claimant of this argument is himself bound by it: he believes in his own belief that he does not lie, but every believer knows that the claimant is by this statement a liar. Likewise, Allah knows the truth of everything as it is, not by external signs, and He knows the sincerity of the original belief. If someone later apostasies, then the claimant's statement would make him a liar in Allah's sight and before witnesses, deserving the judgment of disbelief upon himself—since his statement aims to establish the disbelief of others, but instead establishes his own.

Finally, Maturidi cites the Quranic verse 'Indeed, those who believed then disbelieved then believed then disbelieved' (4:137). If the opponent's claim were true, such people could never have faith established for them after repeated disbelief. But the verse acknowledges their faith after disbelief, showing the corruption of the opponent's argument. Maturidi concludes: if this argument fails regarding disbelief, how much more so for lesser sins? And the differing states in creation do not necessitate the corruption of their opposites in other states. This comprehensive refutation upholds the sincerity of initial belief despite later apostasy.
