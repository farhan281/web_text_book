Intellect's Limits: The Senses Analogy
Maturidi compares intellect to sensory faculties to show their common limitation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi argues that the intellect, like the senses, has inherent limits that necessitate revelation.

Core Idea
The intellect, like the senses, has a limited range and cannot reach everything alone.

In this passage, Maturidi builds on his earlier arguments about the need for messengers. He now uses a powerful analogy: just as each sense has its own instrument and limitations, so too the intellect is a created cause with fixed boundaries. This sets the stage for understanding why reason alone is insufficient for ultimate truth.

This analogy is the core of the argument.
3
Understand the conclusion
After establishing the analogy, Maturidi directly states that the intellect likewise has a limit and is affected by obscurity. Consider how this supports the need for divine guidance via prophets.
The end of the passage points to the highest human wisdom being that which has proof—revelation.

The intellect's material is reflection on causes, and the best among the philosophers are those whose wisdom is supported by proof (i.e., revelation). Two Ways of Knowing the Unseen
Maturidi explains the two means of perceiving the unseen and their implications for prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's classification of the two means of knowing the unseen (inference and report) and how this demonstrates the necessity of prophethood.

Core Idea
The unseen is known either by inference from the seen or by report from a truthful messenger.

Maturidi presents a fundamental epistemological framework: all knowledge of things beyond sensory perception comes through one of two channels. One is rational inference from observable connections, the other is reliable testimony. This framework, he argues, is universally accepted by rational people. He then applies it to religious knowledge, showing that divine commands and prohibitions—having no sensory evidence—can only be known through prophetic report, thereby establishing the necessity of messengers.

Guided Path

3
Trace the argument to prophethood
Observe how Maturidi applies the second method (report) to matters of command, prohibition, promise, and threat. He concludes that since these have no sensory evidence, they require a report from a messenger. This establishes the necessity of prophethood.
This is a key argument in kalam for the need for prophets.

Maturidi then applies this to religious knowledge: for matters that have no sensory evidence, such as divine commands, promises, and threats, the only way to know them is through prophetic report. This includes rulings of permissible and prohibited, and the customary practices in different situations. Since these cannot be inferred from nature alone, valid report from a messenger becomes obligatory. Thus, the necessity of prophethood is established from the structure of human knowledge.

The Three Rational Categories: Necessary, Impossible, Possible
Maturidi divides all things into necessary, impossible, and possible, and shows role of revelation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's threefold classification of all things into necessary, impossible, and possible, and why revelation is needed in the realm of the possible.

Core Idea
The possible is the realm where revelation guides reason.

Imam Maturidi begins by laying the foundational classification that governs all of theology: everything that can be rationally considered falls into one of three categories—necessary, impossible, or possible. This division is not just abstract; it determines where reason can decide alone and where revelation must intervene. The passage you are about to read is compact but pivotal for understanding the scope of reason and the necessity of prophethood.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, carefully read the Arabic passage and its English translation. Let the text speak for itself before analyzing.
Notice the three terms: ممتنع (impossible), واجب (necessary), ممكن (possible).
2
Identify the three categories
Find how Maturidi names the three categories: impossible, necessary, and the 'middle' which is possible. Understand their basic definitions.
The term 'واسط' means the intermediate or middle category.
3
Understand the role of intellect
See that for necessary and impossible matters, the intellect judges absolutely—revelation cannot contradict it. For possible matters, the intellect does not mandate one course over another.
4
Understand the role of revelation
Note that in possible matters, messengers come to clarify 'the better' (الأولى) in each case. This shows the necessity of prophethood.

ثم الأصول ثلاثة ممتنع وواجب وواسط وهو الممكن، وعلى ذلك جميع أمر العالم. فالواجب في العقل على جهة لا يجوز مجيء الخبر بغيره، وكذلك الممتنع ؛ ويجئ في الممكن، إذ هو المنقلب من حال إلى حال ويد إلى يد وملك إلى ملك . وفي ذلك ليس في العقل إيجاب جهة ولا امتناع من جهة، فتجئ الرسل ببيان الأولى من ذلك في كل حال. والله الموفق.

Commentary
This passage introduces a threefold classification that is foundational in kalam: the necessary (wajib), the impossible (mumtani'), and the intermediate possible (mumkin). Maturidi states that this classification governs all matters of the world. For necessary and impossible matters, reason determines them absolutely, and revealed reports cannot contradict them. However, in the realm of the possible—things that can change, be transferred, or vary—reason alone does not mandate one option over another. The intellect cannot decide which course is obligatory or prohibited in these contingent matters. Therefore, messengers are sent to explain which possibility is better (al-awla) in each situation—whether it be commanded or prohibited. Thus, revelation guides human choice within the open possibilities.

Refutation of al-Warraq's First Objection
Refutes the first objection concerning eloquence disparity.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Quran's inimitability is proven by the Arabs' natural incapacity, not their lack of effort.

In this passage, Imam Maturidi addresses the first objection claiming that the Arabs could have matched the Quran but simply refrained from doing so due to rivalry. He counters by pointing out that if they had the ability, their failure to produce something similar even after the challenge indicates that their avoidance was natural—meaning it was impossible for them. He then strengthens his argument by citing the Quranic challenge that would otherwise be meaningless.

Commentary
This passage is an extended explanation of the root cause behind the confusion mentioned in the previous lesson. The author first states that Allah created humans with natures (ṭabāʾiʿ) that incline toward immediate pleasures, and these natures become enemies to reason (ʿaql) in moral judgment. He then distinguishes the judgments of reason, which are stable and unchanging, from the judgments of nature, which are mutable through discipline (riyāḍa). He illustrates with animals that can be trained to overcome natural aversion. In humans, even aversion to killing can become easy. Reason perceives both present and absent, while nature only perceives the immediate. Allah made intellects the proof (ḥujja), not natural inclination, and obligated people to follow reason even if nature recoils. Then the author turns to speech and expressions: they may differ in aesthetic quality but do not alter the actual rights (ḥuqūq). The good in itself is not affected by the speaker's eloquence. Therefore the standard (qadr) of good and evil is reason, not nature or eloquence. Reason is like direct visual knowledge (ʿilm al-ʿiyān) – unchanging and a foundation for hidden things. Because natures oppose reason in beautifying and vilifying, many people fail to perceive correctly, confusing the clear with the ambiguous. The author concludes with a prayer for protection from such inversion.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi argues that qiyas (analogical reasoning) is indispensable for theological knowledge of Allah.

Core Idea
Without qiyas, knowledge of the unseen Maker is impossible.

Maturidi begins by addressing the role of qiyas in theology. He argues that if qiyas is not used, then the Mu'tazilite method of knowing the Maker through reason alone collapses, because the senses cannot reach Allah. This sets the stage for his proof that all actions are created by Allah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disjunction
Read the first sentence: Maturidi presents two possibilities: either qiyas is used or it is not. Notice how he frames this as a logical necessity.
This is a common kalam technique to narrow down options.
2
Trace the consequence of not using qiyas
If qiyas is not used, the opponents' method of knowing the Maker fails because Allah is beyond sensory perception. Therefore, qiyas must be used.
Maturidi is countering those who rely solely on empirical evidence.
3
Understand the inference from the witnessed
Maturidi states that qiyas is based on inference from the witnessed (shahid) to the unseen. He observes that all meanings found in the world are also present in human actions.
This establishes a common ground between observable actions and divine creation.
4
Consider the alternative via revelation
If actions are not created by Allah, then only revelation (sam') can tell us about creation, leading to the necessity of the general verse 'Creator of everything' (Quran 6:102).
This shows that either qiyas or the general scriptural statement forces the conclusion.

Commentary
This passage is a complex proof (using qiyas, empirical observation, and scriptural appeal) that Allah creates all actions, with a special focus on the act of faith (iman). Maturidi begins by discussing the role of qiyas (analogical reasoning): either it is used in this matter or not. If it is not used, then the opponents' (Mu'tazilites) method of knowing the Maker fails, because sensory knowledge is impossible; thus qiyas must be used. The method is inference from the witnessed (shahid) to the unseen. We observe that all meanings (properties) found in the world and its accidents are also present in the actions of creatures. Therefore, if these actions are not created by Allah, then knowledge of creation would be limited to revelation alone, which then necessitates using the general statement "Creator of everything" (Quran 6:102) literally, since there is no other way to attribute creation to Him. Alternatively, if qiyas is used, it leads to the same conclusion: the servant does not become a creator by his act, so the act is created by another (Allah). Maturidi then focuses on iman, the best action. If Allah were not its creator, two absurdities follow. First, it would imply preferring the obedient servant over Allah, because the servant would create an excellent action (iman) while Allah creates only vile things like filth and ugly substances. Since excellence is in actions, the servant would surpass Allah in virtue—an impossibility. He notes this is especially true for the Mu'tazilites, who deem disbelief supremely evil; similarly, iman surpasses all good substances. Second, the reward for iman is based on its rational goodness; but if its goodness were merely sensory, it could change, but rational goodness does not. Since reward is proportional to the act, and Allah promises tenfold reward, this shows that the act of iman is created good by Allah. Finally, Maturidi adds an argument from the Quran: Allah condemns those who love praise for what they have not done, and He requires thankfulness to Him for faith. If Allah did not create faith, He would be praising Himself for something He did not create, which is impossible. Thus, Allah must be the creator of iman.

The Creation of Actions and the Excellence of Iman
Maturidi proves actions are created by Allah and extols iman.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses the observed fact that actions contain meanings similar to the world to prove they are created by Allah, and why iman is the most excellent action.

Core Idea
Since the servant does not become a creator, his actions must be created by Allah.

Maturidi continues the qiyas argument by focusing on the traces of actions. He argues that the way to know the agent is through the effects. Actions have observable meanings, so they must have a creator. Since humans do not create their own actions, Allah must be the creator. He then highlights that iman, the act of faith, is the best and most luminous action.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice the two logical steps: servant not becoming creator, and the excellence of iman.
The passage is concise but packed with meaning.
2
Understand the inference from traces
Maturidi says the way to know the agent is through the traces of the act. Since we see actions, they must have an agent. The servant does not create his own act, so the agent is Allah.
This is an application of the qiyas from the previous lesson.
3
Reflect on the nature of iman
Iman is described as the best, most luminous, most perfect action. Why does Maturidi emphasize this? Because if such an excellent act is not created by Allah, it would lead to absurdities (next lesson).
This sets up the argument for the necessity of Allah creating iman.

The Two Absurdities and the Quranic Argument
Maturidi presents two absurdities and a Quranic argument for Allah creating iman.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two absurdities that follow if Allah were not the creator of iman, and the Quranic argument that Allah obligates thankfulness for faith.

Core Idea
If Allah did not create iman, the servant would be superior to Allah and reward would be unjust.

Maturidi now presents two devastating consequences if one denies that Allah creates the act of faith. First, it would imply the servant creates something better than any good creature, making the servant superior to Allah. Second, the rational goodness of iman would not change, proving its divine origin. He then adds a Quranic argument: Allah requires gratitude for faith, impossible if He did not create it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two 'amran' (matters)
Maturidi says 'two matters enter upon us'. Read carefully to find both absurdities. The first is about superiority, the second about reward.
The Arabic uses 'ahaduhuma' and 'al-thani'.
2
Analyze the first absurdity
If the servant creates iman, which is better than any good substance created by Allah, then the servant would be superior in virtue. Maturidi notes this especially applies to the Mu'tazilites.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Analyze the second absurdity
The goodness of iman is rational and immutable, unlike sensory goodness. Reward is proportional, and Allah promises tenfold reward, showing iman's goodness is created by Allah.
This argument links the nature of goodness to divine recompense.
4
Understand the Quranic argument
Allah condemns being praised for what one didn't do and requires thankfulness for iman. If Allah did not create iman, He would be soliciting praise for something He didn't create, which is impossible.
This is a direct scriptural proof.

Commentary
This passage is a complex proof (using qiyas, empirical observation, and scriptural appeal) that Allah creates all actions, with a special focus on the act of faith (iman). Maturidi begins by discussing the role of qiyas (analogical reasoning): either it is used in this matter or not. If it is not used, then the opponents' (Mu'tazilites) method of knowing the Maker fails, because sensory knowledge is impossible; thus qiyas must be used. The method is inference from the witnessed (shahid) to the unseen. We observe that all meanings (properties) found in the world and its accidents are also present in the actions of creatures. Therefore, if these actions are not created by Allah, then knowledge of creation would be limited to revelation alone, which then necessitates using the general statement "Creator of everything" (Quran 6:102) literally, since there is no other way to attribute creation to Him. Alternatively, if qiyas is used, it leads to the same conclusion: the servant does not become a creator by his act, so the act is created by another (Allah). Maturidi then focuses on iman, the best action. If Allah were not its creator, two absurdities follow. First, it would imply preferring the obedient servant over Allah, because the servant would create an excellent action (iman) while Allah creates only vile things like filth and ugly substances. Since excellence is in actions, the servant would surpass Allah in virtue—an impossibility. He notes this is especially true for the Mu'tazilites, who deem disbelief supremely evil; similarly, iman surpasses all good substances. Second, the reward for iman is based on its rational goodness; but if its goodness were merely sensory, it could change, but rational goodness does not. Since reward is proportional to the act, and Allah promises tenfold reward, this shows that the act of iman is created good by Allah. Finally, Maturidi adds an argument from the Quran: Allah condemns those who love praise for what they have not done, and He requires thankfulness to Him for faith. If Allah did not create faith, He would be praising Himself for something He did not create, which is impossible. Thus, Allah must be the creator of iman.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the claim that human actions are necessitated (jabr). Maturidi begins by stating that the claim of necessity is invalid because every person knows intuitively that they are free and make choices. If one were to claim that everyone knows something by heart that contradicts this, it would lead to absurdity. Then he addresses an objection: if actions are not necessitated, then there is no governance in them. He responds by distinguishing between two aspects: from the perspective of creation (khalq) the action is according to God's will and creation, which is a kind of necessity, but from the perspective of acquisition (kasb) the action is the servant's choice. These two aspects do not contradict each other. He gives an example: the statement of disbelief is itself false, but as an indicator of the speaker's foolishness it is true. Similarly, an action is chosen from the aspect of acquisition, but not from the aspect of creation. The aspect of creation does not negate choice. He clarifies that whether God creates the action or creates the heavens and earth, it does not negate the servant's choice. Finally, he emphasizes that calling God's creation of actions 'creation' does not imply compulsion for the servant, because the power (qudra) for the action is also created by God, and that power is what makes the servant free, not compelled.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of a Mu'tazili argument about God's creation of evil. Maturidi begins by accusing his opponent (likely al-Ka'bi) of lying against his opponents—they never claimed that God insults Himself. He then argues that if God created an act of insult from a disbeliever, the act is lying, injustice, and foolishness from the disbeliever's side, not from God. God, knowing the act as it truly is, is wise and truthful. The act itself, being an accident or movement, is not inherently insulting. The blame attaches to the doer, not the creator of the doer. Next, he addresses the objection that prophets were killed by God's decree, yet this does not remove God's wisdom. He refutes the claim that such matters did not occur in the Prophet's time, arguing that this would invalidate the Qur'an which often clarifies events before they happen. He then turns to a specific verse that the Mu'tazila misinterpret to deny that God creates evil. He shows their inconsistency: they deny attributing the reality of created acts to God, yet they use revelation to prove such attribution is impossible. This is circular. Finally, he presents four aspects that demonstrate why God's creation of all acts is praiseworthy: (1) it shows all creatures' dependence on God; (2) true power is over acts that others have no power over; (3) it exposes the foolishness of those who misunderstand God's creation; (4) it shows that God is beyond praise or blame from His acts. This refutes the Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Second Proof: The Argument from Dominion
Maturidi argues from dominion that God's will is effective in all realms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the proof from dominion and authority, which argues that God's will must be effective because initiating an action in another's domain without permission is a sign of weakness, incompatible with divinity.

Core Idea
Acting in another's domain without consent indicates weakness—impossible for God.

Maturidi's second argument shifts from personal experience to a logical principle about authority and dominion. He posits that if someone acted in another person's territory without their consent, that would show weakness or coercion. Since God is perfect and sovereign, He cannot be described by such weakness; therefore His will must always be effective, even in the actions of others. This passage presents that reasoning succinctly.

Guided Path
1
Read the second proof passage

The argument: (1) producing an effect in another's realm without their will indicates weakness or subjugation; (2) such a description is impossible for God because it contradicts His lordship; (3) therefore, God must be described as having effective will in all domains.
This is a reductio ad absurdum: if God's will were ineffective, He would be weak, which is false.

Reflect on how this proof supports the doctrine that everything that happens occurs by God's will, without negating human responsibility.
Maturidi’s emphasis is on God's authority, not on determinism versus free will.

God's Will is Not Wishful Thinking: Choice, Compulsion, and Divine Will
Maturidi distinguishes God's will from human tamanni and argues that God's will cannot be ineffective.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God's will cannot be ineffective or reduced to human wishful thinking, and how Maturidi distinguishes between divine will and human tamanni.

Core Idea
God's will determines all actions; otherwise it would be mere wishing.

Maturidi sharpens the distinction between God's will and human wishful thinking. He argues that if God's will were ineffective — if things could occur against His will — then His will would resemble human tamanni, which is a weak, fruitless wishing. But God's will is necessarily effective and all-encompassing. This passage establishes the logical necessity that God wills every existing thing.

The Impossibility of Willing the Impossible
Maturidi uses the example of asking guidance for Iblis to show we cannot will the impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why, according to Imām Māturīdī, it is impossible to genuinely will something that one knows will never occur, and how this principle applies to both humans and Allāh.

Core Idea
You cannot sincerely will what you know will not happen—this is true for you and for Allāh.

This passage from Imām Māturīdī’s Kitāb al-Tawḥīd tackles a subtle point about the relationship between knowledge and will. He argues that if you know with certainty that something will not occur, it is impossible (muḥāl) for you to genuinely will it. He then extends this principle from human beings to Allāh, showing that Allāh’s will is perfectly aligned with His knowledge. The example of asking guidance for Iblīs illustrates the point.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it, then read the literal translation. Note the flow of the argument.
The passage is dense, so take your time.
2
Identify the hypothetical scenario
The passage begins with a hypothetical: if a prophet's prophethood were confirmed by a human statement, willing that statement would be a sin. This is a setup to highlight that God's knowledge prevents willing the impossible.
This is not the main point; it's an analogy.

The passage then says that a servant cannot sincerely ask God to guide someone (like Iblīs) if he knows that person will not be guided. Because his knowledge makes it impossible for him to genuinely will that guidance.
This is the key principle: knowledge of impossibility precludes sincere willing.

The passage concludes that if it is impossible for us to will what we know will not happen, then it is even more impossible for God, because His knowledge is perfect. So God's will only concerns what He knows will occur.
This refines our understanding of God's will (irādah) in relation to His knowledge.
