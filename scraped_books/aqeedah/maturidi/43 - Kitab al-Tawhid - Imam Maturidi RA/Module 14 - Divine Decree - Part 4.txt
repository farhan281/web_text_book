Who is More Praiseworthy: The Prophet or Iblis?
Maturidi answers which of two figures is more praiseworthy using the same distinction of ability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses the distinction between two types of ability to answer a paradoxical question about moral praiseworthiness.

Core Idea
Moral praiseworthiness depends on the type of ability, not just the action.

Maturidi continues his dialectic by posing a provocative comparison: a prophet who would sin if able versus Iblis who would obey if able. The answer hinges on what we mean by 'ability'. This passage refines the earlier distinction and applies it to a concrete case to show the absurdity of deterministic premises.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the two questions posed and the two-part answer. Pay attention to the final phrase 'لا قوة إلا بالله'.
The passage is short but dense; read it twice.
2
Identify the two meanings of ability
The respondent distinguishes between 'the causes' (الأسباب) and 'the power that is with the act' (القوة التي معها الفعل). Understand how each leads to a different judgment of praiseworthiness.
The first meaning makes the prophet more praiseworthy; the second makes the question impossible.
3
Examine the parallel question about obedience
The second question mirrors the first: who is more obedient, one who obeys if befriended or one who disobeys if shown enmity? See how the same logic applies symmetrically.
This shows the consistency of the author's argument.
4
Reflect on the purpose
Ask yourself why Maturidi brings up this comparison. It serves to expose the absurdity of the deterministic view that ability determines action. If ability is taken as a power that necessitates action, the question becomes meaningless.
This connects to the broader theological debate on free will and predestination.

Again the point is that the deterministic premise leads to absurdities.

Inability as Excuse: When It Holds and When It Does Not
Maturidi refutes the claim that inability is always an excuse by distinguishing prevention from waste.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that according to Maturidi, a claim of inability is a valid excuse only when the inability is genuinely prevented and not due to the servant's own waste (tadyi').

Core Idea
Inability excuses only when not self-inflicted.

This lesson focuses on Maturidi's initial refutation of the determinist's claim that 'I couldn't do it' is the ultimate excuse. He argues that such an excuse is valid only in cases where the inability is a genuine prevention from outside, not when the servant himself has wasted his capacity or prevented its occurrence through his own action. This distinction is crucial for understanding divine justice and human responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's claim
Read the opening sentence to see what the determinist argues: that the best excuse is inability, and it applies to the unseen world just as in the witnessed world.
Note the phrase 'فمثله في الغائب' suggesting analogy between seen and unseen.
2
Observe Maturidi's response
Notice how he immediately distinguishes: the excuse works only in what prevents ability from him, not in what he wasted completely (ما ضيعها باتا). This is the core distinction.
3
Understand the concept of waste (tadyi')
Reflect on what it means to 'waste' ability. It implies the servant had the means to act but neglected them, thus forfeiting the excuse.
Compare with the example of ignorance in the next lesson.
4
Apply to the unseen world
Ask yourself: How does this distinction apply to religious obligations? Maturidi argues that the same principle holds in matters of faith and action.

Commentary
This passage refutes the argument that inability is an absolute excuse. The opponent claims that in the witnessed world, the best excuse is 'I couldn't do it', and similarly in the unseen (i.e., regarding divine commands). The author responds that this excuse works only when the inability is not due to the servant's own negligence (tadyi') and when it is a prevention that does not allow new ability to occur. Then he gives another example: ignorance of the command is an excuse only if the servant did not neglect seeking knowledge. If he had the means to know, it is not an excuse. Then a further argument: the servant might say, 'Because You informed that I will not do, so I refrained to avoid making You a liar,' which turns the blame back on God. This is a reductio ad absurdum showing the deterministic position leads to claiming that God owes thanks to the servant. The final conclusion is that any answer the determinist gives is weaker than this counter-argument.

Further Excuses: Ignorance and Divine Foreknowledge Do Not Absolve Negligence
Maturidi refutes two more deterministic excuses: ignorance of the command and reliance on divine foreknowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi refutes the excuses of ignorance and the claim that divine foreknowledge of our actions exempts us from responsibility.

Core Idea
Knowing God knows you won't do something is no excuse to not do it.

Maturidi continues his refutation by addressing two more deterministic arguments. First, the excuse of ignorance: if a person does not know the command, is that a valid excuse? He answers that it is only if the person did not neglect seeking knowledge. Secondly, the determinist might argue that since God knows and informs that a person will not do an act, the person refrains to avoid making God a liar. Maturidi shows this leads to the absurd conclusion that the servant has a favor over God.

Guided Path
1
Read the ignorance example
Notice how Maturidi presents the second example: the excuse 'I did not know' is similarly conditional. He says it is not an excuse if the person was given the means to know but neglected seeking it.
The phrase 'بما أعطى ما لو لم يترك طلبه ليبلغه' indicates that the means were available.
2
Analyze the deterministic argument about foreknowledge
The determinist says: 'You informed that I will not do, and You knew, so I refrained to avoid making You ignorant and lying.' Understand this argument: it tries to shift blame to God's knowledge.
3
See the reductio ad absurdum
The determinist then adds another argument: 'I have the greater favor upon You because You empowered me and placed Your lordship in my hand.' This reduces to claiming that God owes the servant thanks for being able to disobey. Maturidi shows the absurdity.
The conclusion: any answer the determinist gives is weaker than this counterargument.
4
Summarize the lesson
Reflect on how Maturidi systematically shows that deterministic excuses ultimately blame God and are invalid.

Correcting the Question About God Willing Evil
Maturidi corrects a Mu'tazilite question about God willing evil and provides a logical argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the question 'Does God intend to be insulted?' is improper and how Maturidi reframes it to affirm God's wisdom and will.

Core Idea
The proper question is about God's intent for the moral status of actions, not direct intent for evil.

Maturidi addresses a Mu'tazilite objection about divine will and evil actions. He first corrects a misinterpretation of a Quranic verse, then sharpens the theological question. This lesson walks through his correction and the underlying argument about divine wisdom.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the literal translation. Notice the structure: a citation, a correction, a rephrased question, and an argument.
Pay attention to the line 'أيريد الله أن يُشتَم؟' — the key error.
2
Identify the opponent's error
The opponent asks whether God intends to be insulted. Maturidi says this is wrong. Why? Because it anthropomorphizes God's intention as directly related to the insult.
Compare with the corrected formulation.
3
Understand the corrected formulation
Maturidi says the proper question is: 'Does God intend that the act of insult be ugly and displeasing?' This refocuses on the moral quality of the action, not the action's occurrence.
This aligns with divine wisdom (hakim).
4
Follow the argument about divine wisdom
The Shaykh argues that if one denies that God wills evil actions, then either God is not wise (if He wills falsehood) or one is compelled to affirm will, otherwise there is falsehood. This shows the necessity of affirming divine will for all occurrences.
This is a logical dilemma.
5
Conclusion and broader implication
Maturidi concludes that the prohibition and anger are not from the same aspect as will; this suffices for the topic of creation of actions.
This clarifies the distinction between moral and ontological will.

Divine Power and the Prevention of Evil
Maturidi answers an objection about divine power and prevention of evil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that God's ability to prevent evil does not obligate Him to do so, and that His not preventing is due to His will, not impotence.

Core Idea
Power does not compel action; will determines whether power is exercised.

Imam Maturidi addresses a common objection: if God can prevent evil, why doesn't He? The objection seems to imply that God lacks power if He does not prevent. Maturidi's response clarifies the relationship between divine power and will, showing that God's not preventing is an act of wisdom and free choice, not weakness. This lesson examines that response and its proof from the nature of compelled faith.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation once to get the flow of the argument. Note the exchange between the objector and Maturidi.
The passage is a reply to an objection; try to identify each part.
2
Identify the objection and the core response
Find where the objector says 'if He is able to prevent but does not, then He is not prevented' (i.e., He lacks power). Then note Maturidi's counter: power without will does not indicate inability.
The response hinges on the distinction between qudrah (power) and iradah (will).
3
Analyze the proof by analogy
Maturidi gives the example of compelling people to Islam: if God forced them, they would not be genuine Muslims. This shows that not preventing evil is consistent with power and wisdom.
The proof uses a shahid (witnessed) example to clarify the ghayb (unseen).

Commentary
This passage is a theological objection and its response regarding divine will and human action. The objector argues: most people disbelieve because Iblis willed it, yet God wills obedience from them. Thus Iblis's will appears more effective in God's own dominion. The author answers by distinguishing between will and emotions like pleasure and anger. A person can be pleased or angered without acting at that moment, but will is a condition for action; it must exist for the act to occur. Therefore, God does not love or have pleasure in the disbeliever as that would necessitate an action, whereas will is different. The author then states the common principle: action arises from will, coercion, or forgetfulness. God cannot be described by coercion or forgetfulness concerning the servant's act, so it must be by will. The Mu'tazila are criticized for not affirming a real attribute of will in God beyond a mere temporal knowledge without necessity. The text then presents a second objection: Iblis's will is mere wish (tamannī), not true will, because if the servants truly willed faith, they would not disbelieve. The jurist replies that will entails coercion (ghalaba), while wish does not, so how can the enemy's wish overcome God's will? Belief in compulsion (qahr) is rejected. A third objection asks: if a wise master can prevent his servant from an undesired act, why doesn't God do so? The opponent counters with compulsion, but this is wrong because God does will the act, and prevention is not required. The Shaykh explains that if one judges by human custom, prevention only occurs when the agent lacks power or does not will the act. Prevention, if necessary, must be for a cause, not arbitrarily. If the cause would lead to compulsion, then the agent truly lacks power; otherwise, compulsion is not permissible in divine governance. The passage concludes with a statement of reliance on God.

Imam al-Maturidi presents the second proof that qadar is not an excuse because humans act freely.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second proof that God's decree does not compel human actions, and how al-Maturidi argues that humans have genuine choice and responsibility despite qadar.

Core Idea
Divine decree provides context, not compulsion — humans choose freely.

Imam al-Maturidi presents his second argument against using qadar as an excuse for sin. He carefully distinguishes between the decree as a contextual framework and as a compulsion. The passage insists that humans retain the ability to choose the opposite of what they do, proving that qadar is not a deterministic force.

Qadar is not a conscious motive at the time of sin, so it cannot be an excuse.

Imam al-Maturidi answers an objection about divine and human agency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Maturidi responds to the objection that his arguments remove human actions from God's power, affirming both divine creation and human choice.

Core Idea
Human free will is as certain as sensory perception, yet our actions reveal a divine dimension beyond our control.

Imam al-Maturidi anticipates a crucial objection: if God creates all actions, how can humans be responsible? He answers by first establishing that every person knows intuitively that they are free agents. Then he points out that our actions often contradict our own intentions and estimations, showing a divine dimension. This passage is central to understanding the Maturidi doctrine of kasb (acquisition).

After refuting the objection, Maturidi lays out a foundational principle: every person intuitively knows they are a possible agent. But then he shows how actions frequently contradict their own assessments of good and bad, place and time, and involve unintended toil. From these sensory-like discrepancies, he concludes the actions are not truly theirs.

Commentary
This passage begins with a statement: the divine attributes of generosity, bounty, self-sufficiency from punishment, forgiveness, and the fact that He gains nothing from obedience and loses nothing from disobedience cannot be used as an argument to deny punishment or affirm reward. Then he observes that each person finds his actions turning out differently from what his own intellect judges as good or evil, and differently from his estimation of place and time, and often involves unintended toil and pain—despite his power not being deficient. Good and Evil Are from God: The Correct Understanding
Refutation of a misrepresented Muslim creed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise meaning of the Muslim statement 'good and evil are from God' and why it does not imply God is the source of evil.

Core Idea
Muslims affirm predestination of good and evil from God, not evil itself.

Some opponents claimed that Muslims attribute evil directly to God by saying 'good and evil are from God.' In this passage, the author refutes this misrepresentation. He clarifies that Muslims affirm that the predestination (qadr) of good and evil is from God, but the predestination of evil is not the evil itself. This distinction safeguards divine transcendence.

Commentary

Core Idea
Ascription to Allah must convey glorification, not censure.

Maturidi addresses a subtle theological point: although Allah creates all actions, not every action should be directly ascribed to Him. This lesson explains the rationale behind the etiquettes of ascription, distinguishing between what is fitting for divine glorification and what is not.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal explanation of the proper manner of ascribing actions to Allah, particularly regarding evil deeds. The author begins by stating the ruling: it is disliked (makruh) to say that disbelief and sins are by Allah's decree, predestination, and will, giving two reasons. The first reason is the ugliness (qubh) inherent in such acts; they are only mentioned in contexts of censure and contempt, so ascribing them to Allah would be inappropriate. The second reason is that such speech is often used as an excuse or argument, but the author asserts that there is no excuse for sin. He then gives an analogy: people do not address Allah as 'Creator of filth and impurities,' even though He is the Creator of everything. The foundational principle (asl) is then laid out: ascription to Allah is only permissible when the ascription conveys glorification, gratitude, mention of His blessings, or His command; anything else is not ascribed to Him, even if He is its real Creator. The author concludes with a summary (jumla): Allah is described by His action (fi'l), which always falls under justice ('adl) or grace (fadl). Sometimes what is not actually His action is ascribed to Him if it implies a praiseworthy meaning attained through His benevolence; otherwise it is not ascribed. Allah is described only by these two aspects—justice and grace—since His actions contain nothing but these qualities.

The Qadariyya Reinterpretation and Its Logical Inconsistency
Maturidi reports the Qadariyya position and refutes it by showing logical inconsistency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Maturidi refutes the Qadariyya's claim that divine misguidance is merely trial and deprivation, using the principle of consistency in ascription.

Core Idea
If the cause for ascribing good applies, the same logic forces ascribing evil.

The Qadariyya attempted to reinterpret verses that clearly attribute misguidance to Allah as merely trial and deprivation, avoiding direct creation of evil. Imam Maturidi begins his refutation by pointing out a fatal inconsistency: if the cause of ascription of good is command and strengthening, then the same logic would require ascribing evil to Him by the same cause. He then cites Quranic verses to show that misguidance is indeed attributed to Allah in a direct sense.

Guided Path
1
Read the Qadariyya position
Read the first part of the passage where the Qadariyya interpret divine misguidance as trial and deprivation, and good things as command and strengthening.
Notice how they separate good and evil in ascription.
2
Identify the logical argument
The author argues: if the cause for ascribing good is command and strengthening, then by the same token, the cause for ascribing evil would be trial and deprivation. Yet the Qadariyya don't ascribe evil directly.
This is the core inconsistency.
3
Examine the Quranic citations
Read the three verses (Ibrahim 14:4, As-Saff 61:5, At-Tawbah 9:127) that clearly attribute misguidance and turning of hearts to Allah.
These verses show that misguidance is directly from Allah, not just trial.
4
Understand the conclusion
The author concludes that if this logic of ascription were consistent, it would work for both good and evil, but since it doesn't, there must be a different meaning in ascribing misguidance.
