The Impossibility of Spontaneous Generation Proves a Wise Creator
Maturidi argues that things cannot come from nothing; their origin shows a wise Creator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that the origin of complex beings, like humans, cannot be explained by natural processes alone, and therefore proves a wise Creator.

Core Idea
Natural processes alone cannot produce humans; a wise Creator is necessary.

Maturidi presents a powerful argument against spontaneous generation. He shows that the development of a human from a sperm, the movements of stars, and even the mixture of light and darkness all require a wise decree beyond the materials involved. This lesson unpacks that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that Maturidi uses a very compressed style here.
2
Understand the opening premise
Maturidi begins by noting that creating something from nothing is beyond human capacity, yet everyone affirms it for God. This sets up the problem: if humans cannot create from nothing, how do things come to be?
3
Examine the examples
He lists several phenomena: embryological development, stellar influences, the mixture of light and darkness. Each is claimed to occur by nature, but Maturidi argues they are beyond mere materials.
For instance, in sperm, food, and womb there is no trace of human qualities like reason or sight.
4
Reflect on the final analogy
Maturidi argues that if natures and substances of good and evil were left to themselves, no creation would result. Therefore, claiming existence arises spontaneously is even more inconceivable.
This reinforces the need for a wise, knowing decree.

The Embryo: A Proof of a Wise Creator
Maturidi presents two arguments: from embryonic development and from the impossibility of substances creating themselves.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the development of the human embryo from simple components demonstrates the necessity of a wise, knowing Creator.

Core Idea
The embryo's emergence from lifeless matter proves creation requires a wise designer.

Imam Maturidi begins with an observation everyone can make: the human being comes from a sperm-drop that contains no trace of intellect, hearing, or sight. Yet these complex faculties appear. He challenges the view that nature alone could produce them, arguing that without a determining power, such order would be impossible. This passage lays the foundation for a rational proof of God's existence.

This connects to the learning objective.

Commentary
This passage is a multi-part proof and explanation. First, Maturidi presents an argument from the development of the human embryo: in the sperm, food, and womb, there is nothing of human meaning (intellect, hearing, sight), yet these emerge by the determination of a wise, knowing Creator. This shows that creation cannot be from the natural elements themselves. Second, he argues that if different natures or substances of good and evil were left to themselves, they would not produce any substance or creation. Therefore, claiming that things come into being by such natural causes is even more inconceivable. Third, he reaffirms that the wisdom in creating diverse substances lies in placing each thing in its proper place, and that God did not create out of need but by His essence. Fourth, he explains the purpose of creation: to test humans who are given intellect and knowledge of blessings and trials, so that they are tested by placing things in their proper order and showing gratitude. God made all creatures as proofs and tests, so that humans learn piety, caution, and promptness for good outcomes, and avoid disliked outcomes, through witnessing different substances and states. This establishes that divine promise and threat are based on sense and observation, which is the path to knowledge and the comprehension of ends.

The Wisdom Behind Creating Diverse Substances
Maturidi explains the wisdom of creating diverse substances for testing and grounding promise and threat in sensory experience.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the purpose behind God's creation of different substances: to test humans and make divine promise and threat perceptible.

Core Idea
God created diverse substances as a test, grounding reward and punishment in sensory experience.

After establishing that a Creator is necessary, Maturidi now explains why the Creator makes substances different in nature, including good and evil. He shows that this diversity is not arbitrary but serves a profound purpose: it allows humans to be tested, to learn piety, and to perceive divine promise and threat through actual observation. This passage reveals the rational basis for moral responsibility and eschatology.

Reflect on the role of senses

This connects to his epistemology: sensory experience is the foundation of knowledge.

The Fallacy of Denying Creation from Nothing Using Imagination
Maturidi presents the absurd logical consequence of denying creation ex nihilo based on imagination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why it is logically invalid to deny the creation of something from nothing based on the inability to imagine it.

Core Idea
Denying creation because it's unimaginable leads to absurd consequences.

In this passage, Imam Maturidi refutes the claim that creation from nothing is impossible because it cannot be conceived in the imagination. He shows that this argument would also allow anyone with defective senses to deny sensory evidence, and would justify rejecting all unseen realities. This would refute not only rational thought but also the beliefs of other groups, such as the Magians, who blindly follow their predecessors.

Guided Path
1
Read the hypothetical argument
Read the passage and identify the conditional statement Maturidi presents: 'if it were permissible to deny a thing's coming from nothing by what is not conceivable in the imagination...'.
Pay attention to the logical structure: if P then Q.
2
Notice the absurd consequence
Maturidi shows that this would allow anyone with defective senses to deny sensory perception and deny all unseen things. This is an absurd outcome that no rational person would accept.
3
Understand the refutation of the Magians
Maturidi points out that this argument refutes the Magians and others who blindly follow their predecessors without rational basis.
The Magians are Zoroastrians who believed in dualism and rejected creation ex nihilo.

For if He intended all things but did not create them, He would be praising by what is not His, and that is a lie. And in excluding some things is making another equal to Him in that he is a maker of everything, intending what he has no making in, and that is corrupt. Moreover, if that were allowed in transferring [the act] to another which is the act of another, it would be permissible to say: He is not the creator of anything, on the basis that He is not the creator of what is the act of another. So since it is a description of Him by blame and servitude, it is established that the first is a description of Him by praise and lordship, and in specification is affirmation of the first. And also He said: He is the Lord of everything and the God of everything, and He is over everything a guardian, and it is not permissible to exclude anything from that; even if it is not fitting to say it of specification due to ugliness, such as saying: Lord of filths, God of abominations, Guardian of devils and Iblis, and standing over every foul and unclean thing, then the like of it is the first, even if it is ugly in specification in some things by name. Commentary
This passage is a proof that God must be the Creator of all things, including actions, and that any exception would undermine His praise and lordship. Maturidi begins by stating that the verse (such as 6:102) is a praise (imtidaah). If anything among created things were outside God's creation, then the praise would either be for something not His, or He would share it with every weak being—both absurd. If God intended all things but did not create them, His praise would be false. If He created only some, then others are equal to Him as creators, which is corrupt. Moreover, if one transfers the act to another, then one could say God is not the Creator of anything, which is blameworthy. Thus, the first description—praise and lordship—requires that He be Creator of all. Maturidi then cites the verse 'He is the Lord of everything, the God of everything, and over everything a guardian' to show nothing can be excluded. Even if specific designations like 'Lord of filths' sound inappropriate, that does not change the universal meaning. He then notes that the Magians and Zindiqs claimed God did not create harmful things, and this is the root of Mu'tazilism. The Prophet (peace be upon him) declared the Qadariyya the Magians of this community. If anything were outside God's creation, then other exalted names like 'King' would also have exceptions, and His praise would be nullified because everything would have partners in its meaning.

Divine Creation vs. Human Action
Maturidi discusses the establishment of similarity in action and its negation, and distinguishes divine creation from human acquisition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Allah refutes anthropomorphism by establishing that no creature can produce a creation like His, and the distinction between divine creation ex nihilo and human acquisition.

Core Idea

This passage is a doctrinal argument and refutation. Imam Maturidi begins by stating that similarity (tashabuh) between occurents necessarily entails their contingency (huduth). Intellectual Argument for the Temporality of Bodies
Intellectual proof using the principle of accidents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the classic kalam argument from accidents to prove that bodies are temporal (created), and how it establishes the need for a creator.

Core Idea
Bodies are inseparable from temporary accidents, so bodies themselves must be temporary.

Imam Maturidi presents one of the foundational rational proofs for the existence of God by examining the nature of bodies. The argument goes: bodies always have accidents (motion, rest, etc.), these accidents come and go, so they are temporal; whatever cannot exist without temporal things is itself temporal. This lesson unpacks that reasoning and shows why the entire world needs a creator.
