Refuting Dualism: The Mediator Argument
Maturidi presents the Sabian/Manichaean view and refutes the concept of a mediator between light and darkness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Maturidi refutes the dualistic concept of a mediator between light and darkness.

Core Idea
A mediator between opposites must be subordinate, collapsing dualism into monotheism.

Imam Maturidi addresses the dualistic claims of the Sabians and Manichaeans, who posit two eternal principles of light and darkness. He presents their view and then refutes the idea of a mediator that bridges them, showing that any such mediator either implies a single creator or is meaningless.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice the description of the Manichaean view and the two options for the mediator.
The passage uses a dense dialectical style typical of kalam.
2
Understand the dualistic claim
Note how the Manichaeans describe light and darkness meeting from opposite directions with some mixture. This sets up the need for a mediator.
3
Examine the mediator dilemma
Maturidi presents two possibilities: the mediator arises by design (tadbīr) or by coercion (by nature). Trace each option and see where it leads.
The argument is a reductio ad absurdum.
4
Reflect on the implications
Consider how both possibilities undermine dualism. If by design, good and evil come from one source. If by coercion, the mediator is superfluous.
This connects to the learning objective.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of dualistic doctrines, particularly those of the Sabians and Manichaeans, as well as the concept of a mediator (al-wasit) between light and darkness. Maturidi first notes that the Sabians and Manichaeans are similar, with only a slight undefinable difference according to Ibn Shabib. He then describes the Manichaean view: light meets darkness from the north, darkness from the south, and they meet in a certain direction with some darkness entering light, and they are infinite in other directions. Maturidi then addresses the mediator: it must be either a design that caused the world or an accidental meeting. He presents two possibilities: First, if the mediator is by design, then the mixture is invalid because light and darkness have opposite natures (light rises, darkness descends) with a barrier between them; if they are mixed by design, then the designer becomes the source of both good and evil, implying a single designer. Second, if the mediator is overcome by force of nature, then it is helpless and its role as mediator is meaningless, leaving only light and darkness. Thus, the dualistic position collapses.

Refutation of the Magian Myth of God and Iblis
Maturidi narrates and refutes the Magian story of God's amazement and pact with Iblis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi considers the Magian story about God and Iblis to be logically incoherent and worse than dualism.

Core Idea
The Magian myth makes God the source of evil, contradicted by divine perfection.

Maturidi quotes and then refutes a peculiar Magian (Zoroastrian) account of the origin of evil. The story claims that God admired His creation, feared opposition, and through a thought Iblis came into being. Maturidi shows that this narrative, far from solving the problem of evil, actually worsens it by attributing both good and evil to God.

Dualists at least keep good and evil separate, but Magians end up with both from God.
3
Analyze the refutation
Follow Maturidi's logical arguments: the inconsistency of the thought being evil yet from God, the problem of God's ignorance in the truce, and how this leads to contradiction.
The refutation shows the internal inconsistency of the Magian position.

Commentary
This passage presents and refutes the Magian (Zoroastrian) account of the origin of evil. Maturidi begins by quoting their claim: God admired His creation, feared opposition, so He thought, and Iblis (Satan) came from that thought. Some add that God accidentally looked upon Iblis and made a pact with him, giving him a temporary lease, after which God will destroy him. From Iblis comes all evil, from God all good. Maturidi then declares that if this is truly their belief, they are worse than the dualists. He explains that the dualists believed in two eternal principles because they could not conceive creation from nothing and saw both good and evil in the world. They attributed good to one deity and evil to another. However, the Magians accept creation from nothing, yet they balk at the idea that the good God does evil. But in their story, both good and evil come from God: the evil thought is from God, and Iblis is from that thought, so evil ultimately originates from God. Their position is contradictory because they try to separate good and evil but end up making God the source of both. Additionally, if God made a truce out of ignorance of Iblis's future evil, that ignorance is itself evil, compounding the problem. Maturidi refutes the narrative by demonstrating its inconsistency with divine perfection.

This passage is a refutation of the Magian (Zoroastrian) explanation for the origin of evil. It begins by presenting their claim: that God, after creating the world, admired it and feared opposition, so He had a thought from which Iblis (Satan) arose. Some say God gave Iblis the evil eye, then made a truce with him, granting him respite until a set time when God would destroy him, attributing all evil to Iblis and all good to God. Maturidi then argues that if this is truly their belief, they are worse than the Dualists (al-Thanawiyya). He explains that Dualists posited two eternal principles because they could not conceive of creation from nothing and found it difficult to attribute both good and evil to one God; but the Magians actually accept creation from nothing and yet attribute the origin of evil to God Himself (through His thought), thus they fall into greater contradiction. The Magians made God the source (umm) of evil by saying that the base thought is evil, and Iblis came from it. Maturidi points out that this makes God responsible for both good and evil, which is the very problem the Dualists sought to avoid, so the Magians' position is inconsistent. He then examines the truce narrative: if God did not know Iblis would do evil, that is ignorance (evil); if He knew and allowed it, that is also evil. Similarly, if God is able to prevent Iblis but does not, that is evil; if He is unable, then He is not Lord. Maturidi also notes that the truce implies God trusted Iblis to fulfill his promise, but the fulfillment would be good, yet evil results—another contradiction. He then reverses the argument: if God is the source of good and yet evil comes from Him, one could just as well say all good comes from Iblis and all evil from another. Next, he questions how God could feel secure in power over Iblis when Iblis had no helpers while God had many, and those helpers were mingled with God's helpers? He then addresses the objection that the truce was for some benefit, saying this argument would also apply to harmful vermin—they too might have a beneficial aspect. Finally, he attacks the notion that God could be affected by the evil eye: if the eye can harm God, overpower Him, remove His power, and repel His knowledge, then He is not independent but dependent on another, which would make Him a servant, not a lord. The passage ends by noting that no harmful essence fails to benefit some creation.

This passage presents a classical Islamic theological argument against the Christian claim that Jesus performed miracles by God's power. The Flaw in the Honorific Interpretation: 'Father' and 'Brother' Analogies

Learning Objective

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of Christian claims about Jesus (peace be upon him). Maturidi addresses two main aspects: the claim of divinity (rububiyya) and the claim of sonship (bunuwwa). He begins by discussing a possible objection: if God called Jesus 'son' as an honorific, that would imply equality with other honored beings like angels and prophets, which is untenable. He concludes that the verses do not support sonship, as the way to know sonship (through physical attributes) does not apply to God.

Commentary
This passage refutes the Christian claim that Jesus is the son of God through several interconnected arguments. Maturidi first states that there are multiple aspects to the claim. The first aspect is physical birth, which is impossible for God because God is self-sufficient, free from need, desire, and loneliness—the very causes that drive creatures to seek offspring. The Aspect Contradiction: Al-Ka'bi on Pain and Free Will
Maturidi reports al-Ka'bi's view that free agents are compelled in pain and refutes him.

Core Idea
One action cannot be both compelled and free without specifying aspects.

Al-Ka'bi, a Mu'tazili theologian, attempted to reconcile divine compulsion and human free will by claiming that while humans are free in their actions, they are compelled in the resulting pain. Maturidi exposes the inconsistency in this view by showing that al-Ka'bi denied the same principle of aspects when applied to divine creation and human acquisition. This passage is a concise example of the kalam method of exposing internal contradictions.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of al-Ka'bi's argument that an action cannot be both the servant's action and God's creation. Al-Ka'bi claimed it is impossible, and Maturidi responds by calling it ignorance. Al-Ka'bi then argued that dual agency would entail partnership (shirk) because if two agents share an action, each cannot have sole possession of a part, especially if the action is indivisible. He then contradicted himself by considering the opponent's distinction of aspects: partnership only arises when the aspect is one; if aspects differ, it is like property inherited partially and purchased partially. Maturidi points out that al-Ka'bi's argument is flawed because there is no example in the witnessed world that demonstrates that dual agency necessarily entails partnership. Since they cannot provide a clear example, their claim is mere assumption and imagination. Maturidi sarcastically suggests that if they reflect on inheritance laws, they would see their ignorance of actual partnership, and that might excuse their failure to understand what is known by reasoning.

Prevention and the Loss of Ability
Maturidi refutes the Mu'tazila claim that prevention does not negate ability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's refutation of the Mu'tazilite claim that prohibition does not entail loss of ability.

Core Idea
Prevention and loss of ability both render action impossible.

In this passage, Imam Maturidi directly addresses a key Mu'tazilite argument: that a prohibited action remains possible even if ability is intact, because prohibition does not remove ability. Maturidi counters by asserting that prevention and loss of ability are equivalent in rendering action impossible. Let's examine his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation to understand the gist.
Notice the key terms: ممنوع, فوت القدرة, منع.
2
Identify the Mu'tazila claim
Identify what the Mu'tazila claim: that a prohibited action does not result from loss of ability, so the action can occur if not prevented.
3
Follow Maturidi's counter-argument
Maturidi argues that prevention and loss of ability are one in making action impossible. Also, action cannot occur during prevention, but can when ability is lost due to prior ability.
Consider the logical equivalence Maturidi draws.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The final statement 'لا قوة إلا بالله' underscores that all power is from Allah, reinforcing the dependence on divine power.
This is a common closing phrase in theological discussions.

Commentary

Maturidi argues that prevention and loss of ability are equivalent in making action impossible. He points out that action cannot occur during prevention, but can occur when ability is lost due to previous ability. The argument upholds that ability is a prerequisite for action.

Refuting al-Ka'bi's Dilemma
Maturidi presents two options for al-Ka'bi's reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi refutes al-Ka'bi's argument by presenting a dilemma: either al-Ka'bi applies all human attributes directly to God, leading to absurdities, or he considers the underlying meaning, which nullifies his claim.

Core Idea
Analogy requires considering underlying meaning, not just outward similarity.

Al-Ka'bi, a Mu'tazili theologian, argued that if we distinguish between God and humans in terms of actions, then lying from humans could be truth from God. Maturidi shows that this argument forces al-Ka'bi into a dilemma: either he applies all human attributes directly to God, leading to absurdities, or he considers the actual meaning of the action, which nullifies his claim.

Guided Path
1
Read al-Ka'bi's argument
Read the passage and identify al-Ka'bi's claim: if distinction between God and others is allowed, then lying from others could be truth from God.
Notice how Maturidi dismisses it as foolishness.
2
Identify the two options
Maturidi says al-Ka'bi has only two possibilities: either he directly applies everything from the witnessed to the absent, or he considers the underlying meaning.
These are the two horns of the dilemma.
3
Examine the first option
If al-Ka'bi takes the first path, he must accept absurd consequences like creating useless things and punishing without cause. His own argument about lying is turned against him.
Whatever he answers, it binds him.
4
Examine the second option
If he considers the meaning, his statement is nullified by the clear intuition of the intellect that distinguishes what is permissible for God.
This shows his argument has no basis.

So I do not know what drove him to this fancy. And we have already shown by his departure from that and his obstinacy in both claims. If he returns to considering the meanings which are the causes of the realities of things, that is conceded to him, and his saying 'by the intuition of the intellect' has no meaning; that is rather the nature of temperaments and their aversion. Then he denies that there is in God's creation anything truly ugly, evil, or corrupt, despite the existence of countless things with these descriptions in the intellects. Rather, God – exalted be His praise – by making them so, struck a similitude of what is ugly of actions and intended to make horrific the sight with which a person of intellect is threatened. Commentary
This passage is a refutation of a specific argument by al‐Ka‘bī, a Mu‘tazilī theologian. Al‐Ka‘bī claimed: If a distinction is made between God and what comes from other than Him – i.e., if we say that the same kind of act (e.g., lying) is impossible for God but possible for humans – then it would follow that lying from someone else could be truth from God. In other words, he denied the possibility of any qualitative difference between divine and human actions, assuming a strict analogical inference (tamthīl) from the witnessed (shāhid) to the absent (ghā’ib). Maturidi first dismisses this as foolishness and reminds that al‐Ka‘bī's obstinacy has already been shown. He then offers a logical analysis: al‐Ka‘bī has only two options. Either he takes every observable human action as being either wise or foolish and directly applies the same judgment to God (the first alternative), or he considers the underlying meaning (al‐ma‘nā) that makes an action wise or foolish in reality and then applies that meaning to God (the second alternative). If he takes the first path, it would force him to admit that creating useless things, creating something from nothing, and punishing without removal are also wise – which contradicts his position. Moreover, his own argument about lying would be turned against him. Whatever he answers will apply equally to his own claim. If he takes the second path – considering the underlying meaning – then his statement is nullified by the clear intuition of the intellect that distinguishes between what is permissible for God and what is not. Maturidi then criticizes the very idea that realities of things can be grasped by 'the beginnings of intellects' (bidāyat al-‘uqūl) without proper reflection on meanings. Intellects are composed to differentiate things by their specific meanings that necessitate difference, and to unify things by meanings that necessitate coming together. This is the proper function of thought and reflection. Furthermore, no one can know a thing as knowledgeable without knowledge, or powerful without power; the very definition of knowledge and power requires their subjects to be qualified accordingly. If a subject admits the description of power and knowledge, it is knowledgeable and powerful. Thus, God's attributes cannot be denied based on mere analogy from human attributes. Maturidi then turns to al‐Ka‘bī's denial of real ugliness and evil in creation, pointing out that countless things are recognized by intellects as ugly and corrupt. God Himself used such things as similitudes for ugly actions to warn people. The passage concludes by noting that earlier Muslim imams argued against the Dualists (al‐Thanawiyya) that good and evil can come from one source (God) in order to refute dualism.

Critical Role of Meanings in Theology
Maturidi criticizes reliance on intuitive intellect and argues for considering meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's critique of relying on 'beginnings of intellects' and his emphasis on using meanings to know the realities of things.

Core Idea
Intellects are tools for distinguishing by meanings, not for raw intuition.

After refuting al-Ka'bi's analogy, Maturidi criticizes his method of appealing to 'the beginnings of intellects.' He explains that intellects are composed to differentiate things by their specific meanings, and that one cannot grasp realities without proper reflection. He then argues that denying real ugliness and evil in creation is inconsistent with clear intellectual evidence.
