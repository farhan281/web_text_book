Evidence That Eternal Threat Applies Only to Shirk
Maturidi argues on the basis of human nature and intellect that eternal fire is only for shirk.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that eternal punishment is reserved for shirk, based on human nature, intellect, and revelation.

Core Idea
Eternal punishment fits only shirk, as human nature recoils from leaving faith.

Imam Maturidi presents a unique proof from human nature and reason to show that eternal fire is only for shirk. In this passage, he argues that people naturally consider their beliefs permanent, while actions are variable. Therefore, threatening eternity for lesser sins contradicts both innate disposition and divine wisdom.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Begin by reading the Arabic passage carefully, then read the English translation. Notice the structure and key terms.
Pay attention to 'جبلة الخلق' (human nature) as a central concept.
2
Identify the three supports
The passage uses three supports: human nature (jibilla), intellect ('uqul), and revelation (sam'). Identify how each is used.
3
Understand the contrast
Maturidi contrasts beliefs (considered permanent by holders) with actions (variable). This contrast is key to why only shirk merits eternity.
Belief lasts, actions change – so only leaving belief (shirk) can cause eternal punishment.
4
Examine the refutation of Mu'tazila
The passage concludes that the Mu'tazilite position (eternal fire for major sins) goes against both nature and divine management. Consider why.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Think about how this argument protects the believer's hope while upholding divine justice. Summarize the reasoning in your own words.

Then the evidence for the threat of eternity (al-khulud) is that nothing below shirk (polytheism) can bear it—an matter which people are naturally disposed to: their aversion to what would cause them to exit from their religions which they have believed, even if they have criticized them intellectually, by proof, or by imitation, to the existence of lesser slips among them even if they differ. So this indicates that such aversion is part of human nature; rather, intellect confirms this, since beliefs are eternal in the eyes of their holders, but not so the actions that are pointed to, and likewise their opposites. And likewise revelation (al-sam') regarding the actions pointed to is that they are variable. Therefore, their religions are such that they leave them. So what we have mentioned indicates the departure of the Mu'tazilite school from what is naturally disposed to and also driven away by divine management. And there is no power except with God.

Commentary
This passage is a proof (dalala) that the threat of eternal punishment (khalid fi al-nar) applies only to shirk, not to lesser sins. The author argues from human nature (jibilla): people naturally recoil from abandoning their religion, even if their religion is flawed or adopted blindly. They will excuse themselves for minor faults but not for leaving the faith altogether. This natural aversion, supported by intellect (al-'uqul), shows that beliefs are considered permanent by their adherents, whereas actions are seen as variable. Revelation also treats actions differently. Hence, it is unnatural and irrational to think that a believer who commits a sin deserves eternal punishment. Consequently, the Mu'tazilite position—that major sins without repentance lead to eternal hell—goes against both human nature and divine ordering. The author concludes that such a view is 'outside of what people are naturally disposed to' and is also rejected by divine wisdom.

The Unknown Boundary and Calling Oneself a Believer
Maturidi refutes the Mu'tazilite position using their own premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi turns the Mu'tazilite doctrine of the unknown boundary between minor and major sins against them, showing it prevents them from calling themselves believers.

Core Idea
If the boundary is unknown, no one can be sure they are still a believer.

The Mu'tazilites hold that the boundary between minor and major sins is unknown, so a person must remain in fear and hope, never claiming security or despair. Maturidi seizes on this premise to argue that if the boundary is unknown, then one cannot even be sure that one's own sins are minor or major. Since major sin removes the name of faith, the Mu'tazilites cannot consistently call themselves believers. This lesson walks through that argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Mu'tazilite premise
Read the opening of the passage to see what the Mu'tazilites claim about the boundary between minor and major sins.
They say the boundary is unknown, so a person should be in a state of fear and hope.
2
Follow Maturidi's application
Notice how Maturidi applies this premise: if no one knows the boundary, then no one can know if their own sins are major or minor, leading to doubt about the name of faith.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
Consider that if doubt prevents claiming security and despair, it also prevents calling oneself a believer, because the same uncertainty applies.
Maturidi uses their own logic to show inconsistency.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite position on calling oneself a believer. The Mu'tazilites hold that the boundary between minor and major sins is unknown, so a person must remain in a state of fear and hope, never claiming security or despair. The argument uses the Mu'tazilites' own premises to show the inconsistency of their position.

The Dilemma of Righteousness and the Fear of Prophets
Maturidi presents a dilemma and prophetic evidence against the Mu'tazilites.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses the Mu'tazilites' own logic to show that they cannot call themselves righteous without contradiction, and how the fear of prophets supports his position.

Core Idea
If you can't call yourself righteous, you can't call yourself a believer either.

Maturidi continues his refutation by presenting a dilemma regarding self-praise and the claim to righteousness. The Mu'tazilites, by their own principles, cannot call themselves righteous or pious without contradicting the Quran and logic. Moreover, he points out that even prophets feared God—not because of major sins, but because God may punish for minor ones. This lesson explores these further arguments.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dilemma
Read the passage and identify the two options: either the Mu'tazilites call themselves righteous or they don't. See the problems with each.
If they do, they must fear hell, contradicting Quranic verses. If they don't, then by the same logic they cannot call themselves believers.
2
Reflect on self-praise
Note that the Quran warns against self-praise, and calling oneself a believer could be a lie that is a major sin.
This adds another layer of inconsistency.
3
Consider the prophetic example
Maturidi mentions that even prophets feared God, showing that fear is not only due to major sins.
This undermines the Mu'tazilite claim that believers have no fear.

Maturidi's Refutation of Mislabeling the Murji'a
Maturidi refutes the naming of a group by a name not truly theirs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that calling a group by a name not its true name is ignorance or obstinacy and elevates liars while degrading the truthful.

Core Idea
Naming wrongly elevates liars and degrades the truthful.

Maturidi addresses the controversial label 'Murji'a' used by opponents. He argues that such naming is not only inaccurate but carries grave ethical and religious implications. This lesson unpacks his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once through, then read the literal translation. Notice the flow of the argument from naming to consequences.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the core claim about naming
Maturidi begins by stating that using a name not its true name stems from ignorance or obstinacy. Then he questions why this group is singled out.
He implies the name does not truly describe the group.
3
Trace the logical consequences
Follow his reasoning: if the name implies a quality, it should apply to others; by using a non-true name, the namers themselves become deserving of blame; they also invalidate the name for individual good deeds, leading to the conclusion that the naming elevates liars and degrades the truthful.
4
Reflect on the religious significance
Maturidi ends by stating that such mislabeling is 'grievous for those who understand.' Consider why accuracy in naming matters in theological disputes.

Core Idea

This passage clarifies the nuanced Sunni position on major sins and the true meaning of irja'.

Commentary
This passage defines the correct type of irja' (postponement of judgment) concerning the fate of a believer who commits sins. Maturidi argues that it is not possible for God to utterly deprive such a person and consign him to eternal Fire, because he still possesses knowledge of God, enmity towards God's enemies, and veneration of God's friends. Therefore, his affair is deferred to God's will: He may forgive him or make his good deeds expiate his sins, as indicated by Quranic verses. Alternatively, He may recompense him precisely according to his deeds, which is an act of justice, though He is essentially bestowing reward as favor. Maturidi then declares this type of irja' (hope without certainty) to be true and obligatory. He contrasts it with the Mu'tazila's irja', which is a different kind: the Mu'tazila refused to call a grave sinner a believer or a disbeliever, thus deferring the name. Maturidi criticizes this as ignorance of the true nature of the action, whereas the correct irja' acknowledges the limits of human knowledge about God's treatment and relies on revelation, which does not provide a decisive judgment. Thus, the believer must defer to God's will.

Commentary
This passage is a theological discussion about the Murji'ah sect and a related hadith. Maturidi begins by addressing a claim that the Murji'ah are those who postpone judgment about 'Ali and his opponents. He distinguishes between a neutral postponement (stopping short of judgment) and a blameworthy postponement (which might be close to the Mu'tazili view?). He then argues that the virtues of 'Ali were so well-known through hadith, the inclusion of 'Ali in the shura by 'Umar, and the agreement of the Companions that it was clear 'Ali's status was high. Therefore, anyone who suggests that his supporters could be blameworthy is ignorant. Next, he cites the well-known hadith that two groups will not receive the Prophet's intercession: the Qadariyyah (those who affirm human capacity independent of God) and the Murji'ah. Maturidi explains that this hadith can be interpreted in two ways. First, the term 'Murji'ah' here may refer to the Jabriyyah (fatalists), because they are opposite to the Qadariyyah: the Qadariyyah give full power to humans, denying God's will and planning in actions, while the Jabriyyah refer all actions to God, leaving no reality for human agency. Both extremes are blameworthy. The truth is the middle: actions are from humans in terms of their acquisition, but from God in terms of their creation. Second, the hadith may refer to the practice of making exceptions in declaring one's faith (al-istithna'), i.e., saying 'I am a believer if God wills.' This is a form of irja' (postponement) because it withholds full affirmation. The Hashwiyyah (anthropomorphists) are known for this. Maturidi notes that this interpretation is not strongly attested. He then discusses the Mu'tazili view on the status of the grave sinner and the division of people into believer, disbeliever, and hypocrite, concluding that the hypocrite receives outward rulings of faith but inner rulings of disbelief in the Hereafter.

Commentary
This passage is a theological discussion about the Murji'ah sect and a related hadith. Maturidi begins by addressing a claim that the Murji'ah are those who postpone judgment about 'Ali and his opponents. He distinguishes between a neutral postponement (stopping short of judgment) and a blameworthy postponement (which might be close to the Mu'tazili view?). He then argues that the virtues of 'Ali were so well-known through hadith, the inclusion of 'Ali in the shura by 'Umar, and the agreement of the Companions that it was clear 'Ali's status was high. Therefore, anyone who suggests that his supporters could be blameworthy is ignorant. Next, he cites the well-known hadith that two groups will not receive the Prophet's intercession: the Qadariyyah (those who affirm human capacity independent of God) and the Murji'ah. Maturidi explains that this hadith can be interpreted in two ways. First, the term 'Murji'ah' here may refer to the Jabriyyah (fatalists), because they are opposite to the Qadariyyah: the Qadariyyah give full power to humans, denying God's will and planning in actions, while the Jabriyyah refer all actions to God, leaving no reality for human agency. Both extremes are blameworthy. The truth is the middle: actions are from humans in terms of their acquisition, but from God in terms of their creation. Second, the hadith may refer to the practice of making exceptions in declaring one's faith (al-istithna'), i.e., saying 'I am a believer if God wills.' This is a form of irja' (postponement) because it withholds full affirmation. The Hashwiyyah (anthropomorphists) are known for this. Maturidi notes that this interpretation is not strongly attested. He then discusses the Mu'tazili view on the status of the grave sinner and the division of people into believer, disbeliever, and hypocrite, concluding that the hypocrite receives outward rulings of faith but inner rulings of disbelief in the Hereafter.

Maturidi refutes sects on istithna' in faith using Quranic address.
