Refuting the Futility Objection of the Mu'tazila
Maturidi refutes the Mu'tazila objection about giving strength to a known disbeliever.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Maturidi refutes the Mu'tazila argument that giving strength to a known disbeliever is futile, by distinguishing divine wisdom from human analogies.

Core Idea
God's wisdom transcends human analogies; giving strength serves multiple purposes, not solely belief.

The Mu'tazila argued that it is futile for God to grant strength to someone He knows will disbelieve, as it seems like enabling sin. Imam Maturidi meets this objection head-on, showing that divine wisdom operates beyond limited human reasoning. He not only refutes the claim but also establishes a crucial principle: what appears harmful in human experience (al-shahid) does not necessarily apply to the unseen reality of God's actions (al-gha'ib).

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the English translation. Get a sense of Maturidi's argumentative style.
Notice the self-objection style he uses to present the Mu'tazila view.
2
Identify the Mu'tazila claim
The Mu'tazila claim that giving strength to a known disbeliever is futile. Pinpoint the exact phrase in the passage that states this.
The claim is implicit in the objection he counters: 'المعتزلة تزعم أنه أعطى القوة ليؤمن بها'.
3
Analyze Maturidi's two responses
First, he argues that strength can be given for other benefits (like feeding a known disbeliever). Second, he distinguishes between the witnessed world (shahid) and the unseen (gha'ib), stating that what is harmful in the former does not apply to the latter.
The analogy of feeding a disbeliever who will not believe is key.
4
Reflect on the divine transcendence conclusion
Maturidi concludes that God's wisdom is beyond the understanding of a limited intellect, so the objection fails. Consider why this is a powerful theological principle.
This reinforces the incommensurability of divine and human actions.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an objection. Maturidi addresses the Mu'tazila argument that it is futile for God to give strength to someone He knows will disbelieve. He begins by discussing a case where someone gives to a needy person for necessity, and notes that even giving to a known wrongdoer can be wise if it serves other benefits, like feeding a known disbeliever for reasons other than faith. The Mu'tazila assert that God gives strength specifically for belief, which is negated by His foreknowledge. Maturidi counters that God gives strength for various purposes, not solely for belief. He then turns to the objection from common sense: a wise master would not give a servant what will cause disobedience. The response is that what appears ugly in the witnessed world (al-shahid) is due to harm or pain, but this does not apply to the unseen (al-gha'ib). God's wisdom transcends human analogies. Thus, the objection is invalid.

Refuting the Misinterpretation of the Burden Verse
Maturidi refutes the misinterpretation of the burden verse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's refutation of the claim that the verse 'Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity' removes obligation at the moment of action.

Core Idea
Dropping obligation at action time contradicts the verse's affirmation of capacity.

The Qur'anic verse 'Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity' (2:286) is often invoked to discuss human ability. Some argue it means obligation is lifted at the very moment of action, implying that one is not required to perform what they cannot immediately do. Maturidi refutes this by showing that such a view actually burdens the person beyond capacity, making the obligation impossible to fulfill.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the translation. Notice that Maturidi states he has already explained the correct interpretation, and here he focuses on refuting a false claim.
The passage is a single sentence; pay attention to the logical flow.
2
Identify the opponent's claim
The opponent claims that at the time of action, the obligation is dropped and the ability to perform it is nullified. Find this in the Arabic: 'في إسقاط التكليف وقت الفعل وإبطال القدرة عليه'.
This is the misinterpretation Maturidi refutes.
3
Understand the consequence
Maturidi argues that this leads to the obligated person being burdened beyond capacity. In Arabic: 'فيصير في التحصيل المكلف على غير الوسع'. Ask yourself: why does dropping obligation at action time result in burdening beyond capacity?
Because if one is required to do something but the obligation vanishes exactly when they are supposed to do it, they can never fulfill it.
4
Grasp why it is ugly
Maturidi calls this claim 'ugly' (قبح). Reflect on why contradicting the verse's clear statement that Allah does not burden beyond capacity is considered ugly in theological terms.
This reinforces the correct understanding: the verse affirms capacity, it does not excuse performance.

This passage is a concise refutation of a specific misinterpretation of the Qur'anic verse 'Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity' (2:286). In other words, by removing the obligation at the time of action, the opponent inadvertently makes the obligation impossible to fulfill, which contradicts the verse's intent. The author's point is that the verse affirms capacity, not that it removes obligation at the moment of performance.

The Sword Story and the Refutation of Al-Aṣlaḥ
Maturidi argues against al-aṣlaḥ using the sword story.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses the story of the sword to argue that prevention is not always better, thereby refuting the Mu'tazilite obligation of al-aṣlaḥ.

Core Idea
If prevention were always better, Allah would prevent all sin.

Maturidi employs a historical narrative to challenge a rationalist theory about divine justice. The story of a man who drew his sword on the Prophet serves as a test case for whether prevention (manʿ) is always better for a person's religion. By analyzing this example, Maturidi exposes a contradiction in the Mu'tazilite claim that Allah must always do what is best for His servants.

Guided Path
1
Read the story and the question
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how Maturidi presents a specific narrated event and then poses a dilemma: was the prevention better for that person's religion, or would letting him act have been better?
The event is from a hadith where a man attempted to kill the Prophet but Allah prevented him.
2
Analyze the two possible answers
Maturidi considers two answers: 'letting go' (iṭlāq) or 'prevention' (manʿ). For each, he states the implication that follows. Identify each implication in the text.
The first answer implies Allah may not do the best; the second implies prevention can be better.
3
Trace the logical conclusion
If the opponent admits prevention is better, then every sinner who is not prevented has not had the best done for them. This contradicts the Mu'tazilite position that Allah always does the best.
Maturidi ends with a reminder of reliance on Allah's power.
4
Reflect on the theological significance
Understand that this argument does not deny Allah's ability to prevent sin, but refutes the claim that He is obligated to do so. It defends divine wisdom and justice without imposing human notions of 'best'.

From this, the author draws a broader implication: if prevention is better, then every sinner who is not prevented from sinning has not been treated in the best way. The Objection of Resemblance in Power and Its Refutation
Objection-and-answer with a fundamental principle of power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why affirming human power does not imply resemblance to God, and the principle that power is always for an act.

Core Idea
Human power is derived from Allah; His power is unique and independent.

This passage from Imam Maturidi addresses a key objection: if humans have power, don't they resemble God? The answer lies in distinguishing created power from divine power. We'll see how the objection is refuted through two arguments and a fundamental principle that power cannot exist without an act.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and initial answer
Read the Arabic passage and translation carefully. Note how the opponent’s objection is framed and the immediate response from the Muslim theologian.
The response relies on the distinction of being powerful 'by Allah' versus 'by itself'.
2
Analyze the two aspects of the question
The passage presents two aspects: uniqueness in power and independence from God. Understand how each aspect refutes the opponent’s own previous positions or leads to absurd conclusions.
The first aspect uses the opponent's own argument against the Dualists; the second shows that if human power were independent, it would negate need for God.
3
Grasp the principle of power for the act
Imam Maturidi states: 'power is impossible for it to exist not for an act.' This is a key principle in kalam. Understand what it means and how it refutes the claim that power can precede the act (takhalluf).
If power existed before the act, then God would give power for something impossible, which is corrupt.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The passage ends with 'there is no power except by Allah.' Consider how this ties the entire argument back to tawhid and dependence on God.

He said: This is not necessary, because you are powerful by Him, and He is not by other than Him, as is said concerning knowledge by Him. Sheikh Abu Mansur, may Allah have mercy on him, said: If you were powerful by Allah, it would not be permissible for the power of Allah to cease by your power, just as when you know by Him, Allah does not cease by your knowledge. Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer combined with a fundamental principle. The opponent objects: if you (Muslims) affirm power for yourselves, you resemble God in His attribute of power. The answer: no, because your power is from God (bil-lāh), while God's power is not from another. Abu Mansur elaborates: if your power were from God, it would not diminish His power. The question has two aspects. First, claiming uniqueness in power: the opponent himself used this argument against the Dualists, so it applies to him. Second, claiming independence from God: if man were powerful independently, he would not need God for the act. If one says he needs God for preservation, that is ambiguous; if he says God creates another power, that implies temporary independence. Then the principle: power cannot exist without an act (i.e., it must be related to an act); likewise, inability is not unrelated to an act. It is possible to be powerful for an act at one time and incapable at another, but power cannot precede its act. If power existed before the act, then God would give power for something impossible (since the act may never occur). Reason necessitates that power is only for the act, refuting the claim of antecedent power (takhalluf). The passage ends with 'there is no power except by Allah.'

Power Over Faith Does Not Imply Power Over God
Maturidi presents and answers the objection that power to believe implies power over God.

Learning Objective

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an objection raised by al-Ka'bi regarding God's power and inactivity. The argument proceeds in two main parts: first, Imam Maturidi identifies two aspects in which al-Ka'bi erred; second, he provides a detailed theological-physical argument about motion and rest. The first aspect is that power (qudra) exists solely for action (fi'l). If it is free from action for a time, it can legitimately be free for multiple times, as God is described. The lasting times of a body are never empty of them, but power does not endure, so it must act when it exists. Finally, he clarifies that the issue at hand is about action; for a body, we allow that at the moment of its existence it may lack the action that is the name for remaining (i.e., the act of being). Thus, the refutation denies the opponent's analogy.

The Power Argument: Refuting the Objection
Maturidi refutes the initial objection about power and its use.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Power is a favor from God for obedience; misuse is not attributed to God.

Maturidi encounters an objection: if God knows how power will be used and grants it, does that not make Him responsible for misuse? In this passage, he explains that power in human context is not given for specific outcomes, and God's grant is a favor, not a commission. Core Idea
The act is by a power sought from God, not by God's power itself.

The objector poses a direct challenge: if the disobedient acts by God's power, why not say disobedience is from God? Maturidi points out two errors in this question. The power is given specifically for obedience, and misuse does not revert to God.

Commentary
This passage is a theological discussion on the nature of divine will (al-irada) and its relationship to the creation of human acts. Maturidi begins by stating that the question of the will can be linked to the question of the creation of acts. He argues that if the creation of acts is affirmed, then God's will must also be affirmed in the sense that He is described as creating. Conversely, if the creation of acts is denied, then the will is nullified in the sense that it would no longer serve to repel compulsion and forgetfulness, which are the real meanings of will in the observable world (al-shahid). However, Maturidi notes that the term 'will' might be used for wish, command, claim, or pleasure, but God cannot be described with some of these in all cases; such interpretations are invalid. He then presents a second view: the two issues (will and creation) can be separated as the mutakallimun have done, but the first view (that affirming the will necessitates affirming creation) is the truth. 

Maturidi then provides Quranic evidence to support his position. He cites verse 6:125 about God expanding breasts for Islam or constricting them, explaining that God wills guidance or misguidance through human actions. Another verse (6:39) shows God differentiating between people by two willings, indicating that God's will is distinct from command or pleasure. Verses like 32:13, 5:48, and 6:149 are used to show that God's will is effective and would have guided all if He had so willed. Maturidi argues that interpreting these verses as pleasure or command is impossible because those would have required prior conditions. He also refutes the interpretation of compulsion (al-qasr) with two reasons: first, God has taught humanity the true nature of guidance and religion, making it impossible for Him to intend the opposite; second, the path to faith is through ijtihad (effort), not compulsion.

Dialectical Refutation of the Mu'tazila on God's Will
Maturidi uses a dialectical example to refute the Mu'tazila equation of God's will with His creation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Willing an insult can be praiseworthy or blameworthy; God's will is beyond such contradictions.

Maturidi uses a practical example: consider someone who wills the insult of the Messenger of God to be as severe as that of Iblis. That person is a disbeliever. But one who wills the insult to be a great matter (to magnify the Prophet's status) is praised. This shows that the same act (insult) can be willed with different intentions, and the Mu'tazila rejection of a distinct will leads to contradictions.

The Two Meanings of God's Command (Amr)

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between God's creative command and His prescriptive command, and why this distinction resolves an objection about divine will and human action.

In this passage, Imam al-Maturidi responds to an objection that claims since God's command is effective and cannot be repelled, everything that happens must be morally willed by God. He clarifies that the word 'command' (amr) has two distinct meanings: a creative command that brings things into existence, and a prescriptive command that imposes obligations. Understanding this distinction is crucial for reconciling divine sovereignty with human moral responsibility.

The prescriptive command does not entail that God wills the sinful act, because the act is done by a free agent (mukhtār) who has his own will (irāda). It is impermissible to say that God is the one 'commanded' in the sense of being obligated. Thus, the objection fails.

Learning Objective
