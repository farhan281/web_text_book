The Arabs' Inability to Produce Like the Qur'an
Maturidi argues that the Arabs could not meet the Qur'anic challenge despite their eloquence and sacrifices.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi argues for the Qur'an's miraculous nature by highlighting the Arabs' inability to produce its like despite their eloquence and efforts.

Core Idea
The Arabs' failure to meet the Qur'anic challenge, despite their ability, proves it is from God.

This passage from Imam Maturidi presents a key argument for the miraculous nature of the Qur'an. He focuses on the Arabs' characteristics and their response to the challenge. Let's examine his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
2
Identify the premise about the challenge
Notice that the challenge (taqrīʿ) was issued before hostilities and rebuked both humans and jinn. This shows the challenge was serious and universal.
3
Analyze the conditional argument
Maturidi argues that if the Arabs could have produced similar speech, they would have done so with praise and pride. Instead, they submitted and refused, proving their inability.
Look for the word لو (if) and understand the implied conclusion.
4
Consider the universal scope and sign
The challenge was to all humans and jinn, and it spread widely. Additionally, the Prophet's upbringing among the Arabs itself is a sign: he was raised among them yet produced something they could not match.
This final point reinforces the argument from a different angle.

Maturidi refutes the time-based objection to the Qur'an's inimitability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's refutation of the claim that the Arabs could have produced a verse like the Qur'an if given enough time.

Core Idea
Time was not the issue; the Arabs' failure proved divine origin.

This lesson examines Maturidi's response to a common objection: that the Arabs merely needed more time to match the Qur'an. He argues that they had ample time and immense effort, yet still failed. Understanding this refutation strengthens the case for the Qur'an's miraculous nature.

Guided Path
1

3
Identify the refutation logic
Notice how Maturidi lists their extreme efforts (poetry, wars, etc.) and argues that if producing similar verses were possible, it would have been easier than those efforts.
4

Commentary
This passage refutes the objection that the Arabs could have produced something like the Qur'an if they had been given more time. If they truly could have produced a similar text, it would have been much easier for them than these extreme measures. Moreover, the challenge was to produce just a few verses; if it were possible for humans, it would have taken only an hour.

Prophetic Reports Spread Widely Due to Their Extraordinary Nature
Maturidi applies the principle of report spread to the messengers, emphasizing the necessity of their widespread transmission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why prophetic reports must be widely transmitted (mutawatir) based on their extraordinary nature and the universal scope of their message.

Core Idea
Extraordinary reports cannot be hidden; prophets' miracles guarantee widespread transmission.

In this passage, Imam Maturidi applies the earlier principle of report spread to the messengers. He shows that the extraordinary nature of their message and miracles compels widespread transmission, refuting the claim that prophetic reports are merely ahad. The passage grounds this in observable evidence from the life of the Prophet Muhammad and the reactions of kings.

Guided Path

Pay attention to the phrase 'al-umur al-'izam' (great matters).

Maturidi states that because the messengers brought extraordinary matters, their reports spread inevitably. Identify this cause-effect relationship.

The Prophet's influence appeared in every remote region, with people coming from the horizons. This is historical evidence of widespread transmission.
Maturidi uses this to counter the claim of ahad.

Commentary
This passage applies the earlier principle to the messengers. Maturidi argues that prophets come with extraordinary matters (al-umur al-'izam) that break custom, so their reports inevitably spread worldwide because hearers cannot conceal them. He notes that even trivial reports spread; how much more so those with universal significance. In any widespread report, falsehood becomes known and is corrected, while truth stands. During the prophets' lifetimes, falsehood is exposed and erased, leaving only truth. As evidence, he cites Prophet Muhammad: his influence appeared in every distant region, with people coming to him from afar. Thus the objector's claim that prophetic reports are ahad is baseless. On the contrary, even a single report (khabar wahid) about an important or extraordinary matter spreads widely. The message of the prophets involved interfaith call, sending letters, delegations, arguments, and the opposition of kings who feared losing their power. The kings' fear shows their recognition that the prophets came from God. Miraculous signs (ayat) have lasting effects as long as followers remain, and they serve as arguments. The passage concludes with a declaration of reliance on God.

The Quran Surpasses Poetry and Soothsaying
Maturidi proves the Quran's inimitability by comparing it to poetry and soothsaying.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Maturidi proves the Quran's divine origin by showing it surpasses both poetry and soothsaying in their respective excellences.

Core Idea
The Quran outshines both poetry and soothsaying, proving it is not human speech.

How do we know the Quran is from Allah and not from human eloquence? Imam Maturidi presents a powerful argument: he examines the two highest forms of Arabic speech—poetry and soothsaying—and shows that the Quran surpasses both in their own domains. By excelling in both composition and meaningful content, the Quran stands alone as a miracle.

Guided Path
1
Understand the classification of speech
Maturidi begins by dividing eloquent speech into two types: poetry and soothsaying. Notice what defines each type.
Poetry is about form (nazm), soothsaying about content (ma'ani).
2
See the Quran's superiority
He then states that the Quran surpasses poetry in its composition and soothsaying in its meanings. This double superiority is key.
3
Draw the conclusion
Because the Quran excels both forms, its origin must be beyond human capability—it is divine speech.
4
Review the Quranic evidence
Maturidi cites several verses that challenge anyone to produce something like the Quran, reinforcing the proof.
These verses are the basis for the doctrine of i'jaz.

Commentary
This passage presents an argument for the miraculous nature of the Qur'an (iʿjāz). Maturidi first classifies eloquent speech into two types: poetry (shiʿr) and soothsaying (kihāna). Poetry is characterized by beautiful meter and composition, while soothsaying involves foretelling future events through eloquent Arabic expressions. He then asserts that the Qur'an surpasses both: its composition excels poetry, and its meanings excel soothsaying. Since it combines these superiorities in a single text, it cannot be human speech (kalām al-bashar). He then cites several Qur'anic verses that challenge anyone to produce something similar, such as the verse challenging all of mankind and jinn (al-Isra 17:88), and verses denying it is the speech of a poet (al-Haqqah 69:41), and others. Thus the passage establishes the Qur'an's divine origin through its inimitability.

Refutation of al-Warraq's Objection to Miracles
Al-Warraq objects that prophets' miracles could be tricks; Maturidi begins refuting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Warraq's skeptical objection to miracles and Maturidi's initial refutation, which challenges the objector's authority and distinguishes miracles from natural phenomena.

Core Idea
Miracles are not tricks or natural magic; they are unique departures from the world's normal essence.

Al-Warraq, a known skeptical figure, argued that the miracles of prophets might be nothing more than clever tricks or natural phenomena like magnetism. How can we be sure they are divine? Maturidi responds with a two-part refutation: first, he questions the objector's own knowledge, then he clarifies the defining feature of a true miracle. This lesson explores that exchange.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Pay attention to al-Warraq's reasoning and Maturidi's initial reply.
The passage is incomplete on this page, but the core argument is present.
2
Analyze al-Warraq's objection
Identify al-Warraq's points: prophets didn't test natural powers, didn't know limits of tricks, so their 'miracles' could be mere play. He compares it to magnetic attraction.
3
Examine Maturidi's first response
Maturidi first questions al-Warraq's authority: How does he know it's a trick? This is a methodological challenge.
This is a common defensive strategy in kalam: questioning the opponent's epistemology.
4
Understand the second response
Maturidi argues that if miracles were within the normal essence of the world (like the magnetic stone's property), they wouldn't be wonders. True miracles depart from the essence, which makes them unique.

Commentary

The argument is incomplete, but it sets up the principle that miracles are extraordinary events that surpass natural laws.

The Rational Miracles of the Quran
Maturidi enumerates three rational miracles of the Quran.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three rational miracles of the Quran as explained by Imam Maturidi and how they appeal to the intellect.

Core Idea
The Quran's rational miracles are its unmatched eloquence, unique theological arguments, and accurate prophecies.

Imam Maturidi now turns to the rational miracles (al-`aqliyya) of the Quran, which are accessible to the intellect. He presents three distinct aspects that demonstrate the Quran's divine origin: its literary inimitability, its unprecedented arguments for monotheism and resurrection, and its prophecies about future and recurring events. This passage invites us to appreciate the intellectual depth of the Quran beyond emotional appeal.

Guided Path

3
Reflect on the significance

The uniqueness argument is key in establishing divine origin.
4
Connect to the broader theme

Maturidi is building a comprehensive case for the Quran's authenticity.
