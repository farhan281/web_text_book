Third Proof: Form-Giver and Incapacity
Maturidi presents the third proof and additional evidence from self-awareness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's third proof for contingency: every entity and its attribute have forms requiring a form-giver, and everything recognizes its own incapacity, thus cannot be eternal.

Core Idea
Everything has a form and depends on a form-giver; nothing is self-sufficient.

Maturidi's third argument focuses on the nature of entities and their attributes: they are always found with forms, which necessitate a form-giver (muṣawwir). Additionally, everything is aware of its own incapacity and ignorance, indicating its contingency. This lesson unpacks these complementary proofs.

Guided Path
1
Understand the form-giver argument
Every substance (عين) and its attribute are never free from forms (صور). Since they require a form-giver, they cannot be eternal. Consider why this dependence implies temporality.
The argument parallels other kalam proofs: anything that has a cause for its form cannot be eternal.
2
Examine the self-awareness of incapacity
Everything knows of itself its incapacity and ignorance regarding its own states and well-being. This self-awareness is evidence that it is not self-sufficient, hence contingent.
This is a more internal, metaphysical proof compared to the earlier ones.
3
Connect to the overall case
These arguments are part of a cumulative case. How do they reinforce each other? They show that contingency is not just external but also intrinsic.

Commentary
This passage presents a cumulative argument for the temporal origination (ḥudūth) of the world. The author lists several proofs. First proof: In eternity (al-qidam), there is an absence of events (khalā' min al-ḥawādith), but sensory observation testifies to the existence of events; thus the world cannot be eternal. Second proof: Many individual entities (a‘yān) exist and have beginnings in time; they are parts of the whole, so the whole world must likewise be contingent. The author refutes the objection that these entities might have been latent (kāmin) or scattered, showing that such explanations conflict with observable reality. Third proof: Every substance and its attribute are never free of forms, requiring a form-giver (muṣawwir); they are not self-subsistent, so they cannot be eternal. Additional arguments: Everything knows its own incapacity and ignorance, which indicates its contingency. The description of the Maker as powerful and generous in eternity necessitates that He creates; otherwise, the concept of power would become meaningless. Finally, if all were eternal, the attributes of power and action would vanish when a temporal event occurs. Thus, the world must be temporally originated, and God’s creative action is eternal. The passage concludes with 'And Allah is the Grantor of success.'

The Eternal Maker and the Contingency of All Things
Maturidi argues for the necessity of an eternal Creator and the temporal origination of all things.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that describing the Maker with eternal power and generosity necessitates that He creates temporally, and that if all were eternal, divine power would be meaningless.

Core Idea
The Creator's eternal power and generosity require Him to act; if all were eternal, His power would vanish.

After presenting proofs for the world's origination, Maturidi turns to the nature of the Maker. He argues that describing Allah with power (qudra) and generosity (jūd) in eternity necessitates that He creates. Conversely, if the world were eternal, the attributes of power and action would become meaningless when a temporal event occurs. This lesson examines these profound theological implications.

Guided Path
1
Understand the necessity of describing the Maker with power and generosity
Maturidi states that it is necessary to describe the Maker with power in eternity and generosity. This implies that He must bring things into existence. Reflect on why these attributes demand action.
The phrase 'بالقدرة في الأزل والجود لازم' is key.
2
Examine the consequence: eternal creating
God's creating is eternal in the sense that He is always described by it. Maturidi says 'تكوينه أن يكون كل شيء على ما علم أن يكون' — His bringing into existence is that everything is as He knew it would be.
This avoids the problem of change in God.
3
Consider the hypothetical: if all were eternal
If the entire world were eternal, then when a temporal event occurs (which we observe), the description of God's power and action would disappear. This is absurd, so the world must be temporal.
This is a reductio ad absurdum argument.

Ibn Shabib's Argument from Opposition and Contradiction
Ibn Shabib argues that opposition and contradiction necessitate a Creator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Muhammad ibn Shabib's philosophical argument that things characterized by opposition and contradiction cannot be eternal and must have a Creator.

Core Idea
Perpetual opposition cannot sustain existence; an originator is necessary.

Muhammad ibn Shabib, an early theologian, presents a logical argument for the existence of a Creator. He examines things that are characterized by opposition and contradiction, showing that they cannot be eternal. This passage lays the foundation of his proof, using an analogy and two possible scenarios to establish the necessity of a creator.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the dense, technical style typical of kalam.
2
Understand the analogy of the house
Ibn Shabib uses an analogy of a house that cannot be entered until someone else enters. This illustrates the impossibility of an infinite regress or circularity in existence.
The analogy is key to grasping the argument.
3
Follow the two possibilities and their invalidation
He considers two possibilities for opposing things: either they are so by nature (leading to repulsion) or they could depart from their nature by choice (leading to self-annihilation). Both are absurd, so a creator is needed.
This is the core logical move.

Muhammad ibn Shabib said regarding that, the meaning of which according to us is: Since that which subsists upon opposition and contradiction cannot be free from being so forever, so its existence is invalidated insofar as one cannot conceive of any thing from the whole except that it be an existent thing in it, so then every thing that comes to be in it would be the preventer of its existence, so it is invalidated. Commentary
This passage presents Muhammad ibn Shabib's argument for the existence of a Creator. He begins by considering something that subsists upon opposition and contradiction, implying that such a thing cannot be eternal because perpetual contradiction would prevent its existence. He uses the analogy of a house that no one can enter because of a condition of prior entry, illustrating the impossibility of an infinite regress. He then considers two possibilities: either the two principles are opposed by nature, or they could depart from their nature by choice. Both lead to absurdities. Therefore, it must be that they came to be after not being, by an originator. He further argues that because things like seasons change, there must be a creator. Then an objection is raised: if something by itself prevents change at one time, why not when caused by another? He responds that when governed by another, there is a benefit in terms of religion or worldly matters, which is absent when it is by itself. Thus, the two situations differ.

Objection and Response: Time and Purpose
Ibn Shabib responds to an objection about time and governance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection raised against Ibn Shabib's argument and his response regarding the role of time and purpose in distinguishing self-subsisting from externally governed things.

Core Idea
What is governed by another acts for a benefit, unlike what is self-subsisting.

After presenting his main argument, Ibn Shabib addresses an objection: if something by itself prevents change at one time but not another, why would it not do the same when caused by another? His response highlights that things governed externally act for a purpose, while self-subsisting things do not. This lesson examines that dialectical exchange.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic and translation of the objection. It questions why a thing behaves differently at different times if it is self-subsisting, and why that would not apply when caused by another.
The objection is brief but profound.
2
Understand Ibn Shabib's response
He answers that when a thing's existence is governed by another, it has a purpose (benefit in religion or world), but when it is self-subsisting, there is no such purpose.
The key difference is the presence of purpose.
3
Reflect on the distinction
Consider why purpose matters. The objection assumes uniformity, but Ibn Shabib argues that governance introduces intentionality, thereby changing the behavior.
This is a sophisticated response.

Maturidi's Response to Objection on First Creation and Time
Maturidi answers an objection regarding the timing of the first creation and the question of benefit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's logical response to the objection about why the first creation was not created earlier, and why the objection is invalid.

Core Idea
There is no time before the first creation, so the question 'why not earlier?' is meaningless.

In this passage, Maturidi addresses an objection about the timing of the first creation. The objector asks: if God created for Himself without benefit, why did He not create it earlier? Maturidi argues that this question is based on a misunderstanding of time. He shows that the notion of 'first' precludes any time before it, making the question logically incoherent. This lesson explains his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the objection that Maturidi faces: the first creation seems arbitrary if no benefit exists. The objector claims there is no benefit in the act, so why that specific time?
Notice the assumption that time is linear and before the first creation there could be a 'before'.
2
Grasp Maturidi's first response
Identify why he says there is no time to ask 'why not earlier?' because the concept of 'first creation' means there is no time before it.
3
Explore the deeper implication
Consider the alternative: if we allow infinite times before the first creation, then the question becomes infinite (why not earlier ad infinitum), making the objection invalid.
This shows the objection leads to an infinite regress.
4
See the contradiction in claiming eternity
Understand why saying the creation is eternal (i.e., it has always existed) contradicts its origination (it was created). Maturidi says this is a contradiction.
This closes the argument by showing the only alternative is impossible.

Commentary
This passage is a classic logical proof for the oneness of God (tawḥīd) from the impossibility of two omnipotent beings. It presents a trilemma: if there are two gods, then either (1) each can annihilate the other, or (2) neither can annihilate the other, or (3) only one can. In cases (1) and (2), both gods would be imperfect (one would lack power to destroy, or both would be mutually checkmated), leading to incapacity (ʻajz). Additionally, if they needed to use devices to destroy each other, that would imply ignorance (jahl), which is not fit for deity. In case (3), if only one has the power to annihilate the other, then the other is not truly a god, because he would be subject to destruction. Moreover, the truly powerful god would not allow a rival to oppose him in his dominion. Therefore, the only consistent position is that there is precisely one God who has full power over all. This argument is a standard kalam proof for tawḥīd, often attributed to the method of mutual exclusion (tamānuʻ).

Commentary
This passage is a two-part proof for the existence of a wise Creator, likely directed against materialist or dualist views that explain change by natural processes alone. The first part (from 'العالم بالامتزاج' to 'وبالله التوفيق') establishes a general principle: any natured thing (ذي طبع) cannot change from its state to its opposite except by a changer who is either wise (حكيم) or foolish (سفيه). The outcome of the change reveals which kind of changer it is—wise changes lead to good outcomes, foolish ones to bad. The author argues that since the outcome of the world's changes is orderly and good, it must be from a wise changer, thus invalidating the opponents' claim that nature alone can produce such order.

The second part (from 'وأيضاً' to 'والله الموفق') uses the example of fire and burning to illustrate a deeper point: a naturally burning thing only burns what is naturally disposed to combustion; it cannot by itself generate the disposition to be burned in a thing that is not so disposed. In the physical world, achieving such a combination requires knowledge and intention. Therefore, the unseen world (الغائب) must also involve a knowing agent, ruling out the idea that nature alone accounts for the correspondence between causes and effects.

The final paragraph (from 'مع ما إذا كان' to the end) concludes that all the 'principles' (الأصول) the opponents posit—such as atoms, natures, or elements—are lifeless (موات) and act without intelligence. It is impossible for such dead, non-intelligent entities to produce a living, knowing, powerful being like a human. Therefore, the entire universe must depend on the all-knowing Maker (المكون العليم).

Throughout, the author follows the order of argument: first a general principle about change, then a specific example (fire), then a concluding argument from the impossibility of lifeless matter producing intelligent beings.
