Prophetic Narratives Refuting Mu'tazili Interpretation of Will
Maturidi presents three Quranic proofs against Mu'tazili view on divine will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Maturidi uses Quranic narratives about Nuh, Musa, and the destruction of cities to refute the Mu'tazili claim that God's will only encompasses guidance.

Core Idea
God's will is sovereign over both guidance and misguidance.

Imam Maturidi now turns to explicit Quranic evidence to counter the Mu'tazili reinterpretation of divine will. He cites three key passages: the verse about intending destruction before transgression occurs, the words of Prophet Nuh about God willing to lead astray, and the prayer of Prophet Musa about Pharaoh's wealth leading to misguidance. Each proof demonstrates that God's will encompasses all that occurs, challenging the Mu'tazili restriction of His will to good alone.

Guided Path

Read the full Arabic passage and its literal translation. Note the three distinct proofs: the destruction verse, Nuh's statement, and Musa's prayer.
Pay attention to the flow of Maturidi's argument as he moves from one proof to the next.
2
Identify the Mu'tazili position refuted
For each proof, identify what the Mu'tazila claim (e.g., that God does not will misguidance or that He only wills obedience). Maturidi explicitly counters these misinterpretations.
The Mu'tazili view is that God's will is limited to good and guidance.
3
Reflect on the cumulative evidence
Consider how the three proofs together establish that God's will includes all events, whether believers see them as good or evil. This is a cornerstone of Maturidi theology.
This lesson connects to the broader doctrine of qadar (divine decree).

Commentary
This passage uses Quranic narratives to refute the Mu'tazili interpretation of divine will. Maturidi first cites the verse 'وإذا أردنا أن نهلك قرية' to show that God intends destruction due to transgression that will occur. He argues that if God intended obedience and then destroyed them, it would be unjust; thus God's will encompasses what occurs, whether good or evil. Then he cites Prophet Nuh's statement to his people: 'إن كان الله يريد أن يغويكم' (if Allah intends to lead you astray). The Mu'tazila interpret this as saying God does not will misguidance, but Maturidi says they twist Nuh's words beyond their apparent meaning. Finally, he cites Prophet Musa's prayer that Pharaoh's wealth leads people astray, while the Mu'tazila claim God gave that wealth for guidance. Thus, Maturidi demonstrates that the Quranic evidence supports the view that God's will includes both guidance and misguidance.

The Dilemma of God's Will and His Promise
Maturidi presents a rational dilemma to refute denial of God's universal will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical dilemma that forces the opponent to either affirm God's universal will or accuse Him of lying.

Core Idea
Either God intends to fulfill His promise or to break it—no third option.

Imam Maturidi presents a powerful dialectical argument against those who deny that God's will encompasses all things. He forces them into a choice: either God intends to fulfill His promises and threats, or He intends to break them. The first leads to affirming His justice, the second to accusing Him of lying—an obvious absurdity.

1
Identify the opponents' claim
Read the opening where Maturidi cites Quranic verses showing God's will over specific outcomes, and the opponents deny that God willed them.
Notice how Maturidi uses the Quran to establish that God's will is mentioned in the text.
2
Understand the dilemma
Maturidi presents a choice: either God intends to fulfill His promise or to break it. Work through each option and its consequences.
The second option implies God intends lying, which is absurd.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
See how the only consistent position is that God's will encompasses all that occurs, including the fulfillment of His threats.
This conclusion is supported by the verse that God does not intend injustice.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained refutation of those who deny that God's will is universal, particularly the Mu'tazila. The author (Imam Maturidi) begins by citing Quranic verses that affirm God's will over specific outcomes (e.g., refraining from purifying hearts, intending trial, promising Paradise and Hell). He then presents the opponents' view: they claim God does not will such things. Maturidi forces a dilemma: either God intends to fulfill His promises and threats, or He intends to break them. To claim He intends to break them is to accuse Him of foolishness and lying—an absurdity. If they claim He intends to fulfill them, then He also intends that people obey, yet people sometimes disobey; but the fulfillment occurs regardless. If they say God intends injustice (by punishing), that contradicts the verse 'God does not intend injustice for the servants.' So the only consistent position is to affirm that God's will encompasses all that occurs. Maturidi then cites more verses showing that God wills specific outcomes for believers and disbelievers, and that human will is subordinate to God's will. He emphasize the universal Muslim maxim 'Whatever God wills is, and whatever He does not will is not,' which applies to both voluntary and involuntary acts. He argues that if God could will something that does not happen, then the will would not be a necessary attribute of lordship. He further supports this by pointing to common practices: people say 'if God wills' when promising or swearing, showing their belief that outcomes depend on God's will. He concludes that before the Mu'tazila's obfuscation, the consensus of Muslims was firm on this, just as it was on the hadith of the fitra. Thus, Maturidi uses scriptural, rational, and communal arguments to affirm the universality of divine will.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained refutation of those who deny that God's will is universal, particularly the Mu'tazila. The author (Imam Maturidi) begins by citing Quranic verses that affirm God's will over specific outcomes (e.g., refraining from purifying hearts, intending trial, promising Paradise and Hell). He then presents the opponents' view: they claim God does not will such things. Maturidi forces a dilemma: either God intends to fulfill His promises and threats, or He intends to break them. To claim He intends to break them is to accuse Him of foolishness and lying—an absurdity. If they claim He intends to fulfill them, then He also intends that people obey, yet people sometimes disobey; but the fulfillment occurs regardless. If they say God intends injustice (by punishing), that contradicts the verse 'God does not intend injustice for the servants.' So the only consistent position is to affirm that God's will encompasses all that occurs. Maturidi then cites more verses showing that God wills specific outcomes for believers and disbelievers, and that human will is subordinate to God's will. He emphasize the universal Muslim maxim 'Whatever God wills is, and whatever He does not will is not,' which applies to both voluntary and involuntary acts. He argues that if God could will something that does not happen, then the will would not be a necessary attribute of lordship. He further supports this by pointing to common practices: people say 'if God wills' when promising or swearing, showing their belief that outcomes depend on God's will. He concludes that before the Mu'tazila's obfuscation, the consensus of Muslims was firm on this, just as it was on the hadith of the fitra. Thus, Maturidi uses scriptural, rational, and communal arguments to affirm the universality of divine will.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of arguments establishing that God's will (irādah / mashī'a) is not like human will. The fourth is the interpretation of will as 'negation of being overcome' (nafy al-ghalabah) and that the act occurs according to God's determination and will—this is the meaning that is affirmed by consensus. Denying this after understanding it amounts to a misattribution of will. Maturidi grounds this in God's being the Creator of all things, and His attribute of not being compelled or forced in His creation.

Core Idea

Commentary

Refutation of the Mu'tazila on Persistence in Minor Sins
Maturidi presents the Mu'tazila claim and refutes it via reductio ad absurdum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Maturidi refutes the Mu'tazila claim that persistence in minor sins makes them equivalent to major sins by exposing the logical inconsistency in their definition of persistence.

Core Idea
Persistence is not continuous action but abandoning repentance—a distinction that unravels the Mu'tazila position.

The Mu'tazila argued that persisting in a minor sin makes it as severe as a major sin. They defined persistence not as repeating the act but as failing to repent. Imam Maturidi seizes this definition to show that if repentance forgives all sins, their distinction between sin categories collapses, and their view leads to the absurd conclusion that every sin merits eternal punishment.
