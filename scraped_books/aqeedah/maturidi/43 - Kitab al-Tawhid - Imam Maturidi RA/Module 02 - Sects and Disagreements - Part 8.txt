The Theory of an Original Element (Clay)
Maturidi presents the view of an original element (clay).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the view that the world originated from a single primordial element, specifically clay, and how it differs from Islamic monotheism.

Core Idea
The claim that the world came from clay reduces the Creator to a craftsman.

This lesson focuses on one of the ancient theories about the world's origin: that it was fashioned from a pre-existing substance, like clay. Maturidi presents this view to show its flaws and contrast it with Islamic belief in creation ex nihilo. Understanding this helps clarify why theologians rejected materialist origins.

Guided Path
1
Locate the theory in the text
Read the first part of the passage: 'ومنهم من يقول ... أحدث البارى منها هذا العالم'. Identify the phrase that states the original element (طينة).
The word طينة (tina) means 'clay'.
2
Understand the claim
This view posits that the world was crafted from a pre-existing origin, specifically clay. The Creator is described as 'originating' (أحدث) the world from this clay.
3
Compare with Islamic doctrine
Consider how this differs from the Islamic belief that Allah created everything from nothing. If the world is made from pre-existing matter, the Creator is not the ultimate originator.
4
Notice the language
Observe that the term 'أصل' (origin) and 'صنعة' (craft) imply a kind of making that relies on a substrate, which is incompatible with absolute creation.

The Theory of Astral Influence and Hayula
Maturidi presents the astral influence theory and the hayula variant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the astral influence theory and the subgroup that posits hayula (matter) and accidents, along with their self-invalidation due to susceptibility to change.

Core Idea
Those who attribute world-generation to stars or to a prime matter (hayula) end up contradicting themselves.

This lesson explores another ancient cosmology: the idea that the world develops due to the constant motion of stars and sun. Some among them even posited an underlying matter (hayula) that receives accidents. However, they themselves invalidated this idea because such matter would be changeable and thus not eternal or divine. Maturidi presents these views to showcase their internal inconsistencies.

Guided Path
1

2
Identify the sub-views
The first sub-view says the stars and sun run constantly and cause the development (نشوء) of the world. The second sub-view adds that these celestial bodies acknowledge accidents (أعراض) and the world is generated from that, calling the underlying matter hayula.
3
Examine the self-critique
The proponents of the hayula view themselves invalidated it by pointing out that hayula is subject to receiving accidents and changing from state to state. This means it cannot be eternal or the ultimate origin.
4
Reflect on the theological significance
Even non-Muslim thinkers recognized that anything subject to change cannot be the eternal source. This aligns with the Islamic argument that the Creator must be unchanging.

Dualist Views: Light and Darkness as Origins
Maturidi presents two dualist accounts of cosmic origins.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the dualist cosmological view that the world originates from two principles—light and darkness—and the variations among dualist thinkers.

Core Idea
Dualists claim all good comes from light, all evil from darkness.

In this passage, Imam Maturidi presents the dualist theory that the world originated from two eternal principles: light and darkness. This view attributes good to light and evil to darkness. Maturidi notes different versions of this theory and points out that most dualists believe the world comes about through nature rather than deliberate action. Understanding this view is essential for recognizing how early Muslim theologians engaged with competing cosmologies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two principles
Recognize that dualists posit light and darkness as the origins of all existence, with light as source of good and darkness of evil.
Note the binary assignment of moral categories.
2
Compare the two versions
Some dualists say the principles were separate then mixed; others (proponents of matter) say they were one and then separated. Understand the distinction.
The second version derives from the idea of a single primordial matter.
3
Note the role of nature
Observe that most dualists attribute the world's coming-to-be to nature (tabi'a), not to a deliberate act (fi'l). This is a key philosophical commitment.
This contrasts with theistic creation ex nihilo.

The Moral Argument for Dualism
Maturidi presents the moral argument used by dualists.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Judging God by human standards leads to false conclusions.

Some have argued that since good and evil exist in the world, they must come from separate origins. This argument, based on human customs of praising the good and blaming the evil, seems plausible but fails when applied to God. In this passage, Imam Maturidi presents this argument as held by the dualists and materialists.

Guided Path

The Arabic has some unclear parts due to manuscript issues.

Identify the key premise

3
Notice the conclusion
The argument concludes that there must be two origins: one for good (light/God) and one for evil (darkness/Satan).
This leads to the dualist position.

Dualists split reality into two origins: light for good, darkness for evil.

Commentary
This passage presents the dualist doctrines concerning the origin of good and evil. Maturidi begins by describing the view that the world has two origins: light (source of all good) and darkness (source of all evil and harm). Some dualists say these two were originally separate and later mixed; others, following the proponents of matter and clay (hyle), say the two were originally one and then separated, becoming the sources of evil and good. Most of these groups hold that the world operates by nature, not by deliberate action. Then Maturidi presents a third line of reasoning called 'consideration of meanings' (iʿtibār bil-maʿānī). Dualism Makes Knowledge of Wisdom Impossible
Maturidi argues that dualism makes knowledge of wisdom and folly impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi argues that dualism leads to the impossibility of distinguishing wisdom from foolishness.

Core Idea
Dualism destroys the foundation for moral knowledge.

This passage from Imam Maturidi's Kitab al-Tawhid refutes Manichaean dualism. Maturidi demonstrates that if good and evil arise from two independent, eternal principles (light and darkness), then human beings—being composed of both—cannot be certain whether their judgments originate from the good or evil principle. Consequently, all claims about wisdom and folly become unreliable. Let's examine his argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dualist position
First, recognize the dualist claim that good and evil come from separate essences: the essence of light (good) and the essence of darkness (evil). This is the starting point of Maturidi's critique.
Manichaeans held that good and evil are two coeternal principles.
2
See the problem with human nature
According to dualism, every human being is a mixture of both essences. Therefore, any action or judgment a person makes could be influenced by either principle.
Maturidi says 'kull al-insān 'indahum mashūb min al-amrayn' – every human is mixed from the two matters.
3
Recognize the epistemic consequence
Because a person is a mixture, one cannot determine whether their testimony comes from the truthful light or the false darkness. This makes their claims about wisdom and folly unreliable.
If a person is from the essence of darkness, everything they say is false; if from light, everything is true—but we don't know which.
4
Grasp the conclusion
Thus, under dualism, no one can be trusted to know wisdom from folly, and the entire framework for moral knowledge collapses.
Maturidi ends with 'lā yuqāl' – 'so it is not known' – highlighting the uncertainty.

The Triple Dilemma: All Options Refute Dualism

Each option leads to a contradiction or absurdity. He then adds a final argument about light's grief, which further undermines the dualist's characterization of light as pure joy.

Maturidi's Refutation of Dualism: The Self-Undermining Premise
Maturidi argues dualists cannot trust their own reasoning due to their premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi turns the dualists' own explanation of error against them to refute their theology.

Core Idea
If perception can be corrupted by darkness, the dualists' own argument may be corrupted.

Imam Maturidi presents a powerful dialectical argument against the dualists. Rather than rejecting their premise outright, he accepts it for the sake of argument and shows that it undermines their own position. This technique, called argument from internal consistency, is a hallmark of Maturidi's method.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dualist position
Read the first part of the passage where Maturidi presents the dualists' claim: in the witnessed world, the mixture of darkness in light causes errors in perception.
Dualists believe in two eternal principles: light (good) and darkness (evil).
2
See Maturidi's acceptance
Notice that Maturidi does not deny their premise. He says 'قيل' (it is said) and then uses their own logic against them.
This is a dialectical move: accepting the opponent's premise for argument.
3
Grasp the reversal
Maturidi then asks: if afflictions of darkness distort perception, what prevents your own statement (that there are two principles) from being a product of that same distortion? Your claim might be folly and harshness, not wisdom and mercy.
The reversal relies on the dualists' own standard: any statement made under the influence of darkness is unreliable.
4
Reflect on the implication
Conclude that the dualist position is self-refuting: they cannot guarantee the truth of their own foundational claim because they cannot prove their reasoning is free from corruption.
This argument does not require a positive proof of tawhid; it only shows the inconsistency of the dualist position.

He emphasizes that any likeness to the contingent is impossible for the Preeternal. The exception is the Muslims (ahl al-Islam) who consistently maintain the general affirmation of tawhid without contradiction. The passage thus serves as a comparative theological critique, showing how only the Islamic conception of tawhid is coherent.

The Sophist Denial of Knowledge and Ibn Shabib's Rebuttal
Maturidi reports the Sophist claim and Ibn Shabib's self-refutation argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sophist argument against the possibility of knowledge and how Ibn Shabib refuted it by exposing its self-contradictory nature.

Core Idea
The claim 'There is no knowledge' self-destructs: asserting it requires knowledge.

The Sophists argued that all cognition is mere belief because our senses and experiences are contradictory. Ibn Shabib, however, pointed out a fatal flaw: the very statement 'there is no knowledge' must be known to be asserted, thus affirming knowledge. This lesson traces that logical move.

Guided Path
1
Read the Sophist claim
The Sophists list examples of contradiction (e.g., a bat seeing at night but not day) to conclude that knowledge is impossible and only belief exists. Read their argument carefully.
Note that their claim is based on empirical variability, not on a logical premise.
2
Identify Ibn Shabib's question
Ibn Shabib poses a dilemma: either the Sophists know their own statement (thus affirming knowledge) or they do not know it (making their claim baseless). Identify both horns.
This is a classic 'self-refutation' argument (elenchus).
3
Understand the logical force
If they say they know, they have proven knowledge exists. If they say they don't know, they have no ground to call others to their view. Therefore, their position is untenable.
The phrase 'ألزموا السكت' means they are forced into silence.

The Manichaean Doctrine of Light and Darkness
Maturidi expounds the Manichaean cosmology of light and darkness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key tenets of Manichaean dualism as presented by Maturidi, including the nature of light and darkness, their mixture, and the five kinds and senses.

Core Idea
Dualism collapses under its own contradictions.

Maturidi begins by laying out the Manichaean worldview in his own words. He describes a universe composed of two eternal principles: light (good) and darkness (evil). Commentary
This passage is an exposition and refutation of Manichaean dualism. First, Maturidi presents their doctrine: the universe results from the mixing of light (infinite, above) and darkness (finite, below). Darkness invaded light, and the mixture produced the world. Both light and darkness have five genera (colors) and five senses. Light is good, darkness is evil. Light has a spirit; darkness has a spirit called 'Hammāma' (a serpent) that seeks to imprison light. Eventually, each element will separate and return to its own realm. Then Maturidi launches his refutation: the entire doctrine is self-contradictory without needing external evidence. The first contradiction is that the Manichaeans simultaneously affirm finitude and infinity for the same entities. They claim light is infinite in four directions but darkness has finitude at the point of meeting. But if both are infinite, they cannot mix; if one is finite, it implies a limitation imposed by another, indicating createdness. Furthermore, if the parts are finite, the whole cannot be infinite. This reasoning shows the incoherence of the dualistic cosmogony.
