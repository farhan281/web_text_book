The Necessity of Tahqiq and the Consequences of Failure
Rhetorical challenge to those who fail in tahqiq and fall into ta'til or tashbih.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why proper investigation (tahqiq) is essential in theology and how its absence leads to either divesting God of attributes (ta'til) or likening Him to creation (tashbih).

Core Idea
Without tahqiq, theology collapses into error: either ta'til or tashbih.

Imam Maturidi presents a powerful rhetorical challenge to those who fail to achieve tahqiq—rigorous investigation—in matters of God's names and attributes. He argues that such failure inevitably leads to one of two extremes: the negation of divine attributes (ta'til) or anthropomorphism (tashbih). The passage forces us to confront fundamental questions about worship, religion, and the origin of the world.

Guided Path
1

2

3
Analyze the rhetorical questions
The Imam presents a series of questions. Consider their purpose: to expose the theological emptiness of those who fail to investigate.
4
Consider the consequences
According to the passage, what are the two possible outcomes? Either return to a meaning close to understanding, or join the deniers of the world's origination.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
The passage ends with a statement about 'choice of bewilderment'. What does this imply about the state of those who reject tahqiq?

Method of Knowledge and Response to Irrationality Objection
Maturidi answers the claim that something from nothing is irrational, explaining the proper method: rational evidence, not sensory representation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's critique of demanding sensory evidence for metaphysical truths and his two-fold answer to the objection that creation from nothing is irrational.

Core Idea
Demanding sensory proof for non-sensory matters is like trying to see sounds.

Maturidi responds to a common objection: how can we rationally accept creation from nothing? He first points out the category error of using sensory imagination to judge metaphysical realities. Then he offers two complementary answers, grounding the proof in the impossibility of life arising from a dead thing. This lesson unpacks his method.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objector claims that something from nothing is 'outside the rational' because it cannot be imagined in the mind. Maturidi begins by noting this has been addressed before.
The phrase 'خروج عن المعقول' is key.
2
See the methodological critique
Maturidi rebukes the misuse of sensory standards, comparing it to trying to distinguish sounds by sight or colors by hearing. Each domain has its proper method.
This analogy shows the absurdity of demanding sensory evidence for non-sensory matters.
3
Examine the two-fold answer
Maturidi gives two interpretations of 'conception in imagination': either it means proof-based existence (which we affirm) or it means a physical model (which God is beyond). This answers the objection.
This distinguishes between rational evidence and sensory representation.
4
Grasp the proof from life and death
The proof for the world's origination is that life cannot come from a dead thing. Since living things exist, their life is originated, and so is their death. This shows temporal origination.
The proof is concise: life must be created because it cannot arise from lifeless matter.

{ The Shaykh, may God have mercy on him, said: } And what was mentioned of departing from the rational by what is not conceivable in imagination, we have already explained it. Likewise, everything known by a sense, if one loves to comprehend it by other than that, his mind falls short of it. And this answer is also an answer to his saying: 'The existence of a thing from nothing is outside the rational.' And for the two matters there is another answer, which is to say: If you mean by 'conception in imagination' existence by evidence, then that is necessary, and we do not say what does not have that. But if you want a model, our Lord is exalted above that; rather, He is the one who makes for every possessor of a model a likeness, and He is the originator of that. And the proof for the temporal origination of the world is the impossibility of life being in a dead thing, because by it it lives. It is established that the life of things is originated, and likewise their death, since it may occur after life.

Second, if you demand a physical model, God is beyond that; He creates all models. The proof of the world's origination is that life cannot come from a dead thing, yet things live, so life is originated, and death follows.

Each domain of knowledge has its own proper method; the unseen is known by proof, not imagination.

In this passage, Imam Maturidi responds to the objection that kalam arguments about God are invalid because they cannot be imagined or sensed. The Method of Tawhid: Starting with Tashbih and Ending with Tawhid
Maturidi explains that tawhid necessarily starts with tashbih and ends with tawhid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's methodological principle that tawhid necessarily begins with conceptual comparison (tashbih) and ends with affirmation of divine uniqueness (tawhid), and why this approach is necessary for both reason and revelation.

Core Idea
Tawhid starts with creation-based concepts and ends with God's uniqueness — the only way to speak about God without falling into ta'til or tashbih.

Maturidi presents a foundational principle of Islamic theology: we can only speak about God by beginning with familiar concepts from creation, but we must end by negating any similarity. This passage explains why this method is not a compromise but a necessity imposed by both reason and revelation. Let's explore how Maturidi justifies this approach.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify key terms
First, read the Arabic passage and its translation. Pay attention to the terms 'tashbih', 'tawhid', 'ta'til', and 'darurah' (necessity). Note how Maturidi frames his argument.
The passage is dense; read it slowly.
2
Understand the analogy of the afterlife
Maturidi uses the example of perceiving the reward and punishment of the Hereafter through worldly pleasures and pains. This illustrates how we must start with what we know (creation) to infer what we cannot perceive directly (God).
This is a key part of his method: the unseen is understood through the seen.
3
See how the method applies to God's attributes
Maturidi explains that we must affirm attributes like 'Knower' and 'Powerful' based on creation, but then negate likeness by adding 'not like the knowers'. This is how tawhid is achieved: affirmation with negation of similarity.
This avoids both ta'til (negation of attributes) and tashbih (crude anthropomorphism).

The foundation in the matter of tawhid is that its beginning is tashbih and its end is tawhid. Commentary
This passage explains the methodological principle of tawhid. Maturidi states that we must start with tashbih (using terms conceptually familiar from creation) and end with tawhid (affirming God's uniqueness). This is necessary because we can only conceive of the unseen through the seen. For example, we understand heavenly reward by analogy with worldly pleasures, and we describe God as 'Knower' and 'Powerful' because not doing so is ta'til, but doing so without qualification is tashbih. So we add 'not like the knowers' to negate similarity. This method is obligated by both reason and revelation. Naming God with terms unsupported by revelation or reason is a great audacity.

Divine Attributes: Affirmation without Likeness
Maturidi states the principle for attributes like istiwa': affirm revelation, negate likeness, no decisive interpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's method of affirming divine attributes revealed in scripture while negating any likeness to creation, and why he refrains from fixing a specific interpretation.

Core Idea
Affirm the revelation, negate similarity, and leave the how to Allah.

Imam Maturidi sets out a foundational principle for approaching verses that describe Allah in terms that could be misunderstood anthropomorphically. He anchors the entire discussion in the Quranic verse 'There is nothing like unto Him.' This lesson unpacks that principle and shows how it applies to attributes like istiwa' and the vision of Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the English translation. This helps you absorb Maturidi's original phrasing before interpretation.
2
Identify the foundational verse
Notice that Maturidi starts by quoting 'There is nothing like unto Him' (42:11). This verse is the pillar of his entire approach to divine attributes.
This verse negates any similarity between Allah and His creation.
3
Trace the three-step method
Maturidi applies a consistent method to the verse about istiwa': 1) Affirm the revelation as it came, 2) Negate any likeness to creation, 3) Do not settle on a single interpretation because other meanings are possible and unknown.
This is called the method of tafwid — consigning the meaning to Allah.
4
Extend the principle to other attributes
Maturidi explicitly states that this same method applies to all revealed attributes, such as the vision of Allah in the afterlife. The rule is always the same: affirm, negate likeness, believe without specifying.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of rational inquiry (nazar). It begins by presenting the objection of those who argue that refraining from inquiry is safer because the seeker might not attain the truth. The passage ends with 'بل يجب' (but must), indicating the obligation of inquiry.

The Impossibility of Infinite Motion
Maturidi argues that motion must have a beginning because it ends.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's rational argument that motion must have a beginning because every observed motion ends.

Core Idea
Every motion that ends must have a beginning; infinite motion cannot end.

Maturidi presents a powerful rational argument against the possibility of an infinite series of motions. He bases his reasoning on the simple observation that any motion we observe terminates. Because termination is an end, what is terminated cannot be preceded by an infinite series, as an infinite series would have no end. Thus, each motion implies a starting point.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Begin by reading the Arabic passage once without analysing it. Then read the literal translation to understand the surface meaning.
Notice the compressed style; Maturidi assumes prior refutation.
2
Identify the premises
Find two key premises: (1) every motion we observe passes away (ends), (2) what ends is finite. Maturidi states that no motion is in doubt except that it is an end of preceding motions.
The word 'nihaayah' (end) is crucial.
3
Understand why infinite cannot terminate
Maturidi argues that if a motion ends, its termination is the end of the series. An infinite series would have no end to terminate, so it cannot be that motion has an infinite past.
This is a version of the argument from the impossibility of an actual infinite.
4
Appreciate the conclusion
Since motion ends, it must have a beginning. Maturidi writes, 'thus the beginning is established for it.'

Learning Objective

Core Idea
True knowledge of the unseen comes from revelation or inference from the seen.

He asserts that when dealing with claims about stars and natural phenomena, one must rely either on revelation, which is validated by proofs of truth, or on rational inference that moves from the observable present to the unobservable absent. This principle frames the entire discussion of causality and divine attributes.

Commentary
This passage contains an objection-and-answer followed by a refutation. The opponent tries to distinguish dream from wakefulness by noting that in dreams one sees impossible things (e.g., being dead while knowing). He answers that the dreamer does not genuinely believe those impossibilities. He claims that knowledge of wakefulness's soundness is acquired. Maturidi responds by returning to the first principle: the sleeper has an affliction (آفة) known by comparison to wakeful sensory experience. There is no real dispute; the truth of wakefulness is known by necessity (ضرورة). Changes in perception are due to afflictions. Maturidi then generalizes: nature, stars, and food cannot generate such distinctions because they lack the wisdom and precision to produce the observed balance and dominance. This reaffirms that sensory knowledge of wakefulness is immediate and necessary, not acquired through reasoning.
