Maturidi's Distinction Between Names and Attributes
Maturidi distinguishes between essential names and essential attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's terminological distinction between essential names (al-asma' al-dhatiyya) and essential attributes (al-sifat al-dhatiyya).

Core Idea
Essential names are the adjectives; essential attributes are the underlying realities.

Maturidi, like many theologians, needed precise language to discuss God's nature. In this passage, he introduces a crucial distinction between the names we use to address God—like 'the Living'—and the attributes that those names refer to—like 'life'. Grasping this distinction is key to understanding his theological method.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation to get an overview of Maturidi's distinction. Let the author's own words set the stage.
Pay attention to the terms he uses for each category.
2
Identify the two categories
Look for the two terms: 'essential names' (al-asma' al-dhatiyya) and 'essential attributes' (al-sifat al-dhatiyya). Notice how he defines each: names are words that are grammatically attributes, while attributes are the source words.
The category names are clearly stated.
3
Analyze the examples
The passage gives examples: al-Hayy, al-'Alim, al-Qadir, al-Rahman for names; al-hayat, al-'ilm, al-qudra for attributes. See the pattern: the name is the adjective, the attribute is the abstract noun.
This pattern shows the linguistic derivation.
4
Reflect on the broader implication
Consider why this distinction matters. It clarifies that when we call God 'the Knowing', we are referring to the attribute of knowledge inherent in His essence. Also note Maturidi's devotional usage of many names.
This distinction avoids confusion between language and reality.

Maturidi's Verbal Distinction in Divine Attributes
Maturidi explains how to affirm attributes without implying likeness to creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's method of affirming divine attributes while avoiding real anthropomorphism by distinguishing verbal similarity from conceptual likeness.

Core Idea
Using the same words for God and creatures does not imply real likeness—only verbal expression.

Maturidi addresses a central challenge in Islamic theology: how can we call God 'Knower' and 'Powerful' when we use the same words for humans? If we say God knows, does that imply He knows like we do? Maturidi offers a nuanced answer by distinguishing between the words we use and the realities they point to.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let Maturidi's own reasoning come through.
Notice the reference to necessity (al-darura) and the witnessed (al-shahid).
2
Identify the problem of seeming similarity
Maturidi admits that using the same words for God and creatures suggests similarity on the surface. Find where he acknowledges this.
The phrase 'فلعله في الظاهر يوجب التشابه' points to this.
3
Understand Maturidi's solution
He says that necessity (al-darura) forces us to negate the concept of similarity from the witnessed realm. This means we deny any real likeness despite using the same words.
The key is that the similarity is only in speech (القول), not in reality.
4
See the example of multiple languages
Maturidi points out that the same attribute is expressed with different words in different languages, yet the meaning is not identical. This shows the words are just approximations.
This reinforces that the names are not literal descriptions.
5
Reflect on the Quranic principle
He concludes with the verse 'There is nothing like Him' as a foundational principle accepted by all, even anthropomorphists, implying that verbal similarity cannot imply real likeness.
This verse is the ultimate safeguard against anthropomorphism.

Maturidi's Threefold Classification of Divine Attributes
Maturidi introduces his threefold classification of divine attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's threefold classification of divine attributes into negative, affirmative, and active.

Core Idea
Maturidi classifies attributes into three types: negative, affirmative, and active.

This lesson introduces Maturidi's classification of divine attributes, which aligns with later Sunni kalam. He categorizes attributes into three types: negative (salbiyya) that negate imperfection, affirmative (thubutiyya) that connect God to creation, and active (fi'liyya) that describe God's relationship with the world. Understanding this classification is key to grasping Maturidi's theological framework.

Why Not 'Body Not Like Bodies'? Maturidi's Refutation
Maturidi answers an objection about calling God a body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi rejects calling God a 'body not like bodies' even though He is called 'a thing not like things'.

Core Idea
Affirming God as 'thing' does not entail corporeality or require calling Him 'body'.

In this passage, Maturidi responds to a hypothetical objector who questions why the same logic that leads to saying 'a thing not like things' does not also lead to saying 'a body not like bodies'. The answer reveals a subtle point about theological language: not every term that can be negated can be affirmed, and the basis for one description may not apply to another. This lesson unpacks Maturidi's layered reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the opening lines carefully. An objector asks: 'Since you say God is a thing not like things, why don't you say He is a body not like bodies?' Notice the attempt to draw an analogy between 'thing' and 'body'.
The objector assumes that if 'thing' is permissible, then 'body' should also be permissible.

Can Allah Be Called a 'Thing'?
Maturidi justifies applying the term 'thing' to Allah by analogy with accidents in the witnessed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that it is permissible to call Allah a 'thing' (shay') without implying that He is an accident.

Core Idea
Calling Allah a 'thing' is valid because all non-bodies in the witnessed are called accidents, but only by existence.

In the previous discussions, Maturidi established an analogy: in the witnessed world, anything that is not a body is called an accident by virtue of existing. This opens the door to calling Allah a 'thing' without attributing accidenthood to Him. This lesson examines that reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and proof regarding divine power (qudra) versus incapacity (‘ajz). Maturidi states a principle: one whose act does not exceed the actual occurrence (i.e., he can only produce exactly what happens and nothing beyond) is described as incapable; one whose act exceeds the actual and occurs at his behest (i.e., he can also produce the opposite or more) is described as powerful. He uses an analogy: a person who can produce a thing and its opposite is more complete in his action. Similarly, one whose act is confined to a single sphere is less than one whose acts occur in every sphere. For Allah, this description is one of perfection: He can create and not create, bring into being and annihilate, etc. Maturidi then contrasts human and divine action: human acts are bound by time, exertion, and instruments; Allah acts by His essence without instruments. He creates from nothing, so the objections raised against His power are invalid. He then provides another proof from generated acts (al-fi‘l al-mutawallid): in human experience, acts like shooting or killing generate effects after the act is completed; the agent is called killer even after the causal chain ends. This shows that a delay between act and effect does not negate the attribution of the act. This applies a fortiori to Allah. Finally, Maturidi draws a parallel to the term 'thing' (shay’): in the witnessed world, everything non-corporeal is called an accident (by virtue of existence, not by name), so calling Allah a 'thing' is permissible without implying accident. Thus the analogy holds.
