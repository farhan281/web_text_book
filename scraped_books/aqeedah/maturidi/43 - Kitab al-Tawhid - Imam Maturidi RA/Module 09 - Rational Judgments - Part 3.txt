Learning Objective

Core Idea
The strength to act is simultaneous with the act, not prior to it.

The Qadariyya claimed that the Quranic command 'Take it with strength' proves that humans possess an independent power to act before the act occurs. Maturidi presents a sharp logical argument against the Mu'tazila definition of will. The Mu'tazila claim that will is choice that exists only before the act, vanishing at the time of action. Maturidi's Argument for Specificity in Ambiguous Terms

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When a term has multiple distant meanings, specificity must be presumed to avoid contradiction.

Maturidi addresses a key methodological issue in kalam: how to interpret a term that can mean multiple things, especially in theological contexts. He argues that it is irrational to intend two far apart meanings at once, and that the correct approach is to specify the meaning that is free from doubt. This passage presents a tight logical argument for specificity (takhsis) and warns against claiming certainty where ambiguity exists.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical structure of the Ash'ari proof of existence for ru'ya and how it uses qiyās al-ghā'ib 'alā al-shāhid.

Core Idea
Existence, not materiality, is the cause of visibility — and Allah exists.

The Ash'ari school offers a rational proof for the permissibility of seeing Allah. This proof, called dalīl al-wujūd, draws an analogy between the witnessed world and the unseen. By examining what makes physical things visible, they isolate existence as the sole common factor — and then apply it to Allah.

Since existence is shared between seen and unseen, Allah's existence is sufficient cause for visibility.

It is a logical proof structured as an argument from analogy (qiyās al-ghā'ib 'alā al-shāhid). The author begins by stating the conclusion: the possibility of vision in the witnessed world arises solely from existence, and since Allah exists, it is permissible that He be seen. The proof is deductive: it infers from the universal principle (existence is the cause of visibility) to the particular case (Allah's existence makes Him visible).

Bodies are never free from accidents; therefore they are created.

Imam Maturidi presents a rational proof for the creation of bodies based on a foundational principle of kalam: bodies are inseparable from originated accidents. As for the intellect, it is that this world being exclusively for annihilation is not wisdom, and that the action of every possessor of intellect departing from the way of wisdom is repugnant from him. So it is not possible that that from which intellect is a part is founded upon other than wisdom or made in vain. And when that is established, it indicates that the creation of the world is for permanence, not for annihilation. The world in its origin was built upon different natures and opposing aspects, especially that which is intended from the perspective of the intellect that brings together what should be together and separates what should be separated—which the philosophers called the microcosm (al-‘ālam al-ṣaghīr). It is upon different desires and scattered dispositions, and overwhelming passions were instilled in them. If they were left to what they were created upon, they would dispute in the competition for benefits and kinds of honor, nobility, kingship, and authority, leading to mutual hatred and then fighting, resulting in annihilation and corruption—such that if the existence of the world was aimed at this, wisdom in its existence would be invalid. Commentary
This passage presents a rational proof for the necessity of prophethood. Maturidi begins by stating that the intellect judges that the world being created solely for annihilation is not wise, and that any rational being acting unwisely is repugnant. Therefore, the world cannot be founded on wisdom if it were for annihilation, nor can it be created in vain. This establishes that the world is created for permanence. He then observes that the world is composed of diverse natures, especially the microcosm (man), who has conflicting desires and passions. If left unchecked, they would compete for benefits, leading to hatred, fighting, and destruction. Moreover, humans need nourishment to survive; if they were meant only to perish, there would be no need for sustenance. Thus, there must be a unifying principle (a religion) to prevent conflict. The most appropriate principle is to seek a foundation that unites them within their capacity. Reason shows that people have a Director (God) who knows their needs, and He does not leave them ignorant and overwhelmed by desires without guidance. Therefore, God must establish a guide (a prophet) to teach them, and He must provide clear proof to distinguish that guide. This proof is the sign of leadership (imāmah) with which God distinguishes the prophet.

Rational Proof: Impossibility of Two Gods
Maturidi presents a rational argument that more than one god leads to corruption and contradiction in the universe.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Multiple gods cannot coexist; their wills would conflict and the world would not exist in order.

Imam Maturidi presents a foundational rational argument for tawhid. He shows that if there were more than one god, the universe would have to rely on their agreement—a sign of limitation. Moreover, their wills would contradict each other, leading to corruption. This lesson walks through his reasoning and its scriptural foundation.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's deeper argument that wakeful knowledge is necessary and distinct from dream perception due to the sleeper's defect (āfa) and the impossibility of natural causes producing true knowledge.

Core Idea
Wakeful knowledge is established by necessity, while dreams are impaired by a defect that prevents necessary perception.

Maturidi responds to the objector's claim by analyzing the psychological state of the dreamer. He argues that the dreamer does not actually believe the impossible; rather, the dream content is not properly judged. Then he introduces the concept of acquired knowledge versus necessary knowledge, and the jurist adds that the sleeper has a defect that prevents him from having the same certainty as the wakeful person. Finally, Maturidi rejects naturalistic explanations for the difference, affirming that only God can be the source of such necessary knowledge.

He floats in creation, and by this nature is disposed: some gems fly in the sky, another in water, and the third walks on the face of the earth. Its summary is that nature or the stars or foods cannot generate that, nor is there in them that which does so, and that everything has harm and benefit, and what determines domineering and moderation, so the existence of the like of it is not possible by nature nor by the star, because it issues in accordance with the wisdom and perfection in it, and what nature necessitates cannot have that. Commentary
This passage is a refutation of those who argue that the perceptions of wakefulness and dreams are equivalent, thus undermining certain knowledge. It begins by noting that some people try to explain natural phenomena (like birds flying, water flowing) as mere natural contingencies, which is an arbitrary imposition on God's creation. Then Maturidi presents a self-objection: what about dreams? If dream perceptions can be false, how can we trust wakeful ones? The objector claims that the difference is that during sleep the mind imagines impossible things (like seeing oneself dead), which a wakeful person would not accept. Maturidi responds by analyzing the dream state: the dreamer does not actually believe the impossible; for example, seeing oneself dead does not entail believing oneself simultaneously alive and dead. Furthermore, he notes that knowledge of the truth of wakefulness is acquired (iktisāb), but its basis is the necessary knowledge (ḍarūra) that distinguishes it from dreams. The jurist (al-faqīh) adds that the sleeper has a defect (āfa) that prevents true perception, while the wakeful person perceives with necessity. Thus the distinction is not arbitrary but grounded in the nature of cognition. Finally, Maturidi concludes that natural causes like nature, stars, or food cannot produce such knowledge, because they lack the requisite wisdom and perfection; only God can. The passage reinforces the Sunni doctrine of the reliability of wakeful sense perception and rational necessity against skeptics.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some might think that Maturidi denies that dreams can ever be true.
Correction: Maturidi explicitly states that true dreams are possible, but they come from God's kingdom or from a healthy state, not from the standard dream defect.
