Two Arguments: Incapacity and the Command Analogy
Maturidi presents two additional arguments: one from incapacity and one from the analogy of temporal commands.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's two arguments that defend eternal divine attributes against objections of temporal creation.

Core Idea
Eternal divine attributes do not imply temporal creation, just as commands apply at their time.

Maturidi continues to defend the eternity of divine attributes, here presenting two further arguments. First, he argues that the opponents' view implies incapacity in Allah, because a temporal effect would require temporal causes. Second, he uses the example of commands and prohibitions: just as a command applies at the time of revelation without being pre-existent, so too Allah's attribute of takwin is eternal while the created thing is temporal. This lesson unpacks both arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully

The passage is incomplete at the end; this is a known textual issue.
2
Identify the first argument: incapacity
Find where Maturidi says 'what they said is a sign of incapacity' and understand the reasoning: if an effect is not simultaneous with the cause, the cause is limited.
3
Identify the second argument: the command analogy
Look for the discussion of commands and prohibitions. Notice how a command applies at the time of revelation even though the command itself is not newly created. This is analogous to takwin.
Relate this to the overarching debate about eternal attributes.
4
Reflect on the combined force
Consider how both arguments together support the view that Allah's attributes are eternal, even if their effects are temporal.

Commentary
This passage contains two additional arguments. First, the opponents' claim (presumably that divine creation is temporal or that Allah's attributes are not eternal) is a sign of incapacity, because a human being cannot achieve an act whose effect is not realized simultaneously; he must move and still himself. This implies that if Allah's creation were dependent on time as the effect, it would be a limitation. Second, Maturidi uses the example of commands and prohibitions: nobody denies that a person is commanded and prohibited at the time of receiving the revelation, even though the command did not exist before. The revelation makes him commanded at that moment. Similarly, it is not objectionable to say that Allah is 'existing by the bringing into being in pre-eternity' (i.e., the divine act of creation is eternal, and the created thing exists at its time). The text ends mid-sentence, but the analogy is clear: just as a command is applied at the time of revelation without being pre-existent, so too Allah's attribute of takwin is eternal and the creature exists at its appointed time. The passage concludes with 'Allah is described with every existent that it is...' likely continuing that every created thing is described as brought into being at its proper time.

The Perfection of Divine Power
Maturidi contrasts human and divine power to prove God's perfection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi establishes the perfection of God's power by contrasting it with human power, which is limited by sphere and instruments.

Core Idea
God's power is perfect and unlimited, unlike human power.

In this opening part of his argument, Imam al-Maturidi sets the stage by highlighting the contrast between human and divine power. He argues that God's attribute of creation (takwin) is perfect because it is not limited like human acts. The passage uses the example of a person who can act and its opposite to illustrate completeness.

Commentary
This passage is a continuous argument from Imam al-Maturidi establishing the reality of the divine attribute of creation (takwin). It begins by contrasting the perfection of God's power with human power: human acts are confined to a specific sphere and require instruments, whereas God's act is not so limited. The author then presents a second proof from generated acts (al-fi'l al-mutawallad). In human experience, an act like throwing an arrow is attributed to the agent even after the immediate act; the agent is called killer even though the death occurs later. Similarly, God's creation is real even if it is not temporally immediate. Maturidi then argues against those who deny this attribute by noting that their position implies insufficiency: a servant cannot produce an effect without his action being realized. He further draws an analogy with command and prohibition: a command given in the past applies now, so likewise takwin (divine bringing into existence) is eternal yet applies to temporal things. Finally, he explains the meaning of takwin: it is encompassed by the command 'Be' (kun), and it brings everything into existence at its proper time without repetition. The passage concludes that this is a deep topic, and the brief treatment is sufficient for the intelligent.

Proof from Generated Acts
Maturidi proves takwin using the analogy of human generated acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses the analogy of generated acts (like throwing an arrow) to prove that God's act of creation is real even if the effect is not temporally immediate.

Core Idea
Just as a thrower is called a killer after death, God's creation is real even if not instantaneous.

Maturidi presents a second proof for the reality of takwin using generated acts (al-fi'l al-mutawallad). In human experience, an act like throwing an arrow is attributed to the agent even after the immediate act; the agent is called killer even though the death occurs later. Similarly, God's creation is real even if it is not temporally immediate. The passage also responds to a potential objection by showing that denying this leads to the impossibility of generated acts.

Apply the analogy to God

3
Consider the rebuttal
The passage also refutes the objection that God's act cannot be like generated acts because it is not temporal; Maturidi responds that the principle of attribution does not require temporal immediacy.

Al-Ka'bi is asked why attributes of essence cannot be described by their opposites. Maturidi begins his refutation by pointing out that this framework, when followed through, actually denies that God has any real attributes at all.

If God's names are created, then God was ignorant before they existed.

Imam Maturidi presents a logical refutation against those who assert that the divine names are created in time. By examining the implications of such a claim, he shows that it forces us to say either that God was ignorant of His own essence before creation, which is impossible, or that the name is eternal. This passage demonstrates how the names are not mere labels but are rooted in God's eternal attributes.
