Maturidi's Emphasis on Prophethood
The passage notes Maturidi's greater interest in prophethood compared to other mutakallimun.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Imam al-Maturidi showed a distinctive emphasis on prophethood compared to other mutakallimun, and that this interest was continued by later scholars.

Core Idea
Maturidi is the theologian with the greatest interest in prophethood.

This passage from al-Maturidi's Kitab al-Tawhid highlights his unique focus on prophethood (nubuwwa). While the Ash'ari tradition relied heavily on al-Baqillani for this topic, Maturidi himself dedicated significant attention to it, and his approach was adopted by later scholars like Abu al-Mu'in al-Nasafi. The passage sets the stage for understanding Maturidi's broader theological project.

The Five Groups Denying Prophethood
Enumeration of five groups rejecting prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five groups that deny prophethood according to Imam al-Maturidi and understand their core positions.

Core Idea
Five denials of prophethood: from atheism to rationalism to skepticism about miracles.

In this lesson, we explore Maturidi's classification of those who reject prophethood. He identifies five distinct groups, each with a different basis for denial. Understanding these categories is essential for recognizing the variety of objections to divine revelation throughout history.

Guided Path
1
Read the enumeration of the five groups
Read the Arabic passage that lists the five groups. Notice how each group is introduced with 'منهم من' (among them are those who).
The passage comes from a larger discussion of prophethood in Kitab al-Tawhid.
2
Identify each group's position
For each group, identify the specific reason they deny prophethood. The first group denies God entirely; the second acknowledges God but rejects His commands; the third relies on reason alone; the fourth claims miracles can be matched by sorcery; the fifth doubts the inability of opponents.
3
Reflect on the variety of objections
Consider how these five groups represent different levels of opposition: from atheism to deism to rationalism to skepticism. Maturidi's classification shows he addressed a wide spectrum of views.
This variety helps us understand why Maturidi devoted so much space to prophethood.
4
Connect to the lesson's core idea
Pause and summarize in your own words the five denials. This reinforces the core idea that prophethood is denied on multiple fronts.

Issues of prophethood. Indeed, the topic of prophethood in the Ash'ari wing of Sunni theology was particularly addressed by Imam al-Baqillani, who is considered the completer of the founding of the school after its first founder. However, Imam al-Maturidi, who lived before al-Baqillani by nearly a century, is the theologian who showed the greatest interest in issues of prophethood. Perhaps opposition to prophethood began immediately after Islam established its power in the region, so the Islamic position appeared on the scene and presented its proofs on the subject. As for the path that al-Maturidi opened in interest in the topic of prophethood, later authors continued it. Abu al-Mu'in al-Nasafi, who is considered an expounder of al-Maturidi's works in theology and exegesis, we see that he treated prophethood topics independently in his 'Tabṣirat al-Adilla' in a section nearly 100 pages long, and he asked not to be blamed for this large size contrary to his general plan for the book, because of the occurrence of attacks from currents denying prophethood. Al-Maturidi addressed in many places of his 'Ta'wilat al-Qur'an' various topics related to prophethood, foremost of which is the topic of establishing the prophethood of the Messenger, peace be upon him. And in the 'Kitab al-Tawhid' there is a section seventy pages long, more than half of which deals with the need for the institution of prophethood. He said in this section that the message has been confirmed by the imams of guidance, leaders of good, and wise men of humanity, and it was denied by five groups: among them are those who are ignorant of the Maker and deny Him; among them are those who deny the divine command and prohibition despite acknowledging Him, glorified; among them are those who acknowledge all that and claim that reason is independent in its role and independent of the message; among them are those who claim the possibility of matching the miracles of the claimant of prophethood with the work of priests, sorcerers, and conjurers; and among them are those who say that it is possible that the people to whom the claimant of prophethood addresses his message have lacked ability throughout history and have no knowledge or experience in those matters, and have not tested their powers therein, perhaps if they were tested, someone would be found who is able to do the like and oppose him in his deed. And al-Maturidi answered the first of those objections directed against prophethood in the section preceding the issues of prophethood. He also attempted to treat the rest individually by presenting multiple proofs on the subject. In those arguments, he points out that God, exalted, is wise in His actions, and that He did not create man, whom He honored among His creatures, nor the world which He created for his sake, for a purpose aiming at their eternal existence, and that there will be no order nor life without guidance from divine revelation, and that reason cannot continue its intellectual journey to the eternal world alone with its capacities; thus al-Maturidi presents these ideas in order to show behind them what he relies on from reason, human nature, social realities, and what is customary throughout human history.

Commentary
This passage is a description of Maturidi's treatment of prophethood. It begins by contrasting his work with that of al-Baqillani in Ash'arism, noting Maturidi's greater emphasis on this topic. Then it mentions later scholars like al-Nasafi who expanded on Maturidi's approach. The core of the passage identifies five groups that deny prophethood: (1) those who deny God, (2) those who acknowledge God but reject His commands, (3) those who affirm God and commands but claim reason suffices without revelation, (4) those who equate prophets' miracles with sorcery, and (5) those who argue that people might have been able to produce similar miracles if they had tried. Maturidi answered the first objection earlier, and for the rest he provided proofs: he argues that God is wise and created humans for an eternal purpose, that society needs divine guidance for order, and that reason alone cannot reach eternal truths. The passage thus presents Maturidi's rational and social arguments for the necessity of prophethood.

Maturidi's Rational Arguments for Prophethood
Summarizes Maturidi's rational proofs for prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key rational arguments Imam al-Maturidi uses to prove the necessity of prophethood against the five denying groups.

Core Idea
Prophethood is necessary because God is wise, creation has purpose, society needs guidance, and reason alone is insufficient.

After cataloging the five groups that deny prophethood, Maturidi presents multiple rational proofs to affirm its necessity. He argues from God's wisdom, the purpose of creation, the need for social order, and the limitations of human reason. These arguments form a comprehensive defense of prophethood grounded in reason and human nature.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on Maturidi's arguments
Read the Arabic passage that describes Maturidi's rational responses. Identify the key points he makes: God's wisdom, purpose of creation, need for divine guidance, and reason's incapacity for eternal matters.
This passage follows immediately after the enumeration of the five groups in the source text.
2
Analyze each argument individually
Break down each argument: 1) God's actions are wise and purposeful; 2) creation has an eternal purpose; 3) order and life require revelation; 4) reason cannot independently reach eternal truths.
Notice how Maturidi combines theological, teleological, and epistemological arguments.
3
Reflect on the refutation of the five groups
Consider how each argument addresses the objections of the five groups. For example, the argument from God's wisdom refutes the atheist group; the argument from reason's limitation refutes the rationalist group.
This shows the systematic nature of Maturidi's response.
4
Connect to the lesson's core idea
Summarize in one sentence why Maturidi believes prophethood is necessary. This reinforces the core idea that prophethood is essential for human guidance.

Refuting the Claim that Miracles Can Be Countered by Sorcery
Maturidi refutes al-Warraq by redirecting focus to deeper distinctions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi refutes the objection that prophets' miracles are just like sorcerers' tricks by focusing on deeper distinctions of character, influence, message, and societal impact.

Core Idea
Prophets are distinguished not by miracle type but by character, influence, message, and societal impact.

Al-Warraq, a skeptic, argued that the miracles claimed by prophets could be matched by the tricks of priests and sorcerers. Maturidi's response does not deny the external similarity; instead, he shifts the ground to the profound differences in the person of the prophet, the effect on people, the content of the message, and its lasting stability. This lesson examines that refutation.

Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Notice that Maturidi does not argue against the possibility of external similarity but instead points to deeper differences.
The passage is a single sentence that summarizes his approach.
3
Identify the Four Distinguishing Aspects
From the commentary, identify the four elements that Maturidi uses to distinguish prophets: (1) personal character, (2) ability to influence people, (3) meanings contained in the message, (4) stability of the message in society.
These are the core of the refutation.
4
Reflect on the Significance
Ask yourself why these four aspects are more fundamental than the miracles themselves. How do they prove that prophethood is genuine and not mere sorcery?
This connects the argument to the broader proof of prophethood.
5
Test Your Understanding
Answer the checkpoint question to ensure you grasped the basis of Maturidi's distinction.

Core Idea
Prophethood is denied from five distinct angles — not just one.

Imam Maturidi begins his treatment of prophethood by noting that it has been affirmed by righteous leaders and wise men throughout history. However, he identifies five groups that reject it, each for a different reason. This lesson maps out these five positions and sets the stage for his responses.

Connect to Maturidi's response

This lesson only introduces the groups; the responses are covered in later lessons.

Commentary
This passage enumerates the five groups that deny prophethood according to Maturidi and notes his initial response. It first states that prophethood has been affirmed by the righteous leaders and wise, but denied by five groups. The first group denies the existence of the Creator entirely. The second group acknowledges God but denies His command and prohibition. The third group acknowledges everything but claims the intellect is sufficient without revelation. The fourth group claims that the miracles of a prophet can be matched by sorcery or trickery. The fifth group argues that the inability of people to oppose a prophet throughout history might be due to lack of opportunity or testing, not divine support. Maturidi answered the first objection in a previous section.

Maturidi responded that the line of revelation is consistent, because acceptance of the first revelation (given to previous prophets) necessitates acceptance of the subsequent one. This defends the universality of Muhammad's prophethood.

Commentary
This passage provides a practical, almost empirical, argument for the need for prophets. Maturidi argues that worldly matters (amr al-dunya) are intertwined with religion (qiwam al-din). God created humans for trial, grew plants from earth and water as food and medicine, but also grew diseases and lethal poisons. Reason finds it ugly (qubih) to test oneself to distinguish harmful from nutritious because one might die. Reason has no way (laysa fi al-'uqul sabil) to know that. Thus, it is necessary to affirm someone whom God informs (yutli'uhu Allah) about every substance, so that humans may nourish their bodies and establish their religion. Similarly, reason cannot initially know the ways of agriculture. After produce is known, one needs a teacher to know how to use it properly. Food also contains harmful aspects that require knowing their limits and antidotes. The medical sciences, with varying temperaments and lethal poisons, require guidance to know beneficial doses. Crafts for clothing and shelter, and domestication of wild animals, also require knowledge that reason alone cannot provide. All these point to the need for divinely guided teachers—prophets—to teach humans these essential arts and sciences.

The Need for an Arbitrator in Human Conflict
Maturidi proves prophethood from the prevalence of conflict and lack of an impartial judge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's rational proof for the necessity of prophets based on the prevalence of human conflict and the absence of an impartial arbiter.

Core Idea
Conflict reveals humanity's need for a divine messenger.

Maturidi presents a compelling argument drawn from everyday human experience: people constantly disagree, each claiming to be right, yet no one among them is accepted as a neutral judge. This impasse leads to corruption and destruction, which reason recognizes as evil. The only solution is to turn to the Creator, who knows what is best for His creation, and who sends messengers to guide them. This passage lays out that logical necessity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow, then read the literal translation. Notice how Maturidi builds his argument from a common observation.
The passage is concise; every phrase carries weight.
2
Identify the key premise
Find where Maturidi states that conflict is the root of all corruption. Why is that important for his argument?
3

Follow the reasoning from conflict → no arbiter among humans → need for divine guidance → necessity of a messenger. See how each step is linked.
Maturidi uses the phrase 'the outcome of intellects has ended at the One who aids them' to show that reason itself points to God.
4
Reflect on the misconception
Consider the common mistake that humans can solve their conflicts through reason alone. Why does Maturidi's argument show this is insufficient?
He emphasizes that no human is accepted as a neutral judge, so an external divine source is needed.

Then the fundamental principle in that—of what rational necessity imposes the need for messengers—are aspects. One of them is the existence of apparent conflict among creatures, each claiming that he is more entitled to the truth and more likely to be correct, and the agreement that there is none among them to whom one may resort to judge between them, and to show them that by which their hearts are reconciled and their word is united. And it is known that conflict is the root of all corruption and the prelude to all destruction, and all that is ugly in the intellects. So the outcome of intellects has ended at the One who aids them and returns them to what they were created for of rectitude and knowledge. And it is known that no one is more knowledgeable of that than the One who created them and originated them. And in that is the necessity of asserting a messenger who is known that he came from Him. And with God is success.

Commentary
This passage is a social proof for prophethood based on the problem of conflict. Maturidi identifies the fundamental principle that necessitates prophets: the existence of widespread disputes among people, each claiming truth, with no one among them who can be accepted as an impartial judge to reconcile hearts and unify speech. Since conflict is the source of all corruption and destruction, and reason deems it ugly, the intellects must turn to the One who created them and knows best what brings them rectitude. Therefore, a messenger from God is necessary to provide that divine judgment and guidance.
