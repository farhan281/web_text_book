The Linguistic and Logical Conclusion: Sinners Are Believers, Khawarij and Mu'tazila Contradict Themselves
Al-Maturidi turns the table on Khawarij and Mu'tazila, showing their own logic applies to them.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Maturidi's final argument that, according to the Khawarij and Mu'tazila's own criteria, their followers who commit major sins become disbelievers, while Ahl al-Sunnah remain believers because they do not despair of mercy.

Core Idea
Your own logic condemns you: Khawarij and Mu'tazila become disbelievers by their own standards.

Imam al-Maturidi concludes with a powerful rhetorical move. He points out that according to the Khawarij and Mu'tazila themselves, when they commit major sins, they become disbelievers and deserve eternal fire. Why? Because they have consensus on denying mercy, which is a characteristic of disbelief as per the Quran. In contrast, the Ahl al-Sunnah do not fall into this error. Let's unpack this final argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target groups
Read the passage and note which groups al-Maturidi is addressing: the Khawarij and Mu'tazila.
2
Understand the logic
Al-Maturidi says that according to their own claim, when they commit major sins, they become disbelievers deserving eternity in hell. Because they unanimously agree on denying mercy, which is the description of disbelief.
3
Compare with Ahl al-Sunnah
Other groups of Muslims (Ahl al-Sunnah) are not like that because they do not despair of mercy.

And others from among the various sects who claim Islam are not [like that]. Commentary
This passage presents several consecutive arguments. First, al-Maturidi presents a second type of proof: Allah has obligated many acts of worship under the name of faith (al-iman), and the knowledge of what is lawful and unlawful is often tied to the presence or absence of faith. Yet those who commit acts of sin (fisq) are still included in these obligations together with the believers. This shows that the name of faith is not removed from them. Second, he invokes the consensus (ijma') of the community: the practice of praying over deceased Muslims, seeking forgiveness for them, and affirming intercession (al-shafa'ah) proves that they are considered believers, for the community does not pray over disbelievers. Third, he refutes the Mu'tazila: they claim that major sinners are not disbelievers (kuffar) and yet they also say that such sinners are in a state of despair (ya's) from Allah's mercy. But Allah has connected despair with disbelief in the Quran (Surah Yusuf 12:87): 'and none despairs of the mercy of Allah except the disbelieving people.' Therefore, if they affirm despair, they must also affirm disbelief, which they deny – a contradiction. Al-Maturidi explains that in common usage (al-'urf), disbelief is denial (takdhib), but the major sinner affirms (musaddiq) the truth and hopes for mercy, so he is not a denier. Furthermore, the linguistic root of kufr means 'covering' (al-satr), and the sinner does not cover Allah's blessings or deny His rights. Hence he cannot be a kafir. Likewise, faith linguistically and in the revealed law means affirmation (tasdiq), and the sinner has not denied anything, so he remains a believer. Finally, al-Maturidi makes a striking conclusion: according to the logic of the Khawarij and Mu'tazila themselves, when they commit major sins, they become disbelievers (since they hold that major sins expel from faith), and they are deserving of eternal punishment. The Ahl al-Sunnah, however, are not like that, because they do not despair of Allah's mercy, which would be the characteristic of disbelief as per the Quran.

The Name of Faith Remains: Acts of Worship and Consensus
Maturidi presents two proofs that major sinners remain believers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the name of faith remains for major sinners based on their performance of obligatory acts of worship and the community's consensus on intercession and prayers for them.

Core Idea
Major sinners keep the name of faith because they still affirm Allah and perform His commands.

Imam Maturidi begins by asserting that the name of faith is not removed from those who commit major sins. He then provides two key proofs: first, God has tied many acts of worship and rulings to the name of faith, and major sinners still participate in these practices. Second, the entire Muslim community prays for forgiveness for deceased major sinners and asks for intercession, which would be impossible if they were no longer believers. This lesson explores these foundational arguments.

Notice 'ثم شارك [فيها] من أحدث أفعال الفسق مع الإيمان غيره' — they are included.
3
Identify the second proof: consensus
The consensus of the community on intercession and praying over the dead of the qibla shows that major sinners are still considered believers.
The phrase 'إجماع النقلة في إثبات الشفاعة' is key.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Both proofs together establish that the name of faith does not leave major sinners.

Refuting the Mu'tazila: The Contradiction of Despair and the Definition of Faith
Maturidi refutes Mu'tazila by showing their position is contradictory and by defining faith and disbelief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi refutes the Mu'tazila position that major sinners are neither believers nor disbelievers by showing the contradiction between their claim of despair and the Quranic link between despair and disbelief, and by defining faith as affirmation.

Core Idea
If a major sinner is not a disbeliever, he must be a believer because disbelief is denial and he affirms.

Imam Maturidi now turns to the Mu'tazila claim that major sinners are in a state between faith and disbelief. He exposes a contradiction: they assert that such a person despairs of God's mercy, but they refuse to call him a disbeliever, even though the Quran explicitly links despair with disbelief. Furthermore, he presents a linguistic analysis showing that in both common usage and revelation, faith is affirmation (tasdiq), and the major sinner affirms God, so he is a believer.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and translation to understand the argument against the Mu'tazila.
Focus on the Quranic verse cited.
2
Identify the contradiction
The Mu'tazila claim the major sinner despairs of God's mercy, but deny he is a disbeliever. Yet the Quran links despair with disbelief.
Note the phrase 'قول متناقض'.
3
Understand the definition of kufr
Kufr is defined as denial (takdhib) or covering (satr). The major sinner does not deny God or cover His blessings, so he is not a kafir.
4
Understand the definition of iman
Iman is affirmation (tasdiq). The major sinner affirms God and His messengers, so he is a believer.
The conclusion: 'ثبت أنه مؤمن'.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi argues that the Khawarij and Mu'tazila, by claiming that major sinners are cut off from mercy, have described God with the attribute of disbelief.

Core Idea
Despairing of God's mercy is itself an act of disbelief.

Maturidi begins his refutation by turning the Khawarij and Mu'tazila's own reasoning against them. They claim that major sinners are disbelievers deserving eternal punishment. But in doing so, they describe God as one who cuts off mercy—a description that belongs only to those who disbelieve in God's signs. This lesson unpacks the first of three arguments that demonstrates the internal inconsistency of their position.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Khawarij and Mu'tazila positions by turning their own arguments against them. The author presents three reasons why, according to their own principles, they themselves would be considered disbelievers deserving eternal punishment. The first reason is that they all (Khawarij and Mu'tazila) agree on cutting off God's mercy from major sinners, which is the description of disbelief according to the verse: 'And those who disbelieve in the verses of God and in meeting Him, those despair of My mercy.' The second reason is that they narrow God's mercy and make it so that it only encompasses sins that are not major, but then those minor sins do not even require punishment, so mercy and forgiveness become meaningless. They attribute anger and wrath to God as the only attributes that cover all sins, thus denying God's forgiveness and mercy. This makes Him like one who is not generous, but the author notes that a generous being is more inclined to forgiveness than to punishment. The third reason uses the verse 'If they desist, He will forgive them what has passed.' The author argues that if desisting is not defined or possible in this life (because the Khawarij say the major sinner never fully repents until death, and the Mu'tazila say repentance requires immediate desistance from all sin which is impossible), then the promise of forgiveness would be void. Second Reason: Narrowing God's Mercy
Second argument: narrowing mercy deprives God of His attributes of forgiveness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Khawarij and Mu'tazila's restriction of God's mercy to only minor sins leads to a denial of His attributes of forgiveness and mercy.

Core Idea
Narrowing God's mercy makes it meaningless and denies His generosity.

The second argument focuses on how the Khawarij and Mu'tazila define God's mercy. They claim that mercy only applies to minor sins, while major sins are subject only to wrath and punishment. Maturidi points out that minor sins do not even require punishment, so mercy becomes superfluous. Their description of God as mostly wrathful makes Him like a stingy being, contradicting the attribute of generosity.

Third Reason: Desistance and Forgiveness
Third argument: the verse on desistance refutes eternal punishment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the verse about desistance from sin (8:38) proves that major sinners can be forgiven, contradicting the Khawarij and Mu'tazila's claim of eternal punishment.

Core Idea
If desistance is possible, forgiveness is possible.

The third argument centers on the Quranic promise that if people desist from disbelief, God will forgive what has passed. Maturidi shows that both Khawarij and Mu'tazila, by their own definitions of desistance and faith, make it impossible for a major sinner to ever be forgiven, thereby nullifying the divine promise. This reveals the flaw in their doctrine of eternal punishment.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Focus on how Maturidi uses the verse 8:38 and then applies the Khawarij and Mu'tazila's own views to it.
2
Understand the Khawarij position
For the Khawarij, desistance (intiha') is not defined or possible because one must fulfill all obligations without delay, which is impossible in life.
3
Understand the Mu'tazila position
For the Mu'tazila, desistance is possible but they place the major sinner in a station between faith and disbelief, making forgiveness contingent on repentance at death, which is not guaranteed.
4
See the contradiction
Maturidi argues that if the verse promises forgiveness upon desistance, and desistance is impossible by their definitions, then the promise is void. But since God's promise is true, their definitions must be wrong.
5
Conclude the refutation
Thus, the major sinner can be forgiven, and the claim of eternal punishment is invalid.

Why Sin Does Not Prove Initial Faith Was False
Maturidi refutes the argument that sin proves initial faith false using two aspects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's refutation of the claim that committing a major sin proves one's initial faith was insincere, including the two aspects of his argument.

Core Idea
A later sin does not reveal a lie in one's initial faith.

Maturidi addresses a common argument that a person's subsequent sin exposes his earlier faith as false. He refutes this with logical reasoning, showing that internal sincerity is known to the individual and Allah alone. This passage presents two key aspects of his refutation.

Locate where Maturidi says 'وجهان' and note what each aspect argues. The first involves logical consequences; the second involves self-knowledge and divine knowledge.
3

Follow the conditional reasoning: if sin proved insincerity, then the necessity of avoiding sin would be removed, and absurd consequences follow (e.g., no difference between apostate and original disbeliever).
This shows the argument leads to contradictions.

Examine the second aspect

5

This ties back to the core idea: actions do not retroactively invalidate sincere faith.

If someone claims that a believer who commits a major sin becomes a disbeliever, then that claimant is effectively calling the believer a liar about his own belief. The Quranic Threefold Division Refutes a Middle Status

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Maturidi dismisses an objection about the exceptional ruling for women.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi dismisses the objection from the example of a woman as irrelevant for theological argumentation.
