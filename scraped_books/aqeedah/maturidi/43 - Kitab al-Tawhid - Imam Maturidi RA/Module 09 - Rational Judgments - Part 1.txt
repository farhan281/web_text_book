The Necessity of Rational Inference (Naẓar)
Maturidi defines naẓar and states its necessity and functions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi considers rational inference (naẓar) a necessary source of knowledge and how it is used to evaluate sense perception and reports.

Core Idea
Rational inference is indispensable for discerning truth in the unseen and conflicting reports.

This passage introduces naẓar as the third source of knowledge. Maturidi argues that reason is necessary for evaluating the other sources: it helps us know distant sensible objects, resolve conflicting reports, and distinguish miracles from magic. He grounds this necessity in Qur'anic verses that command reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get its flow, then read the English translation. Notice the key terms: النظر (naẓar) and الاستدلال (istidlāl).
2
Identify the three functions of reason
The passage lists three specific tasks for which reason is necessary. Can you find them? They involve knowing distant objects, evaluating conflicting reports, and distinguishing miracles from magic.
These are concrete examples of why naẓar is needed.
3
Notice the Qur'anic basis
Maturidi cites the Qur'an as evidence that God commands reasoning. This shows that naẓar is not just a philosophical tool but a religious obligation.
Look for the phrase 'دلّ الله في كثير من الآيات'.
4
Reflect on the necessity claim
The passage says we are 'compelled' to use naẓar. Why is it not optional? Think about how we could evaluate reports or miracles without reasoning.
This connects to the learning objective.

As for rational inference (naẓar) or reasoning (istidlāl) according to al-Māturīdī, it is an important factor to which we are compelled, especially in evaluating the two sources of knowledge previously mentioned. That means we can use the intellect especially in knowing the sensed object that is distant from the senses or hidden from perception, and in what reaches us from reports whose truths are conflicting, and then differentiating between the miracles of the messengers and the deceptions of magicians and sorcerers. On this basis, God has indicated in many Qur’anic verses the use of reasoning and inference, and clarified that this method is what makes one stop at the truth and shows him the way. It is understood from al-Māturīdī’s style used here in defending the necessity of reasoning and inference that he alluded thereby to the conservative faction as well as the currents opposed to philosophy; so from this angle he says that whoever denies reasoning and inference is at the same time using the same method in his denial. He has paid attention to the necessity of using the intellect both in knowledge of the sensible world and in managing human life and getting rid of the conflicting events in it, and that the intellect in this respect is similar to the two senses of sight and hearing in the confusion of colors and sounds to them. He has declared that man, alongside multiple inclinations and different sensations, has been distinguished by the intellect, and that human nature and the intellect he possesses do not correspond at times, and that in those cases the intellect is given the authority to judge over the faculty of nature. And likewise, al-Māturīdī claims that the sciences perceived by the senses, which are considered the highest sources of knowledge, are realized through mental functions, and he states that a new perception will not be achieved except after the thoughts (khawāṭir) occurring in the mind before this perception; for the intellect must be effective in penetrating those thoughts, and its passivity depends on the manner of its activity in them. Al-Māturīdī holds that those who allow themselves to abandon the use of the intellect in thinking about things are under the pressure of their soul’s desires and satanic thoughts.

Commentary
This passage discusses the third source: naẓar (rational inference). Maturidi presents it as necessary for evaluating the other two sources: to assess sense perceptions that are distant or hidden, to resolve conflicting reports, and to distinguish true miracles from magic. He grounds this in Qur’anic injunctions to reason. He also responds to critics (conservatives and anti-philosophy currents) by noting that denying reasoning itself involves reasoning. He emphasizes the role of reason in managing life and resolving contradictions. He compares reason to sight and hearing, which may be confused. He highlights that reason can judge over natural disposition when they conflict. He asserts that even sensory knowledge involves mental processes, and that new perceptions require prior thoughts; the intellect must be active in engaging these thoughts. He concludes that those who abandon reason are driven by base desires and satanic whispers.

Defending Reason Against Its Detractors
Maturidi refutes opponents and elaborates on reason's functions and dangers of abandoning it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's refutation of those who deny reasoning and his explanation of reason's role in relation to human nature and desires.

Core Idea
Denying reason is self-refuting; reason must govern over nature and desires.

Maturidi addresses critics who reject reasoning, arguing that their denial itself uses reasoning. He compares reason to the senses and asserts reason's authority over natural disposition. He also explains that sensory knowledge involves mental processes and that abandoning reason leads to being driven by base desires and satanic thoughts.

Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Pay attention to the argument against deniers and the comparison to senses.
2
Find the self-refutation argument

This is a powerful rebuttal.
3
Understand the comparison with senses
Maturidi compares reason to sight and hearing, noting that these senses can be confused. What does this imply about reason?
It suggests reason is also needed to correct sensory errors.
4
Note the authority of reason over nature
The passage says reason can judge over human nature when they conflict. Why is this important for knowledge and action?
5
Consider the conclusion about desires
The passage ends by stating that those who abandon reason are under the pressure of desires and satanic thoughts. How does this reinforce the necessity of reason?
This connects reason to moral and spiritual well-being.

The World is Created for Lasting: A Rational Proof
Maturidi gives a rational proof from wisdom to show the world is for lasting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's rational proof that the world was created for lasting, not annihilation, based on the principle of wisdom.

Core Idea
Reason demands wisdom; a world created in vain contradicts reason itself.

Maturidi presents a concise argument from reason (al-‘aql) to establish that the world has a lasting purpose. He argues that since reason finds acting without wisdom repugnant, and the world itself contains reason, it cannot have been created in vain. This leads to the conclusion that creation is for lasting, not annihilation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without overthinking. Then read the literal translation. Let the argument's flow become clear.
Notice how Maturidi starts with 'As for reason' – he is contrasting with other proofs.
2
Identify the key premise
The first part states that the world being only for annihilation is not wisdom, and that reason finds departure from wisdom repugnant.
This is the foundation of the argument.
3
Trace the logic
Since the world contains reason, it cannot be founded without wisdom or be vain. Reason itself would contradict that.
This is a self-refutation argument.
4
Understand the conclusion
Therefore, the world must be for lasting, not annihilation. Wisdom demands purpose and continuity.
This sets the stage for further arguments about prophethood.

And if that is established, it indicates that the creation of the world is for lasting, not for annihilation.

Commentary
This passage is a rational proof (dalīl 'aqlī) based on the concept of wisdom (ḥikma). Maturidi begins by stating that reason judges that acting without wisdom is repugnant (qabīḥ). Since the world includes reason as a part, it cannot be created without wisdom—that would be a contradiction. A creation without purpose (ʿabath/vanity) is inconsistent with wisdom. Therefore, the world must have a wise purpose, and that purpose is lasting (baqāʾ), not annihilation (fanāʾ). This proof establishes a foundational premise for further arguments about prophethood.

Sensory Perception as Necessary Knowledge
Defines sensory perception and refutes its denier through dialectic and coercion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why sensory perception (al-ʿiyān) is considered necessary knowledge and how Maturidi refutes its denial through dialectic and, if needed, physical coercion.

Core Idea
Denial of sensory perception is not ignorance but obstinate stubbornness.

Maturidi here establishes a foundational principle: sensory perception is a source of necessary knowledge that even animals possess. Those who deny it are not sincerely ignorant but willfully obstinate. The passage then outlines a two-pronged response—first a clever dialectical question, and as a last resort, physical pain to break their stubbornness.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic text aloud if possible, then follow with the English translation. Get a feel for Maturidi's direct and forceful style.
Notice how he begins with a definition before moving to the refutation.
2
Identify the definition of al-ʿiyān
Locate the sentence that defines sensory perception. Maturidi says it is what falls under the senses and that it is the foundation for knowledge that has no opposite of ignorance.
This sets up why denial is so problematic.
3
Understand the argument from animal nature
Maturidi argues that even beasts know what benefits or harms them through sensory perception. The denier of such knowledge is thus acting against his own nature.
This shows the universal and necessary character of sensory knowledge.
4
Examine the dialectical trick
The passage presents a clever question: 'Do you know that you are denying?' If the denier says no, his denial is invalid; if yes, he affirms the very knowledge he denies.
This is a classic logical refutation designed to expose self-contradiction.
5
Consider the role of physical pain
Maturidi mentions that as a last resort, the denier may be subjected to physical pain because his denial is obstinate, not based on genuine doubt. This is meant to break his stubbornness.
This is an extreme measure and not the primary response—it comes after dialectic fails.

فالعيان ما يقع عليه الحواس، وهو الأصل الذي لديه العلم الذي لا ضدّ له من الجهل. فمن قال بضده من الجهل فهو الذي يُسمَّي مُنكِرَه كل سامع مكابِرًا؛ تأبى طبيعة البهائم أن يكون ذلك رتبتها؛ إذ كل منها يعلم ما به بقاؤها وفناؤها وما يتلذذ به ويتألّم، وصاحب هذا يُنكر ذلك. وأجْمِعَ أن لا يُناظر مع مَنْ كان ذلك قوله، إذ لا يُثبت إنكاره ولا حضوره بنفسه؛ والمناظرة في مائية الشيء أو هستيته، وهو لهما وللدفع جميعا دافع. لكنه يُمازح، فيقال له: «تعلم بأنك تنفي؟». فإن قال: «لا»، بطل نفيه، وإن قال: «نعم»، أثبت نفيه، فيصير بما يدفع دافعا لدفعه. ويُؤلَم بالألم الشديد من قطع الجوارح ليدع تعنّته، إذ نحن نعلم أنه يعلم العيان إذ هو علم الضرورة، ولكنه يقوله متعنتا، وحق مثله ما ذكرت ليجزع ويضجر، فيُقابل بتعنّت مثله فينهتك لديه ستره. ولا قوة إلا بالله.
Sensory perception (al-ʿiyān) is what falls under the senses, and it is the foundation that possesses knowledge which has no opposite of ignorance. Whoever says its opposite of ignorance is one whom every hearer calls an obstinate denier (mukābiran); the nature of beasts refuses that this be their rank; for each of them knows what pertains to its survival and destruction and what it delights in and is pained by, yet the possessor of this [view] denies that. It is agreed that one should not debate with one whose position is that, for he neither affirms his denial nor his own presence by himself; and debate is about the quiddity (māhiyya) of a thing or its hecceity (hastiyya), and he repels both and even repels repulsion. But he is jested with, and it is said to him: 'Do you know that you are denying?' If he says 'No,' his denial is invalidated; if he says 'Yes,' he affirms his denial, and thus by repelling he becomes a repeller of his repulsion. And he is pained with severe pain by cutting limbs to abandon his obstinacy, for we know that he knows sensory perception (al-ʿiyān) because it is necessary knowledge (ʿilm al-ḍarūra), but he says it obstinately. The proper treatment of such a one is what I mentioned so that he may be distressed and annoyed, and he is met with similar obstinacy so that his veil is torn before him. And there is no power except through Allah.

Commentary
This passage defines sensory perception (al-ʿiyān) as the foundation of knowledge that is immune to ignorance, and it provides a proof of its necessity. This passage is an objection-response unit. Commentary
This passage is an objection-response unit. The objector argues: if perpetual existence (baqa') is possible in the afterlife (as you Muslims claim) through something that does not itself remain (i.e., continuous renewal), then why can't the world be pre-eternal (qadim) through something that does not precede it (i.e., an eternal duration without beginning)? The response provides multiple reasons. First, a linguistic/conceptual distinction: hadath (temporality) means existence after non-existence, so if a thing has no prior non-existence, calling it pre-eternal contradicts the definition. Baqa' means continued existence in subsequent time, regardless of whether it is with another or not, so the two concepts differ. Second, the claim about afterlife is known through revelation (sam‘i), not reason; thus if you accept revelation, it requires that worldly entities are temporal, but if you reject revelation, your argument from it fails. Third, if a thing's existence depends on a preceding different thing, and this condition applies to all others, then nothing could exist at all; but baqa' does not have this condition (it is a continuation, not a new beginning). The analogy of eating: if one says 'don't eat until you eat another', one never eats (requiring an infinite regress); but if one says 'every time you eat a morsel, eat another', one can always continue (like baqa' after a beginning). Fourth, the analogy of doubling numbers: if no starting number is given, no number can exist; but if a start exists, it can increase indefinitely. Fifth, if we suppose no body (no spatial substrate) and permit one accident after another, no accident could exist because there would be no first; but if a first exists, continuation is possible. Sixth, every motion or composition is the end of a past sequence, so there must be a beginning. Seventh, a body can be described with a single baqa' (perpetual existence) even if that baqa' itself does not persist (i.e., it is renewed). The overall point is that pre-eternity and perpetual continuation after a beginning are fundamentally different.

Objection and Answer on Color and Accident
Maturidi refutes an objection about color and temporality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's refutation of the objection that a thing's existence without color does not make it a color, and how he uses the definition of temporality to distinguish it from physical properties like color.

Core Idea
Temporality is about existence after non-existence, not about having a color.

Maturidi addresses a subtle objection that confuses two different kinds of properties: the metaphysical property of being temporally originated (hadath) and the physical property of color. The objector argues that because a thing cannot exist without color, it does not follow that the thing itself is a color. Maturidi shows that this analogy fails because temporality is defined differently.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objector claims that a thing cannot exist without color, but that does not mean the thing is itself a color. Identify the key claim and its apparent logic.

2
Follow Maturidi's rebuttal
Maturidi defines temporality (al-hadath) as 'existence after non-existence.' He argues that if an accident (like color) has the property of being temporally originated, then any substance that inseparably bears that accident also becomes temporally originated.
This is a crucial move: the judgment of temporality transfers from accident to substance due to inseparability.

Maturidi explains that color is not the same kind of accident as temporality. Color inheres in a colored thing, but temporality is a description of existence after non-existence. The body never lacks colors as a whole, but it can precede individual colors one after another.
Thus, the objection fails because it confuses the two different meanings.

Consider how Maturidi uses precise definitions to dismantle an analogy. This method is central to kalam reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is a complex refutation of the Mu'tazila argument for the createdness of the world. Maturidi first states their argument: the world cannot be free of a producer (muḥdith) because it is not free of accidents (ḥawādith). But he notes that their proof only relies on the existence of the world, not on any specific observation. Then he quotes their claim that God in pre-eternity was not Creator, Merciful, or Compassionate, and is so now. This implies that God's attributes are temporal events (ḥawādith). Maturidi then points out the contradiction: the same reasoning they use to prove the world's creation would prove that God is also created, because they consider His attributes as events. He then gives two detailed aspects (wajhān): First, if the world's non-emptiness from events proves its creation, then God's attributes (which are also events) would prove His creation. Second, if God's essence is eternal despite being known only through events, then why cannot the world be eternal despite being full of events? He concludes that the Mu'tazila, by attributing events to God such as mercy, creation, and origination, are forced to apply the same judgment to God as to the world, which is absurd.

The Rational Proof for Divine Command and Prohibition

The intellect's judgment of good and evil requires a Divine Commander.

Imam Maturidi presents a purely rational argument for why humans cannot be left without divine guidance. He begins by noting that Allah distinguished humans from animals with the capacity to know the Commander. Understand the premise

Because actions have objective moral qualities, it becomes necessary to have commands (to do good) and prohibitions (to avoid evil). This is the conclusion: command and prohibition are necessitated by the very nature of good and evil.
This shows that divine law is not arbitrary but grounded in rational morality.

The Rational Dilemma: Intellect Alone vs. Prophetic Guidance
Maturidi presents a dilemma leading to necessity of prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's logical argument that human reason alone cannot resolve all truth, and that a divinely sent messenger with proof is necessary.

Core Idea
Reason alone cannot resolve all truth; a messenger with proof is needed.

Maturidi here presents a powerful logical dilemma that forces us to choose between universalizing individual intellects (which leads to contradiction) and accepting a divinely guided messenger. This argument establishes the rational basis for prophethood.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dilemma's two options
Read the passage and identify the two alternatives: either rely on one's own intellect or accept what a superior intellect shows.
The phrase 'لا يخلو الأمر من أن يرجع إلى ما يدعوه إليه عقله' sets up the dilemma.
2
See why the first option fails
Maturidi argues that if everyone's intellect were correct, contradictory opinions would all be true—an impossibility. This refutes pure rational individualism.
Notice the phrase 'وفي ذلك شهادة بإصابة كل ذي دين' which he says is impossible.
3
Recognize the implication for the second option
If we accept a superior intellect, that person's intellect functions like a messenger. Then we need a proof to validate that messenger, just as we would for any claim of truth.
The key point: 'فلا فصل بين دليل يقوم بصدقه فيما يخبر عن الله أو بإصابة الحق'.

And another proof: The matter is free from either returning to what his intellect calls him to, or [the necessity] that some accept what another of superior intellect has shown them. If the truth is the first, then it would require combining all intellects and declaring everyone correct when he says what his intellect shows him, and in that is a testimony to the correctness of every religious person who relied on his intellect, and that is impossible due to the contradiction of opinions and statements. And if the second aspect, then his intellect becomes like a messenger coming to them from Allah, and that requires a proof to inform us of his person. Commentary
This passage is a dialectical argument that presents a dilemma. It begins with two possibilities: either humans rely solely on their own intellect for guidance, or they must accept what someone of superior intellect (like a potential messenger) shows them. The author quickly dismisses the first option: if everyone's intellect were correct, then all contradictory opinions would be true—an impossibility. Therefore, the second option is necessary. However, if one relies on another's intellect, that person's intellect effectively serves as a messenger. But a true messenger must be validated by a proof (like a miracle). The argument concludes that there is no difference between needing a proof for a messenger and needing proof for any person claiming superior intellect. This leads to the necessity of divinely sent messengers with clear proofs. The phrase 'Allah is the Grantor of success' ends the proof.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The argument dismisses reason entirely and promotes blind faith.
Correction: Maturidi does not reject reason; he shows that reason alone cannot resolve all truths, especially when contradictory claims arise. Reason itself leads to the need for divine guidance validated by proof.
