Maturidi refutes astrologers' determinism by exposing self-contradiction.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Claiming no choice is self-refuting because it undermines the claim itself.

In this passage, Imam al-Maturidi engages with astrologers who argue that humans have no free will and are compelled in all actions. He demonstrates that this very claim is self-defeating: if they have no choice, then their own statement is compelled, and they cannot claim truth for it. Maturidi then presents two logical consequences that force the astrologer into an untenable position.

Commentary
This passage presents a dialectical objection-and-answer between the Mu'tazila and Imam al-Maturidi. The Mu'tazila argue that if God were the creator of human actions, then He would be commanding and forbidding Himself, which is absurd. Maturidi responds by first asking the opponent a clarifying question: do they mean that the servant is commanded to create faith and forbidden to create disbelief? If they say yes, that would imply that God commands people to be creators, which contradicts the Muslim consensus that only God is the Creator. If they say no, then the connection between God's command and His creation of the act is broken: since the command does not require the servant to create, why should God's creating the act require a self-command? Maturidi then examines the nature of faith and disbelief, showing that they have multiple aspects (accidents, indications of temporality, proofs of wisdom/foolishness, etc.). The command and prohibition relate only to certain aspects (like wisdom/foolishness), not to the act of creation itself. Furthermore, many descriptive attributes (like being larger, smaller, good, evil) are not inherently linked to moral categories; they are neutral. Therefore, God can create actions without them being described as obedience or disobedience, and without implying a command or prohibition to Himself. He concludes that similarly, threat and promise (reward/punishment) follow from the act being verified, not from its creation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Denial of God creating actions comes from impossibility, lack of evidence, or fear of compulsion — but all are answerable.

Maturidi begins by categorizing the possible objections to the doctrine that God creates human actions. He identifies three sources of denial: impossibility, lack of evidence, and concern that it leads to compulsion. After laying out this three-fold division, he focuses on the first objection and shows that the claimed impossibility is not rationally necessary.

Actions and Their Locus: A Principle of Kalam
Maturidi states the principle of action's locus and refutes equating divine and human action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the principle that actions are confined to their agent's locus and state, and why ascribing a creature-like action to God is an error.

Core Idea
No action occurs outside its agent's locus; God's act is unlike creation's act.

Maturidi opens with a foundational metaphysical rule: an agent cannot delegate its power to another, nor can it act outside its own locus and state. He then applies this to the Mu'tazilite attempt to describe God's action in terms of created actions, showing it leads to confusion about divine power and the subsistence of acts.

Guided Path
1
Read the principle
Read the first sentence carefully. It states three negations: no one can empower another over his action, no one creates his own action (meaning no creature creates its own acts), and no one can act outside its locus and state.
The term 'locus' (hayyiz) refers to the spatial or ontological domain where an action inheres.
2
Apply to the Mu'tazilite error
Notice how Maturidi applies this principle to criticize those who attribute to God an action that is among the actions of creatures. He says this reflects ignorance about power and subsistence.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
Maturidi concludes that God is exalted above such a notion. Ask yourself: why would comparing God's act to a creature's act be problematic?
This connects to the broader kalam principle of tanzih (transcendence).

Commentary
This passage refutes the Mu'tazilite position on human actions. Maturidi begins by stating a principle: no agent can empower another to act, nor can an agent act outside its own locus (hayyiz) and state (hal). He then mentions the alternative view that creation of a thing is the thing itself, but clarifies that aspects differ. Maturidi argues that this leads to absurdities, especially since such a thing has no parallel in creation. He emphasizes the distinction between divine creation and human action: God's act is simply bringing into existence from nothing, while human actions involve processes and effort. Since both the created act and the human act are real, there is no reason to deny their coexistence. This argument upholds the Maturidi doctrine that God creates human actions while humans acquire them (kasb).

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of Al-Ka'bi's views on human action and divine creation. Maturidi begins by presenting Al-Ka'bi's claim that a free agent is compelled in the suffering resulting from his action, thereby necessitating two contradictory states (freedom and compulsion) in one thing. Core Idea
A single act can be named differently based on aspect, and a report can be from many.

Imam Maturidi refutes the objection that two similar moving reeds require a distinguishing cause.

Core Idea

The Faqih, may God have mercy on him, said: And that [refutation] is against him in two respects. Commentary
This passage classifies two arguments refuting the objector's analogy. The first argument: In the hypothetical case (the two reeds), there is no way to know the true cause because what is from God is not empirically distinguishable from what is from creatures. The objector's demand for a distinguishing mark is meaningless. The second argument: The objector's analogy is invalid because his claim that failure to distinguish arises from neglect of the evidence does not apply here; in our case, there is simply no distinguishing factor to be found. If there were such a factor, it would be in something else external to the actions themselves. The passage reinforces the Maturidi position that divine and creaturely actions are inherently similar in their observable aspect, and distinction is only possible through revealed knowledge.

The Argument That Motion Does Not Prove Creation
Maturidi argues that if motion is not known as created, then no accident can prove creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Imam Maturidi argues that denying that motion is known to be created leads to the collapse of all proofs for creation from accidents.

Core Idea
Denying motion's createdness unravels all proofs for creation.

This lesson presents a critical argument from Imam Maturidi against those who claim that motion itself does not indicate that it is created. By following this reasoning to its logical conclusion, Maturidi shows that such a position would invalidate all accidents as proofs for creation and ultimately undermine the knowledge of substances. He then counters this with the established principle that temporality proves creation.

Commentary
This passage presents an argument against those who deny that motion is known to be created. Maturidi reports a claim that it is not known through the motion itself that it is created; if that is accepted, then the same applies to all accidents like combination and separation, thus no accident can serve as a proof of being created. Consequently, the reality of substances cannot be known, and it would seem that God has not provided any proof of creation through these things. Maturidi calls this an extreme statement that even Satan would not conceive. He then mentions the counter-argument: a thing does not directly indicate God; rather, when its cause is known, it directs towards the Creator. He cites the agreed principle that a body's creation is proven by its temporality, and every originated thing points to that. The passage ends with a request for proof and an attempt to support the view that one can know an originated thing without knowing its creator.
