The Mixture of Benefit and Harm Points to a Single Creator
Maturidi introduces the purpose of harm and interlinking of substances to prove a single manager

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses the observed mixture of benefit and harm in substances to argue for a single, wise Creator.

Core Idea
The interlinking of benefit and harm in creation disproves multiple gods.

Maturidi begins his proof by establishing that harm must have a purpose, otherwise creation would be meaningless. He then observes that every substance is created with the potential for both benefit and harm, and these are interconnected across different substances. This interconnection, he argues, points to a single manager.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof for the existence of a single, wise Creator based on the observed mixture of benefit and harm in the natural world. The author, Imam al-Maturidi, begins by stating that harm (الضرر) must have a purpose in creation, otherwise creation would be meaningless. Allah created different substances with opposing natures: some are beneficial, some harmful. Each substance is created capable of both benefit and harm, and Allah connects the benefit of one substance to the harm of another. This interdependence demonstrates that there is a single manager (مدير) of all things, because if there were multiple gods, their creations would conflict. For example, a substance of pure good cannot produce evil, and a substance of pure evil cannot produce good; but in reality we see both benefit and harm interwoven. This coherence (الاتساق) and interlinking of benefits refutes the notion of multiple creators. Maturidi then argues that if we do not attribute all to one Creator, we cannot validly speak of a number of gods, because none of them could be identified or known, leading to the corruption of all knowledge. He further explains that if one substance benefits and another harms, they would cancel each other out without a single director. The existence of the world and its order—where each thing has its benefit and harm is restrained—proves that the manager is one. This argument is part of Maturidi's rational theology (kalam) to establish divine unity (tawhid).

The Necessity of One Creator for Valid Knowledge and Order

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Without one Creator, we cannot even know who to worship, and all knowledge collapses.

Maturidi continues his proof by highlighting the epistemological consequences of denying a single Creator. If there were multiple gods, we could not identify any of them, leading to complete ignorance. Moreover, the observed restraint of harm in the world demonstrates a single wise manager who controls benefit and harm.

Intellect and the Necessity of Commands
Maturidi argues that rational moral knowledge necessitates commands and prohibitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi grounds the necessity of divine commands and prohibitions in the intellect's natural recognition of good and evil.

Core Idea
The intellect's own moral judgment makes commands and prohibitions rationally necessary.

This passage from Imam Maturidi's Kitab al-Tawhid presents the rational foundation for divine commands. Maturidi argues that the intellect itself recognizes objective moral values—truth and justice as good, falsehood and injustice as evil. From this natural recognition, he deduces that commands to do good and prohibitions against evil are not arbitrary but rationally necessary, and that reward and punishment logically follow. Let's read his argument carefully.

The Necessity of Divine Commands from Creation's Nature
Maturidi argues that creation of harm and benefit implies commands and prohibitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why, according to Maturidi, God's creation of beings capable of harm and benefit necessarily implies the existence of divine commands and prohibitions.

Core Idea
God's wisdom in creation demands commands to guide beings toward benefit and away from harm.

Maturidi, the great Hanafi theologian, offers a rational proof that divine commands are not arbitrary but flow from the very nature of creation. By examining how humans respond to pain and pleasure, he shows that a wise Creator must have intended outcomes that require guidance. This passage lays the foundation for understanding why revelation is necessary.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed refutation of the objection that a wise being (God) cannot command what is rationally evil, which would imply that if prophets were to report such, it would permit injustice and lying. The answer first classifies rational judgments into two types: (1) unchanging judgments, such as the goodness of thanking a benefactor and the ugliness of folly (السفيه), and (2) changeable judgments based on consequences, premises, or context—for example, the permissibility of retribution against a persistent wrongdoer. Revelation can bring commands that fall under the second type. The example of animal sacrifice is given: if all dead animals were permissible, slaughter would be unnecessary; however, slaughter is meant to be easier for the animal and thus falls under permissible things. A second point distinguishes between universal principles (justice always good, injustice always evil) and specific acts whose moral status depends on divine command, like the varying states of a person or the slaughter of animals. This aligns with the messengers' actions, which are always just. Against the dualists (Manichaeans), the argument notes that light permits harm to darkness for an interest. Al-Warraq argues that if prophets follow reason, they are like us; if they contradict it, then reason itself is a proof, and any change would nullify discourse. Ibn al-Rawandi counters with an analogy: if someone sees a head as black then as white, either his sight changed or the object changed. Similarly, what reason deems just for a matter may change due to changing circumstances (like standing, sitting, cupping, eating), without reason itself changing. Finally, the passage notes that people endure great hardships for beneficial outcomes (trade, farming, medicine), and similarly, religious laws may involve choosing a lesser harm for a greater benefit. The conclusion is a tawfiq phrase.

Commentary
This passage is a combination of a classification, an argument, and a refutation. It begins by supporting al-Warraq's point: the intellect condemns harming the innocent and requires loving for others what one loves for oneself; slaughterings are outside that because when one considers consequences and benefits, slaughter becomes acceptable. Then the author (Maturidi) states the central classification: things are of two types. The first type is good in itself, and its opposite and all contradictories are bad. The second type is such that both a thing and its opposite can be good depending on need and the praise or blame of consequences. This implies the necessity of a knower of praise and blame (God or the messenger). Since contradictory differences arise when relying on reason alone, one must refer to a specific part of intellect or to the messenger—hence the necessity of prophethood. The example of slaughter is revisited: its badness cannot be intrinsic, because it can be good for vengeance or for repelling harm; thus it is permissible for prohibition and permission. Then the passage argues against natural aversion as a basis for moral judgment: things deemed bad due to natural repulsion can be overcome by habit, as with butchers and soldiers. This proves that the prohibition is natural, not rational. Habit can change nature, as seen in taming wild animals or training to bear heavy loads. Finally, the passage addresses the dualists: they permit the separation and mixing of light and darkness, which is analogous to slaughter (separation of soul and body). The dualists also argue that pain affects the substance of light.

Proof of Temporality and Necessity of Reason
Maturidi argues for reason's necessity in proving creation's temporality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's dialectical argument that proving the temporality of bodies requires reason, otherwise a circularity arises when relying solely on revelation.

Core Idea
Reason is indispensable in establishing the proof for the world's createdness.

Maturidi presents a tight dialectical argument to show that the proof of the temporality of bodies cannot rely exclusively on revelation without falling into circular reasoning. He demonstrates that unless reason is admitted as a valid source of knowledge, the whole enterprise of proving createdness collapses. This lesson walks through his argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
The passage is short but dense; note the dialectical structure.
2
Identify the circularity
Notice how Maturidi sets up the objection: if one says the proof comes from revelation, he asks for proof of revelation's truth. If the answer is 'the temporality of things', then a circle emerges: temporality is known only through revelation, but revelation's truth is known only through temporality.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
3
Understand the conclusion
Maturidi concludes that the path of knowledge is cut off unless reason is admitted. This establishes reason as necessary for foundational theological proofs.
The reference to Christ shows the polemical context against Christian claims.

Commentary
This passage introduces a dialectical argument about the proof of the temporality of bodies (huduth al-ajsam), a foundational theological topic in kalam. Maturidi states that if one tries to prove the temporality of bodies using reason (al-'aql) alone, it would be binding. However, if someone claims that the proof comes from revelation (al-sam'), then a problem arises: What is the proof that the revelation is true? If they answer 'the temporality of things', then a circularity occurs — the temporality of things is only known through revelation, but the truth of revelation is only known through the temporality of things. Thus the epistemic path is cut off ('fankata'a sabil al-ma'rifa') unless one admits reason as a valid source of knowledge. This then has implications for the Christian claim about Christ: if reason is admitted, it must be applied to Christ as well, implying his temporality and thus refuting Christian doctrine. The passage demonstrates the necessity of reason in establishing foundational truths, even in a scriptural context.

Commentary
This passage explains why the unambiguous (muḥkam) becomes hidden for some people. Maturidi lists six reasons: (1) natural inclination toward pleasure, (2) habit, (3) taqlīd of a trusted authority, (4) lack of effort in seeking, (5) overconfidence in one's own intellect, wanting to make it equal to divine wisdom, and (6) deficiency in research. These lead people to see the muḥkam as ambiguous. The root cause is that human nature (ṭab‘) inclines to immediate pleasures and abhors pain, making it an enemy of the intellect in judging good and evil (taḥsīn wa taqbīḥ). While the intellect's judgments of good and evil are stable and unchanging, nature's judgments are volatile and can be changed through discipline (riyāḍa). Maturidi gives examples: birds and beasts can be trained to accept things they naturally avoid, and humans can overcome aversion to killing animals despite initial repulsion. The intellect's perception of good and evil only increases with awareness.

The Conflict Between Natural Disposition and Intellect in Moral Judgment
Maturidi contrasts the mutable judgments of natural disposition with the stable judgments of intellect, establishing intellect as the moral arbiter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi argues that intellect, not natural disposition, is the authoritative judge of good and evil, and how this grounds objective morality.

Core Idea
Intellect perceives unchanging reality; disposition only sees fleeting pleasure and pain.

How do we determine what is truly good or evil? Our natural instincts often pull us toward pleasure and away from pain, but does that make pleasure good and pain evil? Imam Maturidi delves into this question, explaining that while our dispositions (ṭabʿ) incline us to certain judgments, they are unstable and changeable. The intellect (ʿaql), on the other hand, perceives the unchanging realities of good and evil. In this passage, Maturidi establishes that intellect must be the criterion for moral judgment.

Commentary
This passage is an extended explanation of the conflict between natural disposition (ṭabʿ) and intellect (ʿaql) in judging good and evil. Maturidi begins by stating that God created humans with dispositions inclined toward immediate pleasures and averse to pain, making the disposition an enemy of the intellect in moral judgment. He notes that intellectual judgments of good and evil are stable and unchanging, while natural judgments are mutable through discipline and habit. He gives examples from nature (birds, beasts, human aversion to killing then ease with animals) to show how disposition can be trained. Maturidi then emphasizes that God made intellects the proof (ḥujja), not inclinations. Even though humans share dispositions with those lacking sound intellects, the intellect is obligated to follow what it deems good despite dispositional aversion. The intellect perceives reality of things, while the disposition only sees the present; the intellect brings the absent to the disposition, making judgment easier. He extends this to speech and expressions: their aesthetic variation does not affect the truth of what is expressed; beauty and truth are independent of expression. Therefore, the measure of good and evil is the intellect, which is stable like direct knowledge (ʿilm al-ʿiyān). Because of the opposition between disposition and intellect, many people fail to see what both show them, causing them to mistake muḥkam for mutashābih and vice versa. Maturidi concludes with a prayer for protection from confusion.

Refutation of Deniers of Necessary Knowledge
Maturidi dismisses those who object by denying necessary and self-evident knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that those who deny necessary and self-evident knowledge cannot be engaged in rational debate, and why such debate is pointless.

Core Idea
Denying self-evident truth makes rational argument impossible.

In this brief but powerful passage, Imam Maturidi addresses a group who raised objections based on supposed similarities in divine attributes. He quickly dismisses their objections by pointing out that they have rejected the very foundations of knowledge—necessary and self-evident truths. Without that common ground, no meaningful debate can occur. This lesson unpacks that argument and its implications for theological discourse.

Draw the conclusion about debate
Since they reject these fundamentals, Maturidi says 'لا معنى لمناظرتهم' (there is no point in debating them). Conclude why this is a valid dismissal.
Debate requires shared premises; they have none.

Proof from Good and Bad in Actions
Maturidi argues that ignorance of goodness/badness proves divine origin

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses the fact that humans are ignorant of the true extent of good and bad in their actions to prove that these moral qualities must originate from God.

Core Idea
True goodness and badness of actions are beyond human knowledge, so they must come from God.

In this passage, Maturidi presents a subtle argument based on the moral qualities (husn and qubh) that we observe in actions. He notes that while actions appear good or bad, the doer often does not know the full extent of that quality. This ignorance, he argues, shows that the moral quality cannot originate from the human agent, but must come from a higher source—God. Let's follow his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that Maturidi uses a very compressed style here.
2
Identify the key observation
Maturidi observes that actions emerge with goodness or badness, but the doer does not know that they reach that state. The doer's own perception of beautifying the action may differ from reality.
3
Understand the conclusion
Since humans are ignorant of the true extent of goodness and badness, the action cannot be from themselves. Ignorance does not make an action bad, nor knowledge make it good.
4
Consider the objection and rebuttal
An objector might say that actions are inherently good or bad. Maturidi rebuts that if an action itself has moral quality, then God has more right to that quality, since the thing itself is ignorant.
The phrase 'ignorant of what it is upon' is key.
5
Reflect on the final argument
If moral qualities could exist without an originator, then anything could exist without an originator, which leads to atheism—a departure from Islam.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.

He notes that in the Arabic language, faith is tasdiq (affirmation or assent), and its reality is not what various sects claim—especially those who equate faith with mere verbal declaration under coercion. He argues that the heart is the seat of faith because no human authority can control it, whereas the tongue can be compelled. He then provides a key argument: there are circumstances in which a person has no tongue (e.g., mute) or is forbidden to speak—such as during prayer. In prayer, one is prohibited from making a verbal declaration of faith (e.g., 'I believe in the books, prophets, resurrection'), yet one is still considered a believer. If faith were merely speech, then forbidding it during prayer would mean that the person is not a believer at that moment, which is absurd. Furthermore, the religion of Islam would be corrupted if one's worship were invalidated by the absence of speech. Maturidi concludes that faith is an internal, permanent condition that does not change with the tongue's actions, and it cannot be subject to abrogation.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some believe that faith is simply the verbal utterance of the shahada or other declarations.
Correction: Maturidi shows that this view leads to contradictions, such as a person in prayer being unable to speak yet still being a believer.

Key Takeaway
Faith is an internal affirmation in the heart, not a verbal formula, as shown by the impossibility of maintaining faith if it were tied to speech.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you say 'I believe' with your tongue, is it always a true reflection of what is settled in your heart? Or have you ever spoken words of faith while your heart was distracted or doubtful? What would it take for your heart to truly affirm what your tongue says?
?
In moments when you cannot express your faith—perhaps out of fear, or in a situation where it is forbidden to speak—does your heart remain steady? Or do you feel a gap between your inner certainty and outer silence? How can you strengthen the heart's bond so that no external circumstance can shake it?
?
Consider a time when you performed an act of worship, like prayer, while your tongue was silent but your mind wandered. Was that still an act of faith? Maturidi argues that faith is the heart's permanent condition, not momentary speech. Does your heart's condition change with your moods, or is it anchored in a conviction that does not waver? If it wavers, what are you willing to change today to make it firm?
