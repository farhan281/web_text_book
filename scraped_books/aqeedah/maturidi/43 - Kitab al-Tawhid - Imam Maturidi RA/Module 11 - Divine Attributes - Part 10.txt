Proof from Supplication and Derivation of Power and Will
Maturidi uses supplication to prove choice, then derives power and will from choice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how universal supplication to God points to His choice, and how choice necessarily implies power and will.

Core Idea
People naturally pray to God for help, showing they believe He acts by choice, not compulsion.

Maturidi next turns to a universal human practice: supplication and earnest prayer. People from all cultures seek God's help for specific outcomes, which presupposes that God can freely choose to grant or withhold. He then argues that if God acts by choice, He must possess power (qudra) to act and will (irada) to determine specific acts. This lesson covers the supplication argument and the inference to divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the supplication argument
Read the Arabic and translation describing how people pray to God for relief, victory, and help.
Focus on the phrase 'دل ذلك على أن العالم باختياره'.
2
Understand the inference from choice to power and will
Maturidi says: when choice is established, power and will are established. Ask yourself: why does choice require both ability and specific intention?
Without power, action is disordered; without will, it is random.
3
Relate to the shahid/gha'ib principle
Maturidi uses the witnessed world (shahid) as a basis for understanding the unseen (gha'ib). True action in the witnessed world requires choice, power, and will.
This is a key method in kalam.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of rational proofs that God's actions are by choice (ikhtiyar), then derives the attributes of power (qudra), will (irada), and knowledge ('ilm). The author begins with the observation that God creates things and then annihilates them, then restores them (like the cycle of night and day). This ability to alternate states shows choice, because natural processes cannot reverse themselves in such a purposeful manner. Next, he appeals to creation ex nihilo (huduth al-‘alam la min shay'), which is an act only possible for a being with utmost choice; nature acts by necessity and compulsion. He then points to the universal practice of supplication (du'a) and prayer to God for specific outcomes—people naturally seek help from God, not from a compelled force, indicating that God acts by choice. From choice, he infers power and will: choice implies the ability to perform or not perform an act (qudra) and the specific determination of that act (irada). He argues that without power, action would be disordered, and that true power enables a thing and its opposite. Then he uses the consistent and flawless order in creation to prove that God acts with knowledge ('ilm). The argument is based on the principle that an orderly, purposeful sequence cannot arise from ignorance; it must be known. Finally, he notes that the very fact that creation points to its own createdness and to the oneness of its Creator presupposes knowledge on the part of the Creator. This lesson demonstrates the classical kalam method of proving divine attributes through rational inference from observable phenomena.

Proof of God's Knowledge from Order and Design
Maturidi presents three arguments for God's knowledge: from continuity of action, from creation of substances with their needs, and from the evidential design of creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses the continuity, order, and purposeful design in creation to prove that God possesses knowledge.

Core Idea
Perfect order in creation proves that God acts with knowledge, not ignorance.

Maturidi now turns to the intricate order in creation to establish God's knowledge. He argues that the continuous, well-sequenced actions in the world—and the fact that each thing is created with what sustains it—cannot be the product of an ignorant agent. Moreover, the very fact that creation points to its own createdness and to the oneness of its Creator presupposes knowledge. This lesson explains these arguments.

Notice the phrase 'محكما هو الدليل أنه كان فعله على العلم به'.
2
Analyse the argument from substances
Maturidi mentions that God created substances that are not tested and everything intended for permanence with its sustainment. This shows knowledge of each thing's needs.
Consider how a builder knows which materials and supports are needed.
3
Understand the argument from creation's evidential structure
God created creatures in a way that indicates their origination and their Creator oneness. This could not happen without knowledge.
This is a fine-tuning argument.

Analogical Reasoning from the Witnessed to the Unseen
Maturidi presents an analogical argument from the witnessed to the unseen.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses the analogy between the witnessed world and the unseen to establish that God must have action and speech.

Core Idea
What is true of perfect beings in creation is true of the Creator.

Maturidi begins by inviting us to observe the world around us. In the witnessed realm, any being that is capable and unhindered inevitably performs actions and speech. This observable fact serves as the basis for reasoning about the unseen Creator. If God is capable and not prevented, then He too must possess action and speech. This lesson unpacks that logical step.

And likewise, there does not exist in the witnessed realm a capable being who is not prevented and has no action, nor a capable speaker who has no speech; and the witnessed realm is the evidence for the unseen, so that is incumbent upon Him. And creation may be described with corruption, evil, ugliness, and badness. Commentary
This passage presents two arguments. First, using the method of analogy from the witnessed (shāhid) to the unseen (ghāʾib): in the physical world, every capable being that is not prevented actually performs actions and speech; therefore, God, who is capable, must also have action and speech. Second, Maturidi argues that evil cannot be attributed to God. Creation includes corruption, evil, ugliness, and badness. If these were essentially God's actions (li-dhātihi fiʿl Allāh), then God would be described as a corrupter, evil, etc. Since such descriptions are blasphemous (kufr), it follows that these actions belong to something other than God. He then extends this to birth, obedience and disobedience, and acquisition (kasb): these, if truly from God, would imply a name for God that is not used, so they cannot be directly from Him. This preserves divine transcendence.

The Impossibility of Attributing Evil to God
Maturidi refutes attributing evil to God using a conditional argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that evil and corruption cannot be God's essential acts because naming Him with such qualities would be blasphemy.

Core Idea
God is too transcendent to be named with evil.

If God were the direct author of evil, the logical consequence would be that He could be described as 'corrupter' or 'evil'. But such descriptions are clearly unbelief (kufr). Therefore, Maturidi concludes that these acts must originate from something other than God's essence—namely, human acquisition (kasb) or natural processes. This lesson examines the reasoning and its implications for divine transcendence.

Al-Ka'bi's Classification of Divine Attributes
Al-Ka'bi's two criteria for dividing attributes into essential and active.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Ka'bi's criteria for dividing attributes into essential and active, and the rationale behind his classification.

Core Idea
Al-Ka'bi divides attributes based on variability and scope of divine power.

Al-Ka'bi was a leading Mu'tazilite theologian who proposed a sharp division between attributes that are essential to God and those that are merely acts. His classification rests on two key criteria: whether the attribute can differ with states or persons, and whether it falls under God's power. Understanding his view is crucial before we see Maturidi's refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read Ka'bi's first criterion
Read the definition: attributes that vary with state or person are active; those that do not are essential. Note the examples: providing, mercy, speech vs. power, knowledge, life.
This criterion focuses on variability.
2
Read Ka'bi's second criterion
Now read the second definition: what power can affect is active; what it cannot is essential. Example: mercy and speech are possible for power to affect, while knowledge is not.
This criterion focuses on the scope of divine power.
3
Understand Ka'bi's argument about opposites
Ka'bi argues that essential attributes cannot have opposites because the essence is unchanging. He asks why an essential attribute should not be described by its opposite, and answers that it would imply change in the essence.
This reinforces the fixed nature of essential attributes.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of al-Ka'bi's classification of divine attributes into attributes of essence (صفات الذات) and attributes of action (صفات الفعل). Maturidi first presents Ka'bi's view: attributes that can vary with states or persons (like providing, mercy, speech) are attributes of action; those that do not vary (like power, knowledge, life) are attributes of essence. He argues that attributes of essence cannot have opposites because the essence is uniform. He shows that even attributes like hearing and knowledge are described in varying contexts (hearing one prayer and not another), yet they are still considered attributes of essence. Therefore, speech and mercy likewise do not have to be merely attributes of action. Maturidi's Refutation of Ka'bi's Division
Maturidi's refutation of Ka'bi's division using the examples of hearing and knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that Ka'bi's distinction between essential and active attributes is invalid because even seemingly variable attributes like hearing and knowledge are essential.

Core Idea
Variability in description does not make an attribute non-essential.

After presenting Ka'bi's classification, Maturidi launches a refutation. He points out that the very terms we use to describe God are not independent attributes but descriptions from creatures. He then shows that even attributes like hearing and knowledge are spoken of as varying (e.g., God hears one prayer and not another), yet they are still considered essential. If that is accepted, then speech and mercy can likewise be essential despite being described as active.

Guided Path
1
Read Maturidi's opening critique
Read the first objection: 'From his statement that there is in reality no attribute for Allah...' Maturidi argues that what we call attributes are just descriptions or names applied by creatures.
This undercuts the reality of the division.
2
Follow the analogy with hearing and knowledge
Maturidi points out that we say 'He heard so-and-so's prayer' and 'He did not hear so-and-so's prayer', yet we do not conclude that hearing is not essential. Similarly, knowledge is spoken of as known at one time and unknown at another, yet it is essential.
This shows that variability does not exclude essentiality.
3
Understand the objection and response
Ka'bi might object that such statements refer to the negation of the object, not the attribute. Maturidi replies that the same applies to speech and mercy: we are not negating the attribute but affirming a particular act of goodness.
The refutation is consistent.

Attributes of Essence and Action According to Imam Maturidi
Maturidi defines and distinguishes between attributes of essence and action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between attributes of essence and attributes of action, and why attributes of essence cannot change.

Core Idea
Attributes of essence are unchanging; attributes of action vary with state and person.

Imam Maturidi provides a clear criterion to distinguish between two fundamental categories of divine attributes: those inherent to Allah’s essence and those that relate to His actions. By examining whether an attribute admits variation or falls under divine power, we can classify each attribute correctly. This passage lays the foundation for a proper understanding of Allah’s attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition
Read the Arabic passage once for familiarity. Then read the literal translation to understand Maturidi's wording.
The passage uses technical terms like صفة الذات and صفة الفعل.
2
Identify the two criteria
Notice that Maturidi gives two ways to distinguish attributes of essence from action: (1) variation with state/person, (2) falling under divine power. See how each criterion leads to the same classification.
Examples: knowledge and power do not vary; mercy and speech can vary.
3
Understand the objection
Examine the question about describing an essence attribute by its opposite. Maturidi answers by linking the attribute to the unchanging essence, showing that change would contradict the essence's nature.
This reinforces that essential attributes are immutable.

Commentary
This passage defines the two categories of divine attributes—attributes of essence (ṣifāt al-dhāt) and attributes of action (ṣifāt al-fiʿl). Maturidi begins by stating that whatever can vary according to state or person is an attribute of action, giving examples like providing, mercy, and speech. In contrast, attributes like power, knowledge, and life do not admit such variation; they are attributes of essence. He then adds another criterion: whatever falls under divine power (qudra) is an attribute of action (e.g., mercy and speech in terms of their relational aspect), while whatever does not fall under power (like knowledge itself) is an attribute of essence. A question arises: why is an attribute of essence not described by its opposite (e.g., why not say God is ignorant)? The answer is that the attribute goes back to the Essence, which is unchanging; describing it by an opposite would imply change in the Essence, which is impossible. Therefore, as the Essence is invariable, no change can occur in its essential attributes, just as a thing that is necessary for a cause persists as long as the cause endures.

God's Attributes: Description, Not Reality
Maturidi argues that God's attributes are not real distinct entities but only descriptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's position that God has no attribute as a separate reality, only as description and naming.

Core Idea
God's attributes are not separate entities; they are descriptions of His essence.

In this passage, Imam Maturidi tackles a subtle point: are God's attributes distinct realities in His essence? He argues that they are not. Instead, what we call attributes are simply descriptions or names applied to God. Understanding this is key to avoiding anthropomorphism and affirming divine simplicity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic original and its English translation once to get a feel for Maturidi's concise style and argument.
Focus on the key terms: صفة, وصف, تسمية.
2
Identify the central claim
Maturidi openly states that God in reality has no attribute (صفة). Instead, it is only the description (وصف) or naming (تسمية). Pinpoint this thesis in the text.
3
Analyze the reasoning
Notice how Maturidi supports his claim: describers unanimously describe God with knowledge, power, and action. This consistency shows that description is not adding separate entities.
The phrase 'على غير اختلاف من حيث الوصف' is key.
4
Understand the conclusion
Maturidi concludes that defining God by description is pointless since the reality of these descriptions returns to what is agreed upon (i.e., the essence itself).
The term 'الوفاق' refers to the unanimous agreement on the essence as the reference point.
5
Reflect on the implications
Consider why this matters: it safeguards tawhid by denying that attributes are separate from Allah, preventing any notion of composition in the divine essence.
This aligns with the broader Maturidi emphasis on divine simplicity.

Scriptural Proof for Allah's Speech
Maturidi cites Quranic verses and consensus for Allah's speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses Quranic verses and scholarly consensus to establish that Allah speaks with real speech.

Core Idea
The Quran itself affirms that Allah spoke to Moses, and there is no denial among the creation.

Maturidi begins with the foundation: Allah's attribute of speech is established by both revelation and reason. Here he focuses on the scriptural evidence, citing direct Quranic verses and the agreement of the community. He also addresses those who question why Allah does not speak to them directly.

2

The first verse (4:164) uses the verbal noun 'تكليما' to emphasize the reality of Allah's speech to Moses. Notice how this refutes any denial.

Maturidi states that there is agreement among the creation that Allah is a speaker and has real speech, though they differ on its nature. This consensus is a source of authority.
The nature (mahiyya) of Allah's speech is not the focus here; the reality is affirmed.

Understand the criticism of doubters
Maturidi criticizes those who ask 'Why does not Allah speak to us?' (2:118), calling their question arrogant and ignorant of their own rank. This shows that the demand is improper.

Abu Mansur (may Allah have mercy on him) said: The foundation is that Allah, mighty and majestic, has established His description with speech by the proof of revelation and reason. As for revelation, His saying: 'And Allah spoke to Moses directly' (4:164) mentioned in the verbal noun without any dissent among the creation regarding Allah's speech. And the agreement has been found that He is a speaker and that He has real speech, even if opinions differ on its quiddity. And I do not deny those who said: 'Why does not Allah speak to us?' (2:118) except by description of arrogance and ignorance of their own rank. Similarly, His saying: 'And a group among them used to listen to the words of Allah' (2:75).
As for reason: Every knower and powerful being who does not speak does so due to an impediment, whether from incapacity or [؟] – and Allah is exalted above that – so it is established that He is a speaker. Moreover, the one who does not speak in the witnessed world only does not speak in the sense that he does not hear or see due to an affliction. Allah is free from the meaning that necessitates deafness and blindness, and likewise dumbness. And it is more fitting, for it is the most praiseworthy thing by which He is praised.

Commentary
This passage provides the proof for the attribute of speech (kalam). Maturidi states the foundational principle: Allah's description with speech is established by two types of evidence: sam' (revelation) and aql (reason). The scriptural proof includes the verse 'And Allah spoke to Moses directly' (4:164), which uses the emphatic verbal noun (takliman) to affirm real speech without any denial among the creation. There is agreement that Allah is Mutakallim (Speaker) and has real speech, though people differ on its nature (mahiyya). Maturidi criticizes those who demand 'Why does not Allah speak to us?' (2:118) as arrogant and ignorant of their own status. He also cites the verse 'a group among them used to listen to the words of Allah' (2:75) to show that Allah's speech is heard. The rational proof: every being who knows and has power yet does not speak, does so because of an impediment, either from incapacity or from [؟] – Allah is exalted above such defects – therefore it is established that He is a speaker. In the witnessed world (shahid), one who does not speak fails due to an affliction such as deafness or blindness. Allah is free from any meaning that necessitates deafness or blindness, and likewise dumbness. Speech is more fitting for Allah, as it is the most praiseworthy attribute by which He is praised.

Misconception & Correction

Correction: Maturidi affirms that Allah has real speech (kalam haqiqi) as shown by the emphatic verbal noun and consensus.
