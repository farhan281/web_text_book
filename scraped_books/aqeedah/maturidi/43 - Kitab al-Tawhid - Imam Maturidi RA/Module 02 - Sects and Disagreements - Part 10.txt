Refutation of Dualism: No Helpers for God
Maturidi refutes dualism by arguing harmful things are managed by God alone.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's refutation of dualism by demonstrating that all harmful things are under God's control and serve a wise purpose.

Core Idea
Evil does not prove a second creator; all harm serves a wise purpose.

Maturidi confronts the dualist claim that evil must have a separate source. He argues that harmful creatures, the evil eye, and all injurious things are part of God's creation, managed by a wise director. This lesson unpacks his reasoning, showing how the existence of harm actually confirms God's sole lordship.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let Maturidi's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Note the key terms like 'a'wan (helpers) and 'munshi' al-sharr' (originator of evil).
2
Identify the dualist claims being refuted
Maturidi refutes several dualist assertions: that there are helpers assisting God, that harmful creatures are the result of a truce, and that God fears an opponent. Find each of these in the passage.
These claims are central to the Majus/dualist worldview.
3
Understand the argument about the evil eye
Maturidi argues that if the evil eye could overpower God, it would itself be a lord by another. See how he uses this to show that nothing can diminish God's power or knowledge.
4
Conclude: no independent originator of evil
The passage concludes that all harmful things benefit creation and are directed by a wise manager. Therefore, the claim of a separate originator of evil is far from the truth.
This is Maturidi's key refutation of dualism.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of dualist claims (likely Majus) who posit a separate source of evil. Maturidi argues that harmful things are not independent agents but are created by God with a purpose. He begins by dismissing the idea that there are helpers (a'wan) assisting God, as atheists claim. Then he addresses the objection that harmful creatures were created for some benefit (mawada'a), comparing them to harmful vermin. He then argues that if God feared an opponent, that would imply ignorance of being Lord of all—contradicting divinity. Similarly, the evil eye (al-'ayn) cannot overpower God. If something could remove His power or knowledge, that thing would be lord by another, not by itself. Thus, the objects of worship of the dualists are actually slaves, not gods. Nothing harmful exists purely for harm; they benefit creation. A wise manager (mudabbir) directs them. Therefore, the claim of an independent originator of evil (munshi' al-sharr) is far from the truth.

Refutation of the Ahad Report Objection
Maturidi refutes the ahad claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi rejects the claim that the Quran is transmitted via ahad (single) reports and affirms its mutawatir (mass) transmission.

Core Idea
The Quran was transmitted by the whole community, not isolated individuals.

An objector argued that Quranic reports are ahad (single-chain) and thus unreliable. Maturidi directly refutes this as false, asserting that the entire community (kaffa) transmitted the Quran, making it mutawatir – the highest level of authenticity. This lesson examines his decisive response.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Start by reading the objector's claim that the Quran's reports are ahad. Notice that this is presented as an attack on the reliability of the Quran.
The Arabic text reads: 'وطعن في أخبار القرآن، إنه خبر الآحاد'.
2
Identify Maturidi's refutation
Look for Maturidi's immediate response: 'وذلك كذب، بل رواه كافة كافة'. He calls the claim a lie and states that the entire community transmitted it.
3
Reflect on the significance
Consider why this refutation is powerful: if the whole community transmitted the Quran (tawatur), then it cannot be ahad. This establishes the Quran's authenticity beyond doubt.
This connects to broader Islamic methodology of transmission.

Refutation of the Tawatur Impossibility Objection

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Widespread news cannot be hidden, even from the farthest people.

The objector further argued that mass transmission (tawatur) is impossible because large groups far from an event can be tricked, while those near cannot directly witness except rarely. Maturidi counters by quoting Ibn al-Rawandi, who sarcastically remarks that such matters become widely known and cannot be hidden. This lesson unpacks that exchange.

Ibn al-Rawandi is an opponent, so his words support Maturidi's point.

Understand the refutation
Realize that the quote undermines the objection: if news spreads and is not hidden, then tawatur is possible. Maturidi uses the objector's own associate to refute him.

Classification of Good and Evil in Acts
Maturidi classifies actions into two types and demonstrates slaughter is not inherently evil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's classification of actions into inherently good/evil and context-dependent, and how slaughter falls into the latter category.

Core Idea
Slaughter is not inherently evil; its moral value depends on context and divine permission.

In this passage, Maturidi lays out a foundational principle of Islamic moral epistemology: actions are of two types. Some are good or evil in themselves, while others derive their moral status from outcomes and divine sanction. Slaughter, he argues, belongs to the second type, and thus cannot be condemned as intrinsically evil. This sets the stage for refuting dualist objections.

Guided Path
1
Read the classification of two types of actions
Maturidi states that things are of two types: those good/evil in themselves, and those dependent on need and outcomes. Read this classification carefully.
Note the Arabic terms: 'يحسن لنفسه' and 'على حسب الحاجة'.
2
Apply the classification to slaughter
Maturidi argues that slaughter cannot be evil in itself because it can be good in retaliation or when preventing harm. Notice the reasoning about outcomes.
3
Understand the natural vs. rational distinction
Aversion to slaughter is natural (due to pain), but rational judgment can override it. Maturidi uses habituation as evidence.
The prohibition is natural, not rational, and thus subject to change.
4
Reflect on the animal analogy
Just as wild animals can be tamed, aversion to slaughter can be overcome by habit. This reinforces the point that the prohibition is not inherent.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The part taken from a lamp is created, not eternal, so the analogy fails.

Christians sometimes use the analogy of a lamp: one flame can light another without diminishing the first. They apply this to the son being generated from the Father. Maturidi examines this analogy carefully and exposes its flaws. In this passage, he shows that the transferred part is new and created, not eternal.

5
Conclude the refutation
Maturidi's final question: why allow something originated and created to be called a son? This shows the analogy is invalid for supporting the eternal son.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer regarding the analogy between a single act attributed to two agents and a single statement attributed to multiple speakers. Al-Ka'bi's Objection on Power and Inaction: Maturidi's Refutation
Maturidi refutes al-Ka'bi's objection that a powerful being could remain inactive indefinitely.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
God's power is not constrained by the contradictions that limit creaturely action.

This passage is a dense theological exchange. Al-Ka'bi challenges the notion that God can remain inactive for extended periods, analogizing to human power. Imam al-Maturidi responds by dissecting the question into two aspects and then pointing out a critical oversight regarding the nature of contradiction (tadādd) in divine versus creaturely actions. As you read, notice how Maturidi shifts the ground from temporal duration to the very nature of power and action.

Refuting Al-Ka‘bi: Allah's Decree of Kufr is True and Just
Maturidi refutes al-Kaʻbī's claim that Allah does not decree kufr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Imam Maturidi rejects al-Ka‘bi's claim that Allah cannot decree disbelief, and how the truth of a decree is independent of the nature of the decreed act.

Core Idea
A decree about falsehood is itself true; the nature of the decree is distinct from the nature of the decreed.

In this passage, Imam Maturidi engages with a Mu‘tazili opponent, al-Ka‘bi, who argued that because disbelief (kufr) is false and contradictory, Allah—who only decrees truth—cannot decree it. Maturidi exposes the logical error: the decree of a false thing is a true and just decree, not itself false. He uses the example of a human judge to illustrate the point. Let's examine his argument closely.

Guided Path
1
Understand al-Ka‘bi's claim
Read the first sentence: 'Al-Kaʻbī claimed that Allah does not decree kufr.' Note that his denial is absolute, but Maturidi hints that it could be allowed in some aspects of qada'.
Al-Ka‘bi is a Mu‘tazili figure known for his rationalist theology.
2
Identify al-Ka‘bi's reasoning
He argues that kufr is mutafāwit (disparate/contradictory) and bāṭil (false), while Allah's decree is truth and correctness. Therefore, Allah cannot decree what is false.
This reasoning assumes that the decree must share the nature of the decreed.
3
Grasp Maturidi's counter-argument
Maturidi points out that the decree of falsehood is not itself false: 'the decree of falsehood is not false.' The truth or justice of a decree is independent of the thing decreed.
A decree can be true about a falsehood; e.g., 'This statement is false' is a true statement about falsehood.
4
Appreciate the analogy and conclusion
Maturidi uses judges: a judge's decree about an unjust act is itself just, not unjust. Ignorance of this basic distinction shows a lack of understanding of kalam.
The phrase 'wa lā quwwata illā billāh' emphasizes reliance on Allah.
