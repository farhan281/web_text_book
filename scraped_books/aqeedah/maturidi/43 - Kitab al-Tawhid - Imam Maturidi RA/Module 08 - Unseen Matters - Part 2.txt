The Meaning of Resurrection and Annihilation
Maturidi clarifies the meaning of the act of resurrection and annihilation, rejecting two interpretations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise meaning of Allah's act of resurrection and annihilation, and why assigning time to the act is impossible or corrupt.

Core Idea
The act of resurrection is not tied to a specific moment; it is an eternal attribute manifesting at its appointed time.

In this passage, Imam Maturidi clarifies a subtle theological point about the divine act of resurrection and annihilation. He distinguishes between two possible interpretations of what it means for Allah to 'do' the Hour, showing why both are problematic. This helps us understand the relationship between Allah's eternal attributes and temporal events.

Guided Path
1

Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice the two interpretations Maturidi presents.
The passage is short but dense.
2
Identify the two interpretations
Maturidi mentions two possible meanings of 'doing' the resurrection: making this hour its time, or making a time for takwin. Find these in the text.
The first is dismissed as impossible, the second as corrupt.
3
Understand why each is rejected
Reflect on why the first is impossible (resurrection is not now) and the second is corrupt (it implies temporality in the eternal attribute of takwin).
The key insight: takwin is eternal and cannot be bound by time.

Commentary
This passage is a clarification regarding the divine act of resurrection (qiyama) and annihilation (fana). It addresses a potential misunderstanding of what it means for God to 'do' the resurrection. Maturidi states that the act has a specific meaning. If someone asks about it, their intention could be one of two things: either they mean that God makes the current present hour the time for resurrection, or they mean that God's takwin brings about resurrection at a specific time. The first is impossible because the resurrection is clearly not occurring at the present moment. The second is corrupt (fasid) because it involves assigning a specific time to the attribute of takwin itself, which would imply that takwin is temporal (hadath). Since takwin is eternal, it cannot be tied to a specific time as a temporal event would be. Rather, the resurrection will occur at its appointed time, and God's takwin is the eternal attribute that brings it about at that time without itself being temporal.

The Rational Necessity of Divine Commands and Gratitude
Maturidi presents al-Husayn's rational argument for the necessity of divine commands and gratitude.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why divine commands and prohibitions are rationally necessary and why gratitude is obligatory based on human nature and reason.

Core Idea
Gratitude for God's favors is rationally required, so divine commands and prohibitions are necessary.

Al-Husayn and Maturidi present a rational argument for the necessity of divine commands and prohibitions. They ground these obligations in the nature of humans as rational beings capable of inferring unseen truths from observed evidence, and in the natural duty of gratitude for blessings. This passage establishes that without such commands, ignorance and evil would be permitted.

Guided Path
1
Recognize human endowment
The passage states humans are created disciplined, knowing benefit and harm, and able to infer from the seen to the unseen. This capacity underpins the need for divine guidance.
2
Necessity of imposing knowledge
Given human capacity, it is not permissible that knowledge (ma'rifa) be left unrequired and ignorance allowed, for that would permit lying and every blameworthy thing.
3
Obligation of gratitude
Since God bestows favors in creation, gratitude is rationally necessary, so God requires it. This makes commands and prohibitions meaningful.
Gratitude is a logical consequence of receiving blessings.

Refuting al-Warraq's Objection on Asking the People of the Reminder
Objection and three answers by Imam Maturidi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Maturidi refutes al-Warraq's objection against the Quranic command to ask the People of the Reminder by presenting three distinct answers that harmonize the command with the charge of concealing truth.

Core Idea
God's command to ask can stand even if the asked are accused of concealing truth.

This passage addresses a theological objection raised by al-Warraq: how can the Quran command us to ask the People of the Book when they are also accused of concealing the truth? Imam Maturidi offers three harmonizing answers that preserve the integrity of the Quranic command and demonstrate the compatibility of divine revelations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Read al-Warraq's objection carefully. He points out an apparent contradiction: the Quran commands asking the People of the Reminder, yet elsewhere testifies that they conceal the truth. Note the specific verse references.

Understand the first answer: after God supported the Prophet with clear proofs, the People of the Book inclined to the Book. The command was given because God would subdue them and compel them to agree, making it a sign. See the supporting verse from Shu'ara.
3
Second answer: appeal to a trusted person
The second answer addresses stubbornness: even after proof, a person may only yield when asked by someone they trust. The command is to ask such a trusted individual to break the stubbornness.
4
Third answer and additional interpretation
The third answer restricts the command to those among them who have accepted Islam. Note also the possible interpretation that 'People of the Reminder' means those entrusted with refuting falsehood.
This shows the flexibility of interpretation.

Since the names are divided into three categories, and not all categories are included (i.e., the threat does not apply to all), this proves specificity (khsus). Therefore, one must restrict the application of the threat to what is certain. Any attempt to generalize (ta'mim) would lead to contradiction with the specific reports. Then Maturidi provides evidence that the threat of eternal punishment (khulud) can only be for shirk, because human nature (jibillah) is innately averse to anything that would cause them to exit their religion, even if they have adopted that religion through reason, proof, or blind imitation. The presence of lesser sins among them does not change this. Furthermore, intellects (uql) support this because beliefs are considered eternal by their adherents, unlike actions which are variable. Similarly, revelation (sam') indicates that actions are subject to differing rulings. Therefore, the eternal threat is specific to shirk, the only action that completely uproots the religion.

Commentary
This passage presents a theological argument regarding the principle (الأصل) of all things that are threatened with punishment in the Quran. Maturidi begins by stating that the reality of such threatened actions occurs with varying degrees of ugliness (القبح), which intellects perceive. Similarly, the names given to agents imply different meanings, and not all actions or agents are on the same level of blame. The argument then addresses a methodological issue: when faced with a general threat (عموم) in the Quran, one must not rush to apply it universally without considering context. If one were to interpret all threats as absolute and then find that some individuals who outwardly qualify are not actually punished (e.g., believers who sin), this would lead to an apparent contradiction in the Quran. Such a contradiction would allow heretics to criticize the Quran as not being from God, since the Quran itself claims to have no contradictions (Quran 4:82), that falsehood cannot approach it (41:42), and that God preserves it (15:9). To avoid this, one must understand that many Quranic rulings are directed not at the general outward meaning but at a specific scope (خصوص). Thus, the general term must be qualified. Maturidi then cites the example of the names 'righteous' (الأبرار) and 'wicked' (الفجار) in the Quran. God explicitly describes the absolute wicked (الفاجر المطلق) as those destined for punishment in 'Sijjin' (a place in Hell), implying that the threatened punishment is for the wicked who are absolute in their wickedness, not for every sinner. This shows that Quranic threats must be understood with their proper intended scope, preserving both divine wisdom and the integrity of the Quran against accusations of contradiction.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the Qur'anic verse 18:23-24, where Allah commands His Prophet to say 'in sha' Allah' when intending a future action. The author, Imam al-Maturidi, explains the wisdom behind this command. He begins by noting that the verse's instruction applies whether the promise is made to someone or not. The purpose is to teach people the seriousness and rights of a promise, similar to the command of consultation (shura) which teaches its importance. Alternatively, the exception was commanded because the entry into the mosque was attributed by Allah to Himself, and the Prophet had promised it to a specific group, but some of them might perish before that time. The author then connects this to the verse from Surah al-Fath (48:27), where Allah fulfilled the vision of entering the mosque. He explains that the Prophet's statement about entry was accompanied by an exception (in sha' Allah) because the exact time was not specified. This practice is proper for matters of doubt, not for matters of certainty. Then, Maturidi applies this to believers: those who are certain of their faith and have fulfilled its obligations should express gratitude to Allah by acknowledging His favor, and this is not self-praise because all believers share in that. He concludes by mentioning that outward religion imposes rulings and rights that must be manifested, and ends with a prayer.

Learning Objective

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. Maturidi addresses an apparent contradiction in the hadith of Jibril known to discuss Iman, Islam, and Ihsan. Some narrations present the question about Islam only, while others include Iman. He mentions that Ibn 'Umar reported the Prophet answering according to the question about Islam even when the question about Iman was also asked. Maturidi explains that this narration can be understood in two ways: either the narrator did not hear the entire question (only the part about Islam), or the one from whom he narrated omitted the mention of Iman. He then notes that it is unlikely that Ibn 'Umar would drop part of what Jibril and the Prophet said, and he hints at a further point that the truth is not repelled, but the text is cut off.
