The First Source of Doubt: Blind Following (Taqlid)
Maturidi enumerates the first of three sources of doubt: blind following.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi regards blind following (taqlid) as the primary obstacle to rational acceptance of truth and how it prevents reflection on evidence.

Core Idea
Blind following suffocates reason by substituting habit for evidence.

Maturidi has just presented compelling rational proofs for God's existence. Yet many people still fail to accept these truths. In this passage, he begins to diagnose why: the first and most powerful obstacle is taqlid—blind adherence to inherited beliefs. He explains how this psychological attachment overrides the natural inclination toward truth.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the translation. Let the rhythm of Maturidi's argument come through.
Notice that the passage begins with a statement about three sources of doubt, then details the first.
2
Identify the three aspects mentioned at the start
Maturidi says doubt confronts people from 'three aspects' (أوجه ثلاثة). The passage only elaborates on the first. Keep in mind that two more are to follow in later passages.
3
Focus on the first aspect: taqlid
Taqlid is defined as following those to whom the soul is accustomed and inclined. Notice how Maturidi describes its effects: abandoning reflection on proofs and turning to wishful thinking.
4
Examine the motivations behind taqlid
Maturidi lists four psychological motivations: trust in authorities, desire for companionship, false hope of guidance, and expectation of favors. Each shows how taqlid is sustained by emotional and social factors.
These motivations are not exhaustive but represent common reasons people cling to tradition.
5
Reflect on the outcome of taqlid
The passage concludes that these habits eventually overcome a person. Consider how this outcome blocks rational acceptance of truth.

Commentary
This passage identifies the first of three sources of doubt that prevent people from accepting the truth of divine existence. Maturidi states that after presenting the clear proofs, he observed that doubt arises from three aspects. The first is taqlid (blind following). People become accustomed to following certain leaders or traditions and become attached to them. As a result, they abandon thinking about evidence and instead follow their own wishful thinking. They do this either out of trust in those authorities, or to gain their companionship and fulfill their desires, or in the false belief that those authorities will guide them aright, or in hope of receiving favors from them. Over time, this habit of evil and bad customs becomes ingrained, and they are overcome by it. Thus taqlid is a major barrier to rational acceptance of the truth.

The Importance of Deep Reflection in Refuting Dualism
Maturidi opens refutation by attributing error to lack of deep reflection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Maturidi begins his refutation of dualistic sects by emphasizing the necessity of deep reflection on previously established proofs.

Core Idea
Deep reflection on God's unity is the key to avoiding dualistic errors.

Imam Maturidi opens his refutation of dualism not with a direct argument, but with a methodological point: error stems from insufficient reflection. He urges the reader to consider that if the dualist sects had truly pondered the proofs already presented, they would not have fallen into their mistaken beliefs. This lesson examines that opening statement.

Dualist Arguments for Two Origins: Good and Evil
Maturidi presents the arguments of dualist sects for two origins of good and evil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three main arguments dualist sects used to posit two eternal origins for good and evil.

Core Idea
Dualists argue that a single good God cannot be the source of evil, so good and evil must have separate origins.

In this passage, Imam Maturidi presents the arguments of dualist groups such as the Manichaeans and Zoroastrians. They observe that the world contains both good and evil, and since a wise and merciful God would not create evil, they conclude that there must be two opposing eternal principles. This lesson outlines their reasoning and the sects that adopted it.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation to get the gist of the dualist arguments.
Notice the flow from the first two theories to the detailed moral argument.
2
Identify the three dualist theories
Look for the three explanations dualists give for the mixture of good and evil: (1) two distinct origins mixing, (2) one origin splitting into two, (3) the moral intuition argument.
The third argument is given the most detail.
3
Examine the moral argument in depth
Focus on the third argument based on human custom: a wise and merciful being only does good and avoids causing harm without benefit. Since God is wise and merciful, He cannot be the source of evil.
This is the core of the dualist case.
4
Connect to the named sects
See how the arguments lead to the doctrines of the Thanawiyya (light and darkness), the Magians (Allah and Shaytan), and the Dahriyya (materialists).
Maturidi will refute these arguments in the following lesson.

Commentary
This passage presents the arguments of dualist groups (such as the Manichaeans and Zoroastrians) who posit two eternal origins for the world—one good (light) and one evil (darkness). The author (Imam Maturidi) first outlines several dualist theories: some say the two origins were distinct then mixed; others say they were one then separated, with the separation making each act according to its nature. The majority of these groups, Maturidi notes, hold that the world's existence is by nature rather than by voluntary action. Then he presents their third argument, which is based on moral intuition (consideration by meanings): they observe that the world contains both benefit and harm, good and evil. In human custom, a doer of good is praised as merciful and wise, while a doer of evil is blamed as harsh and foolish. Since they assume God is wise and merciful, they conclude that it is impossible for God to do evil or harm others. They reason that a wise being seeks benefit for himself, not harm; one who harms without gain is not wise. Therefore, they insist that the world's mixture of good and evil must arise from two different origins—either a single origin that later split, or two origins that always were distinct. Maturidi then notes that this line of reasoning leads to the doctrines of the Dahriyya (materialists who deny a Creator) and the dualists (Thanawiyya) who identify good as light and evil as darkness, and the Magians who call good 'Allah' and evil 'Shaytan'. The passage thus sets up the dualist case, which Maturidi will refute in the following lesson.

The Epistemological Self-Defeat of Dualism
Maturidi exposes the epistemological contradictions of dualism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi shows that dualism's own premises make knowledge of good and evil impossible.

Core Idea
Dualism's own logic makes reliable moral knowledge impossible.

Maturidi begins his refutation by turning the dualists' own principles against them. If each essence only perceives its own kind, then a human being – a mixture of both – cannot reliably distinguish wisdom from folly. This leads to a fundamental skepticism that undermines their entire system.

Guided Path
1
Consider the opening remark
Maturidi begins by stating that if dualists reflected, they would realize their minds cannot grasp even human wisdom. This sets the tone for the refutation.
2
Identify the core premise
Dualists hold that the essence of good sees only good, and the essence of evil sees only evil. A human is a mixture of both essences.
This is key to the epistemological problem.
3
Trace the unreliability of statements
Because a human sees by both essences, he may see wisdom as folly and vice versa. His claim 'it is good' is unreliable because we don't know which essence is speaking.
Maturidi uses this to undermine the dualist's ability to make any moral claim.
4
Observe the consequences for hope and fear
Since neither essence has power to harm or benefit the other, there is no basis for hope or fear. The entire purpose of knowing wisdom and folly is lost.
This shows the practical failure of dualism.
5
Understand the nature argument
If each essence acts by nature (not choice), then knowledge of wisdom (which requires deliberate placement) is impossible. This leads to the essence of good being mixed with evil and evil dominating.
The argument culminates in the conclusion that dualism is self-defeating.

Commentary
This passage contains Imam Maturidi's refutation of dualism. He begins by stating that if these sects had truly reflected on the proofs already presented, they would realize their minds cannot grasp even human wisdom, let alone divine wisdom. Furthermore, their own position on choice (that each essence only produces its own kind) prevents them from claiming knowledge of wisdom and folly, since a human who is a mixture of both essences cannot reliably distinguish them—what one essence sees as wisdom, the other may see as folly. Hence, no statement from such a person can be trusted, because it is unknown which essence is speaking. This leads to the destruction of hope and fear, since neither essence has power to benefit or harm the other. He then argues that since each essence acts by nature (طب, not choice), it is impossible for it to have knowledge of wisdom or folly, as wisdom requires deliberate choice and placing things in their proper positions. Light, they claim, does not know folly to avoid it, and darkness does not know wisdom. Consequently, the essence of light itself becomes a mixture of knowledge and ignorance, power and incapacity—so the good essence is tainted with evil, and evil dominates. Maturidi then argues that anything acting by nature is compelled, and this necessitates a Compeller who makes it that way, leading to the affirmation of a single Creator. If one posits two creators, each must either be able to prevent the other or not; if unable, they are not truly powerful; if able but does not act, that is evil. Thus, the dualist position is contradictory and ultimately affirms the oneness of God.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A perfect principle cannot both be all-joy and yet concerned about evil, nor can it change its mind.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of various sects, showing how they all initially affirm tawhid in general but then corrupt it through specific doctrines. Maturidi begins by stating that all humans with reflective capacity were granted a general understanding of tawhid, but each group nullified that general grant through their particular interpretations, except for the Muslims who remained faithful. He then systematically lists the errors of each group:
1. Materialists (al-dahriyya): affirm a Creator and His pre-eternity, but then make all other entities co-eternal with Him, thus negating tawhid.
2. Those who posit pre-eternal clay or matter (hayūlā): make it one, then destroy its unity by deriving countless things from it.
3. Dualists (al-thānawiyya) and Manichaeans: affirm a single knowing being but make all good things parts of that being, and by equating the One with a body (jism), which implies divisibility, they nullify the meaning of the One.
4. Jews: ascribe to God the likeness of creation, leading to multiplicity and even the possibility of a son.
5. Christians: affirm one entity (kayān) and three hypostases (qanūmā), denying each hypostasis division, yet they claim He was non-incarnate then became incarnate, but a body is divisible.
6. Adherents of the natures (aṣḥāb al-ṭabā’i‘): do not make natures act on their own but require a combiner and separator, which they consider pre-eternal.
7. Mu‘tazila: say things exist in non-existence ('adam) and that non-existence is pre-eternal, making things like it, thus falling into the same error as the materialists regarding the pre-eternity of the world. In each case, their specific interpretations undermine the true tawhid.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and refutation of three possible causes for the state of two things (e.g., light and darkness) being either separate or united. Maturidi states that they must be that way either by nature (tab‘), by choice (ikhtiyār), or by an external factor (ākhar). He then refutes each possibility in turn. (A) If by nature, then the property would necessarily intensify over time—like natural motion increasing—but the observed state remains constant, so this is false. Thus the doctrine is corrupt. (C) If by an external cause, then the state (separation or union) is temporally produced (ḥādith), but the two things themselves must be temporally produced as well, leading to the affirmation of tawhid (God's oneness) rather than its negation. Maturidi concludes that one who ignores both doctrines has no valid position and resorts to taqlid. Since the two opposing doctrines are both absurd, the truth—if the materialists' view were correct—would have to lie in one of them, but both have been refuted, so the materialist position is invalid. The passage is a systematic refutation of dualism and an indirect proof of tawhid.

Maturidi refutes external cause and concludes with tawhid.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If an external cause produces the state, then the things themselves are created, proving tawhid.

Maturidi now turns to the third and final possibility: that the state of separation or union is caused by an external agent. He then wraps up by dismissing the position of those who claim ignorance and reaffirming the logical collapse of all alternative explanations.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and refutation of three possible causes for the state of two things (e.g., light and darkness) being either separate or united. Maturidi states that they must be that way either by nature (tab‘), by choice (ikhtiyār), or by an external factor (ākhar). He then refutes each possibility in turn. (A) If by nature, then the property would necessarily intensify over time—like natural motion increasing—but the observed state remains constant, so this is false. (B) If by choice, then each agent would have power to choose and prevent the other, leading to contradiction (since both would simultaneously choose opposite states) and mutual frustration; moreover, the ability to prevent implies knowledge and power, which if absent negates choice and implies ignorance and impotence. Thus the doctrine is corrupt. (C) If by an external cause, then the state (separation or union) is temporally produced (ḥādith), but the two things themselves must be temporally produced as well, leading to the affirmation of tawhid (God's oneness) rather than its negation. Maturidi concludes that one who ignores both doctrines has no valid position and resorts to taqlid. Since the two opposing doctrines are both absurd, the truth—if the materialists' view were correct—would have to lie in one of them, but both have been refuted, so the materialist position is invalid. The passage is a systematic refutation of dualism and an indirect proof of tawhid.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the dualist doctrine (qawl bi-l-ithnayn), specifically the view that the universe is governed by two eternal principles: light (nur) and darkness (zulma). Imam al-Maturidi presents several arguments against this view in sequence. First, he argues that a being that is ignorant and weak (al-ʿajiz al-jahil) is more fitting to be a subjugated servant ('abdun mahrub) than a sovereign lord (rabbun malik). The dualists' light is ignorant of where it falls and is unable to free its part from darkness; darkness is prevented from doing its work (evil). Their parts and states are scattered, and each cannot exercise sovereignty. Allah is exalted above such attributes. Second, he argues that each of the two principles has an end (nihaya) from one side but is unbounded from other sides. If being unbounded is a proof of pre-eternity (qidam), then the side with the end would be temporal; if not, the whole becomes temporal. Third, he argues that the light cannot free its finite part from the infinite parts of darkness, and if the infinite parts could not prevent the fall, how could the light escape after falling? Fourth, if one claims that light possesses all senses and knowledge while darkness lacks them, then light would be a blind god, ignorant, incapable, evil by nature—contradicting divinity. Fifth, he argues that if the two are bodies or accidents, each must occupy a distinct place; separation would destroy an accident, and if they are bodies, either their place is essential (leading to contradictory states) or non-essential (causing mixing of good and evil), which undermines their fundamental claim of duality. All these arguments show the falsity of dualism.
