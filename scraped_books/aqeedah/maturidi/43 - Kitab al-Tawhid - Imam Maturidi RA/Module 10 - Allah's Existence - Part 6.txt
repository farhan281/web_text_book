Maturidi's Proof from Evil for God's Existence
Maturidi's proof from evil for God's existence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that the presence of evil in the world disproves the possibility of the world being self-caused, thereby establishing the necessity of a Creator.

Core Idea
Evil in the world proves the world is not self-caused.

Maturidi presents a concise philosophical proof for God's existence rooted in the observable fact of evil. The argument is a reductio ad absurdum: if the world were self-sufficient and self-causing, it would only produce perfect states, but we see evil, so it must depend on an external maker. This lesson unpacks the logical steps of that proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic text and its translation. Note the conditional structure: 'If... then...' and the conclusion that follows from the observed existence of evil.
The proof is very compressed; pay attention to the logical flow.
2
Identify the premise and conclusion
Identify the conditional premise: if the world were self-caused, there would be no evil. Then identify the observed fact: evil exists. Work out the conclusion.
The conclusion is explicitly stated in the second sentence.
3
Understand the reasoning behind the premise
Reflect on why Maturidi claims a self-caused world would have no evil. The reasoning is that a self-sufficient entity would choose only the best states for itself.
This is a key point: self-sufficiency implies perfection.
4
Connect to the existence of God
Realize that the argument does not directly prove God's attributes, only that the world has a maker. This maker is necessarily external and powerful, aligning with the Islamic concept of God.

This passage presents Maturidi's proof for the existence of God from the presence of evil in the world. It is an argument by contradiction. The reason given is that a self-sufficient entity would only choose for itself the most perfect states and times. Since we observe evil in the world, the antecedent is false—the world did not come to be by itself, implying it has a Maker. The proof is succinct: evil implies dependency, dependency implies a Creator.

Proof of Oneness from the Finitude of the World
Maturidi argues that if there were multiple gods, the world would not be finite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's logical argument that the finitude of the world necessitates the oneness of God.

Core Idea
If the world is finite, there cannot be more than one God.

Maturidi presents a concise yet powerful proof for tawḥīd (God's oneness) grounded in the observable finitude of the world. By considering the logical implications of multiple creators, he shows that only a single Creator can account for the limited nature of creation. This argument is a classic example of kalām reasoning, using rational deduction from observed reality.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its English translation to grasp the flow of the argument. Pay attention to the conditional structure.
The argument is very brief but dense; read it twice.
2
Identify the conditional statement
Notice that the argument begins with 'If God were not one' — this is the hypothetical premise from which the conclusion follows. Understand that the conclusion is that the world would not be finite.
3
Follow the logical chain
The passage then explains why: if multiple gods were possible, infinite gods could be assumed, each producing at least one thing. The cumulative result would make the world infinite. This chain is the core of the proof.
This step shows how multiplicity leads to infinitude.
4
Connect to observed reality
Since we observe that the world is finite, the initial assumption of multiple gods must be false. Therefore, God is one. This is the conclusion that Maturidi implies through the conditional reasoning.
The argument does not explicitly state 'God is one' but it is the logical result.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof for the oneness of God (tawḥīd) based on the finitude of the world. It is a type of argument from impossibility of infinite regress or multiplicity. Maturidi begins with a conditional: if God were not one (i.e., if there were multiple gods), then the world would not be finite. The reasoning is that if multiple gods were possible, we could posit an infinite number of gods. Each of these gods would bring into existence at least one thing. Consequently, the totality of the world would lack finitude, because each god adds an originated being. Since the world is observed to be finite, the premise of multiple gods is false, establishing the oneness of the Creator.

First Proof of Tawhid: Preventing Messengers' Miracles
Maturidi presents a proof of tawhid based on the logical impossibility of a partner preventing messengers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the impossibility of a partner preventing messengers proves the oneness of Allah.

Core Idea
If there were another god, he would stop the prophets — but he didn't.

This lesson examines a concise but powerful argument from Imam Maturidi for tawhid. The proof is based on a logical deduction: if there were another god sharing dominion, he would necessarily prevent the messengers from performing miracles to protect his own claim. Since we know the messengers were not prevented, it proves there is no other god.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic text and its translation. Notice the conditional structure: 'if...then...'
2
Identify the premises
The argument has two premises: (1) If there were a partner, he would prevent miracles. (2) Miracles were not prevented. Therefore, no partner.
3
Reflect on the logic
Consider why the partner would prevent miracles: because allowing them would nullify his divinity. This shows the partner's self-interest.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.

For the oneness of God; for if God had a partner in His kingdom, He would have prevented the messengers from manifesting their miracles because that would nullify His partnership and divinity.

Since we observe that messengers were indeed sent with miracles and were not prevented, it proves that there is no partner. The passage is concise but contains a logical argument based on divine jealousy and protection of sovereignty.

Twofold States of Human Actions
A proof of the twofold nature of human actions

Core Idea
Human actions have a divine side and a human side.

Maturidi provides a concise proof for the Middle Path between fatalism and free will. He points to the two types of states in our actions: those we cannot produce (existence itself) and those we can (choice within commands). This distinction establishes that actions are both from God and from us.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation

The passage uses 'first' and 'second' to contrast the two states.
2
Identify the two states
Identify the two kinds of states (ahwal) Maturidi describes in human actions. What is the first state? What is the second?
The first involves existence from nothing; the second involves intention and command.
3
Understand the conclusion
See how the two states lead to the conclusion that from one aspect actions are not ours, from another they are ours. This is Maturidi's balanced view.
The conclusion is stated explicitly in the passage.

The Necessity of Prophethood from Human Conflict
Maturidi argues for a unifying authority from human conflicting natures and the world's preservation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Human conflict demands a sent guide; the world's preservation proves it.

Maturidi presents a powerful argument from human nature. He observes that the world is full of conflict because people have opposing desires. If left to themselves, they would destroy each other. But the world was created for preservation, so there must be an authority to unite them. This leads to the necessity of prophethood and imamate.

The Arabic phrase 'إنشاء العالم للبقاء لا للفناء' is key.

Humans have different natures and desires. If left to their natural inclinations, they would compete for benefits and status, leading to hatred and fighting.
Maturidi describes humans as having 'أهواء مختلفة وطبائع متشتتة'.
3
Recognize the need for a unifier
Because conflict leads to destruction, there must be an 'origin' (أصل) that unites them and restrains conflict. This is the core need.
The phrase 'لابد من أصل يؤلف بينهم' is central.
4
Identify the director and proof
God provides a director (مدير) who knows their conditions and appoints a guide with a clear proof (برهان) of his imamate. This guide is the refuge for humanity.
The argument culminates in the necessity of a sent prophet or imam.

Maturidi argues from the combination of contrary natures in perceptible things to their createdness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that the combination of contrary natures in perceptible things proves their createdness and dependence on a Creator.

Core Idea
Opposites that naturally repel cannot unite by themselves; their union proves a Creator.

Maturidi presents a concise yet profound proof for the origination of the world based on a simple observation: everything we perceive is made up of elements that by nature are opposed to each other. Why do they stay together? The answer points directly to a cause beyond themselves.

Argument from Parts and Growth
Maturidi argues from parts and growth to the whole's origination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's proof that the temporal origination of the world's parts and their growth implies the origination of the whole world.

Core Idea
If parts are born and grow, the whole cannot be eternal.

This passage presents a key argument from early kalam: the argument from composition and growth. Maturidi observes that we see parts of the world coming into existence and growing. Since the whole is nothing but the combination of these parts, the whole must also have a beginning. This argument connects observable change to the createdness of the entire universe.

The Proof from Change and Diversity
Maturidi enumerates opposites, argues from change to annihilation, refutes an objection, and concludes with divine dependence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that observable diversity and change in the world prove its temporal origination and contingency, and thus the existence of a necessary Creator.

Core Idea
Change and opposites show the world is not eternal; only the Eternal is unchanging.

Maturidi invites us to look at the world around us: good and bad, light and darkness, small and large. These opposites are not fixed; they change and pass away. This simple observation, he argues, reveals that the world cannot be eternal. In this lesson, we will walk through his proof step by step, seeing how change leads to annihilation, and how annihilation points to a beginning and a Creator.

Guided Path
1
Notice the opposites
Read the first sentence where Maturidi lists pairs of opposites. These are observable facts that everyone can see. Why do you think he starts with these examples?
The pairs cover moral, physical, and aesthetic dimensions.
2
Understand the implication of change
Maturidi says these pairs are signs of change and passing away. Change leads to annihilation and destruction. Reflect on why change necessarily implies a potential for non-existence.
If something changes, it is not immutable; what is mutable can cease to be.
3
The combination and dispersion argument
He gives an example: combination strengthens, dispersion weakens. When a thing disperses, it becomes void. This is a sign of annihilation. Think about how this supports the idea that the world is not self-subsistent.
This is reminiscent of the argument from composition.
4
Refute the objection
Maturidi addresses an objection: someone might claim that God is hidden from sight, implying something about eternity. He dismisses this, saying that the world is known by sight, not by such claims. See how this refutation strengthens his proof.
The objection is a straw man; Maturidi cuts it down quickly.
5
Conclude with the source of strength
The final sentence: 'There is no difference between a life that perishes and its essence. And that has not ceased; its strength is only with Allah.' This ties everything back to Allah as the only eternal and self-sufficient being.
The proof ends by affirming tawhid.

Proof from Alternation of Motion and Rest
Maturidi uses motion-rest alternation to prove temporality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the alternation between motion and rest in a body proves its temporal origination.

Core Idea
Opposites can't be eternal together, so the body that alternates must be created.

Maturidi begins a logical proof from the observable fact that every physical body is never free from either motion or rest. Since these two states are opposites that cannot coexist simultaneously, the body must experience both over time. From this alternation, he deduces that the body is temporal and not eternal.

This is a short but dense argument.

This shows that the body cannot be eternal.

Refuting Hidden Eternity and Proving Temporality from Motion and Rest
Maturidi refutes hidden eternity and gives motion-rest proof.

Core Idea
What alternates between opposites cannot be eternal.

Maturidi begins by countering an objection: some argue that the world's eternity is not perceptible, so it cannot be disproved. He responds that the world is known both by sight and by proofs. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Proof from Interdependence of Substance and Accident
Maturidi argues that substances and accidents depend on each other, so the world cannot be self-sufficient.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's proof that substances and accidents depend on each other, and why this implies the world cannot be self-sufficient.

Core Idea
Substances and accidents rely on each other, so the world cannot be independent.

Imam Maturidi presents a concise but profound argument for the existence of God based on the interconnection of substances and accidents. By examining the relationship between physical entities (a'yan) and their transient properties (a'rad), he shows that neither can exist without the other. This mutual dependence points to a creator who brings the whole into being.

3

Commentary
This proof focuses on the relationship between substances (a'yan) and accidents (a'rad). Every individual substance is subject to accidents that happen to it, and those accidents have no existence apart from the substance. Thus, each depends on the other. Since they mutually need each other, the whole cannot be self-sufficient. Therefore, the world must have been brought into existence by another (a creator).

Proof from Opposing Natures
Maturidi argues that opposing natures require an external unifier.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the coexistence of opposing natures in a single entity proves the existence of an external unifier, which is Allah.

Core Idea
Opposing natures cannot unite by themselves; they need a unifier.

Imam Maturidi presents a simple yet profound argument: anything that combines inherently repelling elements cannot bring itself together. This points to a power that overcomes their natural opposition. Let's explore this proof step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation for the first proof. Notice the key terms: opposition natures and unifier.
2
Identify the key claim
The passage says things with opposing natures cannot come together by themselves. Why? Because their natures are repellant.
3
Draw the conclusion
Since they can't unite on their own, they must have a unifier. This unifier is Allah.

Commentary
This lesson contains two proofs. The first proof: every entity that has opposing natures (like hot and cold, wet and dry) cannot bring itself together because these natures naturally repel each other. Therefore, such an entity must have a unifier (jam'i') from outside. The second proof: every living being depends on external nourishment and other things for its survival. The being's own knowledge is insufficient to know what sustains it or how to procure it. Therefore, its existence must be due to an All-Knowing, Wise Creator, not itself.

Proof from Uniformity: If Self-Existent, Would Remain Unchanged
Conditional argument for dependence from lack of uniformity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that if the world were self-existent, it would remain uniform, and since it changes, it must depend on a creator.

Core Idea
Change proves dependence; uniformity would be required if self-existent.

In this passage, Imam Maturidi presents a logical proof based on observable change. He argues that if the world were self-sufficient (existing by itself), it would remain perpetually in a single state. Since we witness constant change and variation, the world cannot be self-existent — it must be brought into being by another.

Guided Path
1
Read the conditional premise
The argument starts: if the world were self-existent, it would remain unchanged and uniform. Understand why a self-existent thing would not change.
2
Observe the conclusion from reality
The world is not uniform but changes. Therefore, it cannot be self-existent; it must be by another (a creator).
This is a modus tollens argument.
3
Reflect on the implication
This proof shows that change is not an argument against God, but rather evidence for His existence as the cause of change.

Proof from Impossibility of Self-Existence Before or After Existence
Reductio ad absurdum argument against self-existence via dichotomy and analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that self-existence is logically impossible because it would require either coming into being after non-existence (contradicting self-existence) or bringing itself into existence from non-existence (impossible for a non-existent agent).

Core Idea
Self-existence is impossible: can't come after non-existence, and can't precede existence.

In this passage, Maturidi provides a second proof against self-existence, using a logical dichotomy. He argues that if something were self-existent, its existence must either be after non-existence or before existence. Both options lead to absurdity. The proof is then reinforced by an analogy from observable crafts.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dichotomy
If X exists by itself, then its being by itself must occur either after it already exists (which would mean its existence was by another) or before it exists (which would require it to create itself when non-existent, impossible).
2
Analyze each branch
After existence: invalid because then it would have been existing by another. Before existence: impossible because a non-existent cannot act.
The term 'before existence' refers to logical priority, not temporal.
3
Connect to the analogy
Just as buildings, writing, and ships require an existing maker, the world requires an existing creator.
This strengthens the argument by analogy.

The Wisdom in Creation as Proof of a Wise Creator
Maturidi reflects on the wisdom in creation as a sign of the Creator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the profound wisdom embedded in every aspect of creation, which even the greatest sages cannot fully comprehend, serves as a clear indication of a wise Originator and Creator.

Core Idea
Creation's unfathomable wisdom points directly to a wise Maker.

Maturidi begins this passage by drawing attention to the astonishing wisdom present in every part of creation. He emphasizes that even the most learned sages are incapable of fully grasping how things come to be as they are. This very limitation becomes a powerful sign that the universe is not self-made but originates from a transcendent, wise Creator.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on wisdom
Read the Arabic passage carefully and then its translation. Notice how Maturidi frames the discussion: he starts with the root principle that everything in creation contains amazing wisdom.
This is a reflective passage, not a logical proof—it appeals to the reader's sense of wonder.
2
Identify the main claim
Underline the phrase 'حكمة عجيبة ودلالة بديعة'. Maturidi claims that the wisdom in creation is so remarkable that even the wisest humans cannot fully understand it. This is not a flaw but a sign of divine origin.
Pay special attention to the word 'مائيته' (quiddity) – it refers to the essential nature of things.
3
Reflect on the implication
Consider why human inability to fully grasp the wisdom actually strengthens the case for a Creator. If a human could fully explain everything, that would imply it is finite; but the depth of wisdom points to an infinite intelligence.
This is a subtle but crucial point: limitation of human knowledge becomes evidence for transcendence.

Commentary

Since it does not perish instantly but undergoes gradual changes through external causes (like death, gathering, growth, and purification), it must be dependent on an external agent. He draws an analogy: a garment's color cannot change without dye, and a ship cannot become something else by itself. Therefore, the world needs a creator.

The Proof from Mutual Hindrance (Tamānuʿ)
Maturidi refutes polytheism by logical deduction from mutual hindrance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical proof of mutual hindrance (tamānuʿ) that demonstrates the impossibility of multiple gods.

Core Idea
Multiple gods inevitably lead to contradiction or impotence, so only One God can exist.

Imam Maturidi presents a rational argument drawn from the Quran to prove that God must be one. The proof of mutual hindrance (tamānuʿ) examines what would happen if two or more gods existed, showing that every possibility leads to an absurdity. This lesson walks you through the argument's logical steps and its Quranic basis.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Quranic foundation
Read the two Quranic verses Maturidi cites. They imply that if multiple gods existed, each would claim exclusive creation, leading to conflict.
These verses are the springboard for the rational argument.
2
Grasp the logical trilemma
Maturidi outlines three possibilities for two gods: both have equal power, both are powerless, or one is powerful and the other weak. Each leads to an absurdity.
This trilemma exhausts all logical scenarios.
3
Explore the implications of each scenario
If both are powerful, they would thwart each other, causing contradiction. If both are powerless, they cannot be gods. If one is powerful, the other is a subject, not a god.
4
See how the argument eliminates polytheism
The only coherent conclusion is that there is one God who possesses all power and knowledge of the unseen.
This proof is a cornerstone of Islamic theology.
