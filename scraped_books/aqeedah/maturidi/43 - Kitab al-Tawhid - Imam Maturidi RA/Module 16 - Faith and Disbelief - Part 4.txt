Faith is Not Forced: Refutation of Coercion
Maturidi uses Quranic verses to prove that faith cannot be coerced, thus it is in the heart.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that faith is an internal conviction of the heart, not merely verbal confession, because it cannot be coerced.

Core Idea
Faith cannot be forced; it is a voluntary act of the heart.

Imam Maturidi grounds his argument on the Quranic prohibition of compulsion in religion. He argues that since faith is addressed to the intellect and requires thought and reflection—acts of the heart—it cannot be reduced to tongue speech, which can be coerced.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get a feel for the argument. Then read the literal translation to understand each part.
The passage uses two Quranic verses as evidence.
2
Identify the logical premises
Maturidi builds his case in steps: faith is known by intellect -> intellect uses thought/reflection -> those are heart actions -> tongue can be coerced -> Quran forbids coercion -> therefore faith is not in the tongue.
Track how each premise leads to the conclusion.
3
Analyze the Quranic verses
Look at how each verse is used: 'No compulsion in religion' negates coercion, so faith cannot be in something subject to force. 'Whoever disbelieves in Taghut...' shows that disbelief is not merely verbal, so faith is similarly not verbal.
Both verses point to the internal nature of belief.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider why this matters: faith must be voluntary and personal. Coercion produces hypocrisy, not genuine belief.
This connects to broader themes of free will and accountability.

He then cites the verse about disbelieving in Taghut and believing in Allah, arguing that disbelief in Taghut is not merely verbal, so similarly faith is not merely verbal.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Quranic address proves faith is in the heart, not just on the tongue.

In this brief but powerful passage, Imam Maturidi draws a simple logical inference from a recurring Quranic phrase. By examining how Allah addresses believers, he demonstrates that faith must already reside in their hearts even when they are not actively speaking. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Faith is a Specific Act, Not All Acts of Obedience
Maturidi proves that faith is a specific act, not the aggregate of all good deeds.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi argues that faith is a specific act (heart's conviction) and not the sum of all acts of obedience, and how he uses Quranic rebukes to prove this.

Core Idea
Faith is a well-defined act, not the entire collection of good deeds.

Maturidi presents a nuanced argument distinguishing faith from general acts of obedience. He examines how the Quran addresses believers who fall short in obligations, showing that calling them 'believers' while they omit duties would be contradictory if faith meant all acts. This lesson unpacks his logical proof that faith is a specific, limited act — the conviction of the heart.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Begin by reading the Arabic passage once, then read the literal translation. Let the structure of Maturidi's argument sink in before analyzing it.
Pay attention to the Quranic reference style: the author uses logic and Quranic address patterns.
2
Identify the key argument
Find Maturidi's central claim: faith is a name for a specific act, not all acts. Notice how he uses the Quran addressing believers despite their shortcomings to refute the alternative view.
The argument hinges on the idea that if faith meant all acts, then omitting an act would break the name 'believer'.
3
Reflect on implications
Think about why this matters for defining faith. This distinction shapes how we understand the relationship between faith and actions in Islamic theology.
Maturidi's conclusion reinforces that faith is primarily inner conviction, not a synonym for all good deeds.

Commentary

Also, the rebuke is not directed at them as 'the pious' or 'the righteous', but as 'believers'. Therefore, faith is a specific act (i.e., inner conviction), not the whole set of acts. The Meaning of Irja' and the Hashwiyya's Suspension of Judgment
Maturidi explains the second interpretation and defines irja'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second interpretation of the hadith as suspending judgment on one's own faith, and the meaning of irja' as suspension and respite.

Core Idea
Irja' means suspending judgment, not only in politics but also in personal faith.

Maturidi examines a second interpretation of the hadith that condemns the Murji'ah. Here, the focus shifts from postponing judgment on others to suspending judgment on one's own status as a believer. The Hashwiyya, by using exceptions like 'if Allah wills,' exemplify this type of irja'.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that faith must be created because it is known, and how this refutes the Hashwiyya.

Core Idea
Faith is known, therefore it is created.

Maturidi engages with a group called al-Hashwiyya who deny the creation of faith. He constructs a logical argument: faith is either known or unknown. If unknown, it cannot be known; but since faith is known through revelation and proof, it must be created. This lesson unpacks that reasoning.

Rational Proofs for the Createdness of Iman
Maturidi presents rational arguments and a dialectical question to prove iman's createdness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Maturidi uses rational inference and dialectical questioning to prove that iman is created.

Core Idea
Reason reveals that iman bears the same marks of creation as all other things.

In this passage, Maturidi extends his argument by appealing to reason's ability to recognize the traces of craftsmanship in iman. He argues that since iman is a temporal occurrence in the servant, it must be created. He then challenges the denier to identify the true nature of iman, forcing them to admit it falls into a created category.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify rational arguments

This is a direct application of the philosophical definition of creation.

Maturidi asks the denier about the true reality of iman, listing options like tasdiq, iqrar, etc. Whichever they choose, it is a created thing.
This forces the opponent to acknowledge iman's createdness through their own admission.

Nullification of Command and Prohibition
Reductio argument against denying command to the disbeliever.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's reductio argument that denying the command to the disbeliever at the moment of disbelief leads to the nullification of all command and prohibition.

Core Idea
If the disbeliever isn't commanded now, he's never commanded — and command becomes meaningless.

This passage presents a powerful reductio ad absurdum against a claim that the disbeliever is not commanded at the moment of disbelief. Imam Maturidi shows that such a position logically destroys the very institution of divine command and prohibition. The argument is central to the Maturidi insistence on universal moral accountability and the ongoing obligation to believe.

Guided Path
1
Read the opponent's claim
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and translation. Notice the opponent's claim that the disbeliever is commanded to faith based on a power that existed before his disbelief.
The opponent seems to separate power from command temporally.
2
Identify Maturidi's first response
Maturidi points out that all Muslims agree the disbeliever is capable (qawī) of his disbelief at the moment. So the same logic applied to command should apply to power — they are one in meaning.
This exposes inconsistency in the opponent's reasoning.
3
Follow the reductio
Maturidi then shows that if the disbeliever is not commanded or prohibited at his current moment, then he is also not commanded in any future moment, ad infinitum.
This leads to the complete nullification of command and prohibition.
4
Understand the conclusion
The argument concludes that the opponent's view renders command and prohibition void, because the command would forever apply to a time when the person is not compliant — an impossibility.
Thus the disbeliever must be commanded at every moment of his disbelief.

Commentary
This passage opens a new question (mas'ala) about the status of those who commit sins and whether they thereby exit faith. Maturidi reports that some scholars group all sins together and use verses like 'Whoever disobeys God and His Messenger' (Q 4:14) and 'It is not for a believing man or a believing woman' (Q 33:36) to argue that any sin expels from faith, since all sins equally constitute disobedience and misguidance, and thus lead to eternal punishment. The first way is that repentance (tawba) expiates sin, citing verses like 'he will remain disgraced except those who repent' (Q 25:69) and 'Repent to God with sincere repentance' (Q 66:8). The second way is that minor sins (al-sagha'ir) committed inadvertently or forgetfully are forgiven, citing verses about unintentional oaths (Q 2:225) and mistakes (Q 33:5). The passage then shifts to a different argument: some give the label of 'disbelief' (kufr) to sin in two ways. These verses show that recompense is for small things, but only the ungrateful are recompensed. Also, those who harm God and His Messenger (Q 33:57) are cursed, and every disobedient person harms the Messenger. The second way is that the very covenant (عقد) of faith obligates a believer not to disobey, so if someone disobeys, his faith was conditional upon fulfilling the covenant, and failure means he did not uphold it. The passage ends abruptly with 'he said: he did not fulfill it.'
