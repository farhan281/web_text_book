Why Creation Is Not a Separate Act: Action as Essence
Maturidi argues that Allah's action is identical to His essence, making questions about the reason for creation invalid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that Allah's action of creation is identical to His essence, making the question of 'why creation' as meaningless as asking why Allah is Lord.

Core Idea
Allah's action is His essence; asking why He created is like asking why He is generous.

This lesson explores a profound Maturidi reply to the question of why Allah created. Instead of giving a purpose-driven answer, Imam al-Maturidi redirects the inquiry by asserting that the action of creation is not separate from Allah's essence. Understanding this principle resolves many theological puzzles about divine motives.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its English translation carefully. Notice the flow: Maturidi gives a possible answer, then declares the basis of the question corrupt.
The passage ends with a traditional formula.
2
Recognize the analogy: action = essence
Maturidi says we consider the action to be the essence, and Allah is described by it in pre-eternity. This means the act of creation is not a separate event but an essential attribute.
This is a key Maturidi doctrine.
3
Reflect on the implication
If action is essence, then asking 'why did He create?' is like asking 'why is He Lord?' Both are essential and uncaused. The question is thus invalid.
This protects divine transcendence.

In Maturidi's theological framework, the attributes (including action) are not separate from the essence; the action of creation is the same as the essence in the sense that it is an eternal attribute. Thus the question is as invalid as asking why Allah is Lord or Knowing.

Maturidi's Response to Objection about Unintelligible Commands
Maturidi answers an objection about God commanding what cannot be understood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi refutes the objection that God could address the servant with utterly unintelligible commands.

Core Idea
God always provides a way to understand every command, whether through intellect or revelation.

This passage presents a theological objection and Maturidi's careful response. The objection attempts to draw a parallelism between intellectual and revealed commands. Maturidi explains that while at first glance there seems to be no difference, in reality God always ensures understanding is possible. The answer highlights the role of intellect and revelation in grasping divine commands.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the first sentence of the Arabic passage which contains the objection. Note the structure: 'If it is said...' (فإن قيل). Then read the translation.
The objection tries to create a dilemma: if God can command through intellect without understanding, why not through revelation?
2
Identify Maturidi's key moves in the answer
Read the answer starting from 'قيل' (It is said). Notice how Maturidi first acknowledges the apparent similarity ('no difference in the first glance') but then rejects the blanket statement. Identify the two ways commands are given: through intellect (بعث العقل) or revelation (خطاب السمع).
Maturidi emphasizes that in every case, God provides a way for understanding.
3
Reflect on the broader principle
Consider the principle: understanding is always possible for those capable. If someone's understanding falls short, they are exempt from the command. Also note the reference to 'aspects of principles' (جهات الأصول) and how reflection (النظر والفكر) determines the type.
This passage guards against the misconception that God might hold people accountable for unintelligible commands.

The answer distinguishes between initial appearance and reality. Maturidi replies that at first glance there is no difference, but one cannot make a blanket statement. In fact, whenever God commands something—whether through intellectual prompting (بعث العقل) or through revealed address (خطاب السمع)—He always provides a way to understand it. If someone's understanding is too limited to bear the command, that person is exempt from the command. However, the various sources of principles (جهات الأصول) differ, and one must use reflection and thought to determine which type a given command belongs to. The passage ends with the formula of reliance on Allah.

Allah Creates All Actions, Good and Evil
Maturidi affirms Allah creates all actions and distinguishes creation from created act.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi affirms that Allah creates both good and evil actions while remaining transcendent and not described by evil.

Core Idea
Allah creates all actions, but His act of creation is not tainted by the act's good or evil.

This passage from Imam al-Maturidi addresses a delicate question: if Allah creates everything, does He create evil? And if so, does that make Him evil? The answer lies in a precise distinction between the act of creation and the created act. Maturidi uses this distinction to uphold divine transcendence while affirming Allah's sole lordship.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and English translation. Notice the key terms: خالق (Creator), جوهر (substance), خلق (creation), فعل (act).
The passage is dense but precise.
2
Identify the two main affirmations
First, Allah creates all actions, both good and evil. Second, there is a necessary distinction between creation and the created act.
These are the pillars of Maturidi's position.
3
Understand the refutation of partners
Maturidi argues that if anything existed without Allah's creation, that would imply a partner or equal in His dominion. This refutes dualism and Mu'tazila.
This is a logical argument from divine sovereignty.
4
Grasp the theological nuance
Allah's act of creation is not the same as the creature's act, and His action is not described as good or evil. Only the creature's act carries moral description.
This preserves Allah's transcendence.
5
Reflect on the implication
Allah is not called 'good' or 'evil' based on the actions He creates; rather, the doer is named by the deed.
This maintains divine justice and sovereignty.

Commentary
This passage is a statement of the Maturidi creed regarding the creation of actions. The author affirms that Allah is the Creator of the substance (jawhar) of both evil and good, and He creates the actions of creatures – whether evil or good. This is a direct refutation of the dualists and a correction of the Mu'tazila. Then he asserts that nothing exists in Allah's dominion that He did not create, otherwise there would be a partner with Him. He then makes a crucial distinction: the creation (khalq) of creation is not identical to the created thing (makhluk); similarly, Allah's act (fi'l) is not the same as the act of the creature. Consequently, Allah's own act (i.e., His creating) is not described as good or evil, because He is described by His act (as Creator, etc.), not by the moral qualities of the created actions. Therefore, we do not say that Allah is 'good' or 'evil' in the sense of being characterized by the moral value of the actions He creates; rather, a creature whose deed is actually evil is called evil. The passage underscores divine transcendence and the rejection of anthropomorphism, while affirming that Allah is the sole creator of all actions.

The Wisdom in Creating Harmful Things
Maturidi explains the divine wisdom in creating harmful substances and sensory defects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that God's creation of harmful and ugly things serves an epistemological purpose: enabling humans to analogize sensory experiences to understand the unseen.

Core Idea
Sensory contrasts are the ladder to knowledge of the unseen.

In this passage, Imam Maturidi addresses a common theological question: why does God create things that cause harm or appear ugly? Rather than being arbitrary or punitive, Maturidi explains that these phenomena are essential for human cognition. By experiencing opposites like pleasure and pain, beauty and ugliness, we develop the conceptual tools needed to reason about realities beyond our senses. Let's explore this profound argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Let the author's rhythm and structure come through before interpreting.

2

Look for the phrase 'li-yumaththilū' (so that they may analogize). This is the core purpose: to enable analogical reasoning from the visible to the invisible.
3

Maturidi argues: Without sensory contrasts (harmful vs. beneficial, ugly vs. beautiful), humans would not know moral and practical distinctions. Therefore, God created them to facilitate reasoning about the unseen.
This is a 'if not... then...' argument.
4
Understand key term: tamthīl
Tamthīl (تمثيل) means drawing an analogy or comparison. Here it means using sensory experiences as a model for understanding what lies beyond the senses.
This word is central to the Maturidi epistemology.
5
Reflect on the broader implication
This argument shows that the existence of evil and ugliness is not a flaw in creation but a necessary feature for human knowledge and spiritual development.
Contrast this with views that see such things as pointless or punitive.

Core Idea
If one part can be a son, then all parts can be sons — absurd.

Maturidi begins his refutation by questioning the very premise that a 'part' of God could become a son. He shows that if we accept that, we must also accept that every smaller part of the universe is a son of the larger, which leads to an infinite regress of sons. This lesson examines that initial logical move.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of dualist and Mu'tazila views on divine action. Maturidi begins by describing a view (likely of the Mu'tazila) that God's action of repelling harm from others is made the cause (ʿilla) of His action, so that His action is not futile. He notes that in this respect they agreed with the Dualists (al-thanawiyya), who refused to consider an action wise unless it brings benefit to the agent. The Dualists instead attributed action to an essence of wisdom (ja whar al-ḥikma) arising from mixture (al-mizāj), so that the agent's action becomes wise only after being saved from folly. They also held that something coming from nothing is impossible in the visible world, so the world must have an originating cause. The People of Tawhid (ahl al-tawḥīd) opposed both the Dualists' requirement of benefit for the agent and their claim that the world must have an origin. Then within the Mu'tazila, differences emerged: some held that every action must have a cause without which it would be futile; others argued that a harmful action without benefit is folly. Finally, the Mu'tazila split on whether harm to the recipient is real or merely apparent: some denied real harm, others affirmed real harm but insisted on compensation to make the action wise, analogous to medical procedures in the visible world. The passage concludes that God cannot do a harmful action without compensation (ʿiwaḍ). This sets up Maturidi's own position that God's actions are wise without needing benefit or compensation.

Maturidi then notes that a single action can have two aspects (just and unjust, wise and foolish) depending on the context, and this is due to ignorance (of the observer). It is possible that the true aspect is hidden from the observer, or he tries to know it through sense perception but cannot. Since both aspects are possible, sense perception and the observer's knowledge cannot decisively attribute wisdom or foolishness, justice or injustice to an action. Therefore, any human judgment in his own law (shari'atihi) based on indicating the action as wise or foolish is invalid. Consequently, humans cannot know the true nature of the two matters (wisdom vs foolishness, justice vs injustice) in a thing through mere reflection, because they lack knowledge of all the causes that change the states of sensed things.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why divine justice is essential for trust in God's promises and threats, and how Maturidi refutes the Mu'tazili position that God could potentially act unjustly.

Core Idea
Justice is not merely possible but necessary for God to be trustworthy.

Maturidi begins by showing the dangerous consequences of allowing even the possibility of injustice in God. Guided Path

Read the opening conditional

Maturidi states the premise: if Allah were NOT the creator of the actions of creatures, then most of the proofs He manifested through His messengers, and the overall planning of His world from beginning to end, would be invalid and corrupt. The reason is that they would depend on the assistance of creation for what He planned (e.g., permanence, bringing into existence progeny). All these events are realized through the actions of creatures; if those actions are not created by Allah, then He would need the help of His creatures to accomplish His plans. But needing assistance for a proof or plan implies that He is not truly wise or powerful—rather, He would be ignorant and incapable. Since this is impossible for Allah (being ignorant and incapable), the original premise is false: Allah must create all actions. The conclusion: it is established that all these events appeared through His creating them at the hands of whomever He willed, however He willed, as He willed. Thus, the argument preserves divine wisdom and power.

Commentary
This passage provides a classification of opinions regarding the doctrine of God's creation of human actions (khalaq al-af'al) and then refutes the first position. The author states that the affirmation of God's creating actions can be denied for three reasons: (أ) because it is impossible (iḥāla), (ب) because there is no evidence for it, or (ج) because it would entail compulsion (ḍarūra) and negate possibility, thus making command, prohibition, promise, and threat unreasonable. The author then addresses the first category: those who deny due to impossibility. He argues that such a denier is required to provide proof, and the only possible proof would be based on assuming that one action cannot really be from two agents, or that the doctrine implies partnership (shirka) with God. The answer is that God's action and the servant's action are different in reality: the servant's action is God's creation (maf'ul) not the servant's own act. The author provides analogies from common experience: two people pulling a rope that breaks—their pulling is a single action in terms of the effect (the break), and they become partners in the effect. Similarly, two people removing an object or carrying an indivisible particle—their combined action results in a single effect. Thus, it is possible for God to create the action while the servant acquires it, without implying partnership or impossibility. The conclusion is that the analogy supports the Maturidi position.
