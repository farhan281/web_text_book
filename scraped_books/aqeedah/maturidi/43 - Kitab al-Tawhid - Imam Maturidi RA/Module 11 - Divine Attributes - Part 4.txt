The Infinite Regress Argument for an Essential Name
Maturidi shows that deriving God's name from His act leads to infinite regress, so the act must be by essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses the argument of infinite regress to prove that God must have an essential name.

Core Idea
If God's name comes from His act, that act itself would require another act, leading to infinite regress.

Having exposed the initial contradiction, Maturidi now takes the opponents' position to its logical conclusion. If they argue that God's name comes from His act of origination (ibda), then that act itself must be explained. The teacher shows this leads to an infinite chain of origination, which is impossible. Therefore, origination must be by God's essence, establishing that He has an essential name.

Guided Path
1
Read the second part of the passage
Focus on the argument starting from 'If he says: The origination was from Him'.
Notice the logical flow: supposition → objection → contradiction → conclusion.
2
Understand the infinite regress argument
If the name depends on a temporal act of origination, then to justify that act, another origination would be needed, and so on ad infinitum. That is impossible.
Infinite regress is a classical argument against derived names.
3
See why origination by essence is necessary
To avoid regress, the origination must be by God's essence, meaning He is eternally originating (lam yazal mubdi'an).
This makes the name essential, not derived.
4
Conclude the necessity of an essential name
The passage ends with the affirmation that the essential name is necessarily required.
Note the phrase 'bi-d-darura' (necessarily).

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of those who claim that God has no essential name (ism dhati) or that His name is derived from something other than His essence. The Invalidity of the Question: Why Did God Create?
Maturidi presents the first group's argument that the question 'why' is invalid based on God's attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a group of scholars regarded the question 'Why did God create creation?' as invalid, based on God's attributes of wisdom, knowledge, and self-sufficiency.

Core Idea
God's actions are inherently wise; the 'why' question is invalid for the Creator.

Imam Maturidi begins by presenting a group who argued that the question 'Why did God create creation?' is itself invalid. Their reasoning draws on God's eternal attributes: He is ever-wise, all-knowing, and utterly self-sufficient. Because these attributes are essential to Him, His actions cannot deviate from wisdom. This lesson explores that argument and the Quranic support for it.

Guided Path
1
Read the first group's argument
Read the Arabic passage and its translation for the first answer. Note how Maturidi introduces the group and their core claim that the question is invalid.
Pay attention to the attributes used: hakim, 'alim, ghani.
2
Analyze the reasoning
Identify the logical steps: God's wisdom prevents deviation; deviation arises only from ignorance or need; God is neither ignorant nor needy; therefore His action cannot lack wisdom.
The argument is a syllogism based on God's attributes.
3
Examine the Quranic proof
Look at the verse cited: 'We did not create the heaven and the earth as players... He is not questioned about what He does.' Understand how this verse supports the claim.
The verse negates play (lahw) and establishes God's unquestionable authority.
4
Reflect on implications
Consider why the question 'why' is problematic: it implies a purpose external to God, which contradicts His self-sufficiency and eternal wisdom.
This connects to the larger theme of God's transcendence.

Commentary

Thus, the correct stance is that God's actions are inherently wise and not subject to human interrogation.

The Mu'tazilite 'most beneficial' parallels wisdom; Maturidi questions its distinctness.

Negation of God's Name and the Avoidance of Anthropomorphism
Imam Maturidi argues that negating God's name avoids tashbih, supported by ibdaʿ and accidents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the negation of God's name from all created things serves to avoid anthropomorphism (tashbih), and why the nature of God's creative act (ibdaʿ) and accidents supports this theological safeguard.

Core Idea
Denying God a name is not a denial of attributes but a shield against anthropomorphism.

In this passage, Imam Maturidi continues his discussion on God's name and the danger of tashbih. He shows that even those who affirm a name for God agree with those who deny it on one crucial point: the name is negated from all created things. He then deepens the argument by examining God's act of creation (ibdaʿ) and the limitations of accidents, reinforcing that attributes like knowledge and power cannot be ascribed to creation in the same way they apply to God.

Commentary
This passage contains the Dualist objection and the author's response. The Dualists argue that in the witnessed world, the admixture of darkness in light causes misperception of things, so observed contradictions do not reflect the true nature of the principles. The author turns the argument back: if the Dualist's own perception is clouded by defects, then his claim that the author's statements are not wisdom could also be due to that admixture. Then the author proceeds to establish the attributes of God: He is powerful by essence, self-sufficient, all-knowing, and perfectly wise. Therefore, there cannot be any disparity or contradiction in His creation. If our intellects fail to perceive the wisdom in some things, it is because they are limited. The author compares the intellect to the senses: each sense has a domain, and may be limited in grasping certain objects. Similarly, the intellect may be prevented from understanding the true wisdom behind things. Then he argues that a human, who is needy and poor, whose judgment is influenced by custom and desire, cannot judge the wisdom of the Lord. Moreover, humans act through limbs and instruments, so they cannot claim incapacity or ignorance over God, who is Powerful and Knowing by essence. This refutes the Dualist claim that observed evil indicates a separate principle.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
God's attributes guarantee wisdom; our failure to see it is our limitation.

Maturidi begins his refutation by grounding the discussion in God's essence. Since Allah is powerful, rich, knowing, and wise by His own essence, nothing in His creation can be without purpose or wisdom. Even when we cannot perceive the wisdom, we must affirm it exists.

Disparity would imply a deficiency in the creator.

This is a key principle: our inability to see wisdom does not negate its existence.

Maturidi responds to an objection about the first creation using the argument from time and infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Maturidi refutes an objection concerning the timing of the first creation by appealing to the impossibility of infinite regress.

Core Idea
No time before the first creation, so the question 'why not earlier?' is meaningless.

The objection suggests that if the first creation had no benefit for anyone, then God could have created something else before it. Maturidi shows that this question relies on the assumption of a specific time, but there is no time prior to the first creation. He then demonstrates that the question leads to an infinite regress, which is impossible. This lesson explores his reasoning.

Refuting the Claim of Eternal Created Things

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi refutes the claim that God must eternally create things, and the difference between the thing's eternity and God's eternal act of creation.

Core Idea
God is eternally a creator, but the world is not eternal.

This passage addresses a common objection: if God is all-powerful, why didn't He create everything from eternity? Maturidi unpacks the ambiguity in the statement 'God everlastingly creates things' and shows that one interpretation leads to contradiction, while the other affirms both God's eternal attribute and the temporal nature of creation. Identify the two interpretations of 'everlastingly creating things': (1) that the things themselves are eternal, or (2) that the act of creation is eternal.

Understand why the first meaning (things eternal) is impossible: it would make the created co-eternal with God, contradicting their temporal origination.

And similar is the situation of the Qadariyya when they are asked about the creation of actions; they revert in the answer to the act of disbelief and sins. That is corrupt, because the way of this is scriptural (samʿī), while the first [question] described is rational. The Shaykh, may Allah have mercy on him, said: The principle with us is that Allah, exalted, did not create any creature except that the trace of His grace upon it is apparent, and the proofs of His generosity in it are clear, and that His wisdom in what it contains of evidence of His oneness exists, and the proof and execution of His will in it are confirmed, and the sign of His power and knowledge of the realities of things are not hidden in that. The question like 'Why did you not bestow grace?' or 'Why did you show your generosity?' or 'Why was there wisdom?' or 'Why did you establish the proof of Your oneness?' - up to the rest of what was mentioned - is absurd, corrupt, which no intellect accepts nor capacity tolerates due to its repulsiveness. Therefore this type of question is invalid. And with Allah is success.

Commentary
This passage begins by drawing a parallel with the Qadariyya (those who deny divine predestination of evil) who, when asked about the creation of actions, try to limit the discussion to acts of disbelief and sins. Imam al-Maturidi says that is wrong because that topic is scriptural (samʿī) while the earlier question about creation in general was rational. Then he states the foundational principle of Ahl al-Sunna: every creature without exception displays the effects of Allah's grace, generosity, wisdom, and proofs of His oneness and will. Consequently, any question about why Allah did not bestow grace or why He showed generosity is inherently absurd and unacceptable to sound intellect. Thus such questioning is invalid.
