Seeing Allah Without Modality
Maturidi explains the modality of vision by denying all bodily attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what it means to say Allah is seen 'without modality' and how this affirms vision while negating all bodily attributes.

Core Idea
Allah is seen truly, but not in a way that resembles any created thing.

After establishing that vision of Allah is not like bodily vision, Maturidi answers the inevitable question: 'How is He seen?' His response, 'Without modality' (bila kayf), is a cornerstone of Sunni theology. He then lists numerous attributes that do not apply to Allah, emphasizing that nothing imagined or conceived can describe Him. This lesson unpacks that answer.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and translation, noting the direct question and answer format.
The phrase 'بلا كيف' is the core response.
2
Understand 'bila kayf'
Realize that 'bila kayf' means vision is affirmed without describing how. It is not a denial of vision but a denial of any modality that can be compared to creation.
This is a standard Ash'ari/Maturidi position.
3
Review the negated attributes
Maturidi lists many physical and spatial attributes (standing, connection, etc.) and denies them. This shows that vision of Allah is unlike any bodily experience.

Commentary
This passage refutes the anthropomorphists (المشبهة) who analogize God to created things. Maturidi argues that the anthropomorphist finds that every agent in the sensible world is a body, and every knower is a body, so he concludes the same for the unseen (God). However, this analogy fails because it only mentions the modality of seeing a body, not the modality of seeing a non-body. Then he cites the verse 'لا تدركه الأبصار' (Sight does not comprehend Him) and argues that since God is eternal and His attributes like creating and omnipotence are necessary, the negation of comprehension is also necessary. Finally, he answers the question 'How is He seen?' with 'بلا كيف' (without modality), because modality applies to things with form. He then negates all bodily attributes: standing, connection, separation, facing, behind, short, long, sitting, leaning, attachment, light, darkness, motion, rest, contact, separation, inside, outside—nothing that the imagination or intellect can conceive applies to Allah.

The Vision of God: Seeing without How
Maturidi refutes anthropomorphism and answers how God is seen.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that God is seen in the afterlife without any modality or physical comparison, and how he refutes anthropomorphic interpretations.

Core Idea
God is seen, but without how—no physical attributes or limitations apply.

In this passage from Kitab al-Tawhid, Imam Maturidi tackles one of the most debated topics in Islamic theology: the vision of Allah (ru'ya). He first refutes anthropomorphists who liken God to created bodies, then answers the key question 'How is He seen?' with the classical formula 'bila kayf' (without how). This lesson unpacks his reasoning and the negations that safeguard divine transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the dense argument and how it shifts from Quranic verses to theological reasoning.
2
Understand the linguistic distinction
Maturidi distinguishes between 'seeing' a person's essence and 'seeing' their actions. This is crucial for interpreting Quranic verses about God's 'seeing'.
3
Identify the anthropomorphism argument
Find where Maturidi presents the anthropomorphist (mushabbiha) claim that God must be a body because all agents in the world are bodies, and then see how he refutes it by pointing out the missing link in their analogy.
4
Examine the 'bila kayf' answer
The passage culminates in the answer to 'How is He seen?'—the answer is 'bila kayf' (without how). Notice the long list of attributes that are negated, ensuring that God is beyond all physical and mental categories.
This is the core of the doctrine: vision without modality.

أَلَمْ تَرَ إِلَى رَبِّكَ كَيْفَ مَدَّ الظِّلَّ وَأَلَمْ تَرَ كَيْفَ فَعَلَ رَبُّكَ بِأَصْحَابِ الْفِيلِ، وأصله أن من قال: رأيت فلانا أو نظرت إلى فلان لم يحتمل غير ذاته، وإذا قال: رأيته يقول كذا ويفعل كذا أنه لا يريد به رؤية ذاته، قتل أمر قصة موسى عليه السلام وهذه الآية. ثم الأصل أن من تأمل الذي ذكر عرف أنه مشتهى النحلة [؟]، لأنه المعنى الذي له يجب أن تكون الرؤية بتلك الشرائط، إنما أخبر أنه كذلك وجد، وهو قول المشبهة أنه وجد كل فاعل في الشاهد جسما، وكذا كل عالم، فيجب مثله في الغائب. ثم ذكر معنى رؤية الجسم ولم يذكر معنى رؤية غير الجسم حتى يكون له دليلا. وبعد الله فإنه نفى بالدقة والبعد، وهما زائلان من سبحانه، ثم احتج بامتداح الله بقوله: لَا تُدْرِكُهُ الْأَبْصَارُ وَهُوَ يُدْرِكُ الْأَبْصَارَ، وقال: لا يجوز أن يزول، فمثله عليه في قوله: خَالِقُ كُلِّ شَيْءٍ وقوله: وَهُوَ عَلَى كُلِّ شَيْءٍ قَدِيرٌ، فلا يجوز أن يزول. ثم قد وصف الله بالرؤية على ذكر، فثبت أن ذلك طريق لا يؤدي على كنه ما به الرؤية إسقاط ما ذكر. فإن قيل: كيف يرى؟ قيل: بلا كيف؛ إذ الكيفية تكون لذي صورة، بل يرى بلا وصف قيام واتصال وانفصال، ومقابلة ومدابرة، وقصير وطويل، وقعود، واتكاء وتعلق، ونور وظلمة، وساكن ومتحرك، ومماس ومباين، وخارج وداخل، ولا يعنى يأخذه الوهم أو يقدره العقل تعالى عن ذلك.

Commentary
This passage is a multi-part argument about the vision of God (ru'ya). It begins with Quranic verses that mention God's actions, then discusses the meaning of 'seeing' in ordinary language: when one says 'I saw someone,' it means the physical vision of their essence, but when one says 'I saw him speaking,' it refers to witnessing actions, not essence. This distinction is applied to Moses' story and the verses. The passage then identifies the anthropomorphist (mushabbiha) argument: since all agents and knowers in the created world are bodies, God must be a body. Maturidi refutes this by noting that they only mentioned the vision of a body, not vision of a non-body, so their analogy fails. He then cites the verse 'Sights do not perceive Him' to negate any physical perception, and uses the other verses to show that God's attributes of creating and power are eternal and unchanging, so His description as being seen must also be eternal but not like physical vision. The core of the passage is the objection-and-answer: 'How is He seen?' The answer is the classic aqeeda formula: 'Bila kayf' (without how). Modality (kayfiyya) applies only to things with form. God is seen without any physical or mental attributes like standing, connection, separation, facing, distance, size, posture, light, darkness, motion, touch, inside/outside. The passage concludes that God is beyond being grasped by imagination or intellect in this regard.

Maturidi's Refutation of the Mu'tazila on God's Attribute of Creation
Maturidi refutes the Mu'tazilite claim and compares them to eternalists.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi refutes the Mu'tazilite claim that God was not creator before creation, exposing their inconsistency in denying eternal divine attributes.

Core Idea
Denying God's eternal attributes leads to self-contradiction about creation's dependence on God.

The Mu'tazila argued that God existed alone and then created the world without any eternal attribute, such as will, being the cause of creation. Maturidi points out the logical flaw: if the world came to be without a cause from God, then it is not truly from God, and using the world as proof of God becomes problematic. He compares their position to that of those who believe the world is eternal, showing the Mu'tazila to be inconsistent.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Note the key terms like إرادة, تكوين, دليل, قدم, حدوث.
The passage is dense; read slowly.
2
Identify the Mu'tazila claim and its implications
See how Maturidi summarizes their view: God was by Himself, the world did not exist, then it came to be without a 'meaning' (attribute) from God. Understand why this leads to contradiction when they also use the world as proof of God.
3
Analyze the comparison with eternalists
Maturidi compares them to those who hold the world eternal. Both deny the world is from another. But eternalists are more consistent because they make the world eternal, while Mu'tazila make it temporal but still not from God. Ask yourself: why is this a refutation?

Maturidi's Critique of the Mu'tazila Proof for the World's Temporality
Maturidi critiques Mu'tazila proof for world's temporality and its implications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's critique that the Mu'tazila argument for the world's temporality logically implies the temporality of God.

Core Idea
If the world's temporality requires a temporal maker, then God would be temporal too.

The Mu'tazila argued that since the world contains events, it must be temporal and have a creator. Maturidi exposes the flaw in this reasoning by showing it leads to a contradiction regarding God's eternity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how Maturidi first states the Mu'tazila argument, then presents his two objections.
The passage is the first half of the source chunk.
2
Identify the Mu'tazila argument
What is the core claim? The Mu'tazila say the world is temporal because it is not free of temporal events, and therefore has a temporally originating agent.
3
Understand Maturidi's two objections
Maturidi presents two logical consequences: (1) if the whole world is temporal, then the same reasoning would make the Creator temporal; (2) if God's essence is eternal, why not also grant eternity to the world?
These show the argument proves too much or too little.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of a group that denies God's names and attributes out of fear of anthropomorphism (tashbih). Maturidi begins by describing their position: they claim that the intellect (al-'aql) was brought into being by origination (ibda'), and that origination is its cause. Everything that exists is manifested in this intellect. Then he argues that it is impossible for such a manifestation to occur from one who does not know what will be, or cannot manifest it, or does not will it. This shows that their view leads to God being described as ignorant, powerless, or unwilling—which is absurd. Consequently, by negating attributes and names to avoid tashbih, they fall into the opposite extreme of ta'til (stripping God of all positive description), making it impossible to establish any proof for God, and their belief becomes mere imitation (taqlid) rather than rational evidence. Next, Maturidi discusses their claim that 'Allah' is a name for something else—specifically, the intellect—and that 'al-Rahman' is the name of the soul. This reduces worship to the worship of number and multiplicity rather than tawhid. Then he presents a direct objection: when they say that God has no name, what exactly do they mean? If they mean no essential name or attribute, they cannot even articulate their own position. They then claim that God has a name derived from another, such as 'the Originator' through an origination that is the cause. Maturidi dissects this: if the name is derived from another, then that other is what actualized the name, and the name is applied metaphorically (majaz) rather than truly (haqiqa). Moreover, if origination from God is the basis for the name, then if origination occurs after not having occurred, it would lead to an infinite regress of origins. The only consistent conclusion is that origination is by God's essence, so He is eternally an originator. This necessarily entails that God has an essential name (al-ism al-dhati). The passage ends by affirming the necessity of this essential name.

The Necessity of God's Essential Name
Maturidi argues for the necessity of God's essential name by refuting derived names.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God must have an essential name, and why derived names alone lead to infinite regress and contradiction.

Core Idea
God's essential name is necessary; derived names imply infinite regress, which is impossible.

In this passage, Maturidi continues his critique by examining the claim that God has only derived names, such as 'Originator' through an act of origination. He demonstrates that this leads to an infinite regress of origins, proving that God must be eternally originator and thus possess an essential name.

Guided Path
1
Understand the opponents' shift
They say God has no essential name but a name from another, e.g., al-Mubdi' from ibda'.
Note the move from absolute denial to derived names.
2
Follow Maturidi's objection
If the name is derived from something else, that thing actualizes the name, making it metaphorical, not real.
3
Analyze the infinite regress argument
If ibda' occurs at a point, then before it God was not originator, so the name depends on a temporal act, leading to an endless chain.
4
Conclude: essential name necessary
Therefore, ibda' must be by God's essence, so He is eternally originator, proving an essential name.

Absolute Names Do Not Imply Anthropomorphism

Maturidi begins by stating a foundational principle: the absolute name (al-ism al-mutlaq) does not necessitate anthropomorphism (tashbih). He supports this with examples from the witnessed world where opposites share the same name yet remain distinct. This lesson explores his reasoning and its implications for divine transcendence.

The Name Creator Necessitates Eternal Creation
Maturidi argues that the name 'Creator' necessitates eternal creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's logical argument that the name 'Creator' implies eternal creation, and why an infinite regress of creations is impossible.

Core Idea
God's name 'Creator' is essential, not acquired; otherwise infinite regress follows.

Imam Maturidi presents a concise yet powerful argument linking the divine name 'Creator' to the necessity of eternal creation. Using reason, he shows that if creation were not eternal, we would fall into an impossible infinite regress. This passage is a classic example of kalam reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation

Notice the key terms: إبداع, اسم ذاتي, لم يزل مبدعا.
2

Break down the argument: premise about the name, consequence of infinite regress, judgment of impossibility, conclusion of eternal creation, and final necessity of the essential name.
3
Understand the concept of 'impossible' (محال)
Why is infinite regress impossible? In kalam, an actual infinite chain of dependent events is rationally impossible because it would require an actual infinite, which is contradictory.
This is a key principle in proofs for God's existence.
4
Connect to the essential name
The argument concludes that the name 'Creator' is essential (ذاتي), meaning it has always been true of God, since He has never ceased to create.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Summarize: Because an infinite regress is impossible, God must be eternally creating by His essence. Therefore, the name 'Creator' is necessary and essential.

Commentary
This passage presents a concise proof by Imam Maturidi regarding the divine attribute of creation. The conclusion is that the name 'Creator' is an essential name (الاسم الذاتي) necessarily belonging to God. The phrase 'لا قوة إلا بالله' is a formula of reliance on God.

Maturidi argues that God is Creator by essence, making the question 'why create?' absurd.

Core Idea
God is Creator by essence; asking 'why He creates' is like asking 'why He knows.'

Maturidi presents a powerful argument: if God is Creator by His very essence, then creating is not an optional act but a necessary expression of His nature. The question 'why create?' becomes as meaningless as asking 'why does God know?' In this lesson, we unpack this argument and see how it grounds the necessity of creation.

Maturidi refutes a claim restricting God's actions.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

In this dense passage, Imam al-Maturidi (referred to as al-faqih) dismantles a theological claim that restricts God's power to only what He actually did. He argues that such a view would force us to attribute injustice, compulsion, and even need to God. Let's trace his logical steps as he turns the claim into a reductio ad absurdum.

Imam Maturidi begins by addressing a fundamental question: what does it mean to say that Allah is a 'thing'? This term often carries physical connotations in everyday language, but in theological discourse it requires precise definition. Maturidi explains that 'thing' is nothing more than affirmation of being, and this affirmation is the path to recognizing the Creator.

God's Attributes and the Perfection of Creation
Maturidi argues from divine attributes to the perfection of creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses God's essential attributes to argue that creation contains no real flaw, and why apparent evils are due to the limitations of human intellect.

Core Idea
God's perfect attributes guarantee the perfection of His creation.

This passage from Imam Maturidi lays a foundation for theodicy from a Sunni perspective. By reflecting on God's essential attributes—power, self-sufficiency, knowledge, and wisdom—Maturidi deduces that creation cannot contain true discrepancy or evil. He then uses an analogy with the senses and intellect to explain why we may not always perceive the wisdom behind creation.
