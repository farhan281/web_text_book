Maturidi's Method for Ambiguous Verses
Maturidi explains the proper approach to istiwa' without specifying interpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi balances reason and revelation when interpreting ambiguous verses about Allah without fixing a single interpretation.

Core Idea
Affirm the verse, negate resemblance, but never fix one interpretation.

Maturidi addresses one of the most debated verses: Allah's istiwa' over the Throne. Instead of choosing a literal or metaphorical interpretation, he sets a method: affirm what reason and revelation teach, but do not lock into one meaning. This lesson explores that principle.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic carefully, noting the key terms like istiwa'. Let the author's own wording set the foundation.
The word istiwa' is central to the discussion.
2
Distinguish the two affirmations
Maturidi first affirms based on reason (Allah existed without place), then based on revelation. Notice how he combines both as valid.
3
Understand the open‑ended conclusion
The final rule: negate tashbih but don't fix a single ta'wil. Ask yourself why leaving the interpretation open is important for divine transcendence.
This connects to the broader Maturidi methodology.

So He is as He was, and He was as He is now, exalted above change, passing away, transformation, and annihilation. And we say it according to what has come in revelation, then we do not definitively interpret it in any one way because of the possibility of other interpretations, and we believe in what Allah intended by it. Commentary
This passage is a statement of the Maturidi hermeneutical principle for dealing with verses that attribute bodily actions or location to Allah (mutashabihat). The specific verse is 'The Most Merciful established Himself over the Throne' (istawa 'ala al-arsh). The author says we affirm this verse based on what reason establishes: Allah existed without any place (la makan) before creation, and places are contingent and can pass away while Allah remains unchanged. Therefore, His istiwa' does not imply change or physical location. Then we also affirm it according to revelation, but we do not fix a single specific interpretation (ta'wil) because the verse could bear other meanings; instead we believe in what Allah intended without settling on one interpretation. This principle extends to all revealed matters like the vision (ru'ya) and others: we must negate any likeness to creation (tashbih) and believe in Allah's intended meaning without insisting on a particular explanation.

Etiquette of Not Attributing Evil to Allah
Maturidi explains the adab of not attributing evil creation to Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi prohibits attributing the creation of evil to Allah and the principle governing proper attribution of actions to Him.

Core Idea
True adab: Affirm Allah's creatorship of all things, but only attribute to Him what evokes veneration or gratitude.

Maturidi teaches us a crucial theological etiquette: while Allah is the Creator of everything, including what we consider evil, we must not describe Him as the creator of filth or sin. This passage lays down the principle that all attributions to Allah must be dignified and express veneration, gratitude, or blessing. Let's explore the reasoning and how to apply it.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic passage from Maturidi once, then the English translation. Notice the examples he gives.
The passage is short but dense; take time to absorb each phrase.
2
Identify the examples of what not to attribute
Maturidi mentions dung, filths, the devil, disbelief, and sins. Note that these are things considered evil or impure.
He also says one should not say 'O Creator of filths' even though Allah is truly their Creator.
3
Understand the principle of attribution
Maturidi gives a general rule: only attribute to Allah what comes out as veneration (ta'zim), gratitude (shukr), mentioning His blessings (ni'am), or His command (amr). Anything else, even if true, is not attributed out of adab.
This principle guides our speech about Allah, preserving reverence.
4
Reflect on the theological nuance
Why does Maturidi affirm Allah's creatorship of all things but prohibit certain verbal attributions? Consider the balance between truth and adab.
This is not a denial of Allah's power but a matter of proper etiquette.
5
Test your understanding
Answer the checkpoint question to ensure you grasp the key point.

Otherwise, even though Allah is truly their Creator, we avoid such attribution out of respect. This is an important theological nuance: while affirming Allah's creatorship of all things, one must observe proper decorum in speech.

The Politeness of Attributing Only Good to God
Maturidi states the principle of attributing only worthy things to God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's principle that while God creates all things, evil should not be directly attributed to Him out of politeness (ta'dib).

Core Idea
We attribute to God only what brings glorification or gratitude, though He creates everything.

This passage from Imām Maturīdī’s Kitāb al-Tawḥīd addresses a subtle point: how to speak about God’s creation of evil. While affirming that God is the Creator of all things, including disbelief and sins, Maturidi teaches that it is improper to ascribe such things directly to God. Instead, attribution should follow the principle of what leads to glorification, gratitude, or mention of His blessings. Let’s examine the text.

By examining the attributes of contingent things—such as change, increase, decrease, and decay—Maturidi shows that God cannot share any of these without negating His perfection. The argument serves as a proof that the world is created and that its Creator is absolutely unique.

Why Allah Cannot Be Called a Body
Maturidi classifies two meanings of 'body' and rules both inapplicable to Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the term 'body' (jism) cannot be applied to Allah according to Imam Maturidi, and the two possible meanings of 'body' that he rules out.

Core Idea
Calling Allah a 'body' either likens Him to creation or becomes meaningless.

Imam Maturidi tackles a key objection from those who might describe Allah as a 'body' in some sense. He carefully separates the term into two possible meanings—each one found to be incompatible with divine transcendence. Let's follow his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Begin by reading the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the English translation. Let the structure of two meanings become clear.
Notice that Maturidi uses a numbered classification (أحدهما / وثانيها).
2
Identify the two meanings of 'body'
The first meaning is based on the quiddity (māhiyya) of body in the witnessed world: something with directions, ends, or three dimensions. The second meaning is body as mere affirmation (ithbāt) without any quiddity.
3
Analyze the refutation of the first meaning
Maturidi argues that the first meaning implies parts and limits, which are signs of being created (ḥadath). Applying it to Allah would make Him like created things, contradicting 'nothing is like Him'. Also, without those specific meanings, the term loses rational meaning and must come from revelation—but it doesn't.
See the argument about 'by way of hash' leading to absurdity.
4
Analyze the refutation of the second meaning
Even if 'body' is taken as pure affirmation (no specific meaning), it is never used that way in language—accidents and attributes aren't called 'body'. So this meaning is also invalid.
The second meaning is a hypothetical that fails due to actual usage.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Both meanings are ruled out. The term 'body' cannot be applied to Allah under any interpretation. This safeguards God's transcendence.
Consider how this argument protects tanzīh (transcendence).

By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi argues that the concept of potentiality in a first principle leads to contradictions, particularly regarding corruption and the eternity of the Qarmatians' eternal origin.

Core Idea
Potentiality implies vulnerability to corruption, undermining claims of eternal origin.

Maturidi begins by attacking the foundational claim that things exist in a first principle 'in potentiality' and later appear 'in actuality.' He shows this leads to absurdities: the first principle becomes susceptible to corruption, contradicting its supposed eternity. The Qarmatians' view of eternal origination faces the same flaw.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of philosophical and sectarian views that posit a primordial substance (matter, clay, universal soul) containing all things potentially, from which the world emerges in actuality. Maturidi argues that such a view leads to absurdities: if the first principle is subject to corruption, then composite things are also; and the Qarāmiṭa's claim of eternal origination faces the same problem. He then presents the tawḥīd view: Allah creates directly by decree and formation, without any latent potentiality. Finally, he notes that if these philosophers actually meant that the origin itself creates the world directly (not from within it), then they would be agreeing with Ahl al-Tawḥīd, but they use different names for the Creator. The argument proceeds by showing the inner contradiction of the potentiality model and affirming divine omnipotence.

Direct Creation Without Potentiality
Maturidi affirms direct creation by Allah's decree and contrasts it with the potentiality model.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's affirmation that Allah creates directly by determination (taqdīr) and formation (takwīn), without any need for potentiality in an eternal substance.

Core Idea
Allah's power suffices to create from nothing, bypassing any latent potentiality.

After refuting the potentiality model, Maturidi presents the correct tawḥīd view: Allah is able to create an origin containing everything already actualized, or he can initiate each thing individually by his decree and power. The key is that creation does not require a transition from potentiality to actuality; rather, Allah's will and power are sufficient.

2

Taqdīr is divine decree, takwīn is formation.
3
Contrast with the potentiality view
See how Maturidi contrasts the tawḥīd view with the claim that things were hidden in the origin's substance and then appeared actually. He argues that if they claim that, it reduces to the same absurdity as sperm drops.
He says 'they too return to what we said' meaning the same refutation applies.
4
Reflect on divine omnipotence
Appreciate that Maturidi emphasizes Allah's absolute power to create as He wills, without any constraints of potentiality or material substrate.
This is central to Islamic theology.

The Name of the Creator
Maturidi notes agreement in concept but misnaming in the philosophers' view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's final point: if the philosophers' origin is said to create directly by its own will, they agree with Ahl al-Tawḥīd but misuse the names of the Creator.

Core Idea
Calling the Creator by other names does not change the truth of tawḥīd.

The passage underscores that the issue is not just rejection but correct naming and attribution of attributes.

The Mu'tazilite Claim of Thingness of Non-Existence and Maturidi's Refutation
Maturidi presents and refutes the Mu'tazilite doctrine of the thingness of non-existents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Mu'tazilite doctrine of non-existent things being things leads to a violation of tawhid according to Imam Maturidi.

Core Idea
If non-existents are things in pre-eternity, they become rivals to God's oneness.

The Mu'tazila proposed that non-existent objects (al-ma'dum) are 'things' (ashya') with a reality that is not from God, only their coming into existence is from God. Imam Maturidi exposes the theological danger of this view: it implies that there are entities co-eternal with God, which compromises the absolute oneness of Allah. In this lesson, we will explore the claim, Maturidi's refutation, and the conclusion that such a doctrine contradicts the very foundation of tawhid.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazilite claim
Read the first sentence: 'al-Mu'tazila said: The non-existent is things, and the thingness of things is not from God, and from God is their bringing out from knowledge to existence.' Understand their position.
Note the distinction they make between thingness (which is not from God) and existence (which is from God).
2
Identify Maturidi's response
Read the rest: 'Abu Mansur said: So upon them...' Identify how Maturidi refutes them: if non-existents are things in pre-eternity, they are distinct entities alongside God.
Notice the phrase 'faj'alu ma'a Allah aghyarun fi al-azal' (they made with God others in pre-eternity).
3
Understand the consequence
Reflect on why this negates tawhid: if there are other eternal things, God is not the sole eternal being. This is a form of hidden shirk.
Maturidi argues that their view introduces multiplicity in pre-eternity, which violates the unity of God.
4
Review the key terms
Look at the key terms: معتزلة, معدوم, شيئية, أزل, توحيد, أغيار. Make sure you understand each in context.
These terms are essential for grasping the debate.

Commentary
This passage presents the Mu'tazilite claim that non-existent things (al-ma'dum) are things (ashya') and that their thingness is not from God, only their bringing from knowledge to existence is from God. Maturidi then refutes this: if non-existents are things in pre-eternity, then they are distinct entities existing alongside God, making God not the sole eternal being. This, he argues, negates tawhid because it introduces 'others' (aghyar) with God in pre-eternity. The key point is that the Mu'tazilite doctrine implies multiple eternal entities, which violates the oneness of God.

The Absolute Name Does Not Imply Anthropomorphism
Maturidi explains why the absolute name does not imply anthropomorphism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that using a common name for God and creatures does not entail anthropomorphism (tashbih) because names signify distinct meanings and opposites have separate names.

Core Idea
Names distinguish opposites; shared names do not imply shared essence.

In this passage, Imam al-Maturidi addresses a common objection against using divine names: if we call God 'Living' or 'Knower', do we not liken Him to creatures? Maturidi refutes this by analyzing the function of names. He shows that names are assigned to distinguish realities, and that sharing a name between God and creation does not require similarity in essence. The argument proceeds through examples and logical reasoning, establishing that the absolute name (al-ism al-mutlaq) does not imply anthropomorphism.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without over-analyzing, then read the literal translation. Note the structure: a principle, then examples, then a conditional argument, then a concluding remark.
2
Identify the main argument
Maturidi's central claim is that the absolute name does not admit tashbih. His proof: if names implied similarity, opposites would not be distinguishable. Since we know opposites have distinct names, names must indicate distinction, not similarity.
The phrase 'فلو لم يكن بالاسم المطلق تشابه' gives the hypothetical.
3
Examine the examples of opposites
Notice the pairs: life/death, light/darkness, evil/good, unbelief/faith. Each pair is opposite in meaning and has a separate name. This shows that names are tied to specific meanings and do not imply sharing in reality.
These are drawn from the witnessed world (shahid).
4
Understand the counterargument about negation
Maturidi anticipates an objection: what if we agree on a name while negating the meaning (e.g., saying God is 'high' but not spatially)? He argues that this agreement on the name alone does not create similarity, because even those who deny God's name agree on negation, which would also imply similarity — an absurd result.
The passage about 'لو كان بموافقة الاسم عند نفي المعنى' addresses this.
5
Reflect on the conclusion about causation and attributes
Finally, Maturidi notes that ibda' (creation) is a cause and not described as a 'thing' in the same way as its effects; accidents (a'rad) are not described with attributes like 'knowing'. Thus, affirming a name like 'Creator' does not entail similarity.
This underscores that names do not force essential likeness.

Shaykh Abu Mansur, may Allah have mercy on him, said: Then the principle with us is that the absolute name does not admit the realization of anthropomorphism because every opposite in the witnessed world falls under the name, such as life and death, light and darkness, evil and good, unbelief and faith—each name separately. So if by the absolute name there were similarity, then no opposition would be known, nor difference by names; it is established that they (names) are made for what is intended of difference and agreement, whose reality is not known if it did not have a name. And if by agreeing on a name when negating the meaning that the effect has from the named there were similarity in the witnessed world, then it would be as if the world were not named 'high' and 'low' and 'first creator' and 'second', and there would be between those who claim that He has a name and others agreement in negating the name with all things, on the condition that he finds in the statement 'one of creation' negation of anthropomorphism, even if from the aspect of the name 'one' there is aggregation. And further, creation with Him is a cause, and it is not described as a thing, because through it things come to be, and all accidents are not described as 'knowing' or 'able' or the like. So if in affirming the name there were similarity, then in negating that there would be similarity as well from the aspect I mentioned. And there is no power except with Allah.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed refutation of the claim that using a common name (like 'wise', 'living') for God and creatures implies anthropomorphism (tashbih). Maturidi establishes that opposite realities (such as life and death, light and darkness) each have distinct names, showing that names correspond to specific meanings. If absolute names implied similarity, then opposites would not be distinguishable. Furthermore, even in the case of negating a meaning (e.g., denying that God is 'high' in a spatial sense), agreement on the name does not imply similarity in essence. Maturidi notes that creation (ibdaʿ) is a cause for the existence of things, but itself is not described as a 'thing' in the same way as its effects. Similarly, accidents (aʿrad) are not described with attributes like 'knowing'. Therefore, affirming a name like 'Creator' does not entail similarity, just as negating it would not. The conclusion is that names do not necessitate anthropomorphism.

God's Purposes in Creation Beyond Benefit
Al-Husayn refutes the claim that God did not create all for a purpose.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that God's creation serves multiple purposes including trial and admonition, and that it is not limited to benefit alone.

Core Idea
Creation is not solely for benefit; it includes trial, admonition, and divine wisdom.

Some argued that if God created everything for a cause, it would imply limitation. Al-Husayn responds by showing that God's purposes are manifold, including evidence, grace, and trial. This lesson explores that response and its implications for understanding divine action.

1
Read the claim and response
Read the Arabic passage and translation carefully. Notice the structure: first the claim of some people, then Al-Husayn's answer.
Pay attention to the shift from 'they said' to 'Al-Husayn said'.
2
Identify the reasons Al-Husayn lists
Find the list of purposes: evidence, admonition, grace, nourishment, etc. Recognize that creation includes blessing for some and trial for others.
This illustrates the diversity of divine wisdom.
3
Understand the refutation of single-purpose
Al-Husayn argues that if creation were only for benefit, it would restrict God's ability to change states. Reflect on why this shows creation is not limited to one purpose.
4
Connect to divine transcendence
The passage concludes that the divine order involves placing things appropriately. Consider how this upholds God's transcendence and wisdom beyond human expectations.
This is a key point in Maturidi theology.

Commentary

Since God has made humans with imaginations and they are veiled from supporting others, it is clear that the divine order is not limited to one purpose but involves placing things appropriately and turning circumstances from benefit to harm and vice versa.

Divine Identity as Existence and Negation of Change
Maturidi defines huwiyya and argues against change in Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's definition of huwiyya as existence and why Allah cannot be subject to change or states.

Core Idea
Allah's identity is pure existence, free from all change and transition.

Maturidi begins by defining what we mean when we talk about Allah's 'identity' (huwiyya). He explains that in our experience, identity points to existence, and for Allah, it means negation of non-existence. He then describes Allah's eternal nature as utterly without change or movement, establishing the basis for divine transcendence. This passage lays the foundation for understanding why Allah cannot be described by originated attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the literal translation. Pay attention to the rhythm of the argument.
Notice how Maturidi uses the term الهوية as a starting point.
2
Examine the definition of huwiyya
Identify that huwiyya in the witnessed world is a metaphor for existence, and its true meaning for Allah is negation of non-existence.
This sets up the positive attribute of existence while negating its opposite.
3
List the negations of change
Find the phrases: 'no change, no passing away, no transition from state to state, no movement, no rest'. Understand that each negates a form of temporal change.
Change implies originated states, which cannot apply to Allah.
4
Follow the argument about states (ahwal)
Understand that if Allah were subject to states, those states are originated (ahdath), which would imply He is originated—impossible. The argument shows that Allah is not subject to any state.
The conclusion is that change in management (tadbir) over others is not change in essence.

Then identity (huwiyya) in the witnessed world is a metaphor for existence, and its interpretation is the negation of non-existence from Him. And God, exalted, has not ceased and will not cease without change, without passing away, without transition from state to state, without movement, and without rest; for change is the description of differing states; states differ with respect to Him, so He is not separate from them. Then [comes] pre-eternity, then [comes] the running of management over others [by Him]? For if a state from among states were for His essence, its change would not be permissible as long as His essence persists. Thus it is established that change of states occurs with respect to Him and moves Him from state to state. And that is evidence of His transcendence above description by place; for it has been established that He was and there was no place. Rather, places are only honored by Him and their degrees differ by His preferring one place over another, making it specific for the best of His creation or for what He made for His worship and veneration therein. As for one's rank being elevated by place among earthly kings or the righteous, it is not so. So how about the All-Compelling King, whose worth no place is elevated except by Him? If that is so, then the attribution [of place to Him] is invalid for glorification. Then what is beyond that is due to need, and He is exalted above it. Therefore, by His saying: 'The Most Merciful established Himself over the Throne' (Q 20:5) it is not necessary to mean dwelling in place; for that expression signifies elevation and majesty, and it is impossible that its like [be interpreted] with...?
