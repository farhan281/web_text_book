Refuting the Analogy Between Creating Accidents and Bodies
Al-Maturidi refutes the objection that if humans create accidents, they should also create bodies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Maturidi's refutation of the objection that if humans can create accidents, they should also be able to create bodies.

Core Idea
Human agency lacks the essential aspect needed to create bodies.

Al-Maturidi addresses an objection raised by his opponents. The objection claims that if humans can bring accidents into existence, they should also be able to create bodies. In this passage, he refutes that reasoning by clarifying the nature of the act required for body creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Read the Arabic passage carefully, then the translation. Notice that the passage begins with an objection and then the author's refutation.
Pay attention to the structure: objection, redefinition, answer, refutation, analogy.
2
Identify the core difference
Al-Maturidi points out that the creation of a body involves a specific aspect (the essence) that humans do not realize in their own acts. Identify this key distinction.
The difference lies in the 'aspect of existence' (وجه وجود الجسم).
3
Understand the Zaid analogy
The analogy with Zaid illustrates that while humans can act with power, they cannot replicate a specific act unless empowered. Reflect on how this supports the refutation.
Zaid does not empower us over his act, but God empowers humans over the meaning of the body.
4
Grasp the logical consequence
Al-Maturidi concludes that the opponent's own criteria would force them to admit that humans create bodies, which they deny, thus their position is inconsistent.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum argument.

Then he objected to himself: if you are able to bring forth accidents from non-existence into existence, why are you not able to do that for the body? Abu Mansur, may Allah have mercy on him, said: 'This is not the manner of the question, but rather because the meaning of the creation of the body is only its emergence from non-existence and its existence after it was not, and by this you described yourselves regarding the act of accidents, how is the description of creating the body not permissible while there is no other [agent]?' Commentary
This passage is an objection and its refutation concerning the ability to create bodies versus accidents. The opponent (al-Ka'bi) raises a self-objection: if you (the Mu'tazila) claim that you can bring accidents from non-existence to existence, why can you not do the same for a body? Abu Mansur responds by redefining the question: the creation of a body means bringing it from non-existence to existence. The Mu'tazila describe their own acts as causing accidents to exist, so why would they not be described as creating bodies, especially since there is no other agent involved? Then the opponent answers by appealing to the act itself, but Abu Mansur deems this answer corrupt. The reason is that in their own acts, the Mu'tazila have not realized the aspect that constitutes the very existence and being of a body (i.e., the essence), so the necessity of that act being creation does not apply to them. However, the opponent (the one who claims accidents are created by humans) has indeed realized that aspect, so the same necessity applies to them. Abu Mansur then uses an example: Zaid's act is merely an act performed by his power, and you can act with your power just as Zaid does. The reply is that Zaid does not enable us over his act (i.e., we cannot replicate his specific act), but God has given you the ability over the meaning by which the body exists, so the same objection we raised (that you create the body) becomes necessary for you.

The Writer and Painter Example
Maturidi presents and refutes the writer/painter example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazili argument from the writer/painter example and Maturidi's initial refutation.

Core Idea
Inability to replicate creation points to divine agency.

In this passage, Maturidi engages with a Mu'tazili analogy: a writer cannot produce an exact copy of his own work, suggesting the original was not truly his. Maturidi begins to dismantle this argument by examining the causes involved.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow of the argument. Then read the translation to understand the key points.
2
Identify the Mu'tazili claim
The opponent argues that if a writer cannot replicate his own work, that shows the first work was not produced by him alone.
This is the claim Maturidi refutes.
3
Understand Maturidi's response
Maturidi points out that human actions require multiple causes (mind, memory, etc.) that rarely align perfectly, so inability is due to incomplete conditions, not lack of agency.
4
Note the counter-objections
The opponent raises that if this argument necessitated another agent, it would also necessitate another painter for the second, and that inability does not prove non-agency.
Maturidi uses these to refine his position.

Commentary
This passage continues the dialectical argument. The opponent (Mu'tazili) uses the example of a writer or painter: if someone cannot produce an identical copy of his own work, that shows the first was not truly his act but from God. The opponent first claims that the power to create the likeness is from God. Then the question is asked: why cannot one produce a similar work? The answer given is that human actions require many causes (memory, mind, etc.) that never fully coincide. The opponent argues that if such an argument necessitated another agent (muhdith) for the first, it would also necessitate another painter for the second. Then he opposes with the point that inability does not prove that we did not act; if it did, then if a second work is better, it would indicate the first is the agent's. Further objection: what prevents one from creating a perfect copy? The response: humans act with instruments, power, treatment, and thought, which are not all equal; if they were equal, they could do it. Then Maturidi presents a dilemma: either these preventing aspects are from God or not. If they are from God, then the opponent has admitted that creation is from God. If they are from the agent, then why are they not equal, given the Qadariyya believe God has given the power? This inconsistency shows that the act emerges from a source other than the human. The inability to control all variables (air, place, hand movements) proves the act is from another (God). Moreover, an act cannot be attributed to a person if it occurs from an aspect he does not know or intend, and this would unravel miracles and other divine signs. Finally, Maturidi equates the painter to the agent and the picture to the action, and dismisses the opponent's argument about the non-permanence of power as meaningless. He concludes that although the opponent claims to choose and know his act, Maturidi has shown that the agent does not truly know that aspect.

The Dilemma of Preventing Factors
Maturidi poses a dilemma to the Mu'tazili about the source of preventing factors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's dilemma argument concerning the factors that prevent a perfect replication.

Core Idea
The factors that prevent replication either come from God or from the agent—both lead to divine involvement.

After the initial exchange, Maturidi presents a critical dilemma. He asks the opponent whether the aspects preventing an exact copy are from God or not. This dilemma exposes the inconsistency in the Mu'tazili position.

Guided Path
1
Read the dilemma passage
Focus on the question: 'Are the preventing factors from God or not?' and the two possible answers.
2
Analyze the first option
If the opponent says the factors are not from God, then he admits that all causes (treatment, thought, etc.) are from God, which means he accepts divine creation.
This contradicts his original denial.
3
Analyze the second option
If the factors are from the agent himself, ask why they are not equal when he has power from God. This shows inconsistency in the Qadariyya belief.
4
Connect to the conclusion
The outcome is that the act emerges contrary to the agent's estimation, indicating it is from another (God).

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of Al-Ka'bi's claim that God's actions are by choice (ikhtiyar). Al-Ka'bi argued that the natural (matbu') has only one type of act (i.e., it acts uniformly), so God's actions being varied implies choice. Then Maturidi presents his own positive evidence (dilalah) for choice: the variation and wisdom in creation point to a single choice and to God's oneness. He then refutes alternative explanations (natures, astrology, celestial spheres, biological generation) by arguing that all such views reduce to one thing causing another, leading to an infinite regress unless an initial state is established, but then that initial state cannot bring itself into existence—this impossibility proves the need for a single divine choice.
