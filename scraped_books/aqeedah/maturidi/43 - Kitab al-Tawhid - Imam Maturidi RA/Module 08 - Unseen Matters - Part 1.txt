The Three Types of Reports
Maturidi defines reports as a means of knowledge and classifies them into three types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's classification of reports into three types and the foundational role of reports in human knowledge.

Core Idea
Reports are the basis of identity and knowledge, coming in three epistemological categories.

Maturidi begins his discussion of reports (akhbar) by highlighting their fundamental role in human knowledge: through reports we know our lineage, names, and even the origin of life. He then introduces a tripartite classification that is essential for evaluating the certainty of transmitted information. Let's examine this classification directly from his words.

This connects to the broader theme of sources of knowledge.

وأما الخبر فهو من وسائل المعرفة، وبه يعرف المرء قبل كل شيء نسبه واسمه ومائيته وكذلك العرق الذي ينتمي إليه، ويصل به إلى معرفة اسمه الشخصي وأسماء كل شيء من الخلق، ويؤمن بذلك العلم بمنشأ الحياة البشرية واستدامتها. فالأخبار ثلاثة أنواع: أخبار الرسل أي الأخبار الآتية عن طريق الرسل، والخبر المتواتر أي الخبر الذي ثبت عن طريق التواتر، وخبر الواحد. إن الآيات والمعجزات هي التي توضح صدق الرسل؛ فالأخبار التي تصل إلينا عن طريقها لزم قبولها بضرورة العقل، إذ الخبر الوارد من قبل هؤلاء الرسل متى بلغ درجة التواتر تنبع منه المعرفة الصادقة فالتواتر يعني النظر بالعقل إلى هؤلاء الناقلين للخبر كما وكيفا ، ثم الحكم بصدقه . وكذلك يذهب الماتريدي إلى الاعتراف بأن الإجماع المبنى على اجتهادات العلماء يعتبر في حد التواتر؛ إذ يقول بأن هؤلاء العلماء، رغم انفرادهم بتفرق الهمم واختلافف الأهواء، فإن اتفاقهم في رأي مشترك لن يكون دون لطف الله العلي الذي يملك إظهار حقه وعصمة خلقه فيما شاء. ويعني ذلك أن الخبر المتواتر الحاصل بالإجماع شبيه عنده بأخبار الرسل.
As for the report (al-khabar), it is a means of knowledge; through it a person knows before everything his lineage, name, essence (māhiyyā), as well as the race to which he belongs, and through it he reaches knowledge of his personal name and the names of everything among creation, and he believes through that knowledge the origin of human life and its continuity. Reports are of three types: reports of the messengers (akhbār al-rusul), i.e., the reports coming via the messengers; the mutawātir report, i.e., the report established by way of tawātur (mass transmission); and the report of a single person (khabar al-wāḥid). Indeed, the signs and miracles are what clarify the truthfulness of the messengers; the reports that reach us through them must be accepted by necessity of reason (bi-ḍarūrat al-ʿaql), for when the report coming from these messengers reaches the degree of tawātur, true knowledge arises from it. Tawātur means rationally examining the transmitters of the report in terms of quantity and quality, then judging its truthfulness. Likewise, al-Maturidi goes to acknowledge that consensus (al-ijmāʿ) built upon the independent reasoning (ijtihādāt) of scholars is considered at the level of tawātur; for he says that these scholars, despite their individuality due to the divergence of intentions and the difference of inclinations, their agreement on a common opinion would not be without the grace of Allah the Most High, Who possesses the power to manifest His truth and protect His creation in whatever He wills. This means that the mutawātir report obtained by consensus is similar in his view to the reports of the messengers.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and epistemological evaluation of reports as a source of knowledge. Maturidi first notes the importance of reports for knowing identity, lineage, and history. He then classifies reports into three types: (1) reports from messengers (prophetic reports), (2) mutawātir reports (continuously transmitted by a large number making collusion impossible), and (3) single reports (khabar al-wāḥid). He explains that miracles establish the truthfulness of messengers, so their reports must be accepted by rational necessity when they reach tawātur. Tawātur itself involves rational scrutiny of the transmitters and their circumstances, leading to a judgment of truth. Maturidi then makes a remarkable claim: scholarly consensus (ijmāʿ) based on ijtihad is equivalent to tawātur. Despite scholars having different motives and inclinations, their unanimous agreement indicates divine grace and protection, making their consensus as reliable as the reports of the messengers. Thus, mutawātir knowledge can arise from both mass transmission and consensus.

Prophetic Reports and the Rational Necessity of Tawatur
Maturidi explains how prophetic reports with miracles and tawatur yield certain knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi argues that prophetic reports, when accompanied by miracles and reaching tawatur, must be accepted by rational necessity.

Core Idea
Prophetic reports become certain knowledge through miracles and mass transmission.

After classifying reports, Maturidi turns to the epistemological grounding of prophetic reports. He explains that the miracles of the prophets verify their truthfulness, and when their reports reach the level of tawatur (mass transmission), they yield necessary knowledge. He then defines tawatur as a rational evaluation of the transmitters. Let's examine this key argument.

Consensus (Ijma) as a Form of Tawatur
Maturidi argues that scholarly consensus (ijma) is equivalent to tawatur.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's view that scholarly consensus built on ijtihad is epistemically equivalent to mutawatir reports.

Core Idea
Consensus of scholars, despite their differences, is a divine safeguard and equals tawatur.

Maturidi makes a striking claim: the consensus of scholars (ijma) based on ijtihad is at the level of tawatur (mass transmission). He argues that despite the diverse intentions and inclinations of scholars, their unanimous agreement can only come from divine grace and protection. This elevates consensus to the same certainty as the reports of the messengers. Let's explore this argument.

The Epistemic Status of Khabar al-Wahid
Maturidi defines khabar al-wahid and its ruling requiring ijtihad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Maturidi's position that khabar al-wahid does not yield certain knowledge but necessitates action through ijtihad.

Core Idea
Khabar al-wahid demands action through ijtihad, not certainty.

Imam Maturidi here tackles a key question in uṣūl al-fiqh: what kind of knowledge does a single-narrator report (khabar al-wāḥid) convey? He clarifies that such reports do not yield certain knowledge (ʿilm) but still obligate action — not blindly, but through careful ijtihad and scrutiny of narrators. This passage lays the foundation for how later scholars approach the authority of solitary hadith reports.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the English translation to get a sense of the argument.
Notice the dense style of Maturidi's prose.
2
Identify the key claims
Identify what Maturidi says about the epistemic status of khabar al-wahid: does it give certainty? What does it obligate? How is ijtihad involved?
Look for phrases like 'لا يبلغ هذا القدر في إيجاب العلم' and 'يجب العمل به والترك، بالاجتهاد'.
3
Reflect on the analogy with sensory knowledge
Consider why Maturidi compares khabar al-wahid to sensory knowledge. How does that analogy support his conclusion that ijtihad is necessary?
The analogy shows that even the most certain path (sensory knowledge) can be uncertain due to weak senses or distant objects.

The single-narrator report and another report that does not reach this level of compelling knowledge and testimony that it is the truth from the Prophet of mercy, so acting upon it and abandoning it are obligatory, by ijtihad and examination of the conditions of the narrators, and the apparent from what has become manifest of its ruling, and its permissibility in the transmitted law which has been encompassed. Then one acts upon what is preponderant in aspect, even if it admits the possibility of error, for sometimes it is acted upon in sensory knowledge, which is the highest of the ways of knowledge, due to weakness of the senses or the distance and subtlety of the perceived object, such that abandoning acting upon it and acting upon it both do not bring a person to complete certainty. And whichever of the two he inclines to, in that there is a turning away from the right of the report. Therefore it is necessary to hold ijtihad in it with both aspects, and there is no power except through Allah.

Commentary
This passage defines the epistemological status of khabar al-wahid, or the single-narrator report. Maturidi begins by stating that such a report does not reach the level of necessitating certain knowledge (ilm) or testimony that it is definitively from the Prophet. Rather, it makes action—either performing or abandoning what it conveys—obligatory through ijtihad and scrutiny of the narrators' conditions. The 'apparent' from what has been established as a ruling and its permissibility in the transmitted law that is already known form the basis. Then, one acts upon the preponderant probability even though error is possible. He draws an analogy with sensory knowledge, which, despite being the most certain path, can still be affected by weak senses or distant objects—so action without certainty is accepted. In both cases, whether one acts or abandons, complete certainty (ihata) is not achieved; yet turning away from the report is an evasion of its right. Thus, ijtihad with both possibilities (acting or leaving) is necessary. The passage guards against two misunderstandings: that khabar al-wahid yields certainty (it does not) and that it can be dismissed entirely (it cannot; it requires ijtihad).

Maturidi begins a powerful scriptural argument by citing the Quranic verse that negates any other god alongside Allah. Commentary
This passage presents Maturidi's scriptural and rational proof for the doctrine that all acts of creatures are created by Allah, a key point against the Mu'tazila who held that humans create their own acts. The passage begins with Quranic evidence: verses where Allah argues from His creation to His knowledge (Al-Mulk 13-14) show that He must be the creator of both hidden and public speech. If He were not, the argument would be invalid. Similarly, verses about causing travel (Yunus 22, Saba' 18) attribute the determination and causing of movement to Allah. Verses about placing affection, mercy, sleep, faith, hardness of heart, and making houses from skins all show that these inner and outer states are acts of Allah — indeed they are called 'signs' (ayat), and it is unlikely that another could originate them. Maturidi then generalizes: Allah is 'Doer of whatever He wills' and among the servants' acts is what He wills. Also, Allah condemns those who want praise for what they did not do, and He obliges believers to praise Him for faith — implying faith is His act.

Then Maturidi adds a rational principle: the evidence for creation of each person's act is even greater than that for creation of the heavens and earth, when considered by reason. No one can know the full capacities of the world's substances to confirm that a creature could create. Rather, we know that things occur beyond a creature's intention and capacity (like flying, penetrating, swimming by nature), showing that the act is determined by another. Thus, by necessity, the one who brings the act into existence is Allah.

Finally, Maturidi engages with Mu'tazila theory: they hold that Allah only brings acts into existence from non-existence (as they consider acts 'things' before existence), but this meaning exists in every act. However, without the revealed command and prohibition, reason would not remove any act from Allah's power and attribute it to the creature. The command and prohibition are true in the sense that they require this attribution; without them, rational knowledge would be overturned by ignorance of wisdom and contingency. Denying this would lead to denying the command itself.

Quranic Proof for Capacity of Means
Maturidi cites the zihar verse and the hypocrites' excuse to prove capacity is of means.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses the zihar verse and the hypocrites' excuse to prove that the Quranic term 'capacity' refers to capacity of means (external resources) rather than capacity of the act (internal ability).

Core Idea
When the Quran says 'cannot,' it means lacks means, not lacks ability.

Maturidi grounds his division of capacity directly in the Quran. He examines two verses where people are described as unable to do something, and shows that in each case the inability is due to a lack of external resources or circumstances, not a failure of their own power to act. This lesson walks through those two proofs and the reasoning behind them.

Third Argument: Hudud as Expiation, No Expiation for Shirk
Maturidi argues that hudud are expiations, but no expiation for shirk, so its punishment is eternal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that prescribed punishments (hudud) are expiations for sins, and that the absence of expiation for shirk implies its eternal punishment.

Core Idea
There is no expiation for shirk in this world, so its punishment is eternal.

Maturidi presents a third argument based on the nature of prescribed punishments (hudud) in this world. He argues that these punishments serve as expiations (kaffarat) for sins. If they were not expiations, they would be increases upon the punishment for disbelief, which is impossible. Therefore, since there is no expiation for disbelief in this world, its punishment must be eternal.

Threat Names: Specificity, Not Generality
Maturidi categorizes threat terms and argues for specific interpretation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Threats are specific; generalizing them contradicts divine pardon.

In this lesson, Imam Maturidi examines the names used in threat verses—such as fisq, fujur, 'isyan, and zulm. He shows that these terms cover three categories of actions, and because they do not always refer to blameworthy acts, the threat must be specific. Furthermore, the existence of verses about Allah's pardon makes a general threat impossible. When ambiguity remains, the proper response is fear, not certainty.

Commentary
This passage contains an objection from Abu Bakr al-Asamm and a threefold refutation by the faqīh (Imam Maturidi). Al-Asamm interprets Quran 4:48 as promising forgiveness for minor sins only, since major sins are already threatened. Finally, the faqīh adds a postscript: al-Asamm claims minor sins are forgiven by wisdom (i.e., rationally) not by promise, but the verse is about defining a category, not about rational judgment.

Ahad Reports Require Ijtihad
Maturidi on the obligation of ijtihad for ahad reports.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why ahad reports do not provide certain knowledge and why ijtihad is necessary to decide whether to accept or reject them.

Core Idea
Ahad reports demand ijtihad because they lack certainty.

Imam Maturidi explains that ahad reports, unlike mutawatir, do not necessitate knowledge or testimony. Instead, they obligate ijtihad—a careful examination of the narrators' conditions and the report's apparent meaning. This lesson unpacks his reasoning and the methodological principle for dealing with such traditions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the nature of ahad reports
Recognize that ahad reports do not reach the degree of certainty that mutawatir reports have. Maturidi states they do not force knowledge or testimony.
This sets the stage for why ijtihad is needed.
2
See the role of ijtihad
Maturidi says action or abandonment requires ijtihad, considering the narrators' conditions and the apparent truth. Note the parallel to sensory knowledge.
3
Grasp the necessity of taking a stance
Abandoning action altogether is not justified because both options involve turning from the report. Ijtihad must weigh both sides.
This principle avoids total rejection.

Commentary
This passage deals with reports that do not reach the level of tawatur (mutawatir) — i.e., ahad reports. Maturidi states that such reports do not give certain knowledge but require ijtihad to either accept or reject them, based on examination of the narrators' conditions. The decision is made according to what is more probable (ghalaba), even though error remains possible. He compares this to sensory knowledge, where even the highest senses can be weak or distant, yet we still act on probability. Abandoning action altogether is not justified because both acting and abandoning involve a turning away from the report; so ijtihad is necessary to weigh the two options. This establishes the methodological principle for dealing with non-mutawatir traditions.

Objection and Answer: Why Can't Entities Be Eternal Like They Last in the Afterlife?
Maturidi answers an objection: if entities can last in the afterlife without enduring, why can't they be eternal without a prior?

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's refutation of the objection that if entities can persist in the afterlife without inherent duration, they could be eternal without prior non-existence.

Core Idea
Revelation and reason operate in different domains; conflating them leads to contradiction.

Maturidi addresses a clever objection: if God can make things last forever in the afterlife even though their continued existence is not intrinsically permanent, then why couldn't things be eternal from the beginning without a prior state of non-existence? He gives two distinct answers—one conceptual and one epistemological—that protect the distinction between creation and afterlife.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice the objection and the two answers.
2
Distinguish the answers
Identify the first answer based on contradiction (tanāquḍ) and the second based on revelation (samʿī).
3
Reflect on the epistemological point
Consider why Maturidi uses the second argument: the objector must accept revelation if he uses a revealed premise.
4
Connect to broader theology
Relate this to the principle that rational proofs of God's existence and creation's temporality are independent of revealed claims about the afterlife.

Second, the claim that entities can last in the afterlife is based on revelation (sam'), not reason, so the objector must accept revelation or his argument collapses.
