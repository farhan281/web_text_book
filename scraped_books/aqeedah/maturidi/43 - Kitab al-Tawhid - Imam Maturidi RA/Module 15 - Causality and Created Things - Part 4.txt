Al-Husayn's Multi-Purpose Answer on Divine Creation
Al-Husayn lists purposes for creation and refutes pure benefit view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Husayn's argument that creation serves multiple purposes beyond mere benefit, including signs, lessons, and trial.

Core Idea
Creation has diverse reasons: indication, lesson, mercy, and trial—not just benefit.

Al-Husayn (al-Najjar) offers a nuanced response to the question 'Why did God create?'. He enumerates several purposes, showing that divine wisdom encompasses more than pure benefit. This passage challenges simplistic views of creation as solely for human ease.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read Al-Husayn's answer carefully, noting the list of purposes he gives.
The list includes: دلالة وحجة, عبرة وعظمة, نعمة ورحمة, غذاء وقوام.
2
Identify the key categories
Notice that Al-Husayn groups purposes into pairs: indication/proof, lesson/majesty, grace/mercy, nourishment/sustenance, and then disposal of needs.
3
Understand the argument against pure benefit
Al-Husayn argues that if creation were only for benefits, no variation (delay, advance, increase, decrease) would be possible. But we see variation, so creation is not purely utilitarian.
He uses the observation of differing outcomes (blessing for one, trial for another) as evidence.
4
Connect to the divine attribute of wisdom
This passage shows that God's wisdom involves placing things in their proper contexts, even turning benefit to harm and vice versa.

Commentary
This passage contains two parts. First, al-Husayn (al-Husayn al-Najjar) gives his answer to the question 'Why creation?'. He enumerates multiple purposes: the creation provides signs and proofs (دلالة وحجة), lessons and majesty (عبرة وعظمة), grace and mercy (نعمة ورحمة), nourishment and sustenance (غذاء وقوام), and general disposal of needs. Moreover, the same thing can be a blessing for one and a trial for another. He argues that if creation were only for benefits, then no change, delay, or variation would be possible. But since we observe variation (misfortune, etc.), it shows that the divine wisdom is not limited to pure benefit but involves placing things in their proper contexts and turning matters from benefit to harm and vice versa. Second, the faqih (Imam al-Maturidi) concludes the chapter by refuting the necessitarian view that God could only do what He actually did. If God had no alternative, then He would have no choice, His action would be unjust (because no act could be better or worse), and He would be dependent on His action for praise — which is impossible for God. The faqih's argument is that if God were compelled to act as He did, then His action would not be freely chosen (مختار), He would be incapable of doing otherwise, and He would need His creation to be praised — all of which are inconsistent with divine perfection.

The Faqih's Refutation of Necessitarian Views
The faqih deduces consequences of necessitarian view: injustice, lack of freedom, and dependence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the faqih's argument that if God could not have done otherwise, His actions would be unjust and He would not be free.

Core Idea
If God had no alternative, He would be unjust and dependent—impossible for the Perfect.

Imam al-Maturidi (the faqih) concludes the chapter by refuting the view that God could only do what He actually did. He argues that such a necessitarian position implies that God's actions are all unjust, that He is not free, and that He would need creation for praise—all contradictions to divine perfection.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the faqih's conclusion carefully, following his logical deductions.
The Arabic has some uncertain readings near the end; focus on the clear parts.
2
Understand the first implication
If God could not do otherwise, then nothing He does can be preferred over another, so He retains the attribute of injustice (الجور).
Because if there is no alternative, then every action is equally necessary, not chosen.
3
Understand the second implication
If God could not do otherwise, He would not be free (مختار). Any other action would be corrupt, implying He cannot reform elsewhere.
This makes Him dependent on a single outcome.
4
Understand the third implication
If God could not do otherwise, He would benefit from His own action and need it for praise—making Him dependent, which is impossible.
Praise is due to God inherently, not because of creation.

Why Reward and Punishment Are Essential for Moral Action
Maturidi explains the rational necessity of promise and threat for moral action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi argues that divine promise and threat are not merely incentives but rationally necessary for humans to act morally.

Core Idea
Promise and threat make moral struggle possible.

Maturidi addresses a deep question: why do we need the promise of heaven and the threat of hell? He explains that human nature shuns what is difficult or unpleasant, so only the prospect of consequences can move us to obey. In this passage, he shows that this is not arbitrary but grounded in how humans are created.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem: the soul's natural aversion
Read the first part of the Arabic passage and notice how Maturidi describes the struggle of the soul against what it naturally dislikes. What is the problem he sets up?
The soul shuns what it dislikes, so commands are inherently difficult.
2
Find the only solution: promise and threat
The passage states that there is no way to overcome the soul's aversion except by bringing to mind promise and threat. See how Maturidi presents this as the sole effective motivator.
This is the core psychological function of reward and punishment.
3
Understand the deeper reason: humans judge actions by consequences
Maturidi then adds that humans are created to consider an action ugly if it does not aim at beneficial consequences or avoid harmful ones. Therefore, actions must have consequences. This is why promise and threat are not arbitrary but fit human nature.
This establishes the rational necessity, not just practicality.
4
Draw the conclusion: without consequences, moral distinctions collapse
The passage concludes that without promise and threat, the outcomes of the obedient and disobedient would be equal, which is unjust. Since people differ in choices, consequences must differ. Summarize the logical flow.
This shows the justice of reward and punishment.

Commentary
This passage explains the psychological function of promise and threat. Maturidi states that commands and prohibitions involve struggling against the soul (mujāhadat al-nafs) and compelling it to do what it naturally dislikes. Since the soul shuns what it dislikes, the only way to overcome this aversion is by contemplating the promised reward and threatened punishment. When a person does that, giving up pleasures and bearing hardships becomes easy. Then Maturidi adds that humans are created to consider an action ugly if it does not aim at beneficial consequences or avoid harmful consequences. Therefore, every action must be assigned such consequences, and this is exactly what promise and threat provide. Without them, the outcomes of the obedient and the disobedient would be equal, which is unjust. Since people differ in their choices and preferences, the consequences must differ accordingly.

The Wisdom Behind Harmful Creatures
Maturidi explains the wisdom behind creating harmful beings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's explanation of the wisdom behind creating harmful creatures, including how they serve as tangible examples for reward and punishment and as a trial that involves reflection.

Core Idea
Harmful creatures are created as tangible examples to help us imagine the consequences of our actions.

This lesson presents Imam al-Maturidi's reasoning on why Allah created harmful beings like snakes and poisonous substances. While human intellect cannot fully grasp divine wisdom, Maturidi highlights two aspects: they serve as a trial by exposing us to harm and benefit, and they provide tangible examples to make the promised reward and punishment imaginable.

The passage has two main points, though the first is not explicitly numbered.

This is the core of the first point.
3
Understand the role of tangible examples

This makes the path easier.
4
Examine the second wisdom

This point is brief and may be incomplete.

Al-Faqih Abu Mansur, may Allah have mercy on him, said: The wisdom in creating snakes and harmful substances (jawahir darra) — even if intellects fall short of reaching the core of divine wisdom, as was previously said about the necessity of wisdom for everything from the aspect which Allah created it, even if its quiddity is not known — is that it is one of the aspects of trial (miḥna) by the harmful and the beneficial present, so that by them one may know the pleasure of reward for obedience and the pain of punishment for disobedience; for creatures are naturally inclined to consider consequences in actions, so He made for them a tangible example (mithāl min al-‘iyān) so that the promised (al-maw‘ūd) may be pictured in imaginations, thereby making the path easy. Commentary
This passage, attributed to 'al-Faqih Abu Mansur' (Imam al-Maturidi), discusses the wisdom (ḥikma) behind the creation of harmful creatures such as snakes and poisonous substances (jawāhir ḍārra). The author acknowledges that human intellects cannot fully comprehend the depth of divine wisdom, but one aspect is that these creatures serve as a trial (miḥna) through the presence of both harmful and beneficial things. This trial allows people to understand the pleasure of reward for obedience and the pain of punishment for disobedience, since humans are naturally inclined to consider consequences of actions. Thus, God provides tangible examples (mithāl min al-‘iyān) so that the promised reward and punishment can be imagined, making it easier to follow the right path. The 'second' point mentions that the trial involves a burden that is easy in some ways but difficult for the body through reflection and thought, and people differ in their capacity for such reflection because there is no immediate benefit.

This passage refutes the second possibility: that composition and separation occur by choice (اختيار). The argument relies on the observed consistency in the world.

Ibn Shabib argues that eternal contradiction is impossible; change proves a temporal originator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ibn Shabib uses the impossibility of eternal contradiction to argue that the world must have a temporal originator.

Core Idea
Eternal contradiction cannot sustain existence; change demands a Creator.

This lesson examines a powerful argument from Muhammad ibn Shabib, an early Islamic theologian. He asks: if the world were eternal and filled with inherent contradictions, could it persist? By analyzing a clever analogy about a house, Ibn Shabib demonstrates that such a world could not exist unless it was brought into being by an external agent. This reasoning challenges the materialist view that the world is eternal.

The Wisdom of Creation: Refuting the Obligation of the Most Beneficial
Maturidi refutes the doctrine of al-aslah and explains that creation is based on wisdom.

Refutation of the Dualists' Claim about Darkness's Movement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's critique of the dualists' claim that darkness moved towards light, questioning whether this movement was eternal or originated.

Core Idea

The dualists claimed that darkness moved towards light and entered it. Maturidi poses a dilemma: either this movement was always (eternal) or it began. Draw the conclusion
Since both alternatives are problematic, the dualists' narrative is untenable. The only valid response is to accept that Allah is the giver of success, indicating dependence on divine guidance.

Commentary
This passage contains several refutations. First, Maturidi criticizes the dualists for claiming knowledge about mixture and separation without having observed or received reliable reports. He demands proof for their claims about the beginning of the world and mixing. Then he presents an alternative: everything tends to combine, not separate. He argues that if one can assume an unseen property without evidence, they could claim anything. Next, he uses an example of a confession to show that actions attributed to good or evil are ambiguous according to their own principles. He also critiques sensory perception: claiming that each sense perceives only its own object leads to contradictions when someone says 'I heard' and 'I saw' using the same faculty. He then poses a question about adding blackness of darkness to blackness of light: if it does not increase, then more equals less; if it does increase, the result is either light or darkness or a third thing, each leading to absurdity. Finally, he argues that if they rely on analogy from the witnessed to the unseen, their claim of eternal separation and infinity is invalid because they have not witnessed the 'five' (possibly the five senses or something else).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Dualists' own principles lead to contradictions in moral and sensory claims.

The Blackness Addition Argument and the Limits of Analogy
Maturidi uses a reductio ad absurdum on blackness addition and critiques the dualists' analogy method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument about adding blackness to blackness and his concluding critique that the dualists' analogy fails because they lack experience of the infinite.

Core Idea
Adding blackness reveals absurdities in dualist ontology, and their analogy method is self-defeating.

Maturidi poses a clever question: if you add the blackness of darkness to the blackness of light, does it increase the blackness? This challenges the dualists' view of light and darkness as separate essences. He explores the logical consequences: if no increase, then more equals less; if increase, then either light or darkness changes into the other, or a third thing emerges. Each option is absurd. Finally, he points out that their entire method of inference from the witnessed to the unseen fails because they have not witnessed the 'five'—likely the five senses or the infinite chains.

Guided Path
1
Read the blackness addition question and the possible answers
Read the passage where Maturidi asks about adding blackness of darkness to blackness of light. Note the two possible answers the dualists could give.
Look for 'فإن قالوا: لا' and 'فإن قالوا: ازداد'
2
Understand why each answer leads to absurdity
Consider why 'no increase' makes 'what is more equal to what is less', and 'increase' forces a change in essence or emergence of a third thing. Each is problematic.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Grasp the final point about the inability to witness the infinite
Read the final part where Maturidi says that the dualists' analogy from witnessed to unseen fails because they have not witnessed the 'five'. Consider what 'the five' might refer to.
The text ends mid-sentence, but the idea is clear: their method is invalid.
