Five Rational Proofs for Prophethood
Al-Maturidi enumerates five rational proofs for the truth of prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the five rational proofs that al-Maturidi presents to establish the truth of prophethood.

Core Idea
Prophethood is rationally established through five observable patterns.

Al-Maturidi distinguishes between sensible signs and rational proofs. While sensible signs are instruments, rational proofs provide the logical foundation. In this passage, he enumerates five arguments, each grounded in divine custom and human need, supported by Quranic verses. These proofs collectively demonstrate that prophethood is neither unprecedented nor arbitrary.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish sensible from rational proofs
Notice that al-Maturidi begins by setting apart the sensible (al-hissiyya) from the rational (al-'aqliyya). Understand why rational proofs are the focus here.
This frames the discussion.
2
Identify the first two proofs
Read the first proof about the continuous custom of sending messengers, and the second about the timing during a period of intermission. See how each is supported by a Quranic verse.
These address the pattern and need.
3
Identify the next two proofs
The third proof focuses on the unlettered recipients, and the fourth on the prominent location of the mission. Note how these show the suitability of the Prophet.
These highlight the audience and place.
4
Understand the fifth proof and conclusion
The fifth proof involves the people's prior oaths and desire for a warner, removing any excuse. Then read the concluding remark about their need.
This completes the five.

And Abu Zayd mentioned that the sensible (signs) are instruments, so the transmitted reports are sufficient. As for the rational (proofs), they are of several aspects: First, that his matter was not unprecedented, but was continuous according to custom in the existence of its like among the nations, therefore the basis for rejecting him is nullified from the outset. Allah Almighty said: 'And there was no nation but that a warner had passed within it' (35:24), and He said: 'Then We sent Our messengers in succession' (23:44). Second, the accordance of his coming at the time of need for him, since it was a time of intermission (fatrah) and the effacement of knowledge, along with Allah's established custom of requiting the means of guidance when its people depart from guidance. Allah said: 'Our messenger has come to you to make clear to you' (5:15). Third, that the one sent among them was in a position of need for him and the emptiness of his kind from the means of knowledge, by His saying: 'It is He who sent among the unlettered a messenger from among them' (62:2) and others. Fourth, his being in the most apparent places for creation, since he is a landmark for the people of the regions in the world, and Allah said: 'And thus We have revealed to you' (42:7). Fifth, the people's wish for that and their showing desire for it, and if a proposer (of an objection) were to propose to his Lord the removal of His proofs, it would not be surprising to cut off his excuse. Allah said: 'And if We had destroyed them with a punishment before it' (20:134), He said: 'And they swore by Allah their strongest oaths that if a warner came to them' (35:42). These five concern their need for him in their circumstances, then their need concerning the circumstances of the Prophet.

Commentary
This passage presents five rational proofs for the truth of prophethood, each supported by Quranic verses. The author begins by distinguishing between sensible signs and rational proofs, then enumerates five aspects. The first proof argues that the coming of prophets is not unprecedented but follows a continuous pattern of sending warners to every nation, so rejecting the Prophet is baseless. The second proof emphasizes the timing of his mission during a period of religious decline and loss of knowledge, fulfilling the need for guidance. The third proof notes that the Prophet was sent to a people lacking scriptural knowledge (the unlettered), so his message was necessary. The fourth proof highlights that his mission occurred in the most prominent region (Mecca), making it universally accessible. The fifth proof refers to the people's prior oaths and desire for a warner, so when he came, they had no excuse. The author concludes that these five aspects address the people's need in their external circumstances, while further proofs relate to the Prophet's personal attributes.

The Prophet's Illiteracy and Divine Revelation
Al-Maturidi argues from the Prophet's illiteracy and Quranic preservation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Prophet's illiteracy and his preservation of the Quran serve as rational proofs for the divine origin of his message.

Core Idea
The Prophet's illiteracy proves his knowledge came from Allah, not human learning.

One of the strongest signs of prophethood is that Muhammad ﷺ grew up in an unlettered environment and never studied books, yet he delivered the Quran with perfect preservation. This lesson examines Al-Maturidi's argument that his illiteracy is not a weakness but a divine proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on illiteracy
Read the Arabic passage and translation, focusing on how Al-Maturidi describes the Prophet's upbringing and his unlettered nature.
Notice the verses cited: 'Do they not recognize their messenger?' and 'These are from the news of the unseen.'
2
Understand the preservation proof
Al-Maturidi argues that the Prophet's extraordinary retention of the Quran, despite being unlettered, is evidence of divine intervention.
He cites 'We will make you recite, and you will not forget' (87:6) and 'Move not your tongue with it' (75:16).
3
Connect to the overarching argument
Reflect on how this proof fits into the broader case for prophethood: only a prophet could have such knowledge without human instruction.

Commentary
This passage continues the rational proofs for prophethood by examining the Prophet's personal qualities. The author presents several arguments: (1) The Prophet grew up among an unlettered people with no books, and he never left them, so he could not have acquired prior knowledge. This is supported by Quranic verses about his recognition and the unseen news he revealed. (2) He was unlettered (ummi), meaning he did not learn from books and could not perfectly memorize from speech; his retention was by spiritual understanding. The evidence is that no one else like him could narrate poetry without error, yet he preserved the Quran perfectly, as Allah promised. (3) He never trained in composing poetry or rhetoric in his youth, yet produced speech that the most skilled orators could not match, as proven by the challenge to produce a surah, which people failed to meet. (4) Allah commanded people to examine his life for any excuse to reject him, and they found none. (5) Unlike false claimants who seek worldly gain, the Prophet rejected offers of wealth and leadership, showing his sincerity. (6) He challenged people to compare his message with other religions, implying that his teachings represent the best of reason. These proofs collectively establish the divine origin of his message.

The Inimitable Speech and Command to Reflect
Al-Maturidi argues from lack of training and the challenge to produce a surah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Prophet's lack of training in poetry and rhetoric, combined with the Quran's inimitability, proves his prophethood.

Core Idea
The untaught Prophet produced speech that experts could not match, confirming divine origin.

Al-Maturidi continues the proofs by noting that before prophethood, the Prophet never practiced composing poetry or rhetoric. Yet the Quran challenged the most skilled orators to produce its like, and they failed. This lesson also highlights the divine command to ponder the Prophet's life for any excuse to reject him.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on rhetorical training
Read the Arabic and translation, focusing on the argument that the Prophet never occupied himself with speechcraft.
The phrase 'ضروب الكلام' refers to the various forms of eloquent speech.
2
Understand the challenge and outcome
Al-Maturidi cites the Quranic challenge 'Bring a surah like it' and notes that the disbelievers remained silent, unable to match it.
This silence is evidence of inimitability.
3
Ponder the command to reflect
The passage also mentions Allah commanding people to examine the Prophet's circumstances, yet they found no valid excuse to reject him.
The verse 'Say: I only advise you with one thing' (34:46) calls for sincere reflection.

Commentary

Commentary
This passage is a composite argument for the prophethood of Muhammad. It begins by citing God's promise 'I will surely prevail, I and My messengers' to establish that the Prophet's victory is divine. Maturidi then contrasts the Prophet's lack of worldly resources—poverty, absence of clan, and initial rejection—with his eventual triumph, proving it is from God. He quotes Quranic verses as evidence. Next, he addresses a potential objection: that prophethood might be confused with soothsaying or sorcery. He distinguishes them by noting that prophets fulfill what they foretell (knowledge of the unseen) without human trickery, whereas soothsayers rely on eavesdropping from devils and mix truth with lies. Sorcery is based on illusion. Prophets receive revelation through angels who convey pure truth, and their actions remain consistent over time. Finally, Maturidi lists many Quranic verses that predicted future events—such as the spread of Islam, victory over enemies, and the fate of disbelievers—all of which were fulfilled. He concludes that for anyone who reflects on these signs, all rational proofs of prophethood are manifest. The passage thus combines logical reasoning from the Prophet's biography and fulfilled prophecies to argue for his divine mission.

The Method of Arguing Against Selective Belief in Prophets
Maturidi outlines the method to argue with those who accept some prophets but reject others.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's methodological approach to arguing against those who accept some prophets but reject others, particularly the Jews and Christians who denied Muhammad.

Core Idea
Consistency in criteria exposes selective belief in prophets as irrational.

Maturidi now turns to a practical challenge: how to engage with people who accept some prophets (e.g., Moses and Jesus) but reject Muhammad. Rather than simply quoting scripture, he proposes a rational method: ask them for the criterion they used to accept the prophets they do accept. Once they give that criterion, it can be tested against Muhammad's prophethood. This passage sets up that logical move.
