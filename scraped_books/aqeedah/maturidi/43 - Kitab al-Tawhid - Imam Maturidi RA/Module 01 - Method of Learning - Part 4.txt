The Analogy of Reflection to the Senses
Maturidi uses an analogy comparing nazar to sense perception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that nazar is for the intellect what the senses are for physical perception—an indispensable tool for reaching realities.

Core Idea
Nazar guides the intellect to truth, just as the senses guide perception of the physical world.

Maturidi strengthens his argument with a powerful analogy: in times of doubt or confusion, people naturally turn to reflection just as they turn to sight when colors are unclear, or hearing when sounds are ambiguous. This shows that nazar is the intellect's go-to tool for clarifying realities. The analogy is not merely illustrative but demonstrates the foundational role of reflection in human cognition.

Guided Path
1
Understand the situation of ambiguity
Read the passage: 'recourse of everyone in the face of calamities and the obstruction of doubts is to reflection'. Think of a time you faced a doubt; what did you do?
2
Analyze the analogy
Maturidi compares nazar to sight when color is ambiguous, hearing when sound is unclear. How does this show the naturalness of reflection?
3
Draw the conclusion
Since reflection is the recourse in difficulties, it must be a reliable guide to realities.

Commentary
This passage is a multi-faceted argument for the necessity of speculative reflection (nazar) in attaining knowledge, particularly of divine matters. It begins by citing Quranic verses that command reflection: showing signs in the horizons, looking at camels, creation, and oneself. These verses demonstrate that God made reflection a means to truth. Then it argues that the only way to refute doubts about the validity of reflection is through reflection itself, implying its foundational role. Next, it states that knowledge of the wisdom in creation—whether it points to a Creator, self-existence, origination, or eternity—requires reflection. Human beings are also entrusted with managing worldly affairs and discerning beneficial from harmful actions, which depends on reflection. The overall argument establishes reflection as the indispensable tool for theological and practical knowledge.

Reflection in Distinguishing Good from Evil
Maturidi argues nazar is necessary for moral discernment and resolving natural-intellectual conflicts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that nazar is necessary to discern the moral qualities of actions and to resolve conflicts between human nature and intellect.

Core Idea
Nazar is required to distinguish good from evil and to harmonize nature and intellect.

In the final arguments, Maturidi shows that reflection is needed to assess the good and bad qualities of actions, especially when reports and senses provide information. Moreover, human beings are composed of both nature (tabi'a) and intellect ('aql), which often disagree. Only through reflection can one determine which inclination to follow. This cements nazar as essential for moral and practical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of knowledge
Read the first part: 'the good qualities and bad qualities of things... the ultimate knowledge after the occurrence of the senses and the arrival of reports'. Why is reflection still needed?
Senses and reports give raw data, but reflection interprets them.
2
Understand the conflict between nature and intellect
The second part: human beings have both nature and intellect, which may disagree. Reflection is needed to evaluate which is correct.
3
Apply to everyday ethics
Think of a situation where your nature desires something but your intellect deems it wrong. How does reflection help decide?

Affirming Names Without Likening
Imam Maturidi explains the method of naming God without tashbih.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Maturidi's method of affirming divine names and attributes without falling into anthropomorphism, using the principle of shahid (witnessed) to gha'ib (absent).

Core Idea
We name God using witnessed terms but always negate similarity.

Imam Maturidi addresses a critical theological puzzle: how can we use human language for God without likening Him to creation? He proposes a method that affirms names and attributes while negating any likeness, grounding our knowledge in the witnessed world (shahid) to speak about the unseen (gha'ib). This passage sets a foundational rule for Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then its translation. Notice the opening assertion and the flow of the argument.
The passage is dense; take your time.

4
Reflect on the negation
Consider why the author insists on negating similarity. This guards against literal anthropomorphism.
5
Appreciate the conclusion

Shaykh Abu Mansur, may Allah have mercy on him, said: There is no likeness in affirming names and verifying attributes to negate the realities of what is in creation from Him, like death and stability. But names do not admit definition nor verification of the essence by the right of lordship except by that? For there is no way to know the absent except by indication of the witnessed. Then when one intends the attribute of highness and majesty, that is the way of knowledge in the witnessed, and the possibility of speech; for we cannot within our capacity have knowledge by naming with other than what we have witnessed, nor indication to what we do not take from the senses and the right of sight. If we could within our capacity do that, we would say that. But we intend by it what removes likeness from our saying: 'Knower not like the knowers', and this type applies to everything we name and describe Him with. And Allah is the granter of success.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological statement by Imam Maturidi on how to affirm names and attributes for God without falling into tashbih (anthropomorphism). He begins by asserting that affirming names and attributes does not imply similarity to creation, because we are not affirming the realities found in creation (like death or stability) for God. Rather, names are used to define and verify God's essence, but only through the method of analogy from the witnessed world (shahid). Since we cannot know the absent (gha'ib) except through the witnessed (shahid), we use familiar terms like 'knower' but with the understanding that God is 'knower not like the knowers' ('alim la ka-l-'ulama'). This negation of similarity is applied to every name and attribute we use for God. The ultimate guidance is from Allah.

Evidence Is Necessary for Reports
Maturidi argues for the necessity of evidence in reports.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi insists that reports require evidence, and how all knowledge beyond direct observation relies on inference.

Core Idea
Without evidence, no report can be preferred over another — even everyday knowledge rests on inference.

Imam Maturidi begins by noting that many reports have proven true, but that does not mean we can accept any claim without evidence. In this passage, he establishes a foundational principle: to distinguish truth from falsehood in reports, we need a clear proof (dalil). He then extends this argument to show that even our basic recognition of things involves inferential reasoning, making evidence an unavoidable necessity.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Revelation cannot prove its own truthfulness without reason.

Do you rely more on what you have heard from others than on what you can observe in your own life? Have you ever accepted a religious claim without checking if it aligns with the observable reality Allah has placed before you? Will you commit to making direct observation—your own senses and reason—a foundation for your faith starting today?
?
Maturidi says hearing (revelation) is known to be true through observable things. Can you name one observable sign in your daily life that confirms the truth of a specific Islamic teaching? If not, what might that say about the strength of your certainty?
?
If direct observation is the foundation for knowledge of the hidden, then your knowledge of Allah begins with what you see in creation. When did you last consciously reflect on an observable part of the universe—a sunset, a cell, a moral law—and let it increase your certainty in the unseen? Commit to doing that this week and see if it changes your heart.
