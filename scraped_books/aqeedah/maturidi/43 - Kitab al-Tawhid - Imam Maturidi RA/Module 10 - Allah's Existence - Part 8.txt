God Existed Without Place – The Principle and Signs of Contingency
Maturidi states the principle and lists signs of contingency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's foundational principle that God's existence precedes all place, and how change, cessation, transformation, and annihilation are signs of contingency that do not apply to God.

Core Idea
God's timeless existence without place is proven by His immunity to the signs of contingency.

Maturidi begins his treatise by laying down a fundamental principle: God existed eternally before any place existed. He then lists the marks of created things—change, cessation, transformation, annihilation—to argue that these cannot apply to the Creator. This lesson walks you through that opening argument, showing how it establishes divine transcendence over time and space.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text once without analysis. Then read the translation to get the literal meaning. Let the author's own phrasing sink in.
Pay attention to the list of four signs—they are central.
2
Identify the core principle
Locate the phrase 'الأصل فيه أن الله سبحانه كان ولا مكان'. This is the thesis: God's existence is prior to all place.
The word 'مكان' (place) is the key term.
3
Spot the four signs of contingency
The passage enumerates 'التغير والزوال والاستحالة والبطلان' as signs of createdness. Understand each one.
These are evidence that something is not eternal.
4
Connect signs to God's nature
Ask yourself: Since God is eternal, why can't these signs apply to Him? The passage argues that if God changed, His first state would not be essential—contradicting His necessity.
This links the signs to the argument for God's unchanging essence.

قال أبو منصور رحمه الله : الأصل فيه أن الله سبحانه كان ولا مكان وجائز ارتفاع الأمكنة وبقاؤه على ما كان ، فهو على ما كان ، وكان على ما عليه الآن ، جل عن التغير والزوال والاستحالة والبطلان ؛ إذ ذلك أمارات الحدث التي بها عرف حدث العالم ، ودلالة احتمال الفناء ؛ إذ لا فرق بين الزوال من حال إلى حال ، ليعلم أن حاله الأولى لم تكن لذاته ؛ إذ لا يحتمل زوال ما لزم ذاته . وبين أنها ليست لذاته ، لما احتمل هو قبول الأعراض وانتقال الأحوال، ولا قوة إلا بالله . و بعد ، فإن فى تحقيق المكان له والوصف له بذاته فى كل مكان تمكين الحاجة له إلى ما به قراره على مثل جميع الأجسام والأعراض التي قامت بالأمكنة وفيها تقلبت وقرب، على خروج جملتها عن الوصف بالمكان ، فمن أنشأها وأمسك كيانها لا يماثلها، ويتعالى عن الحاجة إلى مكان ، أو الوصف بما عليه العالم أن كليته لا في مكان ، وأنه يجزئياته في المكان. إن الله تعالى لو جعل في مكان لجعل بحق الجزئية من العالم، وذلك أثر النقصان ، بل لما استقام قيام جميع العالم لا بالأمكنة للجملة ، فقيمه على ذلك أحق وأولى ، ولا قوة إلا بالله
Abu Mansur (may Allah have mercy on him) said: The principle in this is that Allah, glorified be He, existed and there was no place, and it is possible for places to cease to exist while He remains as He was. So He is as He was, and He was as He is now, exalted above change, cessation, transformation, and annihilation; for those are signs of contingency by which the contingency of the world is known, and an indication of the possibility of perishing; for there is no difference between passing from one state to another, so that one knows that His first state was not by His essence; for it is not possible for that which is necessary for His essence to pass away. And it is clarified that they are not by His essence, because He is subject to the reception of accidents and the transition of states. And there is no power except with Allah. And after that, indeed, in establishing place for Him and describing Him by His essence in every place is enabling need for Him for that by which He is established, like all bodies and accidents that subsist in places and in which they change and draw near, while their entirety is beyond description with place. So the One who originated them and holds their existence is not like them, and is exalted above need for a place, or the description that the world is such that its totality is not in a place, while its particulars are in a place. Indeed, if Allah were placed in a place, He would be placed by right of particularity from the world, and that is an effect of deficiency. Rather, since the existence of the entire world is established without places for the whole, then the One who establishes it is more worthy and deserving of that. And there is no power except with Allah.

Commentary
This passage is a foundational argument for divine transcendence over place. Imam al-Maturidi starts with the principle (الأصل) that God existed eternally before any place existed, and even if all places were to cease, He would remain unchanged. Change, cessation, and transformation are signs of contingency (حدث) and potential annihilation. Since God is eternal and unchanging, He cannot undergo such changes. The author then argues that if God were described as being in a place, He would need that place to provide stability, just as bodies and accidents need places. But God is the Creator and Sustainer of all places, so He is unlike them and has no need for place. Moreover, if God were in a place, He would be a part of the world (جزئية), which is a deficiency. Since the whole world exists without needing a place for its totality (the world is not itself in a place), the Creator is even more entitled to be free from place. The passage concludes by affirming that God is exalted above all spatial limitations.

Refuting the Need for Place – God's Transcendence Over Spatial Limitation
Maturidi refutes that God needs or occupies place, using need and particularity arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that God cannot be in a place because place implies need, limitation, and deficiency, whereas God is the Originator of all places.

Core Idea
Placing God in a space would make Him dependent and a part of the world – an absurd deficiency.

Maturidi now tackles a common but mistaken theology: that God could be literally in a place. He argues that if God were in a place, He would need that place for stability, just as bodies and accidents do. Such need is incompatible with God's self-sufficiency. Moreover, being in a place would reduce God to a particular part of the world (جزئية), implying deficiency. This lesson unpacks that powerful argument, concluding with an analogy: the world as a whole is not itself in a place, so its Creator is even more free from place.

Guided Path

Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text beginning with 'و بعد، فإن في تحقيق المكان له' and follow the argument. Use the translation to check understanding.
The passage is dense – take it slowly.
2
Analyze the 'need' argument
Maturidi argues that if God were in a place, He would need that place for stability (تمكين الحاجة). Identify this reasoning.
This is similar to how bodies need places.
3
Examine the 'particularity' argument
He says that if God were in a place, He would be a 'juz'iyya' (particular) of the world, which is an effect of deficiency (أثر النقصان). Understand why.
Being confined to a specific location implies limitation.
4
Consider the analogy
Maturidi observes that the world as a whole is not in a place (كليته لا في مكان), yet its parts are. So the Creator is even more deserving of being beyond place.
This is a logical inference from the world's own structure.

Proof for Seeing God from the Negation of Idrak
Maturidi affirms the vision of God and gives a proof from the verse negating idrak.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's proof that the negation of idrak in the Quran implies the possibility of seeing God in the hereafter without encompassing.

Core Idea
The verse 'No vision can grasp Him' proves we can see Him, but not encompass Him.

Maturidi opens a crucial topic: the beatific vision of Allah in the hereafter. He insists it is obligatory to believe in, yet clarifies it is not a comprehensive perception (idrak) nor does it require anthropomorphic interpretation (tafsir). Then he offers a striking proof from a well-known Quranic verse, turning the apparent negation into an affirmation of a real but non-encompassing vision.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic text once, then the translation. Notice how Maturidi first states the doctrine, then provides the proof.
Pay attention to the terms 'ru'ya' (vision) and 'idrak' (comprehensive perception).
2
Analyze the proof structure
Identify the verse cited (6:103) and follow Maturidi's reasoning: If God were absolutely invisible, negating 'idrak' would be pointless because other things can be known without being seen. Therefore, the negation of 'idrak' implies that 'ru'ya' is possible but not encompassing.
The key is distinguishing between 'idrak' and 'ru'ya'.
3
Reflect on the implication
Consider what this tells us about the nature of seeing God: it is real, but unlike seeing physical objects, it does not grasp or encompass Him.
This preserves both God's transcendence and the reality of the vision.

قال أبو منصور رحمة الله : القول في رؤية الرب عز وجل عندنا لازم وحق من غير إدراك ولا تفسير . فأما الدليل على الرؤية فقوله تعالى: لَا تُدْرِكُهُ الأَبْصَارُ وَهُوَ يُدْرِكُ الأَبْصَارَ، ولو كان لا يرى لم يكن لنفي الإدراك حكمة؛ إذ يدرك غيره بغير رؤية ، فموضع نفى الإدراك - وغيره من الخلق لا يدرك إلا بالرؤية . لا معنى له ، وبالله التوفيق .
Abu Mansur (may Allah have mercy on him) said: The statement concerning the vision of the Lord, Mighty and Majestic, is necessary for us and true, without [literal] perception (idrak) or interpretation (tafsir). As for the proof for the vision, it is His statement: "No vision can grasp Him, but He grasps all vision." And if He were not to be seen, there would be no wisdom in negating idrak; for other than Him is perceived by other than vision. So the place of negating idrak – while other created beings are not perceived except by vision – would have no meaning. And with Allah is success.

Commentary
This passage presents Maturidi's position on the beatific vision. He begins by stating that the belief in seeing God (in the hereafter) is obligatory and true, but it is not a perception that encompasses God (idrak) nor does it require interpretation in the sense of likening to physical sight. Then he provides a rational proof based on the verse "No vision can grasp Him" (6:103). He argues: If God were absolutely invisible, then negating idrak (which is a form of vision that encompasses) would be meaningless, because other things can be known without being seen. But the verse specifically negates idrak, implying that there is a form of seeing that does not encompass – hence vision is possible. The argument is concise: The verse does not say 'He cannot be seen' but 'no vision can grasp Him', leaving open the possibility of a vision without comprehension.

Mu'tazila Imply Eternity of the World and Agreement with Dahriyya
Maturidi shows the shay'iyya doctrine entails eternity of the world and aligns with Dahriyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Mu'tazilite doctrine of non-existent things leads to the conclusion that the world is eternal and aligns with atheistic materialism.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila's 'things' doctrine makes the world eternal, contradicting monotheism.

Maturidi continues his critique of the Mu'tazila by exposing the logical consequences of their claim that non-existent things are eternal. He shows that this forces them to say the world is eternal, which all monotheists reject. Then he strengthens his point by comparing them unfavorably to the Dahriyya (materialists). In this passage, you will see how a seemingly minor philosophical shift leads to a major theological error.

Guided Path
1

3
See how that implies world's eternity
Since the world is all that is not God, if non-existent things are eternal, then the world as a category must also be eternal. Maturidi argues this undermines creation ex nihilo.
4
Notice the comparison with Dahriyya

The World's Lack of Self-Sufficiency Refutes Deniers
Maturidi refutes the denial of the world as proof for God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that the world cannot be self-sufficient and that denying it as proof for God leads to logical inconsistency.

Core Idea
Nothing in the world is self-caused; its existence demands a Creator.

Maturidi begins by establishing that the world has no inherent meaning or self-existence. Those who deny the world as evidence for God end up making the world self-caused, which is a contradiction. This lesson explores the initial refutation.

Guided Path
1

First Proof Against Dualism: The Argument from Power and Knowledge
Maturidi presents the first logical proof against dualism based on power and knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument that two equal gods would necessarily be ignorant or weak, which negates their divinity.

Core Idea
Two gods cannot both be omniscient and omnipotent without conflict.

Maturidi presents the first of two classic kalam arguments against the possibility of two gods. This argument focuses on the implications of mutual power and knowledge. If two gods exist, each must be able to act independently, leading to ignorance or weakness — both incompatible with true divinity. Let us examine this reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage for the first proof
Read the Arabic text carefully. Notice how the argument is structured as a conditional: if the dualists say 'yes' (each can act independently), they admit ignorance; if they say 'no', they admit weakness.
The key terms are 'جهل' (ignorance) and 'عجز' (weakness).
2
Understand the conditional structure
The proof hinges on a dilemma: either each god can prevent the other's actions (implying they know each other's intentions) or they cannot. But both options lead to a flaw in divinity.
3
Grasp why ignorance and weakness negate lordship
Lordship (rububiyya) requires complete knowledge and power. Ignorance and weakness are direct opposites. Therefore, if the dualists admit either, they have effectively denied the gods' divinity.
Reflect on why even one god being ignorant would be unacceptable.
4
Reflect on the necessity of one God
The only way to preserve perfect knowledge and power is to affirm a single God who is omniscient and omnipotent. This logical conclusion reinforces tawhid.
This proof is a classic example of kalam reasoning.

Abu Mansur (may God have mercy on him) said: And the people of tawhid (Ahl al-Tawhid) argued for the negation of the dualists' statement with two points: one of them is by the power of each of them to prevent something from the other, and to be able to do what the other does not know. So if they say: yes, they made them ignorant, or one of them ignorant; and if they say: no, they made them weak. Ignorance and weakness negate lordship (rububiyya). Commentary
This passage presents two logical proofs from Ahl al-Tawhid (the people of divine unity) against the dualists who claim two eternal gods. The first proof: each of two equal gods would have the power to prevent something from the other and to do something unknown to the other. If the dualists admit that each can do so (say 'yes'), then they have ascribed ignorance to the gods (since one is unaware of the other's action). If they deny this ability (say 'no'), then they have ascribed weakness to them (since they cannot hinder). Both ignorance and weakness are incompatible with lordship (rububiyya), which requires omniscience and omnipotence. The second proof: if two gods exist, what one wills to bring into existence, the other may will to negate or prevent. This leads to contradiction (tanāquḍ) between their wills, which cannot coexist in a perfect being. Therefore, the actual existence of the world—being consistent and orderly—points to the oneness of the Creator (al-Wahid).

The Maturidi Argument: From Human Limitation to Divine Perfection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that knowing one's own ignorance, dependence, and incapacity leads to the necessary knowledge of a perfect Creator.

Core Idea
Our very weakness and ignorance prove our total dependence on a perfect, self-sufficient God.

After rejecting flawed interpretations, Maturidi presents the Sunni position. He argues that true self-knowledge reveals profound limitations: we are ignorant of our own faculties, unable to repair our own corruption, bound by time and place, and incapable of comprehending the full sweep of our own existence. Refuting Change in God: The Argument from Contingency
Maturidi refutes Jahm and others who claim change in God using the argument from contingency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why claiming that God acquires new attributes (like knowledge or power over time) is false, because it would imply change, which is a mark of contingency.

Core Idea
Change belongs to creatures, not to the Creator.

After establishing that God is utterly different from creation, Imam Maturidi turns to refute a specific error: the belief that God was not always knowing, powerful, or active but later became so. He will show that such a claim actually forces God into the very creaturely categories we just used to deny. The same principle—that change and possibility are signs of contingency—serves as the backbone of the refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the refutation passage

The phrase 'upon this is invalidated' shows connection.
2
Identify the two erroneous claims
Jahm claimed God became knowing and powerful after not being so; others claimed He became an agent and speaker after not being so. Both imply change.
3
Understand why change is impossible for God
Change is a property of contingent beings—it comes from the same category as 'states and aspects' that define creatures. If God changed, He would be like creatures.
4
See the positive argument from human possibility
The fact that humans can assume attributes (knowledge, power) and experience varying states (good, evil) proves that they are not self-sufficient, and their management must belong to a being beyond change and possibility.

Commentary
This passage is a complex proof for the oneness of God (tawheed) and a refutation of polytheism and dualism. It begins with a doxology and then presents the core argument: the world shows signs of being created and governed by an external power. Every substance in the world bears witness that it is a contrived manager (mudabbar mafṭūr), meaning it is not self-sufficient but is managed by another. The world is compelled by a wise ordering that prevents contradiction and dissolution. This ordering cannot come from multiple managers because that would inevitably lead to conflict, each wanting to assert his authority, resulting in annihilation and corruption. The only way multiple managers could work is if one is supremely powerful and all others submit, but then that one is effectively the sole manager. The passage then continues onto the next page, discussing the wonder of the world's origin and continuation. The initial creation is as wondrous as its continued existence, and the need for a creator is even more evident from the fact that the world is incapable of managing itself. The argument from the beginning of the world—its coming into being after non-existence—is presented as a clear proof. The weakness and decay observable in things compel the rational person to recognize that the world came into being. Furthermore, the dead and the living all point to the same Manager, because both are sustained by similar causes. Thus, the proof concludes that the world must have a single, wise, and powerful Creator.

Commentary
This passage is Maturidi's refutation of the dualist arguments. He begins by stating that if the dualists had properly considered the earlier proofs, they would realize the limitation of their intellects regarding human wisdom, let alone divine wisdom. The refutation proceeds as follows: First, within their own doctrine, since each essence produces only its own kind (good from light, evil from darkness), and humans are a mixture, a person's judgment about wisdom or foolishness is unreliable—he might call wisdom foolishness and vice versa, because his speech comes from both essences, making his statements untrustworthy. Second, if neither essence has power over the other (light cannot harm, darkness cannot benefit), then hope and fear are eliminated, and the very benefit of distinguishing wisdom and foolishness is lost. Third, since each essence acts by nature, not by choice, wisdom (placing things in their proper place) and foolishness (misplacing them) cannot be attributed to them, because natural action lacks discrimination. Furthermore, if light knows foolishness and avoids it, but darkness does not know wisdom, then light's essence becomes a mix of knowledge and ignorance, power and incapacity—it cannot prevent darkness from harming it. Thus, good becomes mixed with evil, and evil dominates good. But if good does not know evil, how can it produce good and evil after being dominated? Finally, anything acting by nature is subdued and does not control its own effects, which necessitates a subduer who makes one evil by nature and another good. If one postulates two such natures, they still require an external cause (like heating and cooling require a source). This leads to the necessity of affirming a single Creator (al-Wāḥid).

Nature Necessitates a Single Subduer

Whatever acts by nature is subdued and points to a subduer.

In this continuation, Maturidi shifts to the argument from natural action. The dualists claim the two essences act by nature, not choice. Maturidi shows this leads to contradictions: wisdom and foolishness cannot be attributed to natures, light is mixed with ignorance, and evil dominates good. Ultimately, anything acting by nature is subdued, necessitating a subduer who makes them as they are, which leads to affirming the One.

Commentary
This passage is Maturidi's refutation of the dualist arguments. He begins by stating that if the dualists had properly considered the earlier proofs, they would realize the limitation of their intellects regarding human wisdom, let alone divine wisdom. The refutation proceeds as follows: First, within their own doctrine, since each essence produces only its own kind (good from light, evil from darkness), and humans are a mixture, a person's judgment about wisdom or foolishness is unreliable—he might call wisdom foolishness and vice versa, because his speech comes from both essences, making his statements untrustworthy. Second, if neither essence has power over the other (light cannot harm, darkness cannot benefit), then hope and fear are eliminated, and the very benefit of distinguishing wisdom and foolishness is lost. Third, since each essence acts by nature, not by choice, wisdom (placing things in their proper place) and foolishness (misplacing them) cannot be attributed to them, because natural action lacks discrimination. Furthermore, if light knows foolishness and avoids it, but darkness does not know wisdom, then light's essence becomes a mix of knowledge and ignorance, power and incapacity—it cannot prevent darkness from harming it. Thus, good becomes mixed with evil, and evil dominates good. But if good does not know evil, how can it produce good and evil after being dominated? Finally, anything acting by nature is subdued and does not control its own effects, which necessitates a subduer who makes one evil by nature and another good. If one postulates two such natures, they still require an external cause (like heating and cooling require a source). This leads to the necessity of affirming a single Creator (al-Wāḥid).

Maturidi begins by attacking the foundational claim of the Naturalists: that natures are independent causal agents. He points out a simple fact: a nature cannot help but do what it does, yet it can be prevented by something else. This shows its action is not its own. Let's examine his reasoning.

Argument from Opposing Natures
Maturidi uses the coexistence of fire/heat and cold to prove a wise manager.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the coexistence of opposing natural properties like heat and cold in one body points to a wise and powerful manager.

Core Idea
Opposing natures cannot unite without an external wise controller.

Maturidi begins with a simple observation: heat rises, cold descends, yet they combine in a single living body. This cannot happen by nature alone; it demands a manager who overrules these tendencies. In this lesson, you will see how this everyday fact leads to the affirmation of a powerful, knowing Creator.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Note the contrast between the natural tendencies and their unity.
2
Identify the two opposing natures
The passage mentions heat (rising) and cold (descending). Observe how Maturidi uses their natural opposition to set up the argument.
3
Reflect on the logical inference
The conclusion 'that this is because of a manager, subjugator, knowing' is not stated as a proof but as an immediate inference. Ask yourself: why can't nature itself cause this unity?
The key is that opposite natures would repel each other, so something external must force them together.

And whoever says that substances (a'yan) are eternal, we find them not free from originated accidents, which prevents acceptance, for several reasons: one, there is emptiness in eternity, and that is a denial of the testimony of observation; second, the existence of many substances and their beginning for counted durations, and they are from the end of the sum that what the whole can bear, therefore the statement is necessary. Commentary
This passage contains two arguments. First, Maturidi points to the observable fact that heat rises and cold sinks by their natural tendencies, yet they coexist in a single body (e.g., a living organism). This harmony cannot be explained by nature alone; it requires a wise manager (mudabbir qahir 'alim) who overrides natural tendencies. Second, he refutes the eternity of substances (a'yan). He argues that substances are never free from originated accidents (hawadith), so they must themselves be originated. He rejects the theory of 'kumun' (hiddenness) which claims that substances pre-exist in a hidden state and then appear, because that would imply contradiction—e.g., one thing becoming a place for ten. The argument concludes that if substances are originated, then the world's existence from nothing is plausible. Finally, he notes that forms (suwar) require a form-giver (musawwir).

Correction: Maturidi's argument is metaphysical: it relies on the impossibility of contradictory natures forcing unity without an external agent. Natural processes themselves have opposite tendencies, so their coordination points to a wise director.

Refuting the Eternity of Substances
Maturidi gives two reasons against eternal substances and refutes the hiddenness theory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Maturidi argument against the eternity of substances by showing they are never free from originated accidents, and the refutation of the hiddenness (kumun) theory.

Core Idea
Nothing that always has changes can be eternal.

Maturidi now takes on a key rival view: that substances (a'yan) are eternal. He points out that substances are never found without originated accidents, which means they cannot be eternal. He also refutes the theory of hiddenness (kumun) as an alternative explanation. This lesson unpacks these crucial arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the two distinct objections listed with 'one' and 'second'.
The passage is denser; take your time.
2
Identify the two reasons against eternity
First reason: eternal substances would be empty of accidents in pre-eternity, contradicting observation. Second: many substances have a clear beginning in time, so they are not eternal.
3
Understand the rejection of kumun
The theory of hiddenness says substances pre-exist and then appear. Maturidi rejects it because it would imply that one thing can become the place for many, which is impossible. Also, he shows that if kumun were possible, then creation from nothing would also be possible, undermining the opponent's position.

Argument from Forms for a Form-Giver
Maturidi argues that forms require a form-giver.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that all observed forms require a form-giver, leading to the affirmation of Allah as Musawwir (the Form-Giver).

Core Idea
Every form points to a form-giver.

In the final part of this passage, Maturidi uses a simple but powerful observation: every observed form must have a form-giver. Just as we see patterns in nature that indicate a maker, the existence of forms (suwar) in the world necessitates a Designer. This lesson highlights the argument from design at a fundamental level.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation. This is a short but complete argument.
The last part about the copyist may be a scribal addition; focus on the core reasoning.
2
Identify the key premise
Maturidi states that observation and its description are never devoid of forms. This means every observed thing has a form.
3
Draw the inference to a form-giver
If forms exist, they must come from a source that gives forms. Just as any designed object implies a designer, forms imply a form-giver (musawwir).
This is a straightforward a posteriori argument for God as the origin of all forms.

The Principle of Natures and Their Need for an Origin
Maturidi establishes that natures need an origin.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi proves the existence of an origin (منشئ) for natural substances based on the principle that natures only act on prepared recipients.

Core Idea
Natures do not cause themselves; their need for a recipient proves an origin.

Maturidi begins his argument by examining how natural substances operate. He observes that any nature only affects something that is suitably prepared to receive its influence. This principle, he argues, shows that natures themselves are not self-sufficient but require an external origin.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on 'كل ذي طبع'
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the examples of pain, pleasure, and dyes.
These examples illustrate how a nature only acts on something that can accept its effect.
2
Identify the key principle
Maturidi states that it is not the work of nature to make something accept its nature. This implies that natures are not self-originating.
3
Understand the conclusion
The passage concludes that the existence of natures points to an origin (منشئ) beyond them.
This is the first step in the larger argument for a director.

ثم كل ذي طبع لا يعمل في شيء بطبعه إلا أن يكون الآخر مجعولا بحيث يقبل ذلك، نحو الشيء الذي يتأذى لا يؤذيه الفعل الذي في غيره مؤذ، وكذلك المؤلم والملذ، وكذلك الأصباغ. وليس عمل الطبع أن يجعل شيئا يقبل طبعه ويتأثر به، فثبت به كون غير الطبائع. مع ما لو خلى بين ذي الطبع وعمله لكان لا يؤلف ولا يصور، فدل وجودها على غير ذلك: أن لها منشئا. وبعد، فإنه لو خلى بين الأصباغ وانصباغ الأشياء بها ليخرج فاسدا مسمما وإنما يصلح ذلك الحكيم العليم يضع كل شيء موضعه، فمثله أمر الطبائع وهو في شأن الطبائع أحق؛ إذ هي تتنافر وفيها التباعد، أو يقدح في الأشياء بلا حد، وفيه الفساد، فدل الاتساق وقيام الأعيان بها على عليم قاهر جمع بينها ما كان لكل مجتمع الطبائع حامل يحملها ليس هو لهن، فثبت بالضرورة وجودهن. وقد نجا الحرارة ترتفع بطبعها والبرودة تنحدر وقد يجتمعان في جسم، فثبت أن ذلك لمدير قاهر عليم.
Then every possessor of a nature does not act in anything by its nature unless the other is made such that it accepts that, like a thing that is hurt does not hurt it by an action that is hurtful in another, and likewise the painful and the pleasurable, and likewise dyes. And it is not the work of nature to make something accept its nature and be affected by it, so the existence of non-natures is established. With that, if one left between the possessor of a nature and its work, it would not compose nor form, so their existence indicates something else: that they have an origin. And furthermore, if one left between dyes and the dyeing of things with them, it would come out corrupt and poisonous, but only the wise All-Knowing sets everything in its place, and likewise the matter of natures, and He is more entitled in the affair of natures, since they repel each other and have distance, or it would strike at things without limit, and in that is corruption. So the harmony and the subsistence of entities by them indicate a knowing Compeller who brought them together, that every aggregate of natures has a carrier that carries them not being one of them, so their existence is necessarily established. And indeed heat rises by its nature and cold descends, yet they meet in a body, so it is established that that is due to a powerful knowing Director.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof for the existence of a wise, powerful director based on the behavior of natural substances. Maturidi begins by observing that anything with a nature only acts upon something that is prepared to receive its effect. For example, a painful thing only hurts something that can feel pain. This shows that natures themselves do not originate action arbitrarily; they require suitable recipients. He then argues that if left to themselves, natures would not produce orderly forms or compositions—they would cause disorder. For instance, if dyes were left without a wise dyer, they would produce poisonous results. Similarly, the various conflicting natures, which repel each other, could not harmoniously coexist without a wise Compeller who brings them together and sustains them in a single body. As a specific example, heat naturally rises and cold naturally descends, yet they coexist in a single body. This impossible without a director who is both powerful and knowing. Thus, the argument concludes that there must be a directing agent beyond the natures themselves.

Critique of Prime Matter Believers: Eternal Matter That Perishes
Maturidi refutes those who believe in an eternal prime matter that later changes and perishes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's refutation of those who posit an eternal prime matter that later transforms into perishable objects.

Core Idea
Believing in eternal prime matter undermines God's unique pre-eternity.

Maturidi now turns to another group: those who affirm an eternal prime matter (al-ṭīna wa al-hayūlā) as a single eternal substance, but then claim it undergoes change into countless perishable objects. This passage exposes the incoherence of making something eternal and then subject to change and annihilation.

Guided Path
1
Read Maturidi's critique
Read the Arabic and translation. Note the progression: they make it one, then eliminate it, then make countless things that transition and perish.
2
Analyze the logical problem
If prime matter is eternal and one, how can it become many and perishable? This contradicts the nature of the eternal.
The eternal cannot change or cease.
3
Recognize the theological implication
By positing an eternal matter alongside God, they compromise God's unique eternity. Maturidi implicitly shows this is another form of shirk.

Commentary
This passage criticizes two groups: the Dahrīs (materialists) who affirm the Creator and His pre-eternity but then posit that all individual entities are also co-eternal with Him, thereby violating tawhid by associating partners in pre-eternity. The second group believes in an eternal prime matter (al-ṭīna wa al-hayūlā) which they treat as one eternal thing, but then it undergoes change and becomes countless objects that perish, thus undermining the unique pre-eternity of God.

Affirmation of Correct Tawhid: God’s Unique Essence and Transcendence
Maturidi summarizes the correct doctrine of tawhid in positive attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's concise formulation of correct tawhid, including the unique nature of God's essence, His transcendence above limitations, and His essential attributes.

Core Idea
God's oneness is unique, not numerical; He transcends all limitations and is described by pre-eternity and power.

After refuting various erroneous sects, Imam al-Maturidi here presents the positive doctrine of tawhid in a single, tightly packed sentence. This formulation affirms God's unique essence, negates all anthropomorphic and limiting qualities, and establishes the essential attributes that truly describe Him. Let's unpack this rich statement.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the literal translation. Let the words sink in.
Notice the compressed style—every phrase carries theological weight.
2
Identify the three parts
The passage has three main parts: affirmation of essence (unique oneness), negations (of number, change, limitation), and positive attributes (pre-eternity, power, origination).
3
Understand 'wāḥidī al-dhāt'
This phrase means God's essence is unique, not composed, and not subject to quantity. It is different from saying 'one' in a numerical sense.
The common misunderstanding is that this implies atomism.
4
Reflect on the negations
Why are negations of number, change, and limitation necessary? They safeguard God's transcendence and prevent anthropomorphism.
Each negation protects a different aspect of tawhid.
5
Note the essential attributes
The passage mentions three attributes: qidam (pre-eternity), takwīn (origination), and qudra (power). These are fundamental for understanding God's relationship to creation.
These are not exhaustive, but key in Maturidi's system.

Source Text

فحصل قول فريق بالتوحيد أنه واحدى الذات ، إليه حاجات الآحاد ، متعال عن معنى الآحاد عما يوجب صفة الأعداد ويتنكن فيه صفة التغير والزوال أو المحدود والنهاية ، موصوف بالقدم والتكوين والقدرة جل وعز عن التغير والزوال، والحمد لله على كل حال ..
So the correct statement of a group concerning tawhid is that He is unique in essence (wāḥidī al-dhāt), to Him are the needs of individuals, exalted above the meaning of individuals, above what necessitates the attribute of numbers, and cannot be entered by the attribute of change and passing away, nor of limitation and finitude. He is described by pre-eternity (qidam), bringing into existence (takwīn), and power (qudra). He is majestic and exalted above change and passing away. And praise be to Allah in every situation.
Source pages p. 720

Commentary
This passage is a positive statement of correct tawhid after refuting erroneous sects. Maturidi affirms that God is one in essence (wāḥidī al-dhāt), meaning His essence is unique and not composed. All individual beings (āḥād) are in need of Him. He is exalted above the concept of individuals (i.e., not part of a species or genus) and above anything that would imply numerical attributes (like being countable or having parts). The attributes of change (taghayyur), passing away (zawāl), limitation (ḥadd), and finitude (nihāya) cannot apply to Him. Instead, He is described by qidam (pre-eternity), takwīn (the power to originate existence), and qudra (power). He is absolutely transcendent above change and passing away. The passage ends with praise to Allah.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Saying 'wāḥidī al-dhāt' means God's essence is a simple indivisible atom.
Correction: It means His essence is unique and not composed; it does not imply atomism or any physical simplicity.

Key Takeaway
