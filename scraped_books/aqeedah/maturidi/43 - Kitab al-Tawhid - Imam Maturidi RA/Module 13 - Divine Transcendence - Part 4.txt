Negation of Spatial Attributes for God
Maturidi negates spatial attributes and their opposites for God

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi negates all spatial attributes and their opposites from God, and the importance of not affirming the reality of the utterance even in negation.

Core Idea
Allah transcends space and its opposites — even negation must be careful.

This lesson examines a critical passage from Imam Maturidi's Kitab al-Tawhid where he establishes the absolute transcendence of Allah beyond spatial categories. The author not only negates attributes like place, entering, and exiting, but also warns against inadvertently affirming their opposites. This careful approach safeguards tawhid from anthropomorphic misconceptions.

Start by reading the Arabic passage slowly, then the literal translation. Notice the list of spatial terms and the rule that follows.
The passage is dense but concise.
2

The passage mentions two sets: spatial attributes (place, going out, entering, connection, separation, distance) and dynamic attributes (gathering, separating, motion, stillness). List them.
3
Understand the rule of negation
Maturidi says it is not permissible to describe God with these attributes, and even when negating them, one must not affirm the reality of the utterance (i.e., treat the negation as literal description).
This prevents falling into the opposite error.
4
Reflect on the theological significance
Consider why Maturidi emphasizes this caution. How does this protect tawhid? Connect to the idea that Allah is incomparable to creation.
This is a core principle of Maturidi creed.

Commentary
This passage is a theological ruling on divine attributes. The author states that it is not permissible to describe God with spatial attributes such as place, entering, exiting, connection, separation, or distance. Furthermore, it is also impermissible to negate these attributes without affirming the intended meaning. The same applies to concepts like gathering, separating, motion, and stillness. The author concludes that what the opponents (likely anthropomorphists or those who affirm spatial attributes) claimed is not permissible. The passage emphasizes God's transcendence beyond spatial and physical categories.

This sets up a contradiction: they claim God is powerful over everything, yet they deny His power over many things.

The Created Cannot Be Like the Eternal
Initial arguments against creating a like of God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the basic proof that a created being cannot be like the eternal God.

Core Idea
A created thing is originated and transient, so it cannot equal the eternal and immutable God.

The author begins by directly tackling the question of whether God can create a being like Himself. He presents three initial arguments: the ontological gap between created (originated) and Creator (eternal), the impossibility of a like being a body or accident, and the fact that all originated things are subject to annihilation while God is not. These lay the foundation for why such a creation is impossible.

Commentary
This passage is a multi-layered refutation of the question 'Can God create a being like Himself?' It proceeds in several stages. First, an initial proof: a created being is originated, while God is eternal, so they cannot be alike. Any supposed like would be a body with traces of making or an accident—neither can equal God. Also, originated things are subject to annihilation, unlike God. The Shaykh's commentary clarifies the questioner's confusion: the 'like' would have to be uncreated, but then it wouldn't be created, so the question is self-defeating. Next, a comparison with Mu'tazilite views is made: they claim God can lie etc., but that would invalidate His lordship; similarly, they might say He can create a like but doesn't, but the real impossibility is in changing eternal to temporal. Then the author gives his own school's easy refutation: God cannot be under His own power, so bringing another under power to make it like Him is impossible. Two further contradictions are offered: the question is self-contradictory because a created like cannot exist, and the possibility of a like would destroy God's identity and divinity. Finally, the statement implies making the 'like' a made thing, which then cannot be a maker—a logical absurdity. Thus, the question is shown to be impossible from every angle.

The End and Extension Argument Against Dualism
Maturidi argues that the finite side of each principle implies temporality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that if each eternal principle has a limit, that limit implies temporality, contradicting their supposed pre-eternity.

Core Idea
A limited side means a beginning in time, so the whole cannot be eternal.

Maturidi presents another logical argument: each claimed eternal principle must have an end in one direction and extension in others. If the extension indicates pre-eternity (qidam), then the limited side implies temporality (ḥudūth); if extension does not indicate pre-eternity, then the whole thing is temporal. Either way, dualism's claim to eternity fails.

Guided Path
1
Understand the premise: each has an end and extension
Maturidi states that dualists claim each eternal has a 'nihaya' (end) from one side and 'irtifa' (extension) from others. This is a spatial or conceptual boundary.
2
Follow the first conditional
If the extension indicates pre-eternity, then the limited side (the end) must be temporal. This would mean the principle is partly temporal.
3
Consider the alternative
If extension does not indicate pre-eternity, then the whole principle is temporal because no part is eternal.
Thus dualism's claim to eternity fails in both cases.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of dualism (al-thunawiyya), specifically the claim that there are two eternal principles: light (al-nūr) and darkness (al-ẓulma). Maturidi begins by categorizing the possible relations between two eternal beings: either each can destroy the other, neither can, or only one can. If either can destroy the other or neither can, both are marked by incapacity (ʿajz) and ignorance (jahl), which disqualifies them from divinity. If only one is powerful, the other is necessarily subordinate and thus a servant, not a lord. He then applies this to the dualist doctrine: light and darkness are both shown to be deficient—light falls into darkness's trap, darkness is prevented from its own action. Their parts are scattered and states dispersed, so neither can manifest full authority. This refutes their claim to eternality. Second, he argues that each must have a limit in one direction while extending infinitely in others; if that extension indicates pre-eternity (qidam), then the limited side implies temporality (ḥudūth); if not, the whole is temporal. Third, he points out the absurdity that light cannot rescue its finite part from darkness using infinite parts—if infinite parts cannot prevent the initial capture, they cannot later rescue it. Finally, he argues that two entities must occupy separate places. If they are accidents, their separation entails worthlessness; if bodies, their places are either essential or accidental—both cases lead to contradictions (like a cold-preventer coexisting with cold, or good mixing with evil). Maturidi's first argument: al-aslah implies constraints on divine sovereignty.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Absolute sovereignty means God's actions are not constrained by any duty to create the best.

Maturidi begins by refuting the doctrine that God must do what is best for creatures (al-aslah). He argues that if God's sovereignty were limited by such a duty, He would be compelled to prevent all disobedience, but that would be less severe than misguidance, leading to contradiction. This first argument establishes that al-aslah cannot be a necessary attribute of God.

Commentary

قال الشيخ رحمه الله : فنقول : لا نقول الكفر من الله من طريق ، ولا شر بإطلاق القول من الله ، وكذلك من الله . وكذلك لا أحد يقول : إبليس من الله ، أو الشيطان من الله ، أو كل قذر ونتن من الله ، أو كل فساد ثبت أن هذا اللفظ فاسد فيما كانت الخلق أيضا ، ولا قوة إلا بالله . والأصل في ذلك أن القول منه يخرج مخرج دعوى الأمر أو إضافة الأنعام ، وليس فى ذلك واحد منها البتة ، فلا يجوز الإضافة إليه ، وهو كما قلنا : إن الله في التحقيق وإن كان رب كل شيء وإله كل شيء وخالق كل شيء ، وكل شيء له ، لا يقال ذلك فى الأرواث والخبائث والشيطان ونحو ذلك من الأشياء التي لا تذكر أنفسها إلا على الاستحقاق بها ، فإضافتها الواحد يخرج على ذلك ، وإن كانت فى أنها مخلوقة كفرها مما يضاف إلى الله ، فمثله الذي نحن فيه ، ولا قوة إلا بالله وعلى هذا يكره القول فى الكفر والمعاصى إنها بقضاء الله وقدره وإرادته لوجهين : أحدهما ما ذكر من القبح، أو هي لا تذكر إلا على الاستقباح والاستهانة ، والذى ذلك وصفه لا يضاف إلى الله تعالى على ما أخبرت ، وإن كان في التحقيق من قول ووجه آخر إنه يتكلم به على الاعتذار والاحتجاج ، ذلك المفهوم منه ، وقد بينا أن لا عذر لهم في ذلك ، ولا قوة إلا بالله وكذلك عند الناس لا يقال : يا خالق الخبائث والأنجاس ونحو ذلك ، وإن كان هو في الحقية لكل شيء خالقا ، فمثله الذي ذكرنا . وأصل ذلك إنه يضاف إلى الله تعالى كل ما كانت الإضافة إليه تخرج مخرج التعظيم أو مخرج الشكر أو مخرج ذكر نعمه أو أمره ، وما خرج على غير ذلك لا يضاف اليه ، وإن كان فى الحقيقة خلقه ، ولا قوة إلا بالله . وجملة ذلك إن الله يوصف بفعله ، وهو خارج على معنى العدل أو الفضل في الحقيقة ، وربما يضاف إليه ما ليس فى الحقيقة فعله أو صفته ، فإن كان يقتضى معنى محمودا يجوز ذلك ؛ لما نيل ذلك بإنعامه وأفضاله ، وإن لم يكن لم يضف ؛ لما ليس ذلك في الحقيقة فعله فيوصف به ، وهو من حيث فعله ، وذلك الشيء فما عند الخلق بغير هذا الوصف ، والله تعالى يجل ويتعالى عن غير هذين الوصفين ؛ إذ فى أفعاله صفة عدل وحكمة أو فضل وإحسان ، ولا قوة إلا بالله
The Shaykh, may Allah have mercy on him, said: So we say: we do not say disbelief is from Allah in any way, nor evil by absolute speech from Allah, and similarly from Allah. And similarly no one says: Iblis is from Allah, or the devil is from Allah, or every filth and impurity is from Allah, or every corruption—it is established that this expression is corrupt in what creation also had, and there is no power except through Allah. The principle in that is that speech from Him issues in the manner of a claim of command or attribution of blessings, and none of that is in any of them at all, so attribution to Him is not permissible. And it is as we said: Allah in reality, even though He is the Lord of everything, the God of everything, the Creator of everything, and everything belongs to Him, that is not said concerning dung, impurities, the devil, and similar things that are not mentioned except in the context of deserving blame. So their attribution to the One issues on that basis, even if they are created—their kufr is something attributed to Allah, so the like of which we are in, and there is no power except through Allah. And on this basis it is disliked to say concerning disbelief and sins that they are by Allah's decree, determination, and will for two reasons: one is what we mentioned of ugliness, or they are not mentioned except for the purpose of considering them ugly and contemptible, and that which has such description is not attributed to Allah as I informed you, even if in reality it is His creation. And another aspect is that one speaks thereby in the way of excuse and argumentation, which is understood from it, and we have shown that they have no excuse in that, and there is no power except through Allah. Similarly, among people it is not said: O Creator of impurities and filth and the like, even if He in truth is the Creator of everything, so its like is what we mentioned. The principle of that is: whatever attribution to Allah issues in the manner of glorification, or in the manner of gratitude, or in the manner of mentioning His blessings or His command, is attributed to Allah, and whatever issues otherwise is not attributed to Him, even if it is in reality His creation. And there is no power except through Allah. The summary of that is that Allah is described by His action, and it issues upon the meaning of justice or grace in reality. Sometimes what is not in reality His action or attribute may be attributed to Him; if it implies a praiseworthy meaning, it is permissible because that is attained by His favor and bounty. If not, it is not attributed, because that is not in reality His action so as to be described by it, and He is from the aspect of His action, and that thing—what is with creation is other than this description. And Allah, exalted and transcendent above other than these two descriptions, for in His actions there is the attribute of justice and wisdom or grace and beneficence, and there is no power except through Allah.

Commentary
This passage defines the theological and linguistic principle for attributing things to Allah. The author begins by explicitly stating that disbelief, evil, Iblis, the devil, filth, and corruption are not said to be 'from Allah' even though Allah is the Creator of all things. The reason is that the expression 'from Allah' in Arabic implies a praiseworthy or grateful attribution—it issues in the manner of claiming command or attributing blessings. Since none of these evil things are blessings or commands, it is not permissible to attribute them to Allah in that way. The author then explains that although Allah is truly the Lord and Creator of everything, we do not say that dung, impurities, and the devil are from Him because they are only mentioned with blame and contempt. The attribution to Allah is based on how the thing is normally spoken of, not on the fact of creation. He then gives two reasons why it is disliked to say that disbelief and sins are by Allah's decree and will: first, because they are ugly and only mentioned disdainfully, so they should not be attributed to Allah in a positive sense; second, because such speech might be interpreted as an excuse for sin, which is invalid. The author further illustrates with common usage: people do not invoke Allah as 'Creator of filth,' even though He creates everything. The overarching principle is that only attributions that express glorification, gratitude, mentioning of blessings, or command are made to Allah; other attributions are avoided even if He creates them. Finally, the author summarizes that Allah is described by His actions, which are either just or gracious. A thing may be attributed to Him if it implies a praiseworthy meaning, even if not literally His action; otherwise it is not. Thus, Allah's descriptions are confined to justice and grace.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Any action of Allah can be described as 'bad' or 'unjust'.
Correction: Maturidi emphasizes that Allah's actions are always just or gracious; He is transcendent above any other description.

Key Takeaway
The rule for attributing things to Allah is based on linguistic convention of praise and blame, not on the fact of creation; and Allah's actions are always just or gracious.
