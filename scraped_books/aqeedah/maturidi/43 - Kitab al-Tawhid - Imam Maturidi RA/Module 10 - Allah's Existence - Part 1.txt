Maturidi's Proof from Change
Maturidi argues from universal change to the world's creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses observable change and decay in all physical things to argue that the world cannot be eternal and must be created.

Core Idea
Change and decay in every body prove it cannot be eternal.

Maturidi begins with a simple observation: even a perfect human cannot prevent his own body from decay. This pattern applies to all living and non-living things. The presence of change, diversity, and repulsion in bodies shows that they are not self-sustaining and depend on a Creator. This lesson walks through that logical argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get the overall sense, then read the English translation. Note the progression from human to inanimate.
The argument is condensed; take your time.
2
Identify the key observations
Maturidi uses several examples: the inability to prevent bodily decay, the similarity of living and dead things, the gathering of opposing natures, and the variety in nature. List them.
3
Trace the conclusion
Notice how Maturidi moves from these observations to the conclusion that anything bearing accidents cannot be eternal. This is the logical core of his proof.
This conclusion is the foundation for the world's creation.

Maturidi used the word 'attribute' (sifa) instead of 'accident' ('arad) and was convinced that this usage is more correct and closer to Islamic terminology, since the word 'arad occurs in the Noble Qur'an in the sense of the goods of this world and what is meant by it is material things. And Maturidi mentioned the sayings of those who claim the pre-eternity of the world as it is in six following points: a) That something comes from something without an origin, as we observe that in the sensible world, i.e., in the witnessed. b) The existence of things necessarily from a wise origin within the limits of the principle of cause and effect. c) The statement of the pre-eternity of the original clay (al-tinat al-asl) and the occurrence of the making, like what occurs in the example of living beings from the sperm and the egg. d) And the occurrence of the clay by accidents that befell it, then it turned into what the natures are upon of moderation and difference. e) The existence of the world and its occurrence by the Creator, glory be to Him. f) The statement of the existence of the origin for the world and calling it 'hyyle' (hyle).

Commentary
This passage presents both Maturidi's argument for the world's creation based on observable change and a list of six opposing views advocating pre-eternity. The argument begins by noting that even a perfect being cannot prevent decay in his own body, and that the same applies to all living and dead things. The diversity of natures in bodies would lead to disintegration without divine management. The variety in nature (good/evil, etc.) proves change and transience. Anything subject to such accidents cannot be eternal. Then the editorial commentary explains Maturidi's method: he uses a rational inference from observable changes, preferring the term 'attribute' (sifa) over 'accident' ('arad) due to Qur'anic usage. Finally, six arguments for the world's pre-eternity are enumerated: (a) something from nothing without origin, (b) necessary existence from a wise cause, (c) pre-eternal matter with temporal form, (d) accidents changing primordial clay, (e) temporal world from an eternal Creator, and (f) an eternal substrate called 'hyle'. Maturidi refutes these by pointing out that they all improperly analogize from the witnessed to the unseen without proper evidence.

Six Arguments for the Pre-Eternity of the World
Maturidi enumerates six positions that claim the world is pre-eternal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the six main arguments that Maturidi records as claims for the pre-eternity of the world.

Core Idea
Maturidi compiles six philosophical claims for the world's pre-eternity.

After presenting his own proof, Maturidi lists six alternative views that argue for the world's pre-eternity. These range from spontaneous generation to the idea of an eternal hyle. This lesson presents that list.

Guided Path
1
Read the six points in Arabic and English
Read each point slowly. Note the letters (a) to (f) and the structure of each claim.
Each point is a distinct argument from a different philosophical school.
2
Categorize each point by its type
Try to classify each argument: (a) is chance, (b) is necessary causation, (c) is eternal matter with temporal form, (d) is matter altered by accidents, (e) is creation by God (seemingly contradictory), (f) is the Greek concept of hyle.
Point (e) is ambiguous; it may imply the world is created but still pre-eternal in some sense.
3
Reflect on the diversity of views
Notice that Maturidi is presenting these as claims made by opponents. The list shows the range of pre-eternity arguments in his time.

Proving God's Existence Through the Created World
Maturidi explains how the world serves as evidence for God, using both occurrence and order.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi proves God's existence and attributes by using the sensible world as evidence for the unseen, combining arguments from occurrence and order.

Core Idea
The created world is proof of its Creator, both in its coming into existence and in its order.

Maturidi argues that the world we perceive points beyond itself to a Creator. He does not rely solely on the argument that the world began to exist; he also sees the intricate order as evidence that its Creator possesses knowledge, power, and will. This lesson introduces his method of reasoning from the witnessed to the unseen.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to grasp Maturidi's key points about al-shahid and al-gha'ib.
Pay attention to the terms 'دليل النظام والغاية' (proof of order and purpose).
2
Identify the two proofs
Notice how Maturidi uses both the occurrence of the world (حدوث العالم) and the argument from order and purpose (دليل النظام والغاية) as evidence.
He considers both as complementary, not alternative.
3
Connect to attributes
Understand that the same reasoning that proves God's existence also leads to affirming His names and attributes like knowledge, power, and will.
Maturidi uses the same methodology for existence and attributes.
4
Reflect on the method
Consider why Maturidi emphasizes continuous observation of the world and its entities as a starting point for theological reasoning.
This is a distinctive feature of his approach.

And Maturidi devoted a special chapter in his book regarding how the sensible world is evidence for the unseen world, i.e., the indication of the witnessed (al-shahid) on the absent (al-gha'ib). And Maturidi also addressed on many occasions the issue of the occurrence (hadath) of the world and its creation, which is considered a starting point for proving the existence of God. And it is worth mentioning that the proof of establishing the Necessary Being according to Maturidi is not only the proof of occurrence; rather, on various occasions he mentions inferences that enter the domain of the proof of order and purpose (dalil al-nizam wa al-ghaya). So if the evidence for the creation of the world is established, i.e., that the world was not at the beginning and then came into existence under the influence of an external factor, this necessarily leads to acceptance of the existence of its Creator. And we have seen that Maturidi, in proving the Necessary Being, mostly relies on his continuous observation of the world and its entities; so by the same style and method he adopts his position in inferring the existence of His names, the Exalted, and His attributes. For the wonderful creation and the immense order that we observe in the world lead us to say that its Creator has knowledge, power, and will. However, does attributing those attributes to the Creator necessitate similarity to the creatures that are described with the same attributes, and consequently contradict the principle of the doctrine of tawhid? Maturidi held that it is a possibility we cannot avoid; perhaps it apparently necessitates similarity; but necessity has allowed us to negate the concept from the witnessed in order to negate similarity. And we name Him with what I mentioned as a necessity, and even if it is possible, we have the capacity to name Him with what no other is named, but we name Him. However, similarity here is only in speech; do you not see that the expression by which we call Him, the Mighty and Majestic, 'knowing' and 'powerful' in various languages differs while there is no difference in meaning? This means that the names by which we call Him are only expressions and words that bring meanings befitting Allah the Exalted closer to understandings and minds. Thus, the divine statement of attributes in His saying 'There is nothing like unto Him' (Qur'an 42:11) became a principle that everyone adopted, even similarity in such description of the essence of Allah the Exalted.

Commentary
This passage explains Maturidi's method of proving God's existence and attributes. It begins by noting that Maturidi dedicated a chapter to how the sensible world (al-shahid) serves as evidence for the unseen (al-gha'ib). The occurrence of the world is key. However, Maturidi does not rely solely on the argument from occurrence; he also uses the argument from order and purpose (dalil al-nizam wa al-ghaya). The order and creation point to a Creator with knowledge, power, and will. The passage then raises a problem: if we attribute these same qualities (knowledge, power) to God and creatures, does that imply similarity (tashbih) and contradict tawhid? Maturidi answers that necessity forces us to use these terms, but we negate the human concept of these attributes from the witnessed to avoid similarity. The similarity is only in wording, not in reality. Different languages use different expressions for the same meaning. Thus, divine names are approximations for human understanding. The principle from the Qur'an 'There is nothing like unto Him' protects against true anthropomorphism.

Resolving the Problem of Similarity in Divine Attributes

Arguments from Composition and Growth
Maturidi argues from composition and growth to contingency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses the combination of opposing natures and the growth of the world's parts to argue for the contingency of all beings.

Core Idea
Opposites that naturally repel cannot unite without a cause; finite parts cannot yield an infinite whole.

Maturidi presents two further sensory arguments. The first observes that every perceived object contains opposing natures that naturally repel each other, so their combination must be caused by an external agent. The second notes that the world has finite parts that grow and change, and since the whole is composed of finite parts, it cannot be infinite or eternal. These arguments reinforce the contingency of all creation.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the logic of opposing natures

2
Understand the parts-to-whole argument
The world has parts that are observed to be contingent and finite. The whole, being composed of such parts, cannot be infinite or eternal.
The argument uses the principle that the whole shares the nature of its parts.
3
Connect both arguments to contingency
Both arguments show that the world depends on an external cause, so it is contingent and not eternal.

Commentary
This passage presents a second argument from creation, focusing on the interconnectedness of all things. Maturidi points out that despite the immense diversity of creation — the heavens and earth, different regions, and varied provisions — everything is interconnected. The needs of all people are served through a unified system where the earth's produce depends on heavenly causes (like rain), and livelihoods come from various sources. If there were multiple administrators, it would be impossible for all these to converge on a single source. He then brings in the consistency of time (night and day) and quotes the Quranic verse about no discrepancy in creation. The reference to bodies having six directions (up, down, front, back, right, left) underscores the uniform dimensionality of all physical things, further supporting a single Creator. The proof culminates in the assertion that one Manager has brought everything under one meaning (i.e., one purpose and order).
