The Dilemma of God's Knowledge in Eternity
Maturidi presents a dilemma on God's knowledge in eternity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical dilemma that arises if one denies God's eternal knowledge: either God knew His state or He did not, leading to necessary consequences.

Core Idea
Denying eternal knowledge forces a false choice between ignorance and createdness.

Maturidi begins by posing a direct question to those who deny that God's knowledge is eternal. If God did not know His own state in pre-eternity, then He would be ignorant—an impossibility. This introductory dilemma sets the stage for deeper arguments against the denial of attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the translation. Note the structure of the dilemma.
The passage uses a conditional structure.
2
Identify the two horns
The passage presents two possibilities: either God knew His state in eternity or He did not. Find each and see the conclusion drawn.
3
Understand the underlying principle
Notice that the opponent defines 'knower' as negation of ignorance. This is key to the dilemma: if not knowing, then ignorant.
This connects to the learning objective.

ثم يُسأل: كيف كان في الأزل؟ إن علم أنه كان كذلك في الأزل، فيلزمه الاسم كذلك، أو علم أنه لم يكن، فيلحقه اسم الجهل. وهو بقولهم لازم؛ لأن تأويل العالم عندهم نفي الجهل، فإذا لم يكن عالما في القديم فهو إذا عند ذلك كان جاهلا. ولا قوة إلا بالله. ثم يُتكلّم في العلم، إذ لم يكن حتى كان بأن حدث، فيجب ذلك في كل شيء؛ مع ما يقال: كيف حدث به ولم يكن له قدرة؟ أو بغيره، فيبطل به توحيدهم؟ ثم يقال له في الفصل الذي ذكرتُ أنه كان يعلم ذاته قبل الخلق: إذ لم يكن له علم في الحقيقة كيف كان يعلم ذاته؟ فإن علمه عالما بطل قوله بحدث الاسم. وإن قال: غير عالم ولا قادر عليه، دخل عليه جميع ما ذكرت، مع إحالة الوصف له بالعلم به في الأزل مع فساد ما بينا في الحدث. وإن قال من بعد بغيره، رآه ممن يعترض فيه العوارض التي بها تكوّن العالم، وفي ذلك موافقة الدهرية في الطينة، وأصحاب الهيولى والثنوية في كون العالم باعتراض العوارض في الأصل ولا قوة إلا بالله. وهذه المسألة مسألة الصفات في التحقيق، وقد بينا ذلك.
Then it is asked: How was He in eternity? If one knows that He was thus in eternity, then the name (i.e., 'Knower') is necessitated for Him likewise; or if one knows that He was not thus, then the name of ignorance attaches to Him. And that is necessary according to their statement, because the interpretation of 'Knower' with them is the negation of ignorance. So if He was not knowing in pre-eternity, then He was at that time ignorant. And there is no power except with Allah. Then one speaks about knowledge: since it did not exist until it came to be by way of origination, that necessitates the same in everything. Along with what is said: How did it come to be by Him when He had no power? Or by something else, which would invalidate their tawhid? Then it is said to him in the section I mentioned: He knew His essence before creation. Since He did not have actual knowledge, how did He know His essence? If He knows it as knowing, his statement about the createdness of the name is invalidated. And if he says: He is not knowing nor powerful over it, then all that I mentioned applies to him, along with the impossibility of describing Him with knowledge of it in eternity, and the corruption of what we explained about createdness. And if he says after that by something else, he is seen as one who affirms that accidents affect Him, by which the world is constituted. And in that is agreement with the Dahriyya concerning primordial matter, and the supporters of hyle and the dualists concerning the constitution of the world by the intervention of accidents in the origin. And there is no power except with Allah. This question, in reality, is the question of attributes, and we have explained that.

Commentary
This passage is a series of objections and answers against the deniers of eternal attributes. The first objection: either God knew His state in eternity or He did not. If He knew, then the name 'Knower' applies eternally; if He did not know, then He was ignorant. That is a dilemma. Next, discussion about knowledge: if it is originated, then everything would be originated similarly. Then a further objection: how did knowledge come to be if He had no power, or if by another, that invalidates tawhid. Then the final objection: if the opponent says God knew His essence before creation without having actual knowledge, then ask how He knew. If He knew as knowing, then createdness of name is false. If He says He was not knowing, then all contradictions apply. If the opponent attributes this to something else, that leads to the view that accidents affect God, which aligns with materialists (Dahriyya) and dualists. Maturidi concludes that this is truly the issue of attributes, and he has clarified it.

Objections Against Denying Eternal Knowledge
Maturidi presents a chain of objections against originated knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's series of objections against the view that knowledge is originated, culminating in showing that such a view aligns with materialists.

Core Idea
If knowledge is created, then God would need power or another creator, leading to contradictions.

Maturidi continues his refutation by examining the claim that knowledge came into existence through origination. He raises questions about the source of this knowledge—if through God without power, or through something else—and then applies the same reasoning to God's self-knowledge. Ultimately, he shows that such views reduce to agreement with materialists and dualists.

3

The Dilemma of Ignorance
Maturidi presents the core dilemma: no eternal attribute equals ignorance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that denying eternal attributes leads to the absurd conclusion that God was ignorant in pre-eternity.

Core Idea
Denying eternal attributes makes God ignorant.

Maturidi opens his refutation of Jahm by exposing a devastating consequence: if God had no attributes in eternity, then He was not knowing, which for the Jahmiyya definitionally implies ignorance. This dilemma forms the foundation of the entire argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the concise style and the logical moves.
2
Identify the dilemma
The passage presents two options: either one knows that God was knowing in eternity (which attaches the name 'Ālim eternally) or one knows He was not knowing (which attaches the name 'ignorant'). Find these two options and see how they trap the opponent.
The second option leads to an impossibility, forcing the first.
3
Reflect on the definition of 'Ālim
Maturidi clarifies that for the Jahmiyya, 'Ālim means 'not ignorant'. Therefore, if God was not knowing in pre-eternity, He was ignorant. This is the core of the dilemma.
This shows that the denial of attributes contradicts their own definition.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained argument refuting Jahm ibn Ṣafwān's doctrine that God's names and attributes are temporally originated (ḥādith). The author (Maturidi) begins by stating a key principle: positing that God's attribute of knowledge (or any attribute) is 'other' than His essence in a way that implies temporal origination corrupts tawḥīd. The argument proceeds as follows: Jahm denies eternal attributes; this leads to the consequence that in pre-eternity (al-azal) God was neither knowing nor powerful, and then 'became' knowing—which is impossible. The author then poses a dilemma: either one knows that God was knowing in eternity (so the name 'Ālim applies eternally) or one knows He was not knowing (which implies ignorance, since for the Mu'tazila/Jahmiyya 'Ālim is defined as 'not ignorant'). So Jahm's view entails ignorance of God in the past, a clear absurdity. Next, the author addresses the specific case of God's knowledge: if knowledge is temporally originated, then either it arose by God's power (but He had no power before it existed) or by another—both alternatives are problematic. The first implies God was powerless, the second undermines tawḥīd by making an external cause. The author also presses the point that if God knew His essence before creation, that presupposes eternal knowledge; if not, how could He know it? The argument concludes by warning that Jahm's position leads to agreement with the Dahrīs (materialists) and dualists who attribute the world's existence to accidental causes. The author then asserts that this entire discussion is truly about the divine attributes (al-ṣifāt) and that he has already clarified the matter (likely earlier in the book).

The Problem of Origination of Knowledge
Maturidi poses a dilemma about the temporal origination of knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that if God's knowledge is temporally originated, it poses a dilemma: either it came from God's power (impossible before having knowledge) or from another (breaking tawhid).

Core Idea
Originated knowledge implies either impotence or polytheism.

This argument strengthens the necessity of eternal knowledge.

Learning Objective

The Problem of Distinguishing Divine Creation in Mu'tazila Thought
Maturidi critiques Mu'tazila for lacking a divine distinguishing mark.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that the Mu'tazila view fails to provide a distinguishing mark for God's creation, thereby collapsing into a position similar to dualism and naturalism.

Core Idea
Without a divine mark, Mu'tazila cannot prove God's exclusive creation.

This passage continues Maturidi's refutation of the Mu'tazila. He argues that on their view, God did not provide any evidence to distinguish His creation from that of others, leading to an absurdity where the universe could be the work of multiple agents. Maturidi then invokes a Qur'anic verse to highlight the flaw in their reasoning.

Divine Names Do Not Imply Similarity
Al-Maturidi refutes the objection that divine names imply similarity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ahl al-Sunna respond to the objection that divine names imply similarity, and how they use the principle of negation (tanzih) alongside affirmation.

Core Idea
Divine names do not imply similarity; they are used by necessity and accompanied by negation.

This passage from Imam al-Maturidi addresses a key objection raised by those who deny that God can have names or attributes, fearing it would imply similarity to creation. Al-Maturidi explains that while names are drawn from the witnessed world, they are not intended to indicate likeness but are used with the understanding that God is utterly transcendent, as affirmed by the verse 'There is nothing like Him'.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Some people deny essential names and attributes because they think it implies similarity. They argue that if God cannot be compared to anything in reference, then sharing a name would prove similarity even more.

He states that God does have essential names (like 'Allah', 'al-Rahman') and attributes (like knowledge and power). However, our description is limited by our capacity and arises from necessity, drawing from the witnessed world.

To avoid implying similarity, the names are accompanied by negations, such as the verse 'There is nothing like Him'. Thus tawhid is an affirmation of essence within a negation, and a negation within an affirmation.
The names are not God's real names but expressions to bring meanings close to understanding.

Proof from messengers
The prophets used these names; if they implied similarity, they would contradict tawhid. Their use is permissible because the names are accompanied by the negation of likeness.
The verse 'There is nothing like Him' negates any thingness from God.

The Impossibility of a Derived Divine Name
Maturidi refutes the Batiniyya view of a derived divine name via infinite regress.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
God’s name cannot come from another; otherwise, an infinite chain of origination would be required.

Maturidi here refutes the Batiniyya claim that God has no essential name but only a name derived from His creation or attribute. He shows that such a view leads to a logical contradiction and an infinite regress of causes. The only consistent position is that God has an eternal, essential name.
