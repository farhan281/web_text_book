A Tu Quoque Defense of Divine Wisdom
Maturidi rebuts an objection by invoking dualist counter-argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses a tu quoque argument to defend divine wisdom against the objection that God killing prophets while sparing enemies is unwise.

Core Idea
Maturidi turns the opponent's logic against them: the same reasoning proves too much.

In this passage, Maturidi faces a common objection: if God can slay His prophets yet spare His enemies, then His actions seem inconsistent with wisdom. Rather than directly defending the consistency, Maturidi points out that the opponent's own argument can be used by dualists to attack the opponent's position. This is a classic 'you too' argument, showing that the objection is not unique to Maturidi's theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then read the literal translation. Notice the concise style of Maturidi's argument.
The phrase 'أنابهم' may be a variant of 'أصابهم' meaning 'killed'.
2
Identify the objection and response
The opponent claims God's slaying prophets and sparing enemies is unwise. Maturidi's response is not a direct defense but a reversal: he points out that dualists make the same argument against the opponent.
3
Understand the tu quoque structure
Maturidi argues that if the opponent's logic is valid, then the dualists' parallel argument against the opponent is also valid, making the opponent's objection self-defeating. This defuses the criticism without needing to prove divine wisdom is consistent.
This is a logical move: showing the criticism applies equally to the critic's own position.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer regarding God's justice. The opponent (al-Ka'bi) apparently argued that if God could kill prophets and yet spare His enemies (i.e., disbelievers), that would be inconsistent with divine wisdom. Maturidi turns the argument around: he says that the dualists (مجوس) argue that the one who does this (i.e., the opponent's God) is not wise. The point is that the same logic the opponent uses to object to the Ash'ari/Maturidi position can be used by the dualists against the Mu'tazili. So if the opponent cannot answer the dualist, his objection against Maturidi fails. In other words, it is a tu quoque argument: the opponent's own position faces an identical difficulty, so his objection is not valid.

Willing an Act vs. Willing the Sin
Maturidi explains the distinction between willing a sinful act and willing the sin itself, using the example of the Prophet and a Quranic verse.

Core Idea
Willing a sinful act for a good purpose is not the same as willing the sin.

This passage from Imam Maturidi's Kitab al-Tawhid addresses a subtle but critical point: can a prophet or God will something that is sinful? The answer requires distinguishing between the act itself and the sin inherent in it. By examining the Prophet's will for the defeat of polytheists, Maturidi clarifies a principle that preserves divine justice and prophetic infallibility.

This passage presents the second principle for establishing prophethood: the miracles (āyāt) brought by prophets are beyond the capacity of human experts in that field, and cannot be replicated by learning. Even if learning could theoretically produce such wonders, the prophets were not trained in those arts, proving that their power comes from God. Maturidi also distinguishes prophetic miracles from sorcery: sorcery has a heavenly origin but is learned and transmitted, whereas miracles are direct divine gifts. The fact that prophets are given these powers without normal learning shows God's special selection.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Miracles are a form of advanced sorcery.
Correction: Maturidi explains that sorcery is learned and transmitted, whereas prophetic miracles are not acquired through learning, and the prophets were not trained in those arts, proving divine origin.

Checkpoint
How does Maturidi distinguish prophetic miracles from sorcery?
A) Miracles are more powerful than sorcery.
B) Sorcery is learned and transmitted, while prophetic miracles are not acquired through learning.
C) Sorcery is from devils, while miracles are from angels.
D) Miracles are only performed in public, while sorcery is secret.

Key Takeaway
Prophetic miracles are distinguished by being impossible to learn or replicate, proving their divine origin and the special status of prophets.
