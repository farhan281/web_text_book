Proof from the Dual Nature of Substances
Maturidi proves tawhid from the dual nature of substances.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's proof for the oneness of God based on the dual nature of substances.

Core Idea
No substance is purely beneficial or harmful—this shows one Manager.

Maturidi presents a third proof for tawhid from the nature of created substances. He observes that nothing in creation is exclusively good or bad; the same thing can be beneficial in one context and harmful in another. This points to a single wise Manager who combines opposites. In this lesson, we will examine this argument.

2
Analyze the observation
Identify how Maturidi uses the dual nature of substances to argue against multiple gods. Consider the implication of a single Manager gathering benefits and harms.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
Understand that this argument establishes the unity of the Creator by showing coherence in creation.

Commentary
This passage presents a third proof from the nature of created substances. Maturidi observes that no substance is inherently and exclusively beneficial or harmful; the same thing can be beneficial in one context and harmful in another. This indicates that there is a wise Manager who combines opposites in a coherent way. If there were multiple gods, each would have a specific direction, leading to contradiction. But the fact that everything serves multiple purposes and that there is a balance shows that a single Creator is responsible. The argument refutes the possibility of multiple deities by showing the unity of design behind the complexity of creation.

Proof from the Harmony of Opposing Natures
Maturidi argues from the coexistence of opposing natures in bodies for a single administrator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses the coexistence of opposing natures in bodies to argue for a single divine administrator.

Core Idea
Opposing natures held together point to a single Manager.

How can fire and water coexist within the same body without destroying it? Maturidi sees this as a clear sign of a wise Administrator holding them together. In this lesson, we examine his argument that the unity of natural forces implies a single divine manager.

The First Proof from Time and Resources
Maturidi's first proof from the order of times and provisions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's first argument that the ordered distribution of resources and the measured alternation of night and day point to a single Provider and Manager.

Core Idea
The harmony of times and sustenance disproves multiple gods.

In this passage, Maturidi begins a series of arguments demonstrating that the observable order in the universe cannot be the product of several deities. He first draws attention to how human needs and livelihoods are distributed across the earth, and how days and nights interlock with precision. The Quranic verse 'You see not in the creation of the Most Merciful any disparity' (67:3) encapsulates this argument.

Commentary
This passage is a multi-part proof of divine unity (tawḥīd) from the observable order and coherence of the world. Maturidi presents several arguments, each building on the harmony of creation despite diversity and opposition.

First argument: The distribution of needs and livelihoods across all regions, and the interlocking times (day and night, seasons) that follow a measured pattern, point to a single provider and manager. If there were multiple gods, it would be impossible for the resources to originate from one source without conflict. Maturidi cites Quran 67:3 as evidence that there is no disparity in creation.

Second argument (introduced by 'فهذا'): Bodies are created on six directions (dimensions) and yet are unified under one meaning, necessitating a single manager.

Third argument: No atom is purely harmful or beneficial, good or bad; things change in different contexts. This mixture of qualities across all things shows that no object is fixed in its nature, which would be expected if it came from a single essence or from multiple directorial forces each imposing a fixed character. The fact that everything has varying opposites indicates a single director who balances all aspects.

Fourth argument: Entities exist under the category of bodies and possess opposing natures; their natural tendency is conflict and dissolution. Yet we see them coexisting and supporting each other. This survival cannot happen without a single wise manager who restrains harm and preserves order. If there were multiple gods, their natural opposition would lead to chaos.

Fifth argument: Considering the states (aḥwāl) and acts (afʿāl) of gods if there were multiple: either they are equal in lordship, leading to mutual opposition, or one is superior, and the superior alone deserves lordship. The actual harmony of the world, with its many opposing forces working together, cannot be the result of multiple agents but only of a single, wise, all-knowing, and gentle manager who has no rival in determination.

Commentary
This passage is a multi-part proof of divine unity (tawḥīd) from the observable order and coherence of the world. Maturidi presents several arguments, each building on the harmony of creation despite diversity and opposition.

First argument: The distribution of needs and livelihoods across all regions, and the interlocking times (day and night, seasons) that follow a measured pattern, point to a single provider and manager. If there were multiple gods, it would be impossible for the resources to originate from one source without conflict. Maturidi cites Quran 67:3 as evidence that there is no disparity in creation.

Second argument (introduced by 'فهذا'): Bodies are created on six directions (dimensions) and yet are unified under one meaning, necessitating a single manager.

Third argument: No atom is purely harmful or beneficial, good or bad; things change in different contexts. This mixture of qualities across all things shows that no object is fixed in its nature, which would be expected if it came from a single essence or from multiple directorial forces each imposing a fixed character. The fact that everything has varying opposites indicates a single director who balances all aspects.

Fourth argument: Entities exist under the category of bodies and possess opposing natures; their natural tendency is conflict and dissolution. Yet we see them coexisting and supporting each other. This survival cannot happen without a single wise manager who restrains harm and preserves order. If there were multiple gods, their natural opposition would lead to chaos.

Fifth argument: Considering the states (aḥwāl) and acts (afʿāl) of gods if there were multiple: either they are equal in lordship, leading to mutual opposition, or one is superior, and the superior alone deserves lordship. The actual harmony of the world, with its many opposing forces working together, cannot be the result of multiple agents but only of a single, wise, all-knowing, and gentle manager who has no rival in determination.

Equality leads to conflict; inequality leads to the superior deserving sole lordship; harmony demands one God.

Commentary
This passage is a multi-part proof of divine unity (tawḥīd) from the observable order and coherence of the world. Maturidi presents several arguments, each building on the harmony of creation despite diversity and opposition.

First argument: The distribution of needs and livelihoods across all regions, and the interlocking times (day and night, seasons) that follow a measured pattern, point to a single provider and manager. If there were multiple gods, it would be impossible for the resources to originate from one source without conflict. Maturidi cites Quran 67:3 as evidence that there is no disparity in creation.

Second argument (introduced by 'فهذا'): Bodies are created on six directions (dimensions) and yet are unified under one meaning, necessitating a single manager.

Third argument: No atom is purely harmful or beneficial, good or bad; things change in different contexts. This mixture of qualities across all things shows that no object is fixed in its nature, which would be expected if it came from a single essence or from multiple directorial forces each imposing a fixed character. The fact that everything has varying opposites indicates a single director who balances all aspects.

Fourth argument: Entities exist under the category of bodies and possess opposing natures; their natural tendency is conflict and dissolution. Yet we see them coexisting and supporting each other. This survival cannot happen without a single wise manager who restrains harm and preserves order. If there were multiple gods, their natural opposition would lead to chaos.

Fifth argument: Considering the states (aḥwāl) and acts (afʿāl) of gods if there were multiple: either they are equal in lordship, leading to mutual opposition, or one is superior, and the superior alone deserves lordship. The actual harmony of the world, with its many opposing forces working together, cannot be the result of multiple agents but only of a single, wise, all-knowing, and gentle manager who has no rival in determination.

The Error of Anthropomorphists and the Muslim Stance

Learning Objective

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the temporality (huduth) of the world and a refutation of those who liken God to creation (mushabbiha) and materialists (dahriyya). Maturidi begins by stating that attributes understood from creation, when applied to God, are understood differently. He then identifies two groups of anthropomorphists: those who consider God a physical entity and deny the Creator, and those who make Him subject to events (hawadith) while denying His own temporality, claiming that events arise from His power—these are the materialists (ashab al-hayula). Muslims, by necessity, affirm God's 'that-ness' (huwiyya) and negate from Him all that creation admits, such as differentiation in essences, change, increase and decrease. Even stable things are still contingent. Maturidi then argues that the world's stability is by subjugation (taskhir) and involves motion or rest, opposition (tadadd), possibility of annihilation, and likenesses that would negate perfection—all signs of temporality. These aspects are proofs of the world's having an efficient cause (muhdith). If that efficient cause had attributes by which the world's temporality is known, then it would be subject to the same contingencies. The corruption of the world itself testifies to a wise, knowing efficient cause transcendent over likenesses and opposites. Since everything other than God is temporal in all respects, if anything had a likeness to God, that thing would lose its temporality or God would lose His eternity. Likeness in every respect among creatures is impossible because it would make them identical; likeness only occurs in some respect. If God were described as like another in any respect, He would become like that creature in that respect.

Signs of the World's Temporality
Maturidi enumerates six signs proving the world's temporality.

Commentary
This passage classifies the ways in which the world indicates its Creator. Maturidi begins by stating that the world's indication varies according to its different aspects. Refutation of deniers of a wise, knowing Maker. Then whoever says that creation emerges as it is by natures (taba'i') and nutrients, and whoever says that is the work of the star, the sun, and the moon, and whoever says that is by the rotation of the sphere, and whoever says that in procreation it is by the management of fathers and mothers—all of it reduces to something being through something else. If no primacy (awwaliyya) is established for it, it is invalidated by the proofs that have been mentioned. And if primacy is established for it or for each kind of that, then it being by itself is impossible; because [if] it brings itself into existence: a) either at the time of its non-existence, and that is impossible for a non-existent to exist; considering that when the management of everything is referred back to it, it cannot be reached by non-existence; for if it were possible for it to bring itself into existence, then at that time it would be possible for it to annihilate itself, and that is contradictory. b) Or after existence, then existence is established by another. And success is from God. And also, all that is mentioned is only a type of inanimate (mawat) except for what is mentioned of fathers and mothers, of which they know that their management does not reach the beginnings of children, and that they come to be contrary to what they hope, and that if they become corrupt they cannot rectify them, and that their capacity does not reach what is hidden of things, let alone their predetermination there, and other aspects that invalidate that being through them. And the type of inanimate is not aware of the benefits in it, nor of what is likely to prevent them. It is established that what comes to be by nutrition and by natures—that occurs in them only by the making (ja'l) of a wise, knowing One in them, and in the necessity without a making of everything [being] as it is in benefit and harm, [considering] that in animals there are meanings that are not in anything that describes their effects, such as hearing and sight, and especially in humans such as speech, discernment, and comprehension of things. While that [these capacities] at the beginning of states were non-existent, and they do not grow except by the nutrients of fathers and mothers, yet that did not effect them; so how [would it effect] the children? Furthermore, everything has a limit; when it reaches that limit, it does not increase in length, width, hearing, sight, or intellect; rather, everything of that begins to diminish, despite the persistence of nutrients and continuity of upbringing; it is understood that this is so not by what was mentioned, but by One who knows by Himself so that nothing escapes Him, capable.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive refutation of all views that deny a wise, knowing Creator. Maturidi lists four alternative explanations for the existence and order of the world: (1) natures (taba'i') and nutrients, (2) the influence of stars, sun, and moon, (3) the rotation of the sphere, and (4) the management of parents in procreation. He argues that all these reduce to the idea that one thing is caused by another (kulluhu yarji'u ila kawni shay'in bi-shay'in), leading either to an infinite regress if no first cause is posited, or, if a first cause is posited, to the impossibility of self-creation. The impossibility of self-creation is shown through a dilemma: if a thing were to bring itself into existence, it would have to do so either before it exists (impossible) or after it exists (superfluous, proving its existence depends on another). Then he attacks the specific cases: parents lack full control and knowledge of their offspring, and inanimate objects (stars, spheres) are unaware of their effects. The existence of complex faculties like hearing, sight, speech, and intellect in beings that start as helpless infants and grow only through external nutrition points to a wise maker, not blind processes. Finally, he notes that all things have fixed limits beyond which they do not grow, and even with continued nutrition they eventually decline—this indicates a transcendent, all-knowing, and all-powerful agent, not internal mechanisms.

Maturidi presents a concise but profound proof that cuts through the question of origins. He shows that if something could bring itself into existence, it could also annihilate itself — a contradiction. This forces us to recognize that all contingent beings are caused by an external source.

Commentary
This passage presents a brief but fundamental proof for the existence of a creator. Maturidi begins by stating a disjunction: either a thing exists before its own existence (which is impossible, as a non-existent cannot bring itself into existence), or it comes into existence after non-existence, implying that its existence is caused by another. The first option is self-contradictory because if something could cause its own existence, it could equally cause its own non-existence. Thus, the second option is necessary, affirming that everything owes its existence to an external cause. This is a classic kalam argument for a necessary being.

وأيضاً إن كل حي فيما يعاين مبني على الحاجات والشهوات التي تغلبهم وتقهرهم، ولولا ذلك ما احتاجوا إلى الأغذية. ثم كانت هي أسبابًا عند وجودها للغناء، لم يحتمل أن يكون منها تهيج الشهوات وحدوث الحاجات. ولا يجوز أن تكون لأنفسها شهوات وحاجات، لأوجه: أ) أحدها، أن القيام بالذات دون الغير دليل الغناء، لا يجوز أن يصير حاجة؛ ب) ولأن ما كان لذاته على جهة لا يحتمل زواله، وقد يقع لها الغناء من الغير؛ جـ) أو للإحالة أن يكون الشيء من حيث نفسه محوجاً إلى غير، بل إذا أحوج إلى غير من حيث نفسه تدوم الحاجة ما بقيت نفسه. فثبت أن هنالك غيرا أنشأهم على الحاجات وركب فيهم الشهوات، ثم أنشأ لهم ما به الغناء وقضاء الشهوات. فيكون بما أريد به نفي المدبر للعالم تثبيته. ولا قوة إلا بالله. مع ما لا يوجد شيء من أعيان العالم وصفاتها إلا مسخرا به مذللا بما لولا ذلك كان أهون عليه وألذ، نحو القرار الدائم والسير المتتابع مما كان به معاش أحد وما له المعاش فصار العالم بكليته بالمعنى الذي ذكرت ولا يجوز أن يكون المسخر المذلل يملك التدبير حتى يكون به غنى الغير وقيامه، ولا يملك إزالة الذلة عن نفسه والسخرة. ثبت أن لكل ذلك مدبرا عليما علم وجوه حاجاتهم وغناهم فخلقهم على ذلك مع ما أحوج بعضاً إلى بعض.
And also, every living being as is observed is built upon needs and desires that overcome and subdue them; were it not for that, they would not need nourishment. Then when these [needs and desires] exist, they become causes of sufficiency; it is unlikely that from them arise the excitation of desires and the occurrence of needs. And it is not permissible that they should have desires and needs by themselves, for reasons: A) One: existence by essence without another is proof of self-sufficiency; it cannot become need. B) And because that which is by essence in a certain manner does not admit its removal, and yet sufficiency from another occurs to it. C) Or because it is absurd that a thing should need another from its own perspective; rather, if it needs another from its own perspective, the need would continue as long as its essence remains. So it is established that there is another who created them upon needs and implanted desires in them, then created for them that by which is sufficiency and fulfillment of desires. Thus, what was intended to negate the manager of the world becomes its affirmation. And there is no power except with God. Along with that, nothing from the essences of the world and their attributes is found except subjugated and humiliated by that which, were it not so, would be easier and more pleasurable for it—such as permanent rest and continuous movement, by which is the livelihood of one and the life of another. So the whole world became according to the meaning I mentioned; and it is not permissible that the subjugated, humiliated one should possess management such that through it the sufficiency of another and its subsistence occur, while it cannot remove humiliation and subjugation from itself. It is established that all of that has a knowing manager who knew the aspects of their needs and sufficiency, so He created them upon that, along with making some of them need others.

Commentary
This passage presents a detailed argument from the inherent needs and desires of living beings. Maturidi observes that living beings are structured around needs and desires that compel them to seek sustenance. These needs and desires cannot arise from the beings themselves for three reasons: (1) self-sufficiency of essence precludes need; (2) if something is self-sufficient by essence, it cannot acquire need from another; (3) if a thing needed another by its own essence, the need would be permanent. Therefore, needs are imposed by an external creator who also provides means of satisfaction. Additionally, everything in the world is subjugated to certain functions (e.g., rest, movement), which shows it cannot manage itself. Hence, a wise manager (مدبر) is proven, who knows and creates accordingly, even establishing interdependence among beings.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Needs and desires are natural properties of living beings that arise spontaneously without an external cause.
Correction: Maturidi argues that needs cannot be self-originating because a self-sufficient essence cannot acquire need. Instead, needs are imposed by an external creator who also provides for them.

Key Takeaway
The inherent needs of living beings and the subjugation of all things logically require a wise creator who provides and manages.
