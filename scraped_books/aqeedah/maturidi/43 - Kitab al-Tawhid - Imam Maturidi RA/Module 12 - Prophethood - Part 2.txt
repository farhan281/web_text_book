Rational Necessity of Gratitude and Prophethood
Maturidi argues from rational obligation of gratitude to necessity of a messenger.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's rational argument that the intellect requires a messenger to know how to fully thank the Benefactor.

Core Idea
Gratitude to God cannot be fully known by reason alone; a messenger is needed.

This lesson presents Maturidi's foundational argument for prophethood. He starts from a universal rational principle: the intellect recognizes the goodness of thanking the Benefactor. Then he shows that the fullness of blessings and the proper gratitude for each limb surpasses reason, thus necessitating a divine informer.

Guided Path
1

Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the translation. Let the argument's flow come through.
2
Identify the rational premise
Notice Maturidi's opening: it is rationally established that knowing and thanking the Benefactor is good, and denial is evil. This is the foundation.
This premise is taken as self-evident in kalam.
3
Examine the two expressions (ibaratayn)
The passage presents two distinct arguments showing the insufficiency of reason. Find them and see how each leads to the need for a messenger.
The first concerns the scale of blessings, the second concerns the specific gratitude of each limb.
4
Understand the conclusion
Both expressions converge: the intellect cannot fully know the gratitude due, so it necessitates an informer (mukhbi) from God.
This is a rational proof for prophethood, not a traditional one.
5
Reflect on the implication
Consider how this argument shows that prophethood is not arbitrary but rationally required for fulfilling the duty of gratitude.

The Proof from Human Design for Worship and Prophethood
Maturidi argues from human anatomy that worship is the purpose of creation, requiring prophetic guidance.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The human body's extra capabilities prove we are made for worship, not just survival.

Maturidi presents a teleological argument grounded in human anatomy. He observes that our limbs are far more versatile than what is needed for mere worldly benefit — unlike animals. This extra capacity indicates a higher purpose: worship. But since reason alone cannot specify how each limb should worship, a messenger from God is required to explain it. In this passage, we see the logical link between creation and prophethood.

The Social Argument for Prophethood
Maturidi argues that pervasive human conflict necessitates a prophetic arbiter from God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses the universal reality of human conflict to prove the necessity of prophets as divine arbiters.

Core Idea
Human conflict proves we need a judge from God.

Maturidi's argument from social conflict is a powerful demonstration of prophethood's rational necessity. He observes that human beings inevitably disagree, and no one among them can serve as an impartial judge. This leads to corruption, which the intellect itself recognizes as evil. Yet the intellect, by its own logic, points to the need for a messenger from the Creator, who alone knows the remedy for conflict.

The First Proof: Scholars Differ, God Knows More
Maturidi's first proof: gradation of knowledge implies God's exclusive knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that gradation of knowledge among scholars points to God's exclusive knowledge, which He conveys through messengers.

Core Idea
Human knowledge is limited; God's knowledge is complete and conveyed through messengers.

Maturidi begins with a simple observation: scholars vary in their knowledge. If humans lack some beneficial knowledge, it is reasonable that God possesses it and sends messengers to share it. This lesson unpacks that first proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the logical structure: observation, consequence, conclusion.
The proof is based on common experience.
2
Identify the premise
Maturidi states that scholars differ in perceiving what benefits them. This is the starting point.
3
Understand the inference
If scholars differ, it is possible that God has something beneficial that no human knows. Therefore, He would send messengers to convey it.
This is a possibility argument, not a necessity yet.

Commentary
This passage contains two short proofs and a concluding section summarizing obstacles. The first proof: Scholars differ in knowledge; since they have partial knowledge, God may have exclusive knowledge of what is beneficial for humanity, which He conveys through messengers. The second proof: People either follow their own intellect or that of a superior person. If everyone followed their own intellect, contradictions would arise, making it impossible for all to be correct. Therefore, one must follow a superior intellect, which then functions like a messenger, requiring a proof to identify him. The conclusion lists obstacles that hinder the intellect: worldly preoccupations, worries, pains, desires, and pleasures. Hence, a messenger is necessary. Finally, two principles are mentioned: (1) Each sense has its own instrument, but even then, it can be hindered by afflictions and enemies; (2) Distance, smallness, and transparency prevent perfect perception, analogously indicating human limitation.
