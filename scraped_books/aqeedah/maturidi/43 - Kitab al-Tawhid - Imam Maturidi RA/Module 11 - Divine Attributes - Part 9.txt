Maturidi and Ash'ari: Agreement and Difference on Ru'ya
Maturidi and Ash'ari agree on ru'ya being possible without modality, differ on reason's role.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the points of agreement and disagreement between the Maturidi and Ash'ari schools regarding the vision of Allah (ru'ya), particularly the role of reason.

Core Idea
Both schools affirm ru'ya without modality, but differ on whether reason can prove its possibility.

In this passage, we explore a subtle but important theological difference between the Maturidi and Ash'ari schools. While both agree that believers will see Allah in the afterlife without knowing how, they part ways on whether human reason can demonstrate that such vision is even possible. Let's examine their common ground and their point of divergence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own flow come through before you interpret.
2
Identify the agreement
Find what both schools accept: ru'ya without modality (bila kayfiyya), and the obligation of ru'ya by transmitted evidence (sam'an).
Note that the passage says there is no disagreement on the transmitted evidence.
3
Identify the disagreement
Find the exact point of difference: whether reason (al-'aql) can prove the permissibility of ru'ya. Maturidi says no, Ash'ari says yes.
This is the central point of contention.

Al-Maturidi and Al-Ash'ari [agree] that vision (ru'ya) is without modality, but they differ after that among themselves as to whether that is known by a rational proof or whether that is referred to transmitted evidence alone. Commentary
This passage is a classification passage that delineates the points of agreement and disagreement between the Maturidi and Ash'ari schools on the issue of ru'ya (seeing Allah). It begins by stating their common ground: both affirm that ru'ya occurs 'without modality' (bila kayfiyya), meaning they accept its reality without claiming to know how. Then it identifies their central point of contention: whether this truth is known by rational proof (dalil al-'aql) or solely by transmitted evidence (al-naql). The author notes that regarding the transmitted evidence (i.e., the Quran and Sunna), there is no dispute—both schools accept the obligation of ru'ya based on scripture. The passage then clarifies that all Ahl al-Sunna, whether Maturidi or Ash'ari, agree on the interpretation of the relevant Quranic verses to prove the obligation of ru'ya by sam' (transmitted testimony). Their difference lies in the question of intellectual permissibility (jawaz 'aqlan). The author then presents the specific positions: Al-Maturidi holds that ru'ya is wajib sam'an without tafsir (interpretation), meaning we affirm it by revelation but reason cannot demonstrate its possibility. Al-Ash'ari holds that reason can indeed demonstrate its permissibility. This sets the stage for the subsequent discussion.

Maturidi and Ash'ari on the Vision of Allah: Agreement and Disagreement
Introduces Maturidi and Ash'ari positions on ru'ya and their point of difference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that while all Ahl al-Sunnah agree the vision of Allah is obligatory by revelation, the Maturidi and Ash'ari schools differ on whether reason can prove its permissibility.

Core Idea
Revelation obliges the vision; reason is either silent or speaks.

The vision of Allah (ru'ya) is a key attribute discussed by Sunni theologians. Both Maturidis and Ash'aris affirm it based on revelation, but they part ways on whether the human intellect can independently establish its possibility. This lesson introduces their respective positions and sets the stage for the Ash'ari rational proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on agreement
Read the Arabic passage carefully, focusing on the statement that all Ahl al-Sunnah agree on the vision being wajib sam'an. Note the common ground.
Both schools are united on the revealed obligation.
2
Identify the point of difference
Spot where the text says 'different about its permissibility by reason.' Recognize that the disagreement is on rational proof (ʿaql), not on the revealed ruling.
Maturidi denies rational proof; Ash'ari affirms it.
3
Understand Maturidi's position
Al-Maturidi holds that reason cannot prove the possibility or impossibility; we simply accept it by revelation. This is a principled stance on the limits of reason.
This does not mean denying the vision, only that reason cannot confirm it.
4
Preview Ash'ari's proof
The passage introduces Ash'ari's famous proof from existence (dalīl al-wujūd). In the next lesson, we will explore it in detail.
This proof attempts to show rational permissibility using a method called sabr wa taqsīm.

Prophet introduction. Allah, and is it fitting for the station of prophethood that Moses should be ignorant of his Lord's command what the Mu'tazila knew? These are among the conclusive arguments and irrefutable proofs that Ahl al-Sunnah extracted from the Qur'an to prove the vision (ru'ya). In such magnificent interpretations of the Noble Qur'an lies the strength of understanding and intellect of Ahl al-Sunnah. And all Ahl al-Sunnah, both Maturidis and Ash'aris, do not differ among themselves regarding the interpretation of these verses to prove the obligation of the vision by revelation (sam'an). As for al-Maturidi, he held that seeing Allah is obligatory by revelation without rational interpretation, meaning we believe that the vision is obligatory by the Book and the Sunnah, but reason is incapable of providing proof for the possibility of seeing Allah. As for al-Ash'ari, he held that it is possible to give rational proof for the permissibility of seeing Allah, and he has a famous proof for that which the theologians call the proof from existence (dalīl al-wujūd), which we can summarize as follows: The possibility of vision in the witnessed world (al-shāhid) arises only from existence, and Allah is seen. But that visible things in the witnessed world are possible to be seen because of their existence, not because they are existent, so it is permissible that others be seen. The proof for it is that we see things with different realities, such as substances and accidents, and it is not permissible that what corrects vision be something that differs among them, because that would lead to there being two different causes for one judgment, and that is rejected by basic intellects. So it is necessary to find a common description among these different realities that we see, to which the permissibility of this vision is referred, so that the cause is consistent and reversible. And the method of exploration and division (al-sabr wa al-taqsīm) has shown that there is nothing common among these different realities of substances and accidents except existence and temporal origination (ḥudūth), but temporal origination cannot be the cause for the validity of vision, because temporal origination is existence preceded by non-existence, and non-existence has no effect on the judgment. And if temporal origination is invalidated, nothing remains but existence, and existence is common between the witnessed and the absent (al-ghāʾib). So the existence of Allah is a valid cause for the validity of seeing Him, and when the cause obtains, the judgment obtains inevitably, so it is necessary to affirm the validity of seeing Him. Commentary
This passage presents the positions of the two main Sunni schools—Maturidi and Ash'ari—regarding the vision of Allah (ru'ya) and then details the Ash'ari rational proof. It begins by stating that both schools agree that the vision of Allah is obligatory based on revelation (samʿan), i.e., proven by Quran and hadith. Their disagreement is whether reason (ʿaql) can also prove its permissibility (jawāz). Al-Maturidi holds that reason is incapable: we accept it by revelation alone, but rational proof is beyond us. Al-Ash'ari, however, argues that rational proof is possible, and he provides his famous 'proof from existence' (dalīl al-wujūd). The proof starts from the observation that in the witnessed world (al-shāhid), the permissibility of seeing something arises solely from its existence, not from any other attribute. Since we see things of different kinds (substances and accidents), the cause must be something common to all of them. Through the method of exploration and division (al-sabr wa al-taqsīm), we find that the only common qualities are existence and temporal origination (ḥudūth). Temporal origination is invalid because it involves non-existence, which cannot ground a positive judgment. Therefore, existence remains as the sole cause. Since Allah exists, His existence is a valid cause for the permissibility of seeing Him. The conclusion is that it is rationally permissible to see Allah. The footnote cites sources for this argument.

The Ash'ari Proof for the Rational Possibility of Seeing Allah
Presents the full Ash'ari rational proof (dalīl al-wujūd) for the permissibility of seeing Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure of the Ash'ari proof from existence (dalīl al-wujūd) and how it uses the method of exploration and division (sabr wa taqsīm) to establish that existence is the cause of visibility.

Core Idea
Existence alone makes something seeable; since Allah exists, He can be seen.

This lesson unpacks the famous Ash'ari proof for the rational permissibility of seeing Allah. The argument moves from observable realities in the witnessed world (shāhid) to the unseen (ghā'ib) using a rigorous method of identifying a common cause. By the end, you will see how the proof concludes that Allah's existence suffices to make His vision possible.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof in Arabic
Read the entire proof passage from 'إن إمكان الرؤية في الشاهد' to 'فوجب القول بصحة رؤيته'. Try to follow the logical flow without stopping.
The proof is compact; take your time.
2
Understand the starting observation
The proof begins by noting that in the witnessed world, visibility depends solely on existence. We see many different kinds of things (substances and accidents), so the cause cannot be something specific to one kind.
The variety of visible things shows the cause must be common.
3
Follow the method of sabr wa taqsīm
By exploring and dividing possible causes, the proof eliminates all but two: existence and temporal origination (ḥudūth). Then it argues that ḥudūth involves non-existence and cannot ground a positive judgment like visibility.
This leads to the conclusion that existence is the sole cause.
4
Apply to Allah
Since existence is common between the witnessed (shāhid) and the unseen (ghā'ib), and Allah exists, His existence is a valid cause for the possibility of seeing Him. Therefore, it is rationally permissible.
The proof does not prove that we will see Allah, only that it is not impossible.

Maturidi links God's oneness to the finitude of the world.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's logical argument that the finitude of the world necessitates the oneness of God.

Core Idea
If the world were infinite, multiple gods could exist; but it is finite, so there is only one God.

This lesson unpacks a concise but powerful argument from Imam Maturidi. He links the observable fact that the world is finite to the rational necessity of God's unity. The argument works by showing that multiple gods would require an infinite world, contradicting the world's finitude.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Notice how Maturidi structures his argument as a conditional: 'If... then...'.
The passage is brief but dense. Read it twice.
2
Identify the logical structure
The argument has three parts: (1) assumption of multiple gods, (2) consequence of infinite creation, (3) conclusion that world must be infinite, but it is finite, so assumption false.
This is a classical reductio ad absurdum.
3
Consider the premise of world's finitude
Maturidi takes the finitude of the world as a given, based on the cosmology of his time. Notice that the argument's strength depends on this premise.
Later Islamic philosophers debated this premise, but Maturidi accepted it.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The conclusion is that God must be one. Multiple gods would lead to an infinite regress of creators and creations, which is impossible given the finite world.
This proof is sometimes called 'the argument from the impossibility of an infinite series of causes'.

Core Idea
We cannot name Allah except by what He named Himself.

Maturidi begins by establishing a foundational rule for how Muslims speak about Allah: we must restrict ourselves to names that Allah has revealed. Learning Objective

Core Idea

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'shay'' (thing) and proves that it is properly affirmed of God without implying similarity to creation. Maturidi begins by stating that there are two expressions (عبارتان) regarding the statement about a thing (القول بالشيء). He then provides two proofs for affirming that God is called 'thing': the first is scriptural (السمع) citing two verses (42:11 and 6:19) which would be meaningless if 'thing' did not apply to God. Next, he argues that saying 'not a thing' implies negation or diminishment, so saying 'thing' affirms the essence and glorifies it. He contrasts this with saying 'body' (جسم) which does not affirm anything praiseworthy or majestic; similarly, saying 'not knowing' negates majesty while 'knowing' entails it. In the witnessed world (الشاهد), he explains that saying 'thing' conveys existence only, not quiddity, whereas saying 'body' conveys quiddity (dimensions, etc.). The conclusion is that affirming the term 'thing' for God is obligatory based on revelation. The passage guards against the misunderstanding that calling God 'thing' implies anthropomorphism; rather it simply affirms existence.

God's ability to reverse and create from nothing proves His choice, not nature.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of rational proofs that God's actions are by choice (ikhtiyar), then derives the attributes of power (qudra), will (irada), and knowledge ('ilm). The author begins with the observation that God creates things and then annihilates them, then restores them (like the cycle of night and day). This ability to alternate states shows choice, because natural processes cannot reverse themselves in such a purposeful manner. Next, he appeals to creation ex nihilo (huduth al-‘alam la min shay'), which is an act only possible for a being with utmost choice; nature acts by necessity and compulsion. He then points to the universal practice of supplication (du'a) and prayer to God for specific outcomes—people naturally seek help from God, not from a compelled force, indicating that God acts by choice. From choice, he infers power and will: choice implies the ability to perform or not perform an act (qudra) and the specific determination of that act (irada). He argues that without power, action would be disordered, and that true power enables a thing and its opposite. Then he uses the consistent and flawless order in creation to prove that God acts with knowledge ('ilm). The argument is based on the principle that an orderly, purposeful sequence cannot arise from ignorance; it must be known. Finally, he notes that the very fact that creation points to its own createdness and to the oneness of its Creator presupposes knowledge on the part of the Creator. This lesson demonstrates the classical kalam method of proving divine attributes through rational inference from observable phenomena.
