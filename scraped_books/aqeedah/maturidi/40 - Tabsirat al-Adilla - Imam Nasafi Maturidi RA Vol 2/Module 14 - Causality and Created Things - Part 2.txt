Shifting the Burden in the Power Objection
Answer to an objection about power's relation to origination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Maturidi theologian answers the objection that power (qudra) only relates to origination by redirecting the opponent to refute the proof that only Allah has creative power.

Core Idea
The objection is neutralized by forcing the opponent to refute the proof first.

The opponent claims that power can only relate to origination, and that its relation to the manner of origination is inconceivable. The reply does not directly defend the relation; instead, it reminds the opponent of the already-established proof that only Allah has the power of invention. The burden is shifted: the opponent must object to that proof rather than simply assert inconceivability.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the opponent's statement that power only relates to origination and that its relation to the manner of origination is inconceivable. Notice the logical move they are making.
The opponent is trying to limit power's scope to a specific act.
2
Examine the reply
The author responds by referencing the proof that only Allah has the power of invention and origination. He then demands that the opponent object to that proof instead of raising a new point.
This is a methodological argument about where the burden of proof lies.
3
Reflect on the methodological lesson
Consider why this shift is effective: it forces the opponent to engage with the core proof rather than create a side objection. This teaches a key principle of theological debate.

The Objection and Obligation to Follow Proof
Nasafi states the objection and gives the initial proof-based reply.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Nasafi responds to the objection that if the servant cannot originate acts, his power is nullified, and why following the proof is obligatory.

Core Idea
Proof obligates us to affirm the servant's act even when origination is impossible.

The opponents argue that denying the servant power of invention renders his power useless. Imam Nasafi replies by insisting that the rational proof already establishes the servant has an act within his power (maqdur), and we must adhere to the proof even if we cannot fully explain the mechanics. This lesson presents the initial response.

This is a classic objection-and-answer format in kalam.

Understand key terms

The objection claims that without 'اختراع', power has no object.

See the role of proof

This highlights the priority of rational proof over speculative objections.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The author concludes that the two proofs together permit power to relate to an act without origination. Ask yourself why this is sufficient for now.
The detailed mechanism is explained later; here the focus is on accepting the proof.

When the Servant's Act Becomes His Act: A Theological Taxonomy
Nasafi classifies divine creation in beings without choice and with choice, explaining when the act is attributed to the servant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Maturidi distinction between what God creates in beings without choice and in beings with choice, and how the servant's choice and power make the created act his.

Core Idea
The servant's power and choice turn God's creation into the servant's act.

After establishing that the servant has an act, Imam Nasafi provides a detailed taxonomy of divine creation in relation to beings with and without choice. This clarifies why the servant is genuinely the agent of his actions, even though God creates them. This lesson explores that taxonomy and contrasts it with the Ash'ari and Najjariyya views.

Guided Path
1
Read the detailed clarification
Read the second part from 'ثم نقول' to the end. Notice the classification based on whether the being has choice and whether the action is within the power of the locus.
This is a sophisticated theological distinction.
2
Analyze beings without choice
Understand what happens when God creates something in a being that has no choice (e.g., inanimate objects). According to Maturidis, it is not an act for that being, but for Ash'aris/Najjaris it is God's act.
Key point: 'لا يكون فعلاً له' – it is not its act.
3
Analyze beings with choice
For humans, distinguish two cases: (a) when the thing is not within the person's power – then it is not an act of the servant; (b) when the person chooses and has power – then the effect of power makes it an act.
The phrase 'فأثر تعلق قدرته به كونه فعلاً' is crucial.
4
Reflect on the role of choice
Note that God creates the act, but He does so only because the servant chooses and intends to acquire it (لولا اختيار العبد وقصده اكتسابه). The servant's choice is a necessary condition.
This preserves human responsibility while affirming divine creation.
5
Understand the gratuitous nature
The author says this explanation is by way of 'تبرع' (gratuitousness), emphasizing that the obligation to follow the two proofs suffices without such details.
This shows the author's confidence in the earlier proof-based response.

Divine Power Has Two Aspects: Invention and Acquisition
Author presents opponent's analogy and refutes it by distinguishing two modalities of power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the analogy between divine knowledge and divine power fails, and how power relates to its object in two distinct respects.

Core Idea
Divine power relates to acts in two ways; knowledge has only one way.

The opponent claims that just as divine knowledge must match reality, divine power must give the same power to humans. The author rebuts by showing power has two aspects: invention (divine) and acquisition (human). This distinction is essential for the Maturidi theory of kasb.

Guided Path
1
Read the opponent's analogy
Start by reading the first part of the passage, where the opponent draws a parallel between knowledge and power. Notice how he argues from the necessity of truthful information.
The analogy hinges on the idea that if God informs us incorrectly, it becomes ignorance; similarly, if He empowers us differently, it would not be true empowerment.
2
Identify the author's refutation
After the phrase 'we answer this speech', the author directly rebuts the analogy. He asserts that empowerment can still occur even if the servant is not given the power of invention.
Look for the key phrase 'بل يكون ذلك إقدارًا وإن لم يقدرنا على الاختراع'.
3
Understand the two respects of power
The author explains that power can relate to its object in two ways: the aspect of invention (ikhtiraʿ) and the aspect of acquisition (iktisāb). One belongs to God, the other to the servant.
This is the central doctrinal point: maqdur is of two types, mukhtaraʿ and muktasab.
4
Contrast with knowledge
The author emphasizes that knowledge has only one relation: it must correspond to reality as it is. This contrast shows why the analogy fails.
If knowledge related differently, it would be ignorance, not knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the opponent's analogy between divine knowledge and divine power regarding human acts. The opponent argues: if God knows something and informs us, it becomes knowledge for us only if He informs us as it really is; otherwise it is ignorance. Similarly, if God has power over something and empowers us, He must empower us in the same respect (i.e., invention), otherwise it is not true empowering. The author replies by distinguishing the modalities of power from knowledge. Power, he says, relates to its object in two distinct ways: the aspect of invention (ikhtiraʿ) and the aspect of acquisition (iktisāb). The first is exclusive to God, the second to the contingent creature (the servant). This is based on two proofs: the impossibility of invention by the servant, and the establishment of human act. In contrast, knowledge relates to the known in only one way: as it really is. If it related otherwise, it would be ignorance. Therefore, the analogy fails: divine power has two aspects, while divine knowledge has only one.

Refutation of the alternative that the generated effect is by a new power.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If the effect came from a separate power, it would not depend on the cause.

Having ruled out the same power, Imam al-Nasafi now examines the alternative: that the generated effect arises from a different power, one specifically delegated for that effect. He argues that this would sever the necessary connection between cause and effect, making the effect independent of the cause—a position he shows to be absurd. When you face a situation where medical treatment seems ineffective, do you truly believe that God alone is the Harmer and Benefactor, or do you find yourself feeling desperate about the apparent failure of causes? How can you internalize Al-Maturidi’s dialectical method to guard your faith from implicit attribution of power to other than God?
?
The passage says religious goodness has no limit. If you sincerely believed that, how would it change your effort in seeking closeness to God? Are you settling for a limited amount of worship, fearing that more would somehow be too much?
?
Al-Maturidi conceded to opponents only in appearance to expose their falsehood. In your discussions with people who deny God's sole agency, do you sometimes compromise your belief just to avoid conflict? What would it look like to follow his method of outwardly accommodating while inwardly holding the truth, and then gently revealing the fallacy?
