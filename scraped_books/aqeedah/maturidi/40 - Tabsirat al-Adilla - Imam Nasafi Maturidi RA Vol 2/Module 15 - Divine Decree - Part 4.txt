Giving the Disbeliever Power to Disbelieve
Al-Nasafi argues it is not beneficial to give power to those who will misuse it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Nasafi's argument that giving the disbeliever the power to disbelieve is not the most beneficial act from the perspective of divine optimality (al-aslah).

Core Idea
If God knows a person will disbelieve, giving them that power cannot be the most beneficial act.

In this passage, al-Nasafi continues the debate on divine optimality (al-aslah). He questions whether it is truly beneficial for God to grant the disbeliever the power to disbelieve, especially given His foreknowledge. Through a series of logical alternatives, al-Nasafi forces the opponent to confront the implications of their position. Let's follow his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the setup
Al-Nasafi starts by confirming that God gives the disbeliever the power (qudra) to disbelieve. This is the basis of the discussion.
2
Examine the two alternatives
He poses two options: either it is more beneficial not to give the power, or it is more beneficial to give it. Evaluate each.
3
Respond to the first option
If the opponent says it is better not to give, then God's actual act of giving shows He abandoned the optimal, which is problematic.
4
Consider the second option and the distinction of ta'rid
If the opponent says it is better to give despite foreknowledge, that is obstinate. The appeal to ta'rid (conditioning) is already refuted.
5
Address the responsibility argument
The opponent argues that without power there is no moral responsibility. Al-Nasafi counters that exiting responsibility is actually beneficial because it avoids eternal punishment.

God's Power to Cause Death Before Disbelief
Argues that God could cause early death to prevent disbelief, proving power over al-aslah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author uses a hypothetical question about God's power to cause early death to argue for the concept of al-aslah against the Mu'tazilites.

Core Idea
God's power to cause early death before disbelief proves He also has power over what is most beneficial.

In this passage, the author engages in a dialectical argument with the Mu'tazilites over the concept of al-aslah (the most beneficial). He poses a simple question: could God have caused a potential disbeliever to die before reaching puberty, thus preventing his disbelief? Depending on the opponent's answer, either they admit God's incapability or they affirm His power over al-aslah. This short but sharp argument reveals a key Maturidi position on divine power and human destiny.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice the dialectical structure: a question, two possible answers, and a conclusion.
The argument is presented in the form of a dilemma.
2
Identify the key concepts
Look for the terms 'al-aslah' (الأصلح) and 'imata' (الإماتة). Understand how they relate to God's power and human salvation.
Al-aslah is the Mu'tazilite concept that God must do what is most beneficial for His creatures.
3
Trace the logical steps
Follow the argument: If the opponent denies God's power, they attribute incapability to Him. If they affirm it, they must accept God's power over al-aslah. Then note the additional point about withholding intellects.
This is a typical kalam technique using a conditional question.
4
Reflect on the implications
Ask yourself: Why does early death count as 'more beneficial'? How does this argument undermine the Mu'tazilite insistence on al-aslah?
The benefit is salvation from eternal punishment, not worldly comfort.

This passage presents a sharp dialectical argument that turns the Mu'tazilite position on its head. It begins by asking whether God has the power to make all people believe, then forces the opponent into a choice between limiting God's power or admitting that He abandoned what is most beneficial. The argument culminates in a stark comparison between a small reward and eternal punishment, exposing an absurdity in the Mu'tazilite view.

Reward for the believers and in the way of punishment for the disbelievers. Invalid, for we say: what He did from that by way of reward, was there through it an additional assistance towards good and obedience or not? If they say: Yes. It is said: Why did He not give him that before his deserving it by way of reward, while it is more beneficial and more suitable for him, so He prevented the most suitable when [he] accepted the response by his action? And if they say: There is not obtained by it an additional assistance. It is said: There is no benefit in it then, and it is not a blessing, so there is no meaning to making it a reward. And likewise it is said: Is this meaning such that if He did it to the disbeliever, he would believe? If they say: No. Then there is no benefit in it, so it is not suitable as a reward. Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite position regarding reward and punishment and the concept of 'aslah' (most beneficial). The author begins by quoting the Mu'tazilite statement that reward is for believers and punishment is for disbelievers, and then declares it invalid. He then poses a series of dialectical questions. First, concerning the reward: if God's action of rewarding provides additional assistance towards good, then why did He not give it before the person earned it, as it would be more beneficial? If they say it does not provide assistance, then it has no benefit and cannot be a reward. Similarly, if the disbeliever would have believed if given that assistance, then God prevented him; if not, then it is useless. He then turns to the actions done to the disbeliever, such as sealing the heart. If they claim that after such actions the disbeliever is no longer tested, then they would be excused, which contradicts the consensus. If they claim they are still tested, then the author asks whether the presence or absence of these seals is more beneficial for their religion. Either answer leads to a contradiction: either they consider a punishment as beneficial, or they admit God does what is not beneficial. The author concludes that there is no harm in this seal, as it does not cause pain in this world and is a means to benefit in the Hereafter, and the Mu'tazilites have no answer but silence.

Learning Objective
