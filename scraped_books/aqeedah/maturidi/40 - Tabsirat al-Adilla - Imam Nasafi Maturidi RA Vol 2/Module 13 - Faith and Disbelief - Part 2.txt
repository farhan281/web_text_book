The Argument Against Including Actions in Iman
Argument against including actions in Iman using inconsistency in loyalty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument for excluding actions from the definition of Iman, based on the inconsistency it would create in loyalty and enmity.

Core Idea
If actions were part of Iman, loyalty would depend on imperfect behavior, not belief.

In this final part of the passage, the author presents a powerful argument against those who include actions in the definition of Iman. He points out that such a view leads to a contradiction: one would have to love sinful believers and hate righteous unbelievers. Therefore, Iman must be solely a matter of belief (i'tiqad), not actions.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument
Read the Arabic and translation, following the logical flow: premise, consequence, conclusion.
The argument is a reductio ad absurdum.
2
Identify the inconsistency
The author claims that those who include actions show loyalty based on belief alone, not actions, contradicting their own definition.
They love sinful co-believers and hate righteous opponents.
3
Understand the conclusion
The conclusion is that Iman is only belief (i'tiqad), and the judgment of being a believer is based on that, not actions.
This solidifies the position that actions are not part of the essence of Iman.

Commentary
This passage is a presentation of the different opinions on the definition of iman (faith) and a clarification of the author's preferred position. It begins by noting that the hypocrites in the Prophet's time were considered believers outwardly, then introduces the view that iman is only in the heart. Within this, some say iman is ma'rifa (knowledge of the heart) as held by Jahm ibn Safwan and Abu al-Husayn al-Salihi, while others say it is tasdiq (affirmation) by the heart, which is the position of al-Maturidi, Abu Hanifa, al-Bajali, and al-Ash'ari. The author notes that al-Ash'ari at one point preferred al-Salihi's view, but his well-known madhhab is tasdiq. Al-Ash'ari also held that applying the name 'iman' to acts like prayer and fasting is an extended usage (tawassu'), not literal. The author then argues that making actions part of iman leads to inconsistency in loyalty and enmity, since one would have to love those who agree in belief even if they are sinful and hate those who disagree even if they are righteous. Therefore, iman is solely a matter of belief (i'tiqad), and the ruling of iman is based on it, not on actions.

Proof from Opposites: Iman is Tasdiq
First proof: argument from opposites with Quranic support.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that iman must be tasdiq because its opposite (kufr) is denial (takdhib).

Core Idea
Opposites reveal reality: kufr is denial, so iman is affirmation.

The author begins with a logical argument based on the relationship between opposites. Since kufr (unbelief) is defined as denial (takdhib) and rejection (juhud), and it is the opposite of iman, iman must be the opposite of denial — that is, affirmation (tasdiq). This foundational reasoning is supported by the Quranic verse 2:256.

2
Identify the definitions

The verse 2:256 is cited as evidence that kufr and iman are opposites.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
Understand why the author concludes that iman must be tasdiq.
If kufr is denial, iman cannot be anything but affirmation.

Proof from Language: Iman Means Tasdiq in Arabic
Second proof: linguistic argument from the people of language.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the linguistic argument that iman cannot be redefined because it is established in the Arabic language as tasdiq alone.

Core Idea
Language is fixed by convention; changing iman's meaning would destroy language.

The second proof is based on the linguistic convention (ahl al-lisan). The author argues that in the Arabic language, iman is known exclusively as tasdiq. To give it any other meaning would be to distort language, and if that were allowed, no word would have a fixed meaning.

Understand the consequence

Apply the lesson

It shows that redefining iman is not just a theological error but a linguistic one.

Commentary

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Friendship and enmity change with the person's state, not with God's foreknowledge.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the view (attributed to some Ash'aris and Khawarij) that a person's faith or disbelief in the present is determined by their final state (al-ʿāqiba) as known by Allah. Objection and Answer: Divine Knowledge and Friendship/Enmity
Nasafi answers the objection about divine knowledge and present friendship/enmity by clarifying that these are contingent on actual faith and disbelief, not on future knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Nasafi responds to the objection that divine foreknowledge of the final state implies present friendship or enmity with Allah.

Core Idea
Friendship and enmity change with faith; divine knowledge does not alter the present relationship.

After his main refutation, Nasafi anticipates an objection: if Allah knows someone will be sealed with faith, does that not make them His friend now? And if He knows someone will be sealed with disbelief, do they not become His enemy now? Nasafi answers by distinguishing between the relationship that results from faith and the capacity for change.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
The objectors argue that divine knowledge of final state makes a person a friend or enemy of Allah in the present. Identify their reasoning.
They believe that because Allah knows the outcome, the person is currently in that state.
2
Understand the answer
Nasafi responds that friendship and enmity are consequences of faith and disbelief. They change when faith or disbelief changes. So a believer who later disbelieves becomes an enemy. The change occurs on the person, not on the relationship itself.
He distinguishes between the attribute (wilāya) and the person described by it.
3
Apply the analogy
Nasafi compares this to knowledge and the known: knowledge changes according to the state of the known object, but the known object itself changes. Similarly, the person changes, but the relationship of friendship/enmity is derived from their state.
This analogy reinforces his point.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the view (attributed to some Ash'aris and Khawarij) that a person's faith or disbelief in the present is determined by their final state (al-ʿāqiba) as known by Allah. Nasafi begins by establishing a principle: when an attribute (like sitting, standing, black, white) actually exists in its reality, the entity is described by it. Similarly, if affirmation (tasdiq) exists in a person, that person is a believer in reality, regardless of future outcomes. He then states that the contrary view—that present faith has no value and only the final outcome matters—is meaningless. He supports this with an argument: if we judged by future knowledge, then a person who currently denies Allah but is known to die a believer would be called a believer now, and a person who currently affirms but is known to die a disbeliever would be called a disbeliever now—this is absurd because realities are judged by their present existence, not by what they will become. He illustrates with the example of life and death: Allah knows a living person as living, not as dead at present, even though He knows he will die. Likewise for all attributes. Further, he argues that if matters were judged by their end results, we would have to say we are now in the Hereafter (since that is the final state) and every dead person was alive—which is absurd. This reductio ad absurdum shows the doctrine's falsehood. Then Nasafi addresses an objection: 'Doesn't divine knowledge of the final state make a person a friend or enemy of Allah now?' He answers that friendship (wilāya) and enmity (ʿadāwa) are consequential states arising from faith and disbelief; they can change when faith or disbelief changes. The change occurs on the person (who is described by these), not on the relationship itself—just as knowledge and the known, and informing and what is informed about, are distinct. The passage concludes with 'Allah grants success.' Overall, it is a rational argument maintaining that present reality, not future knowledge, determines current judgment of faith and disbelief.

The Invalidity of Saying 'I Am a Believer if Allah Wills'
Nasafi refutes the practice of qualifying 'I am a believer' with 'if Allah wills', using analogy and logical argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the statement 'I am a believer if Allah wills' is invalid when faith is already present, and how Nasafi refutes this practice using analogy and logical consistency.

Core Idea
When faith is actual in the heart, qualifying it with 'if Allah wills' is as false as saying 'I am standing if Allah wills' while standing.

This lesson examines a subtle but important issue in Islamic theology: the practice of suspending one's declaration of faith with the phrase 'if Allah wills.' Nasafi argues that this practice is fundamentally flawed because faith, being internal affirmation (tasdiq), is already a present reality for the believer. The condition implies doubt, which contradicts the certainty of faith. By the end of this lesson, you will see how Nasafi uses a powerful analogy to expose the logical inconsistency in this statement.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation

Notice the phrase 'لما وجد' which emphasizes the existence of faith.
2
Identify the core analogy
Nasafi compares saying 'I am a believer if Allah wills' to saying 'I am standing if Allah wills' while actually standing. Understand why he considers this false.
The condition 'if Allah wills' implies uncertainty about the present state, which is contradictory when the state is certain.
3
Understand the two nuanced positions refuted
Nasafi addresses two groups: those who say 'my faith is true without exception' yet say 'if Allah wills', and those who say 'I am a believer in Allah without exception' yet say 'I am a believer before Allah if Allah wills'. He shows both are meaningless.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the practice of saying 'I am a believer if Allah wills' (istithna') when one actually possesses faith. Nasafi begins by linking this discussion to the previous refutation: by knowing the falsity of the muwafat theory, we also know the falsity of this statement. The argument is straightforward: since internal affirmation (tasdiq) is the reality of faith, and it exists in the believer, then faith is actualized. He asserts that when faith is actualized in its reality, one should simply affirm it without qualification. The passage cuts off with 'he says: I am' but the point is clear: the proper response is to affirm faith directly, not with istithna'. Nasafi is consistent with the Maturidi position that faith is certain and should not be suspended by conditional language.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why saying 'I am a believer if Allah wills' contradicts the reality of present Iman.

Core Idea
Present Iman is a reality; attaching a condition to it is logically invalid.

Many Muslims say 'I am a believer if Allah wills' out of humility. But Imam al-Nasafi explains that this statement is invalid because Iman, being inner affirmation (tasdiq), already exists within the believer. To attach a condition to it is like saying 'I am sitting if Allah wills' while sitting — a logical contradiction. In this lesson, we will examine the core argument and the rebuttals to common objections.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the view that a believer should say 'I am a believer if Allah wills' when asked about his Iman. The author begins by stating that the invalidity of their statement is known by knowing that Iman is the reality of tasdiq. Since tasdiq is already present in the believer, Iman is realized as a reality. Therefore, saying 'I am a believer if Allah wills' while tasdiq exists is analogous to saying 'I am standing if Allah wills' while actually standing—it is logically invalid because it attaches a condition to something that already exists. The author then addresses the claim of some that their Iman is true but they still say 'if Allah wills'. The author argues that if Iman is true, then one is truly a believer, just as if one is sitting, he is truly sitting. Then he addresses the claim of those who say 'I am a believer in Allah without exception' yet also say 'I am a believer with Allah if Allah wills', arguing that if Iman is actualized, then one is truly a believer with Allah. The author explains that the doubt is not about present Iman but about the final state at death. The author refutes this by showing that if the present Iman is not considered, then the present kufr also would not be considered, leading to absurdity. The passage concludes that knowing the invalidity of that view shows the invalidity of this form of istithna.
