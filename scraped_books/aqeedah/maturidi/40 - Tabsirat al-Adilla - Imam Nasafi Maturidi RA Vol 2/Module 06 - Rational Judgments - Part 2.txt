Refuting the Claim that Revelation Permits Breach of Promise
Nasafi rebuts opponents who rely on revelation to permit breach of promise

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Nasafi refutes the opponents who use revelation to justify God's breach of promise, and why this shows that the view of absolute generality (عموم) of threat verses is not Sunni.

Core Idea
Revelation cannot override God's truthfulness; breach of promise remains impossible.

Having established that breach of promise is impossible for God, Nasafi now addresses a counterargument: some opponents claim that God might forgive disbelievers out of generosity, citing revelation. Nasafi challenges them to prove that revelation prevents such forgiveness, and then concludes that the position of generality (عموم) of threat verses is not sound on Sunni principles. Let's examine his rebuttal.

Nasafi concludes that the opponents' position leads to the claim that God can break His promise out of generosity. This, he says, shows that the view of absolute generality (عموم) of threat verses is not consistent with Sunni principles.
The key: if breach of promise is permissible, then the threat verses are not universally applicable.

The Implicit Rational Conditions in Verses of Promise and Threat
The author argues that conditions of dying on faith or unbelief are implicit in divine promises and threats.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi kalām argues that rational conditions are implicitly attached to general verses of divine promise and threat, even when not explicitly stated.

Divine promises and threats are rationally qualified even when textually absolute.

The author anticipates objections from those who hold to textual literalism and systematically refutes them, showing that without such conditions, God's truthfulness would be compromised. Commentary
This passage is a dialectical argument defending the Maturidi position that the general verses of divine promise (وعد) and threat (وعيد) are implicitly qualified by rational conditions, even if those conditions are not explicitly stated in the text. The author presents a series of objections from an opponent (likely a literalist or a Mu'tazilī who maintains absolute generality) and refutes them.

The argument begins with a rhetorical question: 'By God, will he be eternal in the Fire? Then necessarily yes.' This refers to the well-known case of a person who dies unrepentant of a major sin – the verse of threat applies. The author then extends the logic: if a person repents or embraces Islam, the promise of Paradise applies. The opponent then asks: Are not the expressions of promise and threat absolute, without explicit conditions of dying on faith or unbelief? The author responds that one must either (a) keep the conditions unapplied, which makes God a liar because then even repentant sinners would go to Hell and apostates to Paradise, or (b) acknowledge that the conditions of dying on faith or before repentance are attached even if not explicitly mentioned. The opponent is forced to choose the second, which is the Maturidi view.

Then the author notes that any reply the opponent gives for excluding someone from the general promise is equally valid for excluding someone from the general threat. The opponent might argue ignorance of chronology (which verses were revealed first) and treat them as simultaneous, but that would still require specification. The author asks why making the threat verses a restriction on promise verses is more valid than the reverse. Since they were revealed separately, without specification God would be lying if the threats are unfulfilled.

The opponent then appeals to rational evidence (دليل العقل) as a constant qualifier: reason dictates that it is impossible for a believer to be eternally in Hell or a repentant sinner to be eternally punished, so reason restricts both promise and threat. The author counters that the Ash'arites vehemently deny this, claiming reason alone cannot judge such matters; it is not rationally impossible for a believer to be punished eternally or a disbeliever rewarded.

The author then provides a further argument: if it were said that eternal punishment of a believer is rationally repugnant, likewise nullifying all his good deeds due to a single sin is repugnant, especially since each deed should be rewarded tenfold. So reason restricts the threat verses. Moreover, God's attributes of mercy and forgiveness also prevent the general application of threats. The passage concludes with 'والله الموفق' (And God is the granter of success).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two key interpretations of the word 'today' in the verse 'Today I have perfected your religion' and why they avoid the implication of prior imperfection.

Core Idea
The 'today' of perfection is not a single day but the entire prophetic era.

He first clarifies that 'today' is not literal, then provides two main interpretations: either it's a rhetorical opening or it refers to the Prophet's entire era. This lesson unpacks both views.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed interpretation of the verse 'Today I have perfected your religion' (Quran 5:3), aiming to explain away the apparent implication that the religion was incomplete before that specific day. Nasafi first states that the interpretation has several aspects. The first is that the word 'today' does not refer to a literal specific day, just as in the verse 'And I am pleased with Islam as a religion for you' (Quran 5:3 part), the phrase 'today' is not meant literally; rather, the religion was already pleasing to God before that. Then he splits this into two sub-aspects. The first sub-aspect is to completely drop the literal meaning of 'today' and treat it as a customary opening phrase without specifying actual time. The second sub-aspect is to understand 'today' as referring to the entire era of the Prophet (peace be upon him), because before that there was a period of intermission (fatrat) when no prophet was sent, and there was a need for a clear guide. So God sent His Messenger in that era to clarify the truth. Nasafi supports this with an analogy: a king might say to his subjects 'you today are in safety' but he means his whole reign, not just that particular day. Thus, the verse indicates that the religion was perfected over the entire prophetic era, not that it was imperfect earlier. He concludes with 'And God knows best.'

Correction: Nasafi shows that 'today' is either a rhetorical opening or refers to the entire prophetic era, so the perfection of religion was not a sudden event but occurred over time.

Checkpoint
What is the first interpretation given by Nasafi for the word 'today' in the verse?
A) It refers to a specific day.
B) It is used as an opening expression without specifying time.
C) It refers to the era of the Prophet.
D) It refers to the Day of Judgment.

Key Takeaway
Nasafi shows that the perfection of religion in the verse occurred over the Prophet's entire mission, not just a single day.
