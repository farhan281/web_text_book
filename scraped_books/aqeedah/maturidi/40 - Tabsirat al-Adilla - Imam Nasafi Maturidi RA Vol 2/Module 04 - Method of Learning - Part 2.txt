Context and Implicit Meaning in Hadith
Second reconciliation: narrator omitted because context makes it clear.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Maturidi uses the conceptual overlap between iman and Islam to explain why a narrator could omit the word 'shara'i' without causing confusion.

Core Idea
A believer is always a Muslim, so asking about Islam implies its laws.

Another way to reconcile the hadith is to realize that the context already makes the intended meaning clear. Al-Maturidi argues that since every believer is necessarily a Muslim and vice versa, the narrator might have omitted the word 'shara'i' because he knew no one would misunderstand.

Commentary
This passage is a multi-part reconciliation of hadith narrations where the Prophet was asked about iman in one version and about the laws (shara'i') of Islam in another. The author, following al-Maturidi, provides several interpretations to resolve the apparent discrepancy. First, he suggests that the narrator in the version mentioning 'Islam' simply did not hear the word 'shara'i' in the question, and this is more plausible than accusing a narrator of intentionally adding words, because narrators are trustworthy (adala) and would not fabricate. Second, the narrator may have omitted the word 'shara'i' because the context made it clear, since a believer must also be a Muslim and vice versa—so asking about Islam naturally implies asking about its laws. Third, a rhetorical device of Arabic (idad) is invoked: the word 'shara'i' is implied (admar) and the word 'Islam' stands in its place, similar to the Quranic phrase 'and ask the town' meaning 'ask the people of the town', where 'people' is omitted because the context disambiguates. Fourth, the author argues that the answer given (prayer, zakat, etc.) is clearly about the laws of Islam, not Islam itself, because one can have faith (iman) without performing these acts and still be a believer (though imperfect), but one cannot be a Muslim without them. Conversely, performing these acts without faith does not make one a Muslim. Thus, the answer must be about the laws. The author concludes by considering that the Prophet may have simply used 'Islam' to refer to its laws, a common metonymy.

Arabic Rhetoric: Implied Words in Hadith Reconciliation
Third and fourth reconciliations: idmar and metonymy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Maturidi invokes the Arabic rhetorical device of idmar (implication) and the Quranic analogy to reconcile the hadith narration.

Core Idea
Arabs routinely imply words when context clarifies meaning, just as in Quranic usage.

The final reconciliation method draws on a well-known feature of Arabic eloquence: the omission of a word that the context makes obvious. Al-Maturidi compares this to the Quranic verse 'and ask the town' where 'people' is implied. He then provides a theological argument showing that the answer given must refer to the laws of Islam, not Islam itself.

Guided Path
1
Read the third and fourth reconciliations
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice that this method uses the concept of 'idmar' (implication) and supports it with a Quranic analogy.
The Quranic phrase 'wa-li l-qarya' is from Surah Yusuf, meaning 'and ask the town' but implying 'the people of the town'.
2
Understand idmar and the Quranic example
Al-Maturidi explains that Arabs often place the genitive construct (mudaf ilayh) in place of the construct (mudaf) when confusion is impossible. The Quranic example shows that 'the town' stands for 'the people of the town' because the context makes it clear that a town cannot be asked.
Similarly, in the hadith, 'Islam' stands for 'the laws of Islam' because the context shows that the answer is about practices, not the essence.
3
Analyze the theological argument
Al-Maturidi then gives a theological reason: the answer (prayer, zakat, etc.) cannot be about Islam itself because a person can have iman without these acts and still be a believer, but without these acts one is not a Muslim. Therefore, the answer must be about the laws (shara'i').
This reinforces that the omission is not arbitrary but based on logical necessity.

Imamate Is Established by Ijtihad
Author states imamate by ijtihad and rejects nass claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that imamate is determined through ijtihad, not a divine text, and that the Shia claims of a nass for Ali or al-Abbas are false.

Core Idea
Imamate is a matter of human reasoning, not divine appointment.

The author begins by clearly stating the Sunni position: imamate is established through ijtihad, the same method used to derive rulings for novel legal issues. He then immediately dismisses the opposing views of the Rafida and Rawandiyya as false. This lesson sets the foundation for the rest of his argument.

Commentary
This passage contains the author's positive assertion that the imamate is established by ijtihād (independent reasoning) and his proof against the claim of a nass (explicit text). The author begins by stating that the correct way to establish the imamate for a qualified person is through ijtihād, analogous to how rulings are deduced for novel legal questions. He then declares the claims of the Rāfiḍa (Shī‘a) and Rāwandiyya regarding a nass on ‘Alī or al-‘Abbās are false. Instead, they chose the caliph through consultation, and their motives were purely religious—not worldly ambition, as demonstrated by the example of Abū Bakr al-Ṣiddīq's simple lifestyle and lack of personal desire. The argument uses the unanimity of the Companions' practice as evidence that no contrary nass existed.

The Proof from Widespread Transmission
Author provides proof from absence of widespread transmission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that if a nass for imamate existed, it would be as famous as the basic rituals of Islam, and its absence proves no nass.

Core Idea
If a nass existed, it would be universally known like the qibla—but it isn't.

The author now provides his first substantive proof. He reasons that the caliphate concerns all Muslims, so any divine appointment would have been transmitted as widely as the direction of prayer or the number of rak'as. Since no such report exists, there is no nass. This is a powerful argument from common sense and historical fact.

Read the passage and note the analogy

Commentary
This passage discusses the tension between the religious recommendation to remain silent about conflicts among the Companions (based on the hadith 'When my companions are mentioned, withhold') and the practical necessity of addressing them. It begins by acknowledging that complete silence and ignoring the events would be safer and more in line with the hadith. However, it argues that while tongues can be silent, hearts are still affected by the events, and the internal turmoil is more severe than outward speech. Therefore, the situation necessitated that the preservers of the Ummah (huffāẓ) and the theologians (mutakallimūn) record and explain the events, in order to safeguard hearts from believing falsehoods about the Companions, given their high status. The passage then notes that the jurists derived rulings from Ali's conduct, and explains why Ali's actions are authoritative: the Prophet may have taught others, but those rulings came to light only through Ali; Ali's actions were known to all and no one objected, which implies their correctness; and the Prophet's statement 'We fight according to the revelation, and you fight according to the interpretation' proves that Ali's actions were based on knowledge and in harmony with the Sharia.

The Principle of Specification in the Verse of Divine Election
Explanation of the verse about Adam and Noah and its application to Abu Bakr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the principle of specification (takhṣīṣ) is applied to Quranic verses of divine election, demonstrating that certain preferences are limited to specific contexts without implying absolute superiority.

Core Idea
Quranic verses of election are often specific to their context, not absolute rankings.

This passage from Imam al-Nasafi's Tabṣirat al-Adilla addresses a key methodological principle: how to interpret verses that seem to give preference to certain prophets. The author uses the example of the verse 'Indeed, Allah chose Adam and Noah... over the worlds' to show that such 'election' is specific to the people of their time, not including Prophet Muhammad. He then applies this reasoning to the virtues of the Companions, particularly Abu Bakr.
