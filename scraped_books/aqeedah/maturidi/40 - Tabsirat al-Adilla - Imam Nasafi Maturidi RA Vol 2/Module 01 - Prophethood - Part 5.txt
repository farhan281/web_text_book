Prophetic Inheritance is Knowledge, Not Wealth
Imam Nasafi explains the true inheritance of prophets as knowledge and wisdom, citing Quranic evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the true inheritance of prophets is knowledge and wisdom, not material wealth, and how Quranic verses and hadith support this.

Core Idea
Prophets leave knowledge, not gold; scholars are their true heirs.

When we hear about prophets inheriting, it's natural to think of wealth. But the Quran and hadith reveal a deeper reality. In this passage, Imam Nasafi clarifies that prophetic inheritance is spiritual, not material, using the examples of Solomon and Zechariah. Let's examine his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the English translation. Let the author's own argument come through before you interpret.
Notice the citation of Quranic verses and the hadith.
2
Identify the key evidence
List the pieces of evidence Nasafi uses: the Quranic verse from Fatir, the hadith about prophets not inheriting dinar or dirham, the saying about good manners, and the examples of Solomon and Zechariah.
Each piece supports the claim that inheritance is knowledge.
3
Understand the reasoning
Reflect on why the inheritance cannot be wealth. For example, the wealth of Jacob's family would go to all descendants, not just one child, so Zechariah's request must be for knowledge.
This logical point clinches the argument.

Commentary
This passage defines the proper understanding of prophetic inheritance. The author begins by citing the verse from Surah Maryam where Zechariah asks for an heir, and explains that the ignorant misunderstand such verses. The true inheritance is knowledge and wisdom, not material wealth. He supports this with Quranic evidence from Surah Fatir, where Allah says He causes the Book to be inherited by chosen servants. He also narrates hadith that prophets leave no dinar or dirham, only knowledge, leading to the well-known saying that scholars are the heirs of the prophets. A further proof is that the inheritance of Solomon from David was immediately followed by Solomon's mention of being taught the speech of birds and given everything, which points to knowledge and wisdom. Likewise, Zechariah's request for an heir who would inherit him and the family of Jacob cannot refer to wealth, because the family of Jacob's wealth would go to all his descendants, not just one child. Thus the inheritance intended is unmistakably knowledge.

Fatima's Claim: Gift vs Inheritance
Nasafi explains Fatima's gift claim and the witness requirement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Abu Bakr required two witnesses for Fatima's claim on Fadak and how Islamic legal principles applied.

Core Idea
Fatima claimed a gift, not inheritance; Abu Bakr lawfully required two witnesses.

The Fadak incident is often misunderstood as a dispute over inheritance. Imam Nasafi clarifies that Fatima, may Allah be pleased with her, claimed the land as a personal gift (nahl) from the Prophet, not as an inheritance. Since prophets do not leave inheritable wealth, the legal burden fell on proving the gift through proper testimony. Let's examine the exchange and its legal basis.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on Fadak
Read the Arabic passage describing Fatima's claim and Abu Bakr's response. Notice the key terms: نحلة (gift) and شهادة (testimony).
2
Understand the legal principle
Reflect on why inheritance from prophets is impossible. Then see how Abu Bakr applied the rule that a gift must be proven with two witnesses.
Prophetic wealth cannot be inherited, per earlier established doctrine.
3
Analyze the witness testimony
Ali testified, but Abu Bakr asked for another witness. Umm Ayman testified, but as a woman, the law requires two women or a man and two women. So the proof was incomplete.
This shows Abu Bakr's adherence to Islamic legal procedure.
4
Observe the outcome
Fatima left without pressing the claim further, indicating her acceptance of the ruling.
She did not dispute the legal requirement.

فأما أمر فدك ففاطمة رضي الله عنها - كانت لا تدعي فيها الميراث وإنما كانت تدعى أن رسول الله عليه السلام نحلها إياها، وكان لا يجوز لأبي بكر أن يعطيها فدك (٨) مع ما تقدم من امتناع جريان الإرث من أموال الأنبياء عليهم السلام (٩) ما لم يثبت عنده أنه عليه السلام نحلها في حال حياته فشهد لها بذلك علي رضي الله عنه فسألها شاهدًا آخر فشهدت لها أم أيمن مولاة النبي عليه السلام فقال: قد علمت يا ابنة رسول الله أنه لا يجوز إلا شهادة رجل وامرأتين، فانصرفت. والدليل على كون الصديق مصيبا في ذلك أن عليا رضي الله عنه لم يدفع ذلك إلى الحسن والحسين - رضي الله عنهما - وقت خلافته، ولو كان الأمر كما زعمت الروافض من ظلم أبي بكر عليا - رضي الله عنهما – لكان له أولى الناس بدفع تلك الظلامة. ذلك يحققه أن الصحابة بأسرهم امتنعوا عن الاعتراض عليه والتعرض له في أن الله تعالى وصفهم بأنهم يأمرون بالمعروف وينهون عن المنكر، ولو كان ذلك منكراً من أبي بكر رضي الله عنه لكانت الصحابة على الضد مما وصفهم الله تعالى وهو فاسد، وكذا بعد علي لم ير أحد ذلك من أبي بكر ظلمًا سوى المأمون لفرط ميله إلى الرفض وغلوه في ذلك.
As for the matter of Fadak, Fatima, may Allah be pleased with her, was not claiming inheritance in it; rather, she was claiming that the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, had gifted it to her. And it was not permissible for Abu Bakr to give her Fadak, given what had preceded concerning the prevention of inheritance from the wealth of the prophets, peace be upon them, unless it was established to him that he, peace be upon him, had gifted it to her during his lifetime. So Ali, may Allah be pleased with him, testified for her regarding that, then he asked her for another witness. So Umm Ayman, the maidservant of the Prophet, peace be upon him, testified for her, and he said: "I know, O daughter of the Messenger of Allah, that it is not permissible except the testimony of a man and two women." So she left. And the evidence that al-Siddiq (Abu Bakr) was correct in that is that Ali, may Allah be pleased with him, did not give that (Fadak) to al-Hasan and al-Husayn, may Allah be pleased with them, during his caliphate. If the matter were as the Rawafid claim—that Abu Bakr had wronged Ali, may Allah be pleased with them—then he would have been the most entitled of people to remove that wrong. That is confirmed by the fact that all the companions refrained from objecting to him (Abu Bakr) and challenging him regarding that, even though Allah Almighty described them as "enjoining good and forbidding evil." If that were an evil from Abu Bakr, may Allah be pleased with him, the companions would have been the opposite of what Allah described them as, which is corrupt. Similarly, after Ali, no one saw that as a wrong from Abu Bakr except al-Ma'mun due to his extreme inclination towards Rafidism and his excess in that.

Commentary
This passage defends Abu Bakr's ruling on Fadak against claims of injustice. The author explains that Fatima's claim was not for inheritance (which is forbidden for prophets) but for a gift (nahl) from the Prophet during his lifetime. Abu Bakr required two witnesses to prove the gift, consistent with Islamic law. Ali testified, but Abu Bakr asked for another witness; Umm Ayman testified, but since she was a woman, Abu Bakr explained that two women are needed with one man. Fatima then left. The author provides further evidence that Abu Bakr was correct: during his own caliphate, Ali did not restore Fadak to Fatima's descendants, which he would have if Abu Bakr had wronged them. Additionally, all companions remained silent on the issue, despite their duty to enjoin good and forbid evil—if Abu Bakr's action had been evil, they would have objected. Finally, the only later critic was al-Ma'mun, whose inclination towards Rafidism (extreme Shia) is cited as the reason for his dissent, not a valid objection.

Evidence of Abu Bakr's Correctness
Nasafi gives three proofs of Abu Bakr's correctness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three pieces of evidence Imam Nasafi uses to prove Abu Bakr's ruling on Fadak was correct.

Core Idea
Ali's caliphal silence, companion consensus, and Ma'mun's bias confirm Abu Bakr's correctness.

After explaining the legal basis for Abu Bakr's decision, Imam Nasafi provides additional evidence that the ruling was just. He points to Ali's own actions during his caliphate, the silence of the Companions, and the fact that only a biased later caliph criticized Abu Bakr. Let's examine these arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the second passage
Read the Arabic passage that starts with 'والدليل على كون الصديق مصيبا' (The evidence that al-Siddiq was correct). Identify the three arguments: Ali, the Companions, al-Ma'mun.
2
Analyze Ali's argument
If Abu Bakr had wronged Fatima, Ali would have restored Fadak to her children during his own caliphate. He did not, so the claim of injustice is false.
This is a strong practical proof.
3
Analyze the Companions' silence
The Companions were commanded to enjoin good and forbid evil. Their silence on this issue shows they considered Abu Bakr's action correct.
Their consensus (ijma) is a source of authority.
4
Analyze the exception of al-Ma'mun
The only critic was al-Ma'mun, who was known for extreme pro-Shia inclinations. His objection is thus biased and not valid.
This delegitimizes the only opposing voice.

Prophetic Proof that Ali's Fight Was Just
Nasafi employs a prophetic hadith to authenticate Ali's battle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the hadith about Ali fighting on ta'wil serves as proof that his war against the rebels was justified, analogous to the Prophet's war on tanzil.

Core Idea
The Prophet's words to Ali guarantee his fight was on truth, like the Prophet's own fight.

Nasafi presents a key prophetic hadith to settle the question of Ali's war against the Kharijites and rebels. This hadith directly compares Ali's fight on ta'wil (interpretation) to the Prophet's fight on tanzil (revelation). This lesson examines the proof and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith and its translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the literal translation. Notice the core comparison: 'you fight on ta'wil as you fought on tanzil.'
The hadith is narrated from the Prophet to Ali.
2
Understand the key terms
Ta'wil means interpretation of the Quran, while tanzil means the revealed text. Recognize that the Prophet's fight was based on clear revelation, while Ali's fight was based on interpretation.
These terms are central to the analogy.
3
Analyze the analogical deduction
The passage argues: the Prophet fought on tanzil and was in the truth. Ali fights on ta'wil, which the Prophet compared to his own fight. Therefore, Ali is also in the truth in his fighting.
The conclusion uses the term 'muḥaqq' (truth/right).
4
Address a common misunderstanding
The hadith establishes the correctness of Ali's fight, not that it is equally obligatory as the Prophet's fight. Understand the nuance.
This distinction avoids overextending the proof.

Since the Prophet was in the truth in his fight, Ali is likewise in the truth in his fight.
