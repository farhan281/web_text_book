The Ash'ari Solution: Kasb and the Substrate Argument
Nasafi presents the Ash'ari view and the substrate argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Ash'ari concepts of kasb and the impossibility of one act subsisting in two substrates refute the Mu'tazila objection.

Core Idea
Acquisition (kasb) allows an act to be God's creation and the servant's attribute simultaneously.

Nasafi now presents the Ash'ari position as another refutation of the objection. For Ash'aris, all acts are from God, but the servant acquires them. The objection does not apply because the same act is not attributed to two agents in the same way. He then argues that an act can only subsist in one substrate, so two essences cannot share the same motion or blackness.

1
Understand the Ash'ari concept of kasb

Acquisition means the servant's act is God's creation but attributed to the servant as his kasb.
2
Note the attribution of acts to the locus
Observe that the names for acquisition refer to the one in whom the acquisition subsists: the striker is the one in whom striking subsists, not the creator.
This distinguishes the agent from the creator.
3
Understand the argument about one motion in two substrates
Read the argument that one motion cannot subsist in two essences, and similarly for blackness, so two entities cannot be described by the same act.
This reinforces that the objection is impossible.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer unit where Nasafi responds to a Mu'tazila argument. The Mu'tazila argue: if one act can be the act of two agents, then one statement could be the statement of two speakers. They use this to challenge the Maturidi view that one motion can be the act of both God and the servant. Nasafi notes that this argument is posed against opponents, and he has already refuted the claim that one motion is an act of two agents (referring to the previous lesson). He then gives the Maturidi response: this argument does not apply to them because they never claim that one act is the act of two agents absolutely. What is God's act is an eternal attribute in His essence, not an act of the servant. What is the servant's act is not an act of God but rather His effect (maf'ul). The term 'act' (fi'l) is a general name with different specific names and types. God creates the servant's act as a type, but it is attributed to the servant by its generic name. For example, if God creates the servant's speech, that speech is God's creation (maf'ul Allah) and is also called the servant's act by generic name, and the servant's statement by specific name. Nasafi then mentions that according to Abu Isa al-Barghuth and Abu Hasan al-Ash'ari, the objection also fails. They hold that there is no act except from God; the servant has acquisition (kasb). The servant's statement is a name for his acquisition, so it is God's act (in creation) and the servant's acquisition (in acquisition). The names for acquisition refer to the one in whom the acquisition subsists—the striker is the one in whom the striking subsists, not the one who created it, etc. These meanings can only subsist in one substrate, so two cannot be described by them. Finally, he addresses the condition of being an agent: according to the Ash'arites, the act does not have to subsist in the agent. They use the example of necessary motion: the agent of necessary motion is its creator, and the one moved is its locus. Two essences cannot share one motion because it cannot subsist in two, and the same holds for blackness. The passage is cut off here.

The Inherence of Attributes and the Nature of Speech
Imam Nasafi argues that attributes inhere in their locus and refutes opponents' views on two agents.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Attributes and speech inhere in the locus, not in the agent; creation and acquisition are distinct.

This passage from Imam Nasafi clarifies a key Maturidi doctrine: attributes like blackness and speech reside in the subject (locus) that receives them. Nasafi then refutes opponents who claim that a single act can have two agents — a creator and an acquirer — by showing that their reasoning inadvertently supports the Maturidi position.

Core Idea
Kasb is human action within limits; Khalq is divine creation without limitation.

Imam Nasafi has already established that the servant cannot be a creator. Now he presents three ways the early Maturidi scholars defined the difference between what a human acquires (kasb) and what God creates (khalq). These definitions help us grasp how human responsibility and divine power coexist.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal exposition of the Maturidi understanding of kasb (acquisition) and its distinction from khalq (creation). The author begins by stating that after establishing the impossibility of creative power for the servant and affirming that the servant has an act, there is no need to delve into the quiddity (māhiyya) of kasb. The essential task is to prove that the servant is not compelled (majbūr) and that he has an act, with his power attached to its object. Then the author presents three definitions taken from the Maturidi scholars (ashābunā) to clarify the difference between kasb and khalq. The first definition: any object of power (maqdūr) that occurs within the locus (maḥall) of the agent's power is kasb; what occurs outside that locus is khalq; the term fi'l applies to both. The second definition: what occurs through an instrument (āla) is kasb; what occurs without an instrument is khalq. The third definition: if the agent can be independent (infirād) in bringing about the object, it is khalq; if the agent cannot be independent, it is kasb. The author then adopts the third definition, explaining that the servant cannot be independent in attaining his object because he lacks creative power (qudrat al-ikhtirā'). Therefore, the servant is not a creator but an acquirer. Allah alone is unique (mutafarrid) in bringing into existence (ījād) and has no need of others; thus He is the Creator.

Commentary
This passage defines the Maturidi concepts of khalq (creation) and kasb (acquisition), responding to Mu'tazili objections. It begins by giving two earlier opinions on the distinction: first, that an act performed with an instrument is kasb and without an instrument is khalq; second, that what the agent can accomplish alone is khalq, while what cannot be accomplished alone is kasb. The author then clarifies that the term fi'l (act) covers both, and that the servant cannot be alone in producing his intended effect because he lacks the power of invention (qudrat al-ikhtira'), so he is not a creator but an acquirer. God alone is unique in bringing into existence, needing no other, so He is the creator. Next, the Mu'tazili objection is raised: they claim that the concept of kasb is unintelligible. The author responds that it is intelligible by rational proofs, though not imaginable in a sensory way. If the Mu'tazila set aside bias and followed reason, they would see that they themselves hold unintelligible positions. The author then refutes the Mu'tazili doctrine that the non-existent (ma'dum) is a thing existing as an entity, accident, or substance prior to occurrence. He argues that power (qudra) relates only to existence, not to the thingness of the non-existent. Since these attributes were eternally fixed, and existence is not a separate meaning from the essence, power cannot relate to existence either, leading to the absurd conclusion that no power—divine or human—has any relation to an effect, which implies the nullification of God's creative action, the eternity of the world, and the abandonment of religious commands. The author presents the Maturidi alternative: God creates the world and originates existent things from non-existent; in human actions, existence and thingness come from God's power, while their specific qualities (motion, obedience, etc.) are subject to the servant's power. This salvages the reality of both divine creation and human acquisition.

Definition and Rule of Takhsis (Specification)
Defines takhsis and distinguishes it from abrogation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of takhsis (specification) and why it requires a connected proof, distinguishing it from abrogation.

Core Idea
Takhsis requires a connected proof; otherwise, it becomes abrogation.

In this passage, the author establishes a crucial principle: specification of a general statement is valid only if there is a connected proof that the specified item was never intended. Without such proof, later exclusion amounts to changing the ruling, which is abrogation. This distinction is fundamental for interpreting Islamic legal texts.

Identify the key condition

Distinguish takhsis from naskh

Commentary
This passage defines the concept of takhsis (specification) and explains its condition. Takhsis is a proof indicating that a specified item is not included in the general ruling. This proof must be connected to the general statement; without it, the general form remains as it is, indicating generality. If one attempts to exclude some individuals later without such a proof, that is not specification but abrogation (naskh), which terminates the ruling for the excluded ones. The author notes that this logic applies to commands and prohibitions. In the case of reports (akhbar), such an exclusion would render the report false, which is impossible for God. Thus, the passage establishes a key methodological principle for interpreting scriptural language.

The Silence of the Designated: A Proof Against Nass

Learning Objective
