Concludes with conquests and a hadith as proofs.

Learning Objective

The conquests in North Africa and the Companions' consensus confirm 'Uthman's high rank.

The author presents two final arguments: the military expansions during 'Uthman's caliphate, which he considers better than personal combat, and the testimony of Ibn 'Umar that during the Prophet's time the Companions held the order: Abu Bakr, then 'Umar, then 'Uthman. This hadith strongly supports the Maturidi view.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school holds that Uthman is the third best after Abu Bakr and Umar.

This lesson presents the Hanafi doctrinal position on the virtue of Uthman ibn Affan. Imam Nasafi not only states the school's view but also defends it against objections and provides hadith evidence from the Companions' consensus. By studying this passage, you will see how the Maturidi school combines rational argument and transmitted proof to settle a sensitive theological issue.

The apparent doctrine of our companions is the statement of preferring Uthman, may Allah be pleased with him, after Abu Bakr and Umar. Al-Husayn ibn al-Fadl al-Bajali and Muhammad ibn Ishaq ibn Khuzaymah from the people of hadith went to preferring Ali over Uthman, and Abu al-Abbas al-Qalanisi hesitated in that and he held that the imamate of the inferior is permissible. His merits are many, and his spending of wealth in support of the religion of Allah and the Prophet's (peace be upon him) placing one of his hands in place of Uthman's hand at the pledge of Ridwan is well-known, and the reports that the angels are shy of him are famous. Then what appeared of conquests in his days from the direction of the Maghrib at the hands of his governors and army commanders, and that is better for him than killing thousands of the sons of war amidst thrusting and striking. And that which supports what we have gone to is what Abu Dawud al-Sijistani narrated in the book of Sunan with his chain, may Allah be pleased with both of them, from Ibn Umar that he said: We used to say in the time of the Prophet (peace be upon him): No one is equal to Abu Bakr, then Umar, then Uthman. And he also narrated from Ibn Umar that he said: We used to say while the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) was alive: The best of the nation of the Prophet (peace be upon him) after him is Abu Bakr, then Umar, then Uthman. And Abu Dawud also narrated from Muhammad ibn al-Hanafiyyah: He said: I said, O my father, who is the best of people after the Messenger of Allah? He said: Abu Bakr. I said: Then who? He said: Umar. He said: Then I feared that if I said: Then who, he would say: Uthman, so I said: You, O my father? He said: I am but a man from the Muslims. So by these hadiths is established what we claimed of the order of virtue, and Allah is the grantor of success.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal argument for the superiority of Uthman ibn Affan over Ali ibn Abi Talib, as held by the Hanafi-Maturidi school. The author begins by stating the school's position: the apparent doctrine is to prefer Uthman after Abu Bakr and Umar. He then notes dissenting views: al-Husayn al-Bajali and Ibn Khuzaymah preferred Ali over Uthman, while al-Qalanisi suspended judgment but held that an inferior can be imam. Next, the author defends Uthman's merits: his financial support of Islam, the Prophet's high regard for him at the pledge of Ridwan, and the report that angels feel shy of him. He then addresses a potential objection—Uthman's withdrawal at the Battle of Uhud might suggest cowardice. He refutes this by explaining that even a brave hero may retreat due to hidden reasons, and that true bravery is recklessness with death, which Uthman later displayed during his siege (the 'day of the house') when he prevented his supporters from fighting and offered to free slaves if they laid down arms. This, the author argues, shows his courage. He also cites the conquests during Uthman's caliphate as a greater good than personal battlefield heroism. Finally, he provides hadith evidence from Abu Dawud: Ibn Umar reports that during the Prophet's time, the Companions considered the order of virtue as Abu Bakr, then Umar, then Uthman. Another hadith from Muhammad ibn al-Hanafiyyah implicitly affirms this order. The author concludes that these hadiths establish the claimed order of virtue. The lesson thus combines rational argument and transmitted evidence to defend the Hanafi position on the ranking of the first three caliphs.
