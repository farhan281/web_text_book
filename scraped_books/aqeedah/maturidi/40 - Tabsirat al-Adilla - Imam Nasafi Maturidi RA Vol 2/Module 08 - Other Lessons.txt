The Obligation of Gratitude and Its Conditions
Nasafi argues for the obligation of shukr and its condition of commensurability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why gratitude to Allah is obligatory from the very nature of His initiatory blessings and what condition makes gratitude valid.

Core Idea
Gratitude is due because Allah's blessings are purely initiatory, not earned.

This passage from Imam al-Nasafi establishes the foundation of human obligation toward Allah: gratitude for blessings that were never owed. He argues that since all blessings are given without prior right, ingratitude is a form of injustice. Then he sets a high standard: gratitude must match the blessing in essence and measure, though Allah may accept less out of generosity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initiatory nature of blessings
Read the opening lines carefully. Nasafi states that Allah created humans in the best form and subjected all things to them without any prior right from them. This makes the blessings purely initiatory (mubtada'a).
The key Arabic term is 'مبتدأة' (mubtada'a).
2
Derive the obligation of shukr
Nasafi concludes that because blessings are initiatory, there is no justification for dropping gratitude or permitting ingratitude. He says 'فكانت نعماؤه مبتدأه، ولا وجه إلى إسقاط شكرها... فيجب الشكر'. Understand the logical link: since blessings are undeserved, gratitude is a moral duty.
3
Understand the condition of commensurate gratitude
Nasafi then explains that gratitude should be equal to the blessing in essence and measure. He adds that the benefactor may accept less out of generosity, but the obligation remains. Reflect on what this means for your own sense of thankfulness.
The phrase 'ثم ينبغي أن يكون الشكر كفاً للنعمة' sets a high bar.

Then indeed God Almighty created humans in the best mould and subjected to them all that is on the earth without any prior [right] from them that these blessings should have come as recompense or fulfillment of a due right, so His blessings are initiatory. There is no justification for dropping gratitude for them or permitting ingratitude for them, because that involves wasting blessings and wronging them, so gratitude is obligatory. Then it is fitting that gratitude be commensurate with the blessing, balancing it in its essence, equal to it in its measure. If it were not so, it would not be regarded or be counterbalancing it; the benefactor, in His generosity and bounty, would be content with less than His due. Then indeed blessings vary in their measures and differ in their stations according to the variance in the measures of the benefactors and the difference in their stations and degrees. Whoever among them is greater in measure or higher in degree and station, the blessings from him are more sublime in measure and greater in station. For if a single person from the classes of subjects and types of commoners were to gift to a chief of the chiefs or a great one of the greats a swift horse or a stout pony or a beautiful slave-girl or a fine garment, his rank with that chief or great one or in the eyes of the rational who look at it would not be like the rank if such or lesser were gifted by one of great status among kings, great in awe in hearts, whose realm extends, whose might is intense, whose majesty and grandeur fill breasts. So if this premise is true, current among the rational as a principle, then there is no end to God's majesty, grandeur, greatness, and authority, and it is not in the intellect of any human to stand on the essence of the least blessing He has bestowed upon any of His creation. How then, when His blessings have followed in succession and His favors have come one after another upon His servants, such that if any of creation, even if he is a sign of acumen, intelligence, abundant intellect, and penetrating insight, wanted to stand upon the blessings that God Almighty has bestowed upon him and the blessing of repelling calamities from him in a moment, he would be incapable of standing upon that? So how about what has come continuously upon him throughout the length of his life and the period of his existence? And how can he know that? And the intellect by which we attain knowledge of things is a part of the parts of His blessings, and the part does not encompass the whole of which it is a part.

Commentary
This passage is an argument for the obligation of gratitude (shukr) to God and demonstrates the incapacity of human reason to fully comprehend divine blessings. It begins by stating that God created humans in the best form and subjected all things on earth to them, without any prior right from humans, meaning these blessings are purely initiatory (mubtada'a). Therefore, it is obligatory to thank Him, and ingratitude (kufrān) is not permissible because it constitutes wasting and wronging the blessings. The passage then specifies that gratitude must be commensurate and equal to the blessing in essence and measure; otherwise, it is not considered valid gratitude. However, the benefactor may accept less out of generosity. Next, the author explains that the value of blessings varies according to the status of the giver: a gift from a king is far more significant than the same gift from a commoner because of the king's majesty. Since God's majesty is infinite, the least of His blessings is beyond human comprehension. Moreover, the intellect itself is a part of God's blessings, and a part cannot encompass the whole of which it is a part. Thus, humans cannot even fully know a single blessing, let alone the total blessings over a lifetime. The rhetorical questions emphasize the impossibility of grasping God's favors even momentarily.

?

How does that realization make you feel, and what concrete change in your daily attitude of thankfulness will you commit to?
?

The Inability of Reason to Comprehend Divine Blessings
Nasafi demonstrates reason's incapacity to fully comprehend divine blessings using an analogy and an epistemological argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why human reason cannot fully comprehend even the least of Allah's blessings, due to both the infinite greatness of the Giver and the intrinsic limitation of the intellect.

Core Idea
The least of Allah's blessings is beyond human comprehension because the Giver is infinite and the intellect itself is part of those blessings.

After establishing the obligation of gratitude, Nasafi now addresses a profound challenge: how can we fulfill that obligation when we cannot even fully grasp a single blessing? He uses a striking analogy about the disparity in status between givers and then applies it to Allah's infinite majesty, concluding that the intellect, being a part of creation, cannot encompass the whole.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the analogy of varying value
Nasafi presents a social analogy: a gift from a king is far more valuable than the same gift from a commoner. Read the example carefully and understand how the status of the giver affects the significance of the gift.
The key terms are 'تفاوت أقدار المنعمين' (variance in the measures of the benefactors).
2
Apply the analogy to Allah
Since Allah's majesty is infinite, even the least of His blessings is beyond human estimation. Nasafi then asks rhetorical questions to emphasize the impossibility of comprehending blessings even for a moment, let alone over a lifetime.
Notice the rhetorical questions: 'وكيف يعرف ذلك؟'
3
Understand the final argument: the intellect as a part
The passage concludes with a powerful epistemological point: the intellect itself is a part of Allah's blessings. A part cannot encompass the whole of which it is a part. This means we cannot fully know even a single blessing because the tool of knowledge is limited.
The argument is: 'والعقل الذي به نتوصل إلى معرفة الأشياء جزء من أجزاء نعمه والجزء لا يحيط بالكل الذي هو جزء منه'.

The Four Rational Reasons for the Obligation of Prayer
Nasafi enumerates four reasons wisdom necessitates the obligation of prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four rational reasons why wisdom necessitates the obligation of prayer according to Imam al-Nasafi.

Core Idea
Prayer is not a blind ritual; wisdom demands it for four profound reasons.

Imam Nasafi begins his discussion of acts of worship by focusing on prayer. He argues that prayer is not merely commanded by revelation, but that its obligation is rooted in rational wisdom. In this passage, he presents four distinct reasons that demonstrate why any wise being would require prayer of their servants. Let's explore these reasons.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the four reasons
Read the Arabic and translation to get an overview of the four reasons Nasafi lists. Notice how each reason builds on the previous one.
2
Understand the first reason: manifesting servitude
The first reason is that prayer allows the servant to display humility, praise, and awe, distinguishing the obedient from the rebellious.
This reason focuses on the internal state of the servant.
3
Understand the second reason: barrier against sins
Prayer, especially when performed five times daily, acts as a strong barrier against immorality and wrongdoing, as supported by the Quran.
Cite Quran 29:45.
4
Understand the third and fourth reasons: seeking needs and expiation
The third reason is that prayer is the most comprehensive act for seeking needs from Allah with sincerity and trust. The fourth is that prayer expiates minor sins, as good deeds remove evil deeds.
Both reasons emphasize the spiritual benefits beyond ritual.

As for acts of worship, they begin with prayer, for it is that by which the servant draws near to his Lord, and the Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him, came with it according to what wisdom requires, for it is an act of worship that wisdom makes obligatory for four aspects. First: that the servant must manifest the mark of servitude by praising his Master and glorifying his Creator, and displaying humility and awe, so that he may be distinguished from those who are rebellious and have shown arrogance towards His worship. Second: when the servant undertakes the action indicating submission to the Maker and compliance to Him, and commits to humility towards Him, and makes himself feel the greatness of His majesty and the violation of shortcoming in His worship, and occupies his movements in showing lowliness to his Creator, that becomes a stronger barrier between him and sins, and a more protective preventer from all distractions, especially if he undertakes it every day and night five times, and that is the saying of the Most High: 'And establish prayer; indeed prayer forbids immorality and wrongdoing.' Abu Bakr's caliphate was marked by unmatched unity and conquests, signaling divine approval.

In this passage, the author presents a sweeping list of achievements during Abu Bakr's brief caliphate, from unifying the Arabian Peninsula to defeating two mighty empires. This is not just history — it is theological argument: the success of his leadership is seen as a sign of divine approval. The vivid contrasts with the enemy's strength underscore the miraculous nature of these victories.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Let the author's rhythm and the list of blessings sink in before interpreting.
Notice the long, flowing sentences typical of classical Arabic rhetoric.
2
Identify the categories of blessings
The author lists multiple blessings. Find the main categories: internal unity, suppression of apostasy, expulsion of Romans and Persians, and the role of Khalid ibn al-Walid.
Each category is separated by 'و' (and) and builds into a larger picture.
3
Analyze the rhetorical contrast
Observe how the author describes the enemy's strength in detail (numbers, equipment, lineage) before stating their defeat. This contrast highlights the miraculous nature of the victory.
Think: why does the author emphasize the enemy's power?
4
Reflect on the theological purpose
Consider that this enumeration is not mere history; it is a proof of Abu Bakr's righteous leadership and divine support. The blessings are evidence of God's favor.
This connects to the broader Aqeedah argument about the legitimacy of the caliphate.

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration of the many blessings and achievements of Abu Bakr's caliphate. The author, a Maturidi theologian, lists these as evidence of the divine favor upon Abu Bakr's leadership. He begins with internal unity and the suppression of apostasy, then moves to external conquests: expelling the Romans from Syria and the Persians from Iraq, despite their great power. He provides vivid descriptions of the enemy's strength to highlight the magnitude of the victories. The passage culminates with the rout of the enemy armies by Khalid ibn al-Walid, showing that Abu Bakr's caliphate was marked by unprecedented success.
