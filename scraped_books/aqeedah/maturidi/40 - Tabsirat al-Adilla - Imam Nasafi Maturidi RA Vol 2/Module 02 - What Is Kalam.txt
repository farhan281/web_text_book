The Framework of the Debate on Prophethood
Al-Nasafi introduces the debate on sending prophets and states the orthodox position.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Nasafi sets up the debate on prophethood by establishing divine wisdom and then noting disagreement on specific actions.

Core Idea
Prophethood is debated after consensus on God's wisdom.

Al-Nasafi begins with a foundational premise: God is wise and never acts foolishly. After this is agreed upon, scholars still differed on specific actions like sending prophets. This lesson introduces that framework before objections are presented.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then read the English translation. Notice how al-Nasafi structures his introduction.
The passage is the opening of a larger debate.
2
Identify the agreed premise
What is the agreed premise about God’s actions? The passage states that God is wise and never acts foolishly.
This is the foundation upon which the debate is built.
3
Find the point of disagreement
After consensus on God’s wisdom, scholars differed on specific actions. Which action is highlighted? The sending of prophets.
This shows that the debate is not about God’s wisdom per se, but about particular acts.
4
Note the orthodox position
Al-Nasafi states the view of the imams of guidance: sending prophets is wisdom and correctness. He affirms that God has indeed sent messengers with proofs.
This is the position the author will defend.

Defining Risala: Linguistic and Technical
Nasafi defines risala linguistically and technically before proving its possibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between the linguistic and technical definitions of risala and why a clear definition is necessary before proving prophethood.

Core Idea
Risala is not just any message; it is a divine intermediation to remove human excuses.

Before proving the validity of prophethood, Imam Nasafi insists we must first define what risala means. This may seem obvious, but without a precise definition, later arguments would be confused. He gives two definitions: one that covers all human messages, and a technical one specific to divine missions. Understanding both is essential before we can ask whether such a thing is possible.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow, then read the English translation. Notice how Nasafi structures his introduction: he states the need for definition, then gives two definitions, then validates the second one.
The passage uses 'لا بد أن' to express necessity.
2
Identify the two definitions
Find where the linguistic definition ends and the technical definition begins. The linguistic definition is simple and universal; the technical definition is specific to prophethood.
The transition is marked by 'وأما الرسالة التي اختلفنا في ثبوتها'.
3
Understand the purpose of the technical definition
The technical definition includes the idea of removing excuses (ilal). Think about why humans would need an excuse for matters beyond their intellect.
This relates to the Islamic concept of human responsibility and divine guidance.
4
Note the methodological announcement
After defining, Nasafi says we must now prove its possibility. This sets up the next section. Recognize the structure: definition first, then proof.
This is a common pattern in kalam works.

Defining Risala and the Proof Plan
Al-Nasafi defines risala and plans the proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the linguistic and technical definition of risala (prophethood) and the three-stage argumentative plan for proving its existence.

Core Idea
Prophethood is a divine mediation supplying what intellects cannot grasp.

Before arguing for prophethood, we must define it precisely. Al-Nasafi distinguishes the everyday meaning of 'message' from the technical reality of prophetic mission, then maps out a clear logical path: possibility, necessity, and actual proof. This lesson lays that foundation.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage

The passage is dense but well-structured.
2
Identify the two definitions
Find the linguistic definition (conveying speech) and the technical definition (mediation for intellectual beings). Note how each is justified.
The technical definition is the one that matters for theology.
3
Trace the argumentative plan
Al-Nasafi lists three steps: proving possibility (imkān), discussing necessity (wujūb), and proving existence (thubūt). Note the order.
This plan structures the entire discussion of prophethood.

The Impossibility of Fully Thanking Allah
Nasafi argues that full gratitude is impossible due to the infinite regress of tawfiq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why human reason alone cannot fulfill gratitude for even a single blessing, due to the infinite regress of tawfiq and the limited nature of intellect.

Core Idea
Gratitude for one blessing requires infinite gratitude.

Imam al-Nasafi begins by examining the nature of gratitude. He argues that since the intellect itself is a blessing, it cannot fully comprehend any other blessing. Moreover, every act of thanking requires further divine facilitation, leading to an endless chain. This sets the stage for his refutation of those who claim reason is self-sufficient.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the rhetorical questions at the start.
2
Identify the two key arguments
The passage presents two reasons why full gratitude is impossible. Find the argument about the intellect as a part of blessings, and the infinite regress of tawfiq.
The first is a logical premise, the second shows practical impossibility.
3
Reflect on the implications
Ask yourself: If we cannot fully thank Allah for even one blessing, what does that mean for our obligations?
This connects to the need for divine guidance in the next lesson.

The author then applies this to refute the Brahmanas (barāhima) who claimed that reason alone suffices to know good and evil and to derive obligations, arguing that reason is fundamentally incapable of such knowledge and that revealed guidance is necessary.

Reason alone cannot know how, how much, or that gratitude is good.

Having established that gratitude is impossible without divine assistance, Imam al-Nasafi now argues that a revealed statement (bayān samʿī) is necessary to clarify the modality and extent of required gratitude. He then applies this to refute the Brahmanas who claimed that reason alone suffices for moral and religious obligations.

The Maturidi View on Takwīn and the Unnecessary Theory of Jihāt
The author states the Maturidi view on takwīn and why it obviates the need for jihāt al-fi'l.

Life Does Not Require Physical Structure
Imam al-Nasafi cites textual authorities and refutes Mu'tazila doubt about life without structure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Maturidi refutation of the Mu'tazila claim that life depends on physical structure, and why the only condition for an attribute to subsist in a subject is subsistence in the essence (qiyam bi al-dhat).

Core Idea
Life does not require physical structure; it only requires subsistence in the essence.

This passage from Imam al-Nasafi addresses a key objection from the Mu'tazila regarding life after death. The Mu'tazila argued that since the dead body loses its structure, life cannot return. Imam al-Nasafi refutes this by denying that structure is a condition for life and tracing the error to philosophical denial of God's attributes. By reading this passage, you will see how Maturidi theology defends both God's life and the resurrection of the dead.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the literal translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the passage begins with a textual affirmation and then presents a doubt and refutation.
2
Identify the Mu'tazila argument
The Mu'tazila argue that knowledge cannot exist without life, and life cannot be imagined without physical structure. For scattered parts (atoms) that have no structure, life cannot exist, and therefore knowledge, pain, and pleasure cannot exist in them. This leads to a denial of life after death.
3
Understand the refutation
The author denies that structure is a condition for life. He states that the only condition for an attribute to subsist in a subject is that the subject is a self-subsistent entity (qiyam bi al-dhat). This applies to both the dead and to Allah.
This principle is key: attributes do not require physical location.
4
Trace the historical origin
The author points out that the Mu'tazila learned this idea from the 'mu'attila' (deniers) among the philosophers, who denied God's life because He has no structure. The Mu'tazila did not dare to state that explicitly but instead argued that life requires structure, which leads to the same conclusion.
This shows that the Mu'tazila argument is inconsistent even on their own terms.

People have differed concerning what the name 'Iman' applies to, a difference whose mention has no purpose nor means due to the multitude of statements in it. So we will mention from that some statements concisely, so that our book is not devoid of discourse on it, following those who preceded from the Salaf, may Allah have mercy on them. We say: Among people are those who claim that Iman is knowledge in the heart, confession with the tongue, and action with the limbs. This is reported from Malik, al-Shafi‘i, al-Awza‘i, the people of Madina, and the people of al-Zahir, the imams of Hadith like Ahmad ibn Hanbal and Ishaq ibn Rahwayh, and from the theologians among them: all of al-Harith ibn Asad al-Muhasibi, Abu al-‘Abbas al-Qalanisi, and Ibn ‘Ali al-Thaqafi. And among people are those who claim that Iman is by the heart and tongue, without other than these from the limbs; and this is the view of al-Shamiriyya, al-Najjariyya, and al-Ghaylaniyya; and this is reported from many.

Commentary
This passage presents the beginning of a chapter on Iman (faith). The author, Abu al-Mu‘in al-Nasafi, notes that people have extensively differed on what Iman properly refers to, but he will briefly summarize the main positions to avoid prolixity. He first mentions the view that Iman consists of three components: knowledge (ma‘rifa) in the heart, confession (iqrar) with the tongue, and action (‘amal) with the limbs. This comprehensive definition is attributed to major early authorities including Malik, al-Shafi‘i, al-Awza‘i, the people of Madina and the Zahiri school, leading hadith scholars like Ahmad ibn Hanbal and Ishaq ibn Rahwayh, and several theologians, including al-Muhasibi, al-Qalanisi, and Ibn ‘Ali al-Thaqafi. The second view holds that Iman is only of the heart and tongue, excluding bodily actions. This is attributed to the sects of al-Shamiriyya, al-Najjariyya, and al-Ghaylaniyya, and is reported from many. The passage thus sets up the classical debate on whether works are an integral part of faith or merely external requirements.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think that this view completely denies any value to actions.
Correction: The text only says actions are not part of the definition of Iman; it does not necessarily negate their obligation. Actions may still be required, but they are not constitutive of faith itself.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you read that some claim Iman is only in the heart and on the tongue, excluding bodily actions, does that resonate with any part of your own practice? Be honest: are there moments you act as if your actions don't matter for your faith? What would change if you truly believed that every action is part of your Iman?
?
If you were to examine your own Iman right now, would it be defined solely by what you confess with your tongue and believe in your heart, or do your limbs bear witness to it? Think of an action you did today—does it reflect the Iman you claim? What will you do tomorrow to make your actions align with your heart and tongue?
