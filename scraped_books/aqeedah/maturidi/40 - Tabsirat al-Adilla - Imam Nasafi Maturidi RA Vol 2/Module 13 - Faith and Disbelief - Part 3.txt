Refutation of the Mu'tazilite Doctrine of al-Muwafah
Nasafi refutes the Mu'tazilite doctrine of al-muwafah regarding faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite doctrine of al-muwafah and the Quranic evidence that refutes it, establishing that present faith is genuine and not conditional on the state at death.

Core Idea
Present faith is genuine; the final state at death is not the sole criterion.

The Mu'tazilites argued that only a person's state at death matters for true faith, casting doubt on present faith. Imam Nasafi refutes this by showing that the Quran affirms the faith of believers without requiring knowledge of the end, and that legal rulings based on faith do not require exception.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Mu'tazilite position
Read the first part of the passage where Nasafi explains the Mu'tazilite doctrine of al-muwafah: that only the state at death determines true faith, not present faith.
Note that even Mu'tazilites acknowledge present affirmation (tasdiq) but deem it irrelevant.
2
Identify the logical problem
Nasafi points out that since the state at death is hidden, the Mu'tazilites cannot know anyone's true faith, which leads to a contradiction.
This sets up the need for a refutation.
3
Examine the first Quranic proof
Read the verse where Allah testifies to the faith of believers (Quran 2:285) without any exception. This shows that present faith is recognized.
Allah did not condition faith on knowledge of the end.
4
Examine the remaining proofs and conclusion
Read the other verses (Quran 3:193, 2:136) and the argument about legal rulings. Nasafi concludes that the Mu'tazilite position is invalid.
The consistent Quranic command is to affirm faith without exception.

Commentary
This passage refutes the Mu'tazilite position on faith, specifically their doctrine of al-muwafah (consideration of the final state at death). The author begins by explaining the Mu'tazilite view: they doubt whether a person is truly a believer at present because they do not know their state at death; only that final state matters, not the current state. However, the author points out that even the Mu'tazilites do not deny the existence of inner affirmation (tasdiq) in the believer now; their doubt is based on ignorance of the end. The refutation proceeds with Quranic evidence. First, Allah testifies to the faith of believers in Surah al-Baqara (285) without any condition. Second, Allah praises those who respond to the call of faith with certainty (Surah Al Imran 193) without requiring exception (istithna). Third, Allah commands believers to say 'We believe' (Surah al-Baqara 136) without adding 'if Allah wills'. These verses show that present faith is genuine and legally effective. Additionally, in many legal rulings, Allah uses the name of 'iman' to determine permissibility and prohibition, and no one hesitated in applying these rulings based on a doubt about their own authenticity. Therefore, the Mu'tazilite position is invalid. The passage concludes with a doxology.

The Synonymous Nature of Iman and Islam
Nasafi defines iman and islam as synonymous and provides proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why in Maturidi theology iman and islam are considered synonymous, both indicating complete affirmation of tawhid and total submission to Allah.

Core Idea
In Maturidi aqeedah, iman and islam are one and the same reality.

After establishing the definition of iman as tasdiq (affirmation), Imam Nasafi now takes a crucial step: showing that iman and islam are not separate concepts but identical. This clarifies a common confusion and roots Islamic identity in both inner conviction and outward submission. Let's see how he proves this.

Guided Path
1

Notice that Nasafi uses a very compressed style here.
2
Identify the main claim and justification
Nasafi states that since iman is tasdiq, iman and islam are one thing. Look for the reasons he gives: iman affirms tawhid, islam submits entirely to Allah.
3
Understand the linguistic proof
He brings Quranic evidence showing that 'aslama' means 'ikhlas' (sincerity). This supports the idea that iman and islam both refer to exclusive devotion.
Reflect on the verse about Prophet Ibrahim.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Ask yourself: if iman and islam are identical, what implications does this have for understanding who is a believer or a Muslim?
This connects to broader discussions in aqeedah.

Commentary
This passage defines iman and islam as synonymous. The author begins by establishing a premise: since iman is tasdiq (affirmation), and the correct definition of iman has been established earlier, it follows that iman and islam are one and the same. They are synonymous terms. Every believer is a Muslim and every Muslim is a believer. The justification is that iman names the affirmation of the testimony of intellects, signs, and reports (i.e., the proofs) regarding the oneness of Allah, His sole creation and command, and His having no partner. Islam, on the other hand, is the submission of one's entire self and everything to Allah in servitude, also without partnership. Both concepts converge on the same intended meaning: complete devotion to Allah alone. The author then provides linguistic evidence: in Arabic, islam (aslama) carries the sense of ikhlas (sincerity/pure devotion). This is supported by the Quranic verse where Allah commands Ibrahim to 'submit' (aslim) and Ibrahim responds 'I have submitted' (aslamtu), meaning 'I have sincerely devoted myself.' Another verse states that believers are 'Muslimun' (submitted ones) to Allah, implying sincerity and exclusivity of worship. Thus, iman and islam are identical in reality.

Iman and Islam are the same reality, not separate categories.

Refuting the Equivalence of Iman and Outward Islam
Nasafi argues that performing Islamic acts does not guarantee being a Muslim if inner denial exists.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Nasafi's dilemma argument that performing Islamic acts does not guarantee being a true Muslim without inner faith.

Core Idea
Outward Islam without inner belief does not save a person from punishment.

This passage presents a powerful logical refutation of those who claim that simply performing the outward acts of Islam makes one a Muslim, regardless of inner conviction. Nasafi uses a dilemma to expose the contradiction in this view, drawing on the Quranic verdict on hypocrites. Let's examine his argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dilemma
Read the passage and identify the two options Nasafi presents: either performing Islamic acts makes one a Muslim (yes) or it does not (no).
Notice how Nasafi sets up a forced choice.
2
Examine the first horn
If the answer is 'yes', then a person who inwardly denies (hypocrite) would be considered a Muslim saved from punishment. But the Quran says hypocrites are in the lowest depths of Hell, so this is false.
This shows the absurdity of the 'yes' answer.
3
Examine the second horn
If the answer is 'no', then Nasafi asks: why not, since the person performed all Islamic acts? He argues that claiming one can perform an act without being that thing is a denial of realities.
He uses analogies like sitting, eating, walking to illustrate.
4
Reflect on the analogy
Nasafi compares Islamic acts to physical actions: just as performing sitting makes one sitting, performing Islamic acts should make one Muslim if they were identical. But since that's not the case, they are distinct.
This reinforces the separation of inward faith and outward action.
5
Draw the conclusion
Understand that inner belief (iman) is essential; outward Islam alone is not sufficient for salvation in the afterlife.
This is the key takeaway from Nasafi's argument.
