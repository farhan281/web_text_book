Objection: Combining Favors and the Most Beneficial
Objection and initial response on combining favors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that combining multiple favors would either obligate God to provide them or imply finitude of power, and the initial response that this objection obfuscates the issue.

Core Idea
Combining favors does not necessarily bring reform, so God is not obligated.

This passage presents a theological objection regarding God's favors (alṭāf). The objector argues that if multiple favors were combined, they would either be more beneficial (obligating God to provide them) or not bring reform, implying a limit to God's power. The response clarifies that the objection is based on a misunderstanding of power and reform.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and its two horns
Read the passage where the objector presents a dilemma: if combining likes brings reform, God failed to do the most beneficial; if not, God's power is finite.
Notice the use of 'فإن قال نعم' and 'وإن قال لا'.
2
Understand the initial response
The response states that the objector's argument is obfuscation (تمويه) and concludes that God cannot do what is most beneficial beyond what He did at that time.
The response does not accept the premise that combining would be better.
3
Recognize the conclusion
The passage establishes that the objection fails to disprove the view of God's power (taʿjīz) and the limit of what is possible (nihāyat al-maqdūr).

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer in the context of the doctrine of al-luṭf (grace) and al-aṣlaḥ (the most beneficial). The response refutes this by clarifying that the objector's argument is based on the assumption that combining beneficial things necessarily causes harm, which is a naturalist (ṭabīʿī) principle. The speaker (presumably the Ashʿarī or Māturīdī theologian) argues that benefit and harm are directly from God, not intrinsic to substances, and that custom (ʿāda) can change. Therefore, joining beneficial things does not inevitably cause harm. The passage ends by calling the objection an inversion of reason.

The response dismantles this analogy by affirming that benefit and harm are not intrinsic to substances but are directly from God, who is free to change the custom.

Al-Maturidi's Distinction on Benefit and Reform
Al-Maturidi explains the difference between benefit (nafʿ) and reform (ṣalāḥ).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why adding divine favors does not cause harm, based on al-Maturidi's distinction between worldly benefit (nafʿ) and spiritual reform (ṣalāḥ).

Core Idea
Spiritual reform has no limit; worldly benefit does.

Imam al-Maturidi builds on al-Ashʿari's refutation of an objection that infinite favors would harm the recipient. He provides a deeper explanation using a medical analogy. The key insight is that what brings reform (ṣalāḥ) in faith is not like medicine, which has a finite beneficial dose. This lesson unpacks that distinction.

Refutation of the Mu'tazila on the Perception of Life in the Dead
The author refutes the Mu'tazila's claim that life in the dead must be perceptible and provides an analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the absence of observable voluntary actions does not prove the absence of life, and how the author refutes the Mu'tazila's objection using the concept of power (qudra) and the example of a stroke patient.

Core Idea
Life is known by its effects, not by direct observation; God can give life without power.

The Mu'tazila argued that if a dead body had life, it would be perceptible. The author responds by clarifying that life in this world is not observed directly; rather, it is inferred from voluntary actions. However, God can bestow life without the power to act, so no action occurs and life remains hidden. This lesson examines that argument and the analogy of a person in a coma.

Guiding people to Islam is superior to fighting, as it multiplies the community, fulfills the Prophet's boasting, and brings greater reward, as evidenced by the hadith and the example of the messengers.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
You have just read that guiding one person to Islam is better than killing all between East and West. When was the last time you sincerely tried to guide someone? Are you more concerned with your own righteousness or with the salvation of others?
?
If you truly believed that your efforts in da'wah outweigh any military victory, what would you change about your daily priorities? Are you investing your time in activities that multiply the ummah or in pursuits that merely feel comfortable?
?
The author argues that those who cause conversions without war have an exalted rank. Are you content being a spectator while others do the work of guidance, or are you willing to take on the 'trouble of war' in your own life—meaning the effort and discomfort of calling others to Allah?
