Core Idea
The refutation proves the Qur'an's miracle lies in its composition by challenging an-Nazzam to produce even a poem equal to pre-Islamic masterpieces.

Having presented an-Nazzam's claim, the author now refutes it directly. He declares the argument false and proposes a practical challenge: if an-Nazzam thinks producing like the Qur'an is possible, let him produce a poem comparable to the famous odes of Imru' al-Qais and other Arab masters. Commentary
This passage presents and refutes the claim of an-Nazzam (a Mu'tazili) regarding the Qur'an's inimitability. He denied that the composition of the Qur'an is miraculous, arguing that no single phrase like 'al-Hamdu lillah' is individually miraculous, therefore the whole cannot be miraculous. He extended this logic to claim that mutawatir reports are not certain (since individual reports can lie) and that consensus can err. He held that the only miracle is the 'sarfah'—God turning people's resolve away from challenging the Qur'an, despite it being possible. The author refutes this by calling it false and corrupt. The refutation employs a counter-challenge: if an-Nazzam claims he or anyone can produce similar, let him produce a poem comparable to the famous odes of Imru' al-Qais and other master poets. His inability will show his obstinacy. The passage then repeats the same logic: just as an-Nazzam asked 'when are you incapable?' regarding the Qur'an, he is asked the same regarding poetry: is his incapacity at the first word or the next? The text cuts off, but the argument is clear: the challenge demonstrates that even non-revealed literary masterpieces are recognized as beyond human power to imitate, so the Qur'an is even more so.

Al-Nazzam's Sarfa Theory and Its Rationale
Al-Nazzam's claim and argument for sarfa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Nazzam's claim that the Quran's inimitability is due to divine prevention (al-sarfa) and his argument from the parts-whole analogy.

Core Idea
The Quran's inimitability is not inherent but due to God turning people away.

Al-Nazzam, a Mu'tazili theologian, proposed a controversial theory that the Quran is not intrinsically miraculous in its composition. According to him, any ordinary person could produce similar speech if God did not prevent them. This lesson presents his argument and its extension to other fields of knowledge, setting the stage for the refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage presenting Nazzam's claim
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice how Nazzam denies the Quran's inherent inimitability and attributes it to divine prevention.
The term 'sarfa' means turning away or prevention.
2
Analyze the parts-whole argument
Nazzam argues that since individual verses are not considered miracles, the whole Quran cannot be miraculous. Understand this reasoning and its implications.
3
Examine the extension to tawatur and ijma
Nazzam applies the same logic to mutawatir reports and consensus, claiming they are not immune to error. Identify the analogy he draws.
This is a radical claim that undermines traditional epistemology.
4
Understand the beard analogy for sarfa
Nazzam illustrates sarfa with a prophet seizing his beard and challenging others. Recognize that the miracle lies in God's prevention, not in the act itself.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation (radd) of al-Nazzam's theory of sarfa regarding the inimitability (i‘jaz) of the Quran. The author first presents al-Nazzam's claim: the Quran is not inherently inimitable in its composition; rather, any ordinary person could produce something like it. Al-Nazzam argues that since individual verses are not considered inimitable (according to the opponents, because a single verse is not a miracle), the whole collection cannot be inimitable either, because the ruling of the whole follows the ruling of the parts. He extends this logic to mutawatir reports (consensus) and concludes that inimitability is achieved only by sarfa—God turning people's ambitions away from attempting to imitate it. He gives the analogy of a prophet who seizes his beard and challenges others to do the same, and no one does; this is a miracle due to divine prevention, not because the act itself is impossible.

The author then categorically states this is false and corrupt argumentation. The immediate response is to challenge al-Nazzam: produce a qasida (ode) comparable to those of Imru' al-Qais or other master poets. If he cannot, his falsehood is exposed. The author then questions whether al-Nazzam's own incapacity is due to inability or choice, sarcastically suggesting that if he were capable, he would have produced such poetry, but his cold disposition prevents it. This leads to the conclusion that the known difference in eloquence among people (poets vs. ordinary baffled individuals) is intuitive and cannot be denied. Any argument that contradicts such a necessary truth is itself false, because error is possible in reasoning but impossible in intuitive knowledge. Thus, when the two conflict, error must be attributed to the reasoning, not to the necessary knowledge.

Nasafi's Refutation: The Challenge of Poetry and Necessary Knowledge
Nasafi's refutation of Nazzam's sarfa theory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Nasafi's refutation of al-Nazzam's sarfa theory through the challenge to produce comparable poetry and the argument from necessary knowledge.

Core Idea
The Quran's inimitability is proven by the impossibility of producing similar speech, not by divine prevention.

Having presented al-Nazzam's theory, the author now refutes it forcefully. He begins by challenging al-Nazzam to produce even a single line of poetry comparable to master poets, exposing his incapacity. He then argues that the observable difference in eloquence between people is known by necessity, and any rational argument contradicting such necessary knowledge is false.

The author points out that people are clearly divided into eloquent poets and ordinary baffled speakers. If Nazzam's theory were true, this differentiation would vanish.
4
Grasp the argument from necessary knowledge
The author concludes that the difference in eloquence is known by necessity (darura), and any argument against it must be false because error in necessary knowledge is impossible.
This is a key epistemological principle in kalam.

Commentary
