Commentary
This passage addresses the correct Islamic position on hope and despair, and the fate of a believer who commits major sins. The author begins by stating that one should not despair of Allah's mercy even after major sins, because despair is disbelief, specifically the characteristic of the Khawarij (Haruriyya). He then makes a hyperbolic example: even if a believer committed all sins of the entire earth, he should not despair, citing the Quranic verse 'None despairs of the relief of Allah except the disbelieving people' (Quran 12:87). He continues with a more concrete example: a believer who kills a thousand believers, commits adultery with a thousand women, abandons prayer, fasting, zakat, Hajj, ghusl, and does more, as long as he does not commit disbelief (kufr), he remains a true believer. His fate after death without repentance is in Allah's will: He may punish or forgive, and he will ultimately enter Paradise. The author then condemns three deviant sects: (1) The Khawarij (Haruriyya) who declare such a believer a disbeliever; (2) The Mu'tazila who say he will be eternally in Hell; (3) The Murji'a (Marjab) who say sins do not harm. Each of these positions is declared as kufr. He then supports his view with Quranic verses emphasizing Allah's forgiveness, including the famous verse 'Do not despair of the mercy of Allah, indeed Allah forgives all sins' (Quran 39:53).

Definitions of Iman Across Major Schools
Imam Samarqandi enumerates the different views on the definition of iman.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Abu Hanifa, al-Shafi'i, the Mu'tazila, and the Khawarij define iman and the role actions play in each definition.

Core Idea
Iman is defined differently by each school; the key difference is whether actions are part of its reality or not.

Imam Samarqandi begins by clarifying the precise definition of iman according to the major theological schools. Since the nature of faith determines who is a believer and the consequences of sin, this foundational question shapes all subsequent discussions. Let us explore the views of Abu Hanifa, al-Shafi'i, the Mu'tazila, and the Khawarij as presented by the author.

Guided Path
1
Read the Hanafi definition
Carefully read the lines describing Abu Hanifa's position: iman is twofold, comprising heart's affirmation and tongue's confession. Note that action is explicitly excluded from both absolute and perfect iman.
Affirmation is described as the greater pillar, confession as evidence.
2
Compare with al-Shafi'i's view
Notice that al-Shafi'i adds a third element: action with the limbs. However, actions are part of perfect iman, not of its reality, so deficiency of action makes iman incomplete but not invalid.
3
Examine Mu'tazila and Khawarij positions
Both groups consider action part of the reality of iman, so a major sinner leaves iman entirely. The Khawarij label him a disbeliever, while the Mu'tazila place him in a middle station.
This shows how the definition of iman directly affects rulings on major sinners.
4
Summarize the spectrum
Reflect on the spectrum: from Hanafi (action not needed) to Shafi'i (action perfects but not essential) to Mu'tazila/Khawarij (action essential). This illustrates the centrality of definition in kalam.

Know that iman (faith) is twofold according to Abu Hanifa: affirmation in the heart (tasdiq bil-janan) and confession on the tongue (iqrar bil-lisan). Affirmation is the greatest pillar, and confession is like evidence for it. As for action, it is not a part, neither of absolute iman nor of perfect iman; thus iman accepts no increase or decrease at all, and the one who abandons action is a believer but a sinner (fasiq). According to al-Shafi'i, it is threefold: affirmation in the heart, confession on the tongue, and action with the limbs (amal bil-arkan). Action is part of the reality (haqiqa) of iman according to the Mu'tazila and Khawarij, such that the committer of a major sin leaves iman; he enters into disbelief (kufr) according to the Khawarij, and does not enter into disbelief according to the Mu'tazila, so they affirm a station between iman and disbelief. If it is said: acceptance of increase and decrease is definitively established by transmitted texts (naql) and reason (aql). As for transmitted texts, by His saying exalted: 'And when His verses are recited to them, they increase them in faith' (8:2) and by his saying, peace be upon him: 'If the faith of Abu Bakr, may God be pleased with him, were weighed against the faith of all creation, it would outweigh them.' As for reason, because it necessitates equality between the faith of Muhammad, peace be upon him, and that of any one of his community, and the innate judgment of reason contradicts that. We say: iman is affirmation, and people are equal in its foundation. Increase and decrease are only among the fruits (thamarat) and branches (shu'ab) of iman, as we have enumerated, not in the reality of iman which is heart's affirmation (al-tasdiq al-qalbi).

Commentary
This passage defines iman (faith) according to the major theological schools and addresses the objection that faith can increase and decrease. The author, Imam Samarqandi, begins by stating the view of Abu Hanifa: iman consists of two elements: heart's affirmation (tasdiq bil-janan) and tongue's confession (iqrar bil-lisan). Affirmation is the greater pillar; confession serves as evidence. Action is not part of iman at all, whether absolute or perfect, so iman does not accept increase or decrease. The person who abandons actions is a believer but a sinner (fasiq). Next, the view of al-Shafi'i is given: iman is threefold, adding action with the limbs. Then the Mu'tazila and Khawarij views: action is part of the reality of iman, so a major sinner leaves iman entirely; Khawarij say he enters disbelief, while Mu'tazila place him in a station between iman and disbelief (al-manzila bayna al-manzilatayn). For al-Shafi'i, actions are part of perfect iman but not its essence, so lack of action makes iman deficient but not null, allowing increase and decrease by actions. Then an objection is presented: increase and decrease are proven by both scripture (e.g., Quran 8:2 and the hadith about Abu Bakr's faith outweighing all) and reason (otherwise all believers would be equal, including the Prophet). The author's answer distinguishes between the essence of iman — which is affirmation and is equal in all believers — and its fruits and branches (thamarat and shu'ab), which can increase and decrease. Thus the objection is resolved without compromising the definition.

The Objection and Answer on Increase and Decrease of Iman
Imam Samarqandi presents and refutes the objection that iman increases and decreases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Samarqandi responds to the objection that iman increases and decreases, by distinguish between the essence of iman and its fruits.

Core Idea
Iman's essence is equal for all believers; increase and decrease apply only to its fruits and branches.

After defining iman, the author anticipates a strong objection: both scripture and reason seem to prove that iman increases and decreases. The Qur'an says the believers' faith increases when verses are recited, and the Prophet stated that Abu Bakr's faith outweighs all creation. Reason also demands that the Prophet's faith must be greater than that of an ordinary believer. How can this be reconciled with the Hanafi claim that iman does not increase or decrease? Let us follow the author's precise answer.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
The objector presents two proofs: a scriptural proof (Qur'an 8:2 and the hadith on Abu Bakr) and a rational proof (otherwise equality between Prophet and ordinary believer, which is impossible). Note the wording 'maktu'un bihi naqlan wa aqlan'.
2
Identify the author's answer
The author responds with a clear distinction: iman in its essence is affirmation (tasdiq), and all believers are equal in that. Increase and decrease pertain to the fruits (thamarat) and branches (shu'ab) of iman, not the reality.
The phrase 'inna al-iman huwa al-tasdiq' is the key.
3
Connect to the earlier definitions
Notice how this answer supports the Hanafi definition: actions and their effects are fruits, not the essence itself. Thus the scriptural and rational evidences speak about these fruits, not the core affirmation.
4
Reflect on the implication
Understand why this defense is crucial: without it, the Hanafi position would appear contradictory to clear texts. The distinction protects the definition while honoring the apparent meaning of the evidences.

Imam Samarqandi classifies four types of people based on shahada, action, and belief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Samarqandi classifies people into four categories based on shahada, action, and belief, and what each category entails.

Core Idea
Four categories: sincere, hypocrite, disobedient believer, and disbeliever.

This passage from Imam Samarqandi presents a clear classification that distinguishes sincere believers from hypocrites, disobedient believers, and outright disbelievers. By examining three criteria—testimony (shahada), action, and belief—the Imam provides a framework for understanding different states of faith. Let's explore each category and see what distinguishes them.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the repeating pattern: 'Whoever... is...' This is a clear classification by enumeration.
Pay attention to the three criteria: shahada, action, belief.
2
Identify the three criteria
Find the three criteria used to classify people: bearing witness (شهادة), performing actions (عمل), and having belief (اعتقاد). These are the building blocks of the classification.
3
List the four categories
Write down the four categories: sincere (مخلص), hypocrite (منافق), disobedient believer (فاسق), and disbeliever (كافر). For each, note which criteria are present or absent.
Use the translation to confirm your understanding.
4
Reflect on the differences
Compare the categories. Notice that the fasiq has shahada and belief but no action, while the munafiq has shahada and action but no belief. This shows that belief is the core of faith.
Think about why the hypocrite is considered worse than the disobedient believer.
5
Understand the implications
Consider what this classification teaches about the relationship between faith and action. Action is not part of the reality of iman, but its absence does not remove one from iman. However, neglecting the shahada entirely removes one from iman.
This corrects the misconception that a sinful believer is not a believer.

من شهد وعمل واعتقد فهو مخلص ومن شهد وعمل ولم يعتقد فهو منافق ومن شهد ولم يعمل واعتقد فهو فاسق ومن اخل بالشهادتين فهو كافر

Whoever bears witness and does not act but believes, he is a disobedient believer (fasiq). Whoever neglects the two testimonies, he is a disbeliever (kafir).

Commentary
This passage is a concise classification of people into four categories based on three criteria: bearing witness (by the two shahadas), performing actions (amal), and having belief (i'tiqad). The first category includes those who fulfill all three: they are sincere believers (mukhlas). The second category includes those who outwardly bear witness and perform actions but lack inner belief; they are hypocrites (munafiq). The third category includes those who bear witness and believe but do not perform actions; they are disobedient believers (fasiq). The fourth category includes those who fail to bear witness at all; they are disbelievers (kafir). This classification shows that action is not part of the reality of iman but that the absence of action does not remove one from iman, while the absence of belief or shahada does.

Iman and Islam: One or Distinct?
Imam Samarqandi argues for the identity of iman and islam and refutes objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Hanafi position that iman and islam are one in essence, and how they respond to scriptural objections from the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
Iman and Islam are one reality; outward submission without faith is not true Islam.

Imam Samarqandi defends the Hanafi view that iman (faith) and islam (submission) are identical in essence, not separate. He presents Quranic evidence and then addresses the Shafi'i position that distinguishes between them. This passage will teach you how the Hanafis interpret verses and hadith that seem to differentiate, such as the bedouin verse.

Guided Path
1
Read the Hanafi claim and proofs
The passage begins with the statement that iman and islam are one, citing two Quranic verses. Note how the verses equate believers and Muslims.
The story of Prophet Lot is used to show interchangeability.
2
Contrast with the Shafi'i view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds a general-specific relationship: every believer is a Muslim but not vice versa. He uses Quran 49:14 and a hadith.
Identify the key difference in interpretation.
3
Study the Hanafi responses
The author answers both objections. For Quran 49:14, he distinguishes between legally valid islam and mere outward submission. For the hadith, he says it refers to fruits of islam, not its reality.
Notice how the Hanafis defend their position without rejecting the texts.

Commentary
This passage discusses whether iman (faith) and islam (submission) are identical or distinct. The author, representing the Hanafi position, asserts they are one. He provides two Quranic proofs: first, verse 3:85, which states that only Islam is accepted as a religion, implying that iman and islam are synonymous in the sight of God. Second, verses 51:35-36, which refer to believers and Muslims interchangeably in the story of Prophet Lot, indicating they refer to the same people. Then he presents the Shafi'i view that the relationship is a general-specific absolute (umum wa khusus mutlaq): every believer is a Muslim, but not every Muslim is a believer. The Shafi'is cite Quran 49:14, where bedouins are told they have not truly believed but should say they have submitted, and a hadith distinguishing iman and islam. The author answers both objections: regarding the verse, he clarifies that when the Hanafis say iman and islam are one, they mean the islam that is legally valid (al-islam al-mu'tabar fi al-shar'), which cannot exist without iman. The verse's 'islam' refers to outward submission without inner conviction, like saying the shahada without actual faith. Regarding the hadith, he explains that 'islam' mentioned there refers to the fruits and signs of islam, not its reality. Thus the Hanafi position is maintained.
