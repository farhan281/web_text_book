The Hadith on the 73 Sects and the Saved Sect
Imam Samarqandi opens with the hadith on sects and identifies the saved sect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the hadith about the division of the Muslim community into 73 sects, why only one sect is saved, and who that saved sect is according to the Prophet.

Core Idea
The saved sect is the great majority (al-sawad al-a'zam) who follow the Sunnah and the community.

Imam Samarqandi begins his famous work by grounding the concept of orthodoxy in a prophetic hadith. The hadith warns that the Muslim community will splinter into many sects, but only one will be saved. This lesson introduces the hadith and identifies the saved sect as the people of the Sunnah and the community (ahl al-sunnah wa al-jama'ah), also called the great majority (al-sawad al-a'zam). The author also quotes a stern warning against opposing the community.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith and the opening
Read the Arabic passage from the basmala through the hadith and the warning. Notice how the author establishes the authority of the Prophet's way.
The hadith mentions previous communities as a pattern.
2
Identify the saved sect
The Prophet identifies the saved sect as 'ahl al-sunnah wa al-jama'ah' and 'al-sawad al-a'zam'. Reflect on why these names are significant.
3
Understand the warning
The hadith says that opposing the community by a handspan removes the yoke of Islam. Consider what this means for unity.

Commentary
This passage opens the book 'Al-Sawad al-A'zam' with the basmala and praise, then states that the Prophet's way is the straight path. The First Eight Traits of the Saved Sect
Imam Samarqandi enumerates the first eight characteristics of the saved sect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the first eight characteristics that define a member of the saved sect (al-sawad al-a'zam) according to Imam Samarqandi.

Core Idea
The saved sect is defined by specific beliefs and practices, beginning with certainty of faith and unity with the community.

After establishing that the saved sect is the great majority (al-sawad al-a'zam), Imam Samarqandi begins to enumerate the sixty-two traits that characterize its members. This lesson covers the first eight of these traits, which include affirming one's faith without doubt, unity with the community, praying behind any Muslim, not excommunicating others for sins, and affirming divine decree, among others.

Guided Path
1
Read the list of eight traits
Read the Arabic passage that starts with 'وعلامة السواد الأعظم' and lists the first eight traits. Read the translation alongside.
The traits are numbered in the text.
2
Examine each trait individually
Go through each trait one by one. Understand what each means: e.g., not doubting faith, not opposing the community, praying behind righteous and sinful leaders, etc.
The first trait about not saying 'insha'Allah' is a point of creed.
3
Look for common themes
Notice that the traits cover belief, practice, and social interaction. They emphasize unity and avoiding extremism.
4
Reflect on the significance
Why do you think these traits are the first ones mentioned? How do they distinguish the saved sect from others?

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One must never have any doubt in their faith at all, and any doubt invalidates it.
Correction: The intended meaning is that one should affirm their faith with certainty and not use conditional phrases like 'if Allah wills' as a way of doubting. Having passing doubts or questions does not remove one from faith; the trait is about stating faith without qualification.

The Prophetic Promise of a Saved Sect
The author cites hadiths about the 73 sects and identifies the saved group.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the hadith about the 73 sects, the identity of the saved group as Ahl al-Sunna wal-Jama'a (the Sawad al-Azam), and the gravity of separating from the community.

Core Idea
The saved sect is the great majority that follows the Sunnah and the community.

This passage establishes a foundational prophetic warning and promise: the Muslim community will split into many sects, but one sect will be saved. The Prophet explicitly names this saved group as Ahl al-Sunna wal-Jama'a and calls it the Sawad al-Azam (the greatest majority). This lesson unpacks the hadith and its implications for understanding orthodoxy.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice the pattern of previous nations and the repetition of the number of sects.
The hadiths about Jews and Christians serve as a backdrop to the ummah of Muhammad.
2
Identify the saved group
Find the answer the Prophet gave when asked about the saved sect. Note the terms used: 'Ahl al-Sunna wal-Jama'a' and 'al-Sawad al-Azam'.
These are not two different groups but the same group described in two ways.
3
Reflect on the warning
Read the second hadith about the rope of Islam. Ask yourself: what does it mean to separate from the community by a handspan? How does this relate to the saved group?
This warning shows the importance of communal unity within the bounds of Sunnah.

Commentary
This passage presents foundational hadiths about the division of the ummah. The author begins by stating that the Prophet's way is the straight path. He then reports that the Prophet informed that the Jews (people of Moses) split into 71 sects, only one saved; the Christians (people of Jesus) split into 72, only one saved; and this ummah will split into 73, with only one saved. When asked about the saved sect, the Prophet identified it as 'Ahl al-Sunna wal-Jama'a' (the people of the prophetic way and the community), also called 'al-Sawad al-Azam' (the greatest majority). The author then cites another hadith warning that anyone who separates from the community by even a handspan has removed the bond of Islam from his neck, meaning such separation is a serious sin. This establishes the authority and identity of the orthodox group.

Correction: The saved group is defined by its commitment to the Sunnah and unity with the community, not by a particular label or an exact numerical count.

The Virtue of Abu Bakr
Affirms Abu Bakr's excellence and rightful caliphate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni belief that Abu Bakr is the most excellent person after the Prophet and that his caliphate is legitimate.

Core Idea
Abu Bakr is the best of the companions and the rightful caliph after the Prophet.

Imam Samarqandi begins a series of articles affirming key Sunni positions on the Companions. The first establishes the unparalleled merit of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, setting the foundation for a proper understanding of the caliphate. This lesson clarifies that merit and legitimacy go hand in hand.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text for article 25 carefully. Notice the phrase 'أحد من أصحابه ولا من أمته' which emphasizes universality.
The text uses 'يعلم' (knows) indicating certainty.
2
Identify the two claims
The passage makes two claims: 1) Abu Bakr is the most excellent after the Prophet among all Companions and the ummah. 2) His caliphate is true (rightful). Note that both are presented as necessary beliefs.
3
Reflect on the significance
Think about why this article is placed first among the caliphs. It establishes a hierarchy that counters both Shi'a (preferring Ali) and extremist Sunni views (elevating others above Abu Bakr).
This is a foundational article in Sunni political theology.

Commentary
This passage covers articles 25-30. Commentary
This passage is a legal ruling (mas'alah) on the practice of wiping over khuff (leather socks) in place of washing the feet during wudu. The author states the established duration: for a resident (muqim) it is valid for one day and one night (24 hours), and for a traveler (musafir) three days and three nights, counting from the time the hadath occurred. This follows the Hanafi school and is based on authentic hadith. The author then declares that whoever denies the validity of wiping over khuff altogether is from the Rawafid (a pejorative term for the Rafidah, i.e., those who reject this practice, often associated with Shi'a). The phrasing categorically labels denial as a sign of innovation. The passage ends with the standard closing note of sufficiency.

The Status of Denying Wiping over Khuff
Declaration concerning deniers of khuff wiping.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the author labels denial of wiping over khuff as a characteristic of the Rawafid and the implications of such a strong statement in Sunni theology.

Core Idea
Denying a confirmed Sunna can be a sign of sectarian innovation.

After stating the ruling, the author adds a powerful declaration linking the denial of khuff wiping to the Rawafid. This reflects the theological and sectarian context of his time, where certain groups rejected this practice. Let's explore the meaning and background of this assertion.

Guided Path
1
Read the second part of the passage
Read the Arabic sentence: 'ومن انكر المسح على الخفين فهو من الروافض وهذا كفاية للعاقل'
2
Understand the term 'Rawafid'
Rawafid (sing. Rafidi) is a term often used historically to refer to those who 'reject' (rafd) the legitimacy of the first three caliphs and certain established practices. Here it links denial of khuff wiping to that group.
This is a strong sectarian label, not a general takfir.
3
Reflect on the purpose
The author aims to defend a well-attested Sunna by clearly distancing it from those who reject it, reinforcing the Sunni position.
This statement is as much about defining orthodoxy as it is about the ruling itself.
