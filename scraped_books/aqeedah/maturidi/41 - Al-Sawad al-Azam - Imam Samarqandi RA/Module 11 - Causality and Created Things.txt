Tawfiq is Simultaneous With Action
Defines tawfiq as simultaneous with action, refutes two extremes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that divine assistance (tawfiq) coincides with human action, not before or after, and how this balanced view avoids the extremes of Qadariyya and Jabriyya.

Core Idea
Tawfiq accompanies sincere action, not before or after — a middle path between two extremes.

This passage from Imam Samarqandi addresses a key theological question about the timing of divine assistance. Some sects placed tawfiq before action, others after; both miss the mark. The author presents the Sunni position that tawfiq is simultaneous, contingent on the servant's intention. We'll explore the arguments and the proof from the Quran.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic text carefully along with the literal translation. Notice how the author states his thesis at the very beginning: al-tawfiq ma'a al-fi'l mustawiyan (tawfiq and the act are simultaneous).
Pay attention to the condemnatory language: 'Magians of this community'.
2
Identify the two erroneous positions
The passage explicitly names two wrong views: Qadariyya (tawfiq before the act) and Jabriyya (tawfiq after the act). Note the reasoning behind each and why they are deemed heretical.
The author uses a hadith to support that tawfiq is not given before action, but the power to act is.
3
Understand the correct view and its proof
The correct view is that tawfiq occurs simultaneously with the act, based on the servant's intention. The proof is Quran 29:69. Also note the consequences of the extremes: if Jabri view were true, disbelievers would be excused; if Qadari view were true, Allah would be described as incapable.
The passage ends by saying istita'a (capability) is also simultaneous with the act.

The forty-second question: And what we said: That he should know that tawfiq (divine assistance) and the act are simultaneous, and he should not say that tawfiq is before the act, for this is the doctrine of the Qadariyya, and whoever says that tawfiq is after the act, this is the doctrine of the Jabriyya. The Qadari and the Jabari are the Magians of this community. And know that the Prophet, peace be upon him, said: The servant has been given the power of action and charged with it so that the proof is established against him, but he has not been given the power of tawfiq, because tawfiq is from Allah Most High. The Qadari says: Good and evil are all from me, and Allah has no part in it. The Jabari says: Good and evil are from Allah Most High, and the servant has no part in it. So the Qadari attributed lordship to himself, and the Jabari attributed servitude to Allah Most High, and both are innovators. The correct view in this is to know that whoever's aim, effort, and intention is obedience to Allah Most High and His pleasure finds tawfiq from Allah Most High at the moment of the act, and whoever's aim, effort, and intention is disobedience to Allah Most High receives abandonment (khidhlan) from Allah at the moment of the act. The proof is His statement: 'And those who strive in Us, We will surely guide them to Our ways' (Quran 29:69). If he said as the Jabari said, then the disbelievers would be excused before their Lord, and if he said as the Qadari said, that everything is from us and Allah has no part, then he has described Allah with incapability, and this is impossible, so they have disbelieved. And know that capability (istita'a) according to the people of justice is simultaneous with the act, neither preceding nor following. And His statement: 'You are the poor toward Allah' (Quran 35:15) and He has said...

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal clarification on the relationship between divine assistance (tawfiq) and human action. It begins by asserting the correct Sunni position: tawfiq is simultaneous with the act, not before (as the Qadariyya claim) nor after (as the Jabriyya claim). The Qadari and Jabari are condemned as 'Magians of this community'. The author then quotes a prophetic hadith to show that the servant is given the ability to act but not the ability to obtain tawfiq; tawfiq is solely from Allah. The Qadari error is attributing both good and evil entirely to oneself, denying Allah's role; the Jabari error is attributing everything to Allah, denying human agency. Both are innovators. The correct view is that one who sincerely intends obedience will find tawfiq from Allah at the moment of action, while one who intends disobedience will encounter divine abandonment. The proof is Quran 29:69, which promises guidance to those who strive. If the Jabari view were true, disbelievers would be excused for their unbelief; if the Qadari view were true, Allah would be described as incapable, which is impossible. Hence both extremes lead to disbelief. The passage closes by affirming that capability (istita'a) is simultaneous with the act, neither pre-existing nor coming after, and references Quran 35:15, though the text cuts off. This emphasizes human responsibility within divine sovereignty.

The Imam's Hadath and Congregational Prayer
Explains the ruling on praying behind an imam who has hadath and the nullification of the follower's prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the imam's ritual impurity (hadath) invalidates the congregation's prayer and the logical consequences of rejecting this principle.

Core Idea
The imam's hadath is the congregation's hadath – congregational prayer is one act.

In this passage, Imam al-Samarqandi addresses a crucial issue in congregational prayer: if the imam’s wudu breaks, does the congregation’s prayer become invalid? He argues that it does, because the imam’s state is directly linked to the followers. Using a hadith and a logical argument, he shows that rejecting this principle leads to absurd consequences. Let’s examine his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its English translation to get a sense of the argument.
Notice the author's use of a hadith and a hypothetical objection.
2
Identify the core principle
Find the sentence where the author states the principle that the imam's hadath is the congregation's hadath.
3
Understand the reductio argument
See how the author rebuts the objection by showing that if the principle is rejected, it would allow prayer behind non-Muslims, which is invalid.
This is a logical consequence argument.

المسئلة الثانية والخمسون - وما قلنا انه ينبغى له ان يرى ان حدث الامام حدث للقوم من لم يرحدث الامام حدثا للقوم لاتجوز الصلاة خلفه لان النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم قال الامام ضامن والمؤذن مؤتمن فان قال انا اصلى صلاتى والامام يصلى صلاته قتل بايبي يصير القوم مقتدين به وان كان كل واحد منهم يصلى صلاة نفسه ولا يكون حدث الامام حدثا للقوم فبأى شىء يكون فضل الجماعة فاذا كان كذلك فيبتني على قولك انه اذا كان الامام يهوديا او نصرانيا او مجوسيا او امرأة تجوز صلاتك خلفه وهذا يكفي ان شرح الله صدره للاسلام وفيه كفاية للعاقل والله اعلم
The fifty-second issue: And what we have said that it is necessary for him to hold that the hadath (ritual impurity) of the imam is the hadath of the congregation. Whoever does not consider the imam's hadath as the congregation's hadath – it is not permissible to pray behind him, because the Prophet, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, said: 'The imam is the guarantor and the mu'adhdhin is the trustee.' If he says, 'I pray my prayer and the imam prays his prayer,' then I say: By what do the people become followers of him? If each one of them prays his own prayer, and the imam's hadath is not the congregation's hadath, then what is the virtue of congregational prayer? If that is the case, then it follows from your statement that if the imam is a Jew, Christian, Magian, or a woman, your prayer behind him would be permissible. And this is sufficient if Allah expands his heart for Islam. And in it is sufficiency for the intelligent. And Allah knows best.
Source pages p. 32

Commentary
This passage is a legal ruling (mas'alah) on a specific point of fiqh related to congregational prayer. The author asserts the principle that the ritual purity of the imam is considered as the purity of the congregation; if the imam's wudu is broken (hadath), the congregation's prayer is also invalidated because they are following him. He argues that whoever rejects this principle must explain how the congregation's prayer is valid if the imam's hadath does not affect them, and he uses the hadith 'the imam is a guarantor' to support the connection. The author then refutes a hypothetical objection: if someone claims each person prays for himself and the imam's impurity does not affect them, then logically one could pray behind a non-Muslim or a woman, which is invalid. Thus, the principle is necessary to differentiate valid congregational prayer from individual prayer. The passage ends with a note that this suffices for the intelligent.
