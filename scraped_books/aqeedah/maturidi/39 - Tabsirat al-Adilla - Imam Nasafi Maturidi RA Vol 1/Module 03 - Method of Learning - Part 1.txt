Tabsirat al-Adilla by Abu al-Mu'in al-Nasafi j. And [commentary on] differences, vocabulary, kalam and philosophical and scientific terms, clarification of ambiguities, linking parts of the manuscript to each other, linking them to other works of the author or others, adding other evidences, with statement of opinion on the presented ideas whenever possible. The study: we mean by it the introduction of everything related to the author—with kasr before the last letter—and the work—with fath before the last letter—as we also mean by it presenting a brief overview of the edited text if possible without verbosity and analysis of some of its issues with comparison to similar ones in the time before and after the manuscript's author to show influence and being influenced. Commentary
This passage is an editorial preface describing the methodology of the edition. It opens with the book title, 'Tabsirat al-Adilla by Abu al-Mu'in al-Nasafi j', then describes the first aspect: commentary (ta‘līq). The commentary includes addressing differences, vocabulary, technical terms from kalam, philosophy, and sciences; clarifying obscurities; connecting parts of the manuscript to each other and to other works by the same author or others; adding additional evidence; and giving an opinion on presented ideas whenever possible. The second aspect is the study (dirāsah), which the editor defines as introducing everything related to the author (with kasr on the middle letter of mu'allif) and the work (with fath on the middle letter of mu'allaf). It also involves offering a concise presentation of the edited text if feasible, avoiding prolixity, and analyzing some of its issues in comparison with similar works from earlier and later periods to demonstrate influence and influence received. The editor notes that detailed analysis is necessary when the manuscript pages are few; otherwise, it becomes impracticable. The passage ends with a basmalah-style invocation and the editor's name.

Commentary
This passage serves as a methodological conclusion. Nasafi, after presenting multiple proofs that the Mu'tazila are, in his view, enemies of God (by the criteria of the hadith), emphasizes that a fair person should not stop at mere accusation. Instead, one should examine the Mu'tazila's actual statements on human actions and compare them directly with the position of Ahl al-Sunna. Only through this comparison can the truth about the Mu'tazila school be fully understood. This reflects Nasafi's commitment to scholarly fairness despite his strong critique.

Nasafi on the Qualities of the Imam: Ideals vs. Necessities
Nasafi lists additional qualities and states they are not all mandatory, citing historical precedent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the qualities listed by Nasafi for an imam are ideal but not all mandatory, as shown by historical practice after the rightly-guided caliphs.

Core Idea
Leadership qualities are ideals, not absolute requirements.

Nasafi presents a list of desirable qualities for an imam, but immediately clarifies that not all are necessary. He provides historical evidence from the caliphate after the Rashidun. This passage shows the Maturidi emphasis on practicality in leadership qualifications.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and list the qualities
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Note the seven qualities Nasafi mentions: ijtihad in fiqh, justice, piety, political acumen, knowledge of people's ranks, high intelligence, combination of firmness and gentleness, and bravery.
The list is not exhaustive but represents key virtues.
2
Identify the key condition statement
Focus on Nasafi's statement: 'Their presence all in him is not a condition.' This is the main doctrinal point. Understand that he is not dismissing these qualities but saying they are not all compulsory.
3
Understand the historical evidence
Nasafi cites the caliphate after the rightly-guided caliphs, where individuals who had not reached the level of ijtihad were appointed. This shows that perfect scholarship is not an absolute requirement for leadership.
This reflects the Maturidi principle of considering historical practice.

b) Al-Nasafi mentions the attributes which if present in a person make him qualified for the Imamate, and among them: that he should have reached the level of the mujtahids in fiqh, that he be just and pious, that he be guided to the proper policies and measures, knowledgeable of the ranks of people, at the peak of intelligence, combining severity and gentleness, and that he be brave. Then he says: Their presence all in him is not a condition, and the evidence for that is that the caliphate was contracted after the rightly-guided caliphs for those who had not reached the level of ijtihad in knowledge.
Source pages p. 117

Commentary
This passage enumerates the desirable qualities for an Imam but then clarifies that they are not all mandatory. Nasafi lists: reaching the level of ijtihad in fiqh (independent reasoning), being just ('adl) and pious (wara'), being guided in politics and administration, knowing the ranks of people, being highly intelligent, combining firmness and gentleness, and being brave. After listing these, he states that having all of them in one person is not a condition (sharṭ). The evidence is historical: after the rightly-guided caliphs (al-khulafā' al-rāshidūn), the caliphate was given to people who were not of the level of ijtihad in knowledge. This shows that leadership does not require perfect scholarship.
