Al-Nasafi's Proof from the Story of Musa
Al-Nasafi's scriptural arguments for the possibility of seeing Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Nasafi uses the story of Musa (AS) to establish that seeing Allah is rationally possible and not impossible.

Core Idea
God's response to Musa implies possibility, not eternal impossibility.

Al-Nasafi draws five powerful clues from the Qur'anic narrative of Musa asking to see Allah. Each clue, from Musa's own request to God's lack of rebuke, points to the same conclusion: ru'ya is permissible and not impossible. This lesson unpacks these arguments and shows why the verse 'You will not see Me' does not negate all future vision.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then its English translation. Notice how al-Nasafi moves from one argument to the next without interruption.
The passage is a continuous block of reasoning.
2
Identify the five arguments
List the five distinct proofs al-Nasafi extracts from the story. They are: (1) Musa's request itself, (2) God's answer lacking the word 'impossible', (3) conditional suspension on the mountain, (4) absence of rebuke, and (5) the manifestation (tajalli).
Each argument is a separate logical move.
3
Discuss the key terms
Examine the terms 'jawaz', 'mustahil', 'ta'liq', and 'tajalli'. Understand how they function in kalam discourse about divine attributes.
These terms are foundational for the debate on ru'ya.
4
Address the common misunderstanding
Many think 'lan tarani' proves eternal impossibility. Al-Nasafi argues otherwise. Discuss why the absence of 'mustahil' in God's reply is significant.
The common misunderstanding is explicitly countered in the passage.
5
Reflect on the evidence
Consider how al-Nasafi uses the narrative to support the Ash'ari position that ru'ya is possible in the afterlife. Why does he include the interpretation from Ibn Furak about the mountain?
This cements the argument that vision is not inherently impossible.

He establishes the proofs for the permissibility of seeing God from the Qur'an and the purified Sunnah. Among these is the saying of Moses, peace be upon him: 'My Lord, show me [Yourself] that I may look at You' (Q. 7:143). For in the verse there is an acknowledgment of God, because ignorance is negated from the Messenger, and God informed him that he would not see Him, yet God did not inform that seeing Him is impossible, despite the clear need for clarification—so this indicates the permissibility of seeing Him. Likewise, God suspended vision upon a permissible matter, namely the mountain's settling; suspending an action upon something possible indicates its possibility. Also, He did not disappoint Moses nor rebuke him when he asked to see Him; this indicates the permissibility of vision, for if the matter were impossible or Moses were ignorant, there would have been rebuke. And similarly His statement: 'And when his Lord manifested Himself' (Q. 7:143), and al-tajallī is manifestation. Al-Nasafi interprets it, transmitted from Ibn Fūrak from Abū al-Ḥasan al-Ashʿarī, that God gave the mountain a soul and the ability to see, and it saw God.

Commentary
This passage presents al-Nasafi's positive proofs from the Qur'an for the permissibility of seeing Allah. He uses the story of Moses (AS) asking to see God in Surah al-A'raf (7:143). He gives several arguments extracted from the narrative: (1) Moses' request itself is evidence because prophets are free from ignorance—if it were impossible, Moses would not have asked. (2) God answered that Moses would not see Him, but did not say it is impossible (mustaḥīl); given the need for clear clarification, if it were impossible, God would have stated so. (3) God made vision conditional upon the mountain's stability, which is a possible (jāʾiz) event; linking something to a possible act indicates the possibility of that thing. (4) God did not rebuke Moses for asking; rebuke would have come if the request were impossible or ignorant. (5) The phrase 'When his Lord manifested Himself' (tajallā) implies a form of appearance. Al-Nasafi then cites a transmission from Ibn Fūrak from al-Ashʿarī that God gave the mountain a soul and the ability to see, and it actually saw God, demonstrating that ru'ya is possible.

The Wisdom of Sending Prophets: Ahl al-Haqq's Position
Nasafi states the Ahl al-Haqq position on prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Ahl al-Haqq affirm the sending of prophets as wise and permissible, supported by divine proofs.

Core Idea
Sending prophets is wise and permissible, affirmed by Ahl al-Haqq.

This lesson establishes the foundational view of the orthodox Sunni position on prophethood. Before diving into objections, we need to see what Ahl al-Haqq affirm: that sending messengers is not only possible but wise. The passage opens with two key questions and answers them directly.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation carefully to grasp the two questions and the answer from Ahl al-Haqq.
Notice the concise style: the author frames the issue as a debate.
2
Identify the core claim
The passage states that sending messengers is 'wisdom and correctness' (ḥikma wa ṣawāb) and permissible (jāʾiz). Note that Ahl al-Haqq affirm both the moral and legal possibility.
3
Understand the support
Allah sent messengers with good tidings and warnings, and supported them with proofs (ḥujaj). This is the basis for the claim.
These proofs refer to miracles and rational arguments.

الكلام في إثبات النبوة والرسالة. هل من الحكمة إرسال الرسل؟ وهل الإرسال جائز؟ قال أهل الحق: إن إرسال الرسل حكمة وصواب، وهو جائز، وقد أرسل الله تعالى رسلا مبشرين ومنذرين، وأيدهم بالحجج. وقالت طوائف من الخُلَعَاء ومنهم المتصوفة في رأي النسفي بامتناعه إذ فيه أمر ونهي وهو سفه، إذ لا نفع للآمر والناهي، وهنا يهاجم النسفي المتصوفة بقوله: وعند اكتشاف القناع عن مكنون ضمائر كثير من المستترين بالتصوف، وارتفاع الخفاء عن مستور سرائرهم، ويعلم أنه من هذه الفرقة. ومن الذين يقولون بامتناع إرسال الرسل البراهمة، حيث يؤكدون أن العقل يعرف المحاسن فيعملها، ويعرف المساوئ فلا يعملها فالتكليف يكون بالعقل ولا حاجة إلى الرسل لأنهم إن جاءوا موافقين للعقل فبالعقل عنهم غنية، وإن جاءوا مخالفين له كان إرسالهم عبثا لأن العقل حجة الله وحججه تعالى لا تتناقض. ومنهم أيضا الطبيعيون حيث يقولون: إن المتنبي يعارض النبي إذا كان الأمر على مقتضى الواقع، أما إذا خرج عن مقتضى الواقع ففيه تغيير طبائع الأشياء، وطبائع الأشياء لا تتغير وقال بعضهم بأن قلب الطبائع في مقدور الله، إلا أنه أيضا في مقدور السحرة ولا يمكن التمييز بين الأمرين. ومن المنكرين من يقول: بأن ما يقال إنه معجزة لم يعرض على كل البشر، ولو عرض لوجد المعارض، وبذلك لا رسول.
Discussion on establishing prophethood and messengership. Is it from wisdom to send messengers? And is sending permissible? The people of truth said: Sending messengers is wisdom and correctness, and it is permissible. Indeed, Allah sent messengers as bringers of good tidings and warners, and supported them with proofs. And groups of the corrupt – among them the Sufis according to al-Nasafi – said that it is impossible, for it involves command and prohibition which is foolishness, since there is no benefit for the commander and prohibitor. And here al-Nasafi attacks the Sufis with his saying: 'And upon uncovering the veil from the hidden secrets of many of those who conceal themselves under Sufism, and removal of concealment from their hidden inner thoughts, it is known that they are from this group.' And among those who say that sending messengers is impossible are the Brahmins, as they affirm that reason knows good deeds and performs them, and knows evil deeds and does not perform them. So obligation is by reason and there is no need for messengers, because if they come in agreement with reason, then reason suffices for them, and if they come in opposition to it, then their sending is futile, because reason is the proof of Allah, and His proofs do not contradict. And among them are the naturalists, as they say: The one who claims prophethood opposes the prophet if the matter is according to reality; but if it departs from reality, then it involves changing the natures of things, and natures of things do not change. And some of them said that the transformation of natures is in the power of Allah, but it is also in the power of sorcerers, and it is impossible to distinguish between the two matters. And among the deniers are those who say: What is said to be a miracle has not been presented to all people, and if it were presented, it would find opposition, and thus no messenger.

Commentary
This passage begins a new section on prophethood (nubuwwa). It first poses two fundamental questions: Is sending prophets wise? Is it permissible? Ahl al-Haqq (the orthodox) affirm both. The passage then presents three objections from opponents. First, the khula'a (corrupt ones), among whom Nasafi identifies some Sufis (mutasawwifa), argue that commands and prohibitions are foolish because God gains no benefit. Nasafi retorts by unmasking these Sufis as insincere. Second, the Brahmins (Barahima) hold that reason alone is sufficient to discern good and evil, so prophets are unnecessary: if a prophet agrees with reason, reason suffices; if he contradicts reason, his message is invalid because God's proofs do not conflict. Third, the naturalists (tabi'iyyun) claim that if a claimant to prophethood acts within nature, he competes with a prophet; if he changes nature, that alters the immutable natures of things – or if nature can be changed, sorcerers can do the same, making miracles indistinguishable. Finally, a generic denial: miracles are not verified for all, so no prophetic claim is proven. Thus, the passage catalogues rationalist objections that the Ash'ari-Maturidi tradition must refute.

Refuting the Corrupt Sufis' Objection
Nasafi presents and refutes the objection that prophetic commands are foolish.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the corrupt ones (khula'a) consider sending prophets foolish and how Nasafi responds by exposing their hypocrisy.

Core Idea
The claim that divine commands are foolish is refuted by unmasking the insincere.

After establishing the orthodox view, Nasafi turns to the first objection: from a group he calls the 'corrupt ones' (khula'a), who include some claiming to be Sufis. They argue that command and prohibition are useless because God gains no benefit. Nasafi’s response is sharp: he accuses them of hiding their true corrupt beliefs under the cloak of Sufism.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the Arabic and translation to understand the argument that sending prophets involves command and prohibition, which is 'foolishness' (safah) because no benefit accrues to the commander.
This argument assumes God acts only for His own benefit, which is a false premise.
2
Identify the group
The passage identifies the objectors as 'khula'a' (corrupt ones) and singles out some Sufis (mutasawwifa) as part of this group.
Nasafi is not attacking all Sufis, only those who hide corruption under Sufi garb.
3
See the response
Nasafi retorts by saying that when the veil is lifted, many who conceal themselves under Sufism will be revealed as belonging to this corrupt group.
This is a rebuttal ad hominem, but in kalam it's acceptable to expose hypocrisy.

Refuting the Brahmins and Naturalists on Prophethood
Nasafi presents the Brahmins' and naturalists' objections to prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two rationalist objections to prophethood – from the Brahmins (sufficiency of reason) and the naturalists (impossibility of miracles) – and their flaws.

Core Idea
Reason alone cannot replace revelation, and miracles are distinguishable from sorcery.

Nasafi now addresses two more sophisticated objections. The Brahmins argue that reason is sufficient for moral knowledge, making prophets redundant or contradictory. The naturalists argue that miracles would require changing natures, which is impossible or indistinguishable from sorcery. Both objections hinge on overestimating reason or misunderstanding divine power.

Guided Path
1
Read the Brahmin objection
Read the Arabic and translation to understand the Brahmin position: reason knows good and evil, so prophets are either redundant (if they agree with reason) or contradictory (if they oppose).
The Brahmins were a Hindu sect; this is a classic kalam argument.
2
Read the naturalist objections
The naturalists present two versions: one says miracles change natures, which is impossible; the other says God can change natures but so can sorcerers, making miracles indistinguishable.
Notice the two sub-arguments.
3
Consider the generic denial
Some deny prophethood by claiming that miracles are not universally verified, so no claimant is proven.
This is a skeptical argument based on lack of evidence.

Commentary
This passage begins a new section on prophethood (nubuwwa). It first poses two fundamental questions: Is sending prophets wise? Is it permissible? Ahl al-Haqq (the orthodox) affirm both. The passage then presents three objections from opponents. First, the khula'a (corrupt ones), among whom Nasafi identifies some Sufis (mutasawwifa), argue that commands and prohibitions are foolish because God gains no benefit. Nasafi retorts by unmasking these Sufis as insincere. Second, the Brahmins (Barahima) hold that reason alone is sufficient to discern good and evil, so prophets are unnecessary: if a prophet agrees with reason, reason suffices; if he contradicts reason, his message is invalid because God's proofs do not conflict. Third, the naturalists (tabi'iyyun) claim that if a claimant to prophethood acts within nature, he competes with a prophet; if he changes nature, that alters the immutable natures of things – or if nature can be changed, sorcerers can do the same, making miracles indistinguishable. Finally, a generic denial: miracles are not verified for all, so no prophetic claim is proven. Thus, the passage catalogues rationalist objections that the Ash'ari-Maturidi tradition must refute.

Core Idea
Nothing is obligatory upon God; He is the Creator and Bestower.

The Mu'tazila argued that reason can know good and evil and that God must do what is most beneficial for His servants. Ignorance of Definition Does Not Negate Knowledge
Nasafi argues that ignorance of definition does not negate knowledge, using analogy of two witnesses and applying to prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that knowledge is defined by firm conviction free from doubt, not by the ability to define knowledge or explain the proof in detail.

Core Idea
Knowledge is certainty of heart, not mastery of definitions.

In this passage, Nasafi addresses a potential objection: does a person need to know the precise definition of knowledge or the exact mechanism of proof in order to have true knowledge? He argues forcefully that it is the presence of certainty, free from doubt, that constitutes knowledge. Using a vivid analogy of two men hearing the same report from multiple witnesses, he shows that knowledge arises from the heart's settled conviction, not from intellectual analysis. He then applies this directly to the case of miracles and prophethood.

Commentary
This passage continues the argument by focusing on the irrelevance of knowing the formal definition of knowledge or the precise manner of proof (kayfiyyat al-dalala). Nasafi first states the principle: ignorance of the definition (tahdid al-'ilm) does not negate the existence of knowledge when its cause is present, as with the commoner. Then he provides a concrete analogy: two men hear the same report from many witnesses coming from different times and places. One's heart settles and he is convinced there is no collusion (tawatu'), while the other's heart is disturbed by doubt. The first has knowledge, the second does not. Applying this to the issue of prophethood: when a miracle is established, some people may entertain the possibility that the miracle's agent could be foolish or weak, thus doubting the truthfulness of the claimant. Such a person does not have knowledge of prophethood. But a person who does not entertain such doubts and whose heart settles on the truthfulness of the prophet has knowledge, built on evidence. The key point is that the evidence is the same for both, but the presence of doubt prevents knowledge for the first, while its absence allows knowledge for the second. Thus knowledge is defined by firm conviction free from doubt, not by explicit intellectual mastery of the proof.

Prophetic Truthfulness and Divine Foreknowledge
Dialectical argument forcing opponent to accept divine foreknowledge or fall into contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the dialectical argument that forces the opponent to either affirm the truthfulness of prophetic future reports or fall into contradiction.

Core Idea
Prophetic truthfulness about the future is inseparable from divine foreknowledge.

This passage from Imam al-Nasafi presents a powerful dialectical argument against those who deny that Allah's future reports can be considered truthful. Using a dilemma and Quranic examples, the author shows that rejecting the truthfulness of these reports leads to absurdity or disbelief. Let's trace the logic step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the dilemma
The argument opens with a forced choice: is the Prophet truthful or a liar? See the two dead ends.
Notice that the opponent cannot escape without self-contradiction or disbelief.
2
Understand the extension to future reports
The author argues that lying applies to future reports just as to past ones, especially on the opponent's own principle.
The opponent had used a similar argument about divine forgiveness.
3
Examine the Quranic examples
Three Quranic verses that predicted future events now past are cited. Notice how these demonstrate that the reports were true despite being about the future.
The examples show that the events actually took place.
4
See the final trap
The opponent must either admit the verses are true (invalidating his argument) or call them lies (disbelief).
This completes the reductio ad absurdum.
5
Reflect on the theological point
This argument upholds that Allah's knowledge encompasses all times and His reports are truthful.
The passage does not imply that Allah's knowledge changes; rather the known changes.

The Linguistic Rule of 'Ja'ala' with Two Objects
Nasafi establishes the linguistic rule of 'ja'ala' to refute the creationist interpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the linguistic analysis of 'ja'ala' refutes the claim that the Qur'an is created based on verse 43:3.

Core Idea
When 'ja'ala' takes two objects, it means naming, not creating.

The opponents of the Maturidi position cite the verse 'Indeed, We have made it an Arabic Qur'an' to argue that the Qur'an was created. However, a careful linguistic analysis of the verb 'ja'ala' reveals that when it governs two objects—as in this verse—it denotes naming or declaring, not bringing into existence. This lesson unpacks that distinction and shows why the verse does not support the claim of creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the first part of the Arabic passage (from 'فأما حل الشبهة' to 'مفعولين') along with its translation. Focus on how the author introduces the doubt and the linguistic principle.
Notice that the author begins by stating that the opponents have no support from this verse.
2
Understand the linguistic rule
Identify the two cases: 'ja'ala' with one object means creation; with two objects means naming. The author provides Qur'anic examples. Write down the rule.
3
Apply the rule to the disputed verse
The verse 'إنا جعلناه قرآنا عربيا' has two objects: 'ه' and 'قرآنا عربيا'. Therefore, it means 'We named it an Arabic Qur'an', not 'We created it.' Confirm this with the author's interpretation.
The author interprets it as 'جعلنا العبارة عنه بلسان العرب' (We made the expression about it in the Arabic tongue).
4
Review the examples provided
The author cites two additional verses where 'ja'ala' takes two objects: about the angels and about dividing the Qur'an. See how these reinforce the rule.

[The Response to the Scriptural Doubt]
As for the resolution of the scriptural doubt, we say: They have no support in His statement, the Exalted: {Indeed, We have made it an Arabic Qur'an} (43:3) because its meaning—and God knows best—is that We made the expression about it in the tongue of the Arabs and We conveyed the intended meaning and its rulings in the Arabic tongue. Then the linguists said: When "ja'ala" (made) is transitive to one object, it means action and creation, as in His statement: {Praise be to God, who created the heavens and the earth and made the darkness and the light} (6:1). And when it is transitive to two objects, it does not mean creation but rather means declaring (hukm) and naming (tasmiya). God said—informing about the disbelievers—: {And they made the angels, who are servants of the Most Merciful, females} (43:19). The meaning here is naming, not creation. Likewise in His statement: {Those who made the Qur'an into portions} (15:91), the meaning is naming, not creation. And in this verse, it is transitive to two objects.
And the intended meaning of His statement: {No mention comes to them from their Lord that is newly-conveyed} (21:2) is that "dhikr" (mention) could be the Messenger, peace be upon him, as He said: {He has mentioned a messenger} (65:10-11). So its interpretation is that no newly-created (muhdath) messenger comes to them except they listen to him, i.e., they hear his words. And it could mean that what comes to them is an admonition from the Prophet, peace be upon him—this because they did not laugh when reciting the Qur'an but rather revered and magnified its status. And it could be a mention of recent occurrence with respect to them, not that it is in itself new. Like a man who has a servant and buys another; he might say: "Call my new servant," even if he is older than the first. Since it is possible to apply these verses to these newly-created expressions (al-'ibarat al-muhdatha), there remains no proof for the opponents in the point of contention.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of a scriptural doubt (shubha sam'iyya) that the Qur'an is created (muhdath). The author begins by stating that the opponents have no support from the verse {Indeed, We have made it an Arabic Qur'an} because 'ja'ala' here means 'made the expression about it in Arabic', not 'created'. He then cites linguists: when 'ja'ala' takes one object, it means creation; when it takes two objects, it means declaring or naming. Applying this, the verse {And they made the angels females} means they named them females, not created them. Likewise, {Those who made the Qur'an into portions} means they divided it by naming parts. The verse under dispute also has two objects, so it means 'We made it an Arabic Qur'an' i.e., We named it or decreed it as such, not created it. Next, the author addresses the verse {No mention (dhikr) comes to them from their Lord that is newly-conveyed (muhdath)}. He gives three interpretations: (1) 'dhikr' refers to the Messenger, so 'muhdath' means a newly-sent messenger; (2) it refers to renewed admonition from the Prophet; (3) it means a mention of recent occurrence relative to them, not that the Qur'an itself is new. He provides an analogy: a man may call a newly-purchased servant 'my new servant' even if he is older. Since these interpretations are plausible, the opponents have no proof for their claim that the Qur'an is created. This refutation relies on linguistic analysis and contextual reasoning.
