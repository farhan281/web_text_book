Senses Are Reliable When Healthy
Abu al-Mu'in clarifies that senses are reliable when sound and free from defects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the reliability of sense perception is affirmed only when the senses are healthy and free from defects, and that apparent contradictions do not undermine this reliability.

Core Idea
Healthy senses never contradict; defects cause error.

This passage addresses a common objection raised by skeptics: that since our senses sometimes contradict each other (e.g., sweet tastes bitter to a sick person), sense perception cannot be trusted. Abu al-Mu'in clarifies that such contradictions only occur when the senses are impaired. The dispute about the reliability of the senses pertains only to their healthy, defect-free state.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once and then its English translation. Notice the structure: the passage first states the scope of the dispute, then asserts the consistency of healthy senses, and finally acknowledges that defects cause problems.
2
Identify the key point
The passage emphasizes that the dispute between us and the Sophists is specifically about the senses when they are sound ('fi hali salamati-ha'). This is the crucial limitation.
The phrase 'wa qatt la tatanāqaḍu qaḍāyā-hā' (never are their judgments contradictory) is a strong assertion.
3
Understand the sophist's error
The Sophists cite cases of contradictory sense perceptions (e.g., honey tasting sweet to one person and bitter to another) as proof that the senses are unreliable. Abu al-Mu'in here points out that such contradictions only occur when there is a defect or illness affecting the sense organ. In healthy states, contradictions do not occur. Therefore, the Sophist objection is invalid.
The phrase 'innamā yakhtallu idrāku-hā 'inda i‘tirāḍi al-āfāt' indicates the cause of impairment.

Commentary
This passage is a clarification and limitation of the earlier argument about sense perception. Abu al-Mu'in points out that the disagreement between the orthodox and the Sophists pertains to the senses when they are in a state of health and free from defects. In such a state, the judgments of the senses are never contradictory. The contradictions cited by the Sophists (e.g., honey tasting different to a sick person) only occur when there is a defect or illness affecting the sense organ. In those abnormal cases, the sense perception is impaired, and no one claims that such perceptions are reliable or that they constitute a contradiction of the normal, healthy functioning of the senses. Thus, the Sophist objection is invalid because it mistakenly treats defective perceptions as evidence against the senses in general.

The Disagreement on Using 'Hikaya'
Nasafi presents the context, positions, and the Mu'tazila objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the initial disagreement among scholars about whether it is permissible to call the recited Qur'anic expressions a 'hikaya' (narrative) of God's eternal speech.

Core Idea
The term 'hikaya' provoked disagreement because it implies similarity with God's speech.

Imam Nasafi sets the stage: once we accept that God's speech is an eternal attribute and the recited letters are expressions of it, a new question arises: can we call those expressions 'hikaya'? The early Maturidis allowed it, but al-Ash'ari and al-Qalanisi refrained, fearing it suggests similarity. Here we explore the initial positions and the Mu'tazila's objection.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage from the beginning up to the Mu'tazila's objection. Then read the corresponding translation. Notice how Nasafi frames the dispute.
2
Identify the two positions
Find the early Maturidi view that equates 'hikaya' with 'ibara', and the view of al-Ash'ari and al-Qalanisi who abstain due to the connotation of similarity.
The key term is 'mushabaha' (similarity).
3
Understand the Mu'tazila objection
The Mu'tazila argue that if the recited words are a hikaya, and hikaya implies similitude, then God's speech must be created like the recited words. This challenges the orthodox position.
This objection sets up the need for a response.

4. Is it permissible to say that the expressions are a narrative (hikaya) about God's speech? Then, when it is established that God's speech is His attribute subsisting in Him and that these letters are expressions (ibarat) about it, thereafter they differed as to whether applying the term 'hikaya' is permissible to it, so that one says: these expressions are a narrative about God's speech. The early ones among our scholars inclined to the permissibility of that, and they said: the term 'hikaya' and the term 'expression' are the same. But al-Ash'ari and Abu al-`Abbas al-Qalanisi refrained from applying the term 'hikaya' because of what it suggests of similarity (tashabuh). The Mu`tazila raise an objection against our scholars, saying: these words are a narrative about His speech, and the narrative necessitates similitude (mumathala), and these words and expressions are created, so likewise the narrated-about (mahki `anhu) [must be created]; or it is said: since these words are of the kind of letters and sounds, the narrated-about is thus. It is said to them: whoever among our scholars forbids applying the term 'hikaya' — and they are the later ones who adhere to decisiveness — then no objection lies against them. And whoever permits the application, he denies similitude and says: the narrative does not necessitate similitude, and does not necessitate except what news (ikhbar), expression (ibara), and conveyance (ada') necessitate. Its verification is that the translator (tujuman) expresses with an expression other than the first, and with a language other than the first, and it is called a narrative, and there is no resemblance (mushabaha) between the narrative and the narrated-about. Evidence of this is that one who reports (akhbara) about the speech of another — it is said: he narrated (haka) his speech, and it is not said: he imitated (haka) so-and-so unless he performed like his movements and his tone intentionally to imitate him, and without that it is not said 'he narrated' (haki) about him. And if the narrative necessitated similitude like imitation (muhakat), then they would not have refrained from that. The summary is that our scholars refuse the assertion of similitude between God's speech and these expressions, and thereafter they differed concerning the meaning of 'hikaya' linguistically. If it is established that it necessitates similitude, then all refrain; and if it is established that it does not necessitate similitude, then all permit the application. Thus this is a difference of expression without meaning, and no weight is given to it. And the cautious course is to refrain when difference occurs, and for this reason the cautious among our masters choose to say: 'God said, as a report from so-and-so', and they do not say: 'a narrative of so-and-so'. And with God is success.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of whether one may call the recited Arabic expressions (the Qur'an as recited) a 'hikaya' (narrative/account) of God's eternal speech. The author, Imam Nasafi, presents the positions and arguments. First, he sets the context: once it is established that God's speech is an eternal attribute subsisting in His essence, and that the written/recited letters are expressions signifying that attribute, scholars differed on applying the term 'hikaya' to these expressions. The early Maturidi scholars allowed it, equating 'hikaya' with 'ibara' (expression). However, al-Ash'ari and al-Qalanisi refrained due to the term's connotation of similarity (tashabuh), which might imply that the created words resemble God's speech. The Mu'tazila then raise an objection: if the recited words are a 'hikaya' of God's speech, and a hikaya implies similitude (mumathala), then since the recited words are created, the narrated-about (God's speech) must also be created. Alternatively, they argue that since the recited words are letters and sounds, the narrated-about must likewise be letters and sounds. Nasafi gives the response: for those who forbid the term (the later scholars), the objection does not arise. For those who permit it, they deny that 'hikaya' implies similitude. They argue that 'hikaya' only implies what 'ikhbar' (informing), 'ibara' (expression), and 'ada' (conveyance) imply. He provides the example of a translator: when a translator renders speech into another language, it is called 'hikaya', yet there is no resemblance between the translation and the original. Similarly, when one reports someone's speech, one says 'haka kalamahu' (he narrated his speech), but one does not say 'haka fulanan' (he imitated so-and-so) unless there is conscious imitation of tone and movement. Thus, 'hikaya' does not entail similitude. If it did, the scholars who permit the term would still have to deny it because they deny any similitude between God's speech and created expressions. The conclusion: all scholars of Ahl al-Sunna reject similitude between God's speech and the recited words. The remaining disagreement is merely linguistic: does the word 'hikaya' imply similitude or not? If it does, then all should refrain; if it does not, then all may use it. Since there is no consensus on the linguistic meaning, the cautious position is to avoid the term altogether and instead say 'qala Allahu ta'ala khabaran 'an fulan' (God said, as a report from so-and-so). This reflects the prudent choice of the later Maturidi masters.

The Maturidi Response and Linguistic Clarification
Nasafi gives the response and concludes with a cautious recommendation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how later Maturidi scholars responded to the Mu'tazila objection by denying that 'hikaya' implies similitude, using examples from translation and reporting.

Core Idea
'Hikaya' does not mean imitation; it only means conveying information.

The Mu'tazila pressed their objection, but Nasafi now presents the robust Maturidi response. The later scholars either avoided the term altogether or, if they used it, insisted that 'hikaya' does not entail similitude. The proof comes from everyday language: a translator's rendition is called a hikaya without any resemblance, and reporting someone's speech is not imitation.

The later Maturidi masters preferred this cautious phrase.

First Answer: Vision of Opposites and Homogeneous Bodies
Imam al-Nasafi presents the first answer from some scholars on the objection about seeing an elephant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first answer to the objection about seeing an elephant, focusing on how the nature of vision for opposites, different things, and homogeneous bodies explains why we would necessarily see an elephant if it were present.

Core Idea

Learning Objective

Unity of command is essential; one imam suffices for the ummah.

This passage establishes the fundamental rule that the imamate is a single office held by one person. The author provides rational arguments and cites the practice of the Companions to demonstrate that multiple imams lead to division and weakness. He also clarifies the only exception: when a natural barrier prevents mutual support.

Rulings on Conflicting Appointments and Refutation of Lot-Casting
Rulings on conflicting imamate contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rulings when two imams are appointed sequentially or simultaneously, and why casting lots is not a valid method to resolve the conflict.

Core Idea
Conflicting imamate contracts invalidate both, and lots do not establish rights.

Having established the general prohibition, the author now addresses specific scenarios: what if a second imam is appointed after a valid one? And what if two groups appoint different imams at the same time? He explains that the later appointment is rebellious and must be fought, and simultaneous contracts require a fresh start. He also rejects the suggestion to resolve the conflict by casting lots.
