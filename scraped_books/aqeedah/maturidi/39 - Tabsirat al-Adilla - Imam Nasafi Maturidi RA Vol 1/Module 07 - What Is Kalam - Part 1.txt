Mu'tazili Definitions of Knowledge and Their Invalidation
Nasafi reports and invalidates Mu'tazili definitions of knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazili definitions of knowledge presented by al-Ka'bi and Abu Hashim, and why Nasafi invalidates them.

Core Idea
Knowledge as mere belief is insufficient; Nasafi rejects Mu'tazili definitions.

This lesson examines the Mu'tazili approach to defining knowledge, as reported by Nasafi. Al-Ka'bi defines knowledge as 'belief of a thing as it is', and Abu Hashim adds 'tranquility of the soul'. Nasafi argues these definitions are inadequate. You'll see how Nasafi sets up his own preferred definition by first clearing the ground.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on Mu'tazili definitions
The passage presents two definitions. Read the Arabic and translation, noting the specific wording: 'اعتقاد' for al-Ka'bi, and 'سكون النفس' added by Abu Hashim.
Notice that Nasafi mentions these definitions critically.
2
Identify Nasafi's judgment
The passage says 'ويبطل هذه التعريفات' — he invalidates them. Ask: Why might belief alone be insufficient for knowledge?
This connects to later kalam discussions about certainty.
3
Reflect on the significance
Consider how this rejection sets the stage for alternative definitions from Ash'ari and Maturidi, which Nasafi will later evaluate.

Commentary
This passage is a comparative discussion of various definitions of knowledge ('ilm) in kalam. It begins by reporting the Mu'tazili definitions: al-Ka'bi defines knowledge as 'belief of a thing as it is', and Abu Hashim adds 'tranquility of the soul'. Nasafi invalidates these. Then it presents the Ash'ari definition by al-Ash'ari: knowledge is a description (wasf) that makes its possessor knowing. A criticism from al-Mawaqif is noted: this definition leads to circularity. Then Nasafi presents the Maturidi definition from Abu Mansur al-Maturidi: knowledge is an attribute (sifa) by which the known thing is manifested to the knower. This definition is explained further by Zayn al-Din Qasim. Nasafi chooses the Maturidi definition as his preference but also states a definition that agrees with al-Ash'ari. Finally, he specifies that the knowledge under discussion is that of the elite scholars who are devoted to reflection and research, not the common people.

Ash'ari and Maturidi Definitions: Nasafi's Preference
Nasafi presents and evaluates Ash'ari and Maturidi definitions, preferring the latter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari and Maturidi definitions of knowledge, the criticism of circularity against the Ash'ari definition, and why Nasafi prefers al-Maturidi's definition.

Core Idea
Nasafi prefers al-Maturidi's definition: knowledge as an attribute that reveals the known.

After rejecting Mu'tazili definitions, Nasafi turns to the Ash'ari and Maturidi schools. He presents al-Ash'ari's definition (knowledge is a description making one knowing), notes a criticism of circularity, then presents al-Maturidi's definition (knowledge is an attribute that manifests the known). Nasafi clearly chooses the Maturidi definition while also stating a definition that agrees with al-Ash'ari. This lesson clarifies his final position.

Guided Path
1
Read the Ash'ari definition and its criticism
Al-Ash'ari defines knowledge as 'the description by which whoever possesses it is knowing'. Note that al-Mawaqif criticizes this as circular. Consider why.
Circular because 'knowing' is defined by knowledge, and knowledge by 'knowing'.
2
Read the Maturidi definition
Al-Maturidi defines knowledge as 'an attribute by which the mentioned thing is manifested to the one in whom it subsists'. Compare this to the Ash'ari definition.
This definition avoids circularity by focusing on manifestation.
3
Identify Nasafi's choice
The passage explicitly says: 'والنسفي يختار هذا التعريف للماتريدي' — Nasafi chooses al-Maturidi's definition. Also note he states a definition that agrees with al-Ash'ari, indicating compatibility.
Nasafi's final view synthesizes the two schools.
4
Understand the scope of knowledge
Nasafi specifies that this discussion applies to the knowledge of the elite, not common people, who are devoted to reflection and research.

The Ash'ari Definition and the Charge of Circularity
Nasafi presents al-Ash'ari's definition and notes the circularity objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Ash'ari's definition of knowledge as an attribute and the objection that it leads to circularity (dawr).

Core Idea
Defining knowledge as 'the attribute by which one is knowing' risks circularity.

After rejecting Mu'tazilite definitions, Nasafi turns to al-Ash'ari's definition. While he acknowledges its comprehensiveness, he also notes a serious logical objection raised by the author of al-Mawaqif: that it is circular. This lesson examines the definition and the critique.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic excerpt containing al-Ash'ari's definition and the circularity objection.
2
Identify the definition
Al-Ash'ari defines knowledge as 'the attribute by which whoever possesses it is knowing.'
3
Understand the objection
The author of al-Mawaqif says this definition leads to circularity (dawr). Circularity occurs when a term is defined using itself or a synonym.
If 'knowledge' is defined using 'knowing,' then the definition presupposes what it defines.
4
Reflect on why this matters
A definition should be clearer than the term defined. If it is circular, it fails to inform. This critique motivates seeking a better definition.

Then al-Nasafī provides evidence for the existence of istita‛a and its division into these two parts. For the first part he cites the saying of the Exalted: “But if one does not find [the ability], then a fast of three days” [al-Mujādila 4] – and what is meant here is soundness of causes and instruments. For the second part he cites the saying of the Exalted: “They were not able to hear” [Hūd 20] – and what is meant by it is the negation of the reality of power (ḥaqīqat al-qudra), not the negation of causes and instruments, because they were [physically] present. And this warrants blame, because it is squandering the capacity given by Allah Almighty.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification of the concept of istita‛a (capacity). The author first states that istita‛a is defined by major mutakallimūn as qudra, quwwa, and ṭāqa, all synonymous. He then divides it into two types. It is said: 'amantu-hu fa amina' just as it is said 'ajlastu-hu fa jalasa.' So whoever reports a report and then reflects on its evidence and knows by the evidence its truthfulness and then affirms it, it is said: 'amana-hu,' i.e., he affirmed him after he brought himself through reflection on the evidences of his truthfulness into security, without being lied to or deceived or confused about that report. When the matter is this, it becomes clear that plain affirmation without evidence is not faith. Once this is established, then after this Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari and the Mu'tazila say: indeed the evidence must necessarily be rational ('aqli), because there is no way to make the speech of the Prophet evidence for the temporality of the world and the existence of the Maker, since the speech of the Prophet is not a proof (hujja) until his prophethood (risala) is established. And there is no way to affirm his prophethood except after knowing his Sender (mursil), and it will not be possible to know his Sender except after the confirmation of knowledge of the temporality of the world. And if that is the case, obtaining knowledge of the temporality of the world and the existence of the Maker through the speech of the Prophet cannot be conceived, because knowledge of the validity of his speech depends on knowledge of the temporality of the world and the existence of the Maker. This is verified by the fact that the rational person, as long as he does not know that the Maker of the world is [؟] wise (hakim), does not consider the miracle (mu'jiza) to appear at the hand of the false prophet (mutanabbi), as goes the view of those who say that futility (safah) is whatever lacks a praiseworthy outcome; or he knows that the Maker of the world is perfect in power (tamm al-qudra) and incapability ('ajz) is impossible for Him, and the appearance of a miracle at the hand of a false prophet would incapacitate Him from manifesting what is correct among religions and distinguishing between truth and falsehood among them, and this is impossible—as goes the view of those who say that futility is what is forbidden. And when the matter is thus, obtaining knowledge of the temporality of the world, the existence of the Maker, and the perfection of His wisdom and power through the speech of the Prophet cannot be conceived, because the knowledge that his speech is a proof is dependent on these items of knowledge. And when this has been excluded from being suitable as evidence to accompany affirmation—and these items of knowledge are not necessary (darura)—then it is necessary to say that a rational evidence accompanies it.
Source pages p. 165

Commentary
This passage presents a foundational argument in Maturidi epistemology of faith. It begins with an implied premise: if a belief is not necessary (daruri) nor inferential (istidlali), it cannot be knowledge ('ilm). The author applies this to the claim that someone who lacks knowledge of the temporality of the world, the existence and oneness of the Creator, and the truth of prophethood cannot be a true believer. He then distinguishes between mere affirmation (tasdiq) and faith (iman) by arguing linguistically: iman, from the root '-m-n,' means 'to bring oneself into security.' One who reflects on the evidence and thereby affirms the truthfulness of a report is said to 'amanahu'—he entered into security. This security is absent if the affirmation is not based on evidence, because one could be deceived. Therefore, plain tasdiq without evidence is not iman. After establishing this, the author reports the consensus of Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari and the Mu'tazila that the required evidence must be rational, not based solely on the Prophet's words. The reason is circular: to accept the Prophet's statement as evidence for God's existence and the world's temporality, one must first know that the Prophet is truthful; but knowing that requires knowing that God exists and is wise and powerful, otherwise miracles could be performed by false prophets. Thus rational knowledge is prior. The author then supports this with an argument about the definition of 'futility' (safah) in relation to God: a false prophet's miracle cannot succeed because it would imply God is either foolish or powerless. Hence, rational evidence is necessary.

Discourse on the Creation of the World

Then, after establishing the reality of things and distinguishing what is from the means of knowledge from what is not from the means of knowledge, we must direct our attention to explaining the creation (huduth) of the world with its divisions: its accidents (a'rad) and its bodies (ajsam).

Necessary Preliminaries

And it is not possible to achieve that except after knowing what the name 'world' ('alam) applies to, and knowing its divisions and the quiddity (mahiyya) of each division of it, and knowing the meaning of creation (huduth) and eternity (qidam). So we present all of that to make easy the explanation of what is the purpose of the chapter, which is knowing the creation of the world. So we say, and with Allah is success.

What is the world?

As for what is intended by the word 'world' (al-'alam) according to the mutakallimun, it is all existents other than Allah Almighty, from substances (a'yan) and accidents (a'rad). It is called 'world' (al-'alam) because it is a sign ('alam) of the existence of a Maker for it, Living, Hearing, Seeing, Knowing, Powerful, outside of His decree, exalted above what is in it of marks of creation and signs of deficiency, not similar to any of its divisions, nor resembling any part of its parts. There is nothing like Him. And He is the Hearing, the Seeing.

Divisions of the World according to the Mutakallimun

And as for its divisions, the generality of the mutakallimun claim that they are three divisions: jawahir (substances), ajsam (bodies), and a'rad (accidents).
Source pages p. 181

Commentary
This passage is an introduction to proving the creation (huduth) of the world. It begins by noting that after establishing epistemology (distinguishing valid sources of knowledge from invalid), the author will focus on demonstrating that the world is created. He then states three necessary preliminaries: (1) knowing what the term 'world' means, (2) knowing its divisions and the essence of each, and (3) knowing the meanings of 'created' (huduth) and 'eternal' (qidam). He then defines 'world' as everything other than Allah, including substances (a'yan) and accidents (a'rad). He explains that it is called 'alam (world) because it is a sign ('alam) pointing to its Maker, who is described as Living, Hearing, Seeing, Knowing, Powerful, etc. The definition emphasizes that the world is completely unlike its Creator. Finally, he states that the majority of mutakallimun divide the world into three categories: jawahir (substances), ajsam (bodies), and a'rad (accidents). This classification is essential for the subsequent proof of creation, as each category will be analyzed separately.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The term 'world' (alam) refers only to the physical universe (earth and heavens).
Correction: In kalam terminology, 'world' includes all existents other than Allah, even abstract substances and accidents.

?

?

?
