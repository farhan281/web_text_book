Al-Maturidi's Criteria for Imamate: Piety and Insight
Al-Maturidi states that imamate should be given to the most pious and knowledgeable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four qualities that Imam Abu Mansur al-Maturidi identifies as essential for the imamate and how they emphasize merit over lineage.

Core Idea
Leadership in Islam requires piety, insight, and knowledge, not just lineage.

In this passage, Imam al-Maturidi presents his normative religious criteria for selecting an imam. He lists four personal qualities that should be the primary considerations. This reflects his emphasis on capability and righteousness in leadership.

Guided Path
1

Pay attention to the four qualities listed.
2

Find and list the four attributes: الأتقى (most pious), الأورع (most abstinent), الأبصر بالأمور (most insightful in affairs), الأعلم بالمصالح (most knowledgeable of interests). Understand each term.
These qualities form the core of Maturidi's criteria.
3
Understand the phrase 'min tariq al-din'
This phrase indicates that the criteria are derived from the religious perspective, not mere political expediency. Reflect on why he emphasizes this.
It grounds the selection in Islamic values.
4
Consider the Quraysh condition
The commentary notes that Maturidi does not explicitly reject the Quraysh condition but adds merit-based qualifications. Think about how this balances tradition and capability.
This is a common point of discussion in Islamic political thought.
5
Reflect on implications
Consider how these criteria would affect leadership selection today. What does it mean to be 'most insightful in affairs' and 'most knowledgeable of public interests'?

Commentary
This passage presents the opinion of Imam Abu Mansur al-Maturidi on the criteria for imamate in the Muslim community. He states that from a religious perspective (min tariq al-din), the selection of an imam should be based on four personal qualities: al-atqa (the most pious), al-awra' (the most abstinent), al-absar bi al-umur (the most insightful in affairs), and al-a'lam bi al-masalih (the most knowledgeable about public benefits). The phrase 'min tariq al-din' indicates that this is a normative religious principle, not merely a political recommendation. The passage does not explicitly negate the Quraysh condition mentioned earlier, but instead adds a qualitative dimension that emphasizes merit over mere lineage. The concluding phrase 'fa tu'qad lahu al-imamah man kana huwa' (then contract the imamate to whomever is that) implies that such a person, regardless of other considerations, deserves the imamate. This opinion highlights Maturidi's concern for leadership based on capability and righteousness.

Omar's caliphate is validated by his unparalleled virtues and divinely aided conquests.

The author lists a cascade of Umar's virtues—from his early precedence in Islam to his asceticism and care for the needy—and supports them with prophetic hadith. The purpose is to show that such a person could not possibly commit injustice, and that those who claim otherwise are beyond rational argument.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of those who accuse Umar ibn al-Khattab of injustice, specifically the claim that he wrongfully seized the caliphate and usurped the rights of others, including marrying the daughter of Ali against his will. The Principle of Following the Perfect
Explains why Abu Bakr is the one followed despite earlier believers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Abu Bakr is considered the one followed (al-muqtađā) despite others believing earlier, based on his complete virtues of intellect, knowledge, and sound judgment.

Core Idea
People follow the perfect, not the first—Abu Bakr's complete virtues made him the leader.

This passage from Imam al-Nasafi argues that chronological precedence alone does not determine leadership in Islam. Instead, the one who possesses complete intellectual, knowledgeable, and judgmental qualities is worthy of being followed. Sanusi presents this principle to establish Abu Bakr's unique status, despite the earlier faith of Khadija, Ali, and Zayd.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on reward for guiding
Start by reading the opening lines about how guiding someone to faith earns the reward of that person's future believers. This sets up why Abu Bakr's efforts are so significant.
Notice the claim that Umar himself is a 'good deed' from Abu Bakr's good deeds.
2
Identify the order of first believers
The text lists the first believers in each category: women, boys, slaves, free men. Observe that Abu Bakr is last among them in chronological order.
This challenges the idea that being first makes one most virtuous.
3
Understand the principle of 'al-muqtađā'
Read why people do not follow women, boys, or slaves but follow one whose merits are complete. The key qualities are abundance of intellect, perfection of knowledge, and soundness of opinion.
This is the core argument: completeness of virtues matters more than chronological order.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed explanation of Abū Bakr's unparalleled sacrifices and steadfastness in supporting Islam during the Meccan period. It begins by emphasizing that throughout the entire thirteen-year period from the Prophet's mission to the Hijra, Abū Bakr assisted the Prophet with his wealth, to the extent that the Prophet declared that no wealth benefited him as much as Abū Bakr's. He then gave all his wealth, and when asked what he left for his children, he simply replied 'Allah,' indicating complete trust in God. Moreover, he used his wealth to free those being tortured by the polytheists, and also risked his own life. During the Hajj seasons, he would accompany the Prophet to the tribal leaders, praising Islam and calling them to it. In contrast, ʿAlī at that time was too young to be taken seriously, had no wealth to spend, and was not followed. The passage then highlights Abū Bakr's pre-Islamic reputation as a man of opinion, management, and great status, which made the nobles of Quraysh follow him and embrace Islam through his efforts. He endured all kinds of hardships—severities, difficulties, troubles—without his resolve weakening or his firmness softening, without weariness or regret despite the abundance of harm. Instead, he increased daily in support of the religion, strengthening the Prophet and manifesting the symbols of Islam. He vigorously defended the Prophet and the vulnerable believers who had no clan or support. The passage concludes by noting that none of these hardships touched ʿAlī due to his young age, as the enemies did not fear that Islam would spread through his efforts. The final phrase 'and his obvious past merits' ties back to the overall argument for Abū Bakr's precedence.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical narrative praising Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him). It begins by stating that people came to the Prophet and accepted Islam, then focuses on Abu Bakr's role. The author highlights that throughout the entire thirteen-year Meccan period, Abu Bakr supported the Prophet with his wealth, as confirmed by the hadith 'No wealth has benefited me as the wealth of Abu Bakr has benefited me.' The author then mentions that Abu Bakr gave away all his wealth when asked, and that through his wealth Allah saved persecuted Muslims. Next, the passage describes Abu Bakr's activities during the pilgrimage seasons: he accompanied the Prophet to the tents of tribal leaders, praising Islam and inviting them. In contrast, Ali was young and had no influence or wealth. The author then notes Abu Bakr's high status in pre-Islamic times as a man of opinion and management, and that many Quraysh nobles followed him and accepted Islam by his effort. The passage then describes Abu Bakr's steadfastness during the hardships in Mecca: his determination never slackened, his nature never softened, he felt no weariness or regret, and he only increased in supporting the religion and defending the Prophet and the believers, especially those without clan support. The author contrasts this with Ali, who was untouched by those hardships because he was young and the enemies did not fear his influence. The passage concludes with a reference to Abu Bakr's previous good deeds, likening them to bright marks and noble stations that [rise] in fields of [war] over others' merits, with their traces and attack following him.

Abu Bakr's Steadfastness and Contrast with Ali
Description of Abu Bakr's steadfastness and contrast with Ali.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will appreciate Abu Bakr's unwavering steadfastness in the face of hardships and understand how his role differed from that of Ali due to age and circumstances.

Core Idea
Abu Bakr's determination only grew stronger with adversity, never faltering despite prolonged hardship.

The final section of this passage paints a vivid picture of Abu Bakr's endurance and resilience. It describes how he endured various hardships during the Meccan period without any slackening of determination or regret. On the contrary, his support for the religion increased daily. The passage also contrasts his experience with that of Ali, who was spared these difficulties due to his youth. A closing metaphor praises Abu Bakr's earlier deeds as brilliant marks behind him.

Guided Path
1
Read about Abu Bakr's steadfastness
Read the Arabic text from 'ثم إنه تحمل' to the end. Notice the series of negations: his determination did not slacken, his nature did not soften, no weariness, no regret. Instead, he only increased in support.
The text uses parallel negative constructions to emphasize his unyielding nature.
2
Analyze the contrast with Ali
The passage states that nothing of those hardships touched Ali because of his young age and the enemies' lack of concern. Reflect on how the early community experienced different levels of trial.
3
Reflect on the closing praise
The author ends with a poetic description of Abu Bakr's previous good deeds, likening them to bright marks and noble stations that surpass others. This figurative language underscores his unparalleled merits.
The words 'غررها وحجولها' are metaphorical: 'غرر' means white marks on foreheads (of horses), a sign of beauty; 'حجول' means white marks on legs.

Commentary
This passage continues the biographical praise of Abu Bakr. It begins by stating that the Prophet placed his full trust in Abu Bakr, choosing him specifically for the emigration (hijrah) and companionship, and commanding him to use his wealth for the journey. Next, it mentions that Abu Bakr was the first in the two pledges (the pledge of 'Aqaba and the pledge under the tree) and the most eager for them. The author then quotes Quran 48:18 to highlight divine pleasure with those who pledged under the tree, and states that Abu Bakr received the same reward as those from whom he (the Prophet) found benefit. The passage then describes Abu Bakr's constant presence at the Prophet's right side throughout his lifetime, serving as consultant in calamities and vizier in important matters. The author gives a specific example from the Battle of Badr: when a Bedouin reported the battle's intensity, the Prophet affirmed Abu Bakr's role as 'an intimate, a companion, and a vizier,' causing the Bedouin to express praise. The passage then notes that Abu Bakr was the greatest in the eyes and hearts of the companions, evidence of which is the event where Umar proposed Abu Ubayda for the caliphate, but Abu Ubayda refused, saying that Abu Bakr was more deserving. The author concludes that even if there were no other proof of Abu Bakr's virtue and precedence, the fact that after the Prophet's death, amidst fear and dread, the hearts of the companions united upon him and the word was gathered, is sufficient evidence. This clearly indicates his status as the most deserving of the imamate.

Commentary
This passage is a compilation of historical and textual proofs for the virtue of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, used to establish his superiority over all other Companions and thus his rightful claim to the caliphate. The author, al-Nasafi, presents a series of arguments in narrative form, starting with Abu Bakr's unique closeness to the Prophet during the emigration (hijra) and his being chosen as a companion. He then cites the Qur'anic verse about the pledge under the tree (48:18) to show that Abu Bakr participated and earned reward like all believers. Next, he describes Abu Bakr's constant presence at the Prophet's right hand, his role as advisor and vizier, and a specific incident at the Battle of Badr when the Prophet praised him as an intimate companion and vizier. The author then notes that the Companions themselves considered Abu Bakr the greatest and most majestic, as exemplified by Abu Ubayda's statement to Umar during the Saqifa, where Umar was told that he had no distinction in Islam compared to Abu Bakr. Al-Nasafi then makes a key argument: even if the only proof of Abu Bakr's virtue were the fact that after the Prophet's death, when the Companions were terrified and divided, Abu Bakr managed to unite their hearts and bring them together, that alone would be sufficient proof of his excellence. He then elaborates on this by describing the Companions' bewilderment at the Prophet's death, with some denying it and others claiming he was alive. In contrast, Abu Bakr remained firm, recited the verse 'Indeed you are mortal and indeed they are mortal' (39:30), and took decisive action. He sent the army of Usama despite the risk of division, stating he would not untie a banner tied by the Prophet. He also fought the apostates and those who withheld zakat, and by his efforts, the Arabs were guided back to Islam. Finally, al-Nasafi presents additional proofs: the Prophet's delegation of leading prayer to Abu Bakr, which, according to the hadith about the most qualified to lead prayer, indicates he was the most knowledgeable or best among them. He also cites Ali's statement when he was asked to take the caliphate, in which Ali affirmed Abu Bakr's precedence, and Ali's rebuke of Abu Sufyan when the latter questioned Abu Bakr's tribal status. The overall argument is that Abu Bakr's consistent virtue, leadership, and the consensus of the Companions establish his imamate.

Commentary
This passage is a compilation of historical and textual proofs for the virtue of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, used to establish his superiority over all other Companions and thus his rightful claim to the caliphate. The author, al-Nasafi, presents a series of arguments in narrative form, starting with Abu Bakr's unique closeness to the Prophet during the emigration (hijra) and his being chosen as a companion. He then cites the Qur'anic verse about the pledge under the tree (48:18) to show that Abu Bakr participated and earned reward like all believers. Next, he describes Abu Bakr's constant presence at the Prophet's right hand, his role as advisor and vizier, and a specific incident at the Battle of Badr when the Prophet praised him as an intimate companion and vizier. The author then notes that the Companions themselves considered Abu Bakr the greatest and most majestic, as exemplified by Abu Ubayda's statement to Umar during the Saqifa, where Umar was told that he had no distinction in Islam compared to Abu Bakr. Al-Nasafi then makes a key argument: even if the only proof of Abu Bakr's virtue were the fact that after the Prophet's death, when the Companions were terrified and divided, Abu Bakr managed to unite their hearts and bring them together, that alone would be sufficient proof of his excellence. He then elaborates on this by describing the Companions' bewilderment at the Prophet's death, with some denying it and others claiming he was alive. In contrast, Abu Bakr remained firm, recited the verse 'Indeed you are mortal and indeed they are mortal' (39:30), and took decisive action. Finally, al-Nasafi presents additional proofs: the Prophet's delegation of leading prayer to Abu Bakr, which, according to the hadith about the most qualified to lead prayer, indicates he was the most knowledgeable or best among them. He also cites Ali's statement when he was asked to take the caliphate, in which Ali affirmed Abu Bakr's precedence, and Ali's rebuke of Abu Sufyan when the latter questioned Abu Bakr's tribal status. The overall argument is that Abu Bakr's consistent virtue, leadership, and the consensus of the Companions establish his imamate.

Proof from Delegation of Prayer and Companions' Endorsement
Al-Nasafi presents delegation of prayer and Companions' endorsements as final proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Nasafi uses the Prophet's delegation of leading prayer to Abu Bakr and the endorsements of Ali and others as proofs for his superiority.

Core Idea
The Prophet's choice of Abu Bakr to lead prayer and the Companions' refusal to replace him confirm his precedence.

Al-Nasafi presents two additional proofs: first, the Prophet appointed Abu Bakr to lead prayer, which according to a hadith indicates he was the most qualified; second, when Ali was offered the caliphate, he himself endorsed Abu Bakr, and Ali rebuked Abu Sufyan for questioning the choice.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on delegation of prayer
Read the part where Al-Nasafi cites the Prophet's delegation of prayer to Abu Bakr and the hadith about leading prayer. Understand the logical inference that the one who leads prayer is the best.
The hadith: 'The one who recites the Book of Allah most among you should lead you.'
2
Examine Ali's statement when asked to take the caliphate
Find where Ali says 'We will not relieve you nor ask you to be relieved. The Messenger of Allah put you forward; we will not put you back.' This shows Ali's recognition of Abu Bakr's precedence.
Ali's endorsement is crucial because he was a leading candidate himself.
3
Analyze the incident with Abu Sufyan
Read Ali's rebuke to Abu Sufyan when he criticized Abu Bakr's tribal status. Ali defends Abu Bakr's appointment as not based on tribal opinion.
This underscores that Abu Bakr's imamate is divinely ordained, not a matter of tribal preference.
4
Synthesize the proofs
Consider how these proofs complement the earlier ones. They show that even those who might have claim to leadership (like Ali) acknowledged Abu Bakr's superiority.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained argument defending the superiority of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq by demonstrating that guiding people to faith is better than killing enemies. The author begins by stating that through Abu Bakr's blessing, many senior Companions believed and even apostatizing Arabs were guided back. He then asserts without doubt that this guidance is superior to killing, because killing only breaks the enemy's power, whereas guidance brings both that benefit and the multiplication of the community, leading to the Prophet's boasting of his community. He supports this with a prophetic hadith to Ali: guiding one person is better than killing all between east and west. He then amplifies this by noting that messengers were sent for guidance, not killing, so anyone who brings about conversion without war gains a higher rank. If killing vast numbers does not equal guiding one, then killing a few definitely does not. Finally, he addresses an objection: some claimed that Abu Bakr (or another) did not disbelieve in Allah, contradicting the claim that he was the first to embrace Islam. The author answers by analyzing the validity of a child's Islam: if the child's Islam was valid, his prior disbelief counts as disbelief; if his prior state is disregarded due to immaturity, then his Islam at that time would not be valid. Thus the objection is refuted.
