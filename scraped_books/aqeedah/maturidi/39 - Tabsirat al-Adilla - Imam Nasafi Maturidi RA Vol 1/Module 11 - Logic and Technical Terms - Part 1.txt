Division of the World: Bodies, Substances, and Accidents
Al-Nasafi divides the world and presents Abu Mansur's alternative classification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the basic kalam classification of the world into bodies, substances, and accidents, and the Maturidi refinement into accidents and entities.

Core Idea
Kalam divides the world into three categories, but Maturidi reduces them to two: accidents and entities.

This lesson introduces the fundamental ontological categories in Maturidi theology. Al-Nasafi presents the standard tripartite division, then notes Abu Mansur al-Maturidi's refinement, which reduces the categories to two. This sets the stage for understanding how kalam analyzes creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic text once without analysis. Then read the translation. Notice the contrast between the two classifications.
The passage is short but packs two views.
2
Identify the standard division
Note the three terms: bodies (ajsām), substances (jawāhir), and accidents (a‘rāḍ). Understand that 'world' here means all contingent things that are not God.
3
Understand al-Maturidi's adjustment
He says there are only two categories: accidents (a‘rāḍ) and entities (a‘yān). Bodies are included in substances because a body is a substance that occupies space. So bodies are a subset of entities.
The term a‘yān refers to self-subsisting entities.
4
Reflect on the significance
This shows that Maturidi kalam is not merely repeating earlier categories; it refines them for clearer ontological analysis. Ask yourself: why would reducing to two categories be useful?
It simplifies the analysis of creation.

Defining Accident, Entity, and the Division of Bodies
Al-Nasafi defines accident and entity, then classifies entities into atom and body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Nasafi's definitions of accident and entity, and his classification of entities into indivisible atoms and composite bodies.

Core Idea
Accidents depend on entities; entities are either simple atoms or composite bodies.

Al-Nasafi begins by establishing the foundational distinction between what can and cannot subsist by itself. This sets the stage for discussing the nature of substances and bodies, which is essential for later arguments about God's transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Read the definitions
Read the Arabic passage and identify how al-Nasafi defines 'accident' and 'entity'. Notice that accidents cannot subsist by themselves, while entities can.
The term 'accident' here refers to non-substantial attributes, not random events.
2
Examine the discussion with other schools
Al-Nasafi engages with al-Ash'ari and the Mu'tazila. Understand that he is asserting his own view after considering other positions.
3
Learn the classification of entities
Entities are divided into non-composite (the indivisible atom) and composite (the body). Note the definitions of each.
The indivisible atom is the basic building block in kalam physics.

Refuting Corporealism: Why God Is Not a Body
Al-Nasafi refutes corporealism by stating the Maturidi view and refuting two groups.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Maturidi school rejects calling God a body, and how al-Nasafi refutes Hisham ibn al-Hakam and the Karramiyya.

Core Idea
God is not a body because a body implies direction, ends, and dimensions.

After establishing the classification of entities, al-Nasafi applies it to the nature of God. He argues that since a body is defined by having directions, ends, and dimensions, and God transcends all such limitations, the term 'body' cannot be applied to Him. He then refutes two groups who affirm God is a body.

Guided Path
1
Read the Maturidi position
Read the Arabic passage and note that al-Nasafi states the Maturidi view: God is not called a body because body implies directions, ends, and dimensions.
This is a key point of Maturidi theology.
2
Understand the refutation of Hisham ibn al-Hakam
Hisham argued that every existent is a body, therefore God is a body. Al-Nasafi refutes this by implication: God's existence is unique and not subject to bodily limitations.
3
Understand the refutation of the Karramiyya
The Karramiyya argued that God is self-subsistent, and all self-subsistent things are bodies, so God is a body. Al-Nasafi refutes this by showing that self-subsistence does not entail corporeality.
4
Reflect on divine transcendence
Consider why it is important for tawhid that God is not comparable to created bodies.

Al-Nasafi's Critique of Ash'ari's Definition of Accident
Al-Nasafi critiques Ash'ari's definition of accident as composite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Nasafi considered al-Ash'ari's definition of accident contradictory and how it violated Ash'ari's own principles.

Core Idea
A definition cannot be composed of two distinct descriptions for a simple concept.

Al-Nasafi presents al-Ash'ari's definition of accident as something that inheres in a substrate and ceases without the substrate's cessation. While admitting it is valid, he points out a fatal flaw: al-Ash'ari composed the definition from two descriptions, contradicting his own methodological commitment to simplicity. This lesson unpacks that critique and its significance in kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Pay attention to the two parts: definition and critique.
The passage is concise; notice the shift from presentation to evaluation.
2
Identify the two components of Ash'ari's definition
Al-Ash'ari defines accident as 'what inheres in something else and ceases without the cessation of its substrate.' Identify the two descriptions: inherence and cessation.
These are the two 'descriptions' al-Nasafi refers to.
3
Understand why it is contradictory
Al-Nasafi says it is composed of two descriptions, which contradicts al-Ash'ari's own principle that definitions of simple entities should be simple.
This is an internal critique—Ash'ari violates his own rule.

Second, he introduces the Dirariyya and Najjariyya view that bodies are composed of accidents, noting that Abu Mansur al-Maturidi inclined toward this view. The objection also notes that later Mu'tazila who allow a single accident to subsist in two loca would be committing an impossibility. The purpose is to show the difficulty of defining body in terms of composition without falling into contradiction.

Dilemma of Motion Transfer and Refutation of Manifestation and Latency
Sanusi presents a dilemma and refutes the manifestation-latency theory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the dilemma of motion transfer proves that accidents are created, and why the manifestation-latency theory leads to contradiction.

Motion cannot transfer between bodies without new creation; avoiding this leads to impossible opposites.

Why Accidents Cannot Inhere in Accidents
The author proves the impossibility of an accident subsisting in another accident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the kalam proof that an accident cannot subsist in another accident, based on infinite regress and the nature of self-subsistence.

Core Idea
If accidents could inhere in accidents, the chain would go on forever—impossible.

This lesson examines a foundational principle in kalam: that accidents (non-substantial attributes) cannot themselves serve as loci for other accidents. The author presents a concise but powerful argument. By following his reasoning, you will see why this principle is necessary for a coherent ontology of accidents and substances.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text once without translation. Notice the key terms: قيام العرض بالعرض (subsistence of accident in accident) and تسلسل (infinite regress).
The style is dense—read slowly.
2
Identify the two premises
The argument uses two main reasons: (1) infinite regress if allowed, and (2) what is not self-subsistent cannot be a locus. Find them in the text.
3
Understand the regress argument
If accident A could inhere in accident B, then accident B could inhere in accident C, and so on without end. Since such an infinite chain is impossible (in this context), the initial possibility is false.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
4
Understand the self-subsistence argument
An accident by definition is not self-subsistent; it needs a locus. Something that itself depends on a locus cannot serve as a locus for another. This reinforces the conclusion.
5
Apply to the example of latency and manifestation
The author gives the example of latency (كمون) and manifestation (ظهور) supposedly inhering in motion and rest. Show how the principle prevents that.
This connects the abstract proof to a concrete case.

Commentary
This passage proves the impossibility of an accident inhering in another accident. The argument uses a regress: if accident A can subsist in accident B, then accident B can subsist in accident C, and so on ad infinitum. Since infinite regress is impossible for entities in ordered hierarchies, it is impossible. Additionally, any entity that is not self-subsistent (i.e., needs a locus) cannot be the locus for another entity. Therefore, attributes like latency and manifestation cannot subsist in motion and rest if motion and rest themselves are accidents; instead, motion and rest must be accidents directly inhering in bodies.

Distinguishing Manifest and Latent from Motion and Rest
Proof that manifest and latent are distinct from motion and rest, establishing accidentality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why manifestation and latency must be distinct attributes from motion and rest, and how this proves that motion and rest are accidents.

Core Idea
Manifestation and latency are not the essences of motion and rest; they are additional attributes.

In this passage, the author tackles a subtle claim: that the qualities of being manifest or latent are identical to the essences of motion and rest. By showing this leads to contradictions, he proves they are separate and thereby establishes that motion and rest are accidents — things that undergo change. This argument strengthens the Maturidi position that all bodies and their attributes are contingent.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze. Then read the translation. Let the author's argument flow before interpreting.
Notice the author uses a reductio ad absurdum (proof by contradiction) structure.
2
Identify the opponent's claim
The opponent says manifestation and latency are not additional meanings beyond the essence of motion and rest. Understand what this means: they think 'manifest' and 'latent' are the same as the essence of motion/rest.
3
Follow the reductio for rest
If rest is manifest by essence, it cannot become latent (since its essence would have to change). If it is latent by essence, it must have always been latent. Both are impossible. Therefore, manifestation and latency must be distinct attributes.
The same logic is then applied to motion.
4
Connect to accidentality
The author states that this distinction proves motion and rest are accidents (they undergo change). He also notes that all genera of accidents are impermanent, hinting at a future proof.
5
Reflect on the broader argument
Consider why this argument is important: it defends the contingency of the physical world and refutes those who claim that attributes like motion and rest are eternal.
The author promises a later proof for the impossibility of accidents enduring.

Imagination is a product of sensation; it cannot reach the unseen.

This passage from Imam al-Nasafi's Tabsirat al-Adilla addresses a common epistemological error: equating the rational with the imagined. The author defines imagination as a sensory imprint and argues that using it to know the unseen leads to absurdity, like trying to hear colors. This lesson unpacks his argument and its implications for theology.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Jawhar is not self-subsistence; it means a particle that composes bodies.

This passage from Imam al-Nasafi's Tabsirat al-Adilla continues the debate over the term jawhar. The opponents define jawhar as 'self-subsisting' (qa'im bi al-dhat) and argue that since God is self-subsisting, He can be called jawhar. Al-Nasafi refutes this by showing that the definition is arbitrary and that other definitions, such as 'origin of bodies' or 'receptive to accidents', are equally valid. The passage uses logical tools like coextension (ittirād) and co-exclusion (in'ikās) to challenge the opponents' claim.

The passage shows that defining jawhar as self-subsisting is not logically necessary and leads to contradictions, so it cannot be applied to Allah.
This safeguards divine transcendence.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite definition of 'jawhar' (substance/atom) as 'qā'im bi-dhātih' (self-subsistent). Nasafi argues that the correct definition is 'aṣl yutarkabu minhu al-ajsām' (an origin from which bodies are composed). He begins by noting that if a body is composed of jawhar, then the body itself is not a jawhar. Then he addresses the Mu'tazilite claim that 'jawhar' denotes self-subsistence and that this definition is valid through co-extensiveness (ṭard wa 'aks). Nasafi counters: if 'jawhariyya' (substantiality) rotates with self-subsistence, it also rotates with being an origin for bodies. The Mu'tazilites cannot privilege one over the other without justification. He then uses a second argument: in the witnessed world, every jawhar is receptive to accidents, and vice versa. If the Mu'tazilites accept this as a definition, they contradict their own principle that God is not receptive to accidents. If they reject it, they undermine their own method of using co-extensiveness. Next, he presents a third argument based on linguistic indication: the word 'jawhar' itself suggests 'aṣl' (origin) and not 'qā'im bi-dhātih'. Therefore, if one applies the name to God, it must be for the meaning of 'aṣl', not self-subsistence. He supports this with an analogy: a thing is called 'moving' only because motion attaches to it, not because of its existence or self-subsistence. Similarly, attributes like black, white, etc., are named for their specific meanings. He then generalizes: a meaning that is inseparable from a thing may not be causally related; transfer to the unseen requires that the meaning cannot be attributed to anything else. In the case of jawhar, since it can be attributed to being an origin in addition to self-subsistence, the definition as self-subsistent fails. He concludes that this argument is valid against the Mu'tazilites because they themselves define 'accident' by a logical condition rather than linguistic indication, but here they are forced to accept the linguistic evidence.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite definition of 'jawhar' (substance/atom) as 'qā'im bi-dhātih' (self-subsistent). Nasafi argues that the correct definition is 'aṣl yutarkabu minhu al-ajsām' (an origin from which bodies are composed). He begins by noting that if a body is composed of jawhar, then the body itself is not a jawhar. Then he addresses the Mu'tazilite claim that 'jawhar' denotes self-subsistence and that this definition is valid through co-extensiveness (ṭard wa 'aks). Nasafi counters: if 'jawhariyya' (substantiality) rotates with self-subsistence, it also rotates with being an origin for bodies. The Mu'tazilites cannot privilege one over the other without justification. He then uses a second argument: in the witnessed world, every jawhar is receptive to accidents, and vice versa. If the Mu'tazilites accept this as a definition, they contradict their own principle that God is not receptive to accidents. If they reject it, they undermine their own method of using co-extensiveness. Next, he presents a third argument based on linguistic indication: the word 'jawhar' itself suggests 'aṣl' (origin) and not 'qā'im bi-dhātih'. Therefore, if one applies the name to God, it must be for the meaning of 'aṣl', not self-subsistence. He supports this with an analogy: a thing is called 'moving' only because motion attaches to it, not because of its existence or self-subsistence. He then generalizes: a meaning that is inseparable from a thing may not be causally related; transfer to the unseen requires that the meaning cannot be attributed to anything else. In the case of jawhar, since it can be attributed to being an origin in addition to self-subsistence, the definition as self-subsistent fails. He concludes that this argument is valid against the Mu'tazilites because they themselves define 'accident' by a logical condition rather than linguistic indication, but here they are forced to accept the linguistic evidence.

The Linguistic Indication Argument
Nasafi argues from linguistic indication that jawhar means 'origin'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Nasafi uses the lexical meaning of 'jawhar' to argue that it means 'origin' rather than 'self-subsistent'.

Core Idea
The word 'jawhar' itself points to 'origin', not 'self-subsistent'.

Nasafi now turns to linguistic analysis. He argues that the word 'jawhar' contains an indication (dalāla) of being an origin, whereas there is no such indication for self-subsistence. This linguistic fact should determine the definition.

Guided Path
1
Read the linguistic claim
Read the Arabic passage focusing on the statement 'ليس من لفظ الجوهر ما ينبئ عن القيام بالذات، وفيه ما ينبئ عن كونه أصلا'.
Understand that Nasafi is making a claim about the word's inherent meaning.
2
Study the analogy
Nasafi uses the example of 'mutaḥarrik' (moving) to illustrate that a name is applied for the specific meaning that attaches to it.
The analogy shows that if motion attaches to something, it is called moving because of that motion, not for another reason.
3
Apply the principle
Understand that if 'jawhar' linguistically indicates 'origin', then the term should be defined by that meaning, not by self-subsistence.
This argument strengthens Nasafi's case that God is not a jawhar (if jawhar means origin, since God is not composed).
