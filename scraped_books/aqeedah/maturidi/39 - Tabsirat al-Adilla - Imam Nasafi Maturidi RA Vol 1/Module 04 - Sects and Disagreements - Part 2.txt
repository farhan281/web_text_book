Imamate Established by Election: Nasafi's Refutation of Nass
Nasafi argues that imamate is by election and refutes textual designation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Nasafi's argument that imamate is established by the election of the just, and why claims of a textual designation (nass) are refuted.

Core Idea
Imamate is a public matter decided by election, not by inheritance or hidden text.

In this passage, Nasafi addresses a core controversy: how is the leader of the Muslim community appointed? He rejects both hereditary claims and secret prophetic designations, grounding the imamate in the choice of the community's righteous members. Let's examine his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two questions
Read the passage and notice that Nasafi answers two questions: 'By whom is the imamate established?' and 'By what means?'. This structure frames his argument.
2

Nasafi states imamate is established by the choice of the just and righteous. He argues it is a public affair, so election is the natural method.
This refutes any claim of private designation.
3

The Rawandiyya claim inheritance or nass for al-Abbas; the Jarudiyya claim a nass for 'Ali by description then inheritance. Note how Nasafi rejects both.
These are the two main alternatives to election.

Nasafi points out that the Companions, despite their piety, chose the imam without opposition. If a nass existed, it would be known and the designated person would have asserted it. Since neither happened, no nass exists.
This is an argument from silence and historical observation.

Reflect on 'Ali's role
Nasafi notes 'Ali's bravery and integrity. If there had been a nass for him, he would have defended it for the truth, not ambition. His silence further proves no nass.
This refutes any claim that 'Ali was designated but remained silent.

Al-Maturidi's Division and Definition of 'Ayn
Al-Maturidi rejects the jawhar/arad division, proposes a'rad and a'yan, and defines 'ayn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Maturidi rejected the jawhar/arad division, how he redefined 'ayn as qa'im bi-nafsih meaning existence without a locus, and how he refuted al-Ash'ari and Burghuth.

Core Idea
Al-Maturidi’s 'ayn subsists by itself – not independently of God, but without a physical locus.

This passage introduces al-Maturidi’s unique division of the world into a'rad and a'yan, rejecting the standard jawhar/arad split. He redefines what it means for something to be self-subsistent (qa'im bi-nafsih). Al-Maturidi clarifies that this does not imply metaphysical independence from the Creator, but merely that an entity can exist without inhering in a substrate. He then refutes al-Ash'ari's denial of self-subsistent jawahir and Burghuth's absurd claim that God cannot create less than two parts. This lesson will walk through his argument step by step.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of al-Ash'ari and Burghuth's definition of 'ayn (entity), combined with al-Maturidi's own alternative division of the world. The author first explains that al-Maturidi rejected the common division into jawahir (substances) and a'rad (accidents) because of overlap: bodies (ajsam) are themselves composed of jawahir, so the division is not exclusive.
