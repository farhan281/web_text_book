The Pure Grace of Divine Favors
Nasafi uses a kingly analogy to argue divine favors are pure grace.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Nasafi uses a kingly analogy to show that God's blessings are not diminished by a sense of obligation, because they are pure gifts from an infinitely superior Being.

Core Idea
Favors from a superior are pure gifts, not transactions; so they are never tainted.

Nasafi begins by painting a vivid picture of a king bestowing favors on a commoner. The recipient feels immense pride, not obligation. This human experience serves as a stepping stone to a deeper theological truth: if earthly gifts from a superior are cherished, how could the gifts of the infinitely superior God be distasteful? Let's read the passage and see how Nasafi builds this argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the analogy
Read the Arabic passage describing the king and the commoner. Notice the contrast between a gift and a purchase. Nasafi uses this to highlight the pride that comes from a favor.
The keyword 'منة' (minna) is central here; it refers to the sense of obligation that could taint a gift.
2
Apply the analogy to God
After the analogy, Nasafi asks a rhetorical question: how could God's blessings be tainted? Note the language of God's majesty and power.
The phrase 'كيف يتوهم' shows the absurdity of the opposing view.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
The passage concludes that all blessings, even small ones, are from God and cannot be tainted. Think about why this matters for understanding God's generosity.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained theological argument defending the pure graciousness of God's blessings against two potential objections: (1) that divine favors could be tainted by a sense of obligation (minna), and (2) the Mu'tazilite doctrine that obligates God to confer blessings and reward. The author, Imam al-Nasafi, begins with a detailed human analogy. He describes a recipient of a king's special favor who takes pride in it, contrasting this with a mere commercial transaction where no pride is felt. The point is that favors from a superior are more enjoyable precisely because they are pure gifts. He then applies this logic to God: since God is infinitely greater than any earthly king, and everything is under His dominion, how could any of His blessings—however small—be distasteful due to a sense of obligation? To further illustrate, Nasafi presents a hypothetical: even the highest Paradise would be 'tainted' if the objection under consideration were valid, because the inhabitants receive it purely by God's grace. He then reinforces this with two arguments. First, no servant deserves anything from God through worship, because worship is itself a form of gratitude for previous blessings, and fulfilling a due right does not earn additional compensation. Second, even if it did, the servant can never fully thank God for even one blessing, because gratitude itself requires further divine enabling (tawfiq), which constitutes another blessing requiring further gratitude, leading to an infinite regress. Thus, full gratitude is impossible, and all blessings are pure grace. Finally, Nasafi turns to refute the Mu'tazilite position, calling it a 'strange contrivance.' He summarizes their view that God is obliged to grant blessings, gratitude is then obligatory upon the servant, and then God is obliged to reward that gratitude—any breach by either party would constitute injustice. Nasafi dismisses this as irrational, though the passage cuts off mid-sentence at the end of the page, likely continuing the critique.

The Impossibility of Complete Gratitude
Nasafi argues the impossibility of fully thanking God due to infinite regress of tawfiq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Nasafi's argument that humans cannot fully thank God for even a single blessing, because gratitude itself requires further divine enabling (tawfiq), leading to an infinite regress.

Core Idea
Gratitude for one blessing requires further enabling, so complete gratitude is impossible.

Having established that God's blessings are pure grace, Nasafi now faces a potential objection: if we cannot earn God's favors, we should at least be grateful. But he turns this around, showing that even gratitude is impossible to render fully, because every act of gratitude is itself a blessing requiring more gratitude. This deepens the argument that all blessings are unearned grace.

Guided Path
1
Read the Paradise hypothetical
Nasafi begins this section with a hypothetical: if the objection were true, even Paradise would be tainted. Notice how he extends the logic of the previous argument.
This shows that the same principle applies to the highest rewards.
2
Understand the tawfiq argument
Nasafi then argues that no servant deserves anything by worship, because worship is gratitude. Then he introduces tawfiq: even gratitude requires divine enabling, which is another blessing.
The term 'توفيق' (tawfiq) is crucial—it means divine enabling or success granted by God.
3
Follow the infinite regress
Nasafi explains that gratitude for tawfiq requires further tawfiq, ad infinitum. Thus, complete gratitude is impossible.
This refutes any notion that humans can fulfill their duty of gratitude.

The Debate on Khulf in God's Threat
Nasafi presents the view that khulf in God's threat is permissible and the rejection by the mutakallimun.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key arguments for and against allowing khulf (breach) in God's threat, as presented by certain jurists and rejected by the great mutakallimun.

Core Idea
Breaking a threat is not lying, but for God it is still impermissible.

This lesson examines a controversial view held by some jurists that God may break His threat out of generosity, distinguishing it from breaking a promise. It then presents the decisive rejection by the mutakallimun, who argue that any khulf would imply a changeable attribute in God, which is impossible.

Commentary
This passage presents a debate about whether God can break His threat (wa'id). The author then narrates a story from Abu al-Abbas al-Mubarrad about a debate between Abu Amr ibn al-Ala and someone (Abu Uthman al-Mazini?) over whether the Arabs consider leaving a threat unfulfilled as blameworthy. Abu Amr argues from Arabic usage and poetry that the Arabs do not condemn breaking a threat; rather, they praise it as a virtue, while they condemn breaking a promise. The interlocutor initially tries to argue that God fulfills His promises and threats, but Abu Amr retorts that the Arabs do not call someone who leaves a threat 'breaker' in a blameworthy sense; they reserve that term for breaking promises. The interlocutor concedes that one would not call God 'breaker' if He does not fulfill a threat. Certain jurists argued that breaking a threat is permissible because it is generosity, while breaking a promise is baseness. They also claimed that lying applies only to past statements, not future ones; a future broken threat is merely 'khulf' (breach), which is blameworthy only in promises. The author reports that some of our (Hanafi) jurists held this, and al-Baghdadi attributed it to al-Qalanisi, though the author found that al-Qalanisi actually said it by way of specification (takhsis) — that particular individuals might be excluded. The great mutakallimun reject this entirely, arguing that khulf is impermissible for God in either case because if it were allowed, one could rightfully describe God as 'mukhlif al-wa'id' (breaker of the threat), which is not permissible. The author then narrates a story from Abu al-Abbas al-Mubarrad about a debate between Abu Amr ibn al-Ala and someone (Abu Uthman al-Mazini?) over whether the Arabs consider leaving a threat unfulfilled as blameworthy. Abu Amr argues from Arabic usage and poetry that the Arabs do not condemn breaking a threat; rather, they praise it as a virtue, while they condemn breaking a promise. The interlocutor initially tries to argue that God fulfills His promises and threats, but Abu Amr retorts that the Arabs do not call someone who leaves a threat 'breaker' in a blameworthy sense; they reserve that term for breaking promises. The interlocutor concedes that one would not call God 'breaker' if He does not fulfill a threat. The author then provides multiple poetic examples supporting this view from al-Sari, Ka'b bin Zuhair, and an anonymous poet. However, the author immediately qualifies: this reasoning applies only to human beings, not to God. For God, it is impossible to be described as 'mukhlif' because that would entail possibility of change (tabaddul) in God's attributes, which is impossible. Thus, the linguistic argument is irrelevant for the divine case.

Core Idea

The author tackles a delicate theological issue: if God threatens punishment for disbelief, could He still forgive? Some argue that God must fulfill His threat; otherwise, His words would be false. Core Idea
Reason does not override revelation but helps define its scope.

This passage presents a debate on whether rational necessity restricts the literal meaning of Qur'anic verses about reward and punishment. An opponent argues that reason must qualify these verses because eternal punishment of believers is inconceivable. Nasafi responds by showing that the same reasoning would also require qualification, thus undermining the opponent's exclusive claim. Commentary
This passage is a structured argument and rebuttal concerning the interpretation of divine promises (wa'd) and threats (wa'id) in the Qur'an. The author, Imam al-Nasafi, presents an opponent's claim and then responds. The opponent argues that rational evidence (dalli al-'aql) necessarily qualifies (yuqayyid) the absolute wording of these verses. Reason rejects such disproportionate punishment, so this too would restrict the threat verses. Furthermore, God's attributes of mercy and forgiveness also prevent a literal, universal application of the threat verses. Thus, the opponent's own reasoning forces them to accept some qualification, contradicting their claim that the Ash'aris deny all rational qualification. The passage ends with a prayer for divine success.

The Dialectical Argument for Contextual Qualification in Divine Promises
Nasafi uses two scriptural examples to challenge unconditional promise interpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Nasafi uses two scriptural examples to prove that divine promises and threats can be contextually qualified, refuting the literalist claim that absolute wording implies unqualified generality.

Core Idea
Absolute divine promises are qualified by context, not unqualified in all states.

In this passage, Nasafi engages in a dialectical argument (mujadala) against opponents who insist that every divine promise or threat must be interpreted in its absolute, unqualified sense. He presents two clear examples from the Quran—Adam's promise and the mercy verse—to show that such absolutism leads to theological absurdity. As you read, notice how each example forces the opponent into a dilemma: either God's word becomes false or the promise was actually qualified.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own argumentative flow come through before you interpret.
The passage is dialectical—look for the 'if... then' structure.
2
Follow the Adam example
Nasafi first presents the unconditional promise to Adam: no hunger, nakedness, thirst, or sunstroke. After the sin, Adam became naked. Ask: Did the promise cover that state? The opponent's answer leads to either a falsehood or a qualified promise.
This example exposes the flaw in taking 'absolute' wording at face value.
3
Follow the mercy verse example
The second example is the verse 'Do not despair of Allah's mercy; indeed, Allah forgives all sins.' No restriction is mentioned. If the opponent says it covers all sins, they contradict consensus (some sins require repentance). If they say it doesn't, they risk calling God a liar or admit a hidden restriction.
This reinforces the need for contextual qualification.
4
Understand the Maturidi position
Nasafi clarifies that his school does not hold a rigid literalist interpretation. They accept figurative meaning (ta'wil) and a speaker's intended meaning if plausible. He refutes the claim that they enforce absolute generality on God's speech.
This is a defense of the Maturidi methodology.

Then we say to them: Did not God Almighty say to Adam, peace be upon him, when He settled him in Paradise: 'Indeed, it is [promised] for you that you will not be hungry therein nor will you be naked, and that you will not be thirsty therein nor will you be sun-stricken.' Then when the slip occurred from him, he became naked as God Almighty said: 'And they began to fasten over themselves from the leaves of Paradise.' Was that absolute promise inclusive of the state of the occurrence of the slip or not? If he says: it was inclusive, then the falsehood of God Almighty has become apparent because He promised him that he would not be naked yet he became naked. And if he says: it was not inclusive of the state of the slip, then he has claimed that the absolute promise free from restriction was intended with a restriction by a state, and in it is the invalidation of their doctrine and the corruption of the claim that an unqualified formulation free from the evidence of specification and restriction indicates intended generality and absoluteness. And similarly it is said: Did not God Almighty say: 'Say, O My servants who have transgressed against themselves, do not despair of the mercy of Allah. Indeed, Allah forgives all sins.' Does He forgive every sin or not? And no restriction nor specification of a sin accompanied it. If they say: Yes, they invalidate their doctrine and also depart from consensus. And if they say: No, it is said: Does God Almighty thereby become a liar? If they say: Yes, they disbelieve. And if they say: No, they invalidate (their doctrine). And were it not for fear of prolongation, I would have clarified some evidences concerning the issue of generality (al-'umum) and exposed what they obscure to the weak by bringing some issues of our companions and they portray to them that the doctrine of our companions is the obligation to believe in the inclusion of the ruling of the general (al-'amm) in what its wording covers, and to testify about God Almighty that He intended by it everything, and to constrain Him from intending any specification or restriction in it. Commentary
This passage is a dialectical argument (mujadala) against an opponent who insists that divine promises and threats must be taken in their absolute, unqualified sense. Nasafi presents two scriptural examples to show that such absolutism leads to absurdity. First, God promised Adam that he would not be hungry, naked, thirsty, or sun-stricken in Paradise. Yet, after the sin, Adam and Eve became naked and covered themselves with leaves. The question is: did that unconditional promise cover the state of sin? If the opponent says yes, then God's promise was false, which is impossible. If the opponent says no, then he admits the promise was actually qualified (muyqad) by the state of obedience, contradicting his own claim that an unconditional wording indicates absolute generality. Then Nasafi uses the verse 'Do not despair of Allah's mercy; indeed, Allah forgives all sins.' This verse has no explicit restriction. If the opponent says it covers every sin, he invalidates his own position (by absolving all sins) and violates consensus. If he says it does not cover all sins, then God's statement becomes false unless he admits a restriction not stated. If he then says God would be a liar, that is disbelief. Thus, the opponent is caught in a dilemma. Nasafi then explains that his own school (Maturidi) does not hold a rigid literalist interpretation; rather, they allow figurative meaning (ta'wil) and accept a speaker's interpretation if plausible. He refutes the claim that his school believes in enforcing literal generality and constraining God's intent. He mentions that their books contain many cases where a speaker's intended meaning beyond the literal is accepted. The passage ends abruptly due to the author's disclaimer about brevity.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some may think that Nasafi is rejecting all general statements in the Quran, or that the Maturidi school denies the validity of general rulings.
Correction: Nasafi does not reject general statements; he argues that absolute wording can be qualified by context or reason without denying the general rule's validity in its intended scope. The Maturidi school does accept general rulings but allows for contextual qualification and figurative interpretation.

Key Takeaway
Divine promises are contextually qualified, and absolute wording does not preclude specification by state or context.
