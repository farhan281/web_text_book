The Eternity of Divine Speech: Three Arguments
Nasafi enumerates three arguments for the eternity of divine speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three arguments used by Nasafi to prove that divine speech is an eternal attribute and cannot be originated.

Core Idea
Divine speech cannot be an accident; it must be an eternal attribute of God.

Imam Nasafi presents three distinct arguments to demonstrate that God's speech cannot be a created, temporal phenomenon. Each argument eliminates possible alternatives, leading to the conclusion that speech is an eternal attribute subsisting in God's essence. This lesson walks through these arguments step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation. Notice the logical divisions Nasafi presents.
The passage contains three separate arguments.
2
Understand the first argument
Identify the three-part logical division (in God's essence, in another locus, or no locus) and why each possibility is impossible.
This argument eliminates any possible way for speech to be an accident.
3
Understand the second argument
Notice the disjunctive argument: either speech subsists in God or obligations become meaningless.
This shows that denying eternal speech leads to absurdity.
4
Understand the third argument
Consider the argument from deficiency: if not speaking eternally, God would be silent, which is a deficiency.
Deficiency cannot be attributed to God.
5
Connect to theology
Reflect on how these arguments establish that speech is an eternal attribute, not a created accident.
This is foundational for understanding the divine attribute of speech.

حادثا لكان الأمر لا يخلو إما أن يكون حادثا في ذات الله تعالى، وإما أن يكون حادثا في محل آخر سوى ذاته تعالى، وإما أن يكون حادثا لا في محل، ولا تصور لقسم رابع، وهذه الأقسام كلها ممتنعة، ويدلل على امتناع كل قسم منها. وبعد ذلك يقول : إما أن يقال إن كلام الله تعالى قائم بذاته، وهو متكلم آمر ناه، وإما أن ينكر أن يكون الله تعالى كلام وأمر ونهي، وبذلك تبطل فرضية العبادات، لانعدام أمره ونهيه. ويقول : إنه تعالى لو لم يكن في الأزل متكلما لكان موصوفا بضد من أضداد الكلام كالسكوت، واتصافه تعالى بذلك محال لأنه نقص، والنقص من أمارات الحدوث.
If it were originated, the matter would not be free from either being originated in the essence of God Most High, or being originated in another locus besides His essence Most High, or being originated not in a locus, and there is no conception of a fourth division. All these divisions are impossible, and he provides evidence for the impossibility of each division. After that he says: either it is said that the speech of God Most High subsists in His essence, and He is a speaker, commander, prohibitor; or it is denied that God Most High has speech, command, and prohibition, and thereby the obligation of acts of worship is invalidated due to the absence of His command and prohibition. And he says: if He Most High were not in pre-eternity a speaker, He would be described with an opposite of the opposites of speech, like silence, and His being described with that is impossible because it is deficiency, and deficiency is a sign of origination.

Commentary
This passage contains three arguments demonstrating that divine speech cannot be originated and must be an eternal attribute subsisting in God's essence. First, Nasafi presents a logical division: if speech were originated (hadith), it would have to be either (1) an accident in God's essence, (2) an accident in another locus, or (3) an accident not in a locus. A fourth possibility is inconceivable. All three are impossible: the first is impossible because it would imply change in God; the second would make speech a property of another subject; the third is contradictory because accidents cannot exist without a locus. Thus, originating speech in God is impossible. Second, he gives a disjunctive argument: either we affirm that God's speech subsists in His essence, making Him a commanding and prohibiting speaker, or we deny that God has speech, command, and prohibition. Denial would nullify all religious obligations since there would be no divine command or prohibition, which is absurd. Therefore, speech must be an eternal attribute. Third, he argues that if God were not speaking from eternity, He would be described by the opposite of speech, such as silence. But silence is a deficiency, and deficiency is a sign of origination. Since God is beyond deficiency, He must be eternally speaking. These three arguments converge on the conclusion that God's speech is an uncreated, eternal attribute.

The Rational Method and the Core Argument for Vision
Nasafi presents his method and the rational argument for vision.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Nasafi's preference for rational proof and the core Maturidi argument that the cause of visibility is being qaim bi al-dhat.

Core Idea
The absolute cause of visibility is subsistence by oneself, not corporeality.

Nasafi begins by explaining his method: rational proof should precede transmitted evidence, but here he presented the latter first because it establishes both the fact and permissibility of vision. Then he lays out the rational argument: the Mu'tazila claim visibility requires a body, but the Maturidis say the cause is being self-subsistent. This foundational point sets up the entire debate.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage about method
Notice Nasafi's remark about the order of proofs. He usually starts with reason, but here he used transmitted evidence first. Ask yourself why.
This reveals his pedagogical strategy.
2
Identify the two positions
Find the Mu'tazila claim (visibility requires corporeality) and the Maturidi counter (visibility requires qiyam bi al-dhat). Understand the logical connection.
3
Connect to the conclusion
Since Allah is qaim bi al-dhat, the Maturidi conclusion is that He can be seen. Contrast with the Mu'tazila conclusion that He cannot.
This is the core of the rational argument.

After that, he argues for the permissibility of vision by reason, saying: It would have been more suitable to argue by reason first and then support that by transmitted evidence, as is his method – except that he presented the transmitted evidences because they establish both the affirmation and permissibility of vision. And from his rational evidences for the permissibility of vision: that the opponents (al-khusum) established that the absolute cause of vision is corporeality (al-jismiyya); so whatever is a body is visible, and Allah is not a body, therefore He is not visible. However, according to al-Nasafi, his associates said that the absolute cause of vision is His being subsistent by Himself (qa'im bi al-dhat); so whatever is subsistent by itself, its vision is permissible, and whatever is not like that, its vision is impossible. And Allah (Exalted) is subsistent by Himself, so He is possible of vision. And he explains this statement discussing the Mu'tazila in their speech. Then after that he says: We ask the Mu'tazila: why did you say that the attachment of vision to these genera (ajnas) is the attachment of rulings to causes (ilal), rather than these genera being among the attributes of existence (sifat al-wujud)? And they demand proof, so whatever they answer will demolish their own statement. Then he says: And it is advisable that we should not claim that the absolute cause of vision is the subsistence by oneself (al-qiyam bi al-dhat) either, and what al-Maturidi and his associates mentioned was by way of concession and lenience. And we should not occupy ourselves with claiming something as a cause, for they (the opponents) are the ones who claim that the absolute cause of vision is what they alleged. So there is no meaning to occupying oneself with opposing a cause with a cause before being unable to prevent the proof of the opponents and demolishing it.

Commentary
This passage presents the rational argument for the permissibility of seeing Allah. Nasafi begins by noting his methodological preference for rational proof first, but here he presented transmitted evidence first because it simultaneously establishes affirmation and permissibility. Then he presents the core rational argument: the Mu'tazila claim that the absolute cause of visibility is being a body; therefore God, not being a body, is not visible. But the Maturidis counter that the cause is being subsistent by oneself (qaim bi al-dhat). Since Allah is qaim bi al-dhat, He is possible of vision. Nasafi then explains that he discusses this with the Mu'tazila. Next, he challenges the Mu'tazila: why do they consider visibility to be attached to bodies as causal attributes (ilal) rather than as attributes of existence (sifat al-wujud)? He demands proof from them and claims any answer will demolish their position. Finally, Nasafi advises that we should not claim that the absolute cause is qiyam bi al-dhat either; the Maturidi position was a concession. The better approach is to not engage in asserting a cause at all, but rather to first refute the opponent's arguments.

Challenging the Mu'tazila: Cause vs. Attribute of Existence
Nasafi challenges the Mu'tazila's causal theory of visibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Nasafi's challenge to the Mu'tazila: why they consider visibility to be a causal attachment to bodies rather than an attribute of existence, and how this undermines their position.

Core Idea
Visibility is an attribute of existence, not a causal property of bodies.

After presenting the Maturidi alternative, Nasafi questions the Mu'tazila's foundational assumption. He asks: why do you treat visibility as a ruling attached to a cause (corporeality) rather than as a property of existence itself? This move shifts the burden of proof onto the Mu'tazila, demanding they justify their causal framework.

Guided Path
1
Read the challenging question
Find the sentence where Nasafi directly asks the Mu'tazila for their rationale. Notice the key terms: 'ta'alluq al-ahkam bi al-ilal' and 'sifat al-wujud'.
This is a critical dialectical move.
2
Understand the two frameworks
One framework sees visibility as a ruling linked to a cause (corporeality). The other sees it as an attribute of existence. Nasafi suggests the latter is more coherent.
3
Anticipate the Mu'tazila's dilemma
Nasafi claims that whatever they answer will demolish their own statement. Try to think why any answer would be problematic for them.
This is a classic refutation strategy.

Nasafi dismantles this analogy by showing that the human case involves prohibition and lack of praiseworthy outcome, neither of which applies to God. Commentary
This passage refutes the rational objection that if Allah wills kufr, He must be a kafir, based on analogy with human behavior (shahid). Al-Nasafi presents two arguments. First, a subtle point: Allah's willing something that would negate His lordship or divinity is not comparable because His essence is necessarily existent. Second, he cites al-Ash'ari's definition: foolishness (sufh) is what is forbidden. In the witnessed world, one who wills foolishness is prohibited from it, so he becomes foolish by doing it. But in the case of Allah (al-gha'ib), there is no prohibition upon Him, so He is not foolish. Then al-Nasafi gives his own (Maturidi) view: foolishness is that which has no praiseworthy consequence (aqiba hamida). If Allah wills kufr, it has praiseworthy consequences (e.g., punishment of disbelievers, manifestation of justice), so it is not foolish. He then gives examples to support this, which are not detailed here but are said to demonstrate the falsity of the opponent's claim.

Commentary

Proof of Indivisible Atoms
Nasafi proves the existence of indivisible atoms by refuting infinite divisibility and answering objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Nasafi uses rational arguments to prove the existence of indivisible atoms, refuting infinite divisibility and responding to objections.

Core Idea
Infinite divisibility leads to absurdity, proving atoms must exist.

Imam al-Nasafi now turns to a fundamental debate in Islamic theology: whether matter is infinitely divisible or composed of indivisible atoms. He presents a powerful reductio ad absurdum to show that infinite divisibility contradicts sensory experience, and then addresses a sophisticated objection from God's attributes. This lesson unpacks his reasoning and the decisive proof from the nature of aggregation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the density of argument; each sentence carries weight.
2
Identify the first argument
Find the initial argument against infinite divisibility—the comparison of a mustard seed and a mountain. Why does infinite divisibility make them equal?
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
3
Analyze the objection and response
The opponents raise an objection from God's knowledge and power. How does Nasafi respond? Pay attention to the distinction between actual existent things and non-existent things.
This clarifies that infinity can be compared only in the created, finite realm.
4
Understand the aggregation argument
Read the second argument about who created aggregation in bodies. Follow the logical steps: if God can separate parts, then after separation each part becomes indivisible.
This argument ties atoms to the contingency of accidents.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Nasafi concludes that atoms exist and notes the vast literature on the topic. Why does he not delve deeper?
The lesson's key takeaway is that infinite divisibility is rationally impossible.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the existence of indivisible atoms (jawahir) and a refutation of the view that atoms are infinitely divisible. The author first states the disagreement: Hisham ibn al-Hakam, al-Nazzam, and many early thinkers and mathematicians denied the existence of an indivisible part, claiming infinite divisibility. Then he presents a reductio ad absurdum: if a part is infinitely divisible, then a mustard seed and a mountain would have the same number of parts, so neither would be larger than the other, which contradicts sensory evidence. He then handles an objection: the opponents cite God's knowledge and power as cases where one infinite can be greater than another (since God's objects of knowledge are more than His objects of power, yet both are infinite). The author answers by distinguishing between actual existent things (which are finite and can be more or less) and non-existent things (which are infinite and cannot be compared). Thus, the objection does not apply to actual bodies. He then presents a second argument: whoever denies indivisible parts must explain who aggregates parts of a body. They will say God. Then ask: can God separate them? If not, God is impotent; if yes, then after removal of aggregation each part would be indivisible, because divisibility requires aggregation. Hence, parts remain indivisible, proving the existence of atoms. The author concludes by noting the abundance of arguments and says he will not elaborate further for brevity.

Proof for the Existence of Accidents from Color Change
Al-Nasafi presents a proof for the existence of accidents from color change.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Nasafi proves the existence of accidents using the observation of color change in a body and why this proof is foundational for establishing the temporal origination of the world.

Core Idea
Color change proves accidents, not essence.

Al-Nasafi begins by stressing the importance of proving accidents, as they lead to establishing the world's temporal origination. He then presents a clever argument from color change: when a body turns from black to white, the change cannot be due to the body's essence, so accidents must exist. This lesson walks through that argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the importance of proving accidents
Read the opening lines where al-Nasafi mentions the many types of accidents and the sects that deny them. Note why he says proving accidents is crucial: it leads to proving the temporal origination of the world.
This sets the stage for the proof.
2
Follow the proof from color change
Work through the argument: We observe a black body that becomes white while the body remains. Then consider the two possibilities: blackness is due to the body's essence or due to a separate meaning (accident). Trace the reasoning for each branch.
Pay attention to the logical steps and why the essence branch leads to a contradiction.
3
Draw the conclusion
Since the essence branch is impossible, the blackness must be due to an accident that perishes, and the whiteness due to a new accident. This proves that accidents exist.
Reflect on how this argument refutes those who deny accidents.

Why Negation Cannot Explain Blackness
Al-Nasafi refutes the objection that blackness is not due to essence nor accident

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a simple negation (no cause) cannot account for why a body is black rather than white, and why this leads to absurdity.

Core Idea
Negation never specifies; only positive attribution can.

This short but powerful passage presents a typical objection—that blackness might be neither essential nor accidental—and demolishes it with a single logical point: negation does not pick out one of two opposites. Before we can attribute a quality to something, we need a reason, not a denial of reasons.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
The objection claims blackness is not due to essence nor due to an accident (i.e., no cause at all). Notice that this is a pure negation.
The objector thinks 'no cause' is a possible explanation.
2
Spot the refutation's key point
Al-Nasafi says negation cannot necessitate specification (ikhtiṣāṣ) to one of two descriptions. Why? Because saying 'neither X nor Y' doesn't make X more likely than Y.
3
Work through the absurd conclusion
If blackness has no cause at all, then at the moment the body is black, being white would be equally possible. But that contradicts the actual observation that the body is black. So the negation is false.
This shows that every quality must have a cause—either essential or accidental.
4
Reflect on the broader principle
This argument teaches a general rule: denial alone never grounds a positive state. In kalām, all observable attributes must be traced back to a real cause.

Refutation of Dual Causation for a Single Attribute
Al-Nasafi refutes the claim that blackness is due to both essence and accident simultaneously.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a rational judgment cannot have two distinct causes and how this refutes the claim that blackness is due to both essence and accident.

Core Idea
One effect, one cause: rational judgments are single-sourced.

Al-Nasafi here tackles a subtle objection: what if blackness is caused by both the essence and an accident? He shows this is logically impossible because a rational judgment requires a single cause. The objector's position collapses into contradiction and inadvertently admits the existence of accidents.

This passage addresses the objection that blackness is due to both the essence and an accident. The author argues that this is contradictory because a single effect (blackness) cannot have two distinct causes: if it is due to the essence, it cannot be due to an accident, and vice versa. The rational judgment of blackness cannot arise from two principles. Thus, the objection collapses into the already refuted position of no cause. Additionally, by admitting 'meaning' (accident), the objector implicitly concedes the existence of accidents, which is what the proof aims to establish.

Refuting the Maker's Act Objection
Al-Nasafi refutes an objection about blackness coming from a maker's act.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Nasafi refutes the objection that blackness is directly caused by a maker's act, and how the argument reduces to the essential/accidental dichotomy.

Core Idea
A maker's act must work through essence or accident — not outside them.

Having established that blackness is either essential or accidental, a potential objection arises: could blackness be produced directly by a maker's act? Al-Nasafi anticipates this and shows that even if we say blackness comes from a maker's 'making', the making itself must be analyzed as either affecting the essence or creating an accident. Thus the original dichotomy stands.

The Reality of Accidents: A Rational Proof
Nasafi proves the existence of accidents through a numerical example.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Nasafi uses a legal example to prove that accidents have real existence.

Core Idea
Accidents are real; denying them leads to absurdity.

Imam Nasafi begins with a legal ruling on the punishment for adultery to demonstrate a deeper metaphysical truth. The increase from eighty to one hundred lashes is not merely a numerical change; it implies the reality of the act of striking itself. This passage grounds the existence of accidents in rational necessity.
