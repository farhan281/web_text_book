The Principle of Inseparable Attachment and Non-Causality
The author states a principle about inseparable attachment not implying causality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why inseparable attachment between two things does not automatically mean one causes the other, and what additional condition is needed for causality.

Core Idea
Inseparability is not causality—attachment alone does not prove causation.

This lesson explores a foundational principle in Islamic theology: just because two things are always found together, we cannot assume one is the cause of the other. The author, Imam al-Nasafi, uses clear examples to illustrate this point, building up to the critical condition that must be met for causality to be inferred.

These examples are key to grasping the general rule.

Understand the conclusion about causality
From the examples, the author draws a general rule: inseparable attachment (coexistence in existence and non-existence) does not automatically imply causality. One thing attaching to another inseparably does not make it a cause.

Critique of Defining Existence as Substance or Accident
The author criticizes the method of defining existence by dividing it into substance and accident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the author rejects the definition of existence as substance or accident and how this relates to the conditions of a valid definition.

Core Idea
A valid definition must be comprehensive; dividing existence into substance and accident fails this condition.

In this passage, the author continues his critique of alternative methodologies in kalam. He examines the common practice of defining the existent by dividing it into substance and accident, a method he deems corrupt. By analyzing the conditions of a proper definition, he shows why this approach is insufficient and how it reveals deeper methodological disagreements between the Mu'tazila and Ahl al-Haqq.

Commentary
This passage addresses a different kind of argument: defining existence by dividing it into substance and accident. The author calls this 'تحديد مقسم' (divisive definition), i.e., defining a genus by its species. He argues that this is invalid because a proper definition must be comprehensive (جامع) and mutually exclusive (مانع). Here, the two descriptions (substance and accident) cannot be combined in a single subject, so the definition is not comprehensive. Moreover, the definition does not actually define existence itself but divides it. He references al-Jubba'i's definition of knowledge to illustrate a similar error. The author notes that this objection only holds according to the principles of Ahl al-Haqq (the Ash'ari/Maturidi school), not for the Mu'tazila who permit divisive definitions. Thus, the Mu'tazila cannot reject the Christians' claim that God is a substance using this argument.

Resolving Apparent Contradictions Between Revelation and Reason
Nasafi refutes anthropomorphic interpretation by prioritizing rational proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why anthropomorphic scriptural expressions must be interpreted metaphorically to avoid contradiction with definitive rational proofs.

Core Idea
Revelation and reason cannot contradict; ambiguous texts yield to decisive proofs.

Imam al-Nasafi addresses a common challenge: how to handle verses and hadith that seem to describe God in physical terms. He points out that these texts are inherently open to interpretation, whereas rational proofs are unambiguous. Therefore, the literal meaning cannot be taken at face value, as that would pit revelation against reason, both of which are divine proofs.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the premise
Nasafi begins by stating that solving this issue requires a premise: scriptural language can have multiple meanings, while rational proofs are clear. Understand that this premise is the key to the argument.
The premise is that scriptural texts are muhtamila (capable of interpretation) and rational proofs are not.
2
Identify the two types of proofs
Notice the distinction: the words in the Book and Sunna might imply anthropomorphism, but they are open to ta'wil. In contrast, the rational proofs (huquq 'aqliyya) are decisive. Reason itself is a means of knowledge and a proof from God.
Intellects are among the causes of knowledge and are God's proof upon creation.
3
Understand the consequence of literalism
If you take these verses literally, you establish a contradiction between the Book and rational proofs. But both are proofs of God, and God's proofs cannot conflict. Contradiction would imply foolishness and ignorance in God, which is impossible.
Therefore, the only sound approach is to interpret the ambiguous in light of the clear.

Commentary
This passage refutes the anthropomorphic interpretation of scriptural texts (shubha sam'iyya). The author begins by stating that solving this issue requires a premise: the words in the Qur'an and Sunna that seem to imply resemblance (tashbīh) or that God is a body composed of parts (mutabayyiḍ mutajazzi') are all capable (muḥtamila) of interpretations (ta'wīl) beyond the literal meaning. In contrast, the rational proofs ('aqliyya) that the author has presented are not capable of alternative interpretation—they are decisive. Reason (ʿuqūl) is one of the means (asbāb) of knowledge, and it is God's proof (ḥujja) upon creation. Therefore, to take these scriptural expressions literally, as the anthropomorphists (mujassima) and assimilationists (mushabbiha) do, results in a contradiction between revelation and reason. But both the Book and rational proofs are proofs from God, and God's proofs cannot contradict each other. Contradiction in one's proofs is a sign of foolishness (safah) and ignorance (jahl). God is wise (ḥakīm) and not foolish, and He is knowing (ʿālim) and does not ignore. Thus, the correct approach is to interpret these ambiguous verses in a way that harmonizes with the definitive rational proofs, preserving the transcendence of God.

Then, if we have evidence that one of them exists in the unseen, we necessarily know that the other exists. Similarly, if we see a moving body, we know it moves due to motion subsisting in it, and if black, due to blackness subsisting in it. This method is crucial for establishing divine attributes: from the witnessed attributes of creatures, we infer attributes of the Creator in an appropriate manner.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation and a demonstration of inconsistency. The Correct Method: Consensus and Self-Subsistence Argument

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the correct method for arguing that God can be seen: first secure consensus on the invisibility of accidents, then argue from self-subsistence.

Core Idea
To prove God's visibility, first agree that accidents are invisible, then argue from self-subsistence.

This passage from Tabṣirat al-Adilla offers a methodological guide for kalam arguments. The author, Imam al-Nasafi, advises that instead of jumping straight to abstract principles, one should first establish common ground with opponents on a basic point: in this world, accidents are never seen. Only then can one apply the analogy to the unseen.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem
The author starts by noting that reliance on self-subsistence is not possible without first securing consensus on the invisibility of accidents.
This sets the stage for a method that avoids direct abstract reasoning.
2
Identify the agreed point
Both parties agree that accidents are not seen in this world, and that bodies are seen. Also, every body is self-subsistent.
These are the building blocks for the analogy.
3
Trace the analogy
The argument moves from the witnessed world (shahid) to the unseen (gha'ib): bodies are seen because they are self-subsistent, not because they are bodies. Therefore, God, being self-subsistent, can be seen.
The key is to identify the cause (self-subsistence) and apply it to the unseen.
4
Note the exception
If the opponent holds that some accidents are perceptible (like sound), then the consensus argument fails. In such a case, rely on the proof of impossibility of seeing accidents as a category.
This protects the argument from diversity of opinions regarding accidents.
5
Final conclusion
Bodies are seen because they are self-subsistent, not because of composition or corporeality. This avoids attributing materiality to God.
This clarifies the true basis for visibility.

So there is no meaning to occupying oneself with opposing cause with cause before being unable to prevent the opponent's evidence and demolish it. And recourse to that is only taken when unable to do that, and there is no inability, praise be to God, as we will explain after this, God willing. So whoever heeds this advice and adheres to this method has sufficed against the trouble of the opponent and the stigma of their debate is repelled from him.

Commentary
This passage gives a practical procedural warning. The author advises not to claim outright that the absolute cause ('illa mutlaqa) of vision is being self-subsistent (qa'im bi al-dhat). Even though al-Maturidi mentioned this in the previous passage, it was said by way of leniency (musamaha) and facilitation (musahala), not as a primary argument. The correct method is to demand from the opponents that they provide proof for the cause they themselves claim (e.g., bodies or colors). The term 'cause' here refers to the condition or attribute that makes something visible. The opponents assert that the cause of visibility is bodies or colors; the author says: do not start by saying the cause is self-subsistence; instead, force the opponent to prove their claimed cause first. Engaging in counter-causal claims before defeating the opponent's evidence is pointless. Only if one is unable to refute the opponent's evidence should one turn to offering an alternative cause. But the author asserts that there is no such inability, God willing. The passage concludes by saying that whoever follows this advice has sufficiently handled the opponent and avoided the shame of debate.

Key Takeaway
The correct method in theological debate is to first demand proof from the opponent before offering alternative explanations, and only resort to counter-causal claims when unable to refute their evidence.
