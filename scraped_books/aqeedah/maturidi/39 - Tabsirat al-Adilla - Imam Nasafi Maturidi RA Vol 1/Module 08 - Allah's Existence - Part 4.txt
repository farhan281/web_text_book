Why Excluding Disputed Acts Preserves God's Unique Praise
Nasafi counters the objection that the verse is praise by proving exclusion preserves uniqueness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Nasafi defends the necessity of excluding human voluntary acts from the verse's generality to preserve the unique praise of God.

Core Idea
True praise requires exclusivity; if others share the quality, the praise is destroyed.

Nasafi now tackles a key objection: if the verse (e.g., 'Allah is the Creator of everything') is meant as praise, why would it specify human acts? He argues that praise only works when the quality is unique to God. Excluding the disputed acts actually preserves the praise, because including them would mean lowly creatures share in that quality, which would destroy the praise entirely.

The Timeless Scope of Quranic Evidence
Nasafi responds to the objection that 'Creator of all things' refers only to bodies, not actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Nasafi's two arguments that the verse 'Creator of all things' is not limited to bodies but covers all entities, including human actions, and applies to all future disputes.

Core Idea
Quranic verses are timeless and address all future theological issues.

In this passage, Imam Nasafi refutes a common objection against using the verse 'Creator of all things' as evidence for God's creation of actions. The objection claimed the verse only referred to bodies, not actions, because the debate about actions arose later. Nasafi offers two compelling arguments to show the verse's universal and timeless scope.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the first part of the passage where Nasafi states the objection: that the verse is only about bodies, not actions, because the dispute over actions arose later.
Notice the claim: 'Your statement is not sound...'
2
First argument: Historical precedent
Nasafi argues that the dispute over actions already existed before Islam among Jews (the 'Anāniyya) and Christians (jabr), so the verse would cover their views.
3
Second argument: Divine foreknowledge
Even if the dispute hadn't arisen then, God foreknew it and included evidence against it in the Quran.
This emphasizes the comprehensive knowledge of Allah.
4
Conclusion: Timeless relevance
The Quran's clarifications are not limited to the time of revelation but extend to all needs until the end of the world.

Nasafi presents a key Quranic verse as evidence for the Maturidi position on the creation of human actions. Opponents interpret the verse differently, claiming it refers to the objects made (idols). Nasafi relies on a grammatical rule established by Sibawayh to show that the verse speaks of actions, not objects. In this lesson, we will follow his argument verse by verse.

Guided Path
1

Start by reading the verse 'And Allah created you and what you do' and notice how Nasafi immediately explains that 'what you do' means 'your action'. This is his opening claim.
The verse is central to the Maturidi proof for the creation of human acts.

Nasafi introduces the grammatical principle: when 'ma' is attached to a verb without a resumptive pronoun, the combination expresses the verbal noun (masdar). He gives an example: 'a'jabani ma sana'ta' means 'your making pleased me'.

The opponents object that 'ma ta'malun' refers to the product (idols), citing similar verses like 'Do you worship what you carve?' (37:95). Identify how they argue that the verse is about the created objects, not actions.

Nasafi responds by insisting on the default rule: without the resumptive pronoun (ha'), 'ma' + verb means the action. He also provides a proof from another verse: 'Recompense for what they used to do' – here the recompense is for the deed, not the product.

Nasafi proves evil outcomes require a creator

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When actions go against our will, they reveal a greater Will behind them.

This lesson presents a philosophical argument from experience: we often intend good but outcomes are evil. Nasafi uses this to prove that there must be an external creator who brings about the actual quality. This argument, known as the 'proof from contrary outcomes', is a cornerstone of Maturidi theology.

Guided Path

Commentary
This passage is a proof (istidlāl) that human actions which occur contrary to the agent's intention must have an external cause, namely God. This shows that the action has taken on a quality opposite to what was intended. From this he concludes that there must be an originator (mūjid) who brings about the action with that quality. He then applies this to the case of disbelief (kufr) and idol worship: the disbeliever intends these acts to be good and pleasing to God, but they are actually evil and opposed to wisdom. The second leads to the absurdity of things causing themselves. The third alone is coherent, and that is the position of the Ahl al-Sunnah, that God is the creator of all actions, including their qualities.

Paradise Not Diminished by Merit
Imam Nasafi argues that paradise cannot be diminished by merit via an infinite regress argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why human actions cannot independently earn reward in paradise and why all reward is by divine favor alone.

Core Idea
All human obedience is gratitude for prior blessings; gratitude itself is a gift.

Imam Nasafi presents a powerful argument against the view that human actions merit reward. He shows that every act of worship is actually a response to a prior blessing from Allah. This leads to an infinite regress, demonstrating that any reward in paradise is purely by Allah's grace, not by right. This passage is a classic Maturidi refutation of the Mu'tazilite concept of obligation (wujub 'ala Allah).

Guided Path
1
Understand the Mu'tazilite claim
The Mu'tazilites held that human actions can earn reward from Allah by right. Recognize this as the backdrop of the argument.
This claim is what Imam Nasafi refutes.
2
Follow the reductio ad absurdum
Nasafi shows that if reward were based on merit, paradise would be sullied because all good deeds are gratitude for prior blessings.
The term 'sullied' (منغصة) indicates a decrease in perfection.
3
Grasp the infinite regress of gratitude
Every act of gratitude requires tawfiq (divine success) from Allah, and that tawfiq itself is a new blessing requiring further gratitude, ad infinitum.
Thus, no one can fully repay even a single blessing.
4
See the Quranic proof
The passage ends with the verse 'And if you count the blessing of Allah, you cannot enumerate them' (Ibrahim 34), confirming the impossibility of counting blessings.
This supports the argument that reward is by favor, not merit.
5
Conclude: reward is pure grace
Since no one can earn paradise by works, any reward is a gift from Allah's bounty and mercy.
This preserves the perfection of paradise and the sovereignty of Allah.

Mu'tazilite Proof for Eternal Punishment
Mu'tazilites cite Qur'anic verses to prove grave sinners are eternally punished.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Mu'tazila use Qur'anic verses to argue that grave sinners will be eternally punished, and how they defend their position against objections of false threats or specification.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila ground eternal punishment in the irrevocable threat (waʿīd) of the Qur'an.

The Mu'tazilites were known for their emphasis on divine justice and the reality of threats and promises. In this passage, they marshal several Qur'anic verses to prove that the grave sinner will be eternally punished in Hell. They also address two possible objections: that the threat might be false, or that the general commands might be specified. Understanding their reasoning is key to grasping their theological system.

Guided Path
1
Read the Qur'anic citations
Start by reading the three verses the Mu'tazila cite (Q. 4:93, 4:10, 32:18-20). Notice how each verse threatens eternal punishment or contrasts believers and sinners.
These verses are the foundation of their proof.
2
Analyze the classification
The Mu'tazila note that the verses classify people into believers and sinners (fasiq), implying that the grave sinner is not a believer and thus deserves eternal punishment.
This is a key step in their argument.
3
Understand the objections and responses
The passage then presents two objections: (1) the threat could be false (khalaf al-waʿīd), and (2) the general reports could be specified (takhṣīṣ). The Mu'tazila reject both because they would imply lying (kidhb) or abrogation (naskh), which are impossible for God.
These refutations are central to Mu'tazilite theology.
4
Note the internal disagreement on pardon
Within the Mu'tazila, there is a split: some permit God's pardon in wisdom, but still affirm the threat; others deny even the possibility of pardon, arguing it would contradict wisdom.
This shows internal diversity.
5
Consider the final note on minor sins
The passage ends by noting that minor sins are forgiven when major sins are avoided, citing Q. 4:31. This is consistent with their overall position.

As for the ruling of eternity (khulūd), due to His saying - exalted be He: 'And whoever kills a believer intentionally, his recompense is Hell, abiding therein forever' (Q. 4:93) and His saying: 'Indeed, those who consume the property of orphans unjustly...' (Q. 4:10). And He said - exalted be He: 'Is one who is a believer like one who is a sinner? They are not equal.' (Q. 32:18). So Allah - exalted be He - made sin (fisq) opposite to faith (īmān) and made the believer a category and the sinner a category, showing that the fasiq is other than the mu'min. Then He explained the ruling of each: 'As for those who believed and did righteous deeds, for them are the Gardens of Refuge, a hospitality for what they used to do. But as for those who sinned, their refuge is the Fire. Every time they wish to emerge from it, they are returned to it, and it is said to them: "Taste the punishment of the Fire which you used to deny."' (Q. 32:19-20). So in the verse there is evidence of the negation of the name īmān and the establishment of the name fisq, and evidence of the ruling of eternity. Then they say: The reports (akhbār) have come regarding the punishment of the intentional killer of a believer and other than that. There is no basis for claiming that the threat (waʿīd) is false (khalaf), because that would involve affirming lying (kidhb). Nor is there basis for specifying the generality of the reports (takhṣīṣ al-ʿumūm), because it is necessary to adhere to the general expression that is free from evidence of specification (qarīna al-khuṣūṣ). Since the general form without evidence of specification indicates the speaker's intent for generality, it becomes as if he mentioned every individual of the general class by its specific name. What is of this nature cannot be specified; rather, removing it from the ruling of generality would be abrogation (naskh). And there is no basis for claiming abrogation of the reports, because that would involve affirming lying. And a group among them permit pardon (ʿafw) for the grave sinner in terms of wisdom (ḥikma), but they say: the threat (al-waʿīd) is realized due to the reports in the Book (Qur'an) that punishes him. And a group among them says: It is not in wisdom to permit His pardon, because if His pardon were permissible, His punishment would not be permissible, since pardon then would be generosity (karam), and turning away from generosity is not permissible in wisdom. This from them is close to justifying the obligation of reconciliation (ṣulḥ), rather it is the same. As for minor sins, they are forgiven when avoiding the major sins, due to His saying - exalted be He: 'If you avoid the major sins which you are forbidden, We will remove from you your lesser sins' (Q. 4:31).

Commentary
This passage presents the Mu'tazilite proof for the eternal punishment (khulūd) of the grave sinner. They cite three Qur'anic verses: (1) Q. 4:93, which threatens the intentional murderer of a believer with eternal Hell; (2) Q. 4:10, about consuming orphans' property; (3) Q. 32:18-20, which contrasts believers and sinners and assigns each their permanent abode. The Mu'tazila interpret these verses to show that grave sinners are not believers and will be punished eternally. They then argue against two possible objections: (a) that the threat might be false (khalaf al-waʿīd), which they reject because it would imply God lies; (b) that the general reports might be specified (takhṣīṣ), which they reject because the general form without evidence of specification must be taken as universal, and to exclude is abrogation (naskh), which would also imply God lies. Finally, they present two internal Mu'tazilite positions on pardon: one group allows God's pardon in wisdom but insists the threat is real; another denies even the possibility of pardon because it would contradict wisdom. The passage ends with the note that minor sins are forgiven when major sins are avoided, citing Q. 4:31.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That the Mu'tazila believe God must punish the grave sinner (i.e., they deny divine mercy).
Correction: The Mu'tazila ground the necessity of punishment in God's truthfulness (waʿīd must be fulfilled), not in a lack of mercy. Some even allow for pardon, though they affirm the threat is real.

Key Takeaway
The Mu'tazila affirm eternal punishment for grave sinners based on the Qur'an's universal threats, rejecting any possibility of falsehood or specification.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
The Mu'tazila insist that God cannot go back on His word: every threat in the Qur'an must be executed without exception. Do you live as though every single forbidden act carries a guaranteed eternal consequence? If not, what does that say about your trust in God's truthfulness?
?
Some Mu'tazila argue that if God forgave a grave sinner after threatening punishment, He would be turning away from generosity (karam). When you sin, do you silently hope God will be 'generous' and ignore His own threats? What would it cost you to fully reckon with His seriousness?
?
This passage shows that even within a school that affirms strict punishment, there is disagreement over whether God could pardon. Are you more comfortable with a God who must punish or a God who might pardon? Which view makes you take your sins more seriously today?
