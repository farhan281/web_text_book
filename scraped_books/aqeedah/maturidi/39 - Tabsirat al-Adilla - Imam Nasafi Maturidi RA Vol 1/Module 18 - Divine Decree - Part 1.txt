The Creation of Human Acts: Jabriyya and the Middle Path
Nasafi introduces the central issue and states the Jabriyya and mainstream positions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the central issue of the creation of human acts and the distinction between the Jabriyya's denial of human action and the mainstream view that the human is an acquisitor and agent.

Core Idea
The servant is an acquisitor, not the ultimate doer — only God is the true actor.

Nasafi begins by anchoring the discussion of divine justice and human responsibility in the question of who creates human actions. He presents the Jabriyya as outright denying real human action, while the mainstream (including his own school) affirms that the human is an acquisitor and agent, but not the ultimate doer. This lesson lays the groundwork for understanding the Sunni kasb theory.

Human action debates hinge on who creates actions—God alone, humans alone, or both through acquisition.

Meaning and Types of Istita'a in Nasafi
Nasafi defines istita'a, divides it into two types, provides proofs, and refutes opposing views.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of istita'a, its two types according to Nasafi, and the refutations of deviant views.

Core Idea
Istita'a is two-fold: physical readiness and God-given power — neither mere selfhood nor mere tools.

This lesson introduces the concept of istita'a (capacity) as defined by Imam Nasafi. We will explore how he borrows from earlier Ash'ari definitions, then divides it into two essential categories. With Quranic verses and logical arguments, Nasafi refutes several erroneous views, clarifying that human capacity is a created accident, not a part of the self.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of istita'a
Nasafi defines istita'a as synonymous with qudra (power), quwwa (strength), and taqa (energy). This is the same definition used by Ash'ari and Baqillani.
2
Identify the two types
The first type is soundness of causes and health of instruments; the second is an accident created by God in the living being. Note how each type relates to action.
The first precedes action, the second is simultaneous.
3
Examine the Quranic proofs
For the first type, Nasafi cites 58:4; for the second, 11:20. Understand how each verse applies to the respective type.
In 11:20, the disbelievers had hearing organs but lacked true capacity.
4
Analyze the refutations
Nasafi refutes three groups: those who deny any capacity beyond the self (Nazzam, Uswari, Asamm), those who say capacity is only soundness of causes, and those who say capacity is part of the capable one (Dirar, Hafs al-Fard).
The last is impossible because capacity is an accident, not a part of a body.

Commentary
This passage discusses istita'a (capacity/power). It begins by defining it as many theologians like al-Ash'ari and al-Baqillani did: it is synonymous with qudra (power), quwwa (strength), and taqa (energy). Then it divides istita'a into two types: (1) soundness of causes and health of instruments (salama al-asbab wa sihha al-alat), which precede the action and are blessings from God; they are defined as readiness and preparedness to execute the act when the willer chooses. (2) A second type, difficult to define, which is an accident ('arad) that God creates in the living being enabling voluntary actions; this capacity is the cause of the action. The author then provides Quranic evidence: for the first type, he cites the verse on expiation where inability (yastati') refers to lack of physical means. For the second type, he cites the verse about the disbelievers not being able to hear; this negation refers to real power (qudra) not the soundness of causes, since they still had hearing organs. This lack is blameworthy because they wasted the power God gave them. Finally, the passage refutes several wrong views: (a) those who deny any capacity beyond the self, like al-Nazzam, al-Uswari, al-Asamm (they say man is capable by himself); (b) those who say capacity is only soundness of causes; (c) Dirar and Hafs al-Fard who say capacity is a part of the capable one—this is impossible because capacity is an accident and cannot be a part of a body.

Nasafi presents al-Qattan's and al-Ash'ari's views on God's will for evil.

The Identity of Divine Will and Creation of Actions
Nasafi identifies will with creation and presents Mu'tazila proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Nasafi equates the issue of divine will with the creation of human actions, and you will recognize the Mu'tazila's scriptural and rational objections to this view.

Core Idea
Divine will and creation of actions are one issue; Mu'tazila objections are based on analogy and scripture.

In this lesson, we see Imam al-Nasafi asserting a key Maturidi principle: the issue of divine will is essentially the same as the issue of God creating actions. He then presents the two main proofs that the Mu'tazila use to argue that God does not will evil or disbelief. Understanding these objections is crucial before encountering their refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text from 'ثم يقول' up to 'فكذا في الغائب'. Note how Nasafi declares the issues identical and then lists the Mu'tazila's two types of proofs.

Identify the linkage of issues
Observe that Nasafi says the proof for God creating all actions also applies to the issue of divine will. This shows that for Maturidis, will and creation are inseparable.
This is a fundamental principle in Maturidi theology.
3
Examine the scriptural objection
The Mu'tazila cite Q. 51:56, stating that God created jinn and humans only to worship Him. They argue that if God willed disbelief, He would be contradicting His stated purpose.
The Maturidi response is that the verse states the general purpose, not that God cannot will otherwise.

The Mu'tazila use analogy from the witnessed world: one who wills foolishness is foolish, and one who wills unbelief is an unbeliever. They apply this to God, concluding that if God willed disbelief, He would be an unbeliever—which is impossible, so He cannot will disbelief.
This is a key objection that Maturidis refute by denying the validity of such an analogy for God.

The opposing view, held by the author's school (Maturidis), the mutakallimun of Ahl al-Hadith, and the Najjariyya, is that the second capacity is concurrent with the act and cannot precede it. The proof given is that the originated capacity (al-qudra al-haditha) is an accident, and accidents cannot endure (baqa'). If it preceded the act, it would vanish by the time of the act, leaving the act without capacity, which is impossible because it would imply creation without divine power and human action without capacity. Thus, the capacity must be present simultaneously with the act.

Power as Cause: Symmetry Argument

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Power and act emerge equally from non-existence, so neither can cause the other.

The second argument targets the causal relationship between power and the act. Nasafi argues that if power were the cause of the act, then by the same logic the act could be the cause of power, leading to absurdity. This lesson explores the principle of 'qalb' (reversal).

2
Apply the principle of reversal (qalb)
If one is the cause of the other, then by symmetry the reverse is equally valid. That would allow attributing the existence of power to the act.
This leads to an unacceptable circular causality.

Conclude both from another source
Since neither can be the cause of the other, both must be caused by something else—namely, God.

The Vicious Circle of Ability and Belief

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If ability requires belief and belief requires ability, neither ever occurs.

The third argument is a classic reductio ad absurdum: if a person cannot believe until he has the ability to believe, and cannot have the ability until he believes, then he will never believe. Nasafi illustrates this with a powerful analogy of a man trapped in a well. This lesson examines the logical structure of circularity.

2
Analyze the well analogy
Like a man in a well who cannot get out without a rope and cannot get the rope without getting out, the cycle ensures he never gets out.
The analogy makes the impossibility intuitive.
3
Conclude the impossibility
Therefore, the Mu'tazilite position that ability must exist before the act (here, belief) leads to the absurd conclusion that belief is never attained.

If the disbeliever had no ability, he would be excused, contradicting divine justice.

Refuting the Impossibility of Action from the Incapable
Imam al-Nasafi refutes an objection about action being impossible from the incapable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Nasafi refutes the Mu'tazilite claim that action cannot occur from an incapable person, and why power must be simultaneous with the action.

Core Idea
Action is impossible from the incapable not due to incapacity, but due to absence of power.

This passage presents a key dialectical exchange in the debate on divine power and human action. The opponents argue that an incapable person cannot act because incapacity negates power. Imam al-Nasafi turns their own premises against them, showing that their denial of the survival of power leads to a contradiction. By examining this argument, we see the Maturidi insistence that power must be concurrent with the act.

Refuting the Mu'tazilite Proof from the Verse on Strength
Refutes Mu'tazilite claim that capacity precedes action using Quranic verse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Nasafi refutes the Mu'tazilite claim that capacity must precede action by reinterpreting the verse 'Take with strength' and arguing that capacity is simultaneous with the action.

Core Idea
The very verse the Mu'tazila cite proves capacity is simultaneous, not prior.

In this passage, Imam al-Nasafi takes on a key Mu'tazilite proof from the Quran. They claim the verse 'Take what We have given you with strength' shows that capacity (istiṭāʿa) exists before the action. Al-Nasafi turns the tables: the verse requires strength at the time of taking, and because strength is an accident that cannot endure, it cannot exist before the act. Let's see the argument unfold.
