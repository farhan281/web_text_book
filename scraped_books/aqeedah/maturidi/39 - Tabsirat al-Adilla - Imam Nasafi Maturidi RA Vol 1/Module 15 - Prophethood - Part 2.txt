Interpreting 'Muhdath' as Newly Conveyed
Nasafi provides alternative interpretations of 'dhikr muhdath' to refute the creationist claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the multiple interpretations of 'dhikr muhdath' in verse 21:2 that deflect the claim that the Qur'an is created.

Core Idea
The 'new mention' in the verse refers to a messenger or admonition, not the Qur'an's essence.

Another scriptural doubt comes from the verse 'No mention comes to them from their Lord that is newly-conveyed (muhdath).' Opponents take 'muhdath' as proof that the Qur'an itself is created. Nasafi offers three plausible interpretations that shift the meaning: 'dhikr' can refer to the Messenger himself, or to an admonition from him, or to something that is new relative to the people, not in itself. This lesson explores these interpretations and the analogy that supports them.

Guided Path
1
Read the second part of the passage
Read from 'والمراد من قوله تعالى' to the end of the Arabic passage, along with translation. Notice the three interpretations.
2
Identify the three interpretations
List the three possibilities the author gives for 'dhikr' and what 'muhdath' means in each case. Write them down.
Interpretation 1: dhikr = Messenger; Interpretation 2: dhikr = admonition; Interpretation 3: dhikr = something recent relative to them.
3
Understand the analogy
The author gives an example of a man who calls his newer servant 'my new servant' even if he is older than the first. This shows that 'new' can be relative.
4
Conclude the refutation
Since these interpretations are possible, the verse does not necessarily prove the Qur'an is created. The author states that because these applications are possible, the opponents have no proof.

As for the resolution of the scriptural doubt, we say: They have no support in His statement, the Exalted: {Indeed, We have made it an Arabic Qur'an} (43:3) because its meaning—and God knows best—is that We made the expression about it in the tongue of the Arabs and We conveyed the intended meaning and its rulings in the Arabic tongue. Then the linguists said: When "ja'ala" (made) is transitive to one object, it means action and creation, as in His statement: {Praise be to God, who created the heavens and the earth and made the darkness and the light} (6:1). And when it is transitive to two objects, it does not mean creation but rather means declaring (hukm) and naming (tasmiya). God said—informing about the disbelievers—: {And they made the angels, who are servants of the Most Merciful, females} (43:19). The meaning here is naming, not creation. Likewise in His statement: {Those who made the Qur'an into portions} (15:91), the meaning is naming, not creation. And in this verse, it is transitive to two objects.
And the intended meaning of His statement: {No mention comes to them from their Lord that is newly-conveyed} (21:2) is that "dhikr" (mention) could be the Messenger, peace be upon him, as He said: {He has mentioned a messenger} (65:10-11). So its interpretation is that no newly-created (muhdath) messenger comes to them except they listen to him, i.e., they hear his words. And it could mean that what comes to them is an admonition from the Prophet, peace be upon him—this because they did not laugh when reciting the Qur'an but rather revered and magnified its status. And it could be a mention of recent occurrence with respect to them, not that it is in itself new. Like a man who has a servant and buys another; he might say: "Call my new servant," even if he is older than the first. Since it is possible to apply these verses to these newly-created expressions (al-'ibarat al-muhdatha), there remains no proof for the opponents in the point of contention.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of a scriptural doubt (shubha sam'iyya) that the Qur'an is created (muhdath). The author begins by stating that the opponents have no support from the verse {Indeed, We have made it an Arabic Qur'an} because 'ja'ala' here means 'made the expression about it in Arabic', not 'created'. He then cites linguists: when 'ja'ala' takes one object, it means creation; when it takes two objects, it means declaring or naming. Applying this, the verse {And they made the angels females} means they named them females, not created them. Likewise, {Those who made the Qur'an into portions} means they divided it by naming parts. The verse under dispute also has two objects, so it means 'We made it an Arabic Qur'an' i.e., We named it or decreed it as such, not created it. Next, the author addresses the verse {No mention (dhikr) comes to them from their Lord that is newly-conveyed (muhdath)}. He gives three interpretations: (1) 'dhikr' refers to the Messenger, so 'muhdath' means a newly-sent messenger; (2) it refers to renewed admonition from the Prophet; (3) it means a mention of recent occurrence relative to them, not that the Qur'an itself is new. He provides an analogy: a man may call a newly-purchased servant 'my new servant' even if he is older. Since these interpretations are plausible, the opponents have no proof for their claim that the Qur'an is created. This refutation relies on linguistic analysis and contextual reasoning.

Musa's Sufficiency of Signs
Nasafi argues Musa had enough signs, so request was not for a sign

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth argument that Prophet Musa's request was not for a sign, as he already possessed an abundance of miracles.

Core Idea
Abundant signs make further requests unreasonable for any prophet.

In this passage, Nasafi presents his fourth argument against ru'yat Allah based on the principle that the Prophet Musa already had sufficient sensory signs. The logic is that even the most foolish person would not need more evidence after witnessing such clear miracles, so requesting a further sign would be an act of obstinacy—and prophets are protected from that. This sets the stage for interpreting Musa's request as something other than a plea for a sensory proof.

Identify the logical structure
Break down the argument: Premise 1: Musa had many clear signs. Premise 2: These sufficed even the dullest minds. Conclusion: Requesting another sign would be obstinacy, impossible for a chosen prophet.
This is a deductive argument with an implied major premise.
3
Examine the implication
Reflect on why Nasafi uses the example of Musa to argue against the vision of Allah. If Musa's request was not for a mere sign, it must have been for something else—vision of Allah.
This connects to the broader debate about seeing Allah.
4
Consider the objection
Anticipate a possible objection: Couldn't Musa have wanted a new sign? Nasafi's answer is that prophets do not engage in senseless demands; thus the request was for a higher matter.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Prophets refute disbelief instantly; Musa did not refute the vision request, so it is not disbelief.

Nasafi now presents a second argument based on prophetic behavior. If requesting to see Allah were disbelief, then Musa—as a prophet—would have immediately corrected his people, just as he did when they asked for a physical idol. Since he did not reject the vision request but rather asked Allah on their behalf, the request cannot be inherently disbelieving. Let's follow the logic step by step.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the counterfactual premise

When the same people asked to see Allah, Musa did not rebuke them. Instead he asked Allah for the vision. This contrast proves the vision request is not kufr.
If it were kufr, he would have acted as he did with the idol request.

Consider the objection and answer
One might object: maybe Musa did rebuke them but they would not listen, so he asked Allah. The passage begins to address this but is cut off; nevertheless, the core argument stands.

The Ruling on Attributing Sins to Prophets
Nasafi states that attributing sin to a prophet is disbelief.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Attributing sin to a prophet is disbelief because it implies presumption against Allah.

This lesson addresses a serious theological ruling: the status of those who attribute sins to prophets. The passage from Nasafi clarifies that such an attribution stems from presumption (tajassur) against Allah and questioning what is not permissible for Him. It declares without ambiguity that anyone who does this is a disbeliever.

Reflect on why attributing sin to a prophet is equivalent to presumption against Allah. Since prophets are protected from sin (isma), accusing them implies that Allah failed to protect them or that He allowed evil in His chosen messengers—both are impermissible regarding Allah.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Denying miracles leads to the impossible conclusion that God acts foolishly.

Al-Nasafi presents the naturalist argument that miracles cannot serve as proof for prophethood because they are either natural and imitable or supernatural and impossible. He then refutes this by showing the absurd consequence: if a prophet cannot provide proof, God's sending would be futile, which is impossible for God. This lesson unpacks that logical rebuttal.

Nasafi argues that prophethood is logically possible for the owner of all things to send a messenger.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Nasafi argues that prophethood is logically possible given God's absolute ownership of all creation.

Core Idea
Prophethood is possible because the Owner can communicate with His property.

Nasafi begins by narrowing the discussion to those who already accept God as the sole Creator and Owner. He then argues that since God owns everything, He has the full right to impose obligations and send messengers to His servants. This is not rationally impossible; rather, it follows from His sovereignty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent and the precondition
Notice that Nasafi specifies that the discussion only applies to those who affirm God's oneness, ownership, and lordship. If someone denies these, the topic of prophethood cannot be discussed until those fundamentals are proven.
This frames the argument within a shared theological ground.
2
Grasp the key premise: ownership entails disposal rights
Nasafi states that every owner has authority to dispose of their property as they see fit. This is a basic rational principle: ownership implies the right to act upon what is owned.
This premise is used analogically for God's ownership.
3
Apply the premise to God's absolute ownership
God has complete ownership (mulk al-takhlīq) over every part of the world and every human being, as He creates them from nothing. Therefore, He has the right to impose obligations (taklīf) of any kind—command, prohibition, permission, restriction—and to inform people of these obligations by any means, including sending a messenger.
God's ownership is total and direct, so His disposal rights are unrestricted.
4
Conclude that prophethood is not impossible
Since God is the disposer in His own dominion, sending a messenger is not inherently impossible. If it were impossible, that would be due to an external factor, but no such factor exists. Thus, prophethood is rationally possible.
The burden of proof lies on those who claim impossibility.

Commentary
This passage is a proof of the possibility (imkān) of prophethood. Nasafi first specifies the opponents: those who affirm the existence of a single, all-powerful Creator. If someone denies the world's temporality, the Creator, or His oneness, they are excluded from this discussion, as those issues must be settled first. Given that the opponents accept God as the absolute Owner (al-mālik) and everything else as owned (mamlūk), Nasafi argues that the owner has authority to dispose of his property as he sees fit. God's ownership is total: He creates every part of the world and every human being ex nihilo. Therefore, He has the right to impose obligations (taklīf) of any kind—command, prohibition, permission, restriction—on His servants. He can inform them of these obligations by any means He chooses: either by creating direct knowledge in them or by sending a messenger (rasūl) who receives revelation and conveys it. The messenger may be of the same species as the recipients or different; neither is impossible. The impossibility would only arise from some external factor (maʿnā), but since God is the sovereign disposer, no such factor exists. Thus, prophethood is rationally possible.

Commentary
This passage is a rational proof (burhan 'aqli) for the necessity of divine law (shar'). The author, al-Nasafi, presents the third argument in a series. The argument begins by noting that if creation were only for annihilation without a positive outcome, it would be foolish (safah) and contrary to wisdom (hikma). Therefore, divine guidance through a prophet is necessary to give meaning to creation. The author then launches into the third proof: humans naturally love survival and seek means to preserve themselves. If God did not prescribe a law defining rights and obligations, people would scramble selfishly for resources, leading to conflict, fighting, mutual destruction, and ultimately extinction of the human race. This outcome would be absurd (safah) and unworthy of a wise Creator. Next, al-Nasafi extends the argument to material goods and property. People naturally desire beautiful possessions, and without a system of ownership (milk) based on legitimate causes, there would be chaos, theft, and conflict. Law is needed to assign ownership rights and prevent anarchy. The author then concludes that denying the necessity of law amounts to promoting corruption and attributing foolishness to God. Finally, al-Nasafi appeals to common sense: no rational person would allow himself to be driven by base desires and neglect the intellect, which is a divine gift for discerning truth and acquiring knowledge. Thus, the need for law is rationally undeniable.

Commentary
This passage is a rational proof (burhan 'aqli) for the necessity of divine law (shar'). The author, al-Nasafi, presents the third argument in a series. The argument begins by noting that if creation were only for annihilation without a positive outcome, it would be foolish (safah) and contrary to wisdom (hikma). Therefore, divine guidance through a prophet is necessary to give meaning to creation. The author then launches into the third proof: humans naturally love survival and seek means to preserve themselves. If God did not prescribe a law defining rights and obligations, people would scramble selfishly for resources, leading to conflict, fighting, mutual destruction, and ultimately extinction of the human race. This outcome would be absurd (safah) and unworthy of a wise Creator. Next, al-Nasafi extends the argument to material goods and property. People naturally desire beautiful possessions, and without a system of ownership (milk) based on legitimate causes, there would be chaos, theft, and conflict. Law is needed to assign ownership rights and prevent anarchy. The author then concludes that denying the necessity of law amounts to promoting corruption and attributing foolishness to God. Finally, al-Nasafi appeals to common sense: no rational person would allow himself to be driven by base desires and neglect the intellect, which is a divine gift for discerning truth and acquiring knowledge. Thus, the need for law is rationally undeniable.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Reason knows the map, but prophets point out the exact locations.

Nasafi presents a compelling argument for the necessity of prophets that is grounded in the nature of human reason itself. A) Followers of prophets are more intelligent.
B) Philosophers lack the material of revelation.
C) Followers of prophets study longer.
D) Philosophers are not sincere.

Key Takeaway
Revelation provides a sure guide to truth that even the most brilliant intellects cannot guarantee on their own.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Are you, like the philosophers, relying on your own intellect and knowledge to find truth, while neglecting the revealed guidance that even the simple can follow? What specific step will you take today to ensure revelation guides your mind?
?
The followers of prophets are described as dull and busy with livelihood, yet they cling to truth. Do you consider yourself too busy or too intelligent to need constant recourse to revelation? How will you change that?
?
The philosophers devoted years to divine studies yet fell into misguidance. Are you confident that your own study of religion is grounded in revelation rather than your own reasoning? What evidence in your life shows that you submit to revelation over your intellect?
