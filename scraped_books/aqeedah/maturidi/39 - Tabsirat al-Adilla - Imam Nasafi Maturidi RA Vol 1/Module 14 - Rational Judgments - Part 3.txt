Sight Perceives Accidents Directly Unlike Touch
Nasafi contrasts sight and touch to prove accidents are visible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Nasafi argues that accidents like colors and motions are directly visible through sight, in contrast to touch which only infers qualities through the organ's own change.

Core Idea
Sight knows accidents directly; touch only infers them.

The question of whether we can truly see accidents such as colors and motions was contested. Nasafi addresses skeptics by comparing sight and touch. He shows that sight perceives external qualities without affecting the eye, while touch must experience qualities in itself first. This comparison defends the reality of accidents as perceived objects.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the full Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Let the flow of Nasafi's argument sink in without analysis.
This passage is dense; read slowly.
2
Identify the core contrast
Notice how Nasafi contrasts sight and touch: sight knows bodies, colors, and motions without any change in the eye, while touch only knows existence directly and must infer qualities through changes in the hand.
Focus on the sentence: 'This meaning is absent from sight...'
3
Examine the touch examples
Nasafi uses heat, cold, roughness, smoothness, hardness, and softness. He argues that these qualities arise in the hand itself, so we know them by experiencing them in ourselves, then infer them in the object.
Touch is indirect; sight is direct.
4
Understand the final dismissal
Nasafi addresses the deniers of accidents: even they accept sounds as audible. The real dispute is whether sounds and colors are distinct from the essence, not whether they are sensed.
This shows the argument is about ontology, not perception.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of those who deny the visibility of accidents (like color, motion, rest). It begins by stating that no reasonable person can deny that things are seen; one who does contradicts his own perception. The author then explains how sight and touch differ. Sight perceives bodies, colors, and motion without anything entering the eye, so these must be visible. Touch, by contrast, only knows the existence of the touched object directly; to know qualities like heat or roughness, those qualities must arise in the hand itself. For example, heat makes the hand hot, roughness affects its movement. Thus touch knows qualities by experiencing them in itself, then infers them in the object. Sight does not—it perceives qualities directly in the external object without any change in the eye. If these qualities were not visible, we could not distinguish them. The author then addresses an objection: what about those who deny accidents altogether? He counters by using sounds, which even the deniers admit are audible; they only dispute whether sound is a distinct accident or just the essence. Similarly, colors and modes are sensed; the dispute is not about their existence but about their ontological status—whether they are distinct from the essence. So the argument stands.

Refuting the Claim that Motion is Invisible
Nasafi refutes Abu Hashim's objection that ship motion is unseen

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Nasafi uses analogy to defend the visibility of motion against Abu Hashim's objection based on ship motion.

Core Idea
Perception is conditional; absence of perception does not prove invisibility.

Abu Hashim raised a clever objection: if motion were visible, why doesn't a ship passenger see the ship's own motion? Nasafi counters by showing that similar logic would deny the visibility of bodies and colors. This passage sharpens our understanding of how visibility depends on the observer's state, not just the object's nature.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the structure: first the objection, then Nasafi's reply.
The objection is from Abu Hashim; it's a well-known dilemma in kalam.
2
Understand the mirage analogy
Nasafi compares the objection to sophists denying body visibility because a mirage is seen from afar but not up close. Ask: How does this analogy refute the objection?
The key is that failure to perceive in one situation does not negate visibility in general.
3
Understand the color-spinning analogy
Next, Nasafi gives examples of colored objects in motion (arrow, spindle) where multiple colors appear as one. How does this support his argument?
This shows that perception conditions can mask what is actually there.
4
Reflect on the principle
Synthesize: The objection assumes that if motion were visible it would always be perceived. Nasafi shows that perception is relative to the observer's state (e.g., being on the ship).
This lesson reminds us that visibility is not an all-or-nothing property.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection from Abu Hashim: if motion were visible, a passenger on a moving ship would see the ship's own motion, but he does not; therefore motion is not visible. The author replies by drawing an analogy. First, he compares it to sophists who deny bodies are visible because a mirage is seen from afar but not up close — yet no one denies bodies are visible. Second, he uses examples from color: an arrow painted with multiple colors, when shot, appears as one color; similarly a spindle whorl with multiple colors when spun seems a single color, even though the multiple colors exist. So the fact that something is not perceived under certain conditions does not prove it is inherently imperceptible. The motion of the ship is analogous: the passenger does not perceive it due to his own motion relative to the ship (implied), not because motion is invisible. The text cuts off, but the argument is that just as colors are visible despite not being seen in motion, so is motion visible despite not being seen in this case.

Accidents Are Potentially Visible
Imam al-Nasafi argues that tastes, smells, and other accidents are visible in principle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that accidents like tastes and smells are potentially visible, and their non-vision is due to God's custom, not inherent impossibility.

Core Idea
Accidents are visible in essence; God's custom prevents their perception.

Imam al-Nasafi builds on the previous discussion about vision to address a critical question: Are accidents like tastes and smells visible? Many might assume they cannot be seen at all. But al-Nasafi argues they are potentially visible, and the reason we do not see them is not because of any intrinsic impossibility, but because God has not created the vision of them in us. This lesson unpacks his reasoning and the refutation of counterarguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the translation. Notice the author's confident tone and how he builds his argument.
Pay attention to the key terms: الأعراض, الوجود, الرؤية, المقابلة, اتصال الشعاع.
2
Identify the main thesis
Al-Nasafi states clearly that tastes, smells, and other accidents are 'visible' (مرئية) due to existence. Why does he say existence is the cause?
He distinguishes between the cause of visibility (existence) and the cause of non-vision (God's custom).
3
Understand the verification argument
The author then argues that conditions like facing (muqābala) or ray-connection (ittisāl al-shu‘ā‘) are impossible for accidents. Since these conditions are not required for vision of accidents, their absence cannot be the reason we don't see them.
This refutes any objection that such conditions are necessary.
4
Reflect on the objection and response
The passage ends with a powerful rejoinder: if the opponent's reasoning were followed, it would lead to absurd consequences like not seeing elephants or camels due to screens or distance.
Al-Nasafi shows the opponent's logic is self-defeating.
5
Summarize the lesson
Write a one-sentence summary of the key takeaway: Accidents are in principle visible; non-vision is by God's choice, not impossibility.

The Vision of Homogeneous Things: Answering the Elephant Objection
Imam al-Nasafi answers an objection about elephants in presence but not seen.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational basis for why we do not see homogeneous objects even when they are present, using the categories of opposites, different, and homogeneous things.

Core Idea
Vision follows homogeneity: if an elephant were present, you would see it because it is homogeneous with other dense bodies.

This passage addresses a clever objection to the theological principle that perception is not a condition for existence. If you claim that something can exist without being perceived, why not accept an elephant dancing before you, unseen? Imam al-Nasafi responds by analyzing the nature of vision and the classification of objects into opposites, different, and homogeneous. The key insight is that homogeneous objects require equal possibility of vision.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and general response
Read the opening lines of the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how the author first presents the objection and then states that it is a general issue not unique to them.
2
Identify the three categories
The answer uses three categories: opposites (al-mutadādayn), different (al-mukhtalifayn), and homogeneous (al-mutajānisīn). Understand the rule for each.
Opposites require separate parts of the eye; different have distinct visions; homogeneous have the same vision.
3
Apply to the elephant example
Dense bodies like elephants are homogeneous with other dense bodies. Therefore, if an elephant were present, we would necessarily see it due to homogeneity. Accidents like tastes are not homogeneous, so they may exist unseen.
This explains why the objection fails.

The Dialectical Proof of Divine Command
Al-Nasafi uses a dialectical question to prove that God's commands are not foolish.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Nasafi uses a dialectical argument to prove that God's commands and prohibitions are not foolish and are rationally necessary.

Core Idea
A dialectical question forces the opponent to admit the necessity of divine command.

In this passage, Al-Nasafi presents a powerful dialectical argument that refutes anyone who claims that God's commands are unnecessary or foolish. By asking a series of logical questions, he shows that every possible answer leads to a contradiction or admission of divine authority. This method is a hallmark of Islamic theological debate.

Guided Path
1
Read the dialectical question
Begin by reading the opening question carefully: Is commanding without benefit or forbidding without harm considered foolish in the witnessed world?
This question sets up the entire argument.
2
Follow the first branch
If the opponent says 'no', note how Al-Nasafi argues that this invalidates his own position because the same standard applied to the unseen would then not be foolish, contradicting the opponent's claim.
3
Follow the second branch
If the opponent says 'yes', then follow the subsequent questions about whether the foolish act is good or ugly, and then whether it is permissible, prohibited, or obligatory.
Each response leads to a logical dead end or implicit admission of taklif.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Notice that in every scenario, the opponent is forced either to contradict himself or to acknowledge that God's commands are necessary (taklif).
This demonstrates the rational necessity of divine command.

Then we say: Is commanding something that brings no benefit to the commander by its doing, and forbidding something that brings no harm to the prohibitor by its doing, foolish (sufh) in the witnessed realm or not? If he says: No, he invalidates his own statement because making that in the unseen realm is foolish, due to the indication that the like of it is foolish in the witnessed realm. And if he says: Yes, it is foolish. It is said: Is it good (hasan) or ugly (qabih)? If he says: It is good—the rational people know the extent of his intellect since he claims that foolishness is good. And if he says: It is ugly. It is said: Is it permissible (mubah), prohibited (mahzur), or obligatory (wajib)? If he says by obligation or permission, then it is said: Who made it obligatory upon him or permitted it to him? If he says: It is God—exalted is He—then he has uttered a grave statement, since he claims that God permits the ugly or obligates it, despite its falsehood; moreover, he abandons his own school because obligation or permission is legislation (shar') and obligation (taklif). And if he says: Someone other than God made it obligatory upon him or permitted it to him, then he has raved, since he gives to other than God the authority of taklif and does not give that to God. And if he says: It is prohibited and not obligatory nor permissible, then he has also acknowledged taklif, because prohibition can only be established by a prohibition (nahy). And this necessitates the invalidation of his statement. And there is no power except through God.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical argument (munazara) that demonstrates the necessity of divine command and prohibition. The author poses a question: In the witnessed world (al-shahid), is commanding something that brings no benefit to the commander and forbidding something that brings no harm to the prohibitor considered foolish or not? If the opponent says it is not foolish, then he contradicts himself because if the like of this is foolish in the witnessed world, then applying it to God (al-gha'ib) would be foolish. If the opponent says it is foolish, then the author asks: Is that foolishness good or ugly? If the opponent says it is good, then he shows his ignorance. If he says it is ugly, then the author asks: Is this ugly action permissible, prohibited, or obligatory? If the opponent says it is permissible or obligatory, then the author asks who made it so. If the opponent says God, then he attributes to God permitting or obligating what is ugly, which is false and contradicts his own school because obligation and permission are acts of legislation and taklif. If the opponent says someone other than God, then he is giving the authority of taklif to other than God, which is absurd. If the opponent says it is prohibited, then he has admitted that there is a prohibition (nahy), which implies the existence of a prohibitor, i.e., God, thus affirming taklif. In all cases, the opponent's position is refuted.

Power Attaches Only to the Non-Existent
First rational proof: power cannot attach to the existent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite argument that ability must precede the action because it can only bring about what does not yet exist.

Core Idea
Ability cannot bring into existence what already exists.

The first Mu'tazilite proof argues that the power of bringing into existence (qudrat al-ījād) cannot attach to something already existing, for that would be impossible. Therefore, it must attach to the non-existent and thus be prior to the action. This logical premise underlies their entire position on the precedence of ability.

1
Read the Arabic passage

The Arabic uses 'يستحيل' (impossible) and 'محال' (impossible).
2
Identify the key premise
The premise is that power over an existent is impossible. Ask yourself: why do the Mu'tazilites think this?
They argue that bringing into existence what already exists is a contradiction.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
Since power must attach to the non-existent to bring it forth, it cannot be simultaneous with the action (which would make it attach to the existent act). Therefore, ability must be prior.
This argument is foundational for their view.

The Circularity Objection
Second rational proof: concurrent ability leads to circularity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite argument that if ability were concurrent with action, a vicious circularity or infinite regress would result.

Core Idea
If power and act are simultaneous, neither can be the cause of the other.

The second proof attacks the Ash'arite position of concurrent ability. If power and action exist together, neither has priority, so attributing the act's existence to power is arbitrary; they would need a third cause, leading to regress. This argument aims to show the logical absurdity of simultaneous ability.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the second proof carefully. Notice the phrase 'خروجها من العدم إلى الوجود مطابقا' – both power and act emerge from non-existence similarly.
2
Understand the equality claim
The Mu'tazilites claim that if power is concurrent, its emergence is similar to the act's emergence. Since both are equally non-existent before the act, neither has a causal edge.
Thus, saying power causes the act is arbitrary.
3
Consider the consequence
If no causal relation can be established, then both power and act must be caused by something else – leading to infinite regress. This shows concurrent ability is incoherent.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.

This passage recounts a concise response by Abu Mansur al-Maturidi to an objection from al-Ka'bi. The Mu'tazila's Analogy from the Witnessed to the Unseen
Al-Nasafi presents the Mu'tazila's rational analogies from human will to divine will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Mu'tazila use the analogy from human will to argue that God cannot will evil, and why this analogy is contested.

Core Idea
Willing evil makes the willer evil — or so the Mu'tazila claim by analogy.

The Mu'tazila base their rational objections on a method called al-shahid wa al-gha'ib: drawing conclusions about the unseen from the witnessed. In this passage, Al-Nasafi presents their first set of analogies: that if a human wills foolishness, he is foolish; if he wills disbelief, he is a disbeliever. Applying this to God, they argue God cannot will evil. Let's examine their reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the method of shahid/gha'ib
Read the opening sentence: 'اعتبارًا بالشاهد' and 'إذ الشاهد دليل الغائب'. This is their principle: what holds in human experience must hold for God.
This is a key epistemological move.
2
List the analogies given
Notice the specific cases: willing foolishness, disbelief, disobedience, and self-reviling. Each is claimed to make the willer share that quality.
3
Reflect on the implication
If these analogies were true, then God willing disbelief would make God a disbeliever, which is impossible. Hence, they conclude God does not will disbelief.
This leads to the doctrine that God only wills good.
