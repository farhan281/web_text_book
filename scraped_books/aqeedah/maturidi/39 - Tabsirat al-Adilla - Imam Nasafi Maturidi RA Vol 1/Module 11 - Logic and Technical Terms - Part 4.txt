Imam Nasafi defines and refutes Abu Hashim's distinction between 'علة' and 'سبب'.

Imam Nasafi begins by reporting Abu Hashim's definitions of 'علة' (cause) and 'سبب' (reason). He then subjects these definitions to a rigorous critique, pointing out their logical inconsistencies and their failure to align with the Ash'ari view of causality. This lesson unpacks both the definitions and the refutation.

Commentary
This passage defines and refutes the distinction between 'علة' and 'سبب' as held by Abu Hashim. Refuting Abu Hashim on Punishment Without Action
Imam Nasafi refutes Abu Hashim's claim that one can be punished for not doing a commanded act.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Abu Hashim's view that punishment can occur without a positive act is logically and doctrinally problematic.

Core Idea
Punishment without action contradicts both reason and consensus.

Abu Hashim's novel claim that a person can be deserving of punishment without performing any act raises fundamental questions about the nature of moral responsibility. In this passage, Imam Nasafi presents a penetrating objection that exposes the incoherence of this position, showing that even the very concept of 'abandonment' is itself an act. Let's follow the argument closely.

Guided Path
1
Read Abu Hashim's position
Start by reading the passage and noting how Abu Hashim defines the state of the capable person who does nothing. He considers that person a sinner deserving punishment simply for not doing the commanded act.
Notice that he says 'not for an act'.
2
Follow the objection's logic
The objector challenges: if you don't do the commanded act, you have abandoned it, and abandonment is itself a forbidden act. So punishment is for that act, not for nothing. But if you neither do nor abandon, you're neutral.
The objector uses the Arabic terms 'ترك' (abandonment) as an act.
3
Recognize the absurd consequence
The objector points out that Abu Hashim's view implies eternal punishment for no act at all, which contradicts the consensus of the community and even the position of Abu Hashim's own predecessors.
This shows the inconsistency in Abu Hashim's school.

The Double Punishment Objection
Imam Nasafi presents the double punishment objection for zina.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Nasafi uses the example of zina to expose an absurd consequence in Abu Hashim's rational theory of good and evil.

Core Idea
Prohibiting an act implies commanding its opposite, leading to double punishment.

Imam Nasafi continues his refutation of Abu Hashim's theory that good acts are good only due to an additional attribute. He now points out that if an act is prohibited, its omission is implicitly commanded. This leads to the contradiction that a person should be punished both for committing the act and for failing to perform the 'good' of omitting it. The example of zina makes this absurdity concrete.

Guided Path
1
Recall the principle
Remember that Abu Hashim claims acts are good only due to an additional attribute. This objection builds on that.
2
Read the objection
Read the Arabic passage: prohibition of zina implies command to leave it, so commit zina leads to double punishment.
Notice the logical flow: prohibition → command to omit → punishment for both commission and omission.
3
Identify the absurdity
According to Abu Hashim, leaving zina would be a good act, but it is not commanded separately, so punishment for omission is unjust.
This shows the flaw in Abu Hashim's definition of good as a separate attribute.
4
Reflect on the implication
This objection challenges the coherence of Abu Hashim's entire framework by showing it leads to contradictory punishments.

Why 'Shay'' is Not a Common Noun for God and Creation
Imam Nasafi proves that the word 'shay'' is not a common noun encompassing God and creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the word 'shay'' (thing) is not a common noun (ism jins) that encompasses both God and creation, and how this argument safeguards divine transcendence.

Core Idea
'Thing' is not a genus: the gap between Creator and creation is absolute.

Imam Nasafi tackles a subtle but critical point in Islamic theology: can we use the word 'thing' (shay') as a common term for both God and the world? At first glance, both are 'things' since both exist. However, Nasafi argues that this would imply a shared genus, which undermines God's utterly unique nature. In this lesson, we will follow his proof that 'shay'' is actually a homonym, not a genus term, and why claiming otherwise leads to disbelief.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of those who claim that absolute promises (wa'd mutlaq) must apply in all circumstances, and a defense of the Maturidi position on the interpretation of general words (umum) and the possibility of specification (takhsis). The author begins by referring to the story of Adam in Paradise: Allah promised Adam that he would not be uncovered, but after eating from the tree he became uncovered. The latter is the correct view, and the former leads to absurdity. The author then raises a similar argument from the Quranic verse "Allah forgives all sins" — if taken absolutely, it would mean every sin is forgiven, which contradicts consensus; if not, then Allah would be lying unless the verse is restricted. This shows that they do not treat the apparent meaning as definitive when there is evidence of specification. The author concludes that since Allah is not accused of deception, His evidence of specification should be accepted as indicating that He intended the specific meaning from the time of revelation.

Commentary

Al-Maturidi on the Excuse of Ta'wil
Maturidi explains that ta'wil provides a greater excuse than simple ignorance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Imam al-Maturidi considers ta'wil a stronger excuse than simple ignorance for the one who sincerely interprets.

Core Idea
Ta'wil makes the practitioner feel commanded, removing the sense of omission.

In this passage, Imam al-Maturidi explains the unique nature of the excuse for those who engage in ta'wil (interpretation). He argues that when a person sincerely believes his interpretation is correct, it becomes like an act commanded by Allah in terms of his intention, providing an even stronger excuse than simple ignorance. This principle was used to justify the actions of the Companions in disputes.

ولهذا قال شيخنا أبو منصور الماتريدي رحمه الله - الأصل أن التأويل لأهله يجعله لما يرى عنده أنه حق كالمأمور به فهو أعظم من العذر، وأبلغ في معنى الجهالة التي تسقط الكلفة من الجهالة بأصل الخلقة والنشوء أو بالخطأ الذي اعتراه والسهو، لأن هذا النوع من الجهل عند نفسه علمه طلب الكشف والاستعانة بمن يثق به والاجتهاد في التيقظ والتحفظ، وليس مع التأويل في علمه ورأيه أنه قد بقي عليه حق لم يف به أو كان منه ما منعه عن حصول المقصود، فصار هذا لذلك أبلغ في العذر له وأعظم في معنى الجهالة لما معه بعض معاني الناسي عما يكون عليه من الحق في غيره، والله أعلم.
And for this reason our teacher Abu Mansur al-Maturidi (may Allah have mercy on him) said: The principle is that ta'wil (interpretation) for its practitioner makes what he thinks to be true like something commanded. So it is greater than an excuse, and more profound in the meaning of ignorance that drops responsibility than ignorance of the origin of creation and constitution, or the mistake that befell him and forgetfulness. Because this type of ignorance – in his own view – is that he knows the need for investigation and seeking help from those he trusts and striving in vigilance and caution. And with ta'wil, in his knowledge and opinion, it is not the case that there remains upon him a right he did not fulfill, or that something prevented him from attaining the goal. So this is therefore more profound as an excuse for him and greater in the meaning of ignorance because it carries some of the meaning of the one who forgets about what he should have done in other matters. And Allah knows best.

Commentary
This passage explains the profound nature of the excuse for those who engage in ta'wil (interpretation). Imam Maturidi argues that when a person makes a ta'wil and believes his action is correct, it becomes like an action commanded by Allah in terms of his intention. This provides a stronger excuse than simple ignorance (e.g., ignorance of the origin of creation) or a mistake accompanied by forgetfulness. Why? Because the person with ta'wil believes he is fulfilling a duty, so he has no sense of omission. This type of ignorance is coupled with a desire to seek truth, making the excuse even stronger. It ultimately excuses the Companions who fought because they interpreted the situation as requiring war, and they thought they were doing right.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some may think that committing a wrong interpretation removes all blame entirely.
Correction: Maturidi does not say that ta'wil removes all blame; rather, it provides a strong excuse, but the action may still be incorrect. The practitioner is excused for his sincere effort, not declared blameless.

Checkpoint
According to Maturidi, what makes ta'wil a stronger excuse than simple ignorance?
A) It involves a known truth.

Key Takeaway
Maturidi teaches that ta'wil, when sincerely held, provides a stronger excuse than ignorance because the practitioner believes he is commanded and feels no omission.
