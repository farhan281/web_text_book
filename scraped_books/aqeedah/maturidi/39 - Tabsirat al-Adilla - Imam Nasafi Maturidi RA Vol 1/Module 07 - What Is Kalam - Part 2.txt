Introduction and Definition of the World
Nasafi outlines preliminaries and defines the world according to the mutakallimun.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the technical definition of the world (al-ʿālam) in kalam and its role as a sign pointing to the Creator.

Core Idea
The world is everything other than Allah, and it is a sign (ʿalam) pointing to its Maker.

Before proving that the world is temporally originated, Nasafi lays the ground by defining what 'the world' actually means in kalam discourse. The mutakallimūn do not use the term in its everyday sense; they have a precise technical definition that excludes Allah and includes all other existents. This definition is crucial because it sets the scope of the proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get a sense of its flow, then read the translation. Notice that Nasafi begins by stating his overall purpose before giving the definition.
2
Identify the purpose and preliminaries
Nasafi states that the purpose is to demonstrate the temporality of the world. He then lists three necessary preliminaries: knowing what the name 'world' applies to, its divisions, and the meanings of temporality and pre-eternity.
These preliminaries show the systematic approach of kalam.
3
Analyze the definition of the world
The definition states: 'the world is all existents other than Allah Most High, from substances and accidents.' Note that it includes both substances and accidents, and explicitly excludes Allah.
Substances (aʿyān) are self-subsisting entities; accidents (aʿrāḍ) are qualities that inhere in substances.
4
Reflect on the etymology
The world is called ʿālam because it is a sign (ʿalam) pointing to its Maker, who is described with attributes of perfection and is absolutely unlike creation.
This etymological point reinforces the teleological argument for God's existence.

Commentary
This passage is an introduction and definition. It explains that after establishing epistemology (that realities are known and which sources of knowledge are valid), the next task is to prove that the world is temporally originated (ḥudūth). To do this, we must first define the world, know its divisions and the meaning of temporality and pre-eternity. The definition given by the mutakallimūn is that the world is everything other than Allah, comprising substances (aʿyān, entities that subsist by themselves) and accidents (aʿrāḍ, qualities that inhere). The world is called ʿālam because it is a sign (ʿalam) pointing to its Maker, who is described with attributes of perfection and is utterly unlike His creation.

Core Idea
Jawhar is defined as self-subsistent and receptive of opposites — but this definition creates a problem for al-Ash'ari.

This passage from Tabṣirat al-Adilla lays out the foundational division of entities into substances and bodies, then defines 'jawhar' carefully. The author then examines al-Ash'ari's objection to this definition, revealing tensions in Ash'ari's own methodology. By working through this discussion, you will see how kalam definitions are constructed and critiqued.

[Definition of al-jawhar]: As for al-jawhar, in language it denotes the origin (al-aṣl); it is said: 'So-and-so has a noble jawhar (i.e., a noble origin),' and 'This is a jawharī garment (i.e., good in origin).' So this one does not mind, after the purpose is achieved, that it is composed of two or more descriptions. Then he, along with his principle, mentioned the composed definition. And if he had sufficed with his saying: 'receptive of opposites,' it would have been sufficient, and nothing of his principles would have been contradicted, and its correctness would have appeared by testing through coextensiveness (ṭard) and co-exclusiveness ('aks). And it is said: it is that which accepts from every genus of accidents a single accident. And it is said: it is that which occupies space (ḥayyiz). And it is said: it is an originated thing that is independent of a subject (maḥall). And most of these definitions are not consistent with the principles of the people of innovation (ahl al-bida'); if we occupied ourselves with clarifying that, it would extend the book and go beyond the intended purpose.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and a discussion of objections. It begins by presenting the primary division of entities into uncomposed (substances) and composed (bodies), establishing that every body is a substance. Then it defines 'jawhar' linguistically and technically. The technical definition is 'self-subsistent, receptive of opposites.' The author explains 'receptive of opposites' as the capacity to receive opposites alternately, using blackness as an example. Then he discusses al-Ash'ari's criticism: Ash'ari himself uses this definition, but it contradicts two of his own principles. First, Ash'ari holds that only God is self-subsistent, yet he applies 'self-subsistent' to the substance. Second, Ash'ari insists that a definition should consist of a single attribute, yet this definition has two. The author notes that other theologians and philosophers do not mind composite definitions as long as the purpose of distinguishing the defined is achieved. He suggests that Ash'ari could have sufficed with 'receptive of opposites' alone, which would avoid the contradiction. Finally, he lists several alternative definitions of jawhar (e.g., indivisible part, etc.) but does not discuss them in detail, stating that most are incompatible with the principles of innovators and that a full discussion would be too lengthy.

The Definition of Jawhar and Its Inseparable Cause
Nasafi defines jawhar by self-subsistence and proves the inseparable causal relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that for Nasafi, being a substance (jawhar) is defined by being self-subsistent (qa'im bi-dhatih), and that these two are inseparably connected as cause and definition.

Core Idea
A substance is defined by its self-subsistence, and the two are inseparable.

In this passage, Imam al-Nasafi establishes the foundational definition of jawhar (substance/atom) in the Maturidi tradition. He argues that being a jawhar is not an arbitrary label but is causally tied to being self-subsistent (qa'im bi-dhatih). Understanding this inseparable relation is key to later arguments about the nature of God and the universe.

Commentary
