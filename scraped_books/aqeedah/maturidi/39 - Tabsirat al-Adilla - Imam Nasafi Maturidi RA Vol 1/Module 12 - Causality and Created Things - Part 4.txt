The Weakness of the Custom Argument and Initial Absurdities
Author refutes custom argument with first set of reductio examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the opponents' custom argument and why the author considers it weak, as well as the first set of absurd consequences that follow from it.

Core Idea
Custom does not make the effect the human's act; otherwise, absurdities follow.

The opponents claim that because God consistently creates an effect after a human action, that effect becomes the human's act. The author first notes that this premise is not conceded, then shows that even if accepted, it leads to ridiculous outcomes. In this lesson, we examine the initial absurdities involving stones, mountains, and pain.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice how the author begins by stating the weakness of the opponent's argument before launching into hypotheticals.
2
Identify the opponent's assumption
The opponent assumes that if God's custom creates an effect after a human action, that effect is attributed to the human. Find where the author states this assumption.
3
Understand the reductio examples
The author gives three examples: a stone ascending upon separation, a mountain moving upon standing, and pain varying with hit intensity. Ask yourself: why do these examples show the opponent's reasoning is flawed?
These examples are deliberately chosen to be implausible.
4
Reflect on the logical pattern
See how if God's custom were to create an absurd effect, the opponent's logic would still attribute it to the human. This reveals the argument's overreach.

And as for the rational dubious arguments they rely on, they are weak because their opponents do not concede that its occurrence is in accordance with his intention and purpose by the will of the Maker, Exalted and High, and His carrying out of the custom that He does that subsequent to the servant's action within the sphere of his power, due to the preceding proof of the impossibility of that occurring by the servant's power. Then, according to this reasoning, if God had carried out the custom that upon the servant's separating from the stone He creates ascent in the stone, or if He carried out the custom that upon his standing He creates movement in the mountain, that would be the servant's action. So if He carried out the custom that He creates intense pain upon a light hit, and light pain upon a severe hit, each one would be a caused effect of the preceding cause. If they continue on this, they are pretending ignorance and they would be obliged to accept things that increase the appearance of their ignorance, such as it being said: If God had carried out the custom of mountains flying in the sky and their passing in the air like the passing of clouds subsequent to people pointing to them, and the falling of the seven heavens upon people eating and their return to their places upon their refraining from that, then all that would be the making of the agents of these causes, and this is pretense of ignorance. And if they reject this, they invalidate their own argument. And by this, it would require that the occurrence of fatness in a child upon the good care of the nurse in raising him, and the occurrence of fatness in the animal upon the good care of the groom in attending to its welfare and observing the time of food, fodder, and watering, would be from the action of the nurse and the groom. Similarly, the occurrence of crops, vegetables, basil, tree branches, and the fruits of the vine upon the care of the farmer in watering, fertilizing, and pruning would require that to be their action, and then servants would have the power to create bodies, and this error is apparent. And whatever is required from the separation of judgments, all that is related to his acquisition of what occurs in the place of his power from the action that God has made.

Commentary
This passage refutes the opponents' argument based on God's custom (ʿāda). The opponents claim that when God consistently creates an effect after a human action, that effect becomes the human's act. The author first notes that the opponents do not concede that the effect occurs by God's will and custom, and that the proof of the impossibility of the effect being by the servant's power was already given. Then the author reduces their argument ad absurdum: If God's custom of creating an ascent upon the servant's separating from a stone made that ascent the servant's action, then if God had a custom of creating mountains flying in the sky upon people pointing, that would be the people's action, which is absurd. Similarly, if God's custom of creating pain after a hit made the hit the cause of pain, then the same logic would lead to nurses being the cause of fatness in children, and farmers the creators of plants, implying that humans have the power to create bodies—an obvious error. Thus, the custom argument fails.

Further Absurdities: Flying Mountains and Falling Heavens
Author presents extreme reductio examples of flying mountains and falling heavens.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author intensifies the reductio with even more absurd examples, such as mountains flying and heavens falling, to expose the opponent's pretense of ignorance.

Core Idea
The opponent's argument leads to claiming humans cause cosmic events, which is absurd.

The opponents may try to accept the first set of absurdities, but the author then presents even more ridiculous scenarios—flying mountains and collapsing heavens—to force them to either accept the absurdity or abandon their argument. This lesson covers these extreme examples and the charge of 'tajāhul' (pretending ignorance).

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Focus on the hypothetical: what if God's custom made mountains fly upon people pointing, and the heavens fall upon people eating? According to the opponent's logic, who would be the agent of these events?
2
Understand the phrase 'yatajāhalū'
The author says the opponents are pretending ignorance if they accept these consequences. What does this mean?
They know it's absurd but pretend it's acceptable.
3
Connect to the previous lesson
These examples are even more extreme than the previous ones. Why does the author escalate?
To show that the custom argument has no limit and leads to manifest absurdity.
4
Reflect on the rhetorical strategy
The author uses reductio ad absurdum to force the opponent to either accept ridiculous conclusions or reject the original premise. This is a powerful tool in kalam.

Invalidating the Argument and Implications for Human Power
Author concludes refutation with dilemma and implication of human creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author shows that if opponents reject the absurd consequences, they invalidate their own argument, and you will see the implication that their logic would grant humans power to create bodies.

Core Idea
Rejecting the absurdities undermines the custom argument; accepting them leads to false claims of human creation.

The opponents are now caught in a dilemma: accept the absurd consequences (which they cannot) or reject them (which invalidates their argument). The author then shows that if their reasoning held, it would mean that nurses cause fatness in children and farmers create plants, implying humans have the power to create bodies—an obvious error. This lesson concludes the refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Notice the two-part structure: first, the rejection of absurdities invalidates the custom argument; second, accepting it leads to claiming humans create bodies.
2
Analyze the dilemma
Why does the author say 'وإن أبوا هذا نقضوا دليلهم'? If the opponents reject the absurd examples, they are contradicting the logic that led to them.
3
Examine the examples of daily life
The author gives examples of a nurse, a groom, and a farmer. How does the opponent's logic attribute natural growth to these individuals?
These are everyday occurrences where effects follow human care.
4
Understand the final point
The passage ends by linking the acquisition (iktisāb) to the servant's power. What does this tell us about the true relationship?
The servant acquires the action, but God creates the effect.

Refuting the Mu'tazila Doctrine of Generated Acts
Nasafi refutes tawallud by reductio ad absurdum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Mu'tazila theory of generated acts (tawallud) is rationally untenable and leads to attributing creation to creatures.

Core Idea
Generated acts cannot be created by humans; God alone creates all effects.

The Mu'tazila argued that some human actions generate effects that are created by the agent. Imam Nasafi dismantles this by showing that if God's custom were different, the same logic would force absurd conclusions. Understanding his reductio ad absurdum argument will clarify the Ash'ari/Maturidi position on causality and acquisition.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. The argument is dense and uses hypothetical scenarios. Let the examples illustrate the absurdity.
Pay attention to the conditional clauses starting with 'لو أجرى العادة'.
2
Identify the core reductio ad absurdum
Notice how Nasafi uses counterfactual changes in God's custom to show that the Mu'tazila criterion would attribute acts to humans inconsistently.
Examples: stone ascent, pain from blows, flying mountains – all impossible if humans were creators.
3
Recognize the correct position: acquisition
Nasafi clarifies that humans 'acquire' (iktisab) the acts that God creates in the locus of their power. They do not create the effects.
This preserves divine omnipotence while allowing human responsibility.
4
Understand the consequence: shirk
Nasafi concludes that the Mu'tazila theory, if applied consistently, would make humans partners with God in creation, which is false.
This is the ultimate theological refutation.
