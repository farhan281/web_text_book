Refutation of Prayer Objection: Benefit and Harm
Author refutes objection about prayer using dilemma and divine command.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the objection that praying for removal of illness is inconsistent with the nature of benefit and harm.

Core Idea
God's command redefines what is beneficial and harmful.

In this passage, the author continues to defend the rationality of prayer by addressing a specific objection: if illness is a benefit, why pray to remove it? The argument hinges on a dilemma, and the author's response reveals a deeper point about divine command. Let's follow the reasoning carefully.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objection claims that praying for removal of illness is irrational because illness might be a benefit. First, grasp the dilemma: if illness is benefit, why remove it? If not benefit, why pray?
2
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the author's direct rebuttal and the opponent's potential counter.
3
Break down the argument
Identify the three parts: initial dilemma, opponent's counter (illness becomes harm at prayer time), and author's final response that God's command transforms the nature of benefit and harm.
4
Reflect on the theological principle
Consider the implication: God's command itself determines what is beneficial. Human reasoning alone cannot judge before the command.

Commentary
In this passage, the author addresses an additional objection concerning prayer for the removal of illness. He argues that the earlier objection is not valid for this case. The opponent might say that at the time of prayer the illness becomes a harm, so removal becomes beneficial. The author responds that God commanded the prayer, and in it what was beneficial became a harm, and what was not harm became a benefit. But then what benefit is there for the supplicant at that time, when before he was in benefit and harm was absent? The passage ends with 'And God is the giver of success.'

No Due Right of the Servant Over Allah
Nasafi presents a dilemma to refute the claim that a servant has a due right over Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Nasafi denies that a servant has any due right over Allah and how he refutes the claim that withholding what is needed is miserliness.

Core Idea
A servant has no inherent right over Allah; His acts are not obligations but pure bounty.

Nasafi opens by dismantling the notion that a servant has a due right over Allah. He presents a dilemma: either what is withheld is a right of the needy or not. If it is a right, he denies that any creature can have such a right over the Creator. If it is not a right, withholding cannot be miserliness. This sets the stage for a deeper discussion on divine generosity.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening dilemma
Read the first part of the passage carefully: 'ليس للعبد حق مستحق على الله...' up to 'فهو ممنوع'. Notice the two options presented.
The author forces the opponent into a logical corner.
2
Analyze the first option
Consider the first option: if the withheld item is a due right, Nasafi grants the premise but denies that any creature has such a right over Allah. Reflect on why this is a radical theological claim.
3
Analyze the second option
Consider the second option: if it is not a due right, then withholding is not miserliness. Nasafi says this is simply denied (mamnu'). Ask yourself why.
This leads to the definition of generosity.
4
Connect to the next argument
Notice how this dilemma sets up the subsequent discussion on generosity. The opponent cannot escape without admitting something about God's nature.

ليس للعبد حق مستحق على الله: وما تزعمون أن منع ما بالغير إليه حاجة من غير أن ينتفع المانع بالمنع أو يتضرر بالإعطاء بخل فاسد فإنا نقول: منع ما هو حق للمحتاج قبل المانع؟ أم منع ما ليس بحق مستحق للمحتاج؟ فإن قال: بالأول فهو مسلم لكن لا نسلم أن للعبد على الله تعالى حقا مستحقاً وإن قال: بالثاني فهو ممنوع، ثم نقول له: الجود ما هو؟ بذل ما هو واجب على الباذل أم بذل ما ليس بواجب عليه؟ فإن قال: بالأول. ظهرت مكابرته، لما أن من قضى دينا عليه لا يعد جوادًا، وإن قال: بالثاني. قيل: أتقول إنه تعالى جواد متفضل ذو فضل على العالمين؟ فإن قال: لا. فقد أنكر النصوص وخالف الإجماع ووصفه بالبخل إذ كل عالم ليس بجواد فهو بخيل، وإن قال: نعم. أقر أنه تعالى يفعل ما يفعل بعباده، غير مؤد حقًا واجبًا عليه، وأنه لا حق لغيره قبله بل يعطي ما يشاء من يشاء فضلاً منه، ويحرم من يشاء ما يشاء بعدله، وهو الحكيم في ذلك كله. ثم نقول: ما ذكرت فاسد من وجه آخر لأن ما اعتبرت به من الشاهد غير موجود وهو من لا يضره الإعطاء ولا ينفعه المنع لأن بالإعطاء ينتقص ماله وبالمنع لا ينتقص واعتبار الغائب بشاهد لا يتحقق ثبوته فاسد. ثم نقول: الجواد المتفضل من أعطى ما ليس عليه إعطاؤه وكان له أن يعطي وعلى مذهبهم لا يتصور أن يعطي الله أحدًا ما ليس عليه إعطاؤه إذ كل ما يفعل هو الأصلح بخلقه على التعين وفعل ذلك واجب عليه فلم يكن الله تعالى عندهم جوادًا ولا متفضلاً ولا ذا فضل ولا منعماً ولا محسنًا، وهذا كله تكذيب الله تعالى ورسوله.
There is no right for the servant that is due upon Allah: And what you claim that withholding what someone else needs, without the withholder benefiting from the withholding or being harmed by giving, is miserliness, is invalid. For we say: Is it withholding what is a right of the needy before the withholder? Or is it withholding what is not a due right of the needy? If he says: the first, then it is granted, but we do not accept that the servant has a due right over Allah Most High. If he says: the second, then it is denied. Then we say to him: What is generosity? Is it the giving of what is obligatory upon the giver, or the giving of what is not obligatory upon him? If he says: the first, his obstinacy becomes apparent, because one who pays a debt due on him is not called generous. If he says: the second, it is said: Do you say that He Most High is Generous, Bountiful, Possessor of bounty over the worlds? If he says: No, then he has denied the texts and contradicted consensus and described Him with miserliness, for everyone who is not generous is miserly. If he says: Yes, he affirms that He Most High does what He does with His servants, not performing a due right upon Him, and that there is no right for anyone else before Him, but He gives to whom He wills whatever He wills as bounty from Him, and deprives whomever He wills of whatever He wills by His justice, and He is the Wise in all that. Then we say: What you mentioned is invalid from another aspect, because what you analogized from the witnessed world does not exist: it is one who is not harmed by giving nor benefited by withholding, because by giving his wealth decreases and by withholding it does not decrease. And analogizing the unseen to a witnessed whose existence is not established is invalid. Then we say: The generous munificent is one who gives what he is not obliged to give and has the option to give. According to their doctrine, it is inconceivable that Allah gives anyone something He is not obliged to give, since everything He does is the most beneficial for His creation determinately, and doing that is obligatory upon Him. So accordingly, Allah Most High is not generous, nor bountiful, nor possessor of bounty, nor bestower of favors, nor good-doer, in their view. And all of this is a denial of Allah and His Messenger.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical refutation of the claim that a servant has a right (haqq mustahaqq) over God and that withholding what is needed constitutes miserliness. The author begins by framing a dilemma: either the withheld item is a due right of the needy or it is not. If it is a right, the author denies that the servant has any due right over God. If it is not a right, then withholding is not miserliness. Then the author examines the definition of generosity (al-jud): is it giving what is obligatory or giving what is not obligatory? Observing that paying a debt is not called generosity, the author forces the opponent to admit that generosity is giving what is not obligatory. Then he asks whether God is generous and bountiful (jawad mutafaddil). If the opponent denies it, he contradicts revelation and consensus and attributes miserliness to God. If he affirms it, he must admit that God's actions are not due to any obligation, but as pure bounty and justice. The author then criticizes the opponent's analogy from the witnessed world (al-shahid) to the unseen (al-gha'ib) as invalid because the conditions are not met (in the witnessed world, giving decreases wealth, but God's giving does not decrease His wealth). Finally, the author argues that the opponent's position that God must always do the most beneficial (al-aslah) for His creatures would mean God is obliged to do certain acts, which would negate His generosity and bounty, leading to a denial of God and His Messenger.

Defining Generosity and Divine Bounty
Nasafi defines generosity and uses it to affirm God's voluntary bounty and refute the doctrine of al-aslah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Nasafi defines generosity as giving what is not obligatory and why this definition is crucial for affirming God's generosity and refuting the doctrine of al-aslah (the most beneficial).

Core Idea
Generosity is giving what is not obligatory; God's actions are thus voluntary bounty, not forced duties.

After establishing that no servant has a due right over Allah, Nasafi turns to the definition of generosity. He forces the opponent to choose: is generosity giving what is obligatory or what is not? By showing that paying a debt is not considered generosity, he compels the opponent to admit the latter. Then he draws out the implications: if God is generous, His actions are not obligations. This also refutes the Mu'tazili doctrine that God must do the most beneficial (al-aslah), which would negate His generosity.

Guided Path
1
Examine the definition of generosity
Read the question: 'الجود ما هو؟ بذل ما هو واجب على الباذل أم بذل ما ليس بواجب عليه?' Reflect on why Nasafi presents this as a dichotomy.
The answer will determine the nature of divine action.
2
Consider the first option
If generosity is giving what is obligatory, then paying a debt would be generous. Nasafi says this is obstinate, because common sense says paying a debt is not generosity.
3
Consider the second option
If generosity is giving what is not obligatory, then God, if He is generous, must give without obligation. This leads to the affirmation of His bounty.
4
Understand the consequence for God
Nasafi then asks if the opponent admits God is Generous (جواد متفضل). If yes, then God's acts are not duties. If no, he denies revelation and consensus.
This is a powerful argument that opponents cannot escape.
5
Refutation of al-aslah
Read the final part: 'الجواد المتفضل من أعطى ما ليس عليه إعطاؤه...' Nasafi shows that the doctrine of al-aslah (obligating God to do the best) would make God not generous.
This is a direct critique of Mu'tazili thought.

Empirical Refutation of Denial of Children's Pain
Nasafi presents observable signs and rational consequences to refute the denial of childhood pain.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Nasafi uses empirical observation and rational reasoning to refute the claim that children do not feel pain.

Core Idea
Children's pain is proven by observation, logic, and universal human experience.

This passage from Nasafi's Tabsirat al-Adilla tackles a controversial theological claim: that children do not truly feel pain. Rather than engaging in abstract speculation, Nasafi grounds his refutation in observable reality—what we see, hear, and remember. The argument is both empirical and rational, showing that denying such evident facts contradicts common sense and religious morality.

The argument moves from external observation to internal reflection.

If he says: The evidence of the restriction exists, which is the rational indication at the time of revelation of the promise and threat verses, because eternal abiding of one who believed in the Fire is something the mind rejects, likewise eternal abiding of one who repented from sin, likewise eternal abiding of one who ended with disbelief in Paradise is something the mind rejects, likewise eternal abiding of one who committed a sin because by it he becomes an enemy of God the Exalted, and eternal abiding of the enemy in Paradise is impossible. And the rational evidence exists at the revelation of every verse of promise and threat, so all the verses are restricted by rational evidence. We say: The Ash'aris oppose you most strongly in this and claim that there is not in the mind evidence for the impossibility of eternal abiding of obedient believers who have no sin in the Fire, nor eternal abiding of disbelievers and sinners in Paradise. Then if it were said to you that eternal abiding of one who believed and did righteous deeds and did not disbelieve but ended with affirmation – in the Fire, is something the mind rejects, likewise nullification of faith which is ultimate in good and many acts of worship each of which deserves tenfold according to the report of God the Exalted, for committing something not ultimate in evil, and He does not reward except singly according to the report of God the Exalted, along with the presence of fear of punishment and hope for mercy and trust in His generosity, and it is in itself a thing pushed to it by overwhelming desire or force of anger or strong fervor, then this also is something the mind rejects, so it becomes a restriction on the verses of threat connected to eternal abiding. Likewise the existence of evidence that God the Exalted is described with mercy and compassion and pardon and forgiveness prevents the generalization of threat verses also. And God is the grantor of success.

Commentary
This passage continues the refutation of the opponent's attempt to attach implicit conditions to the threat verses via rational evidence. The author first points out that if the opponent claims ignorance of the chronological order of revelation, he cannot arbitrarily assign priority to threat verses. Then the opponent argues that rational reason itself provides the condition, because it is rationally impossible to eternally punish believers or reward disbelievers. The author counters by citing the Ash'ari position, which denies that such rational impossibility exists. Moreover, if rational evidence were accepted, it would also apply to promise verses, leading to a contradiction. The author also notes that other rational considerations, such as God's attributes of mercy, further restrict the threat verses. The argument concludes by affirming that the opponent's rational evidence is neither universally accepted nor consistently applied.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think Ash'aris reject reason entirely in theology.
Correction: Ash'aris deny that reason alone can judge the impossibility of eternal punishment or reward; they do not reject reason but limit its scope in specific matters.
