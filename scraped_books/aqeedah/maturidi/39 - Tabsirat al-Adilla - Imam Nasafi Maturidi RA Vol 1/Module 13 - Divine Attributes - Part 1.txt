The Logical Proof of Tawhid: The Argument from Mutual Hindrance (Tamanu')
Al-Nasafi presents the definitive argument against dualism from mutual hindrance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the impossibility of two gods is proved through the logical principle of mutual hindrance (tamanu').

Core Idea
Two gods cancel each other out; only one can exist.

Al-Nasafi concludes his discussion of dualist sects by presenting a crisp logical argument: if there were two creators, they would inevitably conflict. This conflict would prove either that both are created or that one is weak, and in any case cannot lead to a coherent universe. The argument is a straightforward reductio ad absurdum that every student of kalam must master.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
2
Identify the conditional premise
The passage begins with a hypothetical: 'if there were two makers'. Notice how al-Nasafi builds the argument on this assumption.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Trace the logical consequences
What would happen if two makers existed? Mutual hindrance (tamanu') leads to either both being originated or one being impotent. That is impossible for a true god.
The key terms: التمانع and النقيضان.
4
Conclude the argument
Since the premise leads to impossibility, the premise is false. Therefore, there is only one Maker.
The conclusion is direct: إذا الصانع واحد وهو الله.

الكلام في وحدانية الصانع
يتحدث النسفي مثبتا أن صانع العالم لابد أن يكون واحدا؛ لأن القول بغير ذلك يؤدي إلى الوقوع في المحال، ويدخل في مناقشة طويلة مع المعتزلة، ونراه يتهم أبا هاشم الجبائي بالإلحاد، لأن الأخير قال بأن وحدانية الصانع لا تعرف بالعقل وإنما بالسمع.
يقول أبو المعين: ولعل مراد هذا الملحد إبطال الوحدانية، وإفساد القول بالرسالة، وهذا منه قمة القسوة على أبي هاشم، ولعل ما يخفف وقع هذا الكلام علينا أننا رأينا المعتزلة وعلى رأسهم القاضي عبد الجبار يشنون نفس الحملة ويتهمون الأشاعرة والماتريدية بأنهم المجبرة والقدرية.
وبعد مناقشته للمعتزلة يبدأ في حديث عن المجوس فيعرفنا بهم، وبنشأة فكرة الإثنينية عندهم، ثم يورد شبههم، ويرد عليها، مبينا أن للشرور في العالم فوائد، وهذا يبطل قول المجوس بوجود يزدان إله الخير، وأهر من إله الشر.
ثم يعرفنا بالثنوية، وأقسامهم المانوية، والديصانية، والمرقيونية، ويناقش فكرهم ليبطله، ويطيل في المناقشة ومع الإطالة يقول : إن هذا الكلام تبرع منه إذ لا حاجة بنا إلى شيء من هذا، لأنه لو كان هناك صانعان لوجد التمانع، والتمانع يدل على حدوثهما ، أو حدوث العاجز منهما، لأنه لو حصل ما يريدانه في الشيء لاجتمع النقيضان وهذا محال إذا الصانع واحد وهو الله.
Discussion on the Oneness of the Maker. Al-Nasafi speaks, affirming that the Maker of the world must be one, because saying otherwise leads to absurdity. He enters into a long discussion with the Mu'tazila, and we see him accusing Abu Hashim al-Jubba'i of heresy, because the latter said that the oneness of the Maker is known not by reason but by revelation. Abu al-Mu'in says: Perhaps the intention of this heretic is to nullify oneness and corrupt the assertion of the message. This from him is extreme harshness toward Abu Hashim. Perhaps what alleviates the impact of this statement on us is that we have seen the Mu'tazila, led by al-Qadi 'Abd al-Jabbar, launching the same campaign and accusing the Ash'aris and Maturidis of being Jabriyya (predestinarians) and Qadariyya (free-will advocates). After his discussion with the Mu'tazila, he begins to talk about the Magians, acquainting us with them and the origin of the idea of dualism among them. Then he presents their doubts and refutes them, showing that evils in the world have benefits, and this refutes the Magians' belief in Yazdan, the god of good, and Ahriman, the god of evil. Then he acquaints us with the dualists (Thaniwiyya) and their divisions: Manichaeans, Daysaniyya, and Marqiyuniyya. He discusses their thought to refute it, and he prolongs the discussion, and with the prolongation he says: This speech is a gratuitous addition, for we have no need of any of this, because if there were two makers, there would be mutual hindrance (tamanu'), and mutual hindrance indicates their origination, or the origination of the impotent one, because if both intend something in a thing, two contradictories would combine, and that is impossible. So the Maker is one, and He is Allah.

Commentary
This passage presents al-Nasafi's argument for the oneness of the creator. It begins by stating that the Maker must be one, as multiplicity leads to absurdity (muhal). Al-Nasafi then engages with the Mu'tazila, specifically criticizing Abu Hashim al-Jubba'i for claiming that divine oneness is known through revelation rather than reason, which al-Nasafi considers heretical. The author of the summary (likely Abu al-Mu'in al-Nasafi) notes that the Mu'tazila similarly attack Ash'aris and Maturidis. Next, al-Nasafi discusses the Magians (Majus) and their dualistic idea of two gods (Yazdan and Ahriman), refuting it by arguing that evils have benefits. He then discusses other dualist sects (Manichaeans, Daysaniyya, Marqiyuniyya). Finally, he concludes that if there were two creators, there would be mutual hindrance (tamanu'), which would prove either that both are originated or that one is impotent. If both will the same thing, it would require the combination of contradictories, which is impossible. Therefore, the creator must be one, and He is Allah.

Key Terms
التمانس (tamanu')
النقيضان (contradictories)

Key Terms from Source Passage

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The argument from mutual hindrance requires that two gods actually exist to be refuted.
Correction: It is a reductio: we assume their existence and deduce an impossibility, proving they cannot exist.

Checkpoint
According to al-Nasafi's argument, what follows from the existence of two makers?
A) They would cooperate to create the world.
B) There would be mutual hindrance, leading to impossibility.
C) One would be good and the other evil.
D) They would be unknowable.

Key Takeaway
The logical impossibility of two creators is proven by the unavoidable mutual hindrance they would cause.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
You have now seen that belief in more than one God is logically absurd. But do you still act as if there are other forces controlling your life – like people, money, or your own desires? What is one specific attachment you can cut this week, to prove you truly believe Allah alone is the Creator and Disposer of all affairs?
?
The proof from mutual hindrance shows that any rival to Allah would lead to contradiction and chaos. Look at your daily worries: do you ever fear that something besides Allah can harm or benefit you independently? Repent of that hidden shirk and commit to reciting say, 'Hasbi Allahu la ilaha illa huwa' (Allah suffices me, there is no god but Him) whenever that fear arises.
?
If the existence of two gods is impossible, then your heart's division between love of Allah and love of the world is also a logical chaos. When will you stop trying to serve two masters and fully surrender your heart to the One who alone deserves your entire trust and devotion?

Reason vs. Revelation in Knowing Tawhid: Al-Nasafi's Critique of Abu Hashim
Al-Nasafi criticizes Abu Hashim al-Jubba'i for denying the rational knowability of tawhid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Nasafi insists that the oneness of God must be known through reason, not revelation alone, and why he accuses Abu Hashim of heresy.

Core Idea
Tawhid is a rational necessity, not just a revealed truth.

Before presenting his own arguments, al-Nasafi engages with the Mu'tazila, particularly Abu Hashim al-Jubba'i, who claimed that divine oneness is only known through revelation. Al-Nasafi considers this view heretical because it undermines the rational foundation of belief. This passage highlights the critical debate within Islamic theology about the role of reason in establishing fundamental doctrines.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice the strong language used against Abu Hashim.
Al-Nasafi calls him a heretic (ملحد).
2
Identify the two positions
Al-Nasafi asserts that oneness is known by reason; Abu Hashim says it is known only by revelation. Understand each position.
3
Assess the accusation
Why does al-Nasafi think Abu Hashim's view leads to nullifying oneness? Consider the consequences.
If reason cannot prove tawhid, then revelation could be corrupted.
4
Note the counter-accusation
The Mu'tazila accused Ash'aris and Maturidis of being Jabriyya and Qadariyya. This shows the polemical context.

Refuting Magian Dualism: The Problem of Evil and Its Benefits
Al-Nasafi discusses Magian dualism and refutes it by showing that evil has benefits.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Nasafi refutes the Magian belief in two gods (good and evil) by arguing that evil serves a purpose and has benefits.

Core Idea
Evil in the world has benefits, disproving the need for a separate evil god.

After dealing with the Mu'tazila, al-Nasafi turns to the Magians (Majus), who believe in two eternal principles: Yazdan (good) and Ahriman (evil). Their strongest argument is the existence of evil in the world. Al-Nasafi responds by showing that evils have hidden benefits, thus undercutting the need for a second god of evil. This lesson examines his refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation. Note that al-Nasafi first introduces the Magians and their belief.
2
Understand the Magian argument
The Magians see evil as proof of an evil creator alongside a good creator.
They point to natural disasters, disease, etc.
3
Identify al-Nasafi's response
Al-Nasafi counters that evils have benefits (فوائد) in the larger scheme of things. This undermines the dualist explanation.
He does not deny evil but reinterprets it.
4
Consider the implication
If evil can be explained within a monotheistic framework, dualism is unnecessary.
This leads to the final argument of tamanu'.

The Creator Is Not an Accident
Al-Nasafi proves the Creator is not an accident using definition and attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Creator cannot be an accident based on the definition of accident and the attributes of self-subsistence.

Core Idea
Accidents are fleeting; the Creator is eternal and self-subsistent.

This lesson examines a foundational argument in Islamic theology: the Creator cannot be an accident. Al-Nasafi provides two clear reasons based on the nature of accidents and the unique attributes of the Creator. Let's explore these reasons step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to get the author's direct meaning.
2
Understand the definition of accident
Identify how the passage defines an accident (ʿaraḍ): something that cannot remain (i.e., it is temporary).
This definition is key to the whole argument.
3
Contrast accident with Creator's eternity
Notice that the Creator is described as 'remaining without end' (bāqin lā nihāya). Since accidents cannot remain, the Creator cannot be an accident.
4
Contrast accident's dependence with Creator's self-subsistence
Accidents do not subsist by themselves; they need a substance. The Creator subsists by Himself and nothing else subsists by Him.
This second argument reinforces the first.
5
Conclude the teaching point
Synthesize both arguments: the Creator is eternal and self-subsistent, so He is not an accident.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional argument excluding accidents from God. The Trilemma: Forcing the Mu'tazila to Choose Consistently
Al-Nasafi presents a trilemma forcing the Mu'tazila to choose consistently on the shāhid-ghā'ib analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Nasafi uses a trilemma to show that the Mu'tazila cannot consistently deny attributes without falling into self-contradiction or disbelief.

Core Idea
Mu'tazila must either accept full analogy, reject all analogy, or be inconsistent—each has unacceptable consequences.

After presenting the Mu'tazila's objections, Al-Nasafi turns the tables by offering them a three-way logical division. The argument uses the principle of analogy (tamthīl) between the witnessed world and the unseen divine realm. The Mu'tazila must choose one of three options, but each option either contradicts their own principles or leads to atheism. This is a classic example of kalam's method of refutation through logical enumeration.

Guided Path
1
Understand the setting of the trilemma
Al-Nasafi introduces the trilemma after refuting specific Mu'tazilite objections. He addresses the Mu'tazila directly: 'You are among three matters.'
The trilemma concerns the analogy between shāhid (witnessed) and ghā'ib (unseen).
2
Analyze Option 1: Full analogy
If the Mu'tazila accept full analogy, then the effect in the unseen proves God's power and knowledge—this is the Sunni position, which they reject.
They would have to abandon their school.
3
Analyze Option 2: No analogy
If they reject all analogy, then the effect gives no knowledge of God in the unseen, leading to atheism—which they also reject.
This is 'the height of denial of the existence of the Maker.'
4
Analyze Option 3: Selective analogy
If they accept analogy for power but not for knowledge, they contradict themselves because the same evidence should be treated consistently.
This inconsistency refutes their position.
5
Realize the force of the argument
The trilemma shows that no matter which option the Mu'tazila choose, they cannot maintain their rejection of attributes without logical failure.
Al-Nasafi's method is a strong dialectical tool.

Commentary
This passage introduces the theological discussion on the attribute of takwīn (bringing into existence, creation). Nasafi begins by posing three key questions: Is takwīn identical with the created thing? Is it eternal and unoriginated? Does the eternity of takwīn imply the eternity of the created things? He then provides a definition: takwīn and its synonyms (takhlīq, khalq, ījād, iḥdāth, ikhtirā') all mean bringing the non-existent (ma'dūm) from non-existence into existence. Nasafi notes that he follows his predecessors in affirming takwīn as an attribute. Next, he reports the positions of various Islamic sects. The Mu'tazila and Najjāriyya deny that any attribute can subsist in the Essence of Allah, so they deny takwīn entirely. The majority of them, as well as al-Qalānisī (an early Ash'arī theologian), the Kullābiyya, and the Ashā'ira, hold that takwīn is not a distinct meaning apart from the created thing; rather it is identical with the created thing (ʿayn al-mukawwan). A second group (Abū al-Hudhayl, Bishr ibn al-Muʿtamir, Maʿmar, Ibn al-Rāwandī, and the Karrāmiyya) affirms that takwīn is a meaning (maʿnā) beyond the created thing, but they disagree on its nature; Bishr and Ibn al-Rāwandī go so far as to say it is an event (ḥadath) not subsisting in a subject (lā fī maḥall). Finally, Nasafi presents the position of Ahl al-Haqq (the Maturidi and orthodox Sunni position): takwīn is an eternal attribute (ṣifah azaliyyah) subsisting in the Essence of Allah, just like knowledge, power, and hearing. On this view, takwīn itself is eternal, while the mukawwan (the created thing) is originated (ḥādith). He draws an analogy: power (qudrah) is eternal, while its object (maqdūr) is originated; similarly for other attributes. This establishes the orthodox distinction between the eternal attribute of creation and the temporal created objects.

Commentary
This passage is a comparative presentation of two criteria for distinguishing between attributes of essence (ṣifāt al-dhāt) and attributes of action (ṣifāt al-fi‘l), followed by the author’s own Maturidi position. First, the Mu‘tazila: an attribute is of essence if it is always affirmed of God and cannot be denied; if denial is permissible (like saying God does not create for a particular person), it is an attribute of action. Moreover, if it were originated, it would have to subsist either in no locus (impossible for an accident), in a locus other than God (making that locus the creator), or in God's essence (which would imply change in God and lead to His origination, which is disbelief). Therefore al-takwin must be eternal. Finally, he explains that the eternity of al-takwin does not entail the eternity of the things brought into existence, because things that come into being through al-takwin are by nature originated, while the eternal is self-sufficient. The footnote clarifies that power (qudra) according to the Maturidis is not an attribute of effect but rather the capacity to produce or annihilate; the actual producing and annihilating is the attribute of effect, which is al-takwin.
