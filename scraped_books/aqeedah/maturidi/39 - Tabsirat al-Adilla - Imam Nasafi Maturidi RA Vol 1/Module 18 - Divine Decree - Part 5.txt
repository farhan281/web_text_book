The Dialogue on Infant Death and Apostasy: A Dilemma
The author constructs a dialogue between a child, God, and an adult to create a dilemma for the opponent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author uses a dialogue between a child who died young, God, and an adult who disbelieved to create an irresolvable dilemma for the fatalistic opponent.

Core Idea
The opponent cannot consistently explain why God causes some to die young and spares others known to disbelieve.

This lesson presents the second part of Imam al-Nasafi's refutation, where he constructs a powerful dialectical dialogue. The author imagines a child who died young complaining about not receiving the same reward as an adult believer, and then an adult disbeliever complaining about not being allowed to die young. The opponent is forced into a contradiction from which there is no escape.

Guided Path
1
Set up the dialogue scenario
Read the opening questions that establish the existence of three types of people: a child who died, an adult believer, and an adult apostate. This sets the stage for the dilemma.
2
Understand the child's complaint
The child questions why God did not let him live longer to earn more reward. Consider the reply that dying early was better to avoid future disbelief and punishment.
This reply seems reasonable but leads to a problem.
3
See the adult disbeliever's counter-complaint
The adult uses the same logic: if God knew he would disbelieve, why not let him die as a child? The opponent must now provide a consistent answer.
This exposes the inconsistency.
4
Realize the irresolvable dilemma
The child then points out that if exposure to reward is beneficial for the adult, it should also be beneficial for the child. The opponent cannot answer both, proving their position false.
The author ends with a colorful remark about mice and crows to emphasize the impossibility.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an objection regarding God's wisdom in allowing people whom He knows will apostatize to reach adulthood. The author argues that God's actions are always for the creature's ultimate benefit, even when human reason cannot fully grasp it. He first states a principle: for one known to apostatize, keeping him alive is more beneficial than causing death before apostasy, because God has given him intellect, commanded faith, and guided him, thereby exposing him to the highest reward. This exposure itself is a benefit. The author then mocks the absurdity of the opposite view by comparing it to a delirious person's speech. Next, he presents an analogy: a father who gives money to his son for trade, knowing the son will lose it all—such a father is not acting for the son's benefit. This illustrates that if God acted similarly, it would be irrational. The opponent is forced into a contradiction: either he admits God does what is beneficial (which the analogy contradicts) or he denies it, invalidating his position. Then the author introduces a dialogue: a child who died young complains that he was not given the chance to earn greater rewards like an adult believer. God would answer that dying early was more beneficial because He knew the child would otherwise disbelieve and be punished eternally. But then an adult who disbelieved would counter-argue that God should have let him die young as well. The opponent cannot consistently answer both. Thus the author concludes that the opponent's position is insurmountably flawed.

The Child's Complaint and the Contradiction in the Aslah Doctrine
Nasafi presents a dialectical scenario with a child and an adult to refute the aslah doctrine.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Nasafi uses the hypothetical complaints of a child and an adult to expose a logical contradiction in the claim that God must always do what is most beneficial for each individual.

Core Idea
If killing a child is most beneficial, then sparing an adult who will disbelieve must also be most beneficial—but that leads to contradiction.

In this passage, Imam Nasafi engages in a powerful dialectical argument against those who assert that God is obligated to do what is most beneficial (al-aslah) for every person. He constructs two hypothetical scenarios: a child who died young and an adult who apostatized. By allowing these characters to question God, Nasafi forces the opponent to admit mutually exclusive answers, thereby demonstrating the absurdity of their position. Let us follow the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the scenario of the child’s complaint
Read how Nasafi sets up the child’s lament: he was denied a long life to earn great reward. Note that this is a hypothetical used to challenge the opponent’s view.
2
See how the opponent responds for God
The opponent claims God killed the child because He knew the child would disbelieve. This is the core of the aslah argument: God prevented future harm.
3
Consider the adult apostate’s parallel complaint
An adult who disbelieved or apostatized asks why God did not kill him in childhood. Notice that if the same logic applied, the adult would have been better off dead, but the opponent gives a different answer.
4
Observe the contradiction exposed
The opponent says the adult was spared for exposure to the higher station, but then the child can ask why he was not given that chance. The inconsistency leaves the opponent with no escape.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Nasafi concludes that the opponent has no valid response until impossible events occur. This shows the weakness of the aslah theory.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of theological objections concerning God's wisdom in causing children to die. The author, Nasafi, engages in a dialectical argument against an opponent who holds that God must always do what is most beneficial (al-aslah) for each individual. First, Nasafi sets up a hypothetical scenario: a child who died young complains to God that he was not given a longer life to earn greater reward. The opponent’s answer is that God knew the child would disbelieve if he reached maturity, so killing him was more beneficial (aslah). Argues that the Quranic threat for killing a believer is actually for istihlal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the Quranic threat of eternal punishment for killing a believer (e.g., 4:93) is actually a punishment for istihlal—considering such killing permissible—which is an act of kufr, not for the killing alone.

Core Idea
The threat of eternal hellfire for killing a believer targets istihlal, not the killing itself.

This passage from Imam al-Nasafi's Tabṣirat al-Adilla presents a crucial distinction in interpreting Quranic threats. It argues that the verse threatening eternal punishment for killing a believer is actually a warning against istihlāl, since eternal punishment for a believer is impossible. The passage then defends the possibility of temporary punishment for believers and the validity of intercession and forgiveness.

The Major Sinner with Mu'tazili or Khariji Beliefs Becomes a Disbeliever
Al-Nasafi rules on the major sinner with Mu'tazili or Khariji views.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Despairing of God's mercy and believing oneself to be a disbeliever makes one a disbeliever.

Al-Nasafi addresses a specific theological scenario: the person who commits a major sin while holding the Mu'tazili or Khariji belief that such a sin necessarily removes one from faith. In this lesson, we will examine his ruling that such a person becomes a kafir, drawing on Quranic evidence and the principle that one's own belief about one's faith status is decisive.
