How Miracles Prove Prophethood and Their Certain Transmission
Al-Nasafi explains the proof-value of miracles and their transmission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a miracle serves as proof of a prophet's truthfulness and why its transmission through tawatur yields certain knowledge.

Core Idea
God's enabling of miracles is His tacit confirmation of the prophet.

Al-Nasafi explains that a miracle is not just a wondrous event; it is God's affirmation of the one who performs it. Because the miracle defies natural laws and is witnessed by contemporaries who cannot replicate it, its report reaches us through tawatur, giving us certain knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without pausing to analyze. Then read the translation to get the overall meaning.
Notice the two distinct parts: the proof and the transmission.
2
Understand the proof: miracle = God's confirmation
Identify the phrase 'ysadiqqahu bima yuqaddiruhu 'ala fi'lihi mukhalifan li al-madda'. This is the core: God affirms the prophet by enabling something contrary to nature.
3
Recognize the role of witnesses and inability to oppose
Note that the miracles were witnessed by contemporaries who could not produce anything similar or oppose them. This strengthens the proof.
If they could oppose, the miracle would not be decisive.
4
Grasp tawatur and necessary knowledge
Learn that these reports were transmitted by tawatur (massive parallel transmission), which produces necessary knowledge—certain and requiring no further investigation.
This is why we can be certain of miracles even today.

Al-Nazzam's Theory of Sarfa and Poetry Analogy
Al-Nasafi presents al-Nazzam's theory then refutes it using an analogy with poetry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Nazzam's claim that the Qur'an's inimitability is due to divine diversion (sarfa) and how al-Nasafi refutes it using an analogy with poetry.

Core Idea
The Qur'an's composition is inherently inimitable, not merely due to divine intervention.

Al-Nazzam proposed that the Qur'an is not miraculous in its wording but because Allah prevented the Arabs from matching it. Al-Nasafi begins his refutation by pointing out that even with access to the same words, no one can produce poetry of comparable quality, showing the composition itself is miraculous.

Guided Path
1
Read al-Nazzam's theory
Read the first part of the passage where al-Nazzam's position is presented. Notice his claim that the Qur'an's miracle is due to sarfa (diverting), not its composition.
Al-Nazzam argues that individual verses are not miraculous, so the whole cannot be.
2
Understand the poetry analogy
Al-Nasafi compares the Qur'an to poetry: you have the same words as Imru' al-Qays but cannot produce a qasida like his. This shows the composition itself is the miracle.
The analogy targets the idea that diversion is unnecessary.
3
Reflect on the refutation
Consider why this analogy undermines sarfa. If the words are available but imitation fails, the inimitability lies in the arrangement, not divine prevention.

Source Text

And he continues with al-Nazzam in examination and division (sabr wa taqsim) until he invalidates his statement about sarfa.

Commentary
This passage presents a detailed refutation of al-Nazzam's theory. First, al-Nasafi reports al-Nazzam's position: i'jaz is not due to the Qur'an's composition (since individual verses are not miraculous) but due to sarfa—Allah diverted the Arabs from opposing it, which is a supernatural act. Then al-Nasafi refutes: he first argues by analogy using poetry. If you cannot produce poetry like that of Imru' al-Qays or even contemporaries, despite having the same words, that shows the composition itself is inimitable. Then he turns to the sarfa theory specifically, using a logical dilemma: the diverting (sarf) happened either with the free will of the Arabs or not. If with free will, then Allah is intervening in voluntary actions, which al-Nazzam would deny. If without free will, then it is jabr (compulsion), which al-Nazzam also rejects because he is a Mu'tazili who believes in free will. Thus the sarfa theory is self-contradictory. Additionally, if Allah can divert, why does He not divert disbelievers from disbelief for their own benefit, given the Mu'tazili principle of al-aslah (the best)? Al-Nasafi says he continues with al-Nazzam using the method of sabr wa taqsim (examination and division) until he refutes the sarfa claim entirely.

The Logical Dilemma of Sarfa and al-Aslah
Al-Nasafi uses logical division and the principle of the best to refute al-Nazzam's sarfa theory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Nasafi's logical argument against sarfa, which shows it leads to either compulsion or divine intervention in free will, and why the Mu'tazili principle of al-aslah further refutes it.

Core Idea
Sarfa is self-contradictory because it implies either jabr or invalidates free will, both rejected by al-Nazzam.

Al-Nasafi continues the refutation by examining the nature of the divine diversion. He presents a dichotomy: either the diversion happened with the Arabs' free will or without it. Neither option is acceptable to al-Nazzam. He then adds the argument from al-aslah: if Allah can divert, why doesn't He divert disbelievers from disbelief for their own good? This challenges the Mu'tazili principle.

Guided Path
1
Read the dichotomy passage
Read the passage where al-Nasafi asks whether the sarfa occurred with or without the slave's choice.
This is a classic sabr wa taqsim method.
2
Understand both horns
If with choice, Allah intervenes in voluntary acts (unacceptable to Mu'tazila). If without choice, it is compulsion (jabr), also rejected.
Al-Nazzam, as a Mu'tazili, rejects both.
3
Consider the al-aslah argument
If Allah can divert, why not divert disbelievers from disbelief? This would be best for them, but it doesn't happen, contradicting the Mu'tazili principle of al-aslah.
Al-aslah is a key Mu'tazili doctrine.
4
See the method of refutation
Al-Nasafi uses sabr wa taqsim (examination and division) to systematically refute the sarfa theory.

Refuting the Claim of Lost Imitations
al-Nasafi refutes the argument that imitations existed but were lost.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Nasafi rejects the argument that lost imitations of the Qur'an weaken its miraculous nature.

Core Idea
Lost imitations are no evidence—if they existed, they would have survived.

Al-Nasafi continues to defend the Qur'an's inimitability by addressing a final objection: some claim that opponents did produce imitations, but these were so suppressed by early Muslims that they were lost. This lesson unpacks his concise rebuttal and its logical force.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic sentence and its translation. Note the two key terms: معارضة (opposition/imitation) and اندراس (being lost/obliterated).
This is a single, dense refutation.
2
Understand al-Nasafi's argument
Identify the claim being refuted: that opponents did produce imitations but they were lost. Al-Nasafi invalidates this by implication: if such imitations existed, they would have been preserved by the Prophet's opponents as evidence against his prophethood.
The argument is logical, not empirical.
3
Reflect on the implication
Consider why the complete absence of any competing text strengthens the proof of the Qur'an's inimitability. The onus is on the objector to produce at least one imitation.
This rebuttal completes the defense of the miracle.

Al-Nasafi enumerates the second aspect of i'jaz: prophecies and eternal legal principles.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Many ahaad reports together can yield certainty, like knowing a person is generous from many isolated stories.

Before we can see how the Prophet's miracles are proven, we need a conceptual tool: tawatur ma'nawi. This lesson introduces the idea that many ahaad reports, each insufficient alone, can combine to produce certain knowledge—just as we know of Hatim's generosity or Antarah's bravery from scattered tales. Nasafi presents this analogy to ground his argument.

Commentary
This passage is a theological argument proving that the Prophet's miracles are established with certainty despite many individual reports being ahaad (non-mass-transmitted). The author, Abu al-Mu'in al-Nasafi, begins by stating that the Prophet's sensory miracles are too numerous to count, and he refrains from listing them to avoid making the work too long. He then addresses a potential scepticism: even if many reports of miracles are transmitted through ahaad chains (i.e., not reaching tawatur individually), they can collectively produce certain knowledge when the reports come from so many different people that collusion on falsehood is impossible. To illustrate this, he draws an analogy with well-known figures from Arab history known for generosity, bravery, or wisdom. For example, reports about the generosity of Hatim al-Ta'i or the bravery of Antarah are each ahaad, yet no one doubts that these individuals actually possessed those traits. Similarly, the many ahaad reports of different miracles performed by the Prophet—when considered together—compel knowledge that miracles did indeed occur. The author then clarifies that the tawatur in question is not tawatur of the exact wording (lafzi) but tawatur of meaning (ma'nawi). He explicitly excludes from this analogy such specific miracles as the splitting of the moon, the groaning of the camel trunk, or the sighing of the pillar (which are individually established by tawatur lafzi). Rather, the argument is about the aggregate of many ahaad reports that collectively yield certainty. He concludes by citing the usuli principle that 'from permissible particulars a necessitating universal is composed'—i.e., many contingent, individual reports can combine to yield certain knowledge of a general fact.

The Elephant Event as a Divine Sign for Prophethood
Nasafi enumerates the Elephant event as a divinely prepared sign, proving it via tawātur and concluding Mecca's honor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the historical Elephant event is presented as a divinely arranged sign (irhāṣ) for the coming of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) and why its transmission by tawātur makes it undeniable.

Core Idea
The miracle of the Elephant was Allah's preparation for the Prophet's mission, transmitted beyond doubt.

Imam Nasafi builds a layered proof for prophethood from a famous historical event: the Abyssinian invasion of Mecca (the Year of the Elephant). He argues that the destruction of the army was no accident but a deliberate sign from Allah preparing for the emergence of His final Prophet. Most striking is his claim that this event is so well‑attested by tawātur that even atheists cannot deny it—and the Qur’ān itself challenges the polytheists with it without rebuttal. This lesson unpacks that argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic source passage and its full English translation. Notice the structure: Nasafi begins with the Abyssinians' background, then explains the purpose of their destruction, then argues for its authenticity via tawātur, then cites evidence, and finally draws the conclusion about Mecca's honor.
The passage is quite long; read it in one go to grasp the overall argument.
2
Identify the key claim
Identify the central thesis: the Elephant event was not a natural disaster but a divine sign (irhāṣ) to prepare for the Prophet's mission. Note how Nasafi ties it to the protection of the Prophet's lineage and the honor of Mecca.
irhāṣ is a precursor miracle before prophethood, unlike mu'jiza which comes after.
3
Understand the tawātur argument
Nasafi asserts that the event is transmitted by tawātur, meaning it is reported by so many independent chains that it is impossible to be false. He compares it to other well-known historical events. Ask yourself: why does he emphasize this?
tawātur is a key epistemological concept in Islamic theology for establishing certainty.
4
Examine the supporting evidence
Notice that Nasafi quotes pre-Islamic poets (Nufayl, Tufayl, Umayya) and the Qur'anic sura al-Fil. Also, he notes that the polytheists did not deny it. This multi-layered evidence strengthens the argument.
The poets were not Muslims, so their testimony is unbiased.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Nasafi concludes that a city so honored by such a sign is the most fitting place for a truth-telling prophet. Therefore, the Prophet cannot be a liar. Consider how this argument defends prophethood against skeptics.
This is a form of argument from the sanctity of place (Mecca) to the truth of the prophet.

ومن قصدهم من الحبشان كانوا من أهل الكتاب مؤمنين بموسى وعيسى عليهما السلام متمسكين بكتابيهما التوراة والإنجيل، فلا يظن إهلاكهم لنصرة من كانت حالته تلك بل كان ذلك إرهاصا لأمر نبيه المصطفى محمد عليه السلام إذ كان قرب زمانه ودنا أوان خروجه وظهور دعوته، فصان الله تعالى الآباء لاحترام من علم الله تعالى خروجه من أصلابهم من أنصار دعوة رسول الله عليه السلام وأتباع ملته، ومتبعي دينه وشريعته، وحفظ نبيه الذي كان في سابق علمه قضائه أن يكون قبلة لأمته ومنسكا ومثابة لأهل ملته، ثم إن إنكار أمر الفيل ومبرم غير ممكن لأهل الإلحاد لثبوت ذلك بالتواتر الذي لا يتمكن فيه توهم الكذب كثبوت سائر الأمور الماضية في الأزمنة الخالية التي ينسب جاحدها إلى العناد والمكابرة، وقد ذكر ذلك الشعراء في قصائدهم والخطباء في مواعظهم. فمن ذلك قول نفيل بن حبيب وهو جاهلي، وكانت الحبشة أخذته في طريقها إلى مكة ليدلها فاحتال الهرب منها: ألا ردى ركائبنـ ا ردينـ فإنك لو رأيت ولن تراه حمدت الله إذ عاينت طيـ نعمناكم على الهجران عينا لدى جنب المخصب ما رأينا وحسب حجارة تلقــ علين وكلهم يسائل عن نفيل كأن عليَّ للحبشان دينـ وقال طفيل وهو جاهلي: ترعى مذانب وسمى أطماع لهـ بالجذع حيث عصى أصحابه الفيل وقال أبو الصلت أمية بن أبي الصلت: ان آيات ربنـ ـا بينـ نات جلس الفيل بالمغمس حتى ــا يمارى بهن إلا كفور ـل يحبو كأنه معقور وهذا في الجملة أشهر من أن يمكن إنكاره وقد قال الله تعالى: ﴿أَلَمْ تَرَكَيْفَ فَعَلَ رَبُّكَ بِأَصْحَب الْفِيلِ ﴾ إلى آخر السورة، ولم يعارضه أحد من المشركين بالتكذيب والرد لعلمهم بحقيقة ذلك بل قد كان بقى بعض من عاين ذلك الأمر ثم إن بلدة هذا شأنها أولى البلدان والبقاع بخروج من يدعو الخلق إلى الحق والدهماء إلى المحجة البيضاء، فكيف يستجير عاقل مقر بنبوة من خرج من مكانه لا مزية له على غيره من الأمكنة، ولا ظهر في الخلق له آية بينة تدل على حرمتها عند الله تعالى وكرامتها عليه أن ينسب الخارج عن البلدة المشهور شرفها المذكور فخرها الظاهر آياتها البين علاماتها إلى الكذب والافتعال والدعوة الظاهر عنها إلى التقول والمحال، وهذا واضح لمن ترك العناد وسلك سبيل الرشاد.
And among those intended from the Abyssinians, they were from the People of the Book, believers in Moses and Jesus, peace be upon them, adhering to their two books: the Torah and the Gospel. So do not think that their destruction was for the support of one whose state was that; rather, that was an initial sign (irhāṣ) for the matter of His chosen Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him, since his time was near and the time of his emergence and the appearance of his call had drawn close. So Allah Almighty protected the fathers to honor the one whom Allah Almighty knew would emerge from their loins, from among the supporters of the call of the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, and followers of his religion, and adherents of his faith and law. And He protected His Prophet, whom in His prior knowledge and decree [He willed] to be a qibla for his nation and a place of sacrifice and resort for the people of his religion. Then indeed the denial of the matter of the Elephant and its destruction is not possible for the people of atheism, because it is established by tawātur (mass transmission), in which the suspicion of falsehood cannot occur, like the establishment of all other past matters in previous eras whose denier is attributed to obstinacy and stubbornness. And the poets mentioned that in their odes, and the orators in their sermons. Among that is the saying of Nufayl ibn Habīb, who was a pre-Islamic (Jāhilī) poet: ... [verses omitted in translation] ... And Ṭufayl, who was a Jāhilī, said: ... [verses] ... And Abū al-Ṣalt Umayya ibn Abī al-Ṣalt said: ... [verses] ... And this, in brief, is more famous than that its denial can be possible. And Allah Almighty said: {Have you not seen how your Lord dealt with the Companions of the Elephant?} to the end of the sūrah. And none of the polytheists opposed it with denial and rejection, because of their knowledge of its reality. Rather, some of those who witnessed that event remained. Then indeed a town with such a state is the most deserving of towns and regions for the emergence of one who calls creation to truth and the masses to the white path. So how can a sane person who acknowledges the prophethood of one who emerged from a place that has no merit over other places, and no clear sign appeared among creation indicating its sanctity before Allah Almighty and its honor with Him, attribute the one who emerges from the town whose honor is famous, whose glory is mentioned, whose signs are apparent, whose marks are clear, to falsehood and fabrication, and his call apparent from it to false talk and impossibility? And this is clear for one who abandons obstinacy and follows the path of right guidance.

Commentary
This passage is a proof based on the historical event of the Elephant (the Abyssinian invasion of Mecca). Imam Nasafi argues that the destruction of the Abyssinian army was a divine miracle preparing for the prophethood of Muhammad (peace be upon him). He begins by noting that the Abyssinians were Christians (People of the Book), yet they were destroyed—not because of any sin, but as a sign (irhāṣ) for the coming Prophet. The destruction protected the lineage of the Prophet's supporters. Then he argues that the event is undeniable because it is transmitted by tawātur (mass consensus), just like other well-known past events. He supports this with poetry from pre-Islamic poets (like Nufayl, Ṭufayl, and Umayya) who mention the Elephant. He also cites the Qur'ānic sūrah al-Fīl, which the polytheists did not reject. Then he presents the main conclusion: since Mecca is so honored by this miracle, it is the perfect place for the emergence of a prophet calling to truth. The prophet from such a city cannot be accused of falsehood. The passage ends by stating that this is clear to anyone who abandons obstinacy.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some might think the Elephant event was just a natural incident (e.g., an epidemic) without divine significance.
Correction: The author emphasizes it was a deliberate divine sign (irhāṣ) preparing for prophethood, as evidenced by its transmission and the Qur'anic acknowledgment.

Key Takeaway
The Elephant event stands as an undeniable miracle because it was witnessed, transmitted by countless narrators, and explicitly cited in the Qur’ān without any denial from the polytheists, thereby proving the sanctity of Mecca and the truth of the Prophet who emerged from it.
