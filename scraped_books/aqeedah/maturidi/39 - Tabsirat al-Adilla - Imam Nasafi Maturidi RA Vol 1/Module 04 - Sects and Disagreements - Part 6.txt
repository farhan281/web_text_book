The Mu'tazila's Qur'anic Proof Against the Vision of Allah
Author reports the Mu'tazila's claim and their Qur'anic proof against vision of Allah, along with their argument that the negation of perception is a self-praise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila's argument that the verse 'لا تدركه الأبصار' proves the impossibility of seeing Allah in both this world and the Hereafter, and why they claim it would negate Allah's self-praise if vision were possible.

Core Idea
The negation of vision is a self-praise that would be meaningless if vision were possible.

The opponents of the vision of Allah, including the Mu'tazila, Najjariyya, and Zaydiyya, argue that Allah's statement 'Eyesight does not perceive Him' (6:103) is a self-praise that negates vision absolutely. They reason that if vision were possible, even only in the Hereafter, this negation would not be praise because it would only deny a permissible thing. This lesson unpacks their proof and the underlying logic.

Guided Path
1
Understand the opponents' claim
Read the passage where the Mu'tazila etc. assert that vision of Allah is impossible in both worlds because impossibility does not change with time or place.
They base this on the assumption that vision would imply something impossible for Allah.
2
Analyze the Qur'anic proof
Focus on the verse 'لا تدركه الأبصار' and how the opponents argue it is a self-praise that negates vision absolutely, similar to other self-praises like 'بديع السموات والأرض'.
They use the context of the verse to claim it establishes perfection by negating what is unbefitting.
3
Reflect on the logical implication
Consider why the opponents argue that if vision were possible in the Hereafter, the negation would not be praise. This is because negation of a permissible thing is not a source of praise. Therefore, the verse must indicate impossibility in both worlds.
This is the core of their argument.

This is because what is in the category of permissibility, its existence does not necessitate deficiency, so its negation is not praise. Commentary
This passage reports the position of the Mu'tazila, Najjariyya, and Zaydi Rawafid that vision of Allah is impossible in both this world and the Hereafter. They argue that what is impossible does not become possible over time or place. The author notes that the opponents mistakenly think affirming vision would lead to affirming impossibility for Allah, while the truth is otherwise. Then the author presents the opponents' first proof: the Quranic verse "Eyesight does not perceive Him" (6:103). They argue that Allah praises Himself by negating vision, just as He praises Himself with His beautiful names and high attributes in the same context. Thus, the verse affirms perfection attributes and negates what is unbefitting. If one were to affirm what Allah negated, it would remove the praise and affirm deficiency, which is analogous to negating what He affirmed in other verses like "Originator of the heavens and the earth" etc. The argument then explains that such a deficiency would imply temporality (huduth), which is impossible for Allah. The author then refutes the possibility that negation of vision applies only to this world by arguing that if vision were possible in the Hereafter, Allah's self-praise for negating it in this world would be meaningless, because negation of a permissible thing is not praise, just as one does not praise himself for not having a son (since that is permissible). Therefore, the verse indicates that vision is impossible in both worlds.

The Mu'tazila Argument Against Vision of Allah
Mu'tazila rational argument from analogy against vision of Allah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila's argument that vision requires a body, and why they concluded that Allah—being not a body—cannot be seen.

Core Idea
Visibility requires bodiliness, so Allah—being not a body—cannot be seen.

The Mu'tazila and others who deny the vision of Allah relied on a rational argument based on analogy from the visible world. In this passage, the author presents their reasoning: vision in the witnessed world has a cause and conditions that are specific to bodies, and since Allah is not a body, the ruling of visibility cannot be transferred to Him. Let's examine their argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understanding the Principle of Analogy
Learn that inference from the witnessed (shahid) to the unseen (gha'ib) requires that the cause (illa) of the ruling be transferable. If the cause is specific to the witnessed, the ruling cannot extend.
This principle is key to the Mu'tazila's argument.
2
Identifying the Cause of Visibility
The passage states that the cause of a thing being visible in the witness is its being a body (jism). Every body is seen, but what lies beyond bodies (accidents) cannot be seen.
Visibility is tied to bodiliness.
3
Applying the Principle to Allah
Since Allah is not a body, the cause of visibility does not apply to Him. Therefore, the ruling of visibility cannot be transferred from witness to unseen.
This is the core conclusion.
4
Noting Additional Views
The passage mentions al-Nazzam and his followers, who only allow vision of bodies, and some philosophers who restrict vision to colors. These support the main argument.
These are supplementary positions.

Commentary
This passage presents the rational proof of the Mu'tazila and others who deny the vision of Allah. It begins by stating the logical principle: the cause (illa) and conditions of vision in the witnessed world (al-shahid) cannot be transferred to the unseen (al-gha'ib). The method of inference (istidlal) from the witness to the unseen requires that the cause be transferable; if it is not, the ruling (hukm) does not transfer. The passage then identifies the cause of visibility in the witness: a thing is visible because it is a body (jism). Since every body is seen, but what lies beyond bodies—namely accidents (a'rad)—cannot be seen, and because the body itself cannot be extended to Allah (who is not a body), the ruling of visibility cannot be extended. The passage then mentions al-Nazzam and his followers among the Mu'tazila, who held that only bodies are seen in the witness, and therefore it is impossible to see what is beyond bodies (i.e., non-bodily entities). They rely on empirical observation. Some philosophers further restrict vision only to colors. This argument aims to show that since Allah is not a body, He cannot be seen.

Views on Visible Objects in the Witnessed World
Enumeration of views on visible objects by al-Nazzam, philosophers, al-Najjariyya, al-Jubba'i, and Abu Hashim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the different positions of Mu'tazili and early theologians on what objects are visible in the witnessed world, and why none of these positions imply that God can be seen.

Core Idea
Different groups agreed on the impossibility of seeing God, but disagreed on what can be seen in the world.

In this passage, Imam al-Nasafi presents a detailed overview of several theological schools and their views on which objects are perceptible by sight. Al-Nazzam, al-Jubba'i, Abu Hashim, and others each had distinct criteria for visibility. Understanding their arguments helps clarify why they all concluded that God—being beyond bodies, accidents, and spatial constraints—cannot be seen.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage identifying the different groups
Carefully read the Arabic and translation to identify the views of al-Nazzam, the philosophers, al-Najjariyya, al-Jubba'i, and Abu Hashim. Note what each considers visible.
Pay attention to how each group restricts visibility differently.
2
Compare the restrictiveness of each view
Order the views from most restrictive (only bodies) to least (bodies, colors, and spatial determinations). Consider which view allows the most objects to be seen.
Al-Jubba'i allows the most; Abu Hashim excludes spatial determinations.
3
Notice the common conclusion about the unseen
All groups agree that none of these visible categories transfer to the unseen (God). Reflect on why this is significant for the debate on seeing God.
The passage explicitly states: 'nothing of that transfers to the unseen'.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed enumeration of the views of various theologians on what objects are visible in the witnessed world. It begins with al-Nazzam's view that only bodies are visible; everything beyond bodies (accidents) is not visible. Some philosophers restrict vision to colors. Al-Najjariyya even deny the visibility of bodies and only affirm colors. Al-Jubba'i expands visibility to include certain accidents: colors and spatial determinations (akwan: motion, rest, etc.). Abu Hashim excludes spatial determinations, allowing only bodies and colors. The author then notes that despite these differences, all agree that none of these visible objects (bodies, colors, etc.) transfer to the unseen, so the judgment of visibility does not apply to God. The passage then explains the conditions of vision: distance between seer and seen, and according to al-Nazzam, the necessity of contact (mumassa) between the sense organ and the object. Al-Nazzam's theory of perception is described in detail: all senses require physical contact, with the sense organ receiving particles from the object (e.g., light rays from the eye). Since these conditions cannot be met in the case of God (who is not a body, not at a distance, etc.), vision of God is impossible.

The author declares this interpretation corrupt and provides five arguments. First: The wording of the request uses 'ilayka' (to You), not 'ilayha' (to it). If Musa had asked for a sign, he would have said 'look at it'. Second: The response 'You will never see Me' would have been 'You will never see My sign'. Third: If the request was for a sign, then the response 'You will never see Me' would contradict the fact that Allah did show him a great sign (the mountain crumbling). Fourth: Musa had already seen many miraculous signs, making a request for another sign unnecessary and inappropriate for a prophet. Fifth: The conditional statement 'if it remains in its place, then you will see Me' proves that the vision is of Allah Himself, not a sign, because the sign (the crumbling) occurs when the mountain crumbles, not when it remains. The author adds that Musa already knew Allah intimately through speech and conversation, so asking for a proof of existence would be absurd.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite claim that God's vision of things is identical to His knowledge. Nasafi begins by recalling a previous argument: there is no physical distance or medium between God and us, so the Mu'tazilite objection based on bodily vision fails. He then notes that the Mu'tazilites split: al-Nazzam and al-Ka'bi denied that God sees anything at all, while others interpreted God's attribute 'seeing' as merely knowing, making vision a kind of knowledge. Nasafi argues that if vision were identical to knowledge, then the conditions required for physical vision (like distance, a ray, an impression on an organ) would not be relevant, but they are actually conditions of vision, not knowledge. Furthermore, he provides three proofs that vision and knowledge are distinct. (2) Locus proof: vision resides in the eye, knowledge in the heart; a single attribute cannot occupy two different loci. (3) Opposite proof: the opposite of vision is blindness, and the opposite of knowledge is ignorance; different opposites indicate different things. Nasafi concludes that vision and knowledge are two distinct entities, as established in the witnessed world, and this refutes the Mu'tazilite identification.

The Mu'tazila Argument Against Saintly Miracles
Mu'tazila argument for impossibility of karamat with two reasons.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila rational argument for why saintly miracles (karamat) are impossible.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila argue karamat are impossible because they would confuse with prophetic miracles.

The Mu'tazila, known for their rationalist theology, argued that saintly miracles cannot occur. Their reasoning is based on the need to preserve the distinctiveness of prophetic miracles (mu'jizat). This passage presents their two main arguments, grounded in the purpose of miraculous events.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazila claim
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how the Mu'tazila state their position clearly: they deem karamat impossible.
The opening phrase 'فزعمت المعتزلة أنه ممتنع' sets the tone.
2
Identify the first argument
The first reason is that if karamat were possible, they would be confused with prophetic miracles (mu'jizat), making it impossible to know who is a true prophet.
This argument hinges on the necessity of distinguishing prophets from non-prophets.
3
Understand the second argument
The second reason is that breaking the natural order (naqd al-'ada) is only beneficial when there is a pressing need, such as identifying a prophet. Since there is no such need for recognizing saints, karamat serve no purpose and thus are impossible.
The Mu'tazila view miracles as purposeful signs, not arbitrary displays.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider how this argument reflects the Mu'tazila emphasis on rational coherence and the function of miracles as evidence. How does this differ from the Sunni position that karamat are possible?
This prepares for later lessons on the Sunni response.

Commentary
This passage presents the Mu'tazila argument for the impossibility of karamat (saintly miracles). It begins by stating their claim that karamat are impossible. They give two reasons. First, if karamat were possible, they would be indistinguishable from mu'jizat (prophetic miracles), and then there would be no way to confirm someone's prophethood. This is unacceptable. Second, breaking the natural order (the customary course of events) is only justified when there is a great benefit: enabling people to recognize a true prophet and distinguish him from false claimants. People urgently need this. In contrast, there is no such pressing need to recognize saints (awliya) from non-saints. Therefore, according to the Mu'tazila, God would not grant such extraordinary events to saints, as they serve no necessary purpose.

The Deprivation of Karamat as Proof of Error: The Case of the Mu'tazila
The author argues that Mu'tazila lack of karamat shows error of their beliefs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Mu'tazila's lack of karamat, despite their apparent piety, is presented as evidence for the corruption of their beliefs.

Core Idea
Deprivation of karamat despite piety signals doctrinal corruption.

The author now turns to a practical argument against the Mu'tazila: if their beliefs were true, why do they never experience the miracles of saints that are common among Ahl al-Sunna? Their very earnestness in worship, combined with the absence of karamat, points to a deeper spiritual malady. We will explore this argument and the accompanying description of their outward state.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully

The author claims that the Mu'tazila deny karamat and are deprived of them due to the ill fortune of their innovation.
3
Examine the conditional argument
The author says even if there were no other evidence, their deprivation of karamat despite devotion suffices to show their error. This is a powerful rhetorical move.
This is an argument from absence.
4
Consider the description of their appearance
The passage ends with a vivid description: a sickly yellowness that repels sight, and burdensome company. What does this add to the argument?
It serves as empirical evidence of their spiritual state.
5
Reflect on the theological significance
Karamat are considered signs of God's favor; their absence here indicates divine displeasure. How does this support the Sunni position?
This is a supplementary argument, not a primary one.

Al-Ash'ari's View on the Servant as Acquirer
Al-Ash'ari's view on servant's acquisition and agency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Ash'ari's distinctive position that the servant is called an acquirer and doer but not an agent, and why he reserved the term 'agent' exclusively for Allah.

Core Idea
Al-Ash'ari distinguishes between acquisition and agency—only Allah is the true agent.

This lesson introduces al-Ash'ari's unique contribution to the debate on human action. While most sects agreed that the servant has an act, al-Ash'ari drew a sharp line: the servant acquires and acts, but cannot be called an agent (fa'il). We examine this distinction and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on al-Ash'ari
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse. Then read the translation. Let the author's words come through.
Notice that al-Ash'ari agrees the servant has an act but withholds the title 'agent'.
2
Identify the key terms
Find the terms muktasib (acquirer), `amil (doer), and fa'il (agent) in the passage. Understand how they are used.
3
Understand why al-Ash'ari denied the servant the title of agent
Reflect on why al-Ash'ari reserved 'agent' for Allah. Think about the theological commitment to divine omnipotence.
4
Reflect on the difference between acquisition and creation
Consider how acquisition differs from creation. The servant acquires an act that God creates.

The Qadariyya on the Origin of Human Actions
Historical development of Qadariyya on creation of actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the evolution of the Qadariyya position from early moderates to later extremists like al-Jubba'i and al-Basri.

Core Idea
The Qadariyya increasingly claimed the servant is the true creator of his own actions.

This lesson traces the Qadariyya's stance on human action. They began by affirming the servant as originator but avoiding the term 'creator'. Over time, figures like al-Jubba'i and al-Basri explicitly asserted that the servant is the creator, challenging the orthodox position.

3
Analyse the shift in terminology
Early Qadariyya said 'originator' but not 'creator'. Al-Jubba'i equated them. Al-Basri said the servant is creator and Allah is creator metaphorically.
4
Compare with the orthodox view
Think about how this contrasts with the Ahl al-Haqq view that Allah is the true creator.

The Disagreement on Capability and Its Opposite
Al-Nasafi reports three positions on whether capability applies to both opposites.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three major positions on whether capability (istita'a) is suitable for both opposites and the reasoning behind each.

Core Idea
Scholars disagree: capability for an act may or may not be suitable for its opposite—with profound implications for human agency.

In this passage, al-Nasafi reports a crucial debate in kalam about the nature of human capability. The question is whether the same capability that brings about an act is also capable of bringing about its opposite. Three distinct answers emerge, each with significant implications for how we understand human agency and divine power. By examining these positions, we gain insight into the theological differences that shaped early Islamic thought.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three groups
Read the passage and list the three distinct opinions reported by al-Nasafi. Note who holds each position and the key terminological distinctions (e.g., before the act vs. with the act).
The groups are: those who say capability precedes the act (Mu'tazila), those who say it is with the act (Abu Hanifa, Ash'aris, etc.).
2
Understand the Mu'tazila view
The Mu'tazila argue that capability is like a tool (e.g., a hand) that can be used for either opposite action. They hold this because they consider capability to exist before the act.
3
Explore Abu Hanifa's 'alternation'
Abu Hanifa agrees that capability is suitable for opposites, but only in a sequential sense (badal). The specific instance of capability, once connected to an act, is not simultaneously suitable for the opposite; but if it had been connected differently, it would have been suitable.
This avoids the problem of one capability producing two contradictory acts at once.
4
Analyze the Ash'ari rejection
The Ash'aris and most ahl al-hadith theologians deny that the same capability can be for both opposites. They assign a distinct capability to each act (e.g., capability for faith is different from capability for disbelief).
This position emphasizes divine omnipotence and the specificity of created capabilities.
5
Reflect on theological implications
Consider how each view relates to broader doctrines of free will, divine causality, and moral responsibility. Which view best supports human accountability? Which best safeguards God's omnipotence?
This connects to ongoing debates in kalam about qadar and 'adl.

Commentary
This passage presents a key disagreement in kalam about whether the capability (istita'a) for an action is also suitable for its opposite. Al-Nasafi first notes that all those who hold that capability precedes the act (mainly the Mu'tazila) affirm that it is suitable for both opposites, just as physical instruments (e.g., a hand) can be used for either action. Then he turns to those who hold that capability is simultaneous with the act, and reports Abu Hanifa's view: capability is suitable for opposites 'by way of alternation' (badal). That means a particular instance of capability, once it is actually connected to a specific act, is not simultaneously suitable for its opposite; but if it had been connected to the opposite instead, it would have been suitable for that opposite. This avoids the problem of one capability producing two contradictory acts at once. Al-Nasafi lists supporters: Ibn al-Rawandi, al-Qalanisi, and Ibn Surayj. Then he presents the opposing view of the Ash'aris and most theologians of ahl al-hadith: capability is specific to its act and not suitable for its opposite. They deny that the same capability can be for both faith and disbelief; each act has its own distinct capability. Al-Nasafi ends by attributing this position also to al-Husayn al-Najjar.

Next, the author poses a dilemma to the Karramiyya: if God commands motions and wills to 'subsist', what prevents Him from doing so? The opponent claims subsistence of motions and wills is impossible, but the author demands a distinction between these and other accidents, which the opponent cannot provide. Finally, the author directly addresses the Karramiyya: when God says to an essence 'subsist!', does that essence subsist with a subsistence or without it? If without, it is impossible (since everything that exists requires an attribute to persist). If with a subsistence, then the question is whether that subsistence is an additional meaning (maʿnan zāʾid) over the essence or merely refers back to it. If they say it is additional, they concede the truth and their own doctrine falls because they allow accidents (like subsistence) to qualify other accidents, which is impossible. If they say it refers back to the essence and the essence is subsistent by itself without any added meaning, the author promises to refute this later. Thus, the Karramiyya position is shown to be contradictory.

The dispute about this is: is the existence of action together with power impossible or not? And is the existence of power at the moment of action obligatory or not? And is there or is there not an intermediate between power and incapacity? So whoever wants to make the action, which is evidence of the concomitance of power to it according to his opponents, into evidence of the negation of power has reversed the evidence and made evidence of existence into evidence of non-existence without any proof except his desire to validate his statement. Likewise, if he makes that evidence for the existence of the intermediate, he has reversed the evidence, for it is evidence of the existence of power, not evidence of its removal. For this reason, action is evidence of the power of the agent, not evidence of its removal. Then we say to them: Is the existence of action from the incapable impossible due to the existence of incapacity or due to the absence of power? If they say: due to the existence of incapacity, they are told: it should be that action from inanimate objects and dead things is not impossible, because they lack incapacity. And it should be that action is not evidence of the agent being powerful but evidence that he is not incapable, especially according to your view when there is an intermediate between them in your opinion. All of that is corrupt.

Commentary
This passage refutes the Mu'tazilite claim that there is a middle state between power (qudra) and incapacity ('ajz). Nasafi begins by stating that the opponent posits an intermediate between two things that logically cannot have an intermediate. The opponent bases this on his view that action (fi'l) is impossible when either power or incapacity exists, so the existence of action indicates the negation of both power and incapacity simultaneously. Nasafi points out that the dispute is about three questions: whether action with power is impossible, whether power must exist at the moment of action, and whether there is an intermediate. He then argues that if someone uses the action (which is evidence of the coexistence of power) as evidence for the negation of power, they have reversed the evidence. Similarly, using it to prove the intermediate is also a reversal, because action is evidence of power, not its removal. Nasafi then poses a question to the opponent: Is the impossibility of action from the incapable due to the presence of incapacity or the absence of power? If due to incapacity, then action should be possible from inanimate objects (which lack incapacity), and action would only indicate the absence of incapacity, not the presence of power—which is absurd. Therefore, the opponent's position is invalid.
