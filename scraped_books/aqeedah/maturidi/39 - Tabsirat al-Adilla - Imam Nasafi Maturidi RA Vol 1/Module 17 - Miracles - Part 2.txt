Nasafi then refutes a potential objection: that the Arabs refrained from challenging out of pride or fear of losing face. He argues that they were fiercely proud and would risk everything to protect their honor, and that they indeed spent lives in bitter satire. Their failure to produce anything like the Quran shows they were unable, not unwilling. The passage concludes that they exerted all efforts to extinguish the Prophet's message (لإطفاء نوره وإبطال منهم).

Commentary
This passage is an extended argument for the miraculous nature of the Quran, specifically its inimitability (i'jaz). The author refutes the claim that the Arabs' failure to produce a similar text was due to fear of punishment rather than inability. And from this it follows that the violations of custom are of four types: mu'jiza, karama, ma'una, and ihana, and the distinction between them is as we have explained.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed classification and distinction of four types of supernatural events (violations of custom) that can appear: mu'jiza (miracle of a prophet), karama (gift of honor for a saint/ wali), ma'una (divine aid for an ordinary believer), and ihana (degradation for an impostor or magician). The author first addresses an objection that these types might be confused. He explains that a mu'jiza always accompanies a claim of prophethood and is openly displayed, whereas a karama appears for a wali who does not claim prophethood; if the wali claimed prophethood he would become a disbeliever and no miracle would appear. The wali hides his karama and fears it might be a trap (istidraj). The possessor of a mu'jiza is divinely protected from alteration, while the wali is not. The author then refutes the objection that these events have no benefit, listing several benefits: they confirm God's honor for the wali, strengthen believers' faith in the prophethood of their prophet, motivate the wali to increase worship and avoid sins, and inspire others to strive for that rank. Next, he introduces ma'una as a sudden supernatural help for an ordinary Muslim in distress, and ihana as a supernatural event at the hand of a false claimant or magician, which degrades them. He cites a report from Abu Hanifa about a man afflicted by magic, which is considered ihana. The passage concludes by enumerating the four types and stating that the distinctions have been clarified.

The Four Types of Breaks in Custom

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Not all miraculous events are miracles; they reveal the spiritual rank of the person.
