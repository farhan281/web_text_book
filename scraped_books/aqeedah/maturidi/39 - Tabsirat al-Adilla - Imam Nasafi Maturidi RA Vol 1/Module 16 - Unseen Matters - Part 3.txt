Four Differences Between Kufr and Other Sins
The author enumerates four differences between kufr and other sins.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four rational distinctions the Maturidi school makes between kufr and lesser sins regarding forgiveness and punishment.

Core Idea
Kufr is permanently chosen disbelief; other sins are fleeting acts with redeeming good deeds.

The author explains why divine wisdom demands a distinction between the disbeliever and the believing sinner. He presents four arguments showing that kufr is fundamentally different: the absence of accompanying good deeds, its permanent nature as a belief, its incompatibility with permissibility, and the immense favors God grants to the believer even when he sins. Let us examine these distinctions.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once for rhythm, then the literal translation. Get an overall sense of the four differences.
Notice the Quranic verses that set up the need for a distinction.
2
Understand the context: why the distinction is needed
Identify the two groups mentioned: those who allow forgiveness for disbelievers (mutakallimūn of Ahl al-Ḥadīth) and those who insist on divine wisdom requiring a difference. Focus on the latter's Quranic proof.
3
Analyze the first two differences
The first difference contrasts the presence of good deeds (fear, hope, trust) in the believer with their absence in the disbeliever. The second difference contrasts the permanence of kufr as a belief with the temporariness of other sins.
Both differences show that the believer's situation is not entirely evil.
4
Analyze the third and fourth differences
The third difference states that kufr itself does not admit permissibility (ibāḥa), so its punishment is unforgivable. The fourth difference highlights the immense favors God bestows on the believer who sins, making it unjust to waste them.
5
Reflect on the overall significance
Summarize how these four differences establish that kufr is unique in both its nature and its punishment. Consider how this shapes Maturidi theology.
This lesson touches on the principle of divine wisdom (ḥikma) and justice.

[The difference between kufr and other sins]: Then there is no need for the distinction between kufr and other sins concerning the permissibility of forgiveness, punishment, eternal stay in the Fire, and non-eternity for those who hold the opinion of the mutakallimūn of the people of hadith regarding the permissibility of forgiveness for the disbeliever. But as for those of our companions who say that wisdom requires making a distinction between the wrongdoer and the doer of good, due to God Most High rejecting equality between them in His saying: 'Or do We make those who believe and do righteous deeds like the corrupters in the earth? Or do We make the righteous like the wicked?' and His saying: 'Or do those who commit evil deeds think that We will make them like those who believe and do righteous deeds, [so that] their life and death are equal? Evil is that which they judge.' and His saying: 'Or do We make the righteous like the criminals? What is [the matter] with you? How do you judge?' Then there is no distinction between the two groups in this world, so it is necessary that they be distinguished in the Hereafter; thus these need the distinction between kufr and what is lesser than it among sins concerning the permissibility of forgiveness for what is less than kufr and its impossibility in it, and the establishment of eternal punishment for it, and its removal for what is less than it. And the difference is that there is no one who disobeys God by any kind of major sins other than shirk except that at the time of disobedience he is acquiring acts of obedience from fear of His punishment, hope for His mercy, and trust in His generosity; and that is good deeds which if they were compared with what he committed of opposition due to overwhelming appetite, overpowering anger, or the like, the good in him would outweigh the evil in him. So it is not permissible to deprive him of the benefit of good and impose upon him the punishment of evil. And the one who disbelieves in God Most High and associates partners with Him has no meaning deserving the name of good because he denies Him and rejects His command and prohibition. The second: that kufr is a belief held forever, since beliefs are held forever; therefore its punishment is for all times, while other major sins are at moments when desire dominates, not forever; rather in the belief of everyone who commits them is the resolve to repent from them if he were given a respite and extended delay; therefore their punishment is [only] at those times. The third: that kufr itself does not admit of permissibility and removal of its prohibition; therefore its punishment in wisdom does not admit of removal and forgiveness, unlike other sins. The fourth: that God Most High has bestowed favor upon the possessor of a major sin in religion, at the time when he was harsh, by making His rights greater in his heart than the two worlds, and making His prophets and messengers, peace be upon them, more exalted in his chest than that his soul could allow the desecration of a hair of theirs or reliance on any of their enemies from what he has chosen and preferred of opposition. So it is not permissible in wisdom that this favor be wasted due to a harshness whose measure among sins does not reach a fraction of the countless favors, unlike the disbeliever. And God is the Giver of success.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the differences between kufr and other major sins regarding divine forgiveness and punishment. The author begins by noting that for those who hold the opinion of the mutakallimūn of Ahl al-Ḥadīth that even a disbeliever can be forgiven, there is no need for such a distinction. However, for those (like the author's own school) who insist that divine wisdom requires differentiating between the evildoer and the righteous, as God Himself rebukes equating them in several Quranic verses (38:28, 45:21, 68:35-36), and since there is no full distinction in this world, there must be a distinction in the Hereafter. These scholars thus need to distinguish between kufr and lesser sins regarding the possibility of forgiveness and eternal punishment. Then the author lists four specific differences: The first difference: A believer who commits a major sin other than shirk still, at the time of sin, acquires acts of obedience such as fear of punishment, hope for mercy, and trust in God's generosity. These good deeds outweigh the sin when compared, so it is unjust to deprive him of the benefit of his good deeds and punish him solely for the evil. But the one who commits kufr has no such good deeds because he denies God's commands and prohibitions. The second difference: Kufr is a belief (madhhab) held permanently; beliefs are held permanently. Therefore its punishment is eternal. Other major sins are committed at specific moments due to overwhelming desire, and the sinner intends to repent if given respite, so their punishment is not eternal. The third difference: Kufr itself does not admit of permissibility (ibāḥa) or removal of prohibition; therefore its punishment in wisdom cannot be removed or forgiven, unlike other sins. The fourth difference: God has bestowed great favor upon the sinner who still believes in Him, by making the rights of God and His prophets immense in his heart, such that he would not allow himself to violate even a hair of their rights or side with their enemies. It would be against divine wisdom to waste this favor due to a sin whose weight is negligible compared to the countless blessings. This is not the case for the disbeliever.

The Logical Basis for Intercession
A brief logical argument linking intercession to forgiveness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Maturidi argument for intercession of major sinners is built on the prior possibility of forgiveness.

Core Idea
Intercession is a channel of forgiveness, not a separate divine concession.

In this lesson, we examine a concise passage from Imam Nasafi’s Tabsirat al-Adilla. The author argues that intercession for major sinners is not a new issue but follows logically from the established fact that Allah may forgive major sins by His grace. This short argument clarifies that if forgiveness is possible and wise, then intercession as a means to that forgiveness is also possible.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the author presents a condensed logical chain.
2
Follow the conditional reasoning
Identify the author's 'if… then…' structure: if forgiveness is allowed, then intercession is allowed. Understand that this ties intercession directly to the established principle of divine grace and wisdom.
The author considers intercession as a subset of forgiveness.
3
Reflect on the list of intercessors
The author mentions various categories: prophets, parents, children, relatives, teachers, students. Ask why the list is so broad. This shows that intercession is not limited but open to many righteous individuals.
This underscores the mercy of Allah in providing multiple avenues of forgiveness.

Commentary
This brief passage argues for the permissibility of intercession (shafa'a) for those who have committed major sins. The author states that this issue is essentially the same as the earlier issue of forgiveness. Since it is established that God may forgive a major sinner by His grace and mercy, and that forgiveness is in accordance with wisdom, it follows that He may also forgive through the intercession of prophets, righteous people, and others. The logic is: if forgiveness is possible, then intercession, which is a means of obtaining forgiveness, is also possible. The passage lists various categories of intercessors including prophets, parents, children, relatives, teachers, and students.

Intercession: An Extension of Divine Mercy
Al-Nasafi affirms the possibility of intercession for major sinners.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that intercession for major sinners is a logical extension of Allah's mercy, not a separate doctrine.

Core Idea
Intercession is a channel of Allah's mercy, not a distinct power.

In this passage, Imam al-Nasafi connects the doctrine of intercession with the earlier discussion of forgiveness of major sins. He argues that since Allah can forgive by His mercy directly, it is equally possible for Him to forgive through the intercession of prophets and righteous people. This shows that intercession is not a separate innovation but a natural outcome of divine mercy.

The Mu'tazilite Denial of Intercession for Major Sinners
Al-Nasafi recounts six Mu'tazilite proofs against intercession for major sinners.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the six arguments the Mu'tazila present to deny intercession for unrepentant major sinners.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila argue that intercession only helps the deserving, not the defiant sinner.

In this passage, Imam al-Nasafi presents the Mu'tazilite position on intercession. Before refuting it, he lays out their proofs fairly. These arguments rely on Quranic verses, rational consistency, and practical consequences. Understanding them is essential for appreciating the Sunni response.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazilite claim and first verse proof
Start by reading the passage's opening statement that intercession for the major sinner is impossible, like intercession for a disbeliever. Then note their first proof from the verse 'They do not intercede except for whom He approves.'
The Mu'tazila equate the major sinner with the disbeliever in terms of deserving punishment.
2
Examine the second verse proof about wrongdoers
Read the second proof: 'For the wrongdoers there is no intimate friend and no intercessor to be obeyed.' Identify how they apply this to the major sinner, e.g., the killer of a soul.
They define 'wrongdoer' broadly to include all major sinners.
3
Analyze the third proof from the angelic prayer
Read the verse about angels asking forgiveness for believers who repented. The Mu'tazila argue this shows intercession is only for the deserving, not the unrepentant.
They reason that asking for the undeserving would be like asking God to make enemies His friends.
4
Consider the practical, legal, and consequential arguments
Examine the remaining proofs: (a) Muslims desire intercession and avoid sin, proving sinners don't get it; (b) an oath to do a deed deserving intercession commands obedience, not sin; (c) claiming intercession for major sinners emboldens sin.
These arguments appeal to observable behavior and legal reasoning.

Commentary
This passage presents the Mu'tazilite objections against intercession for the major sinner. It begins by stating that according to the Mu'tazila, since forgiveness without intercession is impossible (because they hold that the major sinner must be punished and cannot be forgiven unless he repents, and intercession is not a factor), then intercession for such a person is like intercession for a disbeliever—impossible. (5) A legal argument: one who swears to do an act deserving intercession is commanded to do righteous deeds, not sinful ones, showing that intercession is for the obedient. (6) A consequentialist argument: claiming intercession for major sinners emboldens people to sin, which is false. Al-Nasafi presents these as the objections to be refuted in the following section.

Commentary
This passage presents and reconciles two versions of a hadith about the pillars of Islam. The author first gives two chains of transmission: one through Abu Hafs al-Bazzar and another through al-Mas'udi, both reaching Ibn Umar, who reported that the Prophet was asked about 'the laws of Islam' (shara'i' al-Islam) and answered listing prayer, zakat, etc. The author then notes that Imam al-Maturidi reported a version where the Prophet was first asked about faith (iman) and then about the laws of Islam. The core of the passage is the reconciliation of these variants. The author offers several explanations: (1) Some narrators may not have heard the word 'laws' in the original question, so they simply reported 'Islam'. This is preferable to accusing other narrators of adding the word deliberately, because trustworthy narrators (thiqat) are presumed not to fabricate additions, though they may omit something. (2) A narrator might have omitted the term 'laws' because he knew no one would confuse the second question as being about generic Islam rather than its specific laws, since faith and Islam are inseparable. (3) The narrator may have implicitly understood the word 'laws' and used 'Islam' in its place, as is a common Arabic rhetorical device (iqamah al-mudaf ilayh muqam al-mudaf), giving the Quranic example of 'ask the village' where 'village' stands for 'people of the village'. The author supports this by arguing that the answer given is clearly about the laws (shara'i'), not about the essence of Islam, because one can have those practices without the faith mentioned in the first answer (but that would not make one a true Muslim) and one can lack those practices while having faith (but that would not make one a non-Muslim). Therefore, the answer is about the laws. Finally, he hints at another possibility: that the phrase 'Islam' was used but intended to include the laws by way of inclusion (bi tariq...), but the text cuts off. Throughout, the author prioritizes maintaining the integrity of all narrators and offering rational and linguistic explanations for the variation.

Commentary
This passage presents and reconciles two versions of a hadith about the pillars of Islam. The author first gives two chains of transmission: one through Abu Hafs al-Bazzar and another through al-Mas'udi, both reaching Ibn Umar, who reported that the Prophet was asked about 'the laws of Islam' (shara'i' al-Islam) and answered listing prayer, zakat, etc. The author then notes that Imam al-Maturidi reported a version where the Prophet was first asked about faith (iman) and then about the laws of Islam. The core of the passage is the reconciliation of these variants. The author offers several explanations: (1) Some narrators may not have heard the word 'laws' in the original question, so they simply reported 'Islam'. This is preferable to accusing other narrators of adding the word deliberately, because trustworthy narrators (thiqat) are presumed not to fabricate additions, though they may omit something. (2) A narrator might have omitted the term 'laws' because he knew no one would confuse the second question as being about generic Islam rather than its specific laws, since faith and Islam are inseparable. (3) The narrator may have implicitly understood the word 'laws' and used 'Islam' in its place, as is a common Arabic rhetorical device (iqamah al-mudaf ilayh muqam al-mudaf), giving the Quranic example of 'ask the village' where 'village' stands for 'people of the village'. The author supports this by arguing that the answer given is clearly about the laws (shara'i'), not about the essence of Islam, because one can have those practices without the faith mentioned in the first answer (but that would not make one a true Muslim) and one can lack those practices while having faith (but that would not make one a non-Muslim). Therefore, the answer is about the laws. Finally, he hints at another possibility: that the phrase 'Islam' was used but intended to include the laws by way of inclusion (bi tariq...), but the text cuts off. Throughout, the author prioritizes maintaining the integrity of all narrators and offering rational and linguistic explanations for the variation.

Nasafi states the Maturidi position on Uthman's rank and refutes objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Maturidi position on Uthman's rank among the Companions and the evidence used to support it, including responses to objections.

Core Idea
Uthman's virtue is confirmed by his sacrifices, the Pledge of Ridwan, and the testimony of the Companions.

In this passage, Imam Nasafi states the Maturidi position that Uthman ibn Affan is the best of the Companions after Abu Bakr and Umar. He then presents the dissenting views of some hadith scholars and refutes a common objection regarding Uthman's withdrawal at the Battle of Uhud. Through a combination of historical evidence and rational argument, Nasafi establishes Uthman's high rank and courage.

[الكلام في فضل عثمان ]: ظاهر مذهب أصحابنا القول بتفضيل عثمان بعد أبي بكر وعمر، وذهب الحسين بن الفضل البجلي ومحمد بن إسحاق بن خزيمة من أهل الحديث إلى تفضيل علي على عثمان، وتوقف أبو العباس القلانسي في ذلك وكان يرى إمامة المفضول جائزة، ومناقبه كثيرة، وبذله الأموال في نصرة دين الله وإقامة النبي عليه السلام إحدى يديه مقام يد عثمان في بيعة الرضوان معروفة، والأخبار بأن الملائكة تستحي منه مشهورة، ثم انحيازه في حرب أحد لا يدل على جبنه إذ قد يتفق ذلك أحيانا للبطل الكمي لعارض أمر خفي، ثم الشجاعة ليست إلا التهاون بالموت وعدم المبالاة من التلف والهلاك، وقد ظهر ذلك منه يوم الدار من منعة ناصريه عن القتال، وتعليق عتق عبيده بإلقاء السلاح على وجه لا تسمح به نفس أشهر خليقة الله بالشجاعة. ثم ما ظهر من الفتوح في أيامه من قبل المغرب على يدي عماله وأصحاب جيوشه، وذلك خير له من قتل ألوف من أبناء الحرب فيما بين الطعن والضرب، والذي يؤيد ما ذهبنا إليه ما روى أبو داود السجستاني في كتاب السنن بإسناده عن ابن عمر أنه قال: كنا نقول في زمن النبي عليه السلام: لا يعدل بأبي بكر أحد ثم عمر ثم عثمان.
[Discussion on the virtue of Uthman]: The apparent position of our companions is to affirm the preference of Uthman after Abu Bakr and Umar. Al-Husayn ibn al-Fadl al-Bajali and Muhammad ibn Ishaq ibn Khuzayma from the people of hadith went to preferring Ali over Uthman, and Abu al-Abbas al-Qalanisi hesitated in that, holding the imamate of the inferior to be permissible. His merits are many: his expenditure of wealth in support of Allah's religion, and the Prophet placing one of his hands in the place of Uthman's hand in the Pledge of Ridwan are well-known, and the reports that the angels are shy of him are famous. Then his withdrawal in the Battle of Uhud does not indicate cowardice, because that can sometimes happen to a courageous warrior due to a hidden cause. Moreover, courage is nothing but recklessness with death and disregard for destruction and perdition, and that was manifested from him on the day of the house (i.e., his siege) through his preventing his supporters from fighting and his conditioning the freeing of his slaves on laying down arms in a manner that even the most courageous of Allah's creatures would not permit in their soul. Then what appeared of conquests in his days from the direction of the Maghreb by the hands of his governors and army commanders, that is better for him than killing thousands of enemies among thrusting and striking. And what supports what we have gone to is what Abu Dawud al-Sijistani narrated in his Sunan with his chain from Ibn Umar that he said: 'We used to say in the time of the Prophet: None is equal to Abu Bakr, then Umar, then Uthman.'

Commentary
This passage is a defense of the Maturidi position on the virtue of Uthman ibn Affan after Abu Bakr and Umar. The author first states the school's position: Uthman is preferred after Abu Bakr and Umar. He then notes dissent: al-Husayn al-Bajali and Ibn Khuzayma (hadith scholars) preferred Ali over Uthman, while al-Qalanisi suspended judgment. The author then lists Uthman's merits: his spending wealth for Islam, his role in the Pledge of Ridwan (where the Prophet used his hand as a substitute), and reports that angels are shy of him. He then addresses the objection that Uthman withdrew from the Battle of Uhud, arguing it does not indicate cowardice because even the brave may retreat due to hidden reasons. He defines courage as recklessness with death, and points to Uthman's behavior during his siege (the Day of the House) where he prevented fighting and offered freedom to slaves if they laid down arms, showing disregard for death. He then notes the conquests that occurred during his caliphate, arguing that is better than killing many enemies. Finally, he cites a hadith from Ibn Umar that the Companions used to say that none equals Abu Bakr, then Umar, then Uthman.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Uthman's withdrawal at Uhud proves he was a coward.
Correction: The author argues that withdrawal can happen to the brave for hidden reasons, and defines courage as disregard for death, which Uthman showed during his siege.

Key Takeaway
The Maturidi school holds that Uthman ibn Affan is superior to all other Companions after Abu Bakr and Umar, supported by his deeds, the Prophet's actions, angelic respect, and the testimony of Ibn Umar.
