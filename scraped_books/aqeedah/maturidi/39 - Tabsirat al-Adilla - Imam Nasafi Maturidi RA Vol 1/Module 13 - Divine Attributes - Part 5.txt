Refutation of the Karramiyya on Power and Death
Nasafi refutes the Karramiyya claim about power and death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Karramiyya's distinction between power and other attributes with respect to death is logically unsustainable.

Core Idea
If power were part of life, death—not incapacity—would be its opposite.

The Karramiyya sect claimed that all attributes except power can remain with a dead person. Nasafi challenges this by showing that their justification—that power is part of life—leads to absurd consequences. This passage demonstrates how logical consistency refutes arbitrary theological distinctions.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
Read the Arabic first, then the translation. Notice the structure: statement of the Karramiyya position, their defense, and two refutation arguments.
2
Identify the two refutation moves
The first move is a counter-example: one could claim the opposite equally. The second is a logical proof: if power is part of life, its opposite is death, not incapacity, but incapacity coexists with life, so contradiction arises.
Focus on the second move as the stronger argument.
3
Reflect on the lesson
Consider why this argument is effective against the Karramiyya position. How does it show the importance of defining attributes and their opposites correctly?
This connects to broader kalam methodology.

Commentary
This passage refutes the Karramiyya sect's position that all attributes except power (qudra) can coexist with death. The author first states the Karramiyya claim: they allow all other attributes—like knowledge, hearing, sight—to remain with death, but power alone cannot. When pressed for a distinction, they argue that power is part of life (hayat) while other attributes are not. The refutation proceeds in two steps. First, a counter-argument is posed: one could just as well claim the opposite—that knowledge, hearing, and sight are part of life and power is not, showing the arbitrariness of their distinction. Second, the author proves the logical consequence: if power were truly part of life, then its direct opposite would be death, not incapacity ('ajz). But we observe that incapacity and life can coexist (e.g., a living being can be incapable), whereas death and life cannot coexist. Thus, since incapacity opposes both power and life, the Karramiyya position leads to contradiction and is invalid.

Refuting al-Salihi's Objection on Attributes Without Life
Nasafi refutes al-Salihi's claim that God cannot create attributes without first creating life.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Nasafi's refutation of al-Salihi's claim that God would need life as a tool to create attributes, and why this argument misunderstands the limits of divine power.

Core Idea
God's power is not limited by rational impossibilities; attributes require a living subject by nature.

This lesson examines a subtle objection raised by al-Salihi, who argued that if attributes like power and knowledge can only exist in living beings, then God would need to create life first, making life a tool. Nasafi refutes this by pointing out that God is not described as having power to create impossible combinations, such as a substance without an accident. Understanding this clarifies the relationship between divine power and the nature of created things.

This distinguishes between incapacity and impossibility.

Thus al-Salihi's objection is invalid because it misunderstands what is within divine power.

Refuting Self-Contradiction in Opposites
Objection and response about self-contradiction in opposites.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A claim that forces a thing to be opposite to itself is self-defeating.

This passage presents a sophisticated objection-and-response typical of kalam reasoning. The author refutes an opponent's argument by showing that it leads to an absurd conclusion—namely, that a thing would be opposite to itself. By tracing the logical implication, the reader sees how the argument collapses.

This is a classic kalam technique.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Not everything that coexists with one thing can coexist with its opposite.

This passage presents a theological objection and its refutation. The objector claims that life and death are known by necessity to be opposites. The author responds by pointing out that the objector's opponents also claim necessary knowledge that knowledge and death are opposites, so the objector must explain why his case is different. Then the author refutes a flawed inference, using the example of blackness and whiteness to show that coexistence with one does not imply coexistence with its opposite.

Commentary
This passage presents four Mu'tazilite arguments (shubah) against affirming real, eternal attributes for God. Al-Nasafi first states their position: God is living without life, knowing without knowledge, etc. Then he explains their reasoning. The first argument: If an attribute is not identical to the essence, it must be 'other' (ghayr). This otherness in pre-eternity implies multiple eternal entities, violating tawhid. The Mu'tazilites therefore call themselves 'Ahl al-Tawhid'. The second argument: If attributes are eternal, they are pre-eternal (qadim). Since pre-eternity is the distinctive quality of God, affirming eternal attributes means affirming additional gods. The third argument: If attributes are eternal, they must be self-subsisting (baqi bi-dhatihi), because an attribute cannot subsist in another attribute. But in the witnessed world, enduring things endure by an accident of endurance (baqa'). This would force the odd conclusion that God is enduring without endurance, contradicting their own principles. The fourth argument: In the witnessed world, we only know that an attribute is a distinct meaning because the essence can be divested of it (e.g., motion only known because sometimes rest). In the Unseen (God), such divestment is impossible, so we cannot know attributes as distinct. Thus all four arguments aim to show that affirming attributes leads to absurdities contrary to tawhid.

Commentary
This passage introduces a methodological approach for proving the attributes of God by analyzing the divine names. Al-Nasafi states that among the methods for the people of truth is reasoning by the 'established name' (al-ism al-thabit) based on unambiguous texts and consensus. He begins by noting that Allah is called by names like Living, Powerful, Knowing, etc. He then classifies naming (tasmiya) into two types: names that indicate mere existence (like 'existent' and 'thing') and names that indicate a meaning beyond existence. Those that indicate a meaning beyond existence are further divided into two categories: the first are names that indicate a quiddity (mahiyya) that distinguishes a species, such as 'human' or 'horse'. This quiddity can be further divided into two subcategories: one where the meaning is additional to the essence (like speech for humans), and another where it indicates a specific attribute (like 'speaker' or 'white'). The second main category consists of names derived from attributes inherent to the named object. Al-Nasafi then discusses the rule of derivation: names derived from attributes are true only if the attribute is present; otherwise, they are false unless the name has been transferred from its literal meaning and used as a nickname or proper name. He gives examples: calling a blind man 'the seer' or a frowning man 'the laugher' as nicknames. He distinguishes between a proper name (which does not indicate a reality) and a descriptive title (which does). The key difference is that a proper name only works by convention and is not understood without knowledge of that convention, whereas a descriptive title directly indicates the attribute. This entire discussion sets the stage for proving that the divine names (like 'Living', 'Knowing') must be descriptive titles that indicate real attributes in God, since they are not mere proper names.

Commentary
This passage is a proof that divine names are derived and indicate real attributes. The author begins by stating the premise that these names are derived from meanings (mashqūqah ʿan al-maʿānī). When applied to God's essence, they are intended to affirm the meaning from which they are derived, not just the essence itself. He then argues by reductio: if they were mere titles (alqāb) or proper names (aʿlām), applying laudatory attributes without corresponding meaning would be mockery, like calling a blind person 'seeing'. Moreover, if each name only affirmed the essence, then saying 'knowing essence, powerful essence' would be mere repetition of 'essence', yielding no new information. But in fact each name conveys a distinct benefit to the listener, so it must be that each intends the specific meaning. He then addresses an objection: the opponent claims that distinct benefit does not prove a meaning beyond the essence, citing examples like 'substance' and 'existent' which refer to the same thing but give different benefits. The author responds by clarifying that his argument is specific: these derived names, if taken as mere labels, would not provide distinct benefits, but they do, so they must retain their derived meanings. He further clarifies that he does not claim this for all names universally, but only for these derived names in this context. Thus, each derived name necessarily implies a real attribute in the essence.

Nasafi confronts an objection that claims names like 'Knowing' and 'Powerful' are just alternative titles for God's essence, not indicators of separate attributes.
