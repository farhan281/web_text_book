Refuting the Claim that the Verse Shows Incomplete Faith
Imam al-Nasafi refutes the claim that the verse 'Today I have perfected your religion' proves faith was incomplete before.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the verse 'Today I have perfected your religion' does not imply that faith was incomplete before, and how this argument preserves the completeness of faith of the early Companions.

Core Idea
The perfection of religion in the Quran does not mean the faith of the early Muslims was deficient.

The verse about perfecting religion is sometimes used to argue that religion was incomplete before. This lesson examines Imam al-Nasafi's refutation of that claim, showing its absurd consequences regarding the status of the Prophet and his Companions.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the English translation to understand the overall argument.
Notice that the passage is a refutation starting with 'That is false'.
2
Identify the opponents' claim
The opponents argue that the verse 'Today I have perfected your religion' shows religion was deficient before. Find this claim in the passage.
3
Understand the reductio argument
The author uses a reductio ad absurdum: if the claim were true, then the earlier Companions and the Prophet himself would have been following a deficient religion. That is absurd.
This is a classic theological argument showing the logical outcome of the opponents' view.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider why the author mentions the specific sacrifices of the Emigrants and Helpers (Badr, pledges, hardships). This highlights the greatness of their faith.

Faith Does Not Increase: The Analogy of Ten Men
The author uses an analogy to argue that faith does not increase or decrease.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Maturidi school uses an analogy to argue that faith (iman) does not increase or decrease in its essence, remaining complete once affirmed.

Core Idea
Faith does not increase because its essence is affirmation, which cannot be more or less.

The author presents a simple analogy to illustrate a profound point: just as adding a woman to a group of men increases the number of persons but not the number of men, so too performing acts of obedience increases one's acts but not the essence of faith. This distinction is critical to understanding the Maturidi position that faith is verified by affirmation alone.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then read the English translation. Get a feel for the author's argument before diving into analysis.
The passage has two parts: an analogy and its application to faith.
2
Understand the analogy
Focus on the analogy: ten men in a house, a woman enters. Why does the number of persons increase but not the number of men? Identify the key distinction between 'persons' (اشخاص) and 'men' (رجال).
The analogy shows that increase in a broader category does not imply increase in a specific subset.
3
Apply the analogy to faith
Now see how the author applies this: acts of obedience are like the woman's entry—they increase the general category of good deeds, but faith (the essence) does not increase because it is like 'men', a specific category (affirmation).
This is the core of the argument: affirmation (tasdiq) is the essence, and it is not quantifiable.
4
Consider the objection about sin
The author then addresses a potential objection: if a believer commits a sin, does faith decrease? He argues that since faith is only affirmation, and affirmation is either present (complete) or absent, sin cannot reduce it.
This refutes the idea of faith increasing or decreasing with deeds.
5
Summarize the key takeaway
Write a one-sentence summary of the argument: Faith is complete at the moment of sincere affirmation; subsequent sins do not diminish it, just as a woman entering a house does not increase the number of men.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the claim that faith can increase or decrease. The author first presents an analogy: if a woman enters a house with ten men, the number of persons increases but the number of men does not. Similarly, faith (iman) is essentially affirmation (tasdiq); affirmation does not admit of increase in its essence. The author then applies this: if a disbeliever becomes a Muslim through affirmation, his faith is complete. If he subsequently commits a sin, his faith does not decrease because it would require a change in the affirmation itself, which would either nullify it if defective or leave it complete. Therefore, any claim of deficiency in faith is false.

The Reality of Faith as Affirmation
Al-Nasafi defines faith as affirmation and analogizes it to other real attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that faith (iman) is a real attribute of inner affirmation, analogous to physical attributes like sitting or blackness, and is defined by its present existence.

Core Idea
Faith is a real present attribute, not a future contingency.

Al-Nasafi begins by establishing the nature of faith as a real and definable reality. Before addressing the controversial doctrine of final outcome, he grounds the discussion in a clear definition: faith is affirmation (tasdiq) of the Prophet's message. This definition anchors the entire refutation that follows.

1
Read the definition of faith
Read the Arabic sentence defining faith as tasdiq. Notice that al-Nasafi calls it a 'real thing with a known limit' (shay' haqiqi ma'lum al-hadd).
The definition is concise: it is affirmation of Muhammad regarding what he brought from Allah.
2
Understand the analogy
Faith is compared to attributes like sitting, standing, blackness, and whiteness. Just as these are real when present, faith is real when affirmation occurs.
The analogy shows that faith is not merely a label but a concrete reality.

If faith is a real attribute, its presence or absence must be judged by the current state, not by future possibilities.
This sets up the refutation of the final-outcome doctrine.

Commentary
This passage refutes the doctrine of al-‘Aqiba (final outcome) held by Ash‘aris and earlier Kharijites. The author first defines faith (iman) as affirmation (tasdiq) of Prophet Muhammad concerning what he brought from God. Faith is a real attribute with a defined limit. When affirmation exists, the person is truly a believer in the present, just as sitting, standing, blackness, and whiteness are real attributes that define a subject when present. The Ash‘ari and Kharijite view is then presented: they claim that present faith or unbelief has no bearing; rather, only the final outcome (al-‘aqiba) matters according to God's foreknowledge. If God knows a person will die a believer, that person is presently a believer even if currently denying God and worshipping an idol. Conversely, if God knows a person will die an unbeliever, that person is presently an unbeliever even if currently affirming God. The author rejects this as meaningless, using the analogy of life and death: God knows the living as living, not as dead presently, though He knows they will die. Similarly, present attributes like sitting, standing, black, white are real now regardless of future change. If the logic of the opponent were valid, it would absurdly imply we are now in the Hereafter and every dead person is alive, since the final outcome of the world is resurrection. Since this is obviously false, the doctrine is false. An objection is then raised: if God knows one's final state, doesn't that make one a friend or enemy of God now? The author responds that friendship (wilaya) and enmity ('adawa) are based on present faith and unbelief. A person changes from friend to enemy by actually committing unbelief. Change applies to the person, not to the relationship itself, just as knowledge and information do not change the known object but reflect its state. Thus, God's knowledge does not alter the present reality of faith or unbelief.

Commentary
This passage refutes the doctrine of al-‘Aqiba (final outcome) held by Ash‘aris and earlier Kharijites. The author first defines faith (iman) as affirmation (tasdiq) of Prophet Muhammad concerning what he brought from God. Faith is a real attribute with a defined limit. When affirmation exists, the person is truly a believer in the present, just as sitting, standing, blackness, and whiteness are real attributes that define a subject when present. The Ash‘ari and Kharijite view is then presented: they claim that present faith or unbelief has no bearing; rather, only the final outcome (al-‘aqiba) matters according to God's foreknowledge. If God knows a person will die a believer, that person is presently a believer even if currently denying God and worshipping an idol. Conversely, if God knows a person will die an unbeliever, that person is presently an unbeliever even if currently affirming God. The author rejects this as meaningless, using the analogy of life and death: God knows the living as living, not as dead presently, though He knows they will die. Similarly, present attributes like sitting, standing, black, white are real now regardless of future change. If the logic of the opponent were valid, it would absurdly imply we are now in the Hereafter and every dead person is alive, since the final outcome of the world is resurrection. Since this is obviously false, the doctrine is false. An objection is then raised: if God knows one's final state, doesn't that make one a friend or enemy of God now? The author responds that friendship (wilaya) and enmity ('adawa) are based on present faith and unbelief. A person changes from friend to enemy by actually committing unbelief. Change applies to the person, not to the relationship itself, just as knowledge and information do not change the known object but reflect its state. Thus, God's knowledge does not alter the present reality of faith or unbelief.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Friendship and enmity are based on actual faith and unbelief, not on foreknowledge.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Ash'ari and Kharijite position that the final destiny (al-'aqiba) determines one's current status of faith (iman) or disbelief (kufr). The author, Imam Nasafi, begins by establishing a principle: when a known reality (mā'lūma al-ḥadd) actually exists with its true reality, the essence is described by that reality (e.g., sitting, standing, black, white). Then he applies this to faith and disbelief: tasdiq (affirmation) is the reality of iman, and takdhib (denial) is the reality of kufr. If these are present in a person now, then that person is truly a believer or disbeliever now, regardless of future knowledge. The Ash'ari claim that only the final state matters leads to absurdities: it would mean that if God knows someone will die a believer, that person is currently a believer even if he is actually denying God and prostrating to idols; conversely, if God knows he will die a disbeliever, he is currently a disbeliever even if he affirms God. Nasafi calls this meaningless because realities are not negated by future knowledge. He gives an analogy: God knows a living person as living, and does not know him as dead now even though He knows he will die. Similarly, all attributes (sitting, standing, etc.) are judged by present actuality, not future expectation. If the Ash'ari view were correct, we would have to consider ourselves now in the afterlife (since we will all die) and every dead person as alive now—which is clearly false. Thus the doctrine is invalid. Then he addresses an objection: If God knows someone will be a believer in the end, doesn't that make him a friend (wali) of God now? Nasafi replies that friendship (wilaya) and enmity ('adawa) are based on actual faith and disbelief. A person who disbelieves after having faith was a friend but becomes an enemy; the reverse also holds. Change occurs on the person (the friend/enemy), not on the relationship of friendship/enmity itself, just as knowledge and the known change but the relation remains. Thus the objection fails.

Commentary
This passage presents al-Nasafi's proof that Islam and Iman (faith) are identical in meaning, not two distinct concepts. The author begins by stating his thesis: since the true nature of Iman and Islam is as previously established (both being inward affirmation and outward submission), it is impossible for one to exist without the other. He compares them to 'sitting' and 'squatting' — two words for essentially the same action. He then argues that it is rationally unacceptable for a person to fulfill all conditions of Iman and not be Muslim, or vice versa. To support this, he cites numerous Quranic verses that use 'Iman' and 'Islam' interchangeably or pair them together. For example, verse 3:19 declares that the religion is Islam, and verse 3:85 warns that any other religion will not be accepted — since Iman is a religion, if it were different from Islam, it would not be accepted. Other verses (51:35-36, 43:69, 10:84, 27:81, 3:102, 2:136-137, 3:20) similarly equate or closely associate the two terms. Al-Nasafi highlights that in 2:136, Allah commands the believers to say 'we have believed' (āmnnā) and then 'we are Muslims,' and in 2:137, guidance is linked to believing 'in the like of what you have believed in.' In 3:20, guidance is linked to submission (aslamtum). Thus, guidance is attributed to both Iman and Islam, proving they are one. He also cites the supplication of Joseph (12:101) asking to die a Muslim and be joined with the righteous — implying that a non-believer (ghayr al-mu'min) cannot be righteous. Further, verse 49:17 shows that guidance to Iman is a favor, linking it to Islam. The author then mentions a hadith that 'none enters Paradise except a believing soul' and another version 'except a Muslim soul,' showing interchangeability. Finally, he addresses an objection: if the two terms were distinct, one could exist without the other — we could conceive of a believer who is not a Muslim or a Muslim who is not a believer. He challenges the opponent to categorize such a person. The overall argument is a comprehensive scriptural proof for the Maturidi position that Iman and Islam are synonymous in the sense that true Iman necessarily entails outward submission (Islam), and vice versa, focusing on the essential unity of the religion.

Logical and Historical Refutation of the Distinction
The author presents logical and historical arguments to prove iman and islam are identical, including a reductio ad absurdum and the absence of a fourth category.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational and historical arguments that refute the claim that iman and islam are distinct, and see how the distinction leads to absurdity.

Core Idea
Separating iman and islam leads to logical contradictions and contradicts Quranic categories.

After presenting scriptural proofs, the author strengthens his case with logical reasoning and historical evidence. He argues that if iman and islam were distinct, the Quran's division of people into two groups would leave no place for Muslims, and the historical reality of the Prophet's time shows only three categories. Understand the argument from Quranic division

If iman and islam are distinct, then one could exist without the other. But in this world and the Hereafter, no rulings distinguish a 'believer who is not a Muslim' from a 'Muslim who is not a believer'. This shows the distinction is meaningless.
The author uses conditional reasoning: if distinct, then there should be separate rulings; there are none, so the claim is false.
3
Apply the historical argument
At the time of the Prophet, people were only three types: believer, disbeliever, and hypocrite. If Muslims were a fourth, they would have been mentioned. Since they are not, Muslims must be believers. If they were disbelievers, their Islam would be rejected.
This argument appeals to the absence of a fourth category in the Quran and history.
