Commentary
This passage is a sustained argument for the necessity of gratitude to God and the insufficiency of human reason to determine how to fulfill it. The author begins by stating that God created humans and subjected all things to them without any prior merit, so His blessings are gratuitous. Therefore, ingratitude is wrong and gratitude is obligatory. He then explains that gratitude must be proportionate to the blessing. Since blessings vary according to the rank of the benefactor, and God is infinitely great, no human can fully comprehend even the least of His blessings. The intellect itself is a part of God's blessings, so it cannot encompass the whole. Moreover, each act of gratitude requires further divine facilitation, leading to an infinite regress. Thus, reason alone cannot determine the proper measure or manner of gratitude. Therefore, a revealed explanation (bayān samʿī) is necessary, because a wise commander must make the command understandable for the commanded. This sets up the refutation of the Brahmins in the next section.

Al-Nasafi distinguishes the Maturidi method from the Mu'tazila on the necessity of sending prophets.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between Maturidi and Mu'tazila reasoning on the necessity of sending prophets and why the Maturidi method avoids the flawed principle of al-aslah.

Prophethood is known by reason, not by God's obligation to do the most beneficial.

In this passage, Imam al-Nasafi draws a sharp methodological boundary between the Maturidi school and the Mu'tazila on how to establish prophethood. The Maturidis affirm that reason can discern good and evil, but they reject the Mu'tazila claim that God must send prophets because it is 'most beneficial' for people. Al-Nasafi warns his companions to avoid falling into this corrupt principle. Let's examine the passage to understand the key dispute.

This passage is a refutation of a claim that a specific Quranic verse (likely referring to a verse where God says something about 'things' or 'scholars' or 'agents') requires specification (takhsis) to exclude God from its apparent scope. The author, Imam Nasafi, begins by arguing that God not being included in the term 'things' (al-ashya') when it appears in the verse does not necessitate that the verse is specified (i.e., that its general meaning is narrowed). He divides words that denote collections into two types: general (amm) and shared (mushtarak). A general term, like 'men' (al-rijal), covers all individuals that share a single definition (i.e., manhood). For such a term, excluding any individual from its ruling would indeed constitute specification (takhsis) and cancel its generality. A shared term, like 'eye' (al-'ayn), covers individuals with different definitions (e.g., the organ, a spring, the sun's disk). When a shared term is used, it does not automatically include every possible referent; only the intended one is meant, and its identification depends on external evidence. Excluding other referents is not considered specification. The key difference is that in a shared term, all possible referents are equally eligible to be the intended one in the absence of other evidence, but the term does not necessitate inclusion of any of them. In a general term, every individual of that type is necessarily included unless specified. After clarifying this, the author applies it to the word 'thing' (shay'). Therefore, when 'thing' is used for God, it cannot include originated things, and vice versa. So 'thing' is not a genus name (ism jins) that would make both God and the world species of a single genus. If it were, that would imply a shared genus between the eternal and originated, which is unbelief. The author, Imam Nasafi, begins by arguing that God not being included in the term 'things' (al-ashya') when it appears in the verse does not necessitate that the verse is specified (i.e., that its general meaning is narrowed). He compares this to the words 'agents' and 'scholars', which also do not imply God's inclusion. He then launches into a detailed linguistic analysis to expose the ignorance of his opponents. He divides words that denote collections into two types: general (amm) and shared (mushtarak). A general term, like 'men' (al-rijal), covers all individuals that share a single definition (i.e., manhood). For such a term, excluding any individual from its ruling would indeed constitute specification (takhsis) and cancel its generality. A shared term, like 'eye' (al-'ayn), covers individuals with different definitions (e.g., the organ, a spring, the sun's disk). When a shared term is used, it does not automatically include every possible referent; only the intended one is meant, and its identification depends on external evidence. Excluding other referents is not considered specification. The key difference is that in a shared term, all possible referents are equally eligible to be the intended one in the absence of other evidence, but the term does not necessitate inclusion of any of them. In a general term, every individual of that type is necessarily included unless specified. After clarifying this, the author applies it to the word 'thing' (shay'). He explains that 'thing' denotes any existent, but it is a shared term, not a general one, because the existence of God (the eternal, necessary of existence) and the existence of created things (the originated, possible of existence) are fundamentally different. The difference between them is far greater than the difference between the various referents of 'eye'—since those referents can share many similarities, but the eternal and the originated cannot share any similarity at all. Therefore, when 'thing' is used for God, it cannot include originated things, and vice versa. So 'thing' is not a genus name (ism jins) that would make both God and the world species of a single genus. If it were, that would imply a shared genus between the eternal and originated, which is unbelief. Commentary
This passage is a multi-part refutation of an unnamed opponent's claim that the verse 'Today I have perfected your religion' indicates that the religion was incomplete before that day. The author presents three interpretations (وجوه) showing that perfection refers to manifestation and victory, not essential completion.

Quoting Quranic verses about God perfecting His light, the author argues that God's light is never imperfect; He perfects it in the creation by making it apparent.

Finite things can be perfected by something else, but the infinite (like God's attributes) cannot be perfected. Therefore, the opponent's claim that the religion was imperfect before this verse is false because religion has no delimitation (حد) in itself; it is from God, who is infinite. Thus, their argument collapses. The author concludes that their reliance on this verse is invalid.

The Behavior of the Companions as Evidence Against Nass
Nasafi uses the Companions' piety and actions to argue against nass.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Nasafi's supporting argument: the pious Companions, especially Abu Bakr, would never have acted contrary to a clear nass, so their choice of Abu Bakr demonstrates there was no nass.

Core Idea
If nass existed, the Companions would have immediately obeyed; they did not.

Nasafi strengthens his case with an argument from the behavior of the Companions. Given their known piety, if there had been a nass designating a specific person, they would have hastened to follow it. Instead, they appointed Abu Bakr through consultation, and his own ascetic life shows he was not seeking power. This lesson examines that historical and moral reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is a proof against the claim that the imamate was established by explicit designation (naṣṣ) from the Prophet. The author first states the correct method: imamate is established by ijtihād, as there is no naṣṣ on the matter. He then declares the claims of both the Rāfiḍa (for ‘Alī) and the Rawandiyya (for al-‘Abbās) to be false. The main proof is an argument from the public nature of the caliphate: it is a matter of universal concern, like the direction of prayer or the number of rak‘as. If there were a naṣṣ, it would have been transmitted so widely that no one could remain ignorant of it. Since no such widespread report exists, there is no naṣṣ. A second supporting argument: the Companions were pious and careful to follow the Sharī‘a; if there had been a naṣṣ, they would have immediately accepted and implemented it, and they would not have chosen Abu Bakr. The author emphasizes that the Companions’ motivations were purely religious, not worldly. He cites the example of Abu Bakr (al-Ṣiddīq) as evidence of his piety and lack of worldly desire, implying that he would not have taken the caliphate if it had been designated for another. The passage follows a logical order: statement of the principle, declaration of error, main proof (public necessity), corollary proof (behavior of the Companions), and an illustrative example.

Reason and Revelation in Islamic Law
Al-Nasafi states that law is not limited to text.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Islamic law is not confined to explicit texts but includes rational obligation and testing.

Core Idea
Divine wisdom includes using reason where there is no text.

Al-Nasafi opens his argument by rejecting the notion that every ruling must be explicitly stated in revelation. He asserts that God's wisdom includes imposing obligations that require people of understanding to use their reason, just as in other matters without a clear text. This sets the foundation for the role of rational thought in Islamic theology and law.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the two sentences slowly to grasp the negation (ليس بمقصور) and the positive statement (بل من الحكمة).
Notice the rhetorical structure: 'not confined... rather from wisdom'.
2
Identify the key principle
Understand that the passage establishes a general principle: texts are not the only source; rational testing and obligation are parts of divine wisdom.
The phrase 'people of understanding' (أولو الألباب) refers to those who reflect.
3
Consider the implication
Reflect on why this principle matters: it defends the use of reason in matters where revelation is silent, including theological reasoning.
This will be applied later to the caliphate issue.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological argument that the way of knowing divine laws is not limited to revealed texts; reason also plays a crucial role. The author (al-Nasafi) begins by asserting that it is not necessary for every ruling to be explicitly in the text; divine wisdom includes obligating people to use their reason in matters where there is no explicit text. He then provides a systematic progression of rational knowledge: every rational person necessarily knows the temporality (huduth) of his own self, then through reflection knows the temporality of all sensed things, then realizes that every temporal thing requires a temporalizer (muhdith), and through further reflection knows the attributes of that temporalizer—whether He is a body, accident, or neither, and whether His attributes like knowledge and power are eternal or temporal. This leads to knowledge of the prophets and messengers, and then to distinguishing truth from falsehood in reports and accepting or rejecting testimonies. Al-Nasafi emphasizes that all this knowledge is attained through reflection and thought (nazar wa fikr) combined with supplication to Allah for success. After establishing this rational method, he applies it to the case of the Companions' selection of the caliph: since they used their reason and acted with care and sincerity, their action is justified and cannot be criticized. He then refutes the Rafida (a sect that reviles the Companions) by arguing that their rejection stems from intellectual shortcoming, not from any fault in the Companions. The passage thus defends the legitimacy of the Companions' ijtihad in choosing Abu Bakr and warns against innovation.
