Refuting the Ash'ari Use of the 'Be' Verse
Nasafi refutes the Ash'ari interpretation of the verse and shows its implication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Nasafi refutes the Ash'ari argument for the eternity of God's speech by showing that their own proof actually supports the Maturidi view of al-takwin as an eternal attribute.

Core Idea
The Ash'ari verse proves takwin's eternity, not just speech.

The Ash'aris cite the Quranic verse 'When We will a thing, We say to it "Be" and it is' to argue for the eternity of God's speech. Nasafi points out that this very verse supports the Maturidi claim that al-takwin is an eternal attribute. Moreover, if the Ash'aris identify takwin with the created thing, they would fall into a contradiction that leads to the eternity of the world.

Guided Path
1
Read the Ash'ari argument as presented
The Ash'aris use the verse to argue that God's speech is eternal. Notice the verse and their inference.
They equate 'Be' with God's speech.
2
Understand Nasafi's counter
Nasafi says the verse actually shows the eternity of takwin. How does he reinterpret the verse?
The command 'Be' is the expression of takwin.
3
Grasp the reductio ad absurdum
If takwin were identical to the thing created, then the thing would be self-caused and eternal, leading to the eternity of the world. Why is this impossible?
The world is clearly originated, so takwin cannot be identical to it.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the eternity of the divine attribute al-takwin (bringing-into-existence) and a refutation of the Ash'ari position that identifies it with the thing brought into existence. The argument proceeds as follows: First, it states that the world's existence through God's power does not eliminate the need for al-takwin, because power (qudra) does not by itself necessitate the actual existence of the thing it is capable of effecting; rather, it is the attribute of al-takwin that brings about existence. Then it refutes the Ash'ari appeal to the Quranic verse 'When We will a thing, We say to it "Be", and it is' which they use to argue for the eternity of God's speech. an-Nasafi points out that the verse actually supports his own position that al-takwin is eternal, because if al-takwin were identical to the thing created, then the thing would be self-caused and therefore eternal, leading to the impossible consequence that the entire world is eternal. Next, he examines the alternatives for al-takwin: either it is eternal or originated. If originated, it would require an infinite chain of creations, which is impossible because the world's existence depends on it and an infinite regress cannot be realized. Moreover, if it were originated, it would have to subsist either in no locus (impossible for an accident), in a locus other than God (making that locus the creator), or in God's essence (which would imply change in God and lead to His origination, which is disbelief). Therefore al-takwin must be eternal. Finally, he explains that the eternity of al-takwin does not entail the eternity of the things brought into existence, because things that come into being through al-takwin are by nature originated, while the eternal is self-sufficient. The footnote clarifies that power (qudra) according to the Maturidis is not an attribute of effect but rather the capacity to produce or annihilate; the actual producing and annihilating is the attribute of effect, which is al-takwin.

Proof that Takwin is Eternal and Its Objects Originated
Nasafi proves the eternity of al-takwin by eliminating the possibility of its origination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Maturidi proof that al-takwin must be eternal, and why this does not imply that the things brought into existence are eternal.

Core Idea
Takwin is eternal; its products are not.

After establishing the necessity and distinctness of al-takwin, Nasafi provides a rigorous proof that it must be eternal. He considers the two possibilities and demonstrates that if takwin were originated, it would lead to impossibilities such as infinite regress or unacceptable loci for its subsistence. He then clarifies that the eternity of takwin does not entail the eternity of the created things, as they depend on it for their origin. A footnote clarifies the Maturidi distinction between power as capacity and takwin as actual effecting.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dichotomy
Nasafi sets up two possibilities: takwin is either eternal (azali) or originated (hadith). Read the opening sentence.
This is a classic dilemma argument.
2
Examine the infinite regress argument
If takwin were originated, it would require another takwin, leading to an infinite chain. Why is this impossible?
The world exists, so its dependence cannot be on an impossible chain.
3
Examine the three impossible loci
If takwin were originated, it must subsist in a locus. Nasafi lists three possibilities and shows each is impossible. Identify them.
No locus, locus other than God, God's essence – all ruled out.
4
Grasp the distinction between eternal attribute and temporal objects
The passage concludes that takwin is eternal, but the things it creates are not. Why? Because they depend on takwin for their origin.
This guards against the misconception that the world is eternal.

The Definition of Divine Will and Its Proof
Nasafi defines irada as an eternal attribute, distinguishes it from mashi'a, and presents a proof that God is described by will in reality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the linguistic and technical definition of God's will (irada), its relation to volition (mashi'a), and the Sunni proof that God is described by will in reality.

Core Idea
God's will is an eternal attribute that specifies acts; the proof is the specification of similar acts.

Nasafi begins by defining the term 'irada' (will) both linguistically and technically, showing that it is distinct from aversion and compulsion. He then clarifies the relationship between irada and mashi'a, and provides an argument that God is described by will in reality, not metaphorically. This lesson covers the foundational definition and the central proof.

Guided Path
1
Examine the linguistic definition
Notice that irada is derived from al-rawd, meaning seeking or inclination. This sets the conceptual root.
2
Understand the technical definition
The technical definition emphasizes that will is incompatible with aversion and compulsion, and necessitates the specific manner of actions.
3
Compare irada and mashi'a
Most theologians treat them as synonyms, but the Karamiyya differentiate. Understand both positions.
4
Analyze the Sunni proof
The proof from specification of actions shows that actions occurring in specific ways, when other ways are possible, indicates a will in the agent.

Moreover, if God were not willing, He would be compelled (mudtarr), and a compelled being is incapable. Nasafi then refutes the Mu'tazila claim that irada is either God's own action or His command to others, showing its inconsistency. Finally, he rejects the idea that irada is the same as appetite (shahwa).

Commentary
This passage is a multi-part discussion on ru'ya (vision of Allah). It begins with a summary of two arguments: first, that Allah is not of a genus, so reasoning from genus is invalid; second, that ru'ya in the hereafter is not habitual like in this world, so it cannot be dismissed. Then it reports al-Maturidi's agreement. Next, it presents al-Qalanisi's view that ru'ya is impossible in this world due to weak eyesight, and al-Nasafi's partial approval with a critique. Al-Nasafi disagrees with al-Qalanisi's claim that preventing a nature from its act is impossible; al-Nasafi holds that Allah can create an opposite, e.g., preventing vision. Then al-Nasafi affirms that Allah sees us without distance or ray, contra the Baghdad Mu'tazila. On ru'ya of attributes, al-Nasafi notes no scriptural text affirms it, but those who permit ru'ya of every existent (including al-Nasafi) deem attributes visible. The core summary follows: ru'ya is linked to wujud (existence), not accidental attributes like distance or facing. Evidence takes precedence over imagination. Opponents reject intellectual proofs and rely on imagination, opening the door to denying the Creator.

Ru'ya of Attributes: Different Positions
Nasafi discusses ru'ya of attributes and lists two views.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the differing opinions on whether Allah's attributes can be seen, and Nasafi's position that any existent can be seen.

Core Idea
For Nasafi, every existent, including attributes, is potentially visible.

Nasafi turns to the vision of divine attributes. He notes that no scriptural text reports seeing attributes. Then he distinguishes two positions: those who think ru'ya can only attach to what subsists in an essence (thus attributes are not visible), and those who allow ru'ya of any existent. Nasafi aligns with the latter.

Guided Path
1
Read the textual note
Nasafi states no transmitted text supports ru'ya of attributes.
2
Identify the two positions
First: ru'ya only of essences. Second: ru'ya of any existent.
Nasafi belongs to the second group.
3
Reflect on implications
If attributes are existent, they can be seen, though no text confirms it.

The Core Disagreement: Evidence vs. Imagination
Nasafi summarizes the core disagreement on ru'ya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fundamental dispute: ru'ya is based on existence (wujud) as a cause, not on accidental concomitants, and evidence must override imagination.

Core Idea
Reliance on evidence, not imagination, is the key to affirming ru'ya.

Nasafi concludes by summarizing the root of disagreement. He argues that ru'ya is attached to existence alone, not to accidental features like distance or facing. Evidence from Allah seeing us shows these are not necessary. Opponents ignorantly cling to imagination and accidental concomitants, opening the door to denying the Creator.

1

2
Identify the key proof
Allah's seeing us without distance shows that such concomitants are not necessary for vision.
3
Understand the opponent's error
Opponents rely on imagination and accidental concomitants, rejecting rational evidence.
Nasafi warns this leads to atheism.

He draws this from the Qur'anic verse that Allah knows even the innermost thoughts, which implies knowledge of things before they exist. This condition is absent in humans; human reason cannot grasp how something emerges from nothing into existence. Therefore, humans are doers (fa'ilun) of actions that are created for them, but they are not creators. This concludes the Maturidi argument against attributing creative power to humans.

Commentary
This passage is an argument from consensus (ijma') refuting the Mu'tazila's doctrine of divine assistance (lutf) and the obligation of al-aslah (the most beneficial).

The argument proceeds as follows:
1. There is consensus among all religious communities and Muslims on supplicating to Allah and asking for His help in obedience and protection from sins.
2. Al-Nasafi (the author) frames a dilemma: either Allah grants what is asked, or He does not.
   - If He grants it, then the request is foolish, because one is asking for what is already given. It would be absurd for Allah to command such foolishness.
   - If He does not grant it, then either it is permissible for Him to withhold it or it is not permissible (i.e., obligatory to grant).
3. If it were obligatory for Allah to grant the request, then withholding it would be injustice (zulm), and the supplicants would be asking Allah not to wrong them by withholding a right. Anyone who says that is an unbeliever (kafir).

Actually, the text says 'because Allah would thereby be a partner in the action of the servant'—meaning that if Allah had the power to grant assistance but does not, then the servant's action is independent of Allah, which contradicts the Mu'tazila's own view? This part is somewhat condensed.

Al-Nasafi then summarizes the Mu'tazila's position: they deny that Allah has a will (mashi'a) or choice (ikhtiyar) in His actions, claiming He is compelled to do what is most beneficial (al-aslah) for His servants. Consequently, they deny that Allah is a benefactor (muhsin) or gracious (mutafaddil) because He is only fulfilling an obligation.

وبين الأشعري خلاف في أن صفات الله تعالى هل توصف بأنها قديمة؟ فالأشعري يصفها بذلك لما أنه لا أول لوجودها، وعبد الله بن سعيد مع قدماء أصحابنا يمتنعون عن ذلك، لاستحالة قيام القدم وهو صفة بما وراءها من الصفات، إذ الصفة لا تقوم بها صفة، وإن كانوا قالوا: لا ابتداء لوجودها، فكأن هذا اختلاف في العبارة. وأما المحدث فهو ما لوجوده ابتداء ويقال ما لوجوده أول، وقيل: هو المبتدأ في الوجود، وقيل: هو ما تأخر بوجوده عن الأزلي. وهذه العبارات كلها تنبئ عن معنى واحد قد يطلق اسم القديم على ما لوجوده ابتداء، فيقال: هذا بناء قديم وشيخ قديم، يراد به التقدم في الوجود على غيره بشرط المبالغة، وهذا النوع ليس بمراد في التكلم في العالم أنه قديم أم محدث، والله الموفق.
And between al-Ashʿarī and [the Māturīdīs] there is a disagreement concerning whether the attributes of Allah—exalted is He—can be described as eternal (qadīm)? Al-Ashʿarī describes them as such because they have no beginning to their existence, while ʿAbd Allāh ibn Saʿīd together with the early ones among our companions refrain from that, due to the impossibility of eternity (al-qidam)—which is a attribute—subsisting in attributes beyond itself, since an attribute does not subsist in another attribute, even though they said: there is no beginning to their existence. So this is as if a disagreement in expression. As for the originated (al-muḥdath), it is that which has a beginning to its existence, and it is said: that which has a first to its existence, and it is said: it is the originated in existence, and it is said: it is that which is subsequent by its existence to the eternal (al-azalī). All these expressions indicate a single meaning. The name 'eternal' (qadīm) may be applied to that which has a beginning to its existence, so it is said: this is an ancient building and an old man, intending precedence in existence over others with the condition of exaggeration. And this type is not intended in the discussion of whether the world is eternal or originated. And Allah is the giver of success.

Commentary
This passage begins by noting a disagreement between al-Ashʿarī and the early Māturīdīs (represented by ʿAbd Allāh ibn Saʿīd al-Qalānisi and others) over whether God's attributes can be described as 'qadīm' (eternal). Al-Ashʿarī permits it because the attributes have no beginning to their existence. The Māturīdīs refrain, arguing that an attribute cannot subsist in another attribute, and since eternity (qidam) is itself an attribute, it cannot be attributed to other attributes. However, both sides agree that the attributes have no beginning; the disagreement is merely verbal. Next, the passage defines 'muḥdath' (originated): it is something with a beginning to its existence, or something that enters existence after non-existence. It then notes that 'qadīm' can colloquially describe something with a beginning but very old (like an 'ancient building' or 'old man'), but this usage is not meant when discussing the world's eternity or origination in theology. The passage ends with 'And Allah is the giver of success.'

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The Māturīdī position implies they believe God's attributes have a beginning.
Correction: Both schools affirm the attributes have no beginning; the Māturīdīs simply avoid the term 'qadīm' because of a logical principle regarding attributes.
