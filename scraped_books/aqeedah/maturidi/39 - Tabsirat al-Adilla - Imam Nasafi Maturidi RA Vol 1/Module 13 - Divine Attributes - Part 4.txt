Core Idea
God's laughter is a metaphor for His pleasure, not a literal action.

When the Prophet ﷺ narrated that God 'laughs' at His friends, some might imagine a human-like laughter. Imam Nasafi, following the Maturidi tradition, clarifies that this is a metaphorical expression. He cites Arabic linguistic usage and poetry to show that 'laughter' can mean 'showing pleasure' or 'manifestation.' This lesson unpacks his evidence and reasoning.

Why Divine Attributes Do Not Admit Superlatives
Nasafi argues that terms like wujud do not admit superlatives, while jism does.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why attributes like existence and self-subsistence cannot take comparative or superlative forms, whereas composite concepts like jism can.

Core Idea
Attributes without increase cannot be compared.

In this passage, Imam Nasafi argues that certain attributes of Allah, such as existence (wujud) and self-subsistence (qiyam bil-dhat), do not admit of increase or decrease. Therefore, they cannot be expressed in comparative or superlative forms. This distinction helps clarify why terms like 'ajsam' (more bodily) are applicable only to created bodies, not to Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the translation. Notice the structure: first a list of attributes without increase, then the example of jism, then the analogy with 'ilm.
Pay attention to the terms تزايد, مبالغة, تفضيل.
2
Identify the two categories
Nasafi distinguishes between attributes that have no increase (e.g., الوجود, القيام بالذات) and those that do (e.g., كثرة الأجزاء for jism). Note why superlatives are impossible for the first category.
The key is that تزايد (increase) is absent in divine attributes.
3
Understand the analogy
Nasafi compares jism with 'ilm: just as 'ālim and a'lam revolve with 'ilm, so jasīm and ajsam revolve with kathrat al-ajzā'. This shows that jism is derived from composition, not from essence.
This analogy reinforces that attributes without increase do not derive terms of comparison.

Commentary
This passage is a proof by Imam Nasafi that attributes like existence and self-subsistence do not admit of increase, and therefore cannot take superlative forms (e.g., awjad, aqwam). He observes that the word jism is used in exaggerated and comparative forms (jasīm, ajsam) precisely because it indicates multitude of parts, which does admit increase. This shows that jism is not a name for what has no increase (existence or self-subsistence) but for what has increase, namely composition. Nasafi then draws an analogy with ʿālim and aʿlam, which are derived from ʿilm and revolve with it, showing that the derivation of jism follows the same pattern—it indicates its meaning. He concludes that the same principle applies to all attributes: those without increase do not take superlatives, whereas composite notions do.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an opponent who, to avoid implication of similarity (mumāthala) between God and creatures, proposes that names applied to both are literal (ḥaqīqa) in one locus and metaphorical (majāz) in the other. The author argues that names are often used literally for opposites without implying similarity, as shown by linguistic examples: words like 'thirsty' apply to both the thirsty and the satiated, 'garment' to both small and large, 'menstrual period' to menstruation and purity. Even apparently synonymous or shared names like 'existence' and 'color' apply to different things without necessitating resemblance. Moreover, metaphor actually requires a strong similarity between the two loci—so strong that linguists say majāz is a simile without the particle 'like' (ka). If one says 'Zayd is a lion', it implies the utmost lion-like courage in him, because the predicate is identical to the subject. Therefore, the opponent's move to use majāz for one locus actually commits him to affirming a necessary similarity between God and creatures, contradicting his aim to avoid similarity. Making both loci literal does not imply similarity, as shown by linguistic usage. So the opponent's position is absurd and shows ignorance of how names really work.

Definition of criterion with illustration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the practical criterion for distinguishing literal (ḥaqīqa) from metaphorical (majāz) usage based on the possibility of truthful negation.

Core Idea
If you can truthfully deny a term, it's metaphorical; if you can't, it's literal.

Before we can discuss whether divine attributes are literal or metaphorical, we need a clear criterion to tell the difference. The author provides a simple test: if a negation of the term is true, then the usage is metaphorical; if false, literal. This lesson unpacks that criterion and its application to the example of 'lion'.

See how the criterion works with 'lion': saying of a real lion 'not a lion' is false (literal), but saying of a brave human 'not a lion' is true (metaphorical).
This concrete example makes the abstract criterion easy to grasp.

This line of reasoning shows that God must possess the attributes of power, knowledge, hearing, and sight.

Commentary
This passage is a combined proof for the essential attributes of Allah (power, knowledge, life, hearing, sight). The author, Imam al-Nasafi, presents two complementary methods. First, he argues from the nature of eternity itself: the Eternal must be free from all deficiencies. The opposites of the attributes (death, ignorance, inability, etc.) are deficiencies. Since the Eternal cannot have deficiencies, He must possess the corresponding attributes of perfection. This is a direct a priori argument. Second, he argues from the observable perfection of creation (a posteriori). The intricate order, precision, and marvels in the heavens, animals, and even inanimate objects cannot be produced except by a being who is living, powerful, knowing, hearing, and seeing. This is so obvious that rational people consider it foolish to attribute such works to a dead or ignorant agent. The author then synthesizes: we know the attributes either by knowing their opposites are impossible for the Eternal (first method), or by seeing the effects that require power and knowledge (second method). Notably, he discusses a subtle point about the attribute of life (hayat). Some of his fellow scholars prove life directly from the act, since voluntary acts in the witnessed world always indicate a living agent. Others hold that life is not directly inferred from the act but is a necessary condition for power and knowledge; since power and knowledge are proven from the act, and they cannot exist without life, life is thus indirectly proven. The author presents both views, showing the depth of Maturidi theological reasoning.

Absurd Consequences of Denying Life as Condition
The author refutes the Salihi and Karamiyya by showing absurd consequences of denying life as condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the reductio ad absurdum argument used to refute those who claim that knowledge and power can exist without life.

Core Idea
If knowledge and power could exist without life, then all beautiful artifacts could be attributed to inanimate objects.

The author now drives home the refutation by showing the absurd consequences of denying that life is a condition for knowledge and power. He paints a vivid picture: if it were possible for the dead to possess knowledge and power, then every precious item might be the work of inanimate matter, and rocks could wage war. This reductio demonstrates that the opposing view is irrational and must be rejected.

Guided Path
1
Read the hypothetical scenarios
The author imagines that if life were not necessary, then precious brocade, palaces, and scientific works could be from inanimates.
2
Note the logical chain
The assumption leads to absurd consequences, which shows the assumption is false.
This is a classic proof by contradiction.
3
Appreciate the force of the reductio
The consequences are so absurd that no rational person would accept them.
The author calls it 'hadyan' (delirium).
4
Understand the refutation of Salihi
The author explicitly states that this proves the invalidity of Abu al-Husayn al-Salihi's position.
Also refutes the Karamiyya who held similar views.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion of divine attributes, focusing on life. It presents two views among the author's school: some infer life directly from contingent acts (since perfect acts require a living agent), while others hold that life is not directly inferred but is a necessary condition for knowledge and power, which are themselves inferred from acts. The author then provides a rational proof: death and inanimateness are opposites of knowledge and power, just as death is opposite to life. Sound intellects find it equally impossible to combine knowledge with death as to combine life with death; therefore, life is a necessary condition. This is reinforced by a reductio: if life were not necessary, delicate artifacts could be attributed to inanimate objects, leading to absurd scenarios. Finally, the author refutes the claim of Abu al-Husayn al-Salihi and the Karamiyya that life is not a condition, declaring it invalid.

Refutation of the Karramiyya on Power and Life
Reductio argument against the Karramiyya view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Karramiyya's claim that power is part of life leads to logical contradictions.

Core Idea
If power were part of life, weakness couldn't coexist with life, but it does.

The Karramiyya argued that power is inseparable from life, so death cannot coexist with power. This seems plausible until we examine its implications. In this passage, the author demonstrates that their position is self-contradictory by showing that weakness, the opposite of power, can indeed coexist with life, which would be impossible if power were part of life's essence.

Guided Path
1
Read the Passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then its translation. Notice the structure: the Karramiyya claim, then the rebuttal, then the verification.
The argument uses reductio ad absurdum.
2
Identify the Karramiyya Claim
The Karramiyya say power is part of life, so it cannot coexist with death. Other attributes can. Ask: why make this exception?
They claim power is from the essence of life.
3
Follow the Reductio
The author shows the claim leads to contradiction: if power is part of life, its opposite should be death, not weakness. But weakness does coexist with life, so the premise is false.
Observe the logical steps carefully.
4
Understand the Final Proof
The verification: weakness also opposes the life that supposedly includes power, so allowing weakness and life together would imply death and life can coexist, which is absurd.
This seals the refutation.

وبطلان قول الكرامية حيث يجوزون ذلك كله إلا القدرة فإنها لا تجامع الموت عندهم، فأما ما وراء القدرة فيجوز اجتماعها مع الموت عندهم، وإذا طولبوا بالفرق زعموا أن القدرة هي من جملة الحياة، فأما العلم وما وراء ذلك من الصفات فليست من جملتها قيل لهم : وبم تنفصلون ممن عكس عليكم فادعي أن العلم والسمع والبصر من جملة الحياة، والقدرة ليست كذلك ؟ ثم نقول لهم : لو كان الأمر على ما تزعمون لكانت القدرة لا تفارق الحياة ولا يجامعها العجز الذي هو ضد القدرة، وحيث كان الأمر بخلافه دل على بطلان هذه المقالة. يحققه : أن القدرة لو كانت من جملة الحياة، لكان ضدها الموت لا العجز، ولو جاز اجتماع العجز والحياة لجاز اجتماع الموت والحياة لأن العجز كما يضاد القدرة يضاد الحياة التي من جملتها القدرة، وحيث لم يكن كذلك دل أن هذا القول باطل .

And the falsity of the statement of the Karramiyya, for they permit all of that except power, for it does not coexist with death in their view. As for what is beyond power, they permit its coexistence with death. And when they are pressed for a distinction, they claim that power is part of life, but knowledge and the attributes beyond that are not part of it. It was said to them: How do you distinguish yourselves from one who reverses it upon you and claims that knowledge, hearing, and sight are part of life, and power is not? Then we say to them: If the matter were as you claim, power would never separate from life, and weakness (al-ʿajz), which is the opposite of power, would not coexist with it. But since the matter is contrary, this indicates the falsity of this doctrine. It verifies that if power were part of life, its opposite would be death, not weakness. And if the coexistence of weakness and life were permissible, then the coexistence of death and life would be permissible, because weakness opposes power and also opposes the life of which power is a part. And since it is not so, this indicates that this statement is false.
And the sophism (al-shubha) that al-Salihi mentions is that the principle is that whatever opposes something opposes its opposite, and whatever does not oppose something does not oppose that thing. For since blackness opposes whiteness, it opposes what is opposite to whiteness of redness, yellowness, greenness, and others. And since movement does not oppose life, it does not oppose death. And if this is the principle, then we see that knowledge and power do not oppose life, so they do not oppose death either. And when opposition is removed, coexistence is permissible. [This] is empty and contradictory speech.

Commentary
This passage first refutes the Karramiyya view. They allow all attributes except power (al-qudra) to coexist with death, claiming that power is part of life (min jumlat al-hayah) while other attributes like knowledge are not. The author rebuts by asking what distinguishes them from someone who reverses the claim and asserts that knowledge, hearing, and sight are part of life while power is not. Then he argues: if power were part of life, it would never separate from life and its opposite, weakness (al-ʿajz), would not coexist with life. But we observe that weakness does coexist with life (e.g., in a living but weak person), and power does separate from life (in death). This shows the falsity of their doctrine. Further reasoning: if power were part of life, its opposite would be death, not weakness. If weakness and life could coexist, then death and life could also coexist, because weakness opposes both power and the life that includes power. Since this is false, their statement is false.

The passage then addresses the sophism (al-shubha) of al-Salihi. His principle is that 'whatever opposes something opposes its opposite, and whatever does not oppose something does not oppose its opposite.' For example, blackness opposes whiteness, so it opposes all colors opposite to whiteness. Movement does not oppose life, so it does not oppose death. Applying this, knowledge and power do not oppose life, so they should not oppose death. When opposition is removed, coexistence would be permissible. The author declares this to be empty and contradictory speech, implying that the principle is misapplied or that the conclusion does not follow, though the detailed refutation is not elaborated here.

Refutation of al-Salihi's Sophism on Opposition
Exposes the flaw in al-Salihi's principle of opposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Salihi's principle about opposition leads to a false conclusion regarding attributes and death.

Core Idea
Opposition logic does not apply directly to divine attributes as al-Salihi assumes.

Al-Salihi proposed a principle: whatever opposes something also opposes that thing's opposite, and whatever does not oppose something does not oppose its opposite. He applied this to life and death: since knowledge and power do not oppose life, they should not oppose death, so they can coexist with death. The author dismisses this as empty and contradictory, hinting that the analogy fails.

Guided Path
1
Read the Sophism
Read al-Salihi's principle and his application. Notice he uses examples like blackness and whiteness, movement and life.
The principle seems plausible at first.
2
Identify the Conclusion
Al-Salihi concludes that knowledge and power can coexist with death because they don't oppose life. This is what the author refutes.
But the author calls it 'empty and contradictory.'
3
Evaluate the Analogy
Consider why the analogy fails: divine attributes like knowledge and power are not natural opposites like colors. The author does not elaborate fully but implies the principle is misapplied.
Think about the difference between essential opposites and relational non-opposition.
