Commentary
This passage answers the question: by which method (bi-ayy ṭarīq) is the Imamate established? Nasafi states it is established by choice (ikhtiyār) because the Imamate is among the public affairs (al-umūr al-ʿāmma). If there had been a specific text (naṣṣ) designating a successor, it would have become widely known (ishtahara), but that did not happen. The proof (dalīl) is that the Companions (ṣaḥāba), despite their great status (jalāla qadrihim) and strong adherence to the Sharia, chose their Imam (after the Prophet) — referring to the election of Abu Bakr — and no one opposed them (lam yuʿāriḍhum aḥad). Nasafi argues that it is inconceivable that the Companions would disregard a clear text, since they were the highest in dignity (al-qimma fi al-jalāl) and farthest from whims (bu'd 'an al-hawā), and they ordained good and forbade evil, and the Prophet himself chose them for his companionship. He then gives a conditional argument: if there had been a text for someone (like 'Ali), that person would have claimed it, brought evidence against the people, and argued with whoever did not follow him. Since none of that happened, there is no text. The final statement about 'Ali being courageous and not fearing blame reinforces that 'Ali would have spoken up if there were a text.

Critique of Dividing Knowledge: Conditions of a Proper Definition
Critique of dividing knowledge; conditions of a proper definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a definition of knowledge cannot be given by simply dividing it into types, and you will learn the logical conditions of a sound definition in Islamic theology.

Core Idea
A definition must cover all instances; dividing knowledge is not defining it.

In this passage, Imam al-Nasafi critiques a common approach to defining knowledge that merely divides it into necessary and acquired. He argues that this is not a true definition because it fails to include all individuals of the defined. Instead, he lays out the rigorous conditions a definition must satisfy: universality and convertibility, which ensure comprehensiveness and exclusiveness. Understanding these conditions is essential for any student of kalam.

Commentary
This passage is a critique of a definition of knowledge that divides it into necessary and acquired. The author first states that such a 'definition' is actually a division (taqsīm) of knowledge, not a proper definition. Thus the divided definition is invalid.

Commentary
This passage argues that taqlid (uncritical acceptance of authority) cannot establish the truth of any religion. The author begins by stating that taqlid is not a valid means to know religious truth. Two possibilities are considered: if one imitates every religion, it is impossible due to contradictions; if one imitates a particular religion, he must explain his choice. If he gives no proof, his choice is baseless; if he gives proof, he has abandoned taqlid in favor of independent evidence. The author then highlights the problem of infinite regress: when asked why his religion is true, the imitator appeals to an authority, but is then asked why that authority's religion is true, leading to an endless chain of claims. The author emphasizes that claims must be established by proof (burhān), not by other claims. He also applies this principle to refute those who use mere correlation (al-ṭard) as evidence for a cause's property. The author then clarifies that if the imitator has independent proof (e.g., proofs of monotheism for the teacher), then both his teacher's and his own religion are verified. However, if the proof is only present in the teacher (e.g., miracles for the Prophet), the imitator's religion is not automatically true unless he demonstrates that his religion is identical to that of the teacher. The example of a Muslim citing Muhammad's miracles shows that the miracle establishes the Prophet's truth, but the imitator himself lacks such a miracle, so his own claim relies on identity of doctrine. The final part is cut off, but the argument continues with an objection.

Commentary
This passage presents a theological-methodological debate between the Ash'ari and Mu'tazili schools regarding the conditions for valid faith-knowledge. The Ash'ari position: a believer who bases his conviction on rational proof has sufficient knowledge; he need not personally be able to rebut every objection. Therefore, requiring personal ability to refute doubts is unnecessary.
