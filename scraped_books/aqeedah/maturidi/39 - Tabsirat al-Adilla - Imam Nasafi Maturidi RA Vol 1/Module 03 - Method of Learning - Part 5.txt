The Rational Path to Knowing God
Al-Nasafi enumerates the rational steps from self to God, prophets, and truth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical progression of rational knowledge from self to God, prophets, and truth, and the role of reflection and supplication.

Core Idea
Reflection leads from self to God, then to prophets and truth.

Al-Nasafi now presents a step-by-step rational journey that every thinking person can take. Starting from the undeniable fact of one's own coming into existence, through contemplation of the world, one arrives at the necessity of a Creator, then learns His attributes, then recognizes prophets, and finally distinguishes truth from falsehood. All of this is achieved through the twin pillars of reflection (nazar) and supplication (tadarru').

Guided Path
1
Follow the rational progression
Read the passage and list the steps: self's temporality → world's temporality → need for a temporalizer → attributes of the temporalizer → knowledge of prophets → truth of reports → acceptance of testimonies.
This is a cumulative argument; each step builds on the previous.
2
Notice the method
The passage concludes that these truths are known through 'reflection and thought along with supplication.' This shows that reason alone is not enough; divine aid is sought.
3
Reflect on the scope
This progression covers theology, prophethood, and epistemology, showing that Islamic doctrine is rationally grounded.

وليس طريق بيان الشرائع بمقصور على النص ليقال إنه لو لم ينص فقد ضيع أمر الأمة بل من الحكمة إيجاب الامتحان والتكليف بوجوه يختلف فيها أولو الألباب كما في سائر المواضع التي لا نص فيها، أليس أن كل ذي عقل يعقل بالضرورة حدوث نفسه ثم يعرف بالتدبير والتأمل حدوث كل محسوس لم يشهد ابتداءه، ثم يعقل كون كل حدوث بمحدث أحدثه بفضل تدبر فيه ونظر، ثم يعرف المحدث بعد طول النظر في أمره أنه يوصف بأنه ما هو أجسم أم عرض أم غير جسم ولا عرض؟ ويوصف بالعلم في الأزل أو الحدوث، وكذا القدرة وجميع الصفات ثم معرفة الرسل والأنبياء عليهم السلام، ثم معرفة صدق الأخبار وكذبها، ثم قبول الشهادات وردها. إن ذلك كله أو تمامه كان طرق الحق في ذلك من الباطل النظر والفكر مع التضرع إلى رب العالمين في التوفيق لذلك والهداية إليه فإنه الجواد الكريم، وإذا ثبت ذا لم يكن لأحد منع جواز محنة الصحابة بعد قبضه عليه السلام بمعرفة الأصلح للخلافة، والعقد له بما سبق من أدلة نظره للمسلمين وشفقته عليهم وتعظيمه أمر الله تعالى وقلة مبالاته في ذات الله تعالى بلومة اللائمين وطعن الملحدين واهتمامه لما عليه من أمر المؤمنين، وليست الروافض بأول طائفة من أهل الزيغ جحدت أمرًا قصرت عقولهم عن الوقوف على وجه الحكمة فيه، غير أنهم الجهالة اختصوا بالطعن على الصحابة، وعلى من أرادوا إثبات الحق له وعلى سلف الأمة بل على الله تعالى وعلى رسوله ما قررنا وبالله العصمة والنجاة عن كل ضلالة وبدعة.
The way of explaining the laws is not confined to the text, so that it be said that if He had not specified (it) by text, He would have neglected the affair of the community. Rather, it is from wisdom to obligate testing and obligation (taklif) through aspects concerning which the people of understanding differ, as in other matters where there is no text. Is it not that every rational person necessarily knows the temporality (huduth) of his own self, then knows through reflection and contemplation the temporality of every sensed thing whose beginning he did not witness, then comprehends that every temporal occurrence has a temporalizer (muhdith) that brought it about through increased reflection and examination, then knows the temporalizer after long consideration of its affair: that it is described as whether it is a body, an accident, or neither body nor accident? And He is described as possessing knowledge eternally or temporally, and likewise power and all attributes. Then (comes) knowledge of the messengers and prophets, peace be upon them, then knowledge of the truthfulness of reports and their falsehood, then acceptance of testimonies and their rejection. Indeed, all of that or its completion was the ways of truth in that from falsehood: reflection and thought along with supplication to the Lord of the worlds for success therein and guidance to it, for He is the Generous, the Noble. And if this is established, then no one can prevent the permissibility of the examination of the Companions after his death, peace be upon him, by knowing the most suitable for the caliphate, and the contract for him based on what preceded of the proofs of his care for the Muslims, his compassion for them, his magnifying the command of Allah, his lack of concern in the essence of Allah Almighty from the blame of the blamers and the criticism of the heretics, and his concern for the affairs of the believers. And the Rafida are not the first sect among the people of deviation that denied a matter which their intellects fell short of comprehending the aspect of wisdom therein, except that they—out of ignorance—are distinguished by criticizing the Companions, and those whom they intended to establish the truth for, and the predecessors of the community, indeed Allah Almighty and His Messenger, as we have established. And with Allah is protection and salvation from every misguidance and innovation.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological argument that the way of knowing divine laws is not limited to revealed texts; reason also plays a crucial role. The author (al-Nasafi) begins by asserting that it is not necessary for every ruling to be explicitly in the text; divine wisdom includes obligating people to use their reason in matters where there is no explicit text. He then provides a systematic progression of rational knowledge: every rational person necessarily knows the temporality (huduth) of his own self, then through reflection knows the temporality of all sensed things, then realizes that every temporal thing requires a temporalizer (muhdith), and through further reflection knows the attributes of that temporalizer—whether He is a body, accident, or neither, and whether His attributes like knowledge and power are eternal or temporal. This leads to knowledge of the prophets and messengers, and then to distinguishing truth from falsehood in reports and accepting or rejecting testimonies. Al-Nasafi emphasizes that all this knowledge is attained through reflection and thought (nazar wa fikr) combined with supplication to Allah for success. After establishing this rational method, he applies it to the case of the Companions' selection of the caliph: since they used their reason and acted with care and sincerity, their action is justified and cannot be criticized. He then refutes the Rafida (a sect that reviles the Companions) by arguing that their rejection stems from intellectual shortcoming, not from any fault in the Companions. The passage thus defends the legitimacy of the Companions' ijtihad in choosing Abu Bakr and warns against innovation.

Justifying the Companions' Choice of Caliph
Al-Nasafi defends the Companions' caliphate selection and refutes the Rafida.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Nasafi applies rational method to justify the Companions' selection of Abu Bakr and refute those who criticize them.

Core Idea
The Companions' ijtihad in choosing the caliph was rational and sincere.

Having established reason as a valid source of guidance, al-Nasafi now applies this principle to one of the most contentious issues in early Islamic history: the selection of Abu Bakr as caliph. He argues that the Companions used their reason, care, and piety to choose the most suitable leader. He then refutes the Rafida (a sect that reviles the Companions) by showing that their rejection stems from intellectual failure, not from any fault in the Companions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the application
Read the passage focusing on how al-Nasafi connects the earlier rational method to the Companions' action. He says 'if this is established' referring to the rational path.
2
Identify the justification
The passage lists the Companions' qualities: care for Muslims, compassion, magnifying Allah's command, and disregard for blame. These show their ijtihad was sincere.
The phrase 'أدلة نظره للمسلمين وشفقته عليهم' indicates rational consideration.
3
Refutation of the Rafida
Al-Nasafi argues that the Rafida denied this matter because their intellects fell short, not because of any error in the Companions. They are like previous deviant sects.

The Imamate by a Single Contract
Al-Nasafi argues that the imamate is established by a single qualified person's contract, citing historical precedents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Maturidi/Ash'ari position that the imamate is established by the contract of a single qualified mujtahid, without requiring unanimous consensus, and the historical precedents that support this view.

Core Idea
The imamate is valid by one qualified person's contract; consensus is not required.

Al-Nasafi addresses a practical question: how is the imam to be chosen? He rejects the requirement of consensus and presents the view that a single mujtahid's contract is sufficient, drawing on the actions of the Companions at Saqifa and Abu Bakr's appointment of 'Umar. This lesson explores the reasoning and evidence behind this position.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the complete Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the full translation to grasp the flow of argument.
Notice the structured argument: invalidity of nass, then election, then al-Ash'ari's ruling, then historical proofs.
2
Identify al-Ash'ari's ruling and the argument against consensus
Focus on the sentence beginning at 'وروي عن الأشعري' and the following lines. Understand why consensus is rejected as a condition.
Al-Ash'ari says one mujtahid's contract is binding; consensus would cause harmful delay.
3
Examine the historical evidences and al-Juwayni's addition
Look at the two proofs: the Saqifa incident with Abu 'Ubayda and Abu Bakr, and Abu Bakr's appointment of 'Umar. Then note al-Juwayni's condition of witnesses.
These examples show that the Companions acted without full consensus, yet the community accepted.

[ الكيفية التي يتم بها اختيار الإمام]
ثم إذا بطل ثبوت الخلافة بالنص ثبت أنها تثبت بالاختيار ثم للناس كلام في كيفية ذلك ولم أقف فيه على مذهب مشايخنا رحمهم الله تعالى. وروى عن الأشعري أن واحدا من أهل الاجتهاد لو اجتهد في زمان موت إمام ورأى أسباب الصلاحية في أحد ثابتة فعقد له الإمامة انعقدت له، وليس لغيره بعد ذلك أن يخالفه ولا وجه إلى اشتراط الإجماع اليوم لما فيه من تأخير الإمامة عن وقت الحاجة إليها لو شرط فيها الإجماع، على أن الصحابة لم يشترطوا فيها الإجماع عند الاختيار والمبايعة، وإنما اعتبروا وجود العقد ثم أوجبوا المبايعة بعد ذلك. ثم الإجماع إذا خرج من أن يكون شرطا لم يكن عدد أولي من عدد فسقط اعتباره لثبوت التعارض وانعدام الترجيح. ويستدل على صحة هذا القول بما روي عن عمر أنه قال يوم السقيفة لأبي عبيدة: ابسط يدك أبايعك فقال له أبو عبيدة: أقول هذا وأبو بكر حاضر فعلم من رأيه أنه يراه أولى، وأبو عبيدة أمين الأمة لا يخون، والأمة تعتمد على قوله فبايع أبا بكر وانعقدت إمامته وانبرمت، فدل هذا على أنها تنعقد بعقد واحد، وكذا استدلوا عليه بعقد أبي بكر لعمر واستخلافه له من غير استعانة بغيره، وجوزت الأمة ذلك وبايعوا عمر فكان هذا دليلا أن الإمامة تنعقد بعقد واحد بإجماع المسلمين، والله الموفق.
قال بذلك أيضا الجويني في الإرشاد ص ٤٢٤. إلا أنه أضاف قال بعض أصحابنا: لابد من جريان العقد بمشهد من الشهود فإنه لو لم يشترط ذلك لم نأمن أن يدع مدع عقدًا سرًا متقدما على الحق المظهر المبطن، وليست الإمامة أحط رتبة من النكاح وقد شرط فيه الإعلان.
[The manner in which the selection of the Imam is completed]
Then if the establishment of the caliphate by text (nass) is invalidated, it is established that it is established by election (ikhtiyar). Then people have discussion concerning the manner of that, and I have not come across a doctrine of our teachers, may Allah have mercy on them, regarding it. And it is narrated from al-Ash'ari that if one of the people of independent reasoning (ijtihad) makes an effort at the time of the death of an Imam and sees the causes of suitability in someone established, and then contracts the imamate for him, it is contracted for him, and it is not for anyone else after that to oppose him. And there is no way to requiring consensus (ijma') today because of the delay of the imamate from the time of need that would be entailed if consensus were stipulated, even though the Companions did not stipulate consensus at the time of selection and oath of allegiance; rather, they considered the existence of the contract and then required the oath of allegiance after that. Then consensus, having ceased to be a condition, (the issue of) number of people is not more important than another number, so its consideration falls due to the existence of conflict and the absence of weighting. One adduces the validity of this statement by what is narrated from 'Umar, may Allah be pleased with him, that he said on the day of Saqifa to Abu 'Ubayda: 'Extend your hand so that I may swear allegiance to you.' Abu 'Ubayda said to him: 'Do you say this while Abu Bakr is present?' So it was known from his opinion that he considered him more deserving. And Abu 'Ubayda is the trusted one of the nation, he does not betray, and the nation relies on his statement, so he swore allegiance to Abu Bakr, may Allah be pleased with him, and his imamate was contracted and concluded. This indicates that it is contracted by the contract of one person. And similarly, they adduced it by the contract of Abu Bakr for 'Umar and his appointing him as successor without seeking help from others, and the nation approved that and swore allegiance to 'Umar, may Allah be pleased with him. So this was a proof that the imamate is contracted by the contract of one person by the consensus of the Muslims. And Allah is the Granter of success.
Al-Juwayni also said this in al-Irshad, p. 424, except that he added: Some of our companions said: It is necessary for the contract to be made in the presence of witnesses, for if that were not stipulated, we would not be safe from someone claiming a contract in secret that is prior to the true one that is manifested and hidden. And the imamate is not of lower rank than marriage, and publicity has been stipulated in it.

Commentary
This passage discusses the procedural question of how the imam (caliph) is to be selected after establishing that the caliphate is not based on explicit textual designation (nass) but on election (ikhtiyar). The author (al-Nasafi) begins by noting that he has not found a definitive ruling from his Maturidi teachers on the exact procedure. He then reports a view attributed to al-Ash'ari: if a qualified mujtahid at the time of an imam's death sees that someone is suitable and contracts the imamate for him, that contract is valid and binding on the community; no one may oppose it afterward. Al-Nasafi argues against requiring consensus (ijma') for the contract, because that would delay the appointment and cause harm. He notes that the Companions themselves did not require consensus at the Saqifa; they only considered the existence of a contract, then required the general oath of allegiance (bay'a) afterward. Once consensus is not a condition, numerical superiority loses its significance. He then provides historical evidence: on the day of Saqifa, 'Umar proposed to swear allegiance to Abu 'Ubayda, but Abu 'Ubayda pointed to Abu Bakr as more deserving, and then swore allegiance to Abu Bakr, thereby establishing the caliphate by the contract of one person (Abu 'Ubayda's action). Similarly, Abu Bakr appointed 'Umar as his successor without consulting others, and the community accepted it. Thus, the imamate is validly contracted by one qualified person. Finally, al-Nasafi cites al-Juwayni, who adds a condition from some scholars: the contract must be made in the presence of witnesses to prevent fraudulent secret claims, just as marriage requires publicity. This passage illustrates the Maturidi/Ash'ari position on imamate selection, emphasizing practicality and precedent.
