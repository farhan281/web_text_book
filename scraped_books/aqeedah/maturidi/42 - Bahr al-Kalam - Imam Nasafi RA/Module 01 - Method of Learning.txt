Historical and Quranic Foundations of Ilm al-Kalam
The author introduces the historical and scriptural foundations of ilm al-kalam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that ilm al-kalam, though not present in its formal form at the time of the Prophet, is deeply rooted in the Qur'an's call to reflection and debate, and in early Muslim interactions.

Core Idea
Kalam is not an innovation; it flows from the Qur'an's demand to think and debate.

This opening passage sets the stage for the entire book by addressing a potential misconception: that ilm al-kalam is a foreign discipline imported later. The author shows that while the formal science did not exist in the Prophet's time, its principles—reflection, inference, and debate—are explicitly commanded in the Qur'an. He also points to early community interactions that naturally led to theological discussions.

Chapter One
Ilm al-Kalam between Methodological Study and Current Reality
The Muslims at the time of the Messenger of Allah, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, had no knowledge of ilm al-kalam in its form that we study today.
At the same time, no one can deny that the texts of the Qur'an call for reflection and use of the intellect to arrive at cosmic and religious truths together. Commentary
This passage introduces the first chapter of the book, titled 'Ilm al-Kalam between Methodological Study and Current Reality.' The author begins by stating a historical fact: the early Muslim community at the time of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) did not possess knowledge of 'ilm al-kalam in the formalized sense that is studied today. However, he immediately counters any possible misconception by affirming that the Qur'an itself repeatedly calls for intellectual reflection (al-nazar) and reasoning to arrive at both cosmic and religious truths. He cites two Qur'anic verses as evidence: the first (Q. 29:19-20) encourages observation of how Allah originates and repeats creation, implying that reflection leads to knowledge of divine power; the second (Q. 29:46) commands Muslims to debate the People of the Book in a manner that is best, which establishes a scriptural basis for dialectical engagement. The author then explicitly states that 'ilm al-kalam is founded upon the principles of reflection (al-nazar), inference (al-istidlal), and debate (al-jadal). He further notes that historical interactions between Muslims and Jews/Christians naturally generated discussions and debates, even if initially simple. He alludes to a famous story of a Jew who approached the Prophet with a question about Allah carrying the earths on a finger, which will be discussed later. Finally, he points out that after the Prophet's death, the first disputes among Muslims arose over the issue of leadership (imamah), which historically gave impetus to theological inquiry. The passage thus establishes both the scriptural and historical roots of kalam while acknowledging its later development as a formal discipline.

Different minds need different proofs; one size does not fit all.

The Prophet himself only demanded affirmation from the Arabs, leaving the manner of belief open. This lesson explores the three categories of people and how to address them.

Commentary
This passage is a quotation from Imam Abdul Ghani al-Maidani's commentary on al-Tahawiyyah, classifying people according to their intellectual capacity in responding to doubts. The author summarizes al-Maidani's view: The Prophet (peace be upon him) only required affirmation (tasdiq) from the Arabs, without distinguishing between traditional belief and certainty based on proof. For the coarse and stubborn person who clings to falsehood, arguments are useless; force is needed. For the doubter who has some intelligence but cannot grasp rational proofs, gentle persuasive speech should be used, not demonstrative proofs, due to their limited understanding. Al-Maidani notes that the ability to grasp abstract rational proofs is given only to a few; most people are deficient. They cannot perceive rational proofs just as dung beetles cannot perceive the light of the sun and are harmed by the scent of roses. Finally, the intelligent person who benefits from rhetorical speech should be argued with using decisive demonstrative proof.

The Need for Kalam and the Early Muslim Stance
Author explains historical necessity for kalam and summarizes early Muslim approach to attributes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the science of kalam emerged as a defensive necessity against intellectual challenges, and that the early Muslims affirmed Allah's attributes without anthropomorphism or negation.

Core Idea
Kalam arose to defend Islamic creed, not as innovation.

Before diving into the principles of kalam, it is important to understand why this science was developed. This passage from Imam Nasafi explains the external challenges that prompted Muslims to engage with rational theology, and it also describes the earliest approach to divine attributes—an approach that avoided both anthropomorphism and negation. By understanding this background, we see that kalam was not a departure from the Sunnah but a necessary tool for its defense.

Therefore the nation stirred to study and know this new thought coming upon it, which is represented in Greek philosophy and what it contains of the science of logic and dialectic and others, and in some other creeds and religions. The nation's acquaintance with these ideas and its study of them was not a matter of intellectual luxury, nor a matter of turning away from the table of the Book and the Sunnah to the tables of disbelief and atheism as some try to portray to us today. Commentary
This passage is a historical explanation establishing the necessity of the science of kalam. The author begins by describing the external threat: Greek philosophy, with its logic and dialectic, along with other creeds, posed intellectual challenges to Islamic beliefs. He stresses that Muslims engaged with these ideas not for intellectual leisure or out of a desire to abandon revelation, but as a defensive necessity. The opponents raised rational doubts about God, His names and attributes, prophets, angels, books, and eschatological matters. The author argues that relying solely on scriptural texts without rational support would have been ineffective; rational arguments must be met with rational arguments, otherwise greater strife would occur. Consequently, Muslims learned, taught, and wrote in these fields, giving rise to theological schools like the Mu'tazila, Ash'ariyya, and Maturidiyya—and these were not innovators but responders to necessity. The author then introduces a summary from al-Maqrizi, which describes the earliest Islamic period: when the Prophet described God, the Arabs understood without questioning, accepting the attributes without anthropomorphism or negation. This state continued through the Companions' era until the first sectarian deviation—the Qadariyya (free will) doctrine—appeared. The passage thus grounds kalam in a historical context of defensive scholarship, not innovation.

Al-Samarqandi's Method for Reconciling Text and Reason in Mutashabihat
Al-Samarqandi refutes a view on mutashabih and establishes rational proof as judge over textual proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Samarqandi's rejection of suspending judgment on mutashabihat and his principle that rational proof governs textual proof in cases of apparent contradiction.

Core Idea
Reason is the arbiter over revelation, not the reverse.

Al-Samarqandi presents a key methodological principle for handling ambiguous Quranic verses (mutashabihat). He first reports a view that when text and reason flatly contradict, one must simply suspend judgment. He then strongly rejects this, arguing that revelation never truly contradicts reason; when an apparent conflict arises, reason must guide the interpretation of the text because textual evidence can accommodate figurative meanings. This passage establishes the foundational hierarchy of evidence in Sunni kalam.

The first point (first issue) establishes that reflection (nazar) is obligatory in knowing the Creator. Indeed, the very first obligation upon a legally responsible person (mukallaf) is to engage in reflection and reasoning that lead to knowledge of Allah, His attributes, tawḥīd, justice, and wisdom. This is followed by successive stages: reflection on the possibility of prophethood and divine command, then reasoning to confirm actual prophethood through miracles, then reasoning to detail the pillars of the Law, and finally acting upon what is required. This sequence shows that intellectual reflection is the foundation of all religious knowledge. The second point clarifies that the obligation of reflection is not tied to the sophisticated proofs of the theologians; rather, each person must use evidence that is accessible to them. This prevents burdening individuals with overly technical arguments.

The Distinction Between Obligatory and Recommended Commands
Nasafi distinguishes obligatory from recommended commands using threat evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Nasafi distinguishes between obligatory and recommended commands in the Quran based on the presence of a threat for abandonment.

Core Idea
A command's obligation is proven by the threat for leaving it.

This passage addresses a key objection in usul al-fiqh: are all Quranic commands merely recommended? The objector points to commands like "eat and drink" that carry no punishment for neglect. Nasafi's answer provides a clear criterion: commands that are accompanied by a threat of punishment are obligatory, while those without are recommended. This lesson unpacks that distinction and its scriptural and rational basis.

Guided Path

Notice the verses cited: 7:31 and 5:2.
2

In the answer, Nasafi states that every command followed by a threat (wa'id) for abandoning it is obligatory. Recognize this as the key distinction.

4
Understand the rational argument

Commentary
This passage presents an objection and its answer concerning the nature of commands (amr) in the Quran. The objector argues that some commands, such as 'eat and drink' and 'hunt after pilgrimage', are not obligatory but merely recommended (nadb or istihbab), because they lack a threat of punishment for omission. The answer clarifies the distinction: commands that are not accompanied by a threat of punishment for leaving them are indeed for recommendation, as the objector said. However, commands that are accompanied by a clear threat of punishment, like prayer and zakat, are obligatory (hutm and ijab). The author supports this with Quranic verses that threaten punishment for neglecting prayer and for hoarding wealth without paying zakat, and concludes with the rational argument that it would be inconsistent with divine wisdom to create humanity without commands and prohibitions, citing verses that deny aimless creation.
