Definitions of Ma'rifa, Tawhid, Iman, Islam, and Din
Imam Nasafi enumerates definitions of five key theological terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinct definitions of the five core Islamic theological terms as explained by Imam Nasafi, and how they interrelate.

Core Idea
True faith is built on knowledge, denial of falsehood, inner belief, outward worship, and steadfastness.

Imam Nasafi, having clarified his creed, is asked to define the foundational terms of Islamic theology. He provides concise, interconnected definitions that progress from knowledge of God to steadfast commitment. This passage lays out the essential vocabulary of Aqeedah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five terms
Read the Arabic and translation. Find the five terms being defined: ma'rifa, tawhid, iman, islam, din.
The terms are presented in a specific order.
2
Analyze each definition
For each term, note the key action: knowing, denying, affirming/worshiping, steadfastness.
Notice that tawhid is defined negatively.
3
Notice the progression
See how the definitions build: from knowledge (ma'rifa) to denial (tawhid) to inner faith (iman) to outer worship (islam) to steadfastness (din).
4

Five Distinctions Between Divine Acts and Their Effects
Nasafi enumerates five distinctions between divine acts and their effects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the principle that each divine act (like making, providing, creating) is distinct from its created effect, and why this distinction matters for preserving God's transcendence.

Core Idea
God's actions are not identical to what they produce—each act-effect pair is distinct.

Imam al-Nasafi now lays down a foundational principle that runs through all of Kalam: divine acts are not the same as their effects. This short but powerful sentence clarifies that when we speak of God 'making', 'providing', or 'creating', we are referring to an eternal attribute or act, not to the temporal thing that results. This distinction prevents any confusion between Creator and creation.

Each pair follows the same grammatical pattern: masdar (verbal noun) + ghayr + ism maf‘ul.
2

3
Understand the principle's purpose
Ask yourself: why does Nasafi stress that these are distinct? Reflect on how this principle answers the earlier objection about ta‘rif and ma‘rifa.
This distinction safeguards God from being equated with His creation.
4
Test your understanding with the quiz

This prevents confusion between the Creator and creation. It reinforces the answer to the previous objection: God's definition (ta'rif) is not the same as the knowledge (ma'rifa) that results in the servant; they are different. This principle is crucial for maintaining divine transcendence and avoiding anthropomorphism or pantheism.

The Ruling on Istithna' in the Shahada (1): Statement of the Ruling
Nasafi states the ruling that istithna' in the shahada is kufr and gives the reason.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the consensus ruling that adding 'in sha Allah' to the testimony of faith constitutes disbelief and the principle that exception is not permissible in matters realized in the present or past.

Core Idea
Adding 'if Allah wills' to the shahada introduces doubt in what is certain and present, which is disbelief.

This lesson introduces the first part of Imam Nasafi's discussion on istithna' in the shahada. The passage establishes a clear ruling: uttering the shahada with 'in sha Allah' makes one a disbeliever because it implies uncertainty about what is certain. We will explore the reasoning and the key distinction between present/past and future matters.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic ruling
Read the first part of the passage where Nasafi states the ruling and the consensus. Notice the examples given: shahada, testimony of Muhammad, belief in angels, books, etc.
2
Identify the reason
The text says 'because he has doubted in his faith'. Understand that introducing an exception into a certain matter creates doubt.
3
Grasp the principle
The author explains that matters realized in the present or past do not admit exception. This is the core principle.
This principle will be contrasted with future matters in the next lesson.

Commentary
This passage begins by stating a ruling: if a person adds 'in sha Allah' (if Allah wills) to the testimony of faith, whether the shahada or belief in the unseen, they become a disbeliever. The reason is that such an exception introduces doubt into what is certain and present. The author then clarifies that this ruling applies only to matters that are realized in the present or past; future matters, like entering Paradise conditioned on dying in faith, may legitimately have an exception because they are not yet realized. Next, the author addresses a doubt: if someone says 'in sha Allah' but is actually a believer in the present, does that make them a disbeliever? The answer is no, as long as no explicit disbelief occurs from them. The analogy is given: just as Allah knows we will die, but we are not considered dead now, similarly, the Hour is coming but not present. The passage then cites the hadith of al-Harithah as proof: the Prophet did not reject al-Harithah's claim of being a true believer, but instead asked for the reality of his faith, and al-Harithah described a deep spiritual state. The Prophet affirmed him, showing that faith in the present is valid without exception. Thus, the ruling stands that adding 'in sha Allah' in present-tense testimony is disbelief.

The Ruling on Istithna' (2): Future Exceptions and Answering the Doubt
Nasafi clarifies that exception is allowed for future matters and answers the doubt that a present believer remains a believer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction that exception is permitted for future matters and the answer to the doubt that a believer in the present remains a believer until disbelief occurs.

Core Idea
Exception is allowed for future matters, and present faith is not negated by a conditional statement unless disbelief follows.

Then he answers a potential doubt: if someone says 'in sha Allah' but is a believer now, they do not become a disbeliever unless they actually commit disbelief. Analogies from divine knowledge illustrate this.

The Hadith of al-Harithah as Proof for the Ruling
Nasafi cites the hadith of al-Harithah as evidence for the ruling on istithna'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the hadith of al-Harithah serves as evidence that claiming true faith without exception is valid and that the Prophet affirmed it.

Core Idea
The Prophet's acceptance of al-Harithah's claim of true faith shows that faith in the present is certain and needs no exception.

This lesson presents the hadith evidence that Nasafi uses to support the ruling. The Prophet asked al-Harithah about the reality of his faith, and al-Harithah described his deep spiritual state. The Prophet affirmed him, showing that faith can be certain and present. This reinforces that adding 'in sha Allah' to the shahada is inappropriate for present matters.

1
Read the hadith
Read the narration from the Prophet to al-Harithah. Notice how the Prophet did not object to al-Harithah's claim of being a true believer.

Commentary
This passage is a short proof (istidlal) for a preceding claim about the superiority of prophets over awliya (saints). The author begins with the particle 'يدل عليه' indicating that what follows is evidence. First, he states that Ali was a wali and the Prophet Muhammad was a nabi, and it is unquestionable that a prophet is superior to a wali. This establishes the general principle. Then he gives a more detailed example: al-Khidr had knowledge from Allah called 'ilm al-ilham (knowledge by inspiration), yet Musa was superior because Musa had a shari'a (revealed law) and a kitab (scripture). The author generalizes that anyone who possesses a book and a law is superior, analogizing to David and Solomon: David is better because the Zabur was revealed to him. The argument uses comparative reasoning (tafdil) based on the nature of prophethood, which involves legislation and scripture, whereas wilaya lacks legislative authority.
