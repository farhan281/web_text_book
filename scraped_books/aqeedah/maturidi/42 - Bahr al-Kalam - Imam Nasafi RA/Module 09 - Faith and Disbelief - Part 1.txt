Nasafi defines Iman as confession, affirmation, and divine guidance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni definition of faith as consisting of verbal confession, heartfelt affirmation, and divine guidance.

Core Idea
Faith is confession with the tongue, affirmation in the heart, and guidance from Allah.

Imam Nasafi begins by clarifying the nature of faith, distinguishing it from the view that it is created or uncreated. He establishes that faith involves human action (confession and affirmation) and divine action (guidance and success). This careful definition guards against reducing faith to mere ritual or mere inner belief.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Iman is what is in the heart and spoken by the tongue, not what is done by the limbs.

This lesson presents the foundational Maturidi definition of iman. Imam al-Nasafi begins by stating that iman is verbal confession and heart-felt affirmation of Allah's oneness. A lengthy footnote then clarifies the Maturidi position that actions are not part of the essence of iman, citing early authorities. By the end, you will grasp a key point of difference within Sunni theology.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Jahmiyya sect’s definition of faith. Nasafi begins by stating their claim: faith is only knowledge (ma'rifa) in the heart, without any need for verbal confession (iqrar). Then he presents the Sunni position: knowledge alone is not faith unless accompanied by verbal confession. He then provides three Quranic proofs. The first verse (al-Ma'idah 5:85) speaks of Allah rewarding those who 'said' something, implying that speech (confession) is a condition for reward, hence for faith. The second verse (al-Baqarah 2:146) describes the People of the Book recognizing the Prophet as they recognize their own sons, yet some conceal the truth; their recognition (knowledge) did not suffice for faith because they did not confess. The third verse (al-Naml 27:14) states that Pharaoh's people rejected the signs even though their souls were certain of them; their certainty (knowledge) did not become faith due to lack of verbal confession. Thus, mere knowledge is not faith.
