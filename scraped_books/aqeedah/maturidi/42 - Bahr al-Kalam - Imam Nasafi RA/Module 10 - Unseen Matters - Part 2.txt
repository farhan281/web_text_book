This brief but profound statement from the sheikh addresses a subtle theological point: the weighing of faith. Core Idea
Paradise and Hell are created now and will never perish.

The author addresses a key theological dispute about the temporal creation of Paradise and Hell. He first presents the objection from the Mu'tazila and Jahmiyya, then provides the Sunni response supported by Quranic verses and rational argument. This passage presents an objection-and-answer argument regarding the punishment of the grave. Commentary
This passage provides two types of evidence for the punishment of the grave: transmitted (hadith) and rational. First, two hadiths: one to 'A'ishah describes the pressure of the grave for a believer as gentle as a mother's embrace, and the questioning as healing like kohl for sore eyes. The second hadith to 'Umar reassures that the believer will retain his intellect during the questioning, so 'Umar says he fears not. Then a rational proof is given: during sleep, the soul leaves the body but remains connected, so the sleeper feels pain or pleasure in dreams and may speak. This shows that connection is sufficient for sensation. Finally, a hadith from the Prophet answers a question about how flesh can hurt without the soul: it is like a toothache, where the tooth hurts because it is connected to the flesh, even though there is no soul in the tooth itself. This analogy demonstrates that physical connection allows sensation even without the soul being present inside the part. Together, these evidences refute the rationalist objection and support the Sunni doctrine.

Rational and Quranic Proofs for Punishment of the Grave
Nasafi presents rational and Quranic proofs for the reality of punishment of the grave.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational and scriptural evidence that establishes the reality of punishment of the grave.

Core Idea
The soul's connection to the body after death makes grave punishment both rational and scripturally confirmed.

Imam Nasafi now turns to direct proofs. He first presents a rational argument using the analogy of sleep and toothache, showing that the soul's connection to the body enables pain even after death. He then cites three Quranic verses and a report from Abu Hanifa to solidify the doctrine from revelation.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the rational analogy of sleep
The author compares the state of the deceased to that of a sleeper: the soul leaves but remains connected, so the sleeper feels pain and speaks in dreams. Similarly, the deceased feels pain in the grave.
This shows that the soul's connection to the body does not require the soul to be inside the body for sensation.
2
Understand the toothache analogy
The Prophet is reported to have said that a tooth can feel pain even though there is no soul in it, because it is connected to the flesh. Likewise, the body of the deceased feels pain due to the soul's connection.
This reinforces that pain can be transmitted through connection, not direct indwelling.
3
Analyze the Quranic verses
Three verses are cited: At-Tawbah 101 ('We will punish them twice...'), Ghafir 46 ('The fire, they are exposed to it morning and evening'), and At-Tur 47 ('For those who have wronged, a punishment before that'). Each is interpreted as referring to punishment of the grave.
The author explains why the 'two punishments' cannot be worldly and Hereafter, because 'then they are returned to a great punishment' refers to the Hereafter.
4
Reflect on Abu Hanifa's reasoning
When asked for evidence, Abu Hanifa cited At-Tur 47, interpreting 'a punishment before that' as the punishment of the grave, distinct from the great punishment of Hell.
This shows the consensus among scholars on this interpretation.

Location of the Sun, Moon, and Stars in the Heavens
Imam Nasafi presents two views on the placement of the sun, moon, and stars, and cites Quranic evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two competing views on the location of the celestial bodies and the Quranic evidence that supports the Sunni position.

Core Idea
The Quran places the stars in the lowest heaven, not the fourth.

This lesson addresses a specific theological question: Where are the sun, moon, and stars located? Astronomers (ahl al-nujum) held one view, but the Ahl al-Sunnah, relying on the Quran, hold another. Imam Nasafi presents both and then brings Quranic proofs to settle the matter. Let's explore the evidence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the two views
Read the Arabic passage once, then its translation. Notice that two distinct groups and their claims are presented: the astronomers and the people of tafsir.
The bracket indicates alternative naming for the second group.
2
Examine the Quranic verses cited
Find the two verses from Surah al-Saffat and al-Mulk. Notice how they explicitly mention the lowest heaven as the location of the stars.
3
Reflect on the Dhu al-Qarnayn argument
Consider the third proof from Surah al-Kahf. Why does the story of Dhu al-Qarnayn reaching the setting place of the sun argue against the sun being in the fourth heaven? This is a logical inference.
The conclusion is that the correct view is the one supported by Quranic evidence.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal classification and proof. Imam Nasafi presents two conflicting views on the location of the sun, moon, and stars: the astronomers (ahl al-nujum) claim they are in the fourth heaven, while the Ahl al-Sunnah or the people of Quranic exegesis (ahl al-tafsir) hold they are in the lowest heaven (al-sama' al-dunya). The author then provides Quranic evidence to support the latter view. First, he cites verse 6 of Surat al-Saffat, which states that Allah adorned the lowest heaven with the beauty of the stars. Second, he cites verse 5 of Surat al-Mulk, which says Allah adorned the lowest heaven with lamps (masabih, i.e., stars). These verses explicitly place the stars in the lowest heaven, not the fourth. Third, he invokes the story of Dhu al-Qarnayn from Surat al-Kahf (verse 86), where Dhu al-Qarnayn travels to the west and finds the sun setting in a muddy spring. Nasafi argues that if the sun were in the fourth heaven, Dhu al-Qarnayn could not have reached it or observed it setting so closely; since he did, the sun must be in the lower heavens, specifically the lowest. The passage concludes with a prayer: 'And Allah is the Guide to the path of right guidance.'
