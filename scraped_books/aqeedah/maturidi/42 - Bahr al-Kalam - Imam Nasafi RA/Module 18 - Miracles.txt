Imam Nasafi now addresses a critical objection raised by three theological sects: that affirming saintly miracles would collapse the difference between prophets and saints. Commentary
This passage enumerates three differences between prophetic miracles (mu'jizat) and saintly miracles (karamat). First, in terms of visibility: prophetic miracles are seen by everyone—believer, disbeliever, obedient, and disobedient—because they serve as public proof of prophethood. Saintly miracles, by contrast, are only witnessed by fellow saints, not by sinners, as they are not meant as public proof. Second, in terms of volition: the prophet can produce his miracle whenever he wishes by supplicating Allah, as he has the ability to bring it forth. The saint, however, does not have such control; his miracle occurs only at specific times determined by Allah, as an encouragement for his obedience. Third, in terms of acknowledgment: the prophet is obligated to recognize and affirm that his miracle is from Allah, and to publicly declare it. Denying it would be disbelief because his prophethood depends on it. The saint, on the other hand, is not required to acknowledge that his experience is his personal miracle; instead, he attributes it to the virtue of another believer, thereby remaining humble and avoiding any claim to special status.
