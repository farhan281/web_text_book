God's Transcendence Over Similarity and Opposites
Nasafi argues that affirming an opposite negates God's divinity and similarity negates His oneness, concluding with the Qur'anic verse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why affirming any similarity or opposite to God negates His oneness and divinity, and how the Qur'an confirms His absolute transcendence.

Core Idea
To affirm an opposite or likeness for God is to deny His divinity and uniqueness.

This passage from Imam Nasafi's creed presents a fundamental argument for God's absolute transcendence. Before we can properly understand divine attributes, we must first clear our minds of any notion that God resembles creation or has an opposite. Nasafi shows that such ideas directly contradict the very meaning of divinity and oneness.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic once to let its rhythm sink in, then read the English translation.
Notice the logical flow: if you affirm a partner, you deny His divinity; if you affirm similarity, you deny His oneness.
2
Identify the two key claims
The passage makes two interconnected arguments: (1) an opposite negates divinity, (2) similarity negates oneness. Underline these in the text.
These are the core of the argument.
3
Connect to the Qur'anic proof
Read the verse from Surah Ash-Shura and reflect on how it serves as the ultimate evidence for God's transcendence.
The verse is the 'tie-off' of the argument.

Commentary

The passage culminates in the Qur'anic verse from Surah Ash-Shura, which is the definitive proof against any anthropomorphism or dualism.

Refuting Anthropomorphism: Body and Form
Al-Lamishi refutes anthropomorphism by logical proof and citation of extreme views.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Lamishi uses logical reasoning (no body → no form) and reports of extreme anthropomorphic claims to affirm God's transcendence.

Core Idea
God's transcendence precludes any form or composition.

Al-Lamishi, a classical Sunni theologian, addresses a dangerous misconception: that God might have a body or human form. He begins with a logical premise—if God is not a body, He cannot have a form, because form requires composition (tarkib). He then reports shocking anthropomorphic claims from extremist groups, showing how such beliefs implicitly attribute originatedness (huduth) to God. This passage is a powerful reminder of the Islamic commitment to tanzih (transcendence).

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the translation. Notice the logical flow from premise to conclusion.
The passage is a quote from al-Lamishi's 'al-Tamhid'.
2
Analyze the logical deduction
Identify the key logical step: if God is not described with a body (jism), then He cannot be described with a form (surah), because form cannot exist without composition (tarkib).
This is a core argument against anthropomorphism.
3
Examine the reported anthropomorphic claims
Note the four reported views: (1) God in human form with limbs, (2) as an old man with white beard, (3) as a beardless youth, (4) Hisham ibn al-Hakam's glistening ingot.
These are extreme examples meant to show the absurdity.
4
Understand the refutation
Al-Lamishi points out that all these descriptions imply huduth (originatedness). This is the decisive objection.
The passage ends with a declaration of negation by Allah's praise.
5
Reflect on common misunderstandings
Consider that denying a body does not deny all attributes—only bodily, composite attributes. God still has attributes like knowledge and power.
This avoids the opposite extreme of ta'til (stripping God of attributes).

The Vision of Allah and Delight According to Disposition
Nasafi affirms the vision of Allah on Judgment Day and explains its delight according to worldly disposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the vision of Allah on Judgment Day is a real event whose delight varies according to each person's spiritual disposition in worldly life, and that true pleasure is in knowledge.

Core Idea
Seeing Allah is a delight shaped by your worldly heart.

This passage from Imam Nasafi's Bahr al-Kalam addresses one of the most profound topics of Islamic eschatology: the vision of Allah (ru'yat Allah) on the Day of Judgment. Nasafi affirms this vision while warning against those who deny it due to rationalistic limitations. He then explains that the joy of seeing Allah is not uniform—it corresponds to what dominated each person in this world, culminating in the recognition that true pleasure lies in knowledge. The passage ends with a heartfelt supplication for guidance.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First read the Arabic passage or its literal translation to get the flow. Notice the warning against denial and the emphasis on personal disposition.
2
Identify the three key elements
Nasafi presents: (a) the reality of the vision, (b) the reason some deny it, (c) the proportional delight. How are they connected? Notice that 'the investigators confined pleasure to knowledge' is the key doctrinal point about true delight.
3
Reflect on the supplication
The passage ends with a personal plea for guidance. Consider why Nasafi includes this after the doctrinal statement.
This shows the practical application of the doctrine in the seeker's life.

God's Transcendence Over Space
Nasafi defines God's relation to space and istawa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Nasafi affirms God's transcendence over space, clarifying that His 'istawa' over the Throne does not imply physical location.

Core Idea
God is not in a place; He is the Creator of all places.

This lesson focuses on a central creedal point: God's relationship with space. Nasafi states that God created the Throne and time, and then 'established Himself over the Throne' (istawa). Immediately, he clarifies that this does not mean God sits or rests on the Throne, for the Throne is not a location for Him. Rather, God is the Creator of the Throne and of all locations, and He is above every place in a non-spatial sense of supremacy.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice the sequence: creation of time and Throne, then istawa, then the negative statements.
The passage uses a very precise theological vocabulary.
2
Identify key terms
Find the words: العرش (Throne), المكان (place), الاستواء (establishing). Note how they are used in the context of God.
The term istawa is often misunderstood.
3
Analyse the doctrine
Understand the positive and negative statements: God is مستعل (elevated) but not the Throne; the Throne is not a resting place; God is the Creator of the Throne and place.
This is a classic formulation of tanzih (transcendence).
4
Reflect on the implications
Ask yourself: Why is it necessary to affirm that God is above every place without being spatial? What does this tell us about His nature?
This protects against anthropomorphism.

God's Knowledge and the Refutation of the Mu'attila
Nasafi describes God's knowledge and will, then uses Surah al-Ikhlas to refute Mu'attila.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Nasafi links God's comprehensive knowledge and will to Surah al-Ikhlas as a refutation of those who deny His attributes (Mu'attila).

Core Idea
God's knowledge and will encompass all, and His uniqueness is affirmed by the Qur'an.

If He were on something, He would be in need of it for support and would be carried, which implies dependency. Each of these implications leads to disbelief (كفر) according to the speaker. The passage also includes a manuscript variant that adds that He has no need and does not settle. This emphasizes that the notion of 'settling' (استقرار) is also negated, further refuting anthropomorphic interpretations of Allah's 'istiwa' (establishing) over the Throne.

Bazdawi on the Metaphorical Ascension of Good Speech
Bazdawi interprets the verse about ascension metaphorically, denying spatial movement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Imam al-Bazdawi interprets the verse about the ascension of good speech metaphorically, and how this argument refutes anthropomorphism.

Core Idea
Good speech ascends in honor, not in space — Allah is beyond direction.

Imam al-Bazdawi encounters a verse that might seem to suggest spatial movement towards Allah. But true tanzih (transcendence) demands careful interpretation. In this passage, he shows that the literal meaning is impossible for speech, and therefore the verse must be understood metaphorically. This lesson unpacks his reasoning step by step.

Commentary
This passage is a proof from Imam al-Bazdawi arguing for a metaphorical interpretation of the verse 'إليه يصعد الكلم الطيب' (To Him ascends good speech). The argument proceeds in several steps: First, he asserts that Allah's attributes are eternal and unchanging, as He was before creation. Then he addresses the verse: the literal meaning (الحقيقة) of 'ascend' cannot apply here because speech, especially human speech, is not a physical entity that can move upwards. Moreover, it has no permanence. Since the literal meaning is impossible, we must understand that the literal intended meaning is not known; the verse becomes ambiguous (مجمل) and cannot be used as a proof for spatial direction. Therefore, the intended meaning is metaphorical (مجازي): it refers to the elevation of the speech in honor and the raising of the doer's rank, making him close to Allah in status, not in spatial proximity. Bazdawi uses an analogy: a person can be 'close' to a king in terms of honor and status without being physically near. Similarly, Allah raises the doer of good speech to a high station. The phrase 'فصاعد بشرفه' (so he ascends in his honor) emphasizes the figurative nature. This refutes anthropomorphists who take the verse literally to prove that Allah is in a direction.
