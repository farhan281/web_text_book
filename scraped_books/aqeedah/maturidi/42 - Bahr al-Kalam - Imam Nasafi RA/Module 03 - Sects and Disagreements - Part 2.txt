Mu'tazila and Khawarij View on Lawful Muslim Blood

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila and Khawarij believed Muslim blood could be lawful for four specific reasons.

This passage presents the view of two early sects on a grave matter: when is it allowed to kill a Muslim? The Mu'tazilites and Kharijites, despite their differences, agreed on four conditions that make a Muslim's blood lawful. Understanding their position highlights the Sunni stance on the inviolability of Muslim life.

The conditions are numbered in the text: one, second, third, fourth.

This shows that not all abandonment is treated equally.

The Three Conditions for Lawful Blood in Sunni Islam
Nasafi cites the prophetic hadith for three capital offenses.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three specific conditions from the hadith that make a Muslim's blood lawful according to Ahl al-Sunna.

Core Idea
Muslim blood is sacred; only three capital offenses permit it.

This lesson examines the foundational hadith that defines when a Muslim may be subject to capital punishment. The Sunni position is based on the Prophet's ﷺ explicit statement limiting lawful blood to three cases: apostasy, adultery after marriage, and premeditated murder.

Textual Designation of Imamate Refuted
Nasafi distinguishes between specific and absolute executorship to refute the Rafidah claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni position that the imamate was not textually designated for Ali and the refutation of the Rafidah claim based on the distinction between specific and absolute executorship.

Core Idea
Ali was executor only for the Prophet's debts, not for all affairs.

The Rafidah (Shi'a) claim that the imamate was explicitly designated for Ali relies on the notion that Ali was the Prophet's executor (wasi). In this passage, Nasafi presents the Sunni counter-argument: the executorship was limited to a specific task—paying debts—and does not imply a general or political succession. This distinction is key to refuting the claim of textual designation.

2

The response shows that being executor for debts does not make Ali the political successor.

First, the author argues that Ali's knowledge came from the Prophet's teaching, using the hadith (though its authenticity is debated among scholars) that the Prophet is the city of knowledge and Ali its gate. Since a gate is not greater than the city, Ali cannot be greater than the Prophet. Second, the author makes a general principle: a prophet (nabi) is superior to a saint (wali), and Ali was a wali while the Prophet was a nabi. Then he addresses the analogy of Khidr and Moses: Khidr had ladunni (direct divine) knowledge, which is knowledge of inspiration (ilham), but Moses was superior because he was a prophet with a scripture (the Torah) and a law (shari'a). The author notes that having a book and a shari'a makes a prophet superior, citing the example of David and Solomon — both were prophets, but David is considered superior because the Zabur was revealed to him. Thus, the claim that Ali was like Khidr and more knowledgeable is refuted. The author concludes that the Prophet Muhammad is superior to all, and the Rafidi claims are baseless denial of clear texts and consensus.
