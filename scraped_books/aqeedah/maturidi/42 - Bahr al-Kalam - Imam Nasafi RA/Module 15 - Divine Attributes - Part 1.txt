Allah's perfection, eternal attributes, and pre-existence.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Allah's perfection and pre-existence are fundamental to Islamic theology.

This passage from Imam Nasafi's Bahr al-Kalam describes the absolute perfection and pre-eternity of Allah. It affirms that Allah is perfect in His essence, eternal in His attributes, and existed before all creation, including space and time. This establishes the foundation for understanding Allah's transcendence and independence from His creation.

Allah's Comprehensive Knowledge and Predestination
Imam Nasafi affirms Allah's comprehensive knowledge and absolute decree.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic belief that Allah's knowledge encompasses all things—past, future, and counterfactuals—and that every event occurs solely by His will, decree, and predestination, while accepting His attributes as described in the Qur'an without modality.

Core Idea
Allah knows everything that happens and everything that could happen, and nothing occurs without His will and decree.

This passage from Imam Nasafi's Bahr al-Kalam lays the foundation for understanding divine predestination (qadar) in Sunni Islam. It affirms that Allah's knowledge is eternal and comprehensive, covering not only actual events but also hypothetical scenarios. Moreover, it declares that every single thing in creation happens through Allah's knowledge, will, desire, decree, and predestination. Finally, it reminds us to accept Allah's self-description without attempting to imagine a form or likeness.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text once to get a feel for its rhythm. Even if you do not understand every word, notice the repeated emphasis on divine attributes.
The passage is short but packed with theological weight.
2
Understand the translation
Read the English translation carefully. Pay special attention to the counterfactual statement: 'what will not be, if it were to be, how it would be.' This is a distinct aspect of divine knowledge.
3
Analyze the attributes listed
Identify the five attributes mentioned: knowledge ('ilm), will (irada), desire (mashi'a), decree (taqdir), and predestination (qada'). Reflect on how each one contributes to Allah's absolute control over creation.
These are not redundant; each emphasizes a different facet of divine authority.
4
Grasp the final point on self-description
Notice the concluding phrase: 'without form' (min ghayri sura). This teaches that we affirm Allah's attributes exactly as He described Himself, without comparing Him to creation or asking 'how.'
This is the principle of tafwid—consigning the modality to Allah.
5
Test your understanding
Answer the checkpoint question to see if you can identify what Allah's knowledge includes according to the passage.
The answer is C, which includes both actual and counterfactual knowledge.

Commentary
This passage discusses Allah's knowledge and decree. His knowledge predates all things. Then it states that nothing occurs in His dominion except by His knowledge ('ilm), will (irada), desire (mashi'a), decree (taqdir), and predestination (qada'). This is a strong affirmation of divine predestination. Finally, it concludes by stating that Allah is exactly as He described Himself in the Qur'an without form (min ghayri sura) and as He defined Himself — meaning we accept His attributes without delving into how (bila kayf) and without tashbih or ta'til.

God's Oneness and Perfection in Nasafi's Essence
Nasafi affirms God's oneness, pre-eternality, and negation of imperfections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Nasafi affirms God's absolute oneness, perfection, and transcendence over space and time through a series of negations and positive attributes.

Core Idea
God is One, perfect, and existed before all creation—no opposite, no peer, no dependence on space or time.

This passage from Imam Nasafi's Bahr al-Kalam lays the foundation for Islamic theology by affirming God's unique oneness and perfection. It uses a series of negations and affirmations to clarify that God is unlike any created being. As you read, notice how each phrase builds a complete picture of divine transcendence and eternality.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic text and its English translation once to get the overall flow. Pay attention to both the negations (لَا, وَلَا) and affirmations (هُوَ, لَمْ يَزَلْ).
The passage is dense but every phrase carries theological weight.
2
Identify the negations
Find the phrases that deny anything comparable to God: 'لَا ضِدَّ لَهُ' (no opposite) and 'وَلَا نِدَّ لَهُ' (no peer). Understand that these negate any equal or rival in essence or attributes.
These establish God's absolute uniqueness (tawḥīd).
3
List the key affirmative attributes
Note the series of adjectives describing God: أَحَدًا (One), صَمَدًا (Self-Sufficient), فَرْدًا (Unique), وَتْرًا (Alone). Also note the phrases 'الكامل في ذاته' (perfect in essence) and 'الأزلي بصفاته' (eternal in attributes).
These affirm God's perfection and self-sufficiency.
4
Notice the negation of imperfection
See how the text says 'المنزَّه عن النقصان' (transcendent above imperfection) and gives examples: 'بلا نسيان' (without forgetfulness) and 'بلا اشتباه' (without confusion).
This underscores that God's knowledge is absolute.
5
Reflect on God's priority over creation
The final sentence asserts that God existed before creating place (المكان) and time (الوقت والزمان). This shows that God is not conditioned by space or time.
This refutes any notion that God is subject to created dimensions.

Allah's Foreknowledge and Counterfactual Knowledge
Nasafi affirms God's foreknowledge and knowledge of counterfactuals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Allah's knowledge encompasses both what will occur and what would have occurred under different circumstances.

Core Idea
Allah knows everything — actual and hypothetical — before it happens.

Imam Nasafi begins by affirming the absolute scope of divine knowledge. He not only states that Allah knows everything that will happen before it occurs, but also that He knows the full nature of events that never come to pass. This challenges any limited view of God's omniscience.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the two distinct clauses about knowledge.
2
Identify the two aspects of knowledge

The second claim is about counterfactuals.
3
Reflect on the significance
Ask yourself why counterfactual knowledge matters. Think about how this guards against limiting God's knowledge to what actually happens.
This relates to the misconception that God only knows actual events.

Source Text

عَلِمَ ما يكون قبل أن يكون، وما لا يكون أن لو كان كيف يكون. قد سبق علمه في الأشياء قبل كونها، ولا يكون في ملكه شيء إلا هو بعلمه وإرادته ومشيئته وتقديره وقضائه.

This passage is a proof of God's omniscience and predestination. First, it states that God knows what will happen before it occurs (mā yakūn qabla an yakūn), and also knows what will not happen—but if it were to happen, He knows how it would be (mā lā yakūn an law kāna kayfa yakūn). This addresses hypotheticals, counterfactuals, and conditions. Then it asserts that God's knowledge preceded all things before their coming into existence (sabaqa ‘ilmuhu fī al-ashyā’ qabla kawnihā). Finally, it declares that nothing occurs in God's dominion except by His knowledge, will (irāda), wish (mashī‘a), decree (taqdīr), and determination (qaḍā’). This refutes the Qadariyya (who deny predestination) and affirms Ash'ari/Maturidi doctrine of divine decree.

The First Two Refutations: Huwa and Ahad
Nasafi explains the refutational force of 'Huwa' and 'Ahad' against four deviant groups.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the words 'Huwa' and 'Ahad' in Surah al-Ikhlas refute those who deny God's existence or attributes and those who associate partners with Him.

Core Idea
The first two divine names in the surah dismantle atheism and polytheism in one breath.

Nasafi opens his tafsir by showing that Surah al-Ikhlas is a comprehensive refutation of false beliefs. He explains that 'Huwa' points to the existent God, refuting those who deny His attributes (Mu'aṭṭila) and those who claim esoteric knowledge (Batiniyya). Then 'Ahad' affirms absolute oneness, refuting polytheists and dualists. This lesson focuses on these first two refutations.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation, noting how each word is linked to a specific group.
2
Identify the two words and their refutations
'Huwa' refutes Mu'aṭṭila and Batiniyya; 'Ahad' refutes polytheists and dualists. Understand what each group believes.
Mu'aṭṭila deny attributes; Batiniyya claim hidden meanings; polytheists worship others; dualists believe in two gods.
3
Reflect on the significance
Why does Nasafi emphasize that the surah is not just a statement but a naqḍ (refutation)?

The Sunni Definition of Knowledge
Definition of knowledge by Ahl al-Sunna with distinction between human and divine.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni definition of knowledge and how it differs for created beings and Allah.

Core Idea
Human knowledge is cognition; divine knowledge is encompassing and eternal.

This passage begins with a question about the definition of knowledge. Ahl al-Sunna provides a precise answer that distinguishes between human and divine knowledge, grounding it in a Quranic verse.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition carefully
Read the Arabic passage where Ahl al-Sunna defines knowledge as 'cognition of the known as it is' for created beings, and as 'encompassing and acquaintance' for Allah.
Notice the Arabic terms used: معرفة for human, إحاطة and خبر for divine.
2
Understand the distinction
Reflect on why Allah is not described as having 'ma'rifa' (cognition). The reason given is that He has always been knowing, so His knowledge is not acquired.
This distinction prevents attributing change or acquisition to Allah.

Connect to the Quranic evidence
The author cites verse 18:91 as evidence for divine encompassing knowledge. Read the translation and see how it supports the definition.
The verse uses the same root (أحطنا) as the term 'ihata'.

Proof of the Eternity of Divine Attributes
Imam Nasafi proves eternity of attributes via reductio ad absurdum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof that Allah's attributes of essence are eternal, based on the impossibility of attributing incapability and ignorance to Him.

Core Idea
If Allah lacked power in eternity, He would be incapable — impossible.

Imam Nasafi here presents a concise objection-and-answer argument. The objector asks for the proof that attributes like power, knowledge, life, etc. are eternal. The answer uses a reductio ad absurdum: if these attributes were not eternal, then before they were created Allah would be described with their opposites — incapability and ignorance — which is impossible. Thus the attributes must be eternal.

This is a classic reductio ad absurdum: assume the opposite, derive a contradiction, so the original must be true.

The Rational Necessity of Calling Allah 'Thing'
Nasafi's rational argument for calling Allah 'thing' and response to Mu'attila.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Denying that Allah is a thing leads to nothingness; affirming thingness saves from ta'til.

This lesson presents the rational argument for why it is permissible and even necessary to refer to Allah as a 'thing' (shay'). Nasafi shows that the opposite of 'thing' is 'nothing', so rejecting thingness implies nothingness.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal dispute over the permissibility of calling Allah 'Nur' (light). It begins as a chapter title. Nasafi reports two positions: the Mushabbiha (anthropomorphists) claim that Allah is a shining light, while Ahl al-Sunnah reject this outright. And in the report: 'O Allah, do not place a hand of a sinful person upon me' meaning a favor. And among them is sin, as His saying the Exalted: 'Because of what your hands have earned.' And among them is the limb, which is the right and left, and Allah is exalted above the other two, and these two [i.e., the hand of Allah and His kingdom] are without how, comparison, form, or limb, and they are among the eternal attributes.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the four meanings of the word 'yad' (hand) as used in the Quran and Arabic language. The author lists them: (1) Kingdom (mulk), exemplified by the verse 'In His hand is the kingdom' and the idiomatic expression 'this village is in the hand of so-and-so'; (2) Grace (minna), as in 'the hand of Allah is above their hands' meaning His favor, and the hadith asking not to let a sinner have a 'hand' (favor) over the speaker; (3) Sin (ma'siya), as in 'because of what your hands have earned'; (4) Limb (jariba), i.e., the physical hand. The author then clarifies that when these meanings are applied to Allah, the last one (limb) is negated, and the first two (kingdom and grace) are affirmed as eternal attributes without modality (bila kayf), comparison (tashbih), form (sura), or limb (jariba). This guards against anthropomorphism while affirming the reality of God's attributes.

Scholarly Critique of the Hadith of Manifestation

Core Idea
The hadith of Manifestation must be understood through transmission-by-meaning and figurative interpretation.

In this lesson, we explore the scholarly critique of the Hadith of Manifestation, a narration that appears to describe Allah in anthropomorphic terms. Trusted scholars like al-Kawthari, Ibn Hazm, and Ibn al-ʿArabī employ rigorous hadith criticism and theological reasoning to demonstrate that the literal wording cannot be taken at face value. Their analysis reveals the mature methodology of Sunni scholars in handling ambiguous texts.
