What Is Kalam?
beginner
6 min
The Qur'anic Foundation of Ilm al-Kalam
The author presents the Qur'anic basis for kalam through two verses and states the three methodological pillars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Qur'an itself commands reflection, use of the intellect, and debate, providing the foundational principles of ilm al-kalam.

Core Idea
Ilm al-kalam is rooted in the Qur'an's call to think and discuss.

The author begins by noting that the early Muslims did not know kalam in its developed form, but he immediately emphasizes that the Qur'an repeatedly invites reflection and reasoned dialogue. This lesson examines the two key verses cited, showing that the very activities of nazar (reflection), istidlal (inference), and jadal (debate) are divinely sanctioned. You will see how kalam’s methodology is not an innovation but a fulfillment of Qur'anic guidance.

Guided Path
1
Read the introductory statement
Notice that the author first states that kalam as a formal discipline did not exist in the Prophet's time. This sets up the need to show its later development.
The author is careful to connect kalam to revelation, not to innovation.
2
Examine the first Qur'anic verse
Read the verse from Surah al-'Ankabut and its translation. Consider how it commands observation and reasoning about creation to infer divine power and resurrection.
This verse supports the use of intellect for theological conclusions.
3
Examine the second Qur'anic verse
Read the second verse which commands debating the People of the Scripture in the best way. This establishes the legitimacy of interfaith dialogue and apologetics.
The phrase 'in the best way' sets a standard for respectful argument.
4
Connect to the three foundations
The author concludes that reflection, inference, and debate are the bases of kalam. Identify how each is supported by the verses or the overall discussion.
These three terms are technical in kalam.

Chapter One
Ilm al-Kalam between Methodological Study and Current Reality
The Muslims at the time of the Messenger of Allah, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, did not have knowledge of ilm al-kalam in the form in which we study it today.
At the same time, no one can deny that the texts of the Qur'an call for reflection and the use of the intellect to reach both cosmic and religious truths. He, exalted, says: 'Have they not seen how Allah originates creation, then repeats it? Indeed, that is easy for Allah. Say: Travel through the land and observe how He began creation. Then Allah will bring into being the final creation. Indeed, Allah is over all things competent.' [Al-'Ankabut: 19-20] Likewise, He calls for debating those of opposing beliefs in the best way and inviting them to Islam. He, exalted, says: 'And do not argue with the People of the Scripture except in a way that is best, except for those who commit injustice among them, and say: We believe in that which has been revealed to us and revealed to you. And our God and your God is one, and we are Muslims to Him.' [Al-'Ankabut: 46]. And upon the foundations of reflection, inference, and debate, ilm al-kalam is based.
Likewise, no one denies that the contact that used to occur between Muslims and others, such as Jews and Christians, would sometimes generate discussions and debates, even if in a simple preliminary form. The story of the Jew who came to the Prophet, peace be upon him, and said to him: 'Indeed, Allah carries the earths upon a finger...' is well-known and famous, and its mention and commentary will come in the book, Allah willing. After the death of the Prophet, peace be upon him, the first disagreements occurred among the Muslims concerning the issue of the imamate. This issue later became one of the most important topics of ilm al-kalam, and it had a great role in the division of the Muhammadan community, followed by the trials that struck the Islamic ranks and created a rift that has not been mended to this day.
Then the expanse of the Islamic state expanded, and the Muslims encountered cultures, ideas, and beliefs foreign to the nature of Arab-Islamic thinking that had prevailed in the Arabian Peninsula. The Muslims met the Persians and Romans, coexisted with them, and new philosophies were transferred to the Arabs. Books of philosophy were translated into Arabic. Here, the Muslims found themselves facing new ideas flowing continuously day by day into their minds until these foreign philosophies and theories reached their beliefs. So it was necessary to confront these new ideas that began to raise questions and inquiries about the Islamic creed. The nature of society had changed, and Arab society was no longer that simple society.
(1) Imam Abd al-Ghani al-Maydani says in Sharh al-Aqida al-Tahawiyya, p. 44, summarizing: Indeed, the Prophet, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, did not demand from the Arabs in his address to them anything more than affirmation, and he did not differentiate whether that affirmation was through faith and traditional commitment or through demonstrative certainty. The harsh, coarse, weak-minded person who is rigid in imitation and insists on falsehood—argument and demonstration are of no use with him; rather, the sword and spear are of use with him. As for the doubters who possess a kind of intelligence but whose minds do not reach the understanding of intellectual demonstration that yields certainty and conviction, they should be treated gently with what is possible of persuasive and acceptable speech, not with certain demonstrative proofs, due to the deficiency of their minds in grasping them. Because guidance by the light of the intellect stripped of customary matters is not granted by Allah, exalted, except to the few of His servants. The majority of creation are deficient and ignorant. Due to their deficiency, they do not perceive the proofs of the intellects just as the light of the sun does not perceive intellectual demonstrative proofs, just as the scent of roses harms the dung beetle, and the eyes of bats harm them. Rather, they are harmed by the intelligent one who benefits from rhetorical speech; then debate with him is obligatory by means of decisive demonstrative proof.

Commentary
This passage is a historical introduction to the study of ilm al-kalam. It begins by noting that during the time of the Prophet (peace be upon him), Muslims did not know kalam in its developed form as studied today. However, the author immediately affirms that the Qur'an itself calls for reflection (nazar) and use of the intellect, citing two verses: one urging observation of God's creation (to infer divine power and resurrection), and another commanding that the People of the Scripture be argued with in the best manner. Thus, the foundations of kalam—reflection, inference (istidlal), and debate (jadal)—are rooted in revelation. The passage then points to early interfaith contacts, such as the story of a Jew questioning the Prophet about God carrying the earth on a finger, as precedents for theological discussion. After the Prophet's death, the first intra-Muslim disputes over imamate arose, and this issue became a major topic of kalam, leading to lasting divisions. Next, the author describes the expansion of the Islamic state and encounters with Persian, Roman, and other cultures, resulting in the translation of philosophical works and the influx of foreign ideas that challenged Islamic beliefs. This necessitated the development of kalam to defend the creed. Finally, a lengthy footnote from Imam Abd al-Ghani al-Maydani's commentary on the Tahawiyya is quoted, summarizing a methodological point: the Prophet himself did not require demonstrative proof from all people; different audiences require different approaches. The coarse, rigid imitator is only deterred by force, while the intelligent inquirer may be persuaded by rhetorical argument, but the person who benefits from kalam (the intelligent seeker) should be engaged with decisive demonstrative proof. This footnote clarifies that kalam is not for everyone, but for those capable of intellectual reasoning.

Early Stimuli: Interfaith Contacts and Imamate Dispute
The author describes early interfaith encounters and the imamate dispute as catalysts for theological reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how early interfaith discussions and the first division over imamate contributed to the development of ilm al-kalam.

Core Idea
Early theological encounters and political disputes planted the seeds for systematic kalam.

The author now turns to historical events that prompted theological reflection. The contact between Muslims and followers of other religions led to informal debates, like the well-known story of the Jew questioning the Prophet. More significantly, the first major internal disagreement after the Prophet's death—over the imamate (leadership)—created lasting divisions and became a central topic of kalam. This lesson explores these early catalysts.

Guided Path
1
Read about interfaith contacts
Notice the mention of debates between Muslims and Jews/Christians. The story of the Jew questioning the Prophet about God carrying the earth is an example.
This shows that theological discussions occurred even in simple forms.
2
Focus on the imamate dispute
The author identifies the first disagreement among Muslims as being about imamate. Understand why this became a major topic of kalam.
The imamate question later split the community into Sunni and Shia streams.
3
Reflect on the significance
Consider how such practical disputes forced Muslims to articulate arguments using reason and revelation, forming the basis for systematic theology.
Kalam developed partly to defend communal positions.

Commentary
This passage presents a theological definition of knowledge (ilm) and refutes the Mu'tazilite view. If we defined knowledge as 'knowing a thing as it is', it would imply that things exist eternally alongside God, which is the doctrine of the atheist philosophers (falasifa). Thus, the Mu'tazilite definition leads to unacceptable consequences.

Commentary
This passage presents three different positions on the ruling of kasb (acquisition/earning of wealth). It begins by stating the topic: clarifying the reality of kasb, seeking wealth, and the reality of tawakkul (trust in God). First, the Qadariyya (a sect that emphasizes human free will) assert that it is an obligation (fard) upon the servant to acquire and seek wealth, implying that not doing so is sinful. Second, Ahl al-Sunna wa al-Jama'a (the mainstream Sunni position) give a nuanced ruling: if one already has sufficient sustenance (qut), then kasb is a sunnah (recommended) and mubah (permissible); if one does not have sustenance but has a dirham to buy it, then it is a rukhsa (concession); and if one is in dire need or has a family, then it is a fard (obligation). Third, the mutaqashshifa (a term that may refer to ascetics or those who affect poverty) and the Karramiyya (a sect known for anthropomorphism and extreme asceticism) declare both kasb and hoarding wealth (wad' al-mal) as haram (forbidden), arguing that tawakkul upon God is wajib (obligatory) and kasb contradicts it. They support their view with two Quranic verses commanding reliance on God (Al 'Imran: 122 and Al-Ma'ida: 23), and they argue that acquisition nullifies tawakkul because God provides from unexpected sources. The passage thus sets up the debate, with the extreme positions of the Qadariyya (obligation) and the ascetics (forbidden) bracketing the balanced Sunni view.

Three Differences Between Mu'jizat and Karamat
Enumeration of three differences between mu'jizat and karamat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three key distinctions between prophetic miracles (mu'jizat) and saintly wonders (karamat) as taught by Imam Nasafi.

Core Idea
Prophets control miracles; saints' wonders are hidden and untimed.

In Islamic theology, it is essential to distinguish the miracles granted to prophets (mu'jizat) from the wonders bestowed upon saints (karamat). Confusing the two could blur the unique status of prophethood. This passage from Imam Nasafi clearly enumerates three practical differences that safeguard this distinction.

And the difference between miracles (mu'jizat) and wonders (karamat) is that miracles for the prophets are seen by the disbeliever, the Muslim, the obedient, and the sinner; whereas wonders of the saints are not seen except by a similar saint, and the sinner does not see them. The second: that the miracle, whenever the prophet wants, he is able to produce it, so he supplicates to Allah Almighty, and He manifests his miracle to him; whereas the wonder occurs only at specific times, [Allah Almighty shows him that as an encouragement for obedience]. Commentary
This passage classifies and contrasts prophetic miracles (mu'jizat) and saintly wonders (karamat) through three distinct differences. First difference: visibility—miracles are witnessed by all people, believers and disbelievers alike, while wonders are only seen by fellow saints, not by sinners. Second difference: control—a prophet can initiate his miracle whenever he wishes by supplicating Allah, but wonders occur only at specific times, given by Allah as an incentive for obedience. Third difference: recognition and obligation—a prophet must recognize and publicly affirm his miracle; denying it would be disbelief. A saint, however, is not required to claim the wonder as his own; rather he attributes it to other believers. These distinctions preserve the unique status of prophethood and the special nature of prophetic miracles.
