The Emergence of Sects According to al-Maqrizi
Al-Maqrizi chronologically lists deviant sects from Qadariyya to Mujassima

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the chronological emergence of major deviant sects in early Islam as recorded by al-Maqrizi, and how each sect represented an extreme reaction against the orthodox position.

Core Idea
Deviant sects emerged gradually as reactions to each other and to orthodoxy.

Al-Maqrizi provides a concise historical survey of how sectarian divisions arose after the early Islamic period. This lesson guides you through his chronological account, from the first free-will advocates to the Corporealists, showing how each deviation sparked a counter-deviation. By following his narrative, you can see how the early community's middle path was challenged from multiple directions.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its literal English translation. Pay attention to the sequence of sects as they appear. Notice the names of founders and key doctrines mentioned.
The passage uses ellipses (...) to indicate abridgment; focus on the main structure.
2
Identify each sect and its founder
As you read, list the six sects in order: Qadariyya (Ma'bad), Khawarij, Shi'a extremists, Jahmiyya (Jahm), Mu'tazila (al-Hasan al-Basri), and Mujassima (Ibn Karram). Note what each group believed and how they deviated from orthodoxy.
3
Notice the chronological and causal links
Observe how al-Maqrizi presents each sect emerging in a specific era, often as a reaction to the previous one. For example, the Mu'tazila opposed the Jahmiyya's negation, and the Mujassima overcorrected against the Mu'tazila.
This shows a pattern of theological pendulum swings.
4
Reflect on the orthodox position
Consider what the orthodox position must be: affirming attributes without likeness (against Jahmiyya and Mujassima), affirming divine justice without denying free will (against Qadariyya and Jabriyya), and maintaining community unity (against Khawarij and extremists).
Implicitly, al-Maqrizi defends the Sunni middle path.
5
Answer the checkpoint question
Test your understanding with the multiple-choice question. Return to the passage if needed.

Commentary
This passage is a chronological enumeration of deviant sects, quoted from al-Maqrizi. The author uses this summary to show how sectarian divisions emerged gradually after the early Islamic period. It begins with the Qadariyya: Ma'bad al-Juhani was the first to advocate free will (qadar). Then the Khawarij (Kharijites) appeared in the time of Ibn 'Umar, declaring excommunication for major sins and legitimizing rebellion against the imam. Ibn 'Abbas debated them, and 'Ali fought them. Next came the Shia extremists (ghulat) who exaggerated in their love for 'Ali; 'Ali himself denounced them and burned some. After the Companions' era, Jahm ibn Safwan emerged in the East, denying all divine attributes (ta'til), causing great tribulation. Then the Mu'tazila arose after 200 AH, pioneered by al-Hasan al-Basri (though the text says al-Hasan ibn al-Husayn al-Basri, likely a corruption). They focused on justice, monotheism, free will, denied vision of God in the afterlife, denied bodily punishment in the grave, and declared the Qur'an created. Finally, the Mujassima (Corporealists) appeared as a counter-reaction, led by Muhammad ibn Karram, who affirmed attributes to the point of anthropomorphism. The passage thus presents a historical chain of deviations, each reacting to previous ones, and underscores that the early community was united on a middle path of affirmation without likeness and transcendence without negation.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That all sects appeared simultaneously.

?

What concrete step will you take to seek the balanced path?

The first theological deviation in Islam was the denial of divine predestination.

Ma'bad al-Juhani is identified as the first to speak about it, and his views were quickly rejected by Umar ibn al-Khattab. This lesson covers that initial controversy.

Each principle is based on a rational argument about what befits God's justice and perfection.

Chapter on Proof of Punishment of the Grave. The Mu'tazila, Najjariyya, and Jahmiyya said: Punishment of the grave and the questioning of Munkar and Nakir are not accepted by reason and analogy, because if He punishes, it is either that He punishes the flesh without the soul, or He causes the soul to enter it and then punishes it. It is invalid that the flesh be punished without the soul, because flesh without the soul does not feel pain. And it is invalid that the soul enters it and then punishes it, because if the soul entered it, it would require a second death. And that is not permissible because Allah Most High said: 'Every soul will taste death' (Qur'an 3:185). He informed that they will not taste death in it except once. So when the two possibilities are invalidated, the third possibility is determined, which is that there is no punishment in the grave.

Commentary
This passage presents the objection of three rationalist sects—the Mu'tazila, Najjariyya, and Jahmiyya—against the doctrine of punishment in the grave. They argue using a dichotomy: if Allah punishes in the grave, it must be either by punishing the flesh without the soul (which is impossible because flesh without the soul cannot feel pain) or by re-entering the soul into the body and then punishing it (which would require a second death, contradicting the Qur'anic verse that every soul tastes death only once). Since both options are invalid, they conclude that grave punishment cannot occur. This sets up the Sunni response that follows.

The Inviolability of Muslim Blood
Classification of reasons for lawful killing of a Muslim by different sects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni position on when a Muslim's blood may be lawfully shed and how it differs from the Mu'tazila and Khawarij.

Core Idea
Muslim life is inviolable except by clear textual evidence.

In this lesson, we examine a crucial issue of Islamic law: when is it permissible to shed the blood of a Muslim? The author presents the views of two deviant sects and then the position of Ahl al-Sunnah, grounded in a prophetic hadith. This comparison highlights the Sunni emphasis on preserving life and strict limitation of killing to specific causes.

Guided Path

Understand the context

The term 'Ahl al-Qibla' refers to all Muslims who pray toward the Kaaba.

Note the four conditions under which they consider a Muslim's blood lawful: major sin, innovation, rebellion, and neglecting obligations.

Fighting a rebel (baghi) is allowed only while he fights; if he ceases, fighting must stop.
This is not a general permission to kill but a defensive measure.

Section on clarifying the inviolability of the blood of the people of the Qibla. The Mu'tazila and the Khawarij said: The blood of the people of the Qibla becomes lawful by one of four meanings: First: if he commits a major sin. Second: if he innovates a bid'ah. Third: if he draws a sword against the ruler. Fourth: if he neglects an obligation, i.e., abandons it; but if he deems its abandonment permissible, his blood becomes lawful by consensus. Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah said: The blood of the people of the Qibla is not lawful except by one of three meanings according to the hadith, which is what was narrated from the Prophet ﷺ that he said: 'The blood of a Muslim man is not lawful except by one of three meanings: disbelief after faith, adultery after marriage, and killing a soul without right.' As for one who rebels against the ruler, it is permissible to fight him as long as he fights, and if he stops, he is left.
Source pages p. 224

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the different doctrinal positions on when a Muslim's blood may be lawfully shed. The author first presents the view of the Mu'tazila and Khawarij, who list four conditions: committing a major sin (kabira), introducing a religious innovation (bid'a), rebelling against the ruler (sall sayfan 'ala al-sultan), and abandoning a compulsory duty (ta'til farida). They add that if one considers the abandonment lawful (istahalla tarkaha), then his blood is lawful by consensus. Then the author presents the position of Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah, who restrict permissibility to three conditions based on a hadith: apostasy after faith (kufr ba'd iman), adultery after marriage (zina ba'd ihsan), and murder without right (qatl nafs bi-ghayr haqq). Finally, the author addresses the case of rebellion (baghi): it is permissible to fight a rebel only as long as he is fighting; if he desists, fighting must stop. This passage highlights the Sunni emphasis on preserving Muslim life and restricting killing to explicit textual evidence.
