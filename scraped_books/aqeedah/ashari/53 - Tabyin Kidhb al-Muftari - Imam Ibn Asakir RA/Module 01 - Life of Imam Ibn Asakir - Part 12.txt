Al-Bayhaqi's Clarification on Al-Shafi'i's Stance on Kalam
Al-Bayhaqi interprets al-Shafi'i's rebuke and clarifies the meaning of 'kalam'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Bayhaqi's explanation of al-Shafi'i's rebuke and why al-Shafi'i's opposition was only to heretical innovations, not all kalam.

Core Idea
Al-Shafi'i's rejection of kalam was a rejection of heresy, not theology itself.

After narrating the story, al-Bayhaqi adds his own commentary to clarify the intent of al-Shafi'i's rebuke. He explains the metaphor of Tharan and Junbulan and shows that al-Shafi'i himself knew how to answer heretical arguments. This lesson examines al-Bayhaqi's analysis and its implications for the proper use of kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read al-Bayhaqi's addition
Read the Arabic and translation of al-Bayhaqi's commentary. Note that it begins with an extra saying of al-Shafi'i, then an explanation of the metaphor, then a conclusion about the meaning of 'kalam'.
Al-Bayhaqi's addition is crucial for understanding the full picture.
2
Understand the metaphor of drowning
Al-Bayhaqi explains that al-Shafi'i compared al-Muzani to someone about to drown in the very place Pharaoh drowned. This emphasizes the mortal spiritual danger of unchecked heresy.
The metaphor extends: al-Muzani had a heretical argument he couldn't answer, and al-Shafi'i taught him the answer.
3
Note al-Bayhaqi's final clarification
Al-Bayhaqi explicitly states that by 'kalam' al-Shafi'i meant the heresies of the people of innovation, not all theological discourse. This corrects a common misunderstanding.
This shows that al-Shafi'i himself was proficient in kalam but disapproved of its misuse.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical narrative (khabar) with subsequent commentary. It recounts an encounter between al-Muzani and Imam al-Shafi'i. Al-Muzani had a question about tawhid (divine unity) and assumed al-Shafi'i could answer it. Al-Shafi'i becomes angry and rebukes him severely, pointing out the danger of the place (Tharan and Junbulan, where Pharaoh drowned) as a metaphor for the danger of theological speculation without proper foundation. He asks al-Muzani whether the Prophet or the Companions engaged in such questioning, and al-Muzani admits they did not. Then al-Shafi'i uses a series of analogies: al-Muzani does not know the number of stars, nor the nature of a single star, nor even a weak creature he sees with his own eye—so how can he speak about the knowledge of the Creator? He then tests al-Muzani on a practical matter (wudu') and he fails. Al-Shafi'i concludes that al-Muzani neglects a religious obligation required five times daily (prayer) while attempting to understand the knowledge of the Creator. He advises him to instead use the Quranic method: infer the Creator from creation, and not to take on knowledge beyond his intellectual capacity. Al-Muzani repents. Al-Bayhaqi then adds his own commentary: he notes al-Shafi'i's warning and explains that al-Shafi'i compared al-Muzani—who had been confronted by a heretic's argument he could not answer—to someone drowning in the same place as Pharaoh. Al-Shafi'i then taught him the answer, showing both his own deep knowledge and that it is obligatory to expose heretical obfuscations when necessary. Al-Bayhaqi clarifies that by 'kalam' al-Shafi'i meant the heretical innovations of the people of heresy and innovation, not all theology. The passage demonstrates al-Shafi'i's mastery of kalam despite his public avoidance of it, and his emphasis on relying on revelation and inference from creation.

Commentary
This passage addresses the historical error concerning Bishr al-Marisi. The author begins by stating that despite his high status in fiqh, Bishr was among those who insisted on the doctrine of the createdness of the Qur'an. Abu Yusuf repeatedly warned him but he did not desist, leading Abu Yusuf to expel him and threaten him with crucifixion. When al-Rashid heard this, he vowed to kill him. Some later authors, like Abu al-'Ala' al-Razi, concluded that the threat was carried out and Bishr was crucified. However, the author corrects this: Bishr remained hidden throughout al-Rashid's caliphate, and the severe search for him did not result in his capture, as reported by reliable authorities. Therefore, the claim that al-Ma'mun brought him close during al-Rashid's reign is false. The author also criticizes al-Bayhaqi for relying on unreliable historians. Finally, he clarifies the true story: al-Shafi'i became acquainted with Bishr in the Hijaz after his first journey to Iraq, and during his second journey in the reign of al-Amin, Bishr hosted him, but al-Shafi'i's mother reproached him, so he moved away. There are also known debates between them.

Correcting a Chronological Error: Al-Shafi'i and Al-Ma'mun
Al-Amidi corrects Ibn Asakir's error that al-Shafi'i met al-Ma'mun in Baghdad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how careful chronology prevents errors in historical reports about scholars.

Core Idea
Chronological precision is essential for verifying historical claims about scholars.

Often, respected historians can make errors when they overlook basic chronological facts. In a marginal note, al-Amidi catches Ibn Asakir in a simple but significant timeline mistake. This passage teaches us the importance of verifying dates even when the source is authoritative.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation to get the gist of the correction. Notice the specific dates mentioned.
The passage is a marginal note correcting an earlier statement.
2
Identify the two key dates
Find the death date of al-Shafi'i (last day of Rajab 204 AH) and the date al-Ma'mun entered Baghdad (14 nights left in Safar 204 AH). Realize that Safar is five months after Rajab in the same year? Actually, Rajab is the 7th month, Safar is the 2nd month. But note: al-Shafi'i died in Rajab 204, al-Ma'mun entered Baghdad in Safar 204. Since Safar comes before Rajab, al-Ma'mun entered after al-Shafi'i's death? Wait: The comment says al-Shafi'i died five months after al-Ma'mun's entry? Let's check: Al-Ma'mun entered Baghdad in Safar 204, al-Shafi'i died in Rajab 204, which is five months later. So actually al-Shafi'i died after al-Ma'mun's entry, but the key is al-Shafi'i never left Egypt, so he could not have met al-Ma'mun in Baghdad. The error is corrected differently in literal translation: 'He verified that he died in Egypt five months after al-Ma'mun's entry into Baghdad, yet he makes such an error.' So the error is saying he attended the assembly. The correction is that he died after the entry but never left Egypt, so he couldn't have been in Baghdad.
Make sure to follow the timeline carefully. Al-Ma'mun entered Baghdad in Safar 204, and al-Shafi'i died in Rajab 204. So al-Shafi'i died after al-Ma'mun's entry, but being in Egypt, he could not have attended.
3
Understand why the error is impossible
Al-Amidi provides evidence: al-Shafi'i never left Egypt after entering it, so he couldn't have been in Baghdad. This contradicts any claim of him meeting al-Ma'mun there.
4
Reflect on the lesson for scholarship
Consider how a great historian like Ibn Asakir could make such an error despite having the correct biographical data. The lesson is that even experts must double-check chronological consistency.

First, it details al-Shafi'i's early life: he heard the Muwatta' from Malik, then went to Yemen due to financial need, worked various jobs, practiced archery, and studied language, poetry, and history for about seventeen years. In 184 AH, he was arrested on suspicion of collusion with the 'Alawids and taken to Baghdad, but was cleared. He then studied under Muhammad ibn al-Hasan and others. This was his first journey to Iraq. His second journey was in 195 AH (al-Amin's reign), and his third and last in 198 AH (after al-Ma'mun's caliphate was acknowledged). During the last two journeys, al-Ma'mun was in Khurasan, so al-Shafi'i never met him in Baghdad. Then the author refutes a story from al-Ma'afa ibn Zakariyya al-Harwani's book 'al-Jalis' that al-Ma'mun tested al-Shafi'i by making him drink wine and he remained unaffected. The author argues that this story, transmitted via a single chain, likely refers to another man named Abu 'Abd al-Rahman Ahmad ibn Yahya al-Shafi'i, a mutakallim who was among al-Ma'mun's inner circle and later joined Ibn Dawud in the mihna. The author says that what is transmitted through a single chain is either to be rejected outright or accepted as a whole, and yet Ibn Hajar included the beginning of this story in his Manaqib, which is odd. He concludes that al-Shafi'i's last journey was indeed during al-Ma'mun's caliphate, so there is no issue there, but al-Shafi'i could not have witnessed al-Ma'mun's assembly in Baghdad because al-Ma'mun was in Khurasan, unless what is meant is that he saw the effects of their dominance in Baghdad.
