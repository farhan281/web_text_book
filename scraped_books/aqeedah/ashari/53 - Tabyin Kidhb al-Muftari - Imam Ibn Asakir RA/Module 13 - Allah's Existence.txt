Proof from Hadith: Only Allah is Eternal
Ibn Asakir extracts a theological proof from the hadith of Imran ibn Husayn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the hadith 'Allah existed and nothing else existed with Him' proves that the Throne and water are created and not eternal.

Core Idea
Only Allah is eternal; everything else, including the Throne and water, is created.

This lesson examines a key hadith used by Imam Ibn Asakir to establish a foundational principle of Islamic theology: that Allah alone is eternal. The passage shows how the Prophet's ﷺ words, recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari, directly refute the idea that the Throne or water are uncreated. As you read the source, pay attention to how the commentary draws out the theological implications of each phrase.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith narrative
Begin by reading the story of the Yemenites asking the Prophet about the beginning of creation. Notice the exact wording of the Prophet's response.
The hadith is narrated by Imran ibn Husayn.
2
Identify the theological claim
Focus on the phrase 'Allah existed and nothing else existed with Him.' What does this imply about the Throne and water?
The commentary will clarify.
3
Analyze the commentary
Read Ibn Asakir's commentary. He explains that the hadith proves nothing coexisted with Allah—not water, not the Throne. All are created.
Note how he interprets 'His Throne was upon the water' as sequential creation.
4
Reflect on the methodology
Consider why the Yemenites' question is used as evidence that discussing the 'principles of religion' is a legitimate practice inherited from the Companions.

Commentary
This passage is an argument and commentary based on a hadith. It begins with a narration of the hadith of Imran ibn Husayn, where the people of Yemen ask the Prophet about the beginning of creation. The Prophet responds with the famous statement that Allah existed before everything, His Throne was upon water, and He wrote all destinies, then created the heavens and earth. The author then provides a commentary on this hadith. First, he notes that the very question of the Yemenites about the beginning of creation serves as evidence that discussing the principles of religion (usul) and the origination of the world is a legitimate and inherited practice from the companions. Then he explains the phrase 'Allah existed and nothing else existed with Him' to mean that absolutely nothing else—neither water, nor the Throne, nor anything else—coexisted with Allah. All of those are created things distinct from Allah. Finally, he interprets 'His Throne was upon the water' in terms of chronological creation: Allah created the water, then created the Throne upon the water, and then wrote all things in the Preserved Tablet. Thus the passage refutes any notion that the Throne or water are eternal; they are created entities after Allah's existence.

Createdness of Paradise and Divine Decree
Ibn Asakir affirms three creedal points on creation and decree.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ibn Asakir's affirmation that Paradise and Hell are created, and that death and provision are decreed by Allah.

Core Idea
Allah has created Paradise and Hell, and every life ends by His decree.

Ibn Asakir begins by affirming foundational beliefs about the afterlife and divine decree. He asserts that Paradise and Hell are created entities, that every death occurs at its appointed time, and that all provision comes from Allah. These points establish a clear Sunni position against those who deny creation of the hereafter or claim human autonomy.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text aloud or silently, noting the three affirmations: created Paradise/Hell, death by appointed term, and provision from Allah.
The language is concise and declarative.
2
Understand each affirmation
Reflect on the meaning of each point: Paradise and Hell are not eternal in the sense of being uncreated; death is predetermined; provision includes both lawful and unlawful.
3
Connect to broader creed
Ask why Ibn Asakir emphasizes these points. They counter the Mu'tazila who denied creation of Paradise/Hell and those who exaggerated human free will in provision.
This lesson establishes the framework for later discussions on qadar.

Commentary

Finally, the author praises the imam who composed this creed for its clarity and eloquence.

Satan's Whisperings and Refuting Deviant Sects
Ibn Asakir affirms Satan's whisperings and refutes Mu'tazila and Jahmiyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ibn Asakir's affirmation that Satan whispers to humans, causing doubt, and how this refutes the Mu'tazila and Jahmiyya.

Core Idea
Satan's whisperings are real, and the Mu'tazila and Jahmiyya are wrong to deny them.

Ibn Asakir defends the reality of Satan's influence on the human soul, citing Quranic verses. This counters the rationalist sects who claimed that Satan cannot affect humans. The passage reminds us that evil whispers are part of divine trial and do not absolve human responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the passage about Satan's whisperings and the two Qur'anic citations. Note the clarity of the refutation.
The verses are from Surah Al-Baqarah and Surah An-Nas.
2
Identify the refuted sects
Ibn Asakir explicitly names the Mu'tazila and Jahmiyya as holding contrary views. Consider why they denied Satan's influence.
These sects emphasized human free will and denied any real effect from Satan.
3
Reflect on the implications
Understanding that Satan can whisper helps explain the origin of evil thoughts while maintaining human accountability through resisting.
This aligns with Sunni theology of kasb (acquisition) and trial.

When you think about Allah's knowledge of 'what would be if it were,' consider a major regret in your life. Do you ever dwell on 'what if' scenarios? How should this belief change your relationship with regret and trust in Allah's wisdom? This week, when a regret surfaces, consciously replace it with gratitude that Allah knows what you don't.
?
Ibn Asakir commands parting from every caller to innovation and avoiding people of desires. Is there any person or group you defend or listen to that openly calls to a belief or practice with no basis in the Quran and Sunnah? What would it cost you to distance yourself from them? Before this day ends, identify one such influence and decide how you will reduce its impact on your faith.
?
The test of the children of polytheists involves jumping into fire on Allah's command. Reflect: if Allah commanded you to do something seemingly irrational or difficult, would you obey without hesitation? Where in your life are you holding back from full obedience because the command doesn't make sense to you? Commit today to obeying one command you have been resisting, trusting that Allah knows what is best.
