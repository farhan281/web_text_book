Commentary

God was without place before creation and remains so after.

Al-Ash'ari steers a careful course between two extremes regarding God's relation to space. On one side, the Najjariyya claimed God is in every place; on the other, the Hashwiyya said He dwells bodily on the Throne. Understand the anthropomorphist claim
The anthropomorphists interpret al-Muqam al-Mahmud as seating the Prophet on the Throne. Ibn Asakir considers this a vile innovation and a corruption of tanzih.
Contrast this with the Sunni consensus that the station is a high rank, not a physical seating.

Commentary
This passage is a scathing critique of anthropomorphist tendencies and their spread. The author expresses distress that a vile faction (the Hashwiyya) persists. He finds it unsurprising that al-Barbahari, known for his ignorance, holds such beliefs. What is surprising is that Ibn al-Qayyim, a respected scholar, recorded the interpretation that the Praiseworthy Station (al-muqam al-mahmud) refers to seating the Prophet on the Throne, and that he listed a group holding that view. The author then attacks modern reformers who advocate following the school of a figure who made an absurd statement about God settling on a mosquito's back. He concludes that if such is reform, then Islam is lost, and criticizes Sunni scholars for not opposing these innovations.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The Praiseworthy Station (al-Muqam al-Mahmud) means seating the Prophet Muhammad on the Throne of Allah.
Correction: Ibn Asakir condemns this as an anthropomorphist innovation; the station is a high rank and intercession, not a literal seating on the Throne.
