Commentary
This passage consists of two parts. He ends with a metaphor: just as precious jewels are rare and only found among elite circles, so too is the jewel of knowledge (jawhar al-ma'rifah) extremely rare.

Al-Qushayri Defends Ash'ari on Laymen's Faith
Al-Qushayri refutes the claim that Ash'ari excludes laymen from faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that al-Ash'ari, as defended by al-Qushayri, does not require laymen to know technical kalam terms like jawhar and 'arad for valid faith; rather, the obligation is to know Allah through His proofs, and technical terms are merely tools for facilitation.

Core Idea
Faith requires knowing Allah by His proofs, not memorizing kalam vocabulary.

Al-Ash'ari was accused of excluding common Muslims from faith because they cannot articulate the technical arguments of kalam. In this passage, al-Qushayri, a leading Ash'ari scholar, clarifies the true position: the essence of faith is reflection on Allah's signs, not the use of specific terminology. This defense shows the continuity between the Salaf and later theologians.

The passage begins with al-Qushayri denying the accusation.

Al-Qushayri states what is actually obligatory (wajib) for the mukallaf: to know Allah through His proofs. Contrast this with what is not required: using the specific terms of kalam like jawhar and 'arad.
3
Understand the role of kalam terminology
Note that the mutakallimin used terms like jawhar and 'arad only as approximations (taqrib) and facilitation (tashil) for learners. The real goal is nazar and istidlal leading to ma'rifah.
This addresses the accusation that Ash'ari made faith dependent on knowledge of these terms.

Reflect on the Salaf and Khalaf
Al-Qushayri argues that the righteous predecessors (salaf) had sound knowledge without using these terms, and the successors (khalaf) who used them did not deviate from truth or introduce innovation. This shows the continuity of correct belief across generations.

Commentary

Commentary
This passage is a defense of the science of kalām against the accusation that it is an innovation (bidʿa). Ibn ʿAsākir begins by quoting al-Qushayrī, who states that al-Ashʿarī does not say the common masses are unbelievers for lacking kalām. Rather, all scholars of verification agree that the obligated person must know God through His evidences; the goal is not the specific terms (jawhar, ʿaraḍ) but attaining rational investigation (naẓar) and inference (istidlāl). The mutakallimūn use these terms only to facilitate learning for students. The Salaf did not use them but had no deficiency; the Khalaf who used them did not thereby innovate. He draws an analogy to other sciences: later jurists introduced terms like ʿilla and qiyās, but this was not innovation; likewise grammarians and hadith transmitters have their own technical vocabulary. If someone claims occupation with kalām is innovation, that charge applies to all mutakallimūn, not only Ashʿarī. Seeking comfort in such objections is the trait of the Hashwiyya (ignorant literalists). It is impossible that the Salaf lacked rational investigation; they were self-sufficient through the Quran and hadith. However, when heretical groups (Khawārij, Jahmiyya, Muʿtazila, Qadariyya) appeared and spread doubts, the imams of Ahl al-Sunna had to defend the religion by refuting them, following the Quranic command to argue in the best way. The passage then argues: those who deny that kalām is in the Quran are mistaken; verses on legal rulings are limited, while verses on uṣūl are abundant. Only two types deny kalām: the ignorant taqlīd-follower who cannot bear the difficulty of verification and forbids others out of his own misguidance, or the heretic who hides his innovations and fears that the people of naẓar will expose him, just as a counterfeiter hates the skilled money-changer who detects counterfeit coins. The passage concludes with a Quranic verse affirming the superiority of those who know.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of ilm al-kalam (dialectical theology) against the accusation that it is an innovation (bid'ah) and contrary to the method of the Salaf. Ibn Asakir begins by noting that each discipline—jurisprudence, grammar, morphology, hadith transmission—has its own technical vocabulary, and the use of such terms by theologians is not an innovation. He then directly addresses the claim that occupying oneself with kalam is bid'ah, pointing out that this accusation is not specific to Ash'aris but could apply to any theologian. He dismisses such criticism as characteristic of the Hashwiyya, a group known for anthropomorphism and lack of critical reasoning. The author then presents a historical argument: the early Salaf, from the Companions onward, did engage in reasoning (nazar) based on the Qur'an and Sunnah regarding Allah's attributes and tawheed. They relied on textual proofs naturally. When heretical sects (Khawarij, Jahmiyya, Mu'tazila, Qadariyya) appeared and spread doubts, the imams of Ahl al-Sunna rose to refute them, using argumentation in accordance with the Qur'anic command to 'argue in the best way.' Ibn Asakir marvels at those who claim the Qur'an has no kalam, noting that while legal verses are limited, verses on usul (foundational principles) are abundant. Finally, he identifies two types of people who deny kalam: the ignorant blind follower who hates what he does not know, and the heretic who hides his corrupt doctrines and fears exposure by the learned. He compares the latter to a counterfeiter who dislikes a discerning money-changer. The passage concludes with a brief statement that this is his praise of kalam and mention of some scholars, leading into the next section.

Learning Objective

Commentary

Finally, he identifies two types of people who deny kalam: the ignorant blind follower who hates what he does not know, and the heretic who hides his corrupt doctrines and fears exposure by the learned. He compares the latter to a counterfeiter who dislikes a discerning money-changer.
