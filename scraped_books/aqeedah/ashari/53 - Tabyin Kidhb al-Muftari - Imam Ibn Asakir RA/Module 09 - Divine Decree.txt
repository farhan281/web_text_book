Al-Ash'ari's mediation between Jabriyya and Mu'tazila on human power.

Human power is real but not creative – that is the Ash'ari middle path.

In debates about free will, two extreme positions emerged: some denied humans any power, others gave them full creative ability. Al-Ash'ari charted a middle course by distinguishing what humans can and cannot do. This lesson presents his nuanced position.

Commentary

Al-Ash'ari presents his middle way on human action: kasb not ijad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Ash'ari's doctrine of kasb as the middle position between Jabriyya and Mu'tazila on human capacity for action.

Al-Ash'ari affirmed human acquisition (kasb) but denied human origination (ijad).

In this passage, Imam al-Ash'ari clarifies his position on human agency. He navigates between two extremes: the Jabriyya who deny any human power, and the Mu'tazila who grant humans independent creative power. Al-Ash'ari's solution is the doctrine of kasb (acquisition), which affirms human responsibility while preserving God's sole creative agency. Read on to see how he articulates this critical distinction.

And that there will not occur on earth anything of good or evil except what Allah wills, and that things occur by Allah's will, and that no one is able to do anything before Allah does it, and that we are not independent of Allah, and that we are not capable of departing from Allah's knowledge, and that there is no creator but Allah, and that the actions of the servants are created by Allah, predestined by Him, as He said (And Allah created you and what you do). Commentary
This passage addresses qadar (divine decree) in a structured manner. It begins by asserting that all good and evil occur only by Allah's will, and that nothing can happen independently of His power or knowledge. It then grounds the creation of human actions in Quranic verses, especially 'Allah created you and what you do' — a key proof for the Sunni position that acts are created by Allah. It rejects the Qadariyya claim that humans create their own acts. The text then discusses guidance and misguidance: Allah guides believers and leads disbelievers astray, not by injustice but because He wills it as per His knowledge. The phrase 'Had He been kind, they would have been righteous' refutes the Mu'tazili claim that Allah must do what is best for all. Finally, it affirms belief in decree—both good and evil—and expresses reliance on Allah alone. The tone is uncompromisingly predestinarian.

The All-Encompassing Divine Will
Ibn Asakir expounds the attribute of divine will, affirming that all events occur by Allah's decree.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that in Sunni doctrine, Allah's will encompasses all events—including evil and disbelief—without exception.

Core Idea
Everything, good and evil, happens only by Allah's will.

Ibn Asakir presents a dense, comprehensive statement on the attribute of divine will. This passage leaves no room for the idea that anything escapes Allah's will, explicitly listing opposites to show the full scope. As you read, notice how the author includes even the most seemingly insignificant events under divine decree.

(Will) That He is the Willer of all things that happen (kāʾināt), the Director of events (ḥādithāt); nothing occurs in the dominion (mulk) and sovereignty (malakūt), be it little or much, small or great, good or evil, benefit or harm, faith or disbelief, recognition (ʿirfān) or denial (nukr), success (fawz) or loss (khusr), increase or decrease, obedience or disobedience, disbelief or belief, except by His decree (qaḍāʾ), predestination (qadar), judgment (ḥukm), and will (mashīʾa). Thus, whatever He wills happens, and whatever He does not will does not happen. No glance of the eye (lafṭat nāẓir) nor fleeting thought (flatat khāṭir) escapes His will. Rather, He is the Originator (mubdiʾ), the Restorer (muʿīd), the Doer of what He wills, the Willer of wisdom (irādat al-ḥikma), and there is no repudiation (muʿaqqib) of His decree, and no escape for a servant from his sin (maʿṣiya) except (…).

Commentary
This passage is a definition of the attribute of divine will (irāda). It affirms that Allah is the Willer of all existing things and the Director of events. Nothing occurs in the entire dominion and sovereignty – whether small or large, good or evil, benefit or harm, faith or disbelief, recognition or denial, success or loss, increase or decrease, obedience or disobedience, belief or disbelief – except by His decree, predestination, judgment, and will. Whatever He wills happens, and whatever He does not will does not happen. No glance or fleeting thought exits His will. The passage then repeats that He is the Originator and Restorer, the Doer of what He wills, and the Willer of wisdom. There is no repudiator of His decree, and no servant can escape his sin without… (the text cuts off here, but the intended completion likely is 'except by Allah's forgiveness').

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think that evil and sin occur outside of Allah's will or against His will.
Correction: This passage explicitly states that 'good or evil, faith or disbelief' all occur by His decree and will, and that no escape from sin is possible except by His mercy.
