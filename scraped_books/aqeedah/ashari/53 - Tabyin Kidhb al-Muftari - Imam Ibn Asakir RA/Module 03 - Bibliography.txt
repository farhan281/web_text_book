A Bibliographic List of the Author's Works
Lists several works by the author arranged alphabetically, preceded by a note on the book's conclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the content and structure of the author's bibliography as presented in 'al-Tabyin'.

Core Idea
A bibliography reveals the scholarly output of a hadith master.

This passage presents a list of works by the author of 'al-Tabyin', arranged alphabetically with the number of volumes for each. It begins by noting that the author concluded his book with a poem. This bibliographic list gives insight into the author's scholarly interests and contributions.

Guided Path
1
Read the Opening Note
Notice that the author mentions concluding his book with a poem, described as among his best.
This provides context for the subsequent list.
2
Observe the Alphabetical Order
The works are listed alphabetically by title. Identify the first few titles to see the pattern.
3
Identify the Types of Works
Most works appear to be collections of hadiths or reports about early scholars. Note the common theme.
For example, 'Hadiths of Shu'bah' and 'Hadiths of Abu al-Ash'ath'.
4
Note the Volume Counts
Each work is followed by a number indicating how many volumes it comprises. Some are single volumes, others multi-volume.
This shows the scope of the author's output.
5
Reflect on the Purpose
Consider why an author would include such a list in his own book. It serves as a record of his scholarly contributions.
This is common in classical Islamic works.

The Scope of Sectarian Study: Roots, Branches, and Key Works
Ibn Asakir explains the limited scope of his work and references key works on sects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ibn Asakir limits his discussion to root sects and the methodological implication that sects are too numerous to be exhaustively enumerated.

Core Idea
Sects branch endlessly; only the roots can be covered.

Ibn Asakir concludes his survey of sects by clarifying its deliberate limitation. He explains that the numerous branches of deviant sects multiply endlessly due to overlapping opinions and evolving passions, making exhaustive enumeration impossible. He also points to a scholarly disagreement on the number of sects and references major classical works on sects.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to understand the overall meaning.
Notice the phrase 'أصول الفرق' (roots of the sects) sets the scope.
2
Distinguish roots from branches
Identify the contrast between 'أصول' (roots) and 'فروع' (branches). Understand why branches are endless.
The passage says branches ramify due to overlapping opinions and renewal of passions.
3
Understand the reason for limitation
Ask yourself: Why did Ibn Asakir only discuss root sects? The answer is in the text: branches do not end at a limited number.
This aligns with the scholarly disagreement on the exact number of sects.
4
Note the scholarly disagreement
The passage mentions 'خلاف مشهور' (well-known disagreement) about the reported number of sects. This indicates that enumeration is not the goal.
5
Identify key works referenced
Look at the list of works: Maqalat al-Islamiyyin, al-Maqalat, Rad Ahl al-Ahwa', and al-Milal. These are major sources for the study of sects.
These works are recommended for further study of detailed sectarian differences.

ولم نتعرض هنا إلا لأصول الفرق من أهل البدع ولها فروع تتشعب منها على حسب ما يقع فيها من تداخل في الآراء وتجدد في الأهواء وهي لا تنتهى عند عدد محدود إلى انتهاء تاريخ البشر وفى العدد المأثور للعلماء خلاف مشهور، وقد قام العلماء في كل طبقة بتفصيل ماجد إلى عصرهم من أصحاب النحل ورد الباطل من آرائهم. ومـقـالات تلك الطوائف مبسوطة فى مقالات الإسلاميين للأشعرى و المقالات » لأبي منصور الماتريدى وه رد أهل الأهواء والبدع لأبى الحسين الطرائفي وه الملل
And we have not discussed here except the roots of the sects of the innovators; they have branches that ramify from them according to what occurs in them of overlapping opinions and renewal of passions, and they do not end at a limited number until the end of human history. And concerning the reported number, there is a well-known disagreement among scholars. Scholars of every generation have detailed what appeared in their time of the people of sects and refuted the falsehood of their opinions. And the articles of those groups are set out in 'Maqalat al-Islamiyyin' by al-Ash'ari, and 'al-Maqalat' by Abu Mansur al-Maturidi, and 'Rad Ahl al-Ahwa' wa al-Bida' by Abu al-Husayn al-Tara'ifi, and 'al-Milal'...
Source pages p. 28

Commentary
This concluding passage explains the scope of the author's discussion: only the root sects of innovators have been covered, as they have many branches that multiply over time and are not finite. There is a known disagreement among scholars about the exact number. Scholars in every generation have recorded the sects of their time and refuted their falsehood. The passage then lists key works on sects: al-Ash'ari's Maqalat al-Islamiyyin, al-Maturidi's al-Maqalat, Abu al-Husayn al-Tara'ifi's Rad Ahl al-Ahwa' wa al-Bida', and 'al-Milal' (probably al-Milal wa al-Nihal), though the text is cut off.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think Ibn Asakir intended to cover all sects exhaustively.
Correction: He explicitly says he only discussed the root sects because the branches are countless.
