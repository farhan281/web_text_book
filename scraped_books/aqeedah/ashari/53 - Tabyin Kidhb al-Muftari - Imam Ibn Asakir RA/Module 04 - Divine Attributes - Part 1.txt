Creedal Poem on Divine Attributes: Tanzih and Rejection of Tashbih
Creedal poem on divine attributes and transcendence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key Sunni beliefs about Allah's attributes as expressed in a creedal poem, emphasizing transcendence (tanzih) and rejecting anthropomorphism (tashbih).

Core Idea
Allah's attributes are affirmed without modality — He is not like His creation.

This creedal poem, recited by the great hadith master Ibn Asakir, summarizes the core Sunni creed on divine attributes. It affirms that Allah is a speaker, that He created the heavens, that He is established over the Throne, and that He sees and hears — all without anthropomorphism. The poem serves as a concise statement of tanzih and a warning against tashbih.

The Distinction Between Name and Meaning in Divine Attributes
Ibn Asakir explains the correct theological principle that the prohibited is sharing in meaning, not name, and applies it to divine attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why using the same attribute names for God and humans does not imply similarity, and how the Jahmī controversy illustrates the misuse of such concepts.

Core Idea
Names can be shared; meanings cannot.

In this passage, Ibn ‘Asākir clarifies a crucial principle for understanding divine attributes: the problem is sharing in meaning, not in name. He then illustrates how this principle was misused in polemics against Jahm. By studying this, you will learn to navigate similar confusions.

The prohibited is sharing in meaning, not name.
3

Summarize: The name may be shared, but the meaning differs. Affirming divine attributes does not imply anthropomorphism.

Commentary
This passage is a theological clarification that establishes the principle regarding divine attributes: it is permissible to use the same names for God and creatures (e.g., knowledge, power) as long as the meaning is not shared. The prohibited is believing that the meaning is identical. The condition is that the name must be revealed in the Law. The example of knowledge is given: both God and creatures are described as 'knowing', but God's knowledge is immediate and uncreated (huduri) while human knowledge is acquired through means (husuli). The passage then shifts to note that many opinions are falsely attributed to Jahm, and no sect claims him as founder. The negative reputation led to pejorative labeling. This shows how theological positions were often distorted in polemics.

Sharing in Name vs. Meaning in Divine Attributes
Ibn Asakir distinguishes between ishtirak fi al-ism and ishtirak fi al-ma'na regarding divine attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between ishtirak fi al-ism and ishtirak fi al-ma'na, and why only nominal sharing is permissible without scriptural evidence.

Core Idea
God and creatures may share names but never share meanings in attributes.

Ibn Asakir clarifies a crucial principle for understanding divine attributes: when we say God is 'Knowing' and a human is 'knowing', we do not mean the same thing. This passage introduces the two types of sharing—in name and in meaning—and sets the condition for permissible usage.

Commentary
This passage defines the distinction between two types of sharing (ishtirak): sharing in name (ishtirak fi al-ism) and sharing in meaning (ishtirak fi al-ma'na). The author states that the prohibited form is the second—sharing in meaning—unless it is specifically legislated in the Law (shar'). He provides the example of knowledge ('ilm): both God and creatures are called 'knowing' (shared name), but there is no shared meaning because God's knowledge is immediate and eternal (huduri) while a creature's knowledge is acquired and temporal (husuli). This principle applies to all other attributes (sifat).

The Mihna and the Mu'tazila Inquisition
Ibn Asakir recounts the mihna under al-Ma'mun and the role of caliphs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the historical context of the mihna, the role of al-Ma'mun and later caliphs, and how the Mu'tazila imposed the doctrine of the created Qur'an and denial of vision.

Core Idea
The mihna was a state-sponsored inquisition to enforce Mu'tazila doctrine.

Ibn Asakir sets the stage by describing how reflective scholars (nuzzar) fell into mental illnesses from debates, while most jurists avoided kalam. He then recounts how al-Ma'mun, upon assuming power, supported the Mu'tazila and compelled people to affirm the created Qur'an and a particular notion of transcendence. The inquisition continued under al-Mu'tasim and al-Wathiq, who added denial of the beatific vision. The persecution ended when al-Mutawakkil lifted the mihna, and Imam Ahmad's steadfastness raised his status.

Commentary
This passage covers multiple historical and theological points. It begins by noting the psychological impact of debates on the mutakallimun, and then contrasts their approach with that of most jurists and hadith scholars, who avoided kalam and adhered strictly to revealed texts. Despite the danger from external enemies who gradually eroded faith, al-Ma'mun came to power and supported the Mu'tazila, imposing the doctrine of the created Qur'an and transcendence (tanzih). The inquisition (mihna) continued under al-Mu'tasim and al-Wathiq, who added denial of the beatific vision (ru'ya). Al-Mutawakkil ended the mihna, and Imam Ahmad's steadfastness raised his status. The passage then describes the reaction: the Hashwiyya and Nawasib (anti-'Alids) rose, while the people of reflection (ahl al-nazar) and Mu'tazila were suppressed. The Hashwiyya are accused of extreme anthropomorphism (tashbih), affirming for God movement, transference, limit, direction, etc., copying from dualists and earlier nations. They cloak themselves in the Sunnah and claim to follow the Salaf, but their proofs are ambiguous statements. The footnote criticizes Mujahid ibn Jabr for two contradictory opinions: one denying vision (used by Mu'tazila) and one on the Praised Station (used by Hashwiyya), noting that both are false and at odds with mutawatir hadiths.

Commentary
This passage is a narrative illustrating the importance of kalam in defending the creed during the Mihna. Zuhayr ibn Harb, a prominent hadith scholar, was tested by the caliph al-Ma'mun regarding the creation of the Qur'an and Jesus. He initially answered incorrectly (agreeing that Jesus was created and perhaps that the Qur'an was created, leading to charges of apostasy). Al-Husayn ibn al-Fadl al-Bajali visited him and corrected his understanding. The dialogue shows the logical connection: Jesus is called the Word of God, yet he is created; the Qur'an is also the Word of God, so if Jesus is created, why not the Qur'an? But the correct position (from the Ahl al-Sunnah) is that the Qur'an is uncreated. Zuhayr had initially accepted the createdness of the Qur'an to avoid persecution, but al-Husayn argued that he could have used the excuse of compulsion (ikrah) based on Quranic verse. The story ends with Zuhayr regretting his answer and asking al-Husayn to teach his son. The author then notes that al-Husayn was a specialist in kalam.

The Obligation to Know God's Attributes
Ibn Asakir states that knowing God as He is is necessary for faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why knowledge of God's attributes is an individual obligation upon every Muslim and how ignorance of even one attribute undermines true faith.

Core Idea
Knowing God's attributes is essential for faith and salvation.

Ibn Asakir opens with a legal classification: the duty to know God's attributes varies—sometimes individual, sometimes communal, sometimes recommended. But he quickly establishes a deeper principle: ignorance of any attribute means ignorance of the Creator Himself, and that spells spiritual ruin.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the literal English translation. Notice the strong conclusion about faith and salvation.
The Arabic uses a conditional structure: 'whoever... does not...'
2
Distinguish the types of obligations
Identify the three categories of religious duty mentioned: individual obligations (fard 'ayn), communal obligations (fard kifayah), and recommended acts (mandub/mustahabb). Ibn Asakir says knowledge of attributes belongs to 'them' (the individual obligations) and others (the other categories).
This means the ruling can vary based on circumstances.
3
Grasp the logical connection
Understand the logic: if you are ignorant of an attribute of something you claim to know, you do not truly know that thing. Applied to God, ignorance of even one attribute means you do not know God as He is.
This is a rational principle: knowledge of a thing entails knowledge of its attributes.
4
Reflect on the consequence
Consider the severe consequence: not knowing the Creator as He is means one does not deserve the name 'faith' nor deliverance from Hellfire. This shows the critical importance of correct knowledge of God's attributes.
Ibn Asakir is emphasizing that incorrect or incomplete knowledge can invalidate faith.

Commentary
This passage establishes the ruling and necessity of knowledge of God. Ibn Asakir states that the science of kalam (or knowledge of God's attributes) falls under the category of obligations (fara'id). He distinguishes between individual obligations (fard 'ayn) and communal obligations (fard kifayah), as well as recommended acts (mandub, mustahabb). The key point is that ignorance of even one attribute of God prevents one from knowing God as He truly is. And whoever does not know the Creator as He is does not deserve to be called a believer nor to be saved from Hellfire on the Day of Judgment. This strongly asserts that knowledge of God's attributes is essential for valid faith and salvation.
