The Conditional Prohibition of Kalam by the Salaf
Ghazali explains the conditional prohibition of kalam by the Salaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the Salaf's prohibition of kalam was not absolute but conditional, targeting specific harmful engagements, and its ultimate aim was to preserve the Sunni creed.

Core Idea
The Salaf did not forbid all kalam; they forbade harmful forms to protect the creed.

Imam al-Shafi'i and the early Salaf are known for cautioning against kalam. Yet kalam is essential for defending Islamic doctrine. How do we reconcile this? Ghazali clarifies that their prohibition was not a blanket condemnation but a targeted measure against misuse. This passage explains the conditions and the noble intention behind their stance.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Begin by reading the Arabic text aloud or silently, then the literal translation. Notice the key terms and the structure of the argument.
The passage uses a conditional structure: the prohibition is interpreted in specific ways.
2
Identify the four categories of prohibition
Look for the four types of people the Salaf forbade from kalam. Write them down. This is a classification that shows the prohibition was not absolute.
The categories are: the fanatic, the incapable of certainty, the corruptor, and the one diving into unnecessary philosophy.
3
Understand the underlying reason
After listing the categories, Ghazali gives the overarching reason: preserving Ahl al-Sunnah creed from confusion caused by innovators. Understand how this reason explains the conditional nature.
4
Reflect on the misconception
Consider the common misunderstanding that the Salaf completely forbade kalam. How does Ghazali's explanation correct this?
The prohibition is seen as protective, not absolute.

Commentary
This passage addresses the apparent conflict between the value of kalam and the famous prohibition by Imam al-Shafi'i and the Salaf. Ghazali explains that the prohibition was not absolute but qualified: it targeted those who are fanatical, incapable of attaining certainty, intend to corrupt beliefs, or delve into unnecessary philosophical subtleties. The essential reason for such prohibition was to protect the Sunni creed from confusion introduced by heretical groups. Thus, the prohibition was precautionary, not a condemnation of kalam itself, which is the basis of obligations.

The Ranking of the Rightly-Guided Caliphs
Imam al-Ghazali states the obligation to believe in the superiority and order of the Rightly-Guided Caliphs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni creed regarding the superiority of the Companions and the specific ranking of the four Rightly-Guided Caliphs.

Core Idea
The best after the Prophet are Abu Bakr, then Umar, then Uthman, then Ali.

Imam al-Ghazali presents a core tenet of Sunni belief: the Companions are all praiseworthy, but they have a divinely ordained ranking. This lesson unpacks that ranking and its significance.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to understand the plain meaning.
2
Identify the three commands
Notice that the passage commands three things: belief in superiority, recognition of the ranking, and having a good opinion of all Companions.
These three form the complete creed regarding the Companions.
3
Reflect on the ranking's basis
Ask yourself: is this ranking based on political succession or actual merit? The Sunni position is that it is based on merit as understood from revelation.
This distinguishes Ahl al-Sunnah from other groups.

Commentary
This passage lays out the Sunni creed regarding the Companions. It first commands belief in their collective superiority. Then it specifies the ranking among the four Rightly-Guided Caliphs: Abu Bakr is the best, followed by Umar, then Uthman, then Ali. This order is based on their merit, not just their chronological succession. Finally, it requires having a good opinion of all Companions and praising them, as they have been praised in the Quran and by the Prophet. This is a fundamental tenet differentiating Ahl al-Sunnah from those who criticize or malign the Companions.

The Virtues of the Companions in Quran and Hadith
Footnotes on rank, caliphate order, and divine praise of Companions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the basis for the elevated rank of all Companions and the evidence for their divine praise.

Core Idea
All Companions are honored; their caliphate order reflects their merit.

In this passage, Imam al-Ghazali's footnotes draw from hadith and Quran to affirm the status of the Companions. We see that the order of the caliphs mirrors their excellence, and that Allah explicitly praises the Companions collectively and specifically. The lesson clarifies the obligation to respect all Companions and avoid criticizing them.

Guided Path
1
Understand the principle of ranking
Read the first part noting that each Companion has a rank deserving veneration and that the order of caliphate reflects their merit, as agreed by Ahl al-Sunnah.
The argument uses historical practice to support theological belief.
2
Examine the hadith evidence
Look at the Bukhari hadith from Ibn Umar showing that the Companions themselves ranked Abu Bakr, then Umar, then Uthman.
This hadith establishes the order of the first three caliphs as based on merit.
3
Review the Quranic praise
The passage cites the Pledge of Ridwan and verses for Muhajirun and Ansar, showing Allah's pleasure with all Companions.
The Pledge involved over 1700 Companions, emphasizing collective honor.
4
Note the prophetic warning
Read the hadith from Tirmidhi warning against making the Companions a target and the duty to maintain the view of them as in the Prophet's time.
This guards against later sectarian criticism.

The Sunni Creed on the Companions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni creed regarding the Companions: their virtue, the ranking of the four caliphs, and the obligation to hold a good opinion of all of them.

Core Idea
The Companions are all virtuous; the best are Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, Ali in that order.

This passage from Ghazali's Qawaid al-Aqa'id summarizes the core Sunni beliefs about the Companions. It states that loving and praising all Companions is essential, and that there is a specific order of superiority among the four rightly-guided caliphs. This lesson will help you understand these foundational points and why they distinguish Ahl al-Sunna from other groups.

Steadfast Faith vs. Fragile Theology
Contrasts two types of faith and critiques imitative theology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference in stability between the imitative faith of a common believer and the dialectical faith of a mutakallim.

Core Idea
True faith is a mountain; debated faith is a thread in the wind.

Imam al-Ghazali opens with a vivid comparison: the common person's faith stands firm like a mountain, while the theologian's faith, built on dialectical divisions, is as unstable as a loose thread blown by the wind. This lesson unpacks that contrast and its implications for the nature of true conviction.

1
Read the passage and translation

Notice the strong imagery: mountain vs. thread.
2
Identify the two types of faith

3
Understand the critique of imitative evidence
The author says that learning evidence by imitation is no different from imitating belief itself. Reflect on why this matters.
This is a key point: true independent reasoning is different from mere transmission.
4
Connect to the learning objective
Ask yourself: why is stability of faith important? How does this apply to your own journey?

Commentary
This passage begins by comparing the faith of a common pious person with that of a mutakallim (theologian). The commoner's faith is stable like a mountain, unmoved by calamities, while the mutakallim's faith, guarded by dialectical divisions, is like a loose thread blown by the wind. The author then notes that those mutakallimun who simply imitate the evidence are no different from those who imitate the belief itself, since imitation of the evidence is still imitation, not true independent reasoning. The author then, in response to a question about what a common person inclined to debate should do, provides a hierarchy of occupations: worship and Quran, then learning other sciences, then crafts, then even play or self-talk about the Day of Judgment, all being safer than delving into theology. He warns that researching Allah's knowledge can lead to immorality or even shirk. He then criticizes the innovations of mutakallimun — such as interpretations and raising problems — as innovations harmful to the public, citing observation and historical fitan as evidence. He notes that the early generations did not engage in such methods, not because they were incapable, but because they knew it was not beneficial. If it were beneficial, they would have elaborated on it more than they did on inheritance laws.

Al-Shafi'i's Condemnation of Kalam
Ghazali reports the view of early imams that kalam is forbidden.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the basis for the early Islamic stance that kalam is forbidden, as narrated by al-Ghazali.

Core Idea

Commentary
This passage presents a threefold classification of approaches to eschatological matters. Ghazali first describes the extremist allegorizing of the philosophers, who reinterpret all physical descriptions of the afterlife (like the burning Fire) as purely intellectual realities, denying bodily resurrection. They are 'the extravagant ones' (al-musrifun). Then he describes the opposite extreme: the rigid literalism of the Hanbalites, who stop at the outward hearing (sam mujarrad). Between these poles lies a subtle moderate path (iqtisad), which is not a compromise but a divinely illumined insight (nur ilahi). Those who possess this light first witness realities as they are, then evaluate revelation: what agrees they confirm, what disagrees they interpret allegorically (ta'wil) in a linguistically sound manner. Those without this light—those who rely solely on transmitted words—cannot attain certainty; their position is that of Ahmad ibn Hanbal, which is to accept without interpretation. The passage thus respects both approaches while pointing to a higher synthesis.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and enumerative passage. Imam al-Ghazali states the ninth principle concerning the conditions of imamate (leadership). He first lists five conditions after the basic prerequisites of Islam and legal responsibility (taklif): manhood (dhukūra), piety (waraʿ), knowledge (ʿilm), capability (kifāya), and Qurayshi lineage (nisba qurayshiyya). Then he explains each condition in detail, providing rational and textual justifications. For the prerequisites 'after Islam and taklif', he notes that a disbeliever cannot lead Muslims, citing the Qur'anic verse, and a non-accountable person (child or insane) cannot manage even his own affairs. He also adds freedom, the need to be free from physical disabilities like blindness, deafness, and muteness, because these would prevent proper execution of duties. For the first condition, manhood, he argues that women are deficient in intellect and religion and are not allowed to attend judicial courts or battlefields, so their imamate is invalid. For piety, he equates it with probity (ʿadāla), the first level of piety which means refraining from anything that leads to sinfulness. For knowledge, he requires ijtihād in both fundamentals (uṣūl) and branches (furūʿ) of religion, enabling the imam to defend the faith, resolve doctrinal doubts, and issue fatwas independently, as the goals of imamate include preserving beliefs, adjudicating governments, and settling disputes. For capability, he interprets it as the ability to manage imamate affairs, which includes strategic planning, military leadership, and courage to enforce punishments. For Qurayshi lineage, he specifies descent from al-Naḍr bin Kināna, the thirteenth grandfather of the Prophet. Finally, he addresses the scenario where multiple qualified candidates exist: the imam is the one who receives the pledge from the majority, and dissenters must submit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the five essential conditions of imamate: manhood, piety, knowledge, capability, and Qurayshi lineage, as well as the rule for selecting among multiple candidates.

Core Idea
The imam must be male, just, learned, capable, and from Quraysh.

Having established the basic prerequisites, Ghazali now details the five core conditions that define who is eligible for the imamate. These conditions cover personal qualities, religious authority, practical ability, and lineage. The passage also explains how to choose among qualified candidates and how to deal with dissent.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the five conditions
Read the Arabic and translation for the five conditions and their explanations.
Notice that Ghazali provides a rationale for each condition.
2
Examine each condition individually
For each of the five, identify the definition and the justification. Piety is equated with probity, knowledge with ijtihad.
Manhood is justified by women's deficiency in intellect and religion.
3

Capability includes strategic planning and courage. It is more comprehensive than mere bravery.
There is a variant reading 'writing' which adds administrative skill.
4
Learn the rule for multiple candidates
If several qualified persons exist, the imam is the one who receives allegiance from the majority; opponents are rebels.
This reflects the Sunni emphasis on communal unity.
5
Reflect on the overall significance
Consider how these conditions together ensure the imam's religious and temporal authority.
These conditions have been debated among Sunni scholars.

الأصل التاسع: إن شرائط الإمامة بعد الإسلام والتكليف خمسة: الذكورة، والورع، والعلم، والكفاية، ونسبة قريش. واحترز بقوله «بعد الإسلام» لأن الكافر لا يصح تقليده لأمور المسلمين لقوله سبحانه: {ولن يجعل الله للكافرين على المؤمنين سبيلا}. واحترز بقوله «والتكليف» لأن غير العاقل من الصبي والمعتوه عاجز عن القيام بأموره، فكيف يقوم بأمر غيره؛ وبعد الحرية لأن العبد مشغول الأوقات بحقوق سيده، فكيف يتفرغ بشأن غيره، وأيضاً أن العبد محتقر في أعين الناس فلا يهاب ولا يمتثل أمره، وبعد سلامته من العمى والصمم والبكم، إذ مع وجود شيء منها لا يمكنه القيام بشأن الإمامة. وأما الذكورة فاشتراطها لأن إمامة المرأة لا تصح إذ النساء ناقصات عقل ودين، ممنوعات من الخروج إلى مشاهد الحكم ومعارك الحرب. وأما الورع فإنه أراد به العدالة، وهي المرتبة الأولى من مراتب الورع التي هي ترك ما يوجب اقتحامه وصف الفسق. وأما العلم فإنه أراد به الاجتهاد في الأصول الدينية والفروع ليتمكن بذلك من القيام بأمر الدين بالحج، وحل الشبه في العقائد، ويستقل بالفتوى في النوازل وأحكام الوقائع نصاً واستنباطاً لأن مقاصد الإمامة حفظ العقائد وفصل الحكومات ورفع الخصومات. وأما الكفاءة (وفي بعض النسخ الكتابة) فإنه أراد بها القدرة على القيام بأمور الإمامة، ويحترز بها عن العجز، وهي أعم من الشجاعة، إذ الكفاءة تتناول كونه ذا رأي بتدابير الحروب والجيوش وحفظ الثغور، وكونه ذا شجاعة وهي قوة قلب بها يقتص من الجناة ويقيم الحدود الشرعية ولا يجبن عن الحروب. والمراد بقوله «نسبة قريش» كونه من أولاد قريش، وهو لقب النضر بن كنانة بن خزيمة بن مدركة بن إلياس بن مضر، والنضر هو الجد الثالث عشر لسيدنا رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم. وإذا اجتمع عدد من الموصوفين بهذه الصفات فالإمام من انعقدت له البيعة من أكثر الخلق، والمخالف للأكثر باغ يجب رده إلى الانقياد إلى الحق.
The ninth principle: Indeed, the conditions of imamate after Islam and legal responsibility are five: manhood, piety, knowledge, capability, and Qurayshi lineage. He guarded by his saying 'after Islam' because the disbeliever is not valid to be entrusted with the affairs of Muslims due to His saying: {And never will Allah grant the disbelievers over the believers a way}. And he guarded by his saying 'and legal responsibility' because the non-rational person, such as the child and the mentally deficient, is incapable of managing his own affairs, so how can he manage the affairs of others? And [also guarded] after freedom because the slave's times are occupied with the rights of his master, so how can he attend to the affairs of others? Also, the slave is contemptible in people's eyes, so he is not feared and his command is not obeyed. And [guarded] after safety from blindness, deafness, and muteness, because with the existence of any of these, he cannot undertake the matter of imamate. As for manhood, its condition is because the imamate of a woman is not valid, since women are deficient in intellect and religion, prevented from going out to judicial assemblies and battlefields. As for piety, he intended by it probity ('adāla), which is the first level of the levels of piety, which is refraining from that which entering into it necessitates the description of sinfulness. As for knowledge, he intended by it independent legal reasoning (ijtihād) in the fundamental principles of religion and the branches, so that he is able to undertake the matter of religion with proof and resolve doubts in beliefs, and independently issue fatwas in calamitous events and rulings of occurrences by text and inference, because the purposes of imamate are preserving beliefs, adjudicating governments, and raising disputes. As for capability (and in some copies 'writing'), he intended by it the ability to undertake the affairs of imamate, and he guards by it against incapacity, and it is more general than courage, for capability encompasses his being possessed of opinion regarding the strategies of wars, armies, guarding frontiers, and his being possessed of courage, which is strength of heart, by which he exacts retribution from criminals and establishes the prescribed legal punishments, and is not cowardly in wars. The meaning of his saying 'Qurayshi lineage' is his being from the descendants of Quraysh, who is the nickname of al-Naḍr bin Kināna bin Khuzayma bin Mudrika bin Ilyās bin Muḍar, and al-Naḍr is the thirteenth grandfather of our master the Messenger of Allah, may Allah bless him and grant him peace. And if a number of those described with these qualities gather, then the imam is the one for whom the oath of allegiance is contracted from the majority of the people; and the one who opposes the majority is a rebel who must be returned to submission to the truth.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and enumerative passage. Imam al-Ghazali states the ninth principle concerning the conditions of imamate (leadership). He first lists five conditions after the basic prerequisites of Islam and legal responsibility (taklif): manhood (dhukūra), piety (waraʿ), knowledge (ʿilm), capability (kifāya), and Qurayshi lineage (nisba qurayshiyya). Then he explains each condition in detail, providing rational and textual justifications. For the prerequisites 'after Islam and taklif', he notes that a disbeliever cannot lead Muslims, citing the Qur'anic verse, and a non-accountable person (child or insane) cannot manage even his own affairs. He also adds freedom, the need to be free from physical disabilities like blindness, deafness, and muteness, because these would prevent proper execution of duties. For the first condition, manhood, he argues that women are deficient in intellect and religion and are not allowed to attend judicial courts or battlefields, so their imamate is invalid. For piety, he equates it with probity (ʿadāla), the first level of piety which means refraining from anything that leads to sinfulness. For knowledge, he requires ijtihād in both fundamentals (uṣūl) and branches (furūʿ) of religion, enabling the imam to defend the faith, resolve doctrinal doubts, and issue fatwas independently, as the goals of imamate include preserving beliefs, adjudicating governments, and settling disputes. For capability, he interprets it as the ability to manage imamate affairs, which includes strategic planning, military leadership, and courage to enforce punishments. For Qurayshi lineage, he specifies descent from al-Naḍr bin Kināna, the thirteenth grandfather of the Prophet. Finally, he addresses the scenario where multiple qualified candidates exist: the imam is the one who receives the pledge from the majority, and dissenters must submit.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think that the requirement of knowledge means the imam must be a mujtahid in all matters, but Ghazali specifies ijtihad in usul and furu'.
Correction: Ghazali defines knowledge as the ability to perform ijtihad in both fundamental principles and branches of religion, which is a specific level of expertise.

Key Takeaway
