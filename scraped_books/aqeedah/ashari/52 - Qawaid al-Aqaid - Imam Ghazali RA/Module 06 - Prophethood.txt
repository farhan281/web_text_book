Prophethood and the Uncreated Quran
Affirms prophethood, revealed books, Muhammad as seal, and Quran as uncreated speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic belief in the sending of prophets and messengers, the seal of prophethood with Muhammad, and the uncreated nature of the Quran.

Core Idea
The Quran is God's uncreated speech, not created by Him nor a creator itself.

This lesson covers key beliefs about prophethood and revelation. It affirms that Allah sent prophets and revealed scriptures, culminating in the final message of Muhammad. A central tenet is that the Quran is the uncreated speech of Allah, distinct from all creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage on prophethood and revelation. Note how it links sending prophets, revealing books, and the finality of Muhammad.
2
Identify the Quran's nature
Find the phrase describing the Quran as 'not created and not creating'. Understand that this is a key doctrinal point against those who say the Quran is created.
3
Reflect on the truthfulness
Note that the Prophet is described as truthful in all he informs. This underpins the reliability of revelation.

Beliefs Concerning What is Realized: Prophets, Books, Angels
Enumerates beliefs about prophets, scriptures, angels, and the Quran.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the specific beliefs Muslims must hold regarding prophets, scriptures, angels, the Quran, the afterlife, and the finality of prophethood.

Core Idea
Faith requires affirming realized truths: prophets, books, angels, and the uncreated Quran.

Imam al-Ghazali now turns from what is impossible and possible for Allah to what must be affirmed as existing. He lists a series of essential beliefs that every Muslim must hold concerning the messengers, the scriptures, the angels, the afterlife, and the nature of the Quran. This passage serves as a concise summary of the articles of faith related to these realized matters.

Prophet Muhammad's Preparation and Mission
Narrative describing the Prophet's preparation and mission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Allah prepared Prophet Muhammad for his prophetic mission through seclusion, revelation, and the command to proclaim tawhid.

Core Idea
The Prophet's retreat in Hira was divine preparation for delivering the message of tawhid.

This passage from Imam Ghazali explains that the Prophet's seclusion in the Cave of Hira was not mere asceticism but a preparation for the great mission of conveying the creed of tawhid. It shows the divine pattern of how prophets are equipped and then sent to warn and give glad tidings.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once for its flow, then follow with the English translation to grasp the narrative.
Notice the shift from a general statement about prophets to the specific account of the Prophet.
2
Identify the general principle
The passage begins with a general description of how the creed possesses prophets, leading them to proclaim openly as warners and bringers of good tidings.
3
Trace the Prophet's preparation
Observe the details of the Prophet's seclusion in the Cave of Hira, his worship, and his return to Khadijah for provisions.
This shows the gradual preparation by Allah.
4
Review the Quranic commands
Notice the three verses cited: Surah al-Muddaththir, Surah ash-Shu'ara, and Surah al-Ma'idah. Each commands a specific aspect of the mission.
The commands escalate from warning to conveying.
5
Understand the core message
The final verse (Q 3:64) summarizes the message: worshiping Allah alone and rejecting all forms of shirk.
This is the essence of tawhid that the Prophet preached.

Commentary
This passage describes the process by which the Prophet Muhammad was prepared for his mission. The author first generalizes: when the creed of tawhid fully possesses the prophets, they proclaim it openly and call people to it, acting as warners so that people have no excuse. Then he applies this to the Prophet: he followed this same divine pattern. He engaged in contemplation and seclusion, spending time in the Cave of Hira, worshipping there for many nights, returning to Khadijah for supplies. Then the truth came to him in the cave, and the mission became clear. He was commanded: 'O you wrapped in garments, arise and warn' (Q 74:1), 'Warn your nearest kinsmen' (Q 26:214), and 'O Messenger, convey what has been sent down to you' (Q 5:67). So he proclaimed the truth openly, and his call rose above all others. He conveyed the message calling people to the common word of tawhid (Q 3:64). The passage emphasizes that the Prophet's pre-mission retreat was part of Allah's preparation, and that the core of his message was monotheism.

Refuting the Objection of a Human Prophet
Refutes the objection that a human cannot be a prophet using the Prophet's integrity and a divine threat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Prophet's known trustworthiness and a divine threat logically prove his truthfulness as a messenger.

Core Idea
A known trustworthy human, threatened by Allah for lying, is the most credible messenger.

Many people question how a human being can be a prophet of Allah. This seems strange because prophets are like us in many ways. In this passage, Imam al-Ghazali addresses this objection by highlighting that the Prophet Muhammad's character was well-known as al-Amin, and that a severe Quranic verse warns him against fabricating. This combination proves his honesty and confirms his prophethood.

This sets up the rebuttal.
3
Find the evidence
Look for two pieces of evidence: the Prophet's known trustworthiness (al-Amin) and the Quranic threat (Ayah 69:44-46). Understand how they together prove his truthfulness.
The evidence is the core of the refutation.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The passage concludes that the Prophet could only speak with absolute clarity. Why does this follow from the evidence?
This ties the argument together.

Commentary
This passage is a proof and clarification regarding the humanity of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) and the nature of prophethood. Commentary
This passage is a continuous argument that clarifies the correct Islamic understanding of prophethood (nubuwwa) and messengership (risala) based on the principle of tawhid (monotheism). The author begins by noting that people misunderstood the nature of the Prophet's mission, demanding supernatural miracles as proof. The Prophet corrected this by emphasizing his humanity and that his role is guidance (hidaya) to the straight path, quoting the verse 'Say, I am only a man like you, revealed to me that your God is one God' (Quran 18:110). He further quotes the Prophet's statement 'Glory be to my Lord, was I but a human messenger' (Quran 17:93) to affirm that even he did not claim divinity or independent power. The author then shows that earlier prophets like Noah also affirmed their humanity, denying possession of God's treasures, knowledge of the unseen, or angelic nature (Quran 6:50). When people asked for miracles, the messengers replied that they are merely human, but God grants miracles by His permission (Quran 14:11). The author then asserts that the Prophet Muhammad could not deviate from the way of previous messengers, citing Quran 4:163 that revelation to him is like revelation to Noah, and Quran 46:9 where he says he is not a novelty among messengers. The author then summarizes the correct understanding: that prophethood is an intermediation (wisata) between God and creation that does not remove the prophet's humanity. Finally, he argues that this understanding is demanded by tawhid: God alone has universal power over miracles and comprehensive knowledge of the unseen; it would violate pure monotheism to attribute these to any human. God alone holds the keys of the unseen (Quran 6:59) and discloses His unseen only to chosen messengers (Quran 72:26-27), teaching them by revelation (Quran 11:49). Thus, prophets are human recipients of revelation, not possessors of independent supernatural power.

Commentary
This passage is a presentation of evidence and explanation. The author begins by citing a Quranic verse (29:43) that establishes that some parables are understood only by the knowledgeable, implying the existence of hidden knowledge. Then he quotes a hadith from the Prophet (peace be upon him) describing knowledge as a hidden treasure (كهيئة المكنون) known only to those who know Allah, and notes that when such scholars speak, only the deluded reject them. This sets the stage for the main hadith: 'If you knew what I know, you would laugh little and weep much.' The author then provides a detailed commentary on this hadith. First, he clarifies the wording variant: many copies have 'لو علمتم' but some have 'لو تعلمون', and this latter version is preferred by al-Hafiz al-'Iraqi. Second, he explains the meaning of 'what I know' as referring to Allah's punishment for criminals and the terrors of the Day of Resurrection. Third, he interprets 'you would laugh little': it could mean very little laughter, or it could mean no laughter at all, which better fits the context because the particle 'law' (if) indicates prevention of the consequence due to prevention of the condition. He then notes the rhetorical beauty: laughter is mentioned first because it is associated with joy, and the hadith employs rhetorical figures (بديع) such as comparison (مقابلة) between laughter and weeping, and between fewness and abundance, and correspondence (مطابقة) of each pair. The overall effect is to anchor the hadith in both scriptural evidence and careful analysis.

Commentary
This passage is a combined proof: first presenting examples of the Qur'an's miraculous nature, then stating the general principle of how miracles indicate prophetic truthfulness, and finally illustrating that principle with a concrete analogy. The author begins by citing two aspects of the Qur'an's miraculous character: its unmatched eloquence and composition, its reports of past nations, and the prophet's illiteracy, and its prophecies—specifically the prophecy that the Romans would be defeated and then victorious, and that the companions would enter Mecca peacefully. These are real-world verifications of the prophet's truthfulness. Then Imam Ghazali states the universal principle: every miracle (mu'jiza) is an act of God that humans cannot perform. When such an act is combined with a clear challenge (tahaddi) from the prophet, it functions as God's explicit endorsement. He then explains this through a vivid analogy: a claimant stands before a king and says, 'I am your messenger.' The king's act of standing and sitting at the claimant's request—contrary to his usual custom—produces necessary knowledge (daruri) in the observers that the king has effectively said 'you are truthful.' Similarly, God's creation of a miracle at the prophet's challenge is a definitive confirmation of his prophethood.
