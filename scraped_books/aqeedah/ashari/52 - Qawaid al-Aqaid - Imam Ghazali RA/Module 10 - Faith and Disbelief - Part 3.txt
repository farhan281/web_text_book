The Distinction Between 'Believer in Allah' and 'Believer with Allah'
Al-Baghdadi distinguishes two ways of describing faith: absolute and conditional.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between declaring faith in Allah without exception and declaring one's status with Allah with exception ('if Allah wills').

Core Idea
Declaring faith in Allah is certain; declaring one's standing with Allah is conditional.

In this passage, Abu Mansur al-Baghdadi presents a nuanced distinction about how believers should phrase their declaration of faith. Some scholars differentiate between saying 'I am a believer in Allah' and 'I am a believer with Allah.' The key lies in whether the statement refers to the reality of one's belief or one's ultimate standing before Allah. This distinction helps clarify the proper use of exception ('if Allah wills') in statements of faith.

Guided Path
1
Read the Passage
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Focus on the overall flow and the two key phrases: 'mu'min billah' and 'mu'min 'ind Allah.'
Notice the reference to hadith scholars and the verse from Surat al-Anfal.
2
Identify the Two Statements
The passage presents two ways of describing faith: one without exception (for 'believer in Allah') and one with exception (for 'believer with Allah'). Identify each and note the reasoning provided.
3
Understand the Reasoning
The scholar explains that saying 'I am a believer with Allah' requires 'if Allah wills' because that statement concerns one's status with Allah, which is known only through His promise. The other statement, 'I am a believer in Allah,' refers to one's own faith and is said without exception.
Consider why this distinction matters for theological precision.
4
Examine the Verse
The passage cites a verse from Surat al-Anfal (8) as evidence that Allah describes some believers with perfection, implying others are not at that level. This supports the need for conditional language when referring to one's standing with Allah.
The verse is not fully quoted but referenced.

Commentary
This passage presents a distinction made by Abu Mansur al-Baghdadi regarding the use of exception (istithna') in declaring faith. He reports that some hadith scholars distinguished between describing one's faith in itself and describing one's status with Allah. The passage cites the verse from Surat al-Anfal to show that there are believers described with perfection, implying that others may not have that perfection.

Two Reasons for Doubt in Perfection of Faith
Ghazali enumerates two reasons for doubt in perfection of faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two reasons Ghazali gives for why one cannot be certain of having perfect faith.

Core Idea
Faith's perfection is hidden from us due to hypocrisy and incomplete acts.

In this passage, Ghazali addresses a subtle yet important point: how can a believer be sure their faith is perfect? He identifies two internal obstacles that make such certainty impossible. These reasons are rooted in the hidden nature of hypocrisy and the unknown completeness of our obedience.

Guided Path

Read the Arabic passage and translation

2
Identify the first reason: hidden hypocrisy
Notice that Ghazali says hypocrisy removes perfection of faith and is hidden, so one cannot be certain of being free from it.
Hypocrisy (nifaq) is internal and concealed.
3
Identify the second reason: unknown completeness of acts
Ghazali states that faith is completed by acts of obedience, but one does not know if they have performed them perfectly.
Perfection of acts is difficult to ascertain.

Commentary

فهما كان الشك بهذه المثابة كان الإستثناء واجباً لأن الإيمان عبارة عما يفيد الجنة . كما أن الصوم عبارة عما يبرىء الذمة ، وما فسد قبل الغروب لا يبرىء الذمة ، فيخرج عن كونه صوماً ، فكذلك الإيمان ، بل لا يبعد أن يسأل عن الصوم الماضي الذي لا يشك فيه بعد الفراغ منه ، فيقال . أصمت بالأمس ؟ فيقول : نعم إن شاء الله تعالى . إذ الصوم الحقيقي هو المقبول ، والمقبول غائب عنه لا يطلع عليه إلا الله تعالى . فمن هذا حسن الإستثناء في جميع أعمال البر ، ويكون ذلك شكاً في القبول ، إذ يمنع من القبول بعد جريان ظاهر شروط الصحة أسباب خفية لا يطلع عليها إلا رب الأرباب جل جلاله ، فيحسن الشك فيه . فهذه وجوه حسن الإستثناء في الجواب عن الإيمان ، وهي آخر ما تختم به كتاب قواعد العقائد . تم الكتاب بحمد الله تعالى وصلى الله على سيدنا محمد وعلى كل عبد مصطفى .
Thus, when doubt is of this nature, the exception becomes obligatory, because faith (īmān) is an expression of what benefits Paradise. Just as fasting (ṣawm) is an expression of what clears the liability, and whatever is invalidated before sunset does not clear the liability, so it leaves the category of fasting; likewise faith. Rather, it is not far-fetched that one be asked about a past fast concerning which there is no doubt after its completion, and he be asked: 'Did you fast yesterday?' and he says: 'Yes, if Allah wills (in shā’ Allāh).' For the true fasting is the accepted one, and the accepted is absent from him, known only to Allah Almighty. From this, the exception is commendable in all good works, and that is doubt concerning acceptance (qabūl), for after the apparent conditions of validity have occurred, there are hidden causes that prevent acceptance, known only to the Lord of lords, may His majesty be exalted; thus doubt concerning it is commendable. These are the aspects of the commendability of the exception in answering about faith, and these are the concluding remarks with which the book Qawā‘id al-‘Aqā’id is sealed. The book is completed by the praise of Allah Almighty, and may Allah bless our master Muḥammad and every chosen servant.

Commentary
This passage is an argument defending the practice of making istithnā’ (exception) by saying 'in shā’ Allāh' when asked about one's faith or good deeds. The author, Imam al-Ghazālī, first states the rule: when doubt is of this nature, istithnā’ becomes obligatory. He then gives an analogy: just as fasting (ṣawm) is defined as what clears one's liability (i.e., valid fasting), and if it is invalidated before sunset it does not clear the liability and thus is not considered fasting, similarly faith (īmān) is defined as what results in Paradise. What is lost before death is not true faith. Then he extends the analogy: a person may be asked about a past fast that is not in doubt, yet he may answer 'Yes, if Allah wills' because the true fasting is the accepted one (al-maqbūl), and acceptance is hidden—known only to Allah. From this, the use of istithnā’ is commendable in all good works, not as doubt about the act itself, but as doubt about its acceptance, since there may be hidden reasons preventing acceptance despite apparent validity. The passage concludes this discussion as the closing of the book Qawā‘id al-‘Aqā’id, followed by the doxology.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Saying 'in shā’ Allāh' about one's faith implies doubt in the existence or sincerity of one's faith.
Correction: Ghazali explains that the doubt is not about the act but about its acceptance, which is hidden and known only to Allah.

Key Takeaway
Istithnā' in matters of faith is not a sign of doubt in one's belief but a recognition that true acceptance is known only to Allah.
