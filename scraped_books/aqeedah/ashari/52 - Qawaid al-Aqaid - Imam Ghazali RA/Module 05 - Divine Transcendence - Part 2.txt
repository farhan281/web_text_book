The Two Worlds and the Danger of Claiming Union
Ghazali distinguishes the visible world from the unseen world and warns against false claims of union.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between the world of shahada and the world of malakut, the subtle connection between them, and why claiming union is a serious error.

Core Idea
The physical and spiritual worlds are distinct yet intimately connected, but never united.

This lesson from Imam Ghazali introduces a foundational concept: the visible world (shahada) and the unseen world (malakut). Ghazali explains that the heart belongs to the unseen, while the limbs belong to the visible, and warns against two dangerous misunderstandings: denying the unseen or claiming union between the two. We will explore his poetic analogy that beautifully illustrates their relationship.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the translation. Let Ghazali's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the compressed style and the poetic ending.
2
Identify the two worlds and their domains
Find how Ghazali defines the world of shahada and the world of malakut. Note that the heart belongs to malakut and the limbs to mulk.
This establishes the fundamental duality.
3
Understand the subtle connection
Ghazali speaks of the 'subtlety and delicacy' of the connection between the two worlds. Consider what this means for how the physical and spiritual interact.
4
Recognize the two errors
The passage mentions two wrong beliefs: claiming union (ittihad) and denying the unseen world entirely. Both are refuted by Ghazali's framework.
These errors arise from misunderstanding the connection.
5
Analyze the poetic analogy
Read the analogy about the glass and wine. Reflect on how it illustrates the distinction and connection without fusion.
The glass represents the physical, the wine the spiritual.

The world of shahada (witnessing) is perceived by the senses, and the world of malakut (dominion) is the unseen world perceived by the light of insight. The heart is from the world of malakut, and the limbs and their actions are from the world of mulk (kingdom). The subtlety and delicacy of the connection between the two worlds reached a point where some people imagined the union of one with the other, and others imagined that there is no world except the world of shahada, which is these sensible bodies. And whoever perceived both matters and perceived their duality and then their connection expressed it by saying: The glass became thin and the wine became clear, and they resembled each other, so the matter became similar, as if it were wine but no cup, and as if it were cup but no wine.

Commentary
This passage classifies two distinct worlds: the world of shahada (the visible, sensory world) and the world of malakut (the unseen, spiritual world perceived by the inner eye of insight). Ghazali explains that the heart belongs to the world of malakut, while the limbs and their actions belong to the world of mulk (the physical kingdom). He then highlights the subtle and delicate connection between these two worlds. This subtlety has led to two opposite errors: some people imagined that the two worlds are united (ittihad), confusing the distinction; others denied the existence of the unseen world altogether, claiming that only the physical world exists. Ghazali then quotes a poetic analogy: when the glass becomes thin and the wine clear, they resemble each other so much that one might think there is only wine or only the cup, yet both remain distinct. This analogy illustrates the fine boundary between the two worlds and warns against the false claim of union. The passage is a clear refutation of both pantheistic union and materialist denial.

The Two Worlds: Shahada and Malakut
Ghazali defines two worlds and critiques two misconceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between the visible and unseen worlds and the subtle connection that leads to common misconceptions.

Core Idea
Two worlds, one connection: the heart links the seen and the unseen.

In this passage, Ghazali lays the foundation for a proper understanding of reality. He defines two distinct realms—the world of the senses and the world of the unseen—and warns against two common errors: either merging them or denying the unseen. This lesson will clarify the distinction and the delicate relationship between them.

Guided Path
1

Notice that Ghazali uses a very compressed style here.
2
Identify the two worlds and their perception
Look for the definitions of shahada and malakut. Note how each is perceived: senses vs. light of insight.
3
Locate the heart and limbs
The passage assigns the heart to malakut and limbs to mulk (part of shahada). Understand how this links the two worlds.
4
Understand the two misconceptions
Identify the two errors: thinking the worlds are united, and denying the unseen world altogether. Reflect on why these errors arise from the subtle connection.
5
Reflect on the poetic analogy
Analyze the glass and wine analogy. What does it teach about the relationship? Why might someone be confused?

The world of witnessing (shahada) perceived by the senses, and the world of sovereignty (malakut) is the world of the unseen perceived by the light of insight. The heart is from the world of malakut, while the limbs and their actions are from the world of dominion (mulk). The subtlety and precision of connection between the two worlds reached a point where some people thought one is united with the other, and others thought there is no world except the world of shahada, which is these sensible bodies. Commentary
This passage is a definitional and argumentative passage. Imam al-Ghazali first defines two worlds: the world of shahada (visible, perceived by senses) and the world of malakut (unseen, perceived by the light of inner sight). He then locates the heart in the malakut world and the limbs and their actions in the mulk world. He highlights the subtle connection between these two realms. This connection is so delicate that some mistakenly claim they are one (union), while others deny the unseen world altogether, reducing reality to mere physical bodies. Those who grasp both and their distinctness, then perceive their connection, express it through a poetic analogy: when glass is thin and wine clear, they resemble each other, leading to confusion—one may say 'it is wine without a glass' or 'a glass without wine'. This illustrates the difficulty of distinguishing the two worlds yet affirms their distinct identities.
