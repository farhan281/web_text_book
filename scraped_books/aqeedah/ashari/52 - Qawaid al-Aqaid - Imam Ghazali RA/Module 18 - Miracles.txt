Miracles of the Prophet and the Inimitability of the Quran
Ghazali lists miracles and argues the Quran is the supreme miracle.

Learning Objective
This lesson establishes that the Prophet's truthfulness is proven through miracles, with the Quran as the greatest miracle due to its inimitability, prophecies, and the Prophet's illiteracy.

Core Idea
The Quran's inimitability proves prophethood beyond eloquence alone.

Imam Ghazali presents a proof of the Prophet's truthfulness through the miracle of the Quran. He lists other physical miracles but centers on the Quran as the enduring sign. This passage explains why the Arabs could not match the Quran and highlights its prophecies as confirmation of divine origin.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice the two main sections: physical miracles and the Quranic miracle.
Pay attention to the list of physical miracles and the shift to the Quran.
2
Identify the factors of inimitability
Ghazali gives several reasons why the Quran is inimitable. List them: its eloquence and composition, content of past stories, the Prophet's illiteracy, and prophecies of future events.
These factors work together to show the Quran is beyond human ability.
3
Examine the prophecies
Look at the two examples of prophecies: the conquest of Mecca and the victory of the Romans. Reflect on how their fulfillment confirms the truth of the Prophet.
These were specific predictions that came true exactly as stated.

شرائع اليهود والنصارى والصابئين، وأيده بالمعجزات الظاهرة والآيات الباهرة كانشقاق القمر وتسبيح الحصى وانطاق العجماء وما تفجر من بين أصابعه الماء. ومن آياته الظاهرة التي تحدى بها مع كافة العرب القرآن العظيم، فإنهم مع تميزهم بالفصاحة والبلاغة تهدفوا لسلبه ونهبه وقتله واخراجه كما أخبر الله عز وجل عنهم، ولم يقدروا على معارضته بمثل القرآن، إذ لم يكن في قدرة البشر الجمع بين جزالة القرآن ونظمه، هذا مع ما فيه من أخبار الأولين، مع كونه أمياً غير ممارس للكتب، والأنباء الغيب في أمور تحقق صدقه فيها في الاستقبال، كقوله عن تعالى: لَتَدْخُلُنَّ الْمَسْجِدَ الْحَرَامَ إِنْ شَاءَ اللهُ آمِنِينَ مُحَلِّقِينَ رُءُوسَكُمْ وَمُقَصِّرِينَ. وكقوله تعالى: الَمَ غُلِبَتِ الرُّومُ فِي أدْنَى الأَرْضِ وَهُمْ مِّن بَعْدِ غَلَبِهِمْ سَيَغْلِبُونَ فِي بِضْعِ سِنِينَ.
He abrogated the laws of the Jews, Christians, and Sabians, and supported him (the Prophet) with manifest miracles and brilliant signs such as the splitting of the moon, the glorification of the pebbles, the speech of the dumb animal, and the flowing of water from between his fingers. And among his manifest signs with which he challenged all the Arabs is the Mighty Quran, for they – despite their distinction in eloquence and rhetoric – sought to rob him, plunder him, kill him, and expel him, as Allah Almighty informed about them, and they were not able to oppose it with anything like the Quran, for it was not within human capability to combine the sublimity of the Quran and its composition, together with what it contains of stories of the ancients, while he was illiterate, not having practiced (reading) books, and the news of the unseen about matters whose truthfulness was realized in the future, such as His statement – exalted is He –: 'You will surely enter the Sacred Mosque, if Allah wills, secure, shaving your heads and shortening (your hair).' And His statement – exalted is He –: 'Alif Lam Meem. The Romans have been defeated in the nearest land, and they, after their defeat, will overcome within a few years.'

Commentary
This passage is a proof of prophethood through miracles, specifically the Quran. The author begins by stating that the Prophet was supported with manifest miracles, listing physical miracles (splitting moon, pebbles glorifying, animal speaking, water flowing). Then he focuses on the Quran as the greatest sign, describing the challenge (tahaddi) to the Arabs, who despite their eloquence could not produce a similar text. He explains that the Quran's inimitability lies in its eloquence and composition, plus its content of past stories and the Prophet's illiteracy, and its prophecies of future events, citing two examples: the conquest of Mecca (Surah Al-Fath) and the victory of the Romans (Surah Ar-Rum). These prophecies were fulfilled, confirming his truthfulness.

Miracles as Divine Confirmation of Prophethood
Ghazali explains the logical reasoning of how a miracle confirms a prophet's truthfulness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a miracle serves as logical proof of a prophet's truthfulness through God's endorsement.

Core Idea
Miracles are God's 'you are truthful' to the prophet's claim.

Ghazali presents a concise logical argument for why miracles are evidence of prophethood. He explains that a miracle is an act beyond human capability, so it must be from God. When paired with the prophet's challenge, it becomes God's direct confirmation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to grasp Ghazali's concise argument.
2
Identify the key components
Notice two parts: the logical principle (miracle as divine act) and the analogy (king's messenger).
3
Reflect on the logical flow
See how the conjunction of miracle and challenge transforms the miracle into divine confirmation.
4
Apply the analogy
Understand the analogy: just as the king's action confirms the messenger, God's miracle confirms the prophet.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
ووجه دلالة المعجزة على صدق الرسل إن كل ما عجز عنه البشر لم يكن إلا فعلاً لله تعالى فمهما كان مقروناً بتحدي النبي ينزل منزلة قوله: صدقت، وذلك مثل القائم بين أيدي الملك المدعي على رعيته، إنه رسول الملك إليهم.
And the way a miracle indicates the truthfulness of the messengers is that everything that humans are incapable of is nothing but an act of Allah Almighty. So whenever it is accompanied by the challenge of the prophet, it takes the status of His saying: 'You have spoken truthfully.' And that is like the one standing before a king claiming to the subjects that he is the messenger of the king to them.
Source pages p. 215

Commentary
This passage explains the logical reasoning (dalīl) behind miracles (mu‘jizāt). The author defines a miracle as an act that humans are incapable of, so it must be an act of God. When such an act is performed in conjunction with a prophet's challenge (taḥaddī), it effectively becomes God's endorsement of the prophet's claim. The analogy of a king's messenger is given: if a claimant stands before a king and claims to be his messenger, and the king performs an action that confirms him (like giving him a sign or escort), it serves as proof. Similarly, God's creation of a miracle at the prophet's challenge is equivalent to God saying 'You have spoken truthfully.'

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think the miracle itself is the proof, independent of the prophet's claim.
Correction: Ghazali clarifies that the evidential value lies in God's act of confirming the prophet through the miracle, not in the miracle per se. The conjunction of miracle and challenge is what makes it proof.

Key Takeaway
Miracles confirm prophethood because they are divine acts that God performs to endorse the prophet's claim when challenged.

The Miracle Analogy: King's Act as Affirmation
Ghazali explains how miracles produce necessary knowledge through a king's analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali uses the analogy of a king performing an unusual act to explain that miracles produce necessary knowledge of a prophet's truthfulness.

Core Idea
Miracles are not just signs—they are equivalent to God's direct affirmation.

Ghazali presents a powerful analogy to explain how miracles (mu'jizat) function as proofs of prophethood. He imagines a claimant saying to a king: 'If you are truthful, do this unusual thing.' When the king does it, observers gain certain knowledge that the king's act is like saying 'you are truthful.' In the same way, when a prophet performs a miracle, it is as if God Himself is affirming the prophet's truthfulness. This analogy reveals that miracles produce necessary knowledge (ilm daruri) rather than merely probable evidence.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic text once carefully, then read the translation. Notice the key terms: علم ضروري (necessary knowledge) and نازل منزلة (equivalent to).
2
Unpack the analogy
Identify the elements: the claimant (prophet), the king (God), the unusual act (miracle), and the observers (people). Understand how the act functions as affirmation.
The king's act is described as 'equivalent to his saying you have spoken the truth.'
3
Understand necessary knowledge (علم ضروري)
Reflect on why the knowledge is 'necessary'—it is immediate and certain, not inferred. How does the analogy convey this immediacy?
The observers directly perceive the king's compliance as an affirmation.
4
Apply to prophethood
Consider how a miracle (like splitting the moon) would function as God's direct affirmation of a prophet's claim. Compare the analogy to examples from Islamic tradition.

Source Text

فانه مهما قال للملك إن كنت صادقاً فقم على سريرك ثلاثاً واقعد على خلاف عادتك ففعل الملك ذلك حصل للحاضرين علم ضروري بأن ذلك نازل منزلة قوله صدقت
For whenever he says to the king: 'If you are truthful, then stand on your bed three times and sit contrary to your habit,' and the king does that, then the attendees acquire necessary knowledge that this is equivalent to his saying 'you have spoken the truth.'
Source pages p. 216

Commentary
This passage presents an argument for the evidentiary value of miracles (mu'jizat). Ghazali uses an analogy: a prophet's claim is like someone saying to a king, 'If you are truthful, perform an unusual act.' When the king performs it, observers gain necessary knowledge (ilm daruri) that the king's action is equivalent to a verbal affirmation (tasdiq). Similarly, when a prophet performs a miracle, it is as if God is affirming his truthfulness through the extraordinary act. The analogy underscores that miracles are not just evidence but produce certainty directly.
