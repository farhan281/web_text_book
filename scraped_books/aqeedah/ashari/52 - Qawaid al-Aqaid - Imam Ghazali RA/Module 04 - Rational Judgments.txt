Classification of Beliefs into Four Categories
Ghazali classifies beliefs into four types and begins explaining the first.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam al-Ghazali's classification of beliefs into four categories and the beginning of the first category: obligatory beliefs about Allah's oneness and attributes.

Core Idea
Beliefs are not all the same; they fall into four categories: obligatory, impossible, existent, and incoming.

This passage from Ghazali's Qawa'id al-‘Aqa’id gives us a structured way to think about creed. Before diving into specific beliefs, he presents a framework: some beliefs are obligatory to hold, some are impossible, some affirm existence, and some affirm future events. We'll explore this framework and then look at the first type—what is obligatory to believe about Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the classification
Notice the four categories and their Arabic terms: وجوب (obligatory), استحالة (impossible), وجود (existent), ورود (incoming).
2
Understand the first category
Read the list of obligatory beliefs: Allah is one, not divided, no partner, Living, Self-Subsisting, no slumber, Creator, Omnipotent, All-Knowing.
3
Reflect on the purpose
Ask yourself: why does Ghazali classify beliefs before stating them? This organizes creed and prevents confusion.

وتفاصيل هذه القاعدة ، يأتي من جهة العقيدة على أنواع كثيرة : منها : ما يعتقد وجوبه ، ومنها : ما يعتقد استحالته ، ومنها : ما يتحقق وجوده ، ومنها : ما يتيقن وروده . أما ما يعتقد وجوبه : فهو أن تعتقد أن الله سبحانه وتعالى واحد غير منقسم في ذاته ، وأنه ليس معه ثان في إلاهيته ، وأنه حي قيوم ، لا تأخذه سنة ولا نوم ، وأنه إله كل شيء وخالقه ، وأنه على كل شيء قدير ، وأنه عالم بما ظهر وما بطن ، لا يعزب
And the details of this principle come, from the perspective of creed, in many types: among them: what is believed to be obligatory (wajib) of it, and among them: what is believed to be impossible (mustahil) of it, and among them: what is confirmed to exist (wujud) of it, and among them: what is certain to arrive (wurud) of it. Commentary
This passage provides a classification of the types of beliefs that fall under the first principle (the shahadatayn). Ghazali states that the details of this principle come in many types from the perspective of creed. He then enumerates four categories: (1) ما يعتقد وجوبه: what is believed to be necessary (wajib) – i.e., beliefs that are obligatory to hold; (2) ما يعتقد استحالته: what is believed to be impossible (mustahil) – i.e., beliefs that must be rejected as impossible for Allah; (3) ما يتحقق وجوده: what is confirmed to exist (wujud) – i.e., things whose existence is affirmed (such as prophets, angels, etc.); (4) ما يتيقن وروده: what is certain to arrive (wurud) – i.e., matters of the unseen like the Day of Judgment. After listing these four, Ghazali begins to explain the first category: what is obligatory to believe. He starts with the belief in Allah's oneness (tawhid) in essence: that He is one, not divided, and that there is no partner in His divinity. He mentions attributes: He is al-Hayy al-Qayyum (the Living, the Self-Subsisting), free from slumber and sleep; He is the God of everything and its Creator; He is omnipotent; He is all-knowing of the apparent and hidden. The passage cuts off at 'لا يعزب' (not absent), which would complete the idea that nothing escapes His knowledge. This classification sets the structure for the detailed creedal exposition to follow.

Ash'ari View on Rational Good and Evil
Ash'ari position on rational judgment of good and evil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari position that good and evil are determined by revelation, not reason.

Core Idea
Good and evil are defined by divine law, not human reason.

The Ash'ari school holds that actions have no inherent moral value; rather, their goodness or badness depends on God's command. This lesson examines their scriptural proof from the Quran and the support they receive from traditionists.

Guided Path
1
Read the Ash'ari passage
Read the quoted passage carefully. Notice the precise claim: 'la tahseen wa la taqbeeh aqlan' (no rational judgment of good or evil).
The passage also clarifies that moral description comes from the attachment of divine speech.
2
Analyze the Quranic proof
Reflect on the verse 'We do not punish until We send a messenger.' How does this support the Ash'ari view? It implies that moral accountability requires revelation.
3
Connect to broader theology
Consider why this position matters for Islamic theology: it emphasizes God's sovereignty in defining morality.
This view contrasts with rationalist schools like the Mu'tazila.

وهذه المسألة معروفة بالتحسين والتقبيح العقليين قالت الأشاعرة : لا تحسين ولا تقبيح عقلا، أي إن الافعال إنما توصف بالحسن والقبح من حيث تعلق خطاب الشرع بها؛ ودليله السمعي قوله تعالى : ﴿ وَمَا كُنَّا مُعَذِّبِينَ حَتَّى نَبْعَثَ رَسُولاً ﴾ وبه تمسك المحدث أيضاً وأما الصوفي : فإنه يقول : الافعال كلها نسبتان نسبة التكوين ونسبة التكليف ، أما نسبة التكوين فعامة، لأن الافعال كلها لله تعالى ، وبهذه النسبة لا توصف بحسن ولا قبح لاستواء الإيجاد، بل هي حسنة من حيث علم الفاعل ، وارادته ، وأما نسبة التكليف وهي الطلب ولا علم بالحقيقة مختصة بأفعال المكلف ، ومن المعلوم أن الطلب للشيء فرع العلم به ، إلا الله تعالى ، فلا تكليف ولا طلب إلا لله تعالى ؛ وأيضاً فإن تعلق الطلب بفعل أو ترك غيب فلا يعلم إلا بالتوفيق السمعي النبوي فهي الحسن والقبح لا يدرك بمجرد العقل فلا حسن ولا قبح عقلا ، وهو المطلوب
This issue is known as rational judgment of good and evil. The Ash'aris said: There is no rational [judgment of] good or evil, i.e., actions are only described as good or bad in terms of the attachment of the speech of the Law to them. Its scriptural proof is the saying of the Most High: 'And We were not tormenters until We sent a messenger.' The traditionist also adhered to it. As for the Sufi, he says: Actions all have two relations: the relation of creation and the relation of obligation. As for the relation of creation, it is general, because all actions are from Allah, and in this relation they are not described as good or evil due to the uniformity of existence; rather they are good in terms of the knowledge and will of the Agent. As for the relation of obligation, which is the request, and there is no knowledge of the reality, it is specific to the actions of the obligated one. It is known that the request for something depends on knowledge of it, except Allah, so there is no obligation or request except from Allah. Also, the attachment of the request to an act or omission is unseen, so it is not known except through the prophetic scriptural guidance. So good and evil are not perceived by mere intellect; thus there is no rational good or evil, and that is what is sought.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of the issue of rational judgment of good and evil (التحسين والتقبيح العقليين). It presents two major positions. First, the Ash'ari position states that actions are not inherently good or evil by reason; rather, they are described as such only by the revealed law. Their proof is the verse 'We do not punish until We send a messenger.' The traditionist (المحدث) also uses this proof. Second, the Sufi position offers a different analysis: actions have two relations. One is the relation of creation (التكوين), applicable to all actions because all come from God, and under this aspect they are not subject to moral judgment. The other is the relation of obligation (التكليف), which applies only to human actions and is known only through revelation. Thus, both schools conclude that good and evil (حسن وقبح) cannot be known by reason alone. The passage ends with the affirmation that this is the sought conclusion.

Sufi View on Rational Good and Evil
Sufi view on rational good and evil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sufi analysis of actions as having two relations (creation and obligation) and why this leads to rejecting rational moral judgment.

Core Idea
Actions have two relations; only revelation can determine moral value.

The Sufi perspective offers a nuanced view: all actions come from God, so they are not inherently good or bad; moral judgment applies only to human actions and requires revelation. This lesson unpacks the two-relation framework.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two relations
The Sufi distinguishes between the relation of creation (nisbat al-takwin) and the relation of obligation (nisbat al-taklif). Grasp the difference.
Creation is general to all actions, obligation is specific to human acts.
2
Examine the creation relation
Under creation, actions are from Allah, so they are not called good or evil; they are uniform in existence and good because of the Agent.
3
Examine the obligation relation
Obligation involves God's command. Since command depends on knowledge, and human reason cannot know what is commanded without revelation, moral judgment requires prophetic guidance.
The conclusion: 'la husna wa la qubha aqlan' – no rational good or evil.

Rational Proof for Metaphorical Interpretation
Author presents rational proof for ta'wil using two examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand rational proof that some scriptural expressions must be interpreted metaphorically, using two examples from the Qur'an and hadith.

Core Idea
Scriptural literalism is impossible when it leads to logical contradiction.

Imam al-Ghazali now introduces rational proof for ta'wil: when taking a text literally results in an impossibility, we must interpret it metaphorically. He illustrates this with two powerful examples: the hadith about the believer's heart between Allah's fingers, and the Qur'anic command 'Be'. Both cases show that literal meaning cannot be upheld logically.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without stopping. Then read the literal translation to grasp the author's argument.
Notice the logical structure: each example is presented and then analysed for impossibility.
2
Analyze the first example: fingers of Allah
Examine the hadith about the believer's heart being between two of Allah's fingers. Why can't this be literal? Because physical fingers are not found in hearts. The author concludes it is a metaphor for divine power.
The metaphor is chosen because 'fingers' powerfully convey perfect control.
3
Analyze the second example: the command 'Be'
Consider the Qur'anic verse 'Be' and it becomes. The author presents a dilemma: if 'Be' is addressed to the non-existent, it cannot be heard; if to the existent, it is unnecessary. Hence literal interpretation is impossible, so it is a metaphor for the immediacy of creation.
4
Understand the principle
Reflect on the principle: whenever literalism leads to an impossibility (muhal), rational proof demands metaphorical interpretation (ta'wil). This is a key methodological rule in kalam.
The two examples serve as concrete evidence for this principle.

Commentary
This passage defines rational proof for metaphorical interpretation. The author gives two examples from hadith and Quran. The first is the prophetic hadith about the believer's heart being between two of the All-Merciful's fingers. Since physically examining hearts reveals no fingers, it cannot be literal; thus it must be a metaphor (kinayah) for divine power. The second example is the Quranic verse 'Be' and it becomes. The author analyses: the command 'Be' is either addressed to the thing before it exists or after. Before existence, the non-existent cannot hear or understand; after existence, the command is superfluous. So literal interpretation is impossible. Therefore, the verse is a metaphor for the immediacy and perfection of divine power. The key point is that rational proof identifies cases where literalism leads to impossibility, necessitating ta'wil.

The Contingency Argument: Premises
Ghazali presents the two premises of the contingency argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure of Ghazali's contingency argument and the rational justification for the first premise that every originated event requires a cause.

Core Idea
Every event's specific time proves it needs a cause.

Ghazali presents a rational proof for God's existence based on two premises. The first premise establishes that anything that comes into existence requires a cause, based on the self-evident principle of specification. The second premise asserts that the world itself is originated. In this lesson, we examine the reasoning behind the first premise and how it leads to the conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get an overall sense of the argument. Then read the translation to understand the meaning.
Notice that the passage is part of a longer argument; the proof for the second premise is incomplete here.
2
Identify the two premises
Find where Ghazali states the first premise (the originated thing needs a cause) and the second premise (the world is originated). Pay attention to the justification for each.
The justification for the first premise is given in detail; the second premise is only introduced.
3
Understand the specification argument
Focus on the reasoning: every originated event occurs at a specific time, which could have been otherwise. This specification requires a specificator (mukhassis). This is the core of the first premise.
This is a self-evident principle according to Ghazali.

Commentary
This passage is a rational proof (burhan) for the existence of God, structured as a syllogism with two premises. The author begins by noting that although fitrah and Quran suffice, he follows the theologians' method by appealing to self-evident intellectual principles (badihah al-'uqul). The first premise is: 'The originated thing (al-hadith) does not dispense in its origination with a cause that brings it about.' He justifies this by saying it is clear (jali) because every originated event occurs at a specific time, whereas it could have occurred earlier or later. This specification requires a specificator (mukhassis). The need for a specificator is a self-evident principle. The second premise is: 'The world is originated (hadith).' The proof given is that the bodies of the world are never free from movement (and rest). Although the full argument is not completed here, the implication is that since movement and rest are both events (hawadith), and bodies are inseparable from them, bodies themselves are temporally originated. From these two premises, the conclusion follows: the world requires a cause (God). The author thus presents the foundational premises of the classical kalam cosmological argument.

Analogies for Divine Speech Without Sound or Letter
Ghazali uses three analogies to explain divine speech is non‑physical.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali uses analogical reasoning to explain that divine speech is heard and written without being a physical substance.

Core Idea
Divine speech, like the vision of Allah, transcends physical attributes.

Ghazali addresses a key theological puzzle: how can the Quran be heard without sound and written without being physically contained? In this passage, he presents three concise analogies that draw on parallel rational principles about perception and containment. Each analogy builds on the previous one to clarify that divine speech is a single, non-physical attribute of Allah.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the problem
The passage starts with someone who finds it hard to accept that Moses heard speech without sound or letters. Note how Ghazali immediately connects this to the parallel problem of seeing Allah without a body in the Hereafter.
This shows a key theological method: understanding the invisible by analogy with the visible.
2
First analogy: Hearing and seeing
Ghazali argues that if one can accept seeing something without color, body, or magnitude (though unseen before), one should also accept hearing without sound. This is a direct comparison between the senses of sight and hearing.
3
Second analogy: Knowledge and speech
Just as Allah has one knowledge that encompasses all existents, He has one attribute of speech that covers everything expressed by words. Here Ghazali moves from the single, all‑encompassing nature of divine knowledge to the similar nature of divine speech.
This reinforces the unity and non‑physicality of the attribute of speech.
4
Third analogy: Writing and containment
The most vivid analogy: the heavens, Garden, and Fire are written on a small paper and seen in the pupil without their essences residing there. Similarly, the Quran is recited, memorized, and written without the essence of divine speech entering those places.
Notice the consequence Ghazali draws: if speech resided in paper, then writing Allah's name would put His essence in the paper – an absurdity.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Ghazali ends with a reductio ad absurdum: if the essence of speech were in the paper, then writing the Fire's name would cause the paper to burn. This proves that divine speech does not physically reside anywhere.
This is a powerful logical move: showing the impossible consequence of the opposing view.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of analogical arguments (qiyas) to make the nature of divine speech comprehensible. The author begins by addressing someone who finds impossible that Moses heard speech without sound or letters; such a person should also reject seeing Allah without body or color, which is accepted. General and Specific in Quranic Interpretation
Ghazali discusses general Quranic verses and their specification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how general Quranic verses can be specified by other verses and hadith, and why neither the Ash'ari nor Mu'tazila extreme positions on general expressions are sustainable.

Core Idea
General Quranic verses require specification by other revealed texts.

This passage from Ghazali's Qawa'id al-Aqa'id tackles a crucial methodological issue: how to interpret general statements in the Quran. Both the Ash'aris and Mu'tazila had different approaches, but Ghazali shows that the middle path is to recognize that general expressions can be specified by contextual clues, other verses, and hadith. Let's explore his argument.

Commentary
This passage is an argument about the interpretation of general (عام) and specific (خاص) expressions in the Quran. Ghazali first gives examples of Quranic verses that appear general but are understood as referring to particular groups or individuals: the verse about the most wretched entering the Fire is either about a specific group or a specific person (Umayya ibn Khalaf), and the verse about 'every time a company is thrown' refers to companies of disbelievers. He notes that from such examples, al-Ash'ari and some theologians denied that words have any inherent general meaning, claiming that they are ambiguous until a contextual clue (قرينة) specifies them. Then Ghazali presents the Mu'tazila position: they use verses that seem to make salvation conditional upon faith and good works, such as 'I am forgiving to him who repents, believes, and does righteous deeds' (Taha 20:82), and the surah al-Asr, and the verse 'There is none of you but will come to it' (Maryam 19:71) followed by 'Then We will save those who feared Allah' (19:72), and the verse threatening hellfire for disobedience, and the verse about having eternal hellfire for murder. The Mu'tazila take these as general statements to support their doctrine that grave sinners will be eternally punished. Ghazali responds that these general statements are themselves specified by other evidence: the verse 'He forgives what is below that (i.e., other than polytheism) for whom He wills' (An-Nisa 4:48) establishes that unforgiven sinners may still be forgiven if they are not polytheists, so the general threats are not absolute. Similarly, the hadith that 'He will bring out of the Fire whoever has a grain of faith' shows that believers who committed grave sins will not be in the Fire forever. Thus general statements must be understood in light of other revealed texts that qualify them.
