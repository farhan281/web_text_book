Defining 'Living' and the Proof from Essence Types
Razi defines 'living' and presents the proof from classification of essences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two definitions of 'living' and the Ash'ari proof that life is a real attribute based on the classification of essences.

Core Idea
Life is not merely the ability to act; it is a distinct attribute making action possible.

Razi begins by presenting two views on divine life: the Mu'tazili view that 'living' is a mere description, and the Ash'ari view that it is a real attribute. He then provides a proof from the observable difference between living and non-living beings, showing that an additional attribute must account for this difference.

Guided Path
1
Read the two definitions
Carefully read the Mu'tazili and Ash'ari definitions of 'living' in Arabic and English. Notice the key difference: the Ash'ari adds that life is an attribute subsisting in the essence.
Abu al-Husayn al-Basri is the Mu'tazili; 'our companions' refers to the Ash'aris.
2
Examine the proof from essence types
The proof divides essences into two kinds: those that can know and have power (living) and those that cannot (inanimates). Both kinds are equal in essentiality, so the difference must be due to an additional attribute.
Ask yourself: why can't the essence itself account for the difference?
3
Reflect on the conclusion
The proof concludes that life is a real attribute (sifa) distinct from the essence. This refutes the Mu'tazili view that life is just a description.
This is the Ash'ari position.

The fourteenth issue: On His being exalted alive. The opinion of Abu al-Husayn is that the living is that which is not impossible for it to know and have power. And our companions said: Life is an attribute subsisting in the essence, due to which it is not impossible for the essence to know, and it is valid for it to have power. And the proof of our companions for their statement: Essences are of two kinds: among them that which knows and has power, and among them that which that is not valid of, and they are the inanimates. And the two kinds are equal in essentiality, and were it not for the distinction of that which it is valid for it to know and have power from that which it is not valid for it, this variation would not occur. Abu al-Husayn al-Basri said: We have proven that His essence is different from other essences by its own specific essence, so why is it not permissible that the attribute of worldliness and poweriness in the case of God be caused by His specific essence? And this is a good question. And the relied-upon [proof] for us is to say: Your saying: The living is that which is not impossible for it to know and have power—this indicates the negation of impossibility, and impossibility is a negation, and the negation of impossibility is a negation of negation, so it becomes a positive matter. Then this positive matter is not the essence itself; because when we know that possible things end in a necessary existent by essence, we have known His essence. And after we have known this matter—that is, our saying: It is not impossible for it to know and have power—and the known is other than what is not existent, it is established that His being exalted alive is a real attribute subsisting in His essence. Commentary
This passage is a theological argument on the attribute of life (hayat) for God. It begins by presenting the position of Abu al-Husayn al-Basri (a Mu'tazili) that 'living' is defined as that which is not impossible for it to know and have power. Then the Ash'ari position (our companions) is given: life is an attribute (sifa) subsisting in the essence, because of which knowledge and power are possible. The proof offered is that essences are divided into two categories: those that can know and have power (like living beings) and those that cannot (inanimates). Since both are equal in essentiality, the difference must be due to an attribute. Abu al-Husayn objects that if God's essence is unique, its uniqueness could suffice to explain knowledge and power. Razi acknowledges this as a good question. Then Razi provides the relied-upon response: the definition of 'living' involves a negation (of impossibility); negation of negation is affirmative. This affirmative thing is not the essence itself, because we know the essence from the contingency argument (ending in a necessary existent), and then we know this additional matter (the negation of impossibility), which is other than the essence. Therefore, life is a real attribute. The argument is a rejoinder to the Mu'tazili position.

Responding to the Mu'tazili Objection: Life as a Positive Attribute
Razi addresses the Mu'tazili objection and provides the relied-upon proof for life as a real attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazili objection that God's unique essence could account for knowledge and power, and the Ash'ari response that life is a positive attribute distinct from the essence.

Core Idea
Life is not a negation but a positive attribute, proven by the logic of double negation.

After presenting the proof from essence types, Razi addresses a powerful objection from Abu al-Husayn: if God's essence is unique, why is an extra attribute needed? Razi acknowledges the objection's strength, then delivers the definitive Ash'ari response based on the logical structure of the definition of 'living'.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and acknowledge its strength
Abu al-Husayn argues that since God's essence is unique, its uniqueness could directly explain knowledge and power without an extra attribute. Razi calls this 'a good question'.
This shows Razi's intellectual honesty.
2
Understand the logical transformation
The definition 'not impossible' involves a negation (impossibility is a negation), and the negation of negation yields an affirmative. Thus 'not impossible' is a positive reality.
This is the key move: turning a seemingly negative description into a positive attribute.
3
Recognize why this positive reality is not the essence
We know God's essence through the contingency argument (possible things end in a necessary existent). The positive reality 'not impossible' is additional to that essence, so it must be a distinct attribute.
Thus life is a real attribute, not a mere negation or the essence itself.

Knowledge and Power: Concepts Other Than the Essence
Razi states the minimal claim and analyzes knowledge as a relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's minimal claim that the concepts of divine knowledge and power are distinct from the essence, and how he grounds this in the relational nature of knowledge.

Core Idea
Knowledge is a relation to the known, and is therefore other than the essence.

Razi begins by lowering the bar: he only insists that the concept of 'knowing' is not identical to the concept of the essence. He then offers a precise analysis of knowledge as a relation between knower and known. This passage clarifies the actual point of dispute between the Ash'aris and Mu'tazila regarding divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read and identify the minimal claim
Read the Arabic passage and translation. Identify Razi's statement of his minimal claim: 'the concept of His being knowing... is not the same concept as His essence.'
Razi suggests that even the Mu'tazila might agree on this.
2
Understand the relational analysis
Notice how Razi defines knowledge: it is a specific relation (nisba) and addition (idafa) between the knower and the known, called 'ta'alluq' by theologians. He claims that this relation is other than the essence.
This is the core of his argument.
3
Compare the two positions
Identify the two views: Razi's own (knowledge = relation, distinct from essence) and the Ash'ari view (three things: essence, attribute as real quality, and then a relation). Observe the nuance.
Razi does not specify which group he belongs to, but notes his companions' view.

The fifteenth issue: On proving that God has knowledge and power. Know that we do not claim in this issue more than that the concept of His being exalted knowing, powerful, living is not the same concept as His essence, but it is something other than His essence. So if the Mu'tazila agree on this much, then agreement is reached and disagreement is removed. Commentary
This passage is a nuanced exposition of the issue of divine attributes. Razi begins by stating the minimal claim he insists upon: the concept (mafhum) of God being knowing, powerful, living is not identical to the concept of His essence; rather, it is something other (mughtayir) than the essence. He suggests that if the Mu'tazila agree on this, then the dispute is resolved, because the Mu'tazila denied distinct attributes. He notes that most theologians have been confused about the precise point of disagreement. Then he provides a precise analysis: knowledge involves a specific relation (nisba) and addition (idafa) between the knower and the known. The theologians call this relation 'ta'alluq'. Razi's own view is that knowledge is exactly this relation and addition, and this relation is other than the essence. Therefore, knowledge (with the relation) and the essence are two things, not one. He then notes that a group of his school (presumably Ash'aris) affirm three things: the essence, knowledge as a real attribute subsisting in the essence, and then a relation (ta'alluq) between that attribute and the known. Thus, they distinguish between the attribute and its relation.

Razi on the Minimum Entities for Divine Attributes
Razi enumerates varying positions on the number of entities: essence, knowledge, worldliness, relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's analysis of different theological positions on the number of entities (essence, knowledge, worldliness, relation) and his conclusion that only essence and a relation (awareness/knowledge) must be affirmed.

Core Idea
Simplicity in divine attributes: only essence and relation are necessary.

Razi examines the views of al-Baqillani and others on how many entities (essence, knowledge, worldliness, relations) must be affirmed. He finds the issue confused and asserts that only the essence and a single relation (awareness/knowledge) are necessary. This lesson walks through his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
The Arabic ends abruptly; focus on the clear part.
2
Identify the positions
The passage presents al-Baqillani's possible view of four entities, and two scenarios leading to four or five. Note how the relation (ta'alluq) is assigned to worldliness or knowledge.
3
Understand Razi's critique
Razi notes that most earlier scholars did not investigate these distinctions, leaving the matter confused. See how he dismisses the complexity.
4
Grasp Razi's own minimal position
Razi asserts that only essence and the relation (shu'ur/ilm) are necessary. Affirming other entities (like worldliness as separate) is not required. This is his main point.
The phrase 'and the addition, which is' is incomplete but the intended meaning is clear.
5
Reflect on the principle of parsimony
Ask yourself why Razi prefers the minimal view. This reflects a methodological principle in kalam: do not multiply entities without necessity.
Connects to the core idea of simplicity.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion on divine attributes by classifying the different positions on how many entities to affirm. Razi begins with al-Qadi Abu Bakr al-Baqillani's view, which appears to imply four things: essence, knowledge (as attribute), worldliness (al-`alimiyya) which knowledge entails, and a relation (ta'alluq). There is ambiguity whether the relation is attached to worldliness or to knowledge. If to one only, then there are four entities; if to both, then five. Razi notes that most earlier scholars did not examine these nuances, leaving the matter confused. Then he states his own position: the minimum necessary is to affirm the essence and the relation, which he calls 'shu'ur' (awareness) or 'ilm' (knowledge). Affirming anything beyond that (like worldliness as a separate entity) is not required. The passage ends with the phrase 'and the addition, which is' suggesting the text is cut off, but the intended meaning is clear: only essence and relation need be affirmed. This lesson presents a key analysis of the different theological positions, with Razi advocating for parsimony.

The Minimal Claim: Attributes as Additional to Essence
Razi introduces the minimal claim and surveys positions on the attribute-essence relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's minimal claim that the concept of God's attributes is additional to His essence, and the different positions of theologians on the number of entities (essence, attribute, relation) involved.

Core Idea
The concept of an attribute is not the same as the concept of the essence.

This lesson begins the fifteenth issue of Razi's Arba'in, where he tackles the nature of God's attributes. Rather than diving into complex proofs, Razi first clarifies the minimal point of disagreement: that the concept of being knowing or powerful is not identical to the concept of the essence itself. He then shows how theologians have confused the issue by multiplying the entities involved.

Guided Path
1
Read Razi's opening claim
Read the first part of the passage where Razi states his minimal claim: the concept of God as knowing, powerful, and living is not the same as His essence, but something additional.
Notice he says 'al-mafhūm' – the concept, not the attribute itself.
2
Understand the role of the Mu'tazila
Razi says if the Mu'tazila agree on this minimal claim, disagreement vanishes. This shows the dispute is about the nature of the addition, not whether attributes exist.
The Mu'tazila are known for denying distinct attributes, but they might accept a conceptual distinction.
3
Grasp the concept of relation (ta'alluq)
Razi explains that knowledge involves a relation to the known. He identifies knowledge itself with this relation, which is additional to the essence.
This is Razi's own view: knowledge is the relation.
4
Survey the different positions
Razi lists the views of other theologians: some affirm three things (essence, attribute, relation), al-Baqillani's view suggests four or five, depending on how the relation is assigned.
These variations show the complexity of the issue.
5
Reflect on the unresolved confusion
Razi concludes that most earlier scholars did not investigate these distinctions, leaving the matter confused. This sets the stage for his own analysis.
Why do you think Razi highlights this confusion?

The fifteenth issue: On proving that God, exalted, is knowing and powerful. I know that we do not claim in this issue more than that the concept of His being knowing, powerful, living is not the very concept of His essence, but it is a matter other than His essence. If the Mu‘tazila assist in this extent, then agreement has been achieved and disagreement has vanished. Know that most of the theologians have been confused in determining the locus of dispute in this issue. The verification of the discourse is that we say: Everyone who knows a thing among things, it is necessary that between the knowledge and the known there occurs a specific relation and a specific addition. This addition is what the theologians express as 'relation' (ta‘alluq), so they say: Knowledge is related to the known. And according to us, knowledge is nothing but this very relation and this very specific addition, and we claim that this addition and relation are other than the essence itself. Thus knowledge with this specific addition is two things, not one thing. And a group of our companions affirmed three things: the essence, and knowledge—which is a real attribute subsisting in the essence—then they affirmed for this attribute this relation, this addition, and this attachment, so this attachment occurs between that attribute and the known. As for Qadi Abu Bakr al-Baqillani, his apparent speech indicates affirming four things: the essence, then knowledge, then knowledge necessitates being-knowing—these are three things—then here is another matter, namely that they affirmed this attachment for the being-knowing (al-‘ālimiyya) not for knowledge, or for knowledge not for the being-knowing. On this estimation, what obtains there is four things. And if they affirm the attachment for both the being-knowing and knowledge, then what obtains there is five things: the essence, knowledge, being-knowing, the attachment of knowledge, and the attachment of being-knowing. Most of those before us did not investigate these distinctions. We said: It remains a confused [matter] not refined.

Commentary
This passage introduces the fifteenth issue concerning the proof that God is knowing and powerful. Razi begins by stating his minimal claim: the concept of God as knowing, powerful, and living is not identical to the concept of His essence; it is something additional. He notes that if the Mu‘tazila agree on this, the disagreement disappears. He then observes that most theologians have been confused in defining the exact point of dispute. To clarify, he explains that whenever someone knows something, there is a specific relation between the knowledge and the known, which theologians call 'ta‘alluq' (relation). Razi declares that according to his view, knowledge is nothing but this relation itself, which is distinct from the essence. Thus knowledge together with this relation constitutes two things, not one. He then surveys the positions of his predecessors: some of his companions affirmed three things (essence, knowledge as an attribute, and the relation of knowledge to the known). Qadi Abu Bakr al-Baqillani's apparent view posits four things (essence, knowledge, being-knowing [‘ālimiyya], and then the relation either attributed to knowledge or to being-knowing), and if the relation is attributed to both, then five things. Razi notes that most earlier scholars did not delve into these distinctions, leaving the matter confused and unresolved.

Razi's Decisive Proof that Attributes are Superadded
Razi gives his claim and the decisive proof for attribute superaddition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's logical proof that God's knowledge and power are not identical to His essence but are superadded attributes.

Core Idea
We can conceive of God's essence without His attributes, proving they are distinct.

Razi begins by stating his position: he affirms the essence and the relation called 'knowledge', but not other claims. He then offers what he calls the 'decisive proof' based on the fact that knowing the existence of the Necessary Existent does not automatically entail knowing that He is knowing and powerful in a specific sense. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Read Razi's claim
Read the first part where Razi states what he affirms: the essence and the relation of knowledge. Note he does not engage with other claims.
2
Understand the decisive proof
The proof: we know that contingent things end in a Necessary Existent. But this knowledge alone does not give us knowledge that He is knowing and powerful in the specific sense. Since the known (the Necessary Existent) is other than the unknown (His specific attributes), the attributes are not identical to the essence.
This is a logical argument based on the distinction between two concepts.
3
Recognize the conclusion
Razi concludes: 'His being knowing and powerful is not identical to His essence.' This is the core claim of the proof.
4
Reflect on the significance
This proof establishes that attributes are superadded (zā'id) to the essence, a key position in Ash'ari theology.
It distinguishes Razi's view from those who say attributes are identical to the essence.

And what we claim and say is that it is necessary to affirm the essence and it is necessary to affirm the relation called 'awareness' and 'knowledge'. As for affirming other matters, that is something we do not claim nor do we engage in. The decisive proof of the existence of this otherness is that when we know that contingent things end in an existent necessary of existence by itself, it does not follow from our knowledge of this extent our knowledge that He, exalted, is knowing and powerful in the meaning we mentioned. And the known is other than what is not known. Therefore His being knowing and powerful is not identical to His essence. Then this additional concept is not a negative matter. Two aspects indicate this: The first: we know necessarily that a knower's being knowing is an expression of a specific relation between the knower and the known, and this relation and this addition are not an expression of the negation of something or the absence of something else. The second: that knowledge is not an expression of the absence of anything whatsoever, but rather an expression of the absence of ignorance. Because if by 'ignorance' is meant the absence of knowledge, then knowledge would be an expression of the absence of absence, so it would be positive. And if by it is meant belief in a thing contrary to what it is, then from the absence of this meaning the occurrence of knowledge does not necessarily follow, because one of us may be free of ignorance of a thing in the sense that he is not knowing of that thing. Commentary
This passage presents Razi's own positive claim and his decisive proof. He first states what he affirms: the essence and the relation called 'knowledge' or 'awareness'. He does not engage with further entities claimed by others. Then he gives the 'decisive proof': we know that contingent things depend on a Necessary Existent; this knowledge alone does not entail knowledge that He is knowing and powerful in the specific sense. Since we can conceive of a Necessary Existent without explicitly conceiving of His knowledge, the two concepts are distinct. He then argues that this additional concept is not merely negative (i.e., not a privation). He provides two aspects: First, we intuitively know that being knowledgeable is a relation between the knower and the known, not a negation. Second, knowledge cannot be the absence of anything, because if ignorance is the absence of knowledge, then knowledge would be the absence of that absence, which is a positive attribute. Alternatively, if ignorance is defined as false belief, then the absence of false belief does not automatically give knowledge (one can be simply unaware). Thus, knowledge is a positive, relational attribute superadded to the essence. Therefore, God's being knowing and powerful is additional to His essence.

Two Arguments that the Superadded Attribute is Positive
Razi provides two arguments to show the added attribute is not negative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two arguments from Razi that prove the superadded attribute of knowledge is a positive relational concept, not a mere negation.

Core Idea
Knowledge is a positive relation, not the absence of ignorance; that would be circular.

After establishing that the attribute is distinct from the essence, Razi preempts an objection: perhaps the added concept is just a negation or absence. In this passage, he provides two arguments to show that knowledge is a positive, relational attribute. The first argument appeals to our intuitive understanding of what it means to be knowing, and the second dismantles the notion that knowledge could be equated with the absence of ignorance.

Guided Path
1
Read both arguments
Read the passage containing the two arguments (wajhān). The first is about the nature of knowledge as a relation; the second refutes the idea that knowledge is absence of ignorance.
2
Analyze the first argument
First argument: we know intuitively that 'being knowledgeable' is a specific relation between the knower and the known, not the negation of something else.
This is a direct appeal to common sense and intuition.
3
Analyze the second argument
Second argument: knowledge cannot be defined as the absence of ignorance. If ignorance means absence of knowledge, then knowledge would be absence of absence (double negation), making it positive. If ignorance means false belief, then absence of false belief doesn't guarantee knowledge (one can be unaware).
This is a logical analysis of definitions.
4
Conclude the lesson
Razi concludes that knowledge (and power) are positive, superadded attributes. This reinforces the conclusion from the decisive proof.

Razi's Refutation of the Unknowable Attribute
Razi refutes Abu Hashim's view that the superadded attribute is unknowable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi refutes the claim that the superadded attribute is unknowable by showing that any judgment about it requires prior conception.

Core Idea
To deny knowledge of an attribute, you must first conceive it—thus contradicting yourself.

The followers of Abu Hashim conceded that there is an additional attribute but claimed it is neither known nor unknown. Razi finds this position self-defeating and provides three arguments that any meaningful discourse about attributes presupposes their conception. This lesson walks through his refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation to grasp the objection and Razi's three proofs. Notice how each proof builds on the necessity of prior conception.
Pay attention to the structure: objection, then three numbered proofs.
2
Understand the opponents' claim
The followers of Abu Hashim say the attribute is 'not known nor unknown'. Ask yourself: how can something be an object of discussion if it is not known?
This is the position Razi calls extremely weak.
3
Analyze the first proof
The first proof: assent (taṣdīq) is preceded by conception (taṣawwur). To judge that the attribute is unknown, you must have conceived it. Thus the claim contradicts itself.
This is a logical refutation: the statement leads to its own negation.
4
Examine the second and third proofs
The second proof: knowing that the essence is qualified by attributes requires conceiving those attributes. The third proof: the opponents distinguish between attributes like 'being-knowing' and 'being-powerful', which requires conception.
These strengthen the argument by showing the opponents implicitly use conception in their own reasoning.

The Third Argument: Analogy of Accidents and Substance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third Mu'tazili argument, which uses the analogy of accidents in substances to reject divine attributes due to lack of spatial distinction.

Core Idea
Without spatial location, attribute and essence become indistinguishable.

The third argument draws from physics: in created beings, an accident (like color) subsists in a substance (like a body) by occupying the same spatial location dependently. But God has no spatial location, so the distinction between attribute and qualified being collapses. This lesson analyses that analogy and its conclusion.

1
Read the third argument passage

2
Understand the analogy from creation

This is based on Aristotelian physics used in kalam.
3
Apply to God
God has no spatial location, so the basis for distinguishing attribute from essence disappears. If we still call one 'attribute' and the other 'essence', it is arbitrary and could be reversed (each could be attribute of the other), which is absurd.
This leads to the impossibility of any real distinction.

The argument focuses on the notion of 'subsistence' (قيام), a relationship between an accident and its substance in the physical world. By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila reductio against eternal attributes: that sharing in qidam forces either an infinite regress of composition or the absurd conclusion of multiple gods and interchangeable attributes.

Core Idea
Sharing eternity forces composition or polytheism — that is the Mu'tazila challenge.

Imam al-Razi presents the fourth Mu'tazilite argument against eternal attributes. The argument is a sharp dilemma: if attributes share eternity with God's essence, they must either be composite (leading to infinite regress) or identical (leading to multiple gods). This passage shows how rational reasoning can be used to critique theological positions, and it is a classic example of reductio ad absurdum in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the full translation to grasp the flow of the argument. Notice the two branches introduced by 'فإما أن يتميز... أو لا يتميز'.
The argument is a dilemma: two mutually exclusive possibilities, both leading to impossibility.
2
Identify the shared attribute and its nature
The argument begins by stating that if eternal attributes subsist in the eternal essence, they share in qidam (eternity). Qidam is described as a positive attribute (wasf thubuti) because it negates prior non-existence, which is a kind of existence. This sets up the shared basis.
The Mu'tazila consider qidam a real attribute; this is a key premise.
3
Follow the first branch: distinction leads to composition and regress
If essence and attribute are distinguished by another consideration (اعتبار), then each is composed of two aspects: what they share (qidam) and what distinguishes them. Each aspect must be eternal, leading to further composition and infinite regress (tasalsul), which is impossible.
This is a regress argument against the possibility of distinct eternal entities.
4
Follow the second branch: no distinction leads to identity and absurdity
If they are not distinguished, they become exactly similar (مثلين). Since the essence is God, the attribute must also be God — implying multiple gods. Moreover, if they are identical, it would be equally appropriate for the essence to subsist in the attribute, swapping roles, and making knowledge and power interchangeable. All are impossible.
This branch attacks the very distinctness of attributes.

The fourth argument: If the eternal attributes subsisted in the eternal essence, then the attributes and the essence would share in eternity (qidam). Qidam is a positive attribute (wasf thubuti) because it is the negation of prior non-existence, and the negation of non-existence is existence. So the essence and attributes share this positive attribute called qidam. Then either the essence is distinguished from the attribute by another consideration or not. If distinguished, then that by which they share is different from that by which they differ, so each of the essence and attributes would be composed of these two considerations—i.e., that by which they share and that by which they differ. Then each of those two considerations must also be eternal, because it is a part of the eternal. And if those two parts share in qidam, they must differ by another consideration, and then each of those two parts would be composed of two other parts, and an infinite regress (tasalsul) follows, which is impossible. But if we say that after sharing in qidam the essence and attributes are not distinguished by a specific essence (mahiyya), then the essence and attribute would be two similars. Since the essence is God, the attribute must also be God, and this is a statement of multiple gods. Also, if the essences and attributes were perfectly similar, then one subsisting in the other would be no more appropriate than the reverse; so the essence would be an attribute and the attribute an essence, knowledge would be power and power knowledge, all of which is impossible. Since the statement affirming the eternal attribute leads to these impossibilities, it must be impossible.

Commentary
This is the fourth Mu'tazilite argument, a complex reductio. It begins with the premise that if eternal attributes subsist in the eternal essence, both share the attribute of qidam (eternity), which is a positive attribute (wasf thubuti) since it negates prior non-existence. The argument then splits into two possibilities: (1) The essence and attributes are distinguished by some other consideration (اعتبار) besides qidam. In that case, each is composed of two aspects: what they share (qidam) and what distinguishes them. But each of those aspects must itself be eternal, as a part of an eternal whole. Then those two aspects also share in qidam and must be distinguished by yet another aspect, leading to an infinite regress (tasalsul), which is impossible. (2) They are not distinguished at all, meaning they are identical in essence. Then they would be like two identical things (مثلين). Since the essence is God, the attribute would also be God, implying multiple gods (تعدد الآلهة), which is absurd. Furthermore, if they are perfectly similar, then it would be equally appropriate for the essence to subsist in the attribute, leading to a reversal of roles: the attribute would become the essence and vice versa, and distinct attributes like knowledge and power would become interchangeable, all absurd. Thus, affirming distinct eternal attributes leads to impossible consequences, so it must be false. This argument attacks the very notion of multiple eternal entities sharing in qidam.

Why God's Knowledge Cannot Be Through an Attribute

In this passage, Imam al-Razi presents a powerful objection against the Ash'ari claim that God knows through an eternal attribute of knowledge. The Second Objection: Dilemma of Similarity or Difference
Second objection using dilemma of similarity or difference

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second objection that uses a dilemma to argue that divine power, if it existed, would be either like or unlike created powers, and in both cases unable to create bodies.

Core Idea
Divine power, if it exists, must be either similar or dissimilar to created powers—both options lead to impossibility.

The opponents present a second argument: if God had a power attribute, that power would be either similar to created powers or different from them. If similar, it would share the inability to create bodies. If different, it would still be unlike every specific created power, but since all created powers—despite their differences—cannot create bodies, any power different from them would also be unable to create bodies. Thus, the conclusion is that God cannot have a power attribute at all.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage for the second objection
Read from 'الشبهة الثانية' to the end of the chunk. Notice how the objection sets up a dilemma with two branches: similarity or difference.
The passage ends with 'فهذه جملة شبه المخالفين في هذه المسألة', summarizing both objections.
2
Analyze the first branch (similarity)
If the supposed divine power is similar to created powers, then it will share their property: inability to create bodies. Thus, God would not be able to create bodies—an absurd consequence.
3
Analyze the second branch (difference)
If it is different, then it differs from each specific created power. But all created powers, despite being different from each other, are still unable to create bodies. So difference does not remove the inability; it is a property of all powers. Hence, even a different power would be unable to create bodies.
The argument assumes that the inability to create bodies is not tied to any particular type of power but is a universal feature of power as such.
4
Reflect on the underlying assumption
Ask: Does the argument assume that any power must be either identical to or different from created powers? And does it assume that 'different from X' still falls under the same general category of power? The Ash'ari would question whether divine power belongs to the same genus as created power.

Razi's Response to the First Objection on Power
Razi responds to the first objection about power by questioning the necessity of the conclusion.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Divine attributes can be contingent in themselves but necessary by another.

In this passage, Razi tackles the first objection against divine power. He proposes a nuanced view: an attribute like power could be contingent in its own nature, but necessary because of the essence that possesses it. When the opponent objects that this makes the essence both receptive and agent—a contradiction—Razi questions why that is impossible, revealing a deeper philosophical principle. He then promises a fuller response later, showing his systematic approach.

Refuting Inherence: The Infinite Regress Argument
Al-Razi presents the first of two proofs against the Mu'tazilite definition of inherence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Razi uses the concept of spatiality to show that the Mu'tazilite definition of inherence leads to an infinite regress, proving it false.

Core Idea
A bad definition forces an absurd infinite chain of occurrences.

Al-Razi begins his response by dissecting the Mu'tazilite definition of inherence. He shows that their definition, if taken literally, would require an endless series of spatial occurrences for any attribute like 'being in a location,' which is impossible. This first proof uses logical reasoning to expose the definition's flaw.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its English translation carefully. Notice the structure: the doubt is stated, then rejected, then a proof is given.
The key term 'ka'iniyya' (spatiality) appears here.

Identify the Mu'tazilite definition
Find the definition of 'inherence' (hulul) as presented by the Mu'tazila. It says: 'the occurrence of a state in a location, following the occurrence of its locus therein.'

Follow the infinite regress
Trace Al-Razi's reasoning: if a body is in a location, its being-in-location (ka'iniyya) is an attribute. If inherence requires a prior occurrence of the locus in the location, then ka'iniyya would need a prior occurrence, leading to infinite regress.

Since infinite simultaneous occurrences are impossible, the definition is false. The attribute actually subsists in the body directly.
This establishes that inherence does not require the Mu'tazilite definition.

Essence and Attribute as Distinct Realities
Razi clarifies the ontological priority of essence over attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that essence and attribute are distinct realities and that priority of one over the other is rooted in their quiddities.

Core Idea
Priority between essence and attribute is real, even if its cause is unknown.

After refuting the spatial definition, Razi addresses a deeper point: the Mu'tazilite claim that there is no inherent priority between essence and attribute. Razi argues that they are distinct quiddities, and priority can be rooted in that very distinction, even if we don't know the reason.

Understand the opponent's claim

Razi's distinction between ignorance and non-existence

3
The possibility of priority in the quiddity
Razi argues that since essence and attribute are different in quiddity (māhiyya), priority could be rooted in that very difference, even if we cannot specify the precise reason.
This is a modal argument: it is possible that priority is intrinsic.
4
Conclusion: the objection collapses
Because the opponent cannot prove that priority does not exist in reality, and Razi has shown it is possible, the objection fails.
This completes Razi's response.

Eternity as a Positive Attribute: Razi's Defense

Learning Objective
