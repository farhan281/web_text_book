The Twenty-Ninth Problem: On the existence of void. Know that the meaning of void is that two bodies exist that do not touch each other and there is nothing between them that touches them. And most philosophers deny void within the world, except Abu al-Barakat al-Baghdadi, for he affirms it. And the strongest arguments given for the existence of void are two: The first argument: that when a body moves from one place to another, the destination is either said to have been empty before the arrival of this moving body, or filled. If we say it was empty, then the affirmation of void is established. And if we say it was filled, then when this body moves to it, either that body remains in it or that body moves from that place to another. If that body remains in it at the time this body moves into it, then two bodies occupying one place would be necessary, and that is impossible. And if that body moves from that place, then either it moves to the place of the body that moved into its place, or to another place. If the first, then circularity is necessary, because this one cannot move from its place unless the place is vacated of the second, and the place will only be vacated of the second if the first place is vacated of the first body. And whatever leads to circularity is impossible to exist. And if it moves to another place, the first division recurs concerning it, and it would be necessary to say that whenever a gnat or an ant moves, it would necessitate the motion of the entire world of bodies, and that is impossible. So it is established that the denial of void leads to false divisions, therefore the denial of void is false, therefore the affirmation of void is true. If it is said: According to this estimation, you would have to say that if an atom moves at the bottom of the encompassing sea, it would push the whole of that sea, or you would establish empty spaces inside the water, and that is far-fetched because water is a heavy fluid substance, and if it finds an empty place, it naturally flows into it. We say: Void inside the water of the sea is not far-fetched, because our Creator is a free agent, and it is not improbable that He prevents the bodies of water from flowing into those empty spaces, except that the objector may say: Why

Commentary
This passage is a new problem (المسألة التاسعة والعشرون) on proving void (al-khala'). Al-Razi defines void as two bodies not touching with nothing between them that touches them. He notes that most philosophers deny void within the world, except Abu al-Barakat al-Baghdadi. Then he presents the strongest argument (two sides, but only one is detailed). The argument proceeds by considering the motion of a body from one place to another. The destination is either empty or filled. If empty, void is proven. If filled, the occupying body either stays (impossible: two bodies in one place) or moves. If it moves, it either goes to the original body's previous place (leading to circularity) or to another place, leading to an infinite regress such that every motion of a gnat would require the motion of the entire universe. Since these consequences are false, the denial of void is false, so void must exist. Then an objection (فإن قيل) is raised: if this argument is accepted, then if a tiny particle moves at the bottom of the ocean, it would either push the entire ocean or create empty spaces in the water, which is unlikely because water is heavy and fluid and would naturally flow into any empty space. The answer (قلنا) appeals to divine omnipotence: the Creator is a free agent (fa'il mukhtar) who can prevent water from flowing into those empty spaces. Proof of Void from Motion and Divine Power
Al-Razi presents the first proof for void from motion and answers an objection about water and divine power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's main argument for the existence of void based on motion and how he answers an objection regarding water and divine power.

Core Idea
Denying void leads to absurd consequences like infinite regress or universal motion, so void must exist.

Al-Razi now presents the strongest argument for void, which proceeds by analyzing what happens when a body moves to a new location. He shows that without void, impossible consequences follow. He then addresses an objection about water filling empty spaces, appealing to Allah's power as a free agent.

1
Read the argument carefully
Read the Arabic passage that contains the first argument. Pay attention to the logical steps and the two possibilities considered.
The argument is a dilemma: destination empty or filled.
2
Identify the logical steps

Each step is designed to force acceptance of void.
3
Understand the objection
The objector says this argument would require either pushing the entire sea or creating voids in water, but water naturally flows to fill voids. Consider why this is a challenge.
The objection relies on natural properties of water.
4
Note al-Razi's answer
Al-Razi answers by invoking divine omnipotence: Allah, as a free agent, can prevent water from flowing. Reflect on the role of God's power in this argument.
This shows the theological dimension of the debate.

First Argument for the Existence of Void with Objection and Reply
Al-Razi presents the first argument for the void, then answers an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's first logical argument for the existence of void and how occasionalist theology addresses objections to it.

Core Idea
If void didn't exist, every movement would be impossible or absurd.

Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi begins his discussion of the void (al-khala') by presenting a rigorous logical proof based on the impossibility of co-location and circularity. This passage demonstrates the dialectical method of kalam: a dilemma is posed, its absurd consequences are drawn, and then an objection and occasionalist reply are examined. Although the argument may seem abstract, it grounds key theological commitments about divine power and creation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dilemma
The argument starts with a simple observation: when a body moves, its destination must be either empty or filled. These two options form the basis of the proof.
Note that 'empty' here means void.
2
Trace the 'filled' branch
If the destination is filled, the occupying body either remains (impossible co-location) or moves. Follow the reasoning for the moving option.
3
Identify circularity and infinite regress
If the occupying body moves to the original body's place, circularity occurs. If it moves elsewhere, an infinite regress of movements would result, requiring all bodies to move with any small motion.
4
Examine the objection and reply
The objector challenges the proof by pointing out that water naturally flows into empty spaces, making void in water impossible. Al-Razi responds by invoking God's power to prevent natural flow, then the objector proposes an alternative occasionalist explanation that would invalidate the proof.

The first proof: When a body moves from one place to another, the destination is either said to have been empty before the movement of this moving body, or it was filled. If we say it was empty, then the claim of void is established. If we say it was filled, then when this body moved to it, either that body remained in it or that body moved from that place to another. If it remained in it at the time when this body moved to it, then two bodies would necessarily occupy a single place, which is impossible. If that body moved from that place, then it is said either it moved to the place of the body that moved to its place, or to another place. If the first, circularity (al-dawar) follows, because this cannot move from its place unless the place is empty of the second, and the place will not become empty of the second unless the first place becomes empty of the first body. Whatever leads to circularity is impossible. If it moves to another place, the same division applies to it, and it would necessitate that whenever a flea or an ant moves, the entire world of bodies would have to move, which is impossible. Therefore, the denial of void leads to false consequences, so the denial of void is false, and the affirmation of void is true. If it is said: According to this reasoning, you are forced to say that if an atom moves in the bottom of the encompassing sea, the entirety of that sea would be displaced, or you would have to admit empty spaces within the water, which is far-fetched because water is a heavy liquid, and if it finds an empty space, it naturally flows into it. We say: The void within the water of the sea is not far-fetched, because we have a Creator of the world who is an agent with free will, and it is not improbable that He prevents the water bodies from flowing into those empty spaces. However, the objector may say: Why is it not allowed to say that the entire world is filled, but when a body moves from one place to another, the free agent annihilates the body that was present in the destination and creates a body in the place from which it moved? On this estimation, your proof collapses.

Commentary
This passage presents the first proof for the existence of void, structured as a dilemma. The argument begins by considering the movement of a body from one location to another. If the destination is empty before movement, then void exists. If it is filled, then either the occupying body remains (causing two bodies in one place, impossible) or it moves. If it moves, it either goes to the original body's vacated place (circularity) or to another place, which leads to an infinite regress of movements affecting all bodies, an absurdity. Hence, void must be affirmed. Then an objection is raised: If the void is accepted in water, it contradicts natural flow. Al-Razi responds that God's power can prevent natural flow, invoking occasionalism. The objector then proposes an alternative: God annihilates and creates bodies to avoid void, which would invalidate the proof. Al-Razi leaves this as a challenge.

First Proof That Souls Are Not Eternal: Unity and Multiplicity
Razi argues that if souls were eternal, they would be either one or many, both impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument that souls cannot be eternal because they cannot be either one or many in pre-eternity.

Core Idea
Souls cannot be eternal because neither unity nor multiplicity is possible without a body.

In this passage, Imam Razi presents the first of two proofs that souls are not eternal. He uses a disjunctive syllogism: if souls were eternal, they would be either one or many. Both possibilities are shown to be impossible. The argument relies on the need for a distinguisher for multiplicity and the absurdity of shared identity if souls were one.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the literal translation to grasp the overall argument.
Notice the logical structure: 'either...or' and 'both are false'.
2
Identify the two branches
The proof splits into two possibilities: souls are one or many. Mark where the author discusses each.
The first branch ends with 'هذا خلف', the second with 'امتنع اختلافها'.
3
Analyze the proof against unity
If souls were one, after attachment to bodies they would remain one, leading to absurd consequences (my soul = your soul, shared knowledge).
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
4
Analyze the proof against multiplicity
If souls were many in pre-eternity, they need a distinguisher. Essential distinguisher fails (same species); accidental distinguisher fails (no substrate before bodies).
The distinguisher (مميز) is key.
5
Conclude the proof
Both unity and multiplicity are impossible, so souls cannot be eternal. This supports their createdness (حدوث).
The conclusion is re-stated at the end.

Two Proofs That Souls Are Not Everlasting
Al-Razi gives two arguments that souls cannot be everlasting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Razi's two arguments for why souls cannot be everlasting and the logical basis for each.

Core Idea
Souls cannot be everlasting because infinite actual numbers are impossible and because they are inherently receptive to non-existence.

After establishing that souls are not pre-eternal, Al-Razi now turns to refute the view that they are everlasting. He offers two distinct proofs: one based on the impossibility of an actual infinite number of souls, and another based on the necessary receptivity to non-existence that comes from their origination.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice the structure: a general claim followed by 'two proofs' (وجهان).
2
Understand the first proof
Trace the logic: celestial cycles have no beginning → bodies originate without beginning → souls originate without beginning. If souls were permanent, they would now be infinite in number, which is impossible because any actual number is finite (can increase/decrease).
This proof assumes the kalām position that celestial cycles have no beginning, not that the world is pre-eternal.
3
Understand the second proof
Focus on the concept of quiddity (ماهية). Souls came from non-existence, so their quiddity has the property of being receptive to non-existence. This property is a necessary concomitant (لازم), so they are always susceptible to non-existence. Therefore, one cannot be certain they will never perish.
The second proof does not prove that souls will perish, only that we cannot be certain of their everlastingness.
4
Compare and reflect
Note that both proofs conclude that souls are not necessarily everlasting (أبدية), but they use different grounds: one from external (infinite accumulation) and one from internal (receptivity to non-existence).
Al-Razi leaves open the possibility that God may preserve them, but their essence does not guarantee permanence.

So if souls were remaining (baqiya), it would entail that now an infinite number of souls have come to exist, and that is impossible; because every existing number is susceptible to increase and decrease, and everything that is such is finite. The second: that these souls, since they came to exist after having been non-existent, their quiddities (mahiyyat) were receptive to non-existence, and this receptivity is among their necessary concomitants, so they must be receptive to non-existence forever. And if that is so, it is impossible to be certain that they will never cease to exist at all.

Commentary
This passage continues the argument after establishing that souls are not pre-eternal. The author now aims to prove that they are not everlasting (abadi). Two proofs are given. The first proof is based on the celestial cycles: since the heavens cycle without beginning, bodies (and hence souls) are originated without beginning. If souls were permanent (baqiya), an infinite number of souls would accumulate now—an actual infinite, which is impossible because any existing number is finite (since it can be increased or decreased). The second proof is based on the intrinsic contingency of souls: they came into existence after non-existence, so their quiddities have the property of being receptive to non-existence. This receptivity is a necessary concomitant, so they are always susceptible to non-existence; hence one cannot be certain they will never perish. Note that the author does not conclude they will perish, only that their permanence is not necessary.

Two Paths of Reasoning: Philosopher vs Theologian on Causality
Razi contrasts the philosopher's and theologian's methods regarding divine action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fundamental methodological difference between the philosopher and the theologian regarding causality and divine action.

Core Idea
One argues from cause to effect; the other from effect to cause.

In classical Islamic thought, philosophers and theologians often reached opposite conclusions about the world's eternity. This passage from Imam Razi pinpoints the exact reason: they start their reasoning from different ends. The philosopher begins with God's eternal nature and deduces that His effect must also be eternal. The theologian begins with the observable fact that acts are preceded by non-existence and concludes that God created the world. By understanding this contrast, you’ll see how starting premises shape entire theological systems.

Commentary
This passage contrasts two methods: The philosopher (faylasūf) argues that God's effectivity is eternal because He is eternal, so His effect (the world) must also be eternal. The theologian (mutakallim) argues from the fact that acts are preceded by non-existence (i.e., the world is created), so God must have brought it into existence after it did not exist. Thus, the philosopher deduces from the cause to the effect, while the theologian deduces from the effect to the cause. Razi identifies the core issue as the premise of necessity (wujūb) and contingency (imkān) in essence and attributes. This is the major point of contention between the two schools regarding the eternity of the world.
