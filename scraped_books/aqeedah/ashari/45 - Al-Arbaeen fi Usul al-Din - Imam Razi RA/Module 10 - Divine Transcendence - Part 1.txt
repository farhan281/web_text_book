The Third Division of Possibles: Immaterial Beings
Imam Razi presents the third category of possibles, the theologians' argument against it, and his logical critique.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third category of possible beings (immaterial entities), the theologians' argument against their existence, and Imam Razi's critique showing why sharing negative attributes does not imply equality of essence.

Core Idea
Sharing negative attributes does not mean sharing the same essence.

Imam Razi continues his classification of possibles by introducing a third category: beings that are neither bodies nor accidents. He notes that most philosophers accept such beings while theologians reject them. In this passage, he presents the theologians' strongest argument and then dismantles it by clarifying a logical point about negative vs. positive attributes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the third category
Read the opening definition: the third category is 'the thing that is neither a body nor subsistent in a body.' Understand that 'mutaḥayyiz' refers to a body occupying space, and 'qāʾim bil-mutaḥayyiz' refers to an accident.
This category includes immaterial souls, intellects, and angels.
2
Understand the theologians' argument
Theologians argue that if such a being existed, it would be equal to God in not being a body or accident, implying equality in essence, which would confuse the Necessary and the possible. Pay attention to the logical chain.
3
Grasp Razi's critique about negative concepts
Razi calls the argument weak. He points out that equality in a negative concept (like 'not being a body') does not necessitate equality in essence. Every two different essences share many negations; that is trivial.
Negative concepts are too broad to determine identity.
4
Reflect on the broader implication
Razi hints that even equality in positive attributes does not entail equality in essence. This opens the door for affirming immaterial beings distinct from God without compromising divine uniqueness.
This is a key move in defending philosophical theology.

Commentary
This passage introduces the third category of possible things (al-mumkināt): entities that are neither bodies (mutaḥayyiz) nor accidents (qāʾim bil-mutaḥayyiz). Imam Razi notes that most philosophers (falāsifa) affirm the existence of such entities (e.g., immaterial intellects, souls), while most theologians (mutakallimūn) deny them. He then presents the strongest argument from the theologians: if such a being existed besides God, it would share with God the negative attribute of being neither a body nor an accident. From that it would follow that they share in the complete essence (al-māhiyya), which would then entail confusion between the Necessary (al-wājib) and the possible (al-mumkin) — i.e., either the Necessary becomes possible or the possible becomes necessary, both absurd. Razi then critiques this argument, calling it weak. He explains that equality in a negative concept (mafhūm salbī) — i.e., both lack certain attributes — does not imply equality in essence. For any two different essences, they must share the negation of everything that is not them (i.e., they both are not what the other is, but that is trivial). Therefore, the premise that equality in a negative attribute implies equality in essence is false, and the argument collapses. He then begins to add a further point: even equality in positive attributes (ṣifāt thubūtiyya) does not require equality in essence — the text cuts off here, but Razi likely argues that two different essences can share some positive attributes without being identical in essence.

Three Proofs That Things Differ by Their Essences
Razi presents three arguments for intrinsic difference of essences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's three arguments demonstrating that some things differ intrinsically by their essences, not by added attributes.

Core Idea
Difference must ultimately be intrinsic; otherwise we face infinite regress or circularity.

Razi begins by establishing a foundational principle: whenever two things differ, that difference cannot go on forever through added attributes. He offers three distinct ways (wujūh) to prove that we must arrive at things that are different by themselves. Each argument targets the same conclusion from a different angle.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem
Razi asks: if two things differ, what makes them different? If it is an added attribute, that attribute itself must be different from the other. This leads to a chain.
This is the starting point of the first wajh.
2
First way: avoiding infinite regress
Follow the first argument that either the added attribute is different or not, and see why it forces an intrinsic difference.
The argument ends with the necessity of ending at two things that differ by themselves.
3
Second way: thought experiment of equal essences
Imagine two equal essences that acquire different qualities. The essences stay equal; the qualities differ by themselves.
This shows that the qualities do not derive their difference from the essences.
4
Third way: essence vs. quality
An essence and its quality must differ; that difference is due to the essence itself, not to something else.
This argument uses the relation between a substance and its attribute.
5
Conclusion
Thus we must accept that some things are essentially different, not by added attributes.
This conclusion is the foundation for the next lesson on God's essence.

... would be something additional. That additional would either be different from the other or not. If it is not different from the other, it is impossible for it to be a cause for something else becoming different from the other. If it is different from the other, then its difference must be due to something else, leading either to infinite regress or circularity, both of which are impossible. So it is established that one must end at two things that differ by themselves, not by an additional thing.

The second way: That when two essences are equal in every respect, and then each is characterized by a quality different from the quality characterizing the other, then at this difference the two essences remain as they were, equal, and the qualities as they were, different. What were equal never become different, and what were different never become equal. Hence it is evident that different things cannot be conceived unless they are different in their very essences. To suppose two things that differ by something additional to their essences is unintelligible.

The third way: If we suppose an essence characterized by a quality, the essence, qua itself, must be different from that quality; otherwise, it would not be more fitting for one to be the qualified and the other the quality rather than the reverse. If that essence is different from that quality, then the difference of that essence from that quality is due to the essence itself, not to something additional. So by these ways it is established that one must acknowledge things that differ from one another by themselves and their essences, as they are in themselves, not in virtue of a quality inherent in them.

Once you understand this, we say: Our statement 'God Most High is different from His creation by His particular essence' means what we have mentioned and summarized.

Now that we have summarized the point of dispute, we say: The proof that He Most High is different from His creation by His particular essence is that if His essence, qua that essence, were equal to other essences—and you know that things equal in every respect must have the same properties—then its being characterized by its particular quality and not by the qualities of contingent things would be a contingent matter. If that contingent matter were preferred over other possibilities without a cause, then a possible would be preferred without a determiner, which is impossible. If it were due to a cause, then the search would revert to its essence's being characterized by that cause, leading either to circularity or infinite regress, both impossible. Thus, the view that His essence (glorified and exalted) is equal to other essences in being an essence leads to these impossibilities, so that view is impossible. And with God is success.

Commentary
This passage is a composite argument: it first gives three ways (wajh) to prove that difference must ultimately be intrinsic to things, not due to added attributes, and then applies this to establish God's essential difference from creation. The first way (incomplete at the start) argues that if two things differ by something additional, that additional either is or is not different from the other; if not, it cannot cause difference; if it is, its own difference would require another cause, leading to infinite regress or circularity. Therefore, difference must be intrinsic. The second way presents a thought experiment: two equal essences each acquire different qualities; the essences remain equal while the qualities differ; thus the qualities differ by themselves, not by essence. The third way argues between essence and quality: since an essence and its quality are different, that difference is due to the essence itself, not to an added attribute. After concluding that intrinsic differences exist, Razi applies this to God: if God's essence were equal to other essences, then His particular attributes would be contingent, requiring a cause for that contingency, leading to regress or circularity; thus God's essence is unique. The argument is a reductio ad absurdum of the opponent's position.

Correction: Razi's conclusion is that we must acknowledge things that differ by themselves, but he does not claim everything differs essentially; rather, he establishes the possibility of essential difference, which he will then apply to God.

Proving God's Essential Difference from Creation
Razi applies the intrinsic difference principle to God's essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi applies the principle of intrinsic difference to prove that God's essence is uniquely different from all creation.

Core Idea
If God's essence were like other essences, His attributes would be contingent, leading to absurdity.

Having established that some differences are intrinsic, Razi now applies this to the most important case: the difference between God and His creation. He argues that if God's essence were equal to other essences in its quiddity, then His special attributes would be accidental, requiring a cause, and that cause would need another cause, leading to impossibility.

Guided Path
1
Recall the principle
We have learned that some things differ by themselves. Now we apply this to God.
This lesson builds directly on the previous one.
2
State the proposition
Razi says God is different from creation by His particular essence. This means His essence is unique.
This is the thesis being proven.
3
Follow the reductio
Assume God's essence is equal to other essences. Then His attributes would be contingent on a cause. If no cause, it's arbitrary; if a cause, it leads to regress.
The argument is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
4
Conclusion
Thus, God's essence must be essentially different from all creation.
This is the conclusion Razi draws.

Divisibility Implies Contingency: Razi's First Proof

Core Idea
If something is divisible, it depends on its parts—and dependence is the mark of contingency.

Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi begins his case for God's transcendence by tackling the notion that God could be a body. His first argument is a crisp syllogism: every body is divisible, and everything divisible is contingent; therefore, a necessary being cannot be a body. The second premise is unpacked: a divisible thing is composite, a composite depends on its parts, and each part is other than the whole. This dependence on something else is exactly what makes it contingent.

God is necessary (wajib al-wujud), so He cannot be contingent. Therefore, He cannot be divisible and hence cannot be a body. That's the conclusion Razi wants.
This proof is just one of several for divine incorporeality.

The Dilemma of Two Sides: Equality in Quiddity
Imam Razi presents the first case of his dilemma: equal sides lead to composition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first part of Imam Razi's argument: if Allah were perceptible by senses, He would have two sides, and if those sides are equal in quiddity, composition would follow, which is impossible.

Core Idea
If two sides are equal, then the infinite could become finite and composition becomes unavoidable.

Imam Razi begins his proof against divine location by considering a hypothetical body with two sides: one finite and one infinite. He proposes two possibilities: either these sides are equal in quiddity or they are not. In this lesson, we examine the first case — equality — and see how it leads to an impossible consequence.

1

Understand the principle of equality

3

If the infinite side can become finite and vice versa, then composition and separation become possible for Allah's essence, requiring a creator. That is impossible.

The Second Case: Sides Differ in Quiddity
Imam Razi presents the second case of his dilemma: differing quiddities lead to composition via simple parts.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Different quiddities force decomposition into simple parts, requiring a composer.

After showing the impossibility of equal sides, Imam Razi considers the second possibility: that the two sides differ in quiddity. He argues that any compound composed of different parts must ultimately consist of simple, indivisible parts. These simple parts, by their nature, must touch other parts, making separation and composition possible. This again requires a composer, which is impossible for Allah.

Conclusion: Finitude Implies Contingency and Not Being Above All
Imam Razi presents two final arguments: finitude implies contingency and not being above all.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi concludes that if Allah were perceptible by senses (and thus finite), He would be contingent and not above all existents, which is impossible.

Core Idea
Finitude demands a specifier; being finite also means not being above all.

Having established that perceptibility by senses implies finitude, Imam Razi now draws two further consequences. First, anything finite must be contingent because its specific measure requires a specifier. Second, a finite being cannot be above all existents, as there would be empty spaces above it. Both conclusions contradict the nature of Allah, proving that He cannot be perceptible by senses.

Guided Path
1

3
Understand the 'above all' argument
If it is finite, there are empty spaces above it, so it is not above all existents. This contradicts the opponent who claims Allah is above everything.
4
Realize the final conclusion
Both consequences are impossible for Allah, so the premise that He is perceptible by senses is false.

A disjunctive proof (hujja) showing the absurdity of Allah being in a place.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Allah's transcendence requires that He is not confined to any place or direction, and how the impossibility of both leaving and not leaving a place proves this.

Core Idea
If Allah were in a place, He would either be subject to change or be deficient — both impossible.

Imam Razi presents a crisp logical argument against the notion that Allah occupies a place. By examining the two possibilities that follow from such a claim — either Allah could leave that place or not — he shows both lead to contradictions with divine perfection. This argument reinforces the Islamic tenet of Allah's transcendence (tanzih) above physical constraints.

The Dilemma of Place: Existent or Non-Existent
Imam Razi presents a dilemma: if the place is existent, it implies pre-eternity of bodies and dependency; if non-existent, it cannot contain an existent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why attributing a place to Allah leads to absurd consequences, whether that place is considered existent or non-existent.

Core Idea
If Allah were in a place, the place would either rival Him in eternity or be unable to contain Him.

Imam Razi begins by presenting a powerful dilemma. If the opponent claims Allah is in a place, that place must either be something that exists or something that does not. Both options lead to conclusions that are impossible for Allah. This lesson walks through each possibility and shows why they fail.

Read the passage

3

If the place is non-existent (pure negation), it has no specificity and cannot contain an existent.

Commentary
This passage is a proof (hujja) with an objection and answer. Imam Razi presents the fourth argument: if Allah were in a place, that place is either existent or non-existent. If it is existent, then it would coexist with Allah from eternity, making it an eternal body—this implies belief in the pre-eternity of bodies, which is false. Moreover, the place does not need the occupant, but Allah would need the place (according to the opponent), making Allah contingent and place necessary—absurd. If the place is nonexistent (pure negation), then it has no specification and cannot contain an existent. Thus both options fail.

Imam Razi answers: The body's being in place means that it has magnitude and parts that are connected and contiguous; pointing to its sides is possible. If the opponent means this by 'Allah is in a place', then they are explicitly saying Allah has parts, which returns to the first problem (composition). So the objection is neutralized.

Refutation of the First Doubt: The Division of Existents
Razi refutes a false dichotomy about existents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi refutes the claim that existents are limited to only two relations (interpenetration or separation by direction) by exposing the logical fallacy of circular reasoning.

Core Idea
A false dichotomy collapses when a third possibility remains open.

In this passage, Imam Razi responds to the first doubt raised by opponents who attempt to limit the possible relations between any two existents to only two categories: interpenetration or separation by direction. He reveals the logical flaw in their reasoning by showing that their argument either assumes the very point in question without proof or falls into circularity, thus rendering it invalid.

Refutation of the Second Doubt: Essence vs. Attribute
Razi argues that a body's specification to direction may be due to its specific essence, not a shared property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that a body's specification to direction is due to its essence, not a separate attribute, thereby refuting the analogy between Allah and bodies.

Core Idea
A body's direction is essential, not attributable; the analogy to Allah fails.

Razi continues his refutation of doubts about divine transcendence. The second doubt argues that since Allah is self-subsistent like bodies, He must also occupy direction. Razi responds by questioning the premise: perhaps a body's direction is inherent to its essence, not a separate attribute. This passage presents that argument using an infinite regress to show that specification cannot be endlessly attributed.
