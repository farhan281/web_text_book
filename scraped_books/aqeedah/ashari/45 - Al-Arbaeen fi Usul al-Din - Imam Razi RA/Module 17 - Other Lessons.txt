The Sixth Argument: Ignorance of God Makes Command Impossible
Imam Razi presents a dilemma arguing that commanding knowledge of God is impossible for the ignorant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Razi's dilemma that a command to know God cannot be directed at the ignorant, and the Ash'ari resolution of affirming divine sovereignty.

Core Idea
A command to know God is impossible if the addressee is ignorant of God.

Imam Razi presents a powerful dilemma: if God commands knowledge of Himself, the addressee must either already know Him or not. Both options lead to impossibility. This argument cuts to the heart of the debate on whether God can oblige what is unbearable. After presenting his case, Razi offers a striking resolution: abandon all stratagems and submit to God's absolute will.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dilemma
Notice the opening: the command to know God is either directed at one who already knows God or one who does not. These are the only two possibilities.
This is a classic logical division (taqsim) used in kalam.
2
Examine the first horn
If the command is for the knower, then it commands acquiring what is already possessed—this is impossible (commanding to attain the attained).
3
Examine the second horn
If the command is for the ignorant, then the person is ignorant of the essence and attributes, so they cannot even understand the command itself, making it an impossible obligation.
This is the core of the argument: ignorance precludes understanding the command.
4
Follow the conclusion and objection
Razi concludes that the problem of obliging the unbearable is necessary for all. Then consider the objection: what stratagem can repel it?
The objection is a common attempt to escape the logical force.
5
Learn the Ash'ari answer
The answer is to abandon all stratagems and confess God's absolute sovereignty: He does as He wills, and He is not questioned about His actions.
This reaffirms divine omnipotence and the limits of human reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is the sixth argument against the possibility of an obligation to know God. The author divides the addressee of the command into two possibilities: either one who already knows God or one who does not. If the former, the command is to attain what is already present, which is impossible. If the latter, the command is directed to an ignorant person, and since the ignorant of the essence is also ignorant of the attributes, such a person cannot know the command itself at the moment it is given. Thus it is a clear case of obligation of what is unbearable (تكليف ما لا يطاق). After presenting these six arguments, the author concludes that this doctrine is necessary for all (i.e., for all theologians). He then addresses a hypothetical objection: 'What stratagem (حيلة) can we use to repel this problem?' The answer is to abandon any stratagem and humbly affirm God's absolute sovereignty: He does what He wills and judges what He desires, and He is not questioned about His actions while all others are. This final point underscores the Ash'ari tenet of divine omnipotence and justice beyond human analogy.

The Third Proof: God Does Not Act for Purposes
Razi argues that God can directly achieve all purposes, so intermediaries are futile.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's third proof that God's actions cannot be motivated by external purposes because He can achieve all outcomes directly.

Core Idea
God's power makes intermediaries unnecessary; using them would be futile.

This lesson presents the third of Razi's proofs against attributing purposes to God's actions. Razi reduces all possible purposes to two categories: acquiring pleasure or repelling pain. He then argues that God, being omnipotent, can directly bring about these outcomes without any means. Using intermediaries would either imply benefit for God or be futile, both impossible for Allah. This proof reinforces the Ash'ari position that God's actions are not subject to external motivations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of purposes
Read the first part of the passage where Razi states that all purposes reduce to two: obtaining pleasure/joy and repelling pain/sorrow. Notice that these cover all possible motivations.
This reduction is a key move in the argument.
2
Understand the premise: God can achieve them directly
Razi asserts that God is capable of obtaining these two desired outcomes directly, without any intermediaries. Reflect on why this premise is crucial.
Omnipotence is the foundation.
3
Analyze the argument about intermediaries and futility
Razi argues that if someone can achieve something directly, using intermediaries is either easier or futile. Since nothing is easier for God, using intermediaries would be futile. Futility is impossible for God.
The logical chain: direct ability → intermediaries unnecessary → futility → impossibility.
4
Conclude that God's actions are not motivated by purposes
The conclusion: Therefore, God's actions and judgments cannot be caused by any purposes or causes. This is the core of the third proof.
This proof does not deny wisdom in God's actions, only external motivation.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof, which focuses on the nature of purposes (aghrad). The author reduces all possible purposes to two: acquiring pleasure or repelling pain. God, being omnipotent, can directly create pleasure or remove pain without any intermediate means. If He uses means, either those means make it easier for Him (which is impossible because God has no ease or difficulty), or they are unnecessary, making the act futile. Futility (abath) is impossible for God. Therefore, God's actions cannot be motivated by external purposes. This proof relies on the premise that God's power is unlimited and that He does not need to use means to achieve ends; if He used means, it would imply that He benefits from them, which is not the case.

Philosophers' Proof for Non-Corporeal Soul
Al-Razi presents a philosophical proof for the soul's incorporeality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' argument that the soul must be non-corporeal because it is the subject of indivisible knowledge.

Core Idea
Indivisible knowledge demands an indivisible subject — the soul cannot be a body.

Al-Razi presents a key philosophical proof that uses the existence of indivisible objects of knowledge to argue that the soul itself is not a body nor a property of a body. The argument proceeds through two rigorous premises, each supported by careful reasoning. Follow each step to see how indivisibility of knowledge leads to the soul's incorporeality.

1
Read the full passage

Notice that Al-Razi is presenting the philosophers' view, not necessarily his own.
2

The argument has a main claim (the soul is non-corporeal) supported by two premises. Identify each premise and how they connect.
The first premise is about indivisible objects; the second is about knowledge of them being indivisible.
3
Examine the first premise
Study the two proofs given for the existence of indivisible objects of knowledge: knowing God's essence/oneness, and the simple/composite distinction.
This establishes that such knowledge exists.

If no additional thing occurs at all, then it would follow that that aggregate is not knowledge of that known thing. And knowledge of a thing is not knowledge of that thing? This is a contradiction. And if an additional state (ḥāla zāʾida) occurs because of that gathering, then if that additional state is subject to division, the aforementioned division applies to it, and an infinite regress (tasalsul) would follow.

Commentary
This passage presents the philosophers' main proof that the soul is neither a body nor corporeal. The argument begins with the claim that there exist indivisible objects of knowledge (maʿlūmāt ghayr munqasima). From this, it follows that the knowledge (ʿilm) of them is indivisible, and therefore the subject (al-mawṣūf) that possesses that knowledge must also be indivisible. Since anything occupying space (mutaḥayyiz) is divisible, this subject cannot occupy space nor be an accident (ḥāl) in something that occupies space. The argument then proceeds to establish two premises. The first premise — that there are indivisible objects of knowledge — is supported by two proofs: (1) We know God's essence, which is indivisible, and we know oneness (waḥda), which is also indivisible. (2) Any known thing is either simple or composite; if simple, the conclusion is immediate; if composite, it is composed of simples, and knowledge of the composite depends on prior knowledge of simples, so we must know simples. The second premise — that knowledge of an indivisible object is itself indivisible — is argued by considering what would happen if that knowledge were divisible. If a part of that knowledge were knowledge of the entire object, the part would equal the whole in essence, which is impossible. If it were knowledge of a part of the object, then the object would be divisible, contradicting the assumption. If it were knowledge of neither the object nor its parts, then when the parts come together, either an additional state arises or not. If no additional state arises, the aggregate cannot be knowledge of the object, which is absurd. If an additional state arises, that state itself must be either divisible or indivisible; if divisible, the same division arguments apply, leading to an infinite regress. The passage ends mid-argument, but the intended conclusion is that knowledge must be indivisible, thus the knower (the soul) must be non-corporeal.

Commentary
This passage is a short deductive proof. It takes the premise already established—that Ali is the most knowledgeable person after the Prophet—and derives the conclusion that he is therefore the best person after the Prophet. The reasoning is based on two Qur'anic verses: (1) In Surah Az-Zumar (39:9), Allah rhetorically asks whether those who know are equal to those who do not know, implying they are not; (2) In Surah Al-Mujadila (58:11), Allah promises to raise the believers and those given knowledge in ranks, showing that knowledge is a means of elevation. If Ali surpasses all others in knowledge, he must be the most elevated in rank, i.e., the best.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some might think that other criteria like piety or seniority are the sole measure of superiority.
Correction: This argument specifically uses knowledge as the criterion, supported by Qur'anic verses that elevate knowledge. It does not deny other criteria but focuses on knowledge.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
If knowledge determines your rank with Allah, are you actively seeking the knowledge that truly matters—or are you distracted by worldly pursuits? What specific step will you take this week to increase your Islamic knowledge?
?
Do you truly believe that a knowledgeable person is not equal to an ignorant one? How does that belief shape who you choose as a teacher, a leader, or a friend? Is there anyone in your life from whom you hesitate to learn due to pride?
?
On the Day of Judgment, you will be raised in degrees based on your knowledge. What have you done today that will elevate your rank tomorrow? Are you content with your current level, or do you feel a pang of urgency to learn more?
