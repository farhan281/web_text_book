Third Quranic Proof: He is the First and the Last
Razi argues from 'the First and the Last' for annihilation and recreation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi uses the divine attributes 'the First' and 'the Last' to argue for the annihilation and subsequent recreation of all creatures.

Core Idea
Being the Last means God is the only existent after annihilation.

Razi now turns to a profound argument based on two of God's names: al-Awwal (the First) and al-Akhir (the Last). He interprets these as meaning that God alone existed in pre-eternity and will alone exist in post-eternity. This implies that at some point all creatures must be annihilated so that God is the only existent. Then, because reward and punishment are eternal, He must recreate them. This lesson examines the reasoning.

If God is the Last, that means in the end nothing else exists with Him. This requires the annihilation of all creatures.
The argument does not specify a timeline; it is a logical implication.
3
See the connection to recreation
Razi then notes that reward and punishment are eternal, so creatures must be recreated after annihilation to experience them.
This combines the attribute of being Last with the eternity of the afterlife.

First Quranic Proof for the Annihilation of Substances
Razi presents a Quranic proof for annihilation and responds to an objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Razi uses the Quranic verse 'Everything is perishing except His Face' to argue that all substances are annihilated, and how he rebuts the objection that 'perishing' only means loss of benefit.

Core Idea
The Quran proves substances are annihilated, not merely transformed.

This lesson presents Imam al-Razi's first proof for the annihilation of all created substances, drawn from the Quran. He interprets the verse 'Everything is perishing except His Face' as indicating absolute non-existence, not just change. He then anticipates and refutes an alternative interpretation that reduces 'perishing' to loss of benefit. By the end, you will see why Razi insists on complete annihilation.

Guided Path

Read the Arabic passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its translation to get the flow of the argument. Note the structure: proof, objection, counter-response.
The passage is dense; read twice.
2
Identify the main claim and its Quranic basis
Razi begins with the Quranic verse from Surah al-Qasas. He defines 'perishing' as non-existence and argues that the general term 'everything' includes all essences (dhawat).
3
Understand the objection: redefining 'perishing'
The opponents claim that 'perishing' means becoming useless, not ceasing to exist. They argue that the separation of parts suffices for perishing.
4
Follow Razi's rebuttal
Razi argues that even if composites become useless, the individual parts remain useful for future compositions and as evidence for the Creator. Therefore, they cannot be said to have perished unless annihilated.
This establishes that the verse requires absolute annihilation.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
The proof concludes that substances (parts) must be annihilated. Ask yourself why this matters for Islamic theology.
It supports the doctrine that Allah alone is eternal.

Razi's Refutation: Identity Requires Attributes
Imam Razi refutes the opponent's argument by analyzing the nature of personal identity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Razi's critique that even if parts are preserved, the loss of attributes prevents the same person from returning, making the opponent's proposal ineffective.

Core Idea
Identity depends on attributes, not just bare parts.

Imam Razi now examines the opponent's claim more deeply. He shows that the problem of identity remains even if parts are not annihilated, because what makes a person 'that person' is not just the parts but the attributes that subsist in them. When parts separate, attributes perish, so a new combination would have different attributes, not the same person.

3
See how attributes are essential
Attributes like life and knowledge are part of the essence (māhiyya) of the person. When parts scatter, these attributes perish.
4
Conclude the refutation

The Reality of Death and Two Views on the Soul
Imam Razi defines death and presents two views on the soul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Razi's definition of death as the soul's cessation of management due to loss of bodily preparedness, and the two main views on the nature of the soul.

Core Idea
Death is not the body's end but the soul's departure when the body loses readiness.

Imam Razi begins the discussion of sam‘iyyat by examining death. He clarifies that whether death means the end of a person depends on what we think a person is. This passage lays out his definition of death and then introduces two competing accounts of the soul's nature, setting the stage for deeper theological debate.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic text carefully along with the literal English translation. Notice how Razi defines death in a conditional way: it happens when the body loses its preparedness for the soul's action.
The key phrase is 'هذا الترك والإعراض هو الموت' – the abandonment and turning away is death.
2
Analyze the definition of death
Focus on the definition: death is the soul's cutting off of management (tadbir) and action (tasarruf) when the body's preparedness (isti‘dad) ceases. This is a functional, not merely biological, definition.
Note that Razi asserts the body undoubtedly dies, but the human may or may not die depending on identity.

Identify the two views on the soul
After defining death, Razi presents two views among those who say the human is not the body. The first: the soul is a simple substance (jawhar mujarrad). The second: the soul is a luminous, transparent body (jism nurani shaffaf).
Both views agree the soul survives death, but differ on its nature.
4
Reflect on the implications
Consider how each view affects the understanding of the afterlife. Razi mentions that the luminous body returns to spheres/lights (for the blessed) or abyss/darkness (for the wretched).
This foreshadows discussions on reward and punishment.

Commentary
This passage demonstrates how Quranic revelation itself supports the three premises of rational possibility. Razi asserts that whenever God mentions resurrection in the Qur'an, He bases it on these same three premises: the possibility of the act, His power, and His knowledge. He then analyzes three verses as examples. The first verse from Surat An-Naml (27:64-65): the phrase 'who originates creation, then restores it' indicates the intrinsic possibility and God's power; the phrase 'none knows the unseen except Allah' indicates God's exclusive knowledge. The third verse from Surat Ar-Rum (30:27): 'He originates creation, then restores it' indicates possibility and power; 'the Almighty' indicates power; 'the Wise' indicates knowledge. Razi concludes by mentioning his own commentary where he explains that every resurrection verse contains these three premises.

Commentary
This passage defines and analyzes the Quranic proof for the resurrection (ma'ad). Razi states that whenever Allah mentions the issue of resurrection in the Quran, He builds its proof upon three premises. These three premises are: (1) the intrinsic possibility of return (i.e., that recreating the body is possible in itself), (2) divine power to bring about that possible thing, and (3) divine knowledge to restore the exact same body. Razi then demonstrates that every specific verse on resurrection contains these three premises. He gives three examples: from Surah al-Naml, Ya Sin, and al-Rum. For each verse, he points out which phrases indicate which premises. For instance, in al-Naml, the phrase 'Is He who originates creation then brings it back' indicates both the possibility of return (since it asks about the one who can do it) and divine power (since He is able to originate, He is able to bring back). The phrase 'None knows the unseen except Allah' indicates divine knowledge. Similarly for the other verses. Razi concludes by noting that he has already explained in his own tafsir that every resurrection verse contains these three premises.

The First Philosophical Objection: Recreating the Non-Existent
Razi presents the first philosophical objection, a syllogism with two premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure of the first philosophical objection against bodily resurrection and its two premises.

Core Idea
Bodily resurrection seems to require bringing back what no longer exists.

Philosophers who deny bodily resurrection often base their argument on a logical puzzle: resurrecting a body that has completely ceased to exist. Imam Razi begins by presenting this objection, which rests on two premises. Let us examine how the objection is constructed, so we can later understand the counterarguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection's conclusion

2
Understand the first premise
The first premise says that gathering bodies (hashr al-ajsad) cannot happen without affirming the validity of recreating the non-existent. Notice that Razi explains this premise in two scenarios: annihilation or disassembly.
Whether God annihilates or disassembles, in either case something that was non-existent must be brought back.
3
Recognize the second premise
The second premise claims that recreating the non-existent is impossible. Razi mentions that he has already discussed the sophisms supporting this claim.
This premise is the crux of the objection.
4
Trace the syllogism
Combine the premises: if P (resurrection) requires Q (recreating non-existent), and Q is impossible, then P is impossible. This is a standard logical form.
The objection is a valid argument; its soundness depends on the second premise.

If a body's parts are eaten by another, both cannot be resurrected with the same matter.

This lesson presents the second objection from Imam Razi's al-Arba'in. It raises a logical difficulty: if a human body is consumed by another, how can both individuals be resurrected with their original material parts? This objection targets the physical continuity needed for bodily identity.

Islamic theologians later answered this objection.

The Third Objection: Identity and Justice in Bodily Resurrection
Razi presents a dilemma about which body parts are restored.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third objection against bodily resurrection based on the dilemma of which body parts are restored and the resulting moral absurdities.

Core Idea
If God restores all body parts, justice becomes impossible.

Imam Razi presents a powerful dilemma against bodily resurrection. If God restores only the parts at death, then the blind and amputee are resurrected defective. If He restores all parts from the entire life, then rewarding or punishing a person becomes unjust. This passage forces us to think about the nature of identity and divine justice.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage carefully, then the literal translation. Notice the structure of the dilemma: two options are presented, each followed by a reason for its invalidity.
The passage uses conditional phrasing (فإما أن... أو) to set up the two possibilities.
2

Razi states that God either restores (1) the parts exactly as they were at the moment of death, or (2) all the parts that ever belonged to the person during his lifetime. Write down these two options in your own words.

On resurrection, God will add other parts to these original particles to form a complete body, and reward or punishment will be given to this enduring self. This solves the identity problem: the same person is rewarded or punished. Razi notes that this explanation works both for the view that the human is a subtle body and for the view that the human is a separate substance (jism mujarrad).

Commentary
This passage presents an objection and its answer. The objection accuses theologians of inconsistency: they freely interpret (ta'wil) scriptural verses that describe God as having a body, but they refuse to interpret verses that describe the resurrection as bodily. Hence no probability remains, so ta'wil is blocked. Thus the theologians' position is not arbitrary but based on authoritative transmission.

Core Idea
Allah's mercy demands that creation be for ease, not pain or vanity.

Razi presents a rational argument for the purpose of creation. He considers three possibilities: ease, hardship, or neither. Through reason and divine attributes, he demonstrates that creation must be for ease, setting the stage for proving bodily resurrection.

Commentary
This passage presents the second rational proof for bodily resurrection. Razi begins by analyzing the purpose of creation: Allah creates creatures either for ease (raha), for hardship (taʿab wal alm), or for neither. It is not appropriate for a generous, merciful, self-sufficient Creator to create for hardship. Since the purpose of creation is pleasure and ease, and that purpose is not fulfilled in this world, there must be a next world (al-dār al-ākhira) where it is fulfilled. Razi notes that this argument is persuasive (iqnāʿiyya) and that the main reliance is on what was presented earlier (the first proof and the transmitted evidence). This concludes the discussion on bodily resurrection.

He gives examples: eating pleasure is the removal of hunger pain – the first bite is best because hunger is greatest; intercourse pleasure is the removal of the discomfort of accumulated semen. All pleasures in this world are of this nature. Moreover, even if some minimal corporeal pleasure existed, it is outweighed by pain. It would be unwise for God to subject animals to a sea of pains for a mere speck of pleasure. Since the purpose of creation is pleasure and ease, and that purpose is not fulfilled in this world, there must be a next world (al-dār al-ākhira) where it is fulfilled. Razi notes that this argument is persuasive (iqnāʿiyya) and that the main reliance is on what was presented earlier (the first proof and the transmitted evidence). This concludes the discussion on bodily resurrection.

In this passage, Imam Razi builds on his earlier discussion to prove that the human soul, though originated, cannot be eternal (azali). Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some might think this view denies bodily resurrection or that all souls are immortal by nature.
Correction: This view does not deny bodily resurrection; it focuses on the soul's survival based on its knowledge. Also, it teaches that not all souls are immortal; ignorant souls perish.

Key Takeaway
The fate of the soul after death depends on its knowledge of God: virtuous souls survive, ignorant souls perish.
