Classification of Schools on the Obligation of the Imam
Razi classifies schools on imamate obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the different classifications of Islamic schools regarding the obligation of appointing an Imam, including those who base it on revelation vs. reason, and the various subgroups.

Core Idea
The obligation of imamate is divided by source (revelation/reason) and subject (people/God).

Imam al-Razi begins the discussion of imamate by classifying the major schools on the fundamental question: Is appointing an Imam obligatory, and how is that obligation known? He presents a detailed taxonomy that maps the positions of the Ash'aris, Mu'tazila, Zaydis, and various Shi'a groups. This lesson guides you through that classification.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the hierarchical structure: first division, then sub-divisions.
2
Identify the main branches
The passage first distinguishes those who say appointment is obligatory vs non-obligatory, then focuses on the obligatory group. Within that, identify the two main groups: those who base obligation on revelation and those who base it on reason.
3
Trace the subdivisions
Among the reason-based group, note two further splits: obligation on the people themselves vs. obligation on God. Then among the 'obligation on God' group, three Shi'a sub-schools are listed.
Pay attention to the specific names: Ash'aris, Mu'tazila, Zaydis, Jahiz, etc.

The thirty-ninth question concerning imamate. It contains chapters. The first chapter: On the obligation of the Imam. People have differed concerning it, and the summary of the schools therein is that the appointment of an Imam is either said to be obligatory or not obligatory. As for those who say it is obligatory, they are two groups: one of them: those who say his appointment is obligatory and the way to know this obligation is revelation, not reason, and this is the saying of our companions (Ash'aris) and most of the Mu'tazila and the Zaydis. The second: those who say the way to know this obligation is reason. Then these are two groups: among them are those who say that it is obligatory by reason upon the people to appoint a leader for themselves, because the appointment of this leader entails repelling harm from oneself, and repelling harm from oneself is obligatory by reason. This is the saying of Abu al-Husayn al-Basri from the Mu'tazila, and among their early figures the saying of al-Jahiz, Abu al-Husayn al-Khayyat, and Abu al-Qasim al-Ka'bi. And among them are those who say: rather it is obligatory upon God to appoint an Imam for the people. These are two groups: the Mulahida (heretics) and the Isma'ilis said: there is no way to know God except through the teaching of the Messenger and the Imam, so it is obligatory upon God the Exalted that He not leave the world empty of an infallible one, so that that infallible one guides the people to knowledge of God the Exalted. The Ithna-asharis said: there is no need in knowing God the Exalted for an infallible one, but it is obligatory upon God the Exalted to appoint the infallible Imam to be a grace (lutf) in performing the intellectual duties and avoiding intellectual abominations, and also to be a preserver of the religion from increase and decrease. And some early Shi'ites said that it is obligatory upon God to appoint an Imam to teach them the conditions of foods and medicines, and to teach them deadly poisons, and to make them know crafts and industries, and to protect them from calamities and fears. This is the detail of the saying of those who say: the appointment of an Imam is obligatory.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the various schools that hold the appointment of an Imam to be obligatory. Razi begins by stating the subject: the thirty-ninth question on imamate, with the first chapter on the obligation of the Imam. He frames the discussion by noting that people differ, and the positions can be summarized as either saying appointment is obligatory or not. He then focuses on those who say it is obligatory, dividing them into two main groups. The first group holds that the obligation is known through revelation (sam') alone, not reason. This is the position of the Ash'aris (his own school), most Mu'tazila, and the Zaydis. The second group holds that the obligation is known through reason. He further subdivides this group. One subgroup says it is obligatory by reason upon the people to appoint a leader, because appointment repels harm from oneself, and repelling harm is rationally obligatory. He attributes this view to Abu al-Husayn al-Basri and earlier Mu'tazila like al-Jahiz, al-Khayyat, and al-Ka'bi. Another subgroup says it is obligatory upon God to appoint an Imam for the people. This subgroup is further divided. The Mulahida and Isma'ilis claim that knowledge of God is impossible without the teaching of a messenger or Imam, so God must provide an infallible guide. The Ithna-asharis (Twelver Shi'a) hold that while knowledge of God does not require an infallible, God must appoint an infallible Imam as a grace (lutf) to help people perform rational duties and avoid rational evils, and to preserve the religion. Finally, some early Shi'ites held that God must appoint an Imam to teach practical knowledge like foods, medicines, crafts, and protection from harms. Razi concludes this section by stating that this completes the detail of those who say the Imam must be appointed.

Three Positions Denying Absolute Obligation of Imamate
Razi enumerates three groups that reject the absolute obligation of Imamate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the nuanced positions of three groups that deny the absolute obligation of Imamate, including their conditional reasoning based on societal conditions.

Core Idea
Imamate's obligation depends on context — not everyone agrees it's always required.

Razi now turns to those who challenge the obligation of Imamate. He carefully records their arguments, showing that even when denying obligation, scholars did so based on conditions like justice or social stability. This passage presents three distinct positions, each with its own logic.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage fully
Read the Arabic passage once to get an overview of the three groups. Then read the translation to confirm your understanding.
Notice that Razi uses enumeration (الأولى, الثانية, الثالثة).
2
Analyze al-Asamm's view
The first group (al-Asamm) makes obligation conditional on fitna. Under fitna, it's obligatory; under security and justice, it's not. Think about why he might have argued this way.
3

The second group reverses the condition: during fitna, appointment is not obligatory (may increase strife), but during justice, it is obligatory. Compare this to al-Asamm.
Note the reasoning that appointment could cause rebellion.

Analyze the Kharijite position
Most Kharijites deny obligation at any time; it is purely permissible. This is the most extreme position. Consider how this differs from the others.
5
Reflect on the classification

This shows Razi's thoroughness as a scholar.

Commentary
This passage classifies the positions of those who deny the absolute obligation of Imamate, presenting three distinct groups. The first group is associated with al-Asamm, who makes the obligation conditional: if there is fitna (strife), appointing an Imam becomes obligatory; but if there is security and justice, it is not obligatory. Proof that God is Not Obliged to Appoint an Imam
Razi constructs a dilemma to disprove divine obligation of imam appointment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational argument (dilemma) that God is not obligated to appoint an imam, and the refutation of the Ismaili and Ithna Ashari positions.

Core Idea
If God were obliged to appoint an imam, He would have sent one accessible to all — but He didn’t, so He is not obliged.

The author, Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, addresses a key sectarian dispute regarding the imamate. The Ismailis and Ithna Ashari claim that God must appoint an imam for guidance. Razi presents a logical dilemma: either the imam is accessible and beneficial, or not. The first is contrary to observable reality, and the second is useless. This passage establishes the Sunni position that the imamate is not a divine obligation but a communal necessity.

The passage is a proof (dalil) against divine obligation.

These are labeled as 'القسم الأول' and 'القسم الثاني'.

The first division is false because no such accessible imam exists empirically. The second is false because an inaccessible imam cannot provide benefit.
The refutation relies on observation and logical necessity.

He now uses a powerful parity argument: if completeness justifies obligation, then many other things—like infallible judges, armies, and deputies—would also be obligatory. But his opponents do not claim that God must appoint such figures, so something must be wrong with the original reasoning. Al-Razi then considers two possible explanations for why these others are not required and shows that both could apply equally to the Imam.

The Basis of Obligation: Preponderant Opinion (Zann) in Determining Lutf
Al-Razi responds that the Mu'tazila's acceptance of knowledge as lutf is based on preponderant opinion, not certainty.

Fifth Objection: Lutf in Shar'iyyat vs. 'Aqliyyat and the Problem of Internal Motives
Razi argues that an imam cannot be a lutf for rational duties because it cannot affect internal motives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's fifth objection against the Twelver claim that appointing an imam is a lutf, and why he argues that the imam cannot affect the internal motives required for rational obligations.

Core Idea
Even if an imam is a lutf, he cannot change what is inside your heart.

In this objection, Imam Razi challenges the Twelver argument that appointing an imam is a divine grace (lutf) that facilitates obedience. He presents a dilemma: either the imam is a lutf for legal duties or for rational duties. Razi systematically dismantles both possibilities, showing that the imam's influence on internal motives is neither necessary nor provable.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dilemma
Razi forces the opponent to choose between two domains: legal (shar'iyyat) or rational ('aqliyyat). Pay attention to how this frames the entire objection.
The dilemma is exhaustive: the imam's lutf must apply to one of these two categories.
2
Analyze the legal branch
If the imam is a lutf for legal duties, then since legal duties can be absent in certain times, the imam can also be absent. This undermines the necessity of an imam.
Razi uses the opponent's own principle of lutf against them.
3
Examine the rational branch
If the imam is a lutf for rational duties, two sub-possibilities arise: either the imam helps bring actions into existence regardless of motive (useless), or he helps motivate the person to act for the right reason.
Razi quickly dismisses the first sub-possibility as irrelevant.
4
Grasp the psychological argument
Razi argues that external inducements like fear or praise may backfire, causing aversion to good acts and desire for forbidden things. Thus, the imam's influence on motives is unprovable and likely counterproductive.
This is a key insight: internal motives are not easily manipulated by external authority.

Razi's Refutation of Collective Infallibility
Razi refutes the argument that the entire ummah must be infallible as transmitter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's refutation of the argument that the entire Muslim community must be infallible as the transmitter of the Sharia, and why he concludes that infallibility must belong to specific individuals.

Core Idea
Infallibility of the entire community is neither rationally proven nor airtight; it leads to circular reasoning.

Razi presents a sophisticated refutation of a common argument used to prove the infallibility of the entire ummah and the authority of consensus. The argument initially seems plausible: the Sharia must be preserved and transmitted, so the transmitter must be infallible. But who is that transmitter? Razi systematically dismantles the claim that it is the community as a whole, exposing a fatal circularity. This lesson walks through his reasoning step by step.

The Third Shubah: Necessity of the Imam's Infallibility
Razi argues that the imam must be infallible because following a sinful imam would entail commanding sin.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's linguistic and logical argument for why the imam must be infallible.

Core Idea
An imam who can sin cannot be an imam.

This lesson presents Razi's third argument for imam's infallibility. He starts with the very meaning of 'imam' as one who is followed, then uses a dilemma to show that allowing sin on the imam leads to either commanding sin or undermining his leadership. This elegant argument ties the imam's infallibility directly to the function of being followed.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic definition of imam
Razi begins by defining 'imam' linguistically as one who is followed and taken as a leader, just as a garment is named for being worn. This definition sets the foundation for the argument.
The analogy with clothing emphasizes that the imam's identity is relational to being followed.
2
Examine the dilemma when the imam sins
Razi poses a dilemma: if the imam sins, either he is followed in that sin or he is not followed. Both options lead to unacceptable consequences.
The dilemma covers all possibilities, so no escape.
3
First branch: following leads to commanding sin
If the imam is followed in his sin, then through the imam's example, Allah would be commanding sin, which is impossible for Allah.
This branch is impossible, so it cannot happen.
4
Second branch: not following negates imamate
If the imam is not followed in his sin, then the people follow their own judgment of good and evil, not the imam. Thus the imam ceases to be an imam in reality.
This branch also destroys the imam's status.
5
Conclude the necessity of infallibility
Since both branches lead to absurdities, it is impossible for the imam to sin. Therefore the imam must be infallible (ma'sum).
The argument does not rely on scriptural proof but on the logical function of imamate.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fifth objection to the necessity of the Imam's infallibility based on Qur'an 2:124 and the nuanced answer that distinguishes between being engaged in sin and absolute infallibility.

Core Idea
The verse only forbids ongoing sin, not all sin, so it does not prove absolute infallibility.

This lesson focuses on the fifth objection raised against the necessity of the Imam's infallibility, which appeals to the famous verse where Allah tells Ibrahim that His covenant does not reach the wrongdoers. While the objection seems strong, Imam Razi provides a precise answer that limits the verse's implication. We will examine the objection and its refutation in detail.

The fifth objection: Adherence to His statement – exalted is He – to Ibrahim: 'He said, “And of my offspring.” He said, “My covenant does not reach the wrongdoers.”' [al-Baqarah 2:124] The verse indicates that the covenant of the imamate does not reach one who is a wrongdoer, and everyone who is a sinner is a wrongdoer. He – exalted is He – said: 'Among them are wrongdoers to themselves' [Fatir 35:32]. Thus the verse becomes a clear text that everyone who is a sinner, whether his sin is apparent or hidden, cannot be an Imam, and if so, it is established that the Imam must be infallible. And the answer to the first objection: It is based on the first issue, that when error is possible for people, they need the appointment of an Imam, and the answer to their speech about it has preceded. And the answer to the second objection: The Sharia is only preserved through the transmission of an infallible transmitter, if that infallible transmitter is such that he can be seen and access to him and recourse to his statement is possible. But if it is not so, the Sharia is not preserved through his transmission, so this objection falls. And the answer to the third objection: There is no dispute that it is obligatory upon every individual of the subjects to follow the deputies of the Imam, such as judges, scholars, and witnesses, even though they are by consensus not infallible, and whatever they say about them is our answer concerning the supreme Imam. And the answer to the fourth objection: It is also a rebuttal by the deputies of the Imam, in that when the infallible Imam delegates command and ministry to some people, that delegate is not infallible, and if sin occurs from him, then the one preventing him is either the greatest Imam alone or with others. The first – that the preventer is the greatest Imam alone – is false because the greatest Imam alone cannot repel that prince with his many armies. Do you not see that Ali, peace be upon him, could not repel Muawiyah from the command with what he had of many armies, let alone that he could do it alone? And the second – that the repeller of that matter is the greatest Imam with his armies – is also false because his armies are not infallible, so perhaps they do not obey the command of the infallible Imam. Thus it is established that delegating command and ministry to a non-infallible is a cause for increase of evil and sedition, and whatever you say here we also say concerning the Imam. And the answer to the fifth objection: The verse indicates that the condition of the Imam is that he not be engaged in sin; but as for being necessarily infallible (wajib al-ismah), there is no indication of it.

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth objection (shubhah) to the necessity of the Imam's infallibility, then provides answers to all five objections in order. The objection uses the Qur'anic verse where Ibrahim asks for his descendants to be included in the covenant, and Allah responds that the covenant does not reach the wrongdoers. The objector argues that since the imamate is a covenant, it cannot go to a wrongdoer, and since every sinner is a wrongdoer, the Imam must be infallible. The author then answers each objection: (1) The first objection is based on an earlier issue that has already been answered. (2) The second objection argues that preservation of Sharia requires an infallible transmitter, but this only works if that transmitter is accessible; if not, it is irrelevant. (3) The third objection raises the point that the Imam's deputies (judges, scholars, witnesses) are not infallible yet people follow them; the author says this same argument applies to the Imam. (4) The fourth objection involves delegation of authority to non-infallible deputies; the author argues that if the Imam tries to prevent their sins alone he cannot, and if with his armies they may not obey, leading to more evil; but this same argument can be turned against the Imam's own infallibility. (5) The answer to the fifth objection is that the verse only shows that the Imam should not be engaged in sin, not that he must be absolutely infallible. Thus, none of the objections prove the necessity of 'ismah.

Answers to Objections One Through Four
Imam Razi answers the first four objections against the necessity of the Imam's infallibility.

Learning Objective

The Different Views on Establishing Imamate
Razi summarizes the positions of the sects on how imamate is established.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the different positions among early Muslim sects regarding how a person becomes an imam, beyond divine designation.

Core Idea
Imamate can be established through designation or, according to some, through other means like bay’a.

This passage from Imam al-Razi sets the stage for a detailed discussion on the causes of imamate. He notes that while all agree on divine designation (nass), there is sharp disagreement on whether other methods exist. This lesson maps out the three main positions that will be debated: the Zaydi, the Ash'ari/Mu'tazili, and the Twelver Imami.

The term 'nass' means explicit designation by God or His Prophet.
2
Identify the three divergent positions
Find the three groups mentioned: Zaydis, Ash'aris & Mu'tazila, and Twelvers. Note their distinctive conditions for imamate (Fatimid qualification, bay'a, and rejection of non-designative causes).

Proving Abu Bakr's Imamate by Consensus and Elimination
Razi presents a two-premise proof for Abu Bakr's imamate by consensus and elimination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi proves Abu Bakr's imamate using consensus and elimination of other candidates.

Core Idea
The rightful imam is proven by consensus and elimination of alternatives.

In this passage, Imam Razi presents a concise proof for the imamate of Abu Bakr. He argues that the Muslim community unanimously agreed that the caliph after the Prophet was one of three people: Abu Bakr, Ali, or al-Abbas. By disproving the claims of Ali and al-Abbas, only Abu Bakr remains. The proof rests on two premises: first, that the consensus limited the candidates to these three; second, that Ali was in a position of incapacity to press his claim, despite his courage and supporters. This argument is foundational in Sunni political theology.

Guided Path
1
Understand the argument structure
Read the passage to see that it presents a two-premise proof leading to a conclusion. The conclusion is that Abu Bakr is the rightful imam because the other two candidates are invalid.
The proof is deductive: if A or B or C, and not B nor C, then A.
2
Examine the first premise
The first premise states that the community agreed the rightful caliph is one of three: Abu Bakr, Ali, or al-Abbas. The author justifies this by noting that the Ansar abandoned their claim after Abu Bakr's argument, leaving only these three.
This is a claim of consensus (ijma).
3
Examine the second premise and its evidence
The second premise asserts that Ali was in a state of incapacity to demand his right. The evidence includes his failure to act despite having courage, family support, and offers from al-Abbas and al-Zubayr.
The evidence is historical reports (riwayat).
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Since the first premise limits candidates and the second removes Ali (and implicitly al-Abbas), Abu Bakr is necessarily the imam. Reflect on how this argument combines consensus and historical circumstance.

Razi concludes that since Ali and Abbas did not claim the imamate, it must be Abu Bakr's right.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

The Argument from the Silence of Ali and Abbas for Abu Bakr's Imamate
Razi argues from the silence of Ali and Abbas to prove Abu Bakr's imamate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi uses the silence of Ali and Abbas as a proof for Abu Bakr's imamate, and the logical structure of this argument.

Core Idea
Silence from a rightful claim implies either inability or sin – either way, Abu Bakr’s imamate stands.

Razi continues his series of arguments for the imamate of Abu Bakr by presenting a third premise based on the observed behavior of Ali and Abbas. He argues that their refraining from contesting Abu Bakr's succession cannot be due to inability, so it must be a conscious choice. This choice, if they had a right to the imamate, would be a sin disqualifying them; if not, the right must belong to Abu Bakr. Thus, in either scenario, Abu Bakr's imamate is confirmed. This lesson unpacks that logical deduction.

1

3

Razi presents a dilemma: either the silence was due to inability (already disproven) or due to ability. Then a second dilemma: either the imamate was their right (so silence is sin) or not (so right is Abu Bakr's).

Commentary
This passage presents a key argument in Razi's refutation of the Shia claim for Ali's imamate.
