The General Answer: Specific Proofs Override General Proofs
Razi presents a general methodological answer to all three doubts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's methodological answer that his specific proofs take precedence over the opponents' general proofs, thereby settling the dispute about intercession.

Core Idea
Specific scriptural evidence overrides general evidence in theological reasoning.

After presenting three proofs against intercession for sinners, Razi anticipates opposing arguments. He offers a concise methodological answer: his own proofs are specific (khāṣṣ) to the case, while the opponents' proofs are general (ʿāmm). Since specific evidence takes precedence over general, his position is preponderant. This lesson explains that principle and its application.

Guided Path
1
Understand the principle: specific vs. general
Razi states that his proofs are specific (khāṣṣ) to certain persons and times, while the opponents' proofs are general (ʿāmm). The principle is that specific evidence overrides general evidence in case of conflict.
This is a standard usul al-fiqh principle.
2
See how it applies to intercession
Razi's proofs show intercession is denied for sinners in particular circumstances, but the opponents' proofs might seem to deny it absolutely. By narrowing the scope, Razi preserves the possibility of intercession for others.
3
Note the limitation of this answer
Razi mentions that detailed answers are in his Tafsir al-Kabir, so this is only a general response.

Fifth premise: absence of ellipsis and interpolation
Razi presents the fifth premise: textual proof requires no ellipsis or interpolation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why textual interpretation requires ruling out ellipsis and interpolation, and how these can invert the apparent meaning of a verse.

Core Idea
Ellipsis and interpolation can flip the meaning of a text from negative to positive and vice versa.

In this passage, Imam al-Razi presents the fifth premise necessary for deriving theological rulings from scripture: the text must be free from ellipsis (hadhf) and interpolation (idmar). Without this premise, we cannot be sure whether a verse means what it appears to mean, because allowing such features can completely reverse its intended meaning.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the translation. Notice how Razi states the premise and the reason for it.
The Arabic uses technical terms: حذف (ellipsis) and إضمار (interpolation).
2
Examine the Quranic examples
For each verse example, identify how the apparent negation is actually affirmation (or vice versa). Notice the verses: Q 75:1, Q 7:12, Q 6:151, Q 4:176.
Razi shows that these examples are used by scholars to argue for the presence of ellipsis.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
Razi concludes that the absence of ellipsis and interpolation is a conjectural (dhanniyya) premise. Think about why this premise, though necessary, is not certain.
This impacts the strength of textual proofs in theology.

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth premise: textual interpretation requires that there be no ellipsis (hadhf) or interpolation (idmar) in the text, because allowing them can invert meanings – negation becomes affirmation and vice versa. Razi gives examples from the Qur'an: in Q 75:1, 'la uqsimu' (I swear) is ostensibly negation but actually affirmation; in Q 7:12, 'ma mana'aka alla tasjuda' (what prevented you from prostrating) uses 'alla' which can be analyzed as containing a negative; in Q 6:151, 'alla tushriku' (that you not associate) is negative but intended as positive. In Q 4:176, 'yubayyinu allahu lakum an' (Allah makes clear to you that) is affirmative but interpreted as a negation. Hence, determining the absence of such ellipsis is a conjectural premise.

Learning Objective

Abu Bakr's bay'a proves that bay'a works.

He asserts that since Abu Bakr's imamate was established by the bay'a of the Companions, and we accept its validity, it follows that bay'a is a legitimate means of becoming imam. The Twelvers argue that bay'a cannot establish imamate due to incapacity, fitna, analogy, and divine deputyship.

As for the Twelvers, they have argued that bay'a cannot be a cause for attaining the imamate by [several] arguments: The first argument: Those who pledge allegiance to the imam have absolutely no power over the least matters or the least persons; and one who has no power over the least matters and the least persons, how can it be rational that he has the power to empower another over all the people of the East and West? The second argument: Establishing the imamate by bay'a and contract leads to fitna; because each people of a town say: 'The imam is from us, more worthy, and the contract issued from us is preferable,' and it is not possible to prefer some over others, so it leads to chaos and inciting fitna. Yet it is known that the purpose of appointing an imam is to remove fitna as much as possible. Thus appointing an imam by the way of bay'a leads to contradiction, so it is invalid. The third argument: The office of imamate is higher and greater than the office of judging (qada) and the office of market inspector (hisba); since the people of bay'a are not able to appoint a judge or a muhtasib, then they are even more unable to appoint the supreme imam. The fourth argument: The imam is the deputy of Allah Almighty and the deputy of His Messenger, and the deputyship of another is not attained...

Commentary
This passage presents a series of objections (shubah) from the Twelvers against bay'a as a means of imamate. The first objection questions the capacity of the electors: they cannot even manage minor affairs, so how can they empower someone over the entire ummah? The second objection argues that bay'a leads to fitna because each group would claim their own candidate, defeating the purpose of imamate which is to prevent fitna. The third objection uses an analogy from lesser offices: if people cannot appoint a judge or muhtasib (market inspector), they certainly cannot appoint the imam, whose office is greater. The fourth objection (incomplete) argues that the imam is the deputy (na'ib) of Allah and His Messenger, and such deputyship cannot be conferred by mere humans. These objections collectively aim to show that imamate cannot be established by human choice but must be by divine designation.
