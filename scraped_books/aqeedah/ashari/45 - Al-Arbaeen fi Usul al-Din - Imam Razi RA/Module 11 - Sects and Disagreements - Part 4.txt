Razi's Refutation of Ijma-Based Argument for Ali's Imamate
Razi refutes the objection that ijma limits imamate to three candidates by questioning the validity of ijma as proof and invoking taqiyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's two-fold refutation of the argument that consensus determines Ali as the exclusive imam: first by challenging the necessity of an infallible imam in every age, and second by invoking the possibility of taqiyya.

Core Idea
Consensus is not proof if the infallible imam may have agreed out of fear.

Razi takes on a central Shi'a proof for Ali's imamate: that the community agreed the imam is one of three, and since only Ali was infallible, he must be the imam. Razi's response cuts deep—he questions whether consensus is even a valid proof, especially if the infallible imam could have dissimulated. This lesson walks through his argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the first paragraph of the Arabic passage, which presents the Shi'a objection. Note the key claim: ijma limits imamate to three candidates, and only Ali qualifies.
The objection relies on the necessity of an infallible imam.
2
Identify Razi's first challenge
Razi begins by questioning the basis of ijma itself: 'Why do you say that ijma is a proof?' This shifts the burden to the opponent.
3
Understand the opponent's justification
The opponents argue that ijma is proof because the age must contain an infallible imam, and his inclusion makes the consensus true.
This is a key premise for their argument.
4
Analyze Razi's two-pronged rebuttal
First, Razi denies that the age must contain an infallible imam. Second, even if granted, the infallible imam might have agreed out of taqiyya and fear, not conviction.
This undermines the reliability of the supposed consensus.

Commentary
This passage addresses the first objection (al-shubha al-ula) raised by the opponents (likely Shi'a). The objection is that the community has consensus (ijma') that the imam is one of three: Ali, Abu Bakr, or al-Abbas. Since Abu Bakr and al-Abbas are not infallible (ma'sum), and the imam must be infallible, they are disqualified, leaving Ali as the only candidate. Therefore, Ali is the rightful imam. Razi responds by first challenging the validity of the supposed consensus. He asks why they consider consensus a proof. The opponents argue that consensus is proof because the age must contain an infallible person (the imam). Their consensus includes that infallible person's opinion, which is true, so the consensus is true. Razi rejects this by denying that the age must contain an infallible imam—he claims they have already shown the weakness of that argument. Even if it were granted, Razi says it does not prove consensus is a proof, because the infallible imam might have agreed with the majority out of taqiyya (dissimulation) and fear, not because the majority opinion is correct. Thus, the consensus argument fails.

Bay'a is a valid basis for imamate, and previous arguments already refute the doubts against it.

The opponents argue that imamate must come either by explicit text (nass) or by bay'a, and they reject both options. This shows that the objection is not new and does not require a fresh refutation.

Refuting the Claim of Explicit Text: The Transmission Argument
Razi challenges the transmission of the alleged explicit text and counters the fame objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's first line of argument against the Shi'a claim of an explicit text for Ali, which challenges the transmission chain and demonstrates that widespread fame is not a reliable indicator of truth.

Core Idea
Widespread fame does not guarantee truth—false rumors spread too.

In this passage, Imam al-Razi confronts one of the main Shi'a arguments for Ali's immediate succession: the claim that there exists an explicit, clear text designating him. Razi's initial response does not address the content of the alleged text but rather its transmission. He argues that the report never reached the level of tawatur, and he counters the objection that it would have become famous by noting that false rumors also become widespread.

3
Understand the counterargument
The opponent says the text would be famous if true. Razi responds with an example of false rumors that become famous.
This is a key logical move: fame ≠ truth.
4
Reflect on the principle
Think about why this argument is important in theological debates: it separates transmission reliability from popularity.
This undermines the opponent's appeal to widespread acceptance.

Commentary

Razi's Refutation of the First Shubha: Ijma' and Infallibility
Razi answers the Shia argument that ijma' and infallibility prove Ali's imamate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi refutes the Shia argument for Ali's imamate based on ijma' and infallibility, by questioning the proof of ijma' and raising the possibility of taqiyya.

Core Idea
Even if consensus existed, fear (taqiyya) can render it unreliable.

Razi now tackles a key Shia proof: the claim that the community agreed that the imam must be one of three, and since only Ali is infallible, he is the imam. In his response, Razi does not simply reject the consensus; he first grants it for argument and then challenges the underlying assumptions about the binding nature of ijma' and the possibility of dissimulation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the structure: a Shia assertion followed by Razi's two-part refutation.
The passage is a classic example of munazara (disputation).
2
Identify the Shia argument
The Shia claim that ijma' points to Ali. Identify the premises: the imam is one of three, Abu Bakr and al-Abbas are not infallible, so Ali must be imam.
3
Understand Razi's first response
Razi grants the premise but asks why ijma' is a proof. The Shia respond by arguing that an infallible imam exists in every age, so his statement is included in the consensus, making it true.
Razi does not accept this premise; he challenges it.
4
Understand Razi's second response
Razi denies the necessity of an infallible imam at all times. Even if accepted, he raises the possibility of taqiyya: the imam might have agreed out of fear, making the consensus unreliable.
This is the decisive refutation.
5
Reflect on the method
Notice how Razi uses the method of munazara: first granting the opponent's premise for argument, then attacking the underlying proof and raising a counter-possibility.
This method is common in kalam.

أما الشبهة الأولى، وهي قولهم : أجمعت الأمة على أن الإمام إما علي أو أبو بكر أو العباس وأبو بكر والعباس لا يصلحان للإمامة؛ لأن الإمام يجب يكون واجب العصمة وهما ما كانا واجبي العصمة، فلم يصلحا للإمامة، فتعين علي للإمامة.
فالجواب: هب أن الأمة أجمعت على أن الإمام أحد هؤلاء الثلاثة، فلم قلتم إن الإجماع حجة قالوا لأنا دللنا على أن الزمان لا يخلو عن وجود المعصوم، وإذا أجمعت الأمة اشتمل إجماعهم على قوله، وقوله حق، والمشتمل على الحق حق، فكان إجماع الأمة حقا من هذا الوجه.

As for the first shubha (doubt), which is their saying: The community (umma) has agreed (ijma'at) that the imam is either Ali or Abu Bakr or al-Abbas, and Abu Bakr and al-Abbas are not suitable for the imamate because the imam must be infallible (wajib al-isma) and they were not infallible, so they are not suitable for the imamate; therefore Ali is specified for the imamate.
The answer: Grant (hab) that the community agreed that the imam is one of these three, but why do you say that ijma' (consensus) is a proof? They said: Because we have proved that time is not free from the existence of an infallible one (al-masum), and when the community agrees, their consensus includes his statement, and his statement is true, and what includes truth is true; so the consensus of the community is true from this aspect.
We said: We do not accept (la nusallim) that time is not free from the existence of an infallible one, and we have shown the weakness of your proof concerning it. Even if we accepted that, it does not follow from this that ijma' is a proof, because of the possibility that the imam feared the people and agreed with them on their falsehood out of dissimulation (taqiyya) and fear. If this is the case, it does not follow from this degree that ijma' is a proof.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer unit. The Shia objection argues, on the basis of ijma' (consensus), that the imam must be one of three individuals, and since Abu Bakr and al-Abbas are not infallible, Ali must be the imam. Razi's answer proceeds in two steps: First, he grants the premise for argument (hab) but questions why ijma' is considered a proof. The Shia respond by claiming that their proof of an infallible imam at all times ensures that the consensus includes his statement, making it true. Second, Razi rejects this by denying the premise that time cannot lack an infallible imam, and even if it were accepted, he argues that ijma' still does not constitute a proof because of the possibility of taqiyya—that the imam might have consented out of fear. Thus, the Shia argument collapses. The passage demonstrates the method of disputation (munazara) by first granting the opponent's premise for argument and then refuting the underlying principles.

The Transmission and Fame of the Alleged Nass Jali
Razi answers the second shubha (bay'a) and begins refuting the third (nass jali) by attacking its transmission and fame.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's arguments that the alleged explicit text (nass jali) for Ali's imamate was not transmitted in a way that ensures certainty, and that its fame is not evidence of authenticity.

Core Idea
Fame does not prove truth; false rumors spread widely, and the nass jali claim originates from known liars.

Razi begins his refutation of the Shia claim of an explicit textual designation for Ali by questioning its transmission. He argues that the transmitters never reached the level of tawatur (mass transmission) required for certainty, and even if they did in some ages, the report should have become universally famous; yet it did not. He counters the fame objection with examples of false rumors and points out that the doctrine's originators were notorious liars.

2

Razi states that the transmitters of the alleged nass jali never reached the threshold of tawatur in any age. This means the report cannot be considered certain.

The Shia object that even if it were ahad in some ages, it should have become famous. Razi gives two rebuttals: (a) false rumors become famous without known origin, (b) the report simply did not reach the opponents.
Consider the example of widespread lies.
4
Trace the doctrine's origin
Razi identifies Ibn al-Rawandi and Abu Isa al-Warraq as the originators of the nass jali claim, both known for lying. This discredits the tradition.
This is an ad hominem but historically significant.

Commentary
This passage answers two Shia objections in sequence. The second shubha is brief: the Shia argue that if Abu Bakr were imam, his imamate must be proven either by nass (explicit designation) or by bay'a (oath of allegiance); they imply bay'a is invalid. Razi dismisses it by stating that bay'a indeed established the imamate, and previous answers have refuted their objections to bay'a. The third shubha is the major claim: that there is an explicit text (nass jali) designating Ali as imam. Razi's refutation proceeds in several steps:
1. He first denies that the transmitters of this alleged text ever reached tawatur (mass transmission) in any period; without tawatur, the report is not conclusive. The Shia counter that if it were at the level of ahad in some periods, it would still have become famous by now. Razi gives two responses: (a) He refutes the necessity of fame by pointing out that many false rumors become famous without known origin; and (b) he argues that the report did not reach the opponents, so it could be that the transmitters were ahad but the report simply did not reach them. Even if fame were conceded, he traces the doctrine's origin to known liars like Ibn al-Rawandi and Abu Isa al-Warraq.
2. He then provides two decisive proofs that the claim is false: (i) If the text were genuine and transmitted widely, it would have been impossible for the community to ignore it and for Ali to be deprived of his right; the very fact that opposition occurred shows the text is spurious. (ii) Ali himself never cited this text in any of his known sermons or arguments, though he used other hadiths like the hadith of al-Mawla. If the nass jali existed, it would be the strongest argument, yet he did not use it—an absurdity for a rational person. Thus the claim collapses.

The Logical and Historical Impossibility of the Nass Jali
Razi provides two definitive proofs that the nass jali claim is false.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's two decisive proofs that the alleged explicit text for Ali is a fabrication: its concealment would be impossible if it were widely known, and Ali himself never cited it.

Core Idea
If the nass jali were real, it would be undeniable and Ali would have used it; his silence proves it false.

Razi presents two powerful arguments against the authenticity of the nass jali. First, if the text had been delivered to the masses, it would have been impossible to conceal and impossible for the community to ignore it and oppress Ali. Second, if it existed, Ali would have cited it in his sermons instead of weaker arguments. The fact that he did not use it is conclusive evidence that it is a fabrication.

Guided Path

Read the two wujuh (proofs)

Razi presents a dilemma: either the text was delivered to tawatur or not. If delivered, it would have spread and could not be concealed; if not, it is not a decisive proof. The historical reality shows it was not widely known, so the claim fails.
Focus on the 'impossible to conceal' and 'impossible for the community to oppress Ali' parts.

Analyze the second proof: Ali's silence
Razi argues that Ali never mentioned this text in any known sermon or appeal, even though he used other hadiths. This is irrational if the text existed and was stronger.
Consider the hadiths Ali actually used: al-Mawla and al-Manila.
4
Reflect on the rationality of the argument
Think about why this argument is considered persuasive in kalam: it uses common sense and historical observation to show the claim is self-contradictory.

The Clear Text Did Not Reach Even Its Opponents

Commentary
This passage presents the first face (wajh) of Razi's refutation of the twelfth shubha, which cites the famous hadith 'He whose mawla I am, 'Ali is his mawla.' Razi lists multiple points within this face. First, he notes the hadith is a khabar wahid (single narration), which only yields probability (zann), not certainty (qat'). The opponents claim the community agreed on its authenticity, but Razi counters: by what standard—certainty or probability? If they claim certainty, it is denied; if probability, it does not prove their claim (which requires certainty). Even if the hadith is authentic, the word 'mawla' may not mean 'first' (awla). They try to prove it from the Qur'anic verse 'the Fire is your mawla' (57:15) meaning 'more worthy of you', but Razi objects that one cannot substitute 'mawla' and 'awla' in usage. Even if 'mawla' can mean 'awla', it is not necessary to interpret it that way here. When they argue that 'mawla' is ambiguous and 'awla' clarifies it, Razi replies that this reasoning is probabilistic and not acceptable in definitive matters (qati'iyyat). Razi supports this by narrating a specific incident: a dispute between Zayd and Ali where Ali called Zayd 'mawla', and Zayd denied being Ali's mawla, claiming only to be the Prophet's mawla. The Prophet then said the hadith, indicating priority in love and respect, not general authority. Therefore, interpreting the hadith as love is more appropriate than imamate, because otherwise it would imply Ali's imamate during the Prophet's lifetime—which is false. Thus the first face dismantles the proof-text by questioning its certainty, the meaning of 'mawla', and limiting its application.

Razi's Refutation: Does 'Mawla' Mean 'First'?
Razi refutes the claim that 'mawla' means 'first' implying imamate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's linguistic and contextual arguments against interpreting 'mawla' as 'first' to prove Ali's imamate.

Core Idea
Linguistic flexibility does not require a word to carry its rare meaning in every context.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi dismantles a key Shi'i argument for Ali's imamate based on the hadith 'man kuntu mawlahu fa Ali mawlahu'. He first questions the certainty of the claim, then challenges the linguistic assumption that 'mawla' can be equated with 'awla' (first/more deserving), and finally contextualizes the hadith to limit its scope.

Guided Path
1
Understand the opponent's claim
Razi begins by questioning whether the community accepted Ali's superiority with certainty or conjecture. This sets the stage for challenging the proof.
He denies certainty and says conjecture does not suffice for a foundational doctrine like imamate.
2
Examine the linguistic argument
Razi argues that 'mawla' does not linguistically mean 'first' (awla). He cites Quranic usage and syntactic differences to show they are not interchangeable.
Note the example: 'This is awla than that' is correct, but 'this is mawla than that' is not.
3
See the contextual argument
Even if 'mawla' could mean 'first', Razi restricts its meaning to the specific occasion of the hadith—a dispute between Zayd and Ali over being the Prophet's mawla.
The context limits the 'firstness' to love and honor, not universal authority.
4
Grasp the consequence
Interpreting 'mawla' as imamate would imply Ali was imam during the Prophet's lifetime, which is absurd and historically false.
This reductio ad absurdum strengthens Razi's refutation.

So he said: 'All people are more knowledgeable than Umar, even secluded women in their homes.' So these incidents occurred for others than Ali, and similar did not happen for Ali.

Commentary
This passage presents the second and third detailed proofs. The second proof is based on the common tafsīr of the verse 'And a listening ear listened to it' (al-Ḥāqqa 69:12), which many commentators understood as revealed concerning ʿAlī (ra). The phrase 'listening ear' is interpreted as a metaphorical description of ʿAlī's deep comprehension, and the fact that the verse singles him out for 'increased understanding' indicates he was granted more knowledge than others. The third proof is a series of historical incidents involving ʿUmar (ra) being corrected by ʿAlī. The first incident: ʿUmar ordered the stoning of a woman who gave birth after six months, believing she had committed adultery. ʿAlī pointed out that the Qur'anic verses about gestation (thirty months) and nursing (two years) together imply that the minimum pregnancy period is six months, so the woman was innocent. ʿUmar acknowledged his error. The second incident: a pregnant woman confessed to adultery, and ʿUmar ordered stoning. ʿAlī objected: 'If you have authority over her, what authority do you have over what is in her womb?'—meaning the unborn child should not be killed. ʿUmar again withdrew. The author then addresses an objection: perhaps ʿUmar ordered stoning without careful examination. The author refutes this by saying that would imply ʿUmar was negligent in bloodshed, which is a graver charge. Then an additional story is cited: ʿUmar proposed capping bridal gifts (mahr), and an old woman corrected him by quoting the Qur'an (al-Nisā 4:20) which allows giving a great amount. ʿUmar accepted the correction. The point is that these errors occurred to others (like ʿUmar and Abū Bakr) but never to ʿAlī, highlighting his infallible knowledge.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you read this verse (69:12) before, did you ever imagine it could be about a specific person? How does knowing that many scholars explain it about Ali affect your attachment to that interpretation?
?
This proof relies on the authority of 'most commentators'. Are you willing to question the opinions of the majority when your personal loyalties are involved? What would it take for you to change your mind on a matter of religion?
?
If you were to learn that this verse is not about Ali at all, would it shake your trust in the Imamate position? Is your faith anchored in verses that might have multiple interpretations, or in something more certain?
