Possibility as an Attribute: The Necessity of Pre-Eternal Matter
Argument that possibility inheres in a subject (hyle) implying eternity of bodies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the philosophers conclude that possibility must inhere in a pre‑existing subject called hyle, leading to the eternity of bodies.

Core Idea
Every possibility needs a bearer—and that bearer is eternal matter.

Having established that possibility is a positive attribute, the philosophers now ask whether it refers to God's power or to the essence of the possible object. They argue that it must be an attribute of the possible object itself, because otherwise there would be no distinction between possible and impossible for divine power. Since attributes require an existing subject, there must be a pre‑existing entity that bears the possibility of the world's existence. They identify this entity as hyle—prime matter—and conclude that bodies are eternal.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage through
Read the entire passage once to get the flow of the argument. Notice how it builds from the nature of possibility to the necessity of a substrate.
2
Examine the two options for possibility
The passage says possibility either refers to the power of the Agent (God) or to the essence of the possible thing. Identify these two options.
3
Understand why the first option is rejected
The argument says: if possibility were just God's power, then there would be no distinction between possible and impossible for His power; He would be equally able to do all things. But we know He cannot bring about impossibilities. Therefore, the distinction must come from the object itself.
This is a key move: it forces possibility to be an attribute of the object, not just of the agent.
4
Follow the inference to hyle
Since possibility is an attribute in the possible object, and an attribute requires a subject, the subject must exist. This subject is called hyle (prime matter). The passage then notes that in philosophy, hyle is either body or inseparable from body, so bodies must be eternal.
Take note of the final conclusion: the world is eternal because matter is eternal.

The Fourth Doubt: Priority, Time, and Eternity
Philosophers argue from priority to infinite time and eternal bodies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' argument that priority before existence is an existent property, leading to an infinite regress identified as time, thereby proving the eternity of time, motion, and body.

Core Idea
Priority before existence is an existent attribute, leading to an infinite regress that is time.

This fourth doubt challenges the Islamic view that the world is originated. The philosophers argue that the concept of 'priority' before existence cannot be mere non-existence, because non-existence itself does not have temporal relations. Instead, priority must be an additional existent quality. Since each such priority is itself originated, it requires another priority, and so on infinitely. This infinite series is identified as time, which must therefore be eternal. From there, they infer the eternity of motion and body.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the starting premise
The argument begins with the idea that every originated thing has its non-existence before its existence. Understand that this 'before' implies a temporal priority.
This priority (qabliyya) is not the same as the non-existence (adam) itself.
2
Distinguish priority from non-existence
Non-existence before and after are the same in essence; only priority distinguishes them. Therefore priority is an additional concept that must be something existent.
3
Follow the infinite regress
Since that existent priority is itself originated, it requires another priority before it, leading to an infinite chain. This chain is identified as time.
The argument claims this infinite regress is not vicious but constitutes time itself.
4
Connect time to motion and body
According to natural philosophy, time is an accident of motion, and motion of body. Hence eternity of time implies eternity of motion and body.
This challenges the Islamic doctrine that the universe is created.

Razi's Refutation of the No-Change Objection
Razi refutes the claim that his argument would imply no change in the world.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi refutes the objection that his cosmological argument leads to no change in the world by presenting a dilemma about the cause of a specific accident.

Core Idea
Razi turns the objection into a dilemma about the cause of change.

In this passage, Razi addresses a key objection: his argument for a necessary being seems to imply that the world is static. He first presents the objection, then a possible rejoinder from the opponent, and finally his decisive refutation. The logical moves here are subtle but crucial for understanding causal necessity in kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read and understand the objection
Read the Arabic passage and its translation once. Identify the initial objection: that Razi's view implies no change in the world. Notice how the objection is stated as a conditional consequence.
The objection is based on the idea that an eternal cause necessitates eternal effects, leading to a static chain.
2
Follow the opponent's rejoinder
After the objection, Razi presents a possible rejoinder: the Necessary Existence is universally effusive, but effects depend on the preparedness of recipients, which vary due to celestial motions. Understand this attempt to save change.
The opponent invokes an infinite temporal series of celestial motions to explain changing preparedness.
3
Analyze Razi's answer
Razi's answer focuses on a specific accident. He presents a dilemma: either the accident occurs without a cause (impossible) or with a cause. If the cause is temporal, we get an infinite regress at one moment (impossible). If the cause is eternal, then an eternal cause produces a temporal effect, which the opponent already accepted for the first event.
This refutes the opponent's appeal to celestial motions by showing it leads to the same problem.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Consider why Razi's refutation is effective. The opponent cannot avoid the conclusion that an eternal cause can produce a temporal effect, so the objection against Razi's argument falls apart.

Refutation of Causal Efficacy as Extra Attribute
Razi proves that the efficacy of the cause is not an extra attribute to avoid regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Razi argues that the cause's effectiveness cannot be an additional attribute, otherwise an infinite regress would follow.

Core Idea
If causal efficacy were an extra attribute, it would itself need a cause—leading to infinite regress.

Razi now refutes the second doubt raised against his position. The opponent claimed that the effect depends on an additional attribute of the cause. Razi shows this leads to an absurd infinite chain, thus the efficacy must be identical to the essence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the literal translation. Notice the logical flow: if-then leading to a contradiction.
The key term 'tasalsul' means infinite regress.
2
Identify key terms
Focus on 'mu'aththiriyya' (effectiveness), 'wasf za'id' (extra attribute), and 'tasalsul' (regress). Understand their roles.
These terms are central to the argument.
3
Understand the conditional argument
Razi assumes the opposite: if effectiveness were extra, it would depend on the essence, making it possible and in need of a cause. That cause's effectiveness would again be extra, leading to an infinite chain.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
4
See the conclusion
Since infinite regress is impossible, the original assumption is false: effectiveness is not an extra attribute but identical to the cause's essence.
This preserves divine self-sufficiency.
5
Reflect on the implication
Consider how this argument supports the idea that God's attributes are not separate from His essence, avoiding multiplicity and dependence.
This is a key point in Ash'ari kalam.

This preserves the principle that the cause is self-sufficient and does not depend on an external attribute to be effective.

The Impossibility of Causal Influence on Essence, Existence, or Attribution
Razi systematically eliminates all possible loci of causal influence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Razi argues that no form of causal influence (ta'thir) can affect essence, existence, or the attribution of existence to essence, thereby proving that the very concept of external causation in this sense is logically impossible.

Core Idea
All possible loci of causal influence are impossible, so causality itself is impossible.

Imam al-Razi presents a rigorous logical proof that the concept of 'influence' (ta'thir) cannot be coherently applied to any aspect of a thing: neither its essence, its existence, nor the connection between them. By systematically eliminating each candidate, he concludes that the very notion of external causation is untenable. This argument is crucial for understanding the Ash'ari critique of causal necessity.

Razi's First Two Proofs That Influence Is Not Identical to Cause or Effect
Razi provides two arguments that the act of causing is not the same as the cause or effect.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Causation is not the same as the cause or the effect.

Razi begins the Third Question by framing a logical division: if influence exists, it must be either identical to the cause, the effect, or a third thing. He then sets out to refute the first two options. This lesson covers the first two proofs, which rely on conceivability and the nature of relations. This passage opens the Third Question (al-su'al al-thalith) which examines the nature of influence (ta'thir) if it were to exist. Agency is an attribute distinct from the cause's essence, proven by logic and explanatory practice.

Razi's Argument Against Influence as a Separate Attribute
Razi argues that if influence were a separate attribute, it would lead to regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Razi argues that influence (mu'aththiriyya) cannot be a real attribute separate from the cause and effect, and why this leads to an infinite regress.

Core Idea
Influence cannot be a separate attribute without causing an infinite regress.

Razi continues his critique of causality by examining the possibility that influence is a real attribute distinct from both the cause and the effect. He systematically eliminates all options, showing that whether influence is a substance or an accident, it leads to contradiction or regress. This passage is a powerful example of Ash'ari reasoning against necessary causal connections.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Get the overall flow of the argument before diving into details.
2
Identify the first division: privative vs. positive
Notice how Razi first determines whether influence is privative or positive. He concludes it must be positive because it is the opposite of pure negation.
This establishes that influence is a real attribute, not a mere absence.
3
Follow the second division: substance vs. accident
Razi then asks whether this positive attribute is a substance or an accident. Pay attention to the two reasons against it being a substance.
The two reasons: (1) agency is always an attribute of something, not self-subsistent; (2) if a substance, it would either relate or not, both problematic.
4
Understand the regress argument for accident
If influence is an accident inhering in the cause, it depends on the cause for existence. But then it needs its own cause, leading to infinite regress.
This completes the argument: all possibilities fail.

Commentary
This passage completes the Third Question by examining the possibility that influence (mu'aththiriyya) is a real additional attribute (zai'da) distinct from both the cause and the effect. Razi first determines whether such an attribute would be privative (adami) or positive (thubuti). He argues it cannot be privative because it is the opposite of non-agency, which is pure negation; the negation of non-existence is existence, so it is positive. Then he asks whether this positive attribute is a substance (jawhar) or an accident (arad). He gives two reasons against it being a substance: first, agency is inherently a description and attribute of something, not a self-subsistent entity; second, if it were a substance, it would either relate to the effect or not. If it relates, then that relation itself would require further analysis (regress). If it does not relate, then it is entirely disconnected and cannot be the cause of the effect. So it must be an accident inhering in the cause. But if it is an accident, it depends on the cause for its existence, and being dependent, it itself needs an agent to cause it. That agent would then need its own agency, leading to an infinite regress. Therefore, assuming agency as a distinct attribute fails. Thus all possibilities are exhausted: influence is neither identical to cause/effect nor a separate attribute, so the very notion of external influence (ta'thir) is false. This is a powerful argument in the Ash'ari tradition against causality (often aimed at philosophers).

The Causal Property Must Be an Existent Attribute
Razi argues that causal property is distinct from essence and effect, and must be existent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the causal property (al-mu'thiriyya) cannot be identified with the agent's essence or effect, and why it must be an existent attribute.

Core Idea
The causal property cannot be pure non-existence; it must be a real existent attribute.

Imam al-Razi begins his fourth argument by clarifying that we do not attribute the effect to the agent's essence, but to its being an agent. He then refutes the idea that the causal property is merely the agent's essence or the effect. The key move is to show that the causal property cannot be a non-existent thing, because it is the opposite of non-agent-property, which is pure negation. Thus, the causal property is necessarily an existent attribute.

This step uses the law of non-contradiction in a simple way.

Why the Causal Property Leads to Infinite Regress
Razi examines substance/accident alternatives for causal property and shows both lead to absurdity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a real causal property (mu'thiriyya) cannot be a substance or an accident without leading to infinite regress.

Core Idea
If causal property is a real attribute, it either is a substance (impossible) or an accident (leading to infinite regress).

In this second part of the fourth argument, Imam al-Razi examines what kind of existent the causal property could be. He argues it cannot be a substance because it is a description and because it would either relate to the effect (leading to regress) or be disconnected (absurd). The only remaining option is that it is an accident dependent on the agent, but then it itself would need an agent, leading to infinite regress. Hence, the notion of a real causal effect is invalid.

Guided Path
1
The substance/accident division
Read where Razi says the causal property must be either jawhar (substance) or 'arad (accident). Pay attention to the two arguments against it being a substance.
A substance in kalam is something that subsists by itself, like a body or atom.
2
Why it cannot be a substance
First reason: a causal property is a description (na't) and attribute, and cannot be conceived except as an attribute of something. So it cannot be self-subsisting. Second reason: if it were a substance, it would either have a relation to the effect or not. If relation, then the same discussion applies to that relation, leading to infinite regress. If no relation, it would be entirely foreign and could not be a cause.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Why it cannot be an accident either
If it is an accident dependent on the agent, then it is possible in itself and needs an agent for its existence. That agent would need its own causal property, leading to infinite regress. So the assumption of a real causal property leads to impossibility.
This is the classic infinite regress argument (tasalsul).
4
Final conclusion
Razi concludes that the notion of a real causal effect (ta'thir) is invalid because whether causal property is identical to agent/effect or distinct, both are impossible.
This does not deny that effects occur; it denies that there is a real attribute of 'being a cause' distinct from the agent.

The Fourth Objection: Symmetry of Existence and Non-Existence
Razi states the fourth objection as a reductio argument.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If possibility requires a cause for existence, it would also require a cause for non-existence – which is impossible.

This lesson presents a crucial objection to the kalam argument that possibility necessitates a creator. The objection uses a symmetry argument: if possibility is a ground for needing a cause, then both existence and non-existence would equally need causes. But non-existence, being pure absence, cannot have a cause. This reductio ad absurdum challenges the very foundation of the argument.

The fifth objection argues that if possibility were the cause of need, things would need an agent even while persisting.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If possibility caused need, things would need a creator even while existing – which is absurd.

This lesson presents the fifth objection against the claim that possibility (al-imkan) is the cause of a thing's need for an agent. The objection raises a powerful logical problem: if possibility were truly the cause, then things would need an agent not only at origination but also throughout their persistence, leading to an unintelligible repetition of creation. The passage then examines and refutes a series of counterarguments, ultimately concluding that possibility cannot be the cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Razi's seventh objection challenges the necessity of an efficient cause for temporal events by considering the totality of events and showing the impossibility of both an originated and an eternal cause.

Core Idea
The totality of originated events cannot have an efficient cause without absurdity.

In this passage, Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi presents a powerful dialectical objection to the claim that every originated event requires an efficient cause. He focuses on the totality of events occurring at a given moment and shows that whether one posits an efficient cause or not, difficulties arise. The objection is structured as a dilemma, systematically eliminating each possibility.

Guided Path
1

Answer to Objection Four: On Possibility and Preponderance
Razi answers the fourth objection about possibility and preponderance.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of circularity (dawr) in causal chains. Al-Razi begins by critiquing a previous argument that attempted to prove the impossibility of circularity by appealing to essential priority (al-taqaddum al-dhātī). He points out that this argument is problematic because it relies on an unclear notion of priority. He first shows that priority need not be temporal, using the example of the finger and the ring moving simultaneously. Then he argues that if 'essential priority' just means 'being effective', then the argument reduces to tautology. If it means something else, it must first be clarified and then proved. Al-Razi then presents a sounder proof: the effect depends on its cause; if each of two things were an effect of the other, each would depend on the other, leading to each depending on itself—an impossibility because dependence is a relation and requires two distinct relata. He then addresses an objection: what if the two things are relative terms (al-iḍāfiyyān) that are mutually entailing? The answer is that either relations have no real existence (so the objection is void) or, if they do, the mutual entailment is explained by the unity of the cause (like the relation of paternity and sonship both depending on the same reality). Thus the proof against circularity stands.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of circularity (dawr) in causal chains. Al-Razi begins by critiquing a previous argument that attempted to prove the impossibility of circularity by appealing to essential priority (al-taqaddum al-dhātī). He points out that this argument is problematic because it relies on an unclear notion of priority. He first shows that priority need not be temporal, using the example of the finger and the ring moving simultaneously. Then he argues that if 'essential priority' just means 'being effective', then the argument reduces to tautology. If it means something else, it must first be clarified and then proved. Al-Razi then presents a sounder proof: the effect depends on its cause; if each of two things were an effect of the other, each would depend on the other, leading to each depending on itself—an impossibility because dependence is a relation and requires two distinct relata. He then addresses an objection: what if the two things are relative terms (al-iḍāfiyyān) that are mutually entailing? The answer is that either relations have no real existence (so the objection is void) or, if they do, the mutual entailment is explained by the unity of the cause (like the relation of paternity and sonship both depending on the same reality). Thus the proof against circularity stands.

The fourth premise among the premises of the proof for establishing knowledge of the Necessary Existent: the refutation of infinite regress. Know that before delving into the establishment of this claim we need to present a premise, namely that the efficient cause must exist at the moment of the existence of the effect. The proof for it is that if it were not so, then at the occurrence of the effect the cause would be non-existent, and so the occurrence of the effect would necessarily be at the non-existence of the cause. Commentary

Thus, the only coherent position is that the complete cause and its effect are simultaneous. This premise is crucial for the subsequent proof against infinite regress.

Why There Must Be a First Cause
Razi disproves infinite regress by analyzing two possibilities, concluding a necessary first cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi's argument against infinite regress demonstrates that any chain of causes must end with a being that is necessary in itself.

Core Idea
Infinite regress leads to contradiction; a necessary first cause is unavoidable.

Razi presents a logical argument that any chain of causes must have a beginning. He considers two scenarios for an infinite sequence, showing both lead to impossibility unless the chain ends with a being that is necessary in itself. This lesson unpacks that reasoning step by step.

Guided Path

Razi examines two cases: an incomplete sequence with no beginning, and a sequence that is cut off at the last end. Identify these in the text.

Commentary
This passage is a proof against infinite regress (tasalsul). Razi presents two scenarios. The argument forces acceptance of a first cause that is necessary in itself.

Razi's Argument for Inherent Possibility

Core Idea

Imam Razi presents a crisp argument showing that the possibility of the world's existence at a given time cannot be caused by an agent, because it would be present even if the agent is assumed absent. This establishes that the concept of a thing coming into existence by itself is rationally conceivable.

The world, even though origination occurs in it, yet that particular condition does not occur in it, so it does not follow that the need for an agent occurs in it.
Source pages p. 90

Commentary
This passage presents three objections (sualat) raised by Razi against the inferential argument of the Mu'tazila. The inferential argument uses human actions as a model: since humans originate their actions, and the world is similar in being originated, the world must need a cause. Razi objects: (1) The premise that humans originate their own actions is disputed; actions may occur spontaneously without a cause. If the opponent claims such spontaneity is impossible, they should apply that directly to the world rather than rely on the problematic analogy. (2) Even if humans originate actions, it does not follow that the cause of need is origination (huduth) itself; Razi refers to earlier arguments that huduth is not a cause, part, or condition of need. (3) Even if huduth is the cause, its effect may be specific to actions with a particular condition (e.g., being human actions); the world's huduth lacks that condition, so the inference fails. These objections target the core of the inferential method.
