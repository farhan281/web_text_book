Ali's High Lineage
Ali's lineage as a proof of his superiority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument for Ali's superiority based on his high lineage, particularly his being a Hashimi from both parents and his close relation to the Prophet.

Core Idea
Ali's lineage was unique: a Hashimi from both sides, closest to the Prophet.

This lesson examines the first argument presented by those who claim Ali ibn Abi Talib is superior to Abu Bakr. The argument focuses on lineage: Ali was a Hashimi from both father and mother, and his grandfathers were the noblest. The author contrasts this with the lineage of al-Abbas and Abu Talib to highlight Ali's distinction.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully to understand the lineage argument.
Note the comparison with al-Abbas and Abu Talib.
2
Identify the key points
The passage makes two points: (a) Ali was closest to the Prophet in lineage, (b) he was a Hashimi from both parents.
3
Reflect on the argument's strength
Consider why lineage was considered a criterion for superiority in early Islam and how this argument fits the broader debate.
This is a sectarian argument, not necessarily the author's own view.

الهام قطع الأقلام ومنها النسب العالي ومعلوم أن أشرف الأنساب هو القرب من رسول الله، وهو كان أقرب الناس في النسب إلى رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم وأما العباس، فإنه وإن كان عم رسول الله إلا أن العباس كان أخا لعبد الله والد رسول الله من الأب لا من الأم، وأما أبو طالب فإنه كان أخا لعبد الله والد رسول الله من الأب والأم.
وأيضًا فإن عليا كان هاشميًا من الأب والأم ؛ لأنه علي بن أبي طالب بن عبد المطلب بن هاشم وأيضًا أم علي بن أبي طالب فاطمة أسد بنت بن هاشم ومنها المصاهرة ولم يكن لأحد من الخلق مصاهرة مثل ما كانت له. وأما عثمان فهو وإن شركه في كونه صهرًا للرسول إلا أن أشرف أولاد الرسول هو فاطمة، ولذلك قال : سيدة نساء العالمين أربع وعدها منهن. ولم يحصل مثل هذا الشرف للبنتين اللتين هما زوجتا عثمان ومنها : أنه لم يكن لأحد من الصحابة أولاد يشاركون أولاده في الفضيلة كالحسن والحسين، وهما سيدا شباب أهل الجنة - ولداه.
ثم انظر إلى أولاد الحسن مثل الحسن - المثنى والمثلث - وعبد الله المثنى، والنفس الزكية وإلى أولاد الحسين مثل زين العابدين، والباقر والصادق، والكاظم، والرضا، فإن هؤلاء الأكابر يقر بفضيلتهم وعلو درجتهم كل مسلم.
ومما يدل على علو شأنهم أن أفضل المشايخ وأعلاهم درجة هو أبو يزيد البسطامي، وكان نشأ في دار جعفر الصادق، وأما معروف الكرخي فإنه أسلم على يد علي بن موسى الرضا، وكان بواب داره وبقي على هذه الحالة إلى آخر عمره، ومعلوم أن أمثال هذه الأولاد لم يتفق لأحد من الصحابة.
ولو أخذنا في الشرح والإطناب لطال الكلام.

The thirty-ninth issue. The hint: cutting of pens, and among them is high lineage. It is known that the noblest of lineages is closeness to the Messenger of God, and he was the closest of people in lineage to the Messenger of God, may God bless him and grant him peace. As for al-Abbas, although he was the uncle of the Messenger of God, al-Abbas was the brother of Abdullah, the father of the Messenger of God, from the father but not from the mother. As for Abu Talib, he was the brother of Abdullah, the father of the Messenger of God, from both father and mother. Also, Ali was a Hashimi from both father and mother, because he is Ali ibn Abi Talib ibn Abd al-Muttalib ibn Hashim; also the mother of Ali ibn Abi Talib, Fatima bint Asad, was from the family of Hashim. Among them is marriage alliance, and no one among creation had a marriage alliance like his. As for Uthman, although he shared with him in being a son-in-law of the Messenger, the noblest of the Messenger's children is Fatima, and therefore he said: 'The mistress of the women of the worlds is four' and he counted her among them. Such honor was not attained by the two daughters who were wives of Uthman. Among them is that none of the Companions had children who shared with his children in virtue like al-Hasan and al-Husayn, and they are the masters of the youth of Paradise—his two sons. Then look at the children of al-Hasan like al-Hasan al-Muthanna and al-Muthallath, and Abdullah al-Muthanna, and al-Nafs al-Zakiyya, and at the children of al-Husayn like Zayn al-Abidin, al-Baqir, al-Sadiq, al-Kazim, and al-Rida, for these great ones, every Muslim acknowledges their virtue and high rank. Among what indicates their high standing is that the best of the shaykhs and the highest in degree is Abu Yazid al-Bistami, and he grew up in the house of Ja'far al-Sadiq. As for Ma'ruf al-Karkhi, he converted to Islam at the hands of Ali ibn Musa al-Rida, and he was the doorkeeper of his house and remained in this state until the end of his life. It is known that the like of these children did not occur for any of the Companions. If we were to elaborate and go into detail, the speech would become lengthy. This is the total of the proofs of those who said that the Commander of the Faithful Ali ibn Abi is superior.

Commentary
This passage enumerates the arguments of those who claim Ali ibn Abi Talib is superior to Abu Bakr. It is presented as the opponent's case, to be refuted later. The arguments are: (1) Ali's high lineage—he was the closest in lineage to the Prophet, being a Hashimi from both parents, unlike al-Abbas or Abu Talib. (2) His marriage to Fatima, the noblest of the Prophet's daughters, who was declared the mistress of the women of the worlds. (3) His children, al-Hasan and al-Husayn, and their descendants, who are universally recognized as virtuous, with great saints like Abu Yazid al-Bistami and Ma'ruf al-Karkhi being connected to them. The passage concludes that this is the sum of their proofs.

Ali's Marriage Alliance
Ali's marriage to Fatima as a proof of superiority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ali's marriage to Fatima, the noblest of the Prophet's daughters, is used as an argument for his superiority.

Core Idea
Ali's marriage to Fatima, the best of women, set him apart.

The second argument for Ali's superiority concerns his marriage alliance (musahara). The author argues that no one had a marriage alliance like Ali's: he married Fatima, the noblest of the Prophet's children, whom the Prophet declared the mistress of the women of the worlds. This honor is contrasted with Uthman's marriage to other daughters.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation focusing on the marriage argument.
Notice the specific honor given to Fatima.
2
Compare with Uthman
The author mentions Uthman also being a son-in-law but contrasts the status of Fatima with Uthman's wives.
This highlights the unique honor of Fatima.
3
Reflect on the criterion
Consider why being the husband of the best daughter would imply superiority.

Ali's Progeny
Ali's children and descendants as proof of his superiority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ali's children and descendants, particularly al-Hasan and al-Husayn and their lines, are used as evidence for his superiority.

Core Idea
Ali's children, the masters of Paradise's youth, and their saintly descendants prove his superiority.

The third argument for Ali's superiority focuses on his progeny. The passage asserts that no other Companion had children as virtuous as al-Hasan and al-Husayn, who are the masters of the youth of Paradise. It then lists their revered descendants, including figures like Zayn al-Abidin and Ja'far al-Sadiq, and notes that great saints like Abu Yazid al-Bistami and Ma'ruf al-Karkhi were connected to them. This lineage of virtue is unique to Ali.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation to understand the argument from progeny.
Pay attention to the long list of descendants.
2
Identify the key figures
Note the specific descendants mentioned: al-Hasan, al-Husayn, their children, and the later Imams.
These figures are widely respected across Muslim traditions.
3
Understand the saintly connections
The passage mentions Abu Yazid al-Bistami and Ma'ruf al-Karkhi to show that even great saints were linked to Ali's descendants.
This underscores their spiritual stature.
4
Reflect on the argument's scope
Consider why the enduring virtue of a person's descendants can be a mark of that person's own merit.

Refutation of the Mubahala Argument for Ali's Preference
Imam Razi refutes using the Mubahala verse exclusively for Ali.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the twofold refutation of the claim that Quran 3:61 (Mubahala) establishes Ali's exclusive superiority over other companions.

Core Idea

الثاني : أن أبا بكر لما أسلم كان أبدا في منازعة مع الكفار والمنافرة معهم، وبقي رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم ثلاث عشرة سنة في مكة ثم انتقل إلى المدينة، وبقي هناك سنة أخرى ثم نزلت آية القتال وأبو بكر في تلك المدة الطويلة في مكة والمدينة كان في الذب عن دين الله ومتابعة رسول الله الله وأما علي فإنه في ذلك الوقت كان صغير السن وما كان يخالط القوم، ثم إن بعد نزول آية القتال اشتغل علي هما بقتال الكفار. يفيد أن فثبت أن ابتداء الجهاد كان لأبي بكر، وآخره لعلي وأما الحجة الخامسة : وهي قولهم : إن إيمان علي سابق على إيمان أبي بكر. من الأحاد[؟] قلنا : وقد ذكرنا أن الأخبار فيه متعارضة وأخبارنا وإن كانت فكذا أخباركم وأيضًا فقد بينا أن إسلام أبي بكر أثر في قوة الإسلام أثرا عظيما، وما كان إسلام غيره كذلك. وأما الحجة السادسة : وهي التمسك بقصة خيبر - فجوابها : أن ذلك الكلام مجموع الصفات المذكورة في مدح الثاني غير حاصلة في مدح الأول، فلما قال : لأعطين الراية رجلًا يحب الله ورسوله ويحبه الله ورسوله، كرارًا غير فرار فهذا يدل على أن هذا المجموع ما كان حاصلا لأبي بكر وعمر ؛ لأن كونه غير فرار ما كان حاصلا فيهما، وكان ذلك المجموع غير حاصل فيهما، وعدم كونه كرارًا غير فرار لا في الفضيلة ألا ترى أن الأنبياء أفضل من الملائكة عند الشيعة، مع أنا نقطع أنه ليس للأنبياء من القدرة الحسية ذرة من القدرة التي للملائكة. نقصانا يوجب وأما بقية الوجوه فهي أخبار ضعيفة وأمثالها موجودة في جانب أبي بكر ، على ما تشتمل عليه الكتب المصنفة في هذا الجنس. فهذا تمام الكلام في هذا الباب، والله أعلم.
The thirty-ninth question. Second: That when Abu Bakr embraced Islam, he was constantly in conflict and opposition with the disbelievers. The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) remained in Mecca for thirteen years, then migrated to Medina, and remained there another year, then the verse of fighting was revealed. And Abu Bakr during that long period in Mecca and Medina was defending the religion of Allah and following the Messenger of Allah. As for Ali, he was young at that time and did not mix with the people. Then after the revelation of the verse of fighting, Ali engaged with them in fighting the disbelievers. This indicates that the beginning of jihad was for Abu Bakr, and its end for Ali. As for the fifth argument: which is their statement that the faith of Ali preceded the faith of Abu Bakr from the hadiths. We said: we have mentioned that the reports on this are contradictory. Our reports, even if they are as they are, so are your reports. Also, we have shown that the Islam of Abu Bakr had a great effect on the strength of Islam, and the Islam of others was not like that. As for the sixth argument: which is holding fast to the story of Khaybar. Its answer is that that statement is a combination of attributes mentioned in praise of the latter (Ali) not found in praise of the former (Abu Bakr). When he said: 'I will certainly give the banner to a man who loves Allah and His Messenger, and Allah and His Messenger love him, one who attacks and does not flee' – this indicates that this combination was not present in Abu Bakr and Umar, because the quality of 'not fleeing' was not present in them, and that combination was not present in them. And the absence of being 'one who attacks and does not flee' does not cause a deficiency in virtue. Do you not see that the prophets are better than the angels according to the Shia, even though we are certain that the prophets do not have a speck of the sensory power that the angels have? It would cause a deficiency. As for the remaining arguments, they are weak hadiths, and their likes exist on the side of Abu Bakr, according to what is contained in the books compiled on this subject. This is the end of the discourse in this chapter. And Allah knows best.

Commentary
This passage is a rebuttal of arguments concerning the precedence of the faith of Ali over Abu Bakr, within the context of the thirty-ninth question. The author first presents a second argument: that Abu Bakr engaged in constant struggle against disbelievers from the beginning of his Islam, while Ali was young and did not mix with the people until after the verse of fighting was revealed. Thus, the beginning of jihad is attributed to Abu Bakr and its end to Ali, implying Abu Bakr's earlier and more sustained effort. Next, the fifth argument is mentioned: the claim that Ali's faith preceded Abu Bakr's based on hadith. The author responds by stating that the reports are contradictory and that both sides have similar traditions. Moreover, the impact of Abu Bakr's Islam on strengthening the religion was immense, unlike that of others. Then the sixth argument is addressed: the story of Khaybar, where the Prophet said he would give the banner to one who loves Allah and His Messenger and who attacks and does not flee. The author explains that this combination of attributes (loving and being loved, attacking without fleeing) was not present in Abu Bakr and Umar, specifically the quality of 'not fleeing' was not present in them. However, the absence of this quality does not diminish virtue, just as prophets, though superior to angels, lack the sensory power of angels. The author concludes that the remaining arguments rely on weak hadiths with parallels on Abu Bakr's side, and then marks the end of the chapter.

The Fifth Argument: Precedence of Faith
Imam Razi refutes the claim of Ali's earlier faith by citing contradictory hadith and Abu Bakr's greater impact.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that Ali's faith preceded Abu Bakr's, and Imam Razi's refutation based on contradictory hadith and the greater impact of Abu Bakr's Islam.

Core Idea
Faith precedence is disputed, but Abu Bakr's Islam had unmatched influence.

The opponents claim that Ali embraced Islam before Abu Bakr, citing hadith evidence. Imam Razi counters by noting that the reports are contradictory, and that the tremendous impact of Abu Bakr's conversion on strengthening Islam outweighs any priority in time.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and translation. Notice the structure: opponent's claim followed by refutation.
The refutation has two parts: contradictory reports and greater impact.
2
Identify the opponent's claim
The opponents claim that Ali's faith preceded Abu Bakr's based on hadith.
3
Understand the refutation
Imam Razi gives two rebuttals: (1) The hadith on this matter are contradictory, so no definitive conclusion can be drawn. (2) Even if Ali was earlier, Abu Bakr's Islam had a much greater impact on the strength of Islam.
Consider why impact is considered more important than timing.

The Sixth Argument: The Story of Khaybar
Imam Razi refutes the Khaybar argument by deconstructing the hadith's attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument from the Khaybar banner hadith and Imam Razi's response that the combination of attributes in the hadith was not present in Abu Bakr and Umar, but that does not diminish their virtue.

Core Idea
A single hadith with a combination of qualities does not prove overall superiority.

The opponents cite the famous hadith of the Prophet giving the banner to Ali at Khaybar, describing him as one who loves Allah and His Messenger and who attacks and does not flee. Imam Razi explains that this combination of attributes was not found in Abu Bakr and Umar, yet the absence of one attribute (not fleeing) does not indicate a lack of virtue, just as prophets are superior to angels despite lacking physical strength.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and translation. This is a complex argument with an analogy.
Pay attention to the structure: opponent's claim, analysis of hadith, the analogy, and concluding remarks.
2
Understand the opponent's argument
The opponents cite the hadith where the Prophet said he would give the banner to one who loves Allah and His Messenger and who attacks and does not flee, implying Ali's superiority.
3
Analyze Imam Razi's response
Imam Razi points out that the hadith describes a combination of qualities not found in Abu Bakr and Umar. However, lacking one quality (like 'not fleeing') does not make them less virtuous.
He uses an analogy: prophets are better than angels despite lacking physical power.
4
Consider the conclusion
The remaining weak hadith are dismissed, and it is noted that similar hadith exist for Abu Bakr.
This shows that argument from specific hadith is not decisive.
