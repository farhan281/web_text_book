No Intermediate Between Affirmation and Negation
Al-Razi refutes the possibility of an intermediate between affirmation and negation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Razi proves the law of excluded middle by defining affirmation and negation in terms of determination.

Core Idea
There is no third state between affirmation and negation.

Al-Razi addresses a subtle objection: while it is obvious that affirmation and negation cannot both be true, is it equally obvious that they cannot both be false? Could there be a middle ground? In this passage, he defines the two terms so precisely that any alleged middle collapses into one of them.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the objection: why can't there be an intermediate between affirmation and negation? Then see Al-Razi's immediate response.
The objection grants non-contradiction but questions excluded middle.
2
Grasp the definitions
Al-Razi defines affirmation as anything with realization (tahaqquq), determination (ta'ayyun), and distinction (tamayyuz) in itself; negation as the complete absence of these. Understand these definitions clearly.
These definitions are the key to the proof.
3
Apply the definitions to the supposed intermediate
Any alleged intermediate either has some determination (thus falls under affirmation) or completely lacks it (thus falls under negation). There is no third option.
This is a case-by-case elimination.
4
Confirm the conclusion
Therefore, no intermediate exists. Affirmation and negation are mutually exclusive and exhaustive.
This establishes the law of excluded middle in logic.

For if it is said: As for affirmation and negation not both being true, it is apparent. But as for them not both being false, it is not at the extreme of apparentness; why is it not permissible that there be an intermediate between them? We say: The mind does not bring to mind the essence of negation and affirmation without judging that there is no intermediate between them. For what is meant by affirmation is everything that has realization, determination, and distinction in itself; and by negation is everything that has no realization, no determination, and no specification at all in itself. When you know this, we say: That intermediate, if it has determination and specification in some respect, then it falls under affirmation; and if it has no realization and specification at all, then it falls under negation. So it is established that there is no intermediate between them.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer followed by a proof. The objector grants that the principle of non-contradiction in the sense of no conjunction of opposites is obvious, but questions the part about no third possibility (they cannot both be false). Could there be a middle state? Al-Razi answers by defining the terms: affirmation is anything with any positive determination; negation is absolute absence of determination. Any alleged intermediate either has some determination (so it is a kind of affirmation) or completely lacks it (so it is negation). Therefore no third state exists. This establishes the law of excluded middle.

The Whole is Greater than the Part
Al-Razi states the first premise and proves it by contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi derives the premise 'the whole is greater than the part' from the principle of non-contradiction and why this premise is fundamental to mathematics and natural sciences.

Core Idea
The whole cannot be equal to its part without violating the law of non-contradiction.

In this passage, al-Razi shows that even our basic understanding of wholes and parts rests on the principle of non-contradiction. If the whole were not greater than its part, the last part would have to be both considered and not considered simultaneously—a contradiction. This simple yet profound observation grounds many mathematical and natural sciences.

Guided Path
1
Read the premise carefully
Read the Arabic passage for the first premise and its translation. Notice how al-Razi sets up the argument: he assumes the opposite and shows it leads to a contradiction.
The premise is derived from the principle of non-contradiction (النفي والإثبات لا يجتمعان).
2
Identify the contradiction
Al-Razi argues that if the whole were not greater than the part, the last part would be 'considered' because it is part of the whole and 'not considered' because it alone suffices for the whole. Spot how this violates the principle.
This shows the premise is necessary.
3
Reflect on the application
The commentary notes that this premise underlies many sciences, especially mathematics. Think of how 'the whole is greater than the part' is used in arithmetic and geometry.
Al-Razi does not elaborate here, but this is a key takeaway.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and proof. Al-Razi lists four premises that follow from the principle of non-contradiction (affirmation and negation cannot both be true). The first is that the whole is greater than its part; otherwise, the last part would be both considered and not considered, violating the principle. He notes that these two premises ground most mathematics (continuous and discrete quantity) and many physics topics (time, body). The third premise is that the judgment of a thing is the judgment of its likeness: if a distinction is not relevant to the judgment, then the essence alone is considered; otherwise contradictory judgments would result. This underlies many theological and philosophical discussions. The fourth premise is that the forms of syllogisms (three figures and two types of conditionals) are derived from the principle: each figure is explained by showing that violation leads to contradiction. This shows how fundamental the principle of non-contradiction is to logic and all reasoning.

Things Equal to the Same Thing are Equal
Al-Razi states the second premise and proves it by contradiction, then notes its broad applications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi derives the premise 'things equal to the same thing are equal' from the principle of non-contradiction and why this premise is foundational to mathematics and physics.

Core Idea
If things equal to the same thing were not equal, their realities would be both one and not one.

Al-Razi now turns to a second premise that follows from non-contradiction: equality is transitive. He shows that if two things are each equal to a third, they must be equal to each other; otherwise, their reality would be both one and not one. This premise, together with the first, grounds most of mathematics and much of physics.

Guided Path
1
Read the second premise
Read the Arabic and translation. Note the logical structure: al-Razi defines equality in terms of reality (حقيقة).
The term 'reality' (حقيقة) here means essence or nature.
2
Understand the argument
If two things are equal to a third, they share the same reality. If they were not equal, their realities would differ, leading to a contradiction.
The contradiction is that their reality is both one and not one.
3
Note the application
Al-Razi states that this premise (with the first) yields most sciences of quantity (continuous and discrete) and many natural investigations. Reflect on how transitivity of equality is used in measurement and physics.
This shows the breadth of the principle of non-contradiction.

The Judgment of a Thing is the Judgment of Its Likeness
Al-Razi states the third premise, gives its logical justification, and notes its applications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi derives the premise 'the judgment of a thing is the judgment of its likeness' from the principle of non-contradiction and why it is crucial for theological and philosophical reasoning.

Core Idea
If two things are identical in essence, they must have the same judgment; otherwise, contradiction arises.

Al-Razi presents a third premise that extends the principle of non-contradiction to judgments about essences. If a distinction between two things is irrelevant to the judgment, then those two things must be judged the same. Denying this would mean both affirming and negating a judgment of the same essence, a contradiction. This premise is widely used in theology and philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Read the third premise
Read the Arabic and translation. Identify the key terms: 'determination' (التعين), 'criterion' (المناط), 'essence' (الماهية).
Think of 'criterion' as the basis for the judgment.
2
Understand the condition
The premise applies when the indication (الدلالة) shows that the particular determination (which distinguishes individuals) is not part of the criterion for judgment. Then only the essence remains.
This means we abstract from accidental differences.
3
Grasp the contradiction
If that same essence is judged differently in two situations, then the essence is both subject to that judgment and not, violating non-contradiction.

Core Idea

Commentary
This passage presents the sixth premise, a key argument in kalam for the reality of accidents (a'rad) as distinct from the substance (jawhar). Al-Razi argues from observation: we see a substance (dhat) move after being at rest. The substance itself persists through both states, but the states of motion and rest themselves change. If the substance were identical to motion, then when it becomes at rest, the substance would cease to exist (since motion no longer exists). But we know the substance continues. Therefore, motion and rest must be additional attributes superadded to the substance. If they were identical, the substance would have to be both motion and rest simultaneously, a contradiction. Al-Razi endorses this premise as sound and strong.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: This argument proves that substances are composed of atoms.
Correction: This argument does not address atomism; it only shows that motion and rest are distinct from the substance, not that the substance is composed of discrete parts.

Key Takeaway
Motion and rest are not identical to the substance but are superadded accidents, as shown by the persistence of the substance through change of states.

Are your deeds merely passing accidents, or do they define who you are? How does this passage force you to reconsider the weight of every action you choose?
?
You claim to have a stable faith, yet your state of iman fluctuates. Are you willing to admit that your faith is an accident that can increase and decrease, requiring constant renewal? Or do you mistakenly identify your essence with your current state, risking despair when weak and pride when strong?
?
If motion and rest are additional to the substance, then what you do is not you—but it is yours to choose. Does this thought terrify you or liberate you? What state will you choose to attach to your essence today, knowing it passes but leaves a mark on your soul?
