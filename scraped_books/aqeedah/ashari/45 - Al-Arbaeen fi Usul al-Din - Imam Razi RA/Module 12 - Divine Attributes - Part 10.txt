The Seventh Proof: Allah Forgives All Sins – Objection and Answer
Razi uses the verse 'Allah forgives all sins' and responds to an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Razi defends the universality of divine forgiveness for believers, responding to an objection based on the apparent meaning of the Qur'anic verse.

Core Idea
God's promise to forgive all sins is certain for every believer, without requiring repentance as a literal condition of the verse.

In this passage, Imam al-Razi presents the seventh proof for the possibility of forgiveness for major sins: the verse 'Indeed, Allah forgives all sins.' An objector argues that the verse seems to promise forgiveness for everyone, including disbelievers, which contradicts the Ash'ari position. Al-Razi responds by clarifying the nature of the verse as a promise of future action, and shows that it applies to all believers who will eventually be purified from Hellfire.

Guided Path
1
Read the verse and the objection
Begin by reading the verse 'Indeed, Allah forgives all sins' and the objector's claim that the verse implies universal forgiveness, which the author does not hold.
Notice the objector points out an apparent inconsistency in the author's position.
2
Consider the objector's concession
The objector concedes the verse might mean forgiveness with repentance, giving two reasons: it applies to all sins in its apparent meaning, and the following verse commands repentance.
These reasons are based on textual context and consistency.
3
Follow al-Razi's answer
Al-Razi responds by interpreting the verse as a promise of future forgiveness. We are certain that God will bring every believer out of the Fire, so forgiveness is certain for all believers.
This answer removes the need to restrict the verse to repentance.
4
Reflect on the implications
Consider how this interpretation defends the Ash'ari view that major sinners are ultimately forgiven, without contradicting the apparent meaning of the verse.

Commentary
This passage presents the seventh proof and an objection-and-answer. The proof is the verse 'Indeed, Allah forgives all sins' (39:53). The objector says the verse seems to promise decisive forgiveness for every sinner, but the author does not hold that position (since he only hopes but is not certain, and excludes polytheists). The objector claims inconsistency. The objector then concedes the verse might mean forgiveness with repentance, but says that interpretation is better for two reasons: (1) It applies to all sins as the apparent meaning; the alternative (forgiveness without repentance) would contradict the verse's apparent sense because God does not forgive disbelief without repentance. (2) The following verse commands turning to God, which is repentance. The author answers that the verse is a promise of future action. We are certain that God will bring all believers out of the Fire, so forgiveness will indeed occur for everyone who believes, without needing to restrict it to repentance. Thus the verse is general regarding believers.

Razi's Proof from the Verse of Wilaya for Ali's Imamate
Razi analyses the word 'wali' and argues the verse indicates Ali's imamate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi uses linguistic analysis, logical deduction, and consensus to prove that the verse of wilaya specifically indicates the imamate of Ali.

Core Idea
The verse of wilaya proves Ali's imamate through the exclusive meaning of 'wali' and consensus of interpretation.

In this passage, Razi presents a detailed logical argument for a key Shi'a proof of Ali's imamate. He begins by analyzing the word 'wali', ruling out the general meaning of 'helper' due to the restrictive particle 'innama'. He then shows that the remaining meaning—'disposer' or 'guardian'—points to an imam, and concludes through consensus and agreed tafsir that this imam is Ali.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two meanings of 'wali'
Read the opening where Razi classifies 'wali' into 'disposer' and 'helper', with examples from hadith and Quran.
Note that he minimizes ambiguity by insisting on one literal meaning.
2
Eliminate 'helper' using 'innama'
Follow Razi's linguistic argument: the word 'innama' restricts the meaning, whereas 'helper' is general among believers. So the verse cannot mean 'helper'.
He cites Surah at-Tawbah 71 to show that 'helper' is general.
3
Conclude that 'wali' means 'disposer'
Since the helper meaning is impossible, the only remaining literal meaning is 'disposer', which implies authority over the community—i.e., imamate.
The disposer over the whole community is the Imam.
4
Examine the two proofs for Ali
Razi presents two aspects: (1) consensus that no one else is claimed for this verse, and (2) exegetes agree that the descriptive phrase refers to Ali.
Both proofs rely on established scholarly agreement.
5
Reflect on the method
Consider how Razi combines lexical analysis, logical elimination, and scholarly consensus to construct a compelling argument for the imamate.
This method is typical of kalam reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is an argument proving that the verse in Surah al-Ma'idah (55) indicates the imamate of Ali ibn Abi Talib. First, Razi classifies the word 'wali' into two meanings: 'mutasarrif' (one who disposes) and 'helper/nasir'. He then argues that in the verse 'innama waliyyukum Allahu wa rasuluhu wa alladhina amanu', the word 'wali' cannot mean 'helper' because the verse uses 'innama' (only) which restricts it, whereas the help of believers is general (as in Surah at-Tawbah 71 where believers are awliya' to each other). So it must mean 'mutasarrif' (disposer). The disposer over the community is the Imam, so the verse points to a specific imam. Then he gives two 'wajh' (aspects) to specify that this person is Ali. The first wajh: the community is split on whether the verse indicates imamate at all—some say no, some say it indicates Ali's imamate. No one says it indicates someone else. So if the verse indicates imamate (as established), it must be Ali, otherwise a third opinion breaks consensus. The second wajh: exegetes agree that the descriptive phrase 'those who establish prayer and give zakah while bowing' refers to Ali. Since the verse speaks of those believers described as such, and the description fits Ali, the verse indicates Ali's imamate.

The Two Premises: Perfection/Deficiency and Good/Evil
Al-Razi introduces two foundational premises of kalam and explains their interrelation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two overarching premises — perfection/deficiency and good/evil — that serve as the rational foundation for most theological arguments in kalam and philosophy.

Core Idea
Two premises anchor all kalam: one on attributes, one on actions.

Al-Razi begins a new section revealing the conceptual scaffolding used by theologians. He identifies two overarching premises that generate most theological arguments. The first concerns perfection and deficiency in attributes; the second concerns good and evil in actions. Understanding these premises is key to following the rest of his discussions.

Commentary

The supporter of tanzih argues that if God were a body or substance or in a place, He would resemble deficient creatures, and resembling the deficient is itself a deficiency. This shows how the premise is applied in theology. The passage cuts off at the beginning of this example.

Perfection/Deficiency in Divine Attributes
Al-Razi presents the Mu'tazila and Sunni arguments on perfection/deficiency in divine attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila and Sunni arguments regarding perfection/deficiency in divine attributes, specifically the nature of God's speech.

Core Idea
Both Mu'tazila and Sunni defend God's perfection, but disagree on what perfection entails.

Al-Razi presents two opposing views on divine attributes, focusing on God's speech. The Mu'tazila argue that if God's speech is eternal, He would have commanded continuously even with no one to command—a deficiency. The Sunni counter that if God acquires an attribute anew, that implies change from deficiency to perfection, also unacceptable. This lesson examines these arguments and their implications.

The Eternity of All Divine Attributes
Razi proves all attributes are eternal via two-case argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why all of Allah's attributes, whether negative or positive, are necessarily eternal according to Razi's second premise.

Core Idea
All divine attributes are eternal because they depend on the eternal essence.

Razi continues building his framework for divine attributes. After establishing the necessity of existence, he now argues that every attribute of Allah must be perpetual. The passage presents a proof by disjunction: whether the essence alone suffices for the attribute or not, the result is the same — the attribute is eternal.

Guided Path
1

First, read the Arabic passage and its literal translation to get a sense of the overall claim and structure.
Notice the concise style — Razi packs a logical argument into a few lines.
2

The proof is built on a disjunction: either the essence suffices for the attribute, or it does not. Identify each case clearly.
The first case is 'in kafat' (if it suffices), the second is 'wa in lam takf' (and if it does not).

For each case, follow how the conclusion of perpetuity (dawām) follows. In the first, the attribute is perpetual because the essence is. In the second, dependence leads to a self-necessary being, which is eternal, so the attribute is eternal too.
Both cases lead to the same result: the attribute is eternal.

Commentary
This passage is a premise (muqaddima) for the discussion of divine attributes. It argues that all of God's attributes, whether negative (like independence from need) or positive (like knowledge and power), are necessary and eternal. That self-necessary being would then necessitate the attribute, so again the attribute is eternal because its cause is eternal. In either case, the attribute is eternal. This argument is used to establish that God's attributes are not acquired or contingent.
