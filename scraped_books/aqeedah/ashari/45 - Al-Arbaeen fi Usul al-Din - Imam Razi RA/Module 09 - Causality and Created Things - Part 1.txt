Refutation of Kawn Causality: The Dilemma
Razi refutes the later scholars' view that ka'iniyya is caused by a meaning (kawn).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's refutation of the theory that spatial occurrence requires a separate cause (kawn), and the two impossible consequences he derives: circularity (dawr) and separation of cause from effect.

Core Idea
Postulating a hidden cause for spatial location leads to circularity or separation of cause and effect — both impossible.

Razi now tackles a specific theological theory: that an atom's being in a particular place is caused by a meaning called kawn. He shows that this theory collapses into one of two logical absurdities. By following his dilemma, you will see how kalam reasoning exposes hidden contradictions.

Guided Path
1
Read the dilemma
Read the first part where Razi sets up the two possibilities. Notice the logical structure: either the meaning depends on location or it does not.
This is the classic 'division' (qisma) technique in kalam.
2
Examine the first horn
See why circularity (dawr) follows if the meaning's subsistence depends on the substance being in that space. Trace the mutual dependency.
Circularity is impossible because it would mean each depends on the other.
3
Examine the second horn
If the meaning does not depend on location, then it could exist without the substance being there, meaning the cause exists without its effect — also impossible.
This is the principle that a cause cannot be separated from its effect.
4
Review the second argument and objection
Razi adds a second argument based on the nature of accident subsistence, then notes an objection that requires further investigation.
This shows Razi's intellectual honesty in acknowledging potential counterpoints.

المسألة الأولى وهذا القول عندنا باطل وذلك لأن المعنى الذي يوجب حصول الجوهر في حيز معين إما أن يتوقف صحة قيامه بالجوهر على كون ذلك الجوهر حاصلا في ذلك الحيز وإما أن لا يتوقف عليه. والأول باطل لأنا إذا عللنا حصول ذلك الجوهر في ذلك الحيز بذلك المعنى كان حصول ذلك الجوهر في ذلك الحيز محتاجا إلى قيام ذلك المعنى به وقيام ذلك المعنى به يكون محتاجا إلى حصول ذلك الجوهر في ذلك الحيز فيلزم الدور وهو محال. والثاني أيضا باطل لأن على هذا التقدير يجوز حصول ذلك المعنى عندما لا يكون ذلك الجوهر حاصلا في ذلك الحيز وذلك يقتضي جواز حصول العلة منفكة عن معلولها وهو محال. فتعين أن تعليل الكائنية بالمعنى يفضي إلى أحد هذين الباطلين. والدليل عليه أنا بينا أن كون العرض قائما بالجوهر معناه حصول ذلك العرض في ذلك الحيز تبعا لحصول محله فيه. فلو كان حصول الجوهر في الحيز معللا بمعنى قائم به لكان حصول ذلك الجوهر في ذلك الحيز تبعا لقيام ذلك المعنى من حيث أن المعلول تبع للعلة. فيلزم كون كل واحد منهما تبعا للآخر وهو محال. ولقائل أن يقول إن عنيت بالتوقف آخر الموقوف عليه لا يلزم وهذه الحجة فيها بحث.
The First Issue: This statement is, in our view, false. This is because the meaning that necessitates the occurrence of the substance in a particular space: either the validity of its subsisting in the substance depends on that substance being occurring in that space, or it does not depend. The first is false, because if we explain the occurrence of that substance in that space by that meaning, then the occurrence of that substance in that space would be in need of the subsistence of that meaning in it, and the subsistence of that meaning in it would be in need of the occurrence of that substance in that space—thus circularity (dawr) follows, which is impossible. The second is also false, because on this assumption it is possible for that meaning to obtain when that substance is not occurring in that space, and that would imply the possibility of the cause being separated from its effect, which is impossible. So it is determined that explaining ka'iniyya by the meaning leads to one of these two impossibilities. The proof of this is that we have shown that the accident's subsisting in the substance means that accident's occurrence in that space as a consequence of the occurrence of its locus in it. If, therefore, the occurrence of the substance in space were caused by a meaning subsisting in it, then the occurrence of that substance in that space would be a consequence of the subsistence of that meaning, since the effect follows the cause. This would entail each of them being a consequence of the other, which is impossible. And someone might say: 'If you mean by dependency [i.e., the last thing depended upon], it does not follow.' And this argument requires investigation.
Source pages p. 12

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the causal theory that ka'iniyya (spatial occurrence) is caused by a meaning called kawn. The author (Razi) presents a dilemma: either the meaning's subsistence in the substance depends on the substance being in that space, or it does not. If it does, circularity arises because the meaning's subsistence requires the substance's occurrence, and the substance's occurrence requires the meaning's subsistence. If it does not, then the meaning could exist without the substance being in that space, which means the cause could exist without its effect—also impossible. Thus both horns lead to absurdity. He adds a second argument: since an accident's existence in a substance means it follows the substance's location, if the substance's location were caused by a meaning, then the substance's location would follow the meaning's subsistence, and simultaneously the meaning's subsistence follows the substance's location (since it inheres), resulting in mutual consequence, which is impossible. The passage ends with an objection: perhaps the opponent means something else by 'dependency'. Razi acknowledges the argument requires investigation.

Razi's Infinite Regress Argument Against Accidents
Razi presents a third argument with objection and refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's infinite regress argument against the possibility of accidents inhering in bodies and why the objection that 'being a locus' is not a real attribute fails.

Core Idea
If a body becomes a locus for an accident, then its 'locus-ness' itself requires an accident, leading to infinite regress.

This passage presents a sophisticated argument that challenges the very notion of accidents inhering in bodies by showing that the attribute of 'being a locus' would itself be an accident, leading to an impossible infinite chain. Razi then answers a natural objection by distinguishing between the essence of an accident and its inherence. The argument is a classic example of reasoning about infinite regress (tasalsul) in kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to grasp the flow: Razi posits a body becoming a locus for an accident, then shows that the 'being a locus' becomes another accident, leading to infinite regress.
Notice the structure: argument, objection, refutation.
2
Identify the infinite regress
Trace the chain: body is not a locus → accident occurs → body becomes locus → that locus-ness is a new accident → then its own locus-ness is another accident, and so on ad infinitum.
This is the core of the argument.
3
Examine the objection
The objection claims that 'being a locus' is merely the occurrence of the accident, not a separate attribute. Understand this counterpoint.
The objection tries to stop the regress by denying the reality of locus-ness.
4
Analyze Razi's refutation
Razi argues that one can know the accident's essence while being ignorant of its inherence, proving that inherence is an additional attribute. This undermines the objection.
This distinction is key: essence vs. attribute of inherence.

Source Text

الوجه الثالث : أن الجسم قبل حدوث العرض المعين فيه ما كان محلا لذلك العرض، ثم إذا حصل ذلك العرض فقد صار محلّا لذلك العرض فيلزم أن يكون كونه محلا لذلك العرض عرضًا آخر ثم إنه على هذا التقدير يصير محلا لذلك العرض الآخر، بعد أنه ما كان كذلك. فيلزم أن يكون كونه محلا لتلك المحلية عرضا آخر ويلزم التسلسل. لا يقال إنه لا معنى لكونه محلا لذلك العرض، وكون ذلك العرض حالاً إلا مجرد حصول ذلك العرض فيه. لأنا نقول : ليس كون الجسم محلّا لذلك العرض، وكون ذلك العرض حالا في الجسم، هو نفس ذلك العرض ولذاته ؛ لأنه يمكن العلم بذات ذلك العرض، وذات ذلك الشك في كون ذلك العرض حالا في ذلك الجسم، وكون ذلك الجسم محلا لذلك العرض والمعلوم مغاير لما ليس بمعلوم. فعلمنا أن المحلية والحالية صفتان مغايرتان لذات ذلك العرض، ولذات ذلك الجسم.
The third point: Before the occurrence of a specific accident in it, the body was not a locus for that accident. Then when that accident occurs, it becomes a locus for that accident. So its being a locus for that accident would be another accident. Then on this assumption, it becomes a locus for that other accident after not being so. So its being a locus for that locus-ness would be another accident, and an infinite regress follows. It cannot be said that there is no meaning to its being a locus for that accident and that accident's being inherent except the mere occurrence of that accident in it. For we say: The body's being a locus for that accident and that accident's being inherent in the body is not the essence of that accident itself; because one can know the essence of that accident and be ignorant of the accident's being inherent in that body and the body's being a locus for that accident. The known is distinct from what is not known. So we know that locus-ness and inherent-ness are two attributes distinct from the essence of that accident and the essence of that body.

Commentary
This passage is a proof against the possibility of accidents inhering in bodies, taken from the perspective of the Ash'ari critique. The argument runs: If a body becomes a locus for an accident, then that 'being a locus' (mahalliyya) is a new attribute that itself must inhere in the body, making it a locus for that attribute, and so on ad infinitum. The objection that 'being a locus' is nothing but the mere occurrence of the accident is refuted: one can know the essence of the accident while being ignorant of its inherence, proving that inherence is an additional attribute. This infinite regress (tasalsul) is considered impossible, thus the initial assumption that accidents inhere in bodies is problematic.

Motion as Transition: Razi's Refutation of Location-Based Definition
Razi refutes the definition of motion as mere location and defines it as transition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that motion is a transitional process rather than a static state of being in a location.

Core Idea
Motion is transition, not arrival.

Razi engages with an opponent who defines motion and rest merely as being in a location, differing only accidentally. The opponent concedes that change implies a positive state but questions why motion cannot be positive. Razi responds by rejecting the reduction of motion to location-occupation. He argues that motion is the transition from one location to another, which is prior to and distinct from arriving at the second location.

Guided Path
1
Understand the opponent's position
The opponent concedes that 'it was not then it was' implies a positive state, and asks why motion cannot be positive and rest its absence. Then they define motion and rest both as 'being in a location', differing only accidentally.
Notice the opponent tries to reduce motion to a static state.
2
Follow Razi's proof that motion ends upon arrival
Razi rejects the definition. He argues that if motion were simply being in the second location, then once that location is reached, motion would cease. But the end of a thing is not the thing itself.
This is a key logical move: the endpoint ≠ the process.
3
Grasp the definition of motion as transition
Razi defines motion as 'being in transition from the first location to the second' (intiqal). This transition precedes arrival and is the essence of motion.
Motion is a process, not a state.

The Possibility of Necessarily Stationary Bodies
Razi objects that necessary rest in bodies could allow eternal bodies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi challenges the proof that all bodies are created by suggesting that some bodies might be necessarily at rest in specific places.

Core Idea
A body that cannot move could be eternal, undermining creation proofs.

In this passage, Razi presents a critical objection to the kalam cosmological argument that all bodies are created. He concedes a premise but then questions the assumption that rest is always possible. By raising the possibility of bodies with necessary rest, he forces the opponent to strengthen their proof. Let's explore his reasoning.

He concedes that an eternal positive does not cease.

The observation of motion does not prove that rest is always possible; some bodies might be necessarily stationary.
4
Grasp the counter-possibility
Razi asks: why can't some bodies be necessarily tied to specific places? If this is possible, such bodies could be eternal, defeating the proof that all bodies are created.
This is the core of the objection.

The Argument from Two Sides
Razi argues that any extended body must have two sides, implying motion is possible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that any extended body must have two sides equal in essence, and that this implies motion is possible.

Core Idea
If a body has two identical sides, motion from one to the other is possible.

This lesson unpacks a concise but powerful argument from Imam al-Razi. At first glance, the idea that every extended thing must have two sides seems obvious, but Razi uses it to build a rational proof for the possibility of motion. By the end, you'll see how this argument fits into larger kalām debates about the nature of bodies and temporal origination.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the English translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the concise style of Razi's argument.
2
Identify the two premises
The passage presents two premises: (a) every extended thing has two sides equal in complete essence, and (b) if two things are equal in essence, whatever is valid for one is valid for the other. Find each one and note how they are stated.
3
Understand the conclusion
The conclusion is that motion is valid for that extended thing. Ask yourself: why does this follow? The commentary will help you connect the premises.
Think about what 'valid' (صحة) means in this context.
4
Reflect on the significance
This argument shows that motion is not inherently impossible for bodies. Consider why Razi might need to prove this — what opposing view is he countering?
This connects to the broader topic of the temporal origination of bodies.
5
Test your understanding
Complete the checkpoint quiz to check if you have grasped the key conclusion.

قوله : كل متحيز فإنه لا بد وأن يفترض فيه جانبان متساويان في تمام الماهية. وكل شيئين متساويين في تمام الماهية فإنه يصح على كل واحد منهما ما يصح على الآخر، وذلك يقتضي صحة الحركة على ذلك المتحيز.

Commentary
This passage presents an argument for the possibility of motion in bodies. The opponent (or the argument itself) states that any extended being (mutahayyiz) must have at least two sides (janiban) that are equal in essence. If two things are equal in essence, then what is possible for one is possible for the other. Therefore, if one side can be the location of the body, the other side can also be the location, implying that the body can move from one side to the other. This proves that motion is possible for any extended body, which is used to argue that bodies are not eternal (since if they were eternal, motion would not be possible, but here it is).

Why Origination Cannot Cause Need
Razi proves that origination cannot be the cause of the effect's need.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Razi argues that origination (huduth) cannot be the cause of the effect's need for a cause, due to logical circularity.

Core Idea
Origination depends on existence; if it caused need, existence would need itself first.

Razi presents a tight logical refutation against those who think the temporal origination of a thing is what makes it need a cause. Third Proof: Temporality Does Not Cause Need for a Cause
Imam Razi presents the third proof that need for a cause is due to possibility, not temporality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why temporality (ḥudūth) cannot be the cause of an effect's need for a cause, based on Imam Razi's dependency argument.

Core Idea
Temporality depends on existence, so it cannot cause the need for a cause.

Imam Razi presents a third proof that the need of an effect for a cause is not due to temporality. He defines temporality as the state of being preceded by non-existence, then shows that this state depends on the existence of the thing itself, which in turn depends on a chain of causation. If temporality were the cause of need, an infinite regress would follow—an impossibility. This passage sharpens our understanding of causality in kalām.

The Fourth Proof: Possibility Demands a Cause
Imam Razi uses a dilemma to link possibility with causation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi uses the concept of specific possibility to prove that the need for a cause is due to possibility itself, not temporality.

Core Idea
Need for a cause stems from possibility, not from being temporal.

Imam Razi now presents his fourth proof, which refines the understanding of causality. He argues that if possibility of nonexistence requires a cause, then even a perpetual nonexistence would need a preponderator. Conversely, if not, then existence would be independent, which is absurd. The proof demonstrates that the need for a cause is intrinsic to the nature of possibility.

Proving That Wajub Is a Positive Attribute
Al-Razi answers an objection by proving wajub is positive with two proofs and a general rebuttal.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Wajub (necessity) is a positive attribute, not a negation—otherwise nothing could be necessary.

In this passage, al-Razi confronts a dilemma about God's knowledge: if it's necessary in itself, there would be two necessary beings; if it's possible and made necessary by God, the cause and effect would be co-eternal. To resolve this, he must first prove that necessity itself is a positive reality. His two proofs—using the concept of affirmation and the principle of contraries—establish that wajub is a positive attribute, thereby safeguarding the foundation of natural theology.

Refuting the First Answer on the Timing of Creation

Core Idea
Razi's refutation exposes that delaying creation by a specific time either entails a regress or forces the world to be eternal.

Imam Razi now turns to refuting the answers theologians gave for why the world was not created in eternity. The first answer claimed that God's will was attached to a specific time not yet present, thus delaying creation. Razi dismantles this from two angles, revealing that such an answer cannot coherently explain temporal creation while preserving God's free choice.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed refutation of the first theological answer, which claimed that God's will was attached to creating the world at a specific time, and that time was not present in eternity, so creation was delayed. Imam Razi refutes this from two aspects. The first aspect considers whether the eternal will is capable of being attached to different times or only to that specific time. If it is capable of multiple attachments, then preferring one time over others either requires no preponderator (leading to preponderance without a cause) or requires a preponderator, leading to infinite regress. If the will is incapable of attaching to other times, then God is not a free agent but a necessitating cause, implying the world must be eternal, not created in time. The second aspect examines whether God's will is conditioned by a specific time. If it is not conditioned, then the will in eternity should have immediately produced the world, making the world eternal. If it is conditioned by a specific time, then either that time is eternal (making the world eternal) or it is originated, leading to an infinite regress of conditions. Thus, the first answer fails because it either entails an impossibility (regress) or leads to the very conclusion (eternity of the world) that the theologians were trying to refute.
