The Third Case and Its Two Refutations
Razi refutes the third case with two arguments regarding incapability and equal powers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the third possibility (one god's object occurs) is impossible due to the equality of powers and the unfitness of incapability for divinity.

Core Idea
If one god's will is fulfilled, the other is incapable—and incapability negates divinity.

Having dismissed the first two outcomes, Imam Razi now tackles the remaining possibility: one god's object occurs while the other's does not. He provides two devastating reasons why this too is impossible. This lesson unpacks those reasons, showing how they rely on the indivisibility of the object and the equality of powers.

Guided Path
1
Read the third case and first reason
Read from 'or the object of one occurs without the other' to the end of the first reason. Note how the first reason points out that the god whose object does not occur would be incapable.
Incapability contradicts the definition of god as omnipotent.
2
Analyze the second reason
The second reason argues that since the object is single and indivisible, the two powers are perfectly equal. There is no basis to prefer one over the other.
This is a more subtle argument based on the nature of the object and power.
3
Connect to the overall proof
Understand that all three cases are impossible, so the original assumption of two gods is false.
This completes the proof by contradiction.

الحجة الثانية : الإله هو الذي يكون قادرا على المقدورات. ولو فرضنا إلهين أراد أحدهما حركة زيد والآخر سكونه. فإما أن يحصل المرادان، وهو محال، أو لا يحصل مراد واحد منهما وذلك أيضا محال لأن المانع لكل واحد منهما عن حصول مراده ليس هو مجرد كون الآخر قادرًا، بل حصول مقدور الآخر. فإذن لا يمتنع حصول مراد هذا . إلا إذا حصل مقدور ذاك ولا يمتنع حصول مراد ذاك، إلا إذا حصل مقدور هذا . فلو حصل مراد كل واحد منهما لحصل مقدور كل واحد منهما. وحينئذ يرجع هذا القسم الثاني إلى القسم الأول. وقد بينا أنه محال . أو يحصل مراد أحدهما دون الآخر.

إذا ثبت هذا، فنقول : لما حصل التساوي في قدرة هذين القادرين، فلو حصل أحدهما دون الثاني، لكان هذا ترجيحا لأحد المتساويين على الآخر، من غير مرجع البتة وذلك محال. فكان هذا القسم محالا . فثبت أن القول بوجود إلهين، يفضي إلى أحد هذه الأقسام الثلاثة ولما ثبت أن كل واحد منهما محال باطل كان القول بوجود إلهين محالا باطلا.
The second proof: The god is the one who is powerful over all things that are objects of power (al-maqdūrāt). And if we suppose two gods, one wanting Zayd's motion and the other wanting his rest, then either both objects will occur—which is impossible—or neither of the two objects will occur—which is also impossible, because the preventer for each of them from the occurrence of his object is not merely the other's being powerful, but rather the occurrence of the other's object. So the occurrence of the object of this one is not prevented unless the object of that one occurs, and the occurrence of the object of that one is not prevented unless the object of this one occurs. Thus if the object of each were to occur, the object of each would occur, and then this second case reverts to the first case, which we have shown to be impossible—or [it means that] the object of one occurs without the other. This too is impossible for two reasons: First, the one whose object occurs is powerful (qādir), and the one whose object does not occur is incapable (ʿājiz), and the incapable is not fit for divinity. Second, since the power (qudra) of each has the capacity to bring about that object (al-maqdūr), we say: it is impossible to say that one of these two capacities is stronger than the other, because the object is a single thing which does not admit of division at all. And if that is so, then it is either existent or nonexistent. As for the third case—that it becomes partly existent and partly nonexistent—that is impossible. And if the matter is so, then bringing about such an object is not susceptible to variation, and if it is not susceptible to variation, it is impossible for one of the two powers to be stronger than the other. Once this is established, we say: since equality holds between the powers of these two powerful beings, if one [object] occurs without the other, this would be a preference of one equal over the other with no basis at all, and that is impossible. So this case is impossible. Thus it is established that the claim of the existence of two gods leads to one of these three cases, and since it is established that each of them is impossible and false, the claim of the existence of two gods is impossible and false.

Commentary
This passage is a proof by contradiction showing the impossibility of two gods (ilāhīn). It is an argument from mutual opposition (burhān al-tamānuʿ). The proof begins by defining God as omnipotent over all things (qādir ʿalā al-maqdūrāt). It then sets up a scenario: suppose two gods, one wills Zayd's motion, the other wills his rest. Three outcomes are possible: (1) both objects occur—impossible because they are contradictory; (2) neither object occurs—impossible because the only preventer would be the occurrence of the other's object, leading to a vicious circle; this case collapses into the first; (3) one object occurs without the other. This third case is also impossible for two reasons. First, the god whose object occurs is powerful, the other is incapable, and incapability disqualifies one from divinity. Second, since the object is unitary indivisible, the two powers are perfectly equal—neither can be stronger. If one object occurs rather than the other, it would be an arbitrary preference of equals with no basis, which is impossible. Thus all three possibilities are impossible, so the assumption of two gods is false.

Objection and Answer: Two Gods Without Disagreement
Imam Razi answers the objection that two gods could exist without disagreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the existence of two gods is rationally impossible, even if they never disagree.

Core Idea
Two gods, even in perfect harmony, are impossible due to wisdom and the singularity of the best action.

Imam Razi continues his proof of divine oneness by addressing a subtle objection. The objector suggests that two gods could coexist if they never disagreed. Razi counters with two arguments: first, disagreement is actually unavoidable; second, even if it were avoidable, the attribute of wisdom would compel both gods to will exactly the same single best action, making one of them superfluous. This lesson unpacks both responses.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the opening objection carefully: why does the objector think two gods could exist without disagreement? Note that he claims the previous proof assumes disagreement must occur.
The objector is trying to find a loophole by postulating perfect harmony.
2
Analyze the first response: disagreement is inevitable
Razi's first argument shows that if each god alone could will opposite actions (e.g., motion vs. rest), and their attributes are eternal and unchanging, then together they still retain those capacities, leading to potential disagreement. Trace this reasoning step by step.
This argument uses the principle that eternal attributes do not change.
3
Examine the second response: wisdom forces identical wills
Even if disagreement were avoided, each god must be wise, and the wise always chooses the single best action. Since the best action in any situation is unique, both gods would will the same thing, eliminating disagreement but implying one god is redundant.
This argument is more direct: it shows harmony leads to a different problem.
4
Reflect on the overall argument
Consider how these two responses together close the loophole. The first makes disagreement likely, the second shows that even without disagreement, the existence of two gods is rationally untenable.
Note that the second argument is incomplete in the text; only the first of two points is given.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer unit. The objector claims that the three impossibilities in the second proof depend on the assumption that two gods would disagree. So why can't two gods exist in perfect harmony without any disagreement? The response first argues that disagreement is inevitable: if each god alone could will opposite things, and their attributes are eternal and unchanging, then even when together they retain those capacities, so disagreement is possible. Then the responder concedes that the objector's argument shows the permissibility of disagreement, but provides an independent argument proving that with two gods, disagreement is impossible anyway. The argument is that each god must be wise (ḥakīm), and the wise always chooses the single best action. Since the best action in any matter is unique, both gods would necessarily will the same thing, making disagreement impossible. This, however, does not solve the problem—if they both will the same thing, then one of them is redundant, and the original proof's third case (one object occurs) would still lead to the same issues. The Third Division: Why Two Gods Cannot Share Creation
Imam Razi proves two gods impossible by three absurd consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third division in Imam Razi's proof that two gods are impossible, and how it leads to the final conclusion.

Core Idea
If two gods were equal, neither could act without the other—impossible.

Imam Razi completes his proof against the existence of two gods by examining the third division: that a single act of creation could be split between two deities. He shows that this is impossible because it would imply variation in power or an arbitrary preference. This final argument seals the case for tawhid.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage carefully. Notice how Razi begins with the third division and then builds his argument.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Understand the third division
The third division is that an object of power (maqdur) is partially created by one god and partially by the other. Razi calls this impossible.
Why is it impossible? Because such an object would not admit variation.
3
Follow the equality argument
If the two powers are equal, their objects cannot vary in strength. This means neither power can be stronger, so any action by one over the other would be arbitrary.
Arbitrary preference (tarjeeh) without a cause is impossible.
4
Grasp the final conclusion
Since all three divisions are impossible, the existence of two gods is impossible. This proves the oneness of Allah.

Commentary
This passage is the final part of Imam Razi's proof against the existence of two gods. He presents the third division, in which a single object of power (maqdur) would be partially created by one god and partially by the other. This is impossible because such an object would not admit variation in its creation—if it did, one power would be stronger, which contradicts equality. Then he argues that if the two powers are equal, it is impossible for one to act without the other without a preponderator (murajjih), which is absurd. Thus all three divisions are impossible, so the existence of two gods is impossible.

The Necessity of Conflict Between Two Gods
Imam Razi responds to the objection that two gods could avoid conflict.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Imam Razi argues that conflict between two gods is unavoidable, based on the principle of divine immutability.

Core Idea
If each god alone could will opposite actions, their unchanging nature makes conflict necessary when together.

In this passage, Imam Razi addresses an objection to the argument against multiple gods. The objector suggests that two gods could coexist without ever conflicting. Razi responds by showing that given each god's ability to will opposite actions in isolation, and the eternal unchanging nature of their attributes, conflict must be possible when they are together. Let's examine this powerful reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice that the objector's challenge is placed first, then Razi's answer.
The objector says: why can't two gods exist without conflict?
2

The objector claims that the three divisions only apply if conflict occurs, but perhaps conflict can be avoided.
3
Understand Razi's reasoning
Razi argues from isolation to union: if each god alone could will opposite actions (e.g., movement vs. stillness of Zayd), and since their attributes are eternal and unchanging, they retain those wills when together, making conflict possible.
Key principle: the eternal cannot change.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Razi concludes that this proves conflict must be possible, which then supports the three divisions he previously set out.
This argument applies to both divine powers and wills.

The Third Proof Against Two Gods: Three Impossible Scenarios
Imam Razi presents the third proof: if two gods, any possible would be under both powers, leading to three impossible scenarios.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Razi's third proof that two gods cannot coexist because any possible would be subject to both equal powers, resulting in three logically impossible outcomes.

Core Idea
Shared omnipotence over one act leads to contradictions.

Imam Razi presents a rigorous logical argument against the possibility of two gods. He begins with the premise that if there were two gods, each would be equally omnipotent over all possible things. Then he considers what would happen if both will the same act. The result is three scenarios, each shown to be impossible. This proof is a classic example of kalam reasoning.

The third proof of the impossibility of the existence of two gods: If we suppose two gods, each one of them would be powerful over all possibles, because the attribute of being powerful (al-qadiriyya) in the case of each one of them is among the necessities of the essence, and the relation of that powerfulness is equal, so it is necessary that He Most High is powerful over all possibles. And if this is established, then it follows that everything that is within the power of one of them is exactly within the power of the other from one angle, namely the angle of bringing into existence and creation, except that this is impossible, because everything that is within the power of a powerful being, its bringing into existence is valid from him. And if each of them intends to bring it into existence, then either that thing within power occurs by both of them together, or it does not occur by either of them, or it occurs by one of them without the other. And the three divisions are false. As for the first division: its occurrence by both of them together—this is impossible because attributing the effect to the cause is only due to its possibility and permissibility, and the effect when taken with the complete cause is necessary of existence, and its being necessary of existence prevents its attribution to the second power. So the existence of the act with the second power prevents the attribution to the first power, so it follows that its state of occurring by each of the two powers is that it is necessarily disconnected from each one of them, which is a combination of two contradictories, and it is impossible. As for the second division: that it does not occur by either of them—this is also impossible, because that which prevents its occurrence by this power is its occurrence by that power, and that which prevents its occurrence by that power is its occurrence by this power. So this is not prevented unless that occurs, and that is not prevented unless this occurs. So if both were prevented, both would occur, and that is impossible, so a combination of negation and affirmation follows, and it is impossible. As for the third division: that it occurs by one of them without the other—this is also impossible for two reasons: first, each one of them we suppose to be powerful over all possibles, so one of them is not more powerful than the other, and if that is so, the preponderance of one over the other is impossible. Second, the occurrence of difference (al-tafawut) in power to bring into existence a single thing within power is impossible, since we have shown that difference in capability (al-iqtidar) requires the possibility of difference in the thing within power, and the thing that is one with a true unity, difference in it is impossible. So difference in power over it is impossible. So it is established that the claim of the existence of two gods leads to one of these three divisions, and it is established that each one of them is impossible, so the claim of the existence of two gods is impossible.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof (al-hujja al-thalitha) against the existence of two gods. Imam Razi argues that if two gods existed, each would be omnipotent over all possibles because the attribute of power is a necessary concomitant of their essence and is equal in both. Consequently, any possible would be within the power of both gods equally. Then, if both simultaneously intend to bring it into existence, three scenarios arise: (1) it occurs by both together, (2) it occurs by neither, (3) it occurs by one only. Razi refutes each. The first is impossible because attributing an effect to a cause depends on possibility; once the effect is actual with a complete cause, it becomes necessary, and a necessary effect cannot be attributed to another power. This would force the effect to be attributed to both yet disconnected from each—a contradiction. The second is impossible because the prevention of one power's effect depends on the other's effect, leading to mutual dependence such that if both are prevented, both must occur—a contradiction. The third is impossible because both have equal power, so no preponderance can occur; also, difference in power over a single object is impossible because difference in capability would require difference in the object, which is impossible for a truly unitary object. Thus all three possibilities are impossible, proving that two gods cannot exist.

The 'I' of each person refers to a unique self; if the soul were eternal, one would remember past states, yet we remember nothing from before birth. Therefore, the soul is not pre-eternal.

As for the second premise—which is the premise of necessity and possibility—this premise is of utmost honor and highness, and it is the ultimate goal of the intellects of the rational. They said: existence is either necessary or possible, and the possible must have a necessitator, and that necessitator must be necessary in essence and in attributes, for if it were possible, it would need another efficient cause. Commentary
This passage introduces the second premise in the overall discussion, which is the premise of necessity and possibility (muqaddimat al-wujub wa al-imkaan). It is described as the ultimate goal of the rational. It states the fundamental division of existence into necessary and possible, and that the possible needs a necessitator which must be necessary in essence and attributes, otherwise it would regress. Then it says there are two matters (amran). The first matter is the premise that God is necessary in essence, and this premise has two necessary consequences (laziman). The first consequence is that God is transcendent (munazzah) from multiplicity. This leads to two further points: first, that He is not a body, because bodies are divisible and thus not individual; second, that He is not in a direction. The second consequence is that there cannot be more than one necessary being, because two would share necessity and differ in identity, making each composite, which contradicts individuality. Then the second matter (the second of the two matters) is that the necessary being cannot be a substrate (mahall) or an indweller (hulul), because that would entail dependence.

Razi's Proof of the Oneness of the Necessary Being
Imam Razi presents a reductio ad absurdum proof that the Necessary Being must be one.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof for God's oneness based on the impossibility of composition in the Necessary Being.

Core Idea
Two Necessary Beings would each be composite, contradicting absolute simplicity.

Imam al-Razi presents a concise philosophical argument that there cannot be more than one Necessary Being. This proof relies on the principle that anything sharing a universal attribute while also having a particular distinction would be composite, but the Necessary Being is absolutely simple. Let's examine his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1

First, read the Arabic text silently, then the English translation. Notice how compact the argument is.
2

The argument begins with a supposition ('if there were more than one') and derives a contradiction ('this is contradictory'). Map out the two parts: assumption and conclusion.
3

Focus on the terms 'sharing' (اشتراك), 'specification' (تعين), and 'composition' (تركيب). Ask yourself: why does sharing and differing lead to composition?
The argument treats the shared aspect and the distinguishing aspect as two distinct constituents of each being.
4
Reflect on the contradiction
The Necessary Being is assumed to be simple (fard). The argument shows that multiplicity forces composition. Why is composition impossible for the Necessary Being?
Composition implies dependence on parts, which contradicts necessary existence.
5

Write down the proof in your own words in two or three sentences. Ensure you capture the logical flow: assumption, analysis, contradiction, conclusion.

وثانيهما : أن لا يكون واجب الوجود أكثر من واحد، إذ لو كان أكثر من واحد لاشتركا في الوجوب وبيانا في التعين وما به المشاركة غير ما به الممايزة، فيلزم كون كل واحد منهما في نفسه مركبًا، وقد فرضناه فردا، هذا خلف.
And the second of them: that the Necessary Existent is not more than one, for if there were more than one, they would share in existence (wujūb) and be distinct in specification (ta‘ayyun), and that by which they share is other than that by which they differ, so it would follow that each one of them is in itself composite (murakkab), while we have assumed it to be simple (fard); this is a contradiction (khalaf).
Source pages p. 474

Commentary
This passage is a proof of God's oneness (tawḥīd). It is the second of two points (the first is not included here). The argument is by reductio ad absurdum. Suppose there were two necessary existents. They would both share the property of being necessary (wujūb) – that is their commonality. But they must also be distinct from each other; their distinctness is due to some specification (ta‘ayyun) that differentiates them. If the shared aspect (participation) is different from the differentiating aspect (distinction), then each necessary existent would have two aspects: one shared, one unique. That would make each composite (murakkab). However, the Necessary Being is assumed to be absolutely simple (fard), not composed of parts. So the assumption of two necessary existents leads to a contradiction. Therefore, it is impossible for there to be more than one Necessary Being.
