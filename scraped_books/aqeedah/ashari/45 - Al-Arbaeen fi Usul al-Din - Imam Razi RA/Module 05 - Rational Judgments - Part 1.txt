The Three Rational Divisions of Possible Beings
Al-Razi enumerates the three rational divisions of possible beings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three rational categories into which all possible beings are divided, and the definition of the first category: that which occupies space (al-mutahayyiz).

Core Idea
All possible beings fall into three rational categories: space-occupying, subsisting in it, or neither.

Al-Razi begins with a foundational rational division that organizes all possible beings. This threefold classification provides the basic framework for ontological discussions in kalam. The first category, mutahayyiz, is crucial because it is further subdivided into body and atom in subsequent discussions.

Guided Path
1
Read the three categories
Read the Arabic passage and identify the three categories listed. Notice the Arabic terms: المتحيز, الحال في المتحيز, and the third category.
The third category is a negative description: neither mutahayyiz nor hall.
2
Understand the definition of mutahayyiz
Al-Razi defines mutahayyiz as that which can be pointed to sensorily. Think about what this means: anything that occupies space and can be located 'here' or 'there'.
This definition is broader than 'body'.
3
Reflect on the division logic
Consider why this division is exhaustive and mutually exclusive. Every possible being must either occupy space, subsist in something that occupies space, or be completely independent of both.
This division sets the stage for discussing God, who is the third category.

And it [the possible being] is, according to rational division, into three categories: that which occupies space (al-mutahayyiz), that which subsists in that which occupies space (al-hall fi al-mutahayyiz), and that which is neither occupying space nor subsisting in that which occupies space. As for the first category, which is that which occupies space, know that the meaning of 'that which occupies space' is that which can be pointed to with a sensory indication, as being here or there. And if you understand the reality of that which occupies space, we say: That which occupies space is either divisible or not divisible. That which occupies space and is divisible is called body (jism). On this basis, the body is that which is composed of two or more parts. The Mu'tazila say: The body is that which is long, wide, and deep, and the minimum of a body is obtained from eight parts. This dispute is linguistic, not rational. As for that which occupies space and is indivisible, it is called the individual atom (al-jawhar al-fard). People have differed about its affirmation, and we will mention this issue in detail, God willing.

Commentary
This passage presents the rational division of possible beings into three categories: (1) that which occupies space (mutahayyiz), (2) that which subsists in what occupies space (hall), and (3) that which is neither. Al-Razi then focuses on the first category. He defines mutahayyiz as that which can be pointed to sensorily. Then he subdivides mutahayyiz into that which is divisible (body) and that which is indivisible (the atom). For a body, he gives the standard kalam definition: composed of two or more parts. He notes the Mu'tazilite definition (length, width, depth from eight parts) and says this is merely a verbal difference. He then introduces the atom (jawhar fard) as the indivisible component, noting that its existence is controversial and will be discussed later.

Body and Atom: Subdivisions of the Occupant of Space
Al-Razi subdivides mutahayyiz into body and atom and discusses their definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi subdivides the category of mutahayyiz into divisible (body) and indivisible (atom), and the nature of the linguistic dispute with the Mu'tazila regarding the definition of body.

Core Idea
Every space-occupant is either divisible (body) or indivisible (atom).

After defining the general category of mutahayyiz, al-Razi now focuses on its internal division. He introduces the concepts of body (jism) and individual atom (jawhar fard). He also addresses a terminological disagreement with the Mu'tazila, emphasizing that their differing definition is merely verbal, not substantive.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subdivision
Read the passage and note that mutahayyiz is divided into two based on divisibility: divisible and indivisible.
This is a dichotomy: every mutahayyiz is either divisible or not.
2
Learn the definition of body
Al-Razi defines body as that which is composed of two or more parts. Contrast this with the Mu'tazilite definition which requires length, width, depth from eight parts.
Al-Razi considers this a linguistic dispute, not a rational one.
3
Understand the atom
The indivisible mutahayyiz is called the individual atom (jawhar fard). Note that its existence is controversial and will be discussed later.
The atom is the smallest possible unit of space-occupation.

The First Proof for the Temporality of Bodies: The Dichotomy of Motion and Rest
Razi presents the first proof for temporal origination and establishes its first premise: the dichotomy of motion and rest.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure of Razi's first proof for the temporality of bodies and the logical necessity of the dichotomy that every body must be either moving or at rest.

Core Idea
Every body occupies space, so it must either stay in one place or move — there is no third option.

Razi now moves from preliminaries to the first actual proof in the book: proving that bodies are temporally originated. He presents a conditional argument: if bodies were eternal, they would have to be either moving or at rest in eternity. Both are impossible, so bodies are not eternal. But before he can disprove each option, he must first establish that the dichotomy itself is exhaustive. This passage introduces that first premise: every body, being spatial, must be either stationary or mobile. This seemingly trivial point is the foundation on which the entire proof rests.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the literal translation. Let the flow of Razi's argument come through before interpreting.
Notice that Razi begins by wrapping up the introduction and then launches into the proof.
2
Identify the overall argument structure
Razi presents a conditional argument: if bodies were eternal, they would be either moving or at rest; both are false; therefore bodies are not eternal. He then says this proof requires three premises.
The first premise is the one detailed in this passage.
3
Understand the basis of the dichotomy
Razi argues that everything that occupies space (mutahayyiz) must be specific to a location. From this, he deduces that in eternity a body is either in one location (rest) or moving between locations (motion).
The key concept is 'occupying space'—any body can be pointed to as 'here' or 'there'.
4
Reflect on why this premise is necessary
Without this dichotomy, someone could claim that an eternal body is neither moving nor at rest, which would sidestep the proof. Razi closes that loophole by showing movement and rest are the only possibilities for a spatial entity.
Later premises will show that both options are impossible for an eternal being.
5
Summarize the first premise in your own words
Try to express the first premise concisely: every body, because it occupies space, must be either in a fixed location (rest) or changing location (motion).
This will help solidify the logical step before moving to the next premise.

Razi's First Proof: Impossibility of Eternal Motion
Razi proves that eternal motion is impossible by essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Razi argues that eternal motion is impossible due to the inherent priority in the definition of motion.

Core Idea
Motion by definition requires a prior state; eternity by definition has no prior — together they contradict.

Razi now presents the first proof within his second premise: that bodies cannot be in motion from eternity. He shows that the very nature of motion entails being preceded by something, while eternity excludes any such priority. This contradiction makes eternal motion impossible in itself.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the essence of motion
Read how Razi defines motion: it is a transition from one state to another. This is the core of the argument.
Focus on the Arabic: 'الحركة ماهيتها وحقيقتها أنها انتقال من حالة إلى حالة'.
2
Identify the necessary implication
Notice that every transition requires the prior existence of the state being left. Thus, motion necessarily involves being preceded by another (musabaqa bi-ghayr).
This is a logical necessity, not an empirical observation.
3
Contrast with the nature of eternity
Eternity (al-azal) means existence with no prior cause or state. Its essence negates being preceded.
Razi asserts that the two realities are mutually exclusive.
4
Conclude the impossibility
Since motion requires priority and eternity precludes it, the combination 'eternal motion' is a self-contradiction. It is impossible by its very essence (mumtani' li-dhatihi).
This is a concise apodictic proof.

This passage presents the first argument within the second premise of the main proof. Razi aims to show that bodies cannot be in motion from eternity. The argument is based on the very essence (mahiyya) of motion: motion is essentially a transition from one state to another, and every transition presupposes the prior existence of the state left behind. Thus, motion inherently requires being preceded (musabaqa) by something else (the initial state). Eternity (al-azal), by definition, means existence without any prior state or cause. Since motion entails priority and eternity precludes it, the two are mutually exclusive. Therefore, the concept of 'eternal motion' is a contradiction in terms—it is impossible by virtue of its own essence (mumtani' li-dhatihi). This is a concise apodictic proof.

Impossibility of Eternal Motion: The Regress Argument
Razi provides a second proof against eternal motion using an infinite regress of rotations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's second proof that an eternal series of rotations leads to a logical contradiction, requiring a beginning for all existences.

Core Idea
Eternal motion forces prior and posterior to coexist, which is impossible.

Razi now presents a more refined argument against the possibility of eternal motion. He considers an endless series of celestial rotations and shows that the non-existences preceding each rotation would all be simultaneous in eternity. This creates a dilemma: either some existence coexists with that totality of non-existences (contradiction) or no existence does, implying existence has a beginning. Follow the careful logical steps to see why eternal motion is rationally impossible.

Guided Path
1
Understand the supposition
Suppose each rotation of the celestial sphere is preceded by another rotation, with no first rotation. This means every rotation has a non-existence (its absence) that itself has no beginning.
This is an actual infinite regress, not a potential one.
2
Collect all non-existences in eternity
Since each non-existence is without beginning, all these non-existences are together in eternity (they are all 'before' any existence in an unordered sense). Only the existences (rotations) have temporal order.
Think of eternity as a single timeless moment containing all these absences.
3
Apply the dilemma
Either some existence (a rotation) occurs together with that totality of non-existences in eternity, or none does. The first option leads to a contradiction because it would make the prior (non-existence) simultaneous with the posterior (existence).
This is the core of the argument.
4
Draw the conclusion
Since the first option is impossible, we accept the second: no existence occurs in eternity. Therefore, existence must have a beginning and a first moment.
This proves that motion cannot be eternal; it must have started.

Motion Cannot Be Eternal: The First Argument
Razi argues that motion's nature requires temporal priority, contradicting eternity.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Motion's essence requires a prior state; eternity excludes any priority.

Razi begins his proof that bodies cannot be moving in pre-eternity by analyzing the very definition of motion. He argues that motion is not a self-sufficient reality; it depends on a prior state from which it transitions. The Fifth Proof: Finite Past via Mental Mapping
Razi uses imaginary mapping to prove that the past must be finite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's fifth proof that the series of past events must be finite, using an imaginary comparison of two infinite sets.

Core Idea
If the past were infinite, a larger infinite set would equal a smaller one, which is impossible.

Imam Razi now presents a subtle yet powerful argument for the finitude of past events. He asks us to imagine two sets of events: one from today back to pre-eternity and another from the time of the Flood back to pre-eternity. Through a mental mapping exercise, he shows that the only consistent conclusion is that both sets have a beginning. This proof avoids direct engagement with the infinite and uses logical impossibility instead.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two sets

The sets are imaginary, not actual historical lists.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. The objector denies that an eternal body must be either moving or at rest, because the evidence for that—namely, that every body must occupy a place—is problematic. The objector implies that the concept of 'place' (hayyiz) is not clear. Razi responds by analyzing the possible meanings of 'place'. He divides it into non-existent and existent. If place is non-existent, it is absurd for a body to be in nothingness. If place is existent, it either cannot be pointed to (impossible, since a body must be pointable) or can be pointed to. If pointable, it is either an independent entity (a body) or a dependent entity (an accident). If it is a body, then 'body in place' means 'body in body', leading either to interpenetration (impossible) or contact implying infinite bodies (denied by the objector). If it is an accident, then 'body in place' means 'body in an accident', but an accident exists in a body, so this causes circularity (accident in body and body in accident). Also, movement from place to place would mean movement from accident to accident, which is nonsensical. Thus all options are impossible, so the original division of body into moving or at rest based on being in a place is invalid. Razi concludes that the objection's foundation is corrupt.

And we say: It is not permissible for the eternity of the body to be additional to its being a body, because that additional attribute, if eternal, would entail that its eternity is additional to it, leading to infinite regress. And if it is originated, every originated thing has a beginning, and every eternal thing has no beginning. So if the eternity of the eternal were that originated thing, it would require that thing to have a beginning and not to have a beginning, which is impossible. If they object by saying it is originated, we say: The originated thing is the sum of existence occurring in the present and prior non-existence, and it is not unlikely to have knowledge of existence while ignorant of prior non-existence, unlike the eternal which has no meaning except its very existence. Thus the difference appears. This is a lofty point, containing subtle investigations. Let this be the end of our speech on explaining proofs of the occurrence of bodies, and God knows best.

Commentary
This passage is a formal proof (burhan) for the temporal origination (huduth) of bodies. It is a classification argument: either the eternity (qidam) of a body is the same as its being a body (jism), or it is an additional attribute. Both are refuted. If additional, then that additional attribute itself must be eternal or originated. If eternal, infinite regress. If originated, then it has a beginning, but the eternal thing has no beginning, contradiction. The proof is considered strong and subtle.

The First Proof: Realization Implies Existence
Razi's first proof that the non-existent is not a thing, using the concept of realization.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's first proof that if the non-existent were a thing, it would be existent, which is contradictory.

Core Idea
If essences were real in non-existence, they would be existent—an impossibility.

Razi begins his refutation by focusing on the concept of 'realization' (taḥaqquq). If the non-existent were a thing, it would have some reality. But reality in the external world is exactly what we mean by existence. This proof shows the absurdity of claiming that non-existent essences have any being.

This reinforces the idea that realization is not inherent to the essence.

Understand the conclusion
Since the hypothesis leads to an impossibility, the claim that the non-existent is a thing is false.
The proof is a reductio ad absurdum.

Commentary
This passage contains three proof's (barāhīn) that the possible non-existent (maʿdūm) is not a thing or entity. The first proof argues from the nature of realization (taḥaqquq) and establishment (taqarrur): if essences were realized externally without existence, their realization would be additional to their specific nature. But realization is exactly existence, so they would be existent and non-existent simultaneously, which is absurd. The intuitive distinction between simply conceptualizing 'blackness established in non-existence' and affirming that it is actually established externally shows that establishment adds something, namely existence. The second proof uses finitude: non-existent essences, if real, must be either finite or infinite. Finite is rejected by agreement (all consider the set of possible non-existent things potentially infinite?). Infinite is rejected by a cardinality argument: the set of all possible non-existent things before any existed is larger than the set after some exist (since some are now existent). But since the remaining set is smaller, it is finite, and the set that came into existence is also finite, so the total is finite, contradicting the assumption of infinity. Thus the hypothesis fails. The third proof is concise: all possible essences are contingent (mumkin) and thus originated (muḥdath). As originated, they are preceded by pure non-existence, so in themselves they are nothing before existence. This establishes the desired conclusion: the non-existent is not a thing.

The Second Proof: Finitude Argument
Razi's second proof that the non-existent is not a thing, based on finitude of the set of non-existent entities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's second proof that the set of non-existent entities cannot be infinite, so the non-existent cannot be things.

Core Idea
If non-existent entities were real, they would be finite—contradicting the infinite number of possibles.

Razi's second proof uses a quantitative argument. If non-existent entities were real, they must be either finite or infinite. The finite option is rejected by consensus, and the infinite option leads to a logical contradiction about cardinalities. Thus, the hypothesis fails.

Guided Path
1
Read the second proof
Read the Arabic and translation to understand the structure: a dichotomy between finite and infinite.
Note that 'by agreement' refers to the consensus that the number of non-existent entities is not finite.
2
Understand the infinite argument
Razi argues that if the set were infinite, then the subset remaining after some exist would be smaller, but any smaller set is finite—contradicting the assumption of infinity.
This is a reductio on the infinite nature.
3
Follow the cardinality reasoning
The totality before any existence is greater than the remainder after some exist. Since the remainder is less, it is finite. Then the sum of finite parts is finite.
4
Conclusion
Both options are impossible, so the original claim that non-existent entities are real is false.

Commentary
This passage contains three proof's (barāhīn) that the possible non-existent (maʿdūm) is not a thing or entity. The first proof argues from the nature of realization (taḥaqquq) and establishment (taqarrur): if essences were realized externally without existence, their realization would be additional to their specific nature. But realization is exactly existence, so they would be existent and non-existent simultaneously, which is absurd. The intuitive distinction between simply conceptualizing 'blackness established in non-existence' and affirming that it is actually established externally shows that establishment adds something, namely existence. The second proof uses finitude: non-existent essences, if real, must be either finite or infinite. Finite is rejected by agreement (all consider the set of possible non-existent things potentially infinite?). Infinite is rejected by a cardinality argument: the set of all possible non-existent things before any existed is larger than the set after some exist (since some are now existent). But since the remaining set is smaller, it is finite, and the set that came into existence is also finite, so the total is finite, contradicting the assumption of infinity. Thus the hypothesis fails. The third proof is concise: all possible essences are contingent (mumkin) and thus originated (muḥdath). As originated, they are preceded by pure non-existence, so in themselves they are nothing before existence. This establishes the desired conclusion: the non-existent is not a thing.

Proof That Non-Existents Are Distinct and Have Quiddities
Razi argues for the distinction of non-existents through possibility and impossibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why non-existent things must have distinct essences (quiddities) even though they do not exist in the external world, and how Razi argues for this using the distinction between possible and impossible.

Core Idea
Non-existents are not nothing—they have determinate essences that make them distinct.

Razi addresses a fundamental question: Do things that do not exist still have some kind of reality? In this passage, he argues that even non-existent entities are distinct from each other in their very essences. He uses the fact that God cannot create the impossible but can create the possible to show that these categories are inherently different, and then refutes the idea that their distinction might only be mental. This lesson unpacks his reasoning step by step.

2

Notice how Razi begins by stating that the possible and impossible are distinct in themselves. He proves this by pointing out that God can create possible things but cannot create impossible things. If they were not distinct, this difference in divine power would not hold.

This final step establishes that non-existents are not mere nothing.

Commentary
This passage presents the second argument of the Mu'tazila for the view that the non-existent (ma'dum) is something (shay'). The argument proceeds from the nature of possible existents. First, there is agreement that possible things exist. The possible is defined as that which can be described with existence and non-existence. For such a proposition to be meaningful, the subject must be present at the same time as the predicate. If the subject is the quiddity itself and the predicate is the possibility of its annihilation, then the meaning would be that the quiddity is established at the time of its annihilation, which is contradictory. Therefore, the correct interpretation of possibility is that the quiddity can be characterized by existence and can lack existence, which implies that the quiddity itself is something even when non-existent. Thus, the non-existent is something.

The third argument: Every existent is either impossible in existence in itself or conceivable in existence in itself, whether what is conceivable in existence is necessary of existence or not necessary of existence. Commentary
This passage presents the third argument of the Mu'tazila for the claim that the non-existent (ma'dum) is something (shay'). The argument uses the concepts of impossibility (imtina') and possibility (imkan) as real attributes. First, the author establishes that every existent is either impossible or possible (conceivable) in itself. These are contradictory opposites, so one must be a positive attribute. He reasons that impossibility cannot be an existing attribute because if it were, the thing described as impossible would have to exist to receive that attribute, but impossibility is supposed to prevent existence. Thus, impossibility is not a real attribute. Therefore, the opposite, conceivability or possibility, must be a real positive attribute. Next, the author applies this to originated things (hawadith). Before they come into existence, they are possible. They cannot be necessary (for then they would exist eternally) nor impossible (for then they could never come to exist). So they must be possible in themselves. Since possibility is a real attribute, it must inhere in a subject (mawsoof). That subject is the quiddity (mahiyya) of the thing, which must be determinate even before the thing exists. Thus, the quiddity is something, and therefore the non-existent (the thing before it exists) is something.

So it is established that we conceive the essence and reality of existence before the essence becomes qualified by existence. And because the opponent also concedes that the essence of existence is not determined in non-existence.

Commentary
This passage is the answer (jawaab) to the first proof that the non-existent is a thing. Razi argues that we have mental perception (shu'ur and idrak) of many things that are agreed to be neither essences (mahiyat) nor realities (haqa'iq), but rather pure negation and absolute non-existence. He provides five examples to demonstrate this.

First example: Knowledge of impossibilities (mumtani'at). We conceive and judge that a partner of God is impossible, that the conjunction of existence and non-existence is impossible, etc. These judgments involve mental concepts, yet all scholars agree that these impossibilities have no essences or realities in the external world. Thus mental awareness does not imply external reality.

Second example: Knowledge of composites (murakkabat) like a sea of mercury or a mountain of emerald. These are composed of substances and accidents in our mind, but such composites do not exist in reality (neither in non-existence nor in existence). Yet we have a clear mental picture.

Third example: Knowledge of relations (nisab) and relative attributes (idafat) such as location or future events. These relations are not fixed in non-existence, as even the opponent concedes, yet we have mental awareness of them.

Fourth example: Knowledge of the distinction between non-existence and existence, and between pure negation and affirmation. The concept of pure negation has no affirmation itself, yet we can distinguish it from affirmation. So we perceive something that has no reality.

Fifth example: Knowledge of the essence of existence (mahiyya al-wujud) before an object is qualified by existence. We can conceive existence itself, but existence is not fixed in non-existence (since non-existence is its opposite). So again, mental perception does not require external reality.

Overall, Razi refutes the opponent's claim that mental perception of the non-existent implies it has a fixed reality in non-existence. These five examples show that we can perceive many things that have no reality in the external world.

Commentary
This passage provides a definition and differentiation between two key terms in kalam: possibility (الإمكان) and origination (الحدوث). Possibility is defined as a property of a thing considered in itself: it is not impossible for it to exist or not exist. In other words, both existence and non-existence are equally possible for it. The definition specifies that the impossibility (of one of the two alternatives) is not essential; rather, the thing itself does not necessitate either. Origination, on the other hand, is defined as a property of existence: it is preceded by non-existence. The difference between the two is clear: possibility is a modal state of the essence, while origination is a temporal state of existence. This sets the stage for later arguments about the need for a determiner.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Confusing possibility with origination
Correction: Possibility is a modal state (neither necessary nor impossible), while origination is a temporal state (existence after non-existence).

Key Takeaway
Possibility concerns the essence's inherent neutrality, while origination concerns existence's beginning in time.
