Second Objection: Generality as Probable, Not Certain
Objection that general forms indicate generality only as probability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the epistemological distinction between certainty and probability in kalam arguments, and why general terms cannot serve as decisive proof in theological disputes even if their usage for generality is granted.

Core Idea
Even if words indicate generality, they only do so probably, not certainly.

This passage presents a key objection in kalam methodology. The Ashab concede to the Mu'tazila that linguistic forms are indeed used for generality, but then raise a deeper question: does that indication rise to certainty or only probability? By accepting probability and denying certainty, they argue that such evidence cannot settle a theological dispute. The argument is cut off here, but the fundamental move is clear: one can grant the opponent's premise and still win the argument by questioning its epistemic weight.

Three Proofs That the Definite Plural Does Not Indicate Universality
Imam Razi presents three arguments that the definite plural does not imply full generality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the definite plural form in Arabic is not automatically universal, and you will learn three arguments from Imam Razi supporting this.

Core Idea
Definite plurals are flexible; they do not always imply total inclusion.

Imam Razi, in his work Al-Arba'in, addresses an important linguistic and juridical principle: whether a definite plural noun necessarily indicates universality (all members of the category). He argues that it does not, and presents three independent proofs to demonstrate that the form can be used for both total and partial inclusion. This lesson examines those proofs.

Guided Path
1
Understand the claim
The issue is whether a definite plural (like 'the men') implies every individual. Imam Razi says no. Read the first sentence and the three proofs listed.
2
Analyze the first proof
Notice that you can say 'all' or 'some' after a definite plural without redundancy or contradiction. This shows the form itself does not imply completeness.
3
Analyze the second proof
Imam Razi argues that Qur'anic usage includes both universal and particular meanings; the default should be the common factor, which is indicating the majority without specifying totality.
4
Analyze the third proof
He uses the permissibility of questioning after a universal statement to show that the form is not specifically for generality. If it were, asking for details would be odd.
The third proof is about the good sense of asking 'which ones?' after a definite plural statement.

Commentary
This passage presents three proofs that the definite plural form does not definitively indicate total inclusion. The first proof shows that because one can add 'all' or 'some' independently to the same expression, the expression cannot itself imply total inclusion. The second proof notes that the Qur'an uses such forms sometimes for total and sometimes for partial inclusion, so the literal meaning must be the common denominator – namely, indicating a majority without specifying totality. The third proof uses the permissibility of asking for details after a universal statement: if the statement were definitively inclusive, specifying particulars would be redundant. Hence, these forms are not placed for definite universality.

Three Arguments That Indefinite Plural Indicates Probability
Imam Razi presents three proofs that indefinite plurals indicate probability, not certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Razi's three arguments that indefinite plural forms indicate probability rather than certainty, and why this distinction is critical in theological reasoning.

Core Idea
Indefinite plurals indicate probability, not certainty — three proofs dismantle claims of certainty.

Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi here presents the second station of objection to those who argue that plural forms indicate generality with certainty. He concedes that they are used for generality, but argues that their indication is only probable, not certain. This distinction is crucial because certain theological issues require certain evidence. Razi offers three independent proofs to establish the probabilistic nature of these forms.

Guided Path
1
Understand the issue at stake
Razi concedes that plural forms indicate generality, but asks whether the indication is certain or probable. Read the opening lines carefully.
This is a concession to opponents; the real argument is about certainty.
2
Analyze the first proof: the impossibility of emphasis
If the plural already indicated totality with certainty, adding emphasis would be impossible (because it would be redundant). Since emphasis is allowed, the indication must be probable.
The phrase 'تحصيل الحاصل' is key.
3
Examine the second proof: the source of knowledge
Knowledge that these words are for totality must come from either mutawatir or ahad reports. Since there is disagreement, it cannot be mutawatir, and ahad only gives probability.
This shows the indication is not established with certainty.
4
Study the third proof: common usage of 'all' for 'most'
People often use 'all' and 'plural' to mean 'most' as exaggeration, as in the Quranic verse. This shows the indication is not certain.
The example from Surah al-Naml 27:23 is cited.
5
Draw the conclusion
Since the indication is only probable, such evidence cannot be used in matters that require certainty, like the ones under discussion.
This closes the objection.

The Thirty-Fifth Issue. The second station of objection: we concede that these forms are placed for generality, but do they indicate generality with certainty or probability? The first is denied, the second is accepted. So we demonstrate that these forms indicate generality probabilistically. If that is the case, it is not permissible to rely on them in this issue. We say that they indicate total inclusion probabilistically, not certainly, for reasons: First, if these forms indicated total inclusion certainly, then the insertion of emphatic words upon them would be impossible, because the acquisition of what is already obtained is impossible, and since they have unanimously agreed that it is good to insert emphatic words upon them, we know that their indication of total inclusion is probable, not certain. Second, knowledge that these words are placed for total inclusion is obtained either by mutawatir report or by ahad report; the first is invalid, otherwise the disagreement would not have arisen, so the second remains, but ahad report only produces probability. Third, we see that people often express the majority by the word 'all' and the plural by way of exaggeration, as God said: 'And she was given of every thing' [Quran 27:23], and it is known that she was not given the Throne, the Footstool, Paradise, and Hellfire. So we know that common custom is to use the word 'all' and the plural for the majority as exaggeration. Since this is a common practice, we know that the indication of these words for total inclusion is a probable, possible indication, not certain. Having established this, we say: it is not permissible to rely on these words in this issue, because this issue is certain and relying on probable evidence in it is absolutely invalid.

Commentary
This is the second objection (المقام الثاني). The author concedes that the plural forms indicate generality, but asks whether they do so with certainty or probability. He argues for probability based on three clear proofs. The first proof uses the permissibility of adding emphatic words like 'all' to the expression; if the expression already indicated totality with certainty, adding an emphasis would be redundant and impossible. The second proof traces the source of knowledge about the linguistic indication: it cannot be from tawatur since there is disagreement, so it must be from ahad reports, which only give probability. The third proof shows that common usage often uses 'all' to mean 'most' as an exaggeration, citing the verse about the Queen of Sheba. Therefore, the indication is probable, and such evidence cannot be used in a matter that requires certainty.

The Objection of Specification and Razi's Response
Razi considers and answers the objection of possible specification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that general terms may be specified, why mere non-finding of specifiers does not prove their absence, and Razi's conclusion that general terms require evidence of no specification to yield certainty.

Core Idea
Not finding specifiers is not proof of their non-existence.

In this passage, Razi engages with a critical objection: even if words convey universality definitively, the possibility of specification (takhsis) remains. The objector argues that the opponent has not proven the absence of specifiers, only that they did not find any. Razi then responds to a potential counter-objection, affirming that without evidence excluding specification, general terms do not provide certainty.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the first part where the objector concedes definitive universality but insists on the condition of no specifiers. The opponent cannot demonstrate that no specifier exists.
Notice the key term 'al-mukhaṣṣiṣāt' (specifiers).
2
Analyze the weak inference
The objector points out that inferring non-existence from non-finding (ʿadam al-wujdān) is extremely weak. This is the core of the objection.
Contrast 'ʿadam al-wujdān' (non-finding) with 'ʿadam al-wujūd' (non-existence).
3
Follow Razi's response to counter-objection
If the objection were accepted, one might say no general term yields certainty. Razi agrees: without evidence of no specification, certainty is not achieved. This clarifies the epistemological status of general terms.
Razi does not reject the objection; rather, he endorses its consequence.

Specific Evidence Precedes General
Razi's first objection: specific verses override general ones.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's first objection: specific scriptural evidence takes precedence over general evidence, so the verses of forgiveness (specific) override the verses of punishment (general).

Core Idea
Specific overrides general in evidence.

Razi offers two detailed objections against relying on general punishment verses. The first is a logical principle: if the punishment verses must be general to support the opposing view, but the forgiveness verses are specific, then the specific evidence prevails. Let's examine this argument closely.

Guided Path
1
Read the first objection passage
Read the Arabic and translation for the first aspect only. Notice how Razi sets up the conditions for both sets of verses.
The key is the comparison: punishment verses must be general; forgiveness verses are specific.
2
Identify the principle
Razi invokes the rule 'al-khas muqaddam 'ala al-'amm' (the specific is given precedence over the general). Understand why this is a standard principle in Islamic legal and theological reasoning.
3
Apply the principle
Once you accept that forgiveness verses are specific and punishment verses are general, the conclusion follows necessarily: the forgiveness verses are given precedence.
This is a logical deduction, not a mere preference.

The Harms of Leadership vs. the Harms of Anarchy: A Rational Balance
Objection and answer weighing harms of leadership against benefits.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how rational weighing of harms (mafasid) and benefits (masalih) resolves the objection that appointing a leader may cause injustice.

Core Idea
When benefits and harms conflict, the decisive factor is the preponderant; avoiding a little evil at the cost of great good is itself a great evil.

Imam al-Razi presents a classic objection to the necessity of leadership: a leader can become oppressive or corrupt. Rather than dismissing these concerns, his answer embraces a rational calculus. By comparing the harms of having a leader with the far greater harms of anarchy, he shows that the rational mind must choose the lesser harm. This lesson unpacks that argument and the principle of preponderance (tarjih).

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Read the objection carefully: what specific harms does the objector list? (disdain for obedience, oppression, extortion). Understand that these are real concerns.
2
Analyze the response
Notice how the answer does not deny that these harms can occur. Instead, it shifts the argument to a comparison: the harms of having no leader are far greater.
This is a key move in kalam – acknowledging partial truths.
3
Understand the principle of tarjih
When benefits and harms conflict, the preponderant (arjah) is the criterion. The passage states: 'when conflict occurs, consideration is given to the preponderant.'
This is a universal rational principle used in Islamic legal theory and theology.
4
Apply the principle to this case
The answer argues that abandoning the great good of social order (from having a leader) to avoid a little evil (from potential leader misconduct) is itself a great evil. Hence, appointing a leader is necessary despite potential harms.

Instead, it uses a comparative weighing argument: every rational person knows that the harms from not having an obeyed leader are far greater than the harms from having one. When benefits and harms conflict, the decisive factor is what is preponderant (arjāh). Abandoning great good to avoid a little evil is itself a great evil. Thus, the potential harms of a leader are outweighed.

The Fifth Premise: Conceptual Distinction
Al-Razi states the fifth premise with a logical proof.

Core Idea
If two concepts were identical, knowledge would contradict itself.

Al-Razi now builds on the impossibility of opposites to establish a key methodological premise. The fifth premise asserts that if we can conceive of two things separately, they must be truly distinct. This simple idea underlies countless arguments in kalam and falsafa.
