Fourth Argument: God's Eternity Would Be Contingent
Fourth argument: God's necessity would be undermined by dependence on duration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth argument that if God's precedence depends on a duration, then God's existence would be contingent on that duration, contradicting His necessary existence.

Core Idea
Dependence on a duration would make the Necessary Existent contingent.

The fourth argument takes a different approach: if God's precedence over the world is constituted by a duration, then God's eternity and continued existence would be dependent on that duration. Anything dependent on something else is contingent (possible in itself). So the Necessary Existent would be contingent—a contradiction. Therefore, the precedence cannot be infinite (nor finite, for that matter).

Guided Path
1
Read the fourth argument
Read the Arabic. Notice the conditional: if precedence is established by a duration, then God's permanence depends on that duration.
2
Understand the meaning of 'dependent'
God's precedence is not just a temporal priority but an ontological priority. If that priority is mediated by a time duration, then God's existence becomes dependent on that time.
3
Apply the principle: dependent things are possible
Whatever depends on another is possible in itself (mumkin li-dhatihi). Therefore, God would be possible, not necessary.
This is a key kalam distinction: the necessary (wajib) cannot depend on anything.
4
Recognize the conclusion
This leads to a contradiction (khulf): the Necessary Existent would be contingent. So infinite precedence is impossible.

المسألة الرابعة. تقولون: الإله قديم والعالم محدث، وإن كان أحدهما قبل الآخر فتقدم أحدهما على الآخر إما أن يكون بمقدار متناه أو غير متناه، فإن كان بمقدار متناه كان كل واحد منهما محدثاً، وذلك محال، وإن كان بمقدار غير متناه فذلك محال لوجوه. الأول: أنه على هذا التقدير قد يكون انقضاء قبل اليوم مدة غير متناهية، وانقضاء ما لا نهاية له غير معقول. الثاني: أنه إن كان يتوقف حصول اليوم على أن يقضي قبله ما لا نهاية - وانقضاء غير المتناهي محال، والموقوف على المحال محال ـ فوجب أن يمتنع حصول اليوم، وحيث حصل اليوم علمنا أن المنقضي متناه لا غير متناه. الثالث: أن المنقضي من زمان الطوفان إلى الأزل أقل من المنقضي من زماننا هذا إلى الأزل، فإذا طبقنا بين هاتين الجملتين من هذين الطرفين اللذين يلياننا وجب أن يظهر التفاوت من طرف الأزل وإلا لكان البعض مثل الكل، فتكون المدة المنقضية متناهية، فيكون تقدم الله تعالى على العالم متناهيا. الرابع: أنه لما كان تقدم الله على العالم أمرًا واجب الثبوت، وهذا التقدم لا يتقرر إلا بهذه المدة كان دوام ذات الله تعالى واستمرار بقائه مفتقرًا إلى تحقيق هذه المدة، والمفتقر إلى الغير ممكن لذاته، فيلزم أن يكون واجب الوجود لذاته ممكن الوجود لذاته، وهذا خلف. الخامس: أن المدة لا تُعقل إلا بتعاقب الآنات وتوالي الأزمنة والساعات، فلو كان الباري تعالى متقدمًا على العالم بمدة لا نهاية لها ولا أول لها لزم حصول الأزمنة المتوالية في الأزل، وذلك محال؛ لأن توالي بعض الأزمنة بالبعض يقتضي كون كل واحد منها مسبوقاً بغيره، والأزل عبارة عن نفي المسبوقية بالغير، والجمع بينهما متناقض.
The fourth problem. You say: The god is eternal and the world is originated, and if one of them is before the other, then the precedence of one over the other is either by a finite measure or an infinite measure. If it is by a finite measure, then each of them would be originated, and that is impossible. And if it is by an infinite measure, then that is impossible for reasons. First: that on this assumption, there would have elapsed before today an infinite duration, and the elapsing of what has no end is inconceivable. Second: that if the coming of today is dependent on the elapsing before it of what has no end—and the elapsing of the infinite is impossible, and that which depends on the impossible is impossible—then the coming of today must be impossible; and since today has come, we know that what has elapsed is finite, not infinite. Third: that what has elapsed from the time of the Flood to pre-eternity is less than what has elapsed from our time to pre-eternity; so if we juxtapose these two sums from these two ends that are near us, the difference must appear from the side of pre-eternity, otherwise the part would be like the whole; thus the elapsed duration is finite, so the precedence of God Most High over the world is finite. Fourth: that since the precedence of God over the world is a necessarily established matter, and this precedence is not constituted except by this duration, then the perpetuity of the essence of God Most High and the continuity of His existence would be dependent on the realization of this duration; and that which depends on another is possible in itself, so it would follow that the Necessary Existent in itself is possible in itself, and this is a contradiction (khulf). Fifth: that duration is not conceivable except by the succession of moments and the sequence of times and hours. So if the Creator Most High were prior to the world by a duration having no end and no beginning, it would necessitate the occurrence of successive times in pre-eternity, and that is impossible; because the succession of some times by others requires that each one of them be preceded by another, and pre-eternity (al-azal) is an expression of the negation of being preceded by another, and combining the two is contradictory.

Commentary
This passage is an objection raised by opponents (likely philosophers) against the Islamic claim that God is eternal (qadim) and the world is originated (muhdath). The objection proceeds by presenting a dilemma: if God precedes the world, the duration of that precedence must be either finite or infinite. If finite, then both would be originated (since each would have a beginning in time). If infinite, then impossible for five reasons. Each reason is a distinct logical argument. The first argument states that an infinite past would mean an infinite duration has elapsed before today, but an infinite cannot be completed. The second argues that if today depends on the completion of an infinite past, and the completion of the infinite is impossible, then today could not occur; but today does occur, so the past must be finite. The third uses a comparison between two periods (from the Flood to pre-eternity and from now to pre-eternity) to show that the elapsed time must be finite, otherwise part would equal whole. The fourth argues that if God's precedence depends on a duration, then God's eternity would be contingent on that duration, implying that the Necessary Existent is contingent—a contradiction. The fifth argues that duration consists of successive moments; an infinite past would mean successive moments in pre-eternity, but pre-eternity by definition negates being preceded, so an infinite sequence in pre-eternity is contradictory.

Fifth Argument: Succession in Pre-Eternity is Contradictory
Fifth argument: infinite succession in pre-eternity is self-contradictory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fifth argument that an infinite succession of moments in pre-eternity contradicts the definition of pre-eternity as having no predecessor.

Core Idea
An infinite sequence of moments cannot exist in pre-eternity, which by definition negates succession.

The final argument focuses on the nature of time and pre-eternity (azal). Duration consists of successive moments. If God's precedence were infinite, then an infinite series of moments would have occurred in pre-eternity. But 'pre-eternity' (azal) means the absence of any preceding thing—it negates the concept of succession. To have both would be contradictory. Hence, infinite precedence is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the fifth argument
Read the Arabic. Note the definition of duration as succession (تعاقب الآنات) and azal as negation of precedence.
2
Understand the contradiction
If there is a succession of moments, each moment is preceded by another. This series would extend infinitely into the past. But azal means 'no predecessor'. So an infinite series in azal would mean each moment has a predecessor, contradicting azal.
3
Recognize the nature of azal
Azal is often defined as the state of having no beginning. It is not a time period but a negation of temporal priority.
4
Conclusion
Therefore, an infinite duration of precedence is impossible.

Refuting Imaginary Duration for Precedence
Razi refutes the opponent's imaginary duration claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Razi insists that the duration of God's precedence over the world must be real and finite, not merely imaginary or supposed.

Core Idea
God's precedence is real, so its duration cannot be merely imaginary.

In this passage, Razi addresses a potential objection: perhaps the time interval between God and creation is only an imaginary construct with no external reality. He refutes this by grounding the discussion in the reality of divine precedence. If the precedence is a real fact, then any associated duration must also be real and not just mental.

The Boundary Between Pre-eternity and Post-eternity
An objection arguing that pre-eternity and post-eternity cannot be distinguished, leading to contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical objection that pre-eternity (azal) and post-eternity (abad) cannot be distinguished because neither has a definite boundary, leading to an apparent contradiction.

Core Idea
If pre-eternity has no end and post-eternity no beginning, how can they be opposites?

Imam Razi presents a clever objection aimed at the rational coherence of eternal attributes. The opponent argues that pre-eternity and post-eternity, being opposites, must be distinct and have a meeting point, but reflection shows that neither has a boundary, making them indistinguishable—a paradox. This passage sharpens our understanding of how theologians think about infinity and distinction.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and its key terms
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Note the terms 'azal', 'abad', 'taqabul', and 'khulf'. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
The passage is dense; read slowly.
2
Follow the logical structure
Identify the premise: opposites must be distinct, implying a boundary. Then follow the reasoning that any proposed boundary fails, so no distinction exists, contradicting the original premise.
The argument is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
3
Reflect on the theological significance
Ask yourself why this objection matters for discussions of God's attributes. How would a theologian respond? The commentary will help clarify.
This connects to broader debates about divine temporality.

السؤال الثالث: أن الأزل والأبد متقابلان تقابل السلب والإيجاب، وتقابل المتناقضين والمتعاندين، وكل أمرين هذا شأنهما فلا بد وأن يتميز أحدهما عن الآخر. فعلى هذا آخر الأزل متصل بأول الأبد إلا أن القول بهذا أيضًا محال؛ لأن كل نقطة فرضناها آخر الأزل وأول الأبد فأول الأبد كان حاصلا قبل ذلك؛ لأنا لو فرضننا نقطة أخرى قبل تلك النقطة بمائة سنة لم يصر الأبد أزلا بسبب زيادة هذا القرن المتناهي عليه فإذن ليس للأزل البتة آخر ولا للأبد البتة أول، فإذن لا يتميز الأول عن الأبد البتة من كون كل واحد منهما مناقضًا للآخر ومعاندا له، هذا خلف.
The third question: That pre-eternity (al-azal) and post-eternity (al-abad) are opposed to each other in the manner of the opposition of negation and affirmation, and the opposition of contradictories and contraries. And any two things of this nature must be distinguished from one another. Accordingly, the end of pre-eternity is connected to the beginning of post-eternity, except that saying this is also impossible; because every point we suppose to be the end of pre-eternity and the beginning of post-eternity, the beginning of post-eternity would have already occurred before that; for if we suppose another point a hundred years before that point, post-eternity would not become pre-eternity due to the addition of this finite century upon it. Thus pre-eternity has absolutely no end, and post-eternity has absolutely no beginning. Thus pre-eternity is not distinguished from post-eternity at all, while each is contradictory and contrary to the other; this is a contradiction (khulf).
Source pages p. 95

Commentary
This passage presents an objection concerning the relationship between pre-eternity (azal) and post-eternity (abad). The opponent argues that since these two are opposites, they must be distinct and have a boundary; the end of pre-eternity should be the beginning of post-eternity. But this leads to an absurdity: if we fix a point as the boundary, then any prior point would also be a candidate, and adding finite time does not turn post-eternity into pre-eternity. Hence neither has a definite boundary, so they cannot be distinguished, yet they are supposed to be opposites—a contradiction.

The Sixth Proof: God's Essence is Not Pure Existence
Razi presents the sixth proof against the identity of God's essence and existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's sixth proof against identifying God's essence with existence, including the key premise and the resulting contradictions.

Core Idea
If God's essence were pure existence, all existents would share His necessary attributes—leading to absurdity.

Razi builds this proof on a premise borrowed from Ibn Sina: whatever is true of one member of a species is true of all. He then applies this to the claim that God's essence is pure existence. The result is a contradiction between the properties of contingent beings and the necessary Being, proving the claim false.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its English translation to get an overview of the argument.
Pay attention to the logical flow: premise → conditional → contradiction.
2
Understand the key premise
Identify the premise about individuals of the lowest species sharing all essential properties. This is a foundational principle in Ibn Sina's metaphysics.
The premise is that for any species, properties that apply to one individual apply to all.
3
Apply the premise to God
Razi applies the premise: if God's essence were pure existence, then whatever is necessary for God's essence (e.g., perpetuity) would be necessary for all existing things, and whatever is impossible for God (e.g., change) would be impossible for all.
This step shows the logical consequences of the hypothesis.
4
Recognize the contradiction
Notice that the consequences contradict observed reality: contingent things are perishable and changing, while God is eternal and immutable. Therefore, the hypothesis is false.
This completes the proof by reductio ad absurdum.

This passage presents a proof by contradiction against the view that God's essence is identical with existence. If they were the same in essence, then either God would be perishable or contingent things eternal. Both are false. Therefore, the premise that God's essence is pure existence is false.

Commentary
This passage presents Avicenna's argument that God's existence cannot be an accidental attribute of His essence, and then an objection and answer. The argument goes: if existence were an accident of essence, then that existence depends on the essence, making it contingent. As contingent, it requires a cause. That cause cannot be the essence itself, because that would require the essence to exist before its own existence (a priority problem). Nor can it be something external, because then the Necessary Being would depend on something else. Thus, existence cannot be an accident. The objector then notes that in possible things, existence is additional to essence, and yet the essence is prior without circularity. The reply is that the same reasoning applies: if the essence can be prior without an existence, then why can't the cause in God's case be prior similarly? The argument forces consistency: if you allow priority of essence without existence for contingents, you must allow it for the Necessary, collapsing the distinction. The exchange highlights the difficulty of explaining the relationship between essence and existence for God and creation.

Commentary
This passage is the Tenth Issue, in which Imam Razi examines the possibility of Allah being a locus for temporal events (maḥall li-l-ḥawādith). All of these, Razi claims, are admissions of change in the divine essence or attributes, thus bringing the Ash'ariyya closer to the view they outwardly reject.

Attributes are either real, real-with-relations, or pure relations — and only the last change.

Commentary
This passage provides a classification of attributes into three kinds, which is essential for understanding the debate about change in God. Razi first defines the first kind: real attributes that are free from any relation, like blackness and whiteness. These are intrinsic qualities that do not involve a relation to something else. The second kind: real attributes that entail relations, such as knowledge and power. Knowledge is a real quality of the knower, but it necessarily involves a relation to the known object. Similarly, power involves a relation to the object over which one has power. The third kind: pure relations and pure correlations, which are not real attributes but merely relative positions, like being before or after, or being to the right or left. Razi illustrates this with an example: if you are to the right of someone and they move, you become left of them. Your essence and your real attributes (like your height) do not change; only the relation changes. After this classification, Razi applies it: change in relations is unavoidable—everyone admits that relations can change even regarding God (e.g., God created the world, so the relation of Creator to creature occurs). Thus the classification clarifies the precise difference between the Karramiyya and others.

Second Argument's First Premise: Receptivity as a Necessary Concomitant

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Razi argues that if God's essence were receptive to temporal attributes, that receptivity must be a necessary concomitant of His essence, and why this leads to a rejection of the Karramiyya view.

Core Idea
Eternal receptivity must be a necessary attribute, not accidental.

Razi's second argument against temporal attributes in God is more intricate. He begins by asserting a key conditional: if God's essence could receive a temporal attribute, then that receptivity would have to be an eternal, necessary feature of His essence. This logical move is crucial because it sets up the tension between eternal receptivity and the temporal nature of events. Core Idea
Razi's defense hinges on the extramental non-existence of relations and a distinction between power's priority and capacity's simultaneity.

Two objections threaten Razi's argument that God's attributes are eternal and unchanging: if relations change, doesn't God change? And if power is eternal, shouldn't its effect always exist? The Karramiyya's Second Argument and Razi's Refutation
Razi presents the second argument of the Karramiyya and his two-part refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Karramiyya's second argument that divine and human attributes differ only in temporality, and Razi's refutation by asserting essential difference and that eternality is not a mere negative qualification.

Core Idea
The Karramiyya argued that only temporality distinguishes divine attributes; Razi countered that they differ in essence and that eternality is a positive attribute.

The Karramiyya's second argument is more subtle: they appeal to a supposed agreement with the Ash'arites that meanings (ma'ani) inhere in God, and claim that the only difference between eternal and originated meanings is temporality. Since temporality is a negative attribute (non-priority), it cannot affect the permissibility of inherence. Therefore, originated meanings can inhere in God. Razi offers a two-pronged refutation: first, the attributes may differ in their essences; second, even if they differ only in temporality, eternality is not a mere negation but implies affirmation.

Then they argue that eternality is a negative qualification (qayd 'adami) and thus cannot be a factor in allowing inherence. Therefore, originated meanings can also inhere.
Note how they use the Ash'arite framework against the Ash'arites.
2
Analyze Razi's first refutation
Razi first denies that there is no essential difference. He asks: why can't the eternal attributes be different in their very essences and specific realities from the originated accidents? If they are essentially different, the analogy fails.
3
Analyze Razi's second refutation
Even if the only difference is eternality, Razi argues that eternality is not a mere negative qualification. He defines eternality as negation of prior non-existence, which is a negation of a negation, thus an affirmation. Therefore, eternality can be a positive factor that distinguishes divine attributes.
This is a sophisticated logical point about double negation.

Consider why this argument matters for Islamic theology: it defends the uniqueness of divine attributes and prevents drawing conclusions from created things to God.

Commentary
This passage contains the second proof of the Karramiyya and Razi's detailed refutation. The Karramiyya argue that both parties (they and the Ash'aris) agree that attributes inhere in God. They claim the only difference between God's eternal attributes and created accidents is that the former are eternal, the latter originated. Since eternality is a negative concept (absence of beginning), it cannot be the reason for inherence. Therefore, the inherence must be based on the fact that they are attributes, and created attributes share that characteristic, so created accidents can also inhere in God. Razi refutes on three levels: 1) He rejects that there is no essential distinction; attributes may differ in their very quiddities (haqa'iq). 2) Even if only eternality is the difference, eternality can be a positive condition (muqtada) because it is not merely negation; it is the negation of prior non-existence, and negating a negation results in affirmation. Thus, eternality is a real attribute that can be a factor. This is a sophisticated argument about the nature of negative terms.

Commentary
This passage presents the first philosophical question and its detailed answer. The philosophers question the nature of the powerful agent. The discussion is divided into two parts. First, it argues that preponderance cannot occur without a preponderating factor, giving two proofs: (1) if it did, a possible thing would arise without a cause, leading to denial of the Creator; (2) empirical observation shows that without inclination in the heart, no action occurs, and with equal inclinations the person remains stationary. This leads to the conclusion that when all conditions are met, the powerful agent necessarily acts, and when they are absent, he necessarily does not act. This seems to erase the distinction between the powerful agent and a necessitating cause. However, the author explains that the difference lies in the rapid change of conditions for created agents, but for Allah, whose essence is unchanging and not dependent on separate conditions, His effect is also unchanging. This is presented as a strong objection against their own view.

Razi's answer is based on the principle of ontological dependence: knowledge (al-'ilm) is subordinate (taba') to the known object. The act occurs first as a result of power and will, then knowledge attaches to it. Therefore, the attachment of knowledge is subordinate to the attachment of power and will. Since knowledge follows the act and does not precede it in the order of causation, it cannot be a hindrance to power and will. This preserves God's free agency.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think that God's eternal knowledge of an act makes the act necessary and limits His freedom.
Correction: Razi shows that knowledge is subordinate to the act, which is subordinate to power and will. Therefore, knowledge cannot constrain power and will; rather, it depends on them.

Key Takeaway
God's knowledge is subordinate to His act, which depends on His power and will, so knowledge cannot constrain His free agency.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Razi argues that God's knowledge is subordinate to His act, which is caused by His power and will. When you reflect on your own life, do you sometimes feel that God's foreknowledge leaves you without real choice? How does this teaching challenge or confirm that feeling? Will you today surrender your actions to God's will, trusting that His knowledge does not negate your responsibility?
?
If knowledge is subordinate to the act, then God's knowledge of your sins does not cause them. How does this affect the way you think about your own failures and God's mercy? Will you stop blaming God for your wrong choices and instead turn to Him in repentance?
?
This lesson shows that God's power and will are primary, and His knowledge follows. If you truly believed that God's will is the ultimate cause of all that happens, how would it change the way you pray and rely on Him? Will you begin this day by acknowledging that every act depends entirely on His power and will, and not on your own strength?
