Two Interpretations of 'Existence as Cause'
Razi presents two meanings of the claim 'existence is the cause' and shows both are problematic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the claim that 'existence is the cause of visibility' leads to two equally problematic interpretations, and how each undermines the proof for seeing Allah.

Core Idea
The claim that existence is the cause of visibility is ambiguous and leads to either skepticism or a broken proof.

Razi's tenth objection targets a key argument of those who deny the vision of Allah by attacking the ambiguous phrase 'existence is the cause.' He shows that this phrase can be understood in two ways, and both lead to unacceptable consequences. This lesson unpacks those interpretations and their flaws.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and note the two interpretations
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Identify the two interpretations labeled 'أما الأول' and 'أما الثاني'.
The objector is showing that the phrase is ambiguous.
2
Analyze the first interpretation and its skeptical consequence
Understand that if only existence is seen, and existence is shared by all existents, then all things would appear identical, making differentiation impossible. This leads to skepticism.
The term 'السفسطة' refers to sophistry/skepticism about knowledge of differences.

Under the second interpretation, the cause is existence plus quiddity (e.g., blackness). But then the cause is not a single shared thing, so the original proof that a shared attribute is the cause collapses.
The proof that existence is the cause was meant to be a single shared attribute.

Razi concludes that if the first interpretation is taken, skepticism follows; if the second, the proof falls entirely. Thus the original argument is unsound.
This is a classic 'either way' refutation.

Abu al-Hudhayl: Musa Sought Transmitted Proofs for Certainty
Third objection: Musa sought transmitted proofs for increased certainty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Abu al-Hudhayl's objection that Musa, already knowing the impossibility of vision, asked to see transmitted proofs for stronger certainty.

Core Idea
Musa's request aimed at harmonizing rational and transmitted proofs, not literal vision.

Abu al-Hudhayl al-Allaf, a Mu'tazili theologian, offers a sophisticated psychological interpretation. He suggests that Musa was already convinced by rational arguments that seeing Allah is impossible. Yet he asked for vision not to see Allah, but to witness the transmitted proofs (the very verses that mention vision) so that rational and scriptural evidence would align, increasing his tranquility and certainty. This objection reframes the request as a search for epistemic harmony.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read Abu al-Hudhayl's argument. He says Musa was already aware of rational proofs for impossibility, but asked to see the transmitted proofs of vision.
Note the phrase 'yara al-dala'il al-sam'iyya min al-ru'ya' (see the transmitted proofs from the vision).
2
Understand the purpose
Why would Musa want to see transmitted proofs? The objection says to make rational and transmitted proofs mutually supportive (muta'addida mutawafiq) and to increase tranquility, certainty, and remove doubt.
3
Consider the broader implication
Abu al-Hudhayl parallels this with the Quran's multiplicity of proofs for tawhid. This objection suggests Musa's request was pedagogical, not literal.

Objection: Musa Might Have Been Ignorant of the Impossibility
Fourth objection: possible ignorance of Musa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that Musa may not have known that seeing Allah is impossible, supported by arguments from prophetic knowledge and Sunni theology.

Core Idea
Musa's ignorance of the impossibility is improbable but not impossible, defended by Sunni principles.

The fourth objection, attributed to unnamed scholars, takes a different tack: perhaps Musa simply did not know that seeing Allah is impossible. While this seems unlikely, the objectors argue it is not rationally impossible. They support this with three arguments: first, knowing this attribute is not necessary for prophethood; second, Sunni theology allows for minor sins (which could include ignorance) by prophets; third, Allah may not have commanded Musa to know this attribute, so his ignorance is not blameworthy.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the fourth objection. It says: why not say Musa was not aware of the impossibility at that time? Then it provides three supporting arguments.
Note the three arguments: (a) knowledge of this attribute is not prerequisite for prophethood, (b) Sunni allowance of minor sins includes ignorance, (c) Allah may not have obligated Musa to know this.
2
Analyze the first argument
The first argument says that knowing each divine attribute does not depend on knowing prophethood, so a prophet might not know a particular attribute.
3
Analyze the second and third arguments
The second cites Sunni permission of sins on prophets, so why not this ignorance? The third says since Allah can do as He wills, He might not have commanded Musa to know this attribute, making ignorance no defect.
4
Implications
If Musa could have been ignorant, then his request does not indicate belief in possibility, as he may have asked out of ignorance.

Core Idea
Perception means encompassing, not seeing; the verse only denies encompassing.

Al-Razi begins his response by challenging the Mu'tazili equation of idrak and ru'ya. He uses Arabic linguistic evidence to show that idrak means reaching or encompassing, not mere vision. Second Aspect: Logical Interpretation of the Negation
Second aspect: logical interpretation using quantification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi uses logical quantification to argue that the verse only denies universal perception, allowing for vision by some.

Core Idea
The verse 'no eyes perceive Him' is a universal negative, whose contradictory is a particular affirmative.

In the second aspect, al-Razi employs logical terminology. Even if idrak meant vision, the verse's form—'la tudrikuhu al-absar'—is a universal negation. Its logical contradictory is a particular affirmative: 'some eyes perceive Him'. Therefore, the verse only denies that all eyes see Allah, not that some do. This allows believers to see Him.

Guided Path
1

Understand the logical rule
A universal affirmative (every A is B) is contradicted by a particular negative (some A is not B). Similarly, a universal negative (no A is B) is contradicted by a particular affirmative (some A is B).

3
Apply the rule to the verse
If the verse is a universal negative ('no eyes perceive Him'), its contradictory is 'some eyes perceive Him' (particular affirmative). Thus the verse only negates that all eyes perceive Him, not that some do.
Al-Razi concludes that disbelievers do not see, but believers may.

Commentary
This passage presents the first four aspects (wujuh) of al-Razi's response to the first Mu'tazili objection, which is based on the verse 'la tudrikuhu al-absar'. The first aspect distinguishes idrak (perception) from ru'ya (vision): idrak means encompassing or reaching, not mere seeing. The verse negates idrak, not ru'ya. The second aspect uses logic: the verse is a universal negation ('no eyes perceive Him'), which is the contradictory of universal affirmation. The fourth aspect argues that even if the verse negates vision for all seers, it is general, whereas the verse about looking at the Lord is specific and takes precedence.

Third Aspect: Consequences of Interpreting 'Absar' as 'Seers'
Third aspect: reductio ad absurdum on the interpretation of 'absar'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's argument that if 'absar' means seers, the verse would imply Allah perceives Himself, which the Mu'tazila deny.

Core Idea
If 'absar' means seers, then 'wa huwa yudriku al-absar' would mean God perceives all seers, including Himself.

In the third aspect, al-Razi considers the Mu'tazili claim that 'absar' in the verse means 'seers' rather than 'eyes'. He then applies this interpretation to the second part of the verse: 'wa huwa yudriku al-absar'. If 'absar' means seers, then God perceives all seers, which includes Himself. This leads to an absurdity that the Mu'tazila reject, thus undermining their objection.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the third aspect
Read from 'الوجه الثالث في الجواب' to 'وأنتم لا تقولون به'. Notice the conditional argument.
Al-Razi is turning the Mu'tazili's own interpretation against them.
2
Identify the Mu'tazili claim
The Mu'tazila argue that 'absar' in 'la tudrikuhu al-absar' means 'seers' (mubsirun), otherwise the verse would be trivial. Al-Razi grants this for argument.
They say: 'If it meant eyes, then the verse would be saying eyes do not perceive Him, which is obvious; so it must mean seers.'
3
Apply the interpretation to the second part
The verse continues: 'wa huwa yudriku al-absar'. If 'absar' means seers, then 'He perceives the seers' — meaning all seers. Since Allah is a seer, He would perceive Himself. This conclusion is unacceptable to the Mu'tazila.
Thus the interpretation leads to a contradiction, so it cannot be maintained.

Fourth Aspect: Specific Over General
Fourth aspect: hermeneutic precedence of specific over general

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's argument that even if the verse is general, the specific verse about believers seeing God takes precedence.

Core Idea
Even if the verse is general, the specific verse about seeing God in the afterlife overrides it.

In the fourth and final aspect, al-Razi concedes for argument that the general verse negates vision for all seers. However, he invokes the principle that a specific text (khas) takes precedence over a general one (amm). The specific verse from Surah al-Qiyama (75:22-23) states that faces will look at their Lord. This specific, positive text overrides the general negation, allowing vision for believers in the hereafter.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the fourth aspect
Read from 'الوجه الرابع في الجواب' to the end. Note the terms 'amm' and 'khas'.
Al-Razi uses a well-known Usul al-Fiqh principle.
2
Understand the principle
In Islamic jurisprudence, when a general text (amm) and a specific text (khas) conflict, the specific is given precedence and restricts the general.
This principle is widely accepted across legal schools.
3
Apply to the verses
The verse 'la tudrikuhu al-absar' is general (negating vision for all). The verse 'wujuhun yawma'idhin nadirah ila rabbiha nazirah' is specific (affirming vision for believers on that day). The specific text overrides the general, so the negation does not apply to believers in the afterlife.
Thus the Mu'tazili objection fails because the specific verse qualifies the general.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof that Allah's essence is unknowable, based on the need for additional proofs beyond attributes. The author states this is a clear syllogism of the second figure (a logical form where the middle term is predicate in both premises). This proof highlights the distinction between knowing attributes and knowing the essence.

Two Arguments That Conflict Is Impossible

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Conflict between two gods is impossible because each would will the same best action and know only one future.

Imam Razi has argued that conflict between two gods is possible in itself. Now an objector responds, conceding that possibility but insisting that given the attributes of wisdom and knowledge, conflict cannot actually occur. The objector presents two arguments that both gods would necessarily will the same thing. This passage shows how the objector tries to defend the impossibility of conflict.

Note that these are not the author's own arguments but those of an imaginary objector.

Core Idea
Either actions depend on motives or they don't; either way, agreement is not necessary.

One might think that if two gods existed, their wisdom would make them always choose what is best, leading to harmony. But Razi challenges this assumption. The twenty-fourth issue: Concerning the explanation that He, exalted, is the Willer of all existent things. The doctrine of the Mu'tazila: that will corresponds to command: so everything as God commanded, He willed it, and everything He forbade, He disliked it. And our doctrine: that will corresponds to knowledge. So everything whose occurrence He knew, its occurrence is willed, and everything whose non-existence He knew, its non-existence is willed. Thus, according to this, the faith of Abu Jahl is commanded but not willed, and his unbelief is forbidden and it is willed.

Commentary
This passage opens the twenty-fourth issue, which is about God's will concerning all created things. Razi first presents the Mu'tazilite position: for them, God's will aligns with His command. Whatever God commands, He wills; whatever He forbids, He dislikes. Then he presents the Ash'arite position: God's will aligns with His knowledge. Whatever God knows will occur, He wills its occurrence; whatever He knows will not occur, He wills its non-occurrence. To illustrate, he uses the example of Abu Jahl: his faith is commanded by God (since God commands all to believe) but it is not willed by God because God knows it will not happen. Conversely, Abu Jahl's unbelief is forbidden by God but it is willed (since God knows it will happen). This shows the Ash'arite view that God's will is not identical to His command; rather it follows His knowledge of what will be.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That God's command and His will are always the same.
Correction: The Ash'arite view distinguishes them: God can command something He does not will (e.g., faith from a disbeliever) and forbid something He does will (e.g., disbeliever's unbelief).

Checkpoint
According to the Ash'arite view presented here, what is the relationship between God's will and His knowledge?
A) God's will corresponds to His command.
B) God's will corresponds to His knowledge.
C) God's will is independent of both command and knowledge.

Key Takeaway
The Ash'arite view distinguishes God's will from His command, basing will on His knowledge of what will actually occur.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
If God wills the unbelief of Abu Jahl even though He forbids it, how does that confront your assumption that God's will always matches what He commands? In what area of your life have you assumed that because God commands something, He must also will it for you? What if He doesn't? Will you still obey?
?
God's will is based on His knowledge of what will happen, not on your efforts. Does this make you feel powerless or free? When you pray for guidance, are you asking God to change His will—or asking for strength to accept what He already knows? Which is more difficult?
?
Consider a sin you commit repeatedly. According to this lesson, God may forbid it but still will its occurrence. Does this truth drive you to despair or to deeper trust in God's wisdom? How does your view of God's justice need to expand to embrace this reality?
