Learning Objective

Core Idea
Not all star-gazing is forbidden, and a prophet never lies.

In this passage, Razi tackles two objections raised against Ibrahim's prophetic integrity. The first concerns his looking at the stars, which critics claim is a forbidden practice. The second targets his statement 'I am sick' as a lie. Razi offers multiple explanations to show that neither action was sinful. This lesson walks through each objection and its refutation.

Commentary
This passage addresses two objections derived from Ibrahim's story: (1) looking into astrology is prohibited, and (2) saying 'I am sick' is a lie. Razi answers each. For the first, he states that looking into stars is not absolutely forbidden; if done to deduce evidence for God's oneness, it is the greatest act of worship. Moreover, Ibrahim may have been informed by God that a specific star's rising would coincide with his illness, so he looked and then said he was sick. Alternatively, he may have appeared to rely on astrology to mislead his people. For the second objection, Razi gives three interpretations: (a) he was actually sick at that moment; (b) he knew he would become sick later, so he said it with future intent, analogous to knowing you will have a fever at noon and saying 'I have a fever'; (c) he meant sick at heart due to grief over his people's stubbornness.

Razi refutes the objection that Ibrahim's star-gazing was sinful.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Intentions transform actions: Ibrahim's star-gazing was an act of tawhid, not sin.

This lesson addresses a key objection to the prophethood of Ibrahim (AS): his looking at stars, which some claim is prohibited. Imam Razi provides a powerful refutation by showing that Ibrahim's action was either an act of tawhid, a response to divine inspiration, or a strategic pretense. Understanding this helps clarify that actions are judged by their intentions.

The second sub-question: Moses asked if Khidr killed a 'pure' soul, but the boy was not pure (since he was destined to be a disbeliever). The answer: Moses asked a question; he did not assert it as fact. Also, assessments are based on outward appearances, which is the norm in legal judgments.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Moses falsely accused Khidr of killing a pure soul.
Correction: Moses asked a question based on what he saw, which is permissible.

Key Takeaway
Prophetic figures, like all humans, judge by what appears, and questions are not accusations.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you question someone's action, do you often assume they are wrong before hearing their explanation? How can you emulate the prophetic adab of seeking clarification with an interrogative tone rather than accusation?
?
You often judge people by what you see on the surface. Are you willing to admit that your judgments are based on outward appearances, not hidden realities? What would change in your relationships if you truly owned that limitation?
?
Moses, a great prophet, was corrected by Khidr. When you are corrected, does your ego resist or do you humbly accept that your perspective was limited? What concrete step can you take this week to seek correction from someone wiser than you?
