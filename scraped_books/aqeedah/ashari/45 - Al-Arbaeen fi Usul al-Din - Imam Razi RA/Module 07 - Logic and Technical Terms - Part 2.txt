Distinguishing Non-Existence from Existence
Razi argues that we conceive pure negation and distinction without those concepts having extra-mental reality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi uses the distinction between non-existence and existence, and between pure negation and affirmation, to show that even the concept of pure negation is mentally conceived without having any existence.

Core Idea
Pure negation is conceived as distinct from affirmation, yet it cannot have any existence.

The fourth image concerns our ability to distinguish non-existence from existence, and pure negation (nafy mahd) from affirmation. The term 'pure negation' is understood as opposite to affirmation, yet if it had any existence, it would be both opposite and identical, a contradiction. The opponent also agrees that the term 'non-existence' is not established in non-existence. Still, we mentally distinguish it. This lesson unpacks this subtle argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the fourth image, paying attention to the terms 'نفي محض' and 'سلب صرف'.
2
Understand the distinction
We clearly distinguish non-existence from existence in our minds.
3
Grasp the contradiction
If pure negation had any existence, it would be both contradictory and identical to affirmation, which is impossible.

That these impossible things are not essences, nor realities, nor quiddities.
The second image: knowledge of composites, such as when we imagine a sea of mercury and a mountain of emerald. For the sea and the mountain are expressions for substances described by composition and arrangement, and described by various colors and specific shapes. According to you, that which is established in non-existence is only the essences of substances and accidents. As for the substance being described by an accident, that is not established in non-existence at all. The mountain is only a mountain because the substances are described by accidents and compositions. Thus, mental awareness is obtained in this image, while the awareness of it is not obtained at all, neither in non-existence nor in existence.
The third image: knowledge of relations and attributes, such as our knowledge of this body being in this place rather than that place, and the sun rising tomorrow from the east not from the west. There is no meaning to the sun rising from the east except the occurrence of its essence in that place rather than this place. There is no doubt or dispute that these relations are not established in non-existence, because the non-existent sun occurring in the non-existent place is impossible in the clarity of reason, and you also agree that there is no occurrence or establishment for such relations in non-existence.
The fourth image: we know the distinction of non-existence from existence and the distinction of pure negation and absolute privation from affirmation. The term 'negation' and 'privation' and 'absolute privation' is judged to be distinct from affirmation, while it is impossible for the term 'pure negation' to have affirmation, because the meaning of pure negation is that which is opposite to the term 'affirmation' and contradictory to it. So if it had affirmation in any respect, then the contradictory of a thing would be the very same thing, which is impossible. And because the opponent agrees that the term 'non-existence' is not established in non-existence.
The fifth image: we judge that the non-existent quiddity is non-existent. Before that quiddity is described by existence, we conceive the quiddity of existence and distinguish in our minds between the term 'existence' and the term 'substance' and 'blackness'. Therefore the opponent says: the term 'substance' and 'blackness' is established in non-existence, and the term 'existence' is not established in non-existence. So it is established that we conceive the quiddity of existence and its reality before the quiddity becomes described by existence. So if from mere mental distinction and intellectual awareness it necessarily follows that the awareness of it occurs in non-existence, then it would necessarily follow that the quiddity of existence is established in non-existence, and that is impossible because non-existence is the opposite of existence, and opposites cannot coexist. And because the opponent also agrees that the quiddity of existence is not established in non-existence.
The sixth image: we conceive the quiddity of effecting and the quiddity of being affected, and we distinguish between each one of them and the other, and between them and others, while the opponent agrees that the non-existent is neither an effecter nor affected, and the term 'effecting' and 'being affected' are not established in the state of non-existence. Thus mental awareness and intellectual perception are obtained while the perceived object is not obtained. Similarly, the thing being above another and below another and right and left, and all relations and attributes enter into that.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation by demonstration through counterexamples. Razi presents six distinct images (suwar) to argue that mental conception and awareness can occur without the object having any real existence in the external world. The overall purpose is to undermine the opponent's claim that the non-existent (ma'dum) must be a thing because it can be known. 

First, Razi asserts that these impossible objects (mumtani'at) are not essences, realities, or quiddities. Then he proceeds to the images:

- Second image: We can imagine composites like a mercury sea or emerald mountain. According to the opponent, only the essences of substances and accidents exist in non-existence, but the accidents and compositions are not there. Yet we have a mental picture of the whole thing.
- Third image: Relations and attributes such as 'being in a place' or 'rising from the east' are clearly understood, but these relations cannot exist in non-existence because that would require the non-existent sun to be in a non-existent place, which is absurd. The opponent agrees such relations are not established in non-existence.
- Fourth image: We distinguish between non-existence and existence, and between pure negation and affirmation. Pure negation cannot have any sort of existence because it is opposite to affirmation, yet we understand it as distinct.
- Fifth image: We conceive the quiddity of existence itself before something exists. If mental awareness implied real existence, then the quiddity of existence would have to be established in non-existence, but that is contradictory because existence and non-existence are opposites.
- Sixth image: We conceive 'effecting' and 'being affected' and distinguish them, yet the non-existent cannot be an agent or patient. The same applies to spatial relations like above/below.

Thus, in every case, mental awareness exists without the object being real. The key is that the opponent must either deny these obvious mental phenomena or admit that being known does not entail being a thing.

This week, identify one fear that is a 'pure negation'—a worry about something that does not exist yet. Write it down, then ask yourself: does this fear have any thubut? ?

Consider a time you strongly believed you were right about someone's intentions, but later found you were wrong. That belief was a mental distinction without external truth. Are you currently holding onto any such 'pure negation'—a judgment or assumption about yourself or others that has no real existence? Will you release it today?
?
You have been taught that 'there is no god but Allah'—a negation that is real because it affirms the opposite. But have you examined your heart for negations that have no affirmation—like the belief that you are hopeless or unloved? Those are pure negations, and they have no thubut. Will you replace them with the affirmation that Allah's mercy encompasses all things, including you?

Conceiving the Quiddity of Existence
Razi argues that we conceive the quiddity of existence without it being established in non-existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi uses the fact that we conceive the quiddity of existence itself before an object exists to argue that mental conception does not entail real existence.

Core Idea
We can conceive existence itself without it existing in the non-existent state.

The fifth image is particularly clever: we judge that a non-existent quiddity is non-existent. But to make that judgment, we must first have a concept of existence itself. We distinguish in our minds between the quiddity of existence and the quiddity of substance or blackness. The opponent agrees that the quiddity of existence is not established in non-existence. Yet we conceive it. This lesson examines how Razi turns the opponent's own admissions against them.

Identify the opponent's concession

3
Grasp the contradiction
If mental awareness implied reality, then the quiddity of existence would have to be in non-existence, which is impossible because existence and non-existence are opposites.

Effecting, Being Affected, and Spatial Relations
Razi uses causal and spatial concepts to show mental awareness without corresponding reality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi uses the concepts of 'effecting' and 'being affected', as well as spatial relations like above/below, to further demonstrate that mental conception does not require the object to exist in the external world.

Core Idea
We conceive causal and spatial relations that cannot apply to the non-existent.

The sixth and final image expands the argument to causal relations (effecting/affected) and spatial relations (above/below, right/left). The opponent agrees that the non-existent cannot be an agent or patient. Nevertheless, we have clear mental concepts of 'effecting' and 'being affected', and we distinguish them. Similarly, we conceive spatial relations that cannot hold in non-existence. This lesson shows how Razi wraps up his refutation with these broad examples.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the sixth image. Note the examples: effecting, being affected, and spatial relations.
2
Understand the opponent's concession
The non-existent is not an effecter or affected.
3
Connect to the overall argument
If mental awareness implied reality, then these concepts would have to belong to the non-existent, which is impossible according to the opponent.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation by demonstration through counterexamples. Razi presents six distinct images (suwar) to argue that mental conception and awareness can occur without the object having any real existence in the external world. The overall purpose is to undermine the opponent's claim that the non-existent (ma'dum) must be a thing because it can be known. 

First, Razi asserts that these impossible objects (mumtani'at) are not essences, realities, or quiddities. Then he proceeds to the images:

- Second image: We can imagine composites like a mercury sea or emerald mountain. According to the opponent, only the essences of substances and accidents exist in non-existence, but the accidents and compositions are not there. Yet we have a mental picture of the whole thing.
- Third image: Relations and attributes such as 'being in a place' or 'rising from the east' are clearly understood, but these relations cannot exist in non-existence because that would require the non-existent sun to be in a non-existent place, which is absurd. The opponent agrees such relations are not established in non-existence.
- Fourth image: We distinguish between non-existence and existence, and between pure negation and affirmation. Pure negation cannot have any sort of existence because it is opposite to affirmation, yet we understand it as distinct.
- Fifth image: We conceive the quiddity of existence itself before something exists. If mental awareness implied real existence, then the quiddity of existence would have to be established in non-existence, but that is contradictory because existence and non-existence are opposites.
- Sixth image: We conceive 'effecting' and 'being affected' and distinguish them, yet the non-existent cannot be an agent or patient. The same applies to spatial relations like above/below.

Thus, in every case, mental awareness exists without the object being real. The key is that the opponent must either deny these obvious mental phenomena or admit that being known does not entail being a thing.

Refuting the Distinctness of Non-Existence via the Object of Power
Razi refutes the argument that the non-existent is distinct because the object of power is distinct.

Core Idea
The object of power is existence, not the quiddity in non-existence.

The opponent argues that objects of power are distinct before existence, implying that non-existents are distinct. Razi responds by defining 'object of power' as that in which the agent has an effect, and showing that effect only applies to existence, not to the quiddity supposed to be in non-existence. First Argument: The Regress/Circularity Proof
The author presents the first proof against the necessity of an additional cause for difference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first argument that refutes the need for an additional cause of difference by showing it leads to infinite regress or circularity.

Core Idea
If difference needed a cause, that cause would itself need a cause, leading to absurdity.

The author now presents his first of three arguments. It is a classic reductio: if every difference required an additional cause, then that cause would itself differ from its effect, requiring yet another cause, and so on. This leads to an unacceptable infinite regress or circularity. The only way to stop is to accept that some things differ in themselves.

Guided Path
1
Understand the conditional premise
The argument assumes that if two things differ due to an additional matter, that matter must either be different or not different from the other.
2
Trace the two branches
If the additional matter is not different, it cannot cause difference; if it is different, then its own difference requires another cause, leading to regress or circularity.
3
Recognize the conclusion
The only escape is to end at two things that differ by themselves, not by an additional matter.

The author presents the second proof using the example of equal essences with differing attributes.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Two equal essences stay equal; only their attributes differ, so difference is in the attributes, not in the essences.

The second argument takes a different approach. Suppose we have two essences that are exactly alike in every way. Then each acquires a different attribute. What happens? Therefore, the difference is not intrinsic to the essences but to the attributes. But the author's point is that if we try to explain all difference by attributes, we end up with a scenario where essences remain equal despite different attributes, showing that difference is not reducible to attributes alone.

Core Idea
An essence and its attribute are intrinsically different; otherwise neither would be more deserving of being the attribute.

The third argument is elegantly simple. Consider any essence and an attribute that subsists in it. The essence, in itself, must be different from the attribute. Why? Because if they were not different, it would be arbitrary which one is the subject and which one is the predicate. This intrinsic difference is not caused by an additional attribute; it is immediate. Thus, we have an example of two things that differ by themselves.

Fourth Proof: Logical Necessity Requires Distinction
Razi uses logic of predication to show necessity qualifies relation, not divine essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi uses the logic of predication to show that necessity qualifies the relation (copula), not the divine essence, thereby establishing the distinction between essence and existence in God.

Core Idea
Necessity is a quality of the relation, not the subject.

This lesson presents Razi’s fourth proof, which draws on logical principles to argue that the necessity of existence must be a quality of the relation between subject and predicate, not of the subject itself. By analyzing the statement 'The Creator must exist,' Razi shows that for necessity to be intelligibly attributed, the Creator’s reality must be distinct from His existence. This proof is foundational for understanding the divine attributes in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Understand the logical framework
Learn that necessity, impossibility, and possibility are qualities of the relation (copula) between subject and predicate, not of the subject itself. This is established in logic.
2
Examine the example
Analyze the example 'Man must be an animal.' Identify subject (man), predicate (animal), copula (subsistence of animal for man), and necessity as a quality of that copula.
3
Apply to the Creator
Apply the same logic to 'The Creator must exist.' Here the subject is the Creator, predicate is existence, and necessity qualifies the attribution relation (copula).
4
Grasp the conclusion
Realize that for the attribution of necessity to be meaningful, the subject (Creator's reality) must be distinct from the predicate (existence). Thus, essence and existence are distinct in God.

You can mentally say something false while knowing it's false — that shows mental judgment is not knowledge.
