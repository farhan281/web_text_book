Refutation of the Claim that the Direction Above is Noblest
Razi enumerates four rational refutations of the third doubt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Razi refutes the claim that Allah must be above because that direction is noblest, using four rational arguments.

Core Idea
Rhetorical honor does not prove direction for Allah.

The third doubt claims that the direction 'above' is the noblest, so Allah must be there. Imam al-Razi dismantles this with four crisp rational arguments, showing that the premise is rhetorical, the world's sphericity destroys absolute 'above', and any attempt to define Allah's location leads to absurdity. Let's study his refutation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four flaws
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that Razi introduces four points (وجوه) to refute the doubt. Try to list them as you read.
2
First flaw: rhetorical premise
Understand that the opponent's statement is a rhetorical (خطابية) premise, which cannot be used in rational (عقليات) discussions. This dismisses the argument at its root.
3
Second flaw: spherical world
Grasp that because the world is a sphere, any direction considered 'above' for one point is 'below' for another. There is no absolute 'above' in the universe.
4
Third flaw: infinite or finite extension
Analyze the dilemma: if Allah's essence extends infinitely upward, no point is absolutely above; if it has a limit, then above that limit is void, making the void higher. Either way, Allah cannot be absolutely above.
5
Fourth flaw: honor belongs to place not occupant
Reflect on the argument that honor from direction belongs primarily to the place, only accidentally to the occupant. If Allah occupied a noble direction, the direction would be nobler than Him—an absurdity.
The text cuts off before completing this point, but the logic is clear.

Razi's Answer: Location as an Essential Property
Razi refutes the claim that location requires a separate attribute, using the infinite regress argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that a body's location can be essential (by its own essence) rather than requiring a separate attribute, and how this avoids the problem of infinite regress.

Core Idea
Some properties are essential, stopping the infinite chain of attributes.

This passage presents Razi's response to an objection: if everything in a location has an attribute of location, then God—if He were in a location—would need such an attribute, implying composition. Razi counters by questioning why location cannot be an essential property of a body itself, without a separate attribute. His reasoning uses the principle that chains of attributes must end at something essential, otherwise infinite regress occurs. Understanding this argument helps clarify how kalam theologians differentiate between essential and accidental properties.

Read the passage and its translation

The key term 'tasalsul' (infinite regress) appears here.
2
Identify the objection being answered
Although the objection is not quoted directly, the passage is a response. Based on the context, the objection claimed that a body's presence in a location is due to an attribute. Razi challenges this assumption.
Razi's strategy is to question the necessity of an attribute for location.
3
Follow the infinite regress argument
Razi argues that if every property required another property to explain it, we would have an infinite regress. Therefore, there must be properties that belong to the essence itself. Location could be one such property.
This is a foundational argument in kalam for establishing essential attributes.

Razi concludes that location may be essential to a body, thus not requiring an additional attribute. This weakens the objection against God (since God is not a body, but the point is about the principle).
The argument is about bodies; the application to God would be by analogy or distinction.

Refutation of Indwelling by Necessity
Razi refutes the possibility of hulul by necessity with two arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Razi's two arguments against the idea that God necessarily indwells in something, and why such indwelling is impossible for the Necessary Being.

Core Idea
If God indwells by necessity, He becomes dependent, which contradicts His necessity.

Having classified Christian views, Imam Razi now begins his refutation. He first considers the possibility that God indwells by necessity (wujub). He gives two decisive arguments: one based on the temporal/eternal incompatibility, and another based on dependence (iftiqar). This passage presents those arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the refutation passage
Read the Arabic passage from 'ونقول : أما القول بالحلول فهو باطل' up to 'والمفتقر إلى الغير ممكن بالذات'. Then read the translation. Note how Razi breaks the case into two divisions: hulul with necessity and with possibility.
This is a deductive argument: if hulul, then either with wujub or jawaz. He refutes both.
2
Understand the first argument
The first argument against necessary indwelling: If God indwells necessarily, then either the indweller (God) becomes temporal or the locus (e.g., Jesus' body) becomes eternal. Both are impossible. Why? Because if the indwelling relation is necessary, the indweller must exist in time if the locus is temporal, or the locus must be eternal if the indweller is eternal.
This argument relies on the incompatibility of temporal and eternal in the same relation.
3
Understand the second argument
The second argument: If God indwells necessarily, His essence becomes dependent on the locus. Anything dependent on something else is contingent (mumkin bi al-dhat), not necessary. Since God is necessary, this is impossible.
This is a classic argument from asma' wa sifat: necessity (wujub) precludes any need for another.

Core Idea
Allah cannot indwell anything because that would make Him dependent—impossible for the Necessary Being.

Through clear rational arguments, he demonstrates that both alternatives lead to contradictions with Allah's necessity and self-sufficiency. Let's follow his reasoning step by step.

Why Allah's Essence Cannot Be Conceived
Razi provides the second proof that Allah's essence cannot be conceived due to lack of direct perception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Imam Razi argues that Allah's essence cannot be conceived by the human intellect, and the two exclusive avenues of conception.

Core Idea
Conception requires direct perception or sensation; Allah transcends both, so He cannot be conceived.

How can we know something if we cannot even conceive it? Imam Razi begins with a logical principle: before we can affirm anything about something, we must have a conception of it. He then restricts the possible ways to form a conception to two: internal necessary perception and external sensory perception. Applying this to Allah, he concludes that since Allah does not fall into either category, His reality remains beyond our intellectual grasp.

Guided Path
1
Understand the logical premise
Read the first sentence: 'Affirmation (tasdiq) is a branch of conception (tasawwur).' This means we cannot affirm or deny anything about something we have no concept of. This is the foundation of the argument.
Notice how the argument builds on this simple logical rule.
2
Identify the two avenues of conception
Razi lists two ways we can conceive a quiddity: (1) necessary internal perception, like pain or joy, and (2) external sensory perception via the five senses, like seeing colors or hearing sounds.
These are the only two ways according to Razi; no third option is given.
3
Apply the rule to Allah
Ask: Does Allah fall under either category? He is not an internal feeling or emotion, nor is He a physical object perceived by the senses. Therefore, neither avenue applies.
This step requires careful reflection on the nature of Allah.
4
Draw the conclusion
Since both avenues are absent, Razi concludes that Allah's reality is not conceived by intellects. This is the core of the second proof.
The conclusion is necessary given the premises.
5
Reflect on theological implications
Think about how this argument supports divine transcendence (tanzih). If we cannot conceive Allah's essence, then any mental image we form is inadequate.
This connects to broader discussions in Islamic theology.

Conception and Assent: The Second Proof
Second proof: conception-assent connection

Core Idea
Assent requires conception; God's essence cannot be conceived.

Apply to God
Since God's essence is not perceived in either way, it cannot be conceived. Therefore, we cannot have assent about it (i.e., we cannot know it).

Commentary
This passage presents three proofs that the essence (ḥaqīqa) of God is unknowable to human intellects, followed by two objections from opponents and an answer. The first proof uses analogy with the magnet: we know the effect but not the specific reality causing it; similarly, we know God's attributes but not His specific essence. The second proof relies on the principle that assent (taṣdīq) depends on prior conception (taṣawwur). Conception occurs either by direct inner awareness (like pain) or by external senses. Since God's essence is not perceived in either way, it cannot be conceived, hence it is unknown. The third proof argues that all we know of God (attributes) are common categories, not the unique essence itself; thus the specific essence remains unknown. This is a syllogism of the second figure. Then two objections are presented: (1) if the essence were unknown, we could not judge it as unknown, since judgment requires conception; (2) we do predicate attributes like existence and pre-eternity to the essence, implying it is known. The answer refutes both by noting that the properties of foods and medicines are unknown in themselves, yet we know their effects; likewise, God's essence is unknown in itself but known through its effects. Razi closes by noting that deeper issues arise but are not addressed here.

The Second Argument: Impossibility of Two Effects from One Aspect
Razi presents the second sophistical argument against multiplicity from one cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument that a single simple cause cannot produce two distinct effects from exactly the same consideration.

Core Idea
One cause, one aspect, one effect — two effects would contradict the oneness.

The philosophers' second argument formalizes a simple intuition: if a cause produces A, then from that same aspect it cannot produce B, because A and B are different. This is not a trivial point; it establishes a key principle in causality that affects arguments about divine simplicity.

The Third Argument: The Suitability Principle
Razi presents the third sophistical argument based on the principle of suitability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that a cause must be suitable for its effect, and how this rules out one cause producing two different effects.

Core Idea
A cause must fit its effect; one cause cannot fit two different effects.

The third argument appeals to the intuitive notion that a cause must be appropriate or 'suitable' (ملائمة) to its effect. If one thing were to necessitate two different effects, it would have to be suitable for both, which would require it to be different from itself—an impossibility.

If one thing produces two different effects, it must be suitable for both. But that which is suitable for two different things must itself be different, leading to a contradiction that the one thing is both itself and different from itself.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Thus, it is impossible for a single simple cause to produce two different effects.
This strengthens the case against multiplicity in the divine essence.

Razi presents the fourth sophistical argument from empirical observation of natural effects.

Learning Objective

The fourth argument moves from observation to principle: we see that fire heats and water cools – different effects from different natures. From this, we infer that difference in effect indicates difference in cause. Therefore, if we observe different effects, they must come from different causes, not one single cause.

Everything Besides God Is Subject to Annihilation: Al-Razi's Proof from Contingency
Al-Razi states that everything besides God is subject to annihilation and gives a proof based on contingency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's rational proof that every created thing inherently possesses the possibility of annihilation.

Core Idea
Every originated thing carries within its essence the permanent possibility of not existing.

Al-Razi now takes up the question of whether anything besides God can be annihilated. The philosophers held that certain entities like human souls and celestial bodies, though originated, are eternal. Al-Razi counters with a clear argument rooted in the nature of contingency: whatever is created has an essence that is equally open to existence and non-existence. This capacity is inseparable from it, so annihilation is always a possibility for everything other than God.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note the philosophers' claim
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Observe what the philosophers assert about human souls, celestial intellects, bodies, and prime matter.
The philosophers admit these are originated but claim they are not susceptible to annihilation.
2
Identify the two premises of al-Razi's argument
Al-Razi's proof has two key premises: (1) everything besides God is originated, and (2) every originated thing has a reality that is susceptible to existence and non-existence. Find these in the text.
3
Understand the concept of 'necessary concomitant' (lāzim)
Al-Razi says the susceptibility to non-existence is a necessary concomitant (lāzim) of the quiddity (māhiyya) of every originated thing. Consider what it means for something to be inseparable from an essence.
Necessary concomitants are always present as long as the essence exists.
4
See how the conclusion follows
Since the susceptibility to non-existence is always present, every originated thing is always capable of being annihilated. Therefore, it is possible for everything besides God to cease to exist.
This does not mean they will be annihilated, only that it is possible.

Therefore, the capacity for non-existence is always present in every created thing, meaning that non-existence is always a possibility. Thus, everything besides God is subject to annihilation.
