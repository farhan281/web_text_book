God is Not a Substance: The Proof from Eternity
Ghazali states the claim and gives a proof by contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's rational argument that the Creator cannot be a substance because that would entail contingency, contradicting His eternal nature.

Core Idea
If God were a substance, He would be contingent—impossible for the Eternal.

In this passage, Ghazali states the fourth claim of his treatise: the Maker of the world is not a substance. He provides a concise proof based on God's eternity and the nature of contingent events (ḥawādith). This lesson unpacks that argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the claim and proof
Read the Arabic text and its translation. Note the conditional structure: 'If ... then ...'
The proof depends on the earlier established eternity of God.
2
Identify the key terms
Focus on 'جوهر' (substance), 'متحيز' (occupying space), and 'حوادث' (contingent events). Understand how they connect.
'متحيز' here means being in a place or having spatial location.
3
Trace the argument
Follow the logical chain: (1) If God were a substance, He would be in space. (2) Whatever is in space is subject to motion or rest, which are contingent. (3) Whatever is not free from contingent things is itself contingent. (4) God is eternal, so He cannot be contingent. Therefore, He is not a substance.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.

Commentary
This passage is an argument and objection-and-answer concerning the fourth claim: that the Creator is not a substance (jawhar). The author first states the claim and provides a brief proof: the eternity of God is established, and if He were not occupying space (mutahayyiz), then He would be subject to motion or rest in space, which are contingent; whatever is not free from contingents is itself contingent, contradicting His eternity. Then an objection is raised: why forbid calling Him 'jawhar' if one does not believe He is a body? The answer distinguishes between rational and linguistic/legal permissibility. Reason does not forbid the word itself; rather, the prohibition comes either from language (if it does not correspond to linguistic convention) or from revelation. The author explains that calling Him 'jawhar' as a real name is a lie against the language because the linguists never used it for Him. The footnotes include references and a note that some scholars like Fakhr al-Rāzī allowed the term based on a different definition (existent independent of a locus), but even that is just a usage, not an attribution of jahariyya in the sense of being a substance. The overall stance is that it is impermissible to call Allah a substance in the sense that implies being in a place.

On the Permissibility of Using 'Jawhar' for God
Ghazali responds to an objection about the use of the term 'jawhar' for God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali allows using the term 'jawhar' for God only under strict linguistic conditions, and why reason alone does not forbid it.

Core Idea
Reason permits the word 'jawhar' for God, but language and revelation may forbid it.

After establishing that God is not a substance in the sense of occupying space, Ghazali addresses an objection: why then do you forbid those who call God 'jawhar' without meaning spatial occupation? He clarifies the roles of reason, language, and revelation in determining permissible terms for God.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Read the Arabic text and translation. Note the objector's premise and Ghazali's distinction between rational and linguistic/legal prohibition.
The objector is likely referring to Christians or philosophers who used 'jawhar' for God.
2
Understand Ghazali's threefold distinction
Identify how Ghazali separates the role of reason (which does not forbid words), language (which may forbid if not conventional), and revelation (shar') which may also forbid.
3
Apply the linguistic test
Consider Ghazali's explanation: if someone claims 'jawhar' is God's real name according to language, it is a lie because linguists never used it for Him. This shows the importance of linguistic convention.
Some scholars like Fakhr al-Razi allowed the term based on a different definition, but Ghazali's main point stands.

Being a substance implies temporality, contradicting God's pre-eternity.

In this passage, Ghazali presents his fourth claim: that the Creator is not a substance that occupies space (jawhar mutahayyiz). He provides a concise proof rooted in the earlier argument that whatever is inseparable from temporal accidents is itself temporal. This lesson examines that proof and its implications for divine transcendence.

The fourth claim concerning substance.
We claim: That the Maker of the world is not a substance occupying space (jawhar mutahayyiz); because His pre-eternity is established, and if the Maker of the world were not occupying space, He would not be free from motion or rest in it, and whatever is not free from temporal events (hawadith) is itself temporal (hadith), as previously established.
If it is said: Why do you object to one who calls Him a substance without believing Him to occupy space? How do you respond to one who names Him thus?
We say: Reason in our view does not necessitate prohibition of using words; rather it prevents [calling Him] substance without believing it about Him only due to the right of language (haqq al-lugha) or the right of the Law (haqq al-shar').
As for the right of language: That is when one claims that it conforms to the linguistic convention... then it is examined.
If the one describing Him claims that it is His name in reality, i.e., the language coiner placed it for Him, then it is slander against the language.
And if he claims it is a metaphor (isti'ara), then one looks at the meaning by which it shares with the borrowed-from: if it is suitable for metaphor, he is not objected to by right of language; if not suitable, it is said to him: You have erred regarding the language. And that is not condemned except to the extent of condemning the action of one who goes far in metaphor, and examining that does not befit the investigations of intellects.
As for the right of the Law and its permissibility and prohibition: it is a jurisprudential investigation that must be sought by jurists, for there is no difference between investigating the permissibility of using words without intending a corrupt meaning and investigating the permissibility of actions. And concerning it there are two opinions: Two opinions regarding applying names to Him, may He be exalted, that have not been conveyed by revelation: either it is said that no name is applied concerning God Almighty except with permission, and this [name] has no permission, so it is forbidden; or it is said that it is not forbidden except by prohibition, and this has no prohibition, so one looks: if it implies a fault (iham khata'), then it must be avoided, because implying fault in God's attributes is forbidden; if it does not imply a fault, we do not judge it as forbidden. Both ways are possible. Then the implication varies by languages and customary usage, for a word may imply fault among some people but not among others.

Commentary
This passage is a composite argument—part proof, part objection-and-answer, and part classification of the criteria for naming God. It begins with Ghazali's fourth claim: that God is not a substance (jawhar) occupying space (mutahayyiz). The proof is that a body (which 'jawhar' often implies) cannot be free from motion or rest, and whatever is not free from temporal accidents is itself temporal—contradicting God's pre-eternity. Ghazali then addresses an objection: why object to calling God a 'substance' if one does not attribute spatial occupancy to Him? He answers that reason does not prohibit linguistic usage per se; the prohibition comes from either language or law. He then examines the linguistic criterion: if someone claims the term is a literal name for God, it is a lie against the language (because no such usage is transmitted). If claimed as a metaphor, one checks whether the shared meaning is appropriate; if not, it is a linguistic error. The legal criterion is a separate jurisprudential issue: two scholarly opinions exist. The first says only revealed names are permissible, so calling God 'substance' is forbidden since no revelation authorizes it. The second says only explicitly prohibited terms are forbidden; if no prohibition exists, one must still avoid the term if it implies a fault in God (iham khata'), otherwise it remains permissible. The implication of fault depends on language and custom. This nuanced discussion balances theological rigor with linguistic and legal flexibility.

Commentary
This passage is a composite argument—part proof, part objection-and-answer, and part classification of the criteria for naming God. He answers that reason does not prohibit linguistic usage per se; the prohibition comes from either language or law. He then examines the linguistic criterion: if someone claims the term is a literal name for God, it is a lie against the language (because no such usage is transmitted). If claimed as a metaphor, one checks whether the shared meaning is appropriate; if not, it is a linguistic error. The second says only explicitly prohibited terms are forbidden; if no prohibition exists, one must still avoid the term if it implies a fault in God (iham khata'), otherwise it remains permissible. The implication of fault depends on language and custom. This nuanced discussion balances theological rigor with linguistic and legal flexibility.

Commentary
This passage is a proof that the Creator is not located in any physical direction. Ghazali begins by stating the claim: He is not in any particular direction among the six (above, below, front, back, right, left). The key point is that direction (jiha) is a relational concept that applies only to bodies and accidents. If an opponent claims a third meaning of direction, it would be unintelligible. Thus, Ghazali concludes that affirming direction for God is impossible.

Commentary

Because nights are times of solitude when hearts are free from distractions and prayers are more likely to be sincere and accepted. Thus, the hadith refers to a special divine attention during seclusion, not a physical descent.

Commentary

We say: If someone were to take the opposite of this path exactly, it would be a firmer statement and more in line with the principle of approval and disapproval that is determined by fancies and imaginations, as previously mentioned. That is, we say: It is deemed ugly for a person to punish for an offense that has passed and whose rectification is difficult, for two reasons:

First: If in the punishment there is deterrence and consideration of future benefit, then it is good out of fear of missing a future objective. If there is absolutely no future benefit, then punishment merely as requital for the past is ugly, because there is no benefit in it for the punisher or for anyone else. The offender is harmed by it, and repelling harm from him is good. Harm is only good if there is a benefit, but there is no benefit, and what is past cannot be rectified; so it is extremely ugly.

The second reason: We say: If the victim is harmed, resentful, and his anger intensifies, then that anger is painful, and relieving the anger is soothing from the pain. The pain is more fitting for the offender. Whenever he punishes the offender, the pain of anger is removed from him and is specific to the offender, so he is more entitled. This also has some basis, although it indicates a deficiency of intellect and the predominance of anger.

As for imposing punishment where there is no future benefit for anyone in God's knowledge, and no removal of harm from the victim, it is extremely ugly. So this is firmer than the saying of one who says: 'Refraining from punishment is extremely ugly.'

But all of it is false and a following of the dictates of fancies that arise from imagining purposes, and God is exalted above them. We only intended to counter falsehood with falsehood so that the invalidity of their imagination may become clear.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an argument about the ugliness of punishment without future benefit. The author first states the argument from the opposite direction, then presents two reasons from human custom why punishment without future benefit is considered ugly: first, because it lacks deterrence and benefit; second, because it serves to relieve the victim's anger. He then applies this to God, saying that imposing punishment without any benefit is extremely ugly, even more so than the opposite view. However, he concludes that all these considerations are based on fancies and imaginations, and God is transcendent above them. The purpose is to counter falsehood with falsehood to expose the invalidity of their imagination.

Proof and Possibility of Punishment of the Grave
Ghazali establishes scriptural proof and intrinsic possibility of punishment in the grave.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the scriptural basis and rational possibility of punishment in the grave as established by Ghazali.

Core Idea
Punishment of the grave is proven by decisive Scriptural evidence and is rationally possible.

Ghazali now turns to a matter of the unseen: punishment in the grave. He does not rely on speculation but invokes the highest category of scriptural proof—mutawatir transmission—and a Quranic verse. He then adds a brief rational note: such punishment is possible, so affirming it is obligatory. In this lesson, you will see how he combines textual authority with rational plausibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the structure: first scriptural proofs, then rational possibility.
2
Identify the three proofs
Ghazali cites three scriptural proofs: mutawatir reports from the Prophet, the specific hadith of the two graves, and the Quranic verse about Pharaoh's people. Find each in the text.
Mutawatir means transmitted by so many narrators that certainty is achieved.
3
Understand the rational move
After the proofs, Ghazali states the punishment is 'possible' (mumkin) and that affirmation is obligatory. Why does he add this? Reflect on why someone might doubt it despite the proofs.
The 'aspect of its possibility is apparent' means no rational contradiction exists.
4
Reflect on the combination
Consider why Ghazali uses both scriptural and rational arguments. How do they work together? This reflects the Ash'ari method of integrating revelation and reason.
The rational argument removes objections; the scriptural proof provides positive evidence.

As for the punishment of the grave: decisive proofs of the Law have indicated it, for the speech of the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) about the punishment of the grave and the seeking of refuge from it in supplications by the Companions (may Allah be pleased with them) is mutawatir, and his saying (peace be upon him) when passing by two graves: 'Verily, they are being punished' is well-known, and the saying of Allah Most High indicates it: 'And there enveloped the people of Pharaoh the worst punishment; the Fire; they are exposed to it morning and evening' (40:45-46). It is possible, so affirmation of it is obligatory, and the aspect of its possibility is apparent.
Source pages p. 275

Commentary
This passage establishes the reality of punishment in the grave ('adhab al-qabr) from two angles: scriptural proof and rational possibility. Ghazali first cites the decisive proofs of the Law (qawati' al-shar'), noting that the Prophet's words about punishment of the grave and the Companions' seeking refuge from it are transmitted via multiple chains (tawatur), meeting the highest standard of certainty. He also cites the famous hadith of the two graves, and the Quranic verse (40:45-46) indicating that the people of Pharaoh are exposed to the fire morning and evening—interpreted as a reference to the intermediate state (barzakh). After establishing the scriptural basis, Ghazali asserts that the punishment is intrinsically possible (mumkin), which makes affirmation obligatory (wajib). The 'aspect of its possibility' (wajh imkanihi) is 'apparent' (zahir), meaning that reason does not find any contradiction in the idea of a soul being punished after death, even if the body appears inactive.
