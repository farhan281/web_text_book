Concluding Supplication for Guidance and Truth
Ghazali's concluding prayer for purification and truth.

Learning Objective
After this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali concludes his theological work with a prayer that encapsulates the goals of seeking truth and avoiding error.

Core Idea
The supplication is the logical conclusion of sound theology: to speak truth and be purified from error.

Imam al-Ghazali ends his 'Iqtisad' with a deeply personal prayer. He has spent the book establishing correct beliefs. Now he turns to God, asking for the purification that makes those beliefs real in the heart and on the tongue. This passage is both a conclusion and a model for the reader to adopt the same spirit of humble supplication.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the shift from gratitude to supplication.
2
Identify the structure
The passage has two parts: first, gratitude for being guided to the true group; second, a direct prayer to God for purification and speech.
3
Reflect on the purpose
Ask yourself why Ghazali ends with a prayer. How does this connect to the overall purpose of theology? Reflect on the integration of knowledge and action.
This passage shows that correct belief should lead to humble supplication.

Commentary
This passage is a concluding supplication and exhortation from Imam al-Ghazali. He begins by stating that the true way (al-tahqīq) belongs to a specific group distinct from others—likely referring to the Ahl al-Sunna wa'l-Jama'a. He then expresses gratitude to God for guiding the reader to follow the path of this group, to join their order, and to be counted among them. He expresses hope that the reader will be resurrected in their company. The passage then turns into a direct prayer: he asks God to purify our innermost beings from the corruptions of misguidance, to fill them with the light of truth, to prevent our tongues from uttering falsehood, and to enable us to speak truth and wisdom. The prayer ends by acknowledging God's generosity, abundant favor, and vast mercy.

The First Error: Subjecting Whims to Judgment
Ghazali explains the first error: judging things ugly due to personal opposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how self-interest can lead to mistaking one's subjective disapproval for an objective judgment of ugliness.

Core Idea
We often judge things ugly simply because they oppose our desires.

Ghazali begins by identifying the first common error in moral judgment: the tendency to label something as 'ugly' solely because it conflicts with one's own purpose, while ignoring that it may serve another's. This error stems from self-absorption and leads to an absolutization of a relative judgment. In this lesson, we explore this mistake and its root cause.

3
Consider the example

Subjecting whims to judgment: The first mistake is that a person may apply the name 'ugly' (qabīḥ) to whatever opposes his own purpose, even if it agrees with the purpose of another, but he does not pay attention to the other. For every natural disposition is engrossed with itself and considers everything else insignificant. Therefore, he judges the act absolutely as ugly. He may say: 'It is ugly in itself' (fī 'aynihi), meaning that it is contrary to his purpose. But his purposes are as if they are the whole world from his perspective, so he imagines that what opposes his right is inherently opposing, and thus he attributes ugliness to the essence of the thing and judges absolutely. He is correct in the original disapproval (istiqbāḥ), but he errs in his judgment of ugliness absolutely and in attributing ugliness to the essence of the thing. The source of this is his heedlessness of paying attention to others, indeed of paying attention to some of his own states; for he may approve in some of his states the very thing he disapproves when it becomes agreeable to his purpose!

Generalization of judgments: The second mistake is that what is contrary to purposes in all states except in a rare state — a person may judge it absolutely as ugly, due to his oblivion to the rare state, and because of the ingrained habit, for example, that lying is absolutely ugly in every state, and that its ugliness is because it is a lie in itself only, not for any additional meaning. The reason for this is his heedlessness of the connection of many benefits with lying in some states. But if that state occurs, perhaps his nature would recoil from approving lying due to his frequent habituation to disapproving it. This is because nature recoils from it from early childhood through discipline and correction, and it is taught that lying is ugly in itself and that one should never lie at all, with the condition that it is ugly as stated, but with a condition that accompanies it in all times, though it is only rarely absent. So that condition is not mentioned, and its ugliness and absolute aversion are ingrained in his nature.

Reverse association: The third mistake is the precedence of illusion (wahm) to the reverse. For what is seen as connected to a thing, the illusion leaps to the assumption that the thing also is necessarily connected to it absolutely, not knowing that the more specific is always connected to the more general, but the more general is not necessarily connected to the more specific.

An example of this is what is said: that the snake-bitten person — I mean, he who has been stung by a snake — fears a speckled rope. This is as said; its cause is that he perceived the harmful thing (the snake) while it was visualized in the form of a speckled rope. So when he perceives the rope, the illusion outruns reason to the reverse and judges that it is harmful, so nature recoils following illusion and imagination, even if reason denies it.

Indeed, a person may recoil from eating yellow saffron pudding because it resembles feces, almost vomiting when someone says 'It is feces,' and finds it difficult to consume it even though reason denies it. That is due to the precedence of illusion to the reverse: he perceived the disgusting thing (feces) as moist and yellow, so when he sees yellow moist substance, he judges it as disgusting.

Moreover, in nature there is something greater than this: the names (asmā') that are applied to Indians and black people (al-Hind wa al-Zunūj) — since ugliness of the named is associated with them — may affect nature to the extent that if the most beautiful Turk or Roman were given that name, nature would recoil from him, because illusion has perceived ugliness associated with this name, so it judges by the reverse: when it perceives the name, it judges ugliness for the named and nature recoils.

Commentary
This passage enumerates and explains three common errors in moral judgment, presenting them as a classification of mistakes people make when evaluating actions or things as good or bad. The author, Imam al-Ghazali, proceeds in order from the first to the third mistake, each with its own heading and detailed explanation.

The first mistake, called 'subjecting whims to judgment' (taḥkīm al-ahwā'), is the error of judging something as ugly (qabīḥ) solely because it conflicts with one's own personal purpose or desire, while ignoring that it may serve the purpose of another. The root cause is self-absorption: each person is engrossed in his own interests and belittles others. Consequently, he projects his subjective disapproval onto the object itself, claiming it is ugly in its essence, when in reality he is only correct about his own disapproval of it. His mistake lies in absolutizing a relative judgment and heeding only his own perspective, even to the point of inconsistently approving the same thing when it suits him.

The second mistake, 'generalization of judgments' (taʿmīm al-aḥkām), occurs when something is mostly contrary to purposes but rarely beneficial. Due to obliviousness to the rare exception, one judges it absolutely ugly. The example given is lying: people are taught from childhood that lying is absolutely ugly in itself, without mentioning the exceptional conditions where it may be permissible or even beneficial (such as reconciling between people). The habitual aversion, inculcated early, becomes so ingrained that the nature recoils from approving it even in those rare cases. The error is overgeneralization based on the common occurrence, neglecting the exceptional contexts in which the act may be good.

The third mistake, 'reverse association' or the precedence of illusion to the reverse (sabuq al-wahm ilā al-ʿaks), is the tendency of the imagination to assume a reverse correlation: if something is frequently perceived together with something else (e.g., snake with speckled rope), then whenever the associated object (rope) is seen, the illusion judges it to have the same quality (harmfulness), even if reason tells otherwise. Examples include the snake-bitten person fearing any speckled rope, recoiling from yellow pudding because it resembles feces, and even the effect of names: if a name is associated with ugliness (e.g., names given to certain ethnic groups), then hearing that name can cause revulsion toward a beautiful person bearing it. The error is that the mind confuses a coincidental or contingent association with a necessary one, leading to irrational judgments.

Throughout, Ghazali emphasizes that these errors stem from psychological mechanisms—self-interest, habit, and associative imagination—that distort rational moral evaluation. He does not provide a systematic remedy here but rather identifies the errors for the reader to be aware of them.

The Second Error: Overgeneralizing Moral Judgments
Ghazali explains the second error: generalizing a judgment to all cases despite rare exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how habitual aversion and neglect of exceptions lead to overgeneralized moral judgments, such as considering lying absolutely ugly.

Core Idea
We often declare something absolutely wrong because we overlook rare exceptions.

The second error arises when we judge an action as absolutely ugly based on its common occurrence, forgetting that there may be exceptional situations where it is beneficial. Ghazali uses the example of lying, which is typically harmful but may be permissible in certain contexts. This lesson examines how early conditioning and habit contribute to this error.

Likewise, he imagines a beautiful image that he desires to have intercourse with. When that is established in the imagination, the faculty that spreads the instrument of action is aroused, it drives winds into the cavities of the nerves and fills them, and the faculty commanded to pour the lubricating fluid that aids intercourse is stirred. All this while being certain by the judgment of the intellect not to refrain from the act at that time. But God Almighty created these faculties according to the habitual course of events, obedient and subservient to the dominion of imagination and fantasy, whether the intellect assists the fantasy or not. This and its likes are a preference of one of the two sides of the action over the other, and all of that goes back to the origin of error in the matter of [؟] purposes.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of the psychological mechanism that leads people to error. Ghazali begins with the example of a person who has a natural aversion to a rope because it is associated with harm, even though intellectually he knows it is not harmful. He draws an analogy: just as a person associates praise with certain acts and thus inclines toward them, or associates harm with a rope and recoils, similarly, the habitual attachment to a place or person (like the poet's love of Layla's dwellings) is not actually due to the place but due to the association. Ghazali then broadens this to show that human nature is full of such learned associations, and those who judge only by appearances are misled because they ignore the deeper realities of the soul's habits. He then gives physiological examples: when imagining food, saliva flows automatically even if the person is fasting and does not intend to eat. Similarly, imagining a beautiful person triggers sexual arousal involuntarily. This shows that the faculties of the body are subservient to imagination and fantasy, not just to rational intellect. Ghazali concludes that these examples illustrate how one side of an action is preferred over another due to these non-rational factors, leading to error if not properly understood.

Commentary
This passage is a jurisprudential argument within a kalām work, addressing the question: Can a wicked person (fāsiq) perform hisba—commanding good and forbidding evil? Ghazālī states that the truth is that they can. He proceeds by gradual elaboration, first posing a hypothetical: Is infallibility (ʿiṣma) from all sins a condition for enjoining good and forbidding evil? If it were, it would break consensus because only prophets are infallible by revelation, and on minor sins there is disagreement. No one would be qualified. Thus the condition is not required. Then he considers a series of objections. If one says that a silk-wearer (committing a sin by wearing silk) can forbid fornication and drinking, Ghazālī asks: Can a wine-drinker forbid a disbeliever from disbelief and fight him? If they say no, they break consensus because Muslim armies included sinners. If they say yes, then can the wine-drinker forbid murder? If no, he asks for the difference between that and the silk-wearer forbidding wine. Sin levels vary. If they try to regulate by saying one can forbid above one's sin but not equal or below, Ghazālī calls this arbitrary: fornication is above drinking, so a fornicator could forbid drinking. Furthermore, one can commit a sin and still command others to avoid it because the obligations are separate. The obligation to perform the act and the obligation to command are distinct; abandoning one does not vitiate the other. Then a strong objection is raised: a man committing rape forbids the woman from uncovering her face—this seems absurd. Ghazālī's answer is that such a ḥisba is permissible in his view; the judgment is not based on whether it seems 'cool' or 'hot' (pleasant or unpleasant). He distinguishes obligations that are independent from those that are conditions. For example, prayer without ablution is invalid because ablution is a condition; likewise, one must refine oneself before others. But in ḥisba, the two obligations (to avoid sin and to command good) are not conditional on each other, so they can be separated. The passage concludes by comparing the case of the disbeliever who cannot call to Islam until he himself submits.

Learning Objective

The truth of a command is independent of the commander's sinfulness.

Ghazali has established that hisbah is an obligation. But a powerful objection arises: how can a sinful person, engaged in evil himself, command others to avoid it? Many truths are cold and burdensome, and many falsehoods are sweet and palatable. So truth is not pleasant, and falsehood is not repulsive.
And the decisive proof (burhan qati') about it is that we say: His saying to her: 'Do not uncover your face; for it is forbidden' – and his forbidding her by action – these are speech and action. This speech and action: either it is said to be forbidden, or obligatory, or permissible.

If you say it is forbidden – then what is the basis for its prohibition when it was obligatory before he engaged in zina? And does the obligatory become forbidden by committing a forbidden act? There is no truth in the latter statement from the Shari'a that it is forbidden. And in his action there is only forbidding from what is forbidden. And to declare any one of them forbidden is impossible.
And by our saying, 'The sinful person has authority (wilayah) of hisbah', we only mean that his speech is truth and his action is not forbidden. And this is not like prayer and ablution; for prayer is commanded and its condition is ablution, so it without ablution is disobedience and is not prayer; rather it ceases to be prayer. But this speech has not ceased to be truth, nor has the action ceased to be forbiddance from the forbidden.
Likewise, suhur is an expression of seeking assistance for fasting by presenting food, and seeking assistance cannot be conceived without the resolve to perform the assisted act. As for your saying that refining oneself is also a condition for refining others – that is the point of dispute; so where did you know that?
And if someone said: Refining oneself from sins is a condition for fighting and preventing disbelievers, and refining oneself from minor sins is a condition for preventing major sins – his statement would be like yours, and it is a violation of consensus (ijma').

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer unit. Ghazali presents an objection against his earlier claim that a sinful person can still enjoin good (hisbah). The objector gives a vivid example: a man committing zina (fornication) with a woman while simultaneously commanding her to cover her face, since she is not a mahram to him. The objector says this is a cold and repulsive act of hisbah that no rational person would do. Then the objector extends the argument by analogy: if both the act of zina and the command are obligatory, the man cannot perform the zina and treat the command as separate; similarly, one cannot pray without wudu, as wudu is a condition for prayer. Likewise, taking suhur (pre-dawn meal) is only meaningful if one intends to fast. The objector argues that refining one's own soul is a condition for refining others, so a sinful person cannot command others.
Ghazali responds systematically. First, he dismisses the aesthetic objection: the discussion is not about whether the act is 'cold' or 'hot' but about whether it is true or false. Truth is not always pleasant. Then he provides a proof: the command and the action of forbidding are either obligatory, permissible, or forbidden. If they were forbidden, what basis could there be for prohibiting something that was obligatory before the zina? The zina does not change the status of the command. The command remains true and the action remains a forbiddance of evil. This is unlike prayer without wudu, because wudu is a condition for the validity of prayer; but the command to forbid evil is not nullified by the sin of the one commanding. Finally, Ghazali challenges the claim that self-reform is a condition for commanding others, calling it a point of dispute and noting that such a condition would lead to absurdities like requiring one to avoid all minor sins before commanding against major ones, which is contrary to consensus.

Worldly Needs as a Condition for Religion

Commentary
This passage is a rational argument for the necessity of the imamate. Ghazali begins by clarifying the term 'dunya' (the world), which is ambiguous. It can mean either excesses beyond need (which is opposed to religion) or the basic necessities of life (which are a condition for religion). He then establishes two premises. First, the proper order of religion (knowledge and worship) depends on worldly necessities like health, security, and provision. Without these, one cannot devote time to religious duties. Second, such worldly order—especially security of life and property—cannot be achieved without an obeyed ruler. He supports this by empirical observation: during times without a ruler, chaos, violence, and economic collapse ensue. He quotes proverbs likening religion and kingship as twins, and the ruler as a guard. The conclusion is that a ruler is necessary for worldly order, worldly order is necessary for religious order, and religious order is necessary for salvation. Therefore, the appointment of an Imam (the caliph) is a religious obligation that cannot be neglected.

The Necessity of an Obeyed Ruler for Worldly Order

Thus it is clear that the ruler is necessary for the order of the world, the order of the world is necessary for the order of religion, and the order of religion is necessary for attaining the happiness of the Hereafter—which is the absolute goal of the prophets. So the obligation to appoint an Imam is among the necessities of the Shari'a that cannot be abandoned.

Commentary
This passage is a rational argument for the necessity of the imamate. Ghazali begins by clarifying the term 'dunya' (the world), which is ambiguous. It can mean either excesses beyond need (which is opposed to religion) or the basic necessities of life (which are a condition for religion). He then establishes two premises. First, the proper order of religion (knowledge and worship) depends on worldly necessities like health, security, and provision. Without these, one cannot devote time to religious duties. Second, such worldly order—especially security of life and property—cannot be achieved without an obeyed ruler. He supports this by empirical observation: during times without a ruler, chaos, violence, and economic collapse ensue. He quotes proverbs likening religion and kingship as twins, and the ruler as a guard. The conclusion is that a ruler is necessary for worldly order, worldly order is necessary for religious order, and religious order is necessary for salvation. Therefore, the appointment of an Imam (the caliph) is a religious obligation that cannot be neglected.
