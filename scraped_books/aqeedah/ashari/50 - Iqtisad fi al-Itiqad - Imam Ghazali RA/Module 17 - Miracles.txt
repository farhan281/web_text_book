The Permissibility of Karamat (Miracles of Saints)
Ghazali answers whether karamat are permissible and distinguishes them from prophethood miracles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's defense of karamat and how he distinguishes them from prophetic miracles using the criterion of tahaddi.

Core Idea
Karamat are possible because they lack the challenge that defines a prophetic miracle.

In this passage, Ghazali addresses a common theological concern: if extraordinary events can occur at the hands of non-prophets, how do we preserve the distinctiveness of prophetic miracles? He resolves this by introducing the concept of tahaddi (challenge) as the defining feature. As you read, focus on how this simple criterion protects the credibility of prophethood while allowing for saintly miracles.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the objection-and-answer format: someone questions the permissibility of karamat, and Ghazali responds.
The passage begins with 'فإن قيل' (if it is said) – a standard dialectical structure.
2
Identify the key question
What is the central issue Ghazali is addressing? He is asked whether karamat (extraordinary events at the hands of non-prophets) are permissible.
The question implicitly worries that karamat might undermine the uniqueness of prophetic miracles.
3
Find the distinction between karamah and mu'jiza
Ghazali defines karamah as an extraordinary event without tahaddi (challenge). If there is tahaddi, it becomes a mu'jiza. Note that this distinction is the key to his argument.
Mu'jiza by definition involves a challenge to produce something similar, which proves the prophet's truth.
4
Understand why karamat are permissible
Ghazali argues that karamat are possible because they do not lead to any impossibility: they do not nullify the evidential value of mu'jizat. They are simply a different category.
He also states that even a sinful person could be the vessel of a karamah, further showing it is not linked to claims of prophethood.
5
Reflect on the theological significance
Consider how this argument protects the credibility of prophethood while affirming the possibility of divine intervention through saints. Why is this distinction important for Islamic theology?
It maintains the uniqueness of prophets while acknowledging the ongoing relationship between God and believers.

Commentary
This passage answers the question about karamat. A miracle (mu'jiza) is an extraordinary event accompanied by a challenge to produce something similar, proving the prophet's truth. A karamah lacks that challenge, so it can occur at the hand of a righteous person or even a sinner. The possibility is not impossible in itself.

What Makes the Quran Inimitable? The Twin Aspects of Eloquence and Composition
Ghazali answers the aspect of Quranic inimitability as eloquence and unique composition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's answer to the question of the Quran's inimitability (i‘jaz) as consisting of two inseparable elements: magnificent eloquence (al-jazala wa al-fasaha) and wondrous composition (al-nazm al-‘ajib).

Core Idea
The Quran's miracle is the fusion of eloquence and unique composition.

This passage presents a direct question about the nature of the Quran's miraculousness. Ghazali offers a concise but profound answer: the inimitability lies not in one feature but in the combination of superb wording and an unprecedented structural arrangement that no human can replicate.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice the structure: a question followed by an answer starting with 'قلنا' (we say).
The objection is implied: 'If it is said...' – a common dialectical form.
2
Identify the two elements
In the answer, locate the two components: first, 'al-jazala wa al-fasaha' (magnificence and eloquence); second, 'al-nazm al-‘ajib' (the wondrous composition). Note that Ghazali emphasizes the composition is 'outside the approaches of Arab speech.'
These two are not separate miracles but two aspects of one miracle.
3
Understand the combination as miraculous
Ghazali says the combination of these two is beyond human capacity (khārij 'an maqdūr al-bashar). Reflect on why having both together is impossible for humans, even if individuals can achieve one aspect alone.
This refutes the idea that any human speech can match the Quran in both eloquence and composition.

The combination of these two—exquisite wording and unique arrangement—is beyond human power. Ghazali acknowledges that some Arab poetry or sermons may exhibit magnificence, but none combine it with this unique composition, and such examples may be transmitted, but they do not match the Quran.

The Quran's Miracle: Sublime Eloquence and Unique Composition
Ghazali defines the Quran's i'jaz as the combination of jalala, fasaha, and nazm 'ajib.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali identifies the Quran's miracle as the unique combination of sublime eloquence and wondrous composition, and how this distinguishes it from all human speech.

Core Idea
The Quran's miracle is not in eloquence or composition alone, but in their perfect union.

Ghazali begins by addressing a direct question: what exactly makes the Quran inimitable? Instead of listing miracles, he focuses on the Quran's own linguistic texture. He argues that the Quran's sublime eloquence combined with its extraordinary composition, which departs from all known Arab speech patterns, produces an effect that no human can replicate. The evidence is empirical: all Arabs, masters of language, were awed and unable to produce a valid criticism.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice the concise argument structure.
The passage begins with a question-and-answer format.
2
Identify the two aspects of i'jaz
Ghazali defines the miracle as a combination of two elements. Find them: sublime eloquence and wondrous composition. Note that he says it departs from all Arab speech.
The term 'jalala' and 'fasaha' refer to the quality of language; 'nazm 'ajib' refers to structure.
3
Examine the counterexample of Musaylima
Ghazali concedes that some imitators tried to mimic the composition but failed in eloquence. Read the example of Musaylima and understand why it is considered weak.
This shows that the miracle is in the combination, not in one aspect alone.
4
Understand the empirical proof
Ghazali argues that all Arabs marveled at the Quran's eloquence and never produced a valid criticism. This failure to challenge proves its miraculous nature.
This is a proof from historical consensus and incapacity (tahaddi).

Objection and Answer: The Sarfa Argument for the Quran's Miracle
Ghazali refutes the war-distraction objection using the sarfa argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali refutes the objection that the Arabs were too busy with wars to oppose the Quran, using the sarfa (divine prevention) argument to affirm the Quran's miraculous nature.

Core Idea
Even divine prevention from opposing the Quran is itself a miracle.

This lesson presents a key objection to the Quran's miraculous nature and Ghazali's powerful rebuttal. The objectors claim the Arabs simply had no time to oppose the Quran due to wars. Ghazali first dismisses this as nonsense, then turns the objection on its head: even if God prevented them, that prevention is a greater miracle. Through a vivid thought experiment, he shows how sarfa strengthens rather than weakens the proof of prophethood.

2
Understand the sarfa argument
Ghazali concedes the possibility of divine prevention but argues that such prevention is itself a miracle.
3
Analyze the thought experiment
The finger-moving example illustrates how even a simple prevention can be a powerful sign when the need is urgent.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection and its answer. The objection claims that the Arabs' failure to oppose the Quran was due to their preoccupation with wars, not inability. Ghazali rebuts this in two ways. First, he calls the objection 'nonsense' because composing speech to meet a challenge is far easier than fighting a war, especially given the severe losses they suffered. If they could have opposed, they would have done so to save themselves. Ghazali illustrates with a thought experiment: if a prophet claims that no one will oppose him moving his finger, and indeed no one does, that is a miracle—because it shows God's power over human actions. Similarly, the Arabs' lack of opposition, despite their urgent need to defend themselves, proves divine intervention. This argument demonstrates Ghazali's willingness to engage with a weaker form of i'jaz (sarf) to refute the objection, while still maintaining the Quran's miraculous nature.

The Objection of Tawatur and Ghazali's Response
Ghazali answers a Christian objection about non-tawatur of Jesus's miracles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali defends the proof of prophethood through tawatur by addressing the objection that miracles are not mutawatir for everyone, and why engaging with the transmitting community is necessary.

Core Idea
Tawatur is not automatic; it requires being part of the transmitting community.

Ghazali anticipates a Christian objection: how can we accept the miracles of Jesus if they are not mutawatir (widely transmitted) for everyone? He responds with a clever analogy involving a Jew in isolation, showing that tawatur is not a given but must be sought out. This lesson unpacks that argument and its implications for the methodology of proving prophethood.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Understand the Christian's claim: the miracles of Jesus are not mutawatir for him, neither as a whole nor individually. This challenges the proof of prophethood.
The objection is a common one from those who reject the Muslim argument that Jesus's miracles are widely transmitted.
2
Examine Ghazali's hypothetical
Ghazali presents a Jew who lives in isolation and never mixes with Christians. This Jew could similarly claim that the miracles are not mutawatir for him, even though they are mutawatir among Christians.
The hypothetical shows that the Christian's claim is not unique; it can be replicated by anyone who isolates themselves.
3
Identify the key requirement
The solution offered: the person must mix with the community among whom the reports are mutawatir. Only then can tawatur be achieved.
Tawatur is not an inherent property of the reports; it requires exposure and transmission within a community.
4
Reflect on the deafness analogy
Ghazali compares the isolated person to a deaf person (asamm) and one who makes himself deaf (mutasammim). Just as a deaf person cannot receive auditory testimony, so an isolated person cannot receive tawatur.
This reinforces that the burden is on the objector to integrate with the transmitting community.

تحريجة: لم تتواتر فإن قال قائل من النصارى : هذه الأمور لم تتواتر عندي لا جملتها المعجزة عند ولا آحادها ؟ فيقال له : ولو انحاز يهودي إلى قطر من الأقطار ولم يخالط النصارى وزعم أنه لم يتواتر عنده معجزات عيسى عليه السلام ، وإن تواترَتْ .. فعلى لسان النصارى وهم متهمون به ؛ فبماذا ينفصلون عنه ؟ ولا انفصال عنه إلا أن يقال : ينبغي أن يخالط القوم الذين تواتر ذلك بينهم حتى يتواتر ذلك إليك ؛ فإن الأصم لا تتواتر عنده الأخبار وكذا المتصامِمُ ، فهذا أيضاً عذرنا عند إنكار واحدٍ منهم التواتر على هذا الوجه ، والله أعلم.
Objection: It is not mutawatir. If a Christian says: 'These matters were not mutawatir for me, neither their totality nor their individual miracles?' It is said to him: And if a Jew withdrew to a region and did not mix with Christians and claimed that the miracles of Jesus, peace be upon him, were not mutawatir for him, even if they were mutawatir according to the Christians, and they are suspect regarding it, then how would they be freed from this? There is no freedom from it except by saying: He should mix with the people among whom it is mutawatir so that it becomes mutawatir for you. For the deaf person (asamm) does not have tawatur of reports, and likewise the one who makes himself deaf (mutasammim). So this is also our excuse when one of them denies tawatur in this manner. And Allah knows best.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection-and-answer regarding the proof of prophethood through tawatur (widespread transmission). The objection is from a Christian who claims that the miracles of Jesus have not reached him via tawatur. The answer uses a hypothetical scenario: if a Jew lived in isolation and never mixed with Christians, he could claim that Jesus's miracles are not mutawatir for him, and that the Christian reports are biased. The solution is that he must mix with the community among whom the reports are mutawatir so that the transmission becomes widespread for him. Ghazali analogizes this to a deaf person who cannot hear reports; just as deafness prevents tawatur, so does willful isolation. He concludes that this is the excuse for denying tawatur in this manner. The passage guards against the misunderstanding that tawatur must be already known to everyone; rather, it can be acquired by engaging with the transmitting community.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The objection assumes that tawatur must be immediately and inherently known to every individual, regardless of their exposure.
Correction: Tawatur requires being part of the transmitting community; it can be acquired by mixing with those who have the reports.

Key Takeaway
Tawatur is not an immediate given; it can be achieved by engaging with the community that transmits the reports.
