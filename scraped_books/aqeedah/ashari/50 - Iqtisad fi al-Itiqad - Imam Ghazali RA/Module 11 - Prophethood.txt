Learning Objective

Core Idea
The fourth pole ties together prophethood, the afterlife, political leadership, and boundary-setting against innovation.

Commentary
This passage is a table of contents for the fourth and final major section (pole) of the book. Ghazali begins by naming the pole as dealing with the messengers of God and what has come on the tongue of the Prophet (peace be upon him) concerning eschatological events: the gathering (hashr), the scattering (nashr), Paradise, Hell, intercession (shafa‘a), punishment of the grave (adhab al-qabr), the balance (mizan), and the bridge (sirat). He then lists four chapters: Chapter 1 on proving the prophethood of Muhammad (peace be upon him), Chapter 2 on the transmitted matters of the afterlife, Chapter 3 on the imamate (leadership of the community) and its conditions, and Chapter 4 on the criteria for judging heretical sects (firaq mubtadi‘a) as unbelievers (takfir). This structure shows that Ghazali’s work culminates in prophethood, eschatology, political theology, and boundary-setting against innovation.

The Priority of Eternal Happiness
Ghazali warns against wasting effort and identifies the ultimate goal.

Core Idea
Eternal happiness is the most important goal; prioritize your efforts.

Ghazali opens his preamble by establishing a fundamental principle: waste not your time on what does not matter. He then reminds us that the ultimate concern for every human being is achieving eternal happiness and avoiding eternal misery. This sets the stage for his argument that investigating prophethood is essential.

The first preamble: on explaining that engaging in this world is important in religion. Know: directing concern to what is not important, and wasting time on what is unavoidable, [this] is the essence of misguidance and the extreme of loss, whether the object of concern is from sciences or from actions; so we seek refuge with Allah from knowledge that does not benefit. And the most important of matters for all creatures is attaining eternal happiness and avoiding perpetual misery. The prophets have come and informed the creation that Allah Almighty has rights and duties upon His servants in their actions, words, and beliefs, and that whoever does not speak truthfully with his tongue, nor conceal truth in his conscience, nor adorn his limbs with good deeds, his end is the Fire, and his consequence is ruin. Then they did not confine themselves to mere informing; rather, they attested to their truthfulness by matters [that establish the messenger?] by his miracle—strange to the intellect, and wondrous acts that break customs, beyond human capability. So whoever witnesses them or hears their accounts through mutawātir reports, there immediately occurs to his mind the possibility of their truthfulness, nay, it overcomes his assumption [of truth] at first hearing before he thoroughly examines distinguishing miracles from marvels of craftsmanship. And this intuitive assumption or necessary estimation removes tranquility from the heart, fills it with apprehension and fear, stirs it to investigation and reflection, strips it of ease and rest, warns it of the consequence of negligence and carelessness, and establishes for it that death is inevitably coming, and that what is after death is hidden from the sight of creation, and that what these [prophets] have reported is not outside the realm of possibility.

Commentary
This passage is a preamble in which Ghazali argues that investigating prophethood is a religious duty. He begins by warning against wasting effort on unimportant matters, whether in knowledge or action, because the ultimate goal is eternal happiness and avoiding eternal misery. The prophets came to inform humanity of God's rights and duties, and that failure in truthfulness, belief, and action leads to Hell. But they did not merely claim; they supported their truth with extraordinary miracles that break natural customs. Upon hearing of such miracles through widely transmitted reports, a person naturally feels an initial assumption of their truth—an intuitive belief or necessary estimation. This initial assumption, however, is not full certainty; it disturbs the heart's tranquility, instills fear and alarm, and drives the person to investigate further. The person becomes aware of death's inevitability and the hidden nature of the afterlife, and realizes that the prophets' reports are not impossible (since they are within the realm of possibility). Thus, the initial unease motivates serious inquiry. The passage emphasizes that the rational response to miracle reports is not blind acceptance nor dismissal, but a quest for certain knowledge.

Ghazali refutes an objection about divine deception using kalām nafsī.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
God's speech is not sounds; it's His knowledge, which cannot lie.

This passage tackles a powerful objection: if lying is not inherently evil, how can we trust any divine revelation? Ghazali's answer shifts the ground from ethics to ontology. He argues that God's speech is not like human speech—it is an eternal attribute of His essence that necessarily corresponds to what He knows. Since lying is a mismatch between speech and reality, and God's mental speech is always identical with His knowledge, deception is impossible.

Guided Path

The objector assumes that if lying is not evil in itself, God could deceive us even in direct revelation. Notice the chain of doubt: even without a messenger, if God speaks directly, how do we know He is truthful?

2

Ghazali does not argue that lying is evil for God. Instead, he redefines the nature of divine speech. Find where he says that God's speech is not sound or letter (laysa bi-ṣawtin wa lā ḥarf).
3
Understand 'mental speech' (kalām nafsī)
Ghazali says God's speech is a meaning (maʿnā) subsisting in His essence. Compare it to human inner knowledge: your own internal knowledge cannot be false. Similarly, God's self-subsisting speech is necessarily true.
4
Reflect on the implications
This argument protects the trustworthiness of revelation without appealing to morality. It is an ontological guarantee: lying is simply impossible in the kind of speech God has.
This is a cornerstone of Ash'ari theology on the Qur'an being uncreated.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the first Jewish sophism concerning abrogation (naskh). Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the first Jewish sophism concerning abrogation (naskh). The author begins by defining naskh: it is a discourse that indicates the lifting of an established ruling, where the continuation of the ruling is conditioned by the absence of a later discourse that removes it. This definition shows that naskh is not a change of mind but a predetermined lifting. Then he provides an analogy: a master commands his servant to stand without specifying duration, knowing that the benefit of standing will eventually end. The servant obeys continuously until the master tells him to sit. When the master tells him to sit, the servant does not accuse the master of ignorance or indecision—the master knew from the start that the command was temporary. Ghazali concludes that just as the Jew should mix with Christians to receive tawatur, the Christian should mix with Muslims. He compares the Christian's denial to deafness—both prevent reception of tawatur.
The analogy with the deaf person emphasizes that the fault lies with the listener, not the report.

If a Christian says: These matters have not reached tawatur for me, neither in their entirety nor individually? It is said to him: Even if a Jew isolated himself in a region and did not mix with the Christians and claimed that the miracles of Jesus (peace be upon him) have not reached tawatur for him, and even if they did reach tawatur, it is upon the tongues of the Christians, and they are accused of bias towards him; how would they extricate themselves from that? There is no extrication from it except by saying: He should mix with the people among whom that (tawatur) occurred so that it may reach tawatur for you. For the deaf person does not receive tawatur of reports, and likewise the one who pretends deafness. This is also our excuse when one of them denies tawatur in this manner. And God knows best.

Commentary
This passage presents a Christian objection and Ghazali's response. The Christian claims that the reports of Muhammad's miracles have not reached the level of tawatur for him, neither individually nor collectively. Ghazali counters with the example of a Jew who isolates himself from Christians and denies the tawatur of Jesus's miracles, claiming that the reports come only from biased Christians. The Christian would have to answer that the Jew should mix with the Christian community to receive the tawatur. Similarly, Ghazali argues, if a Christian denies tawatur of Muhammad's miracles, it is because they have not mixed with the Muslim community that transmits these reports. Just as a deaf person cannot hear reports, someone who avoids contact cannot claim tawatur. Thus, the denial is not a refutation of the miracles themselves but of the individual's failure to seek the transmitted knowledge.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The denial of tawatur by an individual Christian means the reports of Muhammad's miracles are not reliable.
Correction: Ghazali shows that denial stems from isolation or unwillingness to engage with the Muslim community, not from any lack of tawatur among the Muslims.

Denial of tawatur due to isolation does not invalidate the tawatur of the transmitting community.
