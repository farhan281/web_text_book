Learning Objective

Core Idea
Reason itself affirms that order implies a powerful agent, and power is an additional attribute beyond essence.

In this lesson, we continue Ghazali's proof for God's power. He now addresses the second premise: 'Every well-ordered act has a powerful agent.' Ghazali argues that this is known by the necessity of reason, not by observation alone. He then goes further to define power as an extra attribute (sifa za'ida) that makes the act possible, distinguishing it from God's essence. This clarifies the conceptual foundations of the attribute.

Ghazali says the second premise is grasped by 'darurat al-'aql' (necessity of reason). This means it is self-evident and requires no proof.

Ghazali defines 'powerful' (qadir) as one from whom the act proceeds not from essence alone, but from an extra attribute. If it proceeded from essence, it would be eternal (like the essence). Therefore, the attribute is power.
This argument uses the contingency of the world to infer an additional attribute.

Reflect on how this definitional argument complements the earlier syllogism. The syllogism shows that the world has a powerful agent; this argument explains what 'power' means as a real attribute.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for God's power (qudra) based on the order and precision of the world. It begins by stating the claim: the originator of the world is powerful because the world is a well-ordered act (fi'l muhkam), arranged, precise, and full of wonders. This is presented as a syllogism: every well-ordered act comes from a powerful agent; the world is a well-ordered act; therefore, the world comes from a powerful agent.

Ghazali then addresses potential objections. First, an objector asks: 'Why do you claim the world is well-ordered?' The answer: 'Well-ordered' means its arrangement, order, and proportion. Therefore, the act must proceed from an extra attribute (sifa za'ida) beyond the essence, and that attribute is called 'power' (qudra). This further clarifies that the world's contingency (as a temporal act) points to a power in the agent.

Throughout, Ghazali emphasizes that the argument combines sensory evidence (for the world's order) and rational necessity (that order implies power), with a supplementary definitional argument to solidify the concept of power.

One power, infinite possibles: possibility itself is the link.

This passage is the logical heart of Ghazali's argument for the universality of divine power. He begins by stating that God's power relates to all possible things, which are infinite. Then he presents a clever disjunctive proof: either each possible requires its own power (leading to an impossible infinite regress), or one power covers all possibles through their common feature—possibility itself. The conclusion is that every possible is an object of divine power.

Proof of a Thing Capable Between Two Powers
Refutation of preference and proof of maqdur bayn qadirayn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali argues that a single action can be subject to both divine and human power without implying that one power overrides the other.

Core Idea
Creation is equal; no power can be stronger than another in the same act.

Ghazali begins by refuting a mistaken idea that God's power would necessarily prevail over human power, which would lead to denying human agency. He then builds a decisive proof that leads to the conclusion of a single maqdur between two qadirs, showing that the purpose of both powers is creation, and creation is equal.

Guided Path
1
Understand the false notion
Read the passage that starts with 'وإن ظنّ' and see the assumption that God's power being stronger would make His maqdur prevail. Identify why Ghazali calls this impossible.
2
See the equality of creation
Focus on the statement that the benefit (fāʾida) of both powers is creation (ikhtirāʿ), and creation is equal, with no stronger or weaker. This is key to the refutation.
3
Grasp the decisive proof
Realize that the proof of two powers necessarily leads to a single maqdur between two powers. Trace Ghazali's reasoning from the refutation to this conclusion.

And if one supposes that the thing capable of God Almighty is preferred because His power is stronger... then it is impossible; because the attachment of power to a single movement is not superior to the attachment of another power to it, since the benefit of the two powers is creation (ikhtirāʿ), and their strength lies in their ability over something else, and their ability over something else does not give preference in the movement under discussion; since the share of the movement from each of the two powers is that it becomes created by it, and creation is equal, there is no stronger or weaker in it such that there would be preference. So the decisive proof for establishing the two powers leads us to establish a thing capable (maqdūr) between two powers. So whenever He creates the movement and creates with it a power over it... He is the sole possessor of creation of the power and the capable thing together, and it follows from that: that He is unique in creation, and that the movement exists, and that the mover is capable over it, and because he is capable he differs from the state of the trembler; so all the problems are repelled. The summary: that the capable one is vast in power, so He is capable of creating the power and the capable thing together. And since the name 'Creator' (al-Khāliq) and 'Inventor' (al-Mukhtariʿ) is applied to one who brings a thing into existence by his power, and the power and the capable thing are both by the power of God Almighty... He is called Creator. We do not call the servant a creator and inventor, and the capable thing is not by the power of the servant even if it is with it... so he is not called a creator and inventor, but only acquisition (al-kasb). And it became necessary to seek for this type of attribution another name, so the name 'acquisition' (kasb) was sought for it, in imitation of the Book of God Almighty, for its application to the deeds of servants was found in the Qur'an. As for the name 'act' (fiʿl), they hesitated in applying it. And there is no harm in names after the meanings are understood.

Commentary
This passage presents a complete theological argument: a proof, an objection, an answer, and a terminological conclusion. It begins by refuting a false notion that God's power being stronger would make His maqdūr (thing capable) prevail over the servant's. Ghazālī argues that since both powers are for ikhtirāʿ (creation), and creation is equal, no preference arises. The decisive proof for two powers leads to the necessity of a single maqdūr between two qādīrs (powerful beings). An objection follows: this concept is incomprehensible. The answer clarifies: God can create both the movement and the servant's power simultaneously. The servant then has a power (qudra) over the movement, but God alone is its creator (mukhtariʿ). The servant is qādir (capable) and differs from an involuntary agent like a trembler. The summary states that God creates both the power and its object, so He alone is called Khāliq and Mukhtariʿ. The servant is not called khāliq because his power does not bring about the act independently; the act exists with his power but not by it. Hence the term kasb (acquisition) is used, taken from the Qur'an, and there is no dispute over names once meanings are understood.

Defining Kasb: The Servant's Role
Clarification of intelligibility and definition of kasb.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali makes the concept of a maqdur between two powers intelligible and why the servant's act is called kasb, not creation.

Core Idea
The servant is capable but not creator; his act is an acquisition (kasb).

After proving the possibility, Ghazali addresses an objection that the concept of a thing between two powers is unintelligible. He clarifies by explaining that God creates both the act and the servant's power, making the servant genuinely capable without being a creator. This leads to the terminological distinction between Khaliq and kasb, based on Qur'anic usage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Read the objection in the passage: 'If it is said: The proof does not lead to something impossible that is not understood, and what you have mentioned is not understood?' Recognize the challenge against the intelligibility of maqdur bayn qadirayn.
2
Understand the answer
Ghazali says: 'We must make it understood' and then explains that God creates the movement and creates power over it in the servant. The servant becomes capable (qādir) and differs from a trembler. Trace how this makes the concept clear.
3
Grasp the summary
Read the summary: 'The capable one is vast in power, so He is capable of creating the power and the capable thing together.' This reinforces that God creates both the act and the power, so He alone is Creator.
4
Learn the terminological conclusion
Notice why the servant is not called Khāliq or Mukhtariʿ: because his power does not bring about the act independently. The term kasb is adopted from the Qur'an. Understand that the name is secondary after the meaning is clear.

Ghazālī's Defense of Power's Non-Causal Relation
Ghazālī defends power's non-causal relation using will and knowledge as analogies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazālī's argument that the relation of divine power to its object is not limited to causing it, but can exist as a prior capacity, supported by analogies with will and knowledge.

Core Idea
Power's relation does not equal causation; otherwise all relational attributes would collapse.

In this passage, Ghazālī responds to an objection that seems to challenge the very coherence of the concept of 'power' (qudra) if it does not cause its object. The objector insists that relation must be causal. Ghazālī cleverly turns this against the objector, showing that the same logic would undermine other attributes like will and knowledge. The discussion uses a reductio ad absurdum to defend the Ashʿarī position on human power.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Objection
Read the objection carefully: the opponent argues that a power without a causal relation to its capable thing is impossible, as it would be like knowledge without a known object. Notice the insistence that relation must be through effect and bringing into existence.
2
Identify the Response's Structure
Ghazālī's answer has two prongs: first, if relation were confined to causing occurrence, then will and knowledge would be invalid; second, consider power before the act or continuing after it—the objector would have to deny relation, which is impossible.
This shows the reductio strategy.
3
Analyze the Analogies
Examine how Ghazālī compares power to will and knowledge. Both are relational attributes (ṣifāt mutaʿalliqa) that have objects without necessarily causing them. This undermines the objector's narrow definition of relation.
Will is related to something it may not effect; knowledge is related to something that may not exist externally.
4
Reflect on the Theological Stakes
Consider why this matters: if power could only relate causally, then human power (qudra ḥāditha) could not be truly 'power' if it doesn't create its effect. Ghazālī's defense preserves the coherence of power as a real attribute without committing to human creation of acts.
This is central to the Ashʿarī doctrine of acquisition (kasb).

If it is said: The matter is in understanding the meaning, and what you have mentioned is not understood. For the power: what is the power that you affirm—the created, originated one—if it has no relation (taʿalluq) to the capable thing, it is not understood; since a power with no capable thing for the servant and no reality is impossible, like knowledge with no known thing. And if it is related to it, the relation of power to the capable thing is not conceivable except in terms of effect (taʾthīr), bringing into existence (ījād), and the occurrence of the capable thing by it. So the relationship between the capable thing and power is the relationship of the caused (musabbab) to the cause (sabab), which is its being by it. So if it is not by it, there is no relation between them, and then it is not a power; since whatever has no relation is not a power—for power is among the relational attributes (ṣifāt mutaʿalliqa)? We say: It is related. And your statement: 'Relation is confined to occurrence by it' invalidates the relation of will (irāda) and knowledge (ʿilm). And if you say: 'The relation of power is confined to occurrence by it,' that is also false. For power, according to you, if it is supposed to remain, and if it is supposed before the act, is it related or not? If you say: No, then it is impossible.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer in the kalam style. The objector challenges the coherence of a power that is related to a maqdūr (thing capable) but does not cause it. He argues that a power without a maqdūr is impossible, and that relation must be of causation (taʾthīr and ījād). The reply uses a reductio: if relation were confined to causing occurrence, then other relational attributes like will (irāda) and knowledge (ʿilm) would be invalid, since they have objects that may not occur. Ghazālī questions: if power is supposed to continue after the act or exist before the act, is it still related? The opponent would have to say no, which is impossible because a power without any relation is inconceivable. Thus, the relation of power is not limited to causing the act; it can be a prior capacity. This defends the Ashʿarī position that the servant's power does not create but exists concurrently with the act, which God creates.

Attachment of Power Beyond Causation
Ghazali refutes the claim that power is only real if it directly causes the act.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that attachment (ta'aluq) of power to an act is not limited to causing the act, and that there are different modes of attachment.

Core Idea

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer section. The objection asks whether God's objects of knowledge have an end (i.e., are finite). Ghazali answers 'no' and provides two reasons. First, although present existent things are finite, future possible things are infinite, and God knows them (whether He will create them or not). Second, even a single existent thing can have infinite relations and estimations, as illustrated by the example of doubling numbers. Starting from two, doubling produces an infinite series (2,4,8,16...). The series never ends, and a human can only grasp a few steps before death. But God knows all of it. Moreover, this applies to every number, not just two. Thus, God's knowledge encompasses infinite objects. The passage ends with the claim that this knowledge, despite being related to infinite objects, is itself one (a single attribute), a point to be explained later. A marginal note clarifies that the example is intended to show that God's knowledge can relate to infinite possibilities, not that God knows the 'end of number', since number is an imaginary concept with no real existence.

Why God Must Have Will: Refuting al-Ka'bi's Sufficiency of Knowledge
Ghazali proves God has will and refutes al-Ka'bi's sufficiency of knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali argues that God's knowledge alone cannot explain why one possible action occurs over another, and why will is necessary as the preponderator.

Core Idea
Knowledge follows reality; will determines it.

In this passage, Ghazali establishes the divine attribute of will by arguing that neither God's essence, power, nor knowledge can serve as the preponderator that specifies one possible action over another. He directly refutes al-Ka'bi's claim that knowledge alone suffices, showing that knowledge is passive and cannot break the symmetry of equal possibilities. Only will can do so.

Guided Path
1
Read the claim and proof structure
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its translation to grasp the overall claim: God is willing, and the proof is based on the need for a preponderator.
Notice how Ghazali announces his thesis immediately.
2
Identify why essence, power, and knowledge are insufficient
Follow the argument as Ghazali eliminates each candidate for preponderator: essence (relation to opposites is equal), power (same relation), and knowledge (follows the known, cannot affect it).
Focus on the phrase 'نسبة الذات إلى الضدين واحد' — this is key.
3
Apply the argument to the existence of the world
See how the general proof is applied: all possible times for the world's existence are equal in possibility, so knowledge alone cannot specify the actual time. Only will can explain why the world occurred when it did.
This shows the practical theological importance of the proof.

We claim: God, exalted, is willing (murīd) for His actions. Its proof: that the action proceeding from Him is specified with types of possibility, some are not distinguished from others except by a preponderator (murajjiḥ). His essence alone is not sufficient for preponderance, because the relation of the essence to the two opposites is one. So what specifies one of the two opposites to occur in one state rather than another?! Similarly, power (qudra) is not sufficient for it, because the relation of power to the two opposites is one. Likewise, knowledge (ʿilm) is not sufficient, contrary to al-Kaʿbī, who sufficed with knowledge instead of will (irāda), because knowledge follows the known and attaches to it as it is, and does not affect it or change it. For if a thing is possible in itself, equal to the other possible opposite... then knowledge attaches to it as it is, so we do not make one of the two possibles preponderant over the other. Commentary
This passage presents a sophisticated objection against the Ash'ari explanation of how the eternal divine will specifies one temporal event over another, leading to an infinite regress. Ghazali then answers by classifying the opinions of four groups on the relation of God's will to the world's creation.

The objection runs as follows: If will is needed to specify what power and knowledge alone cannot, then the eternal will itself stands in need of another specifier, since it is equally related to two opposites (e.g., motion vs. rest, or one time vs. another). This demand for a specifier can be repeated indefinitely—hence infinite regress. 

Ghazali answers that this question has perplexed all groups except Ahl al-Sunna. He then enumerates four schools of thought on how the world came to be:
1) Philosophers: The world exists necessarily from God's essence, like light from the sun—hence it is eternal.
2) Mu'tazila: The world is originated by an originated will not in a locus (i.e., a created will in no substrate).
3) Anthropomorphists (Karrāmiyya): The world is created by an originated will that occurs in God's essence, making Him a locus for temporal events.
4) Ahl al-Sunna: The world occurs at the moment specified by the eternal will without any new will or change in God's attributes.

Ghazali concludes that only Ahl al-Sunna's position—positing a single eternal will without temporal origination—avoids the infinite regress and is easy to resolve. Each other view has an insurmountable problem.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

In this passage, Ghazali tackles one of the most contested issues in kalam: the nature of God's will. The Mu'tazila claimed that God wills by an originated will that does not subsist in Him. Ghazali counters that such a claim is not just false but linguistically incoherent, because if the will does not inhere in God, then saying 'He wills by it' is like saying He wills by someone else's will. Ghazali presents a dilemma against the Mu'tazila regarding the timing of the will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's second refutation of the Mu'tazila's claim about the timing of the divine will and how it leads to either infinite regress or the possibility of the world occurring without a will.

Core Idea
The timing of the will cannot be explained without infinite regress or arbitrariness.

Ghazali continues his critique of the Mu'tazilite view on the divine will by focusing on a simple but devastating question: why does the will occur at this specific time? The Mu'tazila claim that the will is an accident that originates at a particular moment. But if it originates, there must be a cause for its specific timing. In this passage, Ghazali shows that the Mu'tazilite position either leads to an infinite regress of wills or to the admission that the world could originate without a will—both unacceptable.

Resolving the Tension Between Divine Will and Command
Ghazali answers the Mu'tazila objections by distinguishing command from will and defining good/evil relative to purposes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali resolves the apparent contradiction between God willing evil and commanding against it, by distinguishing will from command and clarifying the nature of good and evil.

Core Idea
Command and will are distinct; God may will what He commands against.

In response to the Mu'tazila objection that it is contradictory for God to command what He does not will, and to will evil, Ghazali provides a twofold answer. He first distinguishes between divine command (amr) and divine will (irada). Then he explains that the concepts of good (husn) and evil (qubh) are relative to purposes (aghrad), and God is free from all purposes. This lesson unpacks this response, which forms a cornerstone of Ash'ari theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Read the Arabic passage containing the Mu'tazila question and Ghazali's answer. Focus on the key distinctions he makes.
The question is: How can God command what He does not will? And how can He will evil without being foolish?
2
Understand the distinction between command and will
Ghazali says that 'command is distinct from will.' What does that mean? Consider that a king may command his subjects to do something, but will that they fail. This is possible if command pertains to obligation, not to actual existence.
3
Grasp the meaning of good and evil as relative to purposes
Ghazali states that good and evil are not intrinsic but are defined by agreement with purposes. Since God has no purposes, actions like evil are not 'evil' in the sense of being contrary to His goals. Thus, God willing evil is not a sign of foolishness.
This is a key Ash'ari doctrine about moral values.
4
Connect to the broader Islamic creed
Reflect on how this defense upholds God's omnipotence and sovereignty while acknowledging that His commands guide human morality.
This lesson prepares for further discussion on why God creates evil.
