The Roles of God, Prophet, Reason, Miracle, and Nature in Obligation
Ghazali resolves the objection by delineating the roles of God, prophet, reason, miracle, and nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's precise delineation of the roles of God, the prophet, reason, miracle, reflection, and nature in the process of obligation.

Core Idea
Obligation originates from God; all other actors are instruments in conveying or understanding it.

Ghazali now responds to the objection by systematically assigning each element its proper role. He defines obligation as preponderance (rujḥān) and identifies God as the sole Obligator. The messenger informs of this preponderance, the miracle proves his truthfulness, reflection verifies the miracle, reason understands the warning, and nature impels action after understanding. This clarifies the entire mechanism and resolves the tension between reason and revelation.

Guided Path
1
Read Ghazali's definition of obligation
Notice that obligation is defined as a type of preponderance in the action, and the Obligator is God alone, who gives that preponderance.
This distinguishes God's role from all others.
2
Identify each role
List the roles: messenger (informer), miracle (evidence), reflection (cause of knowing truthfulness), reason (instrument), nature (impeller). Understand how each contributes.
Each role is necessary and distinct.
3
Understand the necessity of nature
Nature impels to caution after understanding the danger through reason. It must exist for reward or punishment to be deserved.
Nature is the motivational force, not the source of obligation.
4
Summarize the corrected terminology
Ghazali concludes with a clear summary: obligation=preponderance, Obligator=God, informer=messenger, identifier of danger=reason, impeller=nature.
This is the correct understanding, free from ambiguity.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer argument that clarifies the mechanism of obligation (al-wujūb) and the precise roles of God, the prophet, reason (al-ʿaql), and nature (al-ṭabʿ). The passage begins with an analogy: a patient who refuses treatment until it is proven to him by reason or the doctor's word would kill himself, but the doctor is not at fault. Similarly, the prophet's duty is only to convey and guide; people's failure or success is their own. Then an objection is raised: if reason is what makes obligation effective because it anticipates punishment and thus impels caution, then reflection (al-naẓar) becomes obligatory by reason alone. In answer, Ghazali lays out the true picture: obligation is a type of preponderance (rujḥān) in the action, and the Obligator (al-mūjib) is God alone, who gives preponderance. The messenger (al-rasūl) informs of this preponderance. The miracle (al-muʿjiza) proves his truthfulness. Reflection is the cause of knowing that truthfulness. Reason is the instrument for reflection and for understanding the meaning of the message. Nature (al-ṭabʿ) is what impels one to caution after understanding the danger through reason. Nature must exist to make the person deserving of reward or punishment, but it does not impel unless it understands the danger, and it only understands through reason. Reason itself does not perceive preponderance by itself; it learns it from the messenger. The messenger does not create preponderance; God does. The miracle's indication depends on reflection, which is carried out by reason. The passage concludes with a final summary of correct terminology: obligation is preponderance, the obligator is God, the informer is the messenger, the identifier of danger and truthfulness is reason, and the impeller to salvation is nature. This is the truth, and one should ignore conventional speech that fails to resolve the issue.

Denying Only the Impossible: Ghazali's Principle
Ghazali argues that what is not proven impossible should not be denied due to mere improbability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali argues that only proof of impossibility, not improbability, justifies denial of a claim.

Core Idea
Improbability is not a valid reason to deny a claim; only impossibility is.

In this passage, Ghazali tackles a common fallacy: rejecting miraculous or unseen events because they seem unlikely. He draws on examples from revelation, dreams, and human creation to show that subjective improbability is not a sound criterion for denial. Instead, he establishes a key principle of Islamic theology: what is not proven impossible should not be denied.

Guided Path
1

The passage is dense; reading twice helps.
2
Identify the examples
Note the three examples Ghazali gives: the state of revelation, dream experiences, and the creation of man from a drop. Ask why each supports his argument.
3
Understand the logical implication
Ghazali shows that rejecting improbabilities would lead to rejecting universally accepted things like dreams and human creation. This highlights the flaw in the fallacy.
He uses a reductio ad absurdum.
4
Grasp the concluding principle
The final sentence is the key: 'What is not proven impossible should not be denied merely due to improbability.' Memorize this principle.
This is a foundational rule in kalam.

Condition and Cause: Two Types of Necessity
Ghazali defines condition and cause as types of necessary relations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's distinction between a condition (shart) and a cause ('illa) as two different types of necessary relations.

Core Idea
A condition is necessary but not sufficient; a cause produces its effect necessarily.

Ghazali introduces two key concepts for understanding necessity in the world: condition and cause. While both involve a necessary link, they function differently. This passage defines each with an example, preparing you to analyse causal relationships in theology and philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without stopping. Then read the English translation. Notice how Ghazali moves from an example (knowledge, life, will) to a general definition of condition, then to cause.
The passage has some textual issues, but the main ideas are clear.
2
Identify the two definitions
Find where Ghazali defines 'condition' and 'cause'. For condition, note the phrase 'لا بد منه لوجود الشيء' (indispensable for existence) and 'ليس وجود الشيء به بل عنده ومعه' (not by it, but with it). For cause, note the claim about the relationship between cause and effect.
The definition of cause includes a nuance about multiple causes.
3
Apply the distinction to the example
Consider the example: life is a condition for knowledge, and knowledge is a condition for will. Does life cause knowledge? No, it is merely a condition. This illustrates the difference.
Think of other examples: a key is a condition for opening a lock, but does not cause the lock to open by itself.

However, if the effect has multiple causes, then negating one specific cause does not necessitate the negation of the effect absolutely; it only necessitates the negation of the effect that is specific to that cause. Conditions for Takfir Against Deniers of Mutawatir Matters
Ghazali outlines conditions for takfir against deniers of mutawatir matters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the conditions under which denying a mutawatir report leads to takfir, including the exception for recent converts and the distinction between essential and non-essential matters.

Core Idea
Not every denial of a mutawatir report makes one a disbeliever.

Ghazali begins by clarifying a crucial point: while denying matters known by mass transmission (tawatur) generally incurs takfir, there are important nuances. He explains that such matters are grasped by both commoners and elites, but the ruling depends on whether the person has received the tawatur and whether the matter is a fundamental of religion. This lesson unpacks those conditions.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how Ghazali states the general rule and then immediately provides exceptions.
The passage is concise; pay attention to the shift from general to specific.
2
Identify the conditions for takfir
Look for the condition that the person must have received the tawatur (not a recent convert) and that the denied matter must be a fundamental of religion (like Hajj, prayer, pillars of Islam).
Ghazali gives examples of non-fundamentals: specific battles, the Prophet's marriage to Hafsa, existence of Abu Bakr.
3
Ask: Why does Ghazali exempt recent converts?
Consider that knowledge through tawatur requires exposure to widespread reports. A new Muslim may not have encountered them yet. This shows Ghazali's concern for fairness.
This connects to the broader principle of excusing ignorance in matters of detail.

The Distinction Between Tawatur and Ijma in Certainty
Ghazali distinguishes tawatur from ijma in terms of certainty and necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali distinguishes tawatur (which produces necessary knowledge) from ijma (which provides only theoretical certainty) and why this matters for takfir.

Core Idea
Tawatur yields necessary knowledge; ijma yields only theoretical certainty based on revelation.

After discussing the ruling on mutawatir matters, Ghazali turns to the role of consensus (ijma). He argues that contradicting ijma does not automatically incur takfir because ijma is not a source of necessary knowledge. He contrasts the agreement of a large number on a perceived object (tawatur) with the agreement of scholars on a theoretical opinion (ijma). This lesson unpacks that crucial distinction.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on tawatur vs ijma
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. Note how Ghazali defines each and their epistemic value.
The key sentence: 'تطابق العدد الكثير على الإخبار عن محسوس على سبيل التواتر يوجب العلم الضروري' vs 'تطابق أهل الحل والعقد على رأي واحد نظري لا يوجب العلم إلا من جهة الشرع.'
2
Identify the epistemological difference
Tawatur about perceptibles gives necessary knowledge (direct certainty). Ijma on a theoretical matter gives knowledge only through revelation (i.e., it is a legal proof, not rational).
This explains why Ghazali does not immediately declare a person a disbeliever for rejecting ijma.
3
Ask: Why does Ghazali mention the example of the world's temporal origination?
He says tawatur of reports from investigators about the world's origination does not produce necessary knowledge because it is not about a perceptible matter. This reinforces that tawatur only applies to sensory objects.
This is a critique of using tawatur in theological arguments.

1
Read the sixth rank definition
Read the opening lines of the passage where Ghazali defines the sixth rank. Notice that it is about denying something known by mere consensus (ijma mujarrad), not tawatur.
The key phrase: 'ينكر ما علم صحته بالإجماع المجرد'.
2
Analyze the example of an-Nazzam
Ghazali uses an-Nazzam as an example: he denied that consensus is a decisive proof. Ghazali notes that an-Nazzam also violated the consensus of the Successors (tabi'un).
This shows the tension: rejecting the validity of ijma while simultaneously violating an actual consensus.
3
Understand Ghazali's hesitation
Ghazali says 'ولي فيه نظر' (I have difficulty with it), acknowledging that the issue of ijma's authority is open to scholarly debate.
He fears that opening the door to rejecting ijma could lead to harmful innovations.
4
Examine the dangerous consequence
Ghazali presents a hypothetical: if someone claims a prophet after Muhammad, the only proof against it is consensus. The denier could interpret texts to escape literal meaning. The refutation is that the ummah understood by consensus that no prophet comes after Muhammad.
This shows that in such cases, the only safeguard is the consensus of the ummah.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of different levels of denial and their implications for takfir (declaring someone a disbeliever). Imam Ghazali begins by reasserting that a person who denies matters known by tawatur (mass transmission) should be judged a disbeliever, but with a caveat: such matters are grasped by both commoners and elites. However, if the person is a recent convert and the tawatur has not yet reached him, we give him time. Denying something like a specific battle or the marriage of the Prophet does not entail takfir because it is not a fundamental of religion. But denying the Hajj, prayer, or the pillars of Islam does. Then Ghazali turns to consensus (ijma). He says we do not declare a person a disbeliever merely for rejecting consensus because there is a great deal of doubt about whether consensus provides decisive certainty. He distinguishes between tawatur (which produces necessary knowledge) and ijma (which is a theoretical agreement). The sixth rank is when a person does not deny something known by tawatur but denies something known only by mere consensus (ijma mujarrad), such as an-Nazzam denying that consensus is a decisive proof. Ghazali notes that an-Nazzam violated the consensus of the Successors. Ghazali then says this is a matter of ijtihad and he has difficulty with it because there are many problems with consensus being a proof. However, if we open the door to rejecting consensus, it could lead to abominable claims like the possibility of a prophet after Muhammad. Reason alone does not prohibit it; only consensus does. And the denier would interpret the texts. Ghazali argues that the ummah understood by consensus that there is no prophet after Muhammad, and this understanding is based on the Prophet's words and circumstantial evidence. So the denier is essentially a denier of consensus. The passage ends by noting that this issue branches into many interconnected matters.
