Why Logic Was Once Prohibited
Editor explains the historical mixing of logic with philosophy and the resulting prohibition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why some scholars prohibited the study of logic due to its association with philosophy.

Core Idea
Logic was mixed with philosophy, causing scholars to prohibit it.

This lesson sets the historical backdrop for al-Ghazali's work. The passage from the editor explains that logic as studied today was not pure; it was entangled with philosophical doctrines, both divine and atheistic. This mixture led respected scholars like Ibn al-Salah and al-Nawawi to declare its study forbidden. Let's explore why.

The example of the Mu'tazila is given as a warning.

This lesson sets the stage for appreciating Ghazali's contribution.

Pure Logic is Permissible and Ghazali's Role
Editor distinguishes pure logic from mixed logic and defends al-Ghazali's study of it.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Pure logic, like mathematics, is a precise tool never prohibited by scholars.

This lesson examines a key distinction in Islamic scholarship: pure logic versus logic mixed with philosophical errors. The editor defends Imam al-Ghazali against critics like Ibn al-Salah, arguing that al-Ghazali's intellectual stature made him immune to the dangers of mixed logic. Through this passage, we see the nuanced approach to logic in the Islamic tradition.

The Third Type of Premise: Mutawatirat
Ghazali defines mutawatirat with the example of Muhammad's prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how mutawatirat (recurrent reports) function as a premise in theological argument, using the example of Muhammad's prophethood.

Core Idea
Recurrent reports yield certain knowledge, just like sensory evidence.

Ghazali introduces the third type of premise: mutawatirat. He illustrates it with the proof of Muhammad's prophethood, showing how tawatur establishes the attribution of the Qur'an to him. This lesson explores that argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation to grasp the flow of argument.
Notice the syllogistic structure: major premise, minor premise, conclusion.
2
Identify the syllogistic structure
Find the two premises and the conclusion. Then identify the objection and the response using tawatur.
The objection denies the minor premise; the response uses tawatur to prove it.
3
Understand the role of tawatur
Reflect on why tawatur is necessary: it provides certain knowledge of historical facts that cannot be proven by reason or direct observation.
Tawatur applies to the attribution of the Qur'an to Muhammad, just as to the existence of Mecca and past prophets.

Commentary
This passage defines the third type of premise: mutawatirat (recurrent reports). Ghazali gives the example of proving Muhammad's truthfulness: a syllogism with two premises—(1) everyone who brings a miracle is truthful, (2) Muhammad brought a miracle (the Qur'an). The objection is denying that he brought a miracle. The response: the Qur'an is a miracle. If the opponent concedes that the Qur'an is a miracle but denies that Muhammad brought it (i.e., that it is his speech), then Ghazali invokes tawatur: the report that Muhammad came with the Qur'an is so widely transmitted that it yields certain knowledge, just like knowledge of his existence, his claim, and the existence of Mecca and earlier prophets. This demonstrates that tawatur can establish premises that are not otherwise provable by reason or sense.

Refuting the Latency Objection
Ghazali responds to the objection that accidents might be latent rather than originated.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali refutes the objection that accidents might be latent without needing to disprove the latency theory itself.

Core Idea
Ghazali sidesteps the latency objection by showing it still implies originated accidents.

In this passage, Ghazali encounters a clever objection: maybe accidents like motion are not created anew but simply become manifest from a latent state. If so, they would not be 'originated'. Rather than delving into a full refutation of the latency theory, Ghazali takes a more efficient route. He argues that even if latency is true, the atom is never free from either the latent or manifest state, and both states are accidents that come into being, so the atom still never ceases to have originated events.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully.
Notice the objector's challenge: perhaps accidents like motion are not newly created but were latent (kāmin) and then became manifest. This would undermine the argument that the atom always has originated accidents.
The objector uses the term 'kumun' (latency).
2
See how Ghazali chooses not to engage directly.
Ghazali says he could refute the latency theory entirely but it is unnecessary for his purpose. He wants to show that even if latency were true, his argument still holds.
This reveals Ghazali's strategic economy in argument.
3
Understand his alternative argument.
He argues: the atom is never free from either the latency of motion/rest or its manifestation. Both latency and manifestation are accidents (a‘rāḍ) and are temporally originated (ḥādithān). So the atom always has some originated accident.
Even latency itself is an accident that comes into being.
4
Recognize the logical conclusion.
Since the atom always has either a latent or manifest accident, and both are originated, it follows that the atom never ceases to have temporally originated events. Therefore, the original principle still holds.
The objection is neutralized without disproving latency.

Source Text

فإن قيل : فيمَ عرفتم أنها حادثة ؟ فلعلها كانت كامنة فظهرت تحريجة : فلماذا لا نقول بالكمون ؟ قلنا : لو كنا نشتغل في هذا الكتاب بالفضول الخارج عن المقصود لأبطلنا القول بالكمون والظهور في الأعراض رأساً ، ولكن ما لا يُبطل مقصودنا فلسنا نشتغل به ، بل نقول : الجوهر لا يخلو عن كمون الحركة فيه أو ظهورها ، وهما حادثان .. فقد ثبت أنه لا يخلو عن الحوادث

But what does not invalidate our purpose we do not engage with. Rather, we say: The atom is never free from either the latency of motion in it or its manifestation, and both are temporally originated. Therefore, the atom still never ceases to have temporally originated accidents, fulfilling the condition of the principle. The potential refutation of latency is set aside for brevity.

Refuting the Objection of Transference (Intiqal)
Ghazali rejects the suggestion that accidents can transfer from one body to another.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali argues that the transference of accidents is impossible based on the conceptual definitions of accident and transference.

Core Idea
Transference requires an independent location; accidents cannot transfer.

This passage deals with an objection against Ghazali's proof for the creation of accidents. The objector suggests that an accident might simply transfer from another body, avoiding the need for creation. Ghazali responds by clarifying the very meanings of 'accident' and 'transference', showing that the idea is conceptually incoherent.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and Ghazali's response
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to understand the flow of the objection and the response.
Notice that Ghazali begins by dismissing weak proofs before presenting his own approach.
2
Understand Ghazali's approach
Ghazali sets aside weak proofs and focuses on conceptual analysis. This is key to his method.
3
Grasp the definition of transference
Transference is defined as the movement of a substance from one location to another. Understand that this requires an independent subject.
4
Apply to accidents
Accidents are defined as subsisting in substances. They have no independent location, so transference is impossible.
Reflect on why the objector's suggestion fails.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
فإن قيل : فلعلها انتقلت إليه من موضع آخر ، فيمَ يُعرفُ بطلان القول تحريجة: فلم لا بانتقال الأعراض ؟ قلنا : قد ذُكر في إيطال ذلك أدلةٌ ضعيفة لا نطول بنقلها ونقضها الكتاب ، لكن الصحيحُ في الكشف عن بطلانه أن نبينَ أنَّ تجويز ذلك لا يتسع له عقل ما لم يذهل عن فهم حقيقة العرض وحقيقة الانتقال ، ومَنْ فهم حقيقة العرض . . تحقق استحالة الانتقال فيه . وبيانه : أنَّ الانتقال عبارةٌ أُخذتْ من انتقال الجوهر من حيز إلى حيز ، وذلك ثبت في العقل بأنْ فُهِمَ الجوهرُ ، وفهم الحيرُ ، وفُهِم أَنَّ اختصاص الجوهر بحيز دون حير إنما هو بالحالة التي هي كونه في ذلك الحيز، فما بالك بالعرض الذي لا يفهم إلا قائماً بالجوهر، فلا ينتقل؟
If it is said: 'Perhaps they transferred to it from another place. How is the invalidity of this statement known?' An objection: 'Why not transference of accidents?' We say: Weak proofs have been mentioned to invalidate it, and we will not lengthen the book by transmitting and refuting them. Rather, the correct way to uncover its invalidity is to show that the intellect cannot accommodate that possibility unless one is heedless of understanding the reality of accident (ʻaraḍ) and the reality of transference. Whoever understands the reality of accident realizes the impossibility of transference in it. Its explanation: Transference is a term taken from the transference of an atom from one location (ḥayyiz) to another. That is established in the intellect by understanding the atom, understanding the location, and understanding that the atom's specification to a location is by the state of being in that location. So what about the accident, which is only understood as subsisting in the atom? It cannot transfer.
Source pages p. 95

Commentary
This passage is another objection-and-answer. The objector suggests that an accident (like motion) might transfer from another body into the atom, so it would not need to be originated in the atom. Ghazali rejects this by focusing on the nature of accident and transference. He says weak proofs exist but he will instead explain that transference is essentially the movement of a substance (jawhar) from one location to another. This is possible for substances because they can be conceived as occupying different places. However, an accident is defined as something that subsists in a substance; it has no independent location. Transference requires a subject that moves, but an accident cannot move because it does not have its own location—it inheres in the substance. Therefore, the very concept of an accident transferring is incoherent once one understands the realities involved.

Commentary
This passage classifies the possible relationships between two things that are actually connected. Ghazali identifies three types.

1. Mutual relationship (mutakāfi'a): The two things are correlatives (muṭaḍāyifāt) where the concept of one depends on the other, like right/left and above/below. If one is negated, the other is necessarily negated.

2. Conditional relationship (sharṭ/mashrūṭ): One thing is a condition for the other, having precedence. The condition is necessary for the existence of the conditioned, but the conditioned is not caused by it; rather, it accompanies it. Example: life is a condition for knowledge; will is a condition for knowledge? Actually, he uses knowledge with life and will with knowledge: life is a condition for knowledge, and knowledge is a condition for will. Thus, negation of life negates knowledge; negation of knowledge negates will.

3. Causal relationship ('illa/ma'lūl): A cause produces its effect. If there is only one cause, its negation negates the effect. If there are multiple causes, negating all causes negates the effect, but negating only one specific cause does not negate the effect completely—only the effect particular to that cause is negated.

This classification is crucial for analyzing the killing/death issue that follows.
