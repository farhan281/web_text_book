Critique of Verbal Disputes and the Rizq Controversy
Ghazali criticizes verbal disputes using the rizq example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's criticism of theologians who argue over word definitions while neglecting more important issues, using the dispute about rizq as an example.

Core Idea
Not all theological debates are worthwhile; some are just word games.

After establishing the psychological dimension of belief, Ghazali turns to a critique of certain theologians (mutahaddiqun) who engage in lengthy debates about the definitions of terms like 'rizq' (provision). He contrasts the Mu'tazilite and Ash'arite positions, but his main point is that such disputes distract from truly important matters. This lesson explores that critique and its relevance for prioritizing theological inquiry.

Guided Path
1
Read the critique passage
Read the Arabic from 'فهذه أمور يجحدها المتحذلقون في الكلام' to the end of the passage. Then read the translation. Notice the shift from the previous discussion to a polemical tone.
The passage is self-contained in its criticism.
2
Understand the rizq dispute
Identify the two positions: Mu'tazila restrict rizq to what one lawfully possesses; Ash'arites define it as anything beneficial. Note the absurd consequence Ghazali mentions about oppressors.
Ghazali does not side explicitly here but criticizes the prolongation.
3
Analyze Ghazali's criticism
Ghazali calls these disputants 'mutahaddiqun' (pretentious) and says they know only words, not the 'taste of reflection'. He argues such debates waste time that should be spent on more important problems.
This is a methodological critique, not just a doctrinal one.
4
Apply the lesson to your own study
Consider: Are there theological or religious debates today that might be mere verbal disputes? How can we distinguish between essential and trivial discussions?
Ghazali's advice is to prioritize what truly matters.

Commentary
This passage is an argument about how consistent practice (muwazaba) fortifies and deepens belief (i'tiqad) in the soul, followed by a critique of those who engage in purely verbal disputes, such as the disagreement over the definition of 'rizq' (provision). The author begins by stating that this strengthening is known only through introspection: one who regularly performs acts of obedience finds his heart more at ease with his beliefs, making them harder to shake. He illustrates with examples: showing mercy to an orphan through action increases the feeling of mercy; humbling oneself physically increases inner humility. This is why Islamic worship includes repeated physical acts like bowing and prostration—they increase the heart's reverence. Then he criticizes the 'pretentious in kalam' (mutahaddiqun) who know the order of the sciences only from hearing words, not from the 'taste of reflection' (dhawq al-nazar). The dispute over rizq is an example of this: the Mu'tazila restricted it to what one owns or what is lawful, leading to absurdities like claiming oppressors were not provided for. The Ash'arites (as'habuna) define it more broadly as anything beneficial. The author laments that such definitions waste time better spent on important matters, and prays for guidance to focus on what matters.

The Non-Muslim's Command and Kalam's Limits
Ghazali rules on a non-Muslim's command and limits kalam's scope.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's ruling on a non-Muslim commanding another to Islam and his assertion that such detailed issues are outside the scope of kalam.

Core Idea
Kalam is not for every detail; some rulings belong to fiqh, not creed.

Ghazali presents a specific case: can a non-Muslim compel another to embrace Islam? After giving a nuanced ruling, he steps back and makes a methodological observation. This lesson explores both the ruling and why he included it, showing how Ghazali distinguished between theological and legal discussions.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the shift from a specific ruling to a broader methodological point.
2
Understand the ruling
Identify Ghazali's ruling: we do not prevent a non-Muslim from compelling another to Islam. Understand the reasoning about obligation and conditions.
The ruling is based on the obligation to command good, regardless of the commander's faith.
3
Examine the hadith evidence
Read the hadith of the Jewish boy. Reflect on how it supports the ruling and shows the practice of the Prophet.
The hadith confirms that calling a non-Muslim to Islam is valid and praiseworthy.
4
Grasp the methodological conclusion
Ghazali says such issues are not suitable for kalam, especially in concise creedal works. Consider why he makes this point.
Kalam focuses on foundational beliefs; detailed legal rulings belong to fiqh.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on a non-Muslim commanding another non-Muslim to Islam, and then a methodological conclusion. First, Ghazali states that if a non-Muslim compels another non-Muslim to Islam by the sword, we do not prevent him. The reasoning is that the non-Muslim is obligated to utter the shahada and to command others, but it has not been established that his own utterance is a condition for his commanding. Therefore, he may utter without commanding, or command without uttering. After this, Ghazali notes that this is the depth of the issue and that he only included it to show that such detailed questions are not suitable for the discipline of kalam, especially in concise creedal summaries. He then cites a hadith from Sahih al-Bukhari to confirm that the issue is not merely hypothetical: the Prophet (peace be upon him) visited a Jewish boy who served him, invited him to Islam, and the boy converted after his father told him to obey the Prophet. The hadith ends with the Prophet praising Allah for saving the boy from the Fire. This hadith demonstrates the validity of calling a non-Muslim to Islam.

Ghazali's Proof for the Obligation of the Imam (Part 1)

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The obligation of the Imam is proven by a decisive Sharia syllogism.

Ghazali begins by clarifying that the obligation to appoint an Imam is not a matter of pure reason but is derived from divine law. However, he does not rely solely on consensus; he presents a rational argument that reveals the underlying logic of the consensus. This lesson presents the core syllogism that forms the backbone of his proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source of obligation

2
Find the two premises
Ghazali builds a syllogism: (1) The order of religion is certainly intended by the Lawgiver. (2) The order of religion is not achieved except with an obeyed Imam. Identify these in the text.
3
Understand why the first premise is decisive
Ghazali calls the first premise 'decisive' (قطعية) and says no dispute is conceivable about it. Reflect on why this is so — because it is based on clear revealed intent.
4
See the conclusion
From these two premises, the conclusion follows: appointing an Imam is obligatory. Notice how Ghazali explicitly states the conclusion.
This syllogism is the foundation of his entire argument.

Commentary
This passage contains a proof for the obligation of appointing an Imam (nāṣb al-imām). The author, Imam al-Ghazali, begins by clarifying that this obligation is not derived from pure reason but from Sharia, though he will use a rational argument that ultimately rests on a revealed premise. He rejects interpreting 'obligatory' merely as what brings worldly benefit, as that would not be a Sharia obligation. Instead, he builds a decisive Sharia proof based on the consensus of the community, but he goes further to explain the underlying rationale. The proof is a syllogism: (1) The order of religion (niẓām al-dīn) is definitely intended by the Lawgiver (Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him). (2) The order of religion cannot be achieved except with an obeyed Imam (imām muṭāʿ). Therefore, appointing an Imam is obligatory. The first premise is said to be indisputable. The second premise is then defended against an objection. The objection questions why religion cannot be ordered without an Imam. Ghazali responds by breaking the second premise into two further premises: (a) the order of religion cannot be achieved except with the order of this world (niẓām al-dunyā), and (b) the order of this world cannot be achieved except with an obeyed Imam. He then addresses a counter-objection that religion and world are opposites, meaning that building the world ruins religion. Ghazali clarifies that the term 'world' (dunyā) is ambiguous: it can refer to excessive luxury (which is opposed to religion) or to the basic necessities of life (which are a condition for religion). He then explains that religion requires knowledge and worship, and these require a healthy body, life, and basic necessities such as clothing, shelter, food, and security. Without security from oppression and violence, people cannot engage in religious obligations. Historical examples of anarchy after the death of rulers demonstrate that without an obeyed ruler, chaos, violence, and destruction prevail, preventing worship and knowledge. Thus, worldly order in terms of necessities is a condition for religious order. The second premise about the necessity of a ruler for worldly order is supported by empirical observation. The passage ends with famous sayings: 'Religion and sovereignty are twins' and 'Religion is the foundation' (implying that sovereignty is its guardian).

Defending the Need for an Imam: Worldly Order as Condition for Religion
Ghazali defends the need for an Imam by clarifying the role of worldly order.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's defense of the second premise, including the clarification that worldly necessities are a condition for religious order, and the empirical evidence for the necessity of a ruler.

Core Idea
Basic worldly security is a condition for religious practice, not its opposite.

After presenting the syllogism, Ghazali anticipates an objection: perhaps religious order can exist without an Imam. He responds by breaking the second premise into two, then faces a deeper objection that religion and world are opposites. This lesson explores his nuanced response, where he distinguishes between two meanings of 'world' and shows that basic worldly necessities are essential for fulfilling religious obligations.

Read the objection and initial response
Begin with the objection that the second premise is not conceded. Ghazali responds by splitting it into two premises. Identify these two premises.

Understand the counter-objection

Learn the distinction in 'dunya'

See how religion depends on worldly necessities
Religion requires knowledge and worship, which need a healthy body, life, basic needs, and security. Ghazali lists these and shows that without them, religious life is impossible.
5
Examine the empirical proof
Ghazali appeals to historical observation: when rulers die and no successor is appointed, chaos ensues, preventing worship and knowledge. This supports the necessity of an obeyed ruler.
This is a practical, experiential argument.

Yes; it is not permissible that it state that the lie is truth and ignorance is knowledge, and that is not what is sought in this matter. Rather, what is sought is: has the Sharia made this ignorance and lie a cause for revoking his inviolability and ruling that he is eternally in the Fire? And it is like our examination of a child: when he utters the two testimonies, is he an unbeliever or a Muslim after that? That is: this utterance that came from him, which is true, and the belief found in his heart, which is true—has the Sharia made it a cause for the inviolability of his blood and wealth or not? And this is up to the Sharia. As for describing his statement as a lie or his belief as ignorance, that is not up to the Sharia. Therefore, knowledge of lie and ignorance may be rational, but knowledge of his being an unbeliever or a Muslim is only Sharia-based. Rather, it is like our examination in jurisprudence of whether this person is a slave or free. And its meaning: has the cause that occurred been established by the Sharia as invalidating his testimony and guardianship, removing his property, and dropping retaliation from his master who has power over him if he kills him? The established source is: that everyone who denies Muhammad, may God bless him and grant him peace, is an unbeliever; that is, eternally in the Fire after death, and his blood and wealth are permissible in life... and other rulings. However, denial has levels: The first level: the denial of the Jews, Christians, and all people of religion, including the Magians, Christians, and Magians...

Commentary
This passage is an argument establishing that the declaration of someone as an unbeliever (takfir) is a jurisprudential (fiqhi) matter, not a rational one. Imam al-Ghazali begins by noting the excesses and fanaticisms among sects regarding takfir, and then sets out the correct methodology. He states categorically that takfir is a fiqhi matter with no room for rational proof; it is known either definitively or probabilistically from scriptural sources. He then unpacks the meaning of 'unbeliever' (kafir): it involves informing about the person's eternal punishment in the Hereafter and certain worldly rulings (no retaliation, no marriage to a Muslim, blood and wealth not inviolable). He clarifies that while rational proofs can determine that a statement is false or a belief is ignorant, the connection of such falsehood or ignorance to the ruling of unbelief is a separate matter—it is a Sharia-based judgment. The Sharia could theoretically make even a liar or ignorant person eternally in Paradise, but it does not. The core point is that the ruling of unbelief depends on whether the Sharia has made the person's statement or belief a cause for revoking inviolability and eternal punishment. This is analogous to determining whether a child's utterance of the testimony of faith makes him a Muslim—it is a purely Sharia-based determination. Similarly, the question of whether a person is an unbeliever is like asking whether a person is a slave or free in fiqh: it depends on the Sharia's designation. Al-Ghazali then invokes the principles of usul al-fiqh: every Sharia ruling is derived from a source (consensus, transmission, analogy). The established source for unbelief is that anyone who denies the Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) is an unbeliever. However, denial has multiple levels; he begins listing the first level: the denial of Jews, Christians, and all people of religion including Magians. The passage ends mid-sentence, but the main argument—that takfir is a Sharia ruling based on scriptural sources, not rational deduction—is fully established.

Ghazali distinguishes the rational and legal perspectives in determining causes of kufr.
