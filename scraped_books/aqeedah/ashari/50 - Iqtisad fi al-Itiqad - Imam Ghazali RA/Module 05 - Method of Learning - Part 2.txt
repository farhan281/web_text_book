The Third Method: Reductio ad Absurdum
Ghazali defines the third method and provides a worked example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali uses reductio ad absurdum to refute an opponent's claim by showing it leads to an absurdity, without directly proving one's own position.

Core Idea
If a claim leads to absurdity, the claim itself must be false.

In this lesson, Ghazali introduces a powerful logical tool: instead of proving your own view, you demonstrate that your opponent's view inevitably leads to something impossible. This method, called reductio ad absurdum, is a cornerstone of Islamic theological argumentation. The passage provides a clear example and breaks down the argument into its essential components.

Guided Path
1
Read the method description
Start by reading the opening lines describing the third method. Notice that Ghazali explicitly says he does not aim to prove his own claim, but rather to show the opponent's claim leads to absurdity.
The phrase 'لا نتعرض لثبوت دعوانا' is key.
2
Examine the example
Read the example about infinite celestial cycles. Identify the opponent's claim, the absurd consequence, and why that consequence is impossible.
The example uses a physical claim to illustrate the logic.
3
Understand the two roots
Ghazali breaks the argument into two premises (أصلان). Find them in the text and see how they connect to produce the conclusion.
The connection between the two roots is as crucial as the roots themselves.
4
Study the footnote breakdown
Read the footnote (بيان المسألة) which summarises the structure: opponent's claim, first root, second root, third known (conclusion). This reinforces the logical flow.
This clarifies the middle term-like connection.

The third method: we do not address the establishment of our claim, but rather claim the impossibility of the opponent's claim: by showing that it leads to absurdity, and whatever leads to absurdity is itself absurd without exception. Example: our saying: If the opponent's statement that 'the cycles of the celestial sphere have no end' is correct, then it follows the correctness of the statement: 'that which has no end has passed and finished,' and it is known that this consequent is absurd; so from it follows absurdity; so from it follows—necessarily—that that to which it leads is absurd, the opponent's doctrine. So here there are two roots: one of them is our saying: 'If the cycles of the celestial sphere have no end, then that which has no end has passed'—for the judgment of the necessity of the passing of that which has no end on the basis of the claim of negating an end to the cycles of the celestial sphere is knowledge we claim and judge; it is conceivable from the opponent acknowledgment of two roots with a connection between them, like the middle term between the two premises, not merely any two roots; so for reaching this third, two things exist: the existence of the two roots and the existence of a connection between them. And the second: our saying 'this consequent is absurd,' for it is also a root in which denial is conceivable, as when he says: 'I concede the first root, but I do not concede this second, which is the passing of that which has no end.' The third known: the acknowledgment—after the opponent's surrender of both roots together—of the impossibility of the claim, and the falsity of his statement that the cycles of the celestial sphere are infinite, and the proof of its opposite, that they are finite, and that is the sought.

Commentary
This passage presents the third method (al-manhaj al-thālith), which is a form of reductio ad absurdum (burhān al-khalf). Unlike the first two methods, it does not attempt to prove one's own claim directly. Instead, it focuses on demonstrating that the opponent's claim leads to an absurdity (muḥāl). The reasoning is: if the opponent's claim leads to an absurdity, then the claim itself must be impossible (muḥāl). Ghazali provides the example of the opponent asserting that the rotations of the celestial sphere (dawrāt al-falak) are infinite (lā nihāya lahā). This claim implies that something infinite has been completed or passed (inqaḍā), which is impossible. Therefore, the opponent's original claim is false. Ghazali then breaks this argument into two premises (aṣlān): first, that if the rotations are infinite, then the infinite has passed; second, that the passing of the infinite is absurd. If the opponent admits both premises, he must admit the impossibility of his doctrine. The footnote clarifies the structure: the opponent's claim, the first premise (the necessary implication), the second premise (the impossibility of that implication), and the resulting conclusion (the impossibility of the opponent's claim). This method is a powerful tool in kalām for refuting positions that lead to contradictions.

The Structure of Reasoning: Fikr, Talab, and Nazar
Ghazali defines madlul, dalil, and nazar, explaining the roles of fikr and talab.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two components of reasoning (fikr and talab) and how different scholars define nazar based on which component they emphasize.

Core Idea
Reasoning is not just thinking—it is actively seeking the logical link between premises and conclusion.

Imam al-Ghazali concludes his discussion of the three methods of inference by clarifying the technical terms used in reasoning. He explains that the act of reasoning (nazar) comprises two functions: bringing premises to mind (fikr) and seeking the necessary connection to the conclusion (talab). Different definitions of nazar arise depending on which function is emphasized.

The term 'nazar' includes both functions.
3
Observe the three definitions of nazar

Commentary
This passage concludes the discussion of the three methods and provides a conceptual framework for understanding the key terms used in reasoning. Ghazali first asserts that these three methods are clear (jaliyya) and that it is inconceivable to deny the knowledge obtained from them. He then defines the terms: The knowledge obtained and sought is the 'indicated' (madlūl). The pairing (izdiwāj) of the two roots (aṣlān) that necessitate this knowledge is the 'proof' (dalīl). The knowledge of how the conclusion necessarily follows from the pairing is knowledge of the 'aspect of indication' (wajh dalāla) of the proof. Next, he breaks down the act of reasoning (nazar) into two components: first, bringing the two premises to mind, which is called 'thought' (fikr); second, seeking (talab) to realize the necessary connection between the premises and the conclusion. These correspond to two functions (wazīfatān) of the researcher (bāḥith). Different scholars of kalām have defined 'nazar' differently based on which function they emphasize: some define it as 'fikr', others as 'talab', and others as a combination of both—'the thought by which one seeks knowledge or predominant opinion'. This passage thus clarifies the internal structure of reasoning and how technical terms are used in the discipline.

Ghazali enumerates three methods of inference and illustrates them with the example of infinite cycles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three methods of rational inference that Ghazali identifies, how they address an opponent's denials, and how the concession of premises leads to a necessary conclusion.

Core Idea
A proof is established through two premises that, when conceded, yield necessary knowledge.

Imam al-Ghazali opens by illustrating the logical structure of a proof using a concrete example. He describes three methods (manahij) of inference that account for possible denials by an opponent. This passage shows how a properly constructed argument compels acceptance when both premises are granted.

The Terminology of Inference (Dalil, Madlul, Wajh, Fikr, Nazar)
Ghazali defines the components of a proof: madlul, dalil, wajh al-dalala, fikr, and nazar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key terms used in kalam for the components of a proof: madlul (the implied knowledge), dalil (the evidence), wajh al-dalala (the mode of indication), fikr (thinking), and nazar (inference).

Core Idea
A proof consists of a sought knowledge (madlul), the combination of premises (dalil), and the awareness of how they necessitate (wajh); the process of bringing this to mind is fikr, and the effort to grasp the entailment is nazar.

After explaining the three methods of proof, Ghazali defines the technical terms that describe the anatomy of an argument. These definitions provide a precise vocabulary for discussing rational inference in Islamic theology. Understanding these terms is essential for following kalam arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the definitions carefully
Notice how Ghazali defines each term in relation to the others. The madlul is the knowledge sought, the dalil is the pairing of two principles, and wajh is the knowledge of how they necessitate.
2
Distinguish fikr from nazar
Fikr is simply bringing the two principles to mind. Nazar is the active seeking to understand the mode of implication.
This distinction is important: nazar requires effort to discover the logical connection.
3
Relate to the example from the previous lesson
In the infinite cycles example, the madlul is the conclusion that cycles are finite. The dalil is the two principles combined. The wajh is understanding why the principles necessitate the conclusion.
Fikr is thinking about the principles, and nazar is the reasoning process.

Commentary
This passage defines the structure of a proof using a concrete example. Ghazali describes three methods (manahij) of rational inference. The first method is the opponent's denial of the implication: 'I do not concede that such and such is entailed.' The second method is our assertion that the entailment is impossible, but the opponent might deny that impossibility, e.g., the passing away of what has no end. However, if both premises are conceded, the third knowledge necessarily follows: the impossibility of the opponent's doctrine. Ghazali then explicitly states that these three methods are clear and yield necessary knowledge. He then defines the terms: the sought knowledge is the madlul (the implied), the combination of the two principles that entail this knowledge is the dalil (the evidence), and the knowledge of the mode of implication is the mode of indication of the evidence. Finally, he defines fikr (thinking) as bringing the two premises to mind, and the seeking of the mode of implication is nazar. The footnote provides a concrete application: the opponent claims the cycles of the sphere are infinite. The first principle is the necessary consequence: the passing away of what has no end. The second principle denies that consequence as impossible. The third knowledge is then the acknowledgment that the opponent's claim is impossible and that the cycles must be finite. This illustrates how a proper argument works in theology.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological discussion on the nature of proof (dalil), proven (madlul), and theoretical reasoning (nazar). Ghazali begins by emphasizing the correct way to understand these concepts, criticizing lengthy and repetitive works. He then asserts that in reasoning there are only three sciences: two principles (the premises) arranged in a specific order, and a third science that necessarily follows from them (the conclusion). He warns that those who treat the definition of nazar as a point of contention are mistaken, as the underlying meaning is agreed upon. Finally, Ghazali stresses that most errors come from seeking meanings from words, whereas the proper method is to first establish meanings and then consider words as mere terminological labels that do not change the intelligibles.

As you read, consider how this applies beyond theology to any area of knowledge.

Without Revelation, No Obligation to Know God
Ghazali argues against Mu'tazila that reason alone cannot obligate knowledge of God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali argues that reason alone cannot impose an obligation to know God without revelation, and how he refutes the Mu'tazila position.

Core Idea
Reason only commands what has a known benefit; without revelation, benefit is uncertain.

Ghazali continues his systematic refutation of opposing views. Here he takes on the Mu'tazila claim that reason alone obligates knowledge of God. He argues that any rational obligation must be based on a benefit, and without revelation, we cannot be sure of any benefit in this life or the next. This passage shows the careful logical structure of his argument.

1

Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it, then read the English translation. Notice the compact style and the logical connectors.

Ghazali states his claim clearly: without revelation, no obligation to know God. The opponent is the Mu'tazila, who assert reason alone obligates.

The proof proceeds by examining the basis of rational obligation: reason requires a benefit. Then it considers possibilities: no benefit (vain) or benefit; benefit cannot be for God, so for the servant; present benefit is mere exertion; future benefit (reward) is uncertain. Trace each step.

This argument does not deny reason's value but limits its scope in establishing obligations.

The Three Categories of What Is Not Known Necessarily
Al-Ghazali divides non-necessary knowledge into three types and explains each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Ghazali's threefold division of non-necessary knowledge and the logical order of dependence between reason and revelation.

Core Idea
Reason is the foundation; revelation specifies; both confirm.

Al-Ghazali begins his discussion of what must be affirmed through revelation by categorizing all knowledge that is not immediately known by necessity. He identifies three distinct sources: pure reason, pure revelation, and the combination of both. This passage establishes the methodological framework for all subsequent arguments in the book.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the tripartite division
Read the passage and note that Al-Ghazali divides non-necessary knowledge into exactly three categories. Identify each one and the key examples he gives.
The division is a taqsīm, a standard kalam tool for classification.
2
Understand the priority of reason
Notice that Al-Ghazali places rational knowledge first and explains that revelation depends on it. Reason must establish the existence of God and His speech before revelation can be accepted.
This counters the misconception that all religious knowledge comes solely from revelation.
3
See how each category functions
For each category, ask: What does it provide? Reason gives foundational truths; revelation specifies matters beyond reason; both reason and revelation confirm matters that fall within reason's scope.
The vision of God (ru'ya) is an example of the third category.

Commentary
This passage is a classification (taqsīm) by al-Ghazali of all knowledge that is not necessarily known (ḍarūrī). He divides it into three categories. First, knowledge that is purely rational: it does not depend on revelation (sharʿ) and in fact must precede it, because revelation itself is based on the speech of God (kalām Allāh), which must first be established. Examples include the temporality (ḥudūth) of the world and the existence and attributes of God. Second, knowledge that is purely revelational: it concerns matters that reason cannot determine, such as the specifics of the afterlife (resurrection, reward, punishment). These are known only through divine revelation (waḥy) and inspiration (ilhām) transmitted via hearing (samāʿ). Third, knowledge that is attained by both reason and revelation: it consists of matters that reason can consider but which are confirmed by revelation and are posterior to the establishment of God's speech, such as the vision of God (ruʾya) in the afterlife and God's exclusive creation of all movements and accidents. Al-Ghazali shows the order of dependence: rational knowledge is foundational, revelational knowledge adds specifics, and combined knowledge bridges the two.

Obligation of Belief When Reason Permits
Al-Ghazali states the ruling on belief in revealed matters.

Belief is obligatory when reason permits; certainty depends on proof strength.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling (ḥukm) on how to treat revelatory texts when reason does not find them impossible. Al-Ghazali first states a general principle: any revealed matter that reason deems possible (mujawwaz) must be believed. The strength of that belief depends on the level of the revelatory evidence. If the evidence is categorical (qāṭiʿ) in both text (matn) and chain (sanad), then belief is absolutely certain. If the evidence is only probable (ẓannī), then belief is probable—yet it is still an act of the heart and tongue, just like other acts of worship that are based on probable proofs. To illustrate that probable evidence can indeed ground belief, al-Ghazali gives the example of the Companions. They rejected the claim that a human could be a creator of anything, relying on the Quranic verse 'Allah is the Creator of all things.' This verse is general (ʿāmm) and could be specified, so taken alone it is only probable. Yet the Companions still used it as a decisive proof. Only later did rational investigation confirm its certainty. Hence, al-Ghazali concludes that probable grounds are valid in matters of belief, not only in jurisprudence.

When Reason Deems Impossible or Suspends
Al-Ghazali rules on revealed texts when reason deems impossible or suspends.

Core Idea
Reason's judgment of impossibility triggers interpretation; its suspension still demands belief.

Al-Ghazali completes his discussion by laying down clear rules for how a Muslim should treat revealed texts that seem to conflict with reason. Therefore, we are commanded to persist in actions that necessitate the heart's reverence, such as bowing and prostration, so that the heart's reverence increases thereby. These are matters denied by the pretentious in kalam, who have grasped the arrangement of sciences by hearing words but have not grasped them by the taste of reflection. So this is the reality of this issue, and from this type is their disagreement over the meaning of provision (rizq), and the statement of the Mu'tazila: that it is particular to what a person possesses; until they were forced to say that there is no provision of God Almighty in the true sense of provision for beasts? And sometimes they said: it is that which it is not forbidden to consume. So it was said to them: then the oppressors have died after living their entire lives without being provided for! And our companions said: it is an expression for that which is benefited from, however it may be, and [؟] then it is divided into lawful and unlawful. Then they prolonged concerning the definition of provision and the definition of blessing, and wasting time with this—you, I swear by your life, know its worth and its like—is the habit of one who does not distinguish between important and other matters, and does not know the value of the remainder of his life and that it is precious, so it should not be wasted except on the most important. Before the scholars there are problematic matters whose investigation is more important than investigating the implications of words and the requirements of usages. So we ask God Almighty to grant us success in occupying ourselves with what concerns us. And this statement is only for a group of them, and some of them agreed with Ahl al-Sunna in that.

Commentary
This passage is an argument about how consistent practice (muwazaba) fortifies and deepens belief (i'tiqad) in the soul, followed by a critique of those who engage in purely verbal disputes, such as the disagreement over the definition of 'rizq' (provision). The author begins by stating that this strengthening is known only through introspection: one who regularly performs acts of obedience finds his heart more at ease with his beliefs, making them harder to shake. He illustrates with examples: showing mercy to an orphan through action increases the feeling of mercy; humbling oneself physically increases inner humility. This is why Islamic worship includes repeated physical acts like bowing and prostration—they increase the heart's reverence. Then he criticizes the 'pretentious in kalam' (mutahaddiqun) who know the order of the sciences only from hearing words, not from the 'taste of reflection' (dhawq al-nazar). The dispute over rizq is an example of this: the Mu'tazila restricted it to what one owns or what is lawful, leading to absurdities like claiming oppressors were not provided for. The Ash'arites (as'habuna) define it more broadly as anything beneficial. The author laments that such definitions waste time better spent on important matters, and prays for guidance to focus on what matters.
