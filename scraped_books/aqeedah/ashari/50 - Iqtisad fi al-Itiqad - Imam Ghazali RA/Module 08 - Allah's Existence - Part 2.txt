The Threefold Classification of Religious Knowledge
Ghazali introduces the threefold division of unknown matters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's threefold classification of what is not known by necessity: by reason alone, by scripture alone, and by both.

Core Idea
Religious knowledge has three sources: reason, scripture, and both together.

In the introduction to Chapter Two of his work, Ghazali lays out a fundamental classification that structures all subsequent discussion. He teaches that matters not known by direct intuition fall into three categories based on their epistemological source. This lesson unpacks that tripartite division.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the three categories
Read the Arabic passage carefully and note the three types Ghazali enumerates. Then read the translation.
The categories are clearly marked by 'إلى ما يعلم... وإلى ما يعلم... وإلى ما يعلم'.
2
Notice the order of presentation
Observe that Ghazali first mentions reason alone, then scripture alone, then both. This order reflects the epistemological priority he defends.
3
Reflect on why this classification matters
Consider how this division sets the stage for the rest of the chapter, where each category will be explained in detail.
This classification is foundational for understanding Ghazali's method.

Chapter Two: On explaining the necessity of believing matters that come from scripture (sham‘) and reason (ʿaql) judges their permissibility. It contains an introduction and two sections. As for the introduction: what is not known by necessity is divided into: what is known by a rational proof without scripture, what is known by scripture without reason, and what is known by both. As for what is known by rational proof without scripture, it is the temporal origination (ḥudūth) of the world, the existence of the temporal originator (muḥdith), His power, knowledge, and will. For all of that, if it is not established, scripture is not established; because scripture is built upon speech (kalām). If the speech of the self (kalām al-nafs) is not established, scripture is not established. So everything that precedes in rank the speech of the self cannot be established by the speech of the self or what is based on it. And the speech itself, in our chosen view, cannot be established by scripture. Some investigators have taken trouble over that and claimed it, as previously indicated. (1) As for what is known by mere hearing/scripture, it specifies one of the two equally possible things as occurring; for that is beyond the scope of intellects, and it is only known from God through revelation and inspiration. We know it from the one inspired by hearing: such as resurrection, scattering, reward, punishment, and the like. As for what is known by both, it is everything that falls within the scope of reason and is posterior in rank to the establishment of God's speech; such as the issue of vision (ru’ya), and God's exclusive creation of all movements and accidents, and what proceeds in that manner. Then every matter that scripture brings is considered: if reason deems it possible, then belief in it is absolutely obligatory if the scriptural proofs are decisive in text and chain, with no possibility of interpretation. (1) Because if speech is established by scripture, circularity would occur; because scripture is also His speech. See p. 182. Three.

Commentary
This passage is a classification (muqaddimah) introducing Chapter Two. Ghazali divides what is not known by necessity into three types based on their source of knowledge: (1) known by rational proof alone without scripture, (2) known by scripture alone without reason, and (3) known by both. For the first type, he lists the temporal origination of the world, the existence of the Creator, and His attributes (power, knowledge, will). These must be established by reason before scripture can be accepted, because scripture is built on the concept of divine speech (kalām). If divine speech (as an attribute) is not proven, scripture as revelation cannot be proven. He notes a minority view that claims scripture can prove these, but rejects it. For the second type, known by scripture alone, it concerns specifying which of two equally possible events actually occurs (e.g., details of the afterlife). Reason alone cannot determine these; they require revelation. For the third type, known by both reason and scripture, these are matters that reason can grasp but are posterior to the establishment of God's speech (e.g., the beatific vision, God's exclusive creation of motions and accidents). He further notes that if scripture brings something that reason deems possible, then belief is obligatory if the scriptural evidence is decisive (qāṭiʿa) in both text (matn) and chain (sanad) with no possibility of interpretation. The footnote explains a circularity problem if divine speech is proven by scripture. The passage ends abruptly with 'ثلاثة' possibly indicating the start of the next section or page.

The Primacy of Reason: Foundations of Scripture
Ghazali argues that certain truths must be proven by reason because scripture depends on them.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali insists that certain foundational beliefs (like the existence of God) must be proven by reason before scripture can be accepted.

Core Idea
Reason is the foundation; scripture depends on it.

After establishing the three categories, Ghazali explains the first category in detail. He argues that the temporal origination of the world, the existence of the Creator, and His attributes must be known by reason alone. Why? Because scripture itself is built on the concept of divine speech, which must first be proven. This lesson explores that crucial argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the first category
Read the Arabic passage that begins 'أما المعلوم بدليل العقل دون الشرع'. Note the specific items listed.
The list includes 'حدث العالم' and 'وجود المحدث' etc.
2
Follow the argument for priority
Ghazali explains that these items must be established first; otherwise scripture cannot be established, because scripture depends on the concept of divine speech (kalām al-nafs).
Understand the logical dependency.
3
Consider the alternative view
Ghazali mentions that some scholars claimed these could be proven from scripture, but he rejects that.
This reinforces his position.

Scripture's Role and Combined Knowledge
Ghazali defines the second and third categories and sets conditions for accepting scriptural reports.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what role Ghazali assigns to scripture alone and to the combination of reason and scripture, and the condition for believing scriptural reports.

Core Idea
Scripture specifies possibilities; reason and scripture together confirm what is rationally possible.

The second category covers matters that only scripture can specify, like details of the afterlife. The third covers matters within reason's scope but posterior to establishing God's speech, like the beatific vision. Ghazali also imposes a crucial condition: if scripture brings something that reason deems possible, belief is obligatory only if the evidence is decisive. This lesson unpacks these categories.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the second and third categories
Read the Arabic from 'وأما المعلوم بمجرد السمع' to the end of the passage. Identify the examples given.
Examples for second: الحشر والنشر; for third: الرؤية.
2
Understand the function of scripture alone
Scripture specifies which of two equally possible alternatives actually occurs, something reason cannot determine.
This is the 'تخصيص أحد الجائزين'.
3
Analyze the combined category and the condition
Matters known by both are within reason's scope but posterior to divine speech. Then Ghazali adds a general rule: if scripture brings something that reason deems possible, belief is obligatory if the evidence is decisive.
Note the terms 'جائز', 'قطعي', 'متن', 'سند'.
