Metaphorical Interpretation of Scriptural Anthropomorphic Terms
Lexical analysis of six Hebrew Bible terms distinguishing literal and metaphorical usage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how six key scriptural terms (eat, face, back, heart, spirit, leg) are analyzed to distinguish literal from metaphorical meanings, and how this method preserves divine transcendence.

Core Idea
Scriptural descriptions of God using body parts are always metaphorical, never literal.

This lesson examines six Hebrew Bible terms that are also foundational in Islamic scripture. The author demonstrates a lexical method: starting from the concrete physical meaning, then showing how the term expands to abstract concepts. This method is crucial for Islamic theology (kalam) to interpret anthropomorphic descriptions of Allah metaphorically, avoiding tashbih (likening God to creation). The passage provides scriptural evidence for each metaphorical usage.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic source passage once without analysis, then read the full English translation. Notice the consistent pattern: each term begins with the literal meaning, then lists metaphorical usages with biblical examples.
The passage uses Hebrew Bible references, but the method applies to Quranic verses as well.
2
Identify the six terms and their literal meanings
List the six words: akl (eat), wajh (face), akhr (back), qalb (heart), ruh (spirit), rijl (leg). For each, note the stated literal meaning. This establishes the concrete basis.
The literal meaning is the physical or tangible referent.
3
Observe the metaphorical extensions
For each term, the author provides one or more metaphorical meanings with scriptural evidence. For example, 'face' metaphorically means anger, presence, or care. Write down the metaphorical meanings for all six terms.
The pattern shows that body part terms extend to abstract concepts or divine attributes.
4
Connect to divine transcendence
Reflect on how the metaphorical interpretations prevent a literal reading that would ascribe a physical body to God. For instance, God's 'face' is not a physical face but His anger or favor; His 'heart' is His will and care.
This is the theological purpose: to affirm tanzih (transcendence).
5

Commentary
This passage is a lexicological lesson distinguishing the literal (al-haqiqa) and metaphorical (al-majaz) meanings of six key words found in scripture (here, the Hebrew Bible). The purpose is to show that when divine attributes or actions are described using bodily or material terms, these are to be understood metaphorically, preventing anthropomorphism. The author proceeds word by word.

For each term, the author first states the literal meaning, then lists one or more metaphorical usages with scriptural examples. The first word, 'eat' (akl), has two literal senses: consumption causing disappearance, and consumption for growth. Metaphorically, it is used for destruction or benefit. Next, 'face' (wajh) literally means the animal's face. Metaphorically it denotes anger, presence, or care, as in God's face representing His anger or His favor. The third word, 'back' (akhr, likely a typo for 'zahr'), literally means the back. Metaphorically it means 'after' or 'following', used for God's back meaning His trace. Fourth, 'heart' (qalb) literally is the life-organ. Metaphorically it means center, thought, opinion, will, or intellect. Divine 'heart' thus refers to God's care and will. Fifth, 'spirit' (ruh) literally is air or wind. Metaphorically it means the animal's soul, ascending spirit, divine effusion, or purpose. Lastly, 'leg' (rijl) literally is the walking limb; metaphorical usage is truncated but implied. The consistent method is to start from the concrete physical meaning and then show how the term expands to abstract concepts, especially when applied to God, to avoid attributing literal body parts to Him.

Rukb (riding): In the literal sense, it is used for a human riding an animal. In the metaphorical sense, it is used for domination over a thing, because the rider dominates and rules his mount. For example, 'He made him ride on the high places of the earth' (Deuteronomy 32:13). On this metaphorical meaning it is said concerning God Almighty: 'Rider of the heavens for your help' (Deuteronomy 33:26), meaning He dominates the heavens. And the heaven is expressed in the Torah as 'al-barārī.' And 'al-barārī' for them is the highest sphere that encompasses everything. That is because the heavens are seven, and 'al-barārī' is the highest that encompasses everything. The proof that 'al-barārī' are the heavens is that in one place it is written 'Rider of the barārī' (Psalm 68:5), and in another place it is written 'Rider of the heavens' (Haggai 2:12b? [questionable]). And it has reached me: that the first heaven in the speech of the Jews is called 'Fīlūn,' the second 'Raqīʿ,' the third 'Sheḥāqīm,' the fourth 'Zebūl,' the fifth 'Maʿōn,' the sixth 'Mākōn,' and the seventh 'ʿArāfāt.' – And in Tafsīr al-Qurṭubī, the first is named 'Raqīʿ.' – And it reached me that Ibn Maymūn (Maimonides) expressed the seventh heaven as 'ʿArāfāt.' And the Arabic copy I have of Dalālat al-Ḥāʾirīn (Guide for the Perplexed) states that he meant by 'al-barārī' the highest sphere encompassing everything, not that he meant Mount ʿArafāt in the land of Mecca the Honored. And someone else understood – as told to me – that he meant Mount ʿArafāt, because in some manuscripts of Dalālat al-Ḥāʾirīn: 'al-rawkab baʿar būt' instead of 'al-rākib fī al-barārī' (Psalm 68:5) or 'lilrākib fī al-qifār' or 'al-marākib ʿalā al-ghamām.'

Commentary
This passage defines the word rukb (riding) and its metaphorical application to God. It begins with the literal meaning: riding an animal. Then it explains the metaphorical sense of domination, since a rider controls his mount. The author cites Deuteronomy 32:13 as a metaphorical example. He then applies this to the verse 'Rider of the heavens for your help' (Deuteronomy 33:26), interpreting it to mean that God dominates the heavens. Next, he explains that 'al-barārī' (wilderness) in Hebrew refers to the highest heaven. He asserts that there are seven heavens, and al-barārī is the highest. To prove that al-barārī denotes the heavens, he compares Psalm 68:5 ('Rider of the barārī') with Haggai 2:12 (or similar, 'Rider of the heavens') as synonymous. The author then details the Jewish names for the seven heavens, referencing Qurṭubī's tafsīr and Maimonides. He clarifies that Maimonides, in the Guide for the Perplexed, meant the highest sphere, not Mount ʿArafāt. He mentions a variant manuscript reading that led to a misunderstanding. This entire discussion reinforces the necessity of interpreting anthropomorphic expressions metaphorically.

Barari as the Highest Heaven and Clarification of Maimonides
Identifies 'barari' as highest heaven, lists seven heavens, corrects misinterpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that 'al-barārī' in the Torah refers to the highest heaven, and you will learn the Jewish names of the seven heavens and the correct interpretation of Maimonides.

Core Idea
Barari is the highest sphere, not Mount Arafat.
