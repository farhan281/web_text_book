The Salaf's Second Proof: Consensus of the Companions
Razi invokes the consensus of the Sahaba and Tabi'un as evidence for the Salaf's position.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi uses the consensus of the Companions and Followers as a proof that detailed interpretation of mutashabihat is impermissible.

Core Idea
The Companions' silence on ta'wil is a binding proof.

Imam Razi presents the second of three proofs for the Salaf's approach. He argues that because the Companions and Followers did not engage in detailed interpretation despite strong incentives, their collective silence constitutes a consensus that such interpretation is not allowed. This lesson examines that proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice the logical structure: premise, conditional, conclusion.
The passage is a single continuous argument.
2
Identify the key premise
Identify the two key facts: (1) many mutashabihat exist, (2) motivations to research them were abundant. Understand why these are important for the argument.
3
Trace the conditional reasoning
The proof uses a conditional: if detailed ta'wil were permissible, the Companions would have done it. Since they didn't, it must be impermissible. Think about why this reasoning is valid.
The conditional is based on the Companions being the most deserving of engaging in such interpretation.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The conclusion is that delving into ta'wil is not permissible. Consider how this aligns with the Salaf's methodology of avoiding speculative interpretation.

It is permissible. Because this conjunctive reading is a well-known recitation transmitted by mutawatir transmission, so establishing proof of its corruption is an attack on mutawatir transmission, and that is not permissible. It was said: We do not make this issue definitive, but rather speculative and probable. On this basis, the question is removed. The second proof for the correctness of the Salaf's school: Adherence to the consensus of the Companions - may Allah be pleased with them - that these mutashabihat in the Qur'an and narrations are many, and the motivations to research them and ascertain their realities are abundant. So if researching their ta'wil in detail were permissible, then the most deserving of creation for that would be the Companions and the Followers - may Allah be pleased with them - and if they had done that, it would have become widespread and transmitted by tawatur. Since it has not been transmitted from any of the Companions and Followers that they delved into them, we know that delving into them is impermissible. The third proof: We have mentioned that the mutashabih word is of two types: mujmal and mu'awwal. As for the mujmal, it is that which can bear two or more meanings with equal probability. We say: either it can bear only two meanings, or more than two. If it can bear only two meanings, and then evidence indicates the negation of one of them, then it is determined that the intended meaning is the second. For example, 'above' (al-fawq) either means above in direction or in rank. When its being taken in the sense of direction is invalidated, the rank is determined. As for if the word has three concepts, it does not follow from the invalidation of one of them that the second and third are specifically determined. Nor is it possible to apply the word to both of them together, because it has been established that a homonym cannot be used for both of its meanings simultaneously. As for the mu'awwal, we say: when a word has a single literal meaning, and then evidence indicates that it is not intended, it is obligatory to apply the word to its metaphorical meaning. Then if that metaphorical meaning is one, the word is necessarily directed to it, to protect against ta'til (negation of meaning). If it is not determinate, the word remains undecided among those metaphorical meanings. Then the same discussion we mentioned for the mujmal applies here identically. So it is established by what we have mentioned that ta'wil of the mutashabih may be known and may be speculative. And holding speculative opinion is impermissible, as previously established in the chapter that relying on a solitary narration in knowledge of Allah Almighty is impermissible. This is the summary of the discussion in establishing the madhhab of the Salaf. As for the mutakallimun who advocate detailed ta'wils, their proof is what has preceded, that the Qur'an must be understood, and there is no way to that regarding the mutashabihat except by mentioning ta'wils. So resorting to it was obligatory. And Allah knows best.

Commentary
This passage presents a sustained argument for the Salaf's position on the mutashabihat (ambiguous verses) and concludes with the opposing view of the mutakallimun. The passage begins by concluding a previous point (likely the first proof) about a conjunctive reading being mutawatir and therefore not subject to critique; if it were merely speculative, the objection would disappear. Then Imam Razi presents three proofs. The second proof (the first in this selection) invokes the consensus of the Sahaba and Tabi'un: since these companions did not engage in detailed ta'wil despite strong motives, their silence indicates it is impermissible. The third proof is a detailed linguistic analysis. It classifies the mutashabih word into two types: mujmal (equivocal with equally probable meanings) and mu'awwal (having a single literal meaning but requiring metaphorical interpretation due to evidence). For mujmal, if it has exactly two meanings and one is negated by evidence, the other is determined. If it has more than two, negating one does not specify any remaining meaning, and using the word for multiple meanings simultaneously is prohibited. For mu'awwal, if evidence negates the literal meaning, the word must be taken metaphorically. If there is one clear metaphorical meaning, it is used; otherwise, the word remains undetermined, and the same reasoning as mujmal applies. This shows that ta'wil may be certain or speculative, but speculative ta'wil is impermissible, as established earlier regarding the use of ahad reports in knowledge of Allah. The passage then states that this is the summary of the Salaf's madhhab. Finally, it notes that the mutakallimun argue that understanding the Qur'an is obligatory and mutashabihat cannot be understood without ta'wil, so they consider it necessary. The text ends with 'Allah knows best'.

The Salaf's Third Proof: Linguistic Classification of Mutashabihat
Razi classifies mutashabih words linguistically and shows the limits of ta'wil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi's linguistic analysis of mutashabih words (mujmal and mu'awwal) supports the Salaf's position on ta'wil.

Core Idea
Linguistic analysis shows that speculative ta'wil is often inconclusive and thus impermissible.

Imam Razi's third proof is a detailed linguistic argument. He classifies mutashabih words into two categories—mujmal (equivocal) and mu'awwal (requiring metaphorical interpretation)—and shows that in many cases, determining the intended meaning is not possible with certainty. This lesson unpacks that analysis.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the two categories
The passage begins by stating that the mutashabih word is of two types: mujmal and mu'awwal. Read the definitions and note the difference.
Mujmal has equally probable meanings; mu'awwal has a single literal meaning that is negated by evidence.
2
Analyze the mujmal case
Follow Razi's reasoning for mujmal words with two or more meanings. Understand why, if there are more than two meanings, negating one does not determine another.
The example given is the word 'above' (fawq) meaning either direction or rank.
3
Analyze the mu'awwal case
For mu'awwal, when the literal meaning is negated, the word must be taken metaphorically. Understand the condition for determination: if only one metaphorical meaning exists, it is used; otherwise, the word remains undetermined.
Note the principle of avoiding ta'til (negation of meaning).
4
Understand the conclusion
Razi concludes that ta'wil may be certain or speculative, but speculative ta'wil is impermissible, as established earlier. Reflect on how this supports the Salaf's avoidance of detailed interpretation.

The Mutakallimun's Objection and Conclusion
Razi presents the mutakallimun's argument for detailed ta'wil and concludes the discussion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the mutakallimun's argument for detailed ta'wil and how it contrasts with the Salaf's position.

Core Idea
The mutakallimun argue that understanding the Qur'an necessitates ta'wil, but the Salaf respond that certainty is required.

After presenting the Salaf's proofs, Imam Razi briefly states the opposing view of the mutakallimun, who advocate detailed ta'wil. Their core argument is that the Qur'an must be understood, and that requires interpretation of ambiguous verses. This lesson examines that counterargument and places it in context.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the mutakallimun's premise
The passage states: the Qur'an must be understood. Identify this as the key premise. Note that it is presented as an obligation.
2
Understand their conclusion
From the premise, they conclude that ta'wil is necessary for the mutashabihat. Consider why they think this is the only way.
The conclusion is that resorting to ta'wil is obligatory.
3
Contrast with the Salaf's position
Recall the Salaf's proofs. How would the Salaf respond to the claim that the Qur'an must be understood? Think about the earlier linguistic analysis that showed many ta'wils are speculative and thus impermissible.
The Salaf might argue that understanding the Qur'an does not require detailed ta'wil; rather, affirming the texts without modality suffices.
4
Reflect on the phrase 'Allah knows best'
The passage ends with 'Allah knows best.' Consider how this pious conclusion fits with the overall tone of the discussion.

No Replacing Ambiguous Words
Razi forbids replacing ambiguous words and justifies it with precaution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Salaf forbade replacing one ambiguous word with another, and the precautionary principle behind this ruling.

Core Idea
Replacing ambiguous words risks increasing false beliefs, so caution demands abstaining.

Razi now moves from general principles to specific branches of the Salaf's methodology. The first branch addresses a common temptation: to replace a difficult ambiguous word with another that seems clearer. But Razi warns that this is not allowed, because ambiguity varies, and without need, increased illusion is forbidden. He supports this with an analogy from Islamic legal precaution.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice the structure: ruling, reason, difficulty, precaution, and an analogy.
Pay attention to the term 'iham' (illusion/false belief).
2
Identify the central ruling
Find the statement 'لا يجوز تبديل لفظ من الألفاظ المتشابهة بلفظ آخر متشابه'. This is the main ruling: it is not permissible to replace one ambiguous word with another.
3
Understand the rationale
Razi gives the reason: some words cause more false illusion (iham) than others. Increasing illusion without need is forbidden. Then he notes that distinguishing levels of illusion is difficult, so the cautious path is to abstain from all replacements.
This is an application of sadd al-dhara'i' (blocking means to harm).
4
Analyze the analogy
Razi compares this to the waiting period (iddah) in Islamic law. Even though it is hard, it is required to avoid the greater risk of mixing lineages. Similarly, the risk in matters of Allah's essence and attributes is far greater, so caution is even more warranted.
This is a qiyas (analogy) to show the principle of preferring the lesser harm.

The Qur'anic Method for Mutashabihat: Every Ambiguous Verse Has a Qarina
Al-Razi explains that every mutashabih has a qarina removing false understanding, and thus the method is ta'wil with adab.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Razi's principle that every mutashabih verse comes with an internal indicator (qarina) that prevents literal misunderstanding, and how this necessitates respectful ta'wil.

Core Idea
Every ambiguous Quranic verse carries its own key to correct interpretation.

Al-Razi concludes his discussion on mutashabihat by revealing a crucial observation: Allah never leaves a potentially misleading verse without a contextual clue. In this passage, he demonstrates through two key examples how the Qur'an itself resolves apparent anthropomorphism. This lesson unpacks that principle and the method of ta'wil that follows.

Razi's Method for Interpreting Mutashabihat
Razi outlines the ta'wil methodology with two Quranic examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's method of using contextual clues (qarina) to interpret ambiguous Quranic verses (mutashabihat) in a way that upholds divine transcendence.

Core Idea
Contextual clues in the Quran always guide us away from literal falsehood.

Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi presents a key principle for dealing with verses whose apparent meaning might suggest anthropomorphism (tashbih). He argues that Allah never mentions ambiguous expressions without surrounding them with indicators that remove misunderstanding. This lesson walks through his argument and two vivid examples from the Quran, showing why careful interpretation (ta'wil) is required out of respect for Allah's transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get a feel for Razi's style, then read the full translation. Notice the structure: a principle, two examples, and a conclusion.
The passage is dense with reasoning; take your time.
2
Identify the general principle
Razi states that whenever Allah uses the word 'mutashabihat', He couples it with a qarina (clue) that eliminates false understanding. Look for this statement in the Arabic.
3
Examine the first example: 'Light'
Razi uses the verse 'Allah is the light of the heavens and the earth' (24:35). He points out that the following phrase 'like His light' shows that light is attributed to Allah, not identical to Him. Why would claiming identity lead to impossibility?
Self-attribution (adding a thing to itself) is impossible in Arabic grammar and logic.
4
Examine the second example: 'Throne'
The verse 'The Most Merciful established Himself over the Throne' (20:5). Razi shows that the surrounding verses (20:4 and 20:6) indicate that anything associated with a direction is created, so Allah's 'istawa' cannot be literal.
5
Understand the conclusion
Razi concludes that the proper method is to interpret (ta'wil) such expressions figuratively, out of respect for the Necessary Being (Wajib al-Wujud).
This does not mean rejecting the verses but understanding them in a way befitting Allah.

Commentary
This passage presents a methodological argument for dealing with mutashabihat (ambiguous verses) in the Quran. It begins by stating a general principle: whenever Allah uses the word 'mutashabihat' in the Quran, He couples it with a clue (qarina) that removes false understanding. Then two examples are given. First, the verse 'Allah is the light of the heavens and the earth' (An-Nur 35). Razi points out that immediately after, Allah says 'like His light' (mathalu nurihi), which shows that the light is attributed to Allah, not that He is identical to light. If He were literally light, attributing light to Himself would be impossible (self-attribution is impossible). Second, the verse 'The Most Merciful established Himself over the Throne' (Taha 5). Razi notes that before this verse, Allah mentions 'a revelation from the One who created the earth and the high heavens' (Taha 4), and after it, He says 'To Him belongs what is in the heavens...' (Taha 6). These surrounding verses indicate that anything associated with a specific direction (like 'above' or 'over') is created and originated (makhluq muhdath). Therefore, the correct approach to these mutashabihat is to interpret them metaphorically (ta'wil) out of respect for the transcendence of the Necessary Being. The passage concludes with 'With Allah is success.' The author's point is that careful attention to context reveals the intended meaning without anthropomorphism.

The Objection Against Ambiguous Verses
Imam Razi narrates the shubha (doubt) against mutashabihat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two-part objection that mutashabihat could lead to criticism of the Quran, and how Imam Razi frames this shubha.

Core Idea
The Quran's ambiguous verses are not a flaw but a divine wisdom.

In this passage, Imam Razi begins by presenting a powerful objection that critics might raise: that the presence of ambiguous verses opens the Quran to criticism. He then sets the stage for answering it by showing why this objection fails. Understanding this doubt is crucial before exploring the benefits.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer unit. It first presents a shubha (doubt) that the presence of mutashabihat (ambiguous verses) in the Quran leads to criticism. The objection runs: if we interpret them metaphorically, the intended truth is not explicit, causing false beliefs; moreover, there are no explicit verses on tanzih (transcendence) and tawhid (oneness) — even 'Qul huwa Allahu ahad' and 'Laysa kamithlihi shay' only weakly indicate them. Hence, the Quran is open to criticism. The author then transitions by saying 'So this is the narration of this doubt' and states that the verifying scholars have mentioned benefits. Five benefits are listed. (1) Difficulty increases reward, citing Q. 3:142. (2) If the Quran were entirely muhkam (clear), it would only suit one school, alienating others; but with both muhkam and mutashabih, each school hopes to find support, leading them to study and interpret; eventually the muhkam clarifies the mutashabih, guiding the false to truth. (3) The presence of mutashabihat necessitates the use of rational proofs and acquisition of other sciences, freeing one from taqlid (blind following). (4) It necessitates learning tafsir methodology, linguistics, grammar, usul al-fiqh, and methods of tarjih (preference), thus promoting scholarship. (5) The strongest reason: the Quran addresses both khawass (elite) and 'awamm (common people). The common people cannot grasp purely intellectual truths; if they hear of a non-corporeal being, they might think it is nothing. The text cuts off here, but the benefit is that ambiguous verses help gradually convey complex truths to the masses without causing them to fall into disbelief.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer unit. It first presents a shubha (doubt) that the presence of mutashabihat (ambiguous verses) in the Quran leads to criticism. The objection runs: if we interpret them metaphorically, the intended truth is not explicit, causing false beliefs; moreover, there are no explicit verses on tanzih (transcendence) and tawhid (oneness) — even 'Qul huwa Allahu ahad' and 'Laysa kamithlihi shay' only weakly indicate them. Commentary
This passage is a combination of a proof, an objection-and-answer, and a methodological classification. It begins by introducing al-Ghazali's book 'al-Iqtisad' and his method of interpreting ambiguous reports figuratively. Then comes the proof: Allah is transcendent above 'settling' (istiqrar) on the Throne, because settling on a body implies the object settled upon is measurable (larger, smaller, equal), which is impossible for Allah. Then an objection is raised: what about the verse 'al-Rahman 'ala al-'arshi istawa' and the hadith of descent? Ghazali's answer is that the discussion is long, but he offers a key method: people are divided into two groups. For the common people, it is not appropriate to engage in detailed interpretations; rather, we remove from their beliefs any notion that implies anthropomorphism or contingency, and we instill in them the basic tawhid: He exists, nothing is like Him, He is Hearing and Seeing. For scholars, it is appropriate to know and understand the interpretations. However, Ghazali stresses that understanding all the detailed meanings of the Qur'an is not an individual obligation; the only binding duty is tanzih (declaring transcendence). This passage thus emphasizes the priority of tanzih over detailed tawil, especially for laypersons.

Coming (jāʾa): It is used literally for the approach of an animal to a place. And it is used metaphorically for the occurrence of a matter that is not a body at all, like: 'What is coming upon you' (Isa 15:47). And upon this metaphorical meaning, the word 'come' is used in reference to God Almighty—either for the occurrence of His command or for the occurrence of His shekhinah. Like: 'Behold I am coming to you in a thick cloud' (Ex 19:9?), meaning His shekhinah settles. And like: 'And the Lord my God shall come, and all the holy ones with you' (Zech 14:5), meaning His command and the fulfillment of His promises that He promised through His prophets. Going out (al-khurūj): It is used literally for the exit of a body from a place where it was settled to another place. And metaphorically it is used for the manifestation of a spiritual matter that is not a body at all, like: 'The word came out from the mouth of the king' (Eccl 8:4?), and this means the power of the command. And metaphorically the word 'Behold the Lord goes out from His place' (Isa 26:21?) is interpreted, i.e., His command, now hidden from us, becomes manifest. I mean: the occurrence of what happens after it was not. For every event from Him, exalted, is attributed to His command. Like: 'By the word of the Lord were the heavens made, and by the breath of His mouth all their host' (Ps 33:6), analogizing to the actions of kings whose tools execute their will: speech. And He, exalted, is not in need of an instrument to act, but rather acts by His will alone. So there is no speech in any sense, and 'going out' is borrowed for the manifestation of an action of His. 'Returning' (al-rujūʿ) is borrowed for the cessation of that action according to will also, like: 'I will go and return to my place' (Hos 5:15), i.e., He removes His shekhinah from the people, and when He removes His shekhinah, the people remain a target for every attacker to happen upon them, and the good that befalls them is by chance. This is a difficult matter, because if God hides His face from a man, let that man say: 'Farewell to the world.' And evil. Walking or going (al-sayr or al-mashy): It is literally the subject of specific movements issuing from an animal. And metaphorically, walking is used for the spread and manifestation of a matter, even if it is not a body at all. Like: 'Her voice goes like a serpent' (Jer 46:22).

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed semantic analysis of three key terms used in scripture to describe God: 'come' (جاء), 'go out' (الخروج), and 'walk' (السير/المشي). For each term, the author explains the literal meaning (ḥaqīqah) applied to physical entities, and the metaphorical meaning (majāz) applied to non-physical divine actions. For 'come', the metaphorical usage indicates the occurrence of God's command or the settling of His shekhinah. For 'go out', it indicates the manifestation of a spiritual matter, specifically the coming into being of events that were not. For 'walk', it indicates the spread and appearance of a matter. The author also introduces the concept of 'return' (الرجوع) as metaphor for the withdrawal of divine action. Throughout, the emphasis is on affirming divine transcendence by interpreting these terms in a way consistent with God's incorporeality. The passage repeatedly cites biblical verses to support these metaphorical interpretations.

Asas al-Taqdis: Structure and First Premise
Razi introduces the book's structure and states the first preliminary premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure of Razi's Asas al-Taqdis and the content of its first preliminary premise regarding necessary knowledge of existence beyond direction.

Core Idea
The first premise of Asas al-Taqdis is a self-evident truth about divine transcendence.

Razi opens his work Asas al-Taqdis by outlining its four-section structure and immediately delving into the foundational premises. The first premise is presented as a necessary truth that any rational person recognizes: that an existence not located in space is conceivable. This lesson introduces the book's plan and this crucial first step.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of Razi's 'Asas al-Taqdis'. It introduces the book's name and structure: four sections. The first section deals with signs of Allah's transcendence beyond corporeality and direction, containing chapters. The first chapter presents three preliminary premises. Here Razi begins the first premise, which appears to argue that the existence of a being not located in any direction is known by necessity, and that claiming otherwise contradicts necessary reason. The text is partially illegible but conveys the argument that denying this leads to absurdity. Razi emphasizes that impugning necessary truths through theoretical reasoning undermines the foundation.
