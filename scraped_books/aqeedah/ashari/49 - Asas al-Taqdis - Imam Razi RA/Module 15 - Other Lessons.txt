The Benefit of Constant Mindfulness
Concluding remark on benefit of constant thought

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Constant mindfulness is a form of worship with great benefit.

In this concluding passage, Imam Razi wraps up his discussion by affirming that remaining mentally occupied with a certain matter brings a great benefit, and thus the servant worships Allah through that act. He then notes that this is his synthesis of two opposing views.

Guided Path

Three Quranic Descriptions of the Quran
Razi presents three Quranic proofs for the Quran being entirely muhkam, entirely mutashabih, and partly each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi uses three different Quranic verses to support the claims that the Quran is entirely muhkam, entirely mutashabih, and partly both.

Core Idea
The Quran describes itself in three complementary ways, each emphasizing a different aspect.

Imam Razi begins by noting that the Quran itself contains three different descriptions: that it is entirely muhkam (firm and true), entirely mutashabih (similar and harmonious), and that some verses are muhkam and some mutashabih. He then cites specific verses for each claim, showing how they are not contradictory but rather highlight different dimensions of the Quran's perfection. This lesson walks through each proof and its supporting evidence.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening statement
Razi states that the Quran proves three things about itself. Notice the parallel structure: 'indicates that it is entirely muhkam... entirely mutashabih... some muhkam and some mutashabih'.
This sets up the three-part argument.
2
Examine the proof for 'entirely muhkam'
Razi cites two verses (Hud 1 and Luqman 1) and explains that 'muhkam' here means true in wording and meaning, and that the Quran is superior to all other speech.
He also connects to Arabic usage of 'muhkam' for firm construction.
3
Examine the proof for 'entirely mutashabih'
Razi cites Az-Zumar 23 and interprets 'mutashabih' as parts resembling each other in beauty and eloquence, and confirming each other. He also references An-Nisa 82 to support this.
The emphasis is on internal consistency and harmony.
4
Examine the proof for 'partly muhkam and partly mutashabih'
Razi cites Al-Imran 7, which explicitly divides the Quran into muhkam and mutashabih verses. This shows that the three descriptions are not contradictory but apply to different aspects.
This verse is often cited in discussions of interpretation.
5
Reflect on the three descriptions together
Consider how the three proofs complement each other: the entire Quran is muhkam (true and firm), entire mutashabih (harmonious and consistent), and yet some verses are classified as muhkam or mutashabih in another sense.
Razi harmonizes the apparent contradiction by showing different meanings.

Commentary
This passage is a three-part proof from the Quran regarding its own description. Razi begins by stating that the Quran itself shows three things: that it is entirely muhkam, entirely mutashabih, and partly muhkam and partly mutashabih. He then presents a verse for each. For the first, he cites two verses (Hud 1 and Luqman 1) which describe the Quran as having 'muhkamat' verses, interpreting 'muhkam' as true in wording and meaning, and emphasizing the Quran's superiority. He also notes the Arabic usage of 'muhkam' for firm construction. For the second, he cites Az-Zumar 23 ('kitaban mutashabihan'), explaining that it means parts resemble each other in beauty and eloquence and confirm each other. He supports this with the verse from An-Nisa 82 that challenges anyone to find inconsistency. For the third, he cites Al-Imran 7, which explicitly states that some verses are muhkamat and some mutashabihat. Thus, Razi harmonizes these descriptions by showing that each refers to a different aspect: the entire Quran is muhkam in its truth and firmness, entire mutashabih in its mutual resemblance and harmony, and partially each in the specific classification of verses.

Linguistic Roots of Muhkam and Mutashabih
Razi defines the Arabic linguistic roots of muhkam and mutashabih with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Arabic linguistic origins of the terms muhkam and mutashabih, and how their root meanings of 'prevention' and 'resemblance' shape their theological usage.

Core Idea
Muhkam prevents confusion; Mutashabih resembles without distinction.

Razi begins by grounding the Qur'anic terms muhkam and mutashabih in their Arabic linguistic roots. He traces muhkam to the meaning of 'prevention' and firmness, and mutashabih to close resemblance that confuses the mind. This passage provides the essential foundation for later theological discussions about which verses are clear and which are ambiguous.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the literal translation to grasp the overall meaning.
Notice the examples Razi uses for each term.
2
Understand the root of muhkam
Focus on the examples: the judge preventing injustice, the bridle preventing agitation, and the hadith about restraining orphans. They all share the meaning of prevention.
The word 'hikma' (wisdom) is also derived from this root.
3
Understand the root of mutashabih
Examine the examples of close resemblance: the cows in Surah Al-Baqara and the hadith about ambiguous matters. The key idea is that the mind cannot distinguish between the two things.
The term 'mushtabihat' in the hadith is synonymous with 'mutashabihat'.

أما المحكم في اللغة : فالعرب تقول حكمت وأحكمت و حكمت ، بمعنى : رددت و منعت (۷) والحاكم يمنع الظالم عن الظلم ، وحكمة اللجام تمنع الفرس عن الاضطراب . وفي حديث الشخص [؟] : ( أحكم اليتيم كما تحكم ولدك ، أي امنعه من الفساد . وقوله : أحكموا سفهاءكم ، أى امنعوهم . وبناء محكم أي وثيق يمنع من تعرض له . وسميت الحكمة حكمة ، لأنها تمنع الموصوف بها عما لا ينبغي . وأما المتشابه : فهو أن يكون أحد الشيئين متشابها للآخر ، بحيث يعجز الذهن عن التمييز . قال الله تعالى : ( إن البقر تشابه علينا (۹) وقال : « تشابهت قلوبهم (١٠) ومنه إشتبه الأمر . إذا لم يفرق بينهما ويقال لأصحاب المخاريق : أصحاب الشبهات. وقال عليه السلام : الحلال بين والحرام بين وبينهما أمور مشتبهات ، وفى رواية أخرى (۱۱) متشابهات . فهذا تحقيق الكلام فى المحكم والمتشابه ، بحسب أصل اللغة
As for al-muhkam in language: the Arabs say 'hakamtu' and 'ahkantu' and 'hakkamtu', meaning: I turned back and prevented (7). The judge (al-hakim) prevents the wrongdoer from injustice. The bit of the bridle (hikmat al-lijam) prevents the horse from agitation. In the hadith of the person [?]: 'Make the orphan firm (ahkim al-yatim) as you make your child firm', i.e., prevent him from corruption. Wisdom (hikma) is called hikma because it prevents the one described by it from what is not proper. As for al-mutashabih: it is that one of two things resembles the other such that the mind is incapable of distinguishing between them. God Almighty said: 'Indeed the cows are similar to us (9)' and said: 'Their hearts are similar (10).' From it is derived 'ishtabaha al-amr' when one cannot differentiate between them. The practitioners of trickery are called 'ashab al-shubuhat'. Commentary
This passage provides the linguistic definitions of muhkam and mutashabih. Razi begins with muhkam, deriving it from the Arabic root meaning 'to prevent' or 'to turn back'. He cites examples: the judge (hakim) prevents injustice, the bridle (hikma) prevents the horse from agitation, a hadith about restraining an orphan from corruption, and the command to restrain fools. The common theme is prevention and firmness. Wisdom (hikma) is named for preventing one from improper actions. Then he defines mutashabih as a resemblance so close that the mind cannot distinguish between two things. He gives Quranic examples (Al-Baqara 70 and Al-Baqara 118) where 'tashabaha' is used, and notes that 'ishtabaha al-amr' means the matter is confusing. He also mentions the hadith about halal, haram, and ambiguous matters, showing that 'mushtabihat' (or 'mutashabihat') refers to things that are unclear. Thus, linguistically, muhkam emphasizes clarity through firmness and prevention of doubt, while mutashabih emphasizes ambiguity due to close resemblance.

Examples of Muhkam and Mutashabih in the Quran
Razi concludes the discussion on muhkam and mutashabih with Quranic examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi uses Quranic examples to distinguish between muhkam (clear) and mutashabih (ambiguous) verses, specifically in the context of anthropomorphic expressions like 'forgetfulness'.

Core Idea
Muhkam verses clarify the meaning of mutashabih ones, preventing literal misinterpretation.

In this concluding section, Imam Razi provides concrete examples from the Quran to illustrate the relationship between muhkam and mutashabih verses. He shows how verses that might appear problematic are resolved by clear verses that establish Allah's perfection. The passage demonstrates the method of interpreting ambiguous expressions in light of definitive ones.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that Razi uses a very compressed style here.
2
Identify the two examples
The passage presents two main examples: the polytheists' claim and the word 'forgetfulness' (nisyan). Find each one and notice how Razi defines the muhkam and mutashabih within them.
The first example is a claim attributed to polytheists; the second is about forgetting.
3
Distinguish muhkam from mutashabih
For the second example, see how 'They forgot Allah, so He forgot them' is mutashabih, while 'Your Lord is not forgetful' is muhkam. Ask why the former is ambiguous and the latter is clear.
This connects to the method of tafsir.
4
Understand the interpretive principle
The passage shows that mutashabih verses are interpreted in light of muhkam ones. Reflect on how this principle applies to other verses you know.

Commentary
This passage is a concluding summary of a discussion on muhkam (clear) and mutashabih (ambiguous) verses. Razi provides examples from the Quran to illustrate how certain verses are muhkam and others are mutashabih. He cites the polytheists' excuse for their indecency (Quran 7:28) as an example of a claim that might be misinterpreted. Then he discusses the word 'forgetfulness' (nisyan) as applied to Allah: in the verse 'They forgot Allah, so He forgot them' (Quran 9:67), the apparent meaning of forgetting is with knowledge (i.e., disregard), but the mutashabih aspect is that Allah's 'forgetting' cannot be literal ignorance. He contrasts this with muhkam verses like 'And your Lord is not forgetful' (Quran 19:64) and 'My Lord does not go astray nor forget' (Quran 20:52), which affirm that forgetting is not an attribute of Allah. Thus, the passage demonstrates the method of interpreting mutashabih verses in light of muhkam ones.
