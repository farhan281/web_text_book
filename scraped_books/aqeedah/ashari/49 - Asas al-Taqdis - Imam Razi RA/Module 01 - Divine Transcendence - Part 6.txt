Analyzing the Pronoun in the Hadith of Creation in God's Image
Imam Razi introduces the hadith and sets up the pronoun analysis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi uses grammatical analysis of the pronoun in the hadith 'created Adam upon His image' to demonstrate that the hadith does not force a literal anthropomorphic interpretation.

Core Idea
The ambiguous pronoun shows the hadith does not require attributing a literal image to Allah.

This lesson introduces a key hadith often cited by anthropomorphists. Imam Razi begins by noting that the word 'image' (sura) does not appear in the Quran but only in transmitted reports. He then presents two versions of the hadith and reveals his method: a grammatical analysis of the pronoun (ha') in 'upon His image', which yields three possible referents, thereby undermining any forced literal interpretation.

Analyze the three possible referents of the pronoun
Imam Razi identifies three possibilities for the pronoun: referring to something other than Adam and Allah, referring to Adam himself, or referring to Allah. Examine each and see how they affect interpretation.
This grammatical opening is the foundation for Razi's subsequent arguments.

Moses Maimonides says in The Guide of the Perplexed, verbatim: People thought that the word 'surah' in the Hebrew language indicates the shape of a thing and its outline. This led to pure anthropomorphism because of His saying, 'Let us make man in our image, after our likeness' (Genesis 1:26). They thought that God is in the image of a man, meaning his shape and outline, so they fell into pure anthropomorphism and believed it. They thought that if they departed from this belief, they would falsify the text, nay, they would annihilate God if He were not a body with a face and limbs having shape and outline, but larger and more splendid in their view, and that His material is not flesh and blood—the utmost they saw as transcendence in respect of God. As for what should be said concerning the negation of corporeality and the affirmation of true unity, which has no reality except by repudiating corporeality, you will know the proof of all that from this treatise. Commentary
This passage is a quotation from Maimonides' Guide of the Perplexed, included by Razi to explain the proper interpretation of 'image of God' in scripture to avoid anthropomorphism. Maimonides begins by noting that people mistakenly think the Hebrew word 'surah' means shape or outline, leading to pure anthropomorphism (tajsim) because of verses like 'Let us make man in our image.' They believe God has a human form, though larger and more splendid, and think denying this would falsify the text or even negate God's existence. Maimonides then states his intent: to clarify the true meanings of 'surah' and 'mithal'. He distinguishes between the common meaning of 'surah' as shape (called 'to'ar' in Hebrew) and the philosophical meaning of 'surah' as the natural form or essence (al-surah al-tabi'iyya) that makes a thing what it is and is its true reality. In humans, this essence is intellectual apprehension (al-idrak al-'aqli). Thus the verse 'He created him in the image of God' refers to the intellect, not any physical form. Maimonides supports this with scriptural examples: Psalm 73:20 says 'they consume his image' meaning they destroy his dignity attached to the soul, not bodily shape. He extends this to idols and the biblical 'images of hemorrhoids' (1 Samuel 5:5?) as examples where 'image' means intended meaning, not shape. If forced, 'surah' could be considered homonymous between essential form and artificial shape. Then he turns to 'mithal' (likeness), showing from various Psalms that it always means likeness in meaning or quality, not in shape. For example, the throne of God is described in terms of majesty, not dimensions. Ultimately, man's unique intellectual apprehension is compared to God's knowledge (without instrument) as a first-glance analogy, not a real likeness. Thus man is said to be in God's image because of the intellect connected to him, not because God has a body.

Commentary
This passage is a quotation from Maimonides' Guide of the Perplexed, included by Razi to explain the proper interpretation of 'image of God' in scripture to avoid anthropomorphism. Maimonides begins by noting that people mistakenly think the Hebrew word 'surah' means shape or outline, leading to pure anthropomorphism (tajsim) because of verses like 'Let us make man in our image.' They believe God has a human form, though larger and more splendid, and think denying this would falsify the text or even negate God's existence. Maimonides then states his intent: to clarify the true meanings of 'surah' and 'mithal'. He distinguishes between the common meaning of 'surah' as shape (called 'to'ar' in Hebrew) and the philosophical meaning of 'surah' as the natural form or essence (al-surah al-tabi'iyya) that makes a thing what it is and is its true reality. In humans, this essence is intellectual apprehension (al-idrak al-'aqli). Thus the verse 'He created him in the image of God' refers to the intellect, not any physical form. Maimonides supports this with scriptural examples: Psalm 73:20 says 'they consume his image' meaning they destroy his dignity attached to the soul, not bodily shape. He extends this to idols and the biblical 'images of hemorrhoids' (1 Samuel 5:5?) as examples where 'image' means intended meaning, not shape. If forced, 'surah' could be considered homonymous between essential form and artificial shape. Then he turns to 'mithal' (likeness), showing from various Psalms that it always means likeness in meaning or quality, not in shape. For example, the throne of God is described in terms of majesty, not dimensions. Ultimately, man's unique intellectual apprehension is compared to God's knowledge (without instrument) as a first-glance analogy, not a real likeness. Thus man is said to be in God's image because of the intellect connected to him, not because God has a body.

Commentary

He gives several biblical examples where likeness is in meaning (e.g., 'resembles the wild ox' refers to grief, not wings). He concludes that the human is distinguished by rational perception, which is unlike anything in the lower world, and is likened to God's perception, which is not through instruments. He cites Maimonides' Guide for the Perplexed to reinforce that this does not imply God is a body.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the claim that Allah is literally sensory light.
