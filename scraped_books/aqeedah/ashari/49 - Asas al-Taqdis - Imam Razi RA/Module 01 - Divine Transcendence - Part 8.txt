Resolving a Problematic Hadith about the Dajjal
Imam Razi critiques a problematic hadith about the Dajjal and defends it by suggesting missing context.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi defends a problematic hadith by positing missing context, using the analogy of Aisha's critique of Ibn Umar's narration.

Core Idea
Problematic hadith can be reconciled by assuming missing context, not rejecting the text.

Imam Razi presents a challenging hadith that seems to compare God to the Dajjal. Rather than rejecting it, he offers a principle: the speech might have been preceded by other words that clarify the intended meaning. To illustrate, he invokes a famous incident involving Aisha and Ibn Umar. This lesson explores that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own argument unfold.
Pay attention to how Razi introduces the problem and then offers a solution.
2
Identify the problem and solution
The problem is that the hadith's apparent meaning is far-fetched (بعيد). Razi's solution is to assume the speech was preceded (مسبوق) by other words that would clarify the context.
3
Understand the analogy
Razi compares this to the famous incident where Aisha criticized Ibn Umar's narration about the dead being punished by weeping, arguing missing context. Recognize how this analogy strengthens his point.
This analogy shows that the method of assuming missing context is precedent in early Islamic scholarship.

Commentary
This passage addresses an objection concerning a problematic hadith that seems to compare God and the Dajjal by stating that the Dajjal is one-eyed while God is not one-eyed. The author finds this implication far-fetched (بعيد). He suggests the best course is to assume that the hadith was preceded (مسبوق) by another part of speech that would clarify the context and remove the difficulty. He then draws an analogy with a famous incident: Ibn Umar narrated a hadith that 'the dead person is punished by the weeping of his family'. Aisha criticized this narration, arguing that the Prophet's statement was preceded by another context (perhaps referring to a non-believer who was already being punished), and she supported her argument with Quran 35:18 ('no bearer of burdens bears the burden of another'). Had that preceding context been reported, the apparent contradiction would be resolved. Similarly, the hadith about the Dajjal likely had a missing preamble that would explain the intended distinction without implying any imperfection in God. The author concludes that it is very unlikely that the Prophet, whom God chose and commanded to explain His law, would utter such a problematic statement without clarifying context.

Core Idea
"Nafs al-Rahman" means relief from Allah, not a literal breath.

This lesson explores Imam Razi's interpretation of two hadiths that mention "the breath of the Most Merciful." Rather than taking these literally, Razi shows how the word "nafs" is derived from a root meaning relief and alleviation. Understanding this demonstrates the importance of ta'wil in defending God's transcendence.

Now that you know this introduction, we say: As for His saying, exalted: 'The hand of God is above their hands' (48:10), the meaning is that the power of God, exalted, dominates the power of creation. And as for His saying, which was recited as a story about the Jews – that they said: 'The hand of God is fettered' (5:64) – the 'hand' here means blessing (ni'mah). So it is established that the intended meaning is that they believed that the blessings of God, exalted, are withheld from creation, prevented from them. Thus this verse became one of the strongest proofs that the word 'hand' may be intended as blessing.

Commentary
This passage is a verse-by-verse application of the metaphorical framework. The author first interprets 'Yad Allah fawqa aydihim' (48:10) as God's power (qudrah) overcoming the power of creatures. He does not provide a lengthy proof here, simply stating the meaning. Then he turns to the Jewish saying 'Yad Allah maghlulah' (5:64). He argues that the word 'hand' here must mean blessing (ni'mah), not a literal limb, by a two-part dilemma. If the Jews were theists, they could not mean a literal fettered hand for God because that is absurd. If they were atheists, calling God 'fettered' would have no point. So the only sensible interpretation is that they meant God's blessings are withheld. Thus this verse itself demonstrates that 'hand' can mean blessing. The author concludes that this is a strong proof for the metaphorical usage.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation. Imam Razi first presents the opponents' evidence: (1) the Qur'anic verse from Surah al-Qalam that mentions 'the day when the shin shall be uncovered,' and (2) a prophetic hadith narrated in Sharh al-Sunnah in which the Prophet says that on the Day of Resurrection, Allah will uncover His shin, prompting the believers to prostrate while hypocrites cannot. Razi then states that neither the verse nor the hadith provides proof for a literal shin for Allah. He gives three objections (wujuh) in sequence:

First objection: The Qur'anic verse is in the passive voice: 'yukashafu 'an saaq' (the shin shall be uncovered). It does not say that Allah uncovers His own shin. Thus, the literal attribution of a shin to Allah is not asserted by the verse.

Second objection: If we take the shin literally as a limb, attributing a single shin to a living being implies deficiency, because a normal animal has two shins. Such deficiency cannot be attributed to Allah, who is transcendent above all imperfection.

Third objection: In human experience, uncovering the shin is done to avoid soiling one's garment with something forbidden. Allah is far above such considerations. Therefore, the literal meaning is impossible. Razi then provides the correct figurative interpretation: 'saaq' here means the severity of the terrors of the Day of Resurrection, as in the Arabic idiom 'the war stood on its shin' meaning it became intense. So 'uncovering the shin' means 'revealing the severity' of that Day. The attribution to Allah emphasizes that such severity is uniquely within His power.

Razi thus argues that the correct interpretation is metaphorical, not literal.

Negating Attributes: When and How
Razi discusses when and how negation of attributes is permissible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why merely negating an attribute from Allah can be problematic and learn the proper way to negate attributes based on Razi's methodology.

Core Idea
Negating attributes requires careful explanation to avoid denying what is befitting of Allah.

Razi addresses a subtle but critical methodological question: when we say an attribute does not apply to Allah, can we just use the negative form? He shows that simple negation can mislead, because it might imply that the attribute in its praiseworthy sense is being denied.
