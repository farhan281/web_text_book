The First Preliminary: A Being Beyond Sensory Perception
Author states the first preliminary and gives three formulations of the claim.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
God cannot be pointed to, located, or contained—yet He is undeniably existent.

Imam Razi begins his work with a foundational premise: there exists a being that cannot be perceived by the senses. He offers three ways of expressing this same truth. Before engaging with proofs, we must first clarify what we are trying to prove. This passage sets the stage by stating the claim and anticipating the objection that it is self-evidently false.

Fifth Proof: God Cannot Be a Body
Imam al-Razi's fifth proof against God being a body, using a branching reductio ad absurdum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam al-Razi's fifth proof that God cannot be a body, and why composition leads to absurdities like multiplicity of gods.

Core Idea
If God were composed, He would be multiple gods—impossible.

Imam al-Razi presents a powerful logical proof that God cannot be a body. If God were a body, He would be composed of parts. The proof then examines all possibilities for which part possesses divine attributes, showing that each leads to an impossibility. This passage demonstrates the necessity of divine simplicity.

2

3
Reflect on the theological implication

And if He were composed of parts, then either the one described by knowledge, power, and life is a single part of that aggregate, or the one described by these attributes is the aggregate of those parts. Commentary
This passage presents the fifth proof that God cannot be a body. The proof proceeds in steps. First, if God were a body (mutahayyiz), He would be composed of parts, because no one claims He is the size of an atom. Second, if He is composed, then the subject that possesses attributes like knowledge, power, and life is either (a) a single part of the whole, or (b) the whole aggregate. Option (a) is rejected because it would make God extremely small and contemptible, which is absurd. Option (b) then has two sub-possibilities: (b1) a single knowledge and power subsist in the entire aggregate, or (b2) each part has its own knowledge and power. (b1) is impossible because one attribute cannot inhere in multiple distinct loci. (b2) would require each part to be a separate eternal god, leading to polytheism, which is impossible. Thus, all possibilities are ruled out, proving that God cannot be a body.

The Fourth Premise: Existence as a Bridge Between Worlds
Razi argues shared existence entails spatial judgment for God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi argues that shared existence between the Creator and creation necessitates applying the spatial judgment to God, and why this requires existence to be a single reality additional to essence.

Core Idea
Shared existence logically implies shared spatial judgment.

Imam Razi is building a case that the same rules we apply to created beings must apply to God when the cause is the same. Here, the cause is existence itself. Since both the Creator and creation exist, whatever judgment follows from existence in the created realm must also apply to the Creator. This lesson unpacks that reasoning and its key underlying assumption.

Guided Path
1
Read the premise statement
Read the Arabic passage and translation once to grasp the overall claim: the fourth premise asserts a logical transfer of a spatial judgment from the witnessed to the unseen based on shared existence.
Notice the careful wording: 'al-muqtaḍā' (the cause/requisite) is existence.
2
Identify the required demonstration
Razi says we need to prove that existence is a single reality in both the witnessed (shāhid) and unseen (ghā'ib). Find this part and understand why it is crucial.
This is the linchpin: if existence is the same, the judgment must be the same.
3
Understand the implication about essence
The passage states this requires that God's existence is additional (zā'id) to His essence. Think about why that follows: if existence is a shared attribute, it cannot be the essence itself.
This links to the broader debate on whether attributes are identical to or distinct from essence.
4
Reflect on the author's closing remark
Razi ends by contrasting his formulation with others. Consider how his articulation of this 'doubt' (shubha) differs and why that matters for the argument.
This shows self-awareness and methodological sophistication.

The Seventh Objection: Univocal Existence
The seventh objection argues that existence must be univocal for the proof to work.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that the proof from existence requires univocality between God and creation, and the dilemma it poses for the Karramiyya.

Core Idea
Existence must be one in meaning for the proof to work; otherwise it collapses.

The opponent now concedes that existence is the cause but questions why that leads to existence in God. The key is whether 'existence' means the same thing when applied to created beings and to God. If not, the proof fails. Moreover, the Karramiyya are caught in a dilemma: they cannot accept univocality without falling into anthropomorphism or adding existence to essence.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Note the structure: concession, condition, consequence, dilemma.
The objector first concedes that the cause is existence, not temporal origination.
2
Understand the condition of univocality
The conclusion only follows if existence is one thing in the witnessed and the absent. If it is only verbal equivocation, the proof collapses. Grasp this logical hinge.
Univocal means the same meaning; equivocal means different meanings.
3
Analyze the dilemma for the Karramiyya
If existence is univocal, the Karramiyya would have to accept either tashbīh (God similar to created things) or that existence is an addition to essence (ziyāda), both of which they reject. This forces them into a contradiction.
The Karramiyya cannot affirm univocality without violating their own principles.
