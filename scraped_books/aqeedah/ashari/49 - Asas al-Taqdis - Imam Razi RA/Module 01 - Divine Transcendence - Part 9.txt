Arguments 1–3: Istiwa', Fawq, and Uluww
Enumeration of the first three Quranic arguments for direction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first three Quranic arguments that anthropomorphists use to claim Allah has a direction, based on verses of istiwa', fawq, and uluww.

Core Idea
Three families of verses that seem to place Allah spatially above creation.

Al-Razi begins his survey of anthropomorphic arguments by presenting the most obvious ones: verses that literally mention sitting on the Throne, being above, or being high. These provide the foundation for their claim that Allah occupies a direction. This lesson covers the first three of nine arguments, each relying on a specific Arabic word or phrase.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage for argument 1–3
Read the original Arabic for the first, second, and third arguments. Notice the repeated words: istiwa', fawq, and uluww.
The third argument contains multiple verses.
2
Identify the key terms
Identify the three technical terms: istiwa', fawq, and uluww. Understand what each literally suggests.
3
Consider the anthropomorphic reading
Ask yourself: if taken literally, what would these verses imply about Allah? How might an anthropomorphist use them?
This sets up the refutation later.

الفصل الثلاثون
في
ما يتمسكون به في إثبات الجهة لله تعالى
تمسكوا في ذلك بالقرآن والأخبار والمعقول والوجوه المركبة من السمع والعقل (1)
أما القرآن: فمن عشرة أوجه:
الأول: التمسك بالآيات السنة الواردة بلفظ الاستواء على العرش (2).
الثاني: التمسك بالآيات المشتملة على لفظ الفوق. فقد قال تعالى: (وهو القاهر فوق عباده وهو الحكيم الخبير) (3)، وقال: (وهو القاهر فوق عباده ويرسل عليكم حفظة) (4)، وقال: (يخافون ربهم من فوقهم) (5).
الثالث: الآيات المشتملة على لفظ العلو. كقوله تعالى: (وهو العلي العظيم) (6)، وقوله تعالى: (وهو العلي الكبير) (7)، وقوله: (سبح اسم ربك الأعلى) (8)، وقوله: (إلا ابتغاء وجه ربه الأعلى) (9)، وأيضا: تواتر النقل في قوله تعالى: (سبحان الأعلى) (10).
الرابع: الآيات المشتملة على لفظ المروح إليه والصعود. قال تعالى: (تعرج الطيب) (11) (الملائكة والروح) (11) إليه، وقال: (إليه يصعد الكلم الطيب) (12).
الخامس: الآيات المشتملة على لفظ الإنزال والتنزيل. قالوا: وهي كثيرة تزيد على المائتين في حق القرآن المبين، والروح والملائكة المقربين والتوراة والإنجيل.
السادس: الآيات المقرونة بحرف إلى، مع أنها لانتهاء الغاية منها: قوله تعالى: (إلى ربها ناظرة) (13)، وذلك يقتضي انتهاء النظر إليه. وقوله: (ثم إلى ربكم ترجعون) (14)، وقوله: (وإلى المصير) (15)، وقوله: (ارجعي إلى ربك) (16).
السابع: قوله تعالى: (كلا إنهم عن ربهم يومئذ لمحجوبون) (17)، والحجاب إنما يصح في حق من يكون جسما، وفي جهة، حتى يصير محجوبا بسبب شيء آخر.
الثامن: الآيات الدالة على أنه في السماء. قال: (أم أمنتم من في السماء) (18)، وقال: (قل: لا يعلم من في السموات والأرض الغيب إلا الله) (19).
التاسع: الآيات المشتملة على الرفع إليه. قال تعالى في حق عيسى عليه السلام: (إني متوفيك ورافعك إلي) (20)، وقوله: (وما قتلوه بقنا).
Chapter Thirty: On what they cling to in establishing direction for Allah. They cling to the Qur'an, traditions, and rational arguments, and composite arguments from scripture and reason. As for the Qur'an: from ten aspects. First: clinging to the verses that came with the word 'istiwa' upon the Throne (2). Second: clinging to the verses containing the word 'above' (al-fawq). For He said: (And He is the Subduer above His servants, and He is the Wise, the Aware) (3), and He said: (And He is the Subduer above His servants, and He sends guardians over you) (4), and He said: (They fear their Lord from above them) (5). Third: verses containing the word 'highness' (al-‘uluww). Like His saying: (And He is the Most High, the Great) (6), and His saying: (And He is the Most High, the Great) (7), and His saying: (Glorify the name of your Lord, the Most High) (8), and His saying: (except seeking the face of his Lord, the Most High) (9), and also the transmitted frequent reports of His saying: (Glorify the Most High) (10). Fourth: verses containing the word 'al-marwaḥ' (ascension?) to Him and rising. He said: (ascends the good) (11) (the angels and the Spirit) (11) to Him, and He said: (To Him ascends the good word) (12). Fifth: verses containing the word 'descending' (al-inzal) and 'sending down' (al-tanzil). They said: They are many, exceeding two hundred concerning the manifest Qur'an, and the Spirit, and the near angels, and the Torah, and the Gospel. Sixth: verses connected with the preposition 'ila' (to), although it is for the limit of the goal. His saying: (Looking towards her Lord) (13), and that necessitates the end of looking to Him. And His saying: (Then to your Lord you will be returned) (14), and His saying: (And to the destination) (15), and His saying: (Return to your Lord) (16). Seventh: His saying: (Nay, on that Day they will be veiled from their Lord) (17). And the veil is only valid in regard to one who is a body and in a direction, so that he becomes veiled by something else. Eighth: verses indicating that He is in heaven. He said: (Or are you secure from Him who is in the heaven) (18), and He said: (Say: None in the heavens and the earth knows the unseen except Allah) (19). Ninth: verses containing the word 'raising' (al-raf‘) to Him. He said concerning Jesus, peace be upon him: (I will take you and raise you to Me) (20), and He said: (And they did not kill him with certainty).

Commentary
This passage is a classification of nine Quranic arguments used by those who affirm a literal direction (jiha) for Allah. Al-Razi lists them under a heading that also mentions traditions and rational arguments, but here only the Quranic ones are detailed. He states that there are ten aspects in total, but only nine are present in this excerpt. Each argument cites specific verses that anthropomorphists take as proof that Allah is in a direction, such as being above, in heaven, or having a location. The first argument invokes verses about istiwa' (sitting upon the Throne). The second uses verses containing the word 'above' (fawq). The third uses verses with 'highness' (uluww). The fourth, though the Arabic is unclear, likely refers to verses about ascension (mi'raj). The fifth notes the large number of verses about sending down revelation as proof of above-ness. The sixth employs verses using the preposition 'to' (ila) to imply a goal or direction. The seventh uses the verse about being veiled (hijab) from Allah on the Day of Judgment, arguing that veiling only applies to bodies in a place. The eighth uses verses saying He is 'in the heaven'. The ninth uses verses about Jesus being raised to Allah. Al-Razi is here presenting the opposition's case, which he will later refute.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That these verses prove Allah literally sits on a throne or is physically above creation.
Correction: The Ash'ari position interprets these verses metaphorically, affirming Allah's transcendence above spatial location.

Arguments 4–6: Ascension, Descending, and the Preposition 'Ila'
Enumeration of arguments 4–6: ascension, descending, and the preposition 'ila'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the next three Quranic arguments used to claim Allah has a direction: those involving ascension, descending, and the preposition 'ila' indicating a goal.

Core Idea
Verses about movement toward or from Allah imply He is in a specific place.

Moving from static spatial terms, the next three arguments focus on verses that describe movement: things ascending to Allah, things descending from Him, or the preposition 'to' marking a direction. These seem to imply a spatial endpoint. Al-Razi lists them as the fourth, fifth, and sixth proofs.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic for arguments 4–6
Read the Arabic carefully. Note the words 'al-marwah', 'al-inzal', and the use of 'ila'.
The fourth argument contains a typo 'al-marwah', likely meaning 'ascension'.
2
Identify the logic behind each argument
For each, ask: how does the verse suggest direction? Ascension implies an upward direction; descending implies from above; 'ila' implies a goal.
3
Consider the cumulative weight
Anthropomorphists claim these many verses cannot all be metaphorical. Note the large number (over 200) for the fifth argument.
This builds their case.

Arguments 7–9: Veil, In Heaven, and Raising
Enumeration of arguments 7–9: veil, in heaven, and raising.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the final three Quranic arguments for Allah's direction: the veil verse, verses saying He is in heaven, and the raising of Jesus.

Core Idea
Three more specialized verses that seem to imply a physical location for Allah.

The last three arguments are more specific: the verse about being veiled from Allah on the Day of Judgment, which only makes sense if Allah is a body in a place; verses stating 'He is in heaven'; and verses about Jesus being raised to Allah. Al-Razi presents these as further Quranic proof for direction.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic for arguments 7–9
Read the original Arabic. The seventh argument mentions 'hijab', the eighth 'fi al-sama', the ninth 'raf'.
2
Understand the logic of the veil argument
The argument claims that veiling (hijab) only applies to bodies in a direction. If Allah can be veiled, He must be a body in a place.
This is a key anthropomorphic inference.
3
Note the final argument is incomplete
The passage ends mid-sentence for the ninth argument. Recognize that this is due to the page break in the source.
The original lesson continues.

The Dilemma of Direction: Finitude or Infinitude
Razi's dilemma against direction for Allah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why attributing a direction to Allah leads to absurdities, whether we assume He has a limit or not.

Core Idea
Direction for God leads to an inescapable dilemma with impossible consequences.

Imam Razi presents a crisp logical argument against the idea that Allah could be in a direction. He sets up a dilemma: if Allah were in a direction, He would either have a limit (finitude) or not (infinitude) in that direction. Both options lead to contradictions—either something higher than Allah, or division and change in His essence. This lesson unpacks that dilemma.

Guided Path

Read the passage carefully to grasp the two horns: either Allah has a limit in the direction above, or He does not. Notice how Razi presents these as exhaustive possibilities.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum argument.

Empty spaces (al-ahiyaz al-khaliyya) are considered possible locations.

This horn argues that the very notion of 'direction' entails parts in God's essence.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Note that both horns lead to falsehood, so the original assumption (that Allah is in a direction) must be false. This reaffirms tanzih (transcendence).
The argument does not depend on any specific direction; it works for any direction.
