Moses' Answer Proves God's Transcendence
Imam Razi shows that Moses' answer to Pharaoh proves God is not a body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi uses the dialogue between Moses and Pharaoh to prove that God is not a body (mutahayyiz).

Core Idea
Moses' answer to Pharaoh proves God is beyond physical description.

This lesson examines a logical proof from the Quranic dialogue. In Surah Ash-Shu'ara, Pharaoh asks Moses, 'And what is the Lord of the worlds?' Moses replies, 'Lord of the heavens and the earth...' Imam Razi shows that if God were a body, Moses would have answered with bodily attributes. Since he didn't, God is not a body. This argument illustrates the method of prophetic transcendence.

1
Read the passage

The passage is a concise logical proof; read it slowly.
2
Identify the conditional
Look for the 'if-then' structure: 'If God were mutahayyiz, then Moses' answer would have been...' This is the core of the argument.
Imam Razi uses a hypothetical to expose a contradiction.
3
Recognize the conclusion
Since the hypothetical leads to an absurdity (Moses being wrong and Pharaoh correct), the premise is false: God is not mutahayyiz.
The proof is indirect, showing what cannot be the case.
4
Connect to prophetic pattern
The passage ends by linking Moses' answer to Prophet Muhammad's response to the disbelievers, showing a consistent prophetic method of affirming divine transcendence.
This reinforces the theological principle of tanzih.

Abraham's Proof: Bodies Are Similar, So God Cannot Be a Body
Imam Razi gives Abraham's argument that if God were a body, He would be subject to change, which is impossible for a deity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Abraham's rational argument that God cannot be a body because all bodies share the same nature and are subject to change.

Core Idea
If God were a body, He would change like other bodies—but change is impossible for God.

Imam Razi begins his theological proof from Abraham's story by establishing a key principle: all bodies share the same nature. From this, he argues that if God were a body, He would be subject to change, which would contradict His divinity. This concise but powerful argument lays the groundwork for understanding divine transcendence.

Prostration and Nearness: A Logical Proof
Razi presents a logical proof from Quran 96:19 that Allah is not in a spatial direction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi uses the command to prostrate and draw near to argue that Allah is not spatially located above.

Core Idea
Prostration implies nearness; if Allah were above, prostration would increase distance.

Imam Razi continues his series of Qur'anic proofs for divine transcendence. This proof is remarkably concise: it takes a single verse and extracts a logical contradiction in the anthropomorphic view. Let's examine how the command to prostrate and draw near undermines the notion that Allah is physically above.

Guided Path

These terms form the logical pivot of the proof.
3
Unpack the conditional reasoning
Razi uses an 'if...then' structure: if Allah were in the direction of above, then prostration would indicate distance, not nearness. Trace this logic step by step.
Consider why lowering oneself would be moving away from a being that is above.
4
Connect to the principle
Razi concludes by stating that this outcome is 'contrary to the principle' (khilaf al-asl). The principle is that worship, especially prostration, brings one closer to Allah.

وحينئذ نقول: إما أن تكون الذات هي المتحيزية، وتكون تلك الخصوصية صفة لتلك الذات، وإما أن يقال: المتحيزية صفة، وتلك الخصوصية هي الذات.

وأما القسم الثاني وهو أن يقال: إن تلك الخصوصية غير مختصة بشيء من الأحياز والجهات. فنقول: إنه يمتنع أن تكون المتحيزية صفة قائمة بها، لأن تلك الخصوصية غير مختصة بشيء من الأحياز والجهات، والمتحيزية أمر لا يعقل إلا أن يكون حاصلا في الجهات. والشيء الذي يجب أن يكون حاصلا في الجهات، يمتنع أن يكون حاصلا في الشيء الذي لا اختصاص له بالجهات.
The first proof: If He, exalted, were spatial, He would be similar to all other spatial beings in complete essence. And this is impossible. So His being spatial is impossible.
And we say that if He, exalted, were spatial, He would be similar to all other spatial beings in complete essence, because if He were spatial, He would be equal to other spatial beings in being spatial; then after this, it is not free from either it being said that He differs from other bodies in His specific essence, or that He does not differ from them in His specific essence. The first division is false, so the second becomes determined. Then from this it follows that if He, exalted, were spatial, He would be similar to all other spatial beings.
So here we need to show that it is impossible for Him to be equal to other spatial beings in the generality of spatiality, and different from them in His specific essence.
We say: The evidence that this is impossible is that on the assumption that He is equal to them in spatiality and different from them in specificity, then that by which there is commonality would necessarily be different from that by which there is distinction. Then the generality of spatiality would be different from the specificity of the particular essence.
Then we say: Either the essence is spatiality, and that specificity is an attribute of that essence; or it is said: spatiality is an attribute, and that specificity is the essence.
As for the first division, it necessitates the attainment of the objective. Because if mere spatiality is the essence, and it is established that mere spatiality is a common matter between Him and other spatial beings, then from that it follows that on the assumption that He, exalted, is spatial, His essence would be similar to the essences of other spatial beings. And that is exactly what is sought.
As for the second division, which is that it is said: the essence is the specificity, and the attribute is spatiality. We say: This is impossible. That is because that specificity, in itself, without regard to spatiality, either has a connection to space (hayyiz) or does not have that. The first is impossible. Because everything that exists in a space and direction independently is spatial. So if that specificity which we assumed to be free from spatiality existed in space, then that which is free from spatiality would be spatial. And that is impossible.
As for the second division, which is that it is said: That specificity is not connected to any space or directions. We say: It is impossible for spatiality to be an attribute subsisting in it, because that specificity is not connected to any space or directions, and spatiality is something not conceivable except as existing in directions. And a thing that must exist in directions is impossible to exist in that which has no connection to directions.

Commentary
This passage contains the first rational proof that God is not a spatial being. Razi argues that if God were spatial, He would be similar to all other spatial beings in complete essence (tamam al-mahiyya). This is impossible because God is unique. The proof proceeds as follows: (1) If God were spatial, He would share the common property of being spatial with all other spatial things. (2) Either He differs from them in His specific essence or He does not. (3) If He differs, then that common property (spatiality) and the specific essence would be distinct. This leads to two possibilities: either the essence is spatiality and specificity is an attribute, or the essence is specificity and spatiality is an attribute. (4) If the essence is spatiality, then since spatiality is common, God's essence would be identical to other essences—which is the required conclusion (that similarity is entailed, which is impossible). (5) If the essence is specificity and spatiality is an attribute, then this is impossible because the specific essence (without spatiality) must either be connected to space or not. If connected, then it would itself be spatial, contradicting the assumption that it is free from spatiality. If not connected, then spatiality cannot be an attribute of it, because spatiality necessarily exists in directions, while the specific essence has no connection to directions; thus an attribute that requires direction cannot subsist in something not connected to direction. Therefore, the only coherent possibility is that the essence is spatiality itself, leading to similarity, which is impossible. Hence, God cannot be spatial.

Second Branch: Refuting Spatiality as Attribute
Razi refutes the second branch of the argument, showing that spatiality cannot be an attribute of a non-spatial essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's refutation of the possibility that spatiality could be an attribute of a non-spatial essence.

Core Idea
Spatiality cannot be an attribute of anything non-spatial, so God cannot be a body.

In the previous lesson, we saw that if God's essence were spatiality, similarity follows. But there is another logical possibility: maybe God's essence is something unique and spatiality is just an attribute. In this lesson, Razi dismantles that possibility by showing it leads to two absurdities. This completes the first proof against divine corporeality.

Guided Path
1
State the second possibility
Read: 'the essence is the specificity, and the attribute is spatiality'. This is the alternative to be refuted.
2
First subcase: specificity connected to space
If the specific essence has a connection to space, then it would itself be spatial, contradicting the assumption that it is free from spatiality.
This is a contradiction.
3
Second subcase: specificity not connected to space
If the specific essence has no connection to space, then spatiality cannot be an attribute of it, because spatiality requires existence in directions, and that which has no connection to directions cannot support such an attribute.
This is also impossible.
