Two Approaches to Anthropomorphic Verses and the First Proof of Transcendence
Imam Razi defines two types of discussion and gives the first proof of transcendence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam al-Razi's two-fold approach to interpreting anthropomorphic verses and the first rational proof establishing that Allah is transcendent of spatial movement ('coming and going').

Core Idea
Motion and change imply contingency; Allah, being eternal, cannot be described by them.

Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, a master of both kalam and tafsir, introduces a fundamental principle for handling verses that ascribe physical actions to Allah. He distinguishes two necessary steps: establishing transcendence through rational proofs, and then offering allegorical interpretations in line with that transcendence. In this lesson, we examine his first proof, which draws on a core kalam principle: anything subject to change is contingent, and the Eternal is beyond contingency.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice how Razi divides the discussion and the logical flow of the proof.
Pay attention to the structure: two types, then first proof.
2
Identify the two types of discussion
Observe that Razi mentions two approaches: first, proving transcendence by conclusive proofs; second, offering allegorical interpretations. Understand why he prioritizes the first.
This shows his methodology: reason then scripture.
3
Trace the logical steps of the proof
Follow the argument: coming/going implies never free from contingent; whatever is never free from contingent is itself contingent; therefore, it is created. Then apply to Allah: He is eternal, so cannot be subject to coming/going.
This is a classic kalam argument against anthropomorphism.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological statement followed by a logical proof. Imam al-Razi begins by explaining that the discussion of the verse 'Do they wait except that Allah should come to them in canopies of clouds?' (Quran 2:210) and similar anthropomorphic expressions has two aspects. The first is to demonstrate by definitive arguments that Allah is completely beyond any notion of physical movement or spatial change ('coming and going'—al-maji' wa al-dhahab). The second is to provide allegorical interpretations (ta'wil) of these verses, so they are understood in a manner befitting Allah's transcendence.

Then he proceeds to the first type, noting that there are multiple proofs. He presents the first proof, which is based on a foundational principle of theology (usul al-din): anything that can be described as 'coming' or 'going' is necessarily connected to temporal events (muhdath). In other words, motion and displacement are accidents that inhere in bodies and are entailed by change. Whatever is never free from being connected to the contingent (i.e., cannot exist without such contingent attributes) is itself contingent. Since the attribute of 'coming and going' is inseparable from being contingent, anything that possesses this attribute must be contingent and created. The Eternal Being (al-ilah al-qadim) cannot be described as contingent or created; therefore, it is impossible for Him to come or go. This is a classic kalam argument: change implies temporality, and temporality implies need for a creator. Allah, being eternal and self-sufficient, cannot be subject to change.

Four proofs that Allah is above coming and going
Razi provides four rational proofs against the possibility of motion for Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand four rational proofs from Imam Razi demonstrating why Allah cannot be characterized by motion or change.

Core Idea
Allah's transcendence is proven by the impossibility of change in the divine.

Imam Razi presents four rational arguments that refute the attribution of coming and going to Allah. These proofs draw on principles of contingency, limitation, and the need for a specifying agent. They also engage with the prophetic argument of Abraham. Understanding these proofs strengthens one's conviction in Allah's absolute transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the four proofs
Read the Arabic passage carefully, then the translation. Note that Razi lists four distinct proofs (wujuh) against motion for Allah.
Each proof begins with an ordinal: الأول, الثاني, الثالث, الرابع.
2
Understand the first proof (contingency)
The first proof argues that whatever is subject to coming and going cannot be free from contingency (huduth), and whatever is contingent is created. Therefore, Allah, being eternal, cannot be described with motion.
This proof relies on the principle that change entails dependence on an agent.
3
Analyze the second proof (limitation and specification)
The second proof shows that anything that moves from place to place is limited and finite, having a specific size. That size could have been different, so its actual size requires a specifier (mukhassis) and preponderator (murajjih), which is impossible for the self-sufficient God.
This proof uses the kalām principle of specification (takhsis).
4
Examine the third proof (distinction from idols)
The third proof states that if we allowed motion for an eternal god, we could not deny the divinity of the sun and moon, which also move. This undermines the uniqueness of Allah.
This proof is a reductio ad absurdum.
5
Reflect on the fourth proof (Abraham's argument)
The fourth proof cites the Quran: Abraham argued against celestial bodies because they set (afool), meaning they undergo absence and presence. If Allah could also undergo such change, Abraham's argument would fail, and God would be contradicting His own support of Abraham.
This proof combines rational and scriptural evidence.

Commentary
This passage is a series of four rational proofs (wujuh) demonstrating that Allah cannot be characterized by coming and going (maji' wa dhahab). 

Proof 1: From the principle that anything subject to coming and going cannot be free from contingency (al-muhdath), because movement entails change, and change indicates dependence on an agent. Whatever is contingent is created, and the eternal God cannot be created. So movement is impossible for Allah.

Proof 2: Everything that moves from place to place is limited and finite, having a specific size. But that size could have been greater or smaller, so its actual size requires a specifier (mukhassis) and a preponderator (murajjih). Such need for external determination is impossible for the eternal, self-sufficient God.

Proof 3: If we allowed that something subject to motion could be an eternal god, then we could not refute the divinity of the sun and moon, since they also move. This destroys the uniqueness of Allah.

Proof 4: Abraham (al-Khalil) argued against the divinity of celestial bodies because they set (afool), which means they undergo absence and presence. If we allow that Allah can be absent or present (as coming and going implies), then Abraham's argument collapses, and Allah would be accusing Himself of falsehood in supporting Abraham. This shows the absurdity of attributing motion to Allah.

Each proof is independent and builds on different premises: contingency, need for specification, loss of distinction from idols, and undermining prophetic arguments.

The Impossibility of Movement for Allah
Imam Razi presents the second rational proof against literal movement for Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi uses the principle of limitation and the need for a specifier to argue that literal movement is impossible for Allah.

Core Idea
Anything that moves is limited and needs a specifier; Allah is beyond such need.

The Descent of Angels: A Metaphorical Interpretation
Imam Razi presents and interprets the fourth aspect regarding the descent of angels.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Razi's metaphorical interpretation of the descent of angels as an expression of divine honor and care, rather than literal spatial movement.

Core Idea
The descent of angels symbolizes honor, not physical movement.

This lesson explores the fourth aspect presented by Imam Razi in interpreting the hadith about the descent of angels. Rather than taking it literally, Razi argues that the descent is a metaphor for the honor and attention bestowed by Allah. This interpretation preserves divine transcendence while making sense of the scriptural language.

Imam Razi interprets the hadith about seeing Allah like the full moon.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi interprets the hadith of seeing Allah like the full moon, distinguishing comparison of seeing from comparison of the seen.

Core Idea
The hadith compares seeing to seeing, not Allah to the moon.

This lesson examines a famous hadith about the vision of Allah in the afterlife. Imam Razi explains that the prophetic comparison is only about the clarity of sight, not about any similarity between Allah and the moon. He also clarifies the meanings of the words 'la tudamun' and 'la tudarun'. The interpretation protects divine transcendence while affirming the vision.

If 'Face' were a limb, His Face alone would survive — an absurd conclusion.

Imam Al-Razi opens his argument against anthropomorphism by tackling the word 'Face' in the Quran and hadith. But if we interpret 'face' as pleasure (rida'), that becomes consistent. So it is established by these proofs that it is not possible for the 'face' mentioned in these verses and reports to mean the limb and organ.

This passage presents four distinct proofs (second through fifth) that the word 'wajh' (face) in Quranic verses and a prophetic hadith cannot refer to a literal physical limb. The author, Imam Razi, is refuting anthropomorphism (tashbih).

If 'face' were a physical limb, closeness would be spatial and unaffected by location. Interpreting it as 'pleasure' makes sense, as her seclusion increases divine pleasure. Also regarding proving the 'eye' of God Almighty: it is narrated in the supplications: 'Guard us with Your eye that does not sleep,' and it is also said customarily: 'the eye of God is upon you.' And know that the texts of the Quran cannot be taken according to their apparent meanings, for reasons: First: The apparent meaning of His statement (And that you be formed under My eye) requires that Moses, peace be upon him, be settled upon that eye, attached to it, and elevated over it, which no rational person would say. So it is established that it is necessary to resort to figurative interpretation (ta'wil), and that is to interpret these expressions as indicating intense care and protection. The aspect of the goodness of this metaphor is that one whose care for something is great, and his inclination and desire towards it, he frequently looks at it. So the word 'eye,' which is the instrument of that looking, is made a metaphor for intense care.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the necessity of figurative interpretation (ta'wil) of Quranic verses that attribute an 'eye' (عين) to God. It begins by citing two pieces of evidence from established usage: a hadith in supplications asking for protection by God's 'eye that does not sleep', and the common Arabic expression 'the eye of God is upon you'. These show that 'eye' is conventionally understood as care and protection, not a physical organ. Then the author lists three rational arguments why the literal interpretation is impossible. First, the verse about Moses (20:39) 'formed under My eye' would imply Moses was physically located on God's eye, which is absurd. Second, the verse about Noah (11:37) 'build the ark under Our eyes' would mean the eyes themselves were the tools of construction, which is nonsensical. Third, affirming multiple eyes (plural 'أعيننا') in a single face is repulsive to reason. Therefore, one must resort to ta'wil: interpreting these expressions as indicating intense care (عناية) and protection. The metaphor is justified because a person who cares deeply for something looks at it frequently; thus the word 'eye', the instrument of looking, becomes a metaphor for intense care.
