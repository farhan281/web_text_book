Hanabila's Admission Undermines Their Own Position
Razi uses Hanabila's own concessions to refute their objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi uses the Hanabila's own admissions about God's transcendence to argue that their rejection of the mutakallimun's position is inconsistent.

Core Idea
If Hanabila accept God is beyond imagination, they cannot reject the mutakallimun's God.

This passage is a masterclass in internal critique. Razi takes the Hanabila—who affirm that God has parts and dimensions—and shows that they themselves concede that God's essence is utterly unlike creation and beyond imaginative grasp. Having accepted that, they cannot logically object to the mutakallimun's claim that God exists without being inside or outside the world. Razi also points to their own method of interpreting anthropomorphic verses, which affirms a reality beyond imagination, further undermining their position.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without pausing. Then read the translation. Notice the flow: first a concession, then a reductio ad absurdum, then a rhetorical question, then another example from their own school.
The passage is dense; let the argument build.
2
Identify the two arguments
Identify the first argument from the nature of God's essence (if equal to creation, infinite regress or atheism follow). Then identify the second argument from the Hanabila's own method of interpreting anthropomorphic verses.
Both arguments show the Hanabila admit something beyond imagination.
3
Understand the rhetorical questions
After each argument, Razi asks: if you accept this, why do you reject our position? Reflect on how these questions expose inconsistency.
4
Connect to the mutakallimun's position
The passage defends the mutakallimun's claim that God is neither inside nor outside the world—a claim that seems unimaginable. Razi shows that the Hanabila already accept unimaginable things about God.
This is the key takeaway: the Hanabila's own theology allows what they deny.

Commentary
This passage is an argument from the Hanabila's own concessions. Razi first notes that even the Hanabila—who affirm that God has parts and dimensions—admit that God's essence is unlike any created essence, because otherwise infinite regress or denial of a creator would follow. Thus they recognize that the particularity of God's essence is beyond imagination and fantasy. Razi then asks: if they can accept that, why do they reject the notion of an existent that is neither inside nor outside the world? Secondly, Razi points to the Hanabila's own method of interpreting anthropomorphic verses: they affirm a face and hand for God but in a way contrary to created faces and hands, i.e., beyond imagination. If that is acceptable, then the mutakallimun's position—existence beyond inside/outside—is equally acceptable. The argument shows inconsistency in the Hanabila position.

The Tashbih Argument: Indwelling vs. Separation
Razi quotes the Tashbih group's syllogism using indwelling vs. separation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Tashbih group uses the dichotomy of indwelling and separation to argue that God must be spatially distinct from the world.

Core Idea
The Tashbih argue: any two existents are either indwelling or separate; since indwelling is impossible for God, He must be separate.

Razi presents the first of two arguments in this passage. The anthropomorphists (Tashbih) claim a necessary exhaustive dichotomy: any two existents must be either one indwelling in the other or separate from it. They then eliminate indwelling for God, concluding that He is separate and thus occupies space. This lesson examines their reasoning and the flawed assumption behind it.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation carefully to grasp the Tashbih's argument structure: premise, claimed exhaustive division, elimination, and conclusion.
2
Identify the exhaustive division
Note that they claim any two existents are either indwelling (ḥall) or separate (mubāyin). They assert this is known by necessity.
Consider whether this division truly covers all possibilities for non-corporeal beings.
3
Analyze the elimination and conclusion
They reject indwelling for God because it implies imperfection. Then they conclude He is separate by direction, i.e., occupies space.
Pay attention to the leap from 'separate' to 'by direction' — they assume separation implies spatial location.

Commentary
This passage presents two arguments. First, the argument of the Tashbih group (anthropomorphists): they claim a necessary exhaustive division (ḥaṣr) that any two existents are either one indwelling in the other (ḥall) or separate (mubāyin). Since indwelling is impossible for God (because it would imply corporeality), they conclude that God must be separate from the world, i.e., spatially distinct. Second, the argument of the Dahriyya (materialists/eternalists): they use a temporal dichotomy: any two existents are either coexistent (maʿan) or one precedes the other. They claim coexistence of world and Creator is impossible, because it would imply either the world is eternal or the Creator is originated. Therefore, one must have preceded the other (presumably the Creator precedes the world). Razi is likely presenting these to refute them in subsequent text, but the passage here is a straightforward presentation of their views.

The Dahriyya Argument: Coexistence vs. Priority
Razi quotes the Dahriyya's temporal dichotomy argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Dahriyya's temporal dichotomy—coexistence or priority—and how they use it to argue for the priority of the Creator over the world.

Core Idea
The Dahriyya argue: any two existents are either coexistent or one precedes the other; coexistence of world and Creator is impossible, so one must be prior.

Razi presents a second argument from the Dahriyya (materialists/eternalists). They propose a different dichotomy: any two existents are either coexistent (maʿan) or one precedes the other (qabl). Rejecting coexistence leads them to conclude that either the world is eternal or the Creator is originated—both unacceptable—so the Creator must be prior. This lesson examines their reasoning and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation carefully to understand the Dahriyya's argument structure.
2
Identify the dichotomy
The Dahriyya claim any two existents are either coexistent (maʿan) or one precedes the other (qabl).
This is a temporal dichotomy; note that 'coexistent' means simultaneous in time.
3
Analyze the rejection and its consequence
They reject coexistence because it would imply either the world's eternity (if coeternal with God) or God's origination (if coexisting with a temporal world).
Both implications are deemed false, so they conclude one must be prior, likely the Creator.

Imam Razi classifies the various views of anthropomorphists about Allah's form.

Core Idea
Anthropomorphists disagree on Allah's form, and their beliefs led to idol worship.

This lesson explores the third introduction of Imam Razi's Asas al-Taqdis, where he classifies the opinions of those who assert that Allah is a body. The passage reveals internal disagreements among anthropomorphists and traces the historical link between anthropomorphism and idolatry.

Commentary
This passage is the beginning of the third introduction (al-muqaddima al-thalitha) of Imam al-Razi's Asas al-Taqdis. Thus, al-Razi concludes that idolatry is a branch of anthropomorphism. He also notes that many of these anthropomorphists deny that motion or stillness can occur to Allah.

Anthropomorphism and the Origin of Idol Worship
Razi classifies anthropomorphist views and connects them to idol worship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the different anthropomorphic views among those who affirm God has a body, including the Muslim and Jewish anthropomorphists and the 'precise' ones, and how Razi links these views to the origin of idol worship.

Core Idea
Anthropomorphism is not monolithic; it ranges from human form to light, and Razi traces idol worship to this very doctrine.

Imam Razi begins his survey of those who affirm a body for God by detailing the various anthropomorphic opinions. He distinguishes between the common anthropomorphists (mushabbiha) who say God is in human form and the more refined ones who say He is in the form of light. More strikingly, he reports a historical theory that the very practice of idol worship originated from an anthropomorphic conception of God as a great light. This lesson will walk through these views and their implications.

Identify the three anthropomorphic groups
Read the first part of the passage and note the three distinct opinions: Muslim anthropomorphists (young human), Jewish anthropomorphists (old human), and the precise anthropomorphists (form of light).
Each group has a different conception of God's form.
2
Understand the link to idol worship

Commentary
This passage presents the argument of the anthropomorphist (al-mujassim) in defense of his position that asserting God is a body does not constitute tashbih (anthropomorphism). The anthropomorphist begins by stating that although he affirms that God is a body limited to space and direction, he maintains that God is completely different from all other bodies in essence and reality. This uniqueness, he claims, prevents the charge of tashbih. He then argues that establishing equality in forms (musawat fi al-suwar) does not necessarily entail tashbih, and supports this by noting that the Qur'an itself attributes many identical attributes to both God and humans without anyone claiming that this constitutes tashbih. To prove his point, he lists nine examples from the Qur'an where the same term is applied to God and to creatures. The first example is the attribute of hearing and seeing (sami' and basir) applied to God in Surah Taha 46 and to man in Surah al-Insan 2. The second is the phrase 'under Our eyes' used for the construction of the ark (Surah Hud 37) and eyes overflowing with tears for humans (Surah al-Ma'idah 83). The third concerns hands (yad): God's hands are outstretched (Surah al-Ma'idah 64), human hands are mentioned (Surah al-Hajj 10), and God's hands are mentioned again (Surah Ya-Sin 71) and human hands (Surah al-Fath 10). The fourth uses the verb 'istawa' (established) for God on the Throne (Surah Taha 5) and for humans settling on mounts (Surah al-Zukhruf 13). The fifth attributes 'al-Aziz al-Wahhab' (the Almighty, the Bestower) to God (Surah Sad 9) and 'al-Aziz' to Joseph (Surah Yusuf 78), and 'jabbar' (tyrant) to humans (Surah Ghafir 35). The sixth: God is called 'al-Azim' (the Great) and the Throne is described as 'al-Azim' (Surah al-Naml 26). The seventh: God describes Himself as 'al-Hafiz al-Alim' (the Guardian, the Knowing) and Joseph describes himself as 'hafiz 'alim' (Surah Yusuf 55); also 'halim' (forbearing) and 'alim' (knowing) are used for prophets (Surah al-Saffat 101, Surah al-Dhariyat 28). The eighth: God names our greeting 'salam' (peace) (Surah al-Ahzab 44) and names Himself 'Salam', as the Prophet's prayer attests. The ninth: God calls believers 'mu'minun' (believers) (Surah al-Hujurat 9) and calls Himself 'al-Mu'min' (Surah al-Hashr 23). By these examples, the anthropomorphist attempts to show that the mere sharing of descriptive terms in divine and creaturely contexts does not automatically imply tashbih, as the contexts and intended meanings differ.

Learning Objective
