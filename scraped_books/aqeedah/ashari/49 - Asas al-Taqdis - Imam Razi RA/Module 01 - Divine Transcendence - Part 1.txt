God's hand is not a limb but a metaphor for the covenant.

This introductory passage sets the stage for the entire book by tackling one of the most challenging issues in Islamic theology: how to understand verses that seem to attribute physical features to God. The author uses the example of 'hand of God' from the Quran to demonstrate his interpretive approach, emphasizing God's transcendence (tanzih) and the need for metaphorical interpretation (ta'wil).

Introduction to the Book:
Praise be to God, Lord of the worlds, and blessings and peace be upon the seal of the prophets, Muhammad, and upon his family and companions, and upon the earlier prophets, and those who follow their guidance until the Day of Judgment.
To proceed:
In the noble Qur'an is His statement: {Indeed, those who pledge allegiance to you are only pledging allegiance to God. The hand of God is above their hands.} This saying is interpreted by Imam Mahmud ibn Umar saying: He intends that the hand of the Messenger of God – which is above the hands of those pledging allegiance – is the hand of God. And God – exalted is He – is transcendent above limbs and above attributes of bodies. Rather, the meaning is: affirming that the covenant with the messenger is like the covenant with God, with no difference between them. Like His statement: {Whoever obeys the messenger has obeyed God.} This venerable scholar intends to say: The hand of God does not mean that God has a physical hand like human hands, but rather it means that the covenant with the messenger is like the covenant with God. For God has promised to give the mujahideen their reward without account, and who is more faithful to His covenant than God? And this venerable scholar adhered to this meaning because of clear texts in the Qur'an that negate likeness from God, the Mighty and Majestic. Among them is His statement: {There is nothing like unto Him, and He is the All-Hearing, the All-Seeing.} And those among the Muslims who do not excel in interpretation say that God has a hand, because He affirmed for Himself a hand in [?]
(1) See al-Kashshaf in Surah al-Fath.

Commentary
This passage is a book introduction that defines the author's interpretive methodology regarding anthropomorphic verses. The author opens with the basmala and hamdala, then states the book's purpose: to clarify the correct understanding of divine attributes. He uses the Quranic verse about the pledge of allegiance (Quran 48:10) as an example. He cites Imam Mahmud ibn Umar (al-Zamakhshari) who explains that 'the hand of God' above the hands of the believers is not a literal hand but refers to the covenant with the messenger, analogous to obedience to God. The author emphasizes God's transcendence (tanzih) above physical attributes. He then reiterates the interpretation: 'hand' here means the covenant, not a physical limb. He supports this with another verse (Quran 4:80) linking obedience to the messenger with obedience to God. To justify this figurative interpretation, he points to Quranic verses that negate any likeness to God (Quran 42:11). Finally, he contrasts his approach with that of Muslims who lack skill in interpretation (ta'wil) and take the literal meaning. The passage thus establishes the book's commitment to metaphorical interpretation (ta'wil) in line with divine transcendence.

Opening Praise and Metaphorical Interpretation of Divine Attributes
Al-Razi opens with praise of Allah and states the metaphorical interpretation of His attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Razi establishes divine transcendence through metaphorical interpretation of problematic attributes in the opening of his work.

Core Idea
Allah's attributes are transcendent; anthropomorphic terms must be interpreted metaphorically.

Al-Razi begins his masterpiece 'Asas al-Taqdis' with a concise yet profound statement of Islamic theology. In these opening lines, he not only praises Allah but lays the foundation for his entire methodology: the attributes of Allah are beyond literal human understanding and must be interpreted in a way that befits His majesty. This passage is the key to understanding the Ash'ari approach to divine attributes.

بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم الحمد لله الواجب وجوده وبقاؤه الممتنع تغيره وفناؤه العظيم قدره واستعلاؤه العميم آلاؤه ونعماؤه الدال على وحدانيته أرضه وسماؤه المتعالى عن شوائب التشبيه والتعليل صفاته وأسماؤه فاستواؤه قهره واستيلاؤه ونزوله بره وعطاؤه ومجيئه حكمه
In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. Praise be to Allah, whose existence and perpetuity are necessary, whose change and annihilation are impossible, whose power and supremacy are great, whose favors and blessings are universal, who is indicated by His earth and sky as evidence of His oneness, who is exalted above the impurities of anthropomorphism (tashbīh) and causality (taʿlīl) in His attributes and names. So His istiwā' (settling) is His subjugation and domination, and His nuzūl (descending) is His benevolence and giving, and His majī' (coming) is His decree.
Source pages p. 11

Commentary
This passage is the opening of al-Razi's work, functioning as both praise of Allah and a doctrinal statement. It begins with the basmalah and hamdalah, then immediately defines Allah's necessary attributes: His existence and perpetuity are wajib (necessary), while change and annihilation are mumtani' (impossible) for Him. The author then emphasizes His greatness and universal blessings. The phrase 'الدال على وحدانيته أرضه وسماؤه' indicates that the earth and sky are signs of His oneness. Crucially, al-Razi declares that Allah's attributes and names are transcendent above 'شوائب التشبيه والتعليل' – the impurities of anthropomorphism (tashbīh) and causality (taʿlīl). This sets the stage for his methodology of ta'wīl (metaphorical interpretation). He then gives three examples: istiwā' (settling) is interpreted as qahr (subjugation) and istīlā' (domination), not literal sitting; nuzūl (descending) is birr (benevolence) and atā' (giving), not literal movement; and majī' (coming) is hukm (decree), not literal arrival. This passage thus concisely presents the Ash'ari approach to problematic divine attributes, affirming transcendence while rejecting both literal anthropomorphism and complete negation (ta'ṭīl).

Divine Transcendence in Razi's Doxology
Razi praises God and declares His transcendence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi combines praise of God with a strong affirmation of divine transcendence over anthropomorphism and causal reasoning.

Core Idea
God's attributes are beyond human comparison and physicality.

Razi opens his work with a doxology that sets the foundation for his entire theological method: God is necessarily existent, eternal, and utterly transcendent. The passage you will read establishes that God's attributes and names are free from any impurities of anthropomorphism (tashbih) and cause-effect thinking (ta'lil). This prepares the reader to accept the metaphorical interpretations that follow in subsequent sections.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic praise
Read the opening lines slowly, noting the rhythm and the key divine attributes praised.
Notice the contrast between 'necessary existence' and 'impossible change'.
2
Identify the core transcendence claim
Find the sentence where Razi states that God's attributes are exalted above tashbih and ta'lil. This is the heart of the lesson.
Tashbih means likening God to creation; ta'lil means assigning causes to God.
3
Reflect on the implication
Ask yourself: why does Razi begin his book with this declaration? How does it shape the way he will discuss specific attributes later?
This principle of transcendence underlies all his metaphorical interpretations.

بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم الحمد لله الواجب وجوده وبقاؤه، الممتنع تغيره وفناؤه. العظيم قدره واستعلاؤه، العميم آلاؤه ونعماؤه. الدال على وحدانيته: أرضه وسماؤه، المتعالى عن شوائب التشبيه والتعليل: صفاته وأسماؤه. فاستواؤه: قهره واستيلاؤه، ونزوله: بره وعطاؤه، ومجيئه: حكمه وقضاؤه، ووجهه: جوده أو جوده وحباؤه، وعينه: حفظه وهونه واجتباؤه، وضحكه: عفوه أو إذنه وارتضاؤه، ويده: إنعامه أو إكرامه واصطفاؤه. ولا يجرى في الدارين من أفعاله إلا ما يريده ويشاؤه. والعظمة: إزاره، والكبرياء: رداؤه.
In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. Praise be to Allah, whose existence and permanence are necessary, whose change and annihilation are impossible. Great is His power and His exaltation, abundant His favors and His blessings. That which indicates His oneness: His earth and His heavens. Exalted above the impurities of anthropomorphism and cause-effect (tashbih and ta'lil): His attributes and His names. So His istiwa (establishing Himself) is His subjugation and His domination; His nuzul (descending) is His kindness and His giving; His maji' (coming) is His judgment and His decree; His wajh (face) is His generosity, or His generosity and His gift; His 'ayn (eye) is His preservation, His gentleness, and His selection; His dahk (laughing) is His pardon, or His permission and His approval; His yad (hand) is His bestowal, or His honouring and His choosing. And nothing runs in the two abodes from His actions except what He wills and desires. And greatness is His izār (waist-wrapper), and majesty is His ridā' (robe).
Source pages p. 11

Commentary
This passage is a doxology that defines God's essential attributes and then interprets certain anthropomorphic descriptions metaphorically to preserve divine transcendence. The author begins by praising God as necessarily existent and eternal (الواجب وجوده وبقاؤه), and as impossible to change or perish (الممتنع تغيره وفناؤه). He affirms God's power, exaltation, and abundant favors. He then states that God's earth and heavens indicate His oneness, and that God's attributes and names are far removed from anthropomorphism (التشبيه) and cause-effect thinking (التعليل). The core of the passage is the list of interpretations: 'istiwa' (often understood as 'sitting') is reinterpreted as 'subjugation and domination' (قهره واستيلاؤه); 'nuzul' (descending) as 'kindness and giving' (بره وعطاؤه); 'maji'' (coming) as 'judgment and decree' (حكمه وقضاؤه); 'wajh' (face) as 'generosity' (جوده) or 'generosity and gift' (جوده وحباؤه); 'ayn' (eye) as 'preservation, gentleness, and selection' (حفظه وهونه واجتباؤه); 'dahk' (laughing) as 'pardon' (عفوه) or 'permission and approval' (إذنه وارتضاؤه); and 'yad' (hand) as 'bestowal' (إنعامه) or 'honouring and choosing' (إكرامه واصطفاؤه). The passage concludes that nothing occurs in this world or the next except by God's will, and that greatness and majesty are exclusively His, using the metaphors of a waist-wrapper and robe from prophetic hadith. Razi thus affirms the attributes mentioned in scripture but insists on their metaphorical interpretation to avoid corporealism.

Metaphorical Interpretation of Anthropomorphic Attributes
Razi interprets seven anthropomorphic terms metaphorically.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi interprets specific anthropomorphic descriptions of God (like 'face', 'hand', 'eye') in a metaphorical manner that preserves divine transcendence.

Scriptural anthropomorphisms must be understood figuratively to avoid likening God to creation.

He provides metaphorical interpretations for each, showing that they refer to God's actions, not physical parts. This passage is a practical application of the method of tawil (figurative interpretation) widely used in Ash'ari theology.

This being is not located anywhere, not bound by time, and not confined to any direction. He presents three distinct formulations, each emphasizing a different aspect of transcendence, yet all pointing to the same reality. This lesson unpacks that premise and its significance.

Core Idea
If a belief were self-evident, no rational person could deny it. Many do, so it isn't.

Commentary
This passage presents the eighth argument, which is a two-part refutation of anthropomorphic adversaries (the Karramiyya and the Hanabila). Razi first asserts that even the adversaries must admit the existence of something that is not subject to the laws of sense and imagination (i.e., God). He then addresses each group.

**Against the Karramiyya:** Razi presents a dilemma: If God is a sensible object (mushār ilayhi bil-ḥiss), then He must be either divisible (munqasim) or indivisible (ghayr munqasim). If divisible, He would be composite (murakkab)—which the Karramiyya reject. If indivisible, He would be like a point or an atom—extremely small and lowly—which they also reject. In response, the Karramiyya claim that God is one, free from composition, yet not small or lowly. Razi points out that this claim is rejected by sense, imagination, and even reason. He explains: if something has sensible extension in directions, it must have distinct sides, leading to division (by rational intuition). If it has no extension, it is a point, extremely small. The Karramiyya's claim that God is indivisible yet great and infinite in extension is a contradiction (jamʿ bayn al-nafy wa-l-ithbāt) that is refuted by rational intuition. Razi then asks: If they accept such a contradiction, how can they reject the claim that God is neither in-dwelling (ḥall) nor in opposition (mubāyin) with respect to direction? The implication is that their own position is more problematic.

Therefore, the Hanabila must admit that God's essence has a specificity (khuṣūṣiyya) that distinguishes Him from all sensible things, which already implies that God is not subject to the laws of sense and imagination.

The passage ends mid-sentence (indicating the conclusion that God's essence is unique), but the argument is complete in its core: both schools are forced to acknowledge a transcendent reality beyond sense and imagination.

Razi's Refutation: The Dilemma of Imagination
Razi refutes anthropomorphists and materialists via a dilemma on the authority of imagination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi uses a dilemma about the authority of imagination to refute both anthropomorphists and materialists simultaneously.

Core Idea
If imagination is accepted, both sides contradict themselves; if rejected, their arguments collapse.

Imam Razi presents a powerful refutation of two opposing theological errors: anthropomorphism and materialism. He shows that both groups rely on imagination (al-khayāl) to make their claims about God. By posing a simple dilemma, Razi demonstrates that either way, their positions are untenable. This lesson unpacks his argument.

Guided Path
1
Summarize the positions
Identify what each group claims: anthropomorphists say separation from the world must be by direction; materialists say priority must be by time.
2
Pose the dilemma
Razi asks: Is the ruling of imagination accepted regarding God or not? This is the key fork in the argument.
3
If accepted: contradiction
Work through what happens if imagination is accepted. Each group is forced into the other's conclusion, which they reject, so they contradict themselves.
Anthropomorphists must accept eternal time; materialists must accept spatial location.
4
If rejected: premises collapse
If imagination is not accepted, then both groups' foundational claims are baseless imaginative statements. Their arguments fall apart.

Commentary
This passage is the refutation proper. Razi summarizes the positions of both groups: anthropomorphists (mushabbiha) say separation from the world must be by direction; materialists (dahriyya) say priority must be by time. He then poses a dilemma: either the ruling of fantasy (al-khayāl) is accepted regarding God or not. If accepted, each group is forced to accept the other's conclusion – anthropomorphists must admit infinite time (making time eternal), materialists must admit spatial location – but both reject those conclusions, so they contradict themselves. If fantasy is not accepted at all regarding God's essence and attributes, then both groups' premises collapse: the anthropomorphists' dichotomy (indwelling or separation by direction) is false, and the materialists' claim that priority must be by duration is likewise baseless. Razi's resolution is to reject imaginative constructs as authoritative in theology.

The Karramiyya affirm spatial location for God without limbs, but disagree on how.

This is presented as proof that Allah is not in a direction or place. The reasoning is as follows: (1) The word 'ma' in Pharaoh's question asks about the essence (mahiyya). (2) If Allah were spatial, the proper answer would describe that spatiality, because being spatial would be part of His essence. (3) But Moses did not mention spatiality; he mentioned actions. (4) If spatiality were essential, Moses' answer would be incorrect, making Pharaoh's accusation of madness valid. (5) Since we know Moses' answer is correct and Pharaoh's accusation is false, Allah cannot be spatial. Therefore, His essence cannot be defined by location but only by His creative and managerial attributes. Razi concludes that both prophetic responses—Muhammad's and Moses'—establish divine transcendence from spatiality.

Commentary
This passage introduces the argument from the story of Abraham (al-Khalil) in the Qur'an (Surah al-An'am, 6:76-79). Razi states that Abraham reasoned from the changes observed in heavenly bodies to their createdness (ḥudūth). After completing that reasoning, he declared: 'I have turned my face toward He who created the heavens and the earth, as a hanif.' This turning away from celestial bodies to the Creator indicates that Allah is not confined to any direction or place. Razi then tells us to know that this incident proves Allah's transcendence from spatiality, and he begins to list the aspects of this proof. The first aspect, which is cut off on this page, is that bodies are similar (mutamāthila) — a premise to be used in a fuller argument presumably continued on the next page. The passage thus sets up a proof from the absence of directionality in Abraham's final orientation.
