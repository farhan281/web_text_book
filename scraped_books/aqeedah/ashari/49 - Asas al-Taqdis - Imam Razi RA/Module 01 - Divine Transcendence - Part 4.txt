Second Proof: Location is Composite and Contingent
Imam Razi argues that location is composite and contingent, so Allah cannot depend on it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical proof that location is composite and contingent, and why Allah cannot be dependent on location.

Core Idea
Location is composite and contingent; the Necessary Being cannot depend on the contingent.

Imam Razi presents a second argument against Allah being in a location. He shows that location itself is composed of parts and can be measured, making it contingent. If the Necessary Being depended on location, He would depend on a contingent thing—an impossibility. This lesson walks through each step of Razi's proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation once to grasp the overall flow of the argument.
Pay attention to the logical connectors like 'if...then'.
2
Identify the premises
List the key premises: location is composite and measurable, composed things are dependent, dependent things are contingent.
3
Trace the dependency chain
Follow the chain: composite → dependent → contingent → Allah cannot depend on contingent because that would make Him contingent.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The conclusion is that Allah cannot be in a location because location is contingent and Allah is necessary.
This reinforces divine transcendence beyond spatial attributes.

The second [proof]: That which is called location (al-ḥayyiz) and direction (al-jiha) is a thing composed of parts and members. For it has been shown that it can be measured by the cubit and the handspan, and can be described as greater or lesser. And everything that is like that is dependent upon another, and that which depends upon another is contingent in itself. So the thing called location and direction is contingent in itself. If, therefore, Allah the Exalted were dependent upon it, He would be dependent upon the contingent, and that which depends upon the contingent is more fitting to be contingent in itself. Thus the Necessary [Being] in itself would be contingent in itself—and that is impossible.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof that Allah cannot be in a location. The argument proceeds step by step: (1) Location (ḥayyiz) and direction (jiha) are composite—they can be divided into parts, measured by units like cubits, and described as more or less. (2) Anything composite or measurable is dependent on other things (e.g., its parts and a referent for measure). (3) Dependency indicates contingency (mumkin li-dhātihi). Therefore, location itself is contingent. (4) If Allah (the Necessary Being) were to depend on a location, He would depend on something contingent. (5) Depending on the contingent makes one even more deserving of being contingent. (6) This would lead to the absurd conclusion that the Necessary Being is contingent—impossible. The proof establishes that location is inherently contingent, and since Allah is necessarily existent, He cannot be in any spatial context.

Third Proof: Location Would Imply Another Eternal Being
Imam Razi's third proof using the impossibility of a co-eternal location.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third proof that Allah cannot be in a location because it would imply an eternal co-existing entity, which contradicts Islamic consensus.

Core Idea
If Allah were spatial, the space would also be eternal, making two gods.

Imam Razi presents his third and final proof that Allah is not subject to space or direction. This argument appeals directly to the consensus of Muslims that nothing else is eternal besides Allah. Read carefully how Razi builds this concise but powerful proof from the impossibility of a co-eternal location.

(4) Therefore, it is impossible for Allah to be spatial or directional. The proof appeals to Islamic consensus (ijmāʿ) as a decisive authority. The three proofs together establish the transcendence of Allah beyond space and direction.

Refuting the Objection: Location as Abstract Separation
Imam Razi refutes an objection about divine location by clarifying the meaning of separation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi distinguishes between two meanings of 'separation' (essential vs. spatial) to defend God's transcendence and refute the objection that location is merely an abstract distinction.

Core Idea
Separation from the world does not require a spatial location—unless that's what you mean by it.

In this passage, Razi confronts an objection that tries to reduce divine location to a mere abstract distinction. The objection claims that saying Allah is 'separate' from the world does not imply any real location. Razi's answer is layered: first, he reaffirms that location is real; second, he unpacks the ambiguity of 'separation' to show that if spatial separation is meant, it does entail a real location—something impossible for Allah. This distinction is crucial for preserving divine transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Start by reading the objector's argument: that being 'separate' from the world is just an abstract relation, not a real location. Notice how the objector uses the world as an analogy.
The objector's point seems plausible—why should separation imply a real entity?
2
Identify Razi's two-part answer
Razi's answer has two main parts: first, he asserts that location and direction are real things proven by decisive arguments. Second, he addresses the claim about 'separation' by clarifying its possible meanings.
This two-pronged approach is typical in kalam dialectics.
3
Understand the ambiguity of 'separation'
Razi explains that words like 'separate' (mubāyin), 'isolated' (munfarid), and 'distinguished' (mumtāz) can mean either difference in essence (which is fine) or spatial distinction (which implies a real location). The opponent intends the latter, but that requires a real location and dependence.
This is the key logical move: the objection confuses two meanings.
4
See the proof and conclusion
Razi concludes that if the opponent means spatial distinction, then location must be real, and Allah's need for it is impossible. He also notes that bodies' need for location is not problematic because they are contingent.
The difference (farq) between Allah and bodies is that need for location is impossible for the Necessary Being.

Commentary
This passage contains an objection and its detailed answer, forming a dialectical exchange. The objector argues that „being in a location“ is just a metaphor for God‘s separation from the world—it does not imply a real entity. He uses the world as an example: bodies are spatially distinguished, but that only means parts are distinct, not a separate substance called location. Therefore, saying Allah is separate from the world does not entail a real location. Razi‘s answer unfolds in two parts. First, he reaffirms that location and direction are real, existing things, proven by decisive arguments (barāhīn qāṭiʿa). Second, he distinguishes two meanings of words like „separation“ (mubāyana), „isolation“ (infirād), and „distinction“ (imtiyāz): (a) difference in essence/quiddity—that Allah‘s essence is unlike the world, which all Muslims affirm but does not imply direction; and (b) spatial distinction, i.e., being able to point to something as „here“ or „there“. The opponent intends the second meaning, but that meaning necessarily involves a real direction/location, which (as shown earlier) is an existent thing. Furthermore, if Allah were spatially distinct, He would need that location—a need impossible for the Necessary Being. Razi adds that bodies‘ need for locations is not problematic because they are contingent, but Allah‘s need would be. Thus the difference is clear: bodily location involves need, but divine transcendence precludes any such need. The response preserves the transcendence (tanzīh) of Allah.

The author begins by stating the thesis: if Allah were confined to a direction, He would necessarily fall into one of three mutually exclusive cases: (1) infinite from all sides, (2) infinite from some sides and finite from others, or (3) finite from all sides. He then declares all three divisions invalid, so the original claim is false. The bulk of the passage refutes the first two divisions.

For the first division (infinite from all sides), the author gives three proof-wajh (وجوه). The first is a geometrical argument: assume an infinite line and a finite line parallel to it, then rotate the finite line to become aligned (مسامتة) with the infinite one. This would require a first point of alignment on the infinite line, but an infinite line has no first point because above any point there is another. This contradiction proves the impossibility of an infinite extension. The second wajh is a meta-argument: if infinite extension were possible, the proof that the world is finite would collapse, but that proof is accepted by consensus. The third wajh is a theological absurdity: if Allah were infinite in all directions, nothing would be free of His essence, implying mixture with impurities—which no rational person accepts.

For the second division (infinite from some sides, finite from others), the author gives two aspects (وجهان). The first repeats that the proof against infinite extension applies regardless of whether it is from all sides or only some. The second (which is incomplete in the text) considers whether the infinite and finite sides are equal in essence or not; if equal, then the finite would share the property of the infinite (i.e., be infinite as well), which is a contradiction. The text cuts off here, but the conclusion is implied.

The author's argument is rigorous, using logical division, geometrical reasoning, and theological consequences. The proof demonstrates that attributing spatial properties to Allah leads to absurdities, thereby defending divine transcendence (تنزيه).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Temporality cannot cause the immanence/transcendence judgment because non-existence is not a cause.

Imam Razi now addresses a potential objection: perhaps the judgment that a thing is either immanent or transcendent depends on its being created (hadith). To refute this, he presents two compelling proofs. The first is a simple logical analysis of the definition of temporality. The second is a powerful reductio argument involving the materialist who believes in eternal heavens yet still affirms the judgment. This lesson unpacks these proofs.

Third Proof: Determinant is Not Temporal Origination
Razi presents the third proof that the determinant is not ḥudūth, citing Ibn al-Haytham.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why temporal origination cannot be the rational determinant for the necessary judgment of immanence or separation.

Core Idea
Inference cannot ground necessity.

Razi presents a third proof, attributed to Ibn al-Haytham, to show that the determinant of the ruling that an existent must be either immanent or separate is not its temporal origination. The argument hinges on the different epistemic statuses of the two claims: one is known by inference, the other by necessity. This lesson unpacks that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its literal translation to get the overall argument.
2
Identify the two types of knowledge
Notice how Razi distinguishes between knowledge by inference (istidlāl) and knowledge by necessity (ḍarūra). The property of being temporally originated is known by inference, while the judgment of immanence or separation is known necessarily.
3
Understand the epistemic gap
Reflect on why a property known by inference cannot serve as the basis for a judgment known by necessity. Inference yields probable or mediated knowledge, while necessity yields immediate and certain knowledge.
4
Draw the conclusion
Conclude that temporal origination (ḥudūth) is not the determinant (muqtaḍā) of the ruling. This completes the third proof.

Third Objection: Relations as Real Positive Attributes
Imam Razi proves that relations are real positive attributes.

Learning Objective

Relations are not mental constructs but real objective attributes.

In this lesson, Imam Razi begins his third objection by establishing that relations are real existents. He uses the example of fatherhood to show that it is a positive attribute separate from the essence of the father. This argument lays the groundwork for the main objection against the dichotomy of immanence and transcendence.

Read the passage and identify the claim

This shows that the relation is a distinct attribute.

The second proof: Jesus (peace be upon him) had a full essence but no fatherhood or sonship. Thus, the relation is separate from the essence.

The different thing must be either negative or positive. It cannot be negative because fatherhood removes the absence (non-fatherhood), and removal of non-existence is existence. Hence, fatherhood is a positive attribute.
This finalizes that relations are real and positive.

الثالث : إنا نقيم الدلالة على أن الإضافات موجودات في الإعيان ، ثم تبين أنها يمتنع أن تكون محايثة للعالم، أو مباينة عنه بالجهة وذلك يبطل كلامكم . وإنما قلنا: إن الإضافات أعراض موجودات في الأعيان، لأن المعقول من كون الإنسان أبا لغيره ، مغاير لذاته المخصوصة. بدليل: أنه يمكن أن يعقل ذاته مع الذهول عن كونه أبا أو ابنا . والمعلوم غير ما هو معلوم . وأيضا : فإنه يمكن ثبوت ذات منفكة عن الأبوة والبنوة . مثل : عيسى عليه السلام . فإنه ما كان أبا لأحد ، ولا ابنا لأحد . والثابت غير ما هو غير ثابت. فمعنى كونه أبا وابنا ، مغاير لذاته المخصوصة . ثم هذا المغاير إما أن يكون وصفا سلبيا أو ثبوتيا . والأول باطل . لأن عدم الأبوة هو الوصف السلبي . والأبوة رافعة له، ورفع العدم وجود . فثبت : أن الأبوة وصف وجودي مغاير لذات الأب، إذا ثبت هذا فنقول : إنه مستحيل أن يقال : الأبوة محايثة لذات الأب . وإلا لزم أن يقال : إنه قام بنصف الأب نصف الأبوة، وبثلثه ثلث الأبوة : ومعلوم أن ذلك باطل . ومحال أن يقال : إنها مباينة عن ذات الأب ، مباينة بالجهة والحيز . وإلا لزم كون الأبوة جوهراً قائما بذاته مباينا عن ذات الأب بالجهة . وذلك أيضا محال . فثبت بهذا الدليل وجود موجود لا يمكن أن يقال : إنه محايث للعالم، أو يقال إنه مباين عنه بالجهة . وإذا ثبت هذا ، بطل قولهم .
The third: We establish the proof that relations are existent particulars, then we show that it is impossible for them to be immanent in the world or transcendent from it with respect to direction, and that refutes your statement. And we only say that relations are accidents existing in particulars because the conceived meaning of a man being a father to another is different from his specific essence. By evidence: it is possible to conceive his essence while disregarding his being a father or a son. And the known is other than what is known. Also, it is possible to affirm an essence separate from fatherhood and sonship, such as Jesus, peace be upon him, for he was not a father to anyone nor a son to anyone. And the affirmed is other than what is not affirmed. So the meaning of being a father and a son is different from his specific essence. Then this different thing is either a negative or a positive attribute. The first is invalid because the absence of fatherhood is the negative attribute, and fatherhood removes it, and the removal of non-existence is existence. So it is established that fatherhood is a positive attribute different from the essence of the father. When this is established, we say: It is impossible to say that fatherhood is immanent in the essence of the father, otherwise it would be necessary to say that half of fatherhood subsists in half of the father, and a third in a third of the father, and it is known that that is false. And it is impossible to say that it is transcendent from the essence of the father, transcendent with respect to direction and space, otherwise it would entail that fatherhood is a substance subsisting by itself, transcendent from the essence of the father with respect to direction, and that is also impossible. So it is established by this proof that there exists an existent of which it cannot be said that it is immanent in the world nor that it is transcendent from it with respect to direction. And when this is established, their statement is refuted.

Commentary
This passage presents the third objection, which is a detailed proof that relations (al-idafat) are real existents and that they cannot be described as either immanent or transcendent, thus refuting the opponent's dichotomy. The argument begins by asserting that relations are accidents (a'rad) existing in particulars (a'yan). The example of fatherhood (ubuwwa) is used. Fatherhood is conceived as different from the essence of the father; one can think of the essence without thinking of fatherhood. Also, Jesus had no father or son, showing that essence can exist without these relations. Therefore, fatherhood is a positive attribute (wajudi) because it removes the negative attribute of non-fatherhood, and the removal of non-existence is existence. Once established that fatherhood is a real positive attribute, the argument examines whether it is immanent or transcendent. If immanent, it would have to be divisible according to the parts of the father, which is false. If transcendent, it would be a separate substance, which is also false. Therefore, fatherhood is neither immanent nor transcendent. By analogy, there must exist something of which the same holds, refuting the opponent's claim that all existents are either immanent or transcendent.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Relations are merely mental constructs with no external reality.
Correction: Imam Razi proves that relations are real attributes existing in particulars, not just mental concepts.

Key Takeaway
Relations such as fatherhood are real, positive attributes distinct from the essence of the subject.
