Imam al-Razi as Teacher of Ibn Taymiyya
Argues that Ibn Taymiyya was a student of Imam Razi with evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the historical evidence that Ibn Taymiyya was a student of Imam al-Razi and the implications of this teacher-student relationship.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya was a student of Imam al-Razi, not his independent equal.

This passage presents a biographical argument from a traditional Sunni scholar, establishing that Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi was the teacher of Ibn Taymiyya. The author provides concrete evidence and a practical insight for understanding both scholars' works. As you read, consider how this relationship challenges common assumptions about Ibn Taymiyya's intellectual independence.

Guided Path
1
Read the claim and evidence
Start by reading the passage's main claim: Razi is Ibn Taymiyya's teacher. Then look for the evidence that Ibn Taymiyya taught Razi's books and studied Razi's al-Arba'in fi Usul al-Din under him.
Notice the source mentions Ibn Rajab's report about reading a portion of the book.
2
Understand the principle
The passage asserts that a student is not better than the teacher, quoting Jesus and a popular saying. Reflect on why this principle matters in the scholarly tradition.
3
Apply the practical tip
The author gives advice: to understand Razi, read Ibn Taymiyya, and vice versa. Consider how this reciprocity highlights their relationship.
This tip shows that even if Ibn Taymiyya disagreed with Razi, he was still his student.
4
Reflect on the misconception
Commonly, Ibn Taymiyya is seen as independent of Razi. This passage corrects that by showing his direct study and reliance on Razi's works.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical argument establishing that Ibn Taymiyya was a student of Imam Razi. It begins by stating the fact: Imam Razi is the teacher of Ibn Taymiyya, and the student is not better than the teacher. Then it provides evidence: Ibn Taymiyya taught Razi's books to students and earned income from teaching, as he and his family were refugees. Then it quotes Ibn Rajab, who reports that Ibn Taymiyya studied a portion of Razi's 'al-Arba'in fi Usul al-Din'. The passage then supports the principle that a student is not better than a teacher by citing a saying of Jesus from the Bible and a common saying. Finally, the author gives a practical tip: to understand Razi, read Ibn Taymiyya, and vice versa.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Ibn Taymiyya was an independent scholar who rejected Imam al-Razi's influence.
Correction: Ibn Taymiyya studied under Razi, taught his books, and read his works, proving he was Razi's student.

Key Takeaway
