Commentary
This passage is a series of fourteen Quranic proofs (the third through fourteenth in the author's enumeration) demonstrating that the Quran must be comprehensible (معلوم/مفهوم) to its audience. Each proof follows a consistent pattern: the author cites a verse that describes the Quran with an attribute (e.g., 'clear Arabic tongue', 'explanation of everything', 'guidance', 'healing', 'clear book', 'sufficient reminder', 'proclamation', 'proof', 'manifest light', 'guide', 'requires obedience'), and then argues that this attribute would be meaningless if the Quran were not known and understood. The inference is logically necessary: if the Quran is described as guiding, then it must be knowable, because guidance implies comprehension of the guide. Similarly, if it is a 'clear book' (mubin), clarity entails being understood. The cumulative effect of these proofs is to establish that the Quran is inherently comprehensible, and any claim to the contrary contradicts the very descriptions Allah gives of His Book. The conclusion is explicit: 'so it is necessary that the Quran be understood' (فوجب كون القرآن مفهوما).

Know that the Book of Allah indicates that it is entirely Muhkam, and indicates that it is entirely Mutashabih, and indicates that some of it is Muhkam and some of it is Mutashabih. As for that which indicates that it is entirely Muhkam: it is His saying 'Alif Lam Ra. A Book whose verses are made firm... Alif Lam Ra. Those are the verses of the Wise Book.' He mentioned in these two verses that all of it is Muhkam. And the meaning of Muhkam in this sense is that it is true in its words and true in its meanings. And every speech other than the Qur'an, the Qur'an is more excellent in its word and meaning. And that none of the creation is able to produce speech equal to the Qur'an in its word and meaning. And the Arabs say of a solid building and a solid covenant that cannot be dissolved: it is Muhkam. This is the meaning of describing the entire Qur'an as Muhkam.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and proof. The author first states that the Qur'an is described in three ways: entirely Muhkam, entirely Mutashabih, and partly Muhkam partly Mutashabih. He then provides the proof for the first description. He quotes two verses (Surah Hud and Surah Luqman) that describe the Book as having firm verses (آيات محكمات) and as being wise (حكيم). The meaning of Muhkam here is that the Qur'an is true in its words and true in its meanings. Moreover, every other speech is inferior, and no creature can produce words equal to the Qur'an. The author also remarks that Arabs use the term Muhkam for a solid building or covenant that cannot be undone. This clarifies the meaning of describing the entire Qur'an as Muhkam.

Proof that the Entire Qur'an is Mutashabih
Proof from Qur'an that the entire Book is Mutashabih with explanation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Qur'an's internal consistency and mutual confirmation among its verses serve as proof that it is entirely Mutashabih, meaning its parts resemble each other in beauty and eloquence.

Core Idea
The Qur'an's perfect harmony and consistency prove it is entirely Mutashabih — similar and mutually confirming.

In this passage, Imam Razi presents a key proof that the entire Qur'an is described as Mutashabih. He does not mean that it is ambiguous; rather, he shows that the Qur'an's parts resemble each other in beauty and eloquence and confirm each other. The proof is drawn from the Qur'an itself, using two verses that establish its consistency as a sign of divine origin.

Commentary
This passage provides the proof that the Qur'an is entirely Mutashabih. The author quotes the verse 'كتاباً متشابهاً' from Surah Az-Zumar (39:23). The meaning of Mutashabih here is that its parts resemble each other in beauty and eloquence (الحسن والفصاحة) and confirm each other (يصدق بعضه بعضا). The author also references another verse 'ولو كان من عند غير الله لوجدوا فيه اختلافا كثيرا' to indicate that if it were from other than Allah, they would find much contradiction, meaning some parts would contradict others and the style would vary in grandeur and eloquence. Thus, the Qur'an's consistency and mutual confirmation demonstrate its divine origin.
