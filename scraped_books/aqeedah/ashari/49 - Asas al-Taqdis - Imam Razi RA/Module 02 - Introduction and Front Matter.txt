The Book and Its Author: Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's 'Asas al-Taqdis'
Describes the book's purpose and identifies its author.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the purpose of Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's 'Asas al-Taqdis', its interpretive methodology for anthropomorphic attributes, and the scholarly context of its author.

Core Idea
The book affirms God's existence and interprets attributes metaphorically to preserve transcendence.

This lesson introduces the foundational text 'Asas al-Taqdis' and its author, Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi. You will see how the book sets out to affirm God's existence and reinterpret verses that might imply human-like qualities. This opening passage also situates the work within the Ash'ari tradition by citing earlier scholars and listing al-Razi's major contributions.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without pausing. Then read the literal translation to grasp the flow.
Notice the multiple names for the book and the author's full lineage.
2
Identify the book's dual purpose
The book has two aims: affirming God's existence and interpreting (ta'wil) attributes that might imply bodily parts or human actions. Find these in the text.
This is the core of Ash'ari theology—denying anthropomorphism while affirming attributes.
3
Observe the methodological precedent
The author cites three earlier scholars (al-Zamakhshari, al-Ghazali, al-Sha'rani) as following the same interpretive method. Why might this be important?
This shows continuity in the Ash'ari tradition.
4
Note the manuscript and author details
The passage mentions specific manuscripts used for this edition and then presents al-Razi's credentials: his death date, his Ash'ari creed, Shafi'i school, and major works.
This establishes the author's authority and the textual basis of the book.
5
Reflect on the significance
Consider how this introduction frames the entire work: it is a kalam text that defends tawhid using reason and tradition, and it stands on the shoulders of earlier scholars.

Commentary

It begins by stating the book's name, 'Tasis al-Taqdis' or 'Asas al-Taqdis', and its core purpose: to affirm the existence of God Almighty and to interpret (ta'wil) those attributes that might imply bodily parts (like hand, foot, eye, ear) or human-like actions (like anger, wrath, deceit) in a way befitting God's transcendence. Finally, it identifies the author as Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, giving his full name, death date (606 AH), his Ash'ari creed and Shafi'i school, and listing three of his major works: the Great Tafsir (Mafatih al-Ghayb), al-Mahsul in usul al-fiqh, and al-Matalib al-Aliya, which it equates with Greek 'pathologia' and Islamic 'ilm al-kalam' or philosophy. This serves to situate the book within the Ash'ari tradition of kalam and tafsir.

Razi's Purpose in Writing Asas al-Taqdis
Razi states his motivation to write a gift for Sultan Saladin.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Imam Razi wrote Asas al-Taqdis and to whom he dedicated it.

Core Idea
Razi wrote Asas al-Taqdis as a theological gift for Sultan Saladin to strengthen the true religion.

Imam Razi opens his work with a traditional praise of God and the Prophet, then explains his motivation. Despite living far in the East, he heard of Sultan Saladin's great efforts for Islam and decided to compose this book as a noble offering. This lesson explores that context.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic original and its translation to grasp the flow of Razi's introduction.
Notice that Razi begins with standard formulas before stating his purpose.
2
Identify the recipient
Look for the name and honorifics used for Sultan Saladin. Razi describes him with many laudatory epithets.
The recipient is explicitly 'أبا بكر بن أيوب' (Abu Bakr ibn Ayyub), known as Saladin.
3
Understand the purpose
See that Razi calls the book a 'تحفة سنية' (noble gift) and 'هدية مرضية' (pleasing offering) to support the sultan's work.
This shows the work was intended to aid the sultan's efforts in strengthening Islam.
4
Reflect on the title
The book is titled 'Asas al-Taqdis' (Foundation of Sanctification). Consider why Razi might have chosen this name in relation to the sultan's role.
The title emphasizes the theological foundation of tawhid, which aligns with fighting false doctrines.

Commentary
This passage is the author's introduction after the hamdala and shahada, where he explains his reason for writing the book. Razi begins by praising God and then reciting the two testimonies of faith, followed by blessings on the Prophet. He then transitions with 'أما بعد' to state his purpose. He mentions that although he lives in the distant East, he has heard the universal consensus among Easterners and Westerners on the excellence of Sultan Saladin (Abu Bakr ibn Ayyub), describing him with numerous laudatory epithets: the supreme sultan, learned, just, mujahid, sword of the world and religion, sultan of Islam, best of sultans. Razi expresses that the sultan's banners are constantly raised in strengthening the true religion and the truthful creed, and the effects of his power endure perpetually. Out of admiration, Razi decided to send him a noble gift (تحفة سنية) and a pleasing offering (هدية مرضية), which is this book entitled 'Asas al-Taqdis' (Foundation of Sanctification), despite the geographical distance. Thus, the book is a scholarly offering dedicated to a ruler praised for his support of Islam.

Structure of the Book and the First Premise
Introduces the book's division and begins the first of three necessary premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the overall structure of Razi's 'Asas al-Taqdis' and the content of its first preliminary premise regarding the necessity of spatial location or its absence for all existents.

Core Idea
Every existent either occupies space or does not — this dichotomy is the starting point for discussing God's transcendence.

Imam al-Razi begins his work by outlining its four-section structure and then immediately establishes a crucial preliminary point. Before discussing God's transcendence, he argues that we must first accept a self-evident truth: all beings are either physically located or they are not. This premise, though simple, lays the ground for the debate on whether God is bodily or transcendent.

Guided Path
1
Understand the book's architecture
Read the opening lines to see the four-part division and the focus of the first section on signs of God's transcendence.
The book is divided into four sections; the first section contains chapters.
2
Identify the first premise
The first premise states that every existent is either subject to sensory pointing (spatially located) or not. This is presented as a necessary dichotomy.
Note that the premise is about all existents, not just God.
3
Appreciate the premise's status
Razi clarifies that this premise is not demonstrative but apparent and self-evident, needing no proof.
He distinguishes between necessary/intuitive premises and those requiring proof.
4
Engage with the objection
An objection claims that using theoretical reasoning to attack necessary truths undermines both root and branch. Razi responds by reaffirming the premise's intuitive nature.
This objection highlights a methodological concern in theology.

Commentary
This passage is a combination of the book's introduction and the beginning of the first chapter. The author begins by stating that the book is divided into four sections, and the first section deals with signs of God's transcendence over corporeality and having an eye. This section contains chapters, and the first chapter is about presenting three necessary premises before entering into the theological discussion.

The author then states the first premise: every existent is either subject to sensory pointing (like 'here' or 'there') or is not. The claimant of an existent that is not inclined from the world nor in contact with any of the six directions essentially claims something intermediate between being in a direction and not. The author asserts that this premise is known by necessity and intuition; it is a self-evident truth that every existent either occupies a direction or does not. He then mentions that some object that using theoretical arguments to deny necessary truths would undermine both the root and the branch, but he clarifies that this premise is not from the demonstrative premises that require proof; rather it is apparent and needs no proof. The passage ends by stating that there are many ways to show that some premises are intuitive, as sound intellects recognize.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think that the author is trying to prove God's existence through this premise.
Correction: He is actually establishing that spatial location or its absence is a necessary dichotomy for all existents, which is a background for discussing God's transcendence.

Key Takeaway
The foundation for discussing God's transcendence is the self-evident dichotomy that all existents either occupy space or do not.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
The premise says every existent either occupies space or does not. God does not occupy space. Do you truly conceive of God as beyond all space, or do you find yourself imagining Him in a particular direction? Spend a moment in silent reflection, and commit to replacing any spatial image with the understanding that He is absolutely transcendent.
?
The author calls this premise 'apparent' and known by necessity. Yet many people still think of God as being 'up' or 'in heaven' in a physical sense. Where in your own heart do you hold onto such spatial conceptions? Ask yourself: Do I live as though God is limited to a certain place? Pray for the certainty that He is beyond all spatial constraints.
