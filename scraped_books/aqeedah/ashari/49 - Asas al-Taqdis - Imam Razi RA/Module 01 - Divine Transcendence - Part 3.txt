Proof That All Bodies Are Equal in Essence
Imam Razi proves that all bodies are equal in essence by logical division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Razi's proof that if God were a body, His essence would be equal to all bodies, thus refuting anthropomorphism.

Core Idea
If God were a body, He would be identical to every other body.

Imam Razi presents a decisive proof that all spatial beings (bodies) are equal in their complete essence. He uses a logical division argument to show that any distinction would collapse, leading to the conclusion that God cannot be a body. This lesson walks through the proof step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the translation. Notice the formal structure of proof by division.
The passage is dense – take your time.
2
Identify the two components
Look for the two aspects: spatiality (mutahayyiziyya) shared by all bodies, and particularity (khussusiyya) unique to each. The argument assumes God as body would have both.
3
Follow the first division
If the essence is spatiality and particularity is an attribute, then God's essence is merely spatiality – making Him identical to all bodies. This yields the desired conclusion.
This is the straight path.
4
Examine the second division
If spatiality is an attribute and particularity is the essence, the particularity must either have a location or not. Both options lead to impossibility or ultimately to the same equality.
Pay attention to the sub-argument about spatiality requiring direction.
5
Conclude the proof
Both divisions force the conclusion: all spatial beings are equal in complete quiddity. Thus, God cannot be spatial.
This is a qati' (decisive) proof.

لسائرها فى المتحيزية ، ومخالفاً لها فى الخصوصية : كان ما به الاشتراك مغايراً - لا محالة ـ لما به الامتياز. وحينئذ يكون عموم المتحيزية مغايراً لخصوص ذاته المخصوصة . وحينئذ نقول : إما أن تكون الذات هي المتحيزية ، وتكون تلك الخصوصية صفة لتلك الذات ، وإما أن يقال : المتحيزية صفة ، وتلك الخصوصية هي الذات. أما القسم الأول فإنه يقتضى حصول المقصود . لأنه إذا كان مجرد المتحيزية هو الذات ، وثبت أن مجرد المتحيزية أمر مشترك فيه بينه وبين سائر المتحيزات ، فحينئذ يحصل منه أن بتقدير أن يكون تعالى متحيزاً ، تكون ذاته مماثلة لذوات سائر المتحيزات . وليس المطلوب إلا ذلك . وأما القسم الثاني وهو أن يقال : الذات الخصوصية ، والصفة هى المتحيزية . فنقول : هذا محال . وذلك لأن تلك الخصوصية من حيث إنها هي هي ، مع قطع النظر عن المتحيزية . إما أن يكون لها اختصاص بالخبز[؟] ، وإما أن لا يكون لها ذلك (٤) والأول محال . لأن كل ما كان حاصلا في حيز وجهة على سبيل الاستقلال ، كان متحيزاً . فلو كانت تلك الخصوصية التي فرضناها خالية عن التحيز ، حاصلة فى الحيز ، لسكان الخالى عن التحيز متحيزاً . وذلك محال . وأما القسم الثانى وهو أن يقال : إن تلك الخصوصية غير مختصة بشيء من الأحياز والجهات . فنقول : إنه يمتنع أن تكون المتحيزة صفة قائمة بها ، لأن تلك الخصوصية غير مختصة بشيء من الأحياز والجهات ، والمتحيزية أمر لا يعقل إلا أن يكون حاصلا) (ه) في الجهات . والشيء الذي يجب أن يكون حاصلا في الجهات ، يمتنع أن يكون حاصلا في الشيء الذي يمتنع حصوله في الجهة . وإذا لم تكن المتحيزية صفة للشيء ، كانت نفس الذات . وحينئذ يلزم أن تكون الأشياء المتساوية في المتحيزية ، متساوية في (تمام) (۳) الذات . فثبت بما ذكرنا : أن المتحيرات يجب أن تكون كلها متساوية فى تمام الماهية ، وهذا برهان قاطع في تقرير هذه المقدمة
For all other things in being spatial (mutahayyiziyya), and differing from them in particularity (khussusiyya): that by which there is sharing is necessarily different from that by which there is distinction. Then the generality of spatiality is different from the particularity of its specific essence. Then we say: either the essence is the spatiality, and that particularity is an attribute of that essence, or it is said: spatiality is an attribute, and that particularity is the essence. As for the first division, it necessitates the attainment of the objective. Because if mere spatiality is the essence, and it is established that mere spatiality is something shared between Him and other spatial beings, then it results from that that assuming that the Exalted is spatial, His essence would be similar to the essences of other spatial beings. And nothing else is required. As for the second division, which is to say: the essence is the particularity, and the attribute is spatiality. We say: this is impossible. That is because that particularity, in itself, apart from consideration of spatiality—either it has a connection to a place (bal-khubz[?]), or it does not have that [connection] (4). The first is impossible, because everything that exists in a spatial location and direction independently is spatial. So if that particularity which we assumed to be free from spatiality existed in a spatial location, then that which is free from spatiality would be spatial—that is impossible. As for the second division, which is to say: that particularity is not connected to any spatial location or direction. We say: it is impossible for spatiality to be an attribute inhering in it, because that particularity is not connected to any spatial location or direction, and spatiality is inconceivable except as existing in directions. And something that must exist in directions is impossible to exist in something that is impossible to exist in a direction. And if spatiality is not an attribute of the thing, it is the essence itself. Then it follows that things equal in spatiality are equal in (complete) essence (3). So it is established by what we have mentioned: that spatial beings must all be equal in complete quiddity (tamam al-mahiyya), and this is a decisive proof in establishing this premise.

Commentary
This passage is a proof by division (qism) aiming to show that all bodies (mutahayyizat) are necessarily equal in their complete essence. The author first sets up a hypothetical: if God were a body, there would be two components in Him—spatiality (mutahayyiziyya) shared with all bodies, and a particularity (khussusiyya) that distinguishes Him. These two must be distinct. Then he divides: either the essence is the spatiality and the particularity is an attribute, or the spatiality is an attribute and the particularity is the essence. The first division directly yields the desired result: if the essence is merely spatiality, then any body's essence is identical to that common spatiality—so God's essence would be like all bodies. The second division is refuted by considering the particularity apart from spatiality. It either has a location (which would itself be spatial) or does not. If it has no location, then spatiality cannot inhere in it (since spatiality requires direction), so spatiality must be the essence itself, again leading to equality. Thus all bodies are equal in complete quiddity. The proof is presented as a decisive (qati') demonstration of the premise that God cannot be a body.

Proof 1: From Necessary Properties of Similar Things
Razi presents the first proof from the principle of al-mutamathilayn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first proof that God's essence cannot be similar to bodies, based on the principle that similar things share all necessary properties.

Core Idea
If God were like bodies, either God would be temporal or bodies eternal.

Imam Razi begins by laying down a fundamental rule of kalam: two things that are alike in essence must be alike in all their necessary properties. He then applies this rule to the relationship between God and bodies to demonstrate impossibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the translation. Get a feel for the logical flow.
Notice the concise style of Razi.
2
Understand the rule of al-mutamathilayn
The rule states that similar things share all necessary properties (lawazim). This is a key axiom in kalam.
3
Follow the logical consequence
If God were similar to bodies, their necessary properties would match. This leads to a contradiction: either God becomes temporal or bodies become eternal.
Both options are impossible given known truths.
4
Reflect on the impossibility
The conclusion is that God cannot be similar to bodies in essence. This establishes divine transcendence.
This proof is foundational for understanding tanzih.

وإنما قلنا : إنه يمتنع أن تكون ذات الله تعالى مساوية لذوات الأجسام في تمام الماهية لوجوه : الأول : إن من حكم المتماثلين : الاستواء في جميع اللوازم . فيلزم من قدم ذات الله تعالى، قدم سائر الأجسام، أو من حدوث سائر الأجسام، حدوث ذات الله تعالى . وذلك محال . الثاني : إن المثلين يجب استواؤهما في جميع اللوازم . وكما صح على سائر الأجسام خلوها عن صفة العلم والقدرة والحياة ، وجب أن يصح على ذاته الخلو عن هذه الصفات، وحينئذ يكون اتصاف ذاته بحياته وعلمه وقدرته ، من الجائزات . وإذا كان الأمر كذلك ، امتنع كون تلك الذات موصوفاً بالحياة والعلم والقدرة ، إلا بإيجاد موجد ، وتخصيص مخصص. وذلك يقتضى احتياجه إلى الإله . وحينئذ كل ما كان جسماً كان محتاجاً إلى الإله . وهذا يقتضى أن الإله يمتنع أن يكون جسماً . الثالث : إنه لما كان ذاته تعالى مساوية لذوات سائر المتحيزات ، ع في سائر المتحيزات كونها متحركة تارة وساكنة أخرى ، وجب أن تكون ذاته أيضاً كذلك . وعلى هذا التقدير يلزم أن تكون ذاته تعالى قابلة للحركة والسكون . وكل ما كان كذلك ، وجب القول بكونه محدثاً
And we only said: it is impossible that the essence of God Most High be equal to the essences of bodies in complete quiddity, for several reasons: The first: Indeed, from the rule of the two similar things (al-mutamathilayn) is equality in all necessary properties (lawazim). So it would follow from the eternity of the essence of God Most High the eternity of all other bodies, or from the temporality of all other bodies the temporality of the essence of God Most High. And that is impossible. The second: Indeed, the two similar things must be equal in all necessary properties. And just as it is established for all other bodies that they are devoid of the attributes of knowledge, power, and life, it would be necessary that His essence be validly devoid of these attributes. Then the description of His essence with His life, knowledge, and power would be among contingent (ja'izat) matters. And if that is the case, it is impossible for that essence to be described with life, knowledge, and power except by the bringing into existence of an existing thing (mujid) and the specification of a specifier (mukhasis). That necessitates its need for the divine (ilah). Then everything that is a body is in need of the divine. And this necessitates that the divine cannot be a body. The third: Indeed, since His essence Most High is equal to the essences of other spatial beings, and it is established [صح?] for other spatial beings that they are sometimes moving and sometimes at rest, it would be necessary that His essence also be thus. And on this assumption, it follows that His essence Most High is susceptible to motion and rest. And everything that is thus, it is necessary to say that it is temporally originated (muhdath).

Commentary
This passage presents three independent proofs (wujuh) that God's essence cannot be similar to the essences of bodies. The first proof invokes the kalam principle of al-mutamathilayn (two similar things): if two things are similar in essence, they must share all necessary properties (lawazim). If God's essence were like a body's, then either God's eternity would entail the eternity of bodies (impossible, as bodies are contingent), or bodies' temporality would entail God's temporality (also impossible, as God is eternal). Thus similarity is impossible. The second proof applies the same principle to attributes: if God were like bodies, He could be devoid of knowledge, power, and life just as bodies are; but God's attributes are essential, not contingent, so His possession of them would require an external cause, making Him dependent—contradicting divinity. The third proof argues from motion and rest: bodies are subject to change, so if God were like a body, He would be subject to motion and rest, implying temporality (muhdath). All three proofs aim to establish divine transcendence (tanzih) over bodily attributes.

Proof 2: From Contingent Attributes
Razi presents the second proof from contingency of divine attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second proof that God cannot be like bodies: if He were, His essential attributes would become contingent, requiring a creator.

Core Idea
If God were like bodies, His attributes would be contingent, making Him dependent on another.

The second proof extends the rule of similarity to attributes. Just as bodies lack knowledge, power, and life, so too would God if He were like them. But God possesses these attributes necessarily; if they were contingent, He would need a cause, contradicting His divinity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully.
This proof is longer and more detailed.
2
Identify the rule applied to attributes
The same rule of similarity requires that if God and bodies are alike, they must share the possibility of lacking certain attributes.
3
Trace the argument that attributes become contingent
If God could lack life, knowledge, and power, then His actual possession of them becomes contingent (ja'iz), not necessary.
Contingent things require a cause.
4
Understand the conclusion: God cannot be a body
If God's attributes need a cause, then God needs a creator, which contradicts His being the divine (ilah). Therefore, God cannot be a body.
This argument shows that divinity and bodily nature are incompatible.

Proof 3: From Motion and Rest
Razi presents the third proof from motion and rest as properties of bodies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third proof that God cannot be like bodies, because bodies are subject to motion and rest, which would entail temporality in God.

Core Idea
If God were like bodies, He would be changeable, but change implies being created.

The third proof argues from the observable property of bodies: they alternate between motion and rest. If God were similar to bodies in essence, He too would be subject to such changes. But anything subject to change is temporally originated (muhdath), contradicting God's eternity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic and translation. Note the brevity of this proof.
The proof is short but powerful.
2
Identify the reasoning from similarity to motion/rest
If God is like bodies, He must share their property of being sometimes moving, sometimes at rest.
3
Follow the implication to temporality
Being susceptible to motion and rest means being subject to change, which is a sign of temporal origination (muhdath).
Anything changeable is not eternal.
4
Conclude the impossibility
Since God is eternal and not originated, the premise of similarity must be false.
This reinforces divine transcendence.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A body must be differentiated by something external, making it dependent.

Imam Razi presents a sophisticated proof that God cannot be a body. He argues that any body requires a differentiator, either at the level of genus or species, leading to dependence on an external cause. Since God is the Necessary Existent, He cannot be dependent. Read the passage carefully
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the structure: a claim, an explanation of the entailment, and a dismantling of two possibilities.
The argument is a reductio ad absurdum: assuming God is a body leads to a contradiction.
2
Identify the two possibilities

These correspond to the two 'إما' clauses.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi presents a rigorous argument demonstrating that God cannot be associated with spatial location. He builds on the foundational principle that what occupies space depends on space, while God is absolutely independent. In this passage, he offers three distinct proofs, each showing that God's association with space leads to contradictions.

First Proof: Dependence Implies Contingency
Imam Razi presents the first proof that location is impossible for Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first proof that Allah cannot depend on anything, because dependence implies contingency, which contradicts necessary existence.

Core Idea
Dependence on another makes a thing contingent, and what is contingent cannot be God.

Imam Razi begins his proofs for divine transcendence by establishing a basic logical principle: anything that depends on something else for its existence is contingent—it could possibly not exist. Since Allah is the Necessary Existent, He cannot have any such dependence. This short but powerful argument rules out the possibility that Allah occupies a location.
