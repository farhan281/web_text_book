Learning Objective

Core Idea
Adam's final state was known from the beginning — that is the true 'image.'

Imam Razi offers a profound reinterpretation of the well-known hadith about Adam being created on God's image. Rather than understanding it literally, he explains that it refers to the predetermined final state of each person — including their ultimate happiness or misery. This lesson unpacks those two interpretations and their theological significance.

Core Idea
A weak hadith does not override clear theological principles.

In this lesson, we encounter a hadith that appears to suggest that Adam was created in God's image. However, Imam Razi immediately clarifies that the hadith is weak, and even if accepted, it has two interpretations that protect divine transcendence. Let's examine how Ibn Khuzaymah, a rigorous scholar, dealt with this narration.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith and its context
Read the Arabic passage carefully, noting the wording 'خلق آدم على صورة الرحمن'. Understand that this is presented as a second report.
The hadith explicitly mentions 'image of al-Rahman'.
2
Note Ibn Khuzaymah's ruling
Identify that Ibn Khuzaymah declared this narration weak (da'if). This immediately reduces its theological weight.
Weakness in transmission means it cannot be used as proof for belief.
3
Understand the two interpretations
Study the two interpretations: (1) 'image' means attribute, (2) the ascription indicates honor. Both avoid any literal anthropomorphism.
These interpretations are consistent with the principle of tanzih.

Opponents cite the mutashabihat verse with a necessary stop.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how opponents use the verse on mutashabihat (3:7) with a necessary stop to argue that some Quranic meanings are known only to Allah.

Core Idea
The stop before 'illa Allah' is the hinge of the opponents' proof.

The opponents first invoke a key Quranic verse about ambiguous verses (mutashabihat). They argue that a specific grammatical stop in the verse proves that only Allah knows the interpretation. This lesson unpacks that argument and the contested stopping point.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text carefully, noting the verse citation and the claim about the necessary stop (الوقف لازم).
The phrase 'وسيأتي دليله' indicates the proof is deferred.
2
Understand the verse context
Recall that Surah Al 'Imran 3:7 divides Quranic verses into muhkamat (clear) and mutashabihat (ambiguous). The opponents focus on the latter part: 'and none knows its interpretation except Allah.'

Analyze the stop argument
The opponents claim a necessary stop after 'except Allah', meaning the sentence ends there. This implies humans cannot know the interpretation. Reflect on why this matters for theological debate.
The author hints that this stop's proof will come later, showing it is contested.

Allah Hayy: In its literal sense, it is a name for the growing, sensitive being. Metaphorically, it comes with meanings including: 1- If a person becomes severely ill, the severe illness is expressed metaphorically as death, like 'his heart died in his belly and became like a stone' (1 Sam. 25:27?), meaning severely ill. And if a person recovers from his illness, the recovery is expressed metaphorically as life, like 'and he revived from his illness' (Isa. 38:9). 2- Acquisition of knowledge and wisdom, like the saying of Wisdom in the book of Proverbs: 'Now therefore, O sons, listen to me. Blessed are those who keep my ways. Hear instruction and be wise, and do not refuse it. Blessed is the man who watches daily at my gates, waiting at the posts of my doors. For whoever finds me finds life, and obtains favor from the Lord; but whoever sins against me wrongs his own soul; all who hate me love death' (Prov. 8:32-36). That means wisdom is life for those who encounter it. And because wisdom is life metaphorically, correct opinions are expressed as life and corrupt opinions as death, like: 'See, I have set before you today life and good, death and evil' (Deut. 30:15). He clarified that good is life and evil is death. Yasma' Janah: In its literal sense, it is a name for the wing of a flying animal. Metaphorically, it is used in the sense of covering and concealment. 'The one whom you have come to take refuge under His wings' (Ruth 2:13) means: you have sought shelter under His covering.

Commentary
This passage is a lexical analysis of the terms 'hayy' (living) and 'janah' (wing) in scripture, presented by Imam Razi. For 'hayy', the literal meaning is the growing, sensitive being (i.e., sentient life). The author then explains two metaphorical uses. First, severe illness is called 'death' metaphorically, and recovery is called 'life', illustrated by biblical verses. Second, wisdom and knowledge are called 'life', as in Proverbs where wisdom promises life. This extends to correct opinions being 'life' and corrupt opinions 'death', supported by Deuteronomy. Then the term 'janah' is analyzed: literally it means a wing, but metaphorically it means covering or protection, as in Ruth seeking refuge under God's wings, interpreted as under His shelter. The passage demonstrates how scriptural language uses human concepts metaphorically to convey spiritual truths, avoiding anthropomorphism.

The Metaphorical Use of 'Janah' (Wing) in Scripture
Imam Razi explains the literal and metaphorical meanings of 'janah' with an example from Ruth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Arabic word 'janah' (wing) is used metaphorically in scripture to mean protection or shelter.

Core Idea
'Janah' (wing) metaphorically means divine shelter, not a physical wing.

Imam Razi turns to the term 'janah' (wing), which literally refers to the wing of a bird but is used metaphorically in scripture to denote covering and protection. He gives the example from the Book of Ruth to illustrate this. This lesson examines that metaphorical shift.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage for 'janah'
Read the Arabic text from 'يسمع جناح' to the end. Then read the English translation.
Notice the literal definition and the single metaphorical meaning.
2
Understand the metaphor
The literal meaning is the wing of a flying animal. The metaphorical meaning is covering or protection, as in 'under His wings' meaning under His shelter.
3
Apply to avoid anthropomorphism
This reinterpretation prevents the belief that God has literal wings, showing that scriptural language is figurative.
This is a key point in kalam to avoid likening God to creation.
