Dividing Existents: The Eternal and the Originated
The author divides existents into eternal and originated and explains true eternity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between absolute eternity (Allah) and relative priority (creatures) and why Allah's precedence must be without limit.

Core Idea
True eternity means having no beginning; only Allah is eternal in that sense.

Before discussing Allah's attributes, we must clarify what 'eternal' truly means. The word can be used loosely for anything old, but in theology it has a precise meaning: that which has no beginning. This lesson unpacks that definition and shows why it applies only to Allah.

Guided Path

The passage explains that 'qadim' can be used relatively (like an ancient building) or absolutely (Allah). Distinguish these two meanings.
Relative 'qadim' has a beginning but is old relative to something else—this is actually 'muhdath' in absolute terms.
3
Analyze the proof against finite precedence for Allah
The author argues that if Allah were before the world by a finite time, that would imply He was non-existent before that time—impossible for the Eternal.
This logical argument shows why Allah's precedence must be infinite.

Apply the distinction

The Object of Power and the Proof of Accidents
Baqillani argues from human and divine power to further prove accidents.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Power over motion requires an accident as its object, not the body.

Baqillani continues his proof of accidents by analyzing the nature of power (qudra). He notes that both human and divine power have objects, and then enumerates the possibilities for what that object could be when a body moves. By eliminating each possibility except one, he concludes that the object must be an accident, thereby proving accidents exist.

Commentary
This passage extends the proof of accidents by examining the nature of power (qudra). Baqillani notes a known fact: a human sometimes has power to move and sometimes lacks it, and this power must have an object (maqdūr). The same applies to God, who according to monotheists and even atheists who deny accidents can move and still bodies. Baqillani then enumerates the possibilities for what the object of power might be when moving a body: either bringing the body into existence, producing a meaning in it, annihilating the body, annihilating a meaning from it, or something undefined. He rules out the possibility that power has no object, using an analogy with knowledge. He then argues that the object of power cannot be the origination of the body itself, because the body is already existing when it moves; rather, the object is the accident (movement). Finally, he addresses an objection: what if the object of power is the annihilation of a meaning from the body? He replies that such a meaning must be either a body or an accident. If it is a body, then the opponent admits the possibility of a body's non-existence, which implies the body's temporal origination (since the eternal cannot cease). Thus, the argument concludes that accidents are necessary to explain change, and their existence proves that bodies are temporally originated (ḥādith).

Commentary

Similarly, the term of life is the duration Allah knows he will live, and it cannot be increased or decreased. This establishes the Sunni view that all life spans are fixed by God, and even the slain dies at his fixed term.

Proof That God Can Enable Faith: Rational and Quranic Evidence
Baqillani presents a rational and Quranic proof that God can enable faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational and Quranic arguments used by Baqillani to prove that God has the power to enable faith in human beings.

Core Idea
God's power to enable faith is proven by His ability to enable disbelief.

This passage from Baqillani's Tamhid al-Awa'il addresses a theological objection: How can we know that God is able to make people believe? The answer combines a rational argument based on the nature of ability and action, with two Quranic verses that show God withholding blessings to prevent unbelief. This lesson unpacks that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its literal translation to get a sense of the overall argument. Note the objection and the two-part response.
2

Look for the logical structure: God can enable faith because He can enable its opposite (disbelief), and if He created the ability for faith, faith would inevitably occur. Understand the principle that an act necessarily occurs when the ability exists.
This is a key principle in kalam.
3

Read the two Quranic verses cited and see how they serve as evidence. They show that God withholds certain blessings to prevent disbelief and transgression, implying He could also grant ability to produce faith.
The verses are from Surah Az-Zukhruf and Surah Ash-Shura.

Second Proof from Surah Yunus and Defending Divine Ability
Baqillani uses Q. 10:99 and answers objections from Q. 2:145.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani uses Q. 10:99 to prove God's ability to guide all, and how he refutes objections from Q. 2:145.

Core Idea
Quranic proofs for God's power can withstand objections through precise interpretation.

In this passage, Baqillani presents a second Quranic argument for divine omnipotence. He cites the verse that if God willed, all would believe, and then addresses two objections that might seem to limit God's power. The first objection misunderstands the nature of God's ability, and the second misapplies a verse about the Prophet's specific situation.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof from Q. 10:99
Read the Arabic passage and translation. Note how Baqillani infers God's capability from the conditional statement: if He willed, all would believe.
The verse does not say God willed, but that if He had willed, they would believe.
2
Analyze the first objection and response
The objectors claim the verse means God can make them believe unwillingly. Baqillani responds by analogy with provision: just as God can cause misguidance unwillingly, He can cause belief unwillingly, but the point is ability.
The key is that the verse establishes capability, not the mode of belief.
3

The objectors cite Q. 2:145 where God tells the Prophet that even if he brings every sign, the People of the Book will not follow his direction. Baqillani responds by restricting the verse: it only applies to the Prophet's own efforts, not to God's direct action.
Thus the verse does not negate God's ability to guide them through other means.

Reward is Grace, Not Obligation
Baqillani refutes Mu'tazila reward obligation by analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani uses analogies from human social relations to argue that reward from God is a matter of grace, not rational obligation.

Core Idea
Reward is grace, not debt: God's blessings outweigh any service.

In this passage, Imam Baqillani takes on a key Mu'tazila claim: that God is rationally obliged to reward the obedient. Using clear analogies from everyday life, he shows that if a human master is not obliged to reward a slave whose service is already compensated by the master's provision, then how much more does God's infinite grace surpass our worship? This lesson walks through his argument and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the rhetorical style: 'So we compel them...'
2
Identify the analogies
Baqillani presents two analogies: master-slave and honored father-son. In each case, the subordinate does not deserve additional reward because their service is already outweighed by the superior's provision.
The analogies are meant to be intuitive.
3
Apply the logic to God
Notice how Baqillani extends the analogy to the divine realm: God's blessings are infinite, human worship is negligible, and worship itself is a form of gratitude, not a basis for deserving reward.
This leads to the conclusion that reward is by grace, not obligation.
