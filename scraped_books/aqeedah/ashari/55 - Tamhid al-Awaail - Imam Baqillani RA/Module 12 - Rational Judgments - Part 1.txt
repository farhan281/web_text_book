The Five Senses as Sources of Necessary Knowledge
Baqillani enumerates the five senses as sources of necessary knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the five senses as sources of necessary knowledge, including the distinction between sense perception and the physical organs.

Core Idea
The senses give necessary knowledge, and 'sense' means perception, not the physical organ.

Baqillani begins a new chapter on the sources of knowledge. He answers a key question: from how many ways does necessary knowledge come? He identifies six ways, among them the five senses. But he carefully defines what he means by 'sense' to avoid confusion. This passage lays the foundation for understanding how we acquire certain knowledge through sensory perception.

Commentary
This passage opens a chapter on the sources of knowledge (madaarik al-ilm). It begins with a question: from how many ways does knowledge occur when it is necessary? The answer: from six ways. Among them are the five senses: sight, hearing, taste, smell, touch. The author specifies that by 'sense' he means the perception (idrak) found through the senses, not the physical organs (like eye, nose, etc.). So any knowledge obtained through one of these senses is necessary knowledge that compels the soul in a way that no doubt is possible regarding the perceived object. Each sense, at the present time according to the usual course of nature, is specific to perceiving a certain type or types of objects.

The Sixth Type of Necessary Knowledge: Direct Inner Awareness
Baqillani enumerates six examples of the sixth type of necessary knowledge.

Core Idea
Necessary knowledge is not only from the senses; it also springs directly from the soul.

Baqillani completes his classification of necessary knowledge by describing a type that does not depend on the five senses. This knowledge arises directly in the soul through innate awareness, self-reflection, logical necessity, and tawātur reports. In this lesson, we explore the six examples he provides.

And from it (7) also is knowledge that actually occurs concerning the speaker's intention of what he intends by his speech to the exclusion of other things, and that he intends to acquire the kinds of what he can do from speech and other things. And from it also (1) is knowledge that bodies, when they exist, must be either joined, with parts touching, or separated, distinct; and that a report about the existence of a thing and that it has some attributes must be either true or false; and that two contradictory reports about the same subject cannot both be true or both false; and what is like that from matters that are divided in the mind into two [alternatives] with no middle term. And from it also is knowledge of the shame of the bashful and the fear of the fearful, and knowledge of courage and cowardice, piety and impiety, greeting and mockery, occurring upon observing indications. And from it also is knowledge invented in the soul by the tawātur (massively transmitted) report about something and its widespread existence, like the knowledge that occurs upon (10) the reports of reporters about China, Khurāsān, Fārs (11), and Kirmān, and about the appearance of Moses, Jesus, and Muhammad (peace be upon him) (13) and upon all the prophets.

(2) Know that the senses have perceptions of intelligible, absent meanings that are perceived through intermediaries. The meaning of their perception of intelligibles through intermediaries is that they reflect on the states of sensibles and compare some to others, then become aware of correspondences and differences between them, thus perceiving universal meanings in them and judging the relations of some to others, then proceed from them to another place, then continue thus until they complete their essence according to their effort and effort, their striving and striving. It is transmitted from them that they said: 'There is no path to knowledge except the senses,' and therefore they also denied the benefit of tawātur reports. And some philosophers in theology, like Aristotle, said: 'It is not possible to obtain certainty in divine inquiries.'

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration and explanation of the sixth type of necessary knowledge (al-ḍarb al-sādis min al-‘ulūm al-ḍarūriyya). Baqillani classifies this as knowledge that arises in the soul directly, not through the five senses. He gives several examples: (1) self-knowledge and awareness of one's own states (health, sickness, emotions, abilities, etc.); (2) knowledge of the speaker's intention from his speech—i.e., we know when someone speaks that they intend to communicate something specific; (3) knowledge of necessary logical and physical dichotomies, such as that bodies must be either together or separate, reports must be true or false, contradictory reports cannot both be true or false—these are self-evident rational judgments; (4) knowledge of psychological states from outward signs, like shame in a bashful person or fear in a fearful one; (5) knowledge from tawātur (massively transmitted reports), which yields certainty about historical facts like the existence of China or the prophets. The long footnote (2) elaborates on the role of the senses: the senses can perceive intelligible meanings through intermediaries by reflection and comparison, and this process is a cause for knowledge. It then refutes the materialists and the Samaniyya who claimed only sense-perception yields knowledge, denying even tawātur and rational certainty. It also mentions that some philosophers like Aristotle denied certainty in theology. The footnote thus defends the possibility of knowledge beyond the senses, including rational and transmitted knowledge, which is central to the Ash‘ari epistemological project.

Baqillani refutes materialist and skeptical objections to non-sensory knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani refutes the claim that only sense-perception yields knowledge and defends the validity of rational and transmitted knowledge.

Core Idea
Not all knowledge comes from the senses; reason and tawātur are valid sources of certainty.

He explains how the senses themselves can lead to intelligible knowledge through reflection, and he criticizes those who reject tawātur and rational certainty, including Aristotle's skepticism in theology.

Divisions of Known Things: Existent and Non-Existent
Baqillani classifies all known things into existent and non-existent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's classification of all known things into existent and non-existent, including the four types of non-existent and the linguistic basis for equating 'thing' with existence.

Core Idea
All known things are either existent or non-existent — and non-existent has many shades.

Baqillani begins his discussion of epistemology by laying out the fundamental division of everything that can be known. He argues that 'thing' (shay') is synonymous with existence in Arabic usage, and then distinguishes between what exists and what does not, offering a nuanced categorization of non-existents. This passage establishes the conceptual framework for all subsequent theological reasoning.

Proof of Temporal Nature from Dependence on Accidents
Baqillani proves bodies are temporal because they cannot precede accidents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's rational proof that bodies are temporal (muhdath) because they cannot precede accidents.

Core Idea
Bodies cannot precede accidents; therefore they are temporal.

This lesson presents a foundational argument from Imam al-Baqillani demonstrating the temporal origination of bodies. The argument hinges on the necessary relation between bodies and accidents: a body always exists in one of two states—its parts either contiguous or separated—both of which are accidents. Since bodies cannot precede the accidents that inhere in them, and accidents are temporally originated, bodies must be temporal as well.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own argument flow.
Notice that the argument has a clear logical structure: axiom → implication → conclusion.
2
Identify the two states
The passage states that a body must be either with parts contiguous (muttasil) or separated (mutafarriq). There is no third possibility. Recognize that both are accidents.
This is an exhaustive dichotomy based on necessary knowledge.
3
Understand the logical implication
Because the body cannot exist without one of these two states (both accidents), it cannot precede accidents. If accidents are temporal, then the body must also be temporal.
The key is: whatever cannot exist without something temporal is itself temporal.
4
Apply the argument
Consider how this argument refutes the eternity of the physical world. If the world were eternal, it would have to exist without accidents at some point, which is impossible.
This proof is a cornerstone of kalam cosmology.

The Parity Proof for the World's Origination
Baqillani enumerates the parity argument to prove the world's temporality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani uses the parity of numbers to prove that the series of past events must be finite and therefore the world is originated.

Core Idea
An infinite past would force a number to be both even and odd—a logical impossibility.

Baqillani presents a crisp rational proof that the world must have a beginning. He argues that if there were an infinite number of past events, their count would have to be either even or odd, leading to contradictions. This lesson unpacks that argument step by step.

The phrase 'لا نهاية له' means 'without end' or 'infinite'.
2

Focus on why the number cannot be only even or only odd: each can become the other by adding or subtracting one, which is impossible for an infinite set to lack.

3
Connect to the world's origination
Understand that since the infinite series is impossible, the past must have a beginning. Therefore, the world, which is not free of events (hawadith), must itself be originated.
The argument does not directly prove a Creator, only that the world began.

The Logical Proof from the Sun's Movement
Baqillani presents a logical argument from the sun's movement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's logical argument that the movement of the sun from constellation to constellation demonstrates the contingency of celestial bodies.

Core Idea
If the sun's positions were eternal, it would have to be in two places at once.

Having refuted external sources of knowledge, Baqillani now presents a direct logical argument. He observes that the sun's being in one constellation contradicts its being in another. If its positions were eternal, it would have to be simultaneously in two incompatible states—an impossibility. This proves the sun's movements are contingent, and since the celestial bodies never exist without movement, they themselves must be contingent.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. Notice the conditional structure: 'if... then...'
2
Identify the key premise
The key premise is that the sun's being in one constellation (e.g., Aries) is incompatible with being in another (e.g., Taurus) at the same time.
This is an obvious impossibility.
3
Follow the reductio
If the sun's positions were eternal (unchanging), it would have to be in all constellations at once, which is impossible. Therefore, its positions cannot be eternal; they must be temporally originated.
4
Apply to the bodies themselves
Since the celestial bodies never exist without such movements, they themselves are contingent.
Baqillani also references a prior refutation of the possibility of an infinite past of events.

Baqillani presents a dilemma challenging dualist inference from motion to nature.

Motion and rest both indicate nature; if motion proves difference, rest proves similarity.

This puts the dualist in an impossible position.

Commentary
This passage continues the dialectical argument, applying the same principle of conditional rational judgment to two new cases: causing pain to animals and apostasy under duress. First, Baqillani anticipates an opponent who concedes that actions like eating and drinking are permissible with need and prohibited without need, but might hesitate to apply the same logic to causing pain to animals. Baqillani asserts that the same rule applies. He then introduces a more challenging scenario: a person threatened with death by atheists unless he blasphemes and insults God. The question is: what is obligatory? If the opponents say blasphemy is obligatory to save one's life, Baqillani points out that this makes the rationally prohibited (blasphemy) become permissible, contradicting their earlier stance. If they say he must not blaspheme even at the cost of his life, then allowing oneself to be killed becomes permissible, which also overturns a former prohibition. Baqillani then presents a further subtlety: if they claim that uttering blasphemy is preferable, he counters that perhaps forbearance leading to death is even better because it avoids insulting God and the person is not directly killing himself. If they then say forbearance is preferable, Baqillani argues that uttering the word of disbelief could be better if the person does not internally accept disbelief, knowing that God knows his true faith, and that his life is valuable while God is not harmed by outward utterances. By not speaking, he would cause his own death, which is prohibited and transgresses against God's property (his life). The opponents are left with no answer. Finally, Baqillani considers their possible responses: they might say self-destruction is prohibited unless it avoids disbelief, or disbelief is prohibited unless it avoids self-destruction. Either way, Baqillani concludes that the same conditional logic applies, and the same principle holds for causing pain to animals: it is prohibited without need but permissible out of necessity.

If a person does not hold that belief, and lives in a society that does not value truth over falsehood, then he is indifferent and may choose either. Baqillani gives analogies: with two equally useful dirhams, or with equally effective speech and silence, or with either hand, a person is free to choose because there is no preference. The rationalists' argument thus assumes what it tries to prove—that truth is preferable—and fails to show that reason apprehends an objective moral quality. Their proof collapses when we consider scenarios where such beliefs are absent.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Brahmana (a group of Indian rationalists) who claim that reason alone is sufficient for attaining all moral and practical knowledge. The author, Imam al-Baqillani, presents a two-part argument: first, a logical proof that moral obligations cannot be known by reason because it leads to circularity; second, a practical argument that even mundane knowledge like distinguishing food from poison requires experience and divine guidance.

The argument proceeds as follows: The Brahmana initially argued that the goodness of an action (like truthfulness) indicates its obligation. But al-Baqillani shows this leads to a vicious circle: we cannot know the obligation of truthfulness without knowing its goodness, and we cannot know its goodness without knowing its obligation. This circularity makes both impossible to know through reason alone. He illustrates with an analogy: if someone says he cannot know Zayd is in the house without knowing Amr is there, and cannot know Amr is there without knowing Zayd is there, then he can never know either. Therefore, knowledge of moral categories (obligation, prohibition, permission) cannot be derived from rational judgments; it requires divine revelation (sam'). This revelation also provides knowledge of God and the consequences of actions.

Having established that reason fails for moral knowledge, al-Baqillani then challenges the Brahmana on practical knowledge. He asks how humans originally knew which substances are food, medicine, or poison. They did not have innate rational knowledge of this; they had to learn by experience. But this casts doubt on divine wisdom: if God could have directly taught humans these distinctions, why allow them to suffer and die through trial and error? This would be inconsistent with the wisdom attributed to God. The Brahmana might counter that they learned from animals, but al-Baqillani responds that killing animals for experimentation is also morally problematic (ugly) according to their own principles. So the argument concludes that even practical knowledge ultimately points to the necessity of divine guidance.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Brahmana (a group of Indian rationalists) who claim that reason alone is sufficient for attaining all moral and practical knowledge. The author, Imam al-Baqillani, presents a two-part argument: first, a logical proof that moral obligations cannot be known by reason because it leads to circularity; second, a practical argument that even mundane knowledge like distinguishing food from poison requires experience and divine guidance.

The argument proceeds as follows: The Brahmana initially argued that the goodness of an action (like truthfulness) indicates its obligation. But al-Baqillani shows this leads to a vicious circle: we cannot know the obligation of truthfulness without knowing its goodness, and we cannot know its goodness without knowing its obligation. This circularity makes both impossible to know through reason alone. He illustrates with an analogy: if someone says he cannot know Zayd is in the house without knowing Amr is there, and cannot know Amr is there without knowing Zayd is there, then he can never know either. Therefore, knowledge of moral categories (obligation, prohibition, permission) cannot be derived from rational judgments; it requires divine revelation (sam'). This revelation also provides knowledge of God and the consequences of actions.

Having established that reason fails for moral knowledge, al-Baqillani then challenges the Brahmana on practical knowledge. He asks how humans originally knew which substances are food, medicine, or poison. They did not have innate rational knowledge of this; they had to learn by experience. But this casts doubt on divine wisdom: if God could have directly taught humans these distinctions, why allow them to suffer and die through trial and error? This would be inconsistent with the wisdom attributed to God. The Brahmana might counter that they learned from animals, but al-Baqillani responds that killing animals for experimentation is also morally problematic (ugly) according to their own principles. So the argument concludes that even practical knowledge ultimately points to the necessity of divine guidance.

Refutation of Rational Objection Against Seeing God
Baqillani refutes the rational objection by asserting that intellects permit seeing God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Baqillani refutes the rational objection that reason disproves the possibility of seeing God, by affirming that reason actually permits it.

Core Idea
Reason does not disprove seeing God; it permits it.

In this lesson, we encounter a classic objection from rationalist theologians: they claim that reason itself proves the impossibility of seeing God. Baqillani responds by flipping the argument on its head, asserting that in the Ash'ari view, reason actually permits vision of God and invalidates the opponents' claim. This short but dense exchange sets the stage for a deeper discussion of the rational and revealed evidence.

The Certainty of Non-Existence Despite God's Power
Baqillani refutes an argument that we cannot be certain of non-existence by giving examples of God's power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani defends the concept of necessary knowledge of non-existence against the objection that God's power makes everything possible.

Core Idea
We know with certainty that some things do not exist without limiting God's power.

Baqillani addresses a common objection: if God can do anything, how can we be sure something does not exist? He responds that we have necessary knowledge (علم ضروري) that certain things have not occurred, even though they are possible. This lesson unpacks his refutation and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the objection
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how the objection is introduced: 'If we were not compelled to know that does not exist, we would allow it.'
The objection assumes that without necessary knowledge, we would allow anything.
2
List the examples of necessary knowledge
Identify each example Baqillani gives of things we know necessarily do not exist, even though they are possible. Examples include a human without parents, a horse without breeding, etc.
These examples show that we have certainty about specific non-existences.
3
Understand the conclusion
Notice how the argument ends: 'So what you asked about is invalidated.' Baqillani concludes that the objection fails because we do have necessary knowledge.

This passage is a rebuttal to an objection about necessary knowledge of non-existence. Baqillani responds that we do have necessary knowledge of specific non-existences, so the objection is not valid.
