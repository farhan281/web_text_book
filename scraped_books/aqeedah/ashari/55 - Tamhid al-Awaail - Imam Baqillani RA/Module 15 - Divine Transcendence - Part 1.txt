The Creator's Incomparability: Genus and Form
Baqillani argues that the Creator cannot be similar to contingent beings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Creator cannot resemble His creation in genus or in form, and the implications of this for divine transcendence.

Core Idea
God is absolutely unique—resemblance to creation would make Him contingent.

Imam Baqillani begins this chapter by establishing a fundamental principle of Islamic theology: God’s total incomparability to His creation. He argues that any supposed resemblance would either be in genus or in form, and both possibilities lead to absurd consequences. This lesson unpacks that argument and its logical structure.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing it. Then read the translation. Let Baqillani's own argument flow through before you interpret.
Notice that the argument is structured as a conditional: 'if He resembled them...'
2
Identify the two alternatives
The passage presents two ways resemblance could occur: in genus or in form. Find where these are introduced and note how Baqillani defines each.
Resemblance in genus means sharing the same category; resemblance in form means sharing a specific shape or composition.
3
Understand the argument against genus
Focus on the reasoning: if the Creator resembled creation in genus, then both would be of the same kind, making the Creator contingent and creation eternal. Grasp the proof using 'substitutability' (sadd aḥaduhumā masadd ṣāḥibihī).
Think of two blacknesses: they are interchangeable in sight. That shows they are of the same genus.
4
Understand the argument against form
If resemblance were in form, the Creator would not be one thing but composed, requiring a form-giver and making Him contingent. This leads to an infinite regress.
Baqillani references an earlier argument: 'form only occurs from a form-giver.'
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Both alternatives are impossible, so the Creator cannot resemble His creation. This establishes tanzīh (divine transcendence).
This is a cornerstone of Islamic theology: God is unlike anything in creation.

Chapter: That it is not permissible for the Maker of contingent things to resemble them. And it is not permissible for the Maker of contingent things to resemble them, because if He resembled them, He would inevitably either resemble them in genus or in form. Commentary
This chapter establishes divine transcendence (tanzīh). Baqillani argues that the Creator cannot resemble any created being. If He did, the resemblance would be either in genus or in form. Resemblance in genus would imply that both are of the same kind, making the Creator contingent and the creation eternal like Him—but that is impossible. Resemblance in form would mean the Creator has a form, which would require a maker itself, leading to an infinite regress, and would also make Him composed and contingent. Thus, the Creator is absolutely unique and unlike anything in creation. The argument uses the logical principle that two things that are substitutable are essentially the same, which cannot apply to God and the world.

The Creator’s Unlikeness to Creation
Baqillani proves the Creator cannot be similar to creation in genus or form.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani’s logical argument that the Creator cannot resemble originated things in any way, whether in genus or form.

Core Idea
If the Creator resembled creation, absurdities would follow.

Baqillani presents a rigorous logical argument that the Creator of the universe cannot be similar to His creation in any way. Using a simple dichotomy (genus or form), he shows that either the Creator would be originated or creation would be eternal—both impossible. This lesson walks through his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the question
Start with the key claim: the Creator cannot resemble originated things. Ask yourself why this matters for Islamic theology.
This is about divine transcendence (tanzih).
2
Follow the dichotomy
Baqillani splits the possibility of resemblance into two exclusive options: resemblance in genus or resemblance in form. Notice that no third option is considered.
This is a classic proof by contradiction.
3
Examine the first branch
If the Creator resembled creation in genus, then either the Creator would be originated (since genus implies temporality) or creation would be eternal (since the Creator is eternal). Both are false.
The example of similar blacknesses illustrates that two things of the same genus are interchangeable.
4
Examine the second branch
If the Creator resembled creation in form, then He would be composite and require a designer (musawwir), which leads to an infinite regress or origination.
Form implies composition and need for a maker.
5
Conclude the argument
Since both branches lead to impossibilities, the original assumption that the Creator resembles creation is false. The Creator must be utterly unlike anything in creation.
This establishes tanzih as a rational necessity.

If in form and composition, the creator would require a maker (musawwir) and would be a composite substance, hence originated. Both possibilities are impossible, so the creator must be utterly unlike creation.

God's Creation Is Not for a Purpose
Baqillani rejects purpose in divine creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Baqillani argues that God's creation of the world is not motivated by any purpose or inducement.

Core Idea
Purposes imply need, which cannot apply to the Eternal God.

This passage presents a theological objection to the idea that God created the world for a purpose. Baqillani responds by showing that purposes and motives are only relevant to beings with needs, who can benefit or be harmed. Since God is eternal and all-knowing, He is free from such limitations.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Start by reading the questioner's objection carefully. It lists several possible reasons for God's action: inducement, mover, motivator, purpose, or idea.
Notice the structure: the questioner presents a dichotomy—either for something or for nothing.
2
Identify Baqillani's response
Baqillani first seems to concede that God created for something, but then he provides a proof that such reasons cannot apply to God. Read the proof slowly.
The concession may be rhetorical to set up the argument.
3
Understand the proof's two parts
The proof has two main points: (1) Purposes and motives indicate need and temporality, (2) Motives only affect those who are heedless, ignorant, or fear/hope for outcomes. Both are impossible for God.
The passage cuts off, but the conclusion is clear.
4
Reflect on the implication
Ask yourself: What does this argument say about God's attributes? It emphasizes God's self-sufficiency, eternality, and omniscience.

Setting Up the Objection: Is God's Creation Purposeful?
Baqillani presents the objection and initial affirmation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the hypothetical objection that God's creation must have a motive or purpose, and Baqillani's initial response that God did create for something.

Core Idea
The objector assumes that creation must be driven by a motive or purpose.

Baqillani begins by presenting a hypothetical objector who questions whether the Maker of the world created it due to a motive, mover, or purpose. The initial answer is surprising: Baqillani affirms that God created for something. This sets the stage for the deeper argument that such purposes are actually impossible for God.

The objector asks for evidence, which Baqillani will provide. The lesson ends with that demand.

Chapter: That God's making of the world is not for a purpose.
If someone says: Do you say that the Maker of the world made it after not making it due to a motive that called Him to His act, a mover that moved Him, a sender that sent Him, a purpose that disturbed Him, and a thought that necessitated the occurrence of originated things from Him, or did He make it not for any of what I asked about? It is said to him: He, exalted, made the world for something of what you asked about. If it is said: What is the evidence for that? It is said: The evidence for it is that disturbing motives, thoughts, and purposes only occur and are permissible for one who has need, from whom it is possible to seek benefits and repel harms. That is a matter only permissible for one whom pains and pleasures, natural inclination and aversion can befall; all of that is evidence for the originatedness of the one described by it and his need for it, and it is negated of the Eternal, exalted. Likewise, disturbing, moving, sending causes for actions only move the heedless, awaken the ignorant, and occur to the fearful and hopeful who fear harm from leaving actions by bringing about benefit and advantage. And God is exalted above that because He knows what will be before it is and what outcomes of matters will end up, and He knows the secret and what is more hidden. It is not permissible for one with this description that a matter should occur to his mind like someone who was not knowledgeable of it, nor that motives and senders should impel him to his actions. And that is because when we say to one who acts among us: What called you to the action and moved you to perform it, and what is the purpose in it rather than another, and in doing it rather than leaving it and turning away from it? We only ask him to tell us: Did you intend by that to bring a benefit or repel a harm, or not? If he said: I did it neither for gaining benefit nor for repelling harm, while knowing its occurrence, judgment must be passed on his foolishness, because he is one who needs to draw benefits and repel harms and is commanded to do so, and his performing the action devoid of intention for that and pursuit of it is foolishness and contrary to what is obligatory upon him. But the Eternal, exalted, is not a being of need nor one for whom compliance and obedience are obligatory. So it is not permissible that He be compared to one who acts among us.

Commentary
This passage is a theological refutation of the claim that God created the world for a purpose (gharaḍ). Baqillani structures it as an objection-and-answer. The objector asks whether God created due to a motive (dā‘ī), mover (muḥarrik), sender (bā‘ith), purpose (gharaḍ), or thought (khāṭir). The answer is that He did create for something of those, but then the objector demands evidence. The evidence is that motives, thoughts, and purposes only apply to beings with need (dhū ḥāja) — beings who can seek benefit (manfa‘a) and repel harm (ḍarar). Such beings are subject to pain, pleasure, inclination, and aversion, all of which indicate originatedness (ḥudūth) and need. These are negated of the Eternal. Moreover, such causes only affect the heedless (ghāfil), ignorant (jāhil), fearful (khā’if), and hopeful (rājī). God, however, is knowing of all things before they occur and is not subject to these. When we ask a human agent why he acted, we are asking whether he sought benefit or avoided harm. If he did an act without any such purpose, he would be considered foolish (safīh) because he is in need of benefits and commanded to pursue them. But God has no such need and is not commanded. Therefore, God cannot be compared to human agents in this regard.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Baqillani presents a logical dilemma to opponents who claim that doubt (shakk), thought, and punishment are originated. Refuting Bada' from Afflictions on the Innocent
Baqillani refutes the claim that affliction of innocents implies bada'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani refutes the claim that God's infliction of pain on children, animals, and the insane implies bada' (change in divine will).

Core Idea
Affliction of the innocent does not imply bada'; it serves a benefit, just as varied commands do.

In this passage, Baqillani presents a second argument against those who claim that God's changing commands implies bada'. He addresses the objection that God afflicts innocent beings with pain and harm. The opponent must either accept retribution (which leads to metempsychosis) or acknowledge benefit (which parallels the variation in commands). This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation; notice the two conditional arguments the author presents to the opponent.
The argument is structured as a dilemma.
2
Identify the two alternatives
The opponent is given two options: either afflictions are retribution or they are purely for benefit. See what each entails.
Retribution leads to metempsychosis; benefit leads to analogy with commands.
3
Understand the conclusion
The author shows that both alternatives fail to prove bada'. The benefit option directly parallels the variation of commands, which the opponent rejects as implying bada'.

Refutation of Bada' from Changes in Inanimate Objects
Baqillani refutes the claim that God's changes in inanimate objects imply bada'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani uses the example of opposite changes in inanimate objects to refute the doctrine of bada' (change of mind in God).

Core Idea
God's changes in creation reflect wisdom, not bada'.

This lesson continues Baqillani's cumulative argument against bada'. He now turns to the natural world, listing a series of opposite transformations that occur in inanimate objects—such as breaking after composing, or moistening after drying. He then forces the opponent into a dilemma: either these changes imply bada' (which is heretical) or they are for benefit at different times, which then supports the possibility of abrogation in divine commands.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the list of opposite changes (e.g., breaking after composing, making square after hexagonal) and the question that follows.
Pay attention to the rhythm of the questioning—it is meant to corner the opponent.
2
Identify the dilemma
Baqillani presents an either-or question: does God's performing these opposite actions imply bada' or not? Recognize that this is a forced choice with no neutral ground.
3
Analyze the opponent's potential escape
The opponent says 'no bada'' but claims the changes are for benefit at different times. Understand that this is an attempt to avoid the first horn of the dilemma.
Baqillani counters that the same logic applies to commands: God can command one thing at one time and prohibit it at another for the same reason.
4
See how Baqillani closes the argument
Notice that Baqillani declares the opponent has no answer to this analogy. This completes his proof that varying commands do not imply bada'.
This lesson ties together the previous arguments about abrogation and human commands.

First Objection: Why God Cannot Be a Body
Baqillani answers the objection that the Eternal could be a body by defining body as composite

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why classical kalam theologians argued that God cannot be a body, based on the definition of body as essentially composed and aggregated.

Core Idea
If body means composite, then the simple Eternal cannot be a body.

Baqillani responds to an objection from the Anthropomorphists (al-Mujassima) who claimed that the Eternal could be a body. He does not simply deny; he provides a linguistic and conceptual argument. First, he establishes the true definition of 'body' (jism) as that which is composed and aggregated. Then, since God is one and not composed, He cannot be a body. This lesson examines that argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the full translation. Notice the structure: objection, answer with definition, linguistic proof, conclusion.
The passage is a single continuous argument.
2
Understand the objection
The objector asks: Why do you deny that the Eternal could be a body? This reflects the belief of the Mujassima that God has a body. Baqillani takes it seriously.
The objection is not dismissed; it is answered with evidence.
3
Analyze the definition of body
Baqillani claims that the true reality (haqiqa) of body is that it is composed (mu'allaf) and aggregated (mujtami'). He supports this with linguistic evidence: the words 'jaseem' and 'ajsam' refer to composition, not other attributes.
Focus on the phrase 'حقيقة الجسم أنه مؤلَّفٌ مُجتمع'.
4
Connect definition to conclusion
If body is defined by composition, then anything that is not composed cannot be a body. Since the Eternal is one and not composed (not aggregated), He cannot be a body.
The conclusion follows necessarily from the definition.
5
Reflect on the method
Notice Baqillani uses linguistic analysis to establish a theological truth. This is a distinctive method of kalam: using language to define terms before arguing.
Consider how this differs from simply citing a scriptural text.

باب الكلام على المجسمة. إن قال قائل : لم أنكرتم أن يكون القديم سبحانه جسماً؟ قيل له : لِمَا قَدّمناه مِنْ قَبْلُ وهو أن حقيقة الجسم أنه مؤلَّفٌ مُجتمع بدليل قولهم : رجل جسيم وزيد أجسم من عمرو وعلماً بأنهم يقصُرون هذه المبالغة على ضرب من ضروب التأليف في جهة العرض والطول ولا يوقعونها بزيادة شيء من صفات الجسم سوى التأليف فلما لم يجز يكون القديم مجتمعاً مؤتلفاً وكان شيئاً واحداً ثبت أنه تعالى ليس بجسم.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. The objector asks: Why do you (theologians) deny that God (the Eternal) could be a body? The answer begins with a previously established principle: the reality (haqiqa) of 'body' (jism) is that it is composed (mu'allaf) and aggregated (mujtami'). The proof for this definition is linguistic: Arabs say 'rajul jaseem' (a bulky man) and 'Zayd ajsam min Amr' (Zayd is more corporeal than Amr). The superlative form 'ajsam' indicates a degree of composition, not an increase in other bodily attributes like color or weight. Thus, 'body' essentially means something composed of parts. Since the Eternal is one (single, not composed), He cannot be a body. The argument follows: if composition is the defining characteristic of body, and God is not composite, then God is not a body.

Why the Eternal Cannot Be Composite: First Reason
Baqillani gives the first reason why the Eternal cannot be composite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's first argument that if God were composed of parts, He would necessarily have a location and occupy space, which is incompatible with divine transcendence.

Core Idea
Composition implies location, and location is impossible for the Eternal.

The objectors ask why the Eternal cannot be made of parts. Baqillani begins his answer with several reasons. The passage we have gives the first reason: composition would entail that God has a place and occupies space, which implies limitation. This lesson explores that argument and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the structure: objection, then the answer begins with 'from several aspects' (min wujuh).
The answer is incomplete in this passage, but the first aspect is fully given.
2
Identify the key claim
The first reason is that composition would necessitate God being 'possessor of a place' (dha hayyiz) and 'occupation in existence' (shughl). Understand why these imply limitation.
Place and occupation are properties of physical objects, not the Creator.
3
Analyze the argument about mutual touching
Baqillani adds that if God were composed, each part would touch adjacent parts, but it would be impossible for a part to touch two different things from the same direction. This shows composition imposes restrictions.
This is based on the principle of mutual contact (tamass).

Think about how this argument preserves divine transcendence (tanzih). Why did Baqillani present this as the first of several reasons?
This connects to the broader theme of God's uniqueness.

This leads to a dilemma: either only one part is the living, knowing, powerful deity (making worship due to that part alone, which is kufr) or all parts are such (leading to multiple independent deities). The argument is incomplete in the manuscript but the logic is clear.

Refuting the Corporealist Analogy
Baqillani refutes the corporealist analogy with a dilemma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Baqillani refutes the claim that God must be a body by showing that the analogical argument from witnessed agents would force unacceptable attributes onto God, thus proving too much.

Core Idea
Analogy from the created to the Creator proves too much—it proves nothing.

How can we know that God is not a body when every agent we see in the world is embodied? The opponents of tawhid use this very observation to argue that the Creator must also be a body. In this passage, Imam al-Baqillani turns their own reasoning against them, exposing its fatal flaw. Read carefully to see how a simple logical twist dismantles the corporealist argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without pausing for analysis. Then read the literal translation. Notice the structure: a question, the opponents' claim, and Baqillani's rebuttal.
The Arabic uses a conditional structure: 'if they say... then it is said to them...'.
2
Identify the opponents' analogy
The opponents argue: all agents we observe (shahid) are bodies, so the unseen agent (gha'ib) must also be a body. Find this in the text and see how it is presented.
3
Understand Baqillani's counter
Baqillani shows that if the analogy is applied consistently, it forces God to have all the attributes of created agents: being composed, originated, shaped, etc. He then presents the dilemma: accept these or reject the analogy.
The key move is extending the analogy to its logical conclusion.
4
Grasp the dilemma
The opponents face a choice: if they persist, they abandon tawhid; if they refuse the additional attributes, they undermine their own inference. This makes the argument self-defeating.

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'hijab' (veil/barrier) and its two meanings: first as a physical covering that prevents access, and second as an affliction or defect placed in the eye that prevents the perception of visible objects. The root 'hajb' means prevention, and from this root comes the word 'hajib' for a prince's doorkeeper, because he prevents people from reaching the prince. The passage then applies this second meaning to the Quranic verse 'No! Indeed, they will be veiled from their Lord on that Day' (83:15), explaining that the disbelievers are prevented by afflictions in their eyes from seeing Allah on the Day of Resurrection, as a punishment to humiliate them and to distinguish them from the believers who will see Him.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think 'hijab' only means a physical barrier or covering.
Correction: Baqillani shows that 'hijab' also refers to an affliction in the eye that prevents vision, and the root meaning is prevention, not just covering.
