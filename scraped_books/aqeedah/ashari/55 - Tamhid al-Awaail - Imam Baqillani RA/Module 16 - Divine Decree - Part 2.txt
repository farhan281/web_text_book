Pleasure with Divine Decree: Two Answers
Baqillani answers whether one must be pleased with God's decree.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's nuanced answer on being pleased with divine decree, distinguishing between what we must accept and what we are forbidden to approve.

Core Idea
Pleasure with decree means accepting God's commands, not approving forbidden things.

This passage deals with a subtle theological question: are believers required to be pleased with everything God decrees? Baqillani offers a careful answer that balances submission to God's will with moral responsibility. His response clarifies what 'pleasure with decree' actually means, helping Muslims avoid misunderstanding.

The Two Meanings of Qada and Its Relation to the Decreed Thing
Baqillani defines two meanings of qada and resolves the identity question.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the term qada (decree) has two distinct meanings in kalam—creation versus obligation—and that its identity with the decreed thing depends on which meaning is used.

Core Idea
Qada is not always the same as its object—it depends on the meaning.

This passage from Baqillani tackles a crucial distinction in the theology of qada and qadar. Before we can discuss God's decree, we must clarify what the word 'decree' itself means. Baqillani shows that it can refer either to the act of creation (which is identical to what is created) or to a command/obligation (which is distinct from its object). Understanding this distinction prevents confusion in predestination debates.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its English translation. Note the two different uses of qada that Baqillani introduces.
Pay attention to the phrase 'إلا على معنى أنه فرضها وأمر بها' – this is the key exception.
2
Identify the objector's question
Find the question 'فالقضاء عندكم هو المقضي أو غيره؟' and notice how it frames the issue: is the decree identical to its object or something else?
3
Understand the two-part answer
Baqillani answers that qada has two senses: creation (identical to the created thing) and obligation/notification (distinct from its object). Recognize the analogies: command vs. commanded, report vs. reported.
The exception 'إذا لم يكن خبراً عن نفسه' shows a subtle case where a report can be identical to its object.
4
Reflect on the implications
Consider why this distinction matters. If qada is always understood as creation, it would blur the line between God's decree and His commands, leading to theological errors.
This connects to broader debates on divine will and predestination.
5
Test your understanding

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification passage. Baqillani begins by identifying two meanings of the term qada (decree). First, qada can mean judgment and obligation, derived from the judge's verdict. Second, it can mean creation. He clarifies that when we say God decreed sins, we mean He created them and predestined them, not that He commanded them or made them obligatory. Then an objector asks whether qada is identical to the decreed thing (al-maqdi) or something else. Baqillani answers that it depends on the sense: qada in the sense of creation is identical to the created thing (the decreed thing), because the act of creating is the created object. However, qada in the sense of obligation, notification, or writing is distinct from its object, just as a command is not the same as the thing commanded, and a report is not the same as the thing reported about. An exception is when the report is about itself or about something inseparable from it, but generally otherness holds. So the identity or non-identity of qada with its object depends on which meaning is intended.

Pleasure with God's Decree: General vs. Detailed
Baqillani responds to the objection about being pleased with God's decree by distinguishing general from detailed pleasure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani distinguishes between being pleased with God's decree in general versus in detail, and why this distinction is crucial to avoid theological pitfalls.

Core Idea
We accept God's decree in general, but not every detail.

In this passage, Baqillani addresses a challenging question: Are Muslims required to be pleased with everything God decrees, including sins and disbelief? He offers a nuanced answer that distinguishes between general and specific pleasure, using analogies from everyday Muslim speech to clarify his point.

Then they said: Do you accept (be pleased with) God's decree and predestination? It is said to them: We accept God's decree which is His creation which He commanded us to desire and be pleased with. Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer passage. Opponents ask whether Muslims are pleased with God's decree and predestination. Baqillani gives two answers. First, we are pleased with that part of God's decree which is His creation that He commanded us to desire and be pleased with, i.e., good things, and we are not pleased with what He forbade us to be pleased with (sins). Second, we say we are pleased with God's decree in general (الجملة) in all cases. Then a more specific objection: Are you pleased with disbelief and sins which are from God's decree? Baqillani responds that we use the term 'pleasure' (الرضا) in a general sense, but not in detail because of ambiguity. To illustrate, he provides analogies: Muslims say 'Things belong to God' in general, but they do not attribute specific improper things like 'a child belongs to God' (implying a physical offspring) in detail. Similarly, we say 'creation perishes' but do not say 'God's proofs perish' because that would be inappropriate. So some statements are allowed in general but not in detail. Then Baqillani turns the tables: He asks whether God decreed the death of the Prophet, the defeat of Muslims, the assistance of disbelievers, etc. If the opponents say they are pleased with all that, then they have broken consensus (because that would imply being pleased with evil). If they say they are not pleased, then they must also not be pleased with the disbelief and sins they asked about, thus conceding the point. This argument uses a reductio ad absurdum to show that one cannot be pleased with all details of God's decree, only in general.

The Reductio Argument: Consistency in Pleasure
Baqillani uses a reductio ad absurdum to force consistency on opponents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani uses a reductio ad absurdum to show that opponents must either accept being pleased with all calamities (breaking consensus) or deny being pleased with sins, thus conceding his point.

Core Idea
If you demand pleasure in detail, you must approve of all evil.

Baqillani now turns the tables on his opponents. He asks whether God decreed specific calamities like the death of the Prophet and the defeat of Muslims. If they say they are pleased with all that, they break consensus; if not, they contradict their own demand. This argument demonstrates the necessity of the general vs. detail distinction.

3
Understand the conclusion
If they say yes, they break consensus. If no, they agree that pleasure in detail is not required, thus conceding Baqillani's point.

General vs. Specific Approval of Divine Decree
Baqillani refutes the objection that Muslims must be pleased with sins as God's decree.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Muslims affirm divine decree in general but do not approve of specific sins, and how Baqillani defends this position against an objection.

Core Idea
Approving God's decree does not mean approving every specific evil.

How can Muslims affirm that everything happens by God's decree without approving sins? This is a classic objection. Baqillani answers by drawing a crucial distinction: we approve the decree generally, but not in detail. He then turns the question back on the objector to show the absurdity of blanket approval. Read on to see how this argument unfolds.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and initial answer
Read the opening exchange. Notice the question and Baqillani's immediate distinction between general (jumla) and detailed (tafsil) approval.
Pay attention to the term 'ibham' (ambiguity) as the reason for not approving in detail.
2
Examine the analogies
Baqillani gives two analogies to clarify the distinction. Identify them and see how they illustrate the principle: some expressions are allowed generally but not in detail.
The first analogy is about saying 'things belong to God' but not applying that to specific partners. The second is about perishing but not applying to God's proofs.
3
Follow the counter-argument
Read the second part where Baqillani turns the tables. He lists specific painful decrees and asks whether the objector approves of them. This traps the opponent into either breaking consensus or conceding.
This is a typical kalam reductio ad absurdum.
4
Synthesize the lesson
Combine the two parts: the distinction and the reductio. Understand how together they form a complete defence of the Sunni position.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer argument concerning approval of God's decree (al-rida bi al-qada). The objectors ask whether Muslims approve of disbelief and sins since they are decreed by God. Baqillani's answer has two parts. First, he makes a distinction: Muslims approve of the decree in general but not in detail, because affirming divine decree does not mean approving every specific evil act. He gives analogies: Muslims say 'all things belong to God' in general, but they do not say in detail that God has a son, spouse, or partner. Similarly, they say creatures perish, but they do not say God's proofs perish. So some expressions are permitted from one perspective and forbidden from another. Second, he turns the argument back on the questioner. He asks whether they approve of specific decrees such as the death of the Prophet, the suffering of Muslims, and the empowerment of disbelievers. If the questioner approves of all that, then they have broken consensus by applying approval without restriction; if they disapprove, then they have conceded the point against themselves. The structure is classic kalam argumentation: drawing a precise distinction then trapping the opponent in inconsistency. Baqillani thus defends the Sunni position that one must affirm divine decree without approving of evil deeds.

Allah Provides Both Halal and Haram
Baqillani asserts and proves that Allah provides both halal and haram.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Allah is the sole provider of all sustenance, whether lawful or unlawful, and the Qur'anic and rational proofs for this doctrine.

Core Idea
Allah's provision covers both halal and haram; nothing escapes His providence.

Imam Baqillani opens this passage with a direct question: do Muslims believe Allah provides both halal and haram? He answers with a firm 'yes' and marshals Qur'anic evidence and a compelling rational inference. This lesson lays the foundation for the Sunni understanding of divine provision (rizq) as encompassing all that sustains life, regardless of its legal status.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note the question
Begin by reading the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the opening question: 'Do you say that Allah provides the lawful and the unlawful?'
This question sets the stage for the entire discussion.
2
Identify the Qur'anic proofs
Baqillani cites several verses. Find each one and see how they connect creation, provision, and death to establish Allah as the sole Provider.
The verse 'There is no creature but on Allah is its provision' is key.
3
Understand the logical inference
Consider the example of a person who eats only haram his whole life. If haram were not provision, Allah would have failed to provide for him. This proves that haram can be rizq.
This is a powerful rational argument.
4
Reflect on the poetic summary by Ibn Ruslan
Read the line: 'Provision is what benefits, even if unlawful.' This concisely captures the doctrine.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof concerning divine provision (rizq), establishing that Allah provides both halal and haram, and refuting the Qadariyya who limit provision to lawful means. Baqillani begins with the question: do you say Allah provides both? He answers yes, citing Quranic verses that link creation and provision. He then states the principle: Allah is the only true provider. The proof includes verses from Surah Qasas, Surah Saba', and Surah Hud. The key inference: if a person eats only haram his whole life, and haram were not provision, then Allah would not have provided him, which is absurd; therefore haram can be provision. He quotes Ibn Ruslan's line: 'Provision is what benefits, even if unlawful.' Next, he quotes al-Baghdadi to present and refute the Qadariyya position. The Qadariyya claimed provision is only through lawful means like inheritance and spoils, and that a person can miss his provision or eat another's. Baqillani, through al-Baghdadi, affirms the Sunni position that everyone eats his own provision whether halal or haram, and it is impossible to eat another's. He illustrates with a thought experiment: a person who lives only on usurped goods his whole life — if haram is not provision, then Allah never provided him, which contradicts the verse that every creature's provision is from Allah. Thus the correct view is that provision includes both lawful and unlawful, and Allah is the sole provider.

Refuting the Claim that Guidance is Just a Label
Baqillani refutes the claim that divine guidance and misguidance are mere designation through reductio ad absurdum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's argument that divine guidance and misguidance are real acts, not mere labels, based on Quranic verses and consensus.

Core Idea
Divine guidance is a real gift, not just a label.

This passage tackles a subtle objection: maybe God's 'leading astray' is just a name. Baqillani uses the Qur'an and consensus to show this reduces divine favor to human favor and leads to absurdities. Let's read his refutation.

The opponent then argues that 'guidance' and 'misguidance' might be mere judicial or nominal designations, like when people label someone astray without causing the condition.
