Baqillani wrote distinct works to defend prophethood and clarify miracles.

Miracles as Testimony and the Hierarchy of Proofs
Baqillani discusses miracles as testimony and enumerates the types of proofs with their hierarchy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how miracles function as testimony for prophethood and how Baqillani categorizes rational and scriptural proofs, with the latter being subordinate to the former.

Core Idea
Miracles testify to prophethood, and all scriptural proofs rest on rational foundations.

Imam al-Baqillani begins this passage with a subtle point about linguistic testimony, then draws a parallel to miracles as a form of testimony for a prophet's truthfulness. He proceeds to enumerate the accepted sources of evidence in Islamic theology—the Book, Sunnah, consensus, and analogical reasoning—and clarifies that while these scriptural proofs are valuable, they are ultimately branches of the more fundamental rational proofs. This sets the methodological framework for understanding how proofs are evaluated in kalam.

Guided Path
1
Understand the analogy between testimony and miracles
Read how Baqillani compares miracles to testimony (شهادة). Notice that he previously discussed linguistic testimony, and now extends that concept to miracles as a witness to a prophet's truthfulness.
The fragment at the start refers to a prior discussion about the reliability of testimony based on language.
2
Identify the types of evidence mentioned
Baqillani lists the sources of evidence: rational propositions, the Book, Sunnah, consensus, analogical reasoning, and independent reasoning. Make a mental list of these categories.
These are the standard sources in Sunni usul al-fiqh.
3
Grasp the hierarchy between rational and scriptural proofs
Notice the phrase 'فرعاً لأدلة العقول' (branches of the proofs of intellect). This indicates that scriptural proofs are subordinate to rational ones. Reflect on why this matters for theological methodology.
This hierarchy is a key principle in kalam.

Baqillani's Refutation of Astrologers' Epistemological Claims
Baqillani refutes astrologers' three knowledge sources and offers an alternative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani refutes the three possible sources of knowledge that astrologers might claim and presents an alternative causal explanation grounded in observable earthly phenomena.

Core Idea
Astrologers' claims fail on transmission, observation, and inference.

Baqillani tackles astrologers who claim to know the future by the stars. He systematically examines every possible basis for their knowledge—reports, direct observation, and reasoning—and shows each is insufficient. He then offers a more plausible cause-and-effect relationship grounded in observable earthly phenomena.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the translation to grasp the overall argument.
Notice the three refutations and the alternative explanation.
2
Examine the refutation of transmission
Baqillani addresses the astrologers' claim of transmitted knowledge. He argues that the prophets, who have proofs of truthfulness, contradict the astrologers, so they are more deserving of belief.
This shows the priority of prophetic reports over astrological claims.
3
Examine the refutation of sensory observation and inference
Baqillani argues that astrologers did not observe the ancient past or the management of the stars. He also says that nothing in their observations provides evidence for their claims about the stars' management or the eternity of natures.
He points out that inference from observed phenomena does not support astrology.
4
Consider the alternative explanation
Baqillani proposes that changes on earth (trees, seas, waters, etc.) cause changes in the stars, not vice versa. This is closer to direct observation and more plausible.
This counters the astrological causality with a simpler, observable alternative.

Then he said: 'It is said to them: How do you know that it is so? Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the possible sources of knowledge that astrologers might claim. Baqillani addresses three potential sources: (1) transmitted reports (sam'), (2) direct observation/sense (al-'iyan wa al-hiss), and (3) inference (istidlal) from observed phenomena. Against transmitted reports, he says that the reports from the prophets (who are truthful) contradict the astrologers, so the prophets should be believed instead. Against sensory observation, he points out that astrologers themselves do not remember their own past nor have they witnessed the management of the stars, so their sensory claims are false. Against inference from what they observe, he argues that there is nothing observed that proves the stars' management or the eternity of natures. He then offers an alternative: if the argument were turned around, it would be more plausible to say that changes on earth (trees, seas, waters, etc.) cause changes in the stars, rather than vice versa, because that is closer to direct observation. He concludes by affirming reliance on God.

Third Argument: Religious Practices Are Rationally Ugly
The Barāhima argue that religious practices contradict rational good taste.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Barāhima's claim that religious practices are rationally ugly and see the specific examples they cite to support their rejection of prophethood.

Core Idea
The Barāhima argue that a wise God would not command irrational acts.

The Barāhima, a group of Indian atheists, challenge the validity of prophethood by arguing that the practices prescribed by revealed religions contradict rational judgment. They claim that a wise Creator would never command actions that reason deems ugly. In this passage, they enumerate several Islamic rituals to support their argument.

Read the Arabic and translation

Pay attention to the list of practices they cite.
2
Identify the premise

Consider why they assume a wise Maker would avoid such commands.
3

This step helps you see the argument from their perspective.

Commentary

All these, they argue, conflict with rational judgment, so a wise God would not command them, and therefore these religions cannot be from God.

The Barāhima's Equality Objection
The Barāhima present their equality objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Barāhima's argument that a human messenger cannot legitimately command others because they are equals.

Core Idea
If all humans are equal, how can one command another?

The Barāhima reject prophethood on the grounds that a messenger is merely a human like everyone else. They argue that giving one person authority over others is the greatest sin unless there is clear proof. In this passage, they present their objection, claiming that following such a person is like being an inanimate object or an animal.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the rhetorical questions and the comparisons used.
The Barāhima compare the follower to a 'جماد' (inanimate object) and 'حيوان' (animal).
2
Identify the core claims
List the claims: (1) the messenger is identical in nature, (2) following him is the greatest sin, (3) no rational distinction exists.
3
Understand the challenge
The Barāhima ask: what proof does the messenger have? If it's a mere claim, any claim would be equal. If it's a miracle, they claim to have countless miracles too.
This sets up the need for a refutation.

Refutation of the Barahima: Preference is Not Injustice
Baqillani refutes the Barahima's analogy from human favoritism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani refutes the Barahima's argument that God's preference among equals would be unjust, by showing that preference in human affairs is not always foolish.

Core Idea
Preference among equals is not automatically injustice — context and wisdom matter.

The Barahima argued that if God preferred some prophets over others, that would be like a human favoring one equal over another, which is foolish. Baqillani challenges this analogy by examining whether all preference among humans is indeed foolish. He provides everyday examples where such preference is permissible and even wise.

Guided Path
1
Read the Barahima's objection

This objection is the starting point of the passage.
2
Examine Baqillani's counter-question
Notice that Baqillani does not immediately reject the analogy; instead, he questions the premise: 'Why do you say this is foolishness?'
This is a key dialectical move.
3
Look at the examples given
Baqillani provides examples from everyday life—a master favoring one servant, a person favoring a friend with gifts and honors—to show that such preference can be permissible and wise.
These examples demonstrate that the premise is not universally true.
4
Understand how this refutes the analogy
By showing that human preference is not always foolish, Baqillani undermines the Barahima's analogy, allowing that God's preference can be perfectly wise.
The refutation is complete without affirming that God does prefer.

Prophets' merit and the justification of divine favor
Baqillani argues that God's favoring of prophets is based on their merit, not arbitrary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani argues that prophethood is granted based on merit, specifically through an analogy that counters the objection of arbitrary divine favor.

Core Idea
Prophethood is earned through righteousness, not arbitrary whim.

In this passage, Baqillani presents a decisive argument that divine favor in bestowing prophethood is not capricious but based on the individual's merit—their actions and sincerity. He draws a parallel to a principle the objectors already accept: that God favors the repentant and those who accept rational proofs over others because they deserve it. By this analogy, Baqillani forces the objectors to concede that prophethood, too, is a deserved distinction.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice the structure: it begins with a rhetorical question, then presents an analogy, and concludes that the objectors cannot refute this.
Pay attention to the key terms: تفضيل (preference), استحقاق (deserving), إخلاص (sincerity), اجتهاد (striving).
2
Identify the analogy
Baqillani compares God's favoring of prophets to the objectors' own view that God favors the repentant and those who accept rational proofs. Identify how the two cases are parallel.
The objectors already believe that God favors people based on their righteousness. Baqillani applies the same logic to prophethood.
3
Understand the logical force
The argument is that if you accept that divine favor can be deserved in one case (repentance), then you must accept it in the case of prophethood, when the prophet is the best in deeds. This is a consistency argument.
The conclusion is that the objectors have no way to refute this.
4
Address the common misunderstanding
Some might think Baqillani is saying human actions obligate God. But he is not claiming that; he is using the objectors' own principle to show that prophethood is consistent with justice as they understand it.
The distinction: it is about appropriateness, not obligation.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the possibility and actuality of prophethood, and the truthfulness of the prophets. Baqillani presents two main arguments. First, he gives a rational argument: sending messengers does not corrupt the system of obligation (taklif) or trial (mihnah), nor does it conflict with God's attributes or truth; rather, it brings great reward, so it is wise and possible for God to do so. Second, he gives an empirical argument based on consensus (ijma') and tawatur (mass transmission): we know that Jews, Christians, and Muslims have unanimously reported the miracles of Moses, Jesus, and Muhammad. Then he argues that falsehood from such large groups is impossible because: (a) they could not have colluded through correspondence or conspiracy to lie—that would be impossible given their numbers and circumstances; (b) they could not have all gathered in one place to agree on the lie; (c) even if they gathered, collusion on a lie would go against the normal course of habit (adah)—it is not in human nature for such a large, diverse group to agree on a falsehood and keep it secret. Baqillani emphasizes that custom (adah) does not allow for a hundred thousand people to lie about a single report, though it might happen among one or two. Since these ways of falsehood are impossible for the vast multitude of transmitters of prophetic signs, the possibility of falsehood is negated, and their truthfulness is thereby proven. Finally, he adds a conditional: if it were possible for these transmitters to lie, then it would also be possible for the entire population of cities and countries to lie, which is absurd. Thus the argument stands as a proof for the veracity of the prophetic message.

Refuting the Denial of Non-Muslims as Proof Against the Miracle
Baqillani refutes the objection from Jewish/Christian denial.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Imam al-Baqillani argues that the denial of Jews and Christians does not invalidate the miraculous nature of the Quran.

Core Idea
Denial by unreliable witnesses does not disprove a miracle.

Imam al-Baqillani addresses a common objection: if the Quran were truly miraculous, why didn't the Jews and Christians of the time acknowledge it? He explains that their denial carries no weight because they were either absent from the events or were known liars. This passage teaches us how to evaluate the credibility of witnesses in theological arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage carefully
Read the Arabic text once to get a feel for the argument. Don't worry about understanding every word yet.
The passage is a single sentence in the original, so pay attention to the logical connectors.
2
Identify the objection being refuted
What is the claim that Baqillani is responding to? He mentions 'denial of those who denied' – that is the objection that non-Muslims' rejection proves the miracle is false.
This is a classic dialectical argument: the opponent says 'if it were true, they would have believed'.
3
Analyze the two reasons given
Baqillani gives two scenarios: (1) the Jews and Christians were not present at most events, or (2) only a few were present and they are prone to lying and concealment. Understand why each makes their denial invalid as evidence.
4
Consider the conclusion
Since the deniers are not credible witnesses, the objection is baseless. Reflect on how this reasoning can be applied to similar objections today.
The conclusion is stated clearly: 'this objection becomes invalid with clear invalidity'.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an implied objection. The author, Imam al-Baqillani, addresses the claim that if the Quran's miraculous nature were true, even Jews and Christians would have acknowledged it. He argues that their denial is not a valid proof against the miracle because they were not reliable witnesses. First, many of the battles and events where the Prophet's miracles occurred (like the conquest of Khaybar) were not witnessed by Jews or Christians at all. Second, even if a few were present, they are people known for lying and concealing the truth. Therefore, their denial cannot be used as evidence to invalidate the miracle. The author concludes that the objection is baseless and clearly void.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The denial of the Quranic miracle by contemporary Jews and Christians proves that the miracle did not happen.
Correction: Baqillani shows that the deniers either were not present at the events or are unreliable witnesses, so their denial is not a valid proof.

Key Takeaway
Denial of a miracle by unreliable or absent witnesses does not invalidate the miracle's truth.

What one change can you make today so that your life becomes a reliable witness to the truth?

Two Proofs of the Quranic Miracle
Baqillani enumerates the two aspects of the Quranic miracle and elaborates on the composition proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two aspects of the Quranic miracle as presented by Imam al-Baqillani: its unique composition and its reports of the unseen, and how these prove the Prophet's truthfulness.

Core Idea
The Quran's miracle is twofold: its unmatched eloquence and its hidden knowledge.

This passage from Imam al-Baqillani's Tamhid al-Awa'il establishes the foundational evidence for the Quranic miracle. He asserts that the Quran's appearance through the Prophet is known necessarily, but then answers an objection by detailing two independent proofs: the Quran's inimitable composition and its news of the unseen. This lesson unpacks the first proof in depth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two proofs
Read the opening of the passage to find the two ways the Quran indicates the Prophet's truthfulness. Note that Baqillani states them clearly.
2
Examine the composition proof
Study the detailed argument about the challenge to the Arabs. Notice how Baqillani emphasizes their inability despite their motivation.
3
Understand the role of illiteracy
Reflect on the verse from Surah al-Ankabut (48) cited at the end. Understand how the Prophet's illiteracy strengthens the miracle.
This is part of the composition proof, showing the Quran could not be humanly authored.

As for the knowledge of the appearance of the Quran at his hand and its coming from his direction, it is a necessity with no ambiguity in it and no dispute. It was heard and witnessed. If a sayer says: What is the aspect of the evidence of the appearance of the Quran at his hand that indicates his truthfulness? It is said to him: The aspect of that is from two ways: one is its composition and eloquence, and the second is what it contains of reports of the unseen and their knowledge. As for the evidence from the aspect of its composition, it is that we know that he, peace and blessings be upon him, challenged the Arabs to bring something like it in its eloquence, its fluency, its good arrangement, its composition, its grandeur, its solidity, its conciseness, its brevity, and the inclusion of its few words for many meanings. He called them to that and demanded it from them in the seasons and other times, gathered and scattered. He said to them in the text of recitation: {Say: If mankind and jinn gathered to produce the like of this Quran, they could not produce its like, even if they were helpers of one another} [Al-Isra: 88] – meaning allies and assistants. And He said: {Then bring ten surahs like it, fabricated} [Hud: 13]. And He said: {Then bring a surah like it} [Al-Baqarah: 23] – exaggerating in rebuking them for their inability, despite their pride of souls, greatness of arrogance, intensity of zeal, eagerness to deny him, scatter his assembly, disperse people from around him, to refute him and to dishonor and diminish him, and their embarking on war and sword-fighting with him, and exposing their souls to bloodshed, leaving homes, and parting from homelands. If they were, despite that, able to oppose it or oppose a surah from it, they would have hastened to that, and it would have been easier for them and lighter than waging war with him, exile from homelands, enduring terrors, patience in killing, the pain of wounds, bearing humiliation and shame; because he, peace be upon him, enabled them to deny him in two ways: one is his saying: {You will never bring its like}, and the other is his saying: {If you bring it, you would prevail and I would be false and you would be truthful}. This is with his recitation upon them in the text of revelation of His saying: {And you did not recite before it any book, nor did you write it with your right hand, for then the falsifiers would have doubted} [Al-Ankabut: 48]. And if they had known him by that or by companionship with the people of the scriptures, the transmitters of biographies, the frequenting of the people of reports, and sitting with the people of this matter, they would not have delayed to say to him: This is a lie, because you have always been known for companionship with the people of the scriptures, sitting with them, going to their places and homelands, conversing with them, and taking from them and benefiting from them.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive proof of the Quranic miracle (I'jaz al-Quran) as evidence of the Prophet's truthfulness. It begins by asserting that the knowledge of the Quran's appearance through the Prophet is a necessary knowledge, beyond doubt or dispute, as it was heard and witnessed. Then it presents a hypothetical objection: 'How does the Quran's appearance indicate his truthfulness?' The answer is given through two aspects: (1) the Quran's composition and eloquence (nazm), and (2) its news of the unseen (akhbar al-ghuyub). The author then elaborates on the first aspect in detail. He states that the Prophet challenged the Arabs to produce something similar in its eloquence, fluency, arrangement, grandeur, conciseness, and the inclusion of many meanings in few words. This challenge was issued during seasons and gatherings. The Quranic verses of challenge are cited: Surah Al-Isra 88, Hud 13, and Al-Baqarah 23. The author emphasizes that despite the Arabs' extreme pride, zeal, and eagerness to discredit the Prophet, and despite their willingness to go to war and endure hardships, they could not produce a single surah like it. If they had been able, they would have chosen that much easier path instead of war and exile. Moreover, the Prophet gave them two ways to prove him wrong: either produce the like, or if they succeed, they would be victorious. Additionally, the Prophet's illiteracy (ummah) is cited from Surah Al-Ankabut 48 – he never read a book nor wrote with his hand, so he could not have learned from previous scriptures. If the Arabs had known him to be associated with scholars of scriptures, they would have accused him of learning from them, but they did not, because he was known to be unlettered. This further solidifies the divine origin of the Quran.
