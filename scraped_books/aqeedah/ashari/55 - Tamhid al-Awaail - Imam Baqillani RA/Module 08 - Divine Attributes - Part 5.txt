Refuting the Analogy of Foolishness
Baqillani refutes the analogy between divine will and human foolishness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the analogy between divine will and human foolishness fails, and how Baqillani uses the concept of prohibition and a symmetrical argument to defend divine transcendence.

Core Idea
God's will is not bound by human moral categories like foolishness.

This passage presents a key objection against the divine attribute of will: if God wills anything that appears foolish, He would be like a foolish human. Baqillani responds by showing that human foolishness depends on prohibition, and then turns the argument back on the objectors. Let's follow the dialectic.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
The objectors claim: 'Every willer of ugliness and foolishness is foolish and frivolous. So if God wills foolishness, He would be foolish.' Understand their inductive generalization.
2
Identify Baqillani's first reply
Baqillani points out that human foolishness is contingent on prohibition. A child or insane person is not considered foolish for willing foolishness because the prohibition does not apply to them. Similarly, God is not subject to prohibition.
This deconstructs the analogy by highlighting a hidden condition.
3
Follow the second reply: the symmetrical argument
Baqillani turns the objection back: if willing necessarily transfers moral quality, then God's willing obedience would make Him obedient—which is absurd. The objectors cannot accept this without abandoning their religion.
This reveals the inconsistency in their reasoning.
4
Understand the final exchange
The objectors refine: a human is obedient because commanded; God is above commands. Baqillani counters: a human is foolish because prohibited; God is above prohibitions.
The symmetry is complete, and the objection fails.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer unit structured as a dialectical exchange. The objection is based on an inductive generalization: every human who wills something ugly or foolish is themselves foolish and frivolous. If God were to will foolishness, He would be similarly foolish. Baqillani's answer deconstructs the analogy by pointing out that the connection between willing folly and being foolish is contingent on prohibition. A human is considered foolish because they are prohibited from willing folly; if there were no prohibition, the willing would not be blameworthy. He gives the example of children and the insane: they are not deemed foolish for willing foolish acts because the prohibition does not apply to them. Then Baqillani turns the argument back on the objectors using their own reasoning: If willing necessarily transfers the moral quality to the willer, then God's willing obedience, righteousness, and piety would make Him obedient, righteous, and pious—which is absurd. The objectors cannot accept this consequence without abandoning their religion, nor can they reject the consequence without invalidating their own objection. Finally, the objectors attempt to refine their position: a human is called obedient for willing obedience because he is commanded to do so; God is above commands. Baqillani responds symmetrically: a human is called foolish for willing folly because he is prohibited from doing so; God is above prohibitions as well.

Allah Does Not Will Obscenities
Baqillani answers an objection about divine will and the spread of obscenity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani refutes the objection that Allah willing obscenities contradicts His condemnation of those who love their spread.

Core Idea
Allah condemns those who love falsehood, but He does not will falsehood to be true.

This lesson presents a theological objection and its refutation regarding Allah's will. The objector claims that Allah cannot will obscenities because He condemns those who desire their spread. Baqillani responds by distinguishing between what Allah wills and what He condemns, showing that the objection rests on a misunderstanding of divine will. You will see how careful analysis of Quranic verses and logical consistency resolve the apparent tension.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Identify the objection: How can Allah will obscenities when He condemns those who love their spread?
The reference is to Quran 24:19.
2
Analyze Baqillani's first response
Notice that Baqillani clarifies that Allah condemns those who describe pure people falsely. The condemnation is for the false description, not for the obscenity itself.
3
Understand the second point
Baqillani states that Allah did not will slander to be true. He distinguishes between what He wills and what others will.
Allah wills the act of slander as a created event but does not will it as good or true.
4
Grasp the logical conclusion
Since those people are prohibited from willing such things, and it is impossible for Allah to be prohibited, the objection is invalid.
This shows that Allah's will is beyond human categories of permission and prohibition.

Commentary
This passage is a theological objection and answer concerning Allah's will (irada) and the condemnation of those who love the spread of obscenity. The objection states: How can Allah will obscenities (fawahish) when He condemns those who love that obscenity spread among believers (reference to Quran 24:19)? Baqillani responds by first clarifying that Allah's condemnation is directed at those who falsely describe pure people (ahl al-tahara) and love that false description to be true. Allah does not will that slander (qadhf) of chaste women be true. Therefore, He does not will what those people will. Moreover, those people have been prohibited (nuhu) from willing such things. Since it is impossible (muhal) for Allah to be prohibited (i.e., the one who prohibits cannot be subject to prohibition), and His attribute of will is beyond such constraint, the objection collapses. The key point is that Allah's will is not equivalent to human evil desires; He does not will falsehood or injustice, and His condemnation of those who do is consistent with His justice.

The Meaning of 'Hearing' as Acceptance and Divine Justice
The author interprets 'sam'' as acceptance and argues against burdening beyond capacity.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the Quranic term 'sam'' in certain contexts means acceptance, not physical hearing, and that this interpretation supports the doctrine that God does not impose burdens beyond human capacity, reflecting His justice and wisdom.

Core Idea
Hearing in the Quran can mean acceptance, and true divine justice never burdens beyond capacity.

This passage from Imam Baqillani addresses a key theological and exegetical question: does the Quranic phrase 'they were not able to hear' refer to a physical inability or a refusal to accept? The author argues it means the latter, and uses this interpretation to defend the principle that God never imposes an impossible burden, a cornerstone of Sunni theology.

The author argues that if burdening beyond capacity were unjust or foolish, the believers would not have asked God to avoid it, and God would not have praised them for that request. This demonstrates that such burdening is incompatible with divine wisdom and justice.

Second Rational Proof from Unknowable Properties of Acts
Second rational proof that acts have unknowable properties requiring a knowing Creator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Baqillani's second rational argument that human acts possess hidden properties unknown to agents, proving the necessity of a knowing and intending Creator.

Core Idea
Acts contain hidden realities that only an all-knowing Creator can produce.

This lesson presents Imam Baqillani's second rational argument for the creation of human acts. He observes that human acts occur with properties and realities that the agents themselves do not know—such as being accidents, having categories, and serving as evidences. Since the agent is ignorant of these aspects, they cannot be the creator; the true Creator must be God, who knows all realities and intends their existence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice the key observation about acts having unknown properties.
Pay attention to the terms: أعراض (accidents), أجناس (categories), أدلة (evidences).
2
Identify the key premise
Identify the premise: acts occur with rulings, attributes, and realities that servants do not know. Many deny these, but we know they exist.
3
Understand the reductio argument
Imam Baqillani argues that if a heedless, ignorant, non-intending agent could create such acts, then all created things could come from such an agent, undermining the evidence that creation points to a knowing, intending Creator.
This is a logical reduction to absurdity.
4
Conclude with the necessity of God
The proof establishes that the creator of all actions is God, who knows their realities and intends their existence.
This refutes the idea that humans create their own acts.

Commentary
This passage is a set of four Qur'anic proofs presented by Baqillani to demonstrate that human speech is created by Allah, not an uncreated attribute. The argument begins by referencing a prior context about Allah's measure and intent. The first proof is from Surah Ar-Rum verse 22: the diversity of tongues (languages) is counted among Allah's signs (āyāt). Since speech is diverse and a sign, it must be created, as signs are created things. The second proof is from Surah Al-Mulk verses 13-14: Allah asks rhetorically whether He who created would not know hidden speech. This implies that His knowledge of speech is tied to His being its creator. Baqillani explains that if creation were only of the place of speech, it would not necessitate knowledge of the speech itself, but Allah made His creation evidence for His knowledge, so He must be the creator of the speech itself. The third proof is from Surah Fatir verse 3: Allah negates any creator other than Himself. The polytheists argued this only negates a creator who provides, but Baqillani counters by analogy: just as the negation of a god in the verse about night is not limited, so the negation of a creator is absolute. The fourth proof is from Surah An-Nahl verse 20: those invoked besides Allah do not create anything. Since angels were worshipped, and they do not create, then no created being creates. The final proof (embedded) is from Surah Ar-Ra'd verse 16: if there were partners who create like Allah, there would be confusion. Baqillani concludes that if humans created their own speech, movements, will, and knowledge—which resemble Allah's creation—then they would be partners, which is false. Thus, speech is created by Allah alone.

Why God Creating Evil Does Not Make Him Unjust

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This passage presents a classic objection against Ash'ari theology: if God creates all actions, including evil ones, then He would be unjust. Imam Baqillani masterfully refutes this by showing that the analogy between human and divine agency fails. The key lies in the conditions under which an action is deemed unjust: prohibition and self-interest.

Refutation by Linguistic Derivation

Lying and Commanding Sins: Divine Attributes and Moral Categories
Baqillani responds to an objection about divine lying and commanding sins.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Baqillani distinguishes between lying being impossible for God and commanding sins being permissible, and the role of essential attributes and prohibition in defining moral acts.

Core Idea
Truthfulness is essential to God; sin is defined by prohibition, not nature.

This passage tackles a challenging objection: if God can command sins, why can't He commit them? Baqillani carefully separates the two cases. He grounds the impossibility of lying in God's essential attributes, while showing that commanding sins is logically possible because sin is relational, not essential. Let's examine his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and the two-part answer
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice how Baqillani first addresses lying, then commanding sins.
The objection combines two issues; Baqillani treats them separately.
2

Baqillani emphasizes rational proofs, not just ugliness.
3
Understand why commanding sins is possible
Notice the reasoning: if God commanded a sin, the command would be eternal and the act would become obedience. Sin is defined by prohibition.
The example of lying in war illustrates how command changes moral status.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Baqillani states that his argument validates the Ash'ari position. Consider how this distinction preserves divine perfection while allowing moral diversity.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer pair. The objector asks whether it is permissible for God to lie (كذب) and to command lying and other sins. Baqillani distinguishes between two cases. First, lying itself: it is impossible (لا يجوز) for God, not merely because it is considered ugly (قبح), but because truthfulness (صدق) is an essential attribute (صفات النفس) of God. Just as power and knowledge are essential, so is truthfulness; thus, lying is impossible. This impossibility is established by rational proofs (أدلة العقول), not just by judgments of ugliness. Second, commanding sins: it is permissible (جائز) in principle. If God commanded a sin, that command would be eternal (قديماً), and the commanded act would become an act of obedience (طاعة) deemed good (مستحسن) rather than a sin. Sin is sin only because of prohibition (النهي), not because of its inherent nature. Baqillani gives an example: God has commanded lying in some situations (e.g., for the one who fears in war). Thus, the Ash'ari position is validated.
