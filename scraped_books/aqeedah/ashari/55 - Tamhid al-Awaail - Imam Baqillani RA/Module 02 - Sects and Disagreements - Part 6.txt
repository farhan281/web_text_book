Refuting the Solitary Reports Objection for Imamate
Answers the objection that reports about Abu Bakr and Umar are ahad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Baqillani rebuts the claim that reports about Abu Bakr and Umar's imamate are merely solitary (ahad) and therefore unreliable.

Core Idea
Turning the objection back on the opponent exposes their inconsistency.

This passage presents a sharp rebuttal to a common objection: that the reports supporting the imamate of Abu Bakr and Umar are solitary (ahad) and thus not authoritative. Imam Baqillani responds by turning the argument around, showing that the opponents cannot claim a distinction between their own reports and those of the Ahl al-Sunnah. He then exposes the unreasonable demand that clear prophetic statements be dismissed as taqiyya (dissimulation).

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
First, read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how the objection is stated: 'All these reports are ahad.' Then observe the two-part answer: a tuq (rebuttal) and a rhetorical exposure of inconsistency.
The response uses the opponents' own logic against them.
2
Identify the tuq (rebuttal)
Look at the first part of the answer: 'What prevents your opponents from making the same claim about your reports?' This is a classic argumentative technique.
The opponents cannot distinguish their ahad reports from ours.
3
Analyze the second part
The second part shows that the opponents demand acceptance of all their narrations while rejecting ours, and even ask us to interpret clear texts as taqiyya. This is unreasonable.
Taqiyya is invoked as a baseless excuse.
4
Understand the conclusion
Baqillani concludes that this demand is based on wishful thinking and is not a valid argument. Reflect on how this strengthens the Sunni position on imamate.

Imamate Established by Choice (Ikhtiyar)
Baqillani argues that imamate is established by contract, not text.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that according to Baqillani, imamate is established through a contract by qualified electors, not by divine text, and why this is the only valid method.

Core Idea
Imamate is a contract, not a text; when text fails, choice remains.

In this passage, Baqillani addresses a key objection: if textual designation is invalid, how can imamate be established? He clarifies that imamate is established by a contract from the qualified Muslims (ahl al-hall wa al-'aqd). He then uses a simple logical argument: only two methods exist — text or choice — and since text is invalid, choice is proven.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the hypothetical question posed by opponents: 'If the text (nass) regarding a specific imam is invalid, what is the way of establishing imamate and by what does a person become imam?' Notice that they assume the invalidity of text leads to a problem.
This sets up the need for an alternative method.
2
Identify the answer
The answer states that a person becomes imam only by a contract (aqd) from the excellent Muslims who are the people of loosing and binding (ahl al-hall wa al-'aqd). Recognize that this contract is based on choice (ikhtiyar), not inheritance or force.
The term ahl al-hall wa al-'aqd refers to qualified representatives of the community.
3
Grasp the logical elimination
Baqillani argues that there are only two possible methods for establishing imamate: text (nass) or choice (ikhtiyar). Since text is invalid, the only remaining method is choice. This logical argument from elimination shows that choice must be valid.
This reasoning directly answers the objection.
4
Apply the lesson
Reflect on how this passage defends the Sunni position of election over the Shi'a stance of divinely appointed imams. The invalidity of text (in their view) proves the validity of choice.

Source Text

Since text is invalid, the only remaining method is choice (ikhtiyar). This is a logical argument from elimination.

The Minimum Number for Imamate: One Person
Baqillani defines the number and gives a rational proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the evidence that the imamate contract is valid with a single qualified person from the people of loosing and binding.

Core Idea
Imamate is concluded by one, because no evidence requires more.

Imam Baqillani addresses a common objection about the number required for the imamate contract. Using rational and scriptural reasoning, he shows that since no specific number is mandated, the minimum is one qualified individual. This lesson explains his proof based on the absence of evidence for a specific count.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it, then read the translation. Notice the question-and-answer format.
2
Identify the ruling and its proof
The passage first states the ruling (one man from ahl al-hall wal-'aqd) and then gives the evidence. Locate these two parts.
3
Understand the principle of absence of evidence
The proof relies on 'fad al-dalil' (absence of evidence). There is no text or rational proof requiring a specific number, so the minimum is one.

Source Text

باب القول في العدد الذي تنعقد به الإمامة فإن قال قائل : فبكم يتم عقد الإمامة عندكم؟ قيل له : تنعقد وتتم برجل واحد من أهل الحل والعقد إذا عقدها لرجل على صفة ما يجب أن يكون عليه الأئمة فإن قالوا وما الدليل على ذلك؟ قيل لهم : الدليل عليه أنه إذا صح أن فضلاء الأمة هم ولاة عقد الإمامة ولم يقم دليل على أنه يجب أن يعقدها سائرها ولا عدد منهم مخصوص لا تجوز الزيادة عليه والنقصان منه ثبت بفقد الدليل على تعيين العدد والعلم بأنه ليس بموجود في الشريعة ولا في أدلة العقول أنها تنعقد بالواحد فما فوقه .
Chapter on the number by which imamate is concluded. If a questioner says: By how many is the contract of imamate completed according to you? It is said to him: It is concluded and completed by one man from the people of loosing and binding if he contracts it for a man possessing the qualities that an imam must have. Commentary

Then it provides a proof: since the qualified people (afadil) are the ones in charge of the contract, and there is no evidence that it must be by all of them or by a specific number, the absence of evidence for a specific number means it can be as few as one. This is a fall back to the principle of lack of restriction.

Why Imamate Does Not Require Universal Consent
Refutes the requirement of universal participation in imamate contract.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the imamate contract does not require the presence of all qualified Muslims and how early caliphate examples support this.

Core Idea
Gathering all qualified people for imamate is impossible; God does not command the impossible.

This passage addresses an objection that the contract of imamate should involve every excellent Muslim. The response shows that such universal participation is impractical, contrary to divine obligation, and unsupported by early practice. Let's explore the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
First, read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that it begins with an objection (فإن قيل) followed by the response (قيل).
Pay attention to the structure: objection → multiple proofs.
2
Identify the key arguments
The author gives several proofs: consensus of ahl al-ikhtiyar, impossibility of gathering all qualified, God not commanding the impossible, and the practice of the salaf.
Each proof is introduced with 'و' (and) or 'بأن' (that).
3
Examine the caliphal examples
Notice that early caliphs did not require universal presence. Umar limited the selection to six, and Abu Bakr appointed Umar alone.
These examples show practical precedent.
4
Connect to the principle
Understand that the contract's validity does not depend on universal consent. The impossibility of gathering all qualified makes it unnecessary.
This aligns with the broader kalam principle that God does not oblige the impossible.

The Number of Attendees for the Imamate Contract
States that a minimum number of attendees is not obligatory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a specific minimum number of attendees for the imamate contract is not obligatory, and how the analogy from Umar's shura is refuted.

Core Idea
Umar's shura was about excellence, not a fixed number rule.

This passage addresses the question of how many people must attend the contract that appoints an imam. Some scholars analogized from Umar's shura of six to require a fixed number. The author refutes this by clarifying Umar's actual intent, supported by a report of his own words.

Imamate Does Not Require Universal Consensus
Baqillani refutes the claim that the imam must be chosen by all virtuous Muslims everywhere.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Baqillani argues that the imamate contract does not require the agreement of all virtuous Muslims everywhere, based on impossibility and the practice of the early caliphs.

Core Idea
Universal consensus for imamate is impossible and never required.

This lesson presents Baqillani's refutation of a key objection: that the imam must be chosen by all virtuous Muslims of every era. He systematically dismantles this claim using logical impossibility, divine obligation, and historical precedent from the Rightly Guided Caliphs. This passage is a masterclass in Sunni political theology, grounding imamate in practical feasibility rather than utopian idealism.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Begin by reading the full Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how Baqillani structures his answer: he first states the objection, then gives multiple reasons why it is invalid.
The objection is introduced with 'فإن قيل' (if it is said).
2
Identify the key arguments
Identify the four main points: (1) consensus of the people of choice, (2) impossibility of gathering all ahl al-hall wa al-‘aqd, (3) Allah does not oblige the impossible, (4) the precedent of the first four caliphs.
Also note the additional proof from Abu Bakr's designation of ‘Umar.
3
Understand the term 'ahl al-hall wa al-‘aqd'
This term refers to the qualified representatives who can contract the imamate. Baqillani argues that requiring all of them from all cities is impossible.
4
Reflect on the implications
Consider why this argument is important: it establishes a practical, non-utopian basis for political authority in Sunni Islam. The imamate is valid if contracted by a sufficient number of qualified individuals, not by universal acclaim.
This connects to broader Sunni views on legitimacy.

If it is said: 'Did you not make the contract to all the virtuous of the ummah in every age of the Muslims?' It is said to him: The people of choice have agreed on the invalidity of that, and because we know that Allah has obligated upon us the act of contracting for the imam and obeying him when the contract is made for him, and that the gathering of all the people of loosening and binding in all the cities of the Muslims in one location and their concurrence on pledging allegiance to one man is impossible and prevented, and that Allah the Exalted does not impose the impossible act which cannot be done nor left, and because we know that the predecessors of the ummah did not observe in the contract for Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali the presence of all the people of loosening and binding in the cities of the Muslims, nor even in Medina, and that Umar referred the matter to only six persons, even though there were others suitable for the contract; so by these summary points the correctness of what we have said is necessitated. And that is also clarified by the fact that Abu Bakr contracted it for Umar, so his imamate was completed and his covenant was delivered by his contracting for him. Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an objection. The objector asks why the contract of imamate is not given to all virtuous Muslims in every era. The author (Baqillani) gives a multi-part answer: (1) The people of choice (ahl al-ikhtiyar) have agreed that this is invalid. (2) Allah has obligated the act of contracting and obedience, but gathering all people of loosening and binding (ahl al-hall wa al-‘aqd) from all cities in one place to unanimously pledge to one man is impossible (mut‘adhdhir mumtani‘). (3) Allah does not oblige the impossible. (4) The salaf did not require that for Abu Bakr, ‘Umar, ‘Uthman, and ‘Ali; not even in Medina. (5) ‘Umar restricted the shura to six, even though others were qualified. Thus the author's position is necessitated. He further supports it with Abu Bakr's implicit designation of ‘Umar, which was accepted. The author promises to discuss the imamate of ‘Umar later. The argument is based on empirical impossibility and precedent of the Rashidun caliphs.

No Fixed Number for the Imamate Contract
Baqillani rebuts the fixed-number requirement for imamate contract attendees, using 'Umar's shura as counterevidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Baqillani rejects a fixed number of attendees for the imamate contract and how he uses 'Umar's shura as evidence for flexibility rather than a numerical rule.

Core Idea
The imamate contract does not require a fixed number of attendees; 'Umar's shura was about merit, not procedure.

In this passage, Baqillani addresses a common objection: that a minimum of four attendees is necessary for the imamate contract, based on 'Umar's shura. He explains that 'Umar's selection of six was based on their virtue, not as a procedural rule, and that the contract can even be made in seclusion. This lesson clarifies the flexibility of the bay'ah process.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to get the overall argument. Notice the structure: objection, response, analogy rejection, practical point.
2
Identify the objection
The objection says that four attendees are necessary, analogizing to 'Umar's shura. Find where Baqillani states this.
The phrase 'وقد قال قوم...' introduces the objection.
3
Understand Baqillani's rebuttal
Baqillani argues that 'Umar's shura was not about setting a number but about consulting those he considered most virtuous. He quotes 'Umar's statement about Salim to prove this.
The quote shows 'Umar cared about the person, not the count.
4
Note the practical concession
Baqillani adds that the contract can be done secretly to avoid false claims, showing that even one person could suffice.
This underscores that there is no fixed number.
5
Reflect on the lesson
Consider how this flexibility relates to the broader Sunni view of imamate: it's not a rigid process but one based on community consensus.

Commentary

The author then adds a practical point: the contract can be done in seclusion to prevent false claims and chaos, as was done secretly in some cases.

Why the ummah cannot revoke the imam's contract without cause
Baqillani argues that the ability to contract the imamate does not imply the ability to annul it, using analogies from marriage, sale, and ritual acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's argument that the ability to appoint an imam does not imply the ability to remove him without cause, using analogies from marriage, sale, and acts of worship.

Core Idea
In Sharia, the right to contract does not include the right to annul.

Baqillani addresses an objection: if the ummah can select an imam, why can't they dismiss him at will? He responds by showing that Islamic law consistently separates the power to initiate a contract from the power to dissolve it. This lesson unpacks his argument and its analogies.

2

The response is a firm 'No' followed by evidence.

Reflect on why this matters for the imamate: it protects the imam from arbitrary removal and emphasizes that removal requires a valid cause.

فإن قال قائل : فهل تملك الأمة فسخ العقد على الإمام من غير حدث يوجب خلعه كما أنها تملك العقد له؟ قيل له : لا . فإن قيل : فكيف يملك لا يملك فسخه؟ قيل له : هذا في الشريعة أكثر من أن ترى أن العاقد على وليته لا يملك فسخ النكاح من حيث كان يملك عقده وكذلك العاقد البيع على سلعته لا يملك حله وإن ملك عقده وكذلك يملك عقد الصيام إلى مدة ولا يملك فسخه وكذلك يملك كتابة عبده وتدبيره، والمتطوع بالصيام والصلاة إذا دخل فيهما لا يملك حل شيء من ذلك؟ فبطل ما سألتم عنه .
If a speaker said: 'Does the ummah have the right to annul the contract of the imam without a cause that necessitates his removal, just as they have the right to contract for him?' It is said to him: No. If it is said: 'How can they have the right to contract but not to annul?' It is said to him: This is in the Sharia more than you see: that the one who contracts for his female ward cannot annul the marriage even though he could contract it, and similarly the one who contracts the sale of his commodity cannot dissolve it even though he could contract it, and he can contract fasting for a specified period but cannot annul it, and he can contract the kitaba of his slave and his tadbir, and the one who volunteers for fasting and prayer, if he enters into them, cannot dissolve any of that. So your query is invalid.
Source pages p. 473

Commentary
This passage refutes the objection that since the ummah can contract the imamate (select an imam), they should also be able to revoke it without cause. The author responds with a series of analogies from Islamic law: (1) A guardian (al-‘āqid ‘alā waliyyatihi) can contract a marriage for his ward but cannot annul it. (2) A seller can contract a sale but cannot dissolve it unilaterally. (3) One can vow to fast for a period but cannot break it voluntarily. (4) A master can make a kitaba (contract of manumission) or tadbir (freeing after death) for his slave, but cannot undo them. (5) One who voluntarily enters supererogatory fasting or prayer cannot exit at will. Thus, the lack of symmetry between contracting and annulling is well-established in Sharī‘ah. Therefore, the ummah does not have the power to revoke the imam's contract without a legitimate cause (like apostasy or injustice).

Why a Person Cannot Appoint Himself as Imam
Baqillani refutes the possibility of self-appointment to the imamate using legal analogies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical and legal basis for why a member of ahl al-ḥall wa al-‘aqd cannot contract the imamate for themselves, as argued by Baqillani using clear analogies from Islamic law.

Core Idea
Self-appointment in authority is invalid because Islamic law consistently separates the contractor from the beneficiary.

Baqillani addresses a potential objection: if a qualified person can appoint others as imam, why can't he appoint himself? He responds by drawing on well-known legal principles from marriage, sale, and manumission, showing that self-appointment contradicts the very structure of such contracts. This lesson unpacks his argument and the analogies he uses.

Guided Path
1

2

The passage presents three legal examples: marriage guardianship, sale of goods, and manumission contracts. Find each one and note the pattern: the contractor acts for others but not for himself.
In each example, the person cannot contract for themselves in the same capacity.
3
Understand the underlying principle
Ask yourself: what common principle do these examples share? They show that contracts of authority require a separation between the contracting party and the beneficiary. Self-appointment would collapse that distinction.
This principle is rooted in Sharia and recognized by the ummah.
4
Apply to the imamate
Reflect on why Baqillani's analogies apply to the imamate. The imam's authority comes from the delegation of the community (ahl al-ḥall wa al-‘aqd). A person cannot delegate authority to himself.
This reinforces that the imamate is a communal responsibility, not a personal right.

Consensus and the Exception of Necessity
Baqillani uses consensus and historical examples to argue for preferring the most excellent, with a concession for necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how consensus of the early community and the Shura incident support preferring the most excellent, and how necessity (fitna) can override this requirement.

Core Idea
Consensus requires the most excellent, but necessity may permit deviation.

After presenting hadith evidence, Baqillani turns to consensus (ijmāʿ) of the Muslim community, particularly the first generation's practice during the Shura (consultative council) after ʿUmar. He also recounts the incident between ʿUmar and Abū ʿUbayda, which illustrates that while the most excellent is preferred, fear of discord (fitna) can sometimes necessitate a different choice. Understanding this nuance is crucial for a complete picture of Islamic leadership doctrine.

Baqillani explains that ʿUmar accepted Abū ʿUbayda's rebuke and did not insist on his own pledge because he feared fitna (discord) and wanted the community to remain upon its most excellent. This shows that necessity can override the strict requirement.

When a Less Excellent Imam is Permissible
Baqillani permits a less excellent imam to avoid fitna, citing Umar's shura.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a less excellent (al-mafḍūl) imam is permissible when appointing the most excellent (al-afḍal) would lead to fitna and disorder, supported by the precedent of Umar's shura.

Core Idea
Avoiding greater harm justifies appointing a lesser imam.

Baqillani addresses a critical question: is it necessary to always appoint the most qualified person as imam? Drawing on the purpose of the imamate and the practical example of the Companions after Umar, he shows that preserving unity and preventing corruption can override the preference for the excellent. This lesson explores the reasoning and evidence for this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on purpose of imamate
Begin by reading the Arabic passage where Baqillani enumerates the duties of the imam: to repel enemies, protect the sanctuary, implement hudud, etc. Then read the translation.
Notice that the imam's role is functional, not merely ceremonial.
2
Identify the condition for deviation
Pay attention to the clause starting with 'فإذا خيف' (so if one fears). List the negative consequences that permit deviating from the most excellent. Understand that these are collective harms.
The phrase 'اختلاف السيوف' (conflict of swords) is a strong image of civil war.
3
Examine the shura precedent
Read Baqillani's reference to Umar's six-member council. Note that both excellent and less excellent were present, and Umar allowed any of them to be chosen if it led to unity and reform.
This historical example is used as tacit consensus (sukutiyya ijma).

Commentary
This passage is a combined argument and refutation. He states the purpose of the imamate: repelling enemies, protecting, maintaining order, implementing ḥudūd, and securing rights. If appointing the best leads to chaos, the less excellent may be chosen. He cites the precedent of the six-member shura (including Uthman, Ali, etc.), where both excellent and less excellent were present, yet all were considered eligible for the covenant if it served unity and reform. He states that the hadith 'The imams are from Quraysh' implies a general inclusion of all Qurayshi tribes. Reason does not require limitation to one clan. Core Idea
Removal of an imam requires a concrete, impairing condition; reversed conditions do not restore his authority.

In this passage, Imam Baqillani outlines the legal grounds that justify removing a sitting imam. Commentary
This passage is a legal-theological exposition on the conditions that necessitate the removal of an imam and a refutation of the idea that superior virtue in another person alone constitutes grounds for removal. Baqillani begins by enumerating clear incapacities that justify removal: insanity (with loss of intellect for a duration harmful to Muslims or giving no hope of recovery), deafness, dumbness, extreme old age and senility, any condition that prevents the imam from attending to the welfare of Muslims and fulfilling his duties (or even part of them), and captivity by the enemy for a period that endangers the ummah and prevents replacement. These examples show that the timing of a change in condition matters—what would invalidate at the outset does not necessarily invalidate after commencement. Superior Virtue and Fisq Do Not Require Removal
Baqillani argues that superior virtue or fisq in another does not necessitate removal, with analogy to tayammum and expiation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the occurrence of superior virtue or disobedience in another person after an imam's appointment does not necessitate his removal, and how this parallels other Islamic legal principles.

Core Idea
A change in external circumstances after a contract does not undo what has been validly established.

After listing physical incapacities that justify removal, Baqillani turns to a potential objection: what if someone else becomes more virtuous or the imam becomes sinful? He argues that neither requires removal, drawing analogies from the rulings of tayammum and expiation. The key principle is that conditions that would invalidate a contract at its outset do not necessarily invalidate it if they arise later.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection and Baqillani's response
The passage begins 'Wa laysa mimmā yūjibu khalʿ al-imām ḥudūthu faḍlin fī ghayrihī' (And it is not among what necessitates removal...). Read the initial statement and then the reason given.
Baqillani contrasts the scenario if inferiority existed at the time of contract (would require choosing the superior) vs. after (does not).
2
Understand the reasoning
The increase of virtue in another is 'not an innovation in religion nor in himself that would remove him.' In other words, it's not a defect in the imam's person or his religious standing.
3
Examine the parallel with fisq (disobedience)
Baqillani supports the same rule for fisq: occurrence after the contract does not necessitate removal, even if it would have at the start. He notes 'Amthāl hādhā fī al-sharīʿa kathīrah' (examples in Sharia are many).
4
Analyze the examples: tayammum and expiation
The tayammum example: if water is found before prayer, wudu is required; if during prayer, it is not. Similarly for expiation: if wealth comes after starting fasting, the fast is still valid. These show that timing matters—what would invalidate at the start may not disrupt what has begun.
Note the principle: contract validity is based on conditions at inception; later changes do not retroactively invalidate.
5
Apply the principle to the imamate
Thus, the occurrence of virtue or sin in another person after the imam's appointment does not require removal, because the contract was valid at its time and is not undone by subsequent external developments.

Refuting Claims of Delayed Allegiance by Weak Reports
The author refutes claims of delayed allegiance by Ali using weakness of reports and necessity of widespread transmission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why weak isolated reports cannot be used to accuse the Sahaba of sin, using the principles of their established justice and the necessity of widespread transmission for grave matters.

Core Idea
The justice of the Sahaba cannot be overridden by weak isolated reports.

Imam Baqillani addresses a historical claim that certain Companions delayed pledging allegiance to Abu Bakr. He argues that such claims rest on weak, isolated reports that contradict the established justice of the Sahaba. This lesson unpacks his logical and methodological rejection, grounding it in principles of hadith criticism and communal memory.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation

Identify the two main arguments

The first is a principle of adalat al-sahaba; the second is a historical reasoning based on tawatur.
3
Reflect on the principle of adalat al-sahaba
The author asserts that once a Companion's faith, piety, and justice are established, they cannot be accused of sin based on isolated reports. This is a foundational principle in Sunni theology.

Understand the argument from widespread transmission
If 'Ali's delay had truly occurred, it would have been widely known and transmitted. The fact that only weak, contradictory reports exist shows the claim is false.
This is a powerful methodological argument: the absence of widespread transmission for a claim that would necessarily be famous indicates fabrication.

The Delay of Ali and al-Zubayr and Their Return to Bay'ah
Author refutes claims of prolonged delay by Ali and al-Zubayr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that Ali and al-Zubayr's reported delay in pledging allegiance was temporary and they ultimately accepted Abu Bakr's caliphate with apology and praise.

Core Idea
Their delay was followed by return and apology, affirming Abu Bakr's caliphate.

In debates about the early Caliphate, some claim that Ali and al-Zubayr delayed pledging allegiance to Abu Bakr, implying rejection. The author responds by noting that the very narrations that mention the delay also report their return, apology, and recognition of Abu Bakr's virtues. This passage shows that their delay was not opposition.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once through, then read the English translation. Notice the flow of the argument.
2
Identify the author's main rebuttal
The author starts with 'however, we do not know anyone who narrated... except that it was also narrated...' This structure shows he is using the same narrators to prove the return.
This is a common polemical technique: using the opponent's sources against them.
3
List the three elements reported about Ali and al-Zubayr
The passage mentions their return to the pledge, their apology ('no blame...'), and their praise of Abu Bakr's virtues and his companionship in the cave. Note each.
These elements collectively demonstrate their ultimate acceptance.
4
Analyze the apology
They said they delayed only because they disliked being left out of consultation. This frames their delay as a desire for inclusion, not rejection.
The term 'caliph of the Messenger of Allah' shows they recognized Abu Bakr's authority.
5
Reflect on the broader significance
This passage defends the Sunni view of the legitimacy of Abu Bakr's caliphate by showing that even Ali and al-Zubayr accepted it.
It counters sectarian narratives that emphasize their delay as opposition.

Commentary
This passage rebuts an objection concerning the delay of 'Ali and al-Zubayr in pledging allegiance to Abu Bakr. The author argues that even if there are narrations of their delay, the same sources also report that they soon returned, entered into the Muslim community, and apologized to Abu Bakr, calling him 'Caliph of the Messenger of Allah' and praising his merits, especially that he was the companion of the Prophet in the cave (al-ghār). The apology included their reason: they did not want to miss being part of the consultation (mashwara). This shows their delay was not opposition but a desire for inclusion, and they ultimately affirmed Abu Bakr's caliphate.
