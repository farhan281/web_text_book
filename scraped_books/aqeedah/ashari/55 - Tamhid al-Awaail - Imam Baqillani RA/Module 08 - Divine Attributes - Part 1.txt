The Logical Necessity of a First in a Sequence
Al-Baqillani's logical refutation of denying a first in a sequence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Baqillani's argument that any sequence of occurrences must have a first, and denying that leads to sophistry.

Core Idea
Acknowledging a sequence implies a first; denying it is self-contradictory.

This passage from al-Baqillani's 'al-Naqd al-Kabir' tackles a key logical principle: if you admit that one thing comes after another, you have implicitly admitted a beginning. The argument is presented as a refutation of those who deny that certain divine acts have a first. Let's see how al-Baqillani exposes the contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text carefully, noting the sequence of letters (sin, ba, mim) and the logical flow.
The letters are used as an example of a sequence.
2
Understand the argument structure
Notice the conditional: if someone acknowledges one thing after another, they acknowledge a first. Then if they deny the first, they contradict themselves.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
3
Identify the conclusion
Al-Baqillani concludes that such a person has joined the sophists (safasata) and cannot be guided by proof.
Sophists reject self-evident truths.
4
Reflect on the implication
Think about how this argument applies to theological debates about the eternity of attributes or the Qur'an.

Commentary
This passage is a quotation from al-Baqillani's 'al-Naqd al-Kabir', as reported by Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni in his work 'al-Shamil'. It is a logical refutation of someone who posits that a sequence of letters (sin, ba, mim) from God has no beginning. The argument proceeds in steps: 1) If someone claims that sin comes after ba, and mim comes after sin (which comes after ba), then there is a sequence. 2) Admitting that one thing occurs after another implicitly acknowledges a first in that sequence (the ba). 3) If the same person then denies that the sequence has a first, they contradict themselves. 4) Such a contradiction leads to sophistry (safasata), which is the rejection of self-evident truths (badiha). The rhetorical question at the end emphasizes that someone who brazenly denies what is necessary cannot be guided by rational proof. This argument likely targets a theological opponent who denied that the divine attributes or the Qur'anic letters had any beginning, possibly related to the createdness of the Qur'an.

The Maker of the World is One
Baqillani proves oneness through the necessity of impotence in a multiplicity of gods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof for God's oneness based on the impossibility of conflicting wills among multiple gods.

Core Idea
Two gods would conflict; conflict proves impotence; impotence is impossible for God.

This passage from Imam Baqillani lays out a concise logical proof for tawhid. By considering what would happen if there were two gods with opposing wills, Baqillani shows that only one eternal Creator can exist. The argument rests on a simple but powerful principle: the impossibility of contradictory wills being simultaneously fulfilled.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the English translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that Baqillani uses a conditional structure: if A then B.
2
Analyze the hypothetical scenario
Consider the scenario Baqillani presents: two gods willing opposite things for the same body. Ask yourself: can both wills be fulfilled? If not, what does that imply about each god's power?
The key is that the scenario is logically possible, not that it actually happens.
3
Trace the logical conclusion
Follow the argument step by step from the impossibility of fulfilling both wills to the necessity of impotence. Then connect impotence to being created, and finally to the impossibility of multiple gods.
The argument ends with a statement about God's nature: He cannot be impotent.

Impotence (ʿajz) is a mark of being originated, and God, being eternal, cannot be impotent. Therefore, there can only be one God.

The Creator Is Alive
Baqillani proves the Creator is alive through a reductio argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Creator's life is necessarily implied by His knowledge and power, and how Baqillani uses a reductio argument to prove this.

Core Idea
Only a living being can be truly knowing and powerful.

Baqillani presents a concise but powerful proof that the Creator is alive. He begins with the premise that the Creator is all-knowing and all-powerful, which are attributes that can only belong to a living being. To reinforce this, he imagines a world where lifeless things could produce ordered actions — a scenario that would destroy our ability to trust any action as coming from a living agent. This simple yet devastating argument shows why atheistic or deistic positions that deny God's life are rationally untenable.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the English translation. Get a sense of the dialectical style: question, answer, clarification, conclusion.
2
Identify the main proof
Find the core statement: the Creator is knowing and powerful, and such a being must be alive. Notice the logical necessity: 'فالفاعل العالم القادر لا يكون إلا حياً'.
This is the central syllogism.
3
Examine the reductio
Baqillani argues that if lifeless beings could produce ordered actions (أفعال محكمات), then we would not be able to distinguish actions of living vs. dead. Trace this hypothetical and its absurd consequence.
The reductio strengthens the proof by showing the alternative is self-defeating.
4
Reflect on the absurdity
Consider the implication: if the opponent's view were true, even the opponent's own questioning would be unreliable. This shows the argument is not just theoretical but undermines all discourse.
Baqillani calls this 'ignorance and denial'.
5
Conclude with the necessity
The only consistent conclusion is that the Creator is knowing, powerful, and alive (عالماً قادراً حياً). This is necessary (وجب). Reflect on how this fits into the broader Islamic understanding of God.
This conclusion is a cornerstone of divine attributes.

Proving Divine Attributes: Hearing, Seeing, and Speech
Baqillani proves the Creator has hearing, seeing, and speech by elimination of defects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Baqillani proves that the Creator necessarily possesses the attributes of hearing, seeing, and speech, by reasoning from the absence of defects.

Core Idea
The Creator’s perfection requires all positive attributes; their opposites are impossible for the Eternal.

Imam al-Baqillani presents a concise but powerful argument for three essential divine attributes: hearing, seeing, and speech. He begins from the already-established attribute of life and shows that a living being must be capable of these attributes. If the Creator lacked them, He would be described by their opposites – defects that prove contingency. Since the Eternal cannot be contingent, He must be hearing, seeing, and speaking.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage slowly. Then read the English translation. Notice the question-and-answer structure. The passage begins with a hypothetical questioner, then provides the proof.
The question format is common in kalam works to address potential doubts.
2
Identify the core premise
The proof hinges on the fact that the Creator is already established as alive (hayy). Identify where this premise is stated and why it matters.
Baqillani assumes the proof of life was given earlier.
3
Understand the argument from opposites
If the Creator were not described by these attributes, then He would necessarily have their opposites (e.g., deafness, muteness). These opposites are defects (afat) and indicate contingency (hadath). Why are defects signs of contingency?
Contingent things are subject to imperfections; the Eternal must be perfect.
4
Apply the conclusion
Since it is impossible for the Eternal to have any defect, He must have the attributes of hearing, seeing, and speech. Confirm that you can articulate this argument in your own words.

The Impossibility of a Cause for Creation
Baqillani presents a disjunctive argument against creation for a cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's disjunctive argument showing that positing a cause for the world leads to absurd consequences.

Core Idea
If creation had a cause, it must be impossible.

Baqillani strengthens his case with a logical argument. He considers the hypothetical that God did act for a cause. That cause must be either eternal or originated. Both options lead to contradictions, proving that the original position—no cause—is necessary.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two possibilities
Baqillani presents a disjunction: if there were a cause, it must be either eternal (qadīm) or originated (muḥdath). Identify these two branches.
This is a classic kalam argument form called taqsīm (division).
2
Explore the eternal cause scenario
If the cause were eternal, the world would be eternal because an eternal cause necessitates an eternal effect. But the world is originated, so this is impossible.
Note the reasoning: no temporal gap between an eternal cause and its effect except the moment of bringing into existence, which implies the effect is also eternal.
3
Explore the originated cause scenario
If the cause is originated, then its own originator either acted for a cause or not. If for a cause, we get an infinite regress (tasalsul), making the world's existence impossible. If not for a cause, then it is possible for the originator to act without a cause and still be wise; by analogy, God can create all things without a cause.
This branch shows that even if an originated cause exists, it leads either to impossibility or to the very position Baqillani defends.
4
See the conclusion
Since both branches are absurd or lead to the same conclusion, the original position—that God created without a cause—is vindicated.
This is a reductio ad absurdum of the objector's claim.

If the cause is originated, then its own originator must have acted either for a cause or not. If for a cause, there is an infinite regress, making the world's existence impossible. If not for a cause, then it is possible that the originator acted without a cause and is still wise; by the same token, God can create all events without a cause and still be wise. Thus the objection fails.

Baqillani responds to the claim that God is a body meaning self-subsistent.

Proof of Divine Attributes from Human Attributes
Proof of divine attributes by logical analogy with human attributes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the logical relationship between a description and its attribute in humans is used to prove that God possesses real attributes.

Core Idea
If a description is true, its corresponding attribute must exist — for humans and for God.

This lesson addresses a fundamental question: Why do theologians insist that God has seven specific attributes? Imam al-Baqillani presents a rational proof based on the necessary connection between a description and its cause. By examining how we use words like 'living' and 'knowing' for humans, he shows that these descriptions are meaningless without real underlying attributes. The same logic applies to God.
