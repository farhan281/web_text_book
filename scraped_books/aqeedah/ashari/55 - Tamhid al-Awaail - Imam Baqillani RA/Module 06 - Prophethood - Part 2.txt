The Arabs' Inability to Match the Quran
Baqillani argues the Arabs' failure to oppose the Quran is a miracle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Arabs' avoidance of opposing the Quran, despite their eloquence and motivation, serves as the greatest proof of the Prophet's truthfulness.

Core Idea
The greatest proof of prophethood is the Arabs' silence against the Quran.

Baqillani begins by highlighting a striking historical fact: the Arabs, masters of eloquence, made no attempt to produce a surah like the Quran. This avoidance, given their ability and incentive, is a powerful sign of its divine origin. Let's explore his reasoning and see why this is considered a miracle.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice how Baqillani emphasizes the Arabs' knowledge of the Quran's unique composition.
2
Identify the two key observations
The passage makes two points: 1) the Arabs avoided opposing the Quran, 2) the Quran's composition differs from all human speech. Identify these in the text.
These two observations together form the miracle.
3
Understand why this proves truthfulness
Reflect on why their avoidance is a proof: if they could have matched it, they would have, since they wanted to refute the Prophet. Their failure shows it is beyond human capacity.
This is the core of the argument.

And in their avoidance of this altogether and their refraining from attempting to oppose a surah of it or to produce something like it, whether little or much, despite their knowledge that the composition of the Quran departs from all the meters of their speech and their compositions, is the greatest proof of his truthfulness, peace be upon him. Commentary
This passage presents two interconnected arguments for the miraculous nature of the Quran and the prophethood of Muhammad (PBUH). First, Baqillani notes that the Arabs, despite being masters of eloquence and having every motive to refute the Prophet, avoided attempting to produce anything like the Quran altogether. This avoidance, coupled with their knowledge that the Quran's composition surpasses all known poetic meters, is the greatest proof of his truthfulness. Second, he argues that the Prophet himself could not have acquired such eloquence through human means: he grew up among them without studying with scholars or practicing with experts, and his sudden mastery of languages—illustrated by the hypothetical of his going to sleep ignorant of a language and waking up fluent—breaks the natural course of things. Baqillani emphasizes that such acquisition in a short time is not possible by ordinary learning, intelligence, or cunning. He concludes that the very revelation of this speech and the Prophet's ability to articulate it, whether by direct inspiration or by a divinely created faculty, is among the most evident signs. Finally, he states a general principle: God only produces such miracles to prove the truthfulness of His messengers.

The Prophet's Miraculous Eloquence and Language Acquisition
Baqillani details the Prophet's miraculous acquisition of eloquence and languages.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Prophet's overnight fluency in any language is a divine miracle.

This complete transformation in a short time defies all natural laws of learning. Let's see how this becomes another clear sign of his prophethood.

Both are presented as breaks in custom.
3
Connect to the general principle
At the end, Baqillani states that God only shows such miracles to prove the truthfulness of messengers. Understand this as the overarching purpose.
This ties the entire argument together.

Refuting the Objection that the Quran is Mere Human Speech
Baqillani answers the objection that the Quran could be excellent human speech and proves its miraculous nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani refutes the objection that the Quran is merely excellent human speech by showing it exceeds the limits of human skill and belongs to a unique genre.

Core Idea
The Quran exceeds all human capacity and defies genre—proof of its divine origin.

In this passage, Baqillani addresses a common objection from non-Muslims: that the Quran is simply extraordinarily eloquent human speech. He systematically dismantles this by showing that the Quran surpasses any possible human achievement and does not fit any known Arabic literary form. This establishes the Quran as a miracle.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice the structure: an objection followed by two arguments.
The objectors suggest Muhammad's high eloquence could naturally produce the Quran.
2
Analyze the first argument
Identify Baqillani's claim that human skill has a known limit that does not break custom. The Arabs' inability to produce anything like the Quran despite intense motivation shows it is beyond ordinary skill.
This argument uses the concept of 'breaking custom' (kharq al-‘adah) to identify a miracle.
3
Analyze the second argument
Notice Baqillani's second point: the Quran is composed on a meter that does not match any known Arabic poetic or rhetorical form. If it were human, its genre would be recognizable.
The unique meter demonstrates the Quran is not from any known human composition.
4
Consider the evidence from Quraysh
Reflect on how Quraysh, the most eloquent Arabs, were utterly confused—calling the Quran magic, legends, etc. Their inability to classify it confirms its miraculous nature.
Conflicting accusations indicate they were at a loss.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. The objectors (Jews and Christians) propose that the Quran might simply be excellent human speech, with Muhammad being exceptionally eloquent and surpassing all others in rhetoric. Baqillani responds with two arguments. First, he argues that human skill in a craft has a known limit that does not break custom; normally, others can eventually catch up or at least rival it when challenged. But the Quran exceeds that limit entirely—the Arabs, despite their intense desire to refute him, their combined efforts, and their knowledge that he used the Quran as a proof, were utterly unable to produce anything comparable. This places the Quran outside the category of ordinary human achievement. Second, Baqillani points to the unique composition of the Quran: it is in Arabic but on a meter that does not match any known form of their speech (poetry, oratory, rajz, long meters, double meters). If it were within their genres, they would have identified it. Their failure to classify it, and their contradictory accusations (magic, teaching, myths), prove their confusion. Since Quraysh were the most eloquent and knowledgeable, their inability and confusion demonstrate that the Quran is a miracle, not merely excellent human speech.

Rebuttal of the Fear Argument for Mu'arada
Baqillani refutes the claim that fear prevented transmission of the challenge to the Qur'an.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the fear of the sword cannot explain the absence of transmitted challenges to the Qur'an, and how Baqillani extends this reasoning to defend all prophetic miracles.

Core Idea
Fear cannot block transmission if it does not block the claim itself.

Baqillani confronts a common objection: that the Prophet's opponents were too afraid to transmit any successful challenge to the Qur'an. He shows this argument is self-defeating and then extends it by analogy to earlier prophets. Let's read his refutation carefully.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing deeply. Then read the literal translation to grasp the argument flow.
Notice the compact style and the structure of the argument.
2
Identify Baqillani's main argument
Baqillani points out that if fear prevented transmission, it would also prevent the claim of opposition itself. Yet opponents did claim opposition, so fear alone cannot explain the absence of transmitted challenges.
This is the core logical point.
3
Notice the analogy with Moses and Jesus
Baqillani extends the argument by analogy: if the fear argument were valid, it would apply equally to the miracles of Moses and Jesus, leading to absurd consequences.
This strengthens the refutation by parity of reasoning.
4
Consider the implications
The argument is not limited to the Qur'an but defends all prophetic miracles. Reflect on how this reinforces the inimitability of revelation.

Commentary
This passage contains two main arguments. First, Bāqillānī argues that we cannot know with necessity (ḍarūra) that Moses uttered the claimed statement, because knowledge of one's intent in a statement depends on knowing the existence of the statement itself; since we do not know the existence of the statement, we cannot claim to know its intent necessarily. He then turns to the actual Jewish report, which is that Moses said: 'If you obey me, your kingdom will remain firm as the heavens and earth.' This report does not contain any mention of abrogation, perpetuity, or denial of future prophets. Bāqillānī notes that the Jewish claim of perpetuity is borrowed from Christian and Muslim discourse. He then argues that even the reported statement does not imply non-abrogation, because a conditional promise of reward for obedience does not prevent later abrogation of the command. He gives an analogy: a master who says 'If you obey me, I will honor you' can abrogate the command later while still maintaining the honor promised. The historical fact that the Children of Israel disobeyed and were punished—through the 'maskh' (transformation) for Sabbath violation and other punishments—shows that their kingdom did not remain due to disobedience, not because of non-abrogation. Thus, the Jewish claim is baseless.

Commentary
This passage is a continuous argumentative refutation and proof. First, Baqillani refutes the claim that Moses (peace be upon him) stated that his law is eternal and non-abrogable. He argues that the supposed proof from the verse 'If you obey me...' does not imply non-abrogation, because a conditional promise of reward can be made even if the command is later abrogated; the promise is still fulfilled. Moreover, the loss of the Israelites' kingdom was due to their disobedience, not because the law was abrogated. Second, he points out that Moses spoke Hebrew, and the translations into other languages are prone to error, so attributing specific phrases about perpetuity to him is unreliable. Then he addresses a potential objection from the opponent: 'What proof do you have for preventing a prophet after your prophet?' He answers with the mutawātir (mass-transmitted) hadith 'There is no prophet after me,' which the entire community has transmitted continuously from generation to generation. This hadith is unambiguous (muḥkam) and is not subject to specification; it negates all prophets after him, including those who would abrogate his law and those who would not. Finally, the opponent retorts that they have similar claims about Moses. The answer to that objection is already given in the preceding refutation: those claims are baseless due to linguistic and transmission issues.
