Reconciling Attributes: The Face of Allah as an Attribute of Essence and the Analogical Interpretation
Baqillani uses an analogy from common speech to explain the attribute of face.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the analogy of common speech ('the prince is turning towards so-and-so') helps explain that Allah's face is an attribute, not a limb, and that the correct interpretation avoids anthropomorphism while affirming the attribute.

Core Idea
The face of Allah is a real attribute, understood through the language of honor and mercy, not physicality.

After presenting the proof, Imam Baqillani further clarifies the interpretation by using an analogy from everyday Arabic speech. When we say 'the prince turned towards so-and-so,' we mean he showed kindness, not that he physically turned. Similarly, Allah's face and its turning are understood as expressions of mercy. This final lesson ties together the earlier evidence.

2

Baqillani uses this to show that language allows for non-physical attribution.
3
Reflect on the overall conclusion
Understand that the attributes of face and hands are real but free from anthropomorphism, as supported by all the evidence.
The reports from the Prophet and companions on this topic are abundant.

Various chapters on the attributes. Chapter on that Allah has a face and two hands. If a questioner says: What is the proof that Allah, may He be glorified and exalted, has a face and two hands? Know that it is established in the true report, and by it Allah said a description not in terms of form. As for the Book, His saying: 'And there remains the Face of your Lord, Possessor of Majesty and Honor.' Ibn al-Jawzi said in 'Deflecting the Doubt': The commentators said 'and your Lord remains' and likewise they said regarding His saying 'they desire His face' meaning they desire Him. And al-Dahhak and Abu Ubayda said: 'Everything is perishing except His Face' meaning except Him. This is the truth, the proof of which has been established, invalidating the statement of anthropomorphism. And He also said: 'Everything is perishing except His Face.' This verse is textual evidence that what is meant by 'face' is the essence, not an attribute nor a limb. Therefore, saying it is an attribute is not sound, before this verse which interprets the meaning of 'face', such that it leaves no room for speculation from anyone, whether he be a Khalafi or a Salafi. As for the statement about the particular limb, as is the doctrine of the anthropomorphists, it is also rejected by this verse. And the proofs have accumulated that whoever imagines limbs for his deity is upon the first idolatry, even if he outwardly claims guidance. As for the reports, they are many, including what Muslim, al-Bukhari and others narrated. The Messenger of Allah, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, said: 'Two gardens of silver, their vessels and what is in them, and two gardens of gold, their vessels and what is in them. And nothing prevents the people from looking at their Lord, may He be glorified and exalted, except the cloak of pride upon His face in the Garden of Eden.' His saying 'cloak of pride' means the attribute of pride. So He, through His pride and majesty, does not want any of His creation to see Him after the vision on the Day of Resurrection until He permits them to enter the Garden of Eden. When they enter it, He wants them to see Him, so they see Him while they are in the Garden of Eden, and He is not in the Garden—exalted is Allah from being contained by a place. And it is narrated from Ibn Umar, may Allah be pleased with them both, in what al-Bayhaqi narrates, that he saw a man praying while looking around in his prayer. Ibn Umar said: 'Indeed Allah, may He be glorified and exalted, turns towards His servant with His face as long as he turns towards Him. When he turns away, He turns away from him.' And know that there is no turning towards, turning away, or turning aside in the attributes of Allah's essence; rather that is in His attributes of action. And it is as though mercy that belongs to the face is attached to it with the attachment of an attribute by its requirement, coming to him from the direction of the worshipper's face. So by it is expressed the turning towards of that mercy and its turning away, and the turning towards of the face and its turning away, because of the attachment of the face, which is an attribute, to it. And Allah knows best. What shows the correctness of this interpretation is what is narrated from Abu Dharr, may Allah be pleased with him, raising it to the Prophet, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, who said: 'When one of you stands for prayer, mercy faces him, so he should not touch pebbles.' And it is common in people's speech: 'The prince is turning towards so-and-so,' meaning he is turning to him with beneficence, and 'turning away from so-and-so,' meaning he is abandoning his beneficence to him and turning away his favor from him. And Allah knows best. The reports about the meaning of this from the Prophet, his Companions, and their followers, may Allah be pleased with them all, are many.

Commentary
This passage is a structured objection-and-answer argument. It begins with a chapter heading affirming that Allah has a face and two hands. Then an objector asks for the proof. The answer is given in parts: First, it establishes that these are attributes declared in true reports, not physical forms. Then it presents Quranic evidence, specifically the verse 'And there remains the Face of your Lord...', citing interpretations from early scholars (Ibn al-Jawzi, al-Dahhak, Abu Ubayda) that 'face' here means the essence of Allah, not a limb. It argues that this verse refutes anthropomorphism. Then it presents Hadith evidence, including the famous hadith about the two gardens and the 'cloak of pride' on Allah's face, explaining that this does not imply a physical face but an attribute. It also narrates a tradition from Ibn Umar about Allah turning to a worshipper with His face, clarifying that this is an attribute of action, not of essence. Finally, it supports this interpretation with the hadith of Abu Dharr about mercy facing the worshipper, and uses common Arabic speech as an analogy. The conclusion is that these attributes are real but not anthropomorphic.

Refuting Metaphorical Interpretations of 'Hand' in the Quran
Baqillani refutes the metaphorical interpretation of 'yad' as power or favor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Baqillani rejects metaphorical readings of 'yad' (hand) in the verse about Adam's creation and how he establishes that it must refer to actual divine attributes.

Core Idea
Metaphor for 'hand' undermines Adam's honor; only actual attributes preserve it.

In this passage, Baqillani responds to those who claim that 'yad' in the Quranic verse 'I created with My two hands' means power or favor. He systematically shows why such interpretations fail, using linguistic and theological reasoning. Read on to see how he defends the literal attribution of 'hand' as a divine attribute without falling into anthropomorphism.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Learn what the objectors claim: that 'yad' can mean power or favor based on linguistic usage.
The objectors are trying to avoid anthropomorphism by interpreting 'hand' metaphorically.
2
Follow Baqillani's rejection
See why he rejects each possible interpretation, especially the one that would make Iblis share in Adam's honor.
Baqillani argues that interpreting 'yad' as power or favor would imply that both Adam and Iblis share that attribute, nullifying Adam's special status.
3
Grasp the conclusion
Understand that 'yad' here indicates two specific attributes, preserving the special status of Adam.
Baqillani establishes a general principle: whenever 'yad' is mentioned with specification, it indicates an attribute specially attached to the thing mentioned.

For if they say: Why do you not accept that the meaning in His saying: 'I created with My two hands' is that He created him by His power or His favor? Because 'hand' in the language can mean power, ownership, favor, connection, and the limb. As for His saying, the Most High: 'O Iblis, what prevented you from prostrating to that which I created with My two hands?' It is not permissible to interpret it as the limb, because the Creator, exalted is His majesty, is One, and division is not permissible for Him. Nor [can it be interpreted as] power, ownership, favor, or connection, because then partnership (al-ish̲tirāk) would occur between the friend of Adam and his enemy Iblis, thus nullifying the mentioned preference of him due to the nullification of the meaning of specification (al-takhṣīṣ). Then there is no nullification of the place of preference of Adam over Iblis, because if specification is found for him in a meaning not shared by Iblis, it does not harm that someone else shares with him in that meaning, after that Iblis does not share it with him. And Allah knows best.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer unit. The objectors propose that 'hand' in the verse could metaphorically mean power (qudra) or favor (ni‘ma), based on linguistic usage. Baqillani responds by first listing the possible meanings of 'yad' in Arabic: power, ownership, favor, connection (sila), and the literal limb. Then he argues that for the specific verse about Adam, none of these common interpretations work. Interpreting 'yad' as a limb is impossible because Allah is not a body. Interpreting it as power or favor would imply that both Adam and Iblis share in that attribute, thereby undermining the special honor of Adam over Iblis. So the only remaining possibility is that 'yad' here refers to two actual divine attributes (ṣifāt) that are specifically attached to the creation of Adam as an honor. This preserves the distinction between Adam and Iblis. Baqillani concludes with a general principle: whenever 'yad' is mentioned with specification (takhṣīṣ), it indicates an attribute specially attached to the thing mentioned. Thus, the attribute of 'hand' is established without compromising tawhid or the specificity of honor.

Imam Baqillani tackles a key question in Islamic theology: Is God's eternity identical with His essence, or is it a distinct attribute? Commentary
This passage contains a rational proof (dalīl ʿaqlī) that God's eternity (baqā') is an attribute distinct from His essence, i.e., one of the essential attributes (ṣifāt al-dhāt). The objection is phrased as a question: 'What is the proof that eternity is from the attributes of His essence?' The answer proceeds in several steps: First, God has never ceased being eternal, because He has always existed without coming into being (ḥudūth). Second, we observe that any created being that continues to exist (bāqī minnā) does so only by virtue of an attribute of eternity (bi-baqā'). The proof for this is the impossibility of something being eternal at the moment of its origination (istiḥālat baqā' al-shay' fī ḥāl ḥudūthihi). In other words, if something comes into existence, it cannot at that very moment be eternal; eternity requires continued existence beyond the moment of origination. Therefore, if God were eternal by Himself (i.e., without an attribute), He would have to be eternal at the moment of origination, which is contradictory. So it follows that He is eternal through an eternal attribute. The conclusion: since God is pre-eternal (qadīm), it is impossible that His essence itself be eternity or that it be like the attributes (i.e., that His essence is simply the attribute of eternity without distinction). Hence, eternity is a real, distinct attribute of His essence.

Refuting the Mu'tazilite Objection to Beatific Vision
Baqillani refutes the Mu'tazilite objection from the verse 'لا تدركه الأبصار'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's refutation of the Mu'tazilite claim that the verse 'لا تدركه الأبصار' negates the possibility of seeing Allah in the afterlife.

Core Idea
The praise in the verse is about Allah's perception, not about being unseen.

The Mu'tazilites argued that if Allah praised Himself for not being seen, then seeing Him in the afterlife would contradict that praise. Baqillani responds by analyzing the structure of the verse and showing that the actual praise lies in Allah's comprehensive perception, not in the negation. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The Mu'tazilites claim that the verse 'لا تدركه الأبصار' is a praise, so if believers see Allah in the afterlife, that praise would cease, which is impossible.

2

Baqillani points out that the praise is actually in 'وهو يدرك الأبصار' (He perceives all sight), not in the negation. The negation alone is not praiseworthy, as many things are unseen without being praised.

The objector suggests both attributes together form the praise. Baqillani replies that each attribute must be praiseworthy on its own, which 'not being seen' is not.

Baqillani gives an analogy: if Allah said 'I am knowing, known, causing to exist, existent', the praise lies in 'knowing' and 'causing to exist', not in 'known' and 'existent' which are shared by others.
This shows that the negation is not the source of praise.
5
Conclude the argument
Thus, the verse does not negate the beatific vision; it only praises Allah's all-encompassing perception.

Therefore, both objections are invalid, and God's will for all things is established.

Answering the Coercion and Incapacity Objections
Baqillani responds to two objections regarding God's will and incapacity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's symmetrical argument against objections that try to limit God's will to coercive scenarios or that claim incapacity if His will is not fulfilled.

Core Idea
If voluntary will can fail without deficiency, then coercive will can too—so objections prove nothing.

Having established that God wills all actions, Baqillani anticipates two objections. Opponents might argue that God's will for disbelief is only conditional on coercion, or that if His coercive will failed, He would be incapable. Baqillani dismantles both by showing that the same reasoning would apply to voluntary will, thus the objections are self-defeating.

Guided Path
1
Understand the first objection
The opponent suggests that God's will for disbelief might only be through coercion (إكراه), meaning He compels them to disbelieve. Baqillani's response uses a parity argument.
2
Follow the parity argument
Baqillani says: If it is possible that God wills voluntary belief but it does not happen without deficiency, then the same could be said for coercive will. So the objection does not limit God's will.
The key is that deficiency is not attributed to God in either case.
3
Understand the second objection
The opponent then says: If coercive will fails, that shows incapacity to compel. Baqillani again gives a symmetrical answer: if voluntary will fails, that would show incapacity to provide incentives (لطف), which is also impossible for God.
Thus, the objection is invalid because it would apply equally to voluntary will.
4
Reflect on the overall argument
Notice how Baqillani uses logic to show that the opponents' objections are not specific to coercive will—they would also undermine voluntary will, which the opponents accept. So the objections fail.

God can command a test without willing its completion.

This passage addresses a common objection: if God commands something, must He will it? Baqillani argues that the story of Abraham and the near-sacrifice of Ishmael shows that God’s command can be a test, not an expression of His will for the act. By analyzing the event, he demonstrates that God’s wisdom is preserved.

فإن قالوا : فكيف يكون أمراً بما لا يريده ويكون بذلك حكيماً؟
قيل لهم : هذا مما قد ورد به القرآن واتفق عليه سلف الأمة. لأن الله تعالى أمر إبراهيم بذبح إسماعيل، عليهما السلام، ولم يُرد ذلك منه، بل نهاه عنه بعد أمره به وفداه مما أمره بفعله من ذبحه. ولو كان قد فعل الذبح، لم يكن لافتدائه معنى. ولو كان إنما أمره بالإضجاع وإمرار السكين فقط دون الذبح، لم يكن ذلك امتحاناً منه ولم يكن لقوله : إن هذا لهو البلاء المبين معنى ولا كان لافتدائه من إضجاع قد وقع معنى. وكذلك لو ذبحه ثم التحم، لم يكن للفداء معنى ولا لبلائه معنى. وكذلك لو كان قد منعه من ذبحه بقلب صفحة عنقه نحاساً، يقوله بعض جهالهم، لكان عندهم بذلك سفيهاً ومكلّفاً للفعل مع العجز عنه والمنع منه وذلك عندهم باطل.
Issue: If they say: 'How can He command what He does not will and yet be wise?' Say to them: This is something that has been mentioned in the Quran and upon which the predecessors of the community agreed. Because God commanded Abraham to slaughter Ishmael, peace be upon them, and He did not will that from him; rather, He forbade him from it after He commanded him and ransomed him from what He had commanded him to do, i.e., his slaughter. And if he had performed the slaughter, there would be no meaning to his ransom. And if He had only commanded him to lie down and pass the knife without the slaughter, that would not have been a test from Him, and there would be no meaning to His saying: 'Indeed, this was a manifest trial' (Quran 37:106), nor would there be a meaning to his ransom from a lying down that had occurred. And likewise, if he had slaughtered him and then the flesh healed, there would be no meaning to the ransom nor to his trial. And likewise, if He had prevented him from slaughtering him by turning the side of his neck into copper — as some of their ignoramuses say — then according to them He would be foolish and imposing an act while being incapable of it and preventing it, and that is false according to them.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer unit. The objection states: How can God command something He does not will and still be considered wise? The answer begins by grounding itself in revelation and consensus: the Quran and the early community affirm this. Baqillani then provides a detailed theological analysis of the story of Abraham and the near-sacrifice of Ishmael. God commanded Abraham to slaughter his son, but He did not will that the slaughter actually occur; instead, He forbade it and provided a ransom. If the slaughter had taken place, the ransom would be meaningless. If the command was only to put the knife to the neck without slaughter, it would not be a genuine trial, and the Quranic verse calling it a 'manifest trial' would lose meaning. Similarly, if the slaughter had occurred and then the wound healed, the ransom would be pointless. Finally, Baqillani rejects the absurd suggestion that God prevented the act by turning Ishmael's neck to copper, calling it the view of ignoramuses, which would imply God is foolish and imposes impossible tasks.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: God must will whatever He commands.
Correction: God may command something as a test without willing its actual occurrence, as shown in the story of Abraham.

Key Takeaway
God's command is not always an expression of His will; it can serve a testing purpose, and His wisdom is not compromised.
