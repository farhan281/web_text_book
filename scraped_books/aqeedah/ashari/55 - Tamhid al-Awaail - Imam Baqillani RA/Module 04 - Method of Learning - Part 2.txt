Three Meanings of 'Created in Truth'
Baqillani gives three interpretations of the Qur'anic phrase 'except with the truth'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three theological meanings Imam Baqillani extracts from the Qur'anic phrase 'except with the truth' regarding creation.

Core Idea
The phrase 'with the truth' carries layered meanings: divine speech, justice, and purpose.

The Qur'anic verse 'We did not create the heavens and the earth except with the truth' is rich with meaning. Imam Baqillani, a leading Ash'ari theologian, unpacks this phrase in three ways, each highlighting a different aspect of God's relationship to creation. This lesson explores those interpretations.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let Baqillani's own words come through before you interpret.
Notice that the passage is structured as a series of possibilities: 'ya'ni', 'yumkin', 'yuhtamil'.
2
Identify the three interpretations
The passage presents three distinct interpretations. Find each one and note what 'haqq' refers to in each case.
The first is explicit, the second and third are introduced as possible meanings.
3
Reflect on the theological implications
Ask yourself why each interpretation matters. How does each one answer a different question about God's action in creating?
This connects to the broader theme of God's attributes and justice.

وقوله وما خلقنا السموات والأرض وما بينهما إلا بالحق يعني أنه ما خلق ذلك إلا بقوله وكلامه الذي هو الحق. ويمكن أن يكون عنى: إني ما خلقتهما ظالماً لخلقهما ولا تجاوزت بفعلهما أمر آمر ولا زجر زاجر. ويحتمل أن يكون عنى: إني ما خلقتهما وكلّفت أهلهما إلا وأنا مريد لإثابة الطائعين وعقوبة العاصين.

Commentary
This passage is an exegetical discussion. Baqillani presents three possible meanings for the phrase 'إلا بالحق' in the Qur'anic verse. The first interpretation is that 'haqq' refers to God's speech and command—He created by His word which is truth. The second interpretation is that 'haqq' means justice: God did not create unjustly or arbitrarily. The third interpretation links 'haqq' to the purpose of creation: reward for obedience and punishment for disobedience. Each interpretation is distinct and shows different theological emphases.

Three Views on General Plural Forms
Baqillani enumerates three scholarly views on the interpretation of an unqualified plural form.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three main scholarly positions on interpreting an unqualified plural form in Islamic legal theory.

Core Idea
A general plural doesn't reveal its intention—scholars disagree on what it means without context.

Baqillani opens this lesson by surveying how different schools of thought approach a plural form that appears without a specifying indicator. The disagreement reveals a fundamental tension in uṣūl al-fiqh: should we assume generality, particularity, or suspend judgment? This passage introduces three distinct approaches that set the stage for the broader discussion of takhṣīṣ.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three groups
Read the Arabic and translation, then list the three positions: the wāqifiyya, aṣḥāb al-khuṣūṣ, and the majority of jurists.
Pay attention to the Arabic names for each group.
2
Understand each position
For each group, ask: what do they do with the plural? Do they take it as general, particular, or suspend?
3
Connect to the bigger picture
This disagreement is foundational for later discussions on specification. Notice how the majority's view (generality) is the one Baqillani's school adopts.
This lesson sets the stage for the rules of takhṣīṣ.

Commentary
This passage is a survey of scholarly positions on the interpretation of general plural forms and the rules of specification (takhṣīṣ). This lesson shows the nuanced methodology of Islamic legal theory in interpreting general scriptural statements.

The Companions' Approval as Proof of Valid Appointment
Baqillani presents the historical evidence of Companions' unanimous approval of Abu Bakr's appointment.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani uses the Companions' silent approval as evidence for the validity of Abu Bakr's appointment of Umar.

Commentary
This passage is a proof establishing the validity of the caliphal appointment (عهد) of Umar by Abu Bakr, extending to any just imam appointing his successor. The author begins by stating the chapter heading: 'Evidence for the validity of the appointment from Abu Bakr to Umar, and from every just imam to one who is suitable.' He then presents a hypothetical objection: an interlocutor asks for the evidence that Abu Bakr's appointment of Umar is valid and equivalent to the initial contract of imamate. The answer provides several arguments.

First argument: The Companions and Muslims were present when Abu Bakr appointed Umar, and they all approved and deemed his decision correct. No one objected at the time or later, neither in his assembly nor after his death. If the appointment had been a religious error, they would have hastened to correct him. Their silence on this issue, while they did criticize Abu Bakr on other matters (like the appointment of a harsh person), proves that they regarded the appointment as correct. The author notes that discussing the quality of the appointee is secondary to discussing the validity of the appointment itself; if the appointment were invalid, the whole discussion would be moot.

Second argument: The community (أمة) as a whole cannot agree on error. Since all the Companions and Muslims at the time accepted the appointment, it would be impossible for them to have remained silent about a manifest error. Their acceptance implies that the appointment was correct and binding.

Third argument: Consensus of the 'people of choice' (أهل الاختيار), who are the authorities on the imamate, holds that the imam has the right to appoint his successor. No known scholar from this group has denied this, and there is no reliable report otherwise.

Fourth argument (rational): If a just man who is not an imam can initiate a contract for an imam, then an imam, who holds a higher position, certainly has that right. The imam's status does not diminish his capacity to appoint; rather, it reinforces it. The contract is essentially based on the suitability of the person, not on any additional authority needed beyond being qualified.

Thus, the author concludes that the validity of Abu Bakr's appointment of Umar is firmly established by multiple lines of evidence.

And it is also indicated by the consensus of the people of choice (ahl al-ikhtiyar), who are the people of truth, regarding the imamate: that the imam has the right to appoint an imam after him. And we do not know of any of them who denies that, nor is it established from any of them through an anomalous narration or a reported statement that he did not hold it or adopt it. And it is also indicated and clarified by our knowledge that if the just imam were not an imam and were a man from the subjects, he would have the right to initiate the contract for one fit for the imamate. And if that is the case, then his being an imam does not lower him from this rank, so it is necessary that he has the right to contract for an imam after him and appoint him, just as he had the right to initiate the contract for him, because the contract in reality is a contract upon a quality. So by that what we have said is established.

Commentary
This passage is a proof establishing the validity of the caliphal appointment (عهد) of Umar by Abu Bakr, extending to any just imam appointing his successor. The author begins by stating the chapter heading: 'Evidence for the validity of the appointment from Abu Bakr to Umar, and from every just imam to one who is suitable.' He then presents a hypothetical objection: an interlocutor asks for the evidence that Abu Bakr's appointment of Umar is valid and equivalent to the initial contract of imamate. The answer provides several arguments.

First argument: The Companions and Muslims were present when Abu Bakr appointed Umar, and they all approved and deemed his decision correct. No one objected at the time or later, neither in his assembly nor after his death. If the appointment had been a religious error, they would have hastened to correct him. Their silence on this issue, while they did criticize Abu Bakr on other matters (like the appointment of a harsh person), proves that they regarded the appointment as correct. The author notes that discussing the quality of the appointee is secondary to discussing the validity of the appointment itself; if the appointment were invalid, the whole discussion would be moot.

Second argument: The community (أمة) as a whole cannot agree on error. Since all the Companions and Muslims at the time accepted the appointment, it would be impossible for them to have remained silent about a manifest error. Their acceptance implies that the appointment was correct and binding.

Third argument: Consensus of the 'people of choice' (أهل الاختيار), who are the authorities on the imamate, holds that the imam has the right to appoint his successor. No known scholar from this group has denied this, and there is no reliable report otherwise.

Fourth argument (rational): If a just man who is not an imam can initiate a contract for an imam, then an imam, who holds a higher position, certainly has that right. The imam's status does not diminish his capacity to appoint; rather, it reinforces it. The contract is essentially based on the suitability of the person, not on any additional authority needed beyond being qualified.

Thus, the author concludes that the validity of Abu Bakr's appointment of Umar is firmly established by multiple lines of evidence.
