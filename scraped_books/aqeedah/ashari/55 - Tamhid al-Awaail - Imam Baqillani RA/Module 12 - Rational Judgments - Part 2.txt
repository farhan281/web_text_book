The Self-Evident Distinction Proves Ability with the Act
Baqillani argues that the distinction between voluntary and forced actions proves ability is concurrent with the act

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's argument that the inherent distinction between voluntary and involuntary actions demonstrates that human ability (qudra) is concurrent with the act.

Core Idea
Your own experience of choice proves ability is with the act.

Imam Baqillani presents a proof from immediate self-awareness: every person can tell the difference between an action they choose and one forced upon them. He argues that this distinction cannot be explained by genus, knowledge, locus, or will alone. Therefore, the defining factor must be the presence of power (qudra) at the moment of the act.

3
Understand the conclusion
Since the only remaining explanation is the presence of power (qudra) with the chosen act, Baqillani concludes that ability must be concurrent with the act. Reflect on how this supports the Ash'ari view of kasb.
This conclusion is the main theological point.

The Impossibility of Accidental Powers Enduring
Baqillani argues against accident endurance using a two-branch dilemma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Baqillani argues that accidental powers cannot endure and how his dilemma refutes the possibility.

Core Idea
Accidents cannot endure; their endurance would break their nature as transient entities.

In this passage, Baqillani addresses a crucial question in kalam: why can't a power (like the power to act) persist before the act? The opponent challenges the Ash'ari position that powers are created only at the moment of action. Baqillani responds with a tight logical dilemma that exposes the impossibility of accident endurance. By analyzing this argument, you will see how kalam uses basic ontological categories to defend its view of divine omnipotence.

Guided Path

فإن قال قائل : ولم قلتم إنه لا يجوز أن تبقى إلى حين وجود الفعل، ولا شيء من الأعراض؟ قيل له : لأنه لو جاز بقاؤها، لكانت إنما تبقى لنفسها أو لعلة . فلو بقيت لنفسها، لبقيت في حال حدوثها وذلك محال ولو بقيت لعلة، لوجب أن تقوم بها العلل وذلك يوجب أن تكون جسماً أو جوهراً ليس بعرض وذلك فاسد لما قدمناه من قبل.
Then if a speaker says: And why did you say that it is not permissible for them [i.e., powers] to remain until the time of the act's existence, nor any of the accidents? It is said to him: Because if their endurance were permissible, they would either endure by themselves or by a cause. If they endured by themselves, they would have endured at the moment of their occurrence, and that is impossible. And if they endured by a cause, then it would be necessary that causes subsist in them, and that would require them to be a body or a substance, not an accident, and that is invalid according to what we have established previously.
Source pages p. 329

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the possibility that accidents (including the power for acquisition) can endure (baqa') across time. The opponent asks for the reason why powers—indeed any accident—cannot persist until the moment the act occurs.

Baqillani's answer is a two-branch dilemma: if an accident were to endure, its endurance would have to be either (1) self-subsisting (li nafsiha) or (2) caused by something else (li 'illa). 

- If self-subsisting, then the accident would have to endure from the very moment it comes into existence (fi hal huduthiha). But if it endured only later, not from its first moment, that would be arbitrary. However, an accident's existence and non-endurance are simultaneous by definition; hence enduring from the start is impossible because that would contradict its nature as a transient accident.
- If the endurance is caused by a cause, then that cause would need to subsist in the accident itself. But only bodies or substances can have causes subsisting in them (i.e., inhere in them). This would transform the accident into a body or substance (jism or jawhar), which contradicts its definition as an accident. This is invalid based on earlier established principles (that accidents do not subsist in other accidents, and a body is necessary for inherence).

Thus, the endurance of any accident is impossible, and therefore the power to act cannot persist before the act; it occurs anew at the time of the act.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the impossibility of lying by a group that meets the conditions of tawatur (mass transmission). It begins with an objection: 'What is the evidence that lying is impossible for the number of people who establish tawatur?' The answer recalls an earlier point about the transmission of the miracles of the messengers. The core argument is based on 'adah (customary experience). Custom does not allow that such a large group would collectively agree (ijtima') to transmit a lie about something they claim to have witnessed directly, nor would they all agree to conceal what they know. If they were lying, it would be impossible for them to maintain the secret over a long period without the truth emerging. Moreover, when they are alone, they would confess that they fabricated it. This is analogous to the impossibility of a large group agreeing to harm themselves collectively (e.g., disfiguring their faces, exposing their private parts, divorcing their wives). While one or two individuals might do such things, a large group cannot maintain such extraordinary behavior. Thus, the tawatur report must be true.

Commentary
This passage is a proof. It begins by opening a chapter on establishing tawatur and the impossibility of lying for its transmitters. Baqillani's Proof that Knowledge from Tawatur is Necessary
Baqillani proves that knowledge from tawatur is necessarily acquired by direct experience.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's argument that knowledge from tawatur is necessary (daruri) because it is felt as certain as sensory knowledge and shared by all people including non-specialists.

Core Idea
Knowledge from tawatur is as immediate and undeniable as seeing with your own eyes.

Baqillani anticipates a critical objection: how do we know that knowledge from tawatur is necessary? He answers with two powerful observations. First, we experience such knowledge with the same immediacy and certainty as sensory perception. Second, this knowledge is not limited to scholars but is shared even by women, common folk, and detractors who lack theoretical reasoning. This passage establishes the epistemological foundation for relying on tawatur as a source of certain knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Start by reading the objection and the first part of Baqillani's answer. Notice how he compares knowledge from tawatur to sensory and introspective knowledge.
The objection sets up the question: what proves that this knowledge is necessary?
2
Examine the second argument
Read the second argument about women, common people, and disparagers. Ask yourself why their involvement proves necessity.
This argument appeals to universality—if only scholars had it, it might be acquired through reasoning.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
See how both arguments together establish that the knowledge is necessary. Reflect on why this matters for Islamic theology.
Necessary knowledge is indubitable and does not require further proof.
