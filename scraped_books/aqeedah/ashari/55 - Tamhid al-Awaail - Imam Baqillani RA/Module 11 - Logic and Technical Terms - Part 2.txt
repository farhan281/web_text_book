Reports of Impossibilities and Their Sources of Truth
Baqillani explains reports about impossibilities and their sources of truth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani classifies reports about impossibilities and why only infallible sources can be truthful about them.

Core Idea
Reports of impossibilities are always false from ordinary sources but never from God or prophets.

Baqillani begins by examining the most extreme type of report: those that claim something rationally impossible. He explains why such reports are always false when coming from ordinary humans, but then makes an important exception for prophets and God. This lesson unpacks his argument and shows how tawatur and doubt affect reliability.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the examples
Read the Arabic passage and its translation, noting the various examples Baqillani gives of impossible reports: things contrary to senses, habits, or rational necessities.
Examples include resurrection, staff turning into a snake, and two opposites in one place.
2
Distinguish between ordinary and infallible sources
Notice that Baqillani states impossible reports are always lies from ordinary people, but can be true from God, prophets, or those known to never lie.
The key is the certainty of the impossibility versus the trustworthiness of the reporter.
3
Understand the role of tawatur and doubt
Consider why a group achieving tawatur cannot be lying about such reports, but a group under doubt might inadvertently spread falsehoods.
Tawatur requires observation without coercion; doubt undermines knowledge.

Commentary
This passage defines and discusses the first type of report: reports about impossibilities (mumtani'). Baqillani begins by listing examples of impossible reports: things contrary to sensory experience (like claiming non-existence of what we see), contrary to habitual courses (like resurrection, transformation of a staff into a snake, or a river into gold), and contrary to rational necessities (like two opposites in one place or a body in two places). However, such impossible reports may occur from a group that, due to a doubt (shubha) entering their minds, might transmit incorrectly even if they are truthful in intention. Thus, the passage establishes that reports about impossibilities are only reliable when the source is infallible.

Defining Possible Reports and Their Examples
Baqillani defines the third type of report and gives examples of possible matters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what constitutes a possible report (al-mumkin) and be able to identify examples from daily life and theological matters.

Core Idea
A possible report is one that could be true or false in itself.

Imam Baqillani categorizes reports into three types; here he explains the third type: reports about things that are rationally possible. These are matters that can conceivably be either true or false, and he provides a range of examples from mundane events to religious matters. Understanding this category is essential for applying the correct methodology of judgment.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the first part of the passage carefully. Notice how Baqillani defines the third type and lists examples.
The examples include acts of worship, natural events, personal situations, and religious claims.
2
Identify the key term 'ممكن'
Focus on the phrase 'خبر عن ممكن في العقل'. What does it mean for something to be possible in the intellect? Baqillani explains it as something that can be true and can be false.
This is the opposite of necessary or impossible.
3
Reflect on the examples
Consider each example: acts of worship, death, rain, location of a person, designation of a successor. Why are these considered possible? Think about why they could be true or false.
Note that these are not inherently certain; they require evidence to be known.

The Three Rulings for Judging Possible Reports
Baqillani explains the three possible rulings based on evidence and the exception for infallible sources.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three rulings for judging a possible report based on evidence, including the special case where the source is infallible.

Core Idea
Evidence determines whether a possible report is certain truth, certain falsehood, or must be suspended.

After defining possible reports, Baqillani presents the method for determining their truthfulness. The ruling depends entirely on the presence of evidence (dalil). He outlines three scenarios: when evidence supports truth, when evidence indicates falsehood, and when no evidence exists. Additionally, he notes an important exception: reports from infallible sources are accepted without external evidence. This passage provides a clear rational framework for handling contingent information.

2
Identify the three cases

These are based on the principle that judgment follows evidence.
3
Understand the exception
Baqillani adds that reports from God, His Messenger, or an infallible source, as well as reports from a group based on direct observation, are established without further evidence. Why?
This is because such sources are inherently trustworthy or the observation is unmediated.

First Condition of Tawatur: Necessary Knowledge from Direct Perception
Baqillani states the first condition for tawatur: necessary knowledge from direct perception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first condition for tawatur: that transmitters must have necessary knowledge (ʿilm ḍarūra) gained from direct sensory experience, not from reasoning or inference.

Core Idea
Tawatur requires direct, not inferred, knowledge.

Baqillani opens the chapter on the qualities of tawatur transmitters with a question-and-answer format. He establishes the crucial first condition: the transmitters must know what they report through necessary knowledge (ʿilm ḍarūra) gained by observation, hearing, or internal origination without reasoning. This ensures their report produces certain knowledge in the listener.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening Q&A
Notice how Baqillani uses a dialectical question-and-answer style to introduce the topic. The questioner asks whether tawatur transmitters must have specific qualities, and the answer is affirmative.
This format clarifies the logical structure.
2
Identify the first condition
The first condition is that transmitters must know what they transmit with necessary knowledge (ʿilm ḍarūra) from observation, hearing, or something originating in the soul without reflection or reasoning. This is the core of the lesson.
Necessary knowledge is immediate and indubitable, unlike inferential knowledge.
3
Understand the distinction for reasoned matters
Baqillani explains that matters known through reasoning (like the temporality of bodies or the existence of God) are not transmitted by tawatur in the same way. Reports about reasoned matters do not produce certain knowledge for opponents because God's custom does not make it so. Only those who engage in the reasoning can verify it.
This safeguards against conflating direct transmission with theological arguments.

The Second Condition: Number Exceeding Investigable Witnesses

Commentary
This passage presents the second condition for the people of tawatur: their number must exceed certain thresholds that are associated with legal commands to investigate the reporters' integrity. Baqillani argues by looking at divine commands. God commanded us to investigate the truthfulness of a single witness (shāhid wāḥid), and similarly for two witnesses or a man and two women in cases of adultery, and for four witnesses in adultery. The paragraph concludes by restating that the number must be such that anyone who reports from observation and is like them in multitude produces necessary knowledge.

Third Condition: Continuity Across Generations

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Tawatur requires an unbroken chain where every generation is equally reliable.

Baqillani now adds a crucial condition: the chain of transmitters must be continuous from the first generation to the last. Without this continuity, we cannot be certain that the report has been preserved intact. This short passage clarifies that all links, from the earliest to the latest, must be alike in their qualifications.
