Good deeds are signs, not causes of reward
Baqillani concludes that good deeds are signs of felicity, not causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why righteous deeds are signs of divine grace, not rational causes that obligate reward, and how this aligns with the creed of Ahl al-Sunna.

Core Idea
Deeds are signs of felicity, not causes of reward.

This concluding passage from Imam Baqillani ties together the discussion on divine grace and human action. He emphasizes that good deeds are not automatic causes of reward but rather signs that point to God's favor. By citing a prophetic hadith and a Quranic verse, he establishes this as the orthodox Sunni position, rejecting any rational obligation upon God.

God's Forgiveness for Disbelievers: The Role of Prophetic Teaching and Consensus
Baqillani responds to the objection that God might forgive disbelievers, citing prophetic teaching and consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why, according to Baqillani, the possibility of God forgiving disbelievers is ruled out not by reason but by specific prophetic teaching and the consensus of the Muslim community.

Core Idea
Revelation and consensus, not reason alone, close the door to forgiveness for disbelievers.

In the previous lesson, we saw that reason alone permits God to forgive anyone. But does that mean disbelievers could be forgiven? Baqillani now addresses this objection. The answer introduces two crucial sources of theological knowledge: the explicit teaching of the Prophet and the consensus of the community transmitted with certainty.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the objection carefully: if God has threatened disbelievers with Hell, what prevents Him from forgiving them despite the threat? The objector assumes rational permissibility.
Notice that the objection does not deny the threat; it questions its finality.
2
Analyze the two answers
Identify the two sources that prevent forgiveness: (1) the explicit teaching (tawqif) of the Prophet, and (2) the consensus (ijma') of the Muslim community, transmitted via tawatur. Both are infallible.
The consensus is not a fallible agreement; it is a divinely protected transmission of prophetic knowledge.
3
Recognize the implication
Baqillani concludes that without these transmitted evidences, forgiveness would be permissible. This shows that the prohibition is based on revelation, not reasoning.
This reinforces the Ash'ari reliance on transmitted sources for eschatological matters.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. The objector asks: even though God has threatened disbelievers with Hell, what prevents the possibility that He might still forgive some or all of them? The author answers that what prevents this is two things: First, the explicit teaching (توقيف) of the Prophet that disbelievers will not be forgiven. Second, the consensus (إجماع) of the Muslim community, which is protected from error, that the entire nation has transmitted from the Prophet with certainty (تواتر) that all disbelievers are in Hell forever. The community knows this necessarily from the Prophet's religion. The author emphasizes that the consensus covers all disbelievers, and the Prophet's intent was to include all of them in the threat. Without this consensus and teaching, the rational permissibility of forgiveness (established in the previous lesson) would apply even to disbelievers. Thus, the restriction is based on revelation and consensus, not on reason alone.

Consensus on Disbelievers and the Muqallid Objection
Baqillani defends consensus against objection regarding muqallid disbelievers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani defends the consensus that all disbelievers are in Hell against the objection that some theologians exempt muqallids from Hell.

Core Idea
The muqallid objection does not deny punishment; it denies that the muqallid is a disbeliever.

In the previous lesson, Baqillani established a consensus that all disbelievers are in Hell. Now an objector raises a challenge: some theologians claim that muqallid (blind followers) of disbelievers are not in Hell. This seems to break the consensus. Baqillani clarifies that these theologians simply disagree about the definition of disbelief, not about the punishment for those who are conclusively disbelievers. This lesson reveals the importance of precise definitions in theological debates.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Understand the objector's challenge: how can there be a consensus when some theologians say muqallid disbelievers are not in Hell?
This objection targets the previous lesson's claim.
2

See how Baqillani distinguishes between denying that someone is a disbeliever and denying punishment for a known disbeliever. The theologians only did the former due to a doubt.

Baqillani states that the muqallid's status is determined by reason (نظر), not revelation (توقيف) or report (خبر). This reinforces that the consensus applies to those known as disbelievers by revelation.
4
Reflect on the implication
The objection fails because it misidentifies the source of disagreement. The consensus remains intact for those who are undeniably disbelievers.

Commentary
This is another objection-and-answer. If these theologians are from the Muslim community, then the consensus that all disbelievers are in Hell seems false. Rather, they deny that the muqallid is a disbeliever at all, due to some doubt (شبهة) that led them to that conclusion. Their position is about the definition of disbelief (كفر), not about the punishment of known disbelievers. Only shirk and disbelief exclude forgiveness; major sins do not remove faith.

In this passage, Imam Baqillani responds to an objection about the fate of sinful Muslims. Commentary
This passage is a refutation and argument. Baqillani states that a fasiq (sinful believer) cannot be considered an enemy of God simply because of his sin, as long as he acknowledges God's existence, believes in Him, and affirms Him. He concludes that there is no justification for the objectors to revile the fasiq solely on the basis of his sin, because the fasiq is still a believer and not an enemy of God.
