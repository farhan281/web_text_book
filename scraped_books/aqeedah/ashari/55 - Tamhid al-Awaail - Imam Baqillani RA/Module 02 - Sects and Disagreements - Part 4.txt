Barahima's Denial and Refutation of Their Argument
Baqillani reports the Barahima's position and argument against prophethood, then begins refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Barahima's rejection of all prophets except Adam, their argument based on equality of human nature, and Baqillani's initial refutation challenging the assumption that preference entails injustice.

Core Idea
Preference by God is not injustice; it is His wisdom and grace.

Imam Baqillani begins his dialectical engagement with the Barahima, a sect that denied prophethood after Adam. He first reports their position and the argument they use to justify their rejection. Then he launches a critical question that exposes the flaw in their reasoning: why must divine preference be considered unjust? This lesson unpacks that exchange.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Barahima's claim
Read the opening sentences to grasp what the Barahima do and do not believe about prophets. Note that they accept Adam (or Ibrahim) but deny all others.
The text mentions two opinions within the Barahima: some accept only Adam, others only Ibrahim.
2
Understand their argument
Notice the reasoning they present: the messenger is of the same species and essence as humans, so giving one human superiority over another would be injustice (ḥayf) and favoritism (muḥābāh), which cannot be attributed to the Wise God.
Key terms: jins (species), jawhar (substance), tafḍīl (preference), ḥayf (injustice).
3
Examine the refutation
See how Baqillani challenges the premise that preference necessarily implies injustice. He asks: why must it be injustice? God may bestow favor on whom He wills, and all His actions are just and wise.
This is the first step in a longer argument; Baqillani forces the opponent to defend their assumption.

Refutation of Abu Hashim's Theory of States (Aḥwāl)
Baqillani refutes the claim that action indicates a 'state' (ḥāl) rather than an attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Baqillani argues that action cannot indicate a 'state' (ḥāl) but must indicate a real attribute.

Core Idea
A state is either known or unknown; either way, it collapses into an attribute.

This passage presents Baqillani's logical refutation of Abu Hashim's theory of states. The objection suggests that action might indicate a mere state rather than a real attribute. Baqillani's response uses a dilemma: the state must be either known or unknown, and both options lead to impossibility. We'll walk through his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Start by reading the objector's claim: action could indicate a state (ḥāl) rather than an attribute. Understand the Mu'tazilite position.

Identify the dilemma

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite position on divine attributes. Refuting the Excuse of Ignorance in the Qur'anic Challenge
Baqillani refutes the excuse of ignorance by showing it is not compelled and can be overcome.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani refutes the argument that ignorance of producing a similar Qur'an excuses disbelief, by showing that ignorance is not compelled and can be overcome through acquired knowledge.

Core Idea
Ignorance is not an excuse; it is a choice that can be replaced with knowledge.

In this passage, Baqillani addresses a key objection from those who deny the Qur'an's divine origin: they claim that their ignorance of how to compose something like the Qur'an exempts them from the challenge. After all, if they don't know how, how can they be required to produce it? Baqillani's response is sharp and reveals a deeper principle about moral responsibility and the nature of ignorance. Let's see how he dismantles this excuse.

Guided Path

Read the objection carefully

2
Identify Baqillani's initial response
Notice that he does not deny the possibility of ignorance. Instead, he points out that ignorance can be overcome: 'Perhaps they or most of them will acquire knowledge of that and then do it.' He shifts the burden.
He uses 'perhaps' (la'alla) to indicate possibility, not certainty.
3
Analyze the theological premise
Baqillani argues that God does not create ugly actions (qabīḥ), and ignorance is ugly. Therefore, ignorance is not from God; it is from the person. Since they have the ability to acquire knowledge, they can choose to abandon ignorance.
This relies on the Ash'ari principle that God does not create evil.
4
Conclude the refutation
Thus, the objectors have no defense. To persist in the objection amounts to abandoning Islam and attacking the message.
Note the strong language at the end: 'fīhi tark al-Islām wa al-ṭaʿn ʿalā al-risāla'.

The Mu'tazilite Denial of Divine Attributes and Transcendence
Baqillani cites Quranic verses and records Mu'tazilite claims denying divine difference and literal hearing/sight.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite position that God is not different from creation and that His hearing and sight are merely knowledge, and why this is considered heretical.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila reduced God's attributes to deny His transcendence and uniqueness.

Imam al-Baqillani begins his refutation of Mu'tazilite heresies by exposing their erroneous views on divine attributes. He first cites Quranic verses affirming God's hands, then records Abu al-Hudhayl's claim that God is not different from creation, and the Baghdadis' reduction of hearing and sight to mere knowledge. These claims strip God of His unique attributes and reduce Him to the level of creation.

Abu al-Hudhayl claimed that God is not different from creation because difference only applies to bodies. Understand the implication: if God is not different, then He is like creation.
This denies tanzih (transcendence).

Analyze the Baghdadis' view on hearing and sight
They said God is not hearing or seeing in a literal sense, but only knows sounds and persons, making His knowledge no better than a blind or deaf person's. Identify the reductionism here.
This empties God's attributes of their real meaning.

Consider why these views are considered heretical: they undermine God's transcendence and unique perfection.

The Mu'tazilite Claim That God's Speech Is Created and Like Human Speech
Baqillani reports Mu'tazilite views on God's word and speech as created and comparable to human speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite position that God's word is created in a substrate and that His speech is of the same kind as human speech, and why this contradicts Islamic theology.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila made God's speech a created thing, comparable to human words.

Imam al-Baqillani continues enumerating Mu'tazilite heresies by addressing their views on divine speech. He reports that they considered God's word (kalimah) as created, following Christian claims, and that they regarded God's speech (kalam) as of the same kind as human speech, even claiming they could produce its like. This passage exposes a fundamental error about the nature of the Quran as God's uncreated speech.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two claims about speech
The passage first discusses 'kalimah' (word) and then 'kalam' (speech). Note the distinction and the different errors.
Kalimah refers to specific utterances like 'Be!' while kalam is the broader attribute.
2
Understand the creation of kalimah
The Mu'tazila said God creates His word in a physical substrate like a tree, similar to the Christian claim that the word became flesh.
This makes the Quran a created entity.
3
Analyze the claim on kalam
They said God's speech is like human speech and that humans can produce its equal or better, though prevented temporarily by ignorance or evil.
This implies the Quran is not miraculous or unique.
4
Reflect on the implication
If God's speech is created and like human speech, then the Quran is not eternal and not a miracle.
This contradicts the Sunni belief in the Quran as uncreated and inimitable.

The passage begins by stating the Mu'tazilite view: everyone who commits a major sin (kabīra) and dies persisting in it will be eternally punished, even if that person is a Muslim, a monotheist, and believes in Allah and His Messenger. The Mu'tazila hold that such a person's good deeds – including tawḥīd, belief, prayers, and all acts of obedience – are nullified by the single grave sin. They claim that Allah will punish that person for the sin, even a seemingly minor one like drinking a sip of wine or withholding a small amount of zakāt. Al-Baqillani then explains that he could elaborate at length on the errors and innovations of the Mu'tazila, but that would make the book too long and divert from his primary aim. Finally, he ends with a supplication to Allah and a transition: he will now return to establishing the attributes of Allah, which is the main subject of the work. Issue. And if they say: The proof for the eternity of the sinful believer in Hell is that it is established that he deserves punishment, and it is established that what he deserves is perpetual, just as he deserves perpetual blame as long as he does not repent; therefore he is not rewarded. It is said to them: Why do you deny that the sinner is not punished with fire by this same reason? Because it is established that he deserves perpetual reward for his faith and obedience, just as he deserves perpetual praise; therefore he is not punished with fire. Then if they say: If he deserved reward, he would not be cursed nor humiliated in this world, and punishments would be removed from him. It is said to them: These punishments are not punishment; rather, they are a trial. Then it is said to them: And if the sinner did not deserve reward for his obedience, he would not deserve marriage, inheritance, and burial in Muslim cemeteries. If they say: These things are not reward. It is said to them: And the punishments are not punishment because they are applied to the repentant saint. Likewise, our blame and hatred are not punishment, because they are applied to false witnesses when their testimonies differ and their number falls short, even if they are truthful and righteous in the sight of God Almighty. And there is no distinction in any of that.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained argument about the eternal punishment of the sinful believer. The objectors argue that since the sinner deserves eternal blame, he deserves eternal punishment. Baqillani counters by applying the same logic: the sinner also deserves eternal reward for his faith, so he cannot be punished eternally. When the objectors object that if he deserved reward, worldly punishments and curses would not apply, Baqillani explains that those are not punishments but trials. He further argues by analogy: if the sinner did not deserve reward, he would not inherit or be buried as a Muslim. The objectors say those are not reward, and Baqillani retorts that worldly punishments are not punishment either, as they apply even to the repentant saint and to truthful witnesses whose testimony is rejected. He concludes there is no distinction in these examples.

Mu'tazilite Division on Intercession and Three Categories
Classifies Mu'tazila into two factions and three intercession categories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite division on intercession and the three categories of believers for whom intercession is valid according to one faction.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila split into deniers and restrictors of intercession.

This passage from Imam al-Baqillani's Tamhid al-Awa'il presents the Mu'tazilite position on intercession. It shows that they were not monolithic: one group rejected it entirely, while another accepted it but limited it to three specific types of believers. This classification helps in understanding the sectarian debates on intercession.

Guided Path
1

The passage uses technical terms like 'صغائر' and 'كبائر'.
2
Identify the two factions
Find the two groups mentioned: one denies intercession altogether, the other affirms it for three categories.

This is a key point of contention with other Sunni groups.

And the Mu'tazila have divided into two groups. One group denied intercession entirely and burdened themselves with denial of the reports and rejection of the Quran. This passage is a classification of the Mu'tazilite positions on intercession. For these groups, intercession serves to increase their reward in Paradise, not to forgive sins. The passage explicitly excludes intercession for anyone who dies persisting in a major sin without repentance.

The Mu'tazila's Inconsistency on Repentance Acceptance
Baqillani critiques Mu'tazila's interpretation of 'faghfir' and its inconsistency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's refutation of the Mu'tazila's view that God is obligated to accept repentance and why their principles prevent them from adopting the Sunni interpretation.

Core Idea
Acceptance of repentance is God's favor, not an obligation.

Baqillani examines a key difference between Sunni and Mu'tazila theology: whether God must accept repentance. He argues that the Mu'tazila, by making acceptance obligatory, contradict their own principles when they try to interpret a Quranic verse. This passage highlights a fundamental doctrinal divide.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its literal translation to understand Baqillani's argument.
2
Identify the two positions
Notice the Sunni view (acceptance is a favor) and the Mu'tazila view (acceptance is obligatory). Understand why Baqillani says the Sunni interpretation is inconsistent with Mu'tazila principles.
The Mu'tazila would deem God unjust if He rejected repentance.
3
Reflect on the implication
