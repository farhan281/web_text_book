Refutation of the Claim that a Convert Never Changes Belief
Baqillani refutes the argument that a convert never abandons his original belief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani uses internal experience and sensory evidence to refute the claim that religious converts never truly change their beliefs.

Core Idea
Denying religious conversion contradicts what we know from ourselves.

Baqillani encounters a skeptical argument that a person who converts from one religion to another is not the same person who held the original belief — so they never really changed. He dismisses this as absurd, pointing to our own internal experience of changing beliefs and our ability to criticize our past beliefs. This lesson examines his refutation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the opponent's argument
Read the opponent's claim: 'The one who becomes a zindiq and a Jew is not the one who was a Magian.' Notice that they are trying to separate the person's identity from their beliefs.
This is a common skeptical move.
2
See Baqillani's logical consequence
Baqillani points out that if the opponent were correct, then no one would ever change a belief they held as true. Think about why this is a problem.
3
Notice Baqillani's appeal to experience
Baqillani argues that we find in ourselves beliefs that are different from what we previously held, and we even criticize our old beliefs after changing. This is a direct appeal to self-awareness and sense perception.
He calls the opponent's denial 'juhud al-darura wa al-hiss' (denial of necessity and sense).

Mirroring Arguments as a Dialectical Refutation
Baqillani records and refutes an objection about God's knowledge and sending.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani uses the technique of mirroring an opponent's argument to refute it, leaving them without an answer.

Core Idea
Mirroring an opponent's reasoning can be a decisive refutation in kalam.

This lesson examines a short but characteristic example of dialectical argumentation in kalam. Baqillani presents an objection from opponents who interpret certain names and knowledge in a specific way. His response is not to dismantle their argument piece by piece, but to turn it back on them, showing that a parallel counter-argument leaves them speechless. This technique is common in theological debates and reveals the importance of logical symmetry.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice the brevity and the two-part structure: 'They said... It was said to them...'
The passage is a fragment; context is missing, but the structure is clear.
2
Identify the opponent's objection
The opponents claim that a certain name is with Allah before creation and in His knowledge, linking it to sending and empowerment. They offer 'other such interpretations' (تأويلات).
The exact subject is unclear, but the form of an interpretative argument is recognizable.
3
Identify Baqillani's refutation
Baqillani replies by stating that a similar argument was given to them (مثله فيما احتجوا به) and they have no answer for it. This is a mirroring technique: using the opponent's own style of reasoning against them.
The phrase 'ولا جواب عنه' emphasizes the conclusiveness of the refutation.
4
Reflect on the dialectical strategy
Consider why mirroring is effective. It avoids the need to refute each detail; instead, it shows that the opponent's method leads to an unacceptable consequence or a stalemate.
This strategy is common in later kalam works, especially in refutations of skepticism.

Refuting the Indistinguishability of Ritual Acts and the Builder Analogy
Baqillani responds to two objections regarding the distinctiveness of ritual acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Baqillani refutes objections that claim ritual acts are no different from ordinary actions using both direct reasoning and an analogy from a builder.

Core Idea
God's command makes acts worshipful, not inherent qualities.

Imam al-Baqillani addresses critics who argue that there is no intrinsic difference between ritual acts like sa'i or circumambulation and other actions. He first concedes that God could have commanded other acts, then refutes the builder analogy by showing that change is permissible if it benefits the created being. This lesson unpacks his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Note the two objections raised by the critics.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the first objection and response
Find where the critics say there is no difference between Safa-Marwa and other places. Then see Baqillani's response: if God willed, He could command any place.
3
Identify the builder analogy and response
The critics compare God to a wise builder who would not destroy his work. Baqillani responds by introducing the principle of benefit for the created being.
4
Understand the principle of benefit
Baqillani argues that if an action benefits the accountable person, then commanding it is good, even if it involves changes or destruction.
This refutes the objection that destruction is always bad.

The argument concludes that the objectors have no valid response.

Silence as Endorsement: Objection and Two Responses
Baqillani refutes the objection that silence does not always imply endorsement of a report.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani refutes the objection that silence about a report does not necessarily imply endorsement, using the distinction between manifest doctrines and witnessed events.

Core Idea
Silence is contextual; customary practice determines whether silence implies consent.

In this passage, Baqillani confronts a subtle objection: if opponents can remain silent about a false doctrine without approving it, then why should the Companions' silence about miracles imply endorsement? He offers two distinct arguments that rest on the different nature of claims about witnessed events versus opinions. Let's unpack his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Understand the questioner's analogy between silence on false doctrines and silence on reports of miracles.
The objector tries to undermine the argument from silence by showing that silence can occur without approval.
2
Identify the first response
Baqillani appeals to the view of many Muslims that silence about a manifest false doctrine is impossible without denial, so such silence indicates consensus.
This response refutes the analogy by denying that silence about manifest falsehoods is permissible.
3
Understand the second response
He argues that custom differs: silence about opinion-based doctrines (which may be rare or under deliberation) is not the same as silence about claims of witnessed events, where silence indicates truth.
The key distinction is the type of claim: an opinion versus a reported event.
4
Reflect on the implications
Consider how these arguments support the reliability of tawatur (mass transmission) and the argument from silence in historical reports.

Commentary

People may remain silent about a rare opinion because the number of its believers is small or because they are still deliberating. However, about witnessed events, it is not customary for a large group to remain silent about a lie; their silence indicates truth. Thus, the analogy fails.

Refutation of the Claim that Knowledge of Transmission Must Be Necessary
Baqillani refutes the objection that if transmission were true, knowledge would be necessary, showing it leads to absurdity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's argument that requiring necessary knowledge from transmission would destroy the reliability of all reports.

Core Idea
If knowledge must be necessary, no report can be trusted.

The opponent argues that if the Muslim transmission of miracles were true, everyone would be compelled to believe it. Since not everyone believes, the transmission must be false. Baqillani responds by showing the devastating consequences of this reasoning: it would allow us to accuse entire communities of lying, ultimately nullifying all historical knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the opening statement of the opponent: 'If it were as they claim, we would have known their truthfulness necessarily.' Grasp why they think necessary knowledge is required.
The objection relies on the idea that truth from a reliable source produces certainty for everyone.
2
Follow Baqillani's counter
Baqillani points out that accepting the objection means we must allow that today's Muslims could be lying about their chain. Consider the implications of allowing such a possibility.
3
Trace the absurd conclusion
If Muslims can be lying, then Jews, Christians, and all historical transmitters can also be lying. This leads to the nullification of all reports and all knowledge from them.
Baqillani calls this 'something from which there is no escape' – an unavoidable absurdity.

Refutation of the Claim of Necessity in Transmitted Reports
Bāqillānī refutes the claim that knowledge of transmitted statements is necessary by a conditional reductio.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A claim of universal necessity that contradicts universal denial undermines all transmitted knowledge.

This passage is a masterclass in logical refutation. This creates a devastating dilemma: either the Muslims are lying about their denial, or the claim of necessity is false. The Source of Security: Revelation and Consensus
Baqillani explains that revelation and consensus prevent God from punishing believers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Islamic scholars rely on revelation and consensus, rather than reason alone, to guarantee that God will not punish believers.

Core Idea
Revelation and consensus, not reason, assure us of God's justice.

In kalam, one of the most challenging questions is: if God is absolutely powerful and just, what prevents Him from acting contrary to His promises? An objector might doubt the reliability of God's threat and promise. In this passage, Baqillani responds by grounding our certainty in two revealed sources: the prophetic teaching and the consensus of the Muslim community.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. The objector asks: 'What gives you security from God punishing the believers?' Notice the challenge: reason alone does not forbid it.
The objector is assuming that reason permits both possibilities.
2
Identify the two sources of security
Baqillani gives two sources: 'tawqif al-nabi' (prophetic instruction) and 'ijma' al-muslimin' (consensus of Muslims). Find these in the Arabic text.
These are transmitted, not rational, sources.
3
Understand the role of necessary knowledge
Baqillani mentions that God's reports give necessary knowledge (darura) that the events will not occur. Reflect on how revelation creates certainty.
This shows the epistemological foundation of the answer.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer pair. The objector asks: If reason does not preclude God from punishing believers and rewarding disbelievers, then what prevents Him from actually doing so? Baqillani answers that the security (يؤمننا) comes from two sources: first, the prophetic instruction (توقيف النبي) — i.e., revelations and prophetic statements that inform us of God's promise and threat; second, the consensus of the Muslim community (إجماع المسلمين) that God will not do that. Moreover, God has given reports (أخبار) from which the Prophet's community knows with certainty (ضرورة) that this will not happen. Baqillani emphasizes that without these revealed sources, reason alone would not forbid it.
