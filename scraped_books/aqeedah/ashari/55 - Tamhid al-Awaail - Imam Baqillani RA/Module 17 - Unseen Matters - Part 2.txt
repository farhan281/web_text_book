Mu'tazilite Divine Promise and Threat
States the Mu'tazilite doctrine on obligatory reward and necessary punishment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite doctrine that reward is obligatory upon Allah and punishment is necessary for unrepentant grave sinners, and how this differs from the majority view.

Core Idea
For the Mu'tazila, Allah's justice makes reward and punishment binding obligations.

This passage presents a central Mu'tazilite tenet: that divine promise and threat are not merely contingent but carry a binding necessity based on Allah's justice. Before examining the proof, we must grasp their precise claim: reward is a binding obligation (ḥatm) on Allah, and punishment is necessary (wājib) for the unrepentant grave sinner. The author then contrasts this with the majority's view that punishment can be waived.

Commentary
This passage presents the Mu'tazilite position on divine promise (wa'd) and threat (wa'id). It begins by affirming that God's statements about reward and punishment are true. The passage contrasts this with the majority view that punishment is not obligatory in the same sense as reward, because reward cannot be annulled whereas punishment can be forgiven according to the Basran Mu'tazila and some Baghdadis. Ash'ari Refutation via Master-Servant Analogy
Ash'ari argument that the servant does not deserve reward from God

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the master-servant analogy demonstrates that reward from God is not obligatory but a matter of divine grace.

Core Idea
The servant does not deserve reward; worship is gratitude, not a transaction.

This passage presents a key Ash'ari argument against the Mu'tazilite view that God must reward believers. Using everyday analogies from master-slave and father-son relationships, al-Baqillani shows that even in human contexts, service does not obligate extra reward when the master already provides everything. Read on to see how this reasoning applies to God and His servants, ultimately concluding that reward is by grace, not requirement.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the literal translation. Let the author's analogies unfold naturally.
The passage uses two analogies: master-slave and father-son.
2
Identify the two analogies
Notice the first analogy: a master who provides for his slave's needs does not owe extra reward for the slave's service. The second: a father who supports his son does not owe extra for the son's respect. Both show that no additional compensation is due when basic needs are already met.
The analogies are designed to be intuitive and universally understood.
3
Apply the analogy to God and humans
Extend the reasoning: God's blessings are infinite and continuous, while human worship is minimal and itself a form of gratitude. Gratitude cannot logically demand compensation, as that would lead to infinite regress.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Understand that reward (thawāb) is not an obligation due to human merit but a gift of divine grace (faḍl). Worship is a response to blessings, not a transaction that binds God.
This refutes the Mu'tazilite doctrine of 'obligatory reward'.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite doctrine of obligatory reward using analogical reasoning. The author (al-Baqillani) presents examples from human relationships to demonstrate that even in worldly contexts, a servant or child does not deserve extra reward beyond the ongoing care they already receive. The argument proceeds in three steps. First, a master who fully provides for his slave's needs does not owe the slave anything extra for the slave's service, especially if the slave is not even exerting full effort. Second, similarly, a father who constantly provides for his son does not owe the son extra compensation for the son's respectful service, because the son is already benefiting from the father's bounty. Third, applying this to the relationship between God and His servants, the disparity is infinitely greater: God's blessings are overwhelming and continuous, while the servant's worship is minimal. Worship is essentially gratitude (shukr) for blessings, and it is not rationally required that gratitude itself be compensated; if it were, it would lead to an infinite regress where God would also deserve compensation for giving the initial reward. Thus, reward is not obligatory but a matter of divine grace (faḍl).

Legal Punishments Are Trials, Not Punishment
Baqillani responds to the objection that legal punishments prove the sinner deserves punishment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Baqillani argues that legal punishments (ḥudūd) are trials (imtiḥān) and not punishments (ʿiqāb), and how this relates to the sinner's ultimate reward.

Core Idea
Legal punishments in this world are trials, not final judgments.

This passage addresses a common objection: if a sinful believer still deserves reward for his faith, why does he face legal punishments in this world? Baqillani provides a clear response: these punishments are not punishment in the Hereafter sense but are trials for purification. He supports this with parallel examples from Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
The objectors claim that if the sinner deserved reward, he would not be cursed or punished in this world. Baqillani replies that these legal punishments are actually trials (imtiḥān), not punishments (ʿiqāb).
Notice the key distinction between worldly measures and afterlife reward.
2
Examine the parallel argument
Baqillani argues that the sinner still enjoys worldly benefits like marriage, inheritance, and burial in Muslim cemeteries. These are not considered reward (thawāb) in the afterlife sense, so why would legal punishments be considered punishment?
This shows consistency in treating worldly acts differently from eschatological reward.
3
Consider the objection and counter-objection
The objectors retort that those benefits are not reward. Baqillani counters that legal punishments are also not punishment because they apply even to the repentant saint (al-tā'ib al-walī).
The application of ḥudūd to the repentant shows they are not punitive in the ultimate sense.
4
Reflect on the final analogy
Baqillani adds that blame and hatred from the community are not punishment either, using the example of truthful witnesses whose testimony is rejected due to procedural defects.
This reinforces that worldly judgments are not eschatological punishments.

Commentary
This is an objection-and-answer passage. The objectors argue that if a sinful believer still deserves reward for his faith and obedience, then legal punishments (ḥudūd) should not be applied to him in this world—since they would indicate he is being punished. When the objectors object that these are not reward, Baqillani counters that legal punishments are similarly not punishment because they are applied even to the repentant saint (al-tā'ib al-walī). He gives another example: blame and hatred are not punishment because they apply to witnesses whose testimony is rejected due to procedural defects, even if they are truthful and righteous. Thus, there is no valid distinction—worldly legal measures do not reflect the ultimate reward or punishment in the afterlife.

He then refutes the claim that Uthman was the first to compile the Quran, noting that compilation existed in the Prophet's and early caliphs' times, but the need for an official unified version arose only in Uthman's era due to new disagreements. Finally, he refutes the claim that the compilation was a sin, defining sin as an act explicitly forbidden by revelation or reason, and asserts that no such prohibition exists against gathering the Quran. Thus the accusations are baseless.
