Distinguishing Transmission from Interpretation
Baqillani refutes the claim that Christian irrational beliefs invalidate their transmission of Jesus' miracles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's argument that a community's erroneous theological interpretation does not necessarily corrupt the reliability of their historical transmission of factual events.

Core Idea
Factual reports survive flawed interpretations.

When opponents argue that a group's false beliefs discredit all their reports, Baqillani offers a precise rebuttal. He separates the core historical transmission from the later theological construction. This lesson examines that distinction and shows why factual reports can remain trustworthy even when interpretations go wrong.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Notice the objection: Christians added the irrational Trinity to Jesus' miracles, so their transmission is unreliable. Read the Arabic and translation to grasp the claim.
The objection assumes that adding an interpretation invalidates the entire report.
2
Identify Baqillani's key distinction
Baqillani separates the transmitted events (miracles) from the interpretations (Trinity). He says Christians did not transmit the Trinity as a report; they deduced it themselves. This distinction is crucial.
The Trinity is not part of the original narration; it is a later theological inference.
3
Apply the principle
Reflect: if someone misinterprets a story, does that make the story false? Baqillani says no. The transmission of the miracle facts remains sound. This principle has broad applications in evaluating historical reports.

Trinity Does Not Corrupt Christian Miracle Reports
Baqillani answers the objection that Christians' addition of Trinity corrupts their transmission of Jesus' miracles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Baqillani argues that a group's theological errors do not automatically invalidate their factual reports, using the example of Christian transmission of Jesus' miracles.

Core Idea
Theological error does not corrupt factual reporting.

How can we accept miracles reported by a community whose beliefs include what reason deems impossible? This is the objection Baqillani addresses. He draws a crucial distinction between transmitted facts and interpretive doctrines, showing that the two are separate. Read on to see how he defends the reliability of Christian miracle accounts despite their Trinitarian theology.

1
Understand the objection

2

Notice how Baqillani separates the act of transmitting a report from the act of interpreting doctrine. Christians did not transmit the Trinity as a report; they derived it through personal reasoning. Therefore, their theological error does not affect their factual reports of miracles.
This is the key logical move.

Baqillani's second answer: multiple independent witnesses
Observe that Baqillani further argues that the miracles of Jesus are attested not only by Muslims but also by other Christian groups (like the 'Isawiyya and Arians) who differ on doctrine. This multiplicity of witnesses strengthens the case.

Doctrinal errors do not automatically nullify historical testimony.

He shows that if we reject Christian transmission because of their erroneous beliefs, we must also reject Jewish transmission because of their anthropomorphism—leading to a contradiction. This lesson explores his clever use of symmetry to defend the principle that testimony of miracles can remain valid despite theological errors in the transmitters.

Refuting the Qadariyya on Jesus Creating Birds
Baqillani refutes the Qadariyya interpretation of Jesus creating birds

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani interprets the word 'khalq' in the verse about Jesus and the birds to refute the Qadariyya claim that humans can create their own actions.

Core Idea
The word 'khalq' can mean estimation and shaping, not only creation from nothing.

The Qadariyya use the verse where Jesus creates birds from clay as proof that humans can create. Baqillani responds by showing that 'khalq' in this context refers to Jesus' estimation and manual shaping, while God creates the actual formation. This distinction preserves divine sovereignty over creation. Let's examine his argument.

Commentary
This passage refutes a second Qadariyya proof: the verse about Jesus creating birds from clay (Quran 5:110). The Qadariyya argue that Jesus 'created' the birds, implying that humans can create. The author explains that 'takhluq' here means estimation (taqdir) and shaping (taswir), not creating ex nihilo. He clarifies that the word 'khalq' can refer to mental estimation and manual shaping. The author affirms that Jesus indeed moved his hands and thought, but it was God who actually created the coming together of the shaped pieces. Thus, the verse does not prove that humans create their own actions in a metaphysical sense; rather, Jesus merely performed actions that were the occasion for God's creation.
