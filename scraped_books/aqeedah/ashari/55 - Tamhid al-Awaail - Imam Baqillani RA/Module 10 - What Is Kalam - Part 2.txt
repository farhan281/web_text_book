A report is any statement that can be true or false.

This simple but crucial distinction separates reports from commands, questions, and other speech acts. Understanding this will help you analyze theological arguments that rely on reports.

The Third Type of Reports: Possible in Reason
Baqillani defines the third type of khabar and its ruling based on evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third type of report (mumkin fi al-'aql) and how its truth-value depends on evidence, including the special cases that yield certainty.

Core Idea
A report about a possible event is neither true nor false until evidence decides.

Baqillani continues his classification of reports by examining those that are rationally possible. Unlike necessary or impossible reports, these require external evidence to establish their truth or falsehood. This passage lays out the rule and the exceptions, showing how certainty can still be achieved through reliable sources.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition and examples
Read the definition of the third type and the examples given (rain, leadership, Zayd's location, reports about the Prophet). Notice that these are all matters that could be true or false.
The key phrase is 'mumkin fi al-'aql kawnuhu al-ta'abbud bihi' – rationally possible as a legal obligation.
2
Follow the three rulings based on evidence
The passage gives three scenarios: evidence proves truth → certainty; evidence proves falsehood → certainty of falsehood; no evidence → suspension (waqf). Study these carefully.
3
Recognize the special sources of certainty
Baqillani lists cases where certainty is attained without further evidence: reports from Allah, His Messenger, one known not to lie, and tawatur. These bypass the need for external evidence.
Tawatur is a key concept – mass transmission that precludes collusion.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and ruling on the third type of reports (al-akhbar) in Baqillani's epistemology. He defines this type as a report about something that is rationally possible and subject to legal obligation (mumkin fi al-'aql kawnuhu al-ta'abbud bihi). Examples include reports about natural events (rain in a specific town), social facts (leadership, prices), personal states (Zayd in his house), and even reports about the Prophet (peace be upon him) concerning succession or legal obligations. These reports are not inherently true or false; their truth value depends on the evidence (dalil) that accompanies them. If evidence proves truth, certainty (qat') is reached; if evidence proves falsehood, certainty of falsehood is reached. If no evidence exists, one must suspend judgment (waqf) and consider both possibilities. The exception is when the report comes from sources that are guaranteed truthful: from Allah, His Messenger, or one who is known not to lie, or from a group large enough to constitute tawatur (mass transmission). In those cases, certainty is attained directly. The passage concludes by stating that this is a sufficient summary of the discourse on reports.

Baqillani defines khabar al-wahid and states that it obligates action, not knowledge.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the conventional meaning of khabar al-wahid and why it obligates action rather than certain knowledge.

Core Idea
Khabar al-wahid is defined by its epistemic value, not the number of narrators.

In this passage, Baqillani clarifies a key term in Islamic epistemology: the single report (khabar al-wahid). He distinguishes between its linguistic meaning and its technical meaning among jurists and theologians. This distinction is crucial because it determines whether such reports produce certain knowledge or merely obligate action.

باب آخر في خبر الواحد. فإن قال قائل : فما معنى وصفكم للخبر بأنه خبر ؟ قيل له : أما حقيقة هذه الإضافة في اللغة فإنه خبر واحدٍ وأن الراوي له واحد فقط لا اثنان ولا أكثر من ذلك. غير أن الفقهاء والمتكلمين قد تواضعوا على تسمية كل خبر قصر عن إيجاب العلم بأنه خبر واحد وسواء عندهم رواه الواحد أو الجماعة التي تزيد على الواحد. وهذا الخبر لا يوجب العلم على ما وصفناه أولاً ولكن يوجب العمل إن كان ناقله عدلاً ولم يعارضه ما هو أقوى منه على حد ما نذهب إليه مما ليس هذا موضع ذكره.
Another chapter on the solitary report. If a questioner says: What is the meaning of your describing a report as 'solitary'? It is said to him: As for the reality of this attribution in language, it is that it is a report of one, and that the narrator of it is only one, not two nor more than that. However, the jurists and theologians have conventionally agreed to name every report that falls short of necessitating knowledge as a 'solitary report'; in their view, whether it is narrated by one or by a group greater than one. And this report does not necessitate knowledge as we described first, but it necessitates action if its transmitter is trustworthy and it is not opposed by what is stronger than it, according to the position we hold, which is not the place to mention.

Commentary
This passage defines khabar al-wahid (solitary report). It begins with a question about the meaning of 'khabar' in the phrase 'khabar al-wahid'. The answer distinguishes between linguistic and conventional definitions. Linguistically, 'khabar al-wahid' means a report from a single person. However, jurists (fuqaha) and theologians (mutakallimun) have agreed by convention (tawadu'u) to apply the term to any report that falls short of producing necessary knowledge (ilm daruri), regardless of the number of narrators. Thus, even a report narrated by many might be considered khabar al-wahid if it is not mutawatir. The ruling: such a report does not produce knowledge but it does produce action (amal) under two conditions: the narrator is trustworthy (adl) and the report is not contradicted by stronger evidence. Baqillani notes that the full details of this ruling are to be discussed elsewhere.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Khabar al-wahid always means a report narrated by exactly one person.
Correction: Baqillani clarifies that the conventional definition depends on the report's epistemic quality (not producing knowledge), not on the narrator count. A report from many can still be khabar al-wahid if it does not reach the level of tawatur.

Key Takeaway
Khabar al-wahid is defined by its failure to produce necessary knowledge, not by the number of narrators, and it obligates action under specific conditions.
