Proving the Finality of Prophethood from Mutawatir Hadith
Baqillani proves finality of prophethood via mutawatir hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani uses the mass-transmitted hadith 'There is no prophet after me' to prove the finality of prophethood, and how he addresses the objection that similar claims are made about Moses.

Core Idea
The mutawatir hadith is unambiguous and negates all prophets after Muhammad.

Having refuted Jewish claims about Moses, Baqillani now establishes the Islamic proof for the finality of prophethood. He presents the Prophet's statement 'La nabiya ba'di' as a mass-transmitted report (mutawatir) that is clear in its generality, and he preemptively answers the objection that the Jews make similar claims about Moses. The response draws on the earlier arguments about transmission and interpretation.

Commentary
This passage is a continuous argumentative refutation and proof. First, Baqillani refutes the claim that Moses (peace be upon him) stated that his law is eternal and non-abrogable. He argues that the supposed proof from the verse 'If you obey me...' does not imply non-abrogation, because a conditional promise of reward can be made even if the command is later abrogated; the promise is still fulfilled. Moreover, the loss of the Israelites' kingdom was due to their disobedience, not because the law was abrogated. Second, he points out that Moses spoke Hebrew, and the translations into other languages are prone to error, so attributing specific phrases about perpetuity to him is unreliable. Then he addresses a potential objection from the opponent: 'What proof do you have for preventing a prophet after your prophet?' He answers with the mutawātir (mass-transmitted) hadith 'There is no prophet after me,' which the entire community has transmitted continuously from generation to generation. This hadith is unambiguous (muḥkam) and is not subject to specification; it negates all prophets after him, including those who would abrogate his law and those who would not. Finally, the opponent retorts that they have similar claims about Moses. The answer to that objection is already given in the preceding refutation: those claims are baseless due to linguistic and transmission issues.

Proof of Finality of Prophethood and Refutation of Jewish Objections
Baqillani gives proof of finality and refutes Jewish objections with three distinctions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's logical argument for the finality of Muhammad's prophethood and how he distinguishes the Muslim transmission from Jewish claims.

Core Idea
The Muslim report is the Prophet's exact words; Jewish claims rely on translation and error.

Baqillani presents a rigorous argument for why no prophet can come after Muhammad. He begins by challenging the reliability of Jewish transmission of Moses' message, then offers the Muslim proof from mutawatir hadith and Quran. When the Jewish interlocutor objects that they have a similar claim, Baqillani provides three key distinctions that show the Muslim report is uniquely certain and authoritative. This lesson walks through his reasoning.

The passage has a clear structure: critique, proof, objection, refutation.

Understand the Muslim proof
The proof is based on the universally transmitted statement 'There is no prophet after me' and the Quranic verse 'Seal of the prophets.' Recognize why this is considered definitive.
Key term: لا نبي بعدي (lā nabiyya baʿdī).
3
Examine the Jewish objection
The Jews claim a similar statement from Moses. Baqillani presents their objection.
4

The heart of the refutation is three differences between the Muslim report and the Jewish report. Each distinction undermines the Jewish claim.

5
Reflect on the conclusion
Baqillani shows that the Muslim report is self-authenticating and closed prophethood, while Jewish tradition still awaits future prophets.

Commentary
This passage is an argument and refutation. Baqillani begins by criticizing the Jewish transmission of Moses' message: they transmit it through translation and interpretation, which inevitably introduces errors and disagreements. If they claim accuracy, they must produce the exact Hebrew wording for verification, but even then there is great disagreement. Then he presents the Muslim proof for the finality of prophethood: the universally transmitted (mutawātir) statement of the Prophet, 'There is no prophet after me,' which is unambiguous, reinforced by the Quranic verse 'Seal of the prophets,' and accompanied by clear indications that it applies to all prophets forever. If the Jews object that they have a similar claim from Moses, Baqillani responds with three distinctions: First, the Muslim report is the exact wording of the Prophet, not subject to interpretation or translation errors, whereas the Jewish report is translated and thus prone to distortion. Second, the Prophet's statement is explicitly tied to the Quranic verse and commands rejection of any subsequent prophet, while Moses' statement (as they claim) commands belief in all subsequent messengers—indeed, they accept many prophets after Moses, including Joshua, Ezekiel, etc., and some even accept later figures like Abu Isa al-Asbahani. Third, the Jews are still awaiting future prophets, whereas Islam definitively closed prophethood. Thus the Muslim proof is superior and self-authenticating.

First Distinction: Reliability of Transmission
Baqillani explains the first distinction regarding transmission.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Muslim report is verbatim; the Jewish report is a translation open to error.

Baqillani begins his refutation by highlighting a fundamental difference in how the two claims have been preserved. The Muslim report about Muhammad's finality is transmitted in his exact words, while the Jewish claim about Moses comes through translation. This makes the Muslim transmission inherently more trustworthy.

Baqillani's Refutation of the ʿĪsawiyya: The Self-Defeating Denial of Universal Prophethood
Baqillani refutes the ʿĪsawiyya by presenting a dilemma that destroys their position.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani uses a dilemma to show that denying the universal message of a prophet leads to either destroying prophethood or undermining all transmitted knowledge.

Core Idea
Denying a prophet's universal mission unravels the very proofs of his prophethood.

The ʿĪsawiyya sect tried to limit the missions of Muhammad and Jesus to their own peoples, preserving the Torah from abrogation. But Baqillani exposes a fatal flaw: if you accept a prophet based on miracles, you must accept all his statements, including the claim of universal mission. Otherwise, you either accuse the prophet of lying (which destroys prophethood) or accuse the transmitters of lying (which opens the door to rejecting all transmitted reports, including the miracles). This lesson unpacks this powerful argument.
