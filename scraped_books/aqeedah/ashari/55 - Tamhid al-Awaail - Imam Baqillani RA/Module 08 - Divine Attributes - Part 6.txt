The Unforgivable Sin: Shirk vs. Lesser Sins
Baqillani distinguishes unforgivable shirk from forgivable sins, refuting Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's distinction between shirk as unforgivable and lesser sins as forgivable, and the evidence he uses to refute the Mu'tazila position.

Core Idea
Shirk is the only unforgivable sin; God can forgive all others by His will.

In this passage, Baqillani addresses a crucial theological dispute about the fate of sinful believers. He affirms that while shirk is unforgivable unless repented, lesser sins are subject to God's mercy and forgiveness, both rationally and legally. This refutes the Mu'tazila claim that major sins without repentance result in eternal punishment. Let's examine the argument and its evidence.

Commentary
This passage discusses the punishment for sins. It distinguishes shirk (associating partners with God) as unforgivable unless repented, based on Quran 4:48. For sins less than shirk, the author states that forgiveness is permissible both by law and reason, contra the Mu'tazila who hold that while forgiveness is rationally possible, it is legally impossible, and thus they assert eternal punishment for a fasiq (sinful believer). The author refutes the false attribution of this Mu'tazilite view to al-Shafi'i by citing al-Shafi'i's own statement about a deserter being subject to forgiveness. The argument then presents Quranic verses and a hadith to show that forgiveness extends to all sins except shirk, even without repentance (though repentance is generally required). The hadith from Gabriel affirms that tawhid alone suffices for Paradise, even with major sins.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained theological argument defending the Ash'ari position that God is not obligated to punish sin or reward obedience, and that His forgiveness is rationally permissible even without repentance. The author begins by posing the question: Is it rationally permissible for God to forgive all sinners? He answers affirmatively, grounding his reasoning in the nature of divine action: God's infliction of pain is not caused by any external cause or necessity; rather, He makes human deeds signs of His decree, not causes of His action. Punishment is God's right, which He may exercise or waive at His will, analogous to bestowing unmerited favor. The author then appeals to universal moral intuition: humans recognize the goodness of forgoing punishment from one who deserves it for a crime. Muslims and others agree on the goodness of pardon, even for disbelievers. He then addresses a specific objection attributed to the claim that al-Shafi'i held Mu'tazilite views, citing Abu Ali ibn Abi Huraira's argument that the Qur'anic distinction between shirk and lesser sins in the verse 'God does not forgive shirk' implies that forgiveness of lesser sins is possible without repentance, based on the apparent contrast. The author then adduces Qur'anic verses and a hadith (Jibril's report) to show that God forgives all sins except shirk, and that even major sins like fornication and theft are forgiven for those who die without shirk. This leads to the key doctrinal conclusion: good deeds are signs (ʿalamāt) of felicity, not causes (mūjibāt) of reward; similarly, sins are indications (amārāt) of punishment, not causes. This is illustrated by the hadith of Zayd ibn Thabit, which states that God's punishment would not be unjust and His mercy is better than deeds. The author declares this the true creed of Ahl al-Sunna. He then reinforces the argument with Arabic poetry: Ka'b ibn Zuhayr's verse shows that forgiveness is hoped for after a threat, and other verses blame those who always fulfill threats, demonstrating that linguistically, forgiveness after a threat does not make the threat a lie or blameworthy. Finally, he asks rhetorically: How can God not be good in forgiving when He commands and praises forgiveness? He concludes that all agree on this.

Commentary

Commentary
This passage is a sustained argument and objection-answer unit. Baqillani first establishes that only kufr (disbelief) and shirk (polytheism) are unforgivable, citing Q. 4:31 and 4:48 to show that major sins (kabā'ir) here mean disbelief, and that lesser sins are forgiven. He then adduces Q. 12:87 and 39:53 to show that despair of mercy is for disbelievers, not believers. From this he concludes that the sinful Muslim (al-millī al-fāsiq) is neither a kāfir nor a mushrik, and therefore it is possible that he be forgiven even if he dies unrepentant—though repentance would still be required of him. Baqillani then recalls his earlier proof that sin does not negate the knowledge (ma'rifa) that constitutes faith, so the sinner remains a believer. An objector then quotes verses (Q. 10:27, 72:23, 4:93) that seem to threaten eternal punishment for sinners. Baqillani answers that these verses refer to the disobedient disbeliever (al-‘āṣī al-kāfir) who has no faith (īmān) and no good deed (ḥasana), not to the believing sinner. The lesson thus defends the Ash'ari position that faith is not nullified by sin, and that the fāsiq is still a believer subject to God's will—forgiveness or punishment.

God's Enmity and Love for the Fasiq are Compatible
Baqillani proves that God can will punishment and reward for the same person.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Baqillani reconciles God's enmity and love for the same person (the fasiq) by distinguishing between different aspects and deeds.

Core Idea
God's enmity and love for the same person are not contradictory—they relate to different deeds.

In this passage, Imam al-Baqillani addresses a key objection: if God knows that a fasiq (sinful believer) will be punished, does that mean God is his enemy? Baqillani explains that God's enmity towards his sin does not negate His friendship for his faith. He supports this with clear reasoning and Quranic evidence.

2

3
Reflect on the evidence

The verses show God's general love for believers; the analogy shows that punishment and reward can apply to the same person for different acts.

Commentary
This passage is a proof and explanation. Baqillani addresses the objection that if God knows the fasiq will be punished, then He is his enemy. He explains that God's enmity for the fasiq means He intends to punish him for his sin, but at the same time God is a friend to him because of his faith and intends to reward him for it. These are not contradictory because they are from different aspects and for different deeds. He gives an analogy: a human parent can both punish a child for a misdeed and reward him for a good deed. Then Baqillani cites Quranic evidence: 'God is the friend of those who believe' (2:257) and 'Give good news to those who believe that they have a sure footing with their Lord' (10:2). Since the fasiq is a believer (as established earlier), he must be a friend of God. The verse 'And He is merciful to the believers' (33:43) is interpreted to mean God intends to reward believers in the afterlife and to treat them as believers in this world (marriage, inheritance, etc.). Alternatively, it means God is merciful in all matters except where He knows He will punish for their sins. Thus, the objectors' claim that the fasiq is an enemy of God is refuted.

Why God Cannot Curse Conditionally
Baqillani distinguishes human conditional cursing from divine absolute cursing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God's cursing cannot be conditional on unknown outcomes, unlike human cursing which relies on apparent evidence.

Core Idea
God's absolute knowledge makes conditional cursing impossible.

In this passage, Baqillani responds to an objection that seems to equate human and divine cursing. The opponent argues that if humans can curse sinners on the condition that they will be punished, then God could do the same. Baqillani refutes this by pointing to a key difference: we do not know the final outcome, but God does. This distinction preserves God's perfection and shows why conditional judgments apply only to us.

Guided Path
1
Study the objection
Read the first part carefully: the opponent's claim that if humans can curse conditionally, then God can too. Notice the conditional 'shariṭa' (condition) that the sinner be among those punished.
2
Analyze the counter-example
Baqillani offers a parallel analogy: humans cursing false witnesses on condition they are liars. He argues that if this doesn't permit God to curse them conditionally, then the original objection fails.
The analogy is meant to show inconsistency in the opponent's reasoning.
3
Grasp the distinguishing reason
Focus on the phrase 'al-sabab al-mufariq' (the differentiating cause). Baqillani states that we do not know the outcomes of people's affairs, while God knows. Therefore, God's actions must be based on absolute certainty, not conditions.
This is the core of the argument: knowledge difference changes the ruling.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider why this matters for divine attributes. God's cursing is an expression of His justice and knowledge; it cannot be contingent on unknown possibilities. Human cursing, however, is permitted based on apparent sin.
