The Definition of Knowledge: Why 'Known' Not 'Thing'
Baqillani defines knowledge and defends the wording of its definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise definition of knowledge in kalam and why the term 'known' is used instead of 'thing'.

Core Idea
Knowledge is defined as knowledge of the known as it is, not as knowledge of a thing.

Imam Baqillani begins by tackling the foundational question: what is knowledge? He provides a precise definition and defends each word against potential objections. This passage shows how kalam scholars carefully guard definitions to avoid excluding valid objects of knowledge, such as non-existent entities.

Guided Path
1

2
Examine the objection and response
Read the objection: why use 'known' instead of 'thing'? Then read the response: because 'thing' implies existence, which would exclude non-existent knowns.
3
Reflect on the significance
Consider why this distinction matters for kalam. Ask yourself: what kinds of knowledge would be excluded if we used 'thing' instead of 'known'?
Non-existent entities like past events or future contingents are known but not existing things.

Commentary
This passage presents a formal kalam definition of knowledge and defends its precise wording. It is a definition, a proof of its correctness, and an objection-and-answer. 
- First, the definition: Knowledge (al-'ilm) is defined as 'knowledge (ma'rifah) of the known (al-ma'lum) as it is.' 
- Then, a proof of the definition's validity: a proper definition must fully encompass the defined, exclude what is not part of it, and include all of it. The given definition meets this criterion. Therefore, 'known' is the correct term. The passage concludes with 'and with God is success.'

The Twofold Division of Knowledge: Divine and Created
Baqillani divides knowledge into ancient (divine) and created (human).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
All knowledge is either uncreated (God's) or created (ours).

Before proceeding into the deeper proofs of kalam, Baqillani clarifies a foundational distinction: knowledge itself is not a single category. There is knowledge that is eternal and belongs to God alone, and knowledge that is temporal and belongs to creatures. Commentary

هذه المسألة من الفروع ولكن ألحقت بمسائل علم الكلام لما ظهر حينئذ من بدعة الإمامية من قولهم إنها من عقائد الإيمان وإنه يجب على النبي تعيينها والخروج عن العهدة في ذلك لمن له وكذلك على الأمة وقصارى أمر الإمامة انها قضية مصلحية اجتماعية ولا تلحق بالعقائد فلذلك ألحقوها بمسائل هذا الفن. وأما تعريف الإمامة: قال قوم: الإمامة رئاسة عامة في أمور الدين والدنيا. والأولى أن يقال: هي خلافة الرسول في إقامة الدين بحيث يجب اتباعه على كافة الأمة، وبهذا القيد يخرج من ينصبه الإمام في ناحية والمجتهد، والأمر بالمعروف. وإذا عرفت هذا فنقول: نصب الإمام عندنا واجب علينا سمعاً لا عقلاً ولا على الله كما قالت الإسماعيلية.
Chapter on the imamate. And a mention of a summary of rulings of reports. And among what indicates the invalidity of [explicit] textual designation and the validity of choice. He said, may God perpetuate his support: We have previously dictated a summary on the imamate, which we made an introduction to the book 'The Virtues of the Imams and the Refutation of the Defamations against the Predecessors of the Community', we abridged it. This issue is among the derived branches but has been attached to the issues of kalam because at that time the innovation of the Imamiyyah appeared from their saying that it is among the doctrines of faith and that it is obligatory for the Prophet to designate [the imam] and to discharge responsibility in that for whomever it concerns, and likewise upon the community. The sum of the matter of imamate is that it is a social welfare issue and does not attach to doctrines, so they attached it to the issues of this art. As for the definition of imamate: Some people said: Imamate is general leadership in religious and worldly matters. The better thing to say is: It is the deputyship of the Messenger in establishing the religion, in such a way that it is obligatory to follow him for the entire community. By this restriction, those whom the imam appoints in a region, the mujtahid, and the commander of good are excluded. And when you know this, we say: Appointment of the imam is obligatory upon us by revelation, not by reason, nor upon God, as the Isma'iliyyah said.

Commentary
This passage defines imamate and establishes its ruling. It begins by identifying the chapter on imamate, then notes that what follows demonstrates the invalidity of explicit textual designation (nass) and the validity of choice (ikhtiyar). The author, Baqillani, states that he previously dictated a summary on imamate as an introduction to another work. Then a commentatorial note explains why imamate is treated in kalam: it is originally a branch of law but was included because the Imamiyyah considered it a matter of faith requiring prophetic designation. The note emphasizes that imamate is a social welfare issue, not a doctrinal one. Then the definition is given: some define imamate as general leadership, but the preferred definition is that it is the deputyship of the Messenger (khilafat al-rasul) in establishing the religion, with obligatory obedience from the entire community. This excludes appointed governors, independent jurists (mujtahidun), and those commanding good. Based on this definition, Baqillani states that appointing an imam is obligatory upon the community by revelation (sam'an), not by reason, and not upon God, contrary to the Isma'iliyyah who claim it is obligatory upon God.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Imamate is an article of faith (doctrine) that must be designated by the Prophet.
Correction: Baqillani states it is a social welfare issue and its obligation is by revelation, not a doctrinal matter and not obligatory upon God.

Key Takeaway
Imamate is defined as deputyship of the Prophet in religion, and its appointment is obligatory upon the community by revelation, not by reason.
