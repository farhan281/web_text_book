Third Proof: God's Names Are Not Merely Human Conventions
Baqillani argues from the pre-existence of God's names and attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the consensus of the ummah proves that God's names and attributes are real, existing prior to creation, and are not merely human descriptions.

Core Idea
God's names and attributes exist before any human speaks them.

This lesson presents Baqillani's third proof, which draws on the community's consensus to establish that divine names and attributes are not mere human inventions. Instead, they are eternal realities inherent to God, independent of creation. The argument uses a conditional to expose the absurdity of the opposite view.

Therefore, human speech (al-qawl) is not identical to the attribute (al-ṣifa); the description (al-waṣf) and naming (al-tasmiya) are separate from the actual name and attribute.

Names and attributes are real, not just human descriptions.

This passage presents a concise proof from consensus (ijmāʿ). Baqillani shows that if names were merely descriptions, then before creation Allah would have no names—contradicting the Ummah's consensus. Thus, the act of describing is different from the attribute itself.

Refuting That Attributes Are Merely Descriptions
Baqillani refutes that attributes are descriptions by reductio ad absurdum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Baqillani argues that attributes cannot be mere descriptions but must be real properties inherent in the essence.

Core Idea
If attributes were just words, contradictory statements would force impossible realities.

In this passage, Imam Baqillani presents a logical proof from reason to refute the view that divine attributes are nothing more than human descriptions. He shows that if attributes were merely statements, then conflicting statements would require an entity to possess contradictory qualities simultaneously—an absurdity. This establishes that attributes are objective realities inhering in the essence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the translation. Let the author's rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the logical structure: hypothesis, consequence, contradiction, conclusion.
2
Identify the hypothesis and its consequence
Baqillani starts by supposing that an attribute is just a description. Under that assumption, different people could give contradictory descriptions. What would follow?
He uses the example of Zayd being called alive by one and dead by another.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
Since contradictory descriptions would force contradictory attributes to coexist—which is impossible—the initial assumption is false. What then is the true nature of an attribute?
He concludes that attributes exist in the essence itself, like movement in a moving body.

ومما يدل على ذلك أيضاً من جهة المعنى أنه لو كانت الصفة في الحقيقة ليست بمعنى أكثر من وصف الواصف وقوله: «إن زيداً عالم قادر حي»، لوجب أن يكون لزيد في الحقيقة في حالة واحدة صفتان متضادّتان. ولوجب أن يكون حيّاً ميتاً وقادراً عاجزاً وعالماً جاهلاً، إذا قال بعض الواصفين له إنه حي، وقال آخر إنه ميت، وقال قائل إنه قادر عالم، وقال آخر إنه عاجز جاهل. فيجب أن لا يكون العلم من صفته أولى من الجهل لأنه قد وجدت له الصفتان؛ فيجب أن يكون زيد عالماً جاهلاً وحيّاً ميتاً لوجود صفتيه اللتين هما القول. فلما لم يجز ذلك، وكان العالم في الحقيقة هو من وجد به العلم ولوجوده به صار عالماً، بطل أن تكون الصفة هي القول، وثبت أنها ما يوجد بذات الموصوف، كما أن الحركة واللون هما ما وجدا بذات المتحرك والمتلوّن دون القول «إنه متحرك متلوّن».
And among what also indicates that from the perspective of meaning is that if the attribute in reality meant nothing more than the description of the describer and his saying 'Indeed Zayd is knowing, powerful, alive', then it would be necessary that Zayd in reality in one state have two contradictory attributes. And it would be necessary that he be alive and dead, powerful and powerless, knowing and ignorant, if some describers say he is alive, another says he is dead, another says he is powerful [and] knowing, and another says he is powerless [and] ignorant. Then it would be necessary that knowledge not be more entitled to be his attribute than ignorance, because both attributes have come to exist for him; thus Zayd would necessarily be both knowing and ignorant, alive and dead due to the existence of his two attributes which are the utterance. But since that is not permissible, and the knower in reality is the one with whom knowledge exists and by its existence he becomes knowing, it is invalid that the attribute be the utterance, and it is established that it is what exists in the essence of the described, just as movement and color are what exist in the essence of the moving and colored [thing], not the utterance 'he is moving, colored'.

Commentary
This passage presents a reductio ad absurdum argument from reason (min jihat al-maʿnā). Baqillani supposes, for the sake of argument, that an attribute (ṣifa) is nothing more than a description (waṣf) — i.e., the speaker's statement. If that were the case, then if different speakers describe the same person Zayd with contradictory descriptions (e.g., one says 'alive', another says 'dead'), Zayd would actually have both attributes simultaneously in one state, which is impossible. Since knowledge and ignorance, life and death cannot coexist, the premise that attribute equals statement must be false. Instead, the true attribute is a real property inhering in the essence (dhāt) of the described, like movement in a moving body, independent of anyone's statement. The conclusion: the attribute is not the utterance but rather something that exists in the entity itself.

Refuting the Consensus Argument: Derivation of 'Wasif'
Baqillani refutes the Mu'tazilite claim based on consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's linguistic rebuttal to the Mu'tazilite claim that the term 'Wasif' must be derived from creating an attribute.

Core Idea
Wasif is derived from describing (wasf), not from creating an attribute (sifa).

In this passage, Baqillani tackles a Mu'tazilite argument that uses consensus to claim that true attributes are only those by which a describer describes, not the created qualities themselves. He responds by questioning the linguistic derivation of the word 'Wasif', showing that it comes from the act of describing rather than from the act of creating an attribute. This refutation hinges on Arabic verbal patterns.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation (radd) of an objection raised by opponents who argue that attributes (ṣifāt) are not real in the sense of being independent entities inherent in a subject. Their evidence is based on linguistic derivation: they claim that the term 'wāṣif' (describer) is derived from the act of describing a subject with words, not from the act of creating attributes in that subject. For example, if God says 'the body is knowing, black, moving', He is describing it; but if He simply creates knowledge, power, etc. in the body, no one would say He is 'describing' it. Therefore, they conclude, the true attribute (ṣifa) is the act of describing, not the inherent quality. Baqillani responds with two arguments. First, he examines the derivation of 'wāṣif': the verb 'waṣafa' (to describe) yields 'wāṣif', but there is no verb 'faʿala al-ṣifa' (to perform the attribute) from which 'wāṣif' could be derived. Thus 'wāṣif' comes from 'al-waṣf' (the act of describing), not from 'al-ṣifa' (the attribute). Hence the opponents' inference that attributes must be the same as describing is invalid. Second, Baqillani argues by reductio ad absurdum: if we were required to derive names for God from every act He performs in others, we would have to call Him 'mu'dhī' (harmful) for causing menstrual blood and crop damage, and even 'muhawwis' (maddening) etc. — which no one accepts. Conversely, if we were forbidden to derive names from any act, then we could not call Him 'ʿādil' (just) from His justice or 'muḥsin' (beneficent) from His beneficence, which contradicts consensus. Therefore, the principle is selective: God may be named from some acts and not from others. Thus, the fact that He is not called 'wāṣif' from creating knowledge does not negate that knowledge is truly an attribute, just as not calling Him 'mu'dhī' from creating harm does not negate that harm is truly harm. The opponents have no answer to this. The passage concludes with 'And with God is success.'

Core Idea

When you pray, do you truly believe that Allah, in His majesty, turns His face towards you as long as you face Him? Or does your mind wander, turning away from His mercy? Today, commit to praying with the awareness that His face is oriented towards you.
?
The hadith says nothing prevents the people from looking at their Lord except the cloak of pride upon His face. This pride is an attribute of majesty. How does knowing that Allah's pride veils Him affect your awe of Him? Do you live with that reverence?
?
Baqillani emphasizes that Allah is not contained by any place, not even Jannah. Does your heart ever imagine Allah as being 'somewhere'? Repent from that imagination and affirm His transcendence, training your heart to worship Him without spatial boundaries.
