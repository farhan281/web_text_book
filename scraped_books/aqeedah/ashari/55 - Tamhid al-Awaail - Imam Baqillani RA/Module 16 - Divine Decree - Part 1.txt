The Dilemma of the Eternal Source of Evil
Baqillani poses a dilemma to refute the Magian claim about the source of evil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical dilemma used by Imam Baqillani to refute the Magian claim that the source of evil is eternal.

Core Idea
If the source of evil is eternal, the devil must be eternal too.

The Magians argued that evil (the devil) originated from an eternal doubt or thought in God. Imam Baqillani responds with a sharp dilemma: if the source of evil is eternal, then the devil must also be eternal, contradicting their claim that the devil is created. Additionally, an eternal doubt would imply eternal ignorance in God, which is impossible. This lesson walks through that powerful refutation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Magian claim
The Magians believe that evil (the devil) originated from an eternal doubt or thought in God. They consider the source eternal but the devil itself created.
This claim is the target of Baqillani's refutation.
2
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage slowly, then read the translation. Notice the question at the beginning and the two consequences given.
The Arabic uses the terms 'qadīm' (eternal) and 'muḥdath' (originated).
3
Analyze the dilemma
Identify the two consequences of saying the source is eternal: (1) It would make God ignorant, and (2) The devil would also be eternal. Both contradict Magian beliefs.
The argument is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
4
See the inescapability
Note the conclusion: the Magians have no escape. If they accept the source as eternal, they must give up the temporality of the devil. If they reject, the source is originated, contradicting their initial claim.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical refutation of the Magian claim that evil (the devil) originated from a doubt or thought in God. The author presents a dilemma: the source (doubt, thought, or punishment) that produced the devil — is it originated (muḥdath) or eternal (qadīm)? If the Magians say it is eternal, then two consequences follow: (1) It would make it impossible for the Creator to be knowing (because an eternal doubt would mean eternal ignorance), and (2) If the source is eternal, then its effect (the devil) should also be eternal, because an eternal cause necessitates an eternal effect. But the Magians affirm that the devil is originated (ḥādith). So they would have to give up that claim. Thus, they have no escape: they cannot consistently maintain both that the source is eternal and the devil is originated. The argument is structured as a question, leading the opponent to an unacceptable conclusion.

Divine Knowledge and Will: Abu Hanifa's Argument
Abu Hanifa poses two sequential questions to Qadariyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Abu Hanifa Maturidi uses a logical two-step question to refute the Qadariyya by linking God's eternal knowledge and His will to execute it.

Core Idea
If God knows all things, He must will them; denying this implies ignorance of His will.

In this passage, Abu Hanifa Maturidi presents a concise yet powerful argument against the Qadariyya who deny divine predestination. He asks two sequential questions: first about God's knowledge of all that happens, then about His will to actualize that knowledge. The answers force the Qadariyya to either admit God's comprehensive will or fall into blasphemy.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the translation. Notice the two questions and their implied consequences.
This passage is the opening salvo of Abu Hanifa's refutation.
2
Identify the two questions
The first question is about God's knowledge: 'Did Allah know what will be?' The second is about His will: 'Did He will to execute His knowledge?' See how they build on each other.
3
Analyze the logical consequences
If Qadariyya deny the first question, they disbelieve by ignoring God's omniscience. If they accept it but deny the second, they imply God willed ignorance, which is unwise. Only accepting both leads to affirming divine decree.
This shows the power of logical necessity in kalam.

And He said concerning al-qadar in the sense of estimation: 'And He estimated (qaddara) therein its provisions.' And al-qadar also has the meaning of creation. Among that is His saying: 'He determined (qadara) and guided.' And al-qada may be in the sense of command. God Almighty said: 'And your Lord has decreed (qada) that you worship none but Him'—meaning your Lord commanded.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection-and-answer format followed by an enumeration of the meanings of qada and qadar. It begins with a question: 'Do you say that God decrees (qada) and predetermines (qadar) sins just as He creates them?' The answer affirms this but clarifies the meaning: God creates disobedience and makes it according to His intent (qasd), not as a command (amr). That is, His decree of sin is a creative decree, not a legislative command. Then a new question asks on how many aspects (wujuh) qada is divided. The answer gives multiple senses: (1) Qada as creation (khalq), supported by Quranic verses where 'qada' is interpreted as 'creating'. (2) Qada as informing (ikhbar), writing (kitaba), and making known (i'lam), citing the verse to the Children of Israel. (3) Qadar as estimation (taqdir), as in the verse about provisions. (4) Qadar also as creation (khalq), as in 'qadara fahada' (He determined and guided). (5) Qada as command (amr), as in the verse about worshipping none but Him. The lesson thus distinguishes these meanings to prevent confusion: when speaking of God's decree of sin, it is not a command but a creative act.
