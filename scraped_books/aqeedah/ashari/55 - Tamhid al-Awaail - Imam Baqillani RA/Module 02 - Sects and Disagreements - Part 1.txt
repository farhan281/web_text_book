Early Unity and the First Dispute
Describes initial unity and first ijtihādī dispute over leadership.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the nature of the first disagreement among Muslims after the Prophet's death—that it was an ijtihādī matter concerning leadership (imāma and caliphate) and was quickly resolved through consensus and bayʿa.

Core Idea
The first Muslim dispute was over leadership, not doctrine, and it was swiftly resolved.

Imam al-Bāqillānī begins this passage by emphasizing that the Muslim community was united in creed and practice after the Prophet's death, except for hypocrites. He then introduces the first disagreement, explaining that it arose from ijtihād (independent reasoning) aimed at preserving the religion. The example he provides—the famous 'pen and paper' incident—is often misunderstood, but the author shows that it was fundamentally about the imāma (leadership). This lesson unpacks this historical moment and its significance.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic text aloud or silently, then follow the literal translation. Notice the flow: unity → first dispute → example → interpretation → resolution.
The Arabic is relatively straightforward; pay attention to key terms like اختلاف and اجتهادية.
2
Understand the context of the pen and paper incident
The author cites the hadith of the Prophet asking for writing materials during his final illness. Reflect on why ʿUmar said 'The Book of God is sufficient for us' and how this relates to ijtihād.
This incident is well-known; here it is interpreted as a dispute about leadership.
3
Grasp the author's interpretation
Al-Bāqillānī explicitly states that this was a dispute over the imāma and caliphate. However, he notes that it was resolved when ʿAlī gave bayʿa to Abū Bakr, restoring unity.
The author wants to show that early differences were not doctrinal but practical, and they were reconciled.
4
Identify the key takeaway
Notice the author's emphasis on the temporary and ijtihādī nature of the first dispute. This shapes the Sunni understanding of the early community's unity.
Contrast this with sectarian narratives that view the dispute as a major split.

Commentary
This passage is a historical narrative about the state of the Muslim community after the Prophet's death. The author begins by stating that the Muslims were united in belief and practice, except for hypocrites. The first disagreements were ijtihādī (based on independent reasoning) and aimed at preserving religion. The author gives the example of the famous incident during the Prophet's final illness when he asked for writing materials, and ʿUmar said 'the Book of God is sufficient,' leading to a dispute. The author interprets this as a dispute about leadership (imāma and caliphate). However, it was quickly resolved by the pledge of allegiance (bayʿa) of ʿAlī to Abū Bakr, unifying the community. The author then notes that there was no major disagreement during the caliphates of ʿUmar and ʿUthmān, except for some criticisms of ʿUthmān's administrative policies. This sets the stage for the later fitna.

The Great Fitnah and the Birth of Sects
Historical account of the great fitnah and sect emergence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the great fitnah after ʿUthmān's assassination led to the emergence of the Khawarij and Sabāʾiyya sects, fulfilling the Prophet's prophecy of 73 sects.

Core Idea
The great fitnah exposed infiltration and extremism, splitting the ummah into sects.

This passage from Imam al-Baqillani traces the tragic chain of events starting from the murder of ʿUthmān. He shows how political turmoil allowed deviant ideologies to take root, producing the Khawarij and the Sabāʾiyya. As you read, notice how the author links historical events to theological deviation and the prophetic warning about sectarian division.

Guided Path
1
Read the historical narrative
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to grasp the sequence of events from ʿUthmān's death to the emergence of sects.
Pay attention to how the author describes the 'major tribulation'.
2
Identify the two main sects

Understand the role of ʿAbd Allāh ibn Sabaʾ
Observe how the author portrays Ibn Sabaʾ as a deceptive figure who instigated extremism and division.
He is said to have pretended to embrace Islam to cause fitnah.
4
Connect to the prophetic prophecy
The passage ends with the hadith of 73 sects. Reflect on how this early division fits into that prophecy.
One saved, rest in fire.
5
Distinguish sincere ijtihād from deviance
The author defends ʿAlī's supporters as sincere, unlike the extremists. Consider this nuance.
Not all involved in the fitnah were blameworthy.

Unity held until external enemies exploited internal grievances.

Commentary
This passage provides a historical narrative of the early Muslim community's political and sectarian fragmentation. It begins by noting that the initial dispute over the caliphate ended when 'Ali pledged allegiance to Abu Bakr, restoring unity under Abu Bakr's reign. The author states that no significant disagreement occurred during the caliphates of 'Umar and 'Uthman, except for criticisms of 'Uthman's administrative policies, which ultimately led to his assassination. This assassination is described as the 'great fitna' (trial) engineered by enemies of Islam who infiltrated the Muslim ranks. The conflict escalated during 'Ali's caliphacy, with battles such as the Battle of the Camel and Siffin, and the arbitration that followed. Consequently, two factions emerged: those loyal to 'Ali (the supporters) and the Khawarij (those who rebelled against him due to his acceptance of arbitration). He notes that 800 Companions of the Pledge of Ridwan fought with 'Ali at Siffin, and 300 were martyred. As 'Ali's caliphate progressed, the Sabaiyya sect appeared, who cursed most Companions, accused them of disbelief, and dissociated from them—hence also called 'al-Baraiyya' (the dissociators). 'Ali himself dissociated from them. Their leader was 'Abdullah ibn Saba, originally a Jew who pretended to be Muslim to deceive and promote extremism about 'Ali, aiming to divide the ummah. The author compares this to earlier and later Christian divisive tactics, citing enmity with the Prophet and 'Umar's expulsion of Jews from Hijaz as background. Others from Jewish, Christian, polytheist, and Sabian backgrounds aided Ibn Saba. Thus, sects emerged, fulfilling the Prophet's prophecy that the ummah would split into 73 sects, with only one saved group entering Paradise and the rest destined for Hellfire.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of astrologers' claims about the celestial bodies. This necessary knowledge does not apply to stars; we are not compelled to know they are agents, nor can we infer it because evidence is lacking. Therefore, the astrologers have no way to know the stars are alive and powerful.

Proof That Light and Darkness Are Accidents

Learning Objective

Dualist Arguments for Two Eternal Principles
Baqillani presents three dualist arguments for two eternal principles based on empirical observations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three empirical arguments that dualists used to claim that the world originates from two eternal principles: light and darkness.

Core Idea
Dualists argue that bodies' properties prove two eternal origins: light and darkness.

Imam Baqillani, in his refutation of dualism, first presents the opponents' case. The dualists, likely Manichaeans, argue that the diversity of bodies can only be explained by two eternal principles. Their arguments rely on observable properties like shadow, opacity, and heaviness. This lesson examines those arguments as Baqillani recorded them.

Note the term 'tathniya' (dualism).
2
Identify the three arguments
The dualists give three arguments based on shadow, opacity, and motion. Find each and note how they classify bodies.
3
Consider their reasoning
Ask yourself: why do they think these properties indicate different origins? The commentary explains their inference.
This prepares for Baqillani's refutation.

And it is said to the people of dualism: Why do you claim that the entire world is from two eternal origins, one of which is light and the other darkness? If they say: Because we found all bodies are inseparable from being either possessing shadow or not possessing shadow, like fire and the luminous light and other bodies that have no shadow; and whatever is of this kind is from the individuals of light, and whatever is of the first is from the individuals of darkness. And we also found them not free from being either a veil preventing perception of what is behind them, like iron, rock, thick walls that prevent perception of visible and audible things; or not like that, like air, clear water, glass, and everything that allows what is behind it and does not prevent perception of visible and audible things behind it; and whatever is like that is from the individuals of light, and the first kind is from the individuals of darkness. And also because we found it not free from two kinds: either light, pure, whose property is rising, ascending, joining its world, longing for its origin and place of its center; or heavy, dark, whose property is descending, falling, piercing the light, and relying on what is beneath it, like iron, rock, earth, and other heavy bodies that rely on what is beneath them.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of the dualist argument (from the people of tathniya, likely Manichaeans) that the world consists of two eternal principles: light (nūr) and darkness (ẓalām). Baqillani presents their three empirical arguments: (1) bodies either cast shadows (associated with darkness) or do not (associated with light, like fire and luminous objects). (2) Bodies are either opaque (blocking sight/sound, associated with darkness) or transparent (allowing passage, associated with light). (3) Bodies are either light and ascending (light) or heavy and descending (darkness). The dualists infer that all bodies are individuals (ashkhāṣ) of either light or darkness. This sets up the refutation, which Baqillani would continue, but here he merely presents their case. The passage is important for understanding the opponent's position before refutation.

Dualists infer cosmic origins from shadow and transparency.

Refuting Dualism: The Contradiction of Distinct Principles
Dialectical argument refuting dualist principles through logical contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical refutation of dualism by demonstrating that if the two principles are distinct by themselves, then mixing leads to contradictions and collapses fundamental causal distinctions.

Core Idea
If two principles are distinct by themselves, they cannot become mixed without contradiction.

Imam al-Baqillani presents a rigorous dialectical argument against the dualist claim that the world is composed of two eternal principles (light and darkness). By questioning whether these principles are distinct by essence or by an external meaning, he forces the dualist into a logical collapse where the very cause of existence becomes the cause of non-existence. This passage demonstrates the power of rational argument in defending Islamic monotheism.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dualist claim
The dualists assert two eternal principles that mix to form the world. Note that the author begins by questioning the basis of their distinctness.
The dualists typically refer to light and darkness as the two principles.
2
Identify the dilemma
The argument presents two options: the principles are distinct by themselves or by a meaning. The author shows both lead to contradiction. Focus on the first branch.
3
Trace the logical consequence
If distinct by themselves, then mixing does not remove their distinctness, so they would be both distinct and mixed simultaneously. This leads to the absurdity that the world (resulting from mixing) both exists and does not exist.
The argument uses a reductio ad absurdum.
4
Extend the argument
The author extends the argument to show that this logic collapses all causal distinctions, such as motion/rest and eternity/origination, which is false by consensus.
This strengthens the refutation.

A question about the distinctness of the two principles and their mixing. They are asked: inform us about the two principles: are they distinct in pre-eternity by themselves or by a meaning of distinctness? If they say: by themselves, it is said to them: then they must be distinct in the state of their mixing because their selves exist in the state of their distinctness so that they are both distinct and mixed. And what prevents you from saying that they are also mixed by themselves? If they say: it is so, it is said to them: then their mixing is their distinctness, because mixing and distinctness are the same. If they say: yes, it is said to them: if the world exists because of their mixing and not because of their distinctness, then it must be that now it is a world and not a world because distinctness is mixing, and it must be that because of themselves the world existed and because of themselves it did not exist. This necessitates that that by which a thing exists and is is exactly that by which it does not exist and is not. If that is allowed, then it is allowed that that by which a body moves and leaves its place is that by which it is still and settled, and that by which a thing is eternal is that by which it is originated and new. That is false by consensus.

This passage presents a dialectical argument concerning the dualist claim that the world is composed of two eternal principles (light and darkness). The argument shows that such a view collapses fundamental causal distinctions, such as the difference between motion and rest or eternity and origination. The conclusion is that the dualist position is absurd by universal agreement.

The Dualist's Third Entity Dilemma
Al-Baqillani refutes the dualist evasion about distinctness as a third entity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Baqillani refutes the dualist claim that distinctness between light and darkness is a third eternal entity, using logical consequences to corner the opponent.

Core Idea
Claiming distinctness as a third entity destroys dualism and exposes internal contradictions.

This lesson presents a sharp argument from Imam al-Baqillani against the dualists who try to avoid the problem of two eternal principles by positing that the distinctness between them is a third entity. By following the necessary implications of their claim, he shows that it leads to either abandoning their religion or logical deadlock.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the translation. Let the argument flow naturally.
Notice the two-part structure: first a direct contradiction, then a conditional argument.
2
Identify the first argument
The first part shows that claiming distinctness is a third meaning forces the dualist to admit a third eternal principle, contradicting their dualism.
Key phrase: 'أقروا بقدم أصل ثالث' – they admit a third eternal principle.
3
Analyze the second argument
The second part asks whether distinctness ceases when mixing occurs. If it does, then an eternal thing (distinctness) can be annihilated, opening the door for light and darkness to also be annihilated.
The dualist cannot accept that their eternal principles can perish, so they are left without a coherent answer.
4
Understand the dilemma
The final sentence presents the dilemma: either they abandon their religion (by accepting the possibility of annihilation) or they have no way out (no coherent response).
This forces the dualist into an irresolvable contradiction.

This opens the door for the annihilation of light and darkness themselves, which is unacceptable to the dualist. The argument corner the opponent: either they accept the possibility of annihilation of their eternal principles (which undermines their religion) or they cannot find a coherent response.

The Proof That Inseparability from Originated Things Implies Origination

The Dilemma of the Creator: Good or Evil Origin?
Baqillani continues his refutation by examining the creator of the origin and the good/evil nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani refutes the Magian claims that Shaytan's origin is created by Shaytan or by God, and the logical consequences regarding good and evil.

Core Idea
If God created the origin, it must be good or evil, and both options lead to absurdities.

After showing that the origin cannot be eternal or uncaused, Baqillani turns to the possibility that the origin was created by a creator. He then examines two sub-possibilities: the creator is Shaytan himself, or the creator is God. Both lead to contradictions, especially when analyzing whether the origin is good or evil.

Guided Path
1
Read the continuation of the argument
Read from 'وإن قالوا من محدث' to the end. Notice how Baqillani deals with each response.
2
Analyze the claim that Shaytan created his own origin
If Shaytan created the doubt/thought that caused him, then he would exist before his own cause, which is impossible.
This is a logical contradiction.
3
Analyze the claim that God created the origin
Now Baqillani asks whether the origin is good or evil. If good, then good produces evil, which is absurd. If evil, then God created evil, and there is no reason to limit His creation of evil.
Note the reductio ad absurdum with fire cooling and snow heating.
4
Conclude the refutation
Baqillani ends by stating that there is no valid distinction (لا فصل فيه) — the Magian position collapses.

A question concerning refuting the Magians. Then it is said to them: 'Tell us about the doubt, thought, or punishment from which Shaytan occurred—are these created or eternal?' If they say that these matters are eternal, they are forced into the impossibility of the Creator being knowing and they are obliged to accept the eternity of ignorance. Then it is said to them: 'If the doubt, thought, and punishment from which Shaytan was are eternal, then what prevents you from saying that Shaytan is eternal due to the eternity of that from which he was?' If they pass over that, they abandon their statement of his temporality, and they have no escape from that. And if they say that doubt is created, and likewise punishment and thought according to the one who says each, it is said to them: 'Does doubt occur from a creator or not from a creator?' If they say 'not from a creator,' it is said to them: 'What assurance do you have that all other acts and events occur without a creator? And in that is denial and nullification of the Maker.' If they say 'from a creator' these things occurred, it is said to them: 'Who is their creator?' If they say 'Shaytan,' they feign ignorance, and it is said to them: 'Then Shaytan existed before the thought and doubt from which he was; how can a thing exist before its origin and cause from which it was and came to be?' And if they say 'God created doubt and thought,' it is said to them: 'Tell us about doubt and thought—are they evil or good?' If they say 'good,' it is said to them: 'How then did Shaytan, who is evil, occur from them? And what prevents you, if that is permissible, that Shaytan, who is evil, thinks an evil thought that generates good and good occurs? And if that is permissible, then it would be permissible for cooling to occur from fire and heating from snow. This refutes their statement.' And if they say that doubt is evil because it generated Shaytan who is evil, it is said to them: 'Then God has done good by creating doubt, which is evil from evil, and it is the origin of Shaytan. And if that is permissible, why is it not permissible for Him to do all other evils and all harmful entities like beasts, scorpions, snakes, worries, sorrows, and all other evils? And what is the distinction in that? There is no distinction in it.'

Commentary
This is a detailed dialectical refutation of the Magian (مجوس) claim that Shaytan originated from doubt, thought, or punishment. Bāqillānī systematically examines each possible answer the opponent might give, showing that each leads to an absurd consequence. The argument proceeds as follows: (1) If they say these origins are eternal, then Shaytan would be eternal (since effect follows cause), contradicting their claim that he is created. (2) If they say these origins are created, then either they have a creator or not. If no creator, then all events could be uncaused, which is atheism (تعطيل). If they have a creator and they say it is Shaytan, then Shaytan would exist before his own origin (impossible). If they say God is the creator, then either the origins are good or evil. If good, then how can evil come from good? That would also allow absurd reversals like fire cooling. If the origins are evil, then God created evil, and He might as well create all evils, which they must admit—thus no distinction can be made. The passage ends with the conclusion that there is no valid distinction (لا فصل فيه).

Questioning the Punishment Theory and Divine Origin of Evil
Bāqillānī questions those who claim Shaytan originated from a punishment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Bāqillānī uses a regress argument to question those who claim Shaytan arose from divine punishment, exposing a theological inconsistency.

Core Idea
If God creates the sinner, He is ultimately the source of evil.

In this passage, Bāqillānī confronts a particular theological view that claims Shaytan came into existence as a punishment from God. He poses a simple yet penetrating question: who created the disobedient person who deserved that punishment? If the answer is God, then God is implicated in the evil of disobedience just as much as the sinner. This lesson unpacks the logic of that challenge.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the rhetorical question at the end.
2
Understand the opponent's claim
Identify the opponents Bāqillānī addresses: the 'proponents of thought and punishment.' They believe that Shaytan originated as a result of a punishment from God.
This sets up the target of the argument.
3
Trace the regress
Follow the question: 'Who created the disobedient one deserving punishment?' If the answer is God, then God is the source of the evil (disobedience). The rhetorical question forces the opponent to see that God is implicated in evil.
This is the core of the argument.

Commentary
This passage applies a similar line of reasoning to those who claim that Shaytan originated from a punishment imposed by God. Bāqillānī asks: if Shaytan came from a punishment, then who created the disobedient person who deserved that punishment? If the answer is God, then God is the ultimate source of the disobedience that led to the punishment and ultimately to Shaytan. This implies that God is responsible for evil in the same way as the one who directly committed disobedience. The question highlights an inconsistency: if God creates the evildoer, then He is implicated in evil, which the opponent likely denies. The argument is left as a challenge, expecting the opponent to find no valid distinction.
