Can a Sense Perceive Another Sense's Object?
Baqillani argues for the possibility of sensory crossover via divine creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Baqillani affirms the possibility of sensory crossover and how this reflects the Ash'ari principle that God creates all perception directly.

Core Idea
God creates all perception; senses are just occasions.

This passage from Baqillani's Tamhid addresses a subtle theological question: can a sense perceive what normally belongs to another? After affirming the ordinary assignment of senses to objects, he argues that it is possible because perception is directly from God. The following text presents his reasoning and answers a potential objection.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage carefully
Read the Arabic once without analysis. Then read the translation. Note the structure: first the customary assignment, then the question, then the answer with proof, then objection and reply.
Pay attention to the phrase 'بمحض خلق الله تعالى'.
2
Identify the key doctrinal move
Baqillani first affirms the ordinary function of senses (hearing for sounds, etc.), then says the truth is permissibility. Why? Because perception is purely from God, not from the sense itself.
This is a classic Ash'ari occasionalist position.
3
Examine the objection and answer
The objection claims taste perceives both sweetness and heat. Baqillani corrects: sweetness is by taste, heat by touch in the mouth. So the senses remain distinct even when co-occurring.
This reinforces that each sense has its own sphere, but God could override it.

Commentary
This passage is a theological argument about the scope of sensory perception. Baqillani first affirms the customary order: God created each sense for specific objects—hearing for sounds, taste for flavors, smell for odors—and ordinarily a sense does not perceive what belongs to another. Then he addresses the question: could God make a sense perceive what is normally for another? The correct answer (al-ḥaqq) is that it is permissible (jawāz), because perception is purely God's creation, with no effect from the sense itself. Therefore, it is not impossible that God should create the perception of sounds at the moment of directing the sight (i.e., while looking). He then answers an objection: doesn't taste perceive both sweetness and heat? He clarifies that sweetness is perceived by taste, but heat is perceived by touch which exists in the mouth and tongue; it is not that taste perceives heat. This refutes any claim that a sense intrinsically perceives multiple types—rather, when two perceptions occur together, they are via different senses. The argument upholds Ash'ari occasionalism: God creates all perceptions directly, so He is not bound by the ordinary associations of senses and objects.

Proof for the Existence of Accidents
Rational proof for accidents using motion and rest of the body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof for the existence of accidents based on the body's alternation between motion and rest.

Core Idea
A body's change from motion to rest proves it is not self-moving but caused by an accident.

This passage presents a foundational argument in kalam for the existence of accidents. By observing simple changes in bodies—like motion and rest—the theologian deduces that these changes must be caused by something other than the body itself. That something is an accident. Let's examine this proof step by step.

Proof of Accidents from Motion and Rest
Baqillani proves the existence of accidents using the observation of motion and rest.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani uses the observation of motion and rest to prove the existence of accidents and why motion cannot be inherent in bodies.

Core Idea
Motion and rest in bodies reveal that accidents are real and caused.

Baqillani begins by establishing a fundamental proof for the existence of accidents—those transient attributes that inhere in substances. By observing simple changes like a body moving after being still, he demonstrates that such changes cannot come from the body's own nature and must therefore be caused by something else: the accident of movement. This argument becomes a template for proving all accidents.

Commentary
This passage provides a proof for the existence of accidents (a'raad). Baqillani argues that a body can be observed to move after being still and to be still after moving. This change cannot be due to the body's own nature, because if it were inherently moving, it could not rest. Since rest does occur, the movement must be caused by something else, which is the accident of movement (ḥaraka). This same proof also establishes other accidents like colors, tastes, smells, etc., because they too are impermanent. He then strengthens the argument by noting that if a body moved by itself, all bodies of its kind would always be moving, but we find stationary bodies among moving substances. Therefore, movement must be due to an external cause, i.e., the accident of movement.

Motion is not self-subsistent: an analogy
Baqillani proves motion is not self-subsistent using the analogy of blackness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Baqillani uses the analogy of blackness to demonstrate that motion is not an essential property of bodies but an accident caused by an external agent.

Core Idea
Motion is not essential to bodies; it is an accidental property requiring an external cause.

This short passage from Baqillani's Tamhid presents a concise logical argument. Using the example of blackness, he shows that if motion were self-subsistent, all bodies would always be moving, which contradicts our observations. This lesson unpacks the analogy and its implications for causality in kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
First, read the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Pay attention to the key terms: al-mutaharrik (the moving), as-sawad (blackness), bidhdhat (by itself/essence), bil-'arad (accidentally).
The passage is short but dense.
2
Understand the analogy
Notice the rhetorical question about blackness: if blackness is black by itself, can there be a non-black member of its genus? The answer is no. This sets up the standard for self-subsistent properties.
This is a key logical move.
3
Apply the analogy to motion
Baqillani then applies the same logic: among bodies, we find both moving and non-moving examples. Therefore, motion cannot be self-subsistent like blackness. It must be accidental.
This shows motion is caused.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The passage concludes that the mover (the moving body) is not moving by itself but 'by motion was moving'. This underscores the need for a cause of motion.
This is a foundational principle for proving the existence of Allah as the mover.

This passage presents an analogy to prove that motion in bodies is not self-subsistent (i.e., not an essential attribute of the body itself). But we observe that among substances and moving bodies, there are some that are not moving at a given time. This shows that motion is not an essential property of the body; rather, the body becomes moving through motion (an accident). Hence, motion is caused by an external agent.

Six Possible Objects of God's Power Over Motion
Baqillani lists six possible objects of power for moving a body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the six logical possibilities for what God's power to move a body applies to, as enumerated by Imam Baqillani.

Core Idea
God's power over motion has six possible objects; only one can be correct.

Imam Baqillani begins by observing that human power over motion varies, implying that power always has a real object (maqdur). He then applies this to God's power to move a body, listing six possibilities for what that object could be. Understanding these options is crucial for the subsequent arguments in kalam about how God acts in the world.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note the human analogy
Start by reading the Arabic and translation. Notice how Baqillani uses the variability of human power to establish that power must have an object of power (maqdur).
The human case is used as evidence (dalil) for divine power.
2
Identify the six enumerated possibilities
After stating the principle, Baqillani lists six options for what the object of God's power over moving a body could be. Try to list them yourself before reading the commentary.
The list is given in a single long sentence: 'إما أن يكون... أو...'.
3
Understand the last two ambiguous options
The fifth and sixth options are phrased negatively: 'or not for itself and not for a meaning connected to itself'. The commentary explains this likely means the power has no real object, or that the object is the body itself without any accident.
These are refuted in later passages.
4
Ask why this enumeration matters
Reflect on why Baqillani spends time listing possibilities that he will later refute. This is a standard dialectical method: exhaust the alternatives to show that only one is correct.
This leads to the conclusion that God's power to move a body is over producing an accident (meaning) in it, not over the body itself.

Commentary
This passage introduces the problem of what the power (qudra) to move a body actually applies to. The author observes that human power over motion is variable (sometimes able, sometimes unable), so it must have a real object (maqdur). Similarly, God's power to move or still a body must have a maqdur. The author then enumerates the logical possibilities: (1) the maqdur is the creation of the body itself (i.e., the act of moving is actually creating the body anew), (2) the maqdur is the creation of a meaning (accident) in the body, (3) the maqdur is the annihilation of the body, (4) the maqdur is the annihilation of a meaning from the body, (5) the maqdur is nothing at all (neither body nor meaning), or (6) the maqdur is the body itself without any meaning. Note: the last option 'or not for itself and not for a meaning connected to itself' is ambiguous; it likely refers to the possibility that the power has no real object. The subsequent passages refute these alternatives.

The Even-Odd Paradox and the Finitude of the Past

Baqillani tackles a key objection from naturalists who claim that a 'nature' (tab') is the real cause behind the world's events. Commentary
This passage is a sustained refutation of the notion that the world is caused by a 'nature' (tab'). Baqillani presents several arguments. Then he tackles the scenario where nature is created. He compares this to the impossibility of motion and rest coexisting in one locus. Since the world does exist, the premise of an infinite regress of natures must be false. Therefore, the world cannot be originated from any nature. This is a classic kalam argument against naturalistic causation, using the principle of impossibility of infinite regress and the observed fact of the world's origination.

Baqillani's Two Refutations of the Theory of Natures
Baqillani presents two arguments against natures as causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Baqillani's two arguments against the theory that events are caused by natures: a dilemma leading to infinite regress or incoherence, and a challenge to define what kind of accidents natures are.

Core Idea
Natures cannot explain causation without causing infinite regress or being arbitrary.

Baqillani presents two powerful refutations of the claim that events are caused by 'natures' (ṭabā'iʿ) inherent in bodies. The first argument shows the theory leads to an infinite regress or becomes self-defeating. The second argues that proponents cannot even define what these natures are, revealing the concept as incoherent. This lesson examines these arguments step by step.

Identify the dilemma: either natures themselves require another nature (leading to infinite regress) or they do not (making the theory arbitrary). Understand why both options are problematic for the claim that events are caused by natures.
Baqillani references an earlier proof that infinite regress is impossible.
3
Analyze the second argument (definition challenge)
Notice the challenge: proponents cannot specify from which genus or category of accidents these natures come. This shows the concept is not clearly defined and thus cannot be a valid explanation.
This is a direct request for concrete definition.
4

This connects to broader kalām debates on causality.

All Contingent Events Require a Living Agent
Baqillani refutes the theory of natures by parity of reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Baqillani argues that the need for a living, powerful agent is based on an event's contingency, not its specific type.

Core Idea
Contingency, not type, determines the need for a living cause.

Baqillani presents a powerful parity argument against the idea that some events can arise from unconscious natures. He shows that if we allow that for some events, we cannot logically deny it for all, including will and craftsmanship. The argument establishes that the basis for requiring a living agent is the event's contingency, not its specific nature.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
Read the Arabic passage once through, then read the English translation. Notice the structure: a conditional statement begins the argument.
Pay attention to the phrase 'لو جاز' (if it were permissible) which sets up the reductio.
2
Identify the conditional argument
Baqillani argues that if some events (like satiety and pain) could arise from a non-living nature, then logically all events (including will and craftsmanship) could also arise from such a nature. This is the core of the parity reasoning.

Understand the conclusion
Since the consequence is absurd (we know will and craftsmanship require a living agent), the original permission is false. Baqillani then states that the reason all events need a living agent is their common property of being contingent (حادث).
The phrase 'جهة تعلّق هي كونها فعلاً حادثاً' is key.

The Principle of Consistent Causation and Its Implications
Baqillani states the principle of consistent causation and applies it to examples of knowledge, will, and striking.

Core Idea
If natures were real, their effects would be constant; they are not.

Baqillani begins by stating a foundational principle: every effect has a cause, and when the cause repeats, the effect repeats. He then applies this to the theory of natures, showing that natural causes like striking should produce consistent results, but they don't always, pointing to a willful agent behind events.

Commentary
This passage is an empirical argument against the theory of natures. Baqillani first states the principle that every effect has a cause, and when the cause is repeated, the effect is repeated; when causes multiply, effects multiply. He gives as example that a knower knows whenever he has knowledge, in all times and places. Also, those who believe in generation of pain from hitting say that pain increases with more hits. Then he applies this to natures: if the nature of food caused satiety, then eating more should cause more satiety, but beyond a certain point it causes pain, not satiety. If the nature of watering caused plant growth, then more watering would make it grow indefinitely, but in fact overwatering kills it. This shows that the supposed natures are not consistent causes; their effects are not necessary but depend on other conditions, indicating that events are not produced by fixed natures but by a willful agent.

Refuting Natural Causation through Observed Irregularity
Baqillani disproves the theory of natures using examples of regular causation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Baqillani refutes the theory of natural causation by pointing out the lack of uniformity in cause-effect relationships, thereby proving that the true cause is a voluntary agent.

Core Idea
If effects were from natures, they would be uniform; exceptions prove a voluntary Creator.

Baqillani presents a powerful argument against those who claim that natural properties (ṭabā'iʿ) are the real causes behind observed effects. He uses everyday examples to show that if these properties were inherent, their effects would occur consistently, but we see clear exceptions. This leads him to conclude that the true cause is Allah's will.

Commentary
This passage is a proof against the theory of natures (ṭabā'iʿ) as causes of observed effects. Baqillani begins by stating that we know by necessity that every effect has a cause, and that when causes are repeated, effects are repeated accordingly. This is a premise accepted by those who affirm cause and effect. He then gives several examples: the necessary connection between knowledge/will and the act of knowing/willing; the generation of pain from hitting; the production of intoxication, satiety, growth of plants from their supposed natural sources. In each case, if these effects were due to a nature inherent in the object, they would occur whenever the object is present and conditions are met, and they would increase as the object or its intensity increases. But we observe the opposite: excessive watering kills plants, overeating causes discomfort instead of continued satiety, etc. Therefore, the effects are not due to a nature but to a voluntary agent who creates them according to His will. Baqillani then concludes that the natures claimed by opponents are not causes in the true sense, nor are they generated causes as some (like the Mu'tazila) hold. He further states that it has been proven earlier that accidents cannot act, so the claim of natures (which are accidents) acting is false.
