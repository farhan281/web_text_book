Refuting the Linguistic Objection to Moses' Request
Baqillani refutes the linguistic objection to Moses' request using three arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the linguistic objection that Moses' request could mean knowledge or a sign is invalid based on three Arabic linguistic rules.

Core Idea
The word 'nazar' with 'ila' always means physical vision in Arabic.

In this passage, Imam Baqillani responds to an objection that Moses' request 'Show me, that I may look upon You' could be interpreted as asking for knowledge or a sign. He provides three linguistic proofs that such interpretations are impossible in Arabic. This lesson will walk through each argument and show why the objectors are refuted.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and its context
First, read the objection that Moses could have meant something other than seeing Allah. Understand the two alternative meanings proposed: asking for necessary knowledge or a sign of the Hour.
2
Identify the three refutation arguments
The refutation contains three distinct linguistic points. As you read the passage, note each one and how it builds on the previous.
3
Understand the linguistic rule about 'nazar' with 'ila'
Pay special attention to the third argument, which is the strongest: when 'nazar' is transitive with 'ila', it exclusively means vision of the eye. This is a fixed rule in Arabic.
4
Connect the refutation to divine transcendence
Consider why this linguistic argument matters for preserving the transcendence of Allah: if Moses could not ask to see Allah, then no one can, affirming that Allah is not visible in this world.

Refutation of the Doubt about the Israelites' Request to See Allah

The Interpretive Priority of the Vision Verse
Baqillani establishes the interpretive priority of the vision verse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the correct interpretive method for verses about seeing Allah: the affirming verse must be taken literally and the negating verses interpreted in light of it.

Core Idea
The verse affirming vision is the key that unlocks the meaning of negating verses.

When dealing with seemingly conflicting verses about Allah, scholars of kalam prioritize certain verses over others based on established principles. Baqillani presents a foundational rule: the verse that speaks of faces looking at their Lord (75:22-23) must be interpreted literally as seeing with the eyes, and any verse that negates vision must be understood in the context of this literal affirmation. This principle safeguards the possibility of seeing Allah in the Hereafter while respecting all scriptural texts.

The affirming verse is taken as literal.
3
Understand the reason for priority
Baqillani states that the affirming verse 'cannot be other than vision with the eyes'. This decisive certainty gives it interpretive authority over the others.
4
Reflect on the method
Ask yourself: How does this method differ from taking all verses at face value or from philosophical reinterpretation? The principle here is scriptural prioritization based on established meaning.
This is a key methodological tool in Ash'ari kalam.

The Alif of Exaggeration Does Not Necessarily Imply Shared Attributes
Baqillani refutes the claim that the alif of superlative requires shared attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the alif of exaggeration (ألف مبالغة) in Arabic can be used to single out someone as uniquely possessing an attribute, not only to indicate shared participation and gradation.

Core Idea
The alif of exaggeration can deny partners, not just compare equals.

In defending divine unity, Baqillani addresses a linguistic objection: the word 'ahsan' in the Quran might seem to imply that Allah shares the attribute of goodness with others, with comparison. But he shows that the same grammatical form can be used to refute any claim of sharing, making it a tool for tawhid. This lesson unpacks his argument and its evidence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the opponent's claim: the alif of exaggeration only occurs when multiple entities share an attribute and you compare them. This would imply partners for Allah.
2
Baqillani's concession and qualification
Note that Baqillani admits the basic rule, but immediately adds an exception: it can also indicate exclusivity and refute false claims.
This is a classical scholarly move: concede the general principle but show a counter-purpose.

Analyze the first example: Surah an-Naml 27:59
Look at the verse 'Qul Allahu khayrun amma yushrikun'. Baqillani says the word 'khayrun' is used to deny any goodness in what they associate. It does not compare two good things.
The context is challenging idolatry.
4
Examine the poetic evidence
Read the lines by Hassan ibn Thabit and al-Farazdaq. In each, the superlative form is used to assert exclusive superiority, not to measure degrees among equals.
These show the form works for exclusivity in human speech too.

Reflect on the second Quranic example: Surah ar-Rum 30:27
The verse 'wa huwa ahwanu 'alayh' is interpreted as meaning 'in your belief', so it does not imply that another being finds anything easier. It refutes a false notion.
This ties the argument back to tawhid.

This passage contains an objection and Baqillani's response. The opponents argue that the alif (الألف) in the word 'ahsan' (أَحْسَنُ) is the alif of exaggeration (ألف مبالغة) which, according to them, only appears in constructions requiring shared participation in the attribute and establishing gradation—thus implying multiple creators. Then the author concludes that the verse 'فتبارك الله أحسن الخالقين' refutes the claim that others create. Thus false claimants are refuted.

Baqillani responds to an objection that if God creates insults and hatred directed at Himself, He must be worthy of them. Commentary
This passage presents a dialectical objection-and-answer sequence on the problem of divine justice. The objector lists several divine actions that appear unjust: commanding animal slaughter, inflicting pain without benefit, subjugating some animals to others, eternal punishment for finite sins, impossible obligations, and creating people with traits for which they are punished. The initial response is a brief affirmation that these are just and good in God's wisdom. The objector then presses: how can these be good from God while they would be bad from us? The author then inserts a detailed exposition of Mu'tazila theories on pain and compensation, describing their views on whether compensation for pain is necessary, whether God can bestow favors similar to rewards, and the positions of various Mu'tazila scholars. After this background, the answer is given: the actions are bad from us only because God has prohibited them; if He had not prohibited them, they would not be bad. God, as the absolute Owner and Subduer, has no commander or prohibitor above Him. Therefore, His actions cannot be judged bad by analogy to human actions. The argument hinges on the Ash'ari principle that moral value (husn/qubh) is determined by divine command, not by intrinsic properties of acts.
