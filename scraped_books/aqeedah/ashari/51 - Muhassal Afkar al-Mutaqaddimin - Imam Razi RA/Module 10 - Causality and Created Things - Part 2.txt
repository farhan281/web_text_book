Conditioning by Non-Existence vs Causing by Non-Existence
Razi distinguishes conditioning by non-existence from causation by non-existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Razi's distinction between something being a condition for existence and being a cause, especially when the condition is non-existence.

Core Idea
Non-existence can be a condition without being a cause.

In this lesson, we delve into Imam Razi's response to an objection about prior motions being conditions. He clarifies that the non-existence of a prior state can be a condition for the existence of a later state without implying that non-existence causes existence, using everyday examples.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and translation. Pay attention to the objection and Razi's counter-example.
2
Identify the objection
Find where the objector argues that the agency of the prior motion is a new accident requiring a cause, and Razi's response about conditioning.
3
Analyze Razi's examples
Razi gives two examples: absence of clouds conditions sunlight, and absence of grease conditions dyeing. Notice how they illustrate the logical point.
4
Understand the logical distinction
The key is that conditioning existence by non-existence is not the same as causing existence by non-existence. The former is acceptable, the latter impossible.

Refuting the Argument from Eternal Rest
Imam Razi refutes the claim that eternal rest implies a necessitating cause leading to infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi refutes the claim that eternal rest implies a necessitating cause leading to infinite regress.

Core Idea
Eternal rest does not force a regress unless conditions are unconditioned.

This lesson examines Imam Razi’s response to a key objection against the temporality of bodies. The objector argued that if rest is eternal, it requires a cause that leads to infinite regress. Razi shows that the objector’s reasoning depends on an unproven assumption about conditions and that the argument does not require bodies to be similar.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without stopping. Then read the literal translation. Notice the structure: the objector’s claim is stated first, then Razi’s two refutations.
The passage is dense; read slowly.
2
Identify the two refutations
Razi gives two responses. First, he demands proof that conditions cannot be conditioned ad infinitum. Second, he dismisses the need to prove body similarity. Find these in the text.
The second refutation begins with 'وقوله: من أراد تعميم الدلالة...'.
3
Reflect on the logical structure
Consider why the objector's infinite regress fails if conditions can be chained. Also, think about why the proof against something that cannot be free from motion/rest does not require similarity of bodies.
This shows Razi’s method of refuting arguments without conceding unnecessary premises.

Commentary
This passage refutes the second aspect of an argument concerning eternal rest. The objector argued that if rest is eternal and not necessary per se, it requires a necessitating cause. If the cause's effect is unconditional, the rest would never cease; if conditional, the condition leads to an infinite regress. Imam Razi responds that this reasoning only holds if one first proves that every condition cannot itself be conditioned ad infinitum. The objector failed to provide that proof. Furthermore, the objector's claim that one must prove the similarity of bodies to generalize the argument is rejected: the proof against something that cannot be free from motion or rest does not depend on the things being similar—it applies to anything that necessarily has one of these attributes. If it is established that something in pre-eternity has both motion and rest in agreement, then that thing would be temporal (i.e., created), regardless of its nature.

The Four Causes and the Efficient Cause Proof
Imam Razi presents the philosophers' four causes and their proof from the efficient cause against the world's origination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' four causes of everything originated and their argument from the efficient cause that the world cannot have originated.

Core Idea
Every originated thing needs four causes; the efficient cause leads to an impossible dilemma.

Imam Razi begins his critique of the philosophers by presenting their core causal framework. He states their axiom that every event requires four causes and then focuses on the first aspect — the efficient cause — to show how they argue against the world's temporal beginning. This passage sets up the philosophical challenge that later kalam arguments must answer.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice the logical structure: a general statement, then a specific argument.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the four causes
Find the four causes mentioned: الفاعل (efficient), المادة (material), الصورة (formal), الغاية (final). Understand that these are claimed to be necessary for everything originated.
3
Trace the efficient cause argument
The argument runs: if the world is originated, it has an eternal effect. Its origination at a specific time requires a preponderant (مرجح). Either there is a preponderant or not. Both options are deemed impossible, so the world cannot be originated.
This is a dilemma (syllogism).
4
Reflect on the dilemma
Consider why the philosophers reject both options. The first option (existence of a preponderant) is rejected because pure negation (the eternal agent's state before creation) cannot have distinction. The second option (no preponderant) is against the principle that a possibility cannot be preferred without a cause.

They said: We will demonstrate in these aspects the impossibility of the world's origination. With respect to the efficient cause: If the world were originated, it would have an eternal effect, so the specification of its origination to the time in which it was originated would either have a preponderant or not. The first is false because pure negation admits no distinction. Commentary
This passage presents the philosophers' initial claim and then focuses on the first of their four proofs against the world's having a beginning. Imam Razi begins by stating the philosophical axiom that every originated thing requires four causes: efficient (الفاعل), material (المادة), formal (الصورة), and final (الغاية). They then announce that they will demonstrate the impossibility of the world's being originated by examining each of these aspects. The first proof looks at the efficient cause: if the world were originated, it would need an eternal effect, i.e., an eternal creator. But then the fact that the creator brought it into existence at a specific time rather than another requires a preponderating factor (مرجح) that tips the scales. The philosophers argue that such a preponderant is impossible: either it exists (which would mean the creator had a reason to create at that time, but that would mean the creator's will is dependent on something external, which is impossible for an eternal being) or it does not exist (which would mean the occurrence of one possibility over another without any cause, which is impossible). Thus, they conclude that the world cannot have originated.

Refuting the Objection on Priority and Time
Imam Razi refutes the objection that priority of non-existence implies eternity of time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi refutes the objection that priority of non-existence requires the eternity of time by distinguishing between intrinsic and extrinsic priority.

Core Idea
Priority does not always require time; only intrinsic priority does.

In this passage, Imam Razi addresses a subtle objection: if non-existence is prior to existence, doesn't that imply time is eternal? The opponent claims that just as some parts of time are prior to others, the priority of non-existence over existence would require time. Razi responds by showing a flaw in the analogy, clarifying the nature of priority and posteriority.

3
Understand Razi's response
Notice that Razi says priority and posteriority are intrinsic to time, but not to non-existence and existence. Therefore, the priority of non-existence needs a time medium.
This is the key distinction: intrinsic vs. extrinsic priority.
4
Contrast with parts of time
See how parts of time have priority and posteriority built into their concepts, so they don't need an external time.
This contrast shows why the analogy fails.

Refuting the Objection on Motion and Rest Outside Place
Imam Razi refutes the objection that the world cannot be in motion or rest since it is not in a place.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi refutes the claim that the world cannot be in motion or rest because it is not in a place, by exposing the flaws in the opponents' definition of motion and rest.

Core Idea
Motion and rest depend on definition, not on being in a place.

Imam Razi responds to an objection that since the world is not located in any place, it cannot be described as moving or at rest. The opponents propose a novel definition based on contact and separation of substances. Razi shows that this definition leads to absurd implications and ultimately invalidates the original argument. He then recalls the proper interpretation of motion and rest as 'coming-to-be' (al-husul).

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the beginning where the objection is stated: the world is not in a place, so it cannot be in motion or at rest.
This is the claim that Razi will refute.
2
Examine the opponents' definition
Notice how they define motion and rest in terms of contact and separation of two substances.
They define rest as remaining in contact, motion as separation.
3
Follow Razi's first criticism
Razi argues that this definition is new and unhelpful, and implies that a single body cannot be moving or resting.
4
Follow Razi's second criticism
He points out that if a body moves, its parts remain in contact, so they would be at rest, leading to a contradiction.
5
Follow Razi's third criticism and conclusion
Since the world encompasses all bodies, with no external body, it cannot be moving or resting even if its parts are. The proof collapses. Razi reminds that motion and rest were properly interpreted as 'al-husul'.

Annihilation: Possibility, Conditions, and Refutations
The author critiques earlier responses on annihilation and defends its possibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the nuanced arguments about annihilation in Islamic theology, including the possibility of annihilation through opposition or removal of a condition, and the refutation of objections like circularity and mutual dependence.

Core Idea
Annihilation is possible through opposition or removal of conditions, and substance's need for accident is not circular.

In this passage, the author critiques a prior theological response that claimed annihilation is impossible. He defends the majority view that annihilation can occur through the occurrence of a contrary or the removal of a condition, and he refutes several objections including circularity and the nature of conditions. This clarifies the positions of different schools like the Karramiyya and Mu'tazila.

Commentary
This passage is a complex critique of earlier responses regarding annihilation. The author first criticizes the statement that 'the non-existence of the permanent is an effect of the contingent' as insufficient. He then discusses the relative strength of the causing-to-exist vs. preserving cause, arguing that the contingent at origination is stronger. He refutes the idea that the permanent prevents the contingent by pointing out that if it did, it would be stronger, but it is not. He then addresses the possibility of annihilation by occurrence of a contrary and by removal of condition. Regarding removal of condition, he argues that the condition need not be an accident; it could be a substance or even a non-existent. He criticizes the author's (al-Razi's) earlier argument that the condition is necessarily an accident because the Karramiyya and Mu'tazila do not accept that substances need accidents in that way. He then dismantles the circularity objection: need for an unspecified accident does not create circularity. He also criticizes the idea of mutual dependence without need as unintelligible. He ends by referencing the three philosophical objections about necessity of cause, impossibility of time's non-existence, and need for matter.

Commentary
This passage is a complex refutation of arguments against the possibility of bringing about non-existence (إعدام) by an agent. It begins by stating that the claim that an agent can bring about non-existence implies a renewal of non-existence after it was not, and that the distinction between two non-existences is by relation to existences. The author then critiques a response that such a view would mean nothing can cease to exist, calling it not an answer but an intensification of the problem. He notes that many theologians hold that non-existence is only possible through the advent of a contrary or the absence of a condition. The discussion then addresses the strength of the contingent versus the enduring, clarifying that the enduring according to early theologians needs no cause, but even if it needed a preserving cause, the originating agent is stronger. The author rejects the idea that the enduring prevents the contingent from existing. Further, he refutes the objection about the contingent being more numerous, using the principle of impossibility of two similar things. He then turns to the refutation of non-existence by absence of condition, arguing that conditions need not be accidents; they could be substances or even non-existents. He criticizes the author's own explanation about the accident being a condition for non-existence as unhelpful against opponents like the Karramiyya. The next section deals with the charge of circularity between substance and accident: the substance needs an accident in general, not a specific one, so circularity does not apply. The author's suggestion of mutual implication without need is deemed insufficient. Finally, the passage mentions moving to answer three issues from the philosophers: the agent being necessitating, the impossibility of time ceasing, and the need for matter before non-existence.

Razi elaborates on the majority view and its defenses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how most theologians explain non-existence through contrariety or loss of condition, and the debates around which is stronger.

Core Idea
Non-existence is a result of a contrary arrival or a condition's removal, not a direct act.

In this lesson, we explore the mainstream theological view that non-existence (adam) occurs only when a contrary appears or when a condition is absent. Razi analyzes this position, discussing whether the contingent or the enduring is stronger in causal terms.

Guided Path
1
Examine the doctrine of contrariety
Read the part where Razi states that non-existence by contrariety is 'obtained from both sides equally' and the debate on strength.
Contrariety (تضاد) is a key concept: two opposites cannot coexist.
2
Consider the condition argument
Read about the absence of condition: some say only an accident can be a condition. Razi rejects this, giving examples of substance as a condition.
Here 'شرط' means condition, not logical necessity.
3
Understand the critique of the author's example
Razi criticizes the view that the accident is the condition for non-existence because it doesn't convince opponents like the Karramites.
This shows the importance of addressing opponent-specific assumptions.

Commentary
This passage is a complex refutation of arguments against the possibility of bringing about non-existence (إعدام) by an agent. It begins by stating that the claim that an agent can bring about non-existence implies a renewal of non-existence after it was not, and that the distinction between two non-existences is by relation to existences. The author then critiques a response that such a view would mean nothing can cease to exist, calling it not an answer but an intensification of the problem. He notes that many theologians hold that non-existence is only possible through the advent of a contrary or the absence of a condition. The discussion then addresses the strength of the contingent versus the enduring, clarifying that the enduring according to early theologians needs no cause, but even if it needed a preserving cause, the originating agent is stronger. The author rejects the idea that the enduring prevents the contingent from existing. Further, he refutes the objection about the contingent being more numerous, using the principle of impossibility of two similar things. He then turns to the refutation of non-existence by absence of condition, arguing that conditions need not be accidents; they could be substances or even non-existents. He criticizes the author's own explanation about the accident being a condition for non-existence as unhelpful against opponents like the Karramiyya. The next section deals with the charge of circularity between substance and accident: the substance needs an accident in general, not a specific one, so circularity does not apply. The author's suggestion of mutual implication without need is deemed insufficient. Finally, the passage mentions moving to answer three issues from the philosophers: the agent being necessitating, the impossibility of time ceasing, and the need for matter before non-existence.

Circularity and mutual implication
Razi refutes circularity and critiques mutual implication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi refutes the charge of circularity between substance and accident, and why mutual implication without need is insufficient.

Core Idea
Circularity only occurs when each needs the other in the same respect; here the need is generic, not specific.

This lesson tackles the accusation that the relationship between substance and accident leads to circular causality. Razi shows that the substance needs an accident in general, not a specific one, so there is no vicious circle. He also critiques the notion of mutual implication without causal dependence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the charge of circularity
Read the accusation: the substance needs the accident, and the accident needs the substance, creating a loop. Note the context of the Karramiyya argument.
Circularity (دور) is a logical fallacy where A depends on B and B depends on A.
2
Razi's response: generic vs. specific need
Razi clarifies that the substance needs an accident in general, not a particular accident, while each accident needs a specific body. Thus no circularity.
This is a classic solution: the need is not reciprocal in the same way.
3
Critique of mutual implication without need
Razi argues that saying the two are mutually implicating without needing each other is not coherent, as that would just be general companionship, not necessary inseparability.
He provides the example of correlatives (المتضايفان) to illustrate true mutual dependence.

Commentary
This passage is a complex refutation of arguments against the possibility of bringing about non-existence (إعدام) by an agent. It begins by stating that the claim that an agent can bring about non-existence implies a renewal of non-existence after it was not, and that the distinction between two non-existences is by relation to existences. The author then critiques a response that such a view would mean nothing can cease to exist, calling it not an answer but an intensification of the problem. He notes that many theologians hold that non-existence is only possible through the advent of a contrary or the absence of a condition. The discussion then addresses the strength of the contingent versus the enduring, clarifying that the enduring according to early theologians needs no cause, but even if it needed a preserving cause, the originating agent is stronger. The author rejects the idea that the enduring prevents the contingent from existing. Further, he refutes the objection about the contingent being more numerous, using the principle of impossibility of two similar things. He then turns to the refutation of non-existence by absence of condition, arguing that conditions need not be accidents; they could be substances or even non-existents. He criticizes the author's own explanation about the accident being a condition for non-existence as unhelpful against opponents like the Karramiyya. The next section deals with the charge of circularity between substance and accident: the substance needs an accident in general, not a specific one, so circularity does not apply. The author's suggestion of mutual implication without need is deemed insufficient. Finally, the passage mentions moving to answer three issues from the philosophers: the agent being necessitating, the impossibility of time ceasing, and the need for matter before non-existence.

The Breaker and the Broken: Form vs. Quality
Analyzes proper attribution of breaking and being broken to form vs. quality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the quality (not the form) is properly considered the breaker, and the subject as the broken, in physical mixtures.

Core Idea
In causality, the quality breaks, not the form.

This passage tackles a nuanced problem in physics: how can one thing be both breaker and broken? The discussion revolves around attributing breaking to either the form (ṣūra) or the quality (kayfiyya). The author presents an opposing view, refutes it, and then offers the correct analysis using the example of mixing hot and cold water. By the end, you will see how this resolves an apparent paradox.

Causation is Intuitive
Al-Razi argues that causation is intuitively known.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Razi argues that causation is an intuitive concept, not derived from experience.

Core Idea
Causation is intuitive; we know it from simple actions like cutting.

Al-Razi begins a new investigation into cause and effect with a bold claim: the very idea that one thing can affect another is a primitive, intuitive notion. He supports this by pointing to our immediate grasp of actions like cutting and breaking. This passage establishes the foundation for his subsequent discussion on causation.

Commentary
This passage begins a new section on cause and effect. Al-Razi argues that the concept of one thing affecting another is intuitive (بديهي). He gives examples: we understand what it means to cut meat or break a pen; these actions are instances of a specific effect. Since we grasp these specific effects intuitively, the general concept of effect (التأثير) which is part of the definition of those specific effects is even more obviously intuitive. In other words, causality is a primitive notion.

He challenges the objector to define what they mean by priority, showing that on any coherent definition, mutual causation leads to contradiction.

Commentary
This passage is an extended refutation by Imam Razi of two objections: circularity (dawr) and infinite regress (tasalsul). The objector first challenges the claim that cause must be prior to effect, suggesting that if two things were mutual causes, each would be prior to itself. Razi responds by distinguishing types of priority: temporal priority is not required because cause and effect are simultaneous in time; essential priority means the cause is the efficient cause of the effect. If that is what priority means, then mutual causation would lead to each being the cause of the other, which is absurd. If the objector means something else, he must define it. The objector then argues that the collection (of causes) is dependent on each individual, so the series has no external cause. Razi replies that the terms 'collection' and 'whole' presuppose finitude, which is what is being questioned. Even if we grant the description, Razi argues that the objector's reasoning can be turned around: from the existence of sensed events, we can argue that either the series is infinite or it ends in an eternal cause. If it is infinite, that is admitted; if it ends in an eternal, then we must examine how the eternal acts on originated events. Razi then goes through the scenarios: whether the eternal's action depends on a condition or not, whether the condition is eternal or originated, whether it is simultaneous or prior, and so on. In each case, he shows that either circularity or infinite regress follows, concluding that infinite regress is unavoidable for those who deny the first cause. The aim is to force the opponent to accept a first eternal cause without needing to reject infinite regress absolutely.

Refuting the Objection on 'Total' and Proving Infinite Regress
Refutes the claim that a total requires parts and proves infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection against infinite regress based on the concept of a total is refuted, and how the causal analysis leads to a proof of infinite regress.

Core Idea
The opponent's own premises force an infinite regress of conditions.

This passage presents a sophisticated dialectical argument. The opponent claims that if there is an infinite chain of causes and effects, then the total of that chain would require each part, implying finitude. The responder first questions the applicability of the term 'total' in an infinite context, then shows that even if we accept the terms, the opponent's logic backfires and proves infinite regress. Let's follow the reasoning step by step.

Read the Arabic and translation
Read the passage once to get the overall flow. Notice the dialectical structure: the opponent speaks, then the responder replies.
2
Understand the initial objection and response
The opponent says the total depends on each unit. The responder first denies that 'total' applies before finitude is established, then concedes for argument and shows it leads to regress.
The key move: the responder argues that the opponent's premise actually supports infinite regress.
3
Follow the causal analysis
The responder then provides a detailed proof: if the cause of events is originated, regress; if eternal, then its influence either depends on a condition or not, and each option leads to regress or absurdity.
Trace through the branches: eternal with no condition → event eternal; eternal with condition → condition analysis leads to regress, circularity, or impossible.
4
Recognize the dialectical purpose
The responder is not asserting infinite regress as true; rather, he is showing that the opponent's own reasoning commits him to regress, which the opponent rejects. This refutes the opponent's objection.
This is a reductio ad absurdum argument.

Commentary
This passage is a proof that God is powerful over all possible things and that no possible exists except by His power. Imam Razi first states the Ash'ari position: God is powerful over all that is possible (maqduurat). He then gives the basis: the attribute that makes a thing a valid object of divine power is possibility (imkaan). Necessity and impossibility both preclude being an object of power. Since possibility is a common description among all possible things, they all equally can be objects of God's power. If God's power were restricted to some, there would need to be a particularizer (mukhassis) to specify the scope, which is arbitrary. Therefore, God is powerful over all possible things. Then Razi argues that if God is powerful over all possibles, no possible can exist except by His power. He considers the scenario of another agent alongside God. He examines three possibilities: (1) the effect occurs by both agents together — two independent agents acting on one effect is impossible (impossible because the effect would have two sufficient causes independently). (2) The effect occurs by neither — this leads to contradiction because if one agent prevents the other, then as long as the effect does not occur by one, its occurrence by the other is not prevented. If it were prevented by both, then it would have to occur by both to prevent each other, which is circular. (3) The effect occurs by one of them alone — this is a preference without a preferrer, also impossible. Hence, no other agent can be independent; all possible existence is by God's power.
