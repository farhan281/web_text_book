Life as a Positive Attribute: The Ash'ari Argument
Imam Razi presents the dispute on 'living' and argues it is a positive attribute with proof and counterargument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari argument that life is a positive attribute added to the essence, and why the negation-of-impossibility view is considered weaker.

Core Idea
Life is a real attribute, not merely a negation of impossibility.

Imam Razi presents a key dispute in Islamic theology: is God's attribute of 'life' a positive attribute (sifa thubutiyya) or merely a way of saying that He is not incapable? The Ash'ari school argues for the former, while many philosophers and some Mu'tazila adopt the latter. This passage lays out the proof and the stronger counterargument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the two positions
Read the Arabic passage once. Then read the translation. Note the two views: one defines 'living' as the essence entailing negation of impossibility; the other says it is a positive attribute.
The passage begins with agreement on the word 'living' but disagreement on its meaning.
2
Understand the Ash'ari proof
The Ash'ari argument: without a specific quality in the essence that makes it apt for knowledge and power, the occurrence of these attributes would be arbitrary. This supports that life is a distinct attribute.
The proof is presented as a conditional: if not for specificity, then no priority of occurrence.
3
Consider the objection
An objector says: why can't the particular essence itself be sufficient for knowledge and power? Why need an additional attribute?
This objection challenges the need for a distinct attribute of life.
4
Grasp the stronger answer
Razi's stronger answer: impossibility (imtina') is a non-existent thing, so its negation is also non-existent. Therefore, the view that life is merely negation of impossibility reduces to a negative concept, not a real attribute.
This answer reframes the debate around the nature of impossibility.
5
Reflect on the theological implication
Consider how this argument upholds the reality of divine attributes while avoiding anthropomorphism. Life is real yet not a limitation.

Commentary
This passage is a theological problem statement with proof and objection. Imam Razi begins by stating that all rational people agree God is living, but they differ on the meaning of 'living'. The majority of Ash'aris and other Mu'tazila hold that life is a positive attribute added to the essence. Then Razi presents the proof from the Ash'ari school: if not for a specific quality in the essence that makes it suited to be knowing and powerful, then the occurrence of these attributes would not be more likely than their non-occurrence. An objection is raised: why cannot the particular essence itself suffice for these attributes? Razi then gives the stronger answer: impossibility is a non-existent thing (as explained repeatedly), so the negation of impossibility is merely the negation of non-existence, not a positive attribute. This answer reframes the debate around the nature of impossibility.

Allah's will is understood in at least four different ways by Muslim theologians.

This passage from Razi's Muhassal presents a classic theological issue: Muslims agree that Allah is willing (murid), but they disagree on what 'will' actually means. Commentary
This passage is a classification of the different theological views on the meaning of Allah's will (irada). Razi presents the issue (mas'ala) as a point of agreement that Allah is willing, but disagreement on its interpretation. He lists four positions: (1) Abu al-Husayn al-Basri (Mu'tazili) says will is God's knowledge of the benefit in an act that motivates its creation. (2) Al-Bukhari (or al-Baqillani) says it means He is not overcome or compelled — a negative meaning. (3) Al-Ki (al-Ka'bi) distinguishes between God's own acts (will is knowledge of them) and others' acts (will is command). (4) The Ash'ari position (and that of Abu Ali al-Jubba'i and his son Abu Hashim) affirms will as an additional attribute (sifa za'ida) over and above knowledge. Razi thus sets the stage for the subsequent proof of the Ash'ari view.

The Real Dispute Over Divine Speech
Imam Razi defines the issue of divine speech and contrasts the schools.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the real point of disagreement between the Asha'ira and Mu'tazila regarding divine speech, and why the issue reduces to affirming or denying an eternal attribute called 'speech of the soul.'

Core Idea
The real dispute is not about God creating sounds, but about an eternal attribute called 'speech of the soul.'

This passage from Imam Razi's Muhassal introduces the theological debate over the meaning of calling Allah 'speaker.' While all Muslims agree on the term, they differ on its underlying reality. Imam Razi clarifies that both sides agree God can create sounds, but the Asha'ira affirm an additional eternal attribute of speech, which the Mu'tazila deny. Understanding this distinction is crucial for navigating the broader discourse on divine attributes.

3

This agreement is important – the real dispute lies elsewhere.

Commentary
This passage defines the issue of divine speech. It begins by stating the consensus that Allah is called 'speaker' (mutakallim), but disagreement on its meaning. The Mu'tazila hold that it means He creates sounds indicating meanings in bodies. The author (Imam Razi) states that he does not dispute this because he believes all events are by God's power. The remaining dispute is linguistic: does the Arabic term 'speaker' apply to this meaning? This is purely linguistic, not rational. The Asha'ira (our companions) agree that Allah does not speak with letters and sounds, but they affirm 'speech of the soul' (kalam nafsi), a divine attribute. The Mu'tazila deny this essence, and even if they admitted it, they would deny that this attribute subsists in the Essence, and further deny its oneness. So the Asha'ira agree with the Mu'tazila on God creating sounds but affirm an additional eternal attribute of speech, while the Mu'tazila deny its essence, existence, pre-eternity, and oneness. This is the necessary premise.

Commentary

Razi's Argument: Obedience Is Not Part of Iman
Razi presents his initial argument against including acts in iman.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Iman is affirmation, not actions — otherwise Quranic phrasing would be redundant.

Razi begins his refutation of the Mu'tazilah by presenting a simple logical argument from the Quran. If actions were part of iman, then phrases like 'those who believe and do good deeds' would be redundant. This lesson unpacks that argument and its scriptural basis.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic carefully, noting how Razi constructs his conditional argument: 'if obedience were part of iman, then...'.
The argument uses the logical form of reductio ad absurdum.
2
Examine the Quranic evidence
Identify the two verses Razi cites. The first shows iman distinct from wrongdoing; the second joins iman with doing good deeds, indicating they are separate.
The verses treat iman and deeds as separate categories.
3
Understand the conclusion
Razi concludes that restricting 'iman' to obedience would be either repetitive (if deeds are included) or contradictory (if deeds are excluded). Since the Quran avoids both, acts cannot be part of the definition.
This argument relies on the principle that divine speech is not redundant.

Refuting the First Two Mu'tazilah Proofs
Razi refutes the first two Mu'tazilah proofs using the concept of perfection of iman.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi refutes the Mu'tazilah proofs that equate acts to religion and that the highway robber is not a believer.

Core Idea
Both proofs are answered by distinguishing the essence of iman from its perfection.

The Mu'tazilah present two arguments: that obligatory acts are the religion (which is Islam and thus iman) and that the highway robber is disgraced while believers are not, so the robber lacks iman. Razi responds to both with a single principle: these verses refer to the complete and perfect form of iman, not its minimal reality.

Guided Path
1
Read the first proof
The opponent argues that performing wajibat is the 'religion' (din), and din is Islam, and Islam is iman, so acts are iman. Identify the chain of equations.
The proof relies on several Quranic verses equating terms.
2
Read the second proof
The opponent argues that a highway robber will be disgraced in the afterlife, but a believer is not disgraced, so the robber is not a believer. This implies that committing major sins nullifies iman.
This proof uses the concept of disgrace (khizy) to separate believers from sinners.
3
Examine the shared response
Razi answers both by saying the verses refer to the 'perfection' of iman, not its essence. This allows reconciling the verses without changing the definition of iman.
Ta'wil (interpretation) is used to harmonize apparently conflicting evidence.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Specification (takhsis) and reinterpretation preserve the core definition of iman as affirmation.

1
Read the third proof and response

Read the fourth proof and response

This response avoids redefining iman by using the original meaning of affirmation.

Razi uses specification and reinterpretation (ta'wil) to handle problematic proofs without altering the essential definition of iman as tasdiq.
These tools are common in Islamic jurisprudence and theology.
