Classification of Beliefs (Iʿtiqādāt)
Razi defines beliefs and categorizes them as firm/hesitant with subdivisions, then addresses an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi classifies beliefs into firm and hesitant categories, with subcategories based on conformity and causation, and how an objection clarifies the distinction between causal principles.

Core Idea
Beliefs are either firm or hesitant; firm beliefs are distinguished by conformity and cause.

Razi begins by defining belief as a mental state that every living being directly experiences. He then provides a comprehensive classification distinguishing firm (jāzima) from hesitant (mutaraddida) beliefs, with subcategories including ignorance, imitation, self-evident truths, necessary truths, theoretical truths, doubt, opinion, and illusion. The passage also includes an objection and response concerning the concept of 'nourisher' in plant and animal nutrition, which serves to clarify that different causal principles yield different effects.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the main classification.
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice that beliefs are first divided into firm and hesitant. Then firm beliefs are subdivided by conformity and causation.
2
Study the firm belief subcategories.
Understand how firm beliefs are broken down: if not conforming → ignorance; if conforming → either without cause (blind imitation) or with cause (self-evident, necessary, theoretical).
Self-evident truths are based on conception alone, necessary truths on sensation, theoretical truths on inference.
3
Analyze the hesitant belief subcategories.
For hesitant beliefs, if hesitation is balanced → doubt (shakk); if one side preponderates → preponderant is opinion (ẓann), weaker is illusion (wahm).
4
Examine the objection and response.
The objection concerns the difference between plant and animal nourishment. Razi's response argues that the concept of 'nourisher' differs based on the source (vegetative soul vs. animal soul) and that the concept is not identical to life itself.
This part clarifies that different causal principles do not necessarily entail different essences of the effect.

Commentary
This passage defines beliefs (iʿtiqādāt) as mental states that the living being directly experiences and distinguishes from other states. Razi then provides a detailed classification. Beliefs are either firm (jāzima) or hesitant (mutaraddida). Firm beliefs: if not conforming to reality, they are ignorance; if conforming, they are either without a cause (blind imitation, taqlīd) or with a cause. The cause can be: (1) the mere conception of the subject and predicate (self-evident truths, badīhiyyāt), (2) sensation (necessary truths, ḍarūriyyāt), or (3) inference (theoretical truths, naẓariyyāt). Hesitant beliefs: if doubt is balanced, it is doubt (shakk); if one side is preponderant, the stronger is opinion (ẓann) and the weaker is illusion (wahm). The passage then transitions to an objection and response concerning plant and animal nutrition, arguing that the concept of 'nourisher' differs based on the source (vegetative soul vs. animal soul) and that the concept is not identical to life, concluding that this is what was sought.

Editorial Critique of Razi's Definition of Beliefs
The editor critiques Razi's definition of beliefs and offers a corrected version.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the editor's critique of Razi's broad definition of beliefs and the refined definition that restricts beliefs to propositional judgments.

Core Idea
Beliefs require propositional content, not just inner sensations.

The early theologian Fakhr al-Din al-Razi defined beliefs broadly as things the soul finds within itself and distinguishes from others. However, an editor of his work points out that this definition is too inclusive, covering mere sensations like hunger and thirst. In this lesson, we examine the editor's critique and his suggested refinement, which ties beliefs to judgments of truth and falsity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation. Let the editor's argument unfold.
Focus on how each sentence introduces a distinct point of criticism.
2
Identify the problem of broadness
Notice that the editor points out Razi's definition includes all sensations (wijdāniyyāt) like hunger and pain. Recognize why this is problematic: not all internal experiences are beliefs.
Beliefs are typically about propositions, not mere feelings.
3
Understand the proposed correction
The editor states the correct definition: beliefs are things in which judgment of affirmation or negation can be made (i.e., propositional attitudes). This is the core refinement.
This aligns beliefs with truth-apt statements.
4
Examine the exclusion of opinions and illusions
The editor rejects including opinions (ẓunūn) and illusions (awhām) under beliefs, as theologians treat them as separate types. Understand the distinction.
Opinions are probabilistic, illusions are sensory deceptions.
5
Reflect on the critique about necessary truths
Finally, the editor questions equating sensation with necessary truths. Note that necessary truths are broader than sensory experience.
This prevents conflating epistemology with psychology.

Knowledge as a Relational Attribute
Imam Razi defines knowledge as relational and presents his view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why knowledge (ʿilm) is defined as a relational attribute (amr iḍāfī) and how this definition shapes the discourse on divine and human knowledge.

Core Idea
Knowledge is not a solitary quality; it is a relation between a knower and a known.

In this passage, Imam Razi addresses a foundational question in Islamic theology: what is the nature of knowledge? He argues that knowledge is inherently relational, meaning that for something to be 'knowing', there must be a 'known' object. This relational definition sets the stage for later discussions on divine attributes. Let's examine his reasoning and how he distinguishes his view from those of earlier theologians.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage

Pay attention to the key terms used.
2
Identify the author's claim
Notice that the author states the correct view is that knowledge is relational, and provides a brief justification.
The justification is that we cannot conceive of a knower without a known.
3

Both groups affirm more than just the relation.
4
Grasp the author's own position
The author simplifies: he affirms only the relation (taʿalluq) itself, while knowledge and ʿālamiyya are not accepted without proof.
This shows a minimalist approach to attributes.

Commentary
This passage presents the definition of knowledge (ʿilm) as a relational attribute (amr iḍāfī). Thus these two groups affirm three things: the relation, the entity that necessitates it, and the state. The author then states his own position: he affirms only the relation itself (al-taʿalluq), while the existence of 'worldliness' and knowledge as separate entities is not accepted unless proven by evidence.

We have aspects [؟]. First: That the point (nqta) is, by agreement, an existent entity, and because the line is contiguous with it by another line, and that by which something touches another is not pure non-existence. And it is indivisible by agreement, and because it is the extremity of a line, for if it were divisible, the extremity of the line would be one of its halves, so the extremity would not be an extremity. And because the locus of contact of a true sphere touching a true plane surface is indivisible, otherwise the part of it in contact with the plane would be planar, so the sphere would be polygonal. Then this point, if it is extended (mutahayyiz), it confirms the indivisible substance (jawhar fard); if it is an accident (arad), then in its substratum (mahall), if its substratum is divisible, then its division follows from the division of its substratum; if the substratum is not divisible, then that is the desired conclusion. (2) I say: his statement that the point is by agreement an existent entity and then his statement that it is indivisible by agreement contradicts his statement that the extremity of a thing is its non-existence, so it would not be existent. And the point, according to those who affirm it, is the extremity of a line, so this is an 'agreement' without satisfaction of the opponents. If he had said instead 'by acknowledgment of those who affirm it', it would be more correct. His statement: 'if it is an accident in its substratum, if the substratum is divisible, then its division follows from the division of its substratum' is also not granted by his opponents, for they divide accidents into those that permeate their substrata and those that do not, and they count the point among those that do not permeate, and they say that for non-permeating accidents, their division is not necessitated by the division of their substrata. And the contact of a true sphere with a plane artificial surface is, according to them, by a point which is the extremity of a diameter passing through the center of the sphere at the point of contact. Otherwise, if the sphere touched another plane surface at the other extremity of that diameter, and a great circle was drawn on the sphere through the diameter of contact, that circle would be divided by the two contacts into four arcs, two contiguous with the surface and two not contiguous, and from that would follow the correspondence of an arc with a straight line, which is impossible. And the fact that the contact is by a point and the division of the locus of the point does not necessitate the point being divisible, as has been mentioned.

Commentary
This passage presents the first argument for the indivisible atom using the concept of the point. It argues: 1) The point is existent (by agreement) and indivisible because it is the extremity of a line and the contact point of a sphere with a plane. 2) If the point is a substance (mutahayyiz), it is the indivisible atom; if it is an accident, then either its substratum is divisible (so the point divides with it) or not divisible (so the point is indivisible, which is the desired conclusion). Editor’s Critique: The Point and Non-Permeating Accidents
Editor's critique of the point argument

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the editor's critique of the point argument, focusing on the distinction between permeating and non-permeating accidents.

Core Idea
The point argument fails because opponents treat the point as a non-permeating accident, which does not divide with its substratum.

In this marginal note, the editor (likely the author of the commentary) challenges the validity of the first argument for the indivisible substance. He points out two flaws: the false claim of agreement on the point's existence, and the assumption that all accidents divide with their substrata. He introduces the concept of non-permeating accidents (ghayr al-sariya) and provides a geometrical counterexample.

The point is considered a non-permeating accident by opponents.

Proof by Traversal Against Infinite Parts
Proof against infinite parts via traversal and objection

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Infinite parts make motion impossible because you can never reach the end.

This lesson presents a classic kalam proof: if a body had infinitely many actual parts, then to get from one end to the other, you would have to traverse an infinite number of halves, which is impossible. The objector responds that the parts are only potential, not actual. This sets the stage for the refutation of the claim that the divisible is one.

The Third Argument for the Sameness of Bodies and Its Refutation
Razi presents the third argument for the sameness of bodies and its refutation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why equality in a common attribute (like occupying space) does not prove equality in essence, and how the distinction between essence and attribute is crucial in kalam.

Core Idea
Equality in a shared property does not imply equality in essence.

In this passage, Razi presents a third argument that attempts to prove all bodies are essentially the same because they all occupy space. He then refutes it by distinguishing between an essence and its attributes. Understanding this distinction is foundational to the kalam approach to analyzing objects and their properties.

2
Read the refutation
Read the objection: occupying space is not the essence but an attribute. Equality in attributes does not prove equality in essences.
This refutation relies on a prior principle about concomitants (lawāzim) and essences (malzumāt).
3
Reflect on the principle
Consider why this principle is important: it prevents false inferences from shared properties to shared identities.
This is a recurring theme in kalam: attributes do not define essences.

Two bodies cannot occupy one space; it's a rational necessity.

Proof That Bodies Are Visible: Length as Substance
Razi argues that length is a substance, proving bodies are visible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that length cannot be an accident because it would make the indivisible atom divisible, thereby establishing that bodies are inherently visible.

Core Idea
Length is a substance, not an accident — so visibility belongs to the body itself.

In this passage, Razi presents a key argument for the visibility of bodies that relies on the atomic structure of matter. He contends that if length were an accident, it would force the indivisible atom to become divisible. Therefore length must be a substance, and since we see length, we see the substance itself. Let's trace his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the main claim
Start by reading the Arabic and translation. The claim is that bodies are visible, contrary to philosophers. Notice the proof begins with 'لنا أنا زى الطويل'.
The phrase 'أنا زى الطويل' is unclear; it may mean 'we see the long'.
2
Understand the atomic premise
Razi assumes 'body is composed of parts that are indivisible' (الأجزاء التي لا تجزأ). This is the atomic theory of the Mutakallimun. Grasp this premise before continuing.
3
Follow the conditional argument
If length were an accident, it would inhere in a single atom. That atom would then be longer than others, making it divisible — a contradiction. Therefore length is not an accident.
The conclusion 'وهو ال [؟]' may be 'and that is impossible'.
4
Conclude that length is a substance
Since length is not an accident, it must be a substance. And since we see length (long things), we see the substance itself, so bodies are visible intrinsically.
This step completes the proof.

The Debate on the Finitude of Bodies
Razi presents opponent's argument for infinite bodies and two responses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the dialectical argument about whether bodies are finite or infinite, including the opponent's reasoning, the mutakallimun's answer, and the philosophers' stronger solution.

Core Idea
The debate on whether bodies are finite hinges on what lies outside the world.

This passage from Razi's Muhassal presents a classic kalam debate. The opponent argues that if bodies are finite, the space outside must be either distinguished or not, both leading to absurdity. Razi then presents two responses: the mutakallimun's weak answer of 'estimative spaces' and the philosophers' dismissal based on the unreliability of the estimative faculty. Let us follow the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the three main sections: the opponent's argument, the mutakallimun's answer, and the philosophers' answer.
The passage uses technical terms like musamata and ahiyaz.
2
Understand the opponent's argument
The opponent claims that if bodies are finite, what is outside all bodies must either have distinct sides or not. If distinct, it must be a body or magnitude, contradicting the assumption. If not distinct, it contradicts clear reason (north vs south). So bodies must be infinite.
3
Examine the mutakallimun's answer
The mutakallimun posit infinite 'estimative' spaces (ahiyaz taqdiriya) that exist only in the mind. Razi critiques this: if they don't correspond to reality, they are false assumptions; if they do, real spaces exist, reviving the problem.
This shows the weakness of relying on mental constructs without realist grounding.
4
Learn the philosophers' solution
The philosophers (hukama') deny any real distinction outside the world; the apparent distinction is a judgment of the estimative faculty (wahm), not reason, and is thus unacceptable in philosophical proof.
This is presented as the stronger answer because it avoids affirming infinite real spaces.
5
Summarize the debate
Review the three positions: opponent's argument for infinity, mutakallimun's weak infinite estimative spaces, philosophers' denial of distinction. Understand why Razi finds the philosophers' answer superior.
This debate illustrates key differences between mutakallimun and philosophers on ontology and epistemology.

The Logical Contradiction in Mixture Theory
Mutakallimun object to the philosophers' theory of mixture of elements.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the mutakallimun's logical objection to the philosophers' theory of mixture and how they refute a counter-argument.

Core Idea
A quality cannot be both broken and unbroken at the same time.

This passage presents a classic kalam critique of the philosopher's theory of mixture. The mutakallimun argue that if mixing involves each element's quality breaking the other, then simultaneous breaking results in a logical contradiction. They also consider and refute a possible counter-argument involving the form and quality distinction. This lesson will unpack their reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the literal translation. Notice the dialectical structure: claim, objection, counter-objection, rebuttal.
The passage is dense; focus on the logical steps.
2
Identify the philosophers' claim
The philosophers claim that when elements mix, each element's overpowering quality (sūra) breaks the other, resulting in an intermediate quality called temperament (mizāj).

Analyze the mutakallimun's objection
The mutakallimun object using the principle that the cause must coexist with the effect. They pose two scenarios: if both breakings occur simultaneously, each quality is both broken and not broken (contradiction); if they occur separately, the effect exists without its cause (impossible).
The principle of causality is key.

A potential objector says the breaker is the constitutive form, which remains unbroken, while the quality is broken. The mutakallimun respond that the form is broken via the supervening quality, so the problem recurs.

Arguments Against T'ayyun as a Positive Attribute
Razi enumerates three arguments against t'ayyun being a positive attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three arguments theologians use to deny that determination (t'ayyun) is a positive attribute and the logical problems (infinite regress, circularity, multiplication) they raise.

Core Idea
If t'ayyun were positive, it would create infinite regress, circles, or multiplication—so it cannot be real.

In this passage, Imam al-Razi presents a key theological debate about whether the identity (t'ayyun) of a thing is a real positive attribute or merely a conceptual distinction. Theologians argue that if t'ayyun were a real attribute, it would lead to absurd consequences. They give three arguments, each showing a different logical problem: infinite regress, circularity, and the impossibility of one attribute subsisting in two loci. Let's examine these arguments closely.

Guided Path
1
Read the question and the first argument
Start by reading the opening question and the first argument: if t'ayyun were positive, each instance would require a further determination, leading to infinite regress.
Notice the phrase 'إلى غير النهاية' indicating infinite regress.
2
Identify the second argument about circularity
The second argument claims that combining t'ayyun with the essence after the essence exists is impossible, but the essence only exists after t'ayyun, creating a circle or double determination.
The term 'دور' means circular reasoning.
3
Understand the third argument about unity of existence
The third argument states that if t'ayyun is other than the essence, the existence of one cannot be the existence of the other, so a single thing would become two, leading to infinite multiplication.
The conclusion 'بل أمور غير متناهية' stresses the absurdity.

Refuting the Objection: Can a Thing Change from Impossible to Possible?

Core Idea
A thing's essence does not change; possibility is about what the intellect does not deem impossible, not about actual states.

This passage presents a sophisticated objection and answer from Imam Razi regarding the definition of the 'possible' (ممكن). An opponent argues that since originated things pass from non-existence to existence, they must be susceptible to both states, and possibly even change from being essentially impossible to essentially necessary. Imam Razi responds by clarifying the true meaning of 'susceptibility' and showing that the objection rests on a misunderstanding of possibility. This lesson unpacks the dialectical steps and reinforces the correct kalam definition of the possible.
