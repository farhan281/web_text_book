It begins with his birth date: 25 Ramadan 544 AH (with a variant of 543), corresponding to 1149 CE. The passage emphasizes his titles: Imam, Shaykh al-Islam, and his expertise in usul (fundamentals), kalam, disputation, and tafsir. The next section, 'Scholars' opinions about him,' recounts an anecdote from Sharaf al-Din ibn 'Unayn: during a lesson in Khwarazm, a pigeon chased by a predator fell near al-Razi. Out of compassion, he picked it up, prompting Ibn 'Unayn to compose poetry praising al-Razi's generosity and protection. The poetry uses imagery of feeding during famine and snow, and likens al-Razi's assembly to a sanctuary.

Further praise from scholars includes a statement that Allah granted al-Razi a special insight into hidden truths. Imam al-Sakkaki al-Khwarizmi composed a line likening his service to al-Razi to that of Avicenna's servant. The passage then describes al-Razi's preaching in Arabic and Persian, his emotional ecstasy during sermons, and his diverse audience in Herat, including followers of various sects. He answered all questions with skill and attracted nobles and kings. He maintained dignity, wealth, and a good appearance; about three hundred students accompanied him across multiple disciplines. His influence led many Karramiyya to adopt Sunni beliefs. He was honored with the title 'Shaykh al-Islam' in Herat.

Finally, his education began under his father, who was the preacher of Rayy. The text ends abruptly with a mention of his book, likely al-Muhassal or another work.

Commentary
This passage classifies the different views on the nature of the human self (al-huwiyya). The author lists six opinions with their proponents: (1) the indivisible part in the heart—attributed to Ibn al-Rawandi; (2) the sensible structure—held by some theologians; (3) a more precise view of the verifiers that the self is composed of parts to which life pertains; (4) subtle bodies permeating the body—the school of al-Nazzam of the Mu'tazila; (5) the temperament—the school of Galen and some physicians; (6) the rational soul—attributed to a group of theologians and the majority of philosophers. The passage ends by noting that the answer to it (presumably to the sixth or to all) has been mentioned earlier. This enumeration serves to demonstrate the diversity of opinions that were referenced in the initial objection from the Samaniyya.

Key Takeaway
Imam Razi lists six distinct definitions of the human self, each associated with different theological or philosophical schools, demonstrating the diversity of opinions on this metaphysical question.
