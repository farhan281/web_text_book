The Bibliographical Legacy of Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi
Editor enumerates works by al-Razi with notes on completion and attribution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the breadth of Imam al-Razi's scholarly output and the scholarly debates surrounding some of his attributed works.

Core Idea
Imam al-Razi's works span numerous fields, but not all attributions are undisputed.

This passage presents a bibliography of Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's works from an edited edition. It lists his contributions across fiqh, literature, medicine, theology, and more, while also noting that some works attributed to him are contested by scholars. As we read, consider how a scholar's legacy can include both celebrated and disputed writings.

Guided Path
1
Read the list of works
Read the numbered list from 22 to 34. Notice the diversity of fields: fiqh, poetry commentary, i'jaz, logic, medicine, geometry, physiognomy, Shafi'i merits, usul, theology, and the major work al-Muhassal.
The editor includes some notes about incomplete works and planned publications.
2
Identify the fields covered
Identify the various disciplines represented in the list. This shows al-Razi's encyclopedic range.
3
Note editorial comments
Observe which works are marked as incomplete (e.g., number 27 and 28) or awaiting publication (number 25). These comments give insight into the state of the manuscripts.
4
Examine the disputed works
Read the paragraph mentioning works whose attribution is not agreed upon, especially 'Kitab al-Sirr al-Maktum' on astral magic, which the Shafi'is denied.
This shows that scholarly consensus on authorship is not always unanimous.
5
Reflect on Ibn Khallikan's praise
The passage ends with Ibn Khallikan's praise of al-Razi's innovative arrangement. Consider how this reputation enhances al-Razi's scholarly standing.
Ibn Khallikan is a famous biographer; his endorsement is significant.

Commentary
This passage is a bibliography listing twenty-two numbered works by Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, with additional editorial notes. The editor enumerates works from number 22 to 34, covering fields such as fiqh, poetry commentary, i'jaz, logic, medicine, geometry, physiognomy, Shafi'i merits, usul, theology (i'tiqadat), and the major work al-Muhassal. The editor then notes that some works are disputed in attribution, specifically mentioning 'Kitab al-Sirr al-Maktum' on astral magic which the Shafi'is rejected. Finally, the editor praises al-Razi's prolific and methodical authorship, citing Ibn Khallikan's claim that al-Razi pioneered a new arrangement in his books.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you seek knowledge, how carefully do you verify the authenticity of a source or attribution, especially when it comes to matters of ‘aqeedah? This lesson mentions a book on astral magic falsely attributed to al-Razi—could you be unknowingly relying on inauthentic material in your own learning, and what steps will you take to protect yourself from such deception?
?
Al-Razi authored in every field, from fiqh to geometry to medicine. Reflect on your own pursuit of knowledge: is it motivated by sincere desire for Allah’s pleasure, or by the love of being seen as knowledgeable? The editor praised his perfect arrangement—do you organize your learning with the same meticulousness, or do you leave it scattered and superficial?
?
This passage highlights that even great scholars like al-Razi have works that are disputed and rejected. Knowing that scholars before us debated fiercely over attributions, how do you approach disagreements among contemporary scholars about who said what? Are you quick to accept a quote without checking its source, and how can you cultivate a habit of intellectual rigor in this era of easy misinformation?
