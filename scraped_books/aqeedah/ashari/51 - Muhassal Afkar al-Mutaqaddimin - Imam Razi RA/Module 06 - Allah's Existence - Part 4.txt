Three Objections Against the Return of the Non-Existent
al-Razi enumerates three objections against the return of the non-existent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three main objections raised by opponents against the possibility of returning the non-existent: lack of identity, indistinguishability, and time paradox.

Core Idea
Opponents argue that returning the non-existent leads to identity, distinction, and temporal paradoxes.

After presenting and defending the Ash'ari proof, al-Razi lists three objections from his opponents. These objections challenge the coherence of the doctrine by raising problems of identity, distinguishability, and time. Understanding these objections is crucial for further debate.

Guided Path
1
Read the three objections
Read each objection carefully in Arabic and English. Note the numbered structure.
2
Analyze each objection
First: no identity remains. Second: indistinguishable from similar. Third: return would entail the return of the first time.
3
Consider potential responses
Think about how an Ash'ari might respond to each. These objections are not refuted here, but they set the stage.
This is a common pedagogical technique: present objections before refuting them.

Let us now speak about the physical resurrection. Issue: The return of the non-existent is permissible according to our companions, contrary to the philosophers, the Karamiyya, and Abu al-Husayn al-Basri from the Mu'tazila. For us: after non-existence, if it is impossible in its essence or due to something of its concomitants, then the impossibility of its like is necessary. But if the matter is not necessary, then when that accident ceases, that impossibility ceases. It cannot be said: The judgment that it is impossible in itself or due to another is not valid because judgment on a thing requires the distinctness of the judged from other things, and distinctness requires existence, which contradicts non-existence. For we say: The judgment that it is not valid to judge it is itself a judgment upon it, so it is contradictory. And the opponent argued with several matters. First: that after non-existence, the thing is in pure non-existence and its identity does not remain at all, so judgment on the validity of its return is not valid because the judged must be distinct from others, and the distinct is existent. Second: that if it occurs, it would not be distinguishable from its like, and what leads to indistinguishability from its like is invalid. Third: that if it were returned, its first time would have to be returned with it, which would mean it is both initial and returned, and that is a contradiction.

Commentary
This passage is a proof and defense of the Ash'ari position that returning the non-existent is possible. al-Razi first states the position and lists opponents. He gives a proof: if something becomes impossible after non-existence due to its essence, then its like would also be impossible; but the impossibility is due to an accident (non-existence), so removing that accident removes the impossibility. He then anticipates an objection: one cannot judge a non-existent because judgment requires distinctness, which requires existence. He refutes this by noting that the objection itself is a judgment on the non-existent, thus self-contradictory. Next, he presents three objections from opponents: (1) the non-existent lacks identity for judgment; (2) if returned, it would be indistinguishable from a similar existent; (3) returning would entail the return of its first time, leading to contradiction. These are not refuted here but set up for further discussion.

Objections to Bodily Resurrection and Their Answers
Al-Razi enumerates four philosophical objections to bodily resurrection and replies to each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four rational objections to bodily resurrection raised by philosophers and how Imam al-Razi answers them, including the reliance on tawatur and the need to affirm i'ada al-ma'dum.

Core Idea
Bodily resurrection is established by tawatur, which overrides rational objections.

Imam al-Razi presents four objections that philosophers raise against the possibility of bodily resurrection. These objections challenge how scattered parts can be reassembled and question the purpose of resurrection. In response, al-Razi invokes the definitive proof of tawatur from the Prophet, and provides specific refutations.

Guided Path
1
Read the objections carefully
Examine the first objection about a part belonging to two bodies and the fourth objection about the purpose of resurrection. Note how the philosopher argues that worldly pleasures are not true pleasures.
The first objection is implied from context; the fourth is explicitly stated.
2
Analyze the general answer
The main answer is that bodily resurrection is proven by tawatur from the Prophet, which cannot be interpreted away. This is the primary rebuttal.
Tawatur is a strong transmission that yields certainty.
3
Understand the specific answers and note
Review the answers to each objection: vacuum is possible for the second, returning the original part for the third, and prior proof of sensory pleasure for the fourth. Then read the note about i'ada al-ma'dum, which explains the need for returning the non-existent to preserve identity.
The note connects to the broader theological issue of whether non-existent things can be restored.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-answer unit concerning bodily resurrection. The objector raises a difficulty: if a part is shared between two bodies, it can only be returned to one of them, so the other cannot be resurrected with all original parts. The Eternity of the World and Bodily Resurrection
Al-Razi addresses the compatibility of eternal world with bodily resurrection and contrasts theological positions.

Learning Objective
This lesson clarifies that the eternity of the world does not contradict bodily resurrection, and discusses the location of Paradise and Hell and the purpose of resurrection according to different schools.

Core Idea
The world's eternity does not preclude bodily resurrection; God's actions are not bound by obligation.

Some might think that if the world is eternal, bodily resurrection is impossible. Al-Razi's footnote addresses this by defining the world as everything other than God, and argues that bodily resurrection only concerns the specific event of gathering at the end. He also touches on the Mu'tazili view that resurrection is obligatory upon God.

Guided Path
1
Understand the claim about eternity
Read the statement that the eternity of the world does not contradict bodily resurrection. Notice the definition: the world is everything other than God.
The non-existence of other than God is not a condition for resurrection.
2
Note the discussion on location
Al-Razi states that we do not know the location of Paradise and Hell, but it is possible to infer Paradise from the Quranic verse about the Lote Tree.
This shows humility about unknown matters.
3
Contrast theological positions
Compare Ash'ari and Mu'tazili views: for Ash'aris, God's actions are not subject to obligation; for Mu'tazila, resurrection is obligatory for recompense. Both reject the idea that pleasure/pain is the purpose.
This highlights a key difference between the schools.

This lesson explains how Ash'aris and Mu'tazila differ in defining personal identity for resurrection, with Ash'aris focusing on permanent parts and Mu'tazila including accidents.

For Ash'aris, identity is the enduring physical parts; accidents perish and are irrelevant.

A key aspect of bodily resurrection is what constitutes the identity of a person. Al-Razi's second footnote clarifies that, according to the Ash'aris, identity lies in the physical parts that do not perish, while accidents are momentary. The Mu'tazila, however, consider accidents as part of identity, leading to different implications for resurrection.

Contrast with Mu'tazili view

Commentary
This passage is an objection-answer unit concerning bodily resurrection. The objector raises a difficulty: if a part is shared between two bodies, it can only be returned to one of them, so the other cannot be resurrected with all original parts. A fourth objection is then presented: the purpose of resurrection can only be giving pleasure, but worldly pleasures are not true pleasures; only spiritual pleasures are real, so returning the soul to the body is futile. The answer is that bodily resurrection is established by tawatur (continuous transmission) from the Prophet, which cannot be interpreted away. Specific answers follow: the first objection has been answered earlier; the second objection (presumably about vacuum) is answered by affirming that vacuum is possible; the third objection is answered by saying that the original part of one body is superfluous for the other, so returning it to the first is preferable; the fourth objection is answered by referring to earlier proof of sensory pleasure. Then a note (tanbih) states that resurrection as gathering of parts requires affirming the return of the non-existent (i'ada al-ma'dum), because personal identity involves accidents which perish. The footnote expands: the eternity of the world does not contradict bodily resurrection; the location of Paradise and Hell is unknown; and the purpose of resurrection according to Ahl al-Sunna is not subject to obligation, unlike the Mu'tazila. Another footnote clarifies that personal identity according to Ash'aris is only the permanent parts, not accidents, whereas Mu'tazila consider accidents.

Refuting the Proofs for Annihilation of Parts
Imam al-Razi refutes the claim that God annihilates parts and recreates them, responding to three Quranic proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Razi refutes the claim that God annihilates parts and recreates them, and the rational arguments against using certain Quranic verses as definitive proof.

Core Idea
Quranic verses about God's firstness, perishing, and re-creation do not prove the annihilation of parts.

In this passage, Imam al-Razi tackles a theological claim that the Quran proves that God annihilates physical parts and then recreates them. He presents three verses commonly cited and gives logical responses, showing that the verses do not compel that interpretation. This lesson will walk through each proof and its refutation.

Second: His statement 'Everything is perishing except His Face', and perishing is annihilation. If we grant that it is the non-existent, then the verse cannot be applied literally, because describing created things as perishing would require them to be non-existent at present, and that is unanimously false, so it must be interpreted. They interpreted it as meaning that they are perishing in the future, and we interpreted it as meaning that they are capable of perishing, so your interpretation is not more appropriate than ours. Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the claim that it is proven by decisive proof that God annihilates parts and then recreates them. It begins by stating the issue (mas'ala). Then it presents three verses cited by those who affirm annihilation: (1) 'He is the First and the Last' — arguing that just as He was First before creation, He must be Last after creation; (2) 'Everything is perishing except His Face' — taking 'perishing' as annihilation; (3) 'As We began the first creation, We will repeat it' — inferring that re-creation must also be from non-existence like the first creation. The answers: For the first, 'First and Last' may refer to merit, not time. For the second, 'perishing' means becoming useless, not non-existent; even if it meant non-existent, the verse cannot be literal because it would require everything to be currently non-existent, which is false; both sides interpret it, so the opponent's interpretation is not superior. For the third, analogy does not require complete similarity. The footnote adds that the active participle can refer to future, so no special interpretation is needed for 'perishing'; and for 'First and Last' if temporal, it fails because God will still exist after annihilation and then re-creation, so He would not be Last absolutely; thus interpretation is needed, e.g., First as origin, Last as end.
