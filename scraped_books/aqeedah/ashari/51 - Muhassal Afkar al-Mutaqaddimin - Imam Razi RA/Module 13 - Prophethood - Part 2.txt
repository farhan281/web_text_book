Rational acts are not worship; only commanded acts are true worship.

Core Idea
Rational acts are just habits; only commanded acts are pure worship.

This eighth argument from Imam Razi distinguishes between actions driven by human reason and those commanded by God. It explains that reason-based actions are no different from habitual routines, which lack the essence of worship. True worship arises only when we obey a divine command, even if we do not grasp its wisdom. The strange rituals of Hajj exemplify this principle, showing that prophethood is necessary to convey such commands.

The Brahmins' Objection Against Prophethood and Its Refutation
Al-Razi presents the Brahmins' objection and the refutation by distinguishing categories within the two premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Brahmins' objection that prophethood is either unnecessary or unacceptable, and how the response demonstrates that prophets are needed for matters reason cannot grasp independently and for neutral rational matters.

Core Idea
Prophets are needed where reason falls short, not where it suffices.

The Brahmins argued that prophethood is futile because either prophets bring what reason already knows or what it rejects. Imam al-Razi refutes this by dividing both categories: reason may not be independent in some agreeing matters, and opposing matters include neutral ones where guidance is needed. This establishes the necessity of prophethood for human guidance beyond reason alone.

Refutation of Permitting Major Sins for Prophets and Discussion on Minor Sins
Four arguments against major sins and discussion of minor sins with editor's notes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four arguments against allowing major sins for prophets and the subsequent discussion on minor sins, including the editor's reflections.

Core Idea
Major sins for prophets contradict their rank, testimony, rebuke, and the obligation to follow them.

After establishing that prophets cannot disbelieve, Al-Razi refutes those who permit major sins for prophets. He presents four compelling arguments: from honor, witness acceptance, rebuke, and the impossibility of following a sinful act. He then notes disagreements over minor sins and adds his own critical comments on the reasoning.

The first editor's note critiques the analogy of greater abomination, and the second states the fourth argument applies to minor sins too. Consider how these modify the argument.

Commentary
This passage discusses prophetic infallibility from disbelief and major sins. It begins by stating the consensus (except the Fudayliyyah Kharijites) that prophets are infallible from disbelief. The Fudayliyyah considered any sin as disbelief, thus allowing prophets to disbelieve. Al-Razi refutes them: if prophets could disbelieve, following them would be obligatory (since God says 'follow them'), leading to absurdity. Next, some allow manifesting disbelief under taqiyya, arguing that manifesting Islam leads to self-destruction. Al-Razi rejects this because it would conceal religion entirely, and if allowed, prophets like Abraham and Moses—who faced great fear—would have concealed their calls, which they did not. Next, some allow major sins but not disbelief. Al-Razi gives four arguments why this is invalid: 1) Prophets' great honor makes sin from them more abominable, so they would be lower than sinful laymen, impossible by consensus. He cites the verse doubling punishment for the Prophet's wives. 2) A prophet who commits a major sin would be rejected as a witness, but prophets are accepted as witnesses, otherwise they'd be lower than upright believers. 3) If they commit a major sin, rebuke is obligatory, but rebuking a prophet is forbidden (because harming God and His messenger is cursed). 4) If they sin, we must follow them in it, but since sin is forbidden, this combines obligation and prohibition. Finally, he notes disagreement over minor sins: most hold that minor sins that are belittled are not permissible for prophets. Al-Razi adds two comments: one on the logic of greater abomination, and another that the fourth argument applies to minor sins too.

The Fudaliyya View on Prophetic Infallibility and Its Refutation
Imam Razi reports and refutes the Fudaliyya Khawarij view on prophetic infallibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Fudaliyya Khawarij's extreme view on prophetic infallibility and Imam Razi's logical refutation based on the Quranic command to follow the prophets.

Core Idea
If prophets could commit kufr, following them would be following kufr — impossible.

This passage from Imam Razi's Muhassal reports the consensus on prophetic infallibility, then highlights the dissenting view of the Fudaliyya Khawarij. They equated all sin with kufr and allowed prophets to sin, thus permitting kufr on them. The refutation is a crisp logical argument based on the Quranic command to follow the prophets.

Guided Path
1
Understanding the consensus and the exception
Begin by noting the consensus of the Muslim community that prophets are infallible from kufr. The Fudaliyya are the only exception. Ask: Why would a group break this consensus?
The Fudaliyya are a subsect of the Khawarij.
2
Grasping the Fudaliyya's reasoning
The Fudaliyya believed every sin is kufr. Since they allowed prophets to commit sins, they logically had to allow prophets to commit kufr. Trace this chain: sin = kufr → prophets sin → prophets can commit kufr.
Their starting premise is that sin and kufr are identical.
3
Following the refutation
Imam Razi argues that if kufr were permissible for prophets, then the Quranic command 'So follow him' would entail following them in kufr, which is absurd. Therefore the premise that prophets can commit kufr is false.
This is a reductio ad absurdum argument.

Refuting Outward Kufr Under Taqiyya
Imam Razi refutes the claim that prophets may outwardly show kufr under duress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Razi's rational refutation of the claim that prophets may outwardly display disbelief under taqiyya, and why such a concession would undermine the very purpose of prophethood.

Core Idea
Taqiyya cannot justify prophets hiding their faith—it would destroy the message itself.

In this passage, Imam Razi addresses a subtle but dangerous claim: that while prophets never actually commit kufr, they may outwardly express it under duress (taqiyya) to save their lives. Proponents argue that openly professing Islam when it leads to destruction is forbidden by the principle of not throwing oneself into perdition. Razi dismantles this reasoning by showing its logical consequences—if valid, it would have prevented any prophetic mission from ever starting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and its basis
Read the passage and note the view being refuted: that prophets may only outwardly show kufr, not commit it, under taqiyya. The rationale is the principle of not causing self-destruction.
This is a nuanced position—it distinguishes between inner belief and outer expression.
2
Understand Razi's first refutation
Razi argues that if such taqiyya were permissible, the religion would become completely hidden. Reflect on why this is a powerful argument against concessions in public proclamation.
Consider the necessity of public witness in Islam.
3
Examine the second refutation (historical hypothetical)
If taqiyya were allowed, it would have been most needed at the very beginning of prophethood when opposition was strongest. That would have prevented any prophet from ever starting their mission.
This is a reductio ad absurdum argument.
4
Consider the third refutation (historical examples)
Razi cites Ibrahim and Musa who faced extreme fear but never ceased their call. This shows that even in dire circumstances, prophets continued their mission.
Their example disproves the necessity of taqiyya for prophets.
5
Synthesize the lesson
Combine the three arguments to understand why the view is invalid. The core principle: prophetic mission requires public proclamation; taqiyya would negate it.

Four Arguments Against Prophets Committing Major Sins
Imam Razi refutes the view that prophets may commit major sins with four logical proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four rational arguments used by Imam Razi to refute the claim that prophets may commit major sins.

Core Idea
Prophets must be sinless; four logical proofs show major sins contradict their status.

Imam Razi presents four compelling logical arguments to demonstrate that prophets cannot commit major sins. These arguments draw on the unique honor of prophets, their role as witnesses, the prohibition of harming them, and the obligation to follow them. Each proof independently shows that allowing major sins on prophets leads to an impossibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the four arguments
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the translation. Notice that the passage lists four reasons, each beginning with a numbered term (الأول, الثاني, etc.).
Each argument is a conditional proof (if...then...).
2
Understand the first argument: rank
The first argument states that if a prophet committed a major sin, his rank would be lower than sinful members of his community. This is impossible because a sin is more heinous from a person of high station, as shown by Quranic examples and consensus.
The Quranic example of the Prophet's wives and the slave/free punishment illustrate this principle.
3
Analyze the remaining three arguments
The second argument uses witness acceptance: a sinner is not acceptable as a witness, but prophets are accepted, so they cannot be sinners. The third argument concerns rebuking: a prophet must be rebuked if he sins, but rebuking a prophet is forbidden. The fourth argument involves following: if a prophet sinned, we would have to follow him in that sin, which is impossible due to conflicting obligations.
Each argument is independently sufficient to refute the claim.

The fourth argument: if he committed a sin, we would have to follow him in it (because of the general command to follow), but that would require us to do something forbidden, which is impossible.

Commentary
This passage discusses the interpretation (ta'wil) of Adam's sin. It begins by stating that some scholars conceded that Adam was mindful of the divine essence but proceeded to eat through ta'wil. The marginal note (أقول) then provides further support for the view that the disobedience mentioned in the Quran refers to Adam's children, citing the verse about giving partners, and offers a response to the objection that Adam could not have forgotten if reminded. It concludes that when evidences conflict, one must resort to ta'wil or suspension.

Proof That Prophets Are Superior to Angels
Razi presents Quranic and rational proofs for prophets' superiority and an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni argument for the superiority of prophets over angels, including the Quranic and rational proofs, and the counter-argument regarding knowledge and closeness.

Core Idea
Prophets serve Allah under greater hardship, making their obedience more excellent.

Imam Razi presents the Sunni position that prophets are superior to angels, countering the Mu'tazila and others. He provides a Quranic verse and a rational argument based on the difficulty of human worship. However, he also records an objection that challenges the basis of superiority. This lesson unpacks the argument and the objection.

Commentary
This passage asserts the Sunni position that prophets are superior to angels, against the Mu'tazila, the Qadi (likely 'Abd al-Jabbar or al-Baqillani), and the philosophers. Two proofs are given. First, a Quranic verse: 'Indeed, Allah chose Adam and Noah' – if interpreted generally, it includes superiority over angels; if restricted to the scholars of that time, it still shows superiority in that context, analogous to 'I have favored you over the worlds'. Second, a rational argument: humans worship Allah despite many distractions (heedlessness, anger, internal/external obstacles), whereas angels have none, so human obedience is harder, and the Prophet said 'the best deeds are the most strenuous'. The marginal note (أقول) raises an objection: superiority might mean knowledge or closeness. If knowledge, angels have innate knowledge, humans acquire knowledge through effort, so angels may be superior in that regard. If closeness, angels are closer without intermediaries, while prophets need angelic mediation. This objection is not answered here, but the main text's proof stands on the difficulty of worship.
