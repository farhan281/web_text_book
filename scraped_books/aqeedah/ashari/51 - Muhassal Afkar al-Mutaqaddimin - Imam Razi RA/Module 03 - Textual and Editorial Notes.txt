The True Nature of al-Tusi's Talkhis al-Muhassal
Editor explains that al-Tusi wrote a commentary titled 'Talkhis al-Muhassal' which is not a summary but a critique and explanation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that al-Tusi's 'Talkhis al-Muhassal' is not a summary but a critical commentary that includes explanation, supplementation, and sometimes severe criticism.

Core Idea
Al-Tusi's Talkhis is a critique, not a summary — and that's a good thing.

Al-Tusi's work on al-Muhassal is often misunderstood. The editor clarifies that the title 'Talkhis' is misleading: it is not a summary but a detailed engagement with Razi's text. In this lesson, we will examine the editor's explanation and see how al-Tusi balances criticism with praise.

Editor's Critique of the Definition of al-Naẓar
Editor criticizes earlier definition and offers broader definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the editor's critique of the narrow definition of naẓar and the broader definition he proposes.

Core Idea
The definition of naẓar must include both directions of reasoning and conceptual analysis.

The editor of this text challenges the definition of naẓar given earlier. He points out that it only covers one type of reasoning (from premises to conclusion) and omits the reverse direction and the analysis of concepts. He then proposes a broader definition that encompasses all intellectual transitions.

Guided Path
1

3

The editor offers a comprehensive definition: naẓar is the transition from things present in the mind to things to be obtained as goals. Reflect on how this covers both directions and conceptual analysis.

The editor states that fikr (thinking) is synonymous with naẓar in this technical usage. Consider why the editor might equate them.

Substance Classification: Induction vs. Immediate Division
Editor criticizes Razi's induction and offers immediate division of substance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the editor objects to Razi's use of induction and how the alternative immediate division of substance into material and separate categories works.

Core Idea
Induction is unnecessary; substance divides immediately into material and separate.

In this editorial note, the commentator challenges Razi's reliance on induction for classifying substance. Instead, he presents a direct division that philosophers use, distinguishing between material (body and its parts) and separate (soul and intellect). This lesson unpacks that critique and the alternative scheme.

Guided Path
1
Read the editor's objection
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and translation. Notice the editor's criticism: Razi claimed body is 'only by induction' composed of form and matter; the editor says philosophers do not use induction here.
The editor is not rejecting the composition, but the method.
2
Understand the alternative classification
The editor proposes an immediate division: substance is either material (maddi) or separate (mufariq). Material includes matter (hayula), form (sura), and body; separate includes soul (nafs) and intellect ('aql).
This division is based on direct conceptual analysis, not empirical induction.
3
Reflect on the significance
Ask yourself why the method matters. The editor's point is that classification can be determined a priori, without needing to examine all bodies. This shows a key difference in philosophical methodology.
Induction leads to probabilistic knowledge; immediate division yields certainty.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
أقول : في قوله « أو مركبا من الصورة والهيولى وهو الجسم فقط بالاستقراء ، نظر فإن الحكماء لا يستعملون الاستقراء ههنا ولا يحتاجون إليه ، بل يقسمون الموهر إلى الجسم وأجزائه وإلى ما ليس بجسم ولا بالأجزاء وهذه قسمة حاضرة ، ويسمون القسم الأول بالمادى والقسم الثانى بالمفارق ، ويقدمون الأول إلى نفس المادة وإلى ما يقومها وإلى ما يتقوم بها . والأول هو الهيولى . والثانى هو الصورة وهما جزاء الجسم والثالث هو الجسم . وأما المفارق فإما أن يتصرف في الماديات أو لا يتصرف وهما النفس والعقل
I say: There is consideration in his statement: 'or composed of form and matter, and it is the body only by induction.' For the philosophers do not use induction here nor need it. Rather, they divide substance into body and its parts, and into what is neither body nor parts—and this is an immediate division. They call the first division the material (al-maddi) and the second the separate (al-mufariq). They analyze the first into the matter itself, into what subsists by it, and into what is constituted by it. The first is matter (hayula), the second is form (sura), and they are the two parts of body, and the third is the body. As for the separate, it either acts upon material things or does not; these are the soul (nafs) and the intellect ('aql).
Source pages p. 88

Commentary
This passage is an editorial comment (aqul) on Razi's substance classification. The editor takes issue with Razi's claim that body is 'only by induction' (bil-istiqra') composed of form and matter. He argues that philosophers (al-hukama') do not rely on induction here; instead they use an immediate conceptual division: substance is either material (including body and its components) or separate (mufariq). The material is further divided into matter (hayula) which is the substrate, form (sura) which subsists in it, and body which is composed of both. The separate is either soul (governing body) or intellect (purely immaterial). This note shows a critical engagement with Razi's text, typical of later commentaries.

Commentary
This passage is a critical commentary by the author (probably Imam Rāzī himself) on the sources of the doctrines presented earlier. The author begins by quoting from al-Shahristānī's 'al-Milal wa al-Niḥal' regarding Thales of Miletus, presenting a more detailed account of Thales' doctrine involving a primal element containing forms of all things, from which all existents emerge. The author then notes a surprising report that Thales' first originated is water, which aligns with a Torah narrative. The author expresses doubt about the attribution to the Torah, saying it requires scrutiny. He then mentions al-Shahristānī's report that Democritus said the simple bodies are spherical, and he references Ibn Sīnā's 'al-Shifā'' on the same topic, noting that differences among them are not beneficial. Finally, he presents another footnote with further reports about Heraclitus, Anaxagoras, and Empedocles regarding their theories of concealment and manifestation. Overall, this commentary underscores the author's methodological caution about the reliability of historical transmissions.
