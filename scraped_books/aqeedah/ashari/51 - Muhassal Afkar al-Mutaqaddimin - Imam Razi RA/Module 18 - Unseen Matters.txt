Defining Pain and Pleasure: Refuting Ibn Zakariyya
Razi refutes Ibn Zakariyya's definition of pleasure as deliverance from pain.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why pleasure cannot simply be deliverance from pain, and how Razi refutes that definition using the example of first-time perception.

Core Idea
Pleasure is not always relief from pain; new experiences bring pleasure without prior pain.

Razi begins by examining the nature of pain and pleasure. After noting that pain is clearly existent, he tackles the definition of pleasure. He refutes the view of Muhammad ibn Zakariyya that pleasure is merely deliverance from pain, providing a compelling counterexample. He then presents the definitions of Ibn Sina and the Mu'tazila, noting that they do not prove that pain is only perception.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage slowly
Understand the structure: first pain is existent, then Ibn Zakariyya's definition of pleasure and its refutation.
2
Identify the refutation
Razi uses the example of seeing a beautiful form for the first time. Notice how this challenges the idea that pleasure requires prior pain.
The pleasure here is a new experience, not relief from yearning.
3
Compare with other definitions
Read Ibn Sina's and the Mu'tazila's definitions, and note Razi's critique that they do not yield certainty that pain is only perception.

ومنها الألم واللذة أما الألم فلا نزاع في كونه وجوديا ، ثم قال محمد بن زكريا اللذة عبارة عن الخلاص عن الألم وهو باطل بما إذا وقع بصر الإنسان على الإنسان على صورة مليحة فإنه يلتذ بإبصار ما مع أنه لم يكن له شعور بتلك الصورة قبل ذلك حتى تجعل تلك اللذة خلاصا عن ألم الشوق إليها . وزعم ابن سينا أن اللذة: إدراك المرافق والألم إدراك المنافي ، ويقرب قول المعتزلة منه فإنهم قالوا إن المدرك إن كان متعلق الشهوة كالحكة في حق الأجرب كان إدراكه لذة ، وإن كان متعلق النفرة كما فى حق السليم كان إدراكه ألما ، ومثل هذا الكلام لا يفيد القطع بأن الألم ليس إلا الإدراك . واتفقت الفلاسفة على أن تفرق الاتصال موجب للألم في حق الحى وخالفتهم ، لأن التفرق عدمى فلا يكون علة للأمر الوجودى ، وزاد ابن سينا سببا ثانيا وهو سوء المزاج ، قال لأن حد الألم إدراك المنافى وا لحد ينعكس وكل إدراك المنافى ألم وهذه الحجة لفظية لأنه أخذ من العكس . أقول : نقل عن ابن زكريا أنه قال اللذة خروج من الحال الغير الطبيعية وذلك لكون الإدراك إنما يحصل بانفعال للحاسة يقتضيه تبدل حال وأخذ ما بالعرض مكان ما بالذات ، وقول المعتزلة يدل على أنهم يقولون إن اللذة والألم هما الإدراك نفسه ويختلفان باختلاف متعلقهما وهذا الكلام لا يفيد القطع بأن الألم ليس غير الإدراك، ومخالفة المصنف فى أن تفرق الاتصال ليس بموجب للألم في الحي : إنما كان لأنه يقول التفريق يوجب سوء المزاج الذى يقتضيه طبائع المفردات عند تفريقها ، فالسبب الذاتي هو طبائع المفردات و التفريق يقتضي زوال الاعتدال الذى حصل من الكسر والانكسار ، فالتفريق ليس سببا بالذات .
And among them are pain and pleasure. As for pain, there is no dispute that it is existent. Then Muhammad ibn Zakariyya said: Pleasure is an expression of deliverance from pain, and it is false because when a human's sight falls on a beautiful form, he takes pleasure in seeing it, even though he had no awareness of that form before, such that that pleasure could be deliverance from the pain of yearning for it. Ibn Sina claimed that pleasure is perception of what is agreeable and pain is perception of what is harmful. The statement of the Mu'tazila is close to it, for they said: If the perceived is related to desire, like itching in the case of a mangy person, its perception is pleasure; if it is related to aversion, as in the case of a healthy person, its perception is pain. And such speech does not yield certainty that pain is nothing but perception. The philosophers agreed that separation of continuity causes pain in a living being, and (Razi) disagreed with them, because separation is non-existent, so it cannot be a cause for an existent thing. Ibn Sina added a second cause: bad temperament, for he said: The definition of pain is perception of the harmful, and the definition converts, and every perception of the harmful is pain. This argument is verbal because it is taken from the converse. I say: It is reported from Ibn Zakariyya that he said: Pleasure is exiting from an unnatural state, because perception occurs only through an affection of the sense that necessitates a change of state and taking the accidental in place of the essential. The statement of the Mu'tazila indicates that they say pleasure and pain are perception itself and differ by their objects, but this speech does not yield certainty that pain is nothing other than perception. The author's disagreement regarding that separation of continuity is not a cause of pain in the living: that is because he says that separation necessitates bad temperament, which is necessitated by the natures of the separated parts. So the essential cause is the natures of the parts, and separation necessitates the removal of the equilibrium that resulted from composition, so separation is not a cause in itself.

Commentary
This passage addresses the nature of pain (alam) and pleasure (ladhdha). It first states that pain is undisputedly existent (wujudiyya). Then it refutes Muhammad ibn Zakariyya al-Razi's (the physician) view that pleasure is merely deliverance (khulas) from pain, giving the counterexample of seeing a beautiful form for the first time—one experiences pleasure without prior awareness or pain. It then presents Ibn Sina's definition: pleasure is perception of the agreeable (al-mulaim), pain is perception of the harmful (al-munafi). The Mu'tazila have a similar view. Razi notes these definitions do not prove that pain is only perception. The philosophers also held that separation of continuity (tafarruq al-ittisal) causes pain, but Razi disagrees because separation is non-existent (adam) and cannot cause an existent. Ibn Sina added a second cause: bad temperament (su' al-mizaj), but Razi calls this argument verbal because it converts the definition. The commentary then elaborates: Ibn Zakariyya's view is that pleasure is exiting an unnatural state, involving change of state. The Mu'tazila equate pleasure and pain with perception. The author (Razi himself) holds that separation does not directly cause pain; rather, separation leads to bad temperament due to the natures of the parts, so the essential cause is the natures, not separation.

The Cause of Pain: Separation of Continuity and Bad Temperament
Razi refutes the philosophers' view that separation causes pain and provides his own causal explanation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Razi argues that separation of continuity cannot directly cause pain, and how he resolves the issue by attributing pain to bad temperament caused by the natures of separated parts.

Core Idea
Separation is non-existent, so it cannot cause existent pain; the real cause is the ensuing bad temperament.

Razi now turns to the question of what causes pain. The philosophers claimed that separation of continuity directly causes pain. Razi objects because separation is a non-existence and cannot cause an existent like pain. Ibn Sina added a second cause: bad temperament. Razi also examines and refines the understanding of pleasure from Ibn Zakariyya and the Mu'tazila, ultimately explaining that separation only indirectly causes pain by necessitating bad temperament due to the natures of the separated parts.

Guided Path
1
Read the philosophers' claim
They say separation of continuity causes pain in a living being.
2
Understand Razi's objection
Separation is non-existent (adam), so it cannot be a cause of something existent like pain.
3
Examine Ibn Sina's addition of bad temperament
His argument is flawed because it relies on converting the definition of pain.
Razi calls it a verbal argument.
4
Follow Razi's own explanation
Separation leads to bad temperament due to the natures of the parts, and bad temperament is the direct cause. Thus separation is not a cause in itself.

The Order of the Four Elements
Description of elemental spheres and critique of their order

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the philosophers arranged the four elements in concentric spheres and the causal claim that motion generates heat, as well as the inconsistency regarding earth's coldness and fire's wetness.

Core Idea
The elements are nested spheres ordered by fineness, but the theory has internal contradictions.

This passage opens the discussion of bodily substances by presenting the philosophers' model of the four elements as concentric spheres. The arrangement is derived from the principle that motion generates heat, leading to fire nearest the sphere and earth farthest away. However, the author immediately points out inconsistencies: the model would imply that earth is colder than air (contrary to their claim) and that fire should be extremely wet—both of which trouble the philosophers' own definitions. Understanding this sets the stage for the later debates on elemental transformation and mixture.

Guided Path
1
Read the description of the elemental spheres
Read the Arabic passage slowly, noting the concentric order: earth, water, air, fire as spheres enveloping each other, except that water does not fully surround earth (it is said 'except water').
The phrase 'except water' is ambiguous; it may mean water does not completely envelop earth, leaving some dry land.
2
Identify the causal claim
The philosophers claim 'motion heats' (الحركة مسخنة), so the body nearest the moving sphere becomes hottest and finest (fire), and the farthest becomes coldest and densest (earth).
This is a key principle linking motion of the heavens to the properties of sublunar elements.
3
Spot the inconsistency
The author argues that this ordering would make earth colder than air, but the philosophers usually consider water the coldest. Also, fire should be extremely wet given their definition of wetness as ease of accepting shapes.
Note the definition of wetness: it is not about being water-like but about easily taking new shapes. Fire, being formless, should be 'wet' by that definition, which is absurd.
4
Reflect on the purpose of this critique
Consider why the author presents this inconsistency. It shows that even the basic framework of the philosophers has internal problems, preparing for the mutakallimun's objections.

As for the elements: They claimed that earth is surrounded by water, water by air, and air by fire, and that they are spheres one enveloping the other except water. They claimed that motion heats, so the body adjacent to the sphere must be extremely hot and fine, which is fire; that which is at the utmost distance must be extremely cold and dense, which is earth; that which adjoins fire, air, follows in fineness; that which follows earth follows in density. This is the precise description of the order of the elements, except that this statement requires that earth be colder than air, contrary to their claim, and that fire be extremely wet, because wetness according to them is interpreted as ease of accepting shapes, not ease of adhesion to place, and air was not wet. (1) Then they claimed that these four are susceptible to generation and corruption because fire when extinguished turns into air, and when air cools it becomes water; for that reason, water droplets collect at the tip of a jug cooled with snow, and water turns into earth as the alchemists do. (2) As for composites, they claimed that when these elements mix, the force of each one's quality breaks the force of the other, resulting in an intermediate quality which is the temperament (mizaj). The mutakallimun said: The cause is resistant to the effect. So if the breaker of each one's force is the force of the other, and if the two breakings occur simultaneously, then both breakers must obtain in that time, so each of those two qualities at that instant is both broken and unbroken, which is a contradiction; and if they do not occur together, it is absurd because the effect would return after it had been? It cannot be said that the breaker is the constitutive form (sura muqawwima), which remains unbroken, and what is broken is the quality, which admits intensity and weakness; for we say: the form is either broken through the supervening quality, so the problem returns. The Mutakallimun Objection on Mixture of Elements
Philosophers' theory of mixture and mutakallimun's objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' explanation of how elements mix to produce a temperament (mizaj) and the mutakallimun's logical objection that simultaneous breaking of qualities leads to contradiction.

Core Idea
When elements mix, their qualities break each other, but can a quality be both broken and unbroken at once?

This passage addresses a central problem in physics: how do four distinct elements with opposing qualities combine to form a unified substance? The philosophers propose that the qualities (hot, cold, wet, dry) break each other's force, producing an intermediate temperament. The mutakallimun, however, object that if each quality breaks the other, then at the moment of breaking they must be both active (breaking) and passive (being broken), which is contradictory. They also refute a possible defense that the constitutive form (sura muqawwima) does the breaking while the quality is broken. This debate highlights the logical rigor of kalam.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof from the Quranic verse 'Wujuhun yawma'idhin nadirah * ila rabbiha nazirah' (Faces that Day will be radiant, looking at their Lord). Razi analyzes the word 'nazar' (looking). It has two possible meanings: (1) actual vision (ru'ya), or (2) physically turning the pupil toward something to see it (which implies seeking vision). If it means vision, the argument is direct. If it means the physical act of turning the pupil in the direction of something to see it, that cannot be taken literally because there is no direction for Allah, so it must be metaphorical for vision itself, since 'nazar' is a cause of vision and expressing effect by cause is strong metaphor. Razi then refutes two alternative interpretations proposed by opponents: (a) that 'nazar' means 'waiting for bounties' (nazirah = muntazirah). He rejects this because waiting causes grief, but the verse mentions radiance (nudur) which is a blessing. (b) that it means 'looking at reward' (thawab). He argues that 'looking at reward' must itself be interpreted as seeing the reward, so it still requires vision. And if we must assume an implicit object, the simpler assumption is Allah Himself, not reward, because adding reward is without evidence. Thus, the verse supports vision of Allah.

Argument Against Pre-Bodily Multiplicity of Souls
Al-Razi argues that souls cannot be multiple before bodies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument that the human soul cannot be multiple before attachment to bodies, and why both the case of the soul remaining one and the case of it dividing lead to absurdities.

Core Idea
Multiplicity of souls before bodies is impossible because it leads to logical contradictions.

Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi presents a rigorous logical demonstration that souls cannot be multiple prior to their attachment to bodies. He considers the only two possibilities—the soul remaining one or dividing—and shows both are impossible. Then he argues that even if multiplicity were assumed, it would require a distinguishing factor that cannot exist before bodies.

Guided Path
1
Understand the reductio structure
Al-Razi uses a logical proof by contradiction: assume the opposite (multiplicity before bodies) and show it leads to absurdity. Familiarize yourself with this method.
The argument is dense; take it step by step.
2
Examine the case of the soul remaining one
If the soul remains one after attachment to multiple bodies, then all souls would share all knowledge—an evident falsehood. This is one branch of the reductio.
This shows the absurdity of the soul staying undivided.
3
Examine the case of the soul dividing
If the soul divides upon attachment, then the original soul either already had multiplicity (contradiction) or perishes and new souls arise—both impossible.
This covers the other branch, completing the reductio against unity.
4
Consider the requirement for a distinguishing factor
Even if souls were multiple before bodies, they must be distinct by either essential attributes or accidents. Essential distinction is impossible because all human souls share the same species; accidents require matter, which is absent before the body.
Thus pre-bodily multiplicity is impossible on all counts.

First Proof Against Reincarnation: The Createdness of the Soul
Al-Razi gives the first proof against reincarnation based on the soul's creation and bodily preparation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first proof against reincarnation based on the soul's createdness and the necessary condition of bodily preparedness, and how al-Razi defends it against objections.

Core Idea
A body cannot host two souls; reincarnation implies two souls in one body, which is impossible.

Al-Razi presents a decisive argument against reincarnation from the principle that the soul's creation requires a condition—bodily preparedness. If a soul were to reincarnate, the new body would also need to be prepared for a fresh soul, leading to an absurdity of two souls in one body. The objection that this is circular or that souls differ is then rebutted.

Guided Path
1
Understand the premise: the soul is created
The proof begins from the agreed-upon position that the soul is created (hādith). This premise is crucial.
2
Identify the condition for creation
The soul's creation from its eternal source depends on a condition; otherwise, why now rather than earlier? That condition is the existence of a body with preparedness.
3
See the contradiction in reincarnation
A body that receives a reincarnated soul must also be prepared for a new soul, leading to two souls in one body—an impossibility.
4
Examine the objection and its flaws
The objection claims circularity or that souls differ in quiddity. Al-Razi refutes these by showing that if souls are one in species, they cannot preferentially attach to different bodies.
5
Recognize the strength of the proof
Al-Razi concludes that the proof is decisive without needing to refute reincarnation from other angles.

The Philosophers' Proof for the Soul's Immortality
Al-Razi presents the philosophers' proof for the soul's immortality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' argument that the soul cannot be annihilated because annihilation requires matter, which the soul lacks.

Core Idea
Annihilation requires matter, so an immaterial soul cannot perish.

The philosophers present a rational proof for the soul's immortality. They argue that anything that can be annihilated must have a material substrate, and since the soul is not a body, it cannot be annihilated. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Refuting the Philosophers on Afterlife Happiness
Razi presents and critiques the philosophers' argument for afterlife happiness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' argument for the happiness of pure souls after death and the key kalām objections Razi raises against it.

Core Idea
Pleasure is not identical to perception, and causation is not automatic.

Razi presents a philosophical claim about the afterlife and then subjects it to rigorous kalām critique. Before we can accept that pure souls are happy after death, we must examine whether pleasure is simply perception and whether perception necessarily causes pleasure. This passage shows how kalām thinkers scrutinized causal claims even when they seemed plausible.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage carefully, then its literal translation. Notice the structure: a claim, a proof, two objections, and a final concession.
The philosophers' argument is presented neutrally before being critiqued.
2
Identify the philosophers' claim and proof
Find the statement of the philosophers' consensus and the syllogism they use: pleasure = perception of the agreeable, the agreeable is perception of abstract entities, perception occurs after death, therefore pleasure occurs.
3
Understand Razi's first objection
Razi asks: if pleasure is identical to perception, then why do we perceive without pleasure? This shows the identity claim is false.
This is a direct counterexample to the definition.

Even if perception causes pleasure, how do we know? Induction only gives probability, and analogy to other pleasures is weak.
Razi questions the causal link itself.
5
Grasp the final point about cause and effect
Even if the causal link were proven, the presence of a cause does not guarantee the effect if a condition is missing or an obstacle remains. This undermines the necessity of the conclusion.
This is a standard kalām principle.

دليل ثالث : قوله تعالى : ﴿إن الله لا يغفر أن يشرك به ويغفر ما دون ذلك لمن يشاء﴾ وكذا قوله تعالى ﴿وإن ربك لذو مغفرة للناس على ظلمهم﴾ وكلمة على للحال ، يقال رأيت الأمير على أكله أي حال أكله ، فالآية تقتضي حصول المغفرة حال اشتغال العبد بالظلم ، وهو يدل على حصول المغفرة قبل التوبة . دليل رابع : أجمع المسلمون على كونه تعالى عفوا والعفو لا يتحقق إلا عند إسقاط العذاب المستحق ، . عند الخصم ترك العقاب على الصغيرة قبل التوبة وعلى الكبيرة بعدها واجبة ، لا يبقى للعفو معنى إلا إسقاط العقاب على الكبيرة قبل التوبة .
Third proof: His statement, exalted be He, 'Indeed, Allah does not forgive that partners be associated with Him, but He forgives what is less than that for whom He wills' (Quran 4:48) and also His statement, 'And indeed your Lord is full of forgiveness for mankind despite their wrongdoing' (Quran 13:6). The word 'ala (upon) indicates state; one says, 'I saw the Amir upon his eating,' meaning in the state of his eating. So the verse necessitates that forgiveness occurs while the servant is engaged in wrongdoing, and this indicates that forgiveness occurs before repentance. Fourth proof: The Muslims are unanimous that He, exalted be He, is Forgiving ('afuw), and forgiveness is only realized by the cancellation of deserved punishment. According to the opponent, withholding punishment for a minor sin before repentance and for a major sin after repentance is obligatory; so forgiveness has no meaning except canceling punishment for a major sin before repentance.

Commentary
This passage provides two proofs against the Mu'tazilite claim that punishment for major sins cannot be forgiven without repentance. The third proof is from the Quran: verse 4:48 shows that Allah forgives sins other than shirk for whom He wills, without conditioning repentance. Verse 13:6 uses the word 'ala which grammatically indicates a state ('ala ḥāl), meaning forgiveness occurs while the person is in the state of wrongdoing. Thus, forgiveness happens before repentance. The fourth proof is from consensus (ijma'): Muslims agree that Allah is 'afuw (forgiving). The term 'afwu means cancelling a deserved punishment. The Mu'tazilites hold that withholding punishment for minor sins before repentance and for major sins after repentance is obligatory, so the only remaining meaning for 'afwu is the cancellation of punishment for major sins before repentance, which contradicts their position.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think the word 'ala always means 'upon' in a locative sense, implying forgiveness is 'despite' wrongdoing or after repentance.
Correction: Al-Razi explains that 'ala here indicates state (ḥāl), so forgiveness occurs at the very moment of wrongdoing, not after.
