Miracles as Transformations, Not Annihilation
Imam Razi explains that miracles do not involve annihilation of existing beings, only transformation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Imam Razi argues that miracles do not involve annihilation of existing beings, but only transformation of form.

Core Idea
Miracles are changes of form, not instant destructions of created things.

This lesson addresses a potential objection to the principle that existing things are not annihilated: What about the miracles of the prophets? Imam Razi clarifies that miracles such as turning a staff into a serpent are not annihilations but transformations—substitutions of one form for another. This understanding safeguards the consistency of the kalam worldview while affirming the reality of prophetic signs.

Commentary
This passage addresses a potential objection: do not miracles (mu'jizāt) involve annihilating something existing, like turning a staff into a serpent? The author responds that miracles do not involve annihilation (i'dām) of an existing being; they are transformations (tabdīl ṣūra bi-ṣūra) of form. The examples given—staff to serpent and back, she-camel from mountain, water from rock, reviving the dead—are all possible in the intellect and do not require the annihilation of any continuing entity. Even instantaneous creation of an equivalent (ījād mithl lil-mutaqaddim) is mentioned, but the author notes that some miracles have interpretations (ta'wīlāt) that cannot be elaborated here. The key point is that miracles do not violate the principle that existing beings are not annihilated instantaneously; they are rather changes of form or generation from matter.

Refuting Objections to Miracles as Proofs
Imam Razi refutes objections to miracle-based certainty and gives the correct answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Razi defends the certainty of miracles as proofs against objections, and why the mere possibility of deception does not undermine rational certainty.

Core Idea
Possibility does not negate certainty: miracles can be proven despite abstract alternatives.

Imam Razi addresses two objections that skeptics raise against the reliability of miracles as evidence for prophethood. The first objection claims that allowing miracles undermines trust in self-evident truths; the second suggests that miracles might not be from God or might serve a different purpose. Razi refutes both, critiques a weak Mu'tazilite answer, and then provides the definitive response: deeming something possible does not contradict being certain that it did not happen. This lesson walks through his argument step by step.

Guided Path

The passage is dense; take your time.

3
Examine the critique of the Mu'tazilite answer
Razi shows why the Mu'tazilite answer (that God must prevent a false miracle) is weak. Understand his reasoning: God is not obliged to remove all ambiguity because the servant shares responsibility.
He uses analogies: ambiguous verses (mutashabihat) and God allowing disbelievers to harm believers.
4
Grasp the final correct answer
Focus on the key premise: 'that deeming something possible does not negate certainty of its non-existence.' Razi illustrates with the example of creating an adult instantly and the natural sign of anger.
This is the core takeaway – possibility and certainty coexist.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of objections and answers about the reliability of miracles as proofs. The first objection states that if miracles (kharq al-'ada) were possible, then trust in self-evident truths (badihiyyat) would be undermined. Imam Razi replies that this is a necessary consequence for philosophers (falasifa) who admit the possibility of unusual celestial events causing anomalies. The second objection suggests that miracles might not be from God, or if from God, might have a purpose other than verifying the prophet. Imam Razi critiques the Mu'tazilite answer—that God must prevent a miracle if the claimant is false—as weak. Instead, he argues that since miracles can have multiple possible explanations, the individual's categorical adoption of one explanation is their own responsibility, not God's. He gives analogies: ambiguous Qur'anic verses (mutashabihat) are not objectionable, and God's allowing disbelievers to harm believers does not imply truthfulness of the disbelievers' claims. Finally, he presents the correct answer: the possibility of something does not negate certainty of its non-occurrence. For example, we can conceive God creating an adult instantly, yet we are certain it has not happened. Likewise, rational certainty can coexist with abstract possibility.

This passage from Imam Razi's Muhassal addresses two key objections that skeptics might raise against miracles as proofs of prophethood. First, if miracles break the natural order, how can we trust any empirical knowledge? Second, even if a miracle occurs, it might not be from God or might have a purpose other than confirming a prophet. Razi then critiques the Mu'tazili response to the second objection, showing its weakness through analogies with ambiguous verses and unanswered prayers. Let's examine these arguments in detail.

1
Read the first objection and response
The first objection is that if miracles break custom, we can't trust self-evident truths. The author responds that this problem also applies to philosophers because of possible strange celestial events.
Notice how Razi turns the objection back on the objector.
2
Read the second objection and Mu'tazili answer
The second objection: miracles could be from other than God or for a different purpose. The Mu'tazili answer: God would prevent a false miracle to protect people from error.
This is the conventional theological response.

Understand Razi's refutation
Razi argues that the Mu'tazili answer is weak because God is not obligated to prevent false miracles if there are other possible interpretations. He uses the analogy of ambiguous verses (mutashabih) in the Quran and the fact that disbelievers' prayers are sometimes unanswered.
The key point: multiple probabilities exist, so God does not have to remove all doubt.
4

Consider how this passage shows the importance of rational analysis in evaluating miracles, rather than relying on a single interpretation.
This connects to broader themes in kalam about knowledge and certainty.

Possibility Does Not Negate Certainty
Establishes that possibility does not negate certainty and applies to miracles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the possibility of something does not negate certainty of its non-existence, and how this principle applies to miracles as proof of prophethood.

Possibility does not undermine necessary knowledge.

The author now presents his own answer, based on a crucial logical premise: the mere possibility of something does not destroy our certainty that it did not happen. This principle allows us to affirm that miracles confirm prophethood despite alternative theoretical possibilities. The passage develops this with examples and applies it to the proofs of prophethood.

Karamat: The Miracles of Saints in Sunni Theology
Razi asserts karamat's permissibility and defines their distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni position on the permissibility of karamat (saints' miracles) and how they are distinguished from prophetic miracles by the absence of a challenge.

Core Idea
A saint's miracle lacks the challenge that defines a prophet's miracle.

Imam Razi begins this section by asserting the Sunni consensus on the permissibility of karamat (miracles of saints), against the Mu'tazila and some Ash'ari scholars. He provides Quranic proof from the stories of Maryam and Asif. Then he clarifies the key distinction: a karamah, unlike a prophetic miracle, lacks the element of challenge (tahaddi) that proves a prophet's claim. This lesson unpacks that distinction and considers an anticipated objection.
