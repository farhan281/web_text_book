Commentary
This passage presents a proof for the consensus that accidents cannot transfer from one substance to another. The proof is a dichotomy: consider an accident stripped of all necessary attributes. Either it needs a locus (mahall) or it does not. If it does not need a locus, it is self-sufficient, and something self-sufficient cannot acquire a need; this is false because accidents are dependent. If it needs a locus, either the locus is indeterminate or determinate. An indeterminate locus is impossible because existence requires determination. So the locus must be determinate, meaning the accident is inseparable from that specific locus; hence transference is impossible. The objector says: why not allow that it does not need a locus, since self-sufficiency prevents acquisition of need? The answer draws a distinction: the accident is not said to be necessarily not in a locus (which would oppose its being in one); rather, it is said that in its essence it does not necessitate existence in a locus. This does not prevent it from being in a locus due to an external cause (like God creating it in a locus). So the accident's intrinsic attribute is not self-sufficiency, but rather lack of necessity for a locus; it can still be given a locus by divine action.

The Core Proof: Accident Cannot Transfer Substrate
Razi presents the initial proof and first objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument that an accident, stripped of attributes, cannot transfer because it must either need a substrate (leading to determination) or be self-sufficient (impossible for an accident).

Core Idea
An accident's existence depends on a specific substrate; it cannot be self-sufficient.

This lesson unpacks the opening of Razi's proof. He begins by stating the consensus that accidents cannot transfer. He defines transmission and then presents a thought experiment: if an accident were stripped of all essential attributes, it would either need a substrate or not. Each option leads to a conclusion that prevents transfer. We will follow this reasoning and examine an initial objection.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of transmission
Notice that Razi defines transmission as 'obtaining at a time after obtaining in another location,' which immediately restricts it to things that occupy space.
This definition is crucial because accidents are not spatial entities.
2
Understand the supposition
Razi asks us to imagine an accident stripped of all essential attributes. This thought experiment isolates the accident's relationship to a substrate.
3
Analyze the two options
Either the accident does not need a substrate (self-sufficient) or it does. The first is false because then it would be independent, and later needing a substrate would be a change in essence.
The argument uses the principle that what is essential cannot be changed by accident.
4
Examine the objection
An objector suggests that perhaps the accident does not need a substrate at all. Razi responds by clarifying that accidents are not described as 'must not be in a substrate', but rather 'considering its essence, it does not have to be in a substrate'—this leaves room for external dependence.

Commentary

He first explains that transmission means to exist in a place after existing in another place, which is only conceivable for bodies. The proof assumes an accident stripped of all essential qualities. Indeterminate Substrate and Razi's Final Proof
Razi refutes the indeterminate substrate objection and presents his own proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection about indeterminate substrate and Razi's rigorous proof that an accident's individual existence requires a specific substrate.

Core Idea
An accident's individual existence is tied to a particular substrate; no indeterminate cause suffices.

This lesson continues Razi's discussion by addressing a further objection: even if the accident needs a substrate, why can't it be indeterminate? Razi responds with an analogy and then provides his own deeper proof. He distinguishes the accident's dependence from that of bodies and matter, showing that accidents are unique in needing a specific substrate for their very existence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objector grants that the accident needs a substrate but argues it does not need a specifically determined one, similar to a body needing any location.
2
Analyze the refutation
Razi points out that the analogy fails because bodies need a location for a quality (tahayyuz), not for existence itself. Accidents need a substrate for their individual existence.
3
Read Razi's own proof
Razi defines an accident as that whose individual existence is only realized by a substrate. He then argues that it cannot need an indeterminate cause because the indeterminate does not exist externally.
This is a more precise argument based on existence itself.
4
Compare with body and matter
He distinguishes the accident's case from a body's need for location (which is for a property, not existence) and from matter's need for form (which is not determinate).

Two possibilities: either it does not need a substrate (impossible because then it would be self-sufficient and could not later need one) or it needs a substrate. If it needs, either indeterminate (impossible because indeterminate does not exist outside) or determinate (so it cannot leave). An objection: why can't it not need a substrate at all? Razi answers that the accident is not described as 'must not be in a substrate' but rather 'considering its essence, it does not have to be in a substrate'; this does not prevent it from being in a substrate due to an external reason. A second objection: granted it needs a substrate, but not a specific one; analogy with a body needing a place. Razi responds in a note that the real issue is transmission from one specific substrate to another, not from one location to another. He then provides a full proof: an accident's individual existence depends on a particular substrate; it cannot need an indeterminate cause; hence it cannot leave its specific substrate. He distinguishes this from a body's need for a place (not for existence but for a quality), and from matter's need for a form, which is not determinate. Thus the impossibility stands.

Imam Razi's first two proofs for accident immortality.

If an accident could persist, it would either require an accident upon accident or make its own disappearance impossible.

Commentary

Razi's Proof of the Indivisible Moment and the Atom
Razi proves the indivisibility of the present moment of motion and the existence of the atom.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The present moment of motion must be indivisible; otherwise motion itself would be impossible.

Razi presents a logical proof that motion and space are composed of discrete indivisible units. He begins by analyzing the present moment of motion. Proof from Matter: The Dilemma of Possibility
Razi presents a dilemma: matter either originated or eternal.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Possibility requires a subject, and infinite regress is impossible, so matter must be directly created.

The only way out is to posit that matter was created from nothing by God. This passage sets up the dilemma.

Razi presents a dilemma: either God is mukhtar or mujib bi-dhatihi.

Learning Objective

Two Ratio Arguments: Against Void and Proving Inclination
Razi presents two ratio-based arguments: one against void, one for inclination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Fakhr al-Din al-Razi uses proportional reasoning to argue that void cannot exist and that every body must possess inclination (mayl).

Core Idea
Void is impossible because it would make a resisting medium and its absence equivalent.

This lesson examines two related arguments from Razi's note. The first uses a ratio between the consistency of a medium and motion time to disprove void, while the second uses a similar ratio involving inclination to prove that every body possesses inclination (mayl). Both arguments rely on the principle that contradictions arise if certain proportionalities hold.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage slowly
Carefully read the Arabic and English to grasp the structure: a warning statement, then an explanation that the problem applies to both denying void and proving inclination.
Notice the two distinct arguments are separated by 'As for proving inclination'.
2
Understand the first argument (against void)
The argument supposes a body thinner than water with a specific consistency ratio. If the ratio of its consistency to water equals the ratio of motion time in void to motion time in water, then the time in this thin plenum equals time in void, making the presence of the plenum equivalent to its absence—impossible.
This shows that void cannot exist because it would collapse the difference that resistance makes.
3
Understand the second argument (for inclination)
The second argument supposes a body whose inclination (mayl) ratio leads to motion time equal to motion with no inclination. This would mean the presence and absence of inclination are equivalent—contradiction. Therefore every body must have inclination.

Razi presents a proof that bodies are finite by showing infinite lines lead to contradiction.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Infinite dimensions would create impossible contradictions in geometry and motion.

Razi's proof that bodies are finite is a classic theological argument using geometry. He shows that assuming an infinite line leads to a contradiction when paired with a finite parallel line. This lesson walks through the proof and the subsequent discussion by the philosophers about circular motion.

فإنكار
الجواب أما المتكلمون فقد سلموا أحيارا متميزة خارج العالم غير متناهية ، وزعموا أنها أمور تقديرية غير موجودة وهذا ضعيف ، لأن المقدر هو الذي لا وجود له إلا في الذهن والذي لا وجود له إلا في الذهن إن لم يكن مطابقا للخارج كان ذلك فرضا كاذبا وإن كان مطابقا لزم منه وجود الأحياز في نفس الأمر وحينئذ يعود الإلزام وأما الحكماء فإنهم أصروا على أن خارج العالم لا يتميز فيه جانب من جانب وأن الحاكم بهذا التميز هو الوهم لا العقل وحكم الوهم غير مقبول (2) .

(2) أقول : المتكلمون سلموا أحيارا غير متناهية ولم يزعموا أنها تقديرية ، بل زعموا أن التمايز فيها تقديري وذلك هو القول بالخلاء الذي شغلته الأجسام ويكون مكانا حيزا لها .
وأما قوله : الذي يلي القطب الشمالي غير الذي يلي الجنوبي ، يقولون في جوابه أن هذا التمايز في القطبين وهما وجوديان وفي الخلاء الذي يليهما تقدري يتوهم بالقياس إليهما ولولا هما لم يكن مبين أصلا والحكماء القائلون بأن الأمكنة سطوح الحادثات يقولون هذه الأحياز وهمية والحكم بوجودها في الخارج كاذب وما لا وجود له أصلا لا يكون فيه امتياز أصلا
The opponent argued: If bodies were finite, then the exterior of all of them would either be such that a side is distinguished from a side—that would be impossible—or not distinguished. If distinguished, that would not be pure non-existence, because pure negation has no specificity and no actuality, so how can differentiation occur? Rather, it must be an existential thing, and there is no doubt that it is either pointed to, so it is a magnitude, or it is a body, so the exterior of all bodies is a body—a contradiction. And if a side is not distinguished from a side, that is impossible by the obviousness of reason, because sound reason testifies that the side that faces the south pole is not the one that faces the north pole—that is stubbornness against self-evident truths.

Denial.
The answer: As for the theologians, they conceded distinct infinite spaces outside the world, claiming they are estimative things not existent, but this is weak, because the estimated is that which has no existence except in the mind, and what has no existence except in the mind, if it does not correspond to the external, would be a false supposition, and if it corresponds, it would entail the existence of spaces in reality, and then the objection returns. As for the philosophers, they insisted that outside the world no side is distinguished from a side, and that the judge of this distinction is estimation (wahm), not reason, and the judgment of estimation is not accepted. (2)

(2) I say: The theologians conceded infinite spaces and did not claim they are estimative, rather they claimed that the differentiation in them is estimative, and that is the doctrine of the void which bodies occupy, and it becomes a place and space for them.
And as for his statement: 'That which faces the north pole is not that which faces the south pole,' they say in answer that this differentiation is in the two poles, which are existential, and in the void that follows them it is estimative, imagined by analogy to them, and without them there would be no indication at all. And the philosophers who hold that places are surfaces of generated things say that these spaces are imaginary, and the judgment of their existence externally is false, and what has no existence at all has no differentiation in it at all.

Commentary
This passage presents the opponent's objection to the proof that bodies are finite, followed by two answers. The objection argues: if bodies are finite, the space outside them must either have distinct sides or not. If it has distinct sides, that cannot be pure non-existence (because non-existence lacks specificity), so it must be an existing thing—either a magnitude or a body, making the outside a body, which contradicts the assumption that only bodies exist. If sides are not distinct, that contradicts reason, which clearly sees a difference between directions like north and south poles. Thus, finite bodies lead to absurdity. The word 'فإنكار' (denial) may indicate a rejection of this. The answer first gives the view of the theologians (mutakallimūn): they concede infinite distinct spaces outside the world but consider them estimative (taqdīriyya) non-existent. The author criticizes this as weak because what exists only in the mind either corresponds to reality (then spaces exist) or does not (false supposition). Then the philosophers' view: they hold that outside the world there is no real distinction; only estimation (wahm) imagines such distinction, and estimation is not valid. The author adds a marginal note clarifying that theologians actually said differentiation is estimative, not the spaces themselves—they affirm the void as a place. Regarding the pole argument, they respond that the differentiation of poles is based on existing poles, and the void's direction is estimative relative to them; without poles, no basis. The philosophers say spaces are imaginary, not real, so no real differentiation exists.

The Theologians' Answer: Estimative Spaces
Razi presents and critiques the theologians' answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the theologians' response—conceding infinite distinct spaces as estimative—and the critique that this is weak.

Theologians say spaces are estimative, but that only shifts the problem.

The Philosophers' Answer: Estimation, Not Reality
Razi presents the philosophers' answer and adds a clarifying note.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Philosophers deny real distinction outside the world; it's all estimation.

Razi then presents the answer of the philosophers (ḥukamā'). They insist that outside the world there is no real distinction of sides; the apparent differentiation is only a product of estimation (wahm). The World is Not Necessarily Everlasting
Razi gives a brief argument that what is not pre-eternal cannot be everlasting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that anything which begins in time cannot be necessarily everlasting, and why this refutes the philosophers and Karramiyya.

Core Idea
Temporal origin implies possible termination; what is not pre-eternal cannot be everlasting.

Imam Razi presents a concise but powerful argument against the necessity of the world's perpetuity. He establishes a logical link between having a beginning and being susceptible to an end. This brief passage cuts to the heart of the debate about the world's eternity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once and then its translation. Notice how Razi states his position clearly before giving the proof.
The passage is very short but dense; every word matters.
2
Identify the key terms
Find the words 'azalī' (pre-eternal) and 'abadī' (everlasting). Understand their distinct meanings: azalī refers to something without a beginning, abadī refers to something without an end.
These terms are central to the argument.
3
Trace the logical chain
Follow Razi's reasoning: if something is not azalī, its essence is receptive to non-existence (qābil li-l-'adam). Therefore it cannot be abadī. Ask yourself why this connection holds.
This argument is a form of modal proof: contingency implies possible non-existence.

Karamiyya's Argument Against Annihilation of the World
Karamiyya enumerate three ways annihilation could occur and refute each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Karamiyya's rational proof that the world cannot be annihilated, based on three exhaustive possibilities.

Core Idea
The world's annihilation is impossible because every possible means leads to contradiction.

The Karamiyya, a theological sect, argued that the world is eternal a parte post (cannot cease). In this passage, they present a proof by division: they enumerate the only possible ways annihilation could occur and show each is rationally impossible. This lesson walks through their reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the three divisions
Read the Arabic passage and translation. Notice that the Karamiyya begin by stating that if the world were to cease, it would have to be through one of three means: annihilation by an agent, occurrence of a contrary, or removal of a condition.
2
Understand the argument against annihilation by an annihilator (iʿdām)
Focus on the first division. The Karamiyya argue that if the annihilator is an existential act, it would lead to existence being identical to non-existence; if it is purely non-existent, it cannot be attributed to the agent. Both options are absurd.
This refutes the possibility of direct annihilation by an agent.
3
Understand the two arguments against annihilation by occurrence of a contrary
The second division is refuted by two independent arguments: first, a vicious circle would result; second, either the contraries would negate each other (leading to simultaneous existence and non-existence) or they would not (leading to coexistence of contraries). Both are impossible.
4
Reflect on the third division (removal of condition)
The third division is mentioned but not argued separately. The Karamiyya likely considered it reducible to the first two or self-evidently impossible. Note that the argument as a whole is exhaustive.

They begin by dividing the possible ways annihilation could occur into three: (1) by an act of an annihilator (iʿdām), (2) by the advent of a contrary (ṭuriyān ḍidd), (3) by the removal of a condition (intifāʾ sharṭ). They argue each is impossible. For the first: if the annihilator is an existential act, then that act would be the non-existence of the world, leading to the absurdity that existence equals non-existence; if it is purely nonexistent, it cannot be attributed to the agent, as no rational distinction exists between non-action and bringing about non-existence. For the second (occurrence of a contrary): they offer two reasons. First, a vicious circle would result because the occurrence of a contrary depends on the prior nonexistence of the opposite contrary. Second, both contraries are mutually opposed; either each would be negated by the other, which would require both to exist and not exist simultaneously, or neither would be negated, leading to the coexistence of contraries, which is impossible. Thus, the world cannot cease to exist.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some may think the Karamiyya deny any possibility of annihilation, including by God's direct act.
Correction: The Karamiyya only argue that annihilation is impossible through the three specified means; they do not consider direct act as possible because it reduces to those means.

Checkpoint
What are the three possible means of annihilation that the Karamiyya consider and then reject?
A) Annihilation by an agent, by a contrary, or by removal of a condition
B) Annihilation by time, by space, or by motion
C) Annihilation by God's will, by natural decay, or by chance
D) Annihilation by fire, by water, or by air

Key Takeaway
The Karamiyya's proof shows that the world's annihilation is rationally impossible because each possible means leads to an absurdity.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
If you truly believed this world could never cease to exist, would your priorities shift away from the akhira? The Karamiyya argued for its perpetuity—does that thought make you uneasy or complacent? What would it take for your heart to remain attached to Allah even if the world were eternal?
?
The Karamiyya rejected the idea that non-existence can be brought about by an agent. Reflect: have you ever felt that your own actions could 'destroy' something spiritually? How does the rational impossibility of annihilation challenge you to trust more in Allah's power over existence and non-existence?
?
The proof against annihilation hinges on logical absurdities. When you examine your own faith, do you rely more on logical proofs or on direct experience? If a logical argument contradicts a deeply held belief, how do you respond? This lesson demands you submit to reason—are you willing to let go of positions that cannot be rationally defended?
