First proof: the mountain verse with objection and answer
Razi presents the first scriptural proof for the possibility of seeing Allah, refutes an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi uses the mountain verse to argue for the possibility of seeing Allah and how he answers the objection that the condition was impossible.

Core Idea
The mountain verse proves seeing Allah is possible because the condition is possible, not because it actually happened.

In the debate over whether Allah can be seen in the afterlife, scriptural proofs are crucial. Razi's first proof cites the verse where Allah tells Moses, 'If the mountain settles, you will see Me.' The argument hinges on whether the condition is possible. But an objection arises: the mountain was moving, so settling was impossible. How does Razi defend the proof? Let's explore.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation to grasp the flow of proof, objection, and answer.
Notice the logical structure: proof, then 'if it is said' for objection, then 'the answer'.
2
Understand the proof's logic
Identify the core argument: vision is suspended on settling, settling is possible, so vision is possible. Why does Razi consider settling possible?
The verse mentions the mountain's essence, which can be still.
3
Analyze the objection
The objector argues that the condition was impossible because the mountain was moving at the time of speech. Trace how he uses the particle 'in' to claim the condition refers to the future, and that failure of vision implies the mountain remained moving.
The objector asserts no middle state between motion and stillness.
4
Examine Razi's answer
Razi concedes the mountain was moving but distinguishes between the mountain's essence (which can be still) and its accidental state. The verse only mentions the essence, so the condition remains possible.
This answer preserves the proof by focusing on the capacity for stillness, not the actual state.
5

Consider why this distinction matters for the broader debate on seeing Allah. It shows how Kalam scholars defend scriptural arguments against objections by analyzing language and ontology.

Commentary
This passage presents the first scriptural proof for the possibility of seeing Allah, followed by an objection and its answer. The proof is based on the verse where Allah says to Moses, 'If the mountain settles in its place, then you will see Me.' The argument is: Allah suspended vision on the settling of the mountain. Settling is possible (since mountains can be stationary), so that which is suspended on a possible thing is itself possible. Therefore, vision is possible. The objection claims that the condition was actually impossible because at the moment of the speech, the mountain was moving, and settling a moving thing is impossible. The objector argues that the particle 'in' (if) when applied to a past tense verb refers to the future, so 'if it settles' means 'if it becomes settled in the future'. Since vision did not occur (by consensus the mountain did not settle), the mountain must have remained moving, making the condition impossible. The answer distinguishes between the mountain's essence and its temporary state: the verse mentions only the mountain as such, which can be stationary; the impossibility of stillness due to motion is not mentioned in the verse. Thus, the condition remains possible, and the proof stands.

Commentary
This passage presents the opponent's arguments against the possibility of seeing Allah, followed by Razi's answer to the first argument. The opponent raises three proofs: (1) the Quranic verse 'La tudrikuhu al-absar' (No vision can grasp Him). The opponent derives two sub-arguments: (a) the context of the verse is praise, so not being grasped is a perfection; if grasping were possible, it would be a deficiency, which is impossible for Allah. (b) The verse is universal negative, so it negates all instances of grasping; if any instance of vision were true, it would contradict the verse. (2) If Allah were visible, we would see Him now (since there is no obstacle). (3) If visible, He would have to be facing the viewer or in the ruling of facing (like a reflection), which is impossible. Razi answers only the first argument: he affirms the verse but distinguishes 'idrak' from mere 'ru'ya'. 'Idrak' means comprehensive perception from all sides, which is impossible for Allah. But simple vision (ru'ya) does not require such encompassment, so the verse does not negate it. The other two objections are left unanswered in this passage.

Imam Razi presents a common objection raised by those who argue that if there were two wise gods, they would both will the same best action, so no disagreement could occur. Read the objection and reply

Third Argument: Two Conflicting Wills
Imam Razi presents the third argument about conflicting divine and human wills.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Two equal independent powers cannot prefer a single outcome.

Imam Razi presents a logical argument to refute the idea that human will could effectively oppose God's will. He sets up a scenario where the servant wills one thing and God wills the opposite, and shows that no outcome is logically consistent unless one power is superior—which would contradict their assumed equality. This passage is a classic Kalam proof for the Ash'ari position that all acts are created by God.

Guided Path
1
Understand the scenario
Read the scenario: the servant wills to settle a body while God wills to move it. Identify the two opposing wills.
The body is the single object of both wills.
2
Consider the logical outcomes
The passage presents two possibilities: both occur together (impossible because contradictory) or only one occurs. Examine why both occurring is impossible.
Contradictory effects cannot coexist in a real unity.
3
Analyze the equality assumption
Razi assumes the two powers are equal in independence of influence on that single object. Understand why this equality makes one occurring without the other arbitrary.
Without an external factor, no preference can be justified.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal question (mas'ala) proving that God wills all contingent things, in opposition to the Mu'tazila who limit God's will to good acts. Refuting the Obligation of Lutf and 'Iwad
Razi refutes the obligation of lutf and compensation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi refutes the Mu'tazila claims that God is obligated to provide lutf (grace) and compensation for harms.

Core Idea
God can create motives directly; compensation is not obligatory.

The Mu'tazila argue that God must do what is most beneficial for His creatures, including providing lutf (grace that inclines to obedience) and compensating for pains. Razi counters that lutf is not necessary because God can directly create the motive without an intermediary, and compensation is not obligatory because preventing harm does not always incur ugliness.

Refuting Obligations of Reward, Most Beneficial, and Punishment
Razi refutes obligations of reward, most beneficial, and punishment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's arguments against the Mu'tazila obligations of reward, the most beneficial, and punishment.

Core Idea
Reward is not obligatory; most beneficial is subjective; God can waive punishment.

The Mu'tazila also claim that God must reward obedience, always do what is most beneficial, and punish evil. Razi counters that reward is not a necessary compensation, the most beneficial is not universally defined, and punishment is not a required act since God suffers no loss from waiving it.

Commentary
This passage is a complex argument against the Mu'tazila and others who hold that certain things are obligatory upon God. The author, Imam al-Razi, presents the Sunni position that nothing is obligatory upon God. He first states the issue and the opposing views: the Mu'tazila obligate lutf (grace), 'iwad (compensation), and thawab (reward), while the Baghdadis obligate punishment and the most beneficial. Then he gives the Sunni argument: judgments are based only on divine law, and there is no judge over divine law, so nothing is obligatory upon God. He then refutes each claimed obligation in turn. For lutf, he argues that if lutf is a motive that does not compel, God can create that motive directly without the intermediary of lutf, so it is not obligatory. For compensation, if it were obligatory, then preventing harm would be considered ugly, but that is not the case. For reward, God's favors make obedience possible, so reward is not obligatory. For the most beneficial, it is not obligatory because it could involve not creating a disbeliever or creating all in Paradise—these are not necessarily best. For punishment, since God has no benefit or harm from it, waiving it is good. The passage then addresses objections regarding the definition of ugliness and the possibility of lying. The commentator (likely Razi himself) adds a clarification that 'obligation' here is not legal but moral, and then elaborates on the Mu'tazila and others' views on lutf, compensation, reward, and punishment, arguing that all these positions depend on the premise that human acts are created by humans, which Razi rejects. The passage ends by asserting that if that premise is destroyed, all their deductions fall.

Commentary
This passage is a complex argument against the Mu'tazila and others who hold that certain things are obligatory upon God. The author, Imam al-Razi, presents the Sunni position that nothing is obligatory upon God. He first states the issue and the opposing views: the Mu'tazila obligate lutf (grace), 'iwad (compensation), and thawab (reward), while the Baghdadis obligate punishment and the most beneficial. Then he gives the Sunni argument: judgments are based only on divine law, and there is no judge over divine law, so nothing is obligatory upon God. He then refutes each claimed obligation in turn. For lutf, he argues that if lutf is a motive that does not compel, God can create that motive directly without the intermediary of lutf, so it is not obligatory. For compensation, if it were obligatory, then preventing harm would be considered ugly, but that is not the case. For reward, God's favors make obedience possible, so reward is not obligatory. For the most beneficial, it is not obligatory because it could involve not creating a disbeliever or creating all in Paradise—these are not necessarily best. For punishment, since God has no benefit or harm from it, waiving it is good. The passage then addresses objections regarding the definition of ugliness and the possibility of lying. The commentator (likely Razi himself) adds a clarification that 'obligation' here is not legal but moral, and then elaborates on the Mu'tazila and others' views on lutf, compensation, reward, and punishment, arguing that all these positions depend on the premise that human acts are created by humans, which Razi rejects. The passage ends by asserting that if that premise is destroyed, all their deductions fall.

Refuting the Claim that Purposeless Action is Futile
Razi responds to the futility objection by distinguishing meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's response to the objection that an act without purpose is futile.

Core Idea
Calling an act 'futile' just because it lacks purpose is circular reasoning.

The Mu'tazila object that any act without a purpose is futile (عبث), and a wise being does not do futile acts. Al-Razi responds by analyzing the definition of 'futility'. If 'futility' means 'devoid of purpose,' then the objection merely restates the disagreement. If it means something else, it must be clarified. This lesson examines this logical move.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Read the Arabic passage once. Notice that the objection is brief and the response is a logical dissection.
Pay attention to the term عبث (futility).
2
Analyze the meaning of 'futility'
Razi offers two possible interpretations: (1) futility means 'lacking purpose', (2) futility means something else. Understand the implications of each.
3
Understand the logical flaw
If futility simply means 'without purpose', the objection is circular (it assumes what it tries to prove). If it means something else, the opponent must specify.
This is a common technique in kalam: defining terms precisely.

Four Objections to the Possibility of Obligation
Four objections against taklif and the general answer that they demand an unwarranted cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four logical objections raised against the possibility of religious obligation (taklif) and the Sunni response that such objections improperly demand a cause for divine actions.

Core Idea
The four objections to taklif all reduce to demanding a cause, which is not required for God's actions.

Rejecters of obligation raise four dilemmas: divine knowledge necessitates determinism, obligation at equal motives is impossible, timing of command is contradictory, and painful commands lack a proper purpose. Imam Razi dismisses them together as based on an invalid demand for a cause (talab al-‘illiyya).

Guided Path

3
Understand the general answer
The answer is that all objections are based on demanding a cause ('illiyya) for God's actions, which is not required.
This is a key principle in Sunni kalam: divine actions are not subject to external causation.
4
Reflect on the implications

Razi's Argument: God's Actions Have No Cause

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Imam Razi presents a classic argument against causality in divine actions. He reasons that if each act were caused by another, an infinite chain would result, which is impossible. Thus, there must be an ultimate uncaused cause, and that, he asserts, is God's actions themselves.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Names are classified by relation to essence; only revelation permits divine names.

Imam Razi begins by laying out a fundamental classification of names used in kalam. Everything that can be named falls into categories based on its relation to essence. He then asks which of these categories can apply to God's essence. This analysis leads to the conclusion that while many names are logically possible, only those permitted by revelation are actually allowed.

2
Identify the subcategories of external names
Focus on the line where 'external' is subdivided into real, relational, privative, or composite attributes. Understand the difference between a real attribute (like whiteness) and a relational one (like fatherhood).

Read the discussion on whether God's essence can have a name. Note the condition: if His essence is known, then a name is possible; otherwise not. Then note which categories are impossible (part of essence) and which are permissible (other categories).

The final sentence explicitly states that despite the logical possibility of many names, the people of revealed laws (i.e., Muslims) only allow names that have religious authorization. This is a key theological point.
This restricts theological speculation about divine names to scripture and tradition.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Prophetic sin before prophethood is restricted to three exceptional ways and requires repentance.

Razi tackles a delicate theological question: can prophets sin before receiving revelation? He clarifies that any wrongdoing must fall into one of three categories and then surveys the divergent opinions among early sects, from the extreme Fudayliyya to the majority Ahl al-Sunna. This passage is essential for understanding how Islamic orthodoxy balances prophetic infallibility with human fallibility.
