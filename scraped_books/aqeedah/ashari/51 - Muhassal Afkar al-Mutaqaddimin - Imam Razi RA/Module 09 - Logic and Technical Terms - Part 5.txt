Equivocal Predication and Its Logical Implications
Razi explains that equivocal predication does not necessitate equal implications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why equivocal predication (tashkik) does not require equal implications (lawazim) and how this principle answers a key objection in debates about knowledge and the attribute of being knowing.

Core Idea
Equivocal terms differ in implications; sameness in name is not sameness in effect.

This passage from Imam al-Razi clarifies a crucial logical point: when a term is applied to different things equivocally, it does not force all implications to be the same. This principle is used to counter a specific argument about the multiplicity of knowledge and the attribute of being knowing (al-alamiyya). Follow along as Razi untangles this debate.

Guided Path
1
Understand equivocal predication
Read the first part where Razi states that what is predicated equivocally does not necessitate equality in implications. Reflect on what 'equivocal' means in this context.
Equivocal predication means a term is used with different meanings in different contexts.
2
Distinguish between equivocal and similar things
Notice the contrast: similar things (mutamathila) share implications, while equivocal things do not.
3
Follow the answer to the sixth objection
Razi provides a compelling answer: the proof that makes knowledge vary with known objects also makes the attribute of being knowing vary, which is impossible. This answer targets the views of Abu Hashim and Abu Ali.
This is a specific theological context about whether knowledge is superadded to the essence.
4
Grasp the refutation of the six doubts
Understand that the counter-argument by the attribute of al-alamiyya refutes all six doubts raised by those who deny that knowledge is superadded.

In contrast, similar things (mutamathila) share implications. He then gives the answer to a sixth argument (likely a previous objection) about the multiplicity of knowledge and the attribute of being knowing (al-alamiyya). The answer is that the proof requiring knowledge to differ with objects also requires the attribute of being knowing to differ, which is impossible. They raised a compelling argument about the multiplicity of sciences against those who say knowledge is superadded, but the counter-argument using equivocation refutes their six doubts.

The Multiplicity of Speech Styles
Editorial note criticizing reduction of speech to information.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why reducing all speech to information (khabar) is problematic and how primary and secondary indications differ.

Core Idea
Not all speech is information; primary and secondary indication differ.

This editorial note challenges Razi's view that all divine speech can be reduced to information. The commentator argues that styles of speech are many, and their common referent can be one eternal attribute, but the reduction to khabar fails because khabar necessarily involves mentioning the informant, which commands do not. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping the true diversity of divine speech.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once and then its translation. Notice how the commentator introduces his objection.
The passage is dense; read slowly.
2
Identify the two types of indication
The passage mentions 'primary indication' (madlul bi al-dhat) and 'secondary indication' (madlul bi al-'ard). Notice how the commentator uses this distinction to argue against reducing all speech to information.
Primary indication is what the speech directly signifies; secondary is what is implied.
3
Understand the objection to making all speech information
The commentator says information (khabar) is not the reality of the attribute because it is composed of mentioning the informant. Commands and prohibitions do not have that composition, so they cannot be information in the same way.
4
See why command and prohibition are not information
The commentator argues that the claim that command and prohibition are information about reward and punishment is flawed because that would be a secondary indication, not a primary one.
The primary indication of a command is the demand itself, not a report.
5
Reflect on the broader implications
Consider how this distinction preserves the diversity of divine speech styles while still allowing them to refer to a single eternal attribute. Why is this important for theology?
It avoids oversimplification of divine attributes.

Commentary
This editorial note criticizes Razi's reduction of all speech to information. The commentator points out that the styles (uslub) of speech are not limited to five categories; they are many. However, the referent (madlul) of all these styles can be a single eternal attribute. He argues that there is no point in making all speech merely 'information' (khabar), because information is not the essence of the attribute—it necessarily involves mentioning the one informed about. The fact that the eternal attribute can be indicated by different styles, including commands and prohibitions, does not mean they are identical to information. The primary indication (madlul bi al-dhat) is different from the secondary indication (madlul bi al-'ard). Thus Razi's argument that command and prohibition are information about reward and punishment is flawed because that is a secondary indication.

Razi argues for the oneness of eternal speech and defines its reality as report.

The Real Dispute About Seeing Allah
Imam Razi states the issue of ru'yat Allah and clarifies the point of dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari position on ru'yat Allah and what the true disagreement is about.

Core Idea
The dispute about seeing Allah is not about physical vision or necessary knowledge, but about a non-physical state called ru'ya.

Imam Razi begins by stating the Ash'ari thesis that Allah can be seen. He immediately distinguishes his view from those who deny vision entirely (philosophers, Mu'tazila) and those who affirm it only because they believe Allah has a direction (Mushabbiha, Karamiyya). To avoid confusion, he then clarifies what is really being debated: it is not about physical sight or about full knowledge, but about a distinct state that we perceive when we see something after not seeing it, which is not reducible to physical processes. This passage sets the stage for the proofs to follow.

Guided Path
1
Understand the thesis and the sectarian landscape
Read the opening line and the breakdown of opposing sects. Notice who affirms and who denies ru'ya, and why the Ash'aris are unique in affirming a vision free from modality and direction.
The phrases 'al-ru'ya al-munazzaha 'an al-kayfiyya' and 'contrary to all the sects' are key.
2
Analyze the objection that could be raised
The opponent proposes three possible meanings of 'vision'. List them and understand why the first two are not the point of dispute.
The first meaning (full disclosure) is conceded; the second (physical vision) is impossible for Allah.
3
Grasp the response that identifies the real meaning
Imam Razi argues that there is a known distinction between not seeing and seeing, and this distinction is not physical. That state is called ru'ya. This is what we claim can be attributed to Allah.
The argument uses the empirical fact that we perceive a tafarruqa (distinction) that is not reducible to irtisam or shu'a'.
4
Connect to the broader kalam context
Note how this argument uses 'conception precedes assent' (al-tasawwur qabl al-tasdiq) and the empirical basis of distinguishing states. This is a method of kalam.

Issue: Allah the Exalted may be seen, contrary to all the sects. As for the philosophers (falāsifa) and the Muʿtazila, there is no difficulty in their opposition. As for the Mushabbiha and the Karāmiyya, they only permitted seeing Him because of their belief that He the Exalted is in a place and direction; but on the supposition that He the Exalted is transcendent of direction, they forbid seeing Him. So it is established that this vision free from modality (al-ruʾya al-munazzaha ʿan al-kayfiyya) is not affirmed by anyone except our companions (aṣḥābinā). And you have known that that distinction cannot be referred to the imprinting of the phantom (shabah) in the eye nor to the emission of the ray from it, so it refers to another state called vision (al-ruʾya). So we claim that the attachment of this attribute to the essence of Allah the Exalted is permissible. This is the investigation of the point of dispute.

Commentary
This passage defines the issue of whether Allah can be seen (ruʾyat Allāh) and clarifies the precise point of dispute. The author states the thesis: Allah the Exalted can be seen, contrary to all other sects. He then categorizes the opponents: the philosophers and Muʿtazila reject vision absolutely; the Mushabbiha and Karāmiyya affirm it but only because they believe Allah is in a place and direction, so they do not accept a vision free from modality. Thus only the Ashʿarīs (our companions) affirm vision without modality or direction. Before giving proofs, the author delineates the point of dispute (maḥall al-nizāʿ) to avoid misunderstanding. An opponent could object: 'Vision' could mean three things: (1) full disclosure (kashf tāmm) – but that is conceded because on the Day of Judgment cognitions become necessary. (2) the ordinary state of seeing bodies, which involves physical processes like imprinting an image or emitting a ray – that is impossible for Allah. (3) a third meaning – but then we need its conception to discuss it. The answer is that we know a distinction between not seeing and seeing something, and this distinction cannot be reduced to physical processes, so it must be a separate state called 'vision' (ruʾya). We claim this state can be attributed to Allah's essence. This is the real dispute, not about physical vision or necessary knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of a position that tries to avoid both human independence and pure compulsion. Razi begins with a logical disjunction: either the servant is independent in causing something to exist, or he is not. There is no middle ground. If the servant is independent, that is the Mu'tazilite position (which is rejected). If he is not independent, then the servant is compelled, and all the problems of compulsion return. Then Razi addresses the theory of kasb (acquisition), calling it a name without a real referent. He examines a statement that the servant's choice causes the act: but then the question is whether that choice itself occurs by the servant's power or not. If yes, it's the opponent's view; if no, it doesn't solve the problem. Then he considers an argument that obedience and disobedience are attributes of the act occurring by the servant's power, while the essence of the act is by God's power. Razi counters that this acknowledges that the originated power has an effect, which is exactly the Mu'tazilite position.

Philosophers' Proof: The First Emanation is a Pure Intellect
Razi presents the philosophers' proof that the first emanation is a pure intellect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' deductive argument that the first creation must be a simple intellect, not a body, accident, or soul.

Core Idea
The first effect must be a pure intellect because all other options lead to contradiction.

This passage presents a philosophical argument from the principle 'from the one only one proceeds.' The philosophers attempt to deduce the nature of the first creation through a process of elimination: it cannot be an accident, a body, matter, form, or a soul, so it must be a pure intellect. Follow the logical steps carefully.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the main claim
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that the philosophers start with a premise and then eliminate possibilities to conclude that the first effect is a pure intellect.
2
Trace the elimination process
Identify each step: first it is determined to be a substance, not an accident; then not extended; then not matter or form; then not a soul.
Note the use of disjunctive syllogism.
3
Understand why each option is impossible
For each excluded option, understand the reasoning: accident leads to circularity, body implies composition, matter would be both agent and receptive, form would be independent of matter, soul acts through bodies.
The key is the principle that the first cause must be simple and independent.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The conclusion is that the first creation is a pure intellect, known as the 'First Intellect' in philosophical cosmology.
This is a famous proof in Islamic philosophy.

Commentary

So the first effect is neither extended nor matter nor form; it is a simple substance. It is not a soul because a soul's acts are through bodies, but the first effect is the cause of all bodies, so it is a pure intellect.

Names reveal realities; God's essence is simple, so no name can indicate a part of it.

Commentary
This passage from the fourth section discusses the classification of names and their application to God. The Body Perceives Both Particulars and Universals: The Proof
Razi presents the two-argument proof that the body perceives both particulars and universals, along with a refutation of the opposing view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two arguments used by the trustworthy to prove that the body perceives both particulars and universals, along with the refutation of opposing views.

Core Idea
The body perceives universals because it perceives particulars, and the universal is part of the particular.

This passage presents a proof (ḥujjat al-thiqāt) that the body, not a separate soul, is the perceiver of both particulars and universals. It begins with a self-evident sensory example and then offers two logical arguments. The first ties the perception of universals to the perception of particulars through the act of predication, while the second draws on the part-whole relationship between universals and particulars. An objection and its refutation are also included.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening premise
The proof starts by stating that the perceiver of particulars is the body, a fact known necessarily from sensation. Notice how it uses the example of sensing heat with a finger.
This establishes the foundation.
2
Understand the first argument
The first argument shows that when we perceive a particular heat, we can predicate the universal heat of it, which requires perceiving both. Since the body perceives the particular, it must also perceive the universal.
Pay attention to the idea that assent (taṣdīq) is preceded by conceptualization (taṣawwur).
3
Understand the second argument
The second argument uses the part-whole relation: the universal (e.g., man) is a part of the particular (e.g., this man). Perceiving the composite implies perceiving the simple, so perceiving this man means perceiving man, which is universal.
This refutes the opponent who might claim only the particular's individual determination is perceived.
4
Study the refutation of the objection
The opponent might say the body perceives only particulars, and the soul perceives both. The argument refutes this by showing it would lead to redundant perception of particulars. Also, the attempt to limit perception to the individual determination ('this-ness') is refuted by two sub-arguments.
Understand why determination cannot be an existential attribute and why it cannot be the sole object of sense.

The Proof That Human Souls Are One Species
Aristotle's proof for the unity of the human soul species.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the core proof given by Aristotle and his followers for the unity of human souls, based on the principle that difference in essence would require composition and thus corporeality.

Core Idea
If souls differed in essence, they would be composite; but every composite is a body, so souls must be one species.

This lesson presents the foundational argument that human souls are of one species, as held by Aristotle and his school. The proof relies on a logical principle: if two souls shared the definition of 'human soul' but differed in their specific essence, they would be composite (having a shared element and a distinguishing element). Since every composite is a body, and souls are not bodies, they must be identical in species. Understanding this argument is key to grasping the broader kalam debate on the nature of the soul.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Note the compressed style; the premise 'every composite is a body' is stated without justification here.
2
Identify the logical steps
Break down the argument: (1) Doctrine stated, (2) If souls differed in essence while sharing the definition, they would be composite, (3) Because the shared element is distinct from the distinguishing element, (4) Every composite is a body, (5) Therefore the soul would be a body, which is false, so souls must be one species.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
Ask yourself: why is it important that souls are not bodies? This connects to broader discussions about the soul's immateriality.
The conclusion is that all human souls are of the same species.

Objection and Refutation to the Unity Argument
An objection and refutation of the unity argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection raised against the unity argument—that being a human soul might be accidental—and the response that composition does not necessarily imply being a body.

Core Idea
Being a human soul may be an accident, and composition does not always mean a body.

The previous lesson presented the argument that souls are one species, based on the idea that difference would lead to composition and then to body. In this lesson, we encounter an objection that challenges two key points: first, that 'being a human soul' might be an accidental attribute (like being a manager of bodies), so souls could differ in essence while sharing that accident; second, that not every composite is a body, because even according to Aristotle, bodies are composed of matter and form, and souls fall under the genus of substance without being bodies. Understanding this objection is crucial to see how the debate unfolds.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection passage
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. Note the two parts: the alternative explanation of 'being a human soul' as accidental, and the challenge to 'every composite is a body'.
The objection uses terms like 'عوارض' (accidents) and 'الهيولى والصورة' (matter and form).
2
Analyze the first objection
The objector suggests that 'being a human soul' might mean 'managing human bodies', which is an accident. So souls could differ in essence while sharing that accidental function, like two opposites that share the property of being opposites.
This undermines the premise that sharing the definition implies sharing the genus.
3
Analyze the second objection
The objector then questions why every composite must be a body. According to Aristotle, bodies are composed of matter and form, but souls are not composed in that way; they fall under the genus of substance, which is not a body.
The objection also notes that if souls fall under a genus, they would be composite in definition (genus + differentia), but that does not make them bodies.

Alternative View: Souls Differ in Quiddity
An argument that souls differ in quiddity due to moral/intellectual differences, labeled persuasive.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Differences in morality and intellect suggest different soul essences, but Razi says this argument is only persuasive.

Razi's editorial corrections and defense of the unity argument.

And they argued that if they differed in essence [?] after sharing in being human souls, they would be composite, because that by which they share is other than that by which they are distinguished, and every composite is a body, so it is a body. Rather your doctrine is that the body is composed of matter and form, but the universal affirmative proposition [؟] is not convertible as itself. And how? According to them, substance is the genus of souls and intellects, and everything that falls under a genus has its quiddity composed of genus and differentia. And among them there is one who claimed that they differ in quiddities, and they argued that they differ in chastity and licentiousness, intelligence and stupidity, and that is not among the consequences of temperament, because a person may have a cold temperament and be extremely intelligent, and conversely, and temperament may change while the psychical attribute remains, nor is it due to external causes because they may be such as to require a disposition while the result is opposite. So we know it is among the necessary concomitants of the soul, and difference of concomitants indicates difference of the things to which they are attached. This argument is persuasive. I say [Razi]: He mentioned in places that those who affirm the soul say that the perceiver of particulars is other than the perceiver of universals, and that is a slander against those who affirm the soul, and the argument is based on that. The entirety of his words in this argument is confused, of no benefit, and does not apply to any rational person. I say [Razi]: Their argument that human souls are of one species is that one definition includes them both, and that is sufficient. As for the claim that every composite is a body, if they mean by it intellectual composition, it is not so, and what is composed of genus and differentia is not a body as he mentioned. Commentary
This passage is a combined argument and refutation regarding the unity of human souls. It begins by stating Aristotle's doctrine that all human souls are of the same species. The proof given is that if souls differed in essence while sharing the definition of being human souls, they would be composite, because the factor of sharing is distinct from the factor of distinction, and every composite is a body. The objection questions this, suggesting that 'being human souls' might be an accidental attribute (managing bodies), so difference in essence could coexist with sharing in that accidental attribute, just as opposites share in opposition. The objector also challenges the premise that every composite is a body, noting that matter-form composition (as per Aristotle) does not make a body, and that according to the Aristotelians, substance is the genus of souls, so souls are not bodies. Then the passage introduces another group who argue that souls differ in quiddity based on differences in ethical and intellectual qualities, claiming these are not from temperament or external causes but are necessary concomitants of the soul, so their difference indicates difference in essence. Razi labels this argument as merely persuasive. In his editorial comments, Razi first criticizes a misrepresentation of those who affirm the soul, clarifying that the soul perceives both particulars and universals, but particulars through instruments. He then defends the original argument for unity of species, stating that a single definition suffices, and that composition (if understood intellectually or from substance or accidents) does not necessarily imply being a body. Thus, the argument for unity of species stands firm.

The Proof for the Creation of the Soul
Razi gives a disjunctive proof for the soul's creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof that the soul is created, based on the alternatives of oneness or multiplicity.

Core Idea
If the soul were eternal, it would either be one or many—both lead to contradiction.

Razi presents the argument that the soul cannot be eternal. He examines two possibilities: that the soul is one or that souls are many. Both possibilities lead to absurdities, thereby proving that the soul must have been created in time.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and translation, noting the structure of a disjunctive proof.
2
Identify the two horns
The proof considers two cases: the soul is one or many. For each, find the contradiction.
3
Understand the critique of oneness
If the soul is one, attaching to many bodies leads either to universal knowledge or to division—both impossible.
4
Understand the critique of multiplicity
If souls are many, they must be distinguished by essence or accident—both impossible before embodiment.

Question: Aristotle and his followers claimed that the soul is created, contrary to Plato and those before him. The proof of those who affirm creation is that if it were eternal, it would either be one or many. If it is one, then upon attachment to bodies, if it remains one, then everything known by one would be known by every one and vice versa, which is absurd; or it does not remain one, so it has divided, and that is impossible, because the two identities that we have after division, if they existed before, then multiplicity existed before its occurrence, which is absurd; and if we say that they were existing and have now occurred [?], then two souls have now occurred, and the soul that existed before has perished [؟]. And if it is many, then distinction is necessary by something, which is either by essential attributes or their concomitants, and both are impossible because human souls are of one species. And if not all are of one species, then at least from each species there would be two individuals. As for distinction by accidents, it is impossible because distinction by accidents only occurs when there is diversity of matter, and before the body there is no matter, so distinction by accidents does not occur. The objection: We do not concede that two souls exist from one type, and its explanation has passed. We grant, but why do you say that distinction must be by something additional? Its explanation has passed. We grant, but why may it not be distinction by accidents? His statement: Before this body there is no matter. We say: We do not concede; why may it not be that before its attachment to this body it was attached to another body, and then transferred from it to this by way of metempsychosis?

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the creation of the soul, presented in a dialectical form. It begins by stating the doctrine that the soul is created, contrary to Plato. The proof proceeds by disjunction: if the soul were eternal, it would be either one or many. If it is one, then upon attaching to many bodies, either it remains one (which would entail that all bodies know the same things, which is false) or it divides (which is impossible because that would imply multiplicity before division or annihilation and recreation). If it is many, then we need a principle of individuation. This cannot be by essential attributes because all souls share the same species definition, nor by accidents because accidents require matter, and before the body there is no matter. The objector then challenges: first, he denies that two souls of the same type can exist (i.e., he denies the premise that souls are of one species). Then he concedes but questions why distinction must be by something additional. Then he concedes again but raises the possibility of distinction by accidents, and when told that before the body there is no matter, he suggests that the soul might have been attached to another body before (transmigration). The passage thus presents a sophisticated dialectic, and the arguments are left partly unresolved in this segment, hinting at further discussion.

Razi's First Argument Against Transmigration
Razi gives the first reason against transmigration and responds to an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's first argument against transmigration based on the soul's creation and how he refutes the objection of circularity and difference in quiddity.

Core Idea
Transmigration leads to two souls in one body, which is impossible.

Razi presents the first of several reasons why transmigration (tanasukh) is impossible for those who affirm the soul's creation. He argues that the soul's origination depends on bodily readiness, so a body receiving a soul via transmigration would require a new soul's origination, resulting in two souls. He then anticipates and responds to an objection that the argument is circular.

Guided Path
1
Read Razi's main argument
Carefully read the first part of the passage where Razi presents the argument that the soul's creation depends on bodily readiness, and how transmigration would lead to two souls in one body.
Notice the logical flow: soul is created → depends on condition (body) → body's readiness causes emanation → transmigration implies two souls.
2
Understand the objection
Read the objection that the argument is circular because the proof for the soul's creation itself relies on rejecting transmigration. Also consider the objection that souls might differ in quiddity.
The objector tries to break the argument by challenging its premises.
3
See Razi's response
Razi responds by asserting that even if souls are of one species, multiplicity is impossible because they would be equal. Thus the argument stands without needing to refute transmigration.
Razi strengthens the original argument by showing the objection fails under the agreed assumption.
4
Reflect on the dialectic
Consider why this argument is considered decisive and how it fits into the broader discussion of transmigration in kalam.
The argument does not rely on disproving transmigration beforehand; it is independent.

So either the total of ten is nullified—which is oppression—or nothing of it is nullified—which is what is desired. The fourth: If he becomes entitled to ten portions of reward, then does an act by which he becomes entitled to ten portions of punishment, then the later (entitlement) either nullifies the first and is not itself nullified, as Abu Ali holds, or it nullifies and is nullified, as Abu Hashim holds in the balancing (al-muwāzana). The first is invalid because it makes the former act of obedience purely futile, with no effect in bringing benefit or repelling harm, and it is false in light of His statement, 'Whoever does an atom's weight of good will see it' (Quran 99:7). The second is invalid because the cause of the removal of the first entitlement is the occurrence of the second entitlement; if the second entitlement does not exist, the first does not cease; and if the second entitlement exists and the first ceases by it, it is impossible for this second entitlement to cease because it has no remover, so it becomes the first position which was Abu Ali's doctrine, and we have disproved it. It remains to be said: each of the two entitlements is removed by the other at once, but this is impossible because the cause of the non-existence of each is the existence of the other; if both were non-existent at once, they would both exist at once. But the cause exists at the moment of the effect's occurrence, so they would both exist at the moment they are non-existent—this is a contradiction. These are proofs indicating the corruption of their doctrine of nullification, and when that is established, the cessation of punishment is established.

Commentary
This passage presents three arguments against the Mu'tazilite theory of habt (nullification of reward and punishment). The second argument assumes reward and punishment are opposites; if so, the later entitlement requires removal of the prior, leading to circularity. The third argument addresses a scenario of unequal portions: if one deserves 10 reward and 5 punishment, nullifying only part of the reward is arbitrary, so either all is nullified (unjust) or none (the desired conclusion). The fourth argument examines equal portions: Abu Ali's view (only previous nullified) makes good deeds futile, contradicting Quran 99:7; Abu Hashim's balancing view (both nullify each other) fails because the later entitlement has no remover, reverting to Abu Ali's discredited position. Finally, concurrent mutual nullification is impossible as it would require both to exist and not exist simultaneously. The conclusion: habt is false, and punishment can cease.

Learning Objective

Fourth Argument Against Habt: Mutual Nullification Impossibility
Al-Razi presents the fourth argument: refutation of two Mu'tazilite views and impossibility of mutual nullification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Razi's critique of both Abu Ali's and Abu Hashim's positions on mutual nullification, and why simultaneous nullification is logically impossible.

Core Idea
Neither one-sided nor mutual nullification avoids logical contradiction.

In this final argument, Al-Razi examines the case of equal portions. He refutes Abu Ali's view (only prior nullified) as making good deeds futile, and Abu Hashim's balancing view (both nullified) as leading to an irremovable later entitlement. He also shows that simultaneous mutual nullification is impossible.
