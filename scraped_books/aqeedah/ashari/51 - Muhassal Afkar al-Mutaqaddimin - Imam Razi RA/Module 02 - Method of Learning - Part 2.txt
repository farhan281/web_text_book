Razi concludes with remarks on existence and dismisses further debate.

Commentary
This is an editorial note, likely by Razi himself, commenting on the previous reports. First, he clarifies that the philosophers' view that quiddities are 'not unknown' does not mean they are uncreated; rather, quiddity's self-identity is not due to a maker but is a necessary consequence of supposing that quiddity. Then he criticizes the Mu'tazila: they claim the agent's effect is not to make essences essences, yet they treat non-existent essences as eternally established without any agent, which is problematic. To avoid all essences being identical, they introduced genus-attributes. Then he lists the ten life-dependent accidents (beliefs, opinions, etc.) and notes that Abu 'Ali adds death. He defines spatial occupation (tahayyuz) as a substance-specific attribute requiring a place, and distinguishes it from the 'kainiyya' (the actual being in a state like motion or rest). He concludes that the arguments on these topics are many but of little benefit, and he will not delve into them. This note shows Razi's critical and sometimes dismissive attitude towards excessive dialectic.

Objection: The Principle is Not Self-Evident
The objector argues that the principle of preponderator is not self-evident and demands a proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objector's challenge to the self-evidence of the principle of preponderator and how it sets the stage for further debate.

Core Idea
Is the principle of preponderator self-evident? The objector says no.

We have encountered a proof that relies on the principle 'when two sides are equal, preponderance requires a preponderator.' Now an objector questions the epistemic status of this very principle. Is it self-evident? Or does it require demonstration? This lesson presents that objection and the concessive reply that leads to further objections.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two claims the objector denies
The objector first denies that the principle is self-evident, then denies that it is demonstrative (unless a proof is provided). Notice the structure: 'if you claim it is self-evident... if you claim it is demonstrative...'
The objector does not outright reject the principle, but questions its epistemic foundation.
2
Understand the comparison with a more obvious truth
The objector compares the principle to the statement 'one is half of two' and finds the latter more certain. This difference indicates that the principle is not as obvious as claimed.
This is a standard method in kalam: comparing propositions to test self-evidence.
3
Note the concessive phrase 'we accept'
After presenting the challenges, the objector says 'we accept the correctness of what you mentioned' (sana). This shows he does not reject the principle outright but is about to oppose it with several counterarguments.
The word 'sana' (or 'sanna') is critical: it signals a concessive move before raising further objections.

Foundations: Mode, Substrate, and the Primary Division
Razi defines mode, substrate, subject, and divides possible into accident and substance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definitions of mode (hal), substrate (mahall), and subject (mawdu‘), and how possible things are primarily divided into accident and substance.

Core Idea
Everything possible is either an accident in a subject or a substance without one.

Before classifying possible things, the philosophers establish key terms: mode, substrate, and subject. They explain how a mode can sustain its substrate without leading to an infinite regress. This foundation clarifies that accidents reside in subjects, while substances exist independently.

We say: A mode (al-hal) may be a cause for the subsistence of a substrate (al-mahall) either by the mode necessitating the existence of the substrate, then itself becoming a mode in it, or by the effect necessitating the indwelling of its cause in it. On both suppositions, [infinite] regress does not follow. The substrate that is self-subsistent and that sustains what subsists in it is called the subject (al-mawdu‘), and it is more specific than substrate, so its non-existence is more general than the non-existence of the substrate.

I say: The substrate is receptive to the mode, so according to them it is not an agent in it. The statement that the effect necessitates the indwelling of its cause in it is accepted by them. What is meant here by the mode that is a cause for the subsistence of the substrate is the form (al-sura), and by the substrate is the matter (al-hayula). They intend by this explanation that the impossibility of separation between them due to each needing the other does not entail regress. And the mode that does not sustain its substrate is the accident and its substrate is the subject.

When you understand this, we say: The possible either exists in a subject – and it is an accident – or does not – and it is a substance. Substance either is in a substrate – and it is form – or is a substrate – and it is matter – or is composed of form and matter – and it is body, only by induction – or is neither a mode nor a substrate nor composed of them: either related to bodies with the relation of governance – and it is soul – or not – and it is intellect.

I say: Regarding his statement "or composed of form and matter, and it is body, only by induction", there is a consideration. The philosophers do not use induction here nor need it. Rather, they divide existents into body and its parts, and into what is neither body nor parts; this is a present division. They call the first division material and the second division separate. They take the first to the matter itself, to what sustains it, and to what is sustained by it. The first is matter, the second is form – both are parts of body – and the third is body. As for the separate, either it acts in material things or does not act; they are soul and intellect. Names of types of quality (al-kayf): The first type is called affectivities and passions; the first are stable like the redness of blood, the second are unstable like the redness of shyness. The second type is called state and disposition; state is quickly vanishing like the anger of a mild person, disposition is slowly vanishing like the health of the healthy. The third type is called power and non-power; the name 'power' is not only for repulsion, for hardness is a power – an aptitude not to be quickly affected – and slowness is non-power, not being affected by something but rather that which cuts off having little motion. So 'power' is a name for a relative preparedness to do a thing easily or be affected with difficulty; 'non-power' is a name for a relative preparedness to do with difficulty or be affected easily. The fourth type has no name other than what was mentioned.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of possible things (tawjih al-mumkinat) according to the philosophers. It begins with a definition of 'mode' (al-hal) as something that may cause the subsistence of a substrate. The author distinguishes between two ways: the mode necessitates the existence of the substrate and then becomes a mode in it, or the effect necessitates the indwelling of its cause. In both cases, no infinite regress follows. Then the terms 'subject' (al-mawdu‘) and 'substrate' (al-mahall) are defined: a substrate that is self-subsistent and sustains what subsists in it is called subject. The marginal note clarifies that the substrate is receptive, not an agent, and identifies the mode as form and the substrate as matter in this context. The main classification then divides possible into accident (exists in a subject) and substance (does not exist in a subject). Substance is further divided into form (in a substrate), matter (is a substrate), body (composed of form and matter), soul (related to bodies by governance), and intellect (not related). The marginal note on page 88 criticizes the use of 'by induction' (bil-istiqra’) in the body definition, stating that philosophers prefer a different division. The note then digresses into a classification of qualities (kayfiyyat) into four types: affective qualities (stable and unstable), states and dispositions, powers and non-powers, and quantities-specific qualities. This quality classification seems to be an explanatory comment.

We say: A mode (al-hal) may be a cause for the subsistence of a substrate (al-mahall) either by the mode necessitating the existence of the substrate, then itself becoming a mode in it, or by the effect necessitating the indwelling of its cause in it. As for proving the impossibility of an infinite in existence by the argument of matching (mutābaqa) as they say in the philosophical books, it is not valid. The proof is that one takes from the infinite a finite part and imagines matching the remainder with the whole before the subtraction, and it is said that one of the two sets must be less than the other by a finite number, so the two sets are infinite, as previously explained. But it is not valid by what we say about temporal events; it is valid by something like what came before: that from the world's director ad infinitum there is an infinite series of causes, all existing and ordered; the two sets (causes and effects) are matched in external existence without needing imagination and are equal on the side that is upon the world; and it is necessary that the set of causes exceed the set of effects by one cause on the other side supposed infinite; that entails the effects ending before the causes end, necessitating their finitude while they are supposed infinite—a contradiction. So if the [causes] are infinite, it is invalid, so the [series] is invalid.

Handling Apparent Anthropomorphic Verses
Imam Razi explains the two valid approaches to anthropomorphic verses.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Apparent anthropomorphic verses must be understood in harmony with definitive reason, either by consigning the modality to God or by interpretation.

The Imamate: Critique of Rational Proof and Sunni Alternative
Critique of rational proof for imamate and presentation of Sunni scriptural-consensus proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the rational proof for the imamate is problematic within the Ash'ari framework and how the Sunni school grounds the imamate in scripture and communal consensus.

Core Idea
The imamate is established by divine command and community consensus, not rational necessity.

Imam al-Razi presented a proof for the necessity of the imamate that mixes rational and scriptural elements. However, as this passage shows, the rational part conflicts with Ash'ari theology. The commentator then points to a stronger, purely scriptural and historical argument preferred by the Ahl al-Sunna. This lesson unpacks both the critique and the alternative proof.
