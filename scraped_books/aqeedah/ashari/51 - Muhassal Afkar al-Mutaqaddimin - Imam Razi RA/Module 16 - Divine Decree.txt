Razi's Objection: All Obligations Are Impossible Under Determinism
Razi presents an objection against the principle that necessary means are obligatory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi objects to the principle that necessary means are obligatory by arguing that it leads to the claim that all obligations are impossible under divine determinism.

Core Idea
Razi's objection turns the principle of necessary means into a deterministic critique of all obligations.

In this passage, Razi presents a sharp objection against a key Ash'ari principle: that anything necessary to fulfill an obligation becomes obligatory itself. He argues that this principle, if accepted, would force us to say that all obligations are impossible—because under divine determinism, everything is either necessary (if God knows it will happen) or impossible (if He knows it won't). This objection challenges the very foundation of obligation.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage once to get a sense of its flow. Note the structure: it begins with 'قلت: سلمنا' indicating a concession, then a challenge with 'لكن لم قلتم'.
The text is short but dense; take your time with each phrase.
2
Identify the two moves
First, the opponent's claim: if the means is not obligatory, the command becomes impossible (taklīf bi-mā lā yuṭāq). Second, Razi's counter: all obligations are like that because of divine determinism.
The word 'بأسرها' means 'all of them'.
3
Reflect on the implication
Consider how Razi's objection undermines the principle. If all obligations are equally impossible under determinism, then singling out the means as problematic becomes moot. This leads to deeper issues about qadar and free will.

The objector concedes the previous point but challenges this principle. The argument is: if fulfilling an obligation requires some means, and that means is not obligatory, then the command would be impossible (since the means might not be done). But the objector counters by asking why that would be impossible—perhaps obligations are impossible anyway under divine determinism, because whatever God knows will occur is necessary, and whatever He knows will not occur is impossible. Thus, all obligations are effectively impossible, so the objection against the means is not special.

Intrinsic Incompatibility of Contradictory Beliefs
Razi argues that the mutual exclusion of contradictory beliefs is due to their own nature, not an external deterrent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between intrinsic and extrinsic impossibility of holding contradictory beliefs, and why certain assent excludes its opposite by nature.

Core Idea
Contradictory certain beliefs cannot coexist because certainty requires the absence of any opposite possibility.

Razi addresses a key question in epistemology: why can't we hold two contradictory beliefs at once? Is it because the beliefs themselves repel each other, or because something external prevents it? In this passage, he argues that for certain knowledge, the mutual exclusion is intrinsic to the nature of certainty. For lesser degrees of belief, an external deterrent may be at work.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage carefully along with the literal translation. Notice the structure: a question, an answer, and a clarification.
The passage is compact; read it twice to absorb the argument.
2
Identify the two positions and Razi's preference
The passage presents two views: (1) impossibility is intrinsic to the beliefs themselves, (2) impossibility is due to an external deterrent. Razi states the more likely view is intrinsic. Find his proof.
The proof is based on the condition of certain assent.
3
Understand the distinction between certain and non-certain beliefs
The comment clarifies that certain (yaqini) assent has intrinsic incompatibility, while non-certain belief (e.g., of a muqallid) is impossible only due to a deterrent. Compare the two cases.
This distinction resolves the apparent disagreement between the two views.

Commentary
This question (mas'alah) addresses the nature of the impossibility of holding two contradictory beliefs. Some say the impossibility is due to the beliefs themselves (linafsihima), while others say it is due to an external deterrent (sarif). Razi states the more likely view is that the incompatibility is intrinsic (dhatiyya). The proof: certain assent (jasm bi al-thubut) requires that the opposite has no possibility (ihtimal); without this condition, it cannot be realized. Hence, the impossibility is intrinsic. The comment clarifies that 'jasm' here means certain (yaqini) assent; belief (i'tiqad) is broader, covering non-certain beliefs like those of a blind follower (muqallid). For non-certain beliefs, the impossibility of coexistence is due to a deterrent, not intrinsically. But for certain assent, the mutual exclusion is intrinsic because the condition of certainty excludes any possibility of the opposite.

The Timing of Choice: A Dilemma for the Mu'tazila
Razi questions the Mu'tazila on the timing of choice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that the Mu'tazilite notion of choice leads to a paradox regarding when choice occurs.

Core Idea
Choice at equal motives is impossible; at preponderant motives, it is necessary — either way, no real choice.

The Mu'tazila famously upheld free choice, but Razi challenges them with a simple question: when exactly does choice occur? In this passage, he presents a dilemma that seems to undermine their entire framework. By examining both possibilities, you will see why the Mu'tazilite concept of choice faces a logical dead end.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the literal translation. Let the structure of the argument sink in.
Notice the two-part structure: first option then second option.
2
Identify the two options
Razi asks: when is choice established? Two answers are considered: equality of motives or preponderance of one over the other. Identify both in the text.
These are the two horns of the dilemma.
3
Understand why each option fails
For each option, note the reason given. Equal motives make the act impossible; preponderance makes the act necessary. In both cases, capability (mikna) cannot be affirmed.
This is the core critique.
4
Reflect on the implications
Think about what this means for the Mu'tazilite notion of free choice. If choice cannot be coherently placed at either moment, what does that suggest?
Razi's argument points to the necessity of divine determination.

ويقال للمعتزلة متى ثبت هذا الاختيار عند استواء الدواعي أو عند رجحان أحدهما على الآخر ، الأول باطل، لأن عند الاستواء يمتنع الفعل وعند الامتناع لا تثبت المكنة ، والثانى محال ، لأن عند حصول المرجح يجب الراجح ويمتنع المرجوح ، وعلى هذا التقدير لا نثبت المكنة

Commentary
This passage is an objection directed at the Mu'tazila regarding the timing of choice (ikhtiyar). It asks: is choice established when motives (dawā'ī) are equal or when one preponderates? The first option is false because when motives are equal, the act becomes impossible (since no preference), and capability (mikna) cannot be affirmed in a state of impossibility. The second option is impossible because when a preponderant motive occurs, the preponderant action becomes necessary and the outweighed action becomes impossible, so again capability cannot be affirmed. Thus, on both estimates, capability is not proven. This argument challenges the Mu'tazilite notion of choice by showing that the very definition of choice (as a capacity to do either of two equal actions) leads to paradox.

Reconciling Predestination and Human Choice
Al-Razi defines choice and reconciles it with predestination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Razi reconciles divine predestination with human choice by defining choice as bringing about actions through power or will, both of which are ultimately from Allah.

Core Idea
Human choice is real and meaningful within Allah's decree.

Al-Razi tackles a central theological problem: if everything is decreed by Allah, how can humans be accountable for their choices? In this passage, he offers a nuanced definition of choice that preserves both divine sovereignty and human responsibility. Let's examine his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the passage starts with 'بالتوسط' which may be a heading.
2
Understand the definition of choice
Al-Razi defines choice (ikhtiyar) as 'bringing into existence by means of power or will, whether that power and will are from the act of Allah without intermediation or by means of something else.' This is key to his reconciliation.
3
See the reconciliation
Al-Razi states that it is part of Allah's decree that some actions occur in accordance with the agent's choice. So predestination does not cancel choice; it includes it.
4
Consider the final challenge
The only way to refute this view is to prove that nothing other than Allah has any causal efficacy. Al-Razi implies this is not possible, thus human choice remains real.
This connects to the doctrine of secondary causation.

Thus, predestined actions can still be meaningfully attributed to human choice. The only way to undermine this reconciliation is to prove that nothing but Allah has any causal efficacy whatsoever—a position al-Razi implicitly rejects by asserting the reality of secondary causation.

Al-Ash'ari: No Effect of Human Power
Al-Razi reports al-Ash'ari's denial of causal human power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Ash'ari's position that human power has no causal effect and that both power and act are created directly by God.

Core Idea
Human power is a divine creation with no causal influence.

This lesson presents the first view in al-Razi's survey of Islamic theological schools on human action. Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari holds that the servant's power is entirely passive; God alone is the true agent. Let's examine his claim.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Study the exact words al-Ash'ari uses: 'لا تأثير لقدرة العبد في مقدوره أصلا' (no effect of the servant's power in his intended action at all).
Notice the emphatic 'أصلا' (at all) underscores the denial of any causal role.
2
Identify the key claim
Al-Ash'ari asserts that both the power (قدرة) and the intended action (مقدور) are brought about by God's power alone.
3
Consider the implications
If human power has no effect, then all actions—good and bad—are directly from God. This raises questions about human responsibility.
This position is often misunderstood; al-Ash'ari does not deny the existence of human power, only its causal efficacy.
4
Prepare for contrast
Keep this view in mind as you learn the other schools, which assign varying degrees of efficacy to human power.
Comparison will deepen your understanding of the spectrum of positions.

Commentary

(3) Al-Ustadh Abu Ishaq (al-Isfarayini): Both essence and attributes are from both powers. (4) Imam al-Haramayn (al-Juwayni): God creates power and will in the servant, and these then necessitate the act; this is also the philosophers' view and that of Abu al-Hasan al-Basri from the Mu'tazila. (5) The majority of Mu'tazila: The servant is the creator (mujid) of his actions through free choice (ikhtiyar), not compulsion (ijab).

The First Proof: A Dilemma on Human Refraining
Al-Razi presents the first proof: a dilemma about whether the slave can refrain from action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Razi uses a logical dilemma to argue that human action cannot be truly independent of divine preponderator.

Core Idea
Every choice needs a preponderator, and it cannot come from the chooser.

Al-Razi begins his case against the Mu'tazilite view of human power by setting up a simple but powerful dilemma. The reasoning forces us to ask: if a person can refrain from an action, what tips the scale towards doing it? The answer, he argues, cannot be something the person themselves do, on pain of infinite regress. This passage lays the logical foundation for the Ash'ari doctrine of acquisition (kasb).

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text once without translation. Notice the key terms like 'al-tark' and 'al-tarjih'. Get a sense of the argument's flow.
2
Identify the dilemma's branches
The argument begins with two possibilities: either the servant can refrain or cannot. Work out what each branch implies.
Look for the phrase 'إما أن يمكنه الترك أو لا يمكنه'.
3
Analyze the second branch
If the servant can refrain, then the choice to act requires a preponderator. Notice how Al-Razi eliminates human action as the preponderator through regress.
The key point: the preponderator cannot be from the servant's own act, else the same question recurs.
4
Understand the conclusion
The argument ends by asserting that the preponderator must terminate in something outside the servant — ultimately God. Reflect on how this undermines the Mu'tazilite claim.

Commentary
This passage is a proof (wajh) for the Ash'ari position that human power has no real effect. Al-Razi sets up a dilemma: When a person acts, either he has the ability to refrain or not. If he cannot refrain, then he is compelled, contradicting the Mu'tazilite claim of free choice. If he can refrain, then the choice to act rather than refrain requires a preponderator (murajjih) to tip the scales. If that preponderator is something the person does (i.e., another act), then the same question recurs for that act, leading to a regress. If the preponderator is not from the person, it must be from outside, ultimately terminating in God. Thus human action is ultimately determined by divine preponderator, leaving no real causal role for human power.

The Second Objection: The Theory of Kasb and Its Two Interpretations
Razi presents the second objection: the theory of kasb suffices for responsibility.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If kasb already accounts for human action, why do we need the Quranic evidence?

The second objection is more subtle: even if we deny that humans create their actions, we affirm they are 'acquirers' (kusb). This lesson presents the objection and the two ways kasb is understood within Ash'arism.

Razi addresses three objections regarding divine decree and the obligation to be content.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Contentment is with the decree, not the decreed.

This passage presents a powerful objection: if contentment with God's decree is obligatory, then one must be content even with disbelief, which is itself disbelief. Razi responds with three distinct arguments that distinguish between God's command, His will, and the decree versus the decreed thing.

Four Arguments of the Deniers of Obligation and the General Answer
Imam Razi presents and answers four objections from deniers of obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four objections raised against the concept of obligation and Imam Razi's overarching refutation based on the rejection of infinite regress.

Core Idea
All objections to obligation fail because they wrongly assume God's actions need a cause.

Imam Razi presents four sophisticated arguments from those who deny that humans can be obligated (the deniers of obligation). These arguments draw on divine determinism, the problem of motives, the timing of commands, and the purpose of hardship. Rather than rebutting each in detail, Razi gives a powerful general answer that cuts to the core: the demand for a 'why' behind God's actions is itself invalid, as it leads to an infinite regress. This lesson unpacks each argument and the decisive reply.

Guided Path
1
First argument: Divine determinism
Read the first argument: if all is by God's creation and will, obligation is pointless. Even Mu'tazila admit God's knowledge, which makes the known necessary. Ask: does foreknowledge cause necessity?
Notice Razi lumps both Ash'ari and Mu'tazili views here.
2
Second argument: Equality of motives
Read the second argument: obligation is impossible whether motives are equal or one outweighs. If equal, choice is impossible; if one outweighs, the act is necessary. Consider: is this a valid dilemma?
This is a classic objection against free will.
3
Third argument: Timing of command
Read the third argument: commanding an act either at the time of the act or before it. Both are impossible. Also consider the sub-question about the additional attribute of being a doer.
This deals with the relationship between power and action.
4
Fourth argument: Purpose of hardship
Read the fourth argument: if a difficult command has no purpose, it's defective; if it has a purpose, it cannot benefit God and if it benefits the servant, God could give that benefit directly. Reflect: what is wrong with this reasoning?
This argument assumes God needs a purpose, which Razi will reject.
5
The general answer: Rejecting the why
Read Razi's answer: all these arguments seek a cause ('limiyya'), but not everything needs a cause, otherwise infinite regress. The most fitting thing to be uncaused is God's actions. See how this one answer refutes all four arguments at once.
This is a powerful methodological principle.

Commentary
This passage presents four arguments from the deniers of obligation (likely determinists or philosophers) followed by a general answer. The first argument uses divine determinism: if everything is by God's creation and will, obligation is pointless; even Mu'tazila who reject that still accept God's foreknowledge, so what is known to occur is necessary and what is known not to occur is impossible. The second argument concerns the state of motives: if the two motives are equal, choice is impossible; if one outweighs, the preponderant is necessary. The third argument is about the timing of obligation: if an act does not exist yet, commanding it now is impossible because one cannot be a doer of a future act. A sub-question about an additional attribute of being a doer is addressed—either it is or is not within one's power, both leading to impossibility. The fourth argument concerns the purpose of difficult acts: if no purpose, it is evil; if a purpose, it cannot benefit God, and if it benefits the servant, God could create that benefit directly without the hardship. The general answer cuts through all: these arguments assume that everything must have a cause (talab al-limiyya), but this is false. Not everything needs a cause; otherwise there would be infinite regress. The ultimate endpoint is God's acts and judgments, which have no cause. Thus the deniers' demand for a purpose or cause for obligation is invalid.
