Juwayni's era saw false Sufis; Qushayri defines true Sufism as adherence to Quran and Sunnah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Qushayri defined true Sufism as adherence to Quran and Sunnah, and why Juwayni distinguished it from false claims.

Core Idea
True spirituality is grounded in scripture and moral struggle, not in mere claims.

In Juwayni's time, many appeared claiming to be Sufis while lacking genuine devotion. Abu al-Qasim al-Qushayri responded by defining Sufism in terms of adherence to the Quran and Sunnah, coupled with internal struggle against the soul. This lesson explores that definition and its significance.

This highlights that authentic spirituality is not a separate label but the essence of Islam itself.

The lesson guards against antinomianism and emphasizes balance between inner struggle and outer adherence.

The Nature of Divine Speech: Refutation of Transfer
Juwayni refutes the Hashwiyya's transfer theory and states the correct doctrine of divine speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Juwayni argues that divine speech cannot be transferred to books or become sounds, and that it is an eternal attribute subsisting in God's essence.

Core Idea
God's speech is an eternal attribute, not a transferable physical entity.

In this passage, Juwayni confronts the Hashwiyya's belief that God's speech consists of letters and sounds that can be transferred. He asserts that even basic reason shows the impossibility of such transfer. He then affirms the orthodox doctrine that kalam Allah is written, recited, and memorized but is actually an eternal attribute in God's essence.

Guided Path
1
Read Juwayni's diagnosis
Read the opening lines where Juwayni notes how the erroneous view spread among the Hashwiyya. Understand the context of the refutation.
2
Analyze the rational argument
Identify Juwayni's core rational point: speech does not transfer from a speaker to a book, nor does inner meaning become sounds or shapes. This is a self-evident truth.
Notice the phrase 'لا يخفى على من معه مسكة من عقل' — it underscores the obviousness of the argument.
3
Grasp the positive doctrine
Read the concluding sentence where Juwayni states that God's speech is written in mushafs, recited, and memorized, but it subsists in God's essence. Understand that the attribute is eternal, not the created letters.
The phrase 'قائم بذات البارى وجودا' is key: the speech attribute exists in God's essence.

Learning Objective

Proofs 17-18 and Refutation of Khawarij Hadiths
Khawarij present hadith and rational proof, then al-Mawaqif refutes the hadiths as ahad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Khawarij's use of hadith and rational argument to equate sin with disbelief, and how al-Mawaqif refutes them using hadith methodology.

Core Idea
The Khawarij's strongest proof is refuted by the principle that solitary hadith cannot override consensus.

The Khawarij's seventeenth proof relies on two hadiths that seem to equate abandoning prayer or Hajj with disbelief. They also present a rational argument that God's friendship and enmity are opposites. The author of al-Mawaqif responds by pointing out the hadiths are solitary (ahad) and therefore cannot contradict the consensus of the community, which holds that grave sinners are not kafir.

Guided Path
1
Read the section on proof 17 and 18
Read the Arabic and translation for proofs 17 and 18. Note the two hadiths and the rational argument.
The hadith about prayer is well-known; the one about Hajj is less so.
2
Understand the rational argument
The eighteenth proof argues that God's friendship (wilayah) and enmity (adawah) are opposites with no middle ground. Since wilayah is faith, enmity must be disbelief. The Khawarij then conclude that the fasiq, who is not a friend of God, must be an enemy and thus a kafir.
This argument assumes that all people are either friends or enemies of God, ignoring the possibility of believers who sin.
3
Read the refutation
Al-Mawaqif responds by stating that the two hadiths are solitary (ahad) and cannot override the consensus (ijma) of the Muslim community. The consensus is that a person who commits a major sin is not a kafir unless he considers the sin permissible.
This shows the importance of hadith classification in theological debates.
4
Reflect on the methodology
Consider why ahad hadiths are not sufficient to overturn established consensus. This is a key principle in both fiqh and kalam.
Consensus is considered a decisive proof, while ahad hadiths are probabilistic.

And the twelfth: His statement, the Exalted: (But as for he who is given his record in his left hand) until His statement: (Indeed, he did not believe in Allah, the Most Great) (al-Haqqah 69:25-33). And the thirteenth: His statement, the Exalted: (Indeed, the curse of Allah is upon the wrongdoers) (al-A'raf 7:44). And the fourteenth: His statement, the Exalted: (But as for those who defiantly disobey, their refuge is the Fire) (as-Sajdah 32:20). And the fifteenth: His statement, the Exalted: (They will question each other about the criminals: "What put you into Saqar?"... until: "And we used to deny the Day of Recompense") (al-Muddaththir 74:40-46). Commentary
This passage completes the Khawarij's proofs from nine to eighteen and includes a critical note on two hadiths. The ninth proof uses the verse from Surah an-Nur where 'fasiqun' is applied to disbelievers, arguing that the fasiq is a kafir. The tenth uses the verse that only disbelievers despair of mercy, claiming the fasiq despairs. The eleventh combines two verses about disgrace to link the fasiq to disbelievers. The twelfth uses the description of those given their book in the left hand, who are described as not believing. The thirteenth declares curse upon wrongdoers (zalimin), equating them with disbelievers. The fourteenth directly states that the fasiq's refuge is the Fire. The fifteenth uses the dialogue of the people of Saqar who admit to denying the Day. The sixteenth contrasts the driving of disbelievers with the leading of the righteous. The seventeenth presents two hadiths: abandoning prayer intentionally is kufr, and dying without Hajj is like dying as a Jew or Christian. The eighteenth is a rational argument: God's friendship (wilayah) and enmity (adawah) are opposites with no intermediate; since wilayah is faith, enmity is kufr, so the fasiq must be a kafir. The passage ends with a note from the author of al-Mawaqif, who responds to the two hadiths in the seventeenth proof, arguing that they are ahad (solitary) narrations and thus cannot override the consensus (ijma) of the Muslim community. This shows that the Khawarij's scriptural proofs are not decisive, as the consensus upholds the view that grave sinners are not kafir.

The Second Mu'tazila Argument: Wasil ibn Ata's Principle
Second proof using the principle of taking the agreed upon.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second Mu'tazila proof for the intermediate position, based on Wasil ibn Ata's logical principle of focusing on agreed matters.

We should set aside disputed matters and rule based on what is agreed upon.

The second argument attributed to Wasil ibn Ata, the founder of the Mu'tazila, presents a logical principle: since the fasiq's sin is agreed upon while his faith is disputed, we should take the agreed fact (that he is a fasiq) and leave the disputed (his faith).
