The Principle of Impossible Attributes
Juwayni states the rule: attributes requiring a specifier are impossible for God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the general principle that any attribute that requires a specifier in creatures is impossible for God, and how this rule applies to attributes like direction.

Core Idea
Attributes needing a specifier in creatures are impossible for God.

In this passage, Imam al-Juwayni establishes a foundational principle for understanding divine transcendence: whatever requires a cause or specifier in creation cannot be attributed to God. He explains that attributes like temporality, composition, and spatial location are possible in creatures – they could be otherwise – so they imply need for a specifier. Since God is absolutely independent, such attributes are impossible for Him. Juwayni then connects this to the famous hadith 'Whoever knows himself knows his Lord,' and applies the principle to the issue of God being restricted to a direction.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that Juwayni uses a very structured argument: principle, rule, examples, hadith, application.
2
Identify the general principle
Find the sentence that states the core principle about attributes that require a specifier (mukhaṣṣiṣ). Understand what a specifier is and why it matters.
3
Understand the rule linking possibility (jawāz) to need
Juwayni says that every attribute that is possible (could be otherwise) depends on a specifier. Why does possibility imply need?
Remember: God is eternal (qadīm) and possibility (jawāz) is a characteristic of temporal things.
4
Examine the examples
Look at the attributes listed: temporality, composition, spatial occupation, limitation, measure, etc. Why are these impossible for God?
These are all attributes that in us are possible, so they require a specifier.
5
Reflect on the hadith and its application
Read Juwayni's explanation of the hadith. Then see how he applies the principle to direction (jiha). Ask yourself: why is direction impossible for God?
This shows the practical use of the principle to refute anthropomorphism.

Discourse on what is impossible for God, Mighty and Majestic. A concise statement containing the goal has preceded. If we see it sufficient, we will be content with it, and if we see that we should expand a portion of the discourse, we will proceed in it according to what the circumstances dictate. God, exalted, is the Facilitator. So we say: Every attribute in created things whose existence indicates a specifier (mukhaṣṣiṣ) that effects them and wills them and whose existence is inconceivable without that, it is impossible for the deity (ilāh), for if it were established for Him, it would indicate His need for a specifier just as it indicates in the case of the temporally originated created thing. And the rule regarding the attributes that are in need [of a specifier] is: what was first established from the estimation of the ruling of possibility (jawāz), so every attribute from which the ruling of possibility departs is impossible in the description of God, exalted. For eternity (qidam) and possibility (jawāz) are contradictory. And its detail: that temporality (ḥudūth) in us is described by possibility, so we sanctify the deity from it. And composition (tarkīb) and spatial occupation (taṣawwur) and limitation (taqaddur) in our attributes are marked by possibility, so [there is] no composition, nor is it permissible to assume its contrary. And no measure (qadd), no power (qadr), no boundary (ḥadd), no length, no width, except that the intellect permits their like and their opposite. And these attributes, due to their possibility, depend on the specification of their Creator, so the Maker is exalted above them. And this is the meaning of the saying of the master of mankind, the Seal of the Prophets, Muhammad, may God bless him and grant him peace, when he said: 'Whoever knows himself knows his Lord.' He meant: whoever knows himself by the attributes of need knows the independence of the Lord from attributes. For He—exalted are His names—is the ultimate of need, and He is free from them. And upon this principle it is necessary that the Maker of the world be sanctified from being restricted to some directions (jihāt), for the intellect judges the possibility of [His] bringing into existence (takwīn) in one direction without its likes, just as it judges the possibility of spatial occupation (taṣawwur) and limitation (taqaddur).

Commentary
This passage establishes the general principle for determining which attributes are impossible for God. Juwayni begins by stating that every attribute in creatures that requires a specifier (mukhaṣṣiṣ) to bring it about is impossible for God, because if it were attributed to God, it would indicate that He needs a specifier, which is impossible. The rule is that any attribute that is subject to possibility (jawāz)—meaning it could be otherwise—is impossible for God, because possibility implies contingency and need for a specifier. Eternity (qidam) and possibility are contradictory. He gives examples: temporality, composition, spatial occupation, and limitation are all possible in creatures, so they are impossible for God. He then quotes the hadith 'Whoever knows himself knows his Lord,' explaining that it means that when a person recognizes his own attributes of need, he recognizes that God is free from such attributes. Finally, he applies this principle to the issue of direction (jihāt): since the intellect judges that creation could occur in any direction, direction is a possible attribute, so God cannot be restricted to a particular direction.

Application of Contingency to Direction
Juwayni applies the contingency principle to negate direction from Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Juwayni applies the principle of contingency to negate direction from Allah, establishing divine transcendence.

Core Idea
Direction is contingent; the Necessary Being cannot be confined.

Juwayni now applies the foundational principle of contingency to a specific attribute: direction. Since direction is a contingent matter, it cannot be attributed to the necessary Being. This leads to the crucial Islamic doctrine of divine transcendence beyond spatial confines.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once. Notice how Juwayni starts with 'upon this principle' and then states the conclusion.
2
Identify the principle being applied
The phrase 'upon this principle' refers to the contingency of the world. Juwayni uses it to argue about direction.
3
Understand the reasoning
The intellect judges that origination in one direction is permissible, just as being pictured or measured is permissible. Since direction is permissible (ja'iz), it cannot be for the necessary Being.
This is a direct application of the earlier distinction between necessary, impossible, and contingent.
4
Note the consequence
The consequence is that Allah must be sanctified from being particularized with any direction. This is a core doctrine of Islamic theology.
5
Recognize the divisive nature
Juwayni notes that this issue is a 'slippery place' that caused division. This sets up the refutation of deviant sects in subsequent lessons.

And upon this principle, the Maker of the world must be sanctified from being particularized with some directions, for the intellect judges the permissibility of origination in one direction without its likes, just as it judges the permissibility of being pictured and measured. Then the negation of particularization with measures necessarily follows inasmuch as it was permissible, and specialization with directions and regions in the case of contingency is like particularization with measures. This is a slippery place for feet, and a cause of trouble among people, and because of it the masses of creation split into two groups. And the true, saved group was established. It is necessary to alert to the cause of the division, and to clarify what impelled the people of truth to steadfastness and avoidance of dispersion. Some groups went to describing the Lord (exalted is His majesty above that) with confinement to a direction, until the extremists ended up in doubt or anthropomorphism or [؟] — God is exalted above the saying of the deviators. And what called them to that was their seeking their Lord through sensory things, and what takes shape in imaginations and is measured in the metaphors of whispers and thoughts of obsessions. This is a complete deviation from the attributes of divinity. And what difference is there between these and those who worship some celestial bodies? And even if the former and the latter gathered to perceive the spirit through this path — and it is a creation of God Almighty — they would not find a way to it, for it is known intellectually, not sensibly. And God Almighty said in the firm text of His Book, which falsehood cannot come to it from before it or behind it: 'And they ask you about the spirit. Say: the spirit is from the command of my Lord, and you have not been given of knowledge except a little.' And another group went to negation (ta'til) because their intellects fell short of grasping the reality of the Divine, so they thought that what the mind does not encompass is nonexistent. Had they been guided, they would have known that knowledge of an existent is not far-fetched while being incapable of grasping its reality. And what we have struck as an example of the spirit is used to refute these.

Commentary
This passage applies the previous principle to the issue of direction and refutes two deviant sects. Juwayni first states that based on the principle of contingency, the Creator must be free from being confined to any particular direction, because the intellect considers direction as permissible (ja'iz). Just as the intellect can conceive of God being in any direction or none, direction is a contingent attribute and thus impossible for God. He then notes that this issue is a cause of major division among people; the saved group adheres to the correct negation of direction. He identifies two misguided groups: those who describe God with confinement to a direction (anthropomorphism), including extremists who fall into doubt (tashkik), anthropomorphism (tamthil), or [؟]. Their error stems from trying to perceive God through the senses and imagination. Juwayni compares them to idol worshippers and argues that even if they tried to perceive the spirit — a created thing — through sensory means, they could not, because the spirit is intellectually known (ma'qul) but not sensible (ghayr mahsus). He cites the Quranic verse about the spirit to support this. The second group is the mu'attila who negate God's attributes and existence because their intellects cannot grasp His true reality. They erroneously conclude that what cannot be fully conceived does not exist. Juwayni counters by pointing to the spirit: we know it exists despite not knowing its true nature. Thus, the example of the spirit refutes both sides.

Refutation of Anthropomorphists (Mushabbiha)
Juwayni refutes those who attribute direction and anthropomorphism to Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why describing Allah with direction leads to anthropomorphism and how Juwayni refutes it using the spirit example.

Core Idea
Seeking God through the senses leads to anthropomorphism.

Juwayni now turns to those who mistakenly described Allah with direction and even anthropomorphism. He explains that their error stems from trying to perceive the divine through sensory faculties, and he compares them to idolaters. The spirit example serves as a powerful refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage describing the first deviant group and Juwayni's refutation.
2
Identify the error of the first sect
They describe Allah with confinement to a direction, leading to doubt, anthropomorphism, or worse. Their root cause is seeking Allah through sensory things.
Juwayni uses strong language: 'complete deviation from the attributes of divinity'.
3
Understand the comparison to idolaters
Juwayni asks: what difference is there between these people and those who worship celestial bodies? This highlights the gravity of their error.
4
Examine the spirit example
The spirit is a created thing that is known intellectually but not sensibly. Even if they tried to perceive it through the senses, they could not. This shows that existence can be beyond sensory perception.
Juwayni cites the Quranic verse about the spirit to reinforce this point.
5
Apply the spirit example to refute anthropomorphism
If even a created thing like the spirit cannot be perceived by the senses, how can one expect to perceive the Creator through the senses? Thus the anthropomorphist approach is invalid.

Refutation of the Negators (Mu'attila)
Juwayni refutes those who negate Allah due to inability to comprehend.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Juwayni refutes those who deny Allah's existence due to inability to fully comprehend Him, using the spirit example.

Core Idea
Inability to fully comprehend does not negate existence.

The second deviant group are those who negated God's attributes and existence altogether, because their intellects could not grasp His true reality. Juwayni refutes them by showing that existence does not require full comprehension. He uses the same spirit example to demonstrate that something can be known to exist even if its true nature is unknown.

1
Read the passage
Read the short Arabic passage describing the mu'attila and Juwayni's refutation.

A miracle is a sign only when it confirms the claim it is meant to prove.

Commentary
This passage is a demonstration of how miracles indicate prophethood. Juwayni first lays down the general principle: a miracle does not indicate in virtue of being extraordinary per se, but rather because it occurs in accordance with the specific claim of prophethood. If an extraordinary event is attached to another claim, it indicates the truth of that claim, not necessarily prophethood. He illustrates this with an analogy: a man in a court claims to be close to the king, and the king tells him to stand and sit; when the king does so, it confirms the man's claim. Similarly, if an extraordinary event is linked to a claim of prophethood, it confirms the claim. However, Juwayni clarifies that this does not mean every extraordinary event confirms any claim; he acknowledges that God sometimes causes miracles (كرامات) for saints without any deliberate request, but that is a matter of God's grace, not a basis for proof. He also warns against confusing miracles with extraordinary events that may accompany false claimants, such as the trials of the Antichrist (الدجال) or the splitting of the Nile for Pharaoh—these are not genuine miracles (معجزات) because they do not proceed from a true claim of prophethood. The core point is that a miracle (معجزة) does not prove truth by its own nature; it proves only when it aligns with the claim of prophethood. The passage ends with the assertion that whoever understands this can easily answer all objections on the topic.
