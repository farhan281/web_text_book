The Criterion for Impossible Attributes
Juwayni defines impossible attributes and states the rule of contingency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Juwayni's criterion for determining which attributes are impossible for God: they must be contingent, requiring a specifier.

Core Idea
Contingent attributes imply need; God is beyond need.

Juwayni begins by defining the key concept: an attribute that in created things always requires a specifier (mukhassis) to exist cannot be attributed to God, because that would imply dependence. He then states the rule: every attribute characterized by contingency (jawaz) is impossible for God, since eternity and contingency are mutually exclusive. This lesson establishes the foundational principle.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of an impossible attribute
Juwayni says that any attribute in creatures whose existence indicates a specifier (mukhassis) that brings it about and intends it, and cannot be conceived without that, is impossible for God.
The key is the need for a specifier.
2
Understand why that attribute cannot apply to God
If such an attribute were established for God, it would indicate His need for a specifier, which is impossible for the Divine.
3
Learn the rule of contingency
Juwayni states the precise criterion: every attribute that is characterized by contingency (jawaz) is impossible for God, because eternity (qidam) and contingency are contradictory.
Contingency means the possibility of existence or non-existence.

Discussion on what is impossible for God Almighty. We present a concise statement that contains the purpose; if we see it as sufficient we suffice with it, and if we see to expand a portion of the discourse we proceed in it according to what destinies determine, and God is the granter of ease. So we say: Every attribute in created things whose existence indicates a specifier that brings it about and intends it, and its existence cannot be conceived without that, is impossible for the Divine; for if it were established for Him, it would indicate His need for a specifier just as it indicates it in the case of the originated creature. And the precise statement concerning attributes that are in need: what was first established from the estimation of the ruling of contingency, so every attribute from which the ruling of contingency departs is impossible in the description of God Almighty. For eternity and contingency are contradictory. And the detail of that is that temporality in us is characterized by contingency; so we declare God free from it. And composition, being pictured, and measurement in our attributes are stamped with contingency, so there is no composition nor is it permissible to assume otherwise. And there is no measure, no quantity, no limit, no length, no width, except that the intellect deems their likes and their opposites permissible. And these attributes, because of their contingency, are in need of specification by their Creator, so the Maker is exalted above them. Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof. Juwayni begins by introducing the topic of attributes that are impossible for God. He then defines an impossible attribute: any attribute in creatures whose existence requires a specifier (mukhassis) that brings it about and intends it, and which cannot be conceived without such a specifier. Such attributes cannot be attributed to God because if they were, they would indicate that God needs a specifier, just as they do for originated beings. He then formulates a rule: the key to identifying such attributes is the judgment of contingency (jawaz). Every attribute characterized by contingency is impossible for God, because eternity (qidam) and contingency are contradictory. He elaborates: in us, temporality (huduth) is characterized by contingency, so God is free from it. Similarly, composition (tarkib), being in a direction (tasawwur), and measurement (taqdir) in our attributes are contingent, hence impossible for God. He gives examples: any measure, quantity, limit, length, or width is subject to mental variation, thus contingent. Such attributes need a specifier, so the Creator is beyond them. Juwayni then interprets the prophetic saying 'Whoever knows himself knows his Lord' as meaning that whoever recognizes his own contingent and needy attributes comes to know that God is utterly free from them and self-sufficient.

Impossible Attributes in Detail: Examples and the Prophetic Wisdom
Juwayni elaborates examples of contingent attributes and interprets the hadith 'Who knows himself knows his Lord'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will recognize specific examples of contingent attributes (temporality, composition, measurement) that are impossible for God, and understand the hadith about self-knowledge.

Core Idea
Everything in creation that is variable or dependent is impossible for God.

After establishing the general rule, Juwayni delves into concrete examples: in humans, temporality (huduth) is contingent, composition (tarkib) is contingent, and all forms of measurement (length, width, etc.) are contingent because the intellect can conceive of their opposites. He then explains the famous prophetic saying 'Whoever knows himself knows his Lord' as a lesson in recognizing our own need and God's perfect independence.

Guided Path
1
Read the detailed examples of impossible attributes
Juwayni lists temporality, composition, being pictured, measurement, quantity, limit, length, width – all are contingent and thus impossible for God.
Each of these in creatures requires a specifier.
2
Reflect on the underlying logic
For each attribute, the intellect can imagine its opposite, so it is contingent. Hence it needs a specifier and cannot be attributed to God.
3
Understand the hadith interpretation
Juwayni explains that 'knowing yourself' means recognizing your attributes of need (contingency), which leads to knowing God's independence from such attributes.

The Definition of Miracle and the Miracle of the Quran
Juwayni defines the conditions of a miracle and states that the Quran's miracle is not its eloquence but the divine prevention of imitation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Juwayni's definition of a miracle and why the Quran's inimitability is based on divine prevention rather than superhuman eloquence.

Core Idea
The Quran's miracle is not its eloquence but God's prevention of imitation.

Juwayni begins by setting a strict definition for what constitutes a miracle: it must exceed ordinary custom in a way that human effort cannot achieve. He then applies this definition to the Quran, arguing that eloquence is within human capability, so the true miracle lies in the fact that God prevented the Arabs from producing something similar. This passage establishes the doctrine of sarfah, a key concept in Ash'ari theology.

This is the key doctrinal point: the miracle is in the prevention, not the text itself.

Commentary
This passage defines the conditions of a miracle (mu'jiza) and applies it to the Qur'an. Juwayni first states the prerequisite: a true miracle must exceed the ordinary breaking of custom in such a way that it cannot be achieved by human knowledge, talent, or effort. Instead, the miracle is that God challenged the Arabs to produce something similar, and they failed despite being capable. The proof is the verse from Surah al-Isra'. This aligns with the doctrine of sarfah (divine prevention). Juwayni emphasizes that the miracle lies in the prevention, not in the text's inherent quality surpassing human ability.

Most People Are Believers, Not Knowers
Juwayni argues that firm belief, not knowledge, is required for salvation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why, according to Juwayni, a firm belief suffices for salvation and why most people are not required to attain demonstrative knowledge of God.

Core Idea
Firm belief saves; demonstrative knowledge is for the few.

In this passage, Juwayni addresses a critical question: is everyone required to have deep, demonstrative knowledge of God? He argues that most people are not—their firm, correct belief is sufficient. He supports this with the example of the early Muslims and the famous hadith about the last words. This lesson unpacks his reasoning and its implications.

Commentary
This passage argues that most people are not required to have demonstrative knowledge (ma‘rifa) of God; instead, a firm, correct belief (aqd) suffices. Juwayni begins by stating that necessity (absolute certainty) is not attained by everyone, nor can everyone independently use proofs—even those who study kalam. Therefore, most beliefs are not true knowledge but are stable and correct. He then asserts that Allah did not burden people with attaining true knowledge and certainty in religion; rather, they were only required to have a firm belief, to testify, and to adhere to rulings. As evidence, he cites the early Muslims (al-awwalūn) who were not required to pursue proofs but only to maintain a firm conviction. If they remain faithful to that conviction until death, they are saved, as the hadith states: 'Whoever’s last words are ‘There is no god but Allah’ enters Paradise.' From this, he concludes that the people of knowledge (arbāb al-ma‘ārif) are the minority; the rest are people of beliefs (ahl ‘aqā’id). Thus, knowledge is not necessary for salvation; a sound belief suffices.

Iman, Islam, and Din are one reality; outward confession ensures Islamic rulings.

Imam Juwayni presents the correct Sunni view that they are identical in essence, supported by Quranic proofs. Commentary
This passage defines the relationship between Iman, Islam, and Din, and clarifies the status of a Muslim who commits sins. Juwayni begins by explaining that the term 'Islam' can be used in two ways: sometimes it means Iman (faith), and sometimes it means outward submission (al-istislam) without necessarily implying the inner reality of Iman. He then clarifies that whoever pronounces the two shahadahs is subject to the rulings of Islam (e.g., washing, shrouding, burial) and that the name 'Iman' does not disappear due to sin. The correct position according to Ahl al-Sunnah is that performing obligatory acts (fard) is 'al-Din', and al-Din is al-Islam, and al-Islam is al-Iman—meaning they are identical in essence. Juwayni supports this with three proofs from the Quran: (1) That performing obligatory acts is al-Din is proven by the verse 'That is the correct religion' after mentioning prayer and zakah (al-Bayyinah 98:5). (2) That al-Din is al-Islam is proven by the verse 'Indeed, the religion with Allah is Islam' (Aal-e-Imran 3:19). (3) That al-Islam is al-Iman is proven by two arguments: (a) If Iman were different from Islam, then seeking Islam as a religion would not be accepted, but the verse says 'Whoever seeks a religion other than Islam, it will never be accepted from him' (Aal-e-Imran 3:85), implying that Iman and Islam are the same. (b) The story of Prophet Lut (peace be upon him) distinguishes between 'believers' and 'Muslims' in the same house, showing that the terms can be used interchangeably—otherwise, the exception would be meaningless. Juwayni then addresses the status of a committer of a major sin (fasiq): he is not a believer because a believer is not disgraced, while the fasiq is disgraced (as in the case of a highway robber, whom Allah threatens with punishment and disgrace). However, despite this, the fasiq who dies upon his sin is still treated as a Muslim in this world—he is washed, shrouded, prayed over, and buried in Muslim cemeteries. Thus, the passage affirms the Ahl al-Sunnah position that Iman decreases with sin but does not vanish entirely, and that the fasiq remains within the fold of Islam outwardly.

The Ruling on the Sinner (Fasiq): Believer or Not?
Juwayni explains the status of the major sinner and its worldly implications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the committer of a major sin (fasiq) is not called a believer but is still treated as a Muslim in this world according to Juwayni.

Core Idea
The major sinner is not a believer in the full sense yet remains within the community of Muslims.

Imam Juwayni addresses a crucial issue: the status of a Muslim who commits major sins. Using the example of a highway robber, he shows that such a person is not a believer because disbelief entails disgrace, and the believer is not disgraced. However, he still receives the funeral rites of a Muslim. This lesson clarifies the Sunni position between Kharijites (excommunication) and Murji'ites (deeds irrelevant).

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic and English carefully. Notice the logical flow from the definition of the fasiq to the practical ruling.
2
Understand the argument against the sinner being a believer
Juwayni argues that a major sinner (like a highway robber) is not a believer because he is disgraced in the Hereafter, and the believer is never disgraced.
He uses Quranic verses to connect disgrace with punishment.
3
Identify the two Quranic proofs
First proof: The highway robber is threatened with great punishment (al-Ma'idah 5:33). Second proof: Being thrown into the Fire means disgrace (Aal-e-Imran 3:192), and the believer is not disgraced (at-Tahrim 66:8).
4
Note the practical ruling for the deceased sinner
Despite not being a believer, the fasiq who dies upon his sin is washed, shrouded, prayed over, and buried in Muslim cemeteries.
This shows he remains within the fold of Islam outwardly.
5
Reflect on the theological middle ground
This position avoids both extremes: declaring the sinner a kafir (Kharijite) or denying that sin harms his faith (Murji'ite).
The fasiq is a Muslim with defective faith, but not a disbeliever.
