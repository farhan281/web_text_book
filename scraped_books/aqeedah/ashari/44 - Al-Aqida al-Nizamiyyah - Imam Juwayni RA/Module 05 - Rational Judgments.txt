The Three Rational Judgments and Their Division
Juwayni defines the three rational judgments and divides each into intuitive and theoretical.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three rational judgments (contingency, necessity, impossibility) and their division into intuitive and theoretical knowledge.

Core Idea
Rational reflection yields three fundamental judgments: contingent, necessary, impossible.

Imam Juwayni begins his foundational work by establishing the categories of rational judgment. He explains that sound intellectual reflection leads to knowledge of three possibilities: contingency, necessity, and impossibility. He then divides each of these into what is grasped intuitively and what requires theoretical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening passage
Read the Arabic text from the beginning to the explanation of the division. Pay attention to the three terms: جواز, وجوب, استحالة.
These terms are foundational for all subsequent discussions.
2
Identify the three rational judgments
The passage states that reflection leads to knowledge of three categories: contingency (جواز), necessity (وجوب), and impossibility (استحالة). Note that each is defined by its relation to the intellect.
3
Understand the division into intuitive and theoretical
Juwayni divides each judgment into two types: بديهي (intuitive, grasped without reflection) and نظري (theoretical, requiring prior reasoning). Reflect on why this division matters for theological argumentation.
Intuitive knowledge still requires a necessary basis (مستند ضروري).

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification passage. Juwayni begins by stating that sound intellectual reflection leads to knowledge of three types of judgment: contingency (jawāz), necessity (wujūb), and impossibility (istiḥāla). These are the three rational judgments foundational to kalām. He then notes that these sciences are grasped only by those with sound intellects and upright discernment. Next, he divides each of the three judgments into two types: badīhī (a priori, intuitive) and naẓarī (theoretical, requiring reflection). For the a priori, the intellect grasps it immediately without any reflection; for the theoretical, it requires prior reasoning. He then provides an example for contingency: when one sees a building, the intellect immediately knows that its existence is contingent—it could have been otherwise. The intellect spontaneously applies this contingency to all attributes of the building (height, width, shape, etc.) and recognizes that any state could have been different. This is an a priori perception of contingency. Then he mentions the theoretical example: the contingency of the rotation of celestial spheres in various directions, which requires reasoning. The passage thus establishes the classification of rational judgments and gives concrete examples to clarify.

The Intuitive Example of Contingency
Juwayni exemplifies intuitive contingency using a building.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The building's existence is known as contingent without any reflection.

He uses the example of a building: when we see it, we immediately know that its existence is not necessary—it could have been otherwise. This example shows how the intellect spontaneously applies contingency to all attributes without any reasoning.

The Three Rational Judgments and Their Division
Juwayni states the three rational judgments and their division into badihi and nadhari.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three rational judgments – possible, necessary, impossible – and that each is known either intuitively (badihi) or through acquired reflection (nadhari).

Core Idea
Judgments are possible, necessary, or impossible; each known intuitively or reflectively.

Juwayni opens the core of his theological method by establishing the fundamental categories of rational judgment. Before we can discuss any specific belief, we need a shared framework for what reason can affirm or deny. This passage provides that framework, dividing all knowledge into intuitive and acquired.

Understand the role of necessary basis
Note that every reflection must have a necessary (ضروري) basis and an intuitive (بديهي) foundation. This prevents infinite regress.
This is a key methodological point: all arguments rest on self-evident truths.

Commentary
This passage defines evil and good in terms of harm and benefit, and argues that since God is transcendent over both, all His actions are equal in His decree. The passage begins by stating the overall division: what is permissible regarding God's rulings is divided into discussion of His actions and discussion of the possibility of seeing Him. Juwayni then addresses the actions of God. He notes that whatever reason deems possible, God has power over it. He then focuses on the issue of declaring good and evil (al-tahsin wa al-taqbih). He says that the various schools on this matter would be lengthy, so he restricts himself to a decisive point. Refuting the Analogy from Human Nobility
Juwayni refutes the analogy of a great person doing good without benefit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Juwayni rejects the analogy between human noble behavior and divine actions, and how he grounds his refutation in human psychology.

Core Idea
Human nobility is driven by praise and sympathy—God is beyond both.

Juwayni confronts an objection that appeals to noble human actions to argue that God, too, may act without personal benefit. The objection sounds plausible at first, but Juwayni dissects it ruthlessly. He shows that what seems like selfless virtue in humans actually depends on specific psychological and social causes—causes that cannot apply to the transcendent Creator. This passage is a masterclass in avoiding anthropomorphism by examining the roots of human behavior.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Start by reading the objector's claim: a great person may honor a stranger without benefit, so why can't God act similarly? Recognize the intuitive appeal.
Juwayni dismisses it as 'sophistry that yields nothing'—take note of his strong language.
2
Examine the first cause: praise and habit
Juwayni argues that noble acts are often motivated by desire for praise, and repeated actions become habitual. This makes them human-specific.
The term 'الجبليات' refers to innate dispositions shaped by habit.
3
Examine the second cause: natural sympathy
Humans feel compassion and would be harmed by inaction. God feels no such sympathy or harm.
Notice how this undermines the analogy completely.
4
Understand the conclusion: transcendence and warning
Juwayni declares God transcendent over all these attributes and warns that attributing differentiated motives to God leads to anthropomorphism.
He connects this error to corporealism and direction (tajsim).
5
Reflect on the method
Juwayni uses reduction: break a seemingly parallel case into its human components, then show they don't apply to God.
This is a key kalam method for refuting analogies.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection and its refutation. The objector argues that a great and majestic person may treat a lowly stranger with honor and shelter without any personal benefit, simply because wisdom (or noble character) impels him. By analogy, God might also be expected to act in certain ways despite being unaffected by benefit or harm. Juwayni dismisses this as sophistry. He breaks down the example into two underlying causes. First, such noble behavior in humans is often motivated by a desire for praise and reputation; moreover, habits can create a second nature that makes it difficult to act otherwise. Second, humans are moved by natural sympathy (compassion) to rescue others, and if they fail to do so, they themselves would feel harmed (e.g., guilt). Both causes are rooted in human psychology and vulnerability. God is utterly transcendent over these attributes—He does not seek praise, is not subject to habit, and feels no sympathy or psychological harm. Therefore, the analogy fails. Juwayni warns that imagining that actions are differentiated in God's right (i.e., that some actions are good and others evil from God's perspective) leads to anthropomorphism (tashbih). He concludes that those who fall into corporealism and affirming direction for God ultimately rely on such analogies, which lead to tashbih.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer followed by a methodological explanation and a transition to the next topic. The objector claims that the author's position—that obligation is perceived through sensing punishment—makes him like the Qadariyya, who held that reason independently imposes obligations on Allah. Juwayni strongly denies this: the Qadariyya believed that intellects obligate reward and punishment upon Allah and that they alone perceive obligations by reason. In contrast, Juwayni affirms that nothing is obligatory upon Allah and no obligation is known by reason directly. Rather, when Allah intends to oblige His servants, He issues commands and threats; then reason prompts the servant to avoid the threatened harm. The author then draws on his broad study of rational sciences, noting that all rational people agree on fundamental principles (like the need for a Director for changing things) but differ on details. The Qadariyya's error lies not in their starting point (that souls seek benefit and avoid harm) but in their mishandling of details, leading to the false conclusion that Allah is obligated. The author then transitions, stating that since the rulings of lordship and servitude have been covered and it is clear that the source of obligations depends on revelation, the next chapter will explain that the source of laws is reception from prophets.
