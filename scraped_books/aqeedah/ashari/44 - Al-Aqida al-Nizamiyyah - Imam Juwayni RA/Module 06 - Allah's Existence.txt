Juwayni's Proof of the World's Temporality from Possibility
Juwayni defines the world and argues for its temporality via the judgment of possibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Juwayni's argument that the world is temporally originated because it is possible and therefore requires an external effector.

Core Idea
The possible cannot be eternal; the world is possible, so it is not eternal.

Juwayni begins by defining the world as everything besides God, consisting of bodies and accidents. He then observes that every part of the world can be imagined differently—its shape, motion, position, etc. This shows that the world is not necessary but possible. Since the possible cannot be self-sufficient, it must have an effector. This lesson unpacks that logical chain.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of the world
Carefully read Juwayni's definition: 'The world is every existent other than God, composed of bodies and accidents.' Note that this includes everything we observe and what is absent.
This definition sets the scope of the argument.
2
Observe the proof of possibility
Go through the examples: shapes can change, rest can become motion, planets can rearrange. The conclusion is that reason judges all these as possible (ja'iz).
Possibility means it could be otherwise without logical contradiction.
3
Understand why possibility negates eternalness
Juwayni argues: whatever is possible cannot be necessary, and only the necessary is eternal. Therefore, the world cannot be eternal.
Eternalness (qidam) implies necessity (wujub).
4
Grasp the conclusion: need for an effector
Since the world is possible, it needs an external cause to bring it into existence and determine its specific state among many possibilities. Only the self-necessary needs no cause.
This is the core of the proof for the existence of God.
5
Consider the objection raised
The passage ends with an objection: what if the world is eternal because an eternal necessitator specified it? Think about how you might answer this.
The answer is likely on the next page; this objection highlights the need for a determinant.

The statement on the temporality of the world: The world is every existent other than God Almighty, and it is bodies limited and finite in their extremities, and accidents subsisting in them, such as their colors, their forms, their compositions, and the rest of their attributes. What we have observed of them and what our senses have contacted, and what is absent from the perception of our senses, are equal in the establishment of the judgment of possibility (jawaz) for them. There is no form that is seen or imagined, whether small or large, near or far, absent or present, except that reason judges that those formed bodies are not impossible to imagine being formed in another shape. What is at rest among them, reason does not forbid its movement; what is moving among them, reason does not forbid its rest. What is found raised to the highest depth of the air, the estimation of its lowering is not far-fetched. What revolves on the orbital sphere, the supposition of its rotation away from its path is not far-fetched. The arrangement of the planets in their forms is permissible to be contrary to their shapes and states. Thus it becomes clear with the slightest reflection that the import of possibility (muqtada al-jawaz) continues over all of them. Whatever is established as possible, judgment of its necessity is impossible. It is not permissible in the mind of a successful person to believe in eternalness without specification, while it is possible and not impossible to estimate it contrary to what it is. So if the judgment of possibility is incumbent on the world, the judgment of its eternalness is impossible. And it is established that it is in need of an effector (muqtaḍin) that necessitated it as it is. Only that which reason judges as necessary is independent of an effector, being self-sufficient in its existence and necessity without a necessitator. As for what is established as possible and the aspects of possibility conflict in it, it is impossible for it to occur coincidentally on one of those aspects without a necessitator. If it is said: What do you find objectionable against one who claims that the world and all it contains is eternal, having no beginning of its being nor opening of its existence, due to its specification as it is by an eternal necessitator?

Commentary
This passage presents Juwayni's argument for the temporality (hadath) of the world. He begins by defining the world as every existent other than God, comprising bodies and accidents. Then he argues that all parts of the world are subject to the judgment of possibility (jawaz): they can be imagined differently in shape, movement, rest, position, etc. This shows that possibility (i.e., not being necessary) is a constant feature of all worldly entities. If something is possible, it cannot be judged as necessary, and therefore cannot be eternal (qadim) because eternalness implies necessity. Hence the world must be originated and in need of an effector (muqtaḍin) that determines its existence in one particular state among many possibilities. Only what is necessary in itself is independent of an external cause. The passage concludes by raising an objection: what if someone claims the world is eternal because an eternal necessitator specified it? The answer is presumably continued on the next page. Juwayni's proof relies on the observation of contingency and the principle that the possible requires a determinant.

Why a Necessary Cause Cannot Create the World
Juwayni argues that a necessary cause cannot specify, requiring a free agent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a necessary cause without choice (mujib) cannot account for the world's specific features, and why a free agent (mu'aththir mukhtar) is necessary.

Core Idea
A blind cause cannot choose; only a free will can specify.

Juwayni presents a crucial argument for the necessity of a freely acting Creator. He argues that if the world's cause were a blind necessitator, it would produce all similar effects equally, but we see specification; therefore, the cause must have will. This passage sets the foundation for understanding divine agency in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Notice the structure: a premise, a division into two possibilities, a proof against the first, and a conclusion.
Juwayni uses a condensed style; take your time.
2
Identify the two options for the determinant
Juwayni divides the possible determinant of the world into two: a necessitator (mujib) without choice, or an effective free-chooser (mu'aththir mukhtar). Understand these terms.
The first option is the target of his refutation.
3
Understand the argument against a necessitator
Follow Juwayni's reasoning: a blind cause cannot specify one thing among its likes. He illustrates with the naturalist example of a medicine attracting yellow bile. Ask: why would a blind cause attract one part and not another similar part?
The example shows the absurdity of a blind cause producing specific effects.
4
Apply the same logic to the world's location
Juwayni extends the argument: the universe occupies a specific region of space, but all directions are similar. A blind necessitator would have no reason to place it here rather than there, so a free agent is required.
This is the key application.
5
Conclude with the necessity of a free agent
The passage ends by stating that the impossibility of a necessitator without choice is established, so the determinant must be a free agent with will.
This is the desired conclusion.

فنقول - والله المستعان وعليه التكلان - اذا بطل ثبوت الجائزات من غير مقتضى قسمنا الكلام وراء ذلك . وقلنا : مقتضى العالم لا يخلو اما أن يكون موجبا من غير ايثار واختيار ، وأما أن يكون مؤثرا مختارا فان كان موجبا من غير ايثار كان ذلك مستحيلا • فان الموجب الذى لا يؤثر يستحيل أن يقتضى شيئا دون مماثلة ، وهذا يتضح بأن نضرب فاسد مذهب الطبائعى مثلا . فنقول : اذا قال : من ينتحل القول بآثار الطبائع : ان دواء مخصوصا يجذب المرة الصفراء دون غيرها من الاخلاط يستحيل عنده أن يجذب جزءا من المرة من القطر ، ولا يجذب جزءا آخر فى مثل ) ذلك القطر ( بعد ذلك المجذوب مع ارتفاع المواقع ، واستواء الأحوال .. هذا محال تخيله . واذا تقرر ذلك قلنا : العالم بحملته قار في جو معلوم ، وتقديره واقعا في ذلك الخلاء يماثل تقديره في خلاء عن اليمين أو عن الشمال .. وهذا يقرب من مدارك البدائة ، واذا تماثلت الأحياز والجهات ، استحال اعتقاد موجب يخصص العالم بقطر تمثله سائر الأقطار ، فان الموجب لا يخصص شيئا من أمثاله ، والمؤثر المختار : هو الذى يتحيز بارادته ومشيئته مثلا من أمثال . فلاح بطلان المصير الى موجب قديم لا اختيار له .
We say—and God is the seeker of help and upon Him is reliance—when it is invalid that contingent things exist without a determinant, we divide the discussion beyond that. And we say: the determinant of the world cannot be but either a necessitator (mūjib) without preference and choice, or an effective free-chooser (muʾaththir mukhtār). If it is a necessitator without preference, that is impossible. For the necessitator that does not choose cannot necessitate something without likeness. This becomes clear by giving as an example the corrupt doctrine of the naturalists. We say: if one who advocates the doctrine of the effects of natures says that a particular medicine attracts yellow bile to the exclusion of other humors, it is impossible in his view that it attracts a part of the bile from a region and does not attract another part in a similar region after that part is attracted, with the locations being elevated and the conditions equal—this is inconceivable. And when that is established, we say: the universe with its entirety is positioned in a known space, and its being located in that void is similar to its being located in a void to the right or to the left. This is close to intuitive understanding. And when the spaces and directions are similar, it is impossible to believe in a necessitator that specifies the world in one region while other regions are similar to it; for the necessitator does not specify any of its likes. The effective free-chooser is the one that distinguishes by His will and volition, for example, one of the likes. Thus the conclusion to a pre-eternal necessitator without choice is shown to be false.

Commentary
This passage is a proof demonstrating the impossibility of a necessary cause (mūjib) without choice for the existence of the world. Juwayni begins by stating that since contingent things cannot exist without a determinant (muqtaḍā), we must consider the nature of that determinant. It is either a necessitator that acts without preference (i.e., a blind cause) or an effective free agent (muʾaththir mukhtār). He then argues that a necessitator without preference cannot produce a specific effect among equally possible alternatives; it would necessitate all similar outcomes equally. To illustrate, he gives the example of a naturalist who claims a medicine attracts yellow bile specifically. If the cause were blind, it would attract all similar bile in all similar conditions, which is not observed; therefore, such a cause is impossible. Applying this to the world: the universe occupies a specific location in space (an imagined void), but all directions are equivalent. A blind necessitator would have no reason to place it in one region rather than another. Hence, the necessitator cannot specify; only a free agent can specify by His will. Thus, the notion of a pre-eternal necessitator without choice is refuted.

Why an Eternal World Cannot Be an Act of Will
Juwayni refutes the claim that the world is eternal yet from a free agent and establishes its temporal origination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the world cannot be both pre-eternal and an act of will, and how this leads to the proof of its temporal origination.

Core Idea
Will implies bringing something into being from non-being; an eternal thing cannot be willed.

This passage tackles a key objection against the temporal origination of the world: could it be eternal yet created by God's will? Juwayni shows that the very concept of will excludes the eternal. Understanding this clarifies why the world must have a beginning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the opening objection: some claim the world is pre-eternal (qadīm) yet produced by a free agent. Juwayni declares this absolutely false.
Notice the objection is given as a hypothetical 'if it is said'.
2
Grasp Juwayni's refutation
Juwayni explains that will (irāda) only applies to something that comes into being after not having been. An eternal thing never began, so it cannot be an object of will. Therefore, an eternal world cannot be an act of will.
Key insight: 'what is placed and specified, which was not and then came to be, is what is intended by will.'
3
See the conclusion
Having refuted both blind necessity and free agency for an eternal world, the only remaining possibility is that the world is temporal (ḥādith), brought about by the will of a free agent. Juwayni praises this argument as stronger than other proofs.
This argument is foundational for the proof of the world's temporal origination.

فان قيل : العالم قديم، وموجبه مؤثر مختار . قلنا : هذا باطل قطعا .. فان القديم يستحيل أن يكون ثبوته بارادة ، اذ الموقع المخصص الذي لم يكن فكان . هو المراد . فأما ما لم يزل واقعا ، فيستحیل ارتباط كونه بارادة في الايقاع. ا، ١٧ وعلى الجملة : الواقع بالارادة فعل يؤثره المريد فيوقعه على حسب ارادته وما كان ثابتا أزلا فليس فعلا ، حتى يقال : وقع بالارادة على هذا الوجه . فاذا فسد القول بقدم العالم مع ظهور الجواز في أحكامه ، من غير موجب ومؤثر . بطل كونه قديما عن موجب قديم ، واستحال استناده قدمه الى ارادة . لم يبق الا القطع بأن العالم فعل موقع على وجه من وجوه الجواز ، بارادة مؤثر مختار أوقعه على مقتضى مشيئته .. وهذا الفصل فى اثبات حدث العالم أنجح وأوقع ، من طرق خوتها هو خير مجلدات ، وهو الفاهمه من الدنيا بحذافيرها ، لو ساوقه التوفيق .
If it is said: the world is pre-eternal (qadīm), and its necessitator is an effective free-chooser. We say: this is absolutely false. For the pre-eternal cannot have its existence by will, because that which is placed and specified, which was not and then came to be, is what is intended by will. As for that which has never ceased to be, it is impossible for its existence to be connected to will in bringing about. In summary: that which occurs by will is an act that the willing agent effects and brings about according to His will, and what is eternally established is not an act, such that it could be said to occur by will in this manner. So when the claim of the pre-eternity of the world is corrupted, given the appearance of contingency in its properties, without a necessitator and an effective agent, it is invalid that it be pre-eternal from a pre-eternal necessitator, and impossible to attribute its pre-eternity to will. Nothing remains but the certainty that the world is an act brought about in one of the modes of possibility, by the will of an effective free-chooser who brought it about according to His volition. And this chapter concerning proving the temporal origination (ḥudūth) of the world is more effective and more cogent than methods whose emptiness is... it is the best of volumes, and it is the one that comprehends the whole world, if success accompanies it.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer followed by a conclusion. The objection claims that the world could be pre-eternal (qadīm) yet produced by a free agent. Juwayni refutes this categorically. He explains that the concept of will (irāda) applies only to something that is brought into existence after non-existence. A pre-eternal being that never began cannot be the object of will, because will implies bringing about something that was not. Therefore, if the world were eternal, it could not be an act of will. Thus, the hypothesis of an eternal world from a free will is self-contradictory. Having already shown that the world cannot be from a blind necessary cause, and now showing that it cannot be from a free agent if eternal, the only remaining position is that the world is temporal (ḥādith), brought into existence by the will of a free agent. Juwayni then praises this argument as superior to other proofs for the temporal origination of the world.

Proof of the World's Createdness from Will and Contingency
Juwayni argues for the world's createdness via will and contingency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Juwayni's argument that the world's contingency proves it must be created by a willing agent, not an eternal cause.

Core Idea
Contingency demands a willed act, not just an eternal cause.

Juwayni presents a powerful proof for the temporal creation of the world based on the concepts of will and contingency. He first clarifies what it means for something to be an act of will, then shows why the world's contingent states cannot be explained by an eternal necessitating cause. This argument establishes that the world must be the act of a free chooser.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice Juwayni's compressed style and the logical flow.
2
Understand the definition of act by will
Juwayni begins by defining 'act by will' as something brought about according to the agent's will. Contrast this with what is eternally fixed, which cannot be an act in this sense.
This sets the groundwork: only originated things can be acts of will.
3
See why eternity is invalid given contingency
The world shows contingency (jawāz) in its states, meaning it could be other than it is. If it were eternal from an eternal cause, it would be necessary, not contingent. This contradiction invalidates the claim of eternity.
Contingency is the key evidence against an eternal necessitating cause.
4
Grasp the necessity of a willing agent
Since the world cannot be eternal from an eternal cause, and its eternity cannot be attributed to will (since will pertains only to originated acts), the only conclusion is that the world is a contingent act produced by the will of an efficient agent who chooses freely.
Juwayni concludes that a willing agent (mukhtār) is the only possible explanation.

Core Idea
The world's temporality forces its Maker to be a free chooser, not a blind necessitator.

In this passage, Juwayni builds on the previously established temporality of the world to argue that its agent must be a free chooser (mukhtar). The Burhan al-Mudabbir: Proving a Necessary Existence
Juwayni presents the argument from the director of the world to prove a necessary existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical structure of Juwayni's argument from the director of the world to prove the existence of a necessary being.

Core Idea
The world's director must be necessary, or we fall into circularity or infinite regress.

This lesson presents one of the most elegant proofs in kalam: the Burhan al-Mudabbir. Imam al-Juwayni argues that the director of the world cannot be a contingent being, because that would lead to an infinite chain or circular dependency—both impossible. The only rational conclusion is that the director is necessary in Himself.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the three possibilities Juwayni considers.
2
Identify the logical fork
The argument presents a conditional: if the director is necessary, done; if not, then He is possible and requires an effecter.
3
Understand the two impossible outcomes
If the director is possible, the chain of effecters must end. Otherwise we get dawr (circularity) or tasalsul (infinite regress), both rejected.
4
See the conclusion
The only valid possibility is that the director is necessary of existence in Himself, which is the sought God.

Commentary
This passage presents the Burhan al-Mudabbir (the argument from the director of the world). It is a proof for the existence of a necessary being (wajib al-wujud). The argument begins with the observation that the world has a director (mudabbir). If that director is necessary in Himself, then He is the sought God. But if He is possible (mumkin), then He must have an effecter that brings Him into existence. This second scenario leads either to an infinite chain (tasalsul) or a circular dependency (dawr), both of which are impossible. Therefore, one must eventually arrive at a first effecter that is necessary in His own essence. This is a classic kalam argument for the existence of a First Cause.

General and Specific Provision: Definitions and Quranic Proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Juwayni's distinction between general provision (rizq 'āmm) and specific provision (rizq khāṣṣ) and the Quranic evidence for each.

Core Idea
Provision comes in two forms: general sustenance for all and specific portion earned through effort.

Juwayni begins by clarifying a key concept in the theology of sustenance: there is a difference between the universal provision Allah has placed in the earth and the particular provision that depends on human striving. This distinction is not merely academic; it shapes how we understand the relationship between divine decree and human responsibility.
