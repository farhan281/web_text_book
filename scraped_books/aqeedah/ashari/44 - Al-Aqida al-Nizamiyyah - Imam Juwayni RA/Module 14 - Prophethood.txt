The Barahima's Dilemma: Reason vs. Revelation
Juwayni presents the Barahima's first dilemma and responds.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Barahima's first objection to prophethood and Juwayni's response showing that prophets bring matters reason cannot independently discover, such as promise and threat.

Core Idea
Prophets bring what reason does not deny but cannot discover alone.

This lesson examines the first argument of the Barahima, a group that accepted God but denied prophethood. They posed a dilemma: if prophets contradict reason, reject them; if they agree, reason suffices. Juwayni responds by clarifying the unique role of revelation in providing details of divine promise and threat that reason cannot attain. By studying this passage, you will learn how classical theologians defended the necessity of prophethood while honoring reason's role.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it, then read the translation. Let Juwayni's own argumentative structure come through before you interpret.
Notice the concise style and logical sequence.
2
Identify the Barahima's dilemma
Find the two horns of the dilemma: if prophets come with what contradicts reason, they are rejected; if they come with what agrees, reason is sufficient, making their sending futile.
This is a classic logical dilemma designed to disprove the need for prophets.
3
Understand Juwayni's response
Juwayni argues that prophets bring matters that reason does not deny but also cannot discover on its own—specifically, the promise and threat (wa'd wa wa'id) that form the basis of divine law. Reason cannot perceive these details.
The key phrase is 'la tankiru wa la tahtadi' (do not deny but cannot guide).
4
Consider the additional arguments
Juwayni adds that prophets can serve as reminders of rational truths, and he draws an analogy from creation: just as extra wonders beyond proof of the Maker are not futile, so prophets are not futile even if some of their message aligns with reason.
This strengthens the response by addressing the accusation of futility.
5
Reflect on the lesson's core idea
Ask yourself: How does Juwayni's response preserve both the necessity of revelation and the validity of reason? Write down one sentence summarizing the key insight.
Connect this to the learning objective.

Juwayni's Response to the Barahima's Denial of Prophethood
Juwayni presents the Barahima's objection and gives the first response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Juwayni refutes the Barahima's dilemma that prophethood is either irrational or redundant.

Core Idea
Prophethood is necessary because reason cannot know the details of divine reward and punishment.

The Barahima, a group that affirmed a Creator but denied prophethood, argued that prophets are either irrational or unnecessary. Imam al-Juwayni responds by showing that prophets bring knowledge that reason alone cannot attain—specifically, the details of promise and threat (al-wa'd wa al-wa'īd) that form the basis of divine law. This lesson examines his concise but powerful rebuttal.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation, focusing on the Barahima's dilemma as presented by Juwayni. The dilemma is: if prophets come with something contradicting reason, they are rejected; if they come with something agreeing with reason, reason suffices and sending them is futile.
This dilemma is the core challenge that Juwayni must answer.
2
Identify Juwayni's key response
Notice Juwayni's first statement: 'They only came with what intellects do not deny nor fully grasp.' This is the foundational point—prophets bring matters that reason neither rejects nor can independently discover.
The phrase 'لا تنكره العقول ولا تهتدي' is crucial.
3
Understand the basis of divine law
Juwayni specifies that the basis of laws (manāṭ al-sharā'i') is promise and threat (al-wa'd wa al-wa'īd). Reason cannot comprehend these details on its own; they require revelation.
Even if reason grasps universal benefits, it cannot know their specific applications without prophecy.
4
Consider the broader implications
Juwayni adds that even if prophets only confirmed rational truths, it would not be futile, as reminding is beneficial. He also points out that the wonders of creation beyond what is necessary to prove the Creator are not futile—similarly, prophethood has purpose beyond immediate rational need.
These extra arguments strengthen the overall response.

Responding to the Barahima's Objections: The Rationality of Prophetic Commands
Juwayni responds to Barahima's objections about slaughter, prostration, and similar commands.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Juwayni defends the seemingly irrational commands of the prophets by appealing to God's actions and the inapplicability of human moral categories to God.

Core Idea
What God does is not repugnant, so what He commands cannot be repugnant either.

The Barahima (Brahmins) argued that certain prophetic commands—like slaughtering harmless animals or prostrating in a humiliating posture—are repugnant to reason and thus cannot be from God. Juwayni refutes this by drawing analogies with God's own actions and by reminding us that moral classifications apply only to beings that can be harmed or benefited. This lesson unpacks his arguments.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Barahima's objection

The Barahima claim these are irrational because they seem purposeless or degrading.
2
See Juwayni's first analogy (slaughter)
Juwayni argues that God Himself destroys animals without cause, so commanding slaughter is not repugnant. Compare the logic: if God's action is not repugnant, His command of similar action is also not repugnant.
3
See his second analogy (prostration posture)
If God created a person in a prostrating position without cover, that action would not be repugnant. The same principle applies to the command to prostrate.
This shows that the posture itself is not inherently degrading when created by God.
4
Grasp the underlying principle
Juwayni states that actions are only classified as good or bad for beings that are harmed or benefited. God is above such classification, so what He does or commands cannot be judged by human moral standards.
This is the key doctrinal point: moral categories do not apply to God.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Juwayni ends by saying that if his argument is persuasive, no further objection needs a response. Consider why this closure is important in theological debate.

Definition of Prophethood and the Proof of Miracles
Juwayni defines prophethood and outlines the proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Juwayni's definition of prophethood as a divine designation and the role of miracles as the proof of its occurrence.

Core Idea

Miracles: Definition and Conditions
Juwayni defines miracles and lists their conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of miracles, their conditions, and why they must be divine acts that are extraordinary.

Core Idea
Miracles are divine acts, not attributes, and must be extraordinary to prove prophethood.

Juwayni begins by explaining that the term 'miracle' is used metaphorically; the truly miraculous is the Creator of inability. He then sets out the conditions that make a true miracle, ensuring it is a divine act, not an eternal attribute, and extraordinary. In this lesson, you'll learn these conditions and their significance.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the literal translation. Let Juwayni's own words establish the framework.
Notice the phrase 'tawassu'an wa tajawwuzan' (extension and metaphor).
2
Identify the two main conditions
The passage lists conditions for a miracle. Find the condition that the miracle must be an act of Allah or in the meaning of an act, and the condition that it must be extraordinary. Note the subcondition that it cannot be an eternal attribute.
The text says 'minhā' (among them) then lists conditions.
3
Understand why miracles cannot be eternal attributes
Juwayni argues that eternal attributes like knowledge and power have no specificity; they cannot distinguish the truthful from the liar. Therefore, a claim that an attribute is one's miracle is impossible.
This reinforces that miracles are created acts, not uncreated attributes.
4
Comprehend the requirement of being extraordinary
A miracle must be something that breaks the normal course of events (khāriq lil-‘āda). If it were customary, it could be produced by both truthful and false claimants, so it would not be a distinguishing sign.
Juwayni says clarity suffices from elaboration.
5
Reflect on the phrase 'or in the meaning of an act'
Juwayni gives an example: a prophet claims that Allah prevents people from standing. This is not a physical act but a ruling, considered 'in the meaning of an act'. Understand this expands the scope of divine acts.
This shows that miracles can include divine prohibitions or rulings.

The Extraordinary Nature of Miracles and Distinguishing Them from Hidden Sciences
Juwayni states the second condition of miracles, presents an objection, and answers by dividing miracles and showing how true miracles defy attribution to hidden causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a miracle must be extraordinary and how Juwayni decisively distinguishes genuine miracles from extraordinary acts that could arise from hidden knowledge or natural properties.

Core Idea
True miracles are so extraordinary that they cannot be explained by hidden sciences or natural properties.

Juwayni has already established that miracles must challenge claims of prophethood. Now he adds the second condition: they must break the usual pattern of nature. But what if someone performs an impressive act that might be due to secret knowledge or a rare natural property? Juwayni anticipates this objection and provides a rigorous criterion to distinguish true miracles from merely unusual phenomena.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed explanation of how a miracle (mu'jizah) serves as evidence for the truthfulness of a prophet. Juwayni begins by distinguishing the indication of a miracle from the ordinary indication of an action on its agent: an action naturally points to its agent, but a miracle's special role is to confirm a claim. He stresses that a supernatural event (khāriq li-l-ʿādah) performed by Allah without being linked to a claim does not automatically confirm truthfulness. The key is that the miracle takes the place (manzilat) of a direct verbal declaration of confirmation from God. To illustrate, he presents a parable of a king and a messenger. When it occurs exactly as requested, it is as if Allah said 'You have spoken truthfully; you are My messenger.' Juwayni concludes that this reasoning is grasped by the necessities of intellects (ḍarūrāt al-ʿuqūl), meaning it is a self-evident rational inference.

Commentary
This passage presents the proof of sarf (divine prevention) as the basis for the Quran's inimitability. Juwayni begins by stating that people are prevented from producing anything similar to the Quran, and that this is a more profound miracle than the mere breaking of natural norms by extraordinary acts. He then notes that opponents of the Quran's inimitability either claim it lacks eloquence or, if they admit its eloquence, argue it is not extraordinary in composition. Juwayni counters that regardless of their objections, the fact that God has turned people away from producing its like is more powerful. He explains that the closer speech is to being easily imitated, the more likely it would be challenged; since no challenge occurred despite strong motives, the only explanation is divine prevention. He likens this to a prophet claiming his miracle is the inability of people to stand up at a given time, despite their ability. Juwayni then argues that anyone seeking to prove the Quran's inimitability through its rhetorical qualities alone is misguided, because such qualities are within human capability; the true miracle is that people are prevented by God from imitating it. He calls this section the conclusion of the creed and says it proceeds in rational approaches. He concludes that this argument is more profound than even the miracle of transforming a staff, because one might attribute that to the prophet's special knowledge, whereas the Quran was uttered by an illiterate man and no one can produce its like, which can only be explained by divine prevention.

Our statement is the truth because the descriptions of this mentioned prophet are similar to [those of] Moses. And the Torah has stated that no prophet will appear among the children of Israel like Moses, and since the Torah has stated a blessing for the nations in the lineage of Ishmael, peace be upon him, then this prophet must be from him. As we have mentioned in books we have verified... including Shifa' al-Ghalil on explaining what occurred in the Torah and Gospel of alteration, by Imam al-Juwayni.

Commentary
This passage presents the argument from previous scriptures (akhbar al-anbiya') as a proof of Muhammad's prophethood. Despite the distortion (tahrif) of the Torah, prophecies remain that point to a future prophet. The Jews interpret them as still future, Christians as Jesus, but Muslims as Muhammad. The author argues that the Islamic interpretation is correct because the descriptions of the promised prophet match Moses (like the phrase 'a prophet like Moses' from Deuteronomy 18:15,18). Furthermore, the Torah states that no prophet like Moses would arise in Israel, and that blessing would come through Ishmael's lineage. Therefore, the prophet must be from Ishmael (i.e., Muhammad). The author references his own verified works, including Juwayni's 'Shifa' al-Ghalil' on alterations in the Torah and Gospel.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Many Christians believe the Torah prophecies exclusively refer to Jesus Christ.
Correction: The passage shows that the promised prophet is described as 'like Moses' (a lawgiver and leader), and from the lineage of Ishmael, not Isaac. Jesus was not a lawgiver in the same manner and was from Isaac's line, so the prophecies fit Muhammad better.

Torah prophecies, despite distortion, point to Muhammad as the promised prophet based on the criteria of being 'like Moses' and from Ishmael.
