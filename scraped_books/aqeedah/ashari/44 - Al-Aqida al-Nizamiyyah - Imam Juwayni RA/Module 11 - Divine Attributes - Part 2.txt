The Mu'tazila Position: Attributes Are Identical to Essence
Juwayni presents the Mu'tazila stance that attributes are identical to essence to avoid multiple eternals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila view that God's attributes are identical to His essence, and their argument that separate attributes would imply multiple eternal beings.

Core Idea
Mu'tazila: God is one eternal; adding attributes creates multiple eternals.

Juwayni then presents the Mu'tazila position with a respectful prayer, showing their core concern: maintaining absolute divine unity. They argue that if attributes were additional and separate, they would be co-eternal with God, leading to polytheism. Instead, attributes are within the essence, making God one eternal being described by all perfections.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazila passage
Read the Arabic passage describing the Mu'tazila view and their argument against additional attributes.
Notice the phrase 'may God have mercy on them' indicating respect despite disagreement.
2
Understand the logical problem
The Mu'tazila argue that if attributes are separate from essence, then both God and the attribute are eternal, implying two eternals. This violates tawhid.
Their solution: attributes are within the essence, so only one eternal exists.
3
Contrast with Ash'ari view
Compare this to the Ash'ari position: both reject separation, but Ash'aris say attributes are additional in meaning yet inseparable; Mu'tazila say they are identical.
This is a key point of divergence in kalam.

Commentary
This passage presents the core debate in Islamic theology regarding God's attributes (ṣifāt). Juwayni first frames the question: Are God's attributes identical to His essence (dhāt) or are they additional (zāʾida) yet inseparable? He notes that some hold the former view, arguing that 'knowledge' is just the state of being knowing, and will is identical to volition. This contrasts with Fakhr al-Din al-Rāzī, who held attributes are additional, necessary through God, but possible in themselves. Juwayni then outlines the Ashʿarī position: God has real attributes that are additional to His essence—He is knowing through knowledge, powerful through power, willing through will. Their strongest proof is a logical dilemma: if knowledge were identical to essence and power also identical to essence, then knowledge would be identical to power, collapsing the distinction between them—something absurd because we distinguish knowledge from power. The Ashʿarī then address an objection: we observe one person possessing distinct attributes (knowledge, will, power) that manifest independently, yet they are not separate from him; they are latent in his person. A viewer sees one person, not many. The same applies to God. Next, Juwayni presents the Muʿtazilī position (with the phrase 'may God have mercy on them' indicating respect despite disagreement). They hold that God's attributes are within His essence—He is the same before and after creation. They reject additional attributes because both God and attribute would be eternal, implying multiple eternals if the attribute is separate. Instead, they affirm one eternal described with all perfections. This summary captures the key soteriological and metaphysical stances of the two schools.

The Eternal Speech of God: Juwayni's Analogy
Juwayni defends the eternality of God's speech using the analogy of human planning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Juwayni uses the analogy of a person planning speech to demonstrate that God's attribute of speech is eternal and subsisting in His essence.

Core Idea
God's speech is like a plan in the mind—eternal and not created.

This passage presents one of the most intuitive analogies in Ash'ari theology. Juwayni invites us to reflect on our own experience of planning what to say. The meanings we intend are present in our minds before we utter a single word. He then applies this to God, arguing that His speech—the meanings He will convey to creation—subsists eternally in His essence, just as His power subsists without beginning.

Guided Path
1
Read the Juwayni passage
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice the structure: a human analogy followed by its divine application.
The passage is dense; focus on the flow from human to divine.
2
Identify the human analogy
Juwayni describes a person who resolves to speak to a friend after a month. The meanings of that future speech are already present in his soul. Find this and consider why it matters.
The word 'نفس' (nafs) here means soul or self, not breath.
3
Apply the analogy to God
Juwayni extends the analogy to God: just as the human planner has meanings in his soul, God in His pre-eternity has the meanings of what He will say to His servants. Note how Juwayni emphasises God's freedom from forgetfulness.
This step is the core of the argument.
4
Understand the conclusion
Juwayni concludes that God's speech subsisting in Himself is like His eternal power—neither begins nor ceases. Reflect on how this defends the Ash'ari view of an eternal Qur'an.

Commentary
This passage is a theological argument defending the Ashʿarī view that God's attribute of speech (kalām Allāh) is eternal and subsisting in His essence. Juwayni begins with an analogy: a person who plans to converse with a friend after a month already has the meanings of that speech present in his mind, and when the time comes, he delivers them. Similarly, the knower is not devoid of that intended speech in his soul. God, in His pre-eternity, knew He would address His servants when they exist, and He is free from forgetfulness, so His eternal existence includes the meaning of what will reach them. Juwayni then explicitly states that this speech subsisting in itself (al-kalām al-qāʾim bi-nafsih) is like His eternal power (al-qudra al-qadīma) which has never ceased (lam tazal). He rephrases the analogy: a human arranges speech in himself and then utters it; the human and his attributes are one. Likewise, God's attributes are eternal, and His speech subsists in His essence. Juwayni then addresses a possible objection to the phrase 'has not ceased' (lam yazal). He clarifies that he affirms a speech that never separates from the essence, as in his statement 'the eternal speech of God does not leave the essence nor depart from it.' He interprets this as referring to the ordered expression (al-naẓm) indicating the meaning realized linguistically and conceptually in God's knowledge eternally. The example of a person arranging speech in himself is given to show that there is no objection to this; it is like God's will and power preceding creation. When God creates, the created thing is originated (ḥādith). Similarly, if God says 'Be' (kun), it is like creation after it was an idea. God's power and speech never cease, as evidenced by the continuous appearance of His powers throughout time.

The Phrase 'لم يزل' and the Inseparability of God's Speech

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Juwayni insists on using the phrase 'لم يزل' to affirm that God's speech is an eternal attribute inseparable from His essence, similar to His will and power.

Core Idea
God's speech, like His will and power, is eternal and never ceases.

In discussing God's speech, Juwayni emphasizes that we must use the term 'لم يزل' to affirm its eternity. He argues that just as God's will and power are eternal attributes subsisting in His essence, so is His speech. This analogy clarifies that God's command 'Be' is not a temporal event but an eternal expression that brings about creation in time.

Paradise and Hell can be created before Judgment Day; the Qur'an's past tense indicates certainty.

Commentary

Juwayni notes that this expression could be a metaphor for immense vastness, not literal dimensions. A) Whom Allah wills to forgive, or whoever wills forgiveness for himself through repentance.
B) Only those who never sin, or only the prophets.
C) Those who ask for forgiveness, or those who perform good deeds.
D) Whom Allah wills to punish, or those who disbelieve.

Key Takeaway
Juwayni's interpretation shows that divine forgiveness is both a matter of God's will and open to human repentance, reflecting Ash'ari theological flexibility.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Juwayni presents two interpretations: one where forgiveness depends solely on God's will, and another where it is conditional on your repentance. Which interpretation do you actually live by when you sin? Do you postpone repentance, assuming God's will might forgive you anyway? Or do you rush to repent, knowing that your repentance is the channel through which His forgiveness flows? Be honest: what does your pattern of seeking forgiveness reveal about your real belief?
?
If you truly believed that Allah forgives 'whom He wills' and that He wills forgiveness for those who repent, how would that change the way you approach your worst sin tonight? Not in general—specifically, what step of repentance will you take before you sleep?
?
Juwayni allowed two meanings for the same Qur'anic phrase. Are you as flexible in your understanding of God's mercy? Do you judge others' chances of forgiveness based on your own narrow interpretation, forgetting that God's will and their repentance are both at play? How does this lesson challenge your assumptions about someone you have written off?
