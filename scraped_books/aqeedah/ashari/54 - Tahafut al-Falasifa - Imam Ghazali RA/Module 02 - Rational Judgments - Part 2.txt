Why We Don't Taste Intellectual Pleasure Now
Ghazali explains why intellectual pleasures are not felt now and how they will be experienced after death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why intellectual pleasures are not felt in this life due to bodily immersion, and how the soul can experience them after death or through purification.

Core Idea
Our immersion in vices blinds us to the soul's highest pleasures, which await us after purification.

In this passage, Ghazali addresses a potential objection: if the rational soul is so superior, why do we not feel its pleasures? He explains that our current state—immersed in bodily desires—acts like a sickness that prevents us from tasting true intellectual delight. Only by casting off the yoke of appetite and anger can we catch glimpses, and full experience comes after death.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let Ghazali's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the Arabic text ends mid-sentence, but the argument is still clear.
2
Identify the three reasons we don't feel intellectual pleasure
Find the causes Ghazali gives: immersion in vices, lack of purification from appetite and anger, and the body as a veil. Note how he compares us to a sick person who cannot taste sweetness.
These reasons build on earlier principles alluded to in the text.
3
Examine the common experience proof
Ghazali appeals to everyday choices: even base souls prefer honor, victory, or avoiding shame over immediate desires. This shows intellectual ends are naturally more honorable.
This is a key argument for the inherent nobility of intellectual goods.
4
Understand the afterlife hope
After death, the soul, if awakened in life, will seek its perfection. But if preoccupied with the body, it forgets its true beloved, like forgetting hunger when sick.
The passage ends abruptly, but the point is clear: we must train the soul now.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation and argument about the soul's current state. Ghazali first reaffirms that the rational soul is more perfect in itself and has a more intense perception, because it can grasp many objects, abstract essences, and penetrate both inner and outer aspects. He then addresses the apparent problem: if intellectual pleasures are so superior, why do we not feel them? The answer lies in the three principles established earlier: we are immersed in bodily vices, so we are like the sick person who cannot taste sweetness, or the animal that has no appetite. However, when we purify ourselves from appetite and anger, we get glimpses, especially in moments of intellectual discovery (حل المشكلات). Still, our experience is like smelling food compared to tasting it—vastly inferior. Ghazali then appeals to common experience: even worldly people often prefer intellectual goods (honor, shame, victory) over immediate sensual desires, proving the innate nobility of intellectual ends. But base souls are only aware of lowly things. Finally, Ghazali discusses the state after death: the soul, having been awakened in this life, will at separation from the body be drawn to its perfection. However, if it was preoccupied with the body, it forgets its true beloved, just as a sick person forgets the need for proper nourishment. The passage ends abruptly, but the point is that the soul must be trained to seek its true perfection.

The Five Modes of Existence and Their Role in Defining Tasdiq
Ghazali enumerates five modes of existence to clarify what constitutes tasdiq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the five modes of existence according to Ghazali and how they affect whether one is considered a denier or affirmier of prophetic reports.

Core Idea
Affirming existence in any of its five modes saves one from being labeled a denier.

Before you can judge whether someone truly denies a prophetic report, you must first understand what 'existence' can mean. Ghazali lists five distinct modes of existence, each of which makes an affirmation valid. The passage below introduces these modes and warns against the common mistake of accusing others of denial simply because they affirm a different kind of existence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage attentively
Start by reading the Arabic passage once through. Don't worry about details; just get a sense of the flow. Then read the English translation to confirm your understanding.
Notice that Ghazali begins by linking tasdiq to the acknowledgment of existence.
2
Identify the five modes of existence
As you re-read, list the five modes: essential (dhātī), sensory (ḥissī), imaginative (khayālī), intellectual ('aqlī), and analogical (shabahī). Pay attention to how each is defined.
The definitions are brief; each mode corresponds to a different way something can be 'present'.
3
Understand each mode's distinct reality
For each mode, ask: In what sense does the thing 'exist'? For example, sensory existence is an image in the eye without external reality, while intellectual existence is a meaning grasped by the mind.
Take special note of analogical existence: it is when the thing itself does not exist, but something similar does.
4
Reflect on the implications for tasdiq
Consider how this framework prevents hasty accusations of denial. If someone affirms a report in any of these five ways, they are not a mukadhdhib (denier). This was Ghazali's broader aim.
This reflection connects the lesson to real-world theological debates.

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration and explanation of the five modes of existence that determine whether one is considered a 'musaddiq' (affirmer) or 'mukadhdhib' (denier). It begins by stating that tasdiq fundamentally is acknowledging the existence of what the Prophet reported, but existence has five modes. Whoever affirms existence in any of these modes is not a denier. The five modes are: (1) Essential existence (wujud dhati): real, external existence independent of perception. (2) Sensory existence (wujud hissi): what appears to the senses (e.g., a mirage) but has no external reality. (3) Imaginative existence (wujud khayali): mental images of objects when they are absent. (4) Intellectual existence (wujud 'aqli): the abstract meaning of a thing, e.g., the 'hand' as power, not as a limb. (5) Analogical existence (wujud shabahi): when the thing itself does not exist in any mode, but something else resembling it exists. The author then notes that a full exposition would be lengthy and refers the reader to the original book (Faysal al-Tafriqa).

Ghazali's Refutation: Possibility as Mental Judgment
Philosophers argue for eternal matter; Ghazali responds by redefining possibility as mental judgment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali refutes the philosophical proof for the eternity of matter by redefining possibility as a mental judgment rather than a real attribute.

Core Idea
Possibility is not a thing in the world—it's a judgment of the mind.

The philosophers argue that the world's possibility is eternal and requires an eternal subject, matter. Ghazali responds by redefining possibility, impossibility, and necessity as purely mental categories. This cuts the ground from under the philosophers' argument, because mental judgments do not need to exist in external subjects.

Guided Path
1
Read the philosophers' proof carefully
Read the Arabic passage focusing on the philosophers' argument that possibility is an eternal relational attribute needing a subject. Understand their deduction that matter is eternal.
The proof hinges on the idea that possibility is a real attribute existing in something.
2
Identify Ghazali's redefinition
Notice how Ghazali turns the tables by defining possibility, impossibility, and necessity as mental judgments. These are not real attributes inhering in anything.
Ghazali's move is to shift the ontology from external reality to mental conception.
3
See how the refutation works
Ask yourself: if possibility is just a mental judgment, does it need an eternal subject outside the mind? The answer is no, so the philosophers' proof collapses.
This is a classic example of philosophical therapy—diagnosing a category mistake.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth philosophical proof and Ghazali's initial response. The philosophers argue that the possibility of the world is eternal. They add that possibility differs from being an object of power (مقدور عليه), since the latter is explained by the former. Whatever the mind can conceive without contradiction is 'possible'; what it cannot conceive without contradiction is 'impossible'; what it cannot conceive as non-existent is 'necessary'. These are mental categories, not real attributes needing an external subject.

Objection 1: Mental Judgment is Knowledge
First objection: mental judgment is knowledge, possibility is known.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first objection to Ghazali's theory that possibility is a mental judgment: that mental judgment is knowledge, possibility is something known, and knowledge and the known are distinct.

Core Idea
Possibility cannot be just a mental judgment because it is the object, not the act, of knowledge.

The philosophers object to Ghazali's reduction of possibility to a mental act. They argue that the mind's judgment is a form of knowledge (علم), whereas possibility is the thing known (معلوم). Knowledge and the known are different, so possibility cannot be identical to the mental judgment.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Note the three components: mental judgment is knowledge, possibility is known, knowledge is not the known.
2
Distinguish subject and object
Understand the distinction between the act of knowing (subject) and the thing known (object). The objection claims possibility belongs to the object side.
This is a classic epistemological distinction.
3
Connect to Ghazali's view
Recall that Ghazali argued possibility is a mental judgment. This objection says that makes it the act, not the object — but possibility is clearly an object of thought.

Commentary
This passage presents three objections to Ghazali's view that possibility is a mental judgment. Philosophers' Proof for Soul's Indivisibility and Ghazali's Objections
Philosophers' first proof for soul's indivisibility and Ghazali's two objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' argument that the soul is indivisible because it houses indivisible knowledge, and Ghazali's two objections that challenge the inference from indivisibility of knowledge to indivisibility of the soul.

Core Idea
Divisible bodies cannot host indivisible knowledge? Ghazali says: not so fast.

The philosophers claim that since intellectual knowledge contains indivisible concepts, the soul that hosts this knowledge must itself be indivisible. But Ghazali offers two incisive objections: first, why can't the soul be an indivisible atom? Second, even if we accept that inhering in a divisible body implies divisibility, cases like the sheep's perception of a wolf's enmity show that indivisible judgments exist in divisible bodies. This lesson unpacks both the proof and the objections.

Guided Path
1
Read the philosophers' proof carefully
Read the Arabic passage once. Note the premise: knowledge contains indivisible units. Conclusion: the soul's locus is indivisible.
Notice that the proof assumes that what contains indivisible parts must itself be indivisible.
2
Identify Ghazali's first objection
Ghazali asks: why reject that the locus is an indivisible atom? This challenges the assumption that the soul must be non-physical.
The atom (jawhar fard) is still a body but indivisible, so the conclusion does not rule out a physical soul.
3
Examine the second objection with the sheep and wolf example
The sheep's estimative power perceives the wolf's enmity as a single indivisible judgment, yet the sheep's body is divisible. This refutes the claim that all inherence implies divisibility.
This counterexample targets the underlying principle linking indivisibility of content to indivisibility of locus.
4
Reflect on the overall argument
Ghazali's objections undermine the proof's logic without necessarily denying the conclusion. The soul may still be indivisible, but not for the reasons given.
This is a classic example of refuting an argument rather than a thesis.

The sixth proof: They said: If the intellect perceived by a physical instrument like sight, it would not perceive its instrument, like the other senses, but it perceives the heart and brain and what is claimed to be its instrument, so it indicates that it does not perceive by a physical instrument. Al-Ghazali said: The answer to it is like the answer to the fifth proof. The seventh proof: The powers that perceive by physical instruments are subject to fatigue from persevering in action by continuous perception, while the intellect is opposite to this. Al-Ghazali said: We say: It is not unlikely that the physical senses differ in these matters. The eighth proof: All parts of the body weaken at forty years and after, while the intellectual power in most matters only strengthens after that. Al-Ghazali said: The objection is that we say: The decrease and increase of powers have many causes; some powers may strengthen at the beginning of life, some in the middle, and some at its end. The ninth proof: How can a human be the body with its accidents, while these bodies continually dissolve and food replaces what dissolves? Al-Ghazali said: This is refuted by the animal and tree, when comparing their state at bigness to their state at smallness. The tenth proof: We perceive from one thing both a particular matter and a universal matter. The particular is perceived by sense, and the abstract universal is perceived by intellect. Al-Ghazali said: The universal meaning that you have posited as inhering in the intellect is not conceded; rather, only that which does not inhere in sense inheres in the intellect.

Refutation of the Eighth Proof for Intellect's Immateriality
Ghazali refutes the eighth proof by pointing to varying developmental patterns of powers.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ghazali argues that different powers have different growth trajectories.

The philosophers note that the body weakens after forty, but intellectual power often strengthens after that, suggesting they are separate. Ghazali counters by pointing out that the increase and decrease of powers can vary; some powers peak early, others later.

3
Examine Ghazali's objection
Ghazali says increases and decreases have many causes; some powers strengthen early, some middle, some late.
