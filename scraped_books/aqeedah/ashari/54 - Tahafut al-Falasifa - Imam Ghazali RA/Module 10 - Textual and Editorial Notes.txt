Editor's Discovery of Ibn Sina's Manuscript
Editor recounts dissatisfaction with Ibn Sina and Ghazali and finds Ibn Sina's Risala Adhawiya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the editor's initial dissatisfaction with both Ibn Sina and al-Ghazali, and how discovering Ibn Sina's Risala Adhawiyya clarified the issue of bodily resurrection.

Core Idea
A chance manuscript can resolve long-standing scholarly confusion.

This passage reveals the editor's personal intellectual journey. He was troubled by the contradictions he saw between Ibn Sina's works and al-Ghazali's accusations. Only after finding a key manuscript did the pieces fall into place.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Notice the editor's personal tone and his description of his earlier views.
2
Identify the editor's initial characterizations
The editor describes Ibn Sina as hesitant and confused, and al-Ghazali as audacious and aggressive. Note these terms in Arabic: مضطرب متردد and مفتات متهجم.
These are strong judgments that the editor later revises.
3
Observe the turning point
The discovery of the Risala Adhawiyya changes everything. Note how the editor says he found it explicit in denying bodily resurrection and containing the same proof al-Ghazali mentioned.
This shows the importance of primary sources in resolving scholarly disputes.

Commentary
This passage is a personal reflection by the editor (likely Sulayman Dunya) on his earlier views. He explains that in the first edition he commented on the contradiction between Ibn Sina's 'an-Najah' and 'ash-Shifa', and on al-Ghazali's unclear position. He felt that Ibn Sina was hesitant and confused, while al-Ghazali was audacious and aggressive. However, he remained unsatisfied with this judgment until he discovered Ibn Sina's manuscript 'Risala Adhawiyya on the Matter of the Afterlife'. Reading it, he found it explicitly denies bodily resurrection and contains the same proof that al-Ghazali attributed to the philosophers in his 'Tahafut'. The passage then leads into the quotation from Ibn Sina.

Lexical Note on 'Ilb' (Confederacy)
Lexical explanation of 'ilb' meaning a single group.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the meaning and vocalization of the Arabic word 'ilb' as used in Ghazali's text.

Core Idea
A single 'ilb' means one unified group or confederacy.

In the main text, Ghazali uses the expression 'إلب واحد'. This lexical note clarifies that 'ilb' refers to a single group or confederacy, with two possible vowelizations. Understanding this term helps grasp the unity of the opposition Ghazali addresses.

Guided Path
1
Read the source
Read the Arabic note and its translation. Notice the two vowel points mentioned.
2
Understand the meaning
Recognize that 'ilb' means a single group, and that the phrase is used to describe the confederacy opposing the Prophet.
Consider why Ghazali might clarify this word—perhaps to emphasize the unity of the opponents.

(1) It is said: they are a single ilb, with a kasra on the hamza and fatha is a language [variant], meaning a single group.
Source pages p. 83

Commentary
This is a very brief lexical note found as a footnote on page 83. It explains the word 'إلب' used in Ghazali's main text. It states that the expression 'هم إلب واحد' means they are a single group or confederacy. The word can be vocalized with a kasra on the hamza (إلب) or a fatha (ألب), and it means 'a single collection' (جمع واحد).

Modern Science and Classical Views on the Heavens
Editorial note discussing modern science in relation to Islamic doctrine.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how modern scientific discoveries can challenge and overturn medieval philosophical assumptions about the nature of the heavens, and how this aligns with Islamic teachings.

Core Idea
Modern science corrects medieval errors and confirms religious truths.

This editorial footnote bridges classical Islamic thought with modern science. The author argues that medieval philosophers' belief in celestial incorruptibility is being disproven by space debris, and that such scientific advances actually support doctrines like the Prophet's ascension. The note ends by citing a Quranic promise that God's signs will be shown in the universe, affirming that truth ultimately aligns with revelation.

Guided Path
1
Read the editorial note carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to grasp the author's argument. Note that this is a modern commentary, not part of Ghazali's original text.
2
Identify the medieval belief being criticized
Find the specific claim that the substance of the heavens is incorruptible and does not accept 'tearing and patching'.
This belief was used to deny miracles like the ascension.
3
Understand the modern scientific counterexample
The note mentions space debris falling and burning as evidence that celestial matter can undergo corruption.
This directly contradicts the medieval view.
4
Reflect on the theological implication
The author claims these new scientific views are 'all in favor of religion' and quotes the Quran to emphasize that truth will be revealed.
Consider how science and religion can be complementary.

Discussion regarding His saying, the Exalted: {And Our command is but one, like the glance of the eye} and His saying, the Exalted: {And you will never find in the Sunnah of God any change}. (1) Thus the philosophers of the Middle Ages used to believe, and they thought that the substance of the heavens is of another kind that does not receive generation and corruption, and among their famous phrases is that it does not accept tearing and patching. Perhaps science today is on its way to falsifying this theory because the bodies that now fall from above, which science calls space debris or space junk, have undergone corruption by separating from their origin and burning. And indeed, the theory of no tearing and patching in the past obstructed many religious views, so they denied the ascension of the prophets to the heavens, and they denied that Paradise and Hell can be in the heavens... etc... etc... Indeed, science today is on its way to uncovering the falsehood of some scientific views that were once held as sacred... And it is worthy of consideration that these new views are all in favor of religion, and God the Almighty spoke truth when He says: {We will show them Our signs in the horizons and within themselves until it becomes clear to them that it is the truth}.

Commentary
This is an editorial note appended to the discussion. The editor connects the philosophical arguments about the nature of celestial matter to modern scientific developments. The editor argues that medieval philosophers (like those Ghazali refutes) assumed celestial matter was eternal and incorruptible, a view that modern science is disproving with examples of space debris. The editor claims that such scientific progress actually supports religious doctrines like the ascension of the prophets and the location of Paradise and Hell, contrary to earlier philosophical objections. The note ends by citing the Quranic verse promising that God's signs will be shown in the universe, affirming that truth aligns with divine revelation.
