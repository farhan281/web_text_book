Classification of Animal and Human Powers
Ghazali classifies animal powers and rational soul powers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' detailed classification of animal powers (moving and perceiving) and the two powers of the rational human soul (theoretical and practical).

Core Idea
The soul's powers are hierarchically classified: animal powers are bodily, rational powers are intellectual.

In this passage, al-Ghazali presents the philosophers' detailed classification of the powers that animate living beings. He distinguishes between animal powers—both moving and perceiving—and the human rational soul, which has theoretical and practical capacities. This taxonomy sets the stage for his critique of their proof regarding the soul's nature.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow, then read the English translation to understand the full classification.
Pay attention to the technical terms in Arabic; they will reappear in later discussions.
2
Identify the two main categories
Note that Ghazali first mentions the animal powers (القوى الحيوانية) and then the rational soul (النفس العاقلة). Each has its own subdivisions.
3
Subdivide animal powers
Animal powers are divided into perceiving (مدركة) and moving (محركة). Perceiving has external (five senses) and internal (three: imagination, estimation, thought). Moving has inciting (appetitive/irascible) and agent (nerves/muscles).
The internal perceiving power called 'mutakhayyila' in animals becomes 'mufakkira' in humans.
4
Analyze the rational soul
The rational soul has two powers: practical (عاملة) which moves the body toward crafts, and theoretical (عالِمة/نظرية) which perceives intelligibles.
5
Reflect on the purpose
Consider why Ghazali presents this classification: it prepares his argument that the philosophers cannot prove the soul is a spiritual substance solely through reason.
The classification shows that both animal and human powers are tied to the body, except the theoretical power.

The First Proof for the Soul's Immateriality and Its Refutation
Ghazali critiques the first philosophical proof for the soul's immateriality via two objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Ghazali refutes the first philosophical proof for the immateriality of the soul by exposing its flawed assumptions.

Core Idea
Ghazali deconstructs the proof by showing it confuses knowledge with physical properties.

The philosophers argue that because knowledge contains indivisible concepts, its locus must be indivisible—hence the soul is not a body. Ghazali challenges this on two fronts: first, why reject an indivisible atomic substance? Second, the sheep's perception of wolf enmity is indivisible, contradicting the claim that all states in a body are divisible. He then identifies the real problem: the assumption that knowledge is impressed in the body like color.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the structure: proof, then two objections, then a counter and final analysis.
2
Identify the philosophers' proof
Find the two premises: rational sciences contain indivisible units, and every body is divisible. The conclusion is that the soul is not a body.
3
Understand Ghazali's first objection
Ghazali asks: why reject the possibility that the locus of knowledge is an indivisible atomic substance? This undermines the need for a non-body.
4
Understand Ghazali's second objection
The sheep's estimative faculty perceives wolf enmity as a single indivisible judgment, showing that some states in a body are not divisible.
This refutes the claim that everything in a body is divisible.
5
Reflect on the final equivocation
Ghazali points out that the philosophers assumed knowledge is like color impressed in the body, but this is the point of confusion.
He exposes the flaw rather than denying the soul's immateriality.

The philosophers counter that this is a contradiction in rational matters, but Ghazali responds that his book is specifically written to expose such contradictions. He then identifies the true equivocation: the philosophers assume knowledge is like color—impressed in the body and divisible with it—but this is the point of confusion.

Ghazali's Refutation of the Third Proof
Ghazali refutes the third proof using the analogy of sense perception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali refutes the philosophers' third proof about knowledge and the locus of knowing, using the analogy of sense perception.

Core Idea
Attribution to the whole does not require the attribute to be in every part.

This passage presents the third philosophical proof that knowledge cannot be in a physical part, and Ghazali's sharp refutation. The philosophers argue that if knowledge were in a part, only that part would be called knower, but we call the whole man knowing. Ghazali calls this nonsense and gives a simple analogy: we say man sees, hears, and tastes even though these are localized in specific organs.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Notice the two parts: the philosophers' proof and Ghazali's response.
The philosophers' argument is short and Ghazali calls it 'haws' (nonsense).
2
Understand the philosophers' reasoning
Identify their premise: if knowledge were in a part, only that part would be the knower. Their conclusion: therefore knowledge cannot be in a part because we call the whole person 'knower'.
They assume that the attribute must apply to the specific locus.
3
Follow Ghazali's refutation
Ghazali provides a counterexample: we call a person 'seeing', 'hearing', 'tasting' even though these functions are performed by specific parts (eyes, ears, tongue). So the assumption is false.
This shows that attribution to the whole is common for localized faculties.

Ghazali's fourth proof: knowledge, ignorance, and the turning of the argument
Ghazali turns the fourth proof against the philosophers using the example of conflicting desires.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ghazali refutes the philosophers' fourth proof by showing that their own theory of desire leads to the same contradiction.

Core Idea
Ghazali turns the philosophers' own argument against them using desire and will.

In this passage, Ghazali presents the fourth proof of the philosophers, who argue that knowledge and ignorance cannot coexist in different parts of the heart or brain. Then he turns the argument back on them, showing that their own account of desires and will suffers from the same logical problem. This is a classic example of Ghazali's method of refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the philosophers' proof
Carefully read the first part where the philosophers argue that if knowledge inheres in a part of the heart/brain, then ignorance could inhere in another part, leading to the impossibility of simultaneous knowledge and ignorance of the same thing.
Notice the logical structure: if premise A (knowledge in a part), then possibility of opposite in another part, leading to contradiction.
2
Identify Ghazali's counter
Read Ghazali's response: he says this argument turns against them when applied to desire, longing, and will. These are also 'meanings impressed in the body' and yet it is impossible for aversion and inclination to coexist in the same subject at the same time.
Ghazali is not denying the impossibility; he is showing the same reasoning applies to the philosophers' own concepts.
3
Reflect on the refutation strategy
Think about why Ghazali's refutation is effective. He uses the philosophers' own principle (impossibility of opposites in the same subject at the same time) to challenge their account of emotions, which they also consider bodily.
This is a typical reductio ad absurdum.

These are also 'meanings impressed in the body', and yet it is impossible for a person to simultaneously have aversion and inclination toward the same thing. So if they accept that these opposites cannot coexist, they must admit that desire is not in a divisible part, which undermines their premise.

The Philosophers' Second Proof for the Soul's Eternity
The philosophers present their second proof for the soul's eternity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' second argument for the soul's eternity, which claims that any substance not in a subject cannot perish.

Core Idea
The second proof: what has no location cannot be destroyed.

The philosophers propose a logical argument: if the soul is a substance without a physical location, it cannot undergo annihilation. This lesson presents that proof before Ghazali's refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof statement
Read the Arabic and translation of the philosophers' proof. Let the directness of the claim sink in.
2
Analyze the key terms
Understand 'substance' (jawhar) and 'locus' (mahall). How does location relate to perishability?
The philosophers define substance as something that does not inhere in another.
3
Reflect on the implication
Why does the philosophers' proof depend on the idea that only things in a subject can perish? Consider examples from physical objects.

The second proof: their statement: 'Every substance (jawhar) that is not in a locus (lā fī maḥall) is impossible for it to become nonexistent.' Al-Ghazālī said in refutation of this proof: This is repelled by what we established regarding their denial of the possibility of the origination of matter and the elements, and the impossibility of their non-existence, in the two issues of the world's pre-eternity and its perpetuity. Commentary
This short passage presents the philosophers' second proof and Ghazali's refutation. The philosophers claim that any substance not in a subject (i.e., an immaterial substance like the soul) cannot cease to exist. Ghazali responds by referring back to his earlier arguments against the philosophers' denial of the possibility of matter's origination and annihilation in the discussions on the world's pre-eternity and perpetuity. He identifies the source of their confusion: they assume that possibility (imkān) requires a substrate (maḥall) to subsist in, which is a flawed conception. Since the issue is the same, he does not repeat the full argument, noting that it applies equally to material and soul substances.
