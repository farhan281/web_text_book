The Philosophers' First Miracle: The Imaginative Faculty
Ghazali reports the philosophers' theory of imaginative prophecy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' claim that the imaginative faculty is the source of prophetic knowledge and miracles, and how they distinguish between prophets and ordinary people in this regard.

Core Idea
The philosophers reduce prophecy to a powerful imagination accessing the Preserved Tablet.

Imam al-Ghazali presents the first of three miracles the philosophers accept: the imaginative faculty. They argue that when the imagination is strong and free from sensory distractions, it can directly access the Preserved Tablet and receive knowledge of future events. This lesson examines their claim and sets the stage for Ghazali's critique.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Note that Ghazali is reporting, not endorsing.
Pay attention to the phrase 'ولم يثبت الفلاسفة' which indicates reporting.
2
Identify the key claim
Find the condition for the imaginative faculty to access the Preserved Tablet. What must be true for it to work?
The condition involves the imagination dominating and the senses not being occupied.
3
Distinguish prophets and common people
Notice how this process applies to prophets while awake and to ordinary people only during sleep.
4
Reflect on the implication
Understand that this reduces prophecy to a natural imaginative power, which Ghazali will critique.
This is not Ghazali's own view; it is the philosophers' position.

Second Miracle: The Power of Intuition in the Theoretical Intellect
Ghazali describes intuition as a property of the theoretical intellect, its variations, and its culmination in prophecy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' concept of intuition as a natural faculty that reaches its peak in prophets, and how this is considered a miracle.

Core Idea
Intuition is the speed of transition from known to known; its ultimate form is prophecy.

In this passage, Ghazali presents the philosophers' second type of miracle: an extraordinary property of the theoretical intellect. He explains intuition as a rapid mental transition and describes how people vary in this ability. The peak of intuition is reached by prophets, who know all intelligibles without a teacher.

Guided Path
1
Read and define intuition
Read the passage carefully. Notice how Ghazali defines intuition as the speed of transition from known to known, and how it relates to the middle term in logic.
The definition is key to understanding the entire argument.
2
Identify the classification of people
The passage divides people into categories based on their intuitive ability. Find these categories and note the extremes: those with no intuition and those with perfect intuition.
This classification supports the possibility of prophetic intuition.
3
Connect to prophecy
See how the philosophers use this classification to argue that a prophet can know all intelligibles instantly. This is presented as a natural miracle, not a supernatural intervention.
The Quranic verse 'light upon light' is quoted to illustrate the prophet's luminous soul.

The Practical Soul's Power Over Nature
Ghazali presents the third miracle with examples and an argument for the soul's power over external nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali uses everyday psychological phenomena to argue that the practical soul can influence not only its own body but also external natural bodies.

Core Idea
Your imagination moves muscles—and perhaps even the world outside.

Ghazali continues his defense of miracles by presenting the philosophers' third type: the practical soul's power over nature. He begins with familiar examples of how our own thoughts and imaginations trigger physical responses in our bodies. These common experiences, he argues, reveal a general principle that bodies serve souls. From this, he extends the possibility to external nature, laying the groundwork for explaining extraordinary miracles.

Guided Path

3
Trace the argument
Ghazali uses these examples to show that bodies are created subservient to souls. Then he argues that if the soul can affect its own body, it can affect other bodies because the soul is not confined to a particular body.
Consider the logical leap from internal to external.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider how this argument provides a philosophical basis for miracles: extraordinary events are possible through strong souls.
This connects to the broader theme of Tahafut.

Philosophers Deny the Miracle of Ibrahim
Ghazali reports the philosophers' denial and their two justifications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophical objection to the miracle of Ibrahim (AS) and the two theoretical possibilities the philosophers propose to explain the impossibility of non-burning.

Core Idea
Miracles must be possible by altering nature, not by changing essence.

In this passage, Imam al-Ghazali presents the argument of the philosophers who deny the occurrence of the miracle of Ibrahim (AS) being thrown into the fire without burning. They insist that if fire remains fire, it must burn, and they propose only two ways to prevent burning, both of which they deem impossible. This sets the stage for Ghazali's refutation and defense of the miraculous.

Guided Path

Identify the philosophers' claim
The philosophers deny that Ibrahim could be in fire without burning while fire remains fire. Note their two proposed possibilities.
They only see two theoretical options.
3
Understand why both are impossible
Removing heat from fire would make it not fire; transforming Ibrahim into stone or fireproof material is also impossible in their view.
They argue that both options change the essence of the thing.
4
Reflect on the objection

This passage does not include Ghazali's refutation yet.

Pitch Analogy and Natural Acceleration
Ghazali gives two examples showing that unfamiliar events and acceleration of natural processes are within God's power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali employs empirical examples and a theory of matter transformation to argue that miracles are possible within God's power.

Core Idea
Familiarity does not determine possibility; God can accelerate natural processes to produce miracles.

Ghazali strengthens his case for miracles by pointing to real but unfamiliar phenomena, such as a person surviving fire after anointing with pitch. He then argues that natural transformations (dust to plant to blood to semen to animal) occur slowly by habit, but God can accelerate them instantly. This lesson examines both arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the pitch analogy
Read the first part of the passage describing a person who applies pitch and enters an oven without burning. Notice how Ghazali uses this to challenge denial of the unfamiliar.
This is an empirical counterexample to claims of impossibility.
2
Analyze the acceleration argument
Read the second part where Ghazali describes the slow transformation of elements into living beings. Understand his claim that God can speed up this process.
This shows that miracles are not violations of nature but accelerations of it.
3
Connect to the broader debate
Reflect on how both examples defend the possibility of miracles against philosophers who deny them based on secondary causality.
Ghazali argues that God's power is not limited by observed habits.

Commentary

He then extends the argument to resurrection and turning a rod into a snake, explaining that natural processes like transformation of elements into plants, into blood, into semen, into animal occur over time, and God can accelerate this process, resulting in a miracle.

Ignorance of Preparations Precludes Denial of Miracles
Ghazali argues that ignorance of preparations precludes denial of miracles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's argument that our inability to fully grasp preparations makes it impossible to rationally deny miracles, and that denial stems from narrow-mindedness.

Core Idea
Our ignorance of preparations makes denying miracles impossible.

This lesson presents Imam al-Ghazali's powerful rebuttal to those who dismiss miracles as impossible. He argues that since we cannot fully comprehend the principles of preparations (mabadi' al-istidadat) in bodies, we cannot logically assert that a miraculous transformation is impossible. Denial, he says, is a sign of intellectual limitation, not rational certainty.

This concept is central to the argument.

The Three Types of Miracles Acknowledged by Philosophers
Philosophers limit miracles to three: imaginative, intellectual, and practical.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three types of miracles that philosophers acknowledge according to al-Ghazali.

Core Idea
Philosophers reduce miracles to three innate human powers.

Al-Ghazali presents the philosophers' limited view of miracles. They accept only three types, each rooted in a different psychic faculty: imaginative, theoretical, and practical. This lesson breaks down these three categories.

Commentary
This passage enumerates the three types of miracles that the philosophers admit. The first involves the imaginative faculty (القوة المتخيلة): when it is strong and not preoccupied with sensory input, it can access the Preserved Tablet (اللوح المحفوظ) and receive images of future particular events. For prophets this occurs while awake, for others in dreams. The second involves the rational theoretical power (القوة النظرية العقلية), which is the power of intuition (الحدس) — a rapid transition from one known to another. The third involves the practical psychic power (القوة النفسية العملية), which can reach such intensity that it influences external things. These three are the only miracles the philosophers acknowledge, and they restrict miracles to internal psychic powers.
