The Possibility of a Second Creation
Ghazali argues for the possibility of a second creation after annihilation, dependent on two issues.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali’s argument for the possibility of a second order of creation after the annihilation of the universe, and the two foundational issues on which it depends.

Core Idea
A new creation is possible only if the world is temporal and habits can be broken.

In this passage, Ghazali presents a concise theological argument about what God can do after the end of the world. He imagines a scenario where God exists alone, then creates the world, then initiates a second order (Paradise), and finally annihilates everything except Himself. But he immediately notes that revelation tells us this second order is eternal. Ghazali then identifies two key issues that underpin the entire discussion: the temporal creation of the world and the breaking of habitual causal patterns. As you read, notice how Ghazali ties his argument to previously established conclusions.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once through, then read the translation. Pay attention to the sequence of events Ghazali describes: existence of God alone, creation of the world, second order, annihilation.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the two foundational issues
After stating the scenario, Ghazali says the whole issue depends on two questions. Locate them in the text: ḥudūth al-ʿālam (temporal creation) and kharq al-ʿādāt (breaking of habits).
These are the two issues he says he has already addressed.
3
Connect to the broader argument
Think about why these two issues are necessary for the possibility of a second creation. Without temporal creation, the world would be eternal and could not be annihilated. Without breaking habits, God could not create a new order different from the observed one.
4
Reflect on the role of revelation
Notice that Ghazali says the scenario is possible 'were it not that the Law has come' stating the eternity of Paradise and Hell. This shows that reason alone permits it, but revelation specifies the actual outcome.

The First Proof and Ghazali's Objection on Eternal Will
Ghazali presents the first proof and his first objection via eternal will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's first objection to the philosophers' proof of the world's eternity, specifically how an eternal will can be attached to a temporal event.

Core Idea
An eternal will can choose a moment without absurdity.

This lesson introduces the first proof of the philosophers, who argue that a temporal event cannot come from an eternal cause. Ghazali objects by proposing that an eternal will can be attached to the existence of the world at a specific time, avoiding the philosophers' claimed impossibilities. This objection forms the core of the debate on divine will and creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof and objection
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its translation. Note the structure: first the philosophers' proof, then Ghazali's first objection.
2
Identify the key claim

Focus on the phrase 'إرادة قديمة' (eternal will).
3
Understand the logical move
Ghazali says that an eternal will can be attached to a specific time without contradiction. Why does this refute the philosophers' proof?
4
Consider the implications
If the world is created at a time by an eternal will, then the world is not eternal. This is Ghazali's main point.
This is the first of two objections mentioned.

Ghazali challenges the philosophers' claim and they respond, with Ghazali's counterexample.

Is the impossibility of eternal will intuitive or theoretical? The debate reveals deeper epistemological divides.

The Impossibility of Infinite Cycles with Fractions and Parity
Ghazali questions how infinite cycles can have fractional parts and numerical parity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ghazali's questions about fractional parts and numerical parity challenge the philosophers' notion of an actual infinite series of celestial cycles.

Core Idea
Infinite series cannot have fractions or parity—Ghazali's clever trap.

Ghazali presents a sharp rhetorical objection against the philosophers' claim of infinite past cycles of the celestial spheres. He points out that these cycles are said to have fractional components (a sixth, a quarter) and then asks about the numerical parity of the total count. If the cycles are infinite, how can they have parts? And what is the parity of an infinite number? These questions force the opponent into a logical dead end.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that Ghazali uses two separate but connected arguments: fractions and parity.
The passage is a single sentence with multiple questions.
2
Understand the fraction problem
The philosophers say past celestial cycles are infinite. Yet they assign fractions like a sixth and a quarter to these cycles. An infinite series cannot have fractional parts because fractions imply a finite whole.
A sixth of an infinite quantity is itself infinite, not a fraction.
3
Examine the parity dilemma
Ghazali then asks whether the total number of cycles is even, odd, both, or neither. Any answer leads to a contradiction for an actual infinite.
Even and odd are properties of finite numbers, not infinite ones.
4
Reflect on the rhetorical force
Ghazali is not stating a mathematical theorem; he is using common sense about numbers to expose the absurdity of an actual infinite. The opponent cannot answer without violating basic arithmetic.
This argument is part of a larger refutation of the philosophers' cosmology.

The Fallacy of Necessary Causation
Ghazali refutes the claim that fire is the sole agent of burning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's argument that empirical observation only shows conjunction, not necessary causation.

Core Idea
Observation shows occurrence with fire, not by fire.

In this passage, Ghazali begins his critique of the philosophers' claim that fire is the sole agent of burning. He draws a crucial distinction between 'with' and 'by' to show that causality is not empirically proven.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
2
Identify Ghazali's main claim
Ghazali claims that the true agent of burning is Allah, not fire. Find the sentence where he states this.
3
Understand the 'with' vs 'by' distinction
Ghazali argues that observation indicates burning at the time of fire's presence (عندها), not by fire (بها). This distinction is key to his refutation.
This distinction allows for divine intervention.

Al-Ghazali said: The discussion on this issue has three stations: The first station is that the opponent claims that the agent of burning is only fire. Al-Ghazali said: This we deny, rather we say: The agent of burning is Allah Almighty ... And they have no proof except observation of the occurrence of burning upon contact with fire. ... And observation indicates occurrence with it, and does not indicate occurrence by it. The second station is with one who concedes that these events flow from the principles of events, but the preparedness to receive forms occurs through these observed causes ... except that those principles also bring forth things by necessity, not by deliberation and choice ... And for this reason they denied the occurrence of Ibrahim – peace be upon him – in the fire without burning, while the fire remained fire. Al-Ghazali said: The second answer has two approaches: The first approach: We say: We do not concede that the principles do not act by choice, and that Allah Almighty does not act by will ... And we have already finished refuting their claim in that in the issue of the creation of the world. The philosophers said: Denying the necessary connection of effects from their causes leads to committing hideous impossibilities. For if actions are attributed to a will that has no specific method, then let any one of us be allowed that what is before him of books turns into predatory beasts, solid mountains, and armed enemies. Al-Ghazali said: The answer is that Allah has created for us knowledge that these possibilities did not occur, and the continuation of habit in one pattern establishes in our minds their running on a fixed course, despite the possibility of its being otherwise. The second approach: Al-Ghazali said: We concede that fire is created with a constitution that makes it burn what it contacts, but along with this it is possible that a prophet be thrown into fire and not burn: either by changing the attribute of fire, or by changing the attribute of the prophet, peace be upon him. And the impossible is that which goes back to combining negation and affirmation.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the philosophers' claim of necessary causation. Ghazali lays out three stations (maqamat) of the discussion, though only the first two are detailed here. In the first station, the opponent asserts that fire alone is the agent of burning. Ghazali replies that observation only shows that burning occurs upon contact with fire, not that it occurs by fire. This distinction between 'with' (عند) and 'by' (ب) is crucial: empirical evidence proves conjunction, not causation. In the second station, the opponent concedes that events flow from principles but claims these principles act by necessity, not choice, and therefore deny the miracle of Ibrahim being protected in the fire. Ghazali responds with two approaches. The first approach denies that the principles act without choice, referring to his earlier proof in the discussion of the world's temporal origination. He then addresses the philosophers' objection that denying necessary connection leads to absurdities by stating that Allah has instilled in us knowledge that certain habitual patterns will continue, but this does not preclude occasional exceptions through divine power. The second approach concedes that fire has a nature to burn but asserts that a prophet can be preserved by altering either the fire's attribute or the prophet's attribute. Finally, Ghazali defines the impossible (al-muhal) as the combination of affirmation and negation, indicating that the miraculous preservation does not entail a logical contradiction.

The Philosophers' Objection and the Defense of Miracles
Ghazali addresses the objection that denying necessary causation leads to absurdities, and presents his second approach.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's responses to the objection that denying necessary causation leads to absurdity, and his second approach concerning miracles.

Core Idea
Allah's custom is not absolute; only logical contradiction is impossible.

The philosophers raise a strong objection: if necessary causation is denied, then anything could happen arbitrarily, leading to absurdity. Ghazali responds by explaining the role of habit (adah) in our knowledge and then presents his second approach, conceding fire's nature but allowing miracles through divine intervention.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
2
Understand the philosophers' objection
They argue that denying necessary causation leads to absurd possibilities like books turning into beasts. Find this argument in the text.
3
Examine Ghazali's two answers
First, Ghazali explains that Allah has created knowledge of habitual patterns, so absurdities don't happen. Second, he concedes fire's nature but allows miracles by changing attributes. Finally, he defines the impossible as logical contradiction.
The definition of impossible is key: only combining affirmation and negation is impossible.

The Answer: God Created Knowledge that the Possible Did Not Occur
Ghazali gives the answer regarding God's habit and created knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ghazali reconciles God's habitual pattern of creation with the possibility of miracles, and why habit does not imply necessity.

Core Idea
Habit ingrains expectation, but God's power keeps all possibilities open.

In this passage, Ghazali directly addresses the tension between observed regularity and divine omnipotence. He argues that God has created in us a knowledge that certain possible events—like a prophet not being burned by fire—did not actually occur. Yet, the constant repetition of the same pattern (habit) becomes deeply rooted in our minds, making us assume that events must follow a fixed course. But Ghazali reminds us that this fixed course is not necessary; it remains open to interruption by God's will, which is the basis of miracles.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage once, then its translation. Let the author's own words set the context before you analyze.
2
Identify the two key claims
Ghazali makes two claims: (1) God created knowledge that the possible events did not occur; (2) habit firmly establishes a fixed course in our minds despite the possibility of deviation. Notice how he distinguishes between divine creation and human perception.
3
Understand 'habit' in this context
Ask yourself: What does Ghazali mean by 'habit'? It is not a law of nature but God's typical way of acting. Recognize that 'habit' here is descriptive, not prescriptive.
The term 'al-ʿādah' (habit) is central to Ghazali's occasionalist framework.
4
Reflect on the possibility of deviation
Ghazali emphasizes 'despite the possibility of its deviation'. This is the key point: the habitual pattern can be broken by God's direct intervention. Consider how this allows for miracles without denying normal experience.

Ghazali's Refutation of the First Proof for the Soul's Immortality
Ghazali presents the philosophers' first proof and refutes it with four objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's four objections to the philosophers' first proof that the soul cannot perish, and how each objection undermines the proof's logical structure.

Core Idea
Ghazali dismantles the philosophers' proof by exposing its hidden assumptions.

The philosophers argue that the soul cannot perish because non-existence can only happen through death, a contrary, or divine power—all impossible for the soul. Ghazali responds with four incisive objections, each targeting a different weakness in their reasoning. This passage shows his method of refuting philosophical arguments by challenging their premises and demanding stricter proof.

Guided Path
1
Understand the philosophers' proof
Read the opening lines where the philosophers present their three-fold argument that the soul cannot become non-existent. Notice the three possible causes of annihilation they consider.
The proof is based on exhaustive enumeration of ways something can cease to exist.
2
Examine Ghazali's first two objections
Ghazali attacks the proof's dependence on the soul being an immaterial substance, and points out inconsistency regarding the soul's relation to the body.
The first objection references an earlier refutation; the second highlights Ibn Sina's view versus Plato's.
3
Analyze the third and fourth objections
Ghazali asserts that God can annihilate the soul by His power, and demands a proof that non-existence only occurs through those three means.
This challenges the completeness of the philosophers' enumeration and appeals to divine omnipotence.
