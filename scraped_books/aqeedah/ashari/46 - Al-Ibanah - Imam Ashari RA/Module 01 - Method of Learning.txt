Core Idea
Sunni creed is built on authentic hadith, love for the Companions, and the rightful caliphate of Abu Bakr.

In this foundational passage from Al-Ibanah, Imam al-Ash'ari outlines three key tenets of Ahl al-Sunna: the acceptance of only authentic hadith transmitted by reliable narrators, the obligation to love and honor the Salaf (the early Muslims, especially the Companions), and the affirmation that Abu Bakr al-Siddiq is the most excellent leader after the Prophet. Commentary
This passage lays out the Sunni methodology regarding hadith and the status of the companions. First, the Ash'aris accept authentic hadith that are transmitted by reliable, trustworthy narrators in an unbroken chain back to the Prophet. Second, they affirm love for the Salaf (the early generations, especially the Companions) whom Allah chose, praising them as Allah praised them and showing allegiance to all of them. Finally, they declare that the best and most excellent leader after the Prophet is Abu Bakr al-Siddiq. The justification is given: Allah strengthened Islam through him, he fought the apostates, and the Muslims unanimously chose him as caliph, following the precedent set by the Prophet when he appointed Abu Bakr to lead the prayer. Thus, his caliphate is legitimate and based on both divine support and communal consensus.

Objection and Answer: Specification of General Terms
Imam Ashari answers an objection about specification of general terms using Quranic examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Ash'ari uses Quranic examples to defend the principle that a general term requires specific evidence to be narrowed, and that the Quran itself remains uncreated because it falls under 'al-amr' (command) not 'al-khalq' (creation).

Core Idea
General terms need specific evidence to be narrowed; the Quran is a command, not creation.

In this passage, an objector questions the consistency of interpreting general terms in the Quran. Citing a verse where angels include Jibril and Mikal separately, the objector wonders why the term 'creation' cannot be similarly specified to exclude the Quran. Imam al-Ash'ari responds by distinguishing between specification backed by evidence and unsupported generalization. He shows that the separate mention of Jibril and Mikal actually serves as an implicit specification ('except'), but in the verse about creation and command, no such specification exists. This lesson unpacks this argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Identify the objector's quote and the assumed parallelism between the two verses. Notice how the objector expects that if 'angels' can be specified, so can 'creation'.
The objector is using Quran 2:98 as a precedent.
2
Understand Ashari's opening principle
Imam Ashari states: 'We specify the Quran by consensus and by evidence.' This is key — specification is not arbitrary but requires textual or consensus-based evidence.
3
Analyze the angel example
Ashari explains that the separate mention of Jibril and Mikal after the general term 'angels' functions like an exception ('except'), thus narrowing the general term. This shows that specification can occur when there is evidence.
This is the evidence that justifies specification in that verse.
4
Apply to the creation verse
Contrast that with the verse 'أَلَا لَهُ الْخَلْقُ وَالْأَمْرُ'. Ashari notes that the word 'al-khalq' (creation) is left general with no specifying evidence. The word 'al-amr' (command) is separate, not a specification. Therefore, the Quran, being 'al-amr', is not created.
