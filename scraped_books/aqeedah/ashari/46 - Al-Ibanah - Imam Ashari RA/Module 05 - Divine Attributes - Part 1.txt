The Third State: Affirming Attributes Without Takyif or Tashbih
Describes the third state of affirming divine attributes without takyif or tashbih.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the correct methodology for divine attributes according to al-Ash'ari is to affirm them without asking how or making any likeness, following the path of the Salaf.

Core Idea
Affirm attributes without modality or similitude — that is the way of the Salaf.

In this passage from al-Ibanah, Imam al-Ash'ari explains the third and final state regarding the divine attributes. He emphasizes that attributes like power, will, hearing, sight, speech, and the khabari attributes (face, hands, foot, shin) are to be affirmed without any attempt to specify modality (takyif) or to compare them to creation (tashbih). This approach mirrors the methodology of the Salaf, which al-Ash'ari adopted in his later work.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the literal translation. Notice the list of attributes and the phrase 'إثبات ذلك كله من غير تكييف ولا تشبيه'.
The key phrase is 'without takyif or tashbih'.
2
Identify the attributes mentioned
The passage lists essential attributes (power, will, hearing, sight, speech) and khabari attributes (face, hands, foot, shin). Understand that these are all to be affirmed.
Khabari attributes are those that occur in revelation but may seem anthropomorphic.
3
Understand the third state
The 'third state' is affirming all these attributes without specifying modality (takyif) or making any likeness (tashbih). This is the middle path between negation and anthropomorphism.
The first two states are not mentioned here but traditionally are rejection or interpretation.
4
Connect to the Salaf and al-Ash'ari's methodology
Al-Ash'ari states that this method follows the path of the Salaf and is his approach in al-Ibanah. Reflect on why this is important for avoiding both extremes.

Commentary
This passage describes the three states (al-ahwal al-thalath) regarding the divine attributes. First, it mentions the attributes of power, will, hearing, sight, speech, and the interpretation of the khabari attributes such as face, hands, foot, shin. Then it states the third state: affirming all these attributes without asking how (takyif) or making any likeness (tashbih), following the way of the Salaf. This is the approach of al-Ash'ari in his book al-Ibanah, which he wrote later in his life. The passage emphasizes that the correct methodology is to affirm the attributes without delving into modality or anthropomorphism.

Al-Ash'ari on the Reality of the Qur'an's Multiple Modes
Al-Ash'ari's response on the Qur'an's real existence in multiple modes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari affirms that the Qur'an is truly present in the Preserved Tablet, in hearts, recited by tongues, written in copies, and heard by ears, while maintaining it is the uncreated speech of Allah.

Core Idea
The Qur'an is really in the Tablet, hearts, tongues, and copies—all without being created.

This passage from al-Bayhaqi's 'Al-I'tiqad' quotes Imam al-Ash'ari from his work 'Al-Ibanah' to clarify the nature of the Qur'an. Al-Ash'ari responds to a question about the Qur'an's location by affirming its real presence in multiple modes: the Preserved Tablet, the hearts of scholars, recited words, written copies, and audible sounds. This shows that the Qur'an's uncreated reality does not contradict its tangible manifestations.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its translation to get the full context of al-Ash'ari's argument.
Notice the chain of transmission: al-Bayhaqi quoting al-Ash'ari.
2
Identify the question and answer
Find the question posed to al-Ash'ari: 'Do you say that God's speech is in the Preserved Tablet?' and note how he answers with a clear 'Yes' and provides evidence.
3
List the five modes of existence
Al-Ash'ari mentions five ways the Qur'an exists in reality: in the Preserved Tablet, in hearts, recited by tongues, written in copies, and heard by ears. List them and note the Quranic proofs for each.
The phrase 'in reality' (fi al-haqiqah) is key.
4
Examine the Quranic proofs
For each mode, al-Ash'ari cites a verse: Qur'an 85:21-22 for the Tablet, 20:114 for recitation, and 9:6 for hearing. Reflect on how these verses support his point.
The verse for hearts and writing is implied from the overall argument.
5
Understand the underlying doctrine
Al-Ash'ari's affirmation counters the idea that the Qur'an becomes created when recited or written. He maintains it is uncreated yet truly present in these created forms.
This is a key Sunni position against those who claimed the recited Qur'an is created.

Commentary
This passage is a direct textual citation from al-Bayhaqi’s book 'Al-I'tiqad' confirming that al-Ash'ari's work 'Al-Ibanah' is authentic. Al-Bayhaqi first reports that al-Shafi'i held that the recited Qur'an is God’s speech. Then he quotes al-Ash'ari from 'Al-Ibanah' answering an objection: Some ask, 'Do you say God’s speech is in the Preserved Tablet?' Al-Ash'ari affirms this, citing Qur'an 85:21-22. He then expands: The Qur'an is both in the Preserved Tablet and in the hearts of scholars; it is recited by tongues, written in copies, and heard by ears—all in a real sense (fi al-haqiqah). He supports this with Qur'an 20:114 (about moving the tongue) and 9:6 (about hearing God’s speech). Al-Bayhaqi concludes that al-Ash'ari used these sections to argue that the Qur'an is uncreated speech. This passage thus shows al-Ash'ari's position on the reality of the Qur'an's multiple modes while maintaining its uncreatedness.

The Methodology of Ahl al-Sunnah
Imam al-Ash'ari answers a hypothetical objector by defining the methodology of Ahl al-Sunnah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Ash'ari defines the methodology of Ahl al-Sunnah as adherence to the Quran, the Sunnah, and the way of the Salaf, particularly Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal.

Core Idea
Ahl al-Sunnah's method is to cling to revelation and the transmitted way of the Salaf.

Imam al-Ash'ari begins his chapter by anticipating an objection: if you reject the positions of the heretical sects, then what is your own position? His response is not a speculative theology but a statement of adherence to the Quran, the Sunnah, and the path of the Companions and the imams of Hadith, embodied by Ahmad ibn Hanbal. This establishes the traditionalist foundation of Ash'ari theology.

Read the objection and response

2

3
Reflect on the role of Ahmad ibn Hanbal
Consider why Imam al-Ash'ari specifically aligns with Ahmad ibn Hanbal. Notice the praise given and how it underscores the Salafi methodology.
This shows that Ash'ari theology is rooted in traditionalism, not rationalism alone.

Commentary
This passage is a creedal statement from Imam al-Ash'ari that opens a chapter dedicated to clarifying the doctrine of Ahl al-Haqq wa al-Sunnah. It begins with a hypothetical objector who notes that the Ash'ari's have rejected the positions of various heretical sects (Mu'tazila, Qadariyya, etc.) and demands a clear exposition of their own belief. The answer first defines their methodology: adherence to the Quran, the Sunnah of the Prophet, and the transmitted reports from the Companions, Followers, and imams of Hadith. They specifically align themselves with the teachings of Ahmad ibn Hanbal, praising him as the imam through whom Allah manifested truth and suppressed innovation. This establishes a traditionalist, Salafi methodology. Then follows a concise summary of beliefs (جملة قولنا): affirmation of Allah, angels, books, messengers, and all authentic traditions without rejection; the oneness of Allah, negating any partner or child; the prophethood of Muhammad; the certainty of the Hour; the resurrection of the dead; and the divine attribute of istiwa (being established upon the Throne) as stated in the Quran without interpretation. The passage thus presents the core Ash'ari creed as one of affirming revelation and the attributes of Allah as they are reported, in line with the Salaf.

Context determines the meaning of 'nazar' — and here it means seeing Allah.

Imam Ash'ari addresses a key dispute with the Mu'tazila over the beatific vision. He employs a rational method called 'division of meanings' to show that the only possible interpretation of the Quranic phrase 'looking at their Lord' is actual sight with the eyes. Commentary
This passage is a proof by division (sabr wa taqsim) to establish that the verse 'Looking at their Lord' (Al-Qiyamah: 23) refers to literal vision (ru'ya) with the eyes in the Hereafter, against the Mu'tazila who interpret it as 'waiting' (intidhar).

The author first states that the word 'nazar' (looking) can have four possible meanings: (1) reflection (i'tibar), as in the camel verse; (2) waiting (intidhar), as in 'await but a single blast'; (3) compassion (ta'attuf), as in 'He will not look at them'; and (4) seeing (ru'ya) with the eyes.

He then eliminates the first three for this context. Reflection is impossible because the Hereafter is not a place for reflection—it is a realm of recompense, not deduction. Waiting is impossible for two reasons. First, when 'nazar' is paired with 'face' in Arabic, it always means sight of the eyes, not an inner mental waiting. Just as 'nazar' of the heart is distinguished from eye-sight, when the face is mentioned, it specifically points to ocular vision. Second, waiting involves anxiety and delay, which contradicts the bliss of Paradise, where inhabitants instantly receive whatever they desire. Compassion is also impossible because it would imply deficiency on the part of the Creator or the creatures, and the context of Paradise excludes it.

With three meanings invalid, the fourth remains: seeing with the eyes. This is the correct interpretation of 'looking at their Lord'.

The author then adds a specific linguistic refutation of the Mu'tazila claim that the verse means waiting. He points out that the Arabic preposition 'ila' (to/at) is not used with the verb 'nazara' when it means 'to wait'. The examples from the Qur'an (Ya-Sin 49 without 'ila', and the story of Bilqis who said 'fanaziratun' without 'ila') and the line of Imru' al-Qais all show that waiting is expressed without 'ila'. Since the verse uses 'ila ila rabbiha', it cannot mean waiting; it must mean actual seeing.

He argues that the word 'ناظرة' here means 'seeing' not 'waiting', because if it meant waiting, the preposition 'إلى' would not have been used; rather, the verb 'انتظر' (to wait) is followed by 'إلى' only when the object of waiting is mentioned? Actually he says 'فلما أراد الانتظار لم يقل إلى' (when He intended waiting He did not say 'to')—this is a grammatical point that the word 'ناظرة' when used for waiting is not followed by 'إلى' in this way. The verse uses 'إلى' indicating direction, and coupled with the mention of 'الوجه' (face) in other verses, al-Ash'ari argues it refers to the physical eyes seeing. He cites the verse about the Prophet turning his face to heaven, noting that 'وجه' is mentioned but the intended meaning is the turning of his eyes. He then addresses an objection: could the verse mean 'looking at the reward of their Lord'? Al-Ash'ari responds that the reward is other than Allah, and the verse says 'to their Lord', not 'to other than Him'. He establishes the principle that the Quran is to be taken on its apparent meaning unless there is a proof to remove it. He gives an example: if Allah had said 'Pray to Me and worship Me', no one could reinterpret it as meaning other than Him. So too with this verse. He then turns the tables on the Mu'tazila: if they can reinterpret 'to their Lord looking' to mean something else, then others could reinterpret 'لا تدركه الأبصار' (sight does not perceive Him) to mean 'it does not perceive other than Him', and they would have no way to distinguish. The argument is a classic reductio ad absurdum, showing the inconsistency of the Mu'tazila position.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the permissibility of seeing Allah (ru'ya) with the eyes, followed by an objection and response. The author first cites the verse where Allah tells Musa: 'If the mountain settles in its place, you will see Me.' Since settling the mountain is within Allah's power, He is able to show Himself to Musa, proving that seeing Allah is possible. The objection then raises that the verse might be a deterrent (tab'id) — i.e., meaning 'you will never see Me' — as a way of discouraging the request. The response refutes this by arguing that deterrent speech in Arabic customarily links the condition to something impossible (muhal), not to something possible (ja'iz). Since settling the mountain is possible for Allah, the condition indicates possibility. The author gives an example from the poet Al-Khansa', who used an impossible condition ('until black pitch becomes white') to express her firm refusal. This shows that if Allah had intended deterrence, He would have used an impossible condition. Thus, the verse actually proves that vision is possible. The conclusion: seeing Allah with the eyes is permissible, not impossible.

Proof for the Vision of Allah and Reconciliation of Apparent Contradiction
Cites Quranic verses supporting vision and explains the verse denying comprehension.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Ash'ari proves the vision of Allah in the afterlife using multiple Quranic verses and reconciles the apparent contrary verse 'La tudrikuhu al-absar'.

Core Idea
Quranic verses affirm believers see Allah; denial refers to this world or disbelievers.

Imam Ash'ari presents another proof for the permissibility of seeing Allah, citing several verses that exegetes interpret as referring to the vision of Allah in Paradise. He then anticipates an objection from the famous verse 'Vision comprehends Him not' and offers two plausible interpretations that harmonize with the affirming verses. This lesson walks through his argument step by step.

Explore the two reconciliations

Notice how Imam Ash'ari argues that the Quran confirms some parts with others, so verses promising vision and the verse denying comprehension must be reconciled, not taken in isolation.

Commentary
This passage presents another proof for the permissibility of seeing Allah, citing multiple Quranic verses, followed by an objection and answer. The author first cites Surah Yunus 10:26, where 'al-husna' (the best reward) is interpreted by exegetes as Paradise, and 'ziyadah' (more) as the vision of Allah (al-nazar ila Allah). This vision is described as the greatest blessing for the people of Paradise. Then Surah Qaf 50:35, where 'mazid' (more) is also interpreted as looking at Allah. Then Surah Al-Ahzab 33:44, where 'meeting Him' implies vision. Then Surah Al-Mutaffifin 83:15, where the disbelievers are veiled from their Lord, implying that believers are not veiled, so they see Him. The objection then raises the apparent counter-verse from Surah Al-An'am 6:103: 'Vision comprehends Him not' (la tudrikuhu al-absar). The author gives two possible interpretations: First, that this refers to the present world (dunya) only, while in the Hereafter believers will see Him. This is supported by the principle that the greatest pleasure belongs in the best abode (the Hereafter). Second, that 'the eyes' here specifies the eyes of the disbelievers, because the Quran interprets itself: verses promising vision are about believers, while the denial of comprehension applies to disbelievers. The author concludes that the verse does not negate vision for believers in the Hereafter.
