The Justice of God in Inflicting Pain on Children
Imam al-Ash'ari argues that just as God inflicts pain on children in this world, He may do so in the next.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Ash'ari uses an argument from analogy to defend God's justice in inflicting pain on children in the afterlife.

Core Idea
God's justice is not limited by human expectations—what is just in this world can be just in the next.

This passage presents a key dialectical argument from Imam al-Ash'ari against those who deny that God can justly inflict pain on children in the afterlife. By drawing an analogy with worldly suffering, he challenges the opponent's assumption and supports his position with prophetic traditions.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own argumentation style come through before you interpret.
Notice the dialogical structure: 'It is said to them...'
2
Identify the initial premise
The Imam starts by establishing a premise that even opponents accept: God inflicts pain on children in this world (e.g., leprosy) and that is considered just. Find this first move in the text.
This is the foundation of the analogy.
3
Follow the opponent's objection and Imam's rebuttal
The opponent argues that worldly pain is for admonition of parents. The Imam then extends the analogy: if that purpose is just, then inflicting pain on children of disbelievers in the afterlife to anger their parents would also be just. Trace this reasoning.
Note the shift in purpose: 'admonition' vs. 'anger'.
4
Examine the hadith support
The Imam cites two hadiths: one about the fire test for children of polytheists, and another about hearing their crying. Consider how these support his argument that God's actions in the afterlife are just and purposeful.
The hadiths indicate a test, not arbitrary punishment.

The Dilemma of God's Command and Foreknowledge
Imam Ash'ari confronts the Mu'tazila with a dilemma about God's command to those He knows will not believe.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical dilemma Imam Ash'ari uses to demonstrate the incoherence of the Mu'tazilite position on free will and divine foreknowledge.

Core Idea
God's command to the non-believer forces a choice between blasphemy and conceding Ash'arite doctrine.

Imam al-Ash'ari presents a crisp logical trap for the Mu'tazilite position on free will. The question seems simple: Does God command belief from those He knows will never believe? But the implications are devastating. Let's see why.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and then the translation. Notice the structure of the dilemma: a question, two possible answers, and the consequences of each.
Pay attention to the logic: if they say No, they agree with us; if Yes, they blaspheme.
2
Identify the two branches
The passage presents two possible answers from the Mu'tazila: 'Yes' or 'No'. Each leads to a different problem for them. Identify the consequences of each.
3
Understand the trap
If they say No, they contradict the Qur'an (since God clearly commands belief). If they say Yes, they imply humans can escape God's knowledge. Both are unacceptable for the Mu'tazila.
This dilemma exposes the incoherence of their view.
4
Reflect on the doctrinal point
This argument shows that affirming divine foreknowledge does not conflict with God's command, and that human free will cannot override God's knowledge.
This is a key Ash'arite proof for predestination within the framework of divine justice.

True Meaning of Qadari: Attributing Power to Oneself
Al-Ash'ari defines Qadari using linguistic analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari redefines the term 'Qadari' based on linguistic usage to refute the Qadariyya's claim.

Core Idea
A Qadari is one who attributes power to himself, not to God.

In this passage, Imam al-Ash'ari addresses a key dispute over the label 'Qadari'. The Qadariyya (Mu'tazila) claimed that since Ash'aris affirm God's predestination of evil, they should be called Qadari. Al-Ash'ari turns the tables by defining the term linguistically: a Qadari is one who claims power over his own actions apart from God, not one who affirms divine qadr. He uses everyday examples of a jeweler and a carpenter to illustrate this point, showing that the real Qadariyya are those who deny divine power.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice the structure: a claim, a definition, examples, and application.
Focus on the logical flow.
2
Understand the definition of Qadari
Identify the key definition: 'القدري هو من يثبت القدر لنفسه دون ربه'. This is the core of al-Ash'ari's argument.
Compare with the opponents' definition.
3
Analyze the linguistic analogy
Examine the examples of the jeweler (صائغ) and carpenter (نجار). How do they show that the name applies to the one who claims action as his own, not the one who attributes it to another?
This analogy is central to the rebuttal.
4
Apply to the dispute
See how al-Ash'ari applies this logic: since the Qadariyya claim they do actions without God, they are the true Qadariyya. The Ash'aris say actions are made possible by God, so they are not.
This refutes the original claim.

Rebuttal: Affirming God's Qadr Entails Qadari
Al-Ash'ari argues that if affirming qadr makes one Qadari, then opponents must also be Qadari.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Ash'ari uses a reductio ad absurdum to refute the claim that affirming predestination makes one a Qadari.

Core Idea
If affirming qadr makes you Qadari, then the opponents themselves are Qadari.

This passage presents a sharp logical rebuttal. Al-Ash'ari turns the opponents' own criterion against them, showing that their accusation is self-defeating. By examining this concise argument, we see the power of consistent reasoning in theological debate.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the logical structure: 'if... then...'.
2
Identify the opponent's claim
The opponent claims that affirming qadr makes one Qadari. Find this claim in the passage.
The phrase 'إذا كان من أثبت التقدير الله عز وجل قدرياً' contains the opponent's premise.
3
Analyze Al-Ash'ari's counter
Al-Ash'ari applies the same criterion to the opponents: they affirm qadr for heavens, earth, and acts of obedience. So by their own rule, they are Qadari. Since they reject this, their rule is inconsistent.
This is a reductio ad absurdum: the opponent's premise leads to a contradiction.

If their worship of idols was from their deeds, that would be created for Allah, and Allah Almighty said: 'A reward for what they used to do' [Ahqaf:14] meaning that He rewards them for their deeds. Likewise, when He mentions their worship of idols and their disbelief in the Most Merciful, and if it was from what they decreed and did for themselves, they would have done and decreed what is beyond the decree of their Lord and His action. How can it be permissible for them to have from decree, action, and power what their Lord does not have? Whoever claims that has rendered Allah incapable, exalted is Allah above the words of those who render Him incapable, a great exaltation. Do you not see that whoever claims that the servants know what Allah Almighty does not know, he would have given them knowledge that did not enter into Allah's knowledge, and made them equals to Allah? Similarly, whoever claims that the servants do and decree what He has not decreed, and have power over what He has no power over, has given them authority, power, and ability that He did not give to the Most Merciful. Exalted is Allah above the words of the people of falsehood, slander, fabrication, and transgression, a great exaltation.

Commentary
This passage is a proof by analogy refuting the view that human actions are not created by Allah. The author begins by referencing Quranic verse (Ahqaf:14) which speaks of rewarding deeds, showing that deeds are attributed to humans. However, if humans truly performed those deeds independently, they would be decreeing and acting beyond what Allah decreed and did. That would imply they have a power and decree that Allah does not possess, which is impossible. The author then draws an analogy with knowledge: claiming that humans know what Allah does not know would make them equal to Allah in knowledge. Similarly, claiming they have independent power over actions makes them equal to Allah in power. The conclusion is that such claims are tantamount to rendering Allah incapable and ascribing partners to Him. The passage ends with an exaltation of Allah above such falsehood.

The Kafir's Disbelief as Created Evil: Ash'ari's Dialectical Argument
Ash'ari argues that the kafir's act of disbelief is created by Allah as corrupt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ash'ari's argument that the kafir's disbelief is created by Allah as corrupt, based on the principle that an action's true agent must know its true nature.

Core Idea
Only Allah can create disbelief because the kafir does not know its evil.

In this passage, Imam Ash'ari employs a dialectical question to challenge those who claim humans create their own actions. He asks whether the kafir's act of disbelief is corrupt and contradictory. When the opponent agrees, Ash'ari exposes a logical inconsistency: the kafir himself believes his disbelief is good. From this, he derives a powerful principle that leads to the conclusion that Allah alone is the creator of disbelief as evil.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage carefully. Notice the question-and-answer format.
This is a typical Ash'ari dialectical style.
2
Understand the initial question
Ash'ari asks: Is the kafir's act of disbelief corrupt, invalid, contradictory? The opponent must agree if they accept the obvious reality.
3
Identify the contradiction
If the act is corrupt, how can the kafir believe it is good? Ash'ari points out the inconsistency between the act's true nature and the agent's belief.
4
Grasp the principle
Ash'ari states that an action is truly an action only from one who knows its true reality. This is a key epistemological principle.
5
Draw the conclusion
Since the kafir does not know the true evil of his disbelief, he cannot be its real agent. Therefore, Allah must be the one who created disbelief as it truly is—corrupt and invalid.

Question 259: And it is said to them: Is the act of the disbeliever in disbelief corrupt, invalid, and contradictory? If they say: Yes. It is said to them: How does he do it as corrupt, contradictory, and ugly while he believes it to be good, correct, and the best of religions? And if that is not possible, because an action is not an action in its true reality except from one who knows it as it truly is, just as it is not possible for it to be an action from one who does not know it as an action, then it is necessarily the case that Allah Almighty is the One who decreed disbelief and created it as disbelief, corrupt, invalid, contradictory, contrary to truth and rectitude.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical argument (مسألة) in which Ash'ari questions the opponent about the nature of the kafir's act of disbelief. First, he asks whether the kafir's act of disbelief is corrupt, invalid, and contradictory. If the opponent agrees, Ash'ari then points out the inconsistency: the kafir himself believes his disbelief to be good, correct, and the best of religions. How could he perform an action as corrupt and ugly while holding such a belief? Then Ash'ari lays down a principle: an action can only truly be performed by someone who knows its true reality. Since the kafir does not know the true evil of his disbelief (he thinks it is good), he cannot be the real agent. Therefore, Allah must be the one who decreed and created that disbelief as it truly is—corrupt, invalid, and contradictory. This refutes the view that humans create their own actions.
