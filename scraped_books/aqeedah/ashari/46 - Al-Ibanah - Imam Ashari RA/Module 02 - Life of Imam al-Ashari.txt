Commentary
This passage is a biographical introduction to Imam Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari, written by Sheikh Hammad al-Ansari as a preface to the second edition of al-Ash'ari's work "al-Ibanah." The author begins with the basmalah and hamdalah, then states his purpose: many people claim to follow al-Ash'ari's creed but know nothing about the imam himself or his final settled creed. He aims to provide information about this imam who is unknown to many of his followers, based on reliable sources.

He then gives a brief biography: al-Ash'ari's full lineage is traced back to Abu Musa al-Ash'ari, the famous Companion. He was born in 260 AH. His biography has been recorded by many prominent scholars: Ibn Asakir, al-Khatib al-Baghdadi, Ibn Khalikan, al-Dhahabi, Ibn Kathir, al-Subki, Ibn Farhun, Murtada al-Zabidi, Ibn al-'Imad, and others.

Al-Ash'ari studied hadith in Baghdad from several renowned scholars, such as Zakariyya al-Saji and others, and narrated from them in his commentary "al-Mukhtazan." However, he also studied kalam (theological speculation) under his stepfather, Abu Ali al-Jubba'i, who was the head of the Mu'tazila. This is the early phase of al-Ash'ari's life when he was a Mu'tazili.

The narrative then describes his famous conversion. Al-Ash'ari became deeply skilled in Mu'tazili kalam, but he began to ask his teacher questions that remained unanswered. This caused him doubt. One night, he felt turmoil in his chest regarding his beliefs. He prayed two rak'as, asked Allah for guidance, and then slept. In a dream, he saw the Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ) and complained to him. The Prophet instructed him to adhere to the Sunnah. Upon waking, he examined the issues of kalam against the Qur'an and prophetic reports (hadith). He affirmed what agreed with them and cast aside all else—meaning he abandoned Mu'tazili doctrines and adopted the Sunni path.

The passage ends with a citation from al-Khatib al-Baghdadi's Tarikh, which likely continues to confirm this story, but the OCR cuts off. The lesson thus covers the biographical background and the key conversion story that shaped al-Ash'ari's final school.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari's personal crisis and prophetic dream led him to abandon Mu'tazilism and commit to the Sunnah.

Core Idea
A prophetic dream turned al-Ash'ari from rationalist confusion to Sunnah-based certainty.

Al-Ash'ari was a leading Mu'tazili scholar, but he became dissatisfied with his teacher's answers. Commentary
This passage is a biographical account of Imam Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari's repentance from Mu'tazilism and his subsequent acceptance by Ahl al-Sunnah. It also includes testimonies from later scholars confirming this event. He dramatically removed a garment and gave away his books, symbolizing his complete break from his previous beliefs. The people of hadith and fiqh accepted his works and elevated him to the rank of imam, so much so that his school became identified with them. The passage then argues that al-Ash'ari's former Mu'tazilism actually enhanced his ability to refute them, using the analogy of a person who leaves a religion being better equipped to expose its flaws. The hadith scholars unanimously regarded him as one of their own, and he is described as a drawn sword against all heretics. He did not innovate a new doctrine but rather defended and clarified the established Sunni position. The passage closes with quotations from Ibn Furak, Ibn Khallikan, Ibn Kathir, and al-Dhahabi, all confirming his repentance and praising his role. The key theological point is that al-Ash'ari's return was a return to the orthodox Sunni creed, not the invention of a new one.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical account of Imam Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari's repentance from Mu'tazilism and his subsequent acceptance by Ahl al-Sunnah. It also includes testimonies from later scholars confirming this event. The passage begins by describing his seclusion of fifteen days, after which he publicly declared on the pulpit in Basra that he had been uncertain and then guided by Allah to the truth, which he compiled in his books. He dramatically removed a garment and gave away his books, symbolizing his complete break from his previous beliefs. The people of hadith and fiqh accepted his works and elevated him to the rank of imam, so much so that his school became identified with them. The passage then argues that al-Ash'ari's former Mu'tazilism actually enhanced his ability to refute them, using the analogy of a person who leaves a religion being better equipped to expose its flaws. This is illustrated with the story of Harun al-A'war, a Jewish convert to Islam who successfully debated an adversary who tried to discredit him for converting. Similarly, al-Ash'ari's background made him a formidable opponent of the Mu'tazila. The hadith scholars unanimously regarded him as one of their own, and he is described as a drawn sword against all heretics. He did not innovate a new doctrine but rather defended and clarified the established Sunni position. The passage closes with quotations from Ibn Furak, Ibn Khallikan, Ibn Kathir, and al-Dhahabi, all confirming his repentance and praising his role. The key theological point is that al-Ash'ari's return was a return to the orthodox Sunni creed, not the invention of a new one.

Al-Khidr Husayn narrates the start of the fitna with the entry of insincere converts and Ibn Saba's activities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how insincere converts and the figure of Abdullah ibn Saba contributed to the early stages of the fitna against Caliph Uthman.

Core Idea
The fitna was not spontaneous; it was ignited by covert propagandists like Ibn Saba.

Imam al-Khidr Husayn begins by painting a picture of Uthman's early popularity, then introduces the entry of insincere elements into Islam. This lesson focuses on the emergence of Abdullah ibn Saba and his secret propaganda, which sowed the seeds of discord.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice how the author describes Uthman's initial popularity before introducing the problem.
The contrast between unity and discord is key.
2
Identify Ibn Saba's role
Find the sentence introducing Abdullah ibn Saba. Note his background (Jewish) and his method: secret propaganda that Ali was the rightful caliph.
He is presented as the first instigator.
3
Trace his movements
List the cities Ibn Saba visited and note how he was expelled from each until he settled in Egypt and recruited followers.
This shows his determination and the spread of the fitna.

The Greatest Imam Muhammad al-Khidr Husayn, Sheikh of al-Azhar, said under the title: How did the fitna arise? Uthman spent most of the days of his caliphate with the people united in love for him and delight in his governance, due to his extreme compassion, vast forbearance, and gentle disposition, and because people's hands were filled with spoils in his era, and they came to enjoy a good life more than they had enjoyed before. After this, some people entered Islam who were not sincere in their Islam, but rather wanted to plot against it and divide its people. Among them was Abdullah ibn Saba, known as Ibn al-Sawda. He was from the Jews of Himyar. He settled in Basra with Hakim ibn Jabalah al-Abdi and began secretly propagating that Ali was more entitled to the caliphate, and called for removing the caliphate from Uthman. A few people adhered to his call. Abdullah ibn Amir, the governor of Basra, heard of his affair and expelled him. He came to Kufa and was expelled from there. Then he came to Syria and was likewise expelled. So he went to Egypt and contacted some of the dregs of people, among them [?] ibn Bishr and Khalid ibn Muljam. He began repeating to them that Ali was the wasi (legatee) of the Prophet, and that Uthman had taken the caliphate without right. He sent from among these people callers to the lands from which he had been expelled. It happened that Uthman had governors from among his relatives, and there were people who would meet from these governors what did not suit their desires, so they would criticize them and use their relationship to Uthman as a means to criticize Uthman himself. So there came to be a faction that agreed with those behind the secret propaganda in criticizing Uthman himself, although the two factions differed in the motive for this criticism. Those behind the secret propaganda wanted to divide the word of the Muslims or corrupt their religion, while the second faction merely wanted to transfer the caliphate from Uthman to someone else, disliking some of his governors or hoping to find in the caliphate of another a position or booty. From the two factions there arose a group that would speak ill of Uthman's governors and then extend criticism to Uthman himself, attributing false things to him and mentioning some of his deeds in a distorted manner. Uthman would confront the leaders of this propaganda with leniency and would only respond with argument. A group from the people of Kufa had shown enmity to Uthman, so Sa'id ibn al-As informed Uthman of their news =

Commentary
This passage is a historical narrative explaining how the fitna (sedition) against Caliph Uthman began. The author, Imam Muhammad al-Khidr Husayn, starts by describing Uthman's early reign as a time of unity and prosperity. He then identifies the root cause: insincere converts, particularly Abdullah ibn Saba, a Jew, who spread propaganda that Ali was the rightful caliph. Ibn Saba moved from city to city, gaining followers, and eventually settled in Egypt. He and his followers used the grievances of people against Uthman's governors (who were his relatives) to incite opposition to Uthman himself. Two groups emerged: one seeking to destroy Islam (the Sabaites) and another seeking political change. Both converged in spreading slander against Uthman. Uthman responded mildly, only with arguments. The narrative breaks off as it mentions the Kufan opposition being reported to Uthman.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?

?

?

The Exploitation of Grievances and the Merging of Factions
Al-Khidr Husayn explains how grievances against governors were used to attack Uthman and how two factions converged.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the secret propaganda exploited grievances against Uthman's governors, leading to a coalition of critics and the spread of slander.

Core Idea
Real grievances were weaponized by secret propagandists to turn people against Uthman.

Building on the previous lesson, Al-Khidr Husayn now describes how Uthman's relatives as governors attracted criticism, and how the secret propagandists merged with those who had political or material motives. This lesson examines the deadly combination that led to widespread condemnation of Uthman.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the two factions
Read the Arabic and translation focusing on the description of the two groups: the secret propagandists and those with personal grievances.
Note the difference in motives: one wants to destroy Islam, the other wants political change.
2
See how criticism escalates
Identify how criticism of Uthman's governors ('ummal) extends to Uthman himself, and how false accusations are made.
The phrase 'attributing false things to him' is key.
3
Observe Uthman's response
Note that Uthman responded with leniency and argument, not force.
This contrasts with the aggression of his opponents.
