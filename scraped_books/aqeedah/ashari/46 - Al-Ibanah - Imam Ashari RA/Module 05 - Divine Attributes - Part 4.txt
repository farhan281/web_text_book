Proof from Ash-Shura: Divine Speech Specifies Humans
Al-Ash'ari argues that the verse on divine speech specifies humans, not other beings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Ash'ari uses the verse from Surah Ash-Shura to argue that Allah's speech is specifically to humans, and the logical implications of this specification.

Core Idea
The verse specifies 'human' to avoid doubt about who receives direct revelation.

Al-Ash'ari presents another proof from the Quran, drawing on a key verse about the modes of divine communication. He argues that the verse is deliberately restricted to humans, and if it were general, it would create confusion. This lesson unpacks his reasoning and its theological significance.

Understand Al-Ash'ari's argument about specification
Al-Ash'ari argues that the verse specifically says 'human' (bashar) to the exclusion of other kinds. He reasons that if it were general (including angels or jinn), it would cause doubt and confusion. The specification removes that doubt.

This supports the Ash'ari position that Allah's speech is a specific attribute and that He reveals only to humans.

Allah as Light and the Vision of Him
Al-Ash'ari uses the Light verse and a hadith to argue that Allah can be seen.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Ash'ari uses the verse 'Allah is the Light of the heavens and the earth' and a hadith to argue that Allah can be seen on the Day of Judgment.

Core Idea
If Allah is Light, and light can be seen, then Allah can be seen.

In this passage, Al-Ash'ari presents a proof for the vision of Allah (ru'ya) on the Day of Judgment. He draws on the Qur'anic verse that calls Allah 'Light' and analyzes its possible meanings, concluding that one meaning implies visibility. This argument shows how early Sunni scholars used linguistic and rational reasoning to affirm divine attributes.

This hadith supports the possibility of vision without speculating on the 'how'.

Commentary

The author then argues that those who deny the vision (ru'ya) of Allah are mistaken, because they contradict the Book and the Sunnah. He supports this with a narration from Ibn 'Abbas that warns against speculating about Allah's essence, but also indicates that Allah is above His creation, which aligns with the possibility of seeing Him. The narration describes immense lights between the seventh heaven and the Footstool, emphasizing Allah's transcendence. Thus, the proof affirms both that Allah is transcendent and that He will be seen by the believers in the afterlife.

Affirming Allah's Face, Eyes, and Sight
Imam Ash'ari quotes verses affirming Allah's face, eyes, hearing, and sight.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Ash'ari uses Qur'anic verses to affirm divine attributes while rejecting anthropomorphism through the principle of 'without modality and without limitation'.

Core Idea
Allah's attributes are real yet beyond human comprehension.

Imam Ash'ari begins a new chapter on divine attributes by grounding them directly in revelation. He cites clear Qur'anic verses that mention Allah's face, eyes, hearing, and sight, and then immediately adds that these are to be affirmed without asking 'how' or imposing limitations. This approach preserves both the literal meaning of scripture and the transcendence of Allah.

This passage is a logical classification (taqsīm) of the possible meanings of the phrase 'bi-yadayya' (with My two hands). The author then rules out the first three: The first (blessings) is rejected because it is linguistically improper—no speaker of Arabic would say 'I worked with my two hands' meaning 'my two blessings'. The third (powers) is rejected because neither the author (Ash'ari) nor his opponents (the Mu'tazila) affirm two distinct powers; the Mu'tazila do not affirm any eternal attribute of power, and Ash'ari affirms one power. Therefore, only the fourth division remains valid: the affirmation of two hands that are not like created hands, not limbs, not powers, not blessings, but described solely as Allah described them. The author emphasizes that these hands are 'outside the three aspects that preceded'—meaning they are unique and incomparable. This is a classic Ash'ari method of affirming divine attributes by negating false interpretations without attempting to explain modality (ta'wīl).

ويقال لهم: أليس إذا لعن الله الكافرين فلعنه لهم معنى، ولعن النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم لهم معنى؟ فإن قالوا: نعم. فيقال لهم: فما أنكرتم من أن الله تعالى إذا علم نبيه شيئاً فكان للنبي علم، والله سبحانه علم، وإذا كنا متى أثبتناه غاضباً على الكافرين فلا بد من إثبات غضب، وكذلك إذا أثبتناه راضياً عن المؤمنين فلا بد من إثبات رضى، وكذلك إذا أثبتناه حياً سميعاً بصيراً فلا بد من إثبات حياة وسمع وبصر.
And it is said to them: Does it not happen that if Allah curses the disbelievers, then His curse upon them has a meaning, and the curse of the Prophet, peace be upon him, upon them has a meaning? If they say: Yes. Then it is said to them: So what prevents you from [affirming] that if Allah teaches the Prophet something, then the Prophet has knowledge and Allah, subhanahu, has knowledge? And whenever we affirm that He is angry with the disbelievers, it is necessary to affirm anger, and likewise if we affirm that He is pleased with the believers, it is necessary to affirm pleasure, and likewise if we affirm that He is living, hearing, seeing, it is necessary to affirm life, hearing, and sight.

Commentary
This passage uses an analogical argument. Al-Ash'ari gets the opponents to agree that Allah's curse on disbelievers has a real meaning, just as the Prophet's curse has a meaning. He then extends this to knowledge: if Allah teaches the Prophet, then both Prophet and Allah have knowledge. He then broadens the principle: whenever we affirm a descriptive attribute of Allah (e.g., angry, pleased, living, hearing, seeing), it necessarily implies the corresponding attribute (anger, pleasure, life, hearing, sight). This establishes that attributes are real and distinct, not just metaphorical.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some deny that attributes like anger and pleasure are real in Allah, claiming they are mere metaphors for His actions.
Correction: Al-Ash'ari shows that if we say Allah curses or is angry, we must affirm the corresponding attribute (curse, anger) as a real meaning, just as with knowledge.

Key Takeaway
If you attribute an action to Allah, you must affirm the corresponding attribute as real.
