Attribution of Al-Ibanah to Ash'ari by Classical Scholars
Bayhaqi, Dhahabi, and others cite Al-Ibanah as Ash'ari's work and quote its content.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that multiple classical scholars, including Bayhaqi and Dhahabi, explicitly attribute the book Al-Ibanah to Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari and quote its content.

Core Idea
Classical scholars unanimously attribute Al-Ibanah to Ash'ari.

The authenticity of Al-Ibanah has been questioned by some. This passage presents direct testimonies from Bayhaqi, Dhahabi, and others who explicitly quote from Al-Ibanah as Ash'ari's work. By reading these citations, we see that the book was widely accepted and transmitted by major scholars.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get an overview of the testimonies. Then read the translation to understand the details.
The passage contains multiple citations from different scholars.
2
Focus on Bayhaqi's testimony
Examine how al-Bayhaqi quotes from Al-Ibanah regarding the Qur'an being in the Preserved Tablet and recited by tongues. Note that he mentions al-Ash'ari used these arguments to prove the Qur'an is uncreated.
Bayhaqi is a major Shafi'i hadith scholar.
3
Examine al-Dhahabi's testimony
Al-Dhahabi quotes from Al-Ibanah on the issue of istiwa' (Allah's establishment over the Throne). He also remarks that Al-Ibanah is among Ash'ari's most famous works, popularized by Ibn 'Asakir and copied by al-Nawawi.
4
Note additional attributions
Ibn Farhun al-Maliki and Ibn al-'Imad al-Hanbali both list Al-Ibanah among Ash'ari's books. This shows consensus across madhhabs.
5
Reflect on the deathbed story
The passage ends with a report that al-Ash'ari on his deathbed cursed the Mu'tazila. This reinforces his opposition to their doctrines.
This story is transmitted by al-Dhahabi via Abu 'Ali al-Daqqaq.

Commentary
This passage is a compilation of historical testimonies from multiple classical scholars attributing the book "al-Ibanah 'an Usul al-Diyanah" to Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari. It begins with an introductory statement praising the impact of Ash'ari's works, then proceeds to cite specific authorities.

The first and most extensive testimony is from al-Bayhaqi (d. 458 AH). He quotes from his own work, stating that al-Shafi'i affirmed that the recited Qur'an is the speech of Allah, and that this is also the view of 'Ali ibn Isma'il (al-Ash'ari) in al-Ibanah. Then al-Bayhaqi directly quotes from al-Ibanah: a question about whether the Qur'an is in the Preserved Tablet is answered affirmatively, with evidence from Qur'an 85:21-22. The passage clarifies that the Qur'an is in the Preserved Tablet, yet also exists in the hearts of scholars, is recited by tongues, written in mushafs, and heard—all in reality (fi al-haqiqa), citing Qur'an 9:6. Al-Bayhaqi then notes that al-Ash'ari used these arguments to prove the Qur'an is uncreated (ghayr makhluq). A manuscript copy dated 1086 AH is noted.

Next, al-Dhahabi (d. 748 AH) in his book "al-'Uluww" quotes from al-Ibanah regarding the attribute of istiwa': Allah is established over His Throne as per Qur'an 20:5. Al-Dhahabi then remarks that al-Ibanah is among Ash'ari's most famous works, popularized by Ibn 'Asakir and copied by al-Nawawi.

Al-Dhahabi also transmits a report from Ahmad ibn Thabit al-Turuqi that he read in al-Ibanah proofs for istiwa'. Then a deathbed story: Zahir ibn Ahmad al-Faqih heard Ash'ari curse the Mu'tazila, calling them deluded and fraudulent.

Additional attributors mentioned: Ibn Farhun al-Maliki in "al-Dibaj" lists al-Ibanah among Ash'ari's works, and Ibn al-'Imad al-Hanbali in "Shadharat al-Dhahab" also attributes it to Ash'ari.

Throughout, the passage functions to defend the authenticity of al-Ibanah by citing multiple chains of transmission and scholarly reliance on the book.

This lesson examines how scholars meticulously preserved and authenticated Kitab al-Ibanah. Ibn Darbas reports copying the book from a reliable Shafi'i jurist and collating it with other copies, demonstrating the careful scholarly practice of verifying texts.

Commentary
This passage is a historical report by Ibn Darbas defending the authenticity of al-Ash'ari's book al-Ibanah. It begins with the chain of transmission: Ibn Darbas reports from multiple authorities from al-Hafiz Abu Muhammad al-Mubarak ibn Ali al-Baghdadi that he had a copy. Ibn Darbas himself copied it from the handwriting of a Shafi'i jurist who had it in a volume, and that jurist relied on it and argued for it. The sheikh affirmed that this book represents his madhhab. The text then mentions that Ibn Darbas saw a complete copy in the handwriting of Abu Muhammad ibn Ali al-Baghdadi, which was in the possession of Abu al-Hasan ibn al-Mufaddal al-Maqdisi, and he copied it and collated it with another copy. When some companions presented this book to a prominent Jahmi who falsely claimed to follow al-Ash'ari, he denied the book, claiming never to have heard of it and that it was not from al-Ash'ari. He even suggested that perhaps al-Ash'ari wrote it when he was a 'Hashwi' (a traditionalist). Ibn Darbas then expresses amazement at the Jahmi's double ignorance: ignorance of the well-known book and ignorance of al-Ash'ari's own history, who was famous for his repentance from Mu'tazilism. Ibn Darbas concludes that those who claim to follow al-Ash'ari but reject his book are more ignorant than the Jahmis regarding the Salaf. He emphasizes that the author of the book (i.e., al-Ash'ari) adopted the first position (i.e., the Ash'ari position after repentance), and contradicting him would undermine the foundation and unity of the school.

Defending al-Ibanah Against Denial

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Denial of al-Ibanah reveals ignorance of both the book and al-Ash'ari's own history.

Ibn Darbas reports a striking incident: a Jahmi who claimed to follow al-Ash'ari denied the existence of al-Ibanah. This lesson unpacks Ibn Darbas's rebuttal, highlighting the irony of the Jahmi's ignorance and his misguided suggestion that al-Ash'ari wrote the book as a Hashwi.
