Benefit Without Beneficiary: A Logical Proof
Al-Ash'ari argues by reductio that God cannot benefit or guide without effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Ash'ari's reductio argument that divine benefit and guidance necessarily require a receptive subject.

Core Idea
Allowing benefit without benefit leads to self-contradictory possibilities about God's power.

Al-Ash'ari presents a concise logical argument to clarify the nature of God's benefit and guidance. He uses reductio ad absurdum and a fortiori reasoning to show that benefits and guidance can only truly occur when the recipient is capable of receiving them. This passage is a masterclass in theological consistency.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage aloud
Read the Arabic text carefully, paying attention to the logical connectors (فإن, فما, ولو, فكذلك). This helps you grasp the flow of argument.
Notice the use of conditional statements.
2
Identify the two arguments
The passage contains two connected arguments: first about benefit and harm, then about guidance and power. Mark where one ends and the other begins.
The first ends at 'منتفعاً', the second continues from 'ولو جاز'.
3
Understand the reductio
The first argument starts from a premise the opponent accepts—God benefits some who are not benefited—and shows it leads to the absurdity that God could also harm without harm, which the opponent denies.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
4
Follow the a fortiori argument
The second argument uses analogy: if benefit without benefit is allowed, then by stronger reason guidance without guidance and power without power would be allowed. Since the latter is impossible, the former is impossible.
This is an argument from the greater to the lesser.

That He benefits them but they are not benefited, and that He reforms them but they are not reformed. And if it is permissible that He benefits one who does not benefit by His benefit, then what prevents you from acknowledging that He harms one who is not harmed? For if He does not harm except one who is harmed, then likewise He does not benefit except a beneficiary. And if it were permissible that He benefits one who is not a beneficiary, and guides one who is not guided, then it would be permissible that He empowers one who is not empowered. And when that is impossible, then it is impossible that He benefits one who is not a beneficiary and guides one who is not guided.

Commentary
This passage is a logical argument (burhan) by reductio ad absurdum. It begins by stating a premise that the opponent grants: God benefits some but they do not actually benefit (as in disbelievers). Al-Ash'ari argues that if one allows that God can benefit someone who derives no benefit, then consistency demands that one also allow that God can harm someone who is not harmed. But the opponent does not accept that God harms without harm reaching the person. Therefore, the original premise is false. The second part is an a fortiori argument: if it is possible for God to benefit a non-beneficiary and guide a non-guided, then by analogy it must be possible for God to empower a non-empowered person. But that is absurd (God cannot make an incapable person capable without changing his nature). Since the latter is impossible, the former is also impossible. The conclusion is that God's benefiting and guiding necessarily require the recipient to be receptive.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think the argument limits God's power.
Correction: The argument defines the nature of benefit and guidance as effects that require a subject fit to receive them, not that God is unable to do something.

Checkpoint
According to the argument, what is the logical consequence if one allows that God can benefit someone who does not actually benefit?
A) God can also harm someone who is not harmed.
B) God cannot benefit anyone.
C) God is not all-powerful.
D) The Quran is not guidance.

Key Takeaway
Al-Ash'ari demonstrates that divine benefit and guidance necessarily imply a receptive subject, otherwise contradictory possibilities arise.

?

?
