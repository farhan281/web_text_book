Divine Names Are Derived from Attributes: Proof of Their Reality
Al-Ash'ari argues that Allah's names are derived from attributes, necessitating their reality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari argues that the derivation of divine names from attributes necessitates the reality of those attributes.

Core Idea
Divine names are not mere titles; they are derived from real attributes.

Al-Ash'ari opens his argument with a linguistic observation: names like 'Alim (Knowing) clearly come from 'ilm (knowledge). He then presents a logical dichotomy: divine names are either derived to convey a meaning or are mere appellations. Since they cannot be mere appellations, they must be derived, and that derivation proves the reality of the attributes. This lesson walks through that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice how al-Ash'ari uses examples of names like 'Alim, Qadir, etc.
2
Identify the examples of derived names
The passage lists several divine names that are clearly derived from attributes: 'Alim from 'ilm, Qadir from qudra, etc. Make a note of each pair.
This establishes a pattern.
3
Understand the dichotomy: derived vs. appellation
Al-Ash'ari states that names must be either derived to convey a meaning (ifada ma'na) or mere appellations (talqib). Consider why appellations are not suitable for Allah.
The consensus of Muslims is cited.
4
Follow the conclusion: attributes are real
Since the names are not appellations but derived, the attributes from which they are derived must be real. For example, because Allah is called 'Alim, He must have knowledge.
This is the key logical step.
5
Reflect on the analogy with 'exists'
Al-Ash'ari draws an analogy: just as the term 'exists' conveys the meaning of existence, so the term 'Alim conveys knowledge. This reinforces the argument.

For if we say that Allah, Mighty and Majestic, is 'Alim and Qadir, it is not an appellation like our saying Zayd and 'Amr; and upon this is the consensus of the Muslims. And if it is not an appellation, and it is derived from 'ilm, then the affirmation of 'ilm is necessarily required; and if that is to convey a meaning, then what is (kadha) for conveying a meaning does not differ. It is necessary. And it is necessary that if the meaning of 'alim among us is that he has knowledge, then every 'alim is possessor of knowledge, just as if my saying 'exists' (mawjud) is beneficial for the meaning of affirmation, then the Creator, Exalted, is necessarily affirmed because He, glorified and exalted, exists.

Commentary
This passage presents an argument for the reality of divine attributes from the nature of divine names. Al-Ash'ari begins by observing that common divine names like 'Alim (Knower), Qadir (Powerful), Hayy (Living), Sami' (Hearer), and Basir (Seer) are all derived from corresponding abstract nouns: 'ilm (knowledge), qudra (power), haya (life), sam' (hearing), basar (sight). He then states that the names of Allah must be either derived to convey a meaning (ifada ma'na) or used as mere appellations (talqib) without conveying meaning. It is not permissible to call Allah by a name that is a mere appellation without conveying meaning and not derived from an attribute. For example, when we say 'Allah is 'Alim and Qadir,' it is not like calling Zayd or 'Amr—mere labels. There is consensus of Muslims on this. Since these names are not mere labels but are derived from attributes, the attribute of 'ilm (knowledge) must be affirmed (wajaba ithbat al-'ilm). Similarly, the meaning of 'alim in created beings is that he has knowledge, so every 'alim is a possessor of knowledge. Just as the term 'exists' (mawjud) conveys the meaning of affirmation, and thus the Creator must be affirmed because He is existent, so too the names derived from attributes require the reality of those attributes.

Quranic Proofs for Allah's Universal Knowledge
Al-Ash'ari cites Quranic verses to prove Allah's universal knowledge.

Core Idea
Allah's knowledge is proven by both general and specific Quranic verses.

In this passage from Al-Ibanah, Imam Al-Ash'ari confronts those who deny that Allah has knowledge of particular events. He challenges them with a simple question and then marshals Quranic evidence to show that denying such knowledge is inconsistent with scripture. Let's examine his argument step by step.

Commentary

Furthermore, general verses like (2:29) 'He is Knower of all things' and (6:59) 'Not a leaf falls but He knows it' necessitate that Allah is 'Alim and knows all things. Thus, if they accept these verses as proof of Allah's knowledge in general, they cannot deny that the specific verses also prove that Allah has knowledge of things. The argument is that the Quran consistently affirms Allah's knowledge, so denying it is contradictory to scripture.

Allah's Knowledge of Distinguishing Friends and Enemies
Al-Ash'ari argues that Allah must know the distinction between believers and disbelievers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Ash'ari's argument that Allah must have knowledge of the distinction between believers and disbelievers, and why denying that leads to an impossible consequence.

Core Idea
If creatures can distinguish friends from enemies, Allah must know that distinction.

This passage presents another polemical argument from al-Ash'ari against his opponents. He forces them to admit that Allah has knowledge of who is a believer and who is a disbeliever, and shows that denying this leads to the absurd conclusion that creatures are superior to the Creator in knowledge.

Identify the logical structure

3
Analyze the reductio ad absurdum
Reflect on the final sentence: if creatures have knowledge that Allah lacks, they would be superior to Him. Why is that impossible?
This is a standard theological argument: any imperfection in Allah is inconceivable.

Commentary
This passage is another objection-and-answer, this time focusing on Allah's knowledge of distinguishing between friends and enemies. If they say that He has a will when He wills faith, then similarly, when He distinguishes, He must have knowledge of that distinction. Al-Ash'ari then uses a reductio: it is impossible that creatures (like the Prophet or believers) could have such knowledge (knowing who is a friend or enemy) while the Creator does not. If that were the case, creatures would have a superiority (maziyya) in knowledge over the Creator, which is an impossible and blasphemous conclusion. Thus, Allah must have knowledge of such distinctions.

Creatures cannot be higher than the Creator in knowledge
Al-Ash'ari uses a reductio to show that denying Allah's knowledge implies creatures are superior

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Ash'ari uses a reductio ad absurdum to prove that Allah must have knowledge.

Core Idea
If denying Allah's knowledge makes creatures superior, the denial is absurd.

This lesson examines a concise but powerful argument from Imam al-Ash'ari. He uses a logical technique called reductio ad absurdum: assume the opposite and show it leads to an unacceptable conclusion. Here, he shows that denying Allah's knowledge would make creatures higher than the Creator, which is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get its rhythm. Then read the translation. Note that this is a single sentence containing a complete argument.
The Arabic is compact; the translation unpacks it slightly.
2
Identify the premise
Notice the premise: among creatures, one who has knowledge is more worthy of high rank than one who lacks knowledge. This is presented as self-evident.
3
Apply the premise to the Creator
The argument applies this same standard to Allah. If the opponents claim Allah has no knowledge, then by the same principle, creatures who have knowledge would be higher in rank than Allah.
This is the reductio: the conclusion is absurd.
4
Understand the conclusion
Since the conclusion is blasphemous (creatures superior to the Creator), the original claim (Allah has no knowledge) must be false. Therefore Allah has knowledge.
The argument does not equate divine and human knowledge in essence, only in logical principle.

This is an absurd and blasphemous conclusion, so the premise must be false: Allah must have knowledge. The argument is simple but powerful, relying on the self-evident superiority of knowledge.

He selects verses that explicitly mention 'knowledge' as an act or possession of God, and then argues by analogy: if verses that say God 'knows all things' prove He is 'Knowing,' then these verses prove He actually possesses knowledge. [An-Nisa 4:78] regarding their saying: 'Whatever good befalls you is from Allah, and whatever evil befalls you is from yourself.' Moreover, what afflicts people is different from what they themselves do? And this demonstrates the invalidity of their clinging to this verse, and it makes the argument binding upon them.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection-and-answer regarding the apparent contradiction between two verses: one attributing evil to the self and the other stating all is from God. The author resolves this by explaining that the verse in question (4:79) is actually a quotation of the disbelievers' words, not a direct statement from God. He shows that the preceding context in 4:78 already establishes that all is from God, and the Quran does not contradict itself. Therefore, those who claim that evil is not from God are refuted, and the argument is binding upon them.

God Can Command the Impossible: The Prostration Argument
Imam al-Ash'ari uses the Day of Judgment prostration to argue that God can command what is impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Ash'ari uses the Day of Judgment prostration to argue that God can command what is impossible, refuting the Qadariyya.

Core Idea
God's command does not require that He grant ability.

This passage addresses a classic theological question: If God commands something, must He also give the ability to obey? Imam al-Ash'ari presents a proof from the Qur'an and hadith that on the Day of Judgment, hypocrites will be commanded to prostrate but will be unable. This demonstrates that God's command is not contingent on human ability.

Guided Path
1
Read the source text carefully
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Note the verse (al-Qalam 42) and the hadith about the hypocrites. Let the Imam's own words set the stage.
Pay close attention to the phrase 'فَلَا يَسْتَطِيعُونَ' (they will not be able).
2
Unpack the argument
The Imam argues: God commands prostration, but the hypocrites are unable. Therefore, God can command what is impossible. This refutes the Qadariyya who say ability must accompany command.
Consider: Does this mean God commands the impossible in this life? Or only in the Hereafter?
3
Reflect on the theological implication
Think about what this means for God's power and human responsibility. If command does not imply ability, then human ability is not a constraint on God's commands.
This is a key point in the Ash'ari view of divine omnipotence.

Commentary
This passage is a response to the objection that God's command implies ability. The Imam cites a verse and a hadith showing that on the Day of Judgment, God will command hypocrites to prostrate, but they will be physically unable. This proves that God can command what is impossible (or at least that ability is not a necessary concomitant of command). The conclusion is that the Qadariyya (who affirm free will and that God only commands what one can do) are refuted.
