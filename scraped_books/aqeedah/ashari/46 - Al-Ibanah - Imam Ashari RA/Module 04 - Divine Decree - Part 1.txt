The Debate with al-Jubba'i on Predestination
Al-Ash'ari refutes Mu'tazili qadar via a logical dilemma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari's logical argument refutes the Mu'tazili doctrine of divine justice (qadar) by exposing its inconsistency.

Core Idea
God's absolute sovereignty cannot be limited by human notions of justice.

This lesson presents a famous debate between al-Ash'ari and his Mu'tazili teacher al-Jubba'i. Through a simple scenario of three brothers with different fates, al-Ash'ari exposes a fatal flaw in the Mu'tazili claim that God must always act in a certain way. Read on to see how logic itself defends Ash'ari orthodoxy.

Guided Path
1
Read the debate scenario
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the setup: three brothers with different lives and deaths.
Pay attention to al-Jubba'i's initial classification of their fates.
2
Follow the dialectical exchange
Trace al-Ash'ari's three questions and al-Jubba'i's responses. Identify how each question pushes al-Jubba'i into a corner.
The final question is the decisive blow.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
Consider Ibn al-'Imad's commentary: the debate proves God's absolute sovereignty. Ask yourself why al-Jubba'i could not answer the final question.
This connects to the core idea of the lesson.

Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari asked his teacher Abu 'Ali al-Jubba'i about three brothers: one of them was a believing, pious, God-fearing man; the second was a disbelieving, immoral, wretched man; and the third was a child. They died: what are their states? Al-Jubba'i said: 'As for the ascetic, he is in the highest ranks; as for the disbeliever, he is in the lowest ranks; and as for the child, he is among the people of safety.' Al-Ash'ari said: 'If the child wants to go to the ranks of the ascetic, will he be allowed?' Al-Jubba'i said: 'No! Because it will be said to him: Your brother only reached these ranks through his many acts of obedience, and you do not have those acts of obedience.' Al-Ash'ari said: 'If he says: That deficiency is not from me—You, (O Lord) did not keep me alive and enable me to obey?' Al-Jubba'i said: 'The Exalted and Glorious Creator says: I knew that if you had remained (alive) you would have disobeyed and become deserving of painful punishment, so I looked after your welfare.' Al-Ash'ari said: 'Then what if the elder brother says: O God of the worlds, just as You knew his state, You knew my state—so why did You look after his welfare and not mine?' So al-Jubba'i was silenced. And Ibn al-'Imad said: 'In this debate is an indication that Allah the Exalted selects whom He wills for His mercy and singles out another for His punishment.' End.

Commentary
This passage is a famous debate between al-Ash'ari and his Mu'tazili teacher al-Jubba'i, demonstrating the flaw in the Mu'tazili doctrine of divine justice (qadar). The debate proceeds in steps: First, al-Ash'ari presents the scenario of three brothers with different fates—a pious believer in paradise, a disbeliever in hell, and a child in a safe state. Al-Jubba'i's Mu'tazili answer assigns each accordingly. Second, al-Ash'ari asks if the child can aspire to the believer's rank; al-Jubba'i says no, because the believer earned his rank through obedience. Third, al-Ash'ari points out that the child did not have the chance to obey because God did not keep him alive—so it is not the child's fault. Al-Jubba'i replies that God knew that if the child had lived, he would have disobeyed and deserved punishment, so God mercifully took him early. Fourth, al-Ash'ari turns the argument: if God knew the believer would obey and the child would disobey, then God also knew the believer's fate; why then did God not let the believer die early as well to spare him from potential sin? Al-Jubba'i is silenced, unable to reconcile God's selective mercy. Ibn al-'Imad concludes that the debate proves God's absolute sovereignty: He grants mercy to whom He wills and punishes whom He wills.

Allah's Will in Guidance and Misguidance
Al-Ash'ari affirms Allah's will in guidance and misguidance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Allah guides whom He wills and leads astray whom He wills, and that His ability to guide all is not constrained, but His will determines destiny.

Core Idea
Allah's will is sovereign over guidance and misguidance; He is able but wills otherwise.

This passage from Imam al-Ash'ari's *Al-Ibanah* addresses one of the most profound questions of faith: why does Allah guide some and not others? Al-Ash'ari refutes the claim that Allah would have guided everyone if He had been kind, affirming that Allah wills both guidance and misguidance according to His eternal knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the repeated emphasis on Allah's ability and will.
2
Identify the two groups and Allah's actions
The passage contrasts believers and disbelievers. Note what Allah does for each group: guidance, success, kindness for believers; abandonment and sealing of hearts for disbelievers.
The claim of deviant sects is mentioned, so identify it.
3
Understand the refutation
Al-Ash'ari refutes the idea that Allah would have guided all if He had been kind. He states that Allah is fully able but willed otherwise. This is the core theological point.
This guards against the misconception that Allah is powerless.

Commentary
This passage is a creedal statement on divine will and predestination (qadr). It begins by affirming that the following beliefs are abundant in the Quran: that Allah granted success and guidance to believers, and led astray the disbelievers—not because He was incapable, but because He willed it. The author refutes the claims of deviant sects that say Allah would have guided everyone if He had been kind. Ash'ari states that Allah is fully able to guide the disbelievers, but He willed them to be disbelievers, as He knew eternally. Then the passage affirms that all good and evil occur by Allah's decree and predestination (qadā' and qadar). The believer accepts both the pleasant and unpleasant aspects of destiny. Next, it echoes the hadith: 'What missed you was not to hit you, and what hit you was not to miss you,' emphasizing that everything happens by Allah's precise measure. Finally, the passage concludes by stating that believers entrust their affairs to Allah and constantly acknowledge their need and poverty before Him, which is the proper response to belief in qadr—not fatalism, but reliance on Allah.

This belief does not lead to fatalism; rather, it drives the believer to entrust all matters to Allah and to acknowledge constant need of Him.

The Qadariyya Accused of Dualism
Al-Ash'ari accuses the Qadariyya of dualistic belief in creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Ash'ari's accusation that the Qadariyya's belief in human creation of evil mirrors Zoroastrian dualism and why this is a grave theological error.

Core Idea
The Qadariyya's belief in human creation of evil mirrors Zoroastrian dualism.

Al-Ash'ari levels a severe charge against the Qadariyya: by affirming that servants create evil, they inadvertently adopt a dualistic framework akin to the Magians (Zoroastrians), who believed in two independent creators—one of good and one of evil. This introduction sets up the comparison and its doctrinal stakes.

Guided Path

The passage is short but packed with a strong comparison.

Identify the analogy al-Ash'ari draws between the Qadariyya and the Magians. The Magians believed in two creators: one for good, one for evil. The Qadariyya claim Allah creates good but servants and Satan create evil.
3
Understand the theological problem
Reflect on why this comparison is so damning. If the Qadariyya assign the creation of evil to beings other than Allah, they limit Allah's exclusive sovereignty and introduce a rival to His power—a form of shirk.
Al-Ash'ari is attacking the core of Qadariyya belief.
4

Think about how the Qadariyya might defend themselves: they want to preserve human moral responsibility. But al-Ash'ari argues that their solution leads to a dualistic universe, which is unacceptable in Islamic monotheism.
