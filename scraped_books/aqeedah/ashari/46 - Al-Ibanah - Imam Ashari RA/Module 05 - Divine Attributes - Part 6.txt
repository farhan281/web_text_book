God's Command to Abu Lahab: Divine Foreknowledge and Ability
Imam Ash'ari refutes Mu'tazilite objection using Abu Lahab example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Ash'ari uses the case of Abu Lahab to argue that God can command what He knows will not happen, refuting the Mu'tazilite objection that divine commands require human ability to comply.

Core Idea
God's command does not depend on human ability to comply.

Imam Ash'ari responds to the Mu'tazilite objection that God only commands what humans can do. He cites the case of Abu Lahab, whom Allah commanded to believe despite revealing that he would not. This lesson examines that argument and its implications for divine command and human free will.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation to grasp the argument's flow. Notice the Quranic citation at the start.
The passage is a jawāb (answer) to an unstated objection.
2
Identify the unstated objection
The passage presupposes a Mu'tazilite objection: that God cannot command what He knows will not be done. Try to reframe the objection in your own words before seeing the author's response.
This objection is rooted in Mu'tazilite theology about divine justice and human capacity.
3
Analyze the logical steps
Break down the argument into steps: the Quranic verse, the command, the divine knowledge, and the incompatibility of faith with certain knowledge of non-occurrence. Map how each step leads to the conclusion.
The argument is compact—each step builds on the previous.
4
Consider the broader implication
Reflect on what this argument means for the relationship between divine omniscience, command, and human ability. How does this differ from the Mu'tazilite view?
This demonstrates that divine commands are not contingent on human ability to comply.

Commentary
This passage is a response (jawāb) to an unstated objection, likely from the Mu'tazila, who argue that God cannot command what He knows will not be done because that would be commanding the impossible. The author cites the case of Abu Lahab: Allah revealed surah Al-Masad, which states that Abu Lahab will perish and enter Hellfire, yet despite that, Allah commanded him to believe. The argument proceeds: since Allah's knowledge is certain and He reported that Abu Lahab will not believe, it follows that Allah knew he would not believe. Faith (īmān) cannot coexist with the certain knowledge that it will not occur. Therefore, Allah commanded Abu Lahab to believe while knowing he would not. The conclusion is that this is a case where God commanded something that He knew would not happen. The author's point is that this does not detract from God's justice or power; rather it highlights that divine commands are not contingent on human ability in the Mu'tazilite sense. This refutes the Mu'tazilite claim that God only commands what the human is capable of doing in a way that is possible.

The Qadariyya Dilemma on God's Command
Al-Ash'ari presents a dialectical argument against the Qadariyya using a dilemma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Ash'ari uses a logical dilemma to refute the Qadariyya position on God's command and predestination.

Core Idea
God's knowledge is infallible; no one can escape what He knows.

Al-Ash'ari presents a sharp dilemma to those who deny predestination. He asks a simple question about God's command to those He knows will not believe. The opponent's answer leads either to agreement with the Ash'ari or to an absurd claim that humans can escape divine knowledge. This short passage cuts to the heart of the debate on qadar.

Reflect on the theological point
The argument shows that God's knowledge is not contingent on human action. It defends divine omniscience against the Qadariyya.

The Ash'ari concludes that this is a great exaltation above Allah, i.e., it is impossible. The argument uses a dilemma to show the inconsistency of the opponent.

The Seal on Hearts and Divine Guidance
Al-Ash'ari questions the Mu'tazila about the seal on hearts and divine guidance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari uses Quranic verses to argue that God's sealing of hearts contradicts the Mu'tazili claim that God guides all.

Core Idea
God's seal on hearts is real and incompatible with universal guidance.

Al-Ash'ari begins by citing two clear Quranic verses that describe God sealing hearts and making them tight for those He wills to misguide. He then challenges the Mu'tazila with a logical dilemma: if they claim God guides those sealed hearts, they contradict scripture; if they deny it, they admit the seal is genuine. This lesson explores this argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the verses
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the two Quranic verses cited: 2:7 and 6:125. Also note the reference to locks from 47:24.
The verses are given with surah and verse numbers.
2
Understand the first question and its implication
Al-Ash'ari asks: Do you claim that those with sealed hearts were guided? If they answer yes, they contradict the verse because a sealed heart cannot be guided. If no, they concede the seal is real.
This is a classic dilemma used in kalam.
3
Analyze the second question about opened and sealed hearts
He then asks how a breast can be both opened to faith and tight/constricted/sealed. These are incompatible, showing the verses are consistent and the Mu'tazili view is inconsistent.
The locks verse reinforces the idea of obstruction.

The Source vs. the Action: Ash'ari's Refutation
Ash'ari refutes the Qadariyya's argument about good and evil sources.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ash'ari uses the Qadariyya's own reasoning to defend divine justice regarding the creation of evil.

Core Idea
God as creator of evil is just; the source of evil is worse only when unjust.

The Qadariyya attempted to trap Ash'ari by comparing good and evil: if the source of good is better, then the source of evil must be worse. But Ash'ari skillfully turns the tables, showing that their logic backfires and actually contradicts their own doctrine. This passage illustrates a key method of theological debate.

Guided Path
1
Read the question-and-answer structure
Notice how the Qadariyya set up two parallel questions about good and evil. Ash'ari first answers the good question, then the evil question. Identify the pattern.
The questions are symmetrical to force a conclusion.
2
Analyze Ash'ari's distinction
Ash'ari distinguishes between 'source of good as a favor' and 'source of evil as something permissible'. What is the key difference?
The modifier 'mutafaddil bihi' (as a favor) vs. 'ja'iz' (permissible) is crucial.
3
Observe the counter-argument
After agreeing that the source of evil is worse, Ash'ari points out that God created Iblis, who is the source of much evil. Therefore, according to Qadariyya logic, God created something worse than evil—which they must reject.
This exposes a contradiction in their position.

Commentary
This passage addresses a logical trap set by the Qadariyya. They ask: which is better, good itself or the one who does good? The answer: the one who bestows good is better than the good itself. Then they ask about evil: which is worse, evil itself or the one from whom evil comes? They expect the answer that the source of evil is worse. Ash'ari agrees with that premise: the one from whom evil proceeds is worse than the evil. However, he turns the argument against them: God creates evil (including Iblis) but He is just in creating evil as a creation. Since God is just, the evil He creates does not make Him evil. The Qadariyya, who claim that man creates his own evil, would then have to say that man is worse than evil because he is the source. Moreover, since God created Iblis, who is the source of much evil, and that source is worse than evil, God created something worse than all evils, which the Qadariyya would have to condemn. But since they affirm God's creation of Iblis, they contradict themselves.

Comparing Good and Evil and Their Sources
Al-Ash'ari argues about the comparative superiority of good and its source.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari refutes the Qadariyya comparison of good and evil by using their own logic against them, while affirming Allah's justice in creating evil.

Core Idea
The source of good is greater, yet Allah's creation of evil is just and incomparable.

This lesson presents a key argument from al-Ash'ari's al-Ibanah against the Qadariyya, who claimed that the one from whom evil comes must be worse than evil itself. Al-Ash'ari first establishes the principle for creatures, then shows that it cannot apply to Allah because His creation of evil is an act of justice. Finally, he turns the argument against them using the example of Iblis.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the two questions

The questions come from the Qadariyya opponents.

Al-Ash'ari accepts the principle for creatures but distinguishes Allah.

Understand the counter-argument
Notice how al-Ash'ari turns the table: if the source of evil is worse, then Allah's creation of Iblis would make Allah worse—an absurd conclusion. This refutes their doctrine.
This shows that the Qadariyya's principle leads to a contradiction.

Therefore, the comparison does not apply to Allah in the same way, because His creation of evil is an act of justice, not a character flaw. Furthermore, al-Ash'ari turns the argument against the Mu'tazila: if they insist that the source of evil is worse than evil, then by their own logic, since Allah created Iblis (who is a source of evil), Allah would be considered worse, but that is absurd. Hence, the Mu'tazila contradict their own principles.

Commentary
This passage is an argument based on the logical relationship between an action and its intended effect. The opponents claim that Allah guided the disbelievers but they were not guided – i.e., the guidance was ineffective. The author first draws a parallel: if guidance can be ineffective, then why not also allow that Allah benefits them but they do not benefit, or sets them aright but they are not set aright? If you allow that Allah can benefit someone who gains no benefit, then by the same logic you must allow that He can harm someone whom no harm befalls. But everyone knows that harm only occurs to someone susceptible to harm. Similarly, benefit only occurs to someone susceptible to benefit. Therefore, if Allah's action (guidance, benefit) does not have its effect, then the action is not truly that action – it becomes meaningless. The argument then escalates: if you permit that Allah does something that has no effect, you could also say He gives power to someone who is not powerful, which is a contradiction. Commentary
This passage provides a proof from the Quran for Allah's comprehensive foreknowledge. The author starts by citing the verse about the people of Hell (6:28), which states that even if they were returned to the world, they would repeat their disobedience. Allah informs us of what would happen in a hypothetical scenario that will never occur, proving that He knows all possibilities. Then he cites the verse about the former generations (20:51-52), where Musa refers to their knowledge being with Allah in a Book. The author concludes logically: whoever does not know something before it happens cannot know it after it passes; but Allah knows both, so He is exalted above the ignorance attributed to Him by wrongdoers.

Quranic Proof that Allah Creates All Actions
Ash'ari presents a Quranic proof for divine creation of all actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Ash'ari uses Quranic verses to demonstrate that Allah creates all human actions, including disbelief, and why denying this leads to attributing power to creatures that belongs only to Allah.

Core Idea
Allah creates every action, even disbelief, because He is the Doer of whatever He intends.

Imam al-Ash'ari presents a concise proof from the Quran that Allah creates all actions, including disbelief. He cites verses about guidance and misguidance, then logically infers that since disbelief is something Allah intends, He necessarily brings it into existence. He also addresses the objection that this might imply human actions are independent of Allah's power, showing that such a claim would falsely elevate creatures to equality with the Creator.

Guided Path
1
Read the Quranic verses cited
Begin by reading the verses Ash'ari quotes: about guidance/misguidance, leading astray, and the verse 'Allah created you and what you do.' Notice how each verse is used as a building block.
The verse 'Doer of what He intends' is key to the argument.
2
Follow the logical inference
Ash'ari argues that if disbelief is something Allah intends, then He must have done it, decreed it, and originated it. Trace this logic carefully.
This step connects divine will to divine action.
3
Understand the rebuttal of independent human action
Ash'ari then refutes the idea that humans create their own actions independently. He explains that if they did, they would have power beyond Allah's, which is impossible.
This step uses analogical reasoning with knowledge.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The passage ends with a declaration of Allah's transcendence above false claims. Consider how this proof upholds tawhid in action.

Commentary
This passage is a proof from the Quran that Allah creates all actions, including disbelief. The author begins by citing several verses: one stating that only Allah can guide or misguide; another that He leads astray many and guides many; another that He leads astray wrongdoers and does what He wills; and a verse describing Him as 'Doer of what He intends.' From these, the author infers that since disbelief is something Allah intends, He necessarily does it, decrees it, brings it into existence, originates it, and invents it. The proof is capped by the verse 'Allah created you and what you do,' which explicitly attributes human actions to Allah's creation. The author then argues that if the idolaters' worship were their own independent actions, it would still be created by Allah (as everything they do is created by Him). He further reasons: if their actions were self-decreed and self-done, they would have done something beyond Allah's decree and action, implying that they have power that Allah does not. This is impossible because it would imply incapacity in Allah or equality between Creator and creature. The author compares this to knowledge: if one claims that creatures know what Allah does not know, that would make them equal to Allah. Similarly, claiming they have independent power outside Allah's would be a form of shirk. The passage concludes with a declaration of Allah's transcendence above such false statements.
