The True Reason for Condemning the Request for Vision
Refutes the objection that condemned request proves vision impossible

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Allah's condemnation of the Children of Israel's request to see Him does not prove that seeing Allah is impossible.

Core Idea
Condemnation of a request does not imply the requested thing is impossible.

Some argue that because Allah rebuked the Children of Israel for asking to see Him, seeing Allah must be impossible. But this passage clarifies that the condemnation was directed at their insolence and disbelief, not at the act of vision itself. Let's examine the text.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it, then read the literal translation. Let the argument unfold naturally.
Notice the Quranic quotes embedded in the text.
2
Identify the objection being refuted
The objection is that since Allah condemned the request for vision, vision must be impossible. Find where this objection is implied in the passage.
The passage begins by stating the objection: 'Moses was greater than that, so they said...'
3
Note the parallel with the request for a book
The author draws an analogy: just as Allah condemned the People of the Book for demanding a book from heaven but that does not make sending a book impossible, so too with vision.
This parallel is key to understanding the refutation.
4
Understand the key distinction
The condemnation is about the manner and motive (disbelief), not the thing requested. Recognize that the request for vision was made 'by way of denying the prophethood of Moses'.
The thing requested (vision) is not inherently wrong or impossible.
5
Reflect on the implication for the possibility of seeing Allah
Conclude that this passage supports the possibility of seeing Allah in the afterlife, as the condemnation does not negate the act itself.
This is a foundational argument in Islamic theology about the beatific vision.

Hadith Proof for Vision of Allah on the Moon Analogy

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Seeing Allah like the moon means literal sight, not metaphor.

This passage presents a key proof for the possibility of seeing Allah in the hereafter. Imam al-Ash'ari cites a famous hadith and then argues for its authenticity by noting its widespread transmission, even exceeding accepted traditions. This lesson unpacks both the content and the argumentative strategy.

Examine the transmission argument

Refuting the Hadith 'Light, How Can I See Him?' as an Objection to Vision
Explains that the hadith 'Nur anna arahu' refers to this world only

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the hadith 'Light, how can I see Him?' does not negate the vision of Allah in the afterlife, but only applies to this world.

Core Idea
The hadith about light only limits seeing Allah in this world, not the hereafter.

Sanusi now addresses a common objection based on the Prophet's statement 'Light, how can I see Him?' Critics use this hadith to argue that vision of Allah is impossible. However, the author shows the context restricts it to this world, where even created light overwhelms the eye. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Notice how the author introduces the hadith and immediately states it is not a proof.
The hadith is quoted: 'Light, how can I see Him?'
2
Identify the objection and response
The objection is that the Prophet's words imply vision is impossible. The response is that the hadith specifically refers to seeing Allah in this world, not the afterlife.
The questioner asked about seeing Allah in this world.
3
Understand the analogy of the sun
The author uses the example of the sun: if human eyes cannot even bear looking at the sun (a created light), then they certainly cannot see the Creator in this world without divine strengthening.
This analogy supports the conclusion.
4
Draw the conclusion
Since the hadith only negates vision in this world, it does not affect the belief that believers will see Allah in Paradise.
The argument is that the hadith is irrelevant to the afterlife.

Therefore, this hadith does not negate vision in the afterlife.

Consensus of the Companions on Seeing Allah in the Hereafter
Establishes Companion consensus on vision in the hereafter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the consensus (ijma) of the Companions is used to affirm the vision of Allah in the hereafter, even though they differed about seeing Him in this world.

Core Idea
Companion consensus on the hereafter settles the vision debate.

This lesson examines a key argument from classical Sunni theology: the Companions of the Prophet (peace be upon him) were unanimous about seeing Allah in the afterlife, even if they disagreed about whether He could be seen in this world. The author uses this reported consensus as a decisive proof, showing that disagreement on one point does not imply disagreement on another. As you read, notice how the argument carefully distinguishes between two different questions.

Establishing Ru'ya: Analogy, Hadith Refutation, and Consensus
Al-Ash'ari argues for afterlife vision via analogy, hadith interpretation, and consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Ash'ari's two-part argument for the vision of Allah in the afterlife: the analogy of sunna and the refutation of the 'How can I see Him?' hadith, culminating in the consensus of the Companions.

Core Idea
The Companions' consensus on afterlife vision is decisive, outweighing disputed earthly vision.

In this passage, al-Ash'ari defends the Sunni doctrine that believers will see Allah in the afterlife. He uses a clever analogy to counter Mu'tazili skepticism and refutes a key hadith often cited against ru'ya. Finally, he grounds his argument in the unanimous agreement of the Prophet's Companions—a powerful proof in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Follow the analogy of sunna
Notice how al-Ash'ari compares the hadiths on ru'ya to other accepted sunna practices (like stoning and marriage prohibitions) that the Mu'tazila themselves accept. He argues that ru'ya hadiths have even more narrators.
This is a rhetorical move to show consistency.
2
Understand the refutation of 'anna arahu'
Read how al-Ash'ari explains that the Prophet's reply 'Light, how can I see Him?' refers only to this world, because the eye cannot bear even created light like the sun, let alone the divine light.
The hadith does not deny afterlife vision.
3
Grasp the consensus argument
Al-Ash'ari states that all Companions affirmed ru'ya in the afterlife, with no dissenting report. This consensus (ijma') establishes the doctrine beyond doubt.
Consensus is a primary source of Islamic law and theology.
4
See the final rhetorical twist
Al-Ash'ari notes that the hadith they cite actually argues against the Mu'tazila because they deny that Allah is light in reality.
This undermines their own position.

The Mu'tazila argued that Allah, the Mighty and Majestic, is not seen with the eyes by His saying, the Exalted: ﴾ Eyesight does not perceive Him, and He perceives eyesight ﴿ [al-An'am 6:103]. Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazila argument against the vision of Allah (ru'ya). The argument is a classic reductio ad absurdum, showing that the Mu'tazila cannot consistently maintain their interpretation.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazila's argument against the possibility of seeing Allah (ar-ru'ya) based on the verse 'La tudrikuhu al-absar' (Vision does not perceive Him). He argues that if the Mu'tazila insist on the general application of the negation, then it would also negate perception by hearts, which they do not hold. Therefore, their argument is inconsistent. He further argues that if they claim the negation is specific (to eyes or to this world), then their own argument collapses. The Mu'tazila then shift to argue that the negation of idrak does not negate ru'ya with hearts. Al-Ash'ari responds that if ru'ya with hearts is not idrak, then ru'ya with eyes can also be not idrak. He challenges the Mu'tazila's claim that ru'ya of the eye is necessarily idrak, pointing out that ru'ya of the heart is not idrak or ihata, so ru'ya of the eyes need not be either. The key distinction is between ru'ya (seeing) and idrak (perception/grasping). The Mu'tazila conflate them; Al-Ash'ari separates them, allowing for seeing Allah without encompassing Him.

The Generality vs. Specificity Dilemma

The Mu'tazila cannot have it both ways.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazila's argument against the possibility of seeing Allah (ar-ru'ya) based on the verse 'La tudrikuhu al-absar' (Vision does not perceive Him). Al-Ash'ari presents the Mu'tazila's proof: they argue that because the verse says 'does not perceive Him' and is conjoined with 'and He perceives vision' which is general for both worlds, the negation is also general for both worlds. Al-Ash'ari then refutes this by first pointing out that 'absar' in the Quran can refer to both eyes and hearts, citing verses where 'absar' means insight. He argues that if the Mu'tazila insist on the general application of the negation, then it would also negate perception by hearts, which they do not hold. Therefore, their argument is inconsistent. He further argues that if they claim the negation is specific (to eyes or to this world), then their own argument collapses. The Mu'tazila then shift to argue that the negation of idrak does not negate ru'ya with hearts. Al-Ash'ari responds that if ru'ya with hearts is not idrak, then ru'ya with eyes can also be not idrak. He challenges the Mu'tazila's claim that ru'ya of the eye is necessarily idrak, pointing out that ru'ya of the heart is not idrak or ihata, so ru'ya of the eyes need not be either. The key distinction is between ru'ya (seeing) and idrak (perception/grasping). The Mu'tazila conflate them; Al-Ash'ari separates them, allowing for seeing Allah without encompassing Him.

Distinguishing Ru'ya from Idrak
Al-Ash'ari refutes the Mu'tazila's final objection by distinguishing ru'ya from idrak.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Ash'ari's key distinction between seeing (ru'ya) and perceiving (idrak), and how this allows for seeing Allah without encompassing Him.

Core Idea
Seeing does not require full comprehension.

The Mu'tazila try to salvage their argument by claiming that the verse only negates 'idrak' (perception), not 'ru'ya' (seeing), but only for the heart. Al-Ash'ari turns this against them: if the heart can see Allah without idrak, then the eye can also see without idrak. The distinction between ru'ya and idrak is crucial: idrak implies encompassing or fully grasping, while ru'ya is mere sight.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazila's new claim
The Mu'tazila say that the negation of idrak does not negate ru'ya with hearts. They admit that we can see Allah with our hearts without perceiving (idrak) Him.
This is a concession that Al-Ash'ari will exploit.
2
See Al-Ash'ari's response
Al-Ash'ari asks: why can't the same be true for the eyes? If seeing with the heart is not idrak, then seeing with the eyes might also be not idrak.
The key is that 'idrak' means full comprehension or encompassment, not mere sight.
3
Understand the final analogy
Al-Ash'ari uses the analogy of the heart: if the heart's knowledge and seeing of Allah is not idrak or ihata (encompassing), then the eye's seeing can also be without idrak. The Mu'tazila have no basis to claim that ru'ya of the eye necessarily equals idrak.
The verse 'La tudrikuhu al-absar' thus negates idrak, not ru'ya.

Reflect on how this refutation uses the opponent's own accepted premises to destroy their argument.
This is a common method in kalam: ilzam (forcing a contradiction).

Commentary
This is a refutation by consistency. The Jahmiyya claim Allah's speech to Moses was created in a tree. But they also accept the hadith that a foreleg (of a poisoned sheep) spoke to the Prophet ﷺ, warning him. If they say Allah created speech in that foreleg, then that speech must also be Allah's speech. But then Allah would be saying 'Do not eat me' according to them, which is absurd and blasphemous. If they deny that the foreleg's speech is Allah's, then by the same logic they must deny that the tree's speech is Allah's. Thus their position is inconsistent.

The Wolf's Speech and the Absurdity of Jahmiyya's View
Imam Ash'ari poses a rhetorical question and a reductio ad absurdum using the wolf's speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Ash'ari uses the example of the speaking wolf to expose a logical flaw in the Jahmiyya's doctrine of created speech.

Core Idea
If the wolf's speech is Allah's, then the wolf surpasses Moses – an absurdity.

Imam Ash'ari continues his refutation of the Jahmiyya by presenting a specific example from the Sunnah: the wolf that spoke by Allah's power. He uses this to show the unacceptable consequences of their claim that Allah's speech is created. This passage is a masterful reductio ad absurdum, forcing the opponent into an impossible position.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and the translation
Read the Arabic and English carefully. Notice the structure: a question, then a reductio, then a supporting hadith.
2
Identify the two parts of the argument
The first part draws a comparison between the wolf and Moses. The second part cites the actual hadith of the wolf.
The hadith is authenticated.
3
Understand the reductio
If the created speech in the wolf is Allah's speech, then the wolf heard Allah directly – making it better than Moses who heard from a tree. This is absurd, so the premise must be wrong.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The Jahmiyya cannot accept that the wolf is better than Moses, so they must reject the idea that created speech is Allah's speech.
This supports the Ash'ari view that Allah's speech is an eternal attribute.

Refutation of Created Speech via the Speaking Arm
Imam Ashari refutes the Mu'tazilite claim that God's speech is created in something else by analogy with the speaking arm.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Ashari uses the analogy of the speaking arm to refute the Mu'tazilite doctrine of created speech, exposing its logical inconsistency.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazilite view leads to the absurd conclusion that God said 'Do not eat me.'

This lesson presents one of Imam Ashari's sharpest refutations. He confronts the Mu'tazilites with a dilemma: if they claim God's speech is created in a tree, then by the same logic, the speech from the arm of a sheep (a hadith) would also be God's speech, leading to a blatantly absurd statement. The argument hinges on consistency.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Mu'tazilite claim
The Mu'tazilites say that when Allah spoke to Musa, He created speech in the tree. Recognize that this is their starting position.
They hold that speech is an accident and cannot be an attribute of God.
2
Recognize the parallel case
Imam Ashari brings up the hadith where a foreleg of a sheep speaks to the Prophet, warning it is poisoned. The Mu'tazilites accept this as a miracle, but they must also consider the speech created in that arm.
3
See the dilemma
If they consider the arm's speech as created, then by their own logic it must be God's speech, making God say 'Do not eat me' – an absurdity. If they deny it's God's speech, then they contradict their own principle about the tree. Either way, their position fails.
This shows their doctrine is internally inconsistent.

If they deny that the arm's speech is God's speech, then they contradict themselves regarding the tree. Thus, the argument exposes the inconsistency: if the arm's speech cannot be God's speech, neither can the tree's speech. The author concludes by affirming that God is far above such attributions.
