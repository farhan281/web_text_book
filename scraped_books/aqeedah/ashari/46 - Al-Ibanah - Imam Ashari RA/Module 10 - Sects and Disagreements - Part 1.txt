The Four Caliphs and the Ten Promised Paradise
Author affirms caliphate of four imams and testifies paradise for ten.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni position on the caliphate of the four caliphs and the testimony of paradise for the ten Companions.

Core Idea
Allah guided the four caliphs and promised paradise to ten Companions.

Imam al-Ash'ari states the Sunni creed regarding the rightly guided caliphs and the ten who were promised paradise. This passage clarifies the proper stance towards the Companions and their disputes. It establishes the basis for loyalty to all Companions and the superiority of the four caliphs.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the concise style and the sequential listing.
2
Identify the four caliphs
The passage lists three caliphs explicitly after mentioning Abu Bakr on the previous page. Note the order and the remark about Uthman's killers.
The author emphasizes that Uthman was killed unjustly.
3
Understand 'caliphate of prophethood'
Reflect on the phrase 'خلافة النبوة' – what does it mean for the caliphate to follow the prophetic model? This is a key Sunni doctrine.
4
Grasp the stance on the ten and all Companions
The author testifies paradise for the ten, shows loyalty to all Companions, and refrains from discussing their disputes. This is the balanced Sunni approach.
The word 'نكف' means we refrain or withhold.
5
Affirm the superiority of the four
The passage concludes that the four imams are the most virtuous, none equals them. This is a clear statement of their rank.

Finally, it affirms that the four rightly guided caliphs are superior and none equals them in virtue.

Rulers and Rebellion: Sunni Political Doctrine
Imam Ash'ari mandates prayer for rulers and forbids armed rebellion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the classical Sunni position on praying for rulers, acknowledging their imamate, and forbidding armed rebellion.

Core Idea
Sunni doctrine requires patience with rulers and forbids revolt, even against unjust ones.

In this passage from Al-Ibanah, Imam Ash'ari articulates a core Sunni political principle: loyalty to the Muslim community's leadership does not mean blind obedience, but it does mean refraining from armed insurrection. The author establishes both a positive duty—to pray for rulers—and a negative prohibition—to avoid rebellion, especially during times of civil strife.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic carefully, then the literal translation. Note the two distinct parts: the positive command and the negative prohibition.
2
Identify the three components
The author lists three obligations: supplication for rulers, acknowledging their imamate, and deeming rebellion misguided when rulers deviate. Find each one.
Also note the second part: renouncing rebellion by sword and refraining from fitnah.
3
Reflect on the implications
Consider why this position was important in Imam Ash'ari's time and how it distinguishes Sunnism from other groups.
This guards against the misunderstanding that Sunni doctrine accepts tyranny unconditionally.

Refutation of Deviant Sects on Three Core Beliefs
Imam Ash'ari enumerates the three denials of the Mu'tazila and Qadariyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three specific beliefs that the Mu'tazila and Qadariyya denied according to Imam Ash'ari, and the foundational evidence from narrations and consensus that he cites.

Core Idea
Three beliefs denied by deviant sects are upheld by Qur'an, Sunnah, and consensus.

Imam Ash'ari opens this chapter by exposing the errors of two deviant groups: the Mu'tazila and the Qadariyya. He first criticizes their method of interpreting the Quran based on personal opinion without prophetic or scholarly authority. Then he pinpoints three key doctrines they rejected—the vision of Allah, the intercession of the Prophet, and the punishment of the grave—all of which are firmly established by transmitted narrations and the consensus of the early Muslims.

Identify the three denials

Each denial is stated in the passage with a brief proof.
3
Analyze the evidence cited
For each denial, note the evidence: for vision, mutawatir narrations; for intercession, narrations from early Muslims; for grave punishment, consensus of Companions and Followers.
4
Reflect on the importance of transmitted knowledge
Imam Ash'ari emphasizes that these beliefs are not based on opinion but on transmitted reports and consensus. Consider why this method is foundational in Sunni Islam.

Al-Ash'ari's Accusation Against the Mu'tazila and Qadariyya
Al-Ash'ari introduces the chapter by accusing the Mu'tazila and Qadariyya of denying the vision due to taqlid and contradictory interpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Ash'ari's critique that the Mu'tazila and Qadariyya denied the vision of Allah due to blind imitation of leaders and rejection of mutawatir reports.

Core Idea
Denial of Allah's vision stems from blind imitation, not from evidence.

In this opening of the chapter, al-Ash'ari sets the tone for his refutation of the Mu'tazila and Qadariyya. He does not start with a theological proof but with a methodological critique: these groups have abandoned the proper sources of knowledge—the Qur'an interpreted with authority and the mutawatir narrations—and instead followed their leaders. This lesson examines that critique.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the translation. Pay attention to the key accusations and the contrast between taqlid and mutawatir narrations.
The passage is densely packed with technical terms.
2
Identify the two sects
Note that al-Ash'ari mentions both al-Mu'tazila and ahl al-Qadr (Qadariyya). He treats them together as deviant groups.
3
Analyze the reason for deviation
Al-Ash'ari says their desires led them to follow their leaders (taqlid) and interpret the Qur'an without divine authority or proof.
The phrase 'fi al-nuzila' means they claimed an interpretation without sanction.
4
Recognize the evidence for the vision
Contrast this with the mutawatir narrations from the Companions about the vision of Allah with the eyes. Al-Ash'ari emphasizes the multiplicity and reliability of these reports.
Mutawatir means mass-transmitted, yielding certainty.
5
Reflect on the lesson
Consider how al-Ash'ari frames the debate as a matter of following transmitted authority versus personal opinion and blind imitation.

The Qadariyyah and the Created Quran
Accusation that Qadariyyah said the Quran is created, like polytheists.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Ash'ari equates the Qadariyyah's claim that the Quran is created with the polytheists' rejection of its divine origin.

Core Idea
Calling the Quran 'created' aligns with polytheists who called it 'human speech'.

In this passage from Al-Ibanah, Imam al-Ash'ari lays out a sharp accusation: the Qadariyyah (including the Mu'tazilah) who say the Quran is created are following the very same reasoning as the polytheists of Mecca. The polytheists dismissed the Quran as mere human speech; the Qadariyyah, by claiming it was created, similarly reduce it to a contingent, human-like product. This lesson examines that comparison and its theological stakes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation

The passage is short but packs a strong rhetorical comparison.
2
Identify the comparison
Al-Ash'ari explicitly compares the Qadariyyah to 'their brethren among the polytheists.' Why would he call them brethren?
Think about the shared belief: both groups deny that the Quran is uncreated divine speech.
3
Reflect on the theological point
Consider why this matters in Aqeedah. For Ash'ari, the Quran being uncreated is a core doctrine; equating it with created speech undermines Allah's attribute of speech.
This passage is part of a larger polemic against rationalist theologies.

The Accusation of Dualism: Two Creators for Good and Evil
Imam al-Ash'ari accuses the Qadariyyah of dualistic belief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the accusation that the Qadariyyah believe in two separate creators for good and evil, and how this parallels Zoroastrian dualism.

Core Idea
Attributing evil to a separate creator leads to dualism.

Imam al-Ash'ari levels a serious charge against the Qadariyyah: that they effectively believe in two gods—one for good and one for evil—just like the Majus (Zoroastrians). This brief passage, though only a sentence, encapsulates a core theological conflict: is evil willed by Allah, or does it have an independent source? Understanding this accusation helps us see why orthodox Sunni theology insists that everything, including evil, falls under Allah's will.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once and its English translation. Notice the two claims made in one sentence.
2
Identify the two claims
The passage contains two distinct claims: (1) that good and evil have two creators, and (2) that some evils occur that God did not will. Underline these in the translation.
These claims are presented as accusations, not as statements of fact.
3
Consider the comparison to Majus
The author compares the Qadariyyah to the Majus (Zoroastrians), who believed in a god of light (good) and a god of darkness (evil). Ask yourself why this comparison is damaging in an Islamic context.
Zoroastrian dualism was seen as a major heresy by early Muslims.
4
Reflect on the significance for Tawheed
Think about why attributing some events to a separate creator violates the Islamic concept of Tawheed (oneness of God). The commentary will explain this further.
