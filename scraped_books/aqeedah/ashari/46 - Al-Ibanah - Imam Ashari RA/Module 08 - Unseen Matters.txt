Eschatological Beliefs of Ahl al-Sunnah
Al-Ash'ari details beliefs on the afterlife: intercession, grave punishment, hawd, mizan, sirat, resurrection, and judgment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the core eschatological beliefs affirmed by Imam al-Ash'ari, including intercession, punishment of the grave, the Basin, the Scale, the Path, resurrection, and the final judgment.

Core Idea
The afterlife is real and detailed: intercession, grave, scales, and path all await.

Imam al-Ash'ari presents a concise summary of Sunni beliefs about the afterlife. These are not abstract ideas but concrete realities that every believer must affirm based on revelation. This passage covers the key events from death to final judgment, emphasizing hope and fear for sinners and the role of prophetic intercession.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice the list of beliefs and the careful phrasing about hope and fear.
The passage begins by clarifying that we do not make definitive judgments about individuals except those explicitly promised Paradise.
2
Identify the list of eschatological events
After discussing intercession, the passage lists: punishment of the grave, the Basin, the Scale, the Path, resurrection, standing, and accounting. Count them and reflect on their order.
3
Understand the nuance about intercession
Al-Ash'ari specifies that intercession brings out people who have been 'charred' – highlighting that even severe sinners may be saved through the Prophet's intercession.
This balances hope and fear: we do not presume salvation for all, but we do not despair of Allah's mercy.
4
Reflect on the purpose of affirming these beliefs
Consider why al-Ash'ari includes these details. They ground our hope and fear in concrete events ordained by Allah and attested by revelation.
This passage is part of al-Ibana, a work defending Sunni orthodoxy against rationalist denials.

وندين بأن لا ننزل أحداً من أهل التوحيد والمتمسكين بالإيمان جنة ولا ناراً إلا من شهد له رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم بالجنة. ونرجو الجنة للمذنبين ونخاف عليهم أن يكونوا بالنار معذبين . ونقول : إن الله عز وجل يخرج قوماً من النار بعد أن امتحشوا بشفاعة الرسول صلى الله عليه وسلم تصديقاً لما جاءت به الروايات عن الرسول صلى الله عليه وسلم . ونؤمن بعذاب القبر ، وبالحوض . وأن الميزان حق ، والصراط حق ، والبعث بعد الموت حق ، وأن الله عز وجل يوقف العباد في الموقف ويحاسب المؤمنين .
And we hold as religion that we do not assign anyone of the people of tawhid (monotheism) and those who adhere to faith to Paradise or Hellfire except for one whom the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, testified to be in Paradise. And we hope for Paradise for the sinners and fear for them that they will be punished in the Fire. And we say: Allah, Mighty and Majestic, will bring out a people from Hellfire after they have been charred, by the intercession of the Messenger, peace be upon him, in confirmation of what the narrations from the Messenger came with. And we believe in the punishment of the grave, and in the Basin (al-hawd). And that the Scale (al-mizan) is true, and the Path (al-sirat) is true, and the resurrection after death is true, and that Allah, Mighty and Majestic, will stop the servants at the standing-place and will call the believers to account.
Source pages p. 24

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive statement of eschatological beliefs. It begins by stating that we do not make definitive judgments about who goes to Paradise or Hell except for those explicitly promised Paradise by the Prophet (such as the ten companions). For other believers, we hope for mercy but also fear punishment. Intercession (shafa'a) is affirmed: some people will be brought out of Hellfire after being reduced to cinders, through the Prophet's intercession. This is based on authentic hadith. Then the passage lists several core beliefs: the punishment of the grave (عذاب القبر), the Basin (الحوض) of the Prophet on the Day of Judgment, the Scale (الميزان) weighing deeds, the Path (الصراط) over Hellfire, resurrection (البعث) after death, and the standing (الموقف) where Allah will hold the believers to account. All these are accepted as true based on revelation.

The Dajjal and the Night Journey
Affirmation of Dajjal and Mi'raj as real events.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Imam al-Ash'ari affirms the emergence of the Dajjal and the reality of the Prophet's Night Journey as core Sunni beliefs based on transmitted reports.

Core Idea
The Dajjal and the Mi'raj are literal events, not metaphors.

Imam al-Ash'ari continues his creedal exposition by affirming two major eschatological and miraculous events: the appearance of the Dajjal (Antichrist) as a major sign of the Hour, and the Prophet Muhammad's Night Journey (Isra and Mi'raj). These are matters of the unseen that must be accepted as true based on authentic narrations.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic affirmation
Read the two sentences in Arabic. Notice the verbs: 'nuqirru' (we affirm) and 'nusaddiqu' (we believe/affirm truth).
The author uses strong verbs of belief.
2
Understand the Dajjal
Reflect on the Dajjal as a major sign of the end times. The author ties his affirmation directly to the prophetic narration.
This counters those who deny the Dajjal.
3
Appreciate the Night Journey
The Mi'raj is affirmed as a physical, literal journey. This is a core Sunni belief against rationalist interpretations.
4
Connect to broader creed
Both beliefs are examples of sam'iyyat (matters known only through revelation). They underscore the principle of accepting what Allah and His Messenger have informed us.

Punishment of the Grave and the Questioning Angels
Belief in the punishment of the grave and the questioning angels.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni belief in the punishment of the grave and the questioning by the angels Munkar and Nakir as part of the unseen realities of the barzakh.

Core Idea
The grave holds real punishment and questioning for the deceased.

Imam al-Ash'ari now turns to the intermediate realm (barzakh) between death and resurrection. He affirms both the punishment of the grave and the specific angels, Munkar and Nakir, who interrogate the dead. These beliefs are based on authentic hadiths and are central to Sunni eschatology.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic text
Notice the phrase 'nu'minu' (we believe) and the specifics: punishment of the grave, the two angels, and the questioning.
2
Understand the punishment of the grave
This is a physical and spiritual punishment experienced by the soul in the grave. It is real, though its nature is partly unknown.
This refutes those who deny any punishment before the Day of Judgment.
3
Learn about Munkar and Nakir
These two angels are sent to question every deceased person about their Lord, religion, and prophet. Their names are derived from hadith.
The belief in them is part of standard Sunni creed.
4
Reflect on the universal scope
The questioning applies to all buried individuals ('the buried in their graves'). It is a universal experience for both believers and disbelievers.

Dreams have meaning, charity benefits the deceased, and sorcery is real.

In this passage, Imam al-Ash'ari addresses three distinct but related matters of the unseen: the interpretation of dreams, the efficacy of charity and supplication for the deceased, and the existence of sorcery. Each counters specific sectarian denials or innovations.

Learn about charity for the dead

4
Acknowledge the reality of sorcery

Funeral Prayer, Creation of Paradise and Hell, and Predestination
Affirmation of funeral prayer, createdness of Paradise and Hell, and predestination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni positions on funeral prayer for all Muslims, the createdness of Paradise and Hell, and the predestination of lifespans and provisions.

Core Idea
Paradise and Hell are created; lifespans and provisions are predestined by Allah.

Imam al-Ash'ari concludes this section by affirming several creedal points related to eschatology and divine decree. He establishes the obligation of funeral prayer for all Muslims regardless of sin, the pre-existence of Paradise and Hell, and that every person's death (whether natural or violent) occurs by a predetermined term, and all provisions come from Allah—whether lawful or unlawful.

3
Grasp the creation of Paradise and Hell
Paradise and Hell are already created and exist now; they are not merely future states. This counters the Mu'tazila view that they will be created later.
4
Accept predestination of lifespan and provision
The author affirms that every death occurs by a fixed term (ajal), and all provisions come from Allah—whether halal or haram. This upholds divine sovereignty over all events.
5
Reflect on the implications
These beliefs reinforce trust in Allah's decree and encourage prayers for all Muslims.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive list of creedal articles that form the core of Ahl al-Sunnah according to Imam al-Ash'ari. It begins by affirming the sunna of wiping over leather socks during travel and residence, countering those who reject it. It then addresses political obedience: supplication for Muslim rulers, acknowledgment of their legitimacy, and condemnation of rebellion even if they deviate. Rebellion with the sword is rejected, and fighting during fitna is abandoned. The passage then moves to eschatological beliefs: the emergence of the Dajjal, punishment of the grave, the angels Nakir and Munkar questioning the dead, and the truth of the Night Journey (Isra and Mi'raj). Dreams are affirmed as having true interpretations. Charity for deceased Muslims is beneficial, and sorcery exists as a reality in the world. Prayer is offered over all Muslims of the qibla regardless of righteousness, and inheritance is recognized. Paradise and Hell are already created. Death occurs by appointed term, and provision is from God, both lawful and unlawful. Satan's whispering and influence on humans is affirmed, supported by Quranic verses against the Mu'tazila and Jahmiyya who deny this. The possibility of miracles for saints is affirmed. The fate of polytheists' children is specified as a test with fire in the afterlife. God's knowledge encompasses all—past, present, future, and even hypotheticals. Obedience to Muslim imams is required. Finally, separation from innovators and people of desires is mandated. The passage ends with 'concluded verbatim' marking the end of the creed.

As for when the promise of bounty precedes, there is no basis for this.
Source pages p. 162

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical argument (objection-and-answer) where Ash'ari refutes the view that intercession is only for the righteous. He begins by stating the consensus on the Prophet's intercession, then poses a question: is it for sinners or the righteous? He presents two possible answers from opponents. If they say it is for sinners, they agree with the correct view. If they say it is for the righteous who are already promised Paradise, Ash'ari raises several objections: (1) Intercession would be meaningless since they are already promised entry, and Allah does not break His promise. (2) Claiming they have deserved Paradise obligates Allah, but Allah is not unjust—delaying them would be injustice, and intercession to prevent injustice is absurd. (3) If they then claim intercession is for increase of reward, Ash'ari counters that Allah has already promised increase (Qur'an 4:173), so intercession would be to ensure Allah does not break His promise, which is ignorance. He concludes by defining the rational meaning of intercession: either removing deserved punishment or granting un-promised favor. Since the righteous are already promised both entry and increase, intercession cannot be for them in this sense, proving it must be for sinners.

Torment of the Grave: Consensus and Hadith
Al-Ash'ari refutes Mu'tazila denial with consensus and hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Ash'ari uses consensus (ijma') and multiple hadiths to affirm the reality of the torment of the grave against the Mu'tazila denial.

Core Idea
Consensus of the Companions confirms the grave's torment.

Al-Ash'ari begins this chapter by naming the Mu'tazila as deniers of the grave's punishment. He then builds a two-pronged case: first, the early Muslim community's unanimous acceptance (consensus), and second, multiple authentic hadiths in which the Prophet himself sought refuge from this torment. The passage shows how transmitted evidence outweighs rational speculation in matters of the unseen.
