Imam Ashari uses the miraculous wolf speech to refute created speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Ashari uses the miraculous speech of the wolf to argue that God's speech is not created in a creature.

Core Idea
If a miracle speaks, its speech must be divine, not created.

Imam Ashari challenges the Mu'tazilite claim that God's speech is created in something else. He brings a powerful example: the wolf that spoke to testify to Muhammad's prophethood. This miraculous speech, if attributed to a created wolf, would lead to absurd consequences. Let's see how he forces the Mu'tazilites into a corner.

Guided Path

2

This is the key premise Ashari uses against them.
3
Apply the premise to the wolf
If God creates speech in another, then the wolf's speech must be God's speech because it is miraculous.
4
Consider the reductio ad absurdum
If the wolf is the speaker, then the tree at Sinai could also be the speaker, leading to the blasphemy that a created tree said 'I am Allah.'
This shows the absurdity of the Mu'tazilite position.
5
Conclude the argument
Thus the Mu'tazilites must admit the wolf's speech is God's own speech, which contradicts their view that all speech is created.
The miraculous nature proves it is divine.

Commentary
This passage is a rejoinder (jawaab) that argues against the view that the Qur'an is created (makhluq). Additionally, if the Qur'an were a created person, it would be subject to death, which is impossible for Allah's speech. Finally, the author argues that even if the Qur'an were created 'in' a created person (like a substrate), it would be indistinguishable from human speech, and it would also be impossible for Allah's knowledge to be created in a person. Commentary
In this passage, Imam al-Ashari offers a logical argument based on Quranic wording. The verse states that Allah does not speak to any human (bashar) except in three ways: revelation, behind a veil, or through a messenger. The key point is that the verse specifically says 'human' (bashar), not 'any being' or 'anyone'. Imam al-Ashari argues that if the verse had been general for all beings, it would have been more straightforward but could raise doubt: the listener might ask whether Allah speaks to jinn or angels in the same way. By specifying humans, the verse eliminates that doubt because it explicitly restricts the modes of divine speech to humans. This suggests that other beings (like angels) may be spoken to in a different manner, which is consistent with Islamic teachings. The argument also implicitly supports the Ashari view that Allah's speech is an eternal attribute, and He speaks to angels without the veil and to prophets in specific ways. The verse thus affirms divine transcendence: Allah is not in direct physical contact with humans; His communication is mediated appropriately.
