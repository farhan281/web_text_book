Proof from Human Development
Ashari presents a proof from human bodily change for the existence of a Creator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari uses the observable transformation of the human body from sperm to fully formed being as a proof for the existence of a Creator.

Core Idea
Human development proves a Creator because we cannot cause even the smallest bodily change in ourselves.

Al-Ash'ari anticipates a question about the evidence for a Maker and Disposer. He answers by pointing to the human being's own journey from a drop of fluid to a complete person. This argument from personal experience is meant to be irrefutable.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the question-and-answer format.
2
Identify the premise
The proof begins with the stages of human development: sperm, clot, lump, flesh and bone. This observable sequence is the starting point.
These stages are mentioned in the Quran as well.
3
Understand the reasoning
The key point: the human cannot transfer himself between these stages, because even at full strength he cannot create a sense or limb. Therefore the change must come from an external agent.
The argument hinges on the inability to cause even a small change.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The only explanation is a Maker (صانع) and Disposer (مدبّر) who orchestrates this transformation. Reflect on why this is considered a rational proof.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the existence of a Creator (صانع) and Disposer (مدبّر) of the creation. It is presented in the form of a question and answer, a method the author uses throughout. The question asks for the rational evidence (دليل) that the world has a Maker. The answer provides an argument from the observable transformation of the human being. The human starts as a sperm (نطفة), then a clot (علقة), then a lump of flesh (مضغة), then fully formed flesh, bone, and blood. This change from one state to another cannot be self-caused, because even when a person is at the peak of his power and intellect, he cannot produce for himself even a single sense like hearing or sight, nor can he create a limb for himself. Therefore, the one who transfers him from state to state must be another—namely the Creator. The argument depends on the impossibility of self-transformation and the observed inability to create basic bodily faculties at will.

Proof from Human Development: The First Argument
Ashari presents the first part of his proof from human development.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Ash'ari's first argument for the existence of a Creator, based on the human being's inability to add new senses or limbs even in a state of full strength, which implies they could not have transformed themselves in a state of weakness.

Core Idea
Our inability to change ourselves in strength proves we couldn't have done it in weakness.

Imam Ash'ari begins his proof by focusing on the most obvious change: a human being's development from a drop of fluid to a fully formed person. He then makes a clever observation: if we cannot even add a single sense or limb when we are at our most powerful, then we certainly could not have built our entire body from nothing when we were weaker. This simple argument forms the core of the first part of his proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and English carefully. Identify the stages of human development mentioned and the reasoning that follows.
2
Understand the 'strength vs weakness' principle
Notice the key logical move: Ash'ari argues that what a person can do in a state of deficiency, they can do more in perfection; but what they cannot do in perfection, they are even more incapable of in deficiency.

الباب الأول الكلام في الإلهيات: الذات والصفات [۱] مسألة الاستدلال علَى وُجودِ ذاتِ الباري - سُبحانه إن سأل سائل فقال : ما الدليلُ علَى أَنَّ لِلْخَلقِ صَانِعًا صَنعَهُ، ومدبّرًا دبَّرَه . قِيلَ : الدليل على ذلك أن الإنسان الذي هو في غاية الكمال ،والتمام كانَ نُطفةٌ ثم عَلَقةً، ثم مُضغة ، ثم لحمًا وعظمًا ودما . وقد عَلِمْنا أنَّه لم يَنْقُلْ نفسَه من حالٍ إلى حال؛ لأنَّا نراه في حال كمالِ قُوَّتِه ، وتَمام عقله، لا يقدر أن يُحدِثَ لنفسِهِ سَمْعًا ولَا بَصَرًا، ولا أن يَخلُقَ لنفسه جارِحةً. فـيدل ذلك على أنه، في حالِ ضعْفِهِ ونُقصانه، عن فعل ذلك أَعْجَرُ ؛ لأنَّ ما قدَرَ عليه في حالِ النُّقصان، فهو في حال الكمال عَلَيْهِ أَقْدرُ. وما عجز عنه في حالِ الكمال فهو في حالِ النقصانِ عنْهُ أَعْجَز. ورأيناه طفلا ، ثُمَّ شابًا ، ثم كهلا ، ثم شَيْخًا . وقد عَلِمنا أنه لم ينقُلْ نفسَهُ ، من حالِ الشبابِ إلى حال الكِبَرِ والهَرَمِ ؛ لأنَّ الإنسانَ لَوْ جَهِدَ أنْ يُزِيلَ عن نفسِهِ الكِبَرَ والهَرَمَ، ويَرُدَّها إلى حالِ الشبابِ، لمْ يُمْكِنه ذلك . فدلَّ ما وصَفْنا على أنهُ ليسَ هُوَ الَّذِي يَنْقَلُ نفسَه في هذه الأحوال، وأَنَّ له ناقِلًا نَقَلهُ من حال إلى حال ودبَّره على ما هو عليه؛ لأنه لا يجوز انتقاله من حالٍ إلى حالٍ بغيرِ ناقل ولا مُدبّر . ومما يُبينُ ذلك أن القُطنَ لا يجوز أن يتحول غَزْلًا مَفْتولا
The first chapter: discourse on divine matters: the essence and attributes. [1] Issue: The proof for the existence of the essence of the Creator, glory be to Him. If a questioner asks, saying: What is the proof that creation has a maker who made it and a governor who governed it? It is said: The proof for that is that the human being, who is at the utmost perfection and completion, was a sperm, then a clot, then a morsel, then flesh, bone, and blood. And we have known that he did not transfer himself from one state to another because we see him, in the state of his strength's perfection and his intellect's completion, unable to produce for himself hearing or sight, nor to create for himself a limb. This indicates that he, in the state of his weakness and deficiency, is more incapable of doing that than in the state of perfection; because what he is capable of in the state of deficiency, he is more capable of in the state of perfection, and what he is incapable of in the state of perfection, he is more incapable of in the state of deficiency. And we saw him as a child, then a youth, then an adult, then an old man. And we have known that he did not transfer himself from the state of youth to the state of old age and decrepitude; because if a person strove to remove from himself old age and decrepitude and return himself to the state of youth, he would not be able to do that. So what we have described indicates that he is not the one who transfers himself in these states, and that he has a transferrer who transferred him from one state to another and governed him as he is; because it is not permissible for him to transfer from one state to another without a transferrer and a governor. And what clarifies that is that cotton cannot become spun thread.
Source pages p. 103

Commentary
This passage is a theological proof (istidlāl) for the existence of a Creator and Governor of the universe, specifically focused on the observable changes in human beings. The argument proceeds as follows: The human being begins as a sperm, then a clot, then a morsel of flesh, then develops bones, flesh, and blood. But we know with certainty that the human does not transform himself from one stage to another, because even in his state of full strength and mature intellect, he is unable to produce for himself a new sense like hearing or sight, or to create a new limb for himself. From this inability in a state of strength, we infer that in the earlier states of weakness and deficiency he was even more incapable. Similarly, we observe the human going through childhood, youth, adulthood, and old age, and we know he cannot by his own effort reverse aging or return to youth. If he cannot control his own passage through these stages, then there must be an external agent (nāqil) that moves him from state to state and a governor (mudabbir) that manages his development. The argument is reinforced with a simple analogy: cotton does not transform itself into spun thread; it requires an external agent. Thus, the observed changes in human beings — which are beyond human power to effect or reverse — demonstrate that there must be a Creator and Governor. This is a standard kalām proof from the principle of change (ḥudūth), though here it is applied specifically to the human lifespan as evidence for a divine agent. The proof avoids circular reasoning by starting from a premise the questioner already accepts: that humans undergo these changes.

Core Idea
We cannot reverse aging, so we cannot be the agents of our own life stages.

He then unifies both arguments to conclude that a transferrer and governor must exist. This lesson covers that second argument and the final conclusion.

Understand the argument from aging

3
Understand the conclusion

Argument from Weakness and Strength
Al-Ash'ari argues that inability proves dependence on a creator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari uses the observable contrast between strength and weakness to argue that human actions are not self-generated but depend on a creator.

Core Idea
What you cannot do when strong, you certainly cannot do when weak.

In this short but powerful passage, al-Ash'ari builds a logical argument from everyday experience. He asks you to consider your own abilities: if you can perform an action when you are weak, you can certainly do it when you are strong. Conversely, if you cannot do something even when strong, you are even less able to do it when weak. This simple observation has profound implications for the source of our actions.

Guided Path

2

The passage has two main parts: first, what one can do in weakness one can do more in strength; second, what one cannot do in strength one cannot do in weakness. Find these two statements.
These form the logical core of the argument.
3
Understand the logical implication
The passage implies that if humans were the true agents of their actions, they would be more capable in strength. But since they fail to act in strength, their actions must come from an external creator.
This is a deductive proof, not just an observation.

The common misunderstanding is that this is about degrees of power, not about origins.

The Argument from Human Life Stages

But sharing in origination does not mean the body must be identical in nature (e.g., movement) to other originated things. This reinforces that shared creation does not remove distinction in attributes.

قيل له: لا يجب ذلك؛ لافتراقهما في معنى الضَّرورة، والاكتساب ؛ لأن الضرورة ما حُمِلَ عليه الشَّيءُ وأُكْرِهَ وجُبِرَ عليه ، ولو جَهِدَ في التَّخلُص منه ، وأراد الخروج عَنْه ، واستَفْرَغَ في ذلك مجهوده ، لم يجد مِنْهُ انفكاكا ، ولا إلى الخروج عنه سبيلا فإذا كانت إحدى الحركتين بهذا الوصف، الذي هو وصفُ الضرورة، وهي حركةُ المُرتعش مِنَ الفَالحِ، والمُرتعدِ من الحُمَّى، كانت اضطرارًا . وإذا كانت الحركةُ الأُخرى بخلاف هذا الوصفِ لم تكن اضطرارًا لأنَّ الإنسانَ في ذَهابه ومجيئه وإقباله وإدباره، بخلاف المرتعش من الفالج، والمرتعد من الحمى. يعلم الإنسانُ التَّفرِقة بينَ الحالين من نفسه ، وغيره عِلْمَ اضطرار، لا يجوزُ مَعَهُ الشَّكُ. فقد وجبَ إذا كانَ العجز في إحدى الحالتين، أن تَكُونَ القدرة التي هي ضِدُّه - حادثةً في الحالِ الأُخْرَى؛ لأن العجز لو كان في الحالين جميعًا، لكان سبيلُ الإنسان فيهما سبيلا واحدة، فلما لم يكن هذا هكذا، وكانت القدرة في إحدى الحركتين، وجبَ أن يكونَ كَسْبا ؛ لأن حقيقة الكَسْبِ أن الشيءَ وقعَ من المكتسب له بقوة محدثة وافتراق الحالين في الحركتين؛ ولأنَّ إحداهما بمعنى الضرورة، وجب أن تكون ضرورة؛ ولأن الأخرى بمعنى الكسب، وجب أن تكون كسبًا، ودليل الخلق : في حركة الاضطرار وحركة الاكتساب، واحد ؛ فلذلك وجب إذا كانت إحداهما خلقا أن تكونَ الأُخرى خَلقًا . ألا ترى أن افتراقهما في باب الضرورة والكسب، لا يُوجِبُ افتراقهما في باب الحدثِ والكون بعد أن لم يكونا ، فكذلك لا يُوجِبُ افتراقُهُما في باب الضرورةِ والكَسْبِ افتراقَهُمَا فِي الخَلْقِ . ترى أنَّ الجسمَ لمَّا لم يَسْبِقِ المحدثاتِ وجبَ حدوثه؛ بدخوله في معنَى الحَدَثِ ولَيْسَ يجب إذا دخل في الحدث، بمشاركة المحدثاتِ في معنى الحدَثِ ، إذا كانَ مِنَ المُحدثاتِ ما هُوَ حركة أن يكون الجسم حركة ، وإذا كانَ مِنْهَا ما هُو جِسْمٌ ليس ألا
Actions of servants are from the creation of God. It was said to him: That is not necessary because of their difference in the meaning of necessity and acquisition. For necessity is what a thing is subjected to, compelled, and forced upon, and even if he strives to get rid of it, and desires to exit from it, and exhausts his effort in that, he finds no escape from it, nor any way to exit from it. So when one of the two movements is of this description—which is the description of necessity—and it is the movement of the one trembling from palsy and the one shivering from fever, it is necessary. And when the other movement is contrary to this description, it is not necessary, because man in his going and coming, and his approach and retreat, is different from the one trembling from palsy and the one shivering from fever. Man knows the distinction between the two states, in himself and in others, with necessary knowledge, with which doubt is not permissible. So it is necessary—since incapability exists in one of the two states—that the capacity (qudra) which is its opposite be originated in the other state; because if incapability were in both states, man's way in them would be one way. Since it is not so, and capacity is in one of the two movements, it must be acquisition (kasb); because the reality of acquisition is that a thing occurs from its acquirer by an originated power, and the two states differ in the movements; and because one of them is in the meaning of necessity, it must be necessity; and because the other is in the meaning of acquisition, it must be acquisition. The proof of creation (khalq) is one in the necessary movement and the acquired movement; therefore, if one of them is creation, the other must be creation. Do you not see that their difference in the category of necessity and acquisition does not necessitate their difference in the category of origination and existence after non-existence? So likewise their difference in the category of necessity and acquisition does not necessitate their difference in creation. Do you see that when the body did not precede originated things, its origination is necessary; but it is not necessary—if it enters into origination by sharing with originated things in the meaning of origination—if among them there is what is movement, that the body should be movement, and if among them there is what is body, not?

Commentary
This passage is a continuous argument distinguishing involuntary (necessary) actions from voluntary (acquired) actions and proving that both are equally created by God. The text begins by addressing an objection: the two types of action differ in being necessary or acquired, which might suggest they are not both created. Al-Ash'ari responds by first defining what necessity (ḍarūra) means: something imposed on a person, such that even with maximal effort they cannot escape it. He gives examples: the trembling of a person with palsy or a feverish shiver. These are compelled movements. In contrast, voluntary movements like coming and going are not compelled; a person can choose to perform them or not. This distinction is known by necessary knowledge (ʿilm ḍarūrī), without doubt. Then he argues: since incapability (ʿajz) is present in the necessary movement, the opposite—capacity (qudra)—must be originated (ḥāditha) in the other movement. If incapability were in both, man's condition would be identical, which is false. Therefore, the voluntary movement is an acquisition (kasb), defined as an act occurring from an agent by an originated power. The proof of creation (dalīl al-khalq) applies to both movements in the same way: just as something's existence after non-existence indicates it is originated (ḥādith), so both movements share in being originated. Difference in necessity/ acquisition does not imply difference in creation. Al-Ash'ari then gives a supplementary analogy: the body is originated because it did not precede other originated things. But sharing in origination does not mean the body must be identical in nature (e.g., movement) to other originated things. This reinforces that shared creation does not remove distinction in attributes.

God's Power to Grant Grace for Belief
Al-Ash'ari affirms that God can grant a lutf that would cause disbelievers to believe, with proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that God is capable of granting a grace (lutf) that would cause disbelievers to believe, but withholds it by wisdom, and the Quranic proof for this.

Core Idea
God's power is absolute; withholding grace is not weakness but wisdom.

In this passage, al-Ash'ari addresses a critical question about divine power and grace: Could God make disbelievers believe if He wanted? The answer reveals a fundamental point about God's absolute capability and the role of wisdom. You'll see how he uses Quranic verses to show that God can indeed grant such grace, but chooses not to for greater purposes.

Connect this to the original question: if God can withhold provision to prevent rebellion, He can also withhold lutf to prevent disbelief?

Commentary
This passage begins a new chapter discussing divine justice (ta'dil and tajwir). The first question is whether God is capable of granting a subtle grace (lutf) to disbelievers that would cause them to believe. The answer is yes, and the proof is that God is capable of doing things to believers that would cause them to rebel, but He refrains from doing so. This is supported by Quranic verses: if God extended provision to His servants, they would rebel; and if He gave immense wealth to disbelievers, people would all become disbelievers. Thus, God's withholding of such grace is not due to inability, but due to divine wisdom. The passage affirms God's absolute power over all things.
