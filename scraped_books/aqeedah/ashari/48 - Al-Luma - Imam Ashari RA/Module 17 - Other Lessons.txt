The Impossibility of Obliging the Impossible
Imam Ash'ari argues that impossible obligation is invalid, using cases of inability and missing limb.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Imam Ash'ari argues that impossible obligation (taklif ma la yutaq) is logically invalid, based on the nature of command and the conditions of ability.

Core Idea
Command requires ability; without it, command is meaningless.

Imam Ash'ari presents a sophisticated argument against the notion that God can command what is impossible for a person to do. He grounds his reasoning in the very purpose of a command: to accept or leave. By examining cases of missing limbs and inherent incapacity, he shows that such commands would be contradictions in terms. This passage from al-Luma builds a logical case that resonates through later kalam.

Guided Path
1
Understand the premise: opposites cannot coexist
Begin by grasping the logical principle that two opposites cannot exist simultaneously. This is the foundation of the argument.
2
Distinguish power from incapacity
Power (qudra) enables one opposite to exist, while incapacity (ajz) negates both the thing and its opposite. This asymmetry is crucial.
Notice that incapacity is not simply a lack of power; it renders both opposites impossible.
3
Analyze the objection about missing limb and incapacity
The opponent questions whether God can command a person who lacks a limb and has incapacity. Ash'ari answers with the purpose of command: acceptance or leaving, which requires the limb.
Without the limb, neither acceptance nor leaving is physically possible.
4
Follow the reductio ad absurdum
Ash'ari argues that if partial inability does not preclude command, then total inability should also not preclude it. But that leads to absurdities, like commanding without knowledge of God.
This reductio shows the original assumption is false.
5
Conclude: impossible obligation is never valid
The argument concludes that any command requiring something impossible (like zakat without wealth) is invalid because it contradicts the very nature of command.

Commentary
This passage is a proof against the possibility of impossible obligation (taklif ma la yutaq). The argument proceeds in several steps. First, the author states that it is impossible for two opposites to exist simultaneously; but in incapacity (al-ajz), one judges by the non-existence of both the thing one is incapable of and its opposite. This is unlike power (al-qudra), which requires one of the opposites. Therefore, power and incapacity are not analogous. Then he addresses an objection: can God command something when the person lacks the necessary limb and has incapacity? The answer is no, because the commanded person is supposed to accept or leave, but without the limb, neither taking nor leaving is possible. Similarly, incapacity precludes both taking and leaving because it extends to the thing and its opposite. He then makes a reductio: if it were necessary when God commands someone without ability for a specific act that He also command without any ability at all, then it would also be necessary when someone lacks some knowledge (like knowledge of God) that He command without any knowledge at all. Since this is absurd (God does not command without knowledge of Himself), the original premise is false. Therefore, it is not necessary that when someone lacks ability for a particular command, that God command despite total inability (e.g., missing limb or presence of incapacity). Finally, the author summarizes that all cases of impossible obligation (like commanding zakat without wealth) are invalid based on this reasoning.

Ibn Khafif enumerates four essential qualities for the spiritual seeker.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four essential qualities that a spiritual disciple must cultivate according to the will of Shaykh Ibn Khafif.

Core Idea
The spiritual path begins with repentance, useful knowledge, silence, and constant awareness of God.

This passage presents a concise spiritual curriculum from the great Sufi master Ibn Khafif. It outlines four essential qualities that any sincere seeker must adopt when embarking on the path. These qualities form a practical guide for inner transformation and discipline.

3

Commentary

It begins with the basmalah and praise, then states the purpose: a set of qualities the disciple must preserve when embarking on the spiritual path. The passage enumerates four qualities in sequence: 1) The first is repentance (nadam) for past heedlessness and abandoning injustices. This shows the necessity of turning away from sin and making amends. 2) The second is learning useful knowledge appropriate to one's time – not all knowledge, but that which can be practically applied. 3) The third is constant silence, solitude, and remembrance of God in all circumstances, indicating the importance of spiritual discipline and presence. 4) The fourth is awareness of God's rights in every posture and state, meaning constant consciousness of divine commands and prohibitions. The will thus provides a concise spiritual curriculum for the beginner.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the six spiritual disciplines that a seeker (murid) must cultivate according to Al-Ash'ari, including their rationale and interconnection.

Core Idea
Discipline of the stomach, tongue, and heart unlocks remembrance of Allah.

Al-Ash'ari outlines ten rules for the spiritual seeker; this passage presents the fifth through tenth. These are not mere external practices but internal disciplines that align the seeker's heart, speech, and actions toward sincerity and mindfulness of Allah. Let's examine each rule and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully

Rule 5 emphasizes acting with consultation and having a sincere brother. Rule 6 requires the heart to agree with the tongue and avoidance of worldly thoughts.
These rules establish external and internal support.

This rule is the longest, showing its importance.
4
Connect the final rules
Rules 9 and 10 promote good manners and moderation in basic needs. See how they complete the set of disciplines.
All rules aim at purifying the soul.

Core Idea
Six rules guard the seeker's heart: lower your gaze, keep ablution, avoid the heedless, and speak only of Allah's decree.

Imam al-Ash'ari continues his list of rules for the spiritual seeker. These six rules cover outward conduct—how to treat women, where to look, when to perform ablution, who to sit with, what to speak about, and how to refer to past events. They are practical disciplines that shape the believer's character and protect the heart from heedlessness and hypocrisy.
