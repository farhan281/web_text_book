The Need for a New Defense of Sunna
Editor explains the rise of sects and need for a new presentation of Sunni creed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the historical circumstances that prompted al-Ash'ari to write a new defense of Sunni orthodoxy, including the rise of sects, political pressures, and external influences.

Core Idea
Al-Ash'ari's work was a prophetic response to protect the creed from distortion.

This lesson sets the stage for al-Ash'ari's project. The passage describes a period of intense theological upheaval: new sects emerging, caliphs enforcing alien doctrines, and foreign philosophies being translated. Against this backdrop, the need for a fresh, authoritative presentation of Sunni creed became urgent. The editor frames al-Ash'ari's work as fulfilling the prophetic promise that scholars would safeguard the faith from corruption.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation to grasp the historical context and the rationale for al-Ash'ari's work.
Notice the vivid description of chaos and the call to preserve orthodoxy.
2
Identify the factors that changed
List the specific changes mentioned: emergence of sects, spread of innovations, confused thoughts, caliphal coercion, translation of philosophies.
These factors collectively created the need for a new defense.
3
Analyze the prophetic hadith
Examine the three corruptions the hadith warns against: distortion by extremists, misinterpretation by the ignorant, and false claims by liars.
This hadith provides the theological justification for al-Ash'ari's project.

Commentary
This passage describes the historical context that necessitated al-Ash'ari's work. This need is justified by the Prophet's hadith that scholars of each generation will inherit knowledge and protect it from three corruptions: distortion by extremists, misinterpretation by ignorants, and false claims by liars. Thus al-Ash'ari's project is shown as fulfilling this prophetic mandate.

The Mu'tazili Challenge and Ash'ari Response to al-Luma
Describes the Mu'tazili reaction to Al-Luma and al-Baqillani's counter-refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari's al-Luma sparked a debate between the Mu'tazila and the Ash'aris, leading to a chain of refutations.

Core Idea
Scholarly refutations reveal the high stakes of theological debate.

This lesson places al-Ash'ari's al-Luma in its historical context. The book was so significant that both supporters and opponents felt compelled to respond. We trace the exchange between the Mu'tazili judge 'Abd al-Jabbar and the Ash'ari chief judge al-Baqillani.

Guided Path
1
Understand the impact of al-Luma

2
Identify the Mu'tazili response
Find the name of the Mu'tazili scholar who wrote a refutation of al-Luma and the title of his work. This is 'Abd al-Jabbar al-Hamadhani and his book al-Mughni.
'Abd al-Jabbar was a student of Abu Hashim al-Jubba'i and a judge under the Buwayhids.
3
Note the Ash'ari counter-refutation
Recognize that al-Baqillani, the chief judge of Baghdad, wrote a response titled Naqd al-Naqd. Understand that this was a direct answer to 'Abd al-Jabbar's critique.
The title 'Naqd al-Naqd' means 'The Refutation of the Refutation.'
4
Reflect on the significance
Consider how this exchange illustrates the intense scholarly engagement between theological schools. Why would a book provoke such a reaction?
This shows the high stakes of theological debates in medieval Islam.

Meaning of Twisting Tongues About the Book
Imam Ashari explains the meaning of Q 3:78 regarding distortion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Q 3:78 refers to the People of the Book distorting the description of the Prophet, not to the creation of human actions.

Core Idea
Twisting tongues means distorting scripture, not creating acts.

Imam Ashari addresses an objection about the meaning of a Quranic verse. The opponent questions how the verse about twisting tongues relates to the topic of human actions. Ashari clarifies that the verse condemns altering the description of the Prophet, not creating one's own actions. Let's examine his explanation.

Guided Path
1
Read the verse and objection
Read the Quranic verse cited by the opponent and note how it is used as an objection. The opponent claims this verse supports the idea that humans create their own actions.
2
Understand Ashari's answer
Ashari explains that 'twisting tongues with the Book' means altering the description of the Prophet to make it appear part of their scripture.
The verse is about textual distortion, not about the origin of actions.
3
Recognize the anti-Qadariyya implication
This interpretation refutes the Qadariyya claim that humans create their own acts, because the verse condemns distorting scripture, not creating actions.

Interpretation of 'Not Created in Vain' and Mu'tazila Inconsistency
Al-Ash'ari interprets 'wa ma khalaqna... bāṭilan' and critiques Mu'tazila inconsistency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two possible interpretations of the verse 'not in vain' and how Al-Ash'ari uses another verse to critique the Mu'tazila for inconsistency in their interpretation.

Core Idea
The verse 'not in vain' does not deny that vain things are created; it denies purposefulness.

In this passage, Al-Ash'ari examines the meaning of the Quranic verse 'We did not create the heavens and the earth and what is between them in vain.' He offers two plausible interpretations and then exposes an inconsistency in the Mu'tazila's approach to interpreting related verses. By the end, you'll see how this argument supports the Ash'ari view that all actions, even vain ones, are created by Allah.

Guided Path
1
Examine the two interpretations
Read the first interpretation: the verse means Allah did not create everything in vain, because vain is itself a creation. Then read the second: Allah did not create them as vain initially; vain occurred later. Notice how both interpretations affirm that vain things are created.
2
Analyze the inconsistency argument
Al-Ash'ari cites another verse about creation in six days. He argues that its generality includes all beings and actions between heaven and earth. The Mu'tazila use one verse to deny that Allah creates vain actions but ignore the other verse that implies He creates all actions. Ask yourself: why is this a problem for them?
3
Reflect on the key takeaway
Consider how this debate relates to the broader issue of divine creation of human actions. The Ash'ari position is that Allah creates everything, including what may appear vain, while the Mu'tazila try to exclude evil acts from divine creation, leading to contradictions.

The Argument from Q3:78 on the Creation of Acts
Al-Ash'ari cites Q3:78 and poses a conditional against the Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Ash'ari uses a Qur'anic verse to expose an inconsistency in the Mu'tazila position on the creation of acts.

Core Idea
If false speech is not created, then obedience must also not be created—but Mu'tazila disagree.

Al-Ash'ari turns the Mu'tazila's own interpretation of Q3:78 against them. They claim that the false statements of the polytheists are not created by Allah because they are evil. But if that logic holds, then acts of obedience, which are good, should also not be created by Allah—yet the Mu'tazila affirm that Allah creates obedience. This lesson examines the first half of his reductio argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text carefully, noting the conditional structure. Then read the translation.
2
Identify the Mu'tazila premise
The Mu'tazila interpret Q3:78 to mean that Allah does not create false speech. Understand why they think this.
They believe Allah only creates good, not evil.
3
Trace Al-Ash'ari's logic
Al-Ash'ari asks: if false speech is not created, then why are acts of obedience created? According to the Mu'tazila, obedience is from Allah. This exposes a contradiction.
4
Reflect on the inconsistency
Consider why the Mu'tazila's position is inconsistent: they cannot consistently hold that Allah creates obedience but not false speech if both are 'from Allah' in some sense.
Al-Ash'ari's point is that creation is univocal: either Allah creates all acts or none.

Source Text

ويقال لهم: إن كان قول الله تعالى في المشركين: "يلوون ألسنتهم بالكتاب لتحسبوه من الكتاب وما هو من الكتاب ويقولون هو من عند الله وما هو من عند الله" معناه لم يخلقه الله فلم لا تكون الطاعات مخلوقة له؛ لأنها عندكم من عند الله تعالى؟ وإن كان الكفر والمعاصي غير مخلوقة لله تعالى؛ لأنها متفاوتة، فلِمَ لا تكون الطاعات مخلوقة له؛ لأنها عندكم غير متفاوتة؟
And it is said to them: If the saying of Allah Almighty concerning the polytheists: "They twist their tongues with the Scripture that you may think it is from the Scripture, but it is not from the Scripture, and they say it is from Allah, but it is not from Allah" means that Allah did not create it, then why are acts of obedience not created by Him, since according to you they are from Allah Almighty? Commentary
This passage presents a reductio ad absurdum argument against the Mu'tazila. Al-Ash'ari first cites Q3:78, which condemns the polytheists for twisting their words and falsely attributing them to Allah. He then poses a conditional: if the Mu'tazila interpret this verse to mean that Allah did not create that false speech (since it is evil), then by their own logic they must also say that acts of obedience are not created by Allah, because those acts are also from Allah according to them. But they affirm that Allah creates obedience. Similarly, if they say disbelief and disobedience are not created because they are diverse (mutaqāwita), then obedience, which is not diverse, should also not be created. The argument highlights the inconsistency in the Mu'tazila position that Allah creates only good acts but not evil ones.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer (mas'ala) concerning the label 'Qadariyya'. The author, Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari, begins by presenting the objection: the Qadariyya ask why the Ash'aris call them Qadariyya. The answer is that they claim to predetermine (tuqaddiruna) their own acts of acquisition (aksab) independently of their Creator.

Ash'ari then gives a precise definition: a Qadari is one who attributes the determination of his actions to himself, not to Allah. He illustrates with analogies of the goldsmith (sa'igh) and carpenter (najjar): the goldsmith is one who acknowledges that he forges, not one who claims that it is forged for him. Similarly, the carpenter is one who claims that he carpenters, not one who acknowledges that it is carpentered for him. Thus, a Qadari is one who claims to do his actions as predetermined for himself, without his Lord, and that his Lord does nothing from his acquisitive acts.

The objector then responds with a potential counter-argument: the Ash'aris would also be Qadariyya because they affirm qadar (divine decree). Ash'ari refutes this by clarifying that Ash'aris affirm that Allah predestines and creates their actions as predetermined for them, but they do not attribute that determination to themselves. Therefore, they are not Qadari. The argument is reinforced by a reductio ad absurdum: if affirming divine predestination for one's actions made one a Qadari, then the objectors themselves would also be Qadariyya for affirming that Allah predestines acts of obedience. Since they do not accept that label, their objection collapses.
