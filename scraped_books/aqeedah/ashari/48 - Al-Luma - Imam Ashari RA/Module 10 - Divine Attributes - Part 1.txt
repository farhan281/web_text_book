Proof for the Possibility of Resurrection
Rational and scriptural proofs for resurrection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational and scriptural proofs for the permissibility of bodily resurrection after death.

Core Idea
If God created once, recreating is even easier; Scripture confirms with vivid analogies.

This lesson examines a classic argument for the possibility of resurrection. The author presents a rational proof based on God's initial creation and then reinforces it with two Quranic verses. By the end, you will see how both reason and revelation affirm that God can indeed bring the dead back to life.

Guided Path
1
Understand the question posed
Read the opening question: 'What is the proof for the permissibility of recreating creation?' This sets up the challenge that resurrection seems impossible.
The question reflects a common doubt among skeptics.
2
Study the rational proof
The author argues: God created the first creation without a prior model. If He could do that, then recreating is not beyond His power. This is a logical inference from what He has already done.
3
Analyze the first Quranic example
Read Quran 36:78-79. God responds to the one who doubts by saying that He who created the first time can create again. The first creation is a direct precedent.
This verse addresses the specific doubt about decayed bones.
4
Analyze the second Quranic example
Read Quran 36:80. God brings fire from green wood – an apparent contradiction. This illustrates God's power to produce opposite effects from opposite causes, similar to giving life to dead bones.
The analogy shows that God can create life where we see only decay.
5
Reflect on the combined argument
The rational proof establishes a general principle; the Quranic examples provide concrete parallels. Together they make a powerful case for resurrection.
Both reason and revelation agree on this point.

Core Idea
The precise order in creation demands a Creator who knows every detail.

This lesson presents one of the classical kalam proofs for divine omniscience. Imam al-Ash'ari begins by addressing a hypothetical question: why do you say God is knowing? He then constructs a logical argument based on the observable harmony and wisdom in the human body and the cosmos. By examining this reasoning, you will see how the order we witness points directly to the knowledge of the Creator.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for God's attribute of knowledge (ʿilm). It begins with a hypothetical objection ('why do you say God is knowing?') and then provides a rational argument. The author first states the premise: well-ordered actions cannot proceed except from a knowing agent. He illustrates this with the analogy of weaving fine brocade—impossible for someone lacking skill and knowledge. Then he applies this principle to creation: observing the intricate harmony in the human body (life, senses, digestion, and completeness) and the celestial sphere (sun, moon, stars, and their motions) shows that their Maker must have full knowledge of their nature and essence. To reinforce the argument, he uses a reductio ad absurdum: if wise works could arise from an ignorant source, then we could not be certain that any animal behaviors or constructions come from knowledge—but that is impossible. Therefore, the conclusion stands: well-made crafts only come from a knower, so God, the Creator of all, is omniscient.

Proof of Life and Power through Crafted Acts
Al-Ash'ari proves that God is living and powerful through the existence of crafted acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari uses the existence of crafted acts to rationally prove that God is both living and powerful.

Core Idea
Crafted acts require a living, powerful agent; otherwise, all agency becomes uncertain.

Before diving into the theological arguments for God's attributes, we must see how reason itself points to their necessity. Al-Ash'ari presents a crisp proof: the very existence of designed actions in the world forces us to conclude that their ultimate source must be alive and capable. This passage lays the groundwork for understanding divine attributes as rationally necessary.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage once
Read the Arabic aloud or silently, letting the argument unfold naturally. Don't analyze yet; just absorb the flow.
Notice the logical connectors: 'likadhalika', 'fa-law', 'fa-lamma'.
2
Identify the core claim
Find the sentence that states the main conclusion: that crafted acts indicate God is living and powerful.
3

Al-Ash'ari uses a 'reductio ad absurdum' (if not, then...). Follow the conditional: if crafted acts could come from a non-living, powerless being, then we couldn't distinguish real agency from mere accidents. This leads to absurdity, so the original claim must be true.
This logical move is typical in kalam.
4
Reflect on the footnote
The footnote adds nuance: basic crafted acts prove life and power; perfected ones also prove knowledge. Consider what this distinction means.

Commentary
This passage is a rational proof for two divine attributes: life (al-hayat) and power (al-qudra). Al-Ash'ari argues that observable crafted acts (al-sana'i') — that is, actions that exhibit design and purpose — cannot occur except from an agent who is both living and powerful. He supports this by a reductio ad absurdum: if it were possible for such acts to come from a non-living or powerless source, then we would have no way to distinguish genuine actions from mere accidents; we could never be certain that any observed act actually came from a capable agent. Since this uncertainty is absurd (muhal), the existence of these acts themselves proves the agent must be living and powerful. The footnote expands: even basic acts indicate life and power, while perfected acts also indicate knowledge ('ilm).

Core Idea
A living being without defects necessarily perceives; Allah is living and defect-free, so He is Hearing and Seeing.

How can we rationally know that Allah is Hearing and Seeing without relying solely on scripture? In this passage, Al-Ash'ari answers that question using a simple logical inference from the attribute of life and the absence of defects. Let's read his argument.

The Impossibility of a Living Being Without Knowledge or Speech
Al-Ash'ari argues by analogy from the witnessed to the unseen.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Ash'ari's argument that a living being cannot be devoid of both a quality and its opposite, and how this applies to God's attributes of knowledge and speech.

Core Idea
A living being must have either a quality or its opposite; God, as Living, must have knowledge and speech.

In this passage, Al-Ash'ari establishes a foundational principle of his theology: that every living being must be characterized either by a quality (like knowledge) or its opposite (ignorance), never by a neutral absence. He then applies this rule to God, arguing that since God is Living, He must possess both knowledge and speech. This analogy from the witnessed world to the unseen is a core method in Sunni kalam.

Chapter on Speech and Will

[The living being] is devoid of knowledge and its opposites, so that it is not described as knowing nor as having the opposite of knowledge. The matter has come together in both: that it is impossible, among us, for a living being to be not knowing and not described with the opposite of knowledge, and that it is impossible among us for a living being to be not speaking and not described with the opposite of speech, and that no indication has been established for other than that in the unseen. So when they agree in impossibility, it must be that whoever permits a speaker in the unseen described with the opposite of speech along with his speech is like one who permits a knower in the unseen described with the opposite of knowledge along with his knowledge.

Thus it is also necessary: since it is impossible among us for a living being to be not knowing and not described with the opposite of knowledge, it must be impossible among us for a living being to be not speaking and not described with the opposite of speech. And it must be impossible in the unseen, and it must be that whoever permits one of the two matters in the unseen is like one who permits the other matter.

Commentary
This passage is an argument by analogy from the witnessed (shahid) to the unseen (ghaib) regarding the impossibility of a living being being devoid of both a quality and its opposite. Al-Ash'ari begins by considering a living being that is devoid of knowledge and its opposites—such a being would be neither knowledgeable nor ignorant, which is impossible in the witnessed world. He then applies the same reasoning to speech: a living being cannot be neither speaking nor having the opposite of speech (muteness or silence). Since no evidence has established a different ruling for the unseen, if it were permissible for God to be without speech and its opposite, then it would also be permissible for Him to be without knowledge and its opposite, which is absurd. Al-Ash'ari further argues that it is impossible in the witnessed world for a knowing being to simultaneously have the opposite of knowledge (ignorance) along with knowledge; likewise for a speaker to have the opposite of speech along with speech. Therefore, anyone who claims that God can be described with the opposite of speech while being speaking is equivalent to claiming that God can be described with the opposite of knowledge while being knowing. The conclusion is forced: since impossibility in the witnessed extends to the unseen, the same impossibility applies to God. Thus, God must be both knowing and speaking, without being characterized by the opposites.

Imam Ashari presents a rational proof that God is eternally willing.

If God were not eternally willing, He would be defective—which is impossible.

This lesson explores one of the earliest rational proofs in Ash'ari kalam. Imam Ashari argues that the absence of will in a living being necessarily implies a defect. Commentary
This passage is a rational proof for the eternity of God's will (إرادة). The author proceeds by a reductio argument. First, he states the analogy: any living being that is not willing anything at all must be described with one of the opposites of will, such as heedlessness, aversion, or languor. Similarly, a living being that is not knowing anything must be described with an opposite of knowledge, like ignorance or forgetfulness. This is a necessary logical consequence. Applying this to God: if God were not eternally willing, He would have to be described with an opposite of will. But for God, being described with any such opposite is impossible because it would imply defect and imperfection. Moreover, if the opposite of will were eternal, it would be a co-eternal entity, which is impossible because only God is eternal. If that opposite were to cease to exist, that would imply change in God, which is also impossible. Therefore, the only possibility is that God is eternally willing. The argument concludes that it is false to say God does not will, so it is established that He is eternal in His will.

Objection and Answer: The Opposite of Action vs. Speech and Will
Imam Ashari refutes an objection about the opposite of divine action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key distinction that inaction is not a real opposite attribute, unlike silence and aversion.

Core Idea
Silence is a real opposite of speech; inaction is not a real opposite of action.

The Consequence of God Creating Speech in Another
Ashari applies the principle of attribute derivation to refute the analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ashari argues that if God created speech in another being, that being would be called a speaker, making the Mu'tazilite analogy invalid.

Core Idea
If God creates an attribute in something, that thing takes its name.

Ashari continues his refutation of the Mu'tazilites by showing a consequence of their analogy. He points out that when God creates an attribute in an entity, that entity becomes described by that attribute. For example, a body receiving power becomes powerful. Applying this to speech would make the recipient a speaker, which is impossible for anything other than God. This lesson unpacks this argument step by step.

Guided Path

The passage is dense; take your time.

The conclusion is that speech cannot be compared to other created attributes.

Commentary
This passage provides an additional proof against the Mu'tazilite analogy. It points out that when God creates an attribute in another entity, that entity becomes described by the most specific description of that attribute. For example, if God creates power in a body, that body is called powerful; if He creates knowledge, it is knowledgeable, etc. By the same logic, if God created speech in another entity, that entity would be called a speaker (i.e., the name 'speaker' would be derived from the attribute of speech). Since it is impossible for anything other than God to be a speaker in the divine sense, the analogy breaks down. Therefore, speech cannot be compared to created acts or favors.
