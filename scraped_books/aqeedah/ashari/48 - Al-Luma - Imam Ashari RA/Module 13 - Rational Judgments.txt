The Argument for the Permissibility of Seeing Allah
Imam Ashari's syllogism for the permissibility of seeing Allah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument for the permissibility of seeing Allah based on the absence of impossibility.

Core Idea
Seeing Allah is possible because it does not entail anything impossible for Him.

Imam Ashari presents a concise logical proof that seeing Allah is permissible. He argues that if a thing does not involve any attribute that is impossible for Allah, then it is not impossible, and thus it is permissible. This lesson will walk through that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
Read the single-sentence Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice the logical flow: a conditional statement leading to a conclusion.
The passage is a complete syllogism in one sentence.
2
Identify key terms
Identify the terms: الرؤية (vision), المستحيل (impossible), الجائز (permissible). Understand their meanings in the context of Allah's attributes.
3
Understand the logic
Break down the argument: The premise is that vision does not involve anything impermissible for Allah. Therefore vision is not impossible. Since it is not impossible, it is permissible.
This is a valid logical inference: if no impossibility, then permissibility.
4
Reflect on significance
Consider why this argument is important in Islamic theology: it establishes that seeing Allah is rationally possible, contrary to some theological schools.

فلما لم يكن في إثباتِ الرؤية شيءٌ مما لا يجوز على الباري لم تكن الرؤية مستحيلة، وإذا لم تكن مستحيلة كانت جائزةً علَى اللَّهِ .
Since there is not in establishing vision something which is not permissible for the Eternal, vision is not impossible, and if it is not impossible, then it is permissible for Allah.
Source pages p. 167

Commentary
This passage is a logical proof for the permissibility of seeing Allah. The Imam begins by stating that establishing vision (ru'ya) does not involve anything that is not permissible (la yajuuz) for the Eternal (al-Bari). Therefore, vision is not impossible (mustaḥīla). Since it is not impossible, it is permissible (jā'iza) for Allah. This is a simple syllogism: if a thing does not entail an attribute that is impossible for Allah, then that thing is possible for Allah. The conclusion is that seeing Allah is possible.

Proof that Ability is Concurrent with the Act
Proves ability is simultaneous with the act via narrative and analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Ash'ari position holds that ability (istitāʿa) is simultaneous with the act, not prior.

Core Idea
Ability is known from the act itself, not before it.

In this passage, Imam Ashari uses a narrative from the story of Moses and a logical analogy to argue that a person's ability for an action exists at the same time as the action itself. This refutes the view that ability must precede the act.

Guided Path
1
Read the narrative example
Read the story of Moses and the woman from Midian. Notice how she judges his trustworthiness only after observing his behavior.
The narrative sets up the question: how did she know he was capable before the act?
2
Identify the key question
The author asks: how did she know he was capable (mustaṭīʿ) of what she saw from him before the act? The answer is that she only knew it after he performed the act.
3
Understand the conclusion
The author concludes that the ability for the act must be simultaneous with the act itself. This counters those who say ability precedes the act.
4
Examine the logical analogy
The analogy of a man praying: we do not know when his ability occurred except from the act of praying itself. This reinforces that ability is known only at the time of the act.
5
Reflect on broader application
The author states that this proof applies to all issues concerning ability. Consider how this unified reasoning shapes the Ash'ari position.

استشكال على مقارنة الاستطاعة للفعل

قال لها : امشي أمامي واهديني الطريق، ففعلت ذلك، فكانت الريح تصفها له، فأدركت موسى -عليه السلام- الخشية؛ فقال لها: امشي خلفي، وعرفيني الطريق بلسانك: يمنة ويسرة وتلقاء، ففعلت ذلك.

فكيف علمت أنه كان مستطيعا لما رأت منه قبل الفعل؛ وإنما ظهر لها ذلك منه بعد فعله إياه؟ فصح عندنا، وصحت الحجة على من خالفنا أن ينبغي أن تكون استطاعته لذلك مع نفس فعله له. والدليل على ذلك من القياس: أنا لو رأينا رجلا في الحال قائما يصلي لما كنا نعلم استطاعته متى حدثت له؟ إلا أنا نعلم من نفس الفعل أنها ظهرت منه للفعل، وهي الصلاة التي كان يفعلها.

وحجتنا على من خالفنا في كل ما يورده من المسائل في باب الاستطاعة كما رسمنا فيما بينا وشرحنا، وبالله التوفيق.
Objection concerning the comparison of ability to the act. He said to her: Walk in front of me and guide me the way, so she did that. The wind was describing her to him, so Moses (peace be upon him) was seized with fear; he said to her: Walk behind me, and describe the way to me with your tongue: right and left and straight ahead, so she did that. When she came to her father, she said to him: 'Indeed he is strong, trustworthy.' He became extremely angry with her, and said to her: O my daughter, as for his strength, you knew it from what you saw of him; how did you know his trustworthiness? So she informed him of what she saw from him. So how did she know that he was capable of what she saw from him before the act? Rather that [knowledge] became apparent to her only after he performed it? So it is established with us, and the proof is established against those who oppose us, that his ability for that should be simultaneous with his act itself. The evidence for that from analogy: if we saw a man currently standing and praying, we would not know when his ability occurred for him? Except we know from the act itself that it appeared from him for the act, which is the prayer he was performing. And our proof against those who oppose us in every issue he brings concerning the chapter of ability is as we have laid out in what we clarified and explained. And with Allah is success.
Source pages p. 243

Commentary
This passage is a proof that ability (istitāʿa) is concurrent with the act, not prior. It begins with a narrative example from the story of Moses and the woman from Midian (Quran 28:26). The woman tells her father that Moses is 'strong and trustworthy'. The father asks how she knows his trustworthiness, since she only saw his strength (drawing water). She explains from his behavior. The author then poses the key question: how did she know Moses was capable (mustaṭīʿ) of what she saw from him before the act? She only knew it after he performed the act. So the author concludes that we must hold that a person's ability for an act is simultaneous with the act itself, not before. This is a refutation of those who claim ability precedes the act. Then the author provides a direct logical analogy: if we see a man standing and praying, we do not know when his ability to pray arose; we only know from the act of praying itself that he had the ability at that moment. Hence, ability is known and exists at the moment of the act. The author ends by stating that this proof applies to all similar issues in the chapter of ability.

Misconception: Some think ability (istitāʿa) must exist before the act as a precondition.
Correction: This passage shows ability is only known and existent at the time of the act, not before.

Key Takeaway
Ability for an action is not known before the action but is known from the act itself; thus ability is simultaneous with the act.

Morality Depends on Divine Command
Ashari argues that commanding lying is permissible and that good and evil depend on command.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ashari's argument that good and evil are not intrinsic to actions but are determined by God's command or prohibition, using the example of lying and truthfulness.

Core Idea
Lying can be good if God commands it – morality depends on revelation.

In this passage, Ashari addresses a challenging question: could God command lying? He uses this to demonstrate a foundational Ashari principle that good and evil are determined by divine command, not by inherent qualities of actions. The passage shows how reported exceptions in revelation prove that moral value is contingent on divine will.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the structure: a conditional claim followed by evidence from reported traditions.
The passage is short but dense with theological implications.
2
Identify the main claim
Find where Ashari states that if God commanded lying, it would not be impermissible. This is the core idea.
3
Examine the supporting evidence
Ashari cites reports that God permitted lying in three situations and prohibited truthfulness in many. Ask yourself: why does he use these examples?
These are traditional exceptions known in Islamic law.
4
Understand the conclusion
The passage concludes that truthfulness became evil because of prohibition, and lying became good because of command. This shows that moral value is not intrinsic.
This is a key Ashari principle against the Mu'tazilite view.
5
Reflect on the misconception
Ashari is not advocating for lying; he is using a hypothetical to demonstrate a theological principle. Reflect on why someone might misunderstand this passage.
The common misunderstanding is that Ashari is justifying lying.

فأما الأمر بالكذب وجواز وروده بذلك. فإنه كان يقول: 'لو ورد الأمر به لم يستحل، مع أنه قد روي أن الله تعالى أباح الكذب في ثلاثة مواضع، ونهى عن الصدق في كثير من المواضع، فقبح صدق لأجل النهي، وحسن كذب لأجل الأمر. فعلم أن ذلك لم يقبح ولم يحسن لأجل أنه صدق وكذب.'
As for commanding lying and the permissibility of its occurrence, he used to say: 'If the command came with it, it would not be impermissible, especially since it has been reported that God Almighty permitted lying in three situations, and prohibited truthfulness in many situations. So truthfulness became evil because of the prohibition, and lying became good because of the command. Thus it was known that it did not become evil or good because it was truthfulness or lying.'
Source pages p. 254

Commentary
This passage addresses the issue of commanding lying. Ashari states that if God commanded lying, it would not be prohibited (يستحل) meaning it would be permissible. He supports this by citing reported instances where God permitted lying in three cases (likely the well-known exceptions: in war, to reconcile between people, and between spouses?) and prohibited truthfulness in many cases. This proves that moral value (حسن وقبح) is not intrinsic to the act itself (lying or truthfulness) but depends solely on the divine command or prohibition. Therefore, lying can be good if commanded, and truthfulness can be evil if prohibited.

Misconception & Correction

Correction: Ashari is not stating that God commands lying; he is using a hypothetical to show that if God did command it, it would become good. He is defending the principle that moral value depends on divine command, not advocating for lying. The reported exceptions are already known in Islamic law.
