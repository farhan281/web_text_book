Affirmation of Eternal Attributes and Their Nature
Ibn Khafif affirms Allah's eternal attributes and distinguishes attribute from described.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Allah's attributes are eternal, distinct from each other, and subsist in His essence without being identical to it or separate from it.

Core Idea
Allah's attributes are neither His essence nor other than it—they are eternal meanings subsisting in Him.

Ibn Khafif's concise statement establishes the Ash'ari position on divine attributes. He first affirms that Allah's attributes are eternal even before their objects exist, then distinguishes between knowledge and vision, and finally articulates the precise relationship between attribute and described. This passage is foundational for understanding how classical Sunni theology balances transcendence with the reality of divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the literal translation. Notice the three distinct sections: the pre-existence of attributes, the distinction between knowledge and vision, and the relation of attribute to described.
The Arabic uses parallelism to emphasize that attributes are prior to their objects.
2
Grasp the eternal pre-existence of attributes
Ibn Khafif affirms that Allah was Knower, Powerful, Seer, Provider, and Creator when nothing else existed. This shows that the attributes are eternal and not dependent on the existence of their objects.
This refutes any notion that Allah acquires attributes over time.
3
Distinguish knowledge from vision
The passage states that knowledge and vision are different attributes. Allah knows things before they exist, but only sees them when they exist. The non-existent is not seen and is not a 'thing' in the sense of being an object of vision.
This clarifies that knowledge is broader in scope than vision.
4
Understand the attribute-described relationship
The final sentence gives the precise Ash'ari formula: the attribute is not the described, nor is it other than the described, but a meaning (معنى) subsisting in the described. This is the middle path between identity and separation.
This avoids both anthropomorphism (making attributes identical to essence) and denial of attributes (making them separate).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Attributes are real meanings in the essence – not the essence itself, nor other than it.

He then clarifies that attributes like knowledge and power exist eternally even without their objects. Affirming Reported Attributes without Anthropomorphism
Al-Ash'ari enumerates four points on affirming reported attributes without anthropomorphism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari affirms reported attributes like istiwa', nuzul, and hand without falling into anthropomorphism or denial.

Core Idea
Affirming divine attributes without tashbih is the Ash'ari middle path.

Al-Ash'ari now addresses the most sensitive area of theology: attributes that seem to imply a body. He shows a balanced approach—affirming the reality of these attributes while negating any human-like qualities. This passage lays out his method for istiwa', nuzul, and the hand, then generalizes to all such reports.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.

Identify the four points

Al-Ash'ari affirms istiwa' and nuzul as attributes, not as physical actions. Compare his wording: 'istiwa' not sitting' and 'nuzul not movement'.

Commentary
This passage presents the Ash'ari approach to the so-called 'attributes of reports' (al-sifat al-khabariyya)—those attributes that, if taken literally, might imply anthropomorphism. Al-Ash'ari outlines four points under this heading. First (a): Affirming istiwa' (usually interpreted as 'establishing' or 'ascending') but negating that it means sitting (julus); similarly affirming nuzul (descent) but negating that it means bodily movement (intiqal). This is the principle of tafwid or ta'wil? Here al-Ash'ari seems to affirm the words as attributes without their apparent physical meanings. Second (b): He explicitly states that Allah's 'istiwa' over the Throne' and 'nuzul to the lower heaven' are to be understood as attributes (bi ma'na al-sifa), not as physical locomotion. Third (c): The 'Hand' of Allah is affirmed as a real attribute, not as a bodily limb (jaraha); when Allah created Adam with His Hand, it does not mean with the hand of His power (qudra) but with the hand of His attribute—implying that the Hand is a distinct attribute. Fourth (d): For all other reported attributes, the methodology is to accept the revelatory texts at face value while denying any anthropomorphic interpretation; such matters belong to the unseen and are to be taken as they come (hakaidha jami' al-akhbar). This is the classic Ash'ari stance: affirm the wording and the reality, but negate any literal anthropomorphism.

He concludes with a doxology affirming God's oneness, attributes, and glory.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think that affirming attributes compromises God's oneness.
Correction: Al-Ash'ari affirms both transcendence and attributes, showing they are not contradictory.

?
