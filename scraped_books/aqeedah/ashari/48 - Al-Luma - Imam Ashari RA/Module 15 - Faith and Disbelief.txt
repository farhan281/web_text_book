The Servant's Obligations Regarding Blessings and Trials
Ash'ari answers whether a servant is free from a blessing requiring gratitude or a trial requiring patience.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that a servant is always in a state of either blessing or trial, and that trials are of two types: those requiring patience and those requiring rejection (disbelief and sins).

Core Idea
You are never free from a blessing or a trial, but not all trials demand patience.

This passage from Imam al-Ash'ari addresses a fundamental question about the human condition: are we ever in a neutral state? The answer is no – we are always under a blessing requiring gratitude or a trial requiring patience. However, the author makes a critical distinction among trials: some must be patiently endured, while others, like disbelief and sin, must be actively rejected. Let's explore this teaching.

Faith is inward conviction, not outward speech or actions.

In this opening of the chapter on faith, Imam al-Ash'ari presents his foundational definition. He argues that faith is essentially tasdiq (affirmation) of the heart, and that mere verbal confession without heartfelt belief does not constitute true faith. This passage establishes the inner core of faith and distinguishes it from hypocrisy.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Ash'ari's argument that the linguistic meaning of iman is tasdiq (affirmation), and why this definition serves as the foundation for his theology.

Core Idea
Iman in Arabic means inner affirmation, not outward works.

Al-Ash'ari begins his discussion of faith by grounding it in the language of the Qur'an. He argues that since the Qur'an was revealed in Arabic, the meaning of key religious terms must be understood according to Arabic linguistic usage. In this lesson, we examine his two proofs—from the Qur'an itself and from common speech—that iman means tasdiq (affirmation).

2
Identify the two proofs for tasdiq
Al-Ash'ari provides two arguments: one from Qur'anic usage (the verse about Joseph's brothers) and one from common Arabic speech (e.g., 'he believes in the punishment of the grave'). Identify each.
The common speech example shows that 'believing in' something means 'affirming' it.
3
Reflect on why this definition matters

قال الله - تعالى -: ﴿وَمَا أَرْسَلْنَا مِن رَّسُولٍ إِلَّا بِلِسَانِ قَوْمِهِ﴾ [إبراهيم: ٤]، وقال - تعالى -: ﴿بِلِسَانٍ عَرَبِيٍّ مُّبِينٍ﴾ [الشعراء: ١٩٥]. فلما كان الإيمان في اللغة التي أنزل الله -تعالى- بها القرآن هو التصديق، وقال الله - تعالى -: ﴿وَمَا أَنتَ بِمُؤْمِنٍ لَّنَا وَلَوْ كُنَّا صَادِقِينَ﴾ [يوسف: ١٧] أي: بمصدق لنا. وقالوا جميعًا: «فلان يؤمن بعذاب القبر والشفاعة» يريدون يصدق بذلك، فوجب أن يكون الإيمان هو ما كان عند أهل اللغة إيمانًا، وهو: التصديق.
فإن قال قائل: فحدثونا عن الفاسق من أهل القبلة أمؤمن هو؟ قيل له: نعم، مؤمن بإيمانه فاسق بفسقه وكبيرته. وقد أجمع أهل اللغة أن من كان منه ضرب فهو ضارب، ومن كان منه قتل فهو قاتل، ومن كان منه كفر فهو كافر، ومن كان منه فسق فهو فاسق، ومن كان منه تصديق فهو مصدق، وكذلك من كان فيه إيمان فهو مؤمن. ولو كان الفاسق لا مؤمنا ولا كافرا، لم يكن منه كفر ولا إيمان، إذ لا كان [إبطال لكان؟] لا موحدا ولا ملحدا، ولا وليا ولا عدوا، فلما استحال المنزلة بين ذلك استحال أن يكون الفاسق لا مؤمنا ولا كافرا، كما قالت المعتزلة المنزلتين. وأيضا: فإذا كان الفاسق مؤمنا قبل فسقه بتوحيده، فحدوث الزنا، بعد التوحيد، لا يبطل اسم الإيمان الذي لم يفارقه. وأيضا: فقد كان الناس قبل حدوث واصل بن عطاء رئيس المعتزلة، على مقالتين: منهم: خوارج يكفرون مرتكبي الكبائر، ومنهم: أهل استقامة، يقولون: هو مؤمن بإيمانه فاسق بكبيرته.
Ruling on the committer of a major sin. Allah - exalted - said: {And We did not send any messenger except with the language of his people} [Ibrahim: 4], and He - exalted - said: {in a clear Arabic tongue} [Ash-Shu'ara': 195]. Since faith (al-iman) in the language with which Allah revealed the Qur'an is affirmation (al-tasdiq), and Allah - exalted - said: {And you are not a believer (bi-mu'min) in us, even if we were truthful} [Yusuf: 17], meaning: a confirmer (musaddiq) of us. And they all said: 'So-and-so believes in the punishment of the grave and intercession,' meaning he affirms that. So it is necessary that faith be what it is among the people of language, and it is: affirmation (al-tasdiq). If a questioner says: Inform us about the sinful person (al-fasiq) from the people of the qibla, is he a believer? It is said to him: Yes, a believer by his faith, sinful by his sin and his major sin. And the people of language have agreed that whoever has from him striking is a striker, whoever has from him killing is a killer, whoever has from him disbelief is a disbeliever, whoever has from him sin is a sinner, whoever has from him affirmation is an affirmer, and likewise whoever has in him faith is a believer. And if the sinful person were neither a believer nor a disbeliever, there would not be from him disbelief nor faith, since he would be [؟] neither a monotheist nor an atheist, neither a friend nor an enemy. Since the intermediate position is impossible, it is impossible that the sinful person be neither a believer nor a disbeliever, as the Mu'tazila said regarding the two positions. And also: Since the sinful person was a believer before his sin by his monotheism, the occurrence of adultery after monotheism does not invalidate the name of faith which he has not left. And also: The people before the advent of Wasil ibn 'Ata', the leader of the Mu'tazila, were upon two statements: among them, the Khawarij who declare the committers of major sins to be disbelievers, and among them, the people of uprightness who say: he is a believer by his faith, sinful by his major sin.

Commentary
This passage is a combined definition and argument. Al-Ash'ari first defines iman (faith) as tasdiq (affirmation or belief) based on the Arabic language in which the Qur'an was revealed. He cites the verse 'We did not send any messenger except with the language of his people' and the verse 'in a clear Arabic tongue' to establish the linguistic basis. He then gives two proofs: first, the Qur'anic usage where 'mu'min' means 'musaddiq' (affirmer) as in the story of Joseph, and second, the common speech of the Arabs (e.g., 'he believes in the punishment of the grave') which means 'he affirms it'. Therefore, iman linguistically is tasdiq. Then he addresses the question about the fasiq (sinner) from the people of the qibla (the Muslim direction of prayer). The answer: he is a believer by his iman and a sinner by his sin. Al-Ash'ari then presents an argument from Arabic usage: if someone performs an action, he is called by that action (e.g., striker, killer, disbeliever, sinner, affirmer). Since the fasiq has iman (affirmation) in his heart, he must be called a believer. If he were neither believer nor disbeliever, he would have neither iman nor kufr, which is absurd because he must be one or the other. The Mu'tazila's position of a 'manzila bayn al-manzilatayn' (intermediate position) is thus impossible. Al-Ash'ari then gives another argument: the fasiq was a believer before his sin (by means of tawhid), and the occurrence of a sin like adultery does not remove the name of iman because iman persists. Finally, he notes a historical fact: before Wasil ibn 'Ata', the founder of Mu'tazila, there were only two positions among Muslims: the Khawarij who declared major sinners to be disbelievers, and the Ahl al-Istiqama (people of uprightness) who said he is a believer by his iman and a sinner by his sin. Thus the Mu'tazila innovated a third position.

The Believer Who Sins: The Status of the Fasiq
Al-Ash'ari argues the fasiq remains a believer, refuting the Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Ash'ari's arguments that a Muslim who commits a major sin remains a believer, and how he refutes the Mu'tazila intermediate position.

Core Idea
A fasiq is a believer by his faith, sinful by his sin.

After defining iman as inner affirmation, Al-Ash'ari tackles a pressing question: What happens to a believer who commits a major sin? The Mu'tazila claimed such a person is in an intermediate state—neither believer nor disbeliever. Al-Ash'ari uses linguistic principles, logical reasoning, and historical precedent to argue that the fasiq remains a believer, though sinful.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the fasiq's status
Read the Arabic passage and translation, paying attention to the question and answer, and the series of arguments that follow.
Note the key phrase: 'mu'min bi-imanihi, fasiq bi-fisqihi'.
2
Understand the linguistic argument from action-naming
Al-Ash'ari argues that if a person performs an action, he is called by that action. Since the fasiq has iman (affirmation), he must be called a believer.
This is based on Arabic grammar: a person who strikes is called a striker, etc.
3
Analyze the reductio ad absurdum against the intermediate state
Al-Ash'ari shows that if the fasiq were neither believer nor disbeliever, he would have neither iman nor kufr, which is impossible because he must be one or the other.
This refutes the Mu'tazila position of a middle state.
4
Examine the temporal and historical arguments
The passage also argues that since the fasiq had iman before sinning, sin does not remove iman. Plus, historically, before Wasil ibn 'Ata', there were only two positions: Khawarij (disbeliever) and Ahl al-Istiqama (believer-sinner).
This shows the Mu'tazila innovation.

Commentary
This passage is a combined definition and argument. Al-Ash'ari first defines iman (faith) as tasdiq (affirmation or belief) based on the Arabic language in which the Qur'an was revealed. He cites the verse 'We did not send any messenger except with the language of his people' and the verse 'in a clear Arabic tongue' to establish the linguistic basis. He then gives two proofs: first, the Qur'anic usage where 'mu'min' means 'musaddiq' (affirmer) as in the story of Joseph, and second, the common speech of the Arabs (e.g., 'he believes in the punishment of the grave') which means 'he affirms it'. Therefore, iman linguistically is tasdiq. Then he addresses the question about the fasiq (sinner) from the people of the qibla (the Muslim direction of prayer). The answer: he is a believer by his iman and a sinner by his sin. Al-Ash'ari then presents an argument from Arabic usage: if someone performs an action, he is called by that action (e.g., striker, killer, disbeliever, sinner, affirmer). Since the fasiq has iman (affirmation) in his heart, he must be called a believer. If he were neither believer nor disbeliever, he would have neither iman nor kufr, which is absurd because he must be one or the other. The Mu'tazila's position of a 'manzila bayn al-manzilatayn' (intermediate position) is thus impossible. Al-Ash'ari then gives another argument: the fasiq was a believer before his sin (by means of tawhid), and the occurrence of a sin like adultery does not remove the name of iman because iman persists. Finally, he notes a historical fact: before Wasil ibn 'Ata', the founder of Mu'tazila, there were only two positions among Muslims: the Khawarij who declared major sinners to be disbelievers, and the Ahl al-Istiqama (people of uprightness) who said he is a believer by his iman and a sinner by his sin. Thus the Mu'tazila innovated a third position.

Commentary
This passage presents a reductio ad absurdum argument against the Mu'tazilī claim that the grave sinner is neither a believer nor a disbeliever (and thus not a believer). The argument is structured as follows: If one were allowed to say that a person with faith who commits a major sin is neither a believer nor a sinner (the Mu'tazilī position), then by the same logic one could say that he is only a believer due to his faith and should not be called a sinner at all. The parallel shows that denying the subject status of a person who possesses both faith and sin leads to logical contradictions. Therefore, the only consistent position is to acknowledge both: the person is a believer because of his faith and a sinner because of his sin. Imam Ash'ari's View on the Fate of Sinful Believers
Ibn Furak reports Ash'ari's position that the punishment of a fasiq is not eternal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Ash'ari's stance that the punishment of a sinful Muslim (fasiq) is not eternal, based on the contingency of divine promise and threat upon final states.

Core Idea
The fasiq's good deeds guarantee eventual Paradise, despite temporary punishment.

This lesson presents Imam Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari's nuanced position on the fate of Muslims who die in sin without repentance. Reported by Ibn Furak, the passage explains why Ash'ari rejected eternal punishment for such individuals, grounding his view in the conditionality of divine promises and threats. We will explore his key arguments from the Qur'an.

Guided Path
1

Ash'ari states that Allah's promise and threat are contingent upon 'awaqib al-ahwal' (final states). This means they apply based on how a person dies.

Ash'ari argues that the fasiq possesses the greatest acts of obedience (ma'rifa, tawheed, love, veneration). Qur'anic verses promise reward for these and state that Allah does not waste good deeds.
The proof leads to the conclusion that punishment cannot be eternal.

Ash'ari concludes that the fasiq's punishment is not eternal; he will inevitably return from the Fire and enter Paradise.

It is reported by Ibn Furak, a major transmitter of Ash'ari's teachings. The passage begins by stating that Ash'ari held that Allah's promise (wa'd) and threat (wa'eed) are contingent upon the outcomes of states (awaqib al-ahwal), meaning that they apply based on the final state of the person at death. For example, the promise of paradise to believers is conditioned upon dying in that state of faith, and the threat of hell to the polytheist is conditioned upon dying in shirk. Based on this, Ash'ari suspended judgment (tawaqquf) on the fate of the fasiq who dies without repentance. He allowed two possibilities: either Allah forgives him or punishes him temporarily (a measure of punishment) and then admits him to Paradise. He argued that the fasiq possesses the greatest acts of obedience: knowledge (ma'rifa) of Allah, tawhid, love for Allah, and veneration. Since Allah has promised reward for these and does not waste any good deed (as per verses like {Whoever does an atom's weight of good will see it} and {We do not waste the reward of those who do good}), it follows that the fasiq must eventually see his reward. Therefore, his punishment cannot be eternal (mu'ayyad); he will inevitably return from the Fire and enter Paradise. Ash'ari concluded that the apparent meanings (zawaahir) of Quranic verses concerning promise and threat do not necessitate a definitive judgment in this world (al-qadaa), because they are conditioned on final states.

Fourth, shaytan does not know the heart's contents; his only power is whispering (waswasa). Finally, it distinguishes nafs (soul), ruh (spirit), and hayat (life): nafs is not ruh, ruh is not hayat. The ruh departs the body during sleep, while life only leaves at death.
