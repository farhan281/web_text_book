Al-Ash'ari's Linguistic Argument for Vision
Al-Ash'ari proves nazar means vision via linguistic coupling and sabr wa taqsim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari uses the linguistic coupling of 'nazar' with 'faces' and the method of division and elimination (sabr wa taqsim) to prove that the word means vision by the eye.

Core Idea
When 'nazar' is tied to faces, it means vision, not waiting.

Al-Ash'ari begins his proof for the vision of God (ru'ya) by examining a key Quranic verse: {s}إِلَى رَبِّهَا نَاظِرَةٌ{/s}. The word 'nazar' can mean several things, but the context determines its precise meaning. In this passage, he argues that because the verse mentions faces, the intended meaning must be vision by the eye, not mental contemplation or waiting. He then applies the logical method of sabr wa taqsim — enumerating all possible meanings and eliminating all but one.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic carefully, noting how al-Ash'ari connects 'nazar' with 'wujuh' (faces) and draws an analogy with 'qalb' (heart).
Pay attention to the parallel structure: nazar+face → eye's look; nazar+heart → heart's look.
2
Understand the linguistic rule
Al-Ash'ari states a rule: when a word like nazar is coupled with a bodily part, it takes the specific meaning associated with that part. Identify this rule and see how it applies.
3
Trace the sabr wa taqsim
He says nazar has four modes; he invalidates three. List the four modes from the passage and see which are eliminated.
The fourth mode is 'nazar ru'ya' — vision by the eye.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Ask yourself: why is this argument considered a rational proof? How does it establish that the verse affirms vision of God?
The conclusion is that 'nazar' means 'seeing' (ra'iya).

Chapter on the Vision. It is not permissible that He means 'waiting' because when 'nazar' is coupled with mention of faces, its meaning is not the heart's nazar which is waiting, just as when nazar is coupled with mention of the heart, its meaning is not the eye's nazar. For if one says 'Look with your heart at this matter,' its meaning is the heart's look. Likewise, when nazar is coupled with the face, its meaning is only the face's look, and the look by the face is the look which is vision (ru'ya) that is by the eye which is in the face. So it is established that the meaning of His saying - exalted - 'To their Lord looking' is 'seeing', since it is not permissible that He means any of the other modes of nazar. And since nazar does not escape four modes, and three modes are invalid, the fourth mode is valid: it is the look of vision by the eye that is in the face. If someone says: 'Did not Allah - exalted - say: "And faces on that day frowning, expecting that an affliction will be done to them", and expectation (zann) is not by the face? Likewise His saying: "Faces on that day radiant, to their Lord looking" meant the heart's look.' It is said to him: No. Because expectation (zann) is not by the face, and it is only by the heart. So when expectation is coupled with mention of the face, its meaning is the heart's expectation, since expectation is only by it. So if nazar were only by the heart, then when He mentions it with mention of the face, it would be necessary to refer it to the heart. But since nazar can be by the face and by other than it, it is necessary when He couples it with mention of the face that He means by it the face's look, just as when He couples it with mention of the heart, it is necessary that He means by it the heart's look.

Commentary
This passage is a proof and refutation. Al-Ash'ari begins by establishing the meaning of 'nazar' in the verse 'إِلَى رَبِّهَا نَاظِرَةٌ'. He argues that it cannot mean 'waiting' (nazar al-qalb) because the word nazar is coupled with faces. When nazar is coupled with the face, it means vision by the eye; when coupled with the heart, it means mental contemplation. This is a linguistic rule. He then uses the method of division and elimination (sabr wa taqsim): nazar has four possible meanings; three are invalidated, leaving only the fourth: vision by the eye. The objection brought is that in another verse, 'ظن' (expectation/thinking) is attributed to faces but it means heart's expectation, so similarly 'nazar' could mean heart's look. Al-Ash'ari refutes this by pointing out that 'zann' is only possible by the heart, so its attribution to faces forces a metaphorical interpretation. But nazar can be both by the face and by the heart, so when it is coupled with the face, the literal meaning (face's look) is intended. The argument preserves the literal meaning of the verse affirming the vision of God for the believers in the afterlife.

Refuting the Objection from Zann
Al-Ash'ari refutes an objection using the difference between zann and nazar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari refutes the objection that 'nazar' could mean heart's look by distinguishing between the words 'zann' and 'nazar' and their respective couplings.

Core Idea
Because zann is only heart-based, its coupling with faces forces metaphor; nazar is not so restricted.

An opponent raises a clever objection: In another verse, 'zann' (expectation/thinking) is attributed to faces, but clearly it means a heart's action. So why can't 'nazar' also mean a heart's action when attributed to faces? Al-Ash'ari responds by pointing out a crucial difference: zann can only be performed by the heart, so when the Quran says 'faces that are expecting', it must be metaphorical. But nazar can be performed by both the face (eye) and the heart, so when the verse specifically ties it to faces, the literal meaning (vision) is intended.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the objection from the opponent: they cite the verse about 'zann' with faces and argue that since zann is heart's work, the phrase is metaphorical; similarly nazar could be metaphorical.
The opponent uses an apparent parallel to challenge al-Ash'ari's conclusion.
2
Understand al-Ash'ari's response
Al-Ash'ari points out the key difference: zann is only possible with the heart, so when it is mentioned with faces, it must be interpreted as heart's action. But nazar can be both by the face (eye) and by the heart.
This distinction is critical.
3
Learn the rule he applies
He states: 'Since nazar can be by the face and by other than it, when it is coupled with the face, it must be taken as face's look.' Contrast with zann which is exclusively heart-based.
4
Reflect on the overall argument
Ask yourself: How does this refutation strengthen the original proof? Why is it important to defend the literal meaning of the verse?
It preserves the doctrine that believers will see Allah in the afterlife.

al-Ash'ari explains Quran 3:78 as a distortion of the Prophet's description.

Distortion is in the description, not the Book.

The verse in question describes a group who twist their tongues to make their speech appear as scripture. Al-Ash'ari addresses a potential objection: does this verse imply that some parts of revelation are not from Allah? His answer clarifies that the distortion is about the description of the Prophet, not the text of the Book itself. This lesson unpacks his argument.

Not Created in Vain: The Purpose of Reward and Punishment
Al-Ash'ari explains that creation has a purpose: reward and punishment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari uses the Quran itself to argue that the purpose of creation is the ultimate reward and punishment, refuting the disbelievers' denial of the afterlife.

Core Idea
Creation is not random; it points to an ultimate reckoning.

When the Quran says creation was not made 'in vain,' it raises a theological question: what purpose does it serve? Al-Ash'ari addresses this by linking the verse to the disbelievers' assumption about no afterlife, grounding divine justice in the very act of creation.

Guided Path

The Two Meanings of Lām: Will and Consequence
Explanation of the two meanings of the lām and their application.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between lām al-ʿāqibah (consequence) and lām al-irādah (will), and how Al-Ash'ari applies this to verses about worship and creation for Hell.

Core Idea
Lām in divine actions indicates Allah's will, not mere consequence.

Al-Ash'ari, as reported by Ibn Furak, teaches that the phrase 'Allah did X for Y' can mean either that Y is the consequence or that Y is what Allah willed. In the verse of worship, the correct meaning is will, because Allah cannot will what He knows will not happen. This lesson unpacks this crucial distinction and its application to other verses.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the two meanings
Read the Arabic passage from 'قال ابن فورك' to the end. It presents two possible meanings of the lām: consequence (like the verse about Pharaoh) and will (like building a house to live in).
The passage then applies the second meaning to the worship verse.
2
Identify the two types of lām
The first type is lām al-ʿāqibah (لَام العاقبة), indicating the outcome of an action without necessarily being the intention. The second is lām al-irādah (لَام الإرادة), indicating the agent's will.
Al-Ash'ari opts for the second for the worship verse.
3
Understand the proof from impossibility
Al-Ash'ari argues that it is impossible for Allah to will something He knows will not happen. Since He knows that some will not worship, He cannot will their worship. Therefore the lām must indicate will for those He knew would worship.
This proof relies on the principle that divine will is always fulfilled.
4
Apply to other verses and general principle
The same logic applies to the verse about creating many for Hell: Allah willed their entrance into Hell. The passage concludes with a general rule: the lām of purpose is the default, and one only resorts to lām of consequence with evidence, as in the example of benefit and harm.
Thus, divine actions are interpreted with the lām of will unless there is clear evidence for mere consequence.
