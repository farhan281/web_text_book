This passage is a proof for the validity of rational reasoning (nazar) in theological matters. It begins with an objection: someone requests more evidence for the soundness of reasoning. The author responds by quoting the Quranic story of Prophet Ibrahim (AS). Then he observed the moon and similarly rejected it when it set. The conclusion: both the star and the moon share the attribute of setting (ufool), which proves they cannot be divine—since God does not change or pass away. The author then explicitly states: 'This is the reasoning and inference.' Thus, the prophet himself used rational deduction from observable phenomena. This refutes those who reject nazar (the deniers) and those who deviate from it (the deviators). The argument argues that nazar is not a blameworthy innovation but a method used by prophets.

He begins by establishing a parallel between prayer and humility, forcing them to either accept inherence for both or face absurd consequences. Commentary
This passage is a dialectical refutation of opponents who deny that actions like prayer, humility, lying, and obedience inhere in human agents. The argument proceeds through a series of analogies and reductio ad absurdum. First, al-Ash'ari establishes that if a praying person is called 'praying' because prayer inheres in him, then logically a humble person is called 'humble' because humility inheres in him—since humility is in the heart and the whole person is humble. If the opponents object that only the heart is humble, not the whole person, al-Ash'ari forces them to the conclusion that the tongue must then be the real speaker and the heart the real willer (since attributes inhere in specific parts). Next, if they deny that the humble person is humble due to inherence, al-Ash'ari argues that this leads to the absurdity that if God commands humility, He Himself must be humble (since on their view the commander does the act). When they reply that God creates humility in another without being humble Himself, al-Ash'ari retorts that the same logic would permit God to create lying in another if He commanded lying. If they then claim that a liar is a liar because he performs the lie, al-Ash'ari says the same applies to the humble person. The second part, under the chapter heading 'On Justification and Injustice', continues: if the opponents say the humble person is not humble by inherence nor by his own action, then the same applies to the liar. Then al-Ash'ari moves to the example of motion: if God commands us to move, it is permissible that He make us moving; similarly, if He commanded lying, He could make us liars. Finally, he draws on their own admission that prayer consists of movements and that the mover is moving by inherence of motion—so obedience should also be by inherence of obedience when one performs a commanded movement. The opponents are forced to agree, showing the consistency of al-Ash'ari's position.

Ash'ari's Linguistic Method for Defining Faith
Ibn Furak explains Ash'ari's reliance on pre-Shari'ah linguistic usage for defining faith.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Shar'i terms keep their language; 'iman' always meant tasdiq.

Ibn Furak explains a cornerstone of Ash'ari's theology: the Shari'ah did not change the meanings of Arabic words. Since the Qur'an was revealed in Arabic, its terms must be understood according to their original language. Here, Ash'ari applies this principle to 'iman' (faith), showing that before Islam it meant only tasdiq (affirmation), while actions were called obedience, not faith.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological exposition attributed to Ibn Furak explaining Ash'ari's approach to defining faith. Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer unit. The objection raises three Quranic verses that seem to promise certain punishment for specified actions (e.g., wicked being in Hellfire, those who do injustice, those who consume orphans' wealth). The challenge is: Do these verses necessarily apply to everyone who commits these acts? Imam al-Ash'ari answers by examining the linguistic forms used. He points out that the word 'man' (من) in Arabic can denote either generality ('whoever' meaning all) or specificity ('someone' meaning some). Its form alone does not indicate which; context is needed. Similarly, the word 'inna' (إن) with a relative clause (e.g., 'inna alladhina...') can also be ambiguous. Therefore, one cannot make a definitive judgment (qat') that these verses apply to all offenders or only to some based solely on the wording. He then argues that if someone claims that such forms inherently imply generality until evidence for specificity appears, that claim is no stronger than the opposite claim that they imply specificity until evidence for generality appears. Since both positions are equally supported by the language, both must be set aside (mulghayayn) unless external evidence (dalala) resolves the ambiguity. Finally, he quotes a line of poetry by Zuhayr illustrating that whoever does not adapt to circumstances suffers harm, possibly as an analogy for the need to consider context in interpretation.

Original Permissibility: The Default Ruling
Ash'ari states the principle of original permissibility and its application.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the foundational legal maxim of original permissibility and its application to Muslim property and slaughtered meat.

Core Idea
Everything is permissible until proof of prohibition is established.

This lesson introduces a key principle in Islamic legal theory: the default ruling for things is permissibility (ibaha) unless there is clear evidence of prohibition (hazr). Imam al-Ash'ari states this maxim and then applies it to the property and slaughtered animals of Muslims, affirming their lawfulness unless specific transmitted evidence forbids them. This principle guards against unwarranted suspicion and scrupulosity.
