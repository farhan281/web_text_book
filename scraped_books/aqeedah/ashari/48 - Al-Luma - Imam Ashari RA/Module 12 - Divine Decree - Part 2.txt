Refuting the Analogy: The Reductio Ad Absurdum
Imam Ashari presents a reductio ad absurdum to show that the analogy between power and incapacity leads to impossible consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Ashari uses a reductio ad absurdum to further dismantle the claim that power over a thing implies power over its opposite.

Core Idea
If power were like incapacity, it would necessitate both a thing and its opposite — an impossibility.

After breaking the analogy with the counter-example of assistance, Imam Ashari deepens his refutation by showing that the objector's reasoning leads to an absurd consequence. If power truly mirrored incapacity, then power over a thing would require power over its opposite, and the result would be that a person could simultaneously produce both — which is impossible. This lesson unpacks that reductio.

Guided Path
1
Read the reductio argument
Read the second part of the passage starting with 'Also: if power over a thing...'
Notice Ashari uses the phrase 'لوجب في القدرة ما وجب في العجز'.
2
Understand the logical move
Ashari argues that if the analogy held, then the same consequences would follow for power as for incapacity: where incapacity makes both impossible, power would make both possible. That is absurd.
3
Connect to the concept of acquisition
Ashari brings in the concept of acquisition (kasb) to show that incapacity prevents acquisition of both, so power would necessitate acquisition of both — but humans do not acquire opposites simultaneously.
This shows the analogy fails when applied to human action.

Commentary
This passage continues the debate. Then he presents a reductio: if power over a thing implied power over its opposite, then the same would hold for incapacity: incapacity over a thing would entail incapacity over its opposite, which is false. Actually, incapacity over a thing does not entail incapacity over its opposite. The argument is complex but essentially breaks the false symmetry. Ashari also introduces the concept of 'acquisition' (kasb) to show that incapacity prevents both actions, so by analogy, power would necessitate both actions, which is absurd. Thus, the initial analogy fails.

Refuting the Analogy of Power with Incapacity
Al-Ash'ari refutes the analogy between power and incapacity regarding opposites.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Ash'ari rejects the analogy between power (qudra) and incapacity (ʿajz) concerning opposites, and how he uses reductio ad absurdum to invalidate it.

Core Idea
Power and incapacity are not analogous because they have opposite effects on opposites.

In this passage, al-Ash'ari responds to an opponent who argues that just as incapacity over a thing implies incapacity over its opposite, so power over a thing should imply power over its opposite. Al-Ash'ari dismantles this analogy by showing that if it were valid, it would lead to absurd consequences that violate the logical impossibility of two opposites coexisting. This lesson walks through his refutation step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the opponent's objection
Begin by carefully reading the opponent's claim: that power over a thing should imply power over its opposite, based on an analogy with incapacity. Understand the parallel they are drawing.
Notice that the opponent assumes that power and incapacity share a similar logical structure.
2
Trace the first counter-analogy
Examine al-Ash'ari's first response: if the analogy held, it would also apply to assistance ('awn), leading to an absurd consequence. This shows the analogy cannot be applied universally.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum: extending the opponent's logic to a ridiculous case.
3
Understand the main argument about opposites
Now focus on the detailed argument: if power were like incapacity, then power would necessitate the existence of both opposites simultaneously, which is impossible. In contrast, incapacity causes the absence of both. Therefore, the analogy fails.
The key difference: incapacity can cause non-existence of both opposites, but power can only bring about one at a time.
4
Grasp the conclusion
Al-Ash'ari concludes that the analogy is invalid because there is no common cause (ʿilla) uniting power and incapacity; they are different in genus. The opponent's objection is nullified.

فإن قال : ما أنكرتَ أن يكونَ القادِرُ على الشيء قادرًا على ضده، كما كان العاجِزُ عن الشَّيءِ عاجزا عن ضِدّه .

فإِنْ لم يَجُز هذا فَقَدْ بَطَلتِ العلَّةُ، وانتقضت المعارضَةُ، ولم يَجِبْ أن تُقاس القدرة على العجز، إذ لم تكن علة تجمع بينهما، ولم تكن القدرة من جنس العجز .
Chapter on discourse about ability (al-istiṭāʿa). If he says: What prevents you from saying that the one who has power (al-qādir) over a thing has power over its opposite (ḍidd), just as the one who is incapable (al-ʿājiz) of a thing is incapable of its opposite? It is said to him: If power (al-quwwa) over a thing were power over its opposite, by analogy (qiyās) with incapacity (al-ʿajz), then it would necessarily follow that assistance (al-ʿawn) in a thing is assistance in its opposite, by analogy with the fact that incapacity concerning a thing is incapacity concerning its opposite. And also: If power (al-qudra) over a thing were power over its opposite, by analogy with incapacity, because incapacity concerning a thing is incapacity concerning its opposite, then what holds for incapacity would have to hold for power, namely that the thing and its opposite occur through it, just as the thing and its opposite are impossible (yataʿadhdharu) through incapacity. And if incapacity, when it exists, causes the absence of the thing and its opposite, the two objects of incapacity, despite its existence, so that man does not acquire (muktasiban) either of them, then the like would have to hold for power, when it exists, and it is the power over the thing and its opposite, that the thing and its opposite should exist with it. And that is impossible (muḥāl), because only one of the two opposites (al-ḍiddayn) must exist with it, unlike what we judge in the case of incapacity, because in incapacity we judge the non-existence of the object of incapacity and its opposite despite its existence. Then if this is not permissible, then the cause (al-ʿilla) is invalidated and the objection (al-muʿāraḍa) is nullified, and it is not necessary to analogize power to incapacity, since there is no cause that unites them, and power is not of the same genus (jins) as incapacity.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. The opponent raises an objection based on an analogy: just as incapacity (ʿajz) over a thing implies incapacity over its opposite, so power (qudra) over a thing should imply power over its opposite. Al-Ash'ari responds by refuting the analogy. First, he points out that if this analogy held, it would also apply to assistance (ʿawn), which is absurd. Then he provides a more detailed argument: if power were like incapacity, then just as incapacity makes both a thing and its opposite impossible, power would make both possible. However, this is impossible because power can only bring about one of the two opposites at a time (since two opposites cannot coexist), whereas incapacity can be the cause of both not existing. Therefore, the analogy fails because there is no common cause (ʿilla) that unites power and incapacity; they are different in genus (jins). The opponent's objection is thus invalidated, and the argument that power over a thing implies power over its opposite is rejected.

Ash'ari's Argument Against Obligating the Impossible
Ash'ari argues against obligating the impossible via reductio ad absurdum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ash'ari's reductio ad absurdum argument that God does not command what is beyond a person's capacity, based on the absurd consequence of commanding with total ignorance.

Core Idea
God's commands are just and do not require the impossible.

Imam Ash'ari tackles a crucial theological question: does God ever command something a person is incapable of doing? Using a logical argument, he shows that such a view leads to absurdities. This passage presents his argument with concrete examples.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation, to familiarize yourself with the flow of the argument.
Pay attention to the examples of limb and incapacity.
2
Identify the reductio structure
Notice how Ash'ari starts with a premise (commanding with partial inability implies commanding with total inability), draws an analogous case (partial ignorance implies total ignorance), and concludes the premise is false due to absurdity.
This is a classic logical demonstration.
3
Apply to other cases
Consider how Ash'ari generalizes the argument to all instances of obligating the impossible, such as zakat without wealth.
The same reasoning applies uniformly.

باب الكلام في الاستطاعة. ومع عدم الجارحة لا يوجد أخذ ولا ترك. وكذلك العجز لا يوجد معه أخذ ولا ترك؛ لأن العجز عجز عن الشيء وعن ضده. وأيضًا فلو وجب إذا أمر الله تعالى الإنسان بالشيء مع عدم قدرته عليه أن يأمر به مع عدم القدرة كلها، لوجب: إذا أمر الله تعالى الإنسان، مع عدم بعض العلوم، وهو العلم بالله تعالى وبأنه أمره أن يأمره بالفعل مع عدم العلوم كلها. فإن لم يجب هذا، لم يجب إذا أمر الإنسان، مع عدم القدرة على ما أمره به، أن يأمر مع عدم الجارحة التي إذا عدمت عدمت القدرة كلها، ومع وجود العجز الذي لم تعدم القدرة بوجوده. قال الشيخ أبو الحسن رحمه الله: وكل مسألة في تكليف ما لا يطاق من الأمر بالزكاة مع عدم المال، وغير ذلك من المسائل، فالجواب عنه كما أجبت به عن سؤالهم عن الأمر مع عدم الجارحة، والتكليف مع وجود العجز.
Chapter on Speech about Capacity. With the absence of a limb, there is no taking or leaving. Likewise, with incapacity, there is no taking or leaving with it; because incapacity is inability for the thing and for its opposite. Also, if it were necessary when God—Exalted—commands a person to something while he lacks ability for it that He command him while lacking all ability, then it would be necessary: when God—Exalted—commands a person while lacking some knowledge—which is knowledge of God—Exalted—and that He commanded him—that He command him to the act while lacking all knowledge. If this is not necessary, then it is not necessary, when He commands a person while lacking ability for what He commanded him, that He command while lacking the limb by whose absence all ability is absent, and with the existence of incapacity by whose existence ability is not absent. Al-Shaykh Abu al-Hasan—may God have mercy on him—said: And every issue in obligating what cannot be borne, such as the command for zakat while lacking wealth and other such issues, the answer for it is as I answered for their question about the command while lacking the limb, and obligation with the existence of incapacity.

Commentary
This passage begins the chapter on capacity (istita'a) by stating two foundational observations: absence of a limb prevents action (taking or leaving), and incapacity (ajz) likewise prevents action because it is inability for both the thing and its opposite. Ash'ari then employs a reductio ad absurdum argument: if it were obligatory for God to command a person to an act when the person lacks ability for that specific act (and thus command with total inability), then it would also follow that when commanding a person deficient in some knowledge (like knowledge of God and His command), God would have to command with total ignorance. Since the latter is absurd, the former premise is false. He extends this logic: if commanding with partial incapacity did not oblige commanding with total incapacity, then similarly, commanding with absence of a limb (which entails total incapacity) does not oblige commanding with total incapacity even when incapacity exists but does not remove all ability. Finally, he generalizes: all cases of obligating the impossible (e.g., zakat without wealth) are answered by the same reasoning applied to the limb and incapacity examples.

Refutation of Two Inabilities
Ash'ari refutes the claim that absence of a thing and its opposite is due to two inabilities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ash'ari argues that inability is a single, unified attribute and cannot be multiple distinct inabilities.

Core Idea
Distinct inabilities lead to infinite regress and contradictory life-death states.

In this short but powerful passage, Imam Ash'ari tackles a subtle objection: could the absence of a thing and its opposite be due to two separate inabilities? He shows that such a view unravels into logical absurdities, thereby defending the unity of incapacity in kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Read the Arabic passage and translation to get the flow of the argument. Note the objector's claim and Ash'ari's immediate reply.
Notice the phrase 'fain qāla qā'il' marking the objection.
2
Analyze the first argument: infinite regress
Ash'ari argues that if inabilities were distinct, there would be infinite inabilities in a person, which is impossible. Try to understand why distinct inabilities would lead to infinity.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Analyze the second argument: death and simultaneous opposites
Using death as the greatest inability, Ash'ari shows that distinct inabilities would imply multiple deaths in one part, allowing one death to be removed while another remains, leading to the impossible state of being simultaneously alive and dead.
This reinforces the contradiction.

If someone says: What prevents you that a thing and its opposite are absent due to the existence of two inabilities? It is said to him: Because it is the utmost of what the incapable person who has no ability can be incapable of. For if inability for everything were other than inability for something else, then in the person there would be inabilities without end, and this is impossible. Also, death is the greatest of inabilities; because with it all actions are impossible. So if inability for everything were other than inability for something else, then some of the dead would have actions only absent due to the existence of [multiple] inabilities, and this necessitates that in one part there are two inabilities and two deaths. And if this were possible, it would be possible that one of them could rise to life, so that one part would be alive and dead simultaneously, and this is impossible. So when this is impossible, it is known that the statement of whoever said that inability for everything is other than inability for something else is impossible. And with God is success.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an objection. An objector proposes that the absence of a thing and its opposite could be due to two distinct types of incapacity (ajz). The response begins by stating that there is a limit to what an incapable person can be incapable of—since he has no ability, he cannot be burdened with multiple distinct inabilities. If there were distinct inabilities for each thing, the person would have infinite inabilities, which is absurd. Next, Ash'ari uses death as an example: death is the greatest inability because it prevents all actions. If inabilities were distinct, then a dead person would have multiple inabilities, meaning multiple deaths in one body part, leading to the absurdity that one part could have both a death and a life simultaneously (if one inability were removed). This is impossible, therefore the premise that inabilities are distinct is false. The passage concludes decisively that the claim of multiple distinct inabilities is impossible.

Proof from Yajuj and Majuj: Ability is With the Act
Al-Ash'ari proves that ability exists only with the act using the story of Yajuj and Majuj.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari uses the Qur'anic story of Yajuj and Majuj to demonstrate that human ability does not precede the act but exists only concurrently with it by Allah's will.

Core Idea
Ability is not prior to action; it arises with the act through divine will.

In classical Islamic theology, the relationship between human ability and divine will is central. Al-Ash'ari here presents a unique proof from the story of Dhul-Qarnayn's wall and the nations of Yajuj and Majuj. By examining the tafsir consensus on their failed attempts and eventual success, he argues that ability cannot be possessed before an act – it is only present at the moment of action, contingent on Allah's will. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

3
Relate to capability
Al-Ash'ari uses this to argue that if humans had inherent capability, they would not need to say 'insha'Allah' to guarantee the action. The need for the exception proves that capability is dependent on Allah's will.

And he believes that ability (istita'a) is with the act.
Source pages p. 305

Commentary
This is a brief but important doctrinal statement on the nature of human capacity. The author affirms that the ability to perform an action is simultaneous with the act itself, not prior to it. This is a key position in Ash'ari theology regarding the relationship between human agency and divine power.
