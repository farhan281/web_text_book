Refuting the Qadariyya on divine power over compulsion
Al-Ash'ari argues that if God cannot compel faith, weakness results.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari uses conditional reasoning to show that denying God's power to compel faith logically entails attributing weakness to God, and that the Qadariyya position forces them to deny divine omnipotence.

Core Idea
Denying God's power to compel faith leads to the absurd conclusion that God is weak.

This passage presents a powerful logical argument against the Qadariyya (Mu'tazila) who affirm human free will to the extent that God cannot compel belief. Al-Ash'ari shows that if God cannot make people believe, then He is powerless to achieve His will—a clear contradiction with His omnipotence. Understanding this argument helps clarify the Islamic doctrine of divine power and its relationship to human actions.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic carefully, then the translation. Notice the conditional structure and the key terms like 'compulsion' (iljā') and 'power' (qudra).
2
Identify the two parts of the argument
The passage consists of a conditional argument (if no power to compel then weakness) and a dilemma for the Qadariyya (if humans can always disbelieve, God cannot guarantee faith). Mark these two sections.
The first part uses 'فكما... فكذلك' (just as... similarly).
3
Analyze the logical necessity
Ask yourself: why does inability to compel imply weakness? Al-Ash'ari argues that if something you don't will happens, you are weak; if you cannot make what you will happen, you are also weak. This is a necessary rational judgment.
4
Connect to the Qadariyya position
See how the argument refutes the Qadariyya: they claim humans have power to believe or disbelieve even under compelling signs, so God cannot make them believe inevitably. This leads to denying God's power over that matter, which is absurd.

Commentary
This passage is a proof against the Qadariyya (Mu'tazila) who affirm human free will to choose belief or unbelief, thereby denying that God can compel them to faith. Al-Ash'ari uses a conditional argument: if having something occur that one does not will implies weakness and inability to attain what one wills, then if God were not described as having power to compel (yulji') them to what He wills, He would be subject to weakness. But God is not subject to weakness, so He must have such power. Then he poses a dilemma for the Qadariyya: they claim humans have power to both believe and disbelieve. Therefore, even if God were to send compelling signs (miracles that necessarily produce faith), humans would still have the power to disbelieve, so their faith would not be guaranteed. Thus God would lack power over making them believers, which is absurd. The argument shows that the Qadariyya position forces them to deny God's omnipotence in order to preserve human free will.

God Creates Our Actions: A Quranic Proof and Objection
Ash'ari proves servants' actions are created by God and answers an objection about the interpretation of 'خلقكم وما تعملون'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ash'ari's Quranic proof that human actions are created by God and his refutation of the objection that the verse 'وَاللَّهُ خَلَقَكُمْ وَمَا تَعْمَلُونَ' refers to idols rather than deeds.

Core Idea
God's creation of our actions is proven by the recompense due for them.

In this passage, Imam al-Ash'ari tackles a central question of qadar: Are our actions created by God? He provides a clear Quranic proof and then anticipates a potential misinterpretation of the key verse. This lesson walks through his argument and his response to the objection.

Guided Path

Notice that Ash'ari uses a very compact style, combining proof and objection.
2
Identify the proof and its logic
Find the two Quranic verses Ash'ari cites. Understand how the recompense verse (56:24) supports the claim that actions are created by God.
The logic is: recompense is for actions, recompense is from God, therefore actions are created by God.
3
Examine the objection and refutation
Read the objector's statement and Ash'ari's response. See how he distinguishes between the verse about idols and the verse about actions.
The refutation hinges on the fact that the idols are carved by them, so the carving verse is about idols, not about their general actions.

Commentary
This passage is a combined proof-and-objection section. Ash'ari begins by stating the claim that servants' acquisitions (أكساب العباد) are created by God. He provides a Quranic proof from Sūrat al-Ṣāffāt (37:96): 'And God created you and what you do.' He then adds Sūrat al-Wāqiʿa (56:24): 'A recompense for what they used to do.' The reasoning is that since recompense (جزاء) is given for actions, and recompense is from God, the actions themselves must be created by God (otherwise the recompense would be for something not created by Him). An objector then raises a potential misinterpretation: could the verse 'created you and what you do' refer to the idols they make rather than their actions? He cites the nearby verse 'Do you worship what you carve?' to support this reading. Ash'ari refutes this by noting that the idols are indeed carved by the people (منحوتة لهم), so the phrase 'what you carve' refers back to the idols, not to their general actions. The verse 'created you and what you do' therefore refers to their deeds, as the recompense verse confirms. The answer is cut off at 'and they are not' (وليست), likely concluding that the idols are not the subject of the creation verse.

Imam Ash'ari presents the first objection and its response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Ash'ari refutes the claim that the verse 'Allah created you and what you do' refers to idols rather than human actions.

Core Idea
Your actions are created by Allah, not the objects you make.

Imam Ash'ari begins by establishing the foundational proof that human actions are created by Allah, using clear Qur'anic evidence. He then anticipates and answers an objection that might confuse the meaning of 'what you do' with the idols worshipped by the disbelievers. This lesson breaks down his careful reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of two objections against the doctrine that human actions are created by Allah. The author, Imam Ash'ari, presents the objections and answers them sequentially. The first objection: The objector asks why they claim that human actions (aksab al-ibad) are created by Allah. The answer cites two Qur'anic verses: 'Allah created you and what you do' (37:96) and 'Recompense for what they used to do' (56:24). Since recompense (jaza') is based on actions, the Creator of those actions must be Allah. The objector then raises a second objection: Could the verse 'Allah created you and what you do' refer to the idols that the disbelievers carved, as in the verse 'Do you worship what you carve?' (37:95)? The answer distinguishes between the two verses: 'what you carve' refers to the idols, which are actually carved by humans, but 'what you do' in 37:96 cannot refer to idols because the idols are not 'made' by humans in reality—they are merely carved from pre-existing material. Thus, the verse must refer to their actions. A third objection is presented: In the verse about Prophet Moses' staff swallowing the magicians' ropes (7:117), the phrase 'what they fabricate' (ma ya'fikun) refers to the ropes, not the act of fabrication; so similarly, 'what you do' could refer to something other than actions. The answer explains that 'fabrication' (ifk) here means illusion or deception, not actual creation. The magicians made the ropes appear to be snakes through trickery, but they did not create the ropes; they only made people imagine they were snakes. Therefore, the verse still refers to an action (the act of making illusions), not an object. The passage concludes by stating that if it is not permissible for humans to actually create wood, then the verse cannot refer to the wood, but must refer to actions, consistent with the recompense verse. The text cuts off at the beginning of a new hypothetical objection.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of two objections against the doctrine that human actions are created by Allah. The author, Imam Ash'ari, presents the objections and answers them sequentially. The first objection: The objector asks why they claim that human actions (aksab al-ibad) are created by Allah. The answer cites two Qur'anic verses: 'Allah created you and what you do' (37:96) and 'Recompense for what they used to do' (56:24). Since recompense (jaza') is based on actions, the Creator of those actions must be Allah. The objector then raises a second objection: Could the verse 'Allah created you and what you do' refer to the idols that the disbelievers carved, as in the verse 'Do you worship what you carve?' (37:95)? The answer distinguishes between the two verses: 'what you carve' refers to the idols, which are actually carved by humans, but 'what you do' in 37:96 cannot refer to idols because the idols are not 'made' by humans in reality—they are merely carved from pre-existing material. Thus, the verse must refer to their actions. A third objection is presented: In the verse about Prophet Moses' staff swallowing the magicians' ropes (7:117), the phrase 'what they fabricate' (ma ya'fikun) refers to the ropes, not the act of fabrication; so similarly, 'what you do' could refer to something other than actions. The answer explains that 'fabrication' (ifk) here means illusion or deception, not actual creation. The magicians made the ropes appear to be snakes through trickery, but they did not create the ropes; they only made people imagine they were snakes. Therefore, the verse still refers to an action (the act of making illusions), not an object. The passage concludes by stating that if it is not permissible for humans to actually create wood, then the verse cannot refer to the wood, but must refer to actions, consistent with the recompense verse. The text cuts off at the beginning of a new hypothetical objection.

Ash'ari refutes an objection by clarifying the magicians' verse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ash'ari's argument that the verse 'He created you and what you do' necessarily refers to human actions, not to the physical objects they produce.

Core Idea
The verse 'what you do' refers to deeds, not artifacts.

This lesson examines a key objection raised by those who claim that 'what you do' in the Qur'an includes the objects themselves, like idols. Imam Ash'ari responds by analyzing a parallel verse about the magicians of Pharaoh, showing that the objects were not created by them; only the act of illusion-making was. This clarifies that divine creation encompasses human actions, not their material products.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer

2

Ash'ari explains that the magicians' 'fabrication' (ifk) was the illusion, not the ropes themselves. The ropes were pre-existing objects; the magicians only made them appear as snakes.

The objector's analogy breaks down because the magicians did not create the ropes. So the verse 'He created you and what you do' must refer to the act of carving, not the carved idols.
This argument preserves the Islamic doctrine that Allah creates all actions.

Proof from Analogy: Actions Created by Allah
Ash'ari presents a logical proof from analogy that human actions are created by Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Ash'ari uses a logical analogy (qiyas) to prove that human actions, including their moral qualities, are created by Allah.

Core Idea
The qualities of actions are beyond human control, proving they must be created by a Maker.

Before we dive into the source passage, consider this: How can we know that our actions are not entirely our own? Ash'ari presents a powerful argument from analogy. He observes that both disbelief and faith have fixed qualities that human effort cannot change. From this, he builds a logical proof that every action, with its specific nature, must be created by Allah. The passage is concise but packed with reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two observations
Read the passage and notice what Ash'ari observes about disbelief (kufr) and faith (iman). Disbelief is ugly, corrupt, false, contradictory; faith is good, wearisome, painful.
These qualities are fixed, not subject to human desire.
2
Note the failure of human will
Ash'ari points out that the disbeliever tries to make disbelief good but fails, and the believer cannot make faith free from pain and weariness. This shows that the qualities are beyond human control.
3
Grasp the general principle
He then states a general rule: an action does not occur in its true nature except from a maker (muhdith) who brings it into existence in that state.
This principle forms the core of the analogy.
4
Follow the conditional argument
Ash'ari uses a reductio ad absurdum: if an action could occur in its true nature without a maker, then anything could become an action without a maker, which is impossible. Therefore, actions must have a maker.
This reasoning proves that Allah creates actions with their specific qualities.

Commentary
This passage is a proof from analogy (qiyas) demonstrating that human actions are created by Allah. Ash'ari begins by observing two facts about moral qualities: disbelief (kufr) is ugly (qabih) and painful, while faith (iman) is good (hasan) yet also wearisome and painful. He then notes that the disbeliever tries to make kufr appear good but fails—it remains ugly. Similarly, if a believer wishes his faith to be free from weariness and pain, it does not happen according to his wish. This shows that the qualities (husn, qubh) and the painfulness of actions are not subject to human desire or effort. Next, he states a general principle: a physical action cannot occur in its true nature (i.e., with its specific qualities) except from a maker (muhdith) who brings it into existence with those qualities. He supports this with a conditional argument: if it were possible for an action to occur in its true nature without a maker, then it would be possible for a thing to become an action without any maker at all, which is impossible. Therefore, it is necessary that every action occurs in its true nature only from a maker who creates it with those qualities. This implies that the particular qualities (goodness, ugliness, pain) of actions must also be created by a maker—Allah. Thus human actions, including their moral qualities, are created by Allah.

Scriptural Proof That Actions Are Created by God
Al-Ash'ari argues from Quranic consistency that actions are created by God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Ash'ari uses Quranic consistency to prove that human actions are created by God.

Core Idea
The Quran consistently refers to actions as created, so reinterpreting one verse breaks the pattern.

Imam al-Ash'ari now turns to the Quran to ground his theological position. He shows that interpreting the verse 'He created you and what you do' as referring to something other than the actions would force an absurd interpretation of other verses. This argument from consistency is a powerful tool in kalam.

Trace the logical implication

This is the core of the reductio ad absurdum.
4
Conclude with consistency
Since the implication is absurd, the only consistent reading is that actions themselves are meant. Therefore, actions are created by God.
This shows the power of scriptural consistency in theological argument.

Al-Ash'ari's Rational Proof for Divine Creation of Actions
Al-Ash'ari uses analogical reasoning to prove actions require a creator distinct from the human agent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Ash'ari's analogical argument that human actions, including their moral qualities, are created by God and not by the human agent.

Core Idea
Actions and their qualities require an external intender, not the agent.

Al-Ash'ari presents a rational proof from analogy to establish that God creates all human actions. He observes that the actual qualities of actions (faith as tiring, disbelief as false) are contrary to the agent's desires, showing that the agent is not the efficient cause. He then argues that every action in its true nature must come from an efficient cause that intends it, which for disbelief and faith cannot be the human agent.

Guided Path
1

This observation is the starting point of the proof.
2
Understand the causal principle
Al-Ash'ari lays out a general principle: an action cannot occur in its true nature (حقيقته) without an efficient cause (محدث) that intends it. He argues by contradiction: if it could, then all actions could lack a cause, which is absurd.
The principle is key to the argument.

Since disbelief occurs as bad and false, but the disbeliever desires it to be good, the efficient cause cannot be the disbeliever. Similarly, faith occurs as tiring and painful, but the believer cannot change that. Therefore, the efficient cause must be God.
This yields the conclusion that God creates all actions.

Refuting the Analogy: The Counter-Example of Assistance
Imam Ashari presents a counter-analogy using assistance (al-‘awn) to refute the analogy between power and incapacity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Ashari employs a counter-analogy to break the false symmetry between capability and incapacity.

Core Idea
If power were like incapacity, then assistance would be like power — an absurd conclusion.

The objector tries to draw a parallel between capability and incapacity: just as one incapable of a thing is also incapable of its opposite, so one capable of a thing should be capable of its opposite. Imam Ashari begins his refutation by pointing out that such an analogy would also apply to the term 'assistance' (al-‘awn), leading to an obvious falsehood. This lesson explores the first move in Ashari's argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the objector's claim
Read the first line of the passage where the objector presents the analogy between power and incapacity.
Notice the structure: 'just as ... so ...'
2
Identify Ashari's counter-example
Ashari introduces the concept of 'assistance' (al-‘awn) to show that the analogy fails. Find the line where he says 'if strength over a thing were strength over its opposite...'
He uses the same pattern but with 'assistance' to create a parallel that is obviously false.
3
Understand why the counter-example works
Explain that assistance cannot be over both a thing and its opposite, so the analogy is invalid. This breaks the initial claim.
The key is that the analogy is not universal.

If he says: 'Why do you deny that the one who is capable of a thing is capable of its opposite, just as the one who is incapable of a thing is incapable of its opposite?' It is said to him: 'If strength over a thing were strength over its opposite, by analogy with incapacity, then it would necessarily follow that assistance (al-‘awn) over a thing is assistance over its opposite, by analogy with the fact that incapacity over a thing is incapacity over its opposite. And if incapacity, when it occurs, necessitates the absence of the thing and its opposite—the two things of which one is incapable—while it (incapacity) exists, so that a person does not acquire them, then it would necessarily follow in power, when it exists—and it is power over the thing and its opposite—that the thing and its opposite would exist with it.'

Commentary
This passage continues the debate. The objector argues that just as one who is incapable of a thing is also incapable of its opposite, similarly, one who is capable of a thing should be capable of its opposite. Imam Ashari refutes this by first employing a counter-analogy: if ability were analogous to inability, then 'assistance' (al-‘awn) would also follow the same pattern, but it does not. Then he presents a reductio: if power over a thing implied power over its opposite, then the same would hold for incapacity: incapacity over a thing would entail incapacity over its opposite, which is false. Actually, incapacity over a thing does not entail incapacity over its opposite. The argument is complex but essentially breaks the false symmetry. Ashari also introduces the concept of 'acquisition' (kasb) to show that incapacity prevents both actions, so by analogy, power would necessitate both actions, which is absurd. Thus, the initial analogy fails.
