The Rational Argument for God's Uniqueness
Proves God's dissimilarity to creation through rational division and Quranic evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the method of rational division (sabr wa taqsim) is used to prove that God does not resemble His creation.

Core Idea
If God resembled creation, He would be contingent—impossible for the Eternal.

This lesson presents a classical kalam argument that combines rational reasoning with Quranic confirmation. The argument begins by considering an objection: why do we claim God is unlike creation? The answer uses a powerful logical tool—division into all respects or some respects—to show that any resemblance would entail contingency, which is impossible for God. The Quran then seals the argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and the initial response
Start by reading the passage from the beginning. Notice how the author frames the objection ('Why do you claim...') and gives a concise answer ('Because if He resembled them...'). This sets up the rational method.
The objection is a rhetorical device to introduce the proof.
2
Understand the method of division
The answer uses 'division' (sabr wa taqsim): it considers two exclusive possibilities—resemblance in all respects or in some respects. Both are shown to lead to contingency. This is a key logical tool in kalam.
Watch how each case is handled and the conclusion drawn.
3
Trace the conclusion
Since both possibilities lead to the conclusion that God would be contingent (muhdath), and it is impossible for a contingent thing to be eternal (qadim), the only remaining possibility is that God resembles creation in no respect.
The argument is a reductio ad absurdum.
4
Connect to the Quranic evidence
After the rational proof, the author cites two verses that explicitly affirm God's incomparability. This shows that reason and revelation are in harmony.
The verses serve as confirmation, not as the primary proof.

Commentary
This passage is a rational proof (istidlal aqli) supported by scriptural evidence. It opens with an objection: Why do you claim God does not resemble creation? The answer uses the method of division (sabr wa taqsim): if God resembled creation, either in all respects or in some. Both cases lead to the conclusion that He would be contingent (muhdath), because anything that shares the characteristics of contingent things must itself be contingent. But God is eternal (qadim), so it is impossible for Him to be contingent. Therefore, He resembles creation in no respect. The proof is then confirmed with two Quranic verses affirming God's incomparability.

The Rational Proof for God's Dissimilarity to Creation
Imam Ashari proves God's dissimilarity to creation using rational division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof for God's dissimilarity to creation using the method of division (al-sabr wa al-taqsim).

Core Idea
If God resembled creation in any way, He would be temporal—but the temporal cannot be eternal.

Imam Ashari presents a concise rational proof for a central Islamic doctrine: that God is unlike His creation. He does so by first considering the opposite assumption and then showing its impossible consequences. This lesson walks through the proof step by step, revealing the logical structure behind the verse 'There is nothing like unto Him.'

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic Passage
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Notice the dialectical form (فإن قال قائل... قيل). Let the author's rhythm come through before you interpret.
The opening sets up a hypothetical objection.
2
Understand the Proof Structure
Identify the hypothetical conditional (لو أشبهها) and the division into two cases (all respects vs. some respects). This is a proof by contradiction and division.
Al-sabr wa al-taqsim is the method used.
3
Follow the Consequences
See that both cases lead to the same conclusion: God would be temporal, which is impossible because the temporal cannot be eternal.
The impossibility of combining temporal and eternal is key.
4
See the Quranic Support
The rational proof is reinforced by two Quranic verses affirming God's incomparability. Notice how revelation and reason agree.
The verses are cited after the rational argument.
5
Reflect on Key Terms
Review key terms: hadath (temporality), qadim (eternal), mukhalafah lil-hawadith (dissimilarity to created things), sabr wa taqsim (division).
These terms are foundational for kalam.

Commentary
This passage provides a rational proof for God's dissimilarity to creation (al-mukhalafah lil-hawadith). The argument proceeds by first assuming the opposite: that God resembles created things. If that were the case, then the ruling of temporality (al-hadath) that applies to creation would also apply to Him. The author then employs the method of division (al-sabr wa al-taqsim): either God resembles creation in all respects or in some respects. If He resembles them in all respects, He would be temporal like them. If He resembles them only in some respects, He would be temporal in those respects. In both cases, God would be temporal, but it is impossible for what is temporal to be eternal (qadim). Therefore the initial assumption is false, and God is unlike creation. The proof is then supported by two Quranic verses affirming His incomparability.

Refuting the claim that Allah is a body (first meaning)
Ashari refutes the corporealist claim by analyzing the meaning of 'body' and its implications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the term 'body' cannot be applied to Allah in its literal sense without implying composition, which contradicts His oneness.

Core Idea
A body is always composed of parts; the One God cannot be composed.

Imam al-Ash'ari presents a crisp logical refutation of those who claim Allah is a body. He begins by considering what the corporealist could possibly mean by the term 'jism'. The answer leads to a clear distinction between literal implication and mere naming, and a decisive argument based on the oneness of Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once through, then its literal translation. Let the logical flow of the objection and answer become clear before analyzing.
2
Identify the two possible meanings of 'body'
Notice how Ash'ari splits the claim into two possibilities: either the speaker means a body with dimensions and composition, or they merely use the term as a name without those implications.
This is a common kalam technique: clarifying the meaning before arguing.
3
Understand the argument against composition
Focus on the statement that composition (ijtima') requires at least two things. A single thing cannot be composed with itself. Since Allah is one, He cannot be composed.
The argument rests on the previously established truth that Allah is wahid (one) in a way that precludes parts.
4
Connect to the principle of tawhid
Reflect on why this refutation is essential for preserving tawhid. If Allah were a body, He would have parts, making Him dependent on them. This passage guards against anthropomorphism.

He argues that composition (ijtima') requires at least two distinct things coming together. A single thing cannot be composed with itself. Since it has been established previously (in a prior argument) that God is one thing (wahid), He cannot be composed. Therefore, claiming that God is a body in the first sense is impossible. The second meaning (mere naming) is not addressed here, presumably because it is less problematic or will be dealt with later. The passage is a precise logical argument using the principle of unity vs composition.

Refutation of Corporealism: Two Meanings of 'Body'
Al-Ash'ari refutes corporealism by analyzing two possible meanings of 'body'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Ash'ari's two-pronged refutation of the claim that God is a body, showing that both a physical interpretation and a mere naming interpretation are invalid.

Core Idea
To call God 'body' is either false (if meant physically) or forbidden (if meant as a name without warrant).

In this passage, al-Ash'ari responds to an objection about denying that God is a body. He carefully distinguishes two possible meanings of the term 'body'—a physical composite or a mere name—and refutes both. This approach demonstrates the precision of kalam reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Note the structure: an objector asks why al-Ash'ari denies that God is a body, and al-Ash'ari gives a two-part answer.
This is a common dialectical format in kalam.
2
Identify the first meaning
Al-Ash'ari considers the possibility that the objector means a physical body: long, wide, composed. Notice the argument: composition requires two things, but God is one, so it's impossible.
Key term: مُجْتَمِع (composed).
3
Identify the second meaning
Now consider if the objector merely intends to name God 'body' without physical attributes. Al-Ash'ari rejects this because naming is not up to us—it requires divine or prophetic warrant or consensus.
Key term: تسمية (naming).
4
Reflect on the argument
Consider why both refutations are necessary. If only one were addressed, the opponent might shift to the other. Al-Ash'ari closes both doors.
This shows thoroughness in theological reasoning.

We can only use names that God has given Himself, that His Messenger has used, or that the Muslim community (ijma‘) has agreed upon. Since 'body' is not among these, it is forbidden. Thus both possible interpretations are refuted.

The Principle of Naming Allah: Names Are Not Permissible Without Divine Authority
Imam al-Ash'ari refutes the claim that Allah may be called a body and states the rule for divine names.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why it is not permissible to name Allah with any name that He, His Messenger, or the Muslim consensus have not authorized.

Core Idea
You cannot name Allah based on human reason alone – only revelation authorizes divine names.

Imam al-Ash'ari tackles a subtle objection: if you deny that Allah is a body, why not call Him a body as long as you don't mean a physical extended thing? His answer unveils a foundational principle: naming Allah is not a matter of semantic freedom. The names of Allah are tawqifiyyah – dependent on divine authorization.

This principle is called 'tawqifiyyah al-asma' – divine names reliant on revelation.

The objector argues from empirical observation: everything we see is either a substance or an accident, bounded or inhering in something bounded. Since God is none of these, He cannot be seen. Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of the first obligation upon a responsible individual: correct belief in tawhid. Ibn Khafif states that all other deeds depend on this foundational belief. He then describes the nature of Allah's oneness by negation: Allah is one not in the sense of numerical oneness (like countable objects) nor like individual units that can be grouped. He is a 'thing' but not like other things, meaning He exists but is incomparable to creation. Ibn Khafif then negates likeness, opposition, and rivalry. He then negates accidental descriptions: Allah is not a body (جسم), not an accident (عرض), not a substance (جوهر). He is not a locus in which accidents occur, nor do accidents subsist in Him. He does not inhere in things, nor do things inhere in Him. He does not manifest in anything, nor is He hidden by originated things (i.e., He is beyond spatial and temporal relations). Finally, Ibn Khafif affirms His attribute of knowledge: He knows everything that has happened, will happen, and even things that would happen if they were to occur—emphasizing the completeness of divine knowledge.
