Refutation of the Devoid Capable Objection
Ashari refutes a dialectical objection about capability and speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Ash'ari refutes the objection that a capable speaker can be devoid of speech and its opposites, by exposing an inconsistency in the objector's reasoning.

Core Idea
Having power over speech makes one a speaker — you cannot be capable of speech and devoid of all speech.

This passage is an example of kalam dialectic. An opponent claims that a human who has the power to speak can be in a state without speech and without its opposite (like silence). Imam al-Ash'ari first calls this a contradiction, then uses an analogy with knowledge to show the opponent's inconsistency. Before reading, notice how he forces the opponent to apply the same logic to speech as they do to knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and initial response
Read the first part where the objector states his claim and Ash'ari calls it a contradiction. Note the phrase 'ضرب من الخلاف'.
2
Analyze the knowledge analogy
Ash'ari asks why the objector doesn't apply the same logic to knowledge. The objector's concession highlights his inconsistency.
3
Understand the forced conclusion
Ash'ari forces the objector to admit that the capable person can be devoid of specific speech but not all speech. This shows the objection fails.

Dialectical Comparison of Good and Evil and Their Sources
Dialectical argument comparing good and evil with their sources.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how dialectical argumentation distinguishes between the moral agent and the abstract quality in evaluating good and evil.

Core Idea
The agent's moral character outweighs the abstract quality of good or evil.

In this dialectical exchange, a questioner challenges the relationship between good and evil and their sources. The response clarifies that the one who bestows good as a favor is superior to the good itself, while the one who commits evil unjustly is worse than the evil. This argument underscores the importance of the agent's intention and justice in moral evaluation.

Proof that Human Capacity is Distinct from the Human
Ash'ari proves capacity is distinct from the human using variability argument

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ash'ari argues that human capacity must be an accident distinct from the human being itself.

Core Idea
Human capacity changes while the human remains, so it must be something other than the human.

In this passage, a hypothetical objector challenges the Ash'ari position that capacity (istitāʿa) is a distinct attribute. Ash'ari responds by pointing to the variability of capacity in the same human. By comparing capacity to other attributes like knowledge and movement, he establishes a clear rational proof that capacity is an accident, not identical to the human.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Read the Arabic passage once with its translation. Notice the structure: objector asks, then the author gives a reason.
The passage is an objection-and-answer format.
2
Identify the core analogy
Ash'ari compares capacity to knowledge and movement. Just as these vary, capacity varies. This is key to the proof.
The analogy shows that similar attributes share the same logical status.
3
Reflect on the logical implication
If capacity were identical to the human, the human would always be capable. Since we observe variability, capacity cannot be identical. Conclude that capacity is a distinct accident.
The logic uses contrapositive: if P then Q, not Q, therefore not P.

Why Power Does Not Endure: Ash'ari's Disjunctive Proof
Ash'ari responds to the objection that power might endure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ash'ari's argument that power cannot endure by analyzing the two possibilities—self-endurance or endurance by an attribute—and why both lead to impossibility.

Core Idea
Power cannot endure because it would have to endure by itself or by another attribute—both are impossible.

Imam Ash'ari addresses a critical objection: if power is an accident, why can't it remain over time? He presents a precise disjunctive argument to show that endurance is impossible for power. This argument is foundational for understanding the Ash'ari view that accidents do not persist. Let's follow his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
First, read the Arabic passage and its translation to get the overall argument. Notice that Ash'ari starts with an objection and then gives a precise answer.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the two horns of the dilemma
Ash'ari presents two possibilities if power were to endure: either it endures by itself or by a separate attribute of endurance. Identify these two options in the text.
These are the two branches of the disjunctive argument.
3
Understand why self-endurance is impossible
Analyze the first horn: if power endures by itself, it would have to be enduring at the moment it comes into existence, which is contradictory. Reflect on why this is impossible.
Enduring at the moment of occurrence would mean it never begins, but power does begin.
4
Understand why endurance by an attribute is impossible
Analyze the second horn: if power endures by a separate attribute (endurance), then an attribute (endurance) would subsist in another attribute (power), which is invalid. Also consider the infinite regress consequence.
This violates the principle that accidents only subsist in substances.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Both possibilities lead to impossibility, so power cannot endure. Consider how this argument supports the Ash'ari position on the temporality of accidents.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. The objectors ask why Ash'ari claims that power does not endure (remain over time). The answer is a disjunctive argument: if power endured, it would have to endure either by itself or by a separate attribute of endurance. If it endures by itself, then power would be its own endurance, and it would never exist without being enduring, meaning it would be enduring at the moment it comes into existence (which is contradictory, as enduring implies continuation after coming into being). That is impossible. If it endures by a separate attribute (a 'baqā' or endurance) that subsists in it, then an attribute (endurance) would be subsisting in another attribute (power), i.e., an accident subsisting in an accident, which is invalid because accidents can only subsist in substances. Moreover, if that were allowed, then it would be permissible for power to have another power, life another life, etc., leading to infinite regress, which is corrupt. Therefore, power cannot endure.

The Timing of Capacity: Divorce and Freeing a Slave
Objection-and-answer regarding timing of capacity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Ash'ari position argues that human capacity (istita'a) is concurrent with the action, using examples like divorce and freeing a slave.

Core Idea
Capacity is not a prerequisite; it occurs at the very moment of action.

This passage tackles a subtle objection about when exactly a person has the ability to perform actions such as divorce or freeing a slave. The author uses specific examples to illustrate that capacity is not a separate power that exists before the act, but rather it is present precisely when the act is being performed. Understanding this clarifies the Ash'ari view on the relationship between capacity and action.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and initial answer
Read the Arabic passage once. Notice how the objector asks about the timing of capacity for divorce and freeing a slave, and the author's initial answer places capacity at the moment of the action.
The answer is direct: capacity is at the moment of divorce/freeing.
2
Identify the second objection
The objector then raises a consequence: if capacity is at the action, then the person would have capacity to divorce a non-wife and free a non-slave. Recognize this as a potential problem.
This objection relies on the change of status at the exact moment.
3
Understand the author's resolution
The author resolves by noting the temporal change: the woman was his wife before the divorce, and the slave was his slave before the freeing. Thus capacity applies to the action despite the status change.
The key is that capacity is not about the object's state but the agent's act.
4

The author mentions throwing a stick, moving from sun to shade, and breaking a broken thing. Reflect on how these illustrate the same principle.
These examples reinforce that capacity is simultaneous with the action.

Thus the capacity is rightly applied to the action at the time it occurs, even though the object's status changes at that same moment.

The author then extends the same reasoning to other examples: throwing a stick (which may change its state), moving from sun to shade (the sun is no longer upon him at the moment of moving), and breaking a broken thing (the thing is already broken at the moment of breaking). In all cases, the capacity is concurrent with the action, and the prior state of the object does not affect this simultaneity.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

He shows that two opposite claims about such expressions cancel each other out, and then uses poetry and everyday language to demonstrate that the same words can refer to the whole or a part. This sets the stage for a key principle: no judgment can be made without evidence.

Commentary
This passage is a argument about the interpretation of general expressions (al-‘āmm) in speech, specifically whether their form alone indicates generality over all individuals. Al-Ash‘ari begins by setting up a hypothetical: if a claimant claims that the form (al-ṣūrah) of a word like 'all' is only for the entire set until evidence comes for a subset, then that claimant is no more valid than someone else who claims the opposite—that the form requires judgment on the subset until proof of the whole. Both are equal in their unsupported assertions, so both claims must be canceled. This shows that one cannot rely on mere form without evidence (dalālah).

He then cites a line from the poet Zuhayr to illustrate that a general statement (e.g., 'whoever does not…') may not hold universally. The comment 'but not everyone who does not conciliate is like that' demonstrates that the same expression can be true of some but not all. He provides further everyday examples: 'the one I love came' means one person; 'the merchants came' does not require all merchants; 'my neighbors came' may not be all neighbors; 'the wicked people met me' does not mean all wicked people. These examples show that words like 'al-' (the definite article and similar general expressions) are used sometimes for the whole and sometimes for a part.

Therefore, judgment cannot be made for all or for some without external evidence. He concludes with an additional point about Qur’anic verses: if one were to judge by their form alone, it would require evidence to limit or extend the scope. The passage emphasizes that linguistic usage alone is ambiguous, and rational reasoning or contextual evidence is needed to determine the intended meaning—a key principle in kalām and uṣūl al-fiqh.
