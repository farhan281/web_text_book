Interpreting the Verse on Repentance's Closure
Sawi analyzes Quran 6:158 and its two interpretations regarding repentance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two interpretations of Quran 6:158 that underlie the debate on whether repentance closure is universal or restricted to disbelievers, and the further questions about its scope.

Core Idea
Two interpretations of the verse yield two different rulings on repentance.

The previous lesson introduced the sign and the two opinions. Now Sawi dives into the Quranic verse that is the source of disagreement. He explains how the same verse can be read to support either a general closure or one limited to disbelievers. He also raises additional questions about whether the closure applies only to the morally responsible and whether it lasts until the Resurrection.

Guided Path
1
Read the verse and Sawi's commentary
Read the Arabic and translation from 'قوله' to the end. Notice that Sawi says the verse is the source of the disagreement, not evidence for one side.
The verse is from Surah al-An'am (6:158).
2
Analyze the first interpretation (general)
The first interpretation takes 'لا ينفع نفسا إيمانها' to refer to two categories: a disbelieving soul that had not believed before, and a believing soul that had not earned good deeds. This makes the closure general.
This reading requires an ellipsis: 'or a soul that earned good in its faith' is construed differently.
3
Analyze the second interpretation (specific)
The second interpretation sees the verse as referring to a hypocritical soul whose faith was only outward, thus it applies only to disbelievers.
Here 'أو كسبت في إيمانها خيرا' is taken literally: a hypocritical soul that performed good deeds outwardly but had no inner belief.
4
Consider the further questions
Sawi raises two more issues: is the closure specific to the morally responsible (mukallaf)? And does it continue until the Day of Resurrection? He cites al-Laqani's example of the she-camel as evidence for continuity.

The verse on repentance closure is the source of disagreement, not a proof for one side.

The Quranic verse about the coming of a sign (the sun rising from the west) has been understood in two ways. Some scholars see it as applying to both disbelievers and disobedient believers, while others restrict it to disbelievers. This lesson unpacks those interpretations and their implications.

Say: O Lord, seal our deeds, states, and lifespans with good, so that You do not take us to You except in the most perfect states of tawhid, with longing for You and desire for You, and take our souls in Your hand, and exchange our evil deeds for good deeds, and take our hands at stumbles. Our Lord, we have believed in what You revealed and followed the messenger, so write us among the witnesses. O Merciful, i.e. O Most Merciful of the merciful. In it is an allusion to his saying, peace be upon him: 'The merciful are shown mercy by the Merciful, Blessed and Exalted; have mercy on those on earth and He who is in heaven will have mercy on you.' His saying: 'and take our souls in Your hand': i.e., such that we do not see any angel taking them. His saying: 'at stumbles': i.e., at the occurrence of hardships and troubles. His saying: 'In it is an allusion, etc.': And in it is also an allusion to the hadith: 'When the servant says O Most Merciful of the merciful, Allah says to him: I am the Most Merciful of the merciful; turn to Me and ask.' His saying: 'He who is in heaven will have mercy on you': It is possible that 'he who' refers to the angels, which is apparent, and it is possible that it refers to Allah Almighty; in that case the meaning is he whose command and authority is in heaven.
