What Customary Judgment Indicates: Correlation vs. Causation
Explains that custom only indicates correlation, not causal influence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why customary (habitual) judgments only indicate correlation between events, not the causal influence of one thing over another.

Core Idea
Custom shows that fire and burning happen together, not that fire causes burning.

In this passage, the commentator clarifies a crucial distinction in Islamic theology: the difference between correlation and causation. Customary experience—like seeing fire burn—only tells us that two things occur together. It does not give us certainty about who or what is the true agent behind the effect. This distinction is essential for defending the Sunni position that all causal power belongs to Allah alone.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the key terms that distinguish correlation from causation.
Pay attention to the words 'iqtirān' (correlation) and 'taʾthīr' (influence).
2
Identify the key terms
List the terms: iqtirān, taʾthīr, sabab, muʾaththir, fāʿil. Understand their meanings from the context.
These terms form the conceptual framework of the discussion.
3
Compare the Sunni and other views
The passage contrasts the Sunni position (fire as a cause, sabab) with the view of others (fire as an effectual agent, muʾaththir). Understand why this difference matters.
The Sunni view preserves divine causation; custom only confirms correlation.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The passage ends by stating that knowledge of the true agent (fāʿil) is not obtained from custom. Consider how this aligns with Islamic theology.
This reinforces that only Allah is the true agent.

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory commentary on the original text's statement that custom only indicates correlation, not causation. It clarifies that the most custom (al-ʿāda) can show is the constant conjunction (iqtirān) between two observed phenomena, such as fire and burning. Custom does not demonstrate that fire (or anything else) is the effectual agent (muʾaththir) in producing the burning; that requires additional reasoning. The passage then notes a debate: what is actually derived from custom is the fact that fire is associated with burning, but whether fire is a cause (sabab) or an effectual agent (muʾaththir) is a separate question. The Ahl al-Sunnah affirm that fire is a cause (meaning God creates burning at the time of contact, but fire is not independent), while others (possibly the philosophers or Muʿtazila) say it is an effectual agent. Finally, it concludes that knowledge of the true agent (fāʿil ḥaqīqī), i.e., God, is not obtained from custom; custom only gives the correlation.

The Role of Custom in Causality: Correlation Not Causation
Al-Sawi explains custom's role in causality and handles an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that in Islamic theology, custom ('adah) only indicates consistent correlation (iqtiran) between two events, not that one causes the other, and how this relates to the Sunni position on causality.

Core Idea
Custom shows correlation, not causation.

This passage from Al-Sawi's commentary explains a critical distinction in Islamic theology: custom (al-'adah) does not prove that one thing causes another. Instead, custom only shows that two events consistently occur together. This sets the stage for understanding the Sunni position that God is the true agent, and that natural causes are merely customary.

The Customary Act of Fire Burning and the Rational Possible
Sanusi uses the example of burning by fire to explain customary acts and refute false causal attributions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that regular occurrences like fire burning are customary acts, not rational necessities, and that all effects are from Allah alone, refuting false attributions of causality.

Core Idea
Fire burns by custom, not necessity; only Allah is the true agent.

In this passage, Sanusi uses the concrete example of fire burning to illustrate the category of customary acts within the rationally possible. He clarifies that the intellect, when properly reflecting on God's oneness, recognizes that all effects are from God alone. This lesson unpacks the example, explains the reasoning, and addresses the errors of those who attribute causal power to nature or to a deposited power.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the literal translation. Let the author's own wording sink in.
Notice that the passage includes both the main text and the commentator's explanations.
2
Distinguish customary from rational
Identify how Sanusi differentiates between a customary ruling (حكم عادي) and a rational possibility (جائز عقلي). Ask yourself: why is burning not rationally necessary?
The key is that the intellect, reflecting on God's oneness, sees that all actions belong to God alone.
3
Identify the erroneous views
Find the two wrong attributions of causality: by nature (طبع) and by deposited power (قوة). Note the ruling on each. Also note the Mu'tazila position based on 'obligation of the most beneficial'.
The passage explicitly says the one who says by nature is a disbeliever, and by power is a transgressor.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion of the possible by focusing on the example of fire burning. The author clarifies that the occurrence of burning when fire touches a body is a customary (aadi) judgment, but rationally it is merely possible (ja'iz aqli). He explains that if you consider the burning in itself, without regard to repeated occurrence, it is a rational judgment of possibility because the intellect, reflecting on God's oneness and His exclusive agency, knows that all effects are from God alone and no cause has any real influence. This refutes the error of those who treat burning as a necessary rational judgment, attributing effect to fire either by nature (tab')—which is disbelief—or by a power deposited in it (quwwa)—which is transgression. The passage then notes the Mu'tazilite position that burning is impossible because they hold that God must do what is most beneficial (al-salah wa al-aslah), which would prevent punishing the obedient. The commentator then explains the phrase 'from every customary judgment' with further examples, and notes that the predicate of 'that' is omitted, implying a detailed judgment. Finally, he reiterates the ruling that the one who attributes effect to nature is a disbeliever, and the one who attributes it to a deposited power is a sinner.

Refuting the Philosopher's Denial of Temporality
The author refutes the philosopher's objection by clarifying the meaning of temporal origin.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the philosopher's objection by clarifying that the world's temporal origination is necessary, just as God's eternity is necessary.

Core Idea
The world must be temporal as God must be eternal — the objection becomes irrelevant.

Having established the necessity of the world's temporal origination, the author now addresses the objection from philosophers who deny it. He shows that the intended meaning of 'temporal' is a necessary attribute, not a contingent one, thereby turning the objection into a support.

See how this refutes the objection
If the philosopher's denial of temporal origination is based on the factual claim, it fails because the argument is about necessity. The objection is thus turned aside.
The commentator notes this is 'تنويع في الرد' — a diversification in refutation.

The Proof that Every Created Thing Needs a Creator
Al-Sawi states the proof that every created thing needs a creator and refutes self-creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical proof that every created thing requires a creator and why self-creation is impossible.

Core Idea
Self-creation leads to a logical impossibility: equality and preponderance together.

Al-Sawi presents a concise proof that every created thing (hadith) must have an originator. The argument uses the principle that a well-made product implies a maker. He then shows that denying this leads to an absurdity: a thing causing its own existence, which would require a preponderance without a cause—a logical contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof carefully
Read the Arabic passage once. Notice how Al-Sawi states the conclusion first and then supports it with reasoning.
The proof is structured as a conditional argument.
2
Identify the two premises
The first premise: every created thing is a skillfully made product. The second premise: every such product has a maker. Trace how these lead to the conclusion.
3
Understand the self-creation scenario
If the thing had no maker, it would have to come to be by itself. Consider why this is problematic.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
4
Grasp the impossibility argument
Self-creation would require existence to become preponderant over non-existence without a cause, while they are equal. This combines equality and preponderance—a contradiction.
Equality and preponderance are opposites.

His words: (so the preponderant is the first) i.e., what the mentioned group said is contrary to the preponderant. So it results that whoever says that the customary causes have effect by themselves without any assignment from Allah – disbelief by consensus; whoever says [they have effect] by a power which Allah created in them – an innovator; whoever says that they have effect by Allah's permission but between them and what accompanies them there is a necessary intellectual connection such that non-occurrence is invalid – ignorant, and his belief leads him to disbelief because it necessitates denial of miracles and what the prophets informed of regarding the unseen matters like the states of the grave and the Hereafter, since that is from the category of breaking of customs in which customary causes fail to produce what they accompany; and whoever believes that they have no effect on what they accompany, but rather our Master made them signs and indicators for whatever He wills of events without any intellectual necessary connection between them and what they are made indicators of – he is the true believer and the genuine Sunni, as is indicated by the expression.

Commentary
This passage provides a fourfold classification of beliefs regarding the efficacy of natural causes, based on the preponderant Sunni position. First, those who hold that causes act by their own essence without any assignment from Allah are disbelievers by consensus. Second, those who hold that causes act by a power Allah created in them are innovators (mubtadi'). Third, those who hold that causes act by Allah's permission but with a necessary intellectual connection such that the effect cannot fail are ignorant, and this belief leads to disbelief because it denies miracles (khawariq al-'adat) and prophetic reports about the unseen. Fourth, those who hold that causes have no effect in themselves but are mere signs and indicators placed by Allah for whatever He wills, with no necessary connection, are the true believers and genuine Sunnis. This classification is a standard Ash'ari argument preserving divine omnipotence and the possibility of miracles.

Beliefs About Causality and Their Rulings
Al-Sawi classifies four beliefs about causality with rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four different beliefs about causality and their corresponding rulings from kufr to true faith according to Sunni Islam.

Core Idea
Beliefs about causality range from kufr to orthodoxy based on divine agency.

This passage from Al-Sawi's commentary addresses a critical theological issue: how do we properly understand cause and effect in relation to God's power? Different Muslim sects have held different views, and Al-Sawi categorizes them clearly, showing the ruling on each. As you read, pay attention to the fine distinctions between attributing influence to God alone versus to created objects.

Guided Path
1
Read the full passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its literal translation to get an overview of the four positions and their rulings.
Notice the structure: the passage first explains a nuance, then lists the positions.
2
Identify the four positions
Look for the four distinct beliefs mentioned after 'فتحصل أنَّ من قال'. Each starts with 'من قال' and ends with a ruling.
The positions are: 1) causes act by themselves, 2) by a power God created, 3) by God's permission with rational necessity, 4) no effect, just signs.
3
Understand the first two rulings
The first position is kufr by consensus; the second is innovation (bid‘a). Note why the second is less severe but still wrong.
The second still attributes some power to creation, which is the error of the Mu‘tazila.
4
Understand the third and fourth rulings
The third leads to kufr because it denies miracles; the fourth is true Sunni belief. See how the rejection of rational necessity is key.
The third position is called 'ignorant' but its implication is grave.
5
Reflect on the implications for miracles
Consider how the fourth position alone allows for miracles and prophetic reports, as it breaks the supposed necessary link between cause and effect.
This connects to the broader topic of 'khurq al-‘ādāt' (breaking of norms).

So influence, according to him, is from God alone, even if it is through that power. As for the Qadariyya, they attribute influence to those things through power. So there is a difference between the two beliefs. Despite that, the preponderant [view] is the first, which is that influence is His alone because of them, not by them, even if the custom [of God] is that influence only occurs when they [exist] and by His will; so the act of the servant becomes in reality created by Him Most High. His saying: (So there is a difference between the two beliefs) i.e.: The belief of the Mu'tazili is that influence belongs to things through power, and the Sunni is that influence belongs to God by means of power. His saying: (And despite that) i.e.: Despite the occurrence of the mentioned difference. His saying: (So the preponderant is the first) i.e.: And what some [of the] mentioned said is contrary to the preponderant. And whoever believes they have no effect on what accompanies them, and that our Master has made them merely signs and indications for whatever events He wills, with no rational relation of concomitance between them and that for which they are made indications, he is the true believer and the genuine Sunni, as the expression indicates.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and ruling on different beliefs about causality. Al-Sawi begins by explaining the meaning of 'influence by power' (التأثير بالقوة) according to some Sunni imams: God alone is the true agent, but He creates a power in objects, so the influence is ultimately from God, though by means of that power. In contrast, the Qadariyya (i.e., Mu'tazila) attribute influence to the objects themselves through that power. Despite this difference, the preponderant view among Sunnis is that influence is solely for God—it occurs 'when' the created power is present and by God's will, not 'by' that power. This makes the servant's act truly created by God. Al-Sawi then breaks down the ruling on different positions: 1) One who claims that ordinary causes act by themselves without any divine placement is a disbeliever by consensus. 2) One who claims they act by a power God created in them is an innovator (mubtadi'). 3) One who claims they act by God's permission but with a necessary rational relation (ملازمة عقلية) such that they cannot be separated is ignorant, and his belief leads to kufr because it necessitates denying miracles and revealed unseen matters—since those involve breaking of norms (خرق العوائد) where the usual causes are overridden. 4) The true believer and genuine Sunni is the one who believes that these causes have no inherent effect but are merely signs and indications from God of what He wills to create, with no rational necessity between cause and effect.
