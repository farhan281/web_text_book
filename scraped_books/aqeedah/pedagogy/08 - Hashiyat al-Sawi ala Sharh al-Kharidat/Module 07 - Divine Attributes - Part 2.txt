Qualificative Attributes as Mental Considerations
Sanusi explains the Ash'ari view of attributes as mental considerations and contrasts it with states.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari position that qualificative attributes are mental considerations without external existence, and the contrasting theory of states as intermediaries.

Core Idea
Divine attributes are real in the mind, not as separate entities outside.

This passage from Sanusi clarifies a key Ash'ari position: the qualificative attributes (like power and will) are not independent existents alongside God's essence, but rather concepts we necessarily form in our minds. Sanusi also contrasts this with the theory of 'states' (أحوال). Let's read his precise words and unpack the distinction.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the translation. Notice the key terms: الصفات المعنوية, أمور اعتباريَّة, نفس الأمر, أحوال.
The Arabic uses technical terms that are essential for understanding kalam.
2
Identify the two positions
The passage presents the Ash'ari view first, then contrasts it with the proponents of states (أحوال). Note how each defines the ontological status of attributes.
3
Understand 'mental consideration'
The Ash'ari uses 'أمور اعتباريَّة' to mean something that exists only in the mind, not in external reality. But it is not negated either—it is a necessary conceptual tool.
This is different from saying attributes are unreal or illusions.
4
Grasp the concept of 'state'
For the proponents of أحوال, a state is an intermediary between existence and non-existence—it is established externally but cannot be seen, unlike an existential attribute which can be seen.
This is a more nuanced metaphysical category.

And whoever speaks of states (أحوال) says that they are an intermediary between existence and non-existence. So the existential attribute (الصفة الوجودية) according to them is what is valid to be seen, while the state (الحال) is established externally but is not valid to be seen.

Commentary
This passage classifies the qualificative attributes (الصفات المعنوية) such as power, will, etc. It states that they are mental considerations (أمور اعتباريَّة) that must be assumed in the mind for conceptual analysis, even though they have no external, mind-independent existence. This is the Ash'ari position: these attributes are not real entities in the external world, but they are not negated either—they are considered in the mind. The passage then contrasts this with the doctrine of 'states' (أحوال), held by some theologians, who consider them as intermediates between existence and non-existence. For them, an existential attribute is what can be seen (like power when manifested), while a state is established externally but cannot be seen. The passage clarifies the epistemological and ontological status of divine attributes within Ash'ari theology.

Commentary
This passage is an extended reflection on God's creative power and mercy as manifested in the creation of man. The author first presents the original text (likely from al-Kharidah or its commentary) describing the stages of embryonic development from a drop to a clot to a lump, then the formation of the head, senses, mouth, tongue, neck, internal organs, limbs, and finally the blowing of the spirit. This is followed by a gloss (qāla) from the commentator (al-Ṣāwī) explaining key phrases. The passage begins by noting that the embryo rests 'in a firm resting place' (qarār makīn), which the commentator identifies as the womb. Each stage is specified: the nutfah becomes a clot after forty days, then the clot becomes a mudghah after another forty days. The creation of the tongue is emphasized as a 'soldier from God's soldiers' that translates the sciences and cognitions of the heart. The head is likened to a throne ('arsh) in its loftiness. The fingers are given symbolic meaning related to the letters of the divine name (Allāh). The spirit (rūḥ) is blown after four months and is described as a 'great secret' that gives life to the body, like water in a fresh branch. The Objection on Existence and the Ashʿarī Negation of States
Objection that following Ashʿarī's negation of states should exclude existence, and the reply distinguishing states from considerations.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Negating states does not negate mental considerations; existence is a mental addition needed for building attributes.

This lesson addresses a key objection: if the creed is built on al-Ashʿarī's school that negates states (aḥwāl), why include existence as an attribute? The commentator replies by distinguishing between states and mental considerations (iʿtibārāt), showing that existence is a necessary mental addition. This distinction is central to understanding how kalam handles divine attributes without falling into multiplicity.

The Attribute of Pre-eternality (al-Qidam)
Defines al-qidam, distinguishes qadim bil-dhat from qadim bil-ghayr, and discusses azali vs. qadim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of al-qidam as the negation of beginning, the distinction between essential pre-eternality and pre-eternality through another, and the nuanced relationship between the terms 'qadim' and 'azali'.

Core Idea
Pre-eternality means no beginning; it belongs to Allah by essence, not through another.

This lesson dives into the attribute of al-qidam (pre-eternality). The source passage defines it clearly as the negation of beginning and distinguishes Allah's essential pre-eternality from the philosophical notion of pre-eternality through another. It also clarifies the scope of the term 'everything other than God' and explores the relationship between 'qadim' and 'azali'.

Guided Path
1
Read the Passage
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its translation to grasp the flow. Notice that the author starts by affirming essential pre-eternality and then explains its meaning.
The passage contains a rebuttal of a philosophical position and a discussion of terminology.
2
Identify Key Definitions
Locate the definition of al-qidam as 'negation of beginning' (salb al-awwaliyya) and the distinction between qadim bil-dhat and qadim bil-ghayr. Understand that Allah's pre-eternality is not acquired from an external cause.
The author explicitly rejects the possibility of anything being eternal through another.
3
Clarify the Scope of 'Everything Other than God'
Notice the clarification that 'everything other than God' refers only to existents, not to eternal non-existences (al-a'dam al-azaliyya). This prevents a misunderstanding about the statement that nothing besides God is eternal.
Eternal non-existences are not things; they are absences.
4
Understand the Azali vs. Qadim Distinction
Examine the discussion on whether 'azali' and 'qadim' are synonymous. Note the view of Ibn al-Tilmisani that they are, and al-Sa'd's refinement that 'azali' is more general, applying even to non-existences, while 'qadim' is reserved for self-subsisting entities without beginning.
Allah is described by both terms, but the distinction matters for other existents.

Allah's Qidam: Proof and Refutation of Circularity and Infinite Regress
Al-Sawi presents the proof for Allah's eternality and refutes circularity and infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical proof for Allah's eternality and why both circularity and infinite regress are impossible in the chain of originators.

Core Idea
If Allah were not eternal, the chain of causes would end in absurdity.

Sanusi begins with a straightforward proof: if Allah were not eternal, He would be originated, requiring an originator. That originator, being similar, would also need an originator, leading either to circularity or infinite regress. Both are logically impossible, thus Allah must be eternal. This lesson walks through the argument and its refutations.

Guided Path
1
Understand the main proof
The argument assumes that if something is not eternal, it must have been brought into existence, requiring a cause. This cause, if similar in nature, would also require a cause, leading to a chain.
This is the classic kalam cosmological argument.
2
Identify the two impossible outcomes
The chain can either loop back (circularity) or continue forever (infinite regress). Both are deemed impossible.
3
Learn why circularity is invalid
Circularity requires each to be prior and posterior to the other, and even to itself, which is contradictory.
Impossibility of circularity is considered obvious.
4
Learn why infinite regress is invalid
Infinite regress leads to an infinite number of dependent, originated gods, contradicting the nature of divinity.
This refutation relies on the attributes of God.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Since both outcomes are impossible, the assumption that Allah is not eternal is false; therefore, Allah is eternal.

Proof for Self-Subsistence (Qiyam bi nafsih)
Al-Sawi presents the proof for God's self-subsistence and explains the reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two-part proof that God is self-subsistent, and why alternative possibilities lead to false consequences.

Core Idea
If God subsisted in another, He would be either an accident (temporal) or a dependent attribute — both impossible.

The last elaborated attribute in the passage is self-subsistence (qiyam bi nafsih). Al-Sawi presents a disjunctive proof: if God subsisted in another, two possibilities arise — either He would be an accident (which is temporal) or an eternal attribute subsisting in a subject (which would prevent Him from having attributes of meanings). Both are false, so God must be self-subsistent. This lesson unpacks the logic and the commentary on its terminology.

ثمَّ المعاني أي : ثمَّ بعد أن عرفت ما تقدَّم من النَّفسيَّة والسلبية، يجب عليك معرفة الصفات المسماة بالمعاني؛ لأنَّ كلَّ واحدة منها معنى قائم بذاته تعالى . ومرادهم بصفات المعاني الصِّفاتُ الوجودية؛ أي: التي لها وجود في نفسها، قديمة كانت أو حادثة؛ كعلمه وقدرته تعالى، وكعلمنا وقدرتنا، والبياض والسَّواد. والحاصل : أنَّ الصِّفات إن كانت وجوديَّة سُمِّيت صفات معانٍ، وإن لم تكن وجوديَّة، فإن كان مدلولها عدم أمر لا يليق سُمِّيت سلبية. قوله : (المسماة بالمعاني) : أي : في اصطلاح المتكلمين، وتسمى أيضاً بالصفات الذاتيَّة ؛ لأنَّها لا تنفكُ عن الذات والوجوديَّة؛ لأنَّها متحققة باعتبار نفسها وهي في اللغة: ما قابل الذات، فيشمل النَّفسيَّة والسَّلبيّة والمعنوية، وفي الاصطلاح كلُّ صفة قائمة بموصوف زائدة على الذات، موجبة له حكماً، فخرج بقولنا : (قائمة بموصوف السَّلبيَّة، وبقولنا : (زائدة على الذَّات) النَّفسيَّة ؛ لأنَّها عين الذات، وبقولنا : (موجبة له حكماً) المعنوية ؛ لأنَّها نفسها حكم، وعلى القول بأنَّها أمور اعتبارية، فقد خرجت بقولنا : (قائمة بموصوف)، وهذا التَّعريف للمعاني من حيث هي كانت لقديم أو حادث، وحينئذ فالفرق بين صفات القديم والحادث أنَّ صفات القديم قديمة، ولا تسمى أعراضاً، وصفات الحادث حادثة وتسمّى أعراضاً .
Then the attributes of meanings: that is, then after you have known what preceded of the essential and negative attributes, it is incumbent upon you to know the attributes called 'of meanings' (al-maʿānī); because each one of them is a meaning (maʿnā) subsisting in His essence, exalted. And what they mean by 'attributes of meanings' is the existential attributes (al-ṣifāt al-wujūdiyyah); that is, those that have existence in themselves, whether eternal or temporal; like His knowledge and power, exalted, and like our knowledge and power, and whiteness and blackness. The conclusion: that if attributes are existential, they are called attributes of meanings; and if they are not existential, then if their signification is the non-existence of something unbefitting, they are called negative (salbīyah). His statement: (called 'of meanings'): that is, in the terminology of the theologians (al-mutakallimīn), and they are also called the essential attributes (al-ṣifāt al-dhātiyyah); because they are inseparable from the essence, and existential because they are realized in themselves. And they are in language: what is opposed to the essence, so it includes the essential, negative, and attributive (maʿnawiyyah). And in terminology: every attribute subsisting in a described entity, additional to the essence, necessitating a judgment for it. So by our saying 'subsisting in a described entity' the negative attributes are excluded; by our saying 'additional to the essence' the essential attribute is excluded, because it is identical to the essence; by our saying 'necessitating a judgment for it' the attributive (maʿnawiyyah) is excluded, because it itself is a judgment. And according to the view that they are mental considerations (umūr iʿtibāriyyah), then they are excluded by our saying 'subsisting in a described entity'. And this definition of meanings applies whether they are for the eternal or temporal. Accordingly, the difference between the attributes of the eternal and the temporal is that the attributes of the eternal are eternal and are not called accidents (aʿrāḍ), and the attributes of the temporal are temporal and are called accidents.

Commentary
This passage defines and classifies the attributes called 'ṣifāt al-maʿānī' (attributes of meanings). The author begins by explaining that after knowing the essential (nafsiyyah) and negative (salbīyah) attributes, one must know these attributes because each is a meaning (maʿnā) subsisting in God's essence. He then states that 'attributes of meanings' are existential attributes—those that have existence in themselves, whether eternal (like God's knowledge and power) or temporal (like human knowledge and whiteness). The conclusion: attributes are either existential (called maʿānī) or non-existential; the latter, if signifying a negation of what is unbefitting, are called negative. Next, the author gives a technical definition: in theological terminology, they are called 'dhātiyyah' (essential) because inseparable from the essence, and existential. Linguistically, 'maʿānī' means what is opposed to the essence, thus including nafsiyyah, salbiyyah, and maʿnawiyyah. Technically, it is every attribute subsisting in a subject, additional to the essence, necessitating a judgment. This definition excludes: negative attributes (by 'subsisting'), essential attribute (nafsiyyah, by 'additional to the essence' since it is identical), and attributive (maʿnawiyyah, by 'necessitating a judgment' since it is itself a judgment). On the view that these are mental considerations, they are excluded by the requirement of subsisting. Finally, the definition applies to both eternal and temporal attributes, but eternal attributes are not called accidents, while temporal ones are.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think that 'attributes of meanings' refer only to the seven divine attributes commonly listed (life, knowledge, will, power, hearing, seeing, speech).
Correction: Al-Ṣāwī clarifies that ṣifāt al-maʿānī are any existential attributes, including temporal ones like human knowledge and whiteness. The term is not restricted to the divine.

Key Takeaway
Ṣifāt al-maʿānī are the existential attributes that subsist in a subject, additional to the essence, and necessitate a judgment; they include both eternal and temporal attributes.
