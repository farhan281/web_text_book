Classification of Divine Attributes
The author defines sifat al-ma'ani and classifies attributes into existential, negative, and psychological.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Islamic theologians classify divine attributes into existential, negative, and psychological categories, and the distinction between eternal and contingent attributes.

Core Idea
Divine attributes are either existential, negative, or psychological.

This passage from al-Kharida establishes the foundational classification of God's attributes, known as sifat al-ma'ani (attributes of meanings). The author defines these as existential attributes subsisting in God's essence, then presents a threefold division that organizes all divine attributes and clarifies the difference between attributes of God and created attributes. Understanding this classification is essential for avoiding anthropomorphism and maintaining tawhid.

Guided Path
1
Read and translate
Read the Arabic passage once through, then read its literal English translation. Let the author's words establish the foundation.
Notice the technical terms: صفات المعاني, الصفات الوجودية, etc.
2
Identify the three categories
The passage presents three kinds of attributes. Find each one and note the logical condition that defines it.
The classification is based on existence and necessity.
3
Clarify negative attributes
Pay attention to the correction about negative attributes. They do not mean God lacks attributes, but negate what is unbecoming.
This prevents misunderstanding.
4
Distinguish eternal vs. temporal
Note the final distinction: eternal attributes are not called accidents, while temporal ones are.
This guards against comparing God's attributes to created ones.

It is incumbent upon you to know the attributes called 'attributes of meanings' (sifat al-ma'ani), because each one of them is a meaning subsisting in His Essence, exalted is He. And what is meant by their being negative is not that they are negated from God and denied of Him, but rather what is meant by their being negative is that each one of them negates a matter unbecoming of Him, mighty and majestic. Commentary
This passage defines and classifies the divine attributes. It begins by stating the importance of knowing the attributes called 'sifat al-ma'ani' (attributes of meanings), explaining that each is a meaning (ma'na) subsisting in God's essence. The author then clarifies that 'sifat al-ma'ani' refers to existential attributes (sifat wujudiyya)—those having real existence, whether eternal or contingent—giving examples from both God (knowledge, power) and creatures (knowledge, power, whiteness, blackness). Next, a threefold classification is given: (1) existential attributes are called sifat ma'ani; (2) if not existential, and their denotation is the absence of something unbecoming, they are called negative attributes (sifat salbiyya); (3) if not existential and not negative, but necessary for the essence as long as it exists, not caused, they are called psychological attributes (sifat nafsiyya) or psychological state (ḥāl nafsī), with existence as an example. The author then corrects a potential misunderstanding: negative attributes do not mean that God is stripped of attributes, but rather each such attribute negates an unbecoming quality (e.g., not being subject to origination). Finally, the definition of 'sifat ma'ani' applies to both eternal and contingent attributes, but with a crucial distinction: eternal attributes are eternal and not called 'accidents' (a'rad), whereas contingent attributes are temporally originated and are called accidents. This guards against anthropomorphic or univocal conceptions of God's attributes.

Defining the Attribute of Meaning (al-Ṣifah al-Maʿnawiyyah)
Sanusi defines the attribute of meaning and gives examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what makes an attribute an 'attribute of meaning' and how it differs from self-attributes.

Core Idea
Attributes of meaning are derived from inner causes (maʿānī) and are necessary for the essence due to those causes.

Sanusi begins by distinguishing attributes of meaning from other types. He provides a precise criterion: an attribute is called 'meaning-related' if it is caused by an internal meaning (like knowledge or power) and is necessary for the essence as long as that meaning exists. This lesson unpacks that definition with clear examples.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition
Read the Arabic passage from 'وغير صفات المعاني' to 'نسبة إلى المعاني'. Focus on the words 'معللة بعلة' and 'واجبةً للذات'.
The phrase 'ما دامت عدَّتُها' is technical; the commentary explains it.
2
Identify the examples
The passage gives two examples: 'العالمية' and 'القادرية'. Notice how these are relative to the meanings (knowledge and power).
3
Grasp the logical relationship
Understand that the essence is knowing because it has knowledge; the attribute of being knowing is derived from the meaning (knowledge). Thus, the attribute is 'meaning-related'.

Commentary
This passage defines the attribute of meaning (al-ṣifah al-maʿnawiyyah) and begins its enumeration. The commentator first clarifies that the example 'spatial occupancy for the body' (al-taḥayyuz li-l-jirm) is an example of a self-attribute. Then he transitions to attributes of meanings: if an attribute is caused by a cause (muʿallala bi-ʿillah)—i.e., it is necessary for the essence as long as that cause exists—then it is called a meaning-attribute (maʿnawiyyah). The examples given are 'being knowing' (al-ʿālimiyyah) and 'being powerful' (al-qādiriyyah), which are attributed to the essence because the essence possesses inner meanings (maʿānī) like knowledge and power. These attributes are 'relative to the meanings' (nisbah ilā al-maʿānī), meaning they are derived from those internal meanings. The author then states that these attributes are seven for 'the theologian' (al-rāʾī), i.e., the one who reflects. He begins to list them by saying: 'knowledge' (al-ʿilm), i.e., His knowledge, and what is conjoined to it (the other six). Then he emphasizes that knowledge encompasses all things—both necessary (wājib), possible (jāʾiz), and impossible (mustaḥīl)—so it is not restricted to existents only, as some may assume.

Causal Concomitance in Divine Attributes
Sanusi explains the example of spatial occupancy and redefines 'cause' as logical concomitance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the term 'cause' in the context of attributes of meanings refers to logical concomitance, not efficient causation, and how this applies to the example of spatial occupancy.

Core Idea
In kalam, 'cause' means 'concomitant' — the attribute is necessarily tied to the essence.

This passage of the Kharidat clarifies a crucial conceptual point: when we say that an essence is 'knowing by knowledge,' the causal language does not imply that knowledge produces knowing as an efficient cause. Rather, it means that being knowing is necessarily concomitant with having knowledge. Understanding this distinction is essential for accurate reasoning about divine attributes.

Notice that Sanusi uses a very compressed style here.

These are technical kalam terms that have specific meanings.

Commentary
This passage provides several clarifications on the attributes of meanings. First, the commentator explains the example 'spatial occupancy for the body' (al-taḥayyuz li-l-jirm) which was mentioned earlier. He clarifies that 'body' (jirm) means anything that subsists by itself, whether a body or a single atom, and 'spatial occupancy' means occupying a portion of space. This example illustrates that self-attributes apply universally to both eternal and originated entities. Then he addresses the phrase 'the essence described by knowledge being knowing'. He explains that 'knowing' is caused (muʿallala) by knowledge, but 'cause' here means the concomitant (al-malzūm) and 'effect' means the necessary concomitant (al-lāzim). In other words, the attribute of being knowing is necessarily concomitant with the essence that possesses knowledge—not that knowledge efficiently produces the attribute. Next, he notes that the phrase 'relative to the meanings' (nisbah ilā al-maʿānī) connects to why it is called 'meaning-attribute'. Then he addresses a potential objection: if only knowledge is mentioned, it might imply that the other attributes are not included. The answer is that the phrase 'and what is conjoined to it' (wa-mā ʿuṭifa ʿalayhi) indicates the other attributes are also included. Finally, he explains that the phrase 'their necessary ones, their possible ones, and their impossible ones' responds to an assumed objection: since 'thing' often means 'existent', one might think knowledge only pertains to existents. But the author explicitly includes the necessary, possible, and impossible, showing that knowledge encompasses non-existents as well.

Commentary
This passage is an extended explanation of the two meanings of 'Allah's speech' and a discussion of the nature of the divine address to Prophet Musa. The author first clarifies that the term 'Allah's speech' is used homonymously for two realities: the sensible speech (kalam hissi) that consists of letters and sound, and the self-subsisting eternal speech (kalam nafsi) which is the ancient attribute. The Real Distinction Between the Attributes of Meanings and Refuting the Objection
Al-Sawi refutes the objection of tahsil al-hasil regarding the attributes.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Attributes differ in reality even if we cannot perceive the difference.

Al-Sawi addresses a subtle objection: if hearing, sight, and knowledge all relate to every existent, what prevents them from being identical? He explains that the difference is real but hidden, thereby preserving both divine simplicity and the multiplicity of attributes.

The objection argues that if hearing, sight, and knowledge all relate to the same objects, they must be identical. If they were identical, one attribute already possessing that object would mean the others add nothing (tahsil al-hasil). The difference is affirmed despite our inability to discern it.
4

This shows that attributes can be distinct without implying composition or deficiency, as the distinction is real but not perceived by us. It upholds the doctrine of attributes of meanings.
This is a key point in defending Ash'ari theology against objections.

Resolving the Circularity Objection in Proving God's Speech
Resolves circularity objection regarding proving God's speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the apparent circularity in using transmitted evidence to prove God's speech is resolved by distinguishing the modality of miracles and the nature of divine speech.

Core Idea
Miracles indicate truthfulness without requiring God to utter words.

One common objection to proving God's speech through revelation is that it seems circular: we need a miracle to verify the prophet, but the miracle itself presupposes that God speaks. This passage from Sanusi's commentator shows how this objection is answered by carefully separating what a miracle signifies from what it does not.

Guided Path
1
Understand the circularity objection
Read the objector's claim carefully: proving God's speech via transmitted evidence requires a truthful messenger, but miracles (which prove his truthfulness) depend on God being speaking. Identify each link in the chain.
Notice that the objector assumes the miracle is like God saying 'My servant is truthful.'
2
Grasp the answer: separate modality
The commentator replies that the modality (jiha) is separate: the miracle indicates truthfulness without implying that God actually spoke those words. It functions as a sign, not a speech act.
The term 'جهة منفكة' means the aspect is disconnected – miracle and speech are not necessarily linked.
3
Analyze the gesture analogy
The author compares the miracle to a gesture: a gesture conveys meaning without the gesturer being a speaker. Similarly, the miracle conveys divine attestation without God uttering words.
This clarifies that the miracle does not require prior verbal speech from God.
4
Distinguish internal from verbal speech
The speech being proven is internal speech (kalam nafsi), not verbal speech (kalam lafzi). The miracle does not depend on verbal speech, so there is no circularity.
Internal speech is the eternal attribute; verbal speech is temporal.

Al-Sawi resolves an alleged circularity in proving God's speech through revelation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The miracle is like a pointing gesture—it proves the messenger's truthfulness without presupposing God's speech.

In this passage, Al-Sawi addresses a common objection: that proving God's speech through revelation is circular. He presents the objection clearly and then gives a subtle answer based on the distinction between different aspects (جهة منفكّة). By the end, you'll see how the miracle functions as independent evidence.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Start by reading the objection that Al-Sawi presents. Notice the logical chain: to prove God's speech, we need revelation; to trust revelation, we need a truthful messenger; to know the messenger is truthful, we need a miracle; but a miracle requires God to be speaking. This seems circular.
The objector assumes the miracle is like God's speech.
