The Cave and the Divine Protection
Al-Sawi narrates the cave incident and the spider-pigeon miracle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how God miraculously protected the Prophet and Abu Bakr in the Cave of Thawr by sending a spider and pigeons, and how this event is a proof of prophethood.

Core Idea
The spider's web and pigeon's eggs were divine signs that blinded the Quraysh.

This passage from al-Sawi describes the dramatic moment when the Quraysh trackers reached the Cave of Thawr, only to be misled by a spider's web and pigeons' eggs—miraculous signs that concealed the Prophet and his companion. The narrative also includes the famous verse 'Do not grieve, indeed God is with us,' highlighting the Prophet's trust in Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow of the story. Then read the translation. Notice how the suspense builds as the polytheists approach the cave.
Pay attention to the direct speech: the Prophet's reassurance and the polytheists' dialogue.
2
Identify the miraculous signs
Find the two specific miracles mentioned: the pigeons laying eggs and the spider weaving a web at the cave's mouth. Note how they convinced the polytheists that no one could be inside.
The author explains that these could not have happened after entry, so they were pre-arranged by God.
3
Reflect on the deeper lesson
Consider why this event is a proof of prophethood. The miracle of the cave demonstrates Allah's direct protection of His Messenger and confirms his truthfulness.
Also note the allusion to the poet al-Busiri, which shows how this event is celebrated in Islamic tradition.

Commentary
This passage is a historical narrative that serves as a proof of prophethood by recounting miraculous events during the Prophet's hijra. It begins with the polytheists tracking the Prophet and Abu Bakr to the Cave of Thawr. When they reach the cave, the trace ends, and they search. One suggests looking into the cave, but they claim there is no one, even though a glance would have revealed them. Abu Bakr fears, but the Prophet reassures him with Quranic words: 'Do not grieve, indeed God is with us.' God blinds their physical eyes as He had blinded their inner sight. The author then reports a tradition that God sent two pigeons to lay eggs at the cave's mouth and a spider to weave a web, so the polytheists argue that the cave cannot be entered in that state, implying no one could be inside. The poet al-Busiri alludes to this miracle in his Burda, describing the cave's goodness and the blindness of disbelievers. The poem emphasizes that the Prophet (the truthful) and Abu Bakr (al-Siddiq) were in the cave, yet the pagans thought the pigeons and spider were there (i.e., they didn't enter). The author explains that 'al-Siddiq' means the truthful one, referring to the Prophet. Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think the spider web and pigeons were naturally present before the Prophet entered the cave.

The Quraysh Conspiracy and Iblis's Counsel
Al-Sawi narrates the Quraysh meeting in Dar al-Nadwa and Iblis's intervention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quraysh plotted to kill the Prophet and how Iblis influenced their decision, as well as the prophetic protections that foiled their plans.

Core Idea
Iblis disguised as an elder guided the Quraysh to adopt the murder plot.

In this passage, al-Sawi recounts the Quraysh's desperate meeting in Dar al-Nadwa after learning of the Prophet's intention to emigrate. Each tribe proposed a different plan, but it was Iblis, appearing as a wise elder, who endorsed Abu Jahl's proposal of a coordinated assassination to avoid blood feuds.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage in context
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice that this passage follows the cave narrative and leads into the escape. The author first explains the background of the hijra command.
Pay attention to the sequence: first the Prophet commands the companions to emigrate, then Quraysh react.
2
Identify the three proposals
List the three suggestions made by the Quraysh leaders: imprisoning, killing, and tying to a wild camel. Then see how Iblis responds to each.
Iblis only approves the murder plan.
3
Notice Iblis's role
Iblis appears in the form of a Najdi elder. Note how his advice steers the Quraysh toward the most violent option.
This shows the devil's enmity and his role in inciting disbelief.

Commentary
This passage is a historical narrative that serves as a proof of prophethood by recounting miraculous events during the Prophet's hijra. It begins with the polytheists tracking the Prophet and Abu Bakr to the Cave of Thawr. When they reach the cave, the trace ends, and they search. One suggests looking into the cave, but they claim there is no one, even though a glance would have revealed them. Abu Bakr fears, but the Prophet reassures him with Quranic words: 'Do not grieve, indeed God is with us.' God blinds their physical eyes as He had blinded their inner sight. The author then reports a tradition that God sent two pigeons to lay eggs at the cave's mouth and a spider to weave a web, so the polytheists argue that the cave cannot be entered in that state, implying no one could be inside. The poet al-Busiri alludes to this miracle in his Burda, describing the cave's goodness and the blindness of disbelievers. And among them: that he once spoke about asceticism (zuhd), and in the gathering there was a faqir (poor person) wearing ragged clothes, while the sheikh wore fine clothes. The faqir said to himself: How can the sheikh speak about asceticism while wearing this attire? I am the ascetic in the world. The sheikh turned to him and said: These clothes of yours are clothes of desire for the world, because they call out against you with the tongue of poverty, while our clothes call out with the tongue of wealth and self-restraint (ta'affuf). Then the faqir stood up before the people and said: By God, I was speaking about this in my secret, and I ask forgiveness of God and repent to Him. So the sheikh clothed him with good clothing and directed him to a teacher called Ibn al-Dahhan, and said to him:

Commentary
This passage is another karamah narrative, illustrating a spiritual lesson about the true nature of asceticism (zuhd). The sheikh is speaking on asceticism while wearing fine clothes, and a poor man (faqir) in ragged clothes inwardly criticizes him, considering himself more ascetic. The sheikh, possessing spiritual insight (kashf), turns and explains that the faqir's ragged clothes actually indicate attachment to the world, because they call attention to his poverty and thus his concern for worldly appearance. In contrast, the sheikh's fine clothes signal contentment and self-restraint (ta'affuf) since they show no lack or complaint. The faqir recognizes the truth, repents, and the sheikh gives him good clothes and guides him to a shaykh. The lesson is that zuhd is a state of the heart, not outward poverty, and that fine clothing can be a sign of gratitude and detachment if worn without pride.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Asceticism requires wearing ragged clothes and outward poverty.
Correction: True asceticism is a state of the heart, and outward simplicity can sometimes indicate attachment to worldly appearance, while fine clothes worn with gratitude and detachment can be a sign of zuhd.
