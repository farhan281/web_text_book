Prophets and Concealment: Commanded vs. Permitted
Sawi explains the impossibility of prophets concealing commanded revelation and categorizes other matters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that prophets cannot conceal any part of the revelation they were commanded to deliver, and that some divine secrets are either to be concealed or shared only with select individuals.

Core Idea
Prophets must convey what commanded, but may conceal permitted secrets.

This passage addresses a subtle question: do prophets have any secrets? Sawi clarifies that prophets are utterly incapable of concealing any part of the revelation they were commanded to deliver. However, there are divine secrets they are commanded to conceal, and they may share some with select individuals like the four caliphs and Abu Huraira. A marginal note offers an alternative classification that reframes these categories.

Guided Path
1
Read the Quranic evidence
Begin with the opening Quranic proof (2:159) which curses those who conceal revealed guidance. Notice that this establishes a general principle, and prophets must be free from such a curse.
The verse is partially quoted; its message is clear.
2
Identify the main categories
The passage distinguishes matters commanded to be conveyed (which prophets must deliver fully) from other matters. Among the latter, some are optional and some must be concealed.
Look for the terms 'خُيّر' (choice) and 'يجب كتمانه' (must conceal).
3
Examine the special subcategory
Within matters that must be concealed, there is a subset that prophets were permitted to convey to specific individuals, such as the four caliphs and Abu Huraira. These secrets later circulate among saints.
This shows that not all concealed knowledge is absolutely hidden.
4
Consider the alternative classification
The marginal note presents a different view: some scholars treat the permitted-to-individuals category as part of the 'choice' category, resulting in three overall categories: commanded to convey, commanded to conceal, and choice.
Compare this with the two-category division in the main text.
5
Reflect on the overall doctrine
The key point is that prophets are infallible in delivering revelation, but may have knowledge that is not for public dissemination. This balances their mission with divine wisdom.
This lesson connects to the broader topic of prophetic infallibility.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of the classifications of what prophets are required or permitted to communicate. It begins by dividing what they are not explicitly commanded to convey into two subcategories: that which they are given a choice to convey or not (so long as they were not instructed to withhold it), and that which they must conceal because they were commanded to keep it secret, such as certain divine secrets. Then it notes a third subcategory within the second: some secrets they were permitted to disclose to specific individuals like the four caliphs and Abu Hurayra, and these are the secrets that later circulate among saints (awliya'). Then the author quotes his own previous words and adds that some scholars reclassify this third subcategory into the first (i.e., optional), resulting in three categories: (1) what they were commanded to convey — they never concealed a letter of it; (2) what they were commanded to conceal — they never transmitted a letter of it; and (3) what they were given a choice in — they conveyed some and concealed some.
