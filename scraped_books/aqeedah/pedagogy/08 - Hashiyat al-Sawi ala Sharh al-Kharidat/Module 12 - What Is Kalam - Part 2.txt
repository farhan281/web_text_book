Definition of Knowledge of Allah and Its Three Conditions
Al-Sawi defines knowledge of Allah with three conditions: certainty, correspondence, and cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three essential conditions for valid knowledge of Allah: certainty, correspondence to reality, and being based on a cause.

Core Idea
Knowledge of Allah requires certainty, correspondence, and a cause.

Before we can properly study the attributes of Allah, we need a precise definition of what it means to truly 'know' Allah. Al-Sawi provides a technical definition with three conditions that distinguish genuine knowledge from mere opinion, false belief, or blind imitation. This passage lays the groundwork for all theological inquiry.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition carefully
Start by reading the definition of knowledge of Allah: a firm belief (jāzm) that corresponds to reality (muwāfiq lil-wāqiʿ) and is based on a cause (mūjib). Notice that it covers both necessary and theoretical knowledge.
The phrase 'so it includes the necessary and the theoretical' will be explained later.
2
Identify the three conditions and their exclusions
Al-Sawi unpacks each condition: 'firm' excludes mere opinion (ẓann), 'corresponding' excludes false belief (iʿtiqād fāsid), and 'cause' excludes belief without a valid basis. Find the examples for each.
Note that 'cause' includes proof, external sense, and inner sense.
3
Understand the clarification on 'correspondence'
Al-Sawi clarifies that correspondence is about the mental relation (nisba) matching reality, not the firm belief itself being the correspondence. This prevents misunderstanding.
4
Review the example of the Eid prayers
The Sunni belief that the two Eid prayers are sunnah is only knowledge if based on evidence; otherwise it is mere imitation. This illustrates the 'cause' condition.
If the obligation of the prayers is known by tawatur, it becomes necessary knowledge.
5
Connect to the broader study of kalam
Reflect on why defining knowledge precisely matters: it sets the standard for what counts as valid theological arguments and prevents error.
This lesson is foundational for all subsequent discussions.

Commentary
This passage defines the nature of valid knowledge (ma'rifah) of Allah and sets out its essential conditions. The author begins by stating the definition: knowledge of Allah is a firm belief (jāzm) that corresponds to reality (muwāfiq lil-wāqiʿ) and is based on a cause (mūjib). The phrase 'so it includes the necessary and the theoretical' is part of the definition but will be explained in the next lesson. The author then unpacks each condition. The word 'firm' excludes mere opinion (ẓann), which lacks certainty. The word 'corresponding' excludes false belief (iʿtiqād fāsid), such as the philosophers' belief that the world is eternal—this belief does not match reality. The word 'cause' (mūjib) is explained: it is what necessitates belief, whether from a proof (dalīl), external sense (ḥiss), or inner sense (wijdān). An example of correct belief based on a cause is the Sunni belief that the two Eid prayers are sunnah—provided it is based on evidence; otherwise it is mere imitation, not knowledge. The author then clarifies that 'corresponding to reality' refers to the relation (nisba) in the mind matching the relation in reality; it is not that the firm belief itself is the correspondence. Next, he explains that 'ẓann' in the text is a category including doubt (shakk) and illusion (wahm). 'False belief' is identified as compound ignorance (jahl murakkab). He then details the sources: external senses are the five senses, and inner sense (wijdān) includes feelings like hunger and love. Finally, he revisits the example of the Eid prayers: if believed without proof, it is not knowledge; but the widespread tawatur of its obligation makes it necessary knowledge for those aware of that consensus.

Definition of Taqlid and the Infallible Exception
Al-Sawi defines taqlid and clarifies the infallible exception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise definition of taqlid in Islamic theology and why accepting statements from an infallible person is not considered taqlid but rather certain knowledge.

Core Idea
Taqlid is blind following without proof; following an infallible is knowledge, not imitation.

Al-Sawi begins by defining taqlid as accepting another’s statement without evidence. This may seem straightforward, but a crucial nuance follows: following an infallible person, like a living prophet, is not taqlid at all. Instead, it produces necessary knowledge. This lesson clarifies the boundary between blind imitation and genuine knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of taqlid

2
Understand the implied question
The commentator explains that this definition answers whether taqlid suffices for knowledge of God. Consider why one might ask this.
3
Distinguish the infallible case
The passage then clarifies that hearing from an infallible during his lifetime is not taqlid but yields necessary knowledge. Reflect on why this distinction matters.

Faith's Two Levels and the Muqallid
Distinguishes perfect faith from basic faith, validating taqlid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between perfect faith and the basis of faith, and why the faith of a muqallid (one who follows without proof) is considered valid in the Hereafter.

Core Idea
Faith has two levels: perfect faith requires knowledge; the basis requires only decisive belief.

In this passage, the commentator addresses a key debate about the faith of a muqallid (one who accepts religious beliefs without analytical proof). He clarifies that while perfect faith is defined as inner speech following knowledge, the basis of faith is defined as inner speech following decisive belief, which includes taqlid. This distinction resolves the controversy and underscores the mercy of Allah in accepting simple faith.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow. Then read the literal translation to understand the meaning. Note how the commentator first addresses the ruling on the muqallid.
The passage has two clear parts: the worldly and afterlife rulings for the muqallid, and the two definitions of faith.
2
Identify the two definitions of iman
Look for the phrase 'inner speech following knowledge' (perfect faith) and 'inner speech following decisive belief' (basis of faith). Notice that the second definition is broader and includes taqlid.
The basis of faith does not require analytical knowledge; it only requires a firm conviction.
3
Understand the muqallid's validity
The commentator states that according to the majority (contrary to Abu Hashim), the muqallid's faith is valid in the Hereafter. In this world, pronouncing the shahadas is sufficient for Muslim status.
This is an important point: the basis of faith is valid even without deep knowledge.

The Correct Meaning of Knowing Allah
Al-Sawi defines knowledge of Allah and clarifies its scope and ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that knowledge of Allah (ma'rifah) consists of knowing what is necessary, impossible, and permissible for Him, and that this obligation is derived from revelation, while knowledge of His essence is beyond human capacity.

Core Idea
Knowledge of Allah is knowing His attributes, not His essence.

This passage from al-Sawi's Hashiya clarifies what it truly means to 'know Allah.' Many mistakenly think it requires penetrating the divine essence, but the scholars teach that it is about understanding Allah's necessary, impossible, and permissible attributes. The passage also explains that this knowledge is only obligatory because of revelation, and that the highest form of knowledge is realizing our inability to comprehend His essence.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of ma'rifah
Carefully read the opening sentence where al-Sawi defines knowledge of Allah as knowledge of the three rational categories: necessary, impossible, and permissible.
Notice he explicitly says 'not knowledge of the reality of the Supreme Essence.'
2
Understand the source of obligation
The passage states that this knowledge is obligatory by the Law (shar'), not by reason alone. This counters the Mu'tazilite view that reason independently mandates it.
Al-Sawi emphasizes that before revelation, there is no ruling at all.
3
Analyze the refutation of essence-knowledge
Read the section starting from 'Not knowledge of the reality...' to 'the correct view is that it is not permissible by reason.' Understand why knowing the essence is impossible.
The argument: the contingent cannot comprehend the Necessary Being.
4
Reflect on the transmitted proofs
Go through the poetic quote, the hadiths, and the sayings of Abu Bakr and Ali. They all reinforce the theme that Allah is veiled from perception and that acknowledging this incapacity is itself a form of knowledge.
The famous statement 'al-'ajzu 'an idraki idrakun' is key.

The author begins by stating that knowledge of Allah consists of knowing what is necessarily attributable to Him (wajib), what is impossible for Him (mustahil), and what is permissible for Him (ja'iz). Then he explains the statement 'wajib bi al-shar'' (obligatory by the Law): the obligation to seek this knowledge is known only through revelation, not through reason alone, contrary to the Mu'tazila. There is no ruling before revelation—neither primary nor secondary. He then elaborates that knowledge of the essence is not obligatory, nor even possible, whether by reason or by revelation. He supports this with a quote from al-Ghazali stating that the contingent (al-hadith) is naturally incapable of comprehending the Divine. Poetry by al-Sharif al-Maqdisi reinforces the point. Then he cites hadith and sayings of the Companions (Abu Bakr and Ali) and a hadith about the creation in darkness to emphasize that Allah is veiled from perception, and that the highest form of knowledge is to acknowledge one's inability to comprehend His essence. The passage thus establishes the limits of human knowledge concerning Allah: it is obligatory to know His attributes but impossible to know His essence.

The Third Condition: Immediate Abandonment
The third condition of repentance and its practical implications for ongoing sins and interpersonal wrongs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third condition of repentance—immediate abandonment of sin—and its application, including how to handle wrongs committed against others.

Core Idea
True repentance requires not only internal regret but immediate external action to stop the sin.

Having covered the inner dimensions of repentance, Sanusi now turns to the practical requirement: immediate cessation of the sinful act. He explains how this applies to different types of sins, especially those involving harm to others, and outlines the steps to rectify injustices.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage for the third condition

The phrase 'في ذنب لم ينقض' limits the condition to ongoing sins.
2
Identify the examples
Sanusi gives examples of ongoing sins: completing adultery, drinking wine, and harming someone. Understand why these are considered 'not yet elapsed'.
3
Understand the rectification for wronging others
If the sin involves wronging someone, the passage details steps: return injustices, seek forgiveness, or if impossible, pray for them and give charity.
Allah will satisfy the wronged party if the repentance is sincere.

What Makes a Sin Major or Minor?
Sanusi defines major sin and explains how minor sins become major.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi defines major and minor sins and the factors that can escalate a minor sin to a major one.

Core Idea
A minor sin becomes major through persistence, contempt, joy, or pride.

Sanusi begins by defining the categories of sin using a clear example. He then gives a crucial warning: the gravity of a sin is not fixed; it can increase based on the sinner's attitude. This lesson unpacks that shift.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition of major and minor sins
Read the passage and note that Sanusi uses intentional murder as an example of a major sin. Minor sins are defined as those falling short of that criterion.
The criterion for major is not fully specified, but murder is given as a clear case.
2
Identify the four elevating factors
The passage lists four attitudes that can turn a minor sin into a major one: persistence, contempt, joy, and pride. Memorize them.
3
Reflect on the practical implication
Think about how this teaching affects your view of minor sins. Even a small sin can become grave if accompanied by these attitudes.
This connects to the learning objective.

Commentary
This passage provides a definition of major (kabirah) and minor (saghirah) sins. It gives intentional murder (al-qatl al-amd) as an example of a major sin. The criterion (dabit) for a major sin is what goes beyond a certain limit—anything falling below that is a minor sin. The specific major sins are not limited in number. Importantly, a minor sin can become major due to certain factors: persistence (al-israr) in it, treating it lightly (al-tahawun), feeling joy (al-farah) upon committing it, or being proud (al-iftikhar) of it. Thus, the gravity of a sin is not fixed solely by the act itself but can escalate based on the attitude of the sinner.
