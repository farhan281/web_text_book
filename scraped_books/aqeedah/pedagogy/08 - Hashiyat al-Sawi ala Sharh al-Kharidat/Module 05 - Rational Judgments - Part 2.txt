Evidence for the World's Temporality: Observation and Deduction
Sanusi presents two types of evidence for the world's temporality: direct observation and deductive proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two types of evidence (direct observation and deductive proof) for establishing that the world is temporal, and why bodies require deductive proof rather than direct observation.

Core Idea
Temporality is proven by change; we observe change in accidents, and deduce it for bodies.

In this passage, Sanusi explains how we know that the world is temporal (created). He presents two complementary methods: direct observation of changing accidents like motion and stillness, and deductive reasoning for bodies like mountains and planets which appear permanent. By understanding both, we see that nothing in the world is eternal.

Commentary
This passage provides the evidence for the minor premise of the syllogism presented earlier: that the world is temporal. The author explains that temporality is proven by change (taghayyur) in the world, specifically in accidents (a'rad) which are observed to change from non-existence to existence and vice versa. He then distinguishes two types of evidence: direct observation (mushahada) and deductive proof (dalil). Observational evidence includes examples like motion after stillness, light after darkness, etc. However, bodies themselves (ajram) like mountains and planets are not observed to change directly; their temporality is deduced by proof: since it is possible for a body to have its opposite property (e.g., motion for a mountain), and possibility of absence implies impossibility of eternity, bodies must be temporal. The author further elaborates that changes in bodies (e.g., size, life, death) are actually changes in accidents, not the body itself. For example, death is observed as dispersal of parts, and salt dissolving in water is a real annihilation, unlike accidents that can be absent in some individuals but not nonexistent. The commentary clarifies that the observation of change is limited to accidents, while bodies are proved temporal through their association with accidents. The argument from analogy ('no difference between one body and another') reinforces that if one body can accept change, so can all, thus all bodies are temporal.

Proof of Temporality of Accidents from Observed Change
Al-Sawi explains the proof that accidents are temporal based on observed change.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the kalam proof that all accidents are temporal (ḥāditha) based on observed change, and why the possibility of non-existence negates eternality.

Core Idea
Observed change proves accidents are temporal, not eternal.

Al-Sawi presents a key kalam proof: since we observe accidents changing (motion after stillness, light after darkness, etc.), and since any body could experience the opposite, accidents cannot be eternal. This lesson walks through the examples, the logical argument, and the clarifying commentary.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation carefully. Notice the list of examples and the logical argument that follows.
The passage includes both the original proof and brief commentary explaining each phrase.
2
Identify the examples of change
The passage lists several pairs of opposites: motion/sillness, light/darkness, blackness/whiteness, heat/cold. These are observed changes in accidents.
These examples illustrate that accidents undergo change.
3
Follow the logical argument
The argument: for bodies observed in a constant state (like mountains always still), it is possible for the opposite to occur. There is no difference between bodies. If absence is possible, then it cannot be eternal.
The key step: 'If its absence is permissible, then its eternality is impossible.'
4
Understand the commentary
The commentary clarifies each phrase. Note the explanation of 'like motion' and 'no difference between bodies'.
The commentary reinforces that all bodies are equally liable to change.

Commentary
This passage presents the proof that all accidents are temporal (ḥāditha) based on observed change. It begins by listing examples of change: motion after stillness, light after darkness, blackness after whiteness, heat after cold, and the reverse. These are common observable phenomena. The argument then states that for anything observed to be permanently in one state (like mountains always still or planets always moving), it is possible for the opposite state to occur, because there is no intrinsic difference between one body and another in their capacity to receive accidents. Since it is possible for these accidents (like motion or stillness) to be absent (i.e., to change), they cannot be eternal (qadīm). This is because if something can be non-existent, it cannot be eternal—eternal beings must be necessary and unchanging. The subsequent commentary (قوله) explains each phrase: 'like motion' means motion in a body after stillness; 'light after darkness' means the light of a body after its darkness ceases; 'no difference between bodies' means that all bodies are equally liable to change; 'if its absence is permissible' refers to accidents in general, whether observed or not. The proof establishes that accidents are temporal, forming the minor premise (ṣughrā) of the cosmological argument for the temporality of the world.

Core Idea
Huduth is existence after non-existence; qidam is its opposite and belongs only to Allah.

The Seventh Objection: Change in the Maker
Seventh objection and its answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the seventh objection to temporal origination and why kalam scholars distinguish change of actions from change of essence.

Core Idea
God's action does not change His essence.

This passage presents the final and perhaps most subtle objection against the temporal origination of the world: that it would require change in God, which is impossible. The answer follows immediately, drawing a crucial distinction between change of actions and change of essence. Let's examine how Sanusi resolves this.

Change of actions (affections) is permissible; change of essence is not.

This distinction is fundamental in Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Denying God's pre-eternity forces an impossible chain.

Sanusi begins by stating a conditional proof: if God were not pre-eternal, He would be originated and require an originator, leading to an impossible situation. This lesson unpacks the initial argument and introduces the two fatal consequences.

The author does not yet prove they are impossible; that comes next.

The impossibility of the consequences forces the impossibility of the antecedent (lack of pre-eternity). Thus pre-eternity is established.
This is a standard logical form: modus tollens.

The Proof of Mutual Hindrance (Burhan al-Tamanu')

Learning Objective

If two gods existed, contradiction would follow regardless of agreement or disagreement.

Al-Sawi opens this lesson by presenting the Quranic proof for tawhid from Surah al-Anbiya (21:22). He then explains that this proof is known as 'burhan al-tamanu'' (mutual hindrance) and has two variants: one when the gods are assumed to disagree (tatard) and another when they are assumed to agree (tawarud). Commentary
This passage presents the well-known proof of divine unity called 'burhan al-tamanu'' (proof of mutual hindrance), grounded in Quran 21:22: 'If there were in them gods besides Allah, they would both be ruined.' The author explains that this proof is also called 'burhan al-tatard' (repulsion) when assuming disagreement between the two supposed gods, and 'burhan al-tawarud' (concurrence) when assuming agreement.

A significant portion of the commentary is dedicated to analyzing the word 'إلا' in the verse. The author argues that 'إلا' here is not an exception particle but an adjective meaning 'other than' (غير). He gives two reasons: First, if it were an exception, the verse would contradict tawhid by implying that if gods existed including Allah, the heavens and earth would not be ruined. Second, grammatically, exception requires the excluded-from noun to be general, but 'آلهة' (gods) is an indefinite plural in an affirmative context, which does not convey generality, making exception invalid. Therefore, the verse affirms that there were no gods at all; the only deity is Allah.

The passage concludes with the point that the proof assumes the possibility of multiplicity in essence, attributes, and actions, though the text ends abruptly.

Commentary
This passage presents the well-known proof of divine unity called 'burhan al-tamanu'' (proof of mutual hindrance), grounded in Quran 21:22: 'If there were in them gods besides Allah, they would both be ruined.' The author explains that this proof is also called 'burhan al-tatard' (repulsion) when assuming disagreement between the two supposed gods, and 'burhan al-tawarud' (concurrence) when assuming agreement.

A significant portion of the commentary is dedicated to analyzing the word 'إلا' in the verse. The author argues that 'إلا' here is not an exception particle but an adjective meaning 'other than' (غير). He gives two reasons: First, if it were an exception, the verse would contradict tawhid by implying that if gods existed including Allah, the heavens and earth would not be ruined. Second, grammatically, exception requires the excluded-from noun to be general, but 'آلهة' (gods) is an indefinite plural in an affirmative context, which does not convey generality, making exception invalid. Therefore, the verse affirms that there were no gods at all; the only deity is Allah.

The passage concludes with the point that the proof assumes the possibility of multiplicity in essence, attributes, and actions, though the text ends abruptly.

Textual and Rational Proofs Against Independent Efficacy
Sawi presents textual and rational proofs against creature efficacy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the textual proof from the Quran and the rational proof that refutes the claim that creatures create their own actions.

Core Idea
If servants created their actions, they would know them fully—which they do not.

Having defined the false claims, the author now provides evidence from revelation and reason. The Quran affirms Allah's absolute power and creation. The rational argument demonstrates the impossibility of the servant being the creator of his own actions, using a reductio ad absurdum. This lesson walks through both proofs.

Guided Path
1
Read the textual proof
Read the Quranic verses cited: 'Allah is over all things competent' and 'He created everything and determined it with precise determination.'
These verses establish Allah as the sole creator and agent.
2
Understand the first rational argument
If the servant created his actions, he would know them in detail. Since we lack such knowledge, the claim is false.
This is a conditional argument: if A then B, not B, therefore not A.
3
Understand the second rational argument (dilemma)
Either an action occurs by both God's and the servant's power together (impossible) or by the servant's power alone (implies God is not sole agent).
Both options are impossible, so the servant cannot be a creator.
4
Reflect on the strength of the proofs
Consider how these proofs together show the logical necessity of Allah's exclusive agency.
The proofs are cumulative: textual and rational.

Commentary
This passage is a rational proof (dalīl ʿaqlī) for the permissibility of seeing Allah (ruʾyat Allāh). The author then enumerates the three metaphysical modes: existence (wujūd), temporality (ḥudūth: coming into being after non-existence), and contingency (imkān: equal possibility of existence and non-existence). وقوله : (أو شرعاً) : أي : كالدُّعاء بأنَّ الله يأتيه بمحرَّم؛ كالخمر ونحوه .
وقوله : ( أو عادة) أي : كصعود للسَّماء مثلاً .
قوله : (وعدم حصول إجابة ) : أي : بعين المطلوب.
قوله : (إمَّا لتخلُّف شرط) : أي : من شروط الإجابة بعين المطلوب إذ كثيرة؛ منها : أكل الحلال، والثّقة بالله .
وله آداب ؛ منها : الوضوء، واستقبال القبلة ورفع الأيدي، وتخليله بالصَّلاة على النَّبيِّ ، وختمه بها، وأعظمها حضور القلب لما في الحديث : «إنَّ الله لا يقبل دعاء من قلب لاهي»
The ruling of supplication: it should be regarding what is impossible rationally, legally, or customarily. And it should be accompanied by humility and brokenness, and be in noble times like the pre-dawn hours and after prayers. And there should not be in it restriction upon Allah, like asking for a particular need at a specific time, unless distress is severe, such as deliverance from an oppressor. Then indeed supplication in itself is the essence of worship, because it manifests poverty and neediness to Allah, and that Allah is the Rich, the Powerful over everything, even if response is not obtained. And the non-occurrence of response is either due to a condition being unfulfilled, or due to Allah's knowledge that non-response is better for him or otherwise. His saying: (by what is impossible rationally) i.e. like combining two opposites. His saying: (or legally) i.e. like supplicating that Allah bring him something forbidden, like wine and the like. His saying: (or customarily) i.e. like ascending to the sky, for example. His saying: (and non-occurrence of response) i.e. regarding the exact requested thing. His saying: (either due to a condition being unfulfilled) i.e. from the conditions of response regarding the exact requested thing, for they are many; among them: eating lawful food, and trust in Allah. And it has etiquettes; among them: ablution, facing the qibla, raising the hands, and interspersing it with prayer upon the Prophet, and concluding with it, and the greatest of them is presence of heart, due to the hadith: 'Indeed Allah does not accept the supplication of a heedless heart.'

Commentary
This passage defines the ruling of supplication and its proper etiquette. It begins by stating that supplication should only be for things that are possible in one of three senses: rationally, legally, or customarily. Rationally impossible examples include asking for the combination of two opposites. Legally impossible means asking for something forbidden, like wine. Customarily impossible means asking for something that does not normally occur, like ascending to the sky without a miracle. The passage then lists the proper manner of supplication: it should be done with humility, at noble times such as pre-dawn and after prayers, and without restricting Allah by specifying a particular time for a need unless necessary (like escape from an oppressor). It emphasizes that supplication is the essence of worship because it shows one's need and poverty before Allah, who is Independent and Powerful. The response may not be granted either because a condition was missing or because Allah knows that withholding it is better for the person. The commentary then explains each phrase from the source text: the types of impossibility, the meaning of 'non-response' (i.e., not getting exactly what was asked for), and the many conditions for response, including eating lawful food and trusting in Allah. Finally, it lists etiquettes: ablution, facing the qibla, raising hands, interspersing and concluding the supplication with blessings on the Prophet, and most importantly, having presence of heart, citing the hadith that Allah does not accept a supplication from a heedless heart.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think that if a supplication is not answered, it means Allah is displeased or that du'a is ineffective.
Correction: The passage clarifies that non-response may be due to missing conditions or divine wisdom, and du'a itself is still worship.
