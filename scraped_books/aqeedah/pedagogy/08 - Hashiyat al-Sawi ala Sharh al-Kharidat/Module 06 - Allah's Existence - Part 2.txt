God Does Not Need a Specifier
Al-Sawi proves that God does not need a specifier.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the kalam argument that God does not need a specifier, based on the entailment of origination.

Core Idea
Needing a specifier implies origination, which contradicts God's pre-eternity.

In this passage, Al-Sawi presents a crisp proof for God's independence from any external cause. He uses a simple logical entailment: if God needed a specifier, He would be originated, but He is pre-eternal. This argument builds on the earlier discussion of pre-eternity. Let's examine this proof closely.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once through, then read the literal translation. Notice the concise style: the proof is given in a single line.
The passage references an earlier discussion on pre-eternity (al-qidam).
2
Identify key terms
Identify the three key terms: 'specifier' (مخصص), 'origination' (حدوث), and 'pre-eternity' (قدم). Understand their meanings in this context.
The specifier is defined as 'a bringer into existence and an effecter'.
3
Trace the logical argument
Break down the argument: (1) If God needed a specifier, He would be originated. (2) God is pre-eternal (already established). (3) Therefore, God does not need a specifier.
The proof relies on the earlier established attribute of pre-eternity.
4
Reflect on the implication
Ask yourself: What would it mean if God needed a cause? How does this argument safeguard divine independence?
This argument is foundational for tawḥīd and refuting those who posit external causes for God.

Proof of Oneness from Mutual Frustration (Tamānuʿ)
Sanusi proves divine unity via the impossibility of mutual frustration between two gods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical structure and force of the burhān al-tamānuʿ as a decisive proof for God's oneness.

Core Idea
If two gods could disagree, creation would be impossible; therefore, only one God exists.

Sanusi presents one of the most celebrated arguments in Islamic theology: the proof from mutual frustration. This logical demonstration shows that the existence of two or more gods would lead to a logical contradiction, and therefore only one God can exist. The argument is grounded in the Quranic verse (21:22) and has been a cornerstone of tawhid proofs.

Guided Path
1
Understand the scenario
The argument begins by imagining two gods who might will opposite things, like Zayd's motion and rest. This is possible for anything contingent.
The example uses Zayd's motion and rest as clear opposites.
2
Analyze the outcomes
If both wills happen, opposites coexist (impossible). If neither happens, both or one god is powerless (a sign of contingency). Both lead to impossibility.
Powerlessness contradicts divinity.
3
Refute the objection
The objection says they could always agree. The refutation: the mere possibility of disagreement is enough; actual agreement doesn't remove the logical implication.
Also, the Quran verse (21:22) is a decisive proof.
4
Understand the verse explanation
Sanusi explains that if there were two gods, the universe could not exist because joint creation is either combined (implying incompleteness) or by one alone (making the other redundant).
The verse means the heavens and earth would not exist at all.

Commentary
This passage presents the classical kalam proof for God's oneness called burhān al-tamānuʿ (proof of mutual frustration). It begins by stating a conditional: if there were two gods, it would be possible for them to will opposite things (e.g., one wills Zayd's motion, the other his rest). Then it examines the outcomes: either both contradictory events occur (impossible, as they cannot coexist) or neither occurs, implying incapacity in both or one. Incapacity is a sign of contingency and need, which is incompatible with divinity. Therefore, multiplicity leads to impossibility, so it is impossible. An objection is raised: what if they always agree? The refutation is that the mere possibility of disagreement suffices; actual agreement does not negate the logical implication. The glosses clarify that the verse (Quran 21:22) is a decisive proof, and that denying this argument approaches disbelief. Finally, the passage explains the verse: if there were multiple gods, the universe could not have been created because joint action would be either combined (implying each lacks full power) or by one alone (making the other superfluous or weak). Thus non-existence of the world would follow, which is false. So the verse literally means the heavens and earth would not exist at all.

The Impossibility of God's Origination: Infinite Regress and Circularity
Sanusi argues that God's origination is impossible via two logical absurdities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why attributing origination to God leads to logical absurdities—either infinite regress or circularity—and why both are rationally impossible.

Core Idea
If God were originated, we would face either an endless chain or a vicious circle—both impossible.

Theologians argue that God's eternity is necessary because any alternative leads to logical contradictions. In this passage, Sanusi explains that if God were originated, the chain of causes would either continue infinitely (infinite regress) or loop back on itself (circularity). Both are deemed impossible by rational proof, reinforcing the necessity of God's eternal existence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without pausing, then read the literal translation. Let the logical structure emerge naturally.
Pay attention to the two conditional paths: 'if the number continues' and 'if it does not continue'.
2
Identify the two consequences
The passage presents two possible outcomes of God's origination: an infinite regress (tasalsul) if the chain continues, or circularity (dawr) if the chain returns to the start.
Infinite regress is an unending sequence; circularity is a closed loop where the first becomes later.
3
Understand the manifest impossibility of circularity
Circularity is described as 'al-mustahil al-munjali'—a manifest impossibility—because it makes the first later and the later first, which is clearly absurd.
The clarity of its proof makes it 'manifest' (munjali).
4
Connect to the previous proof
The commentator links back to the proof of mutual hindrance (al-tamānu'), which established the impossibility of multiple gods. This earlier proof also grounds the impossibility of infinite regress and circularity.
The phrase 'as has passed' refers to that earlier argument.
5
Reflect on the theological implication
Since both infinite regress and circularity are impossible, the premise that God is originated must be false. Therefore, God must be eternal (qadīm), not originated.
This is a classic negative proof: rejecting absurd consequences to affirm the opposite.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
God is the free agent who wills to act or refrain, unlike nature or cause.

This lesson lays the foundation by clarifying the correct understanding of God's agency. Al-Sawi defines the 'free agent' (الفاعل المختار) in contrast to the philosophers' claims that God acts by nature or by cause. This distinction is crucial because it affects how we understand creation, divine attributes, and the world's relationship to God.

2
Contrast with false views
Al-Sawi contrasts the free agent with two false views: agent by nature (فاعل بالطبع) and agent by cause (فاعل بالعلة). Understand why these are inadequate for God.
3
Identify the consequences
The passage shows that the philosophers' view leads to the eternity of the world and denial of God's essential attributes. This is declared false and disbelief.

The Diversity of Creation as Proof of Divine Will
Al-Sawi presents the diversity of the world as proof against necessary causation and for divine free agency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the diversity in the world—different kinds, qualities, and forms—cannot arise from a single necessary cause, and thus proves that God is a free agent who creates as He wills.

Core Idea

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The shahada is just a name without theological depth.
Correction: This passage shows that the name 'Allah' and the shahada encompass the entire Islamic creed.

Key Takeaway
The name 'Allah' as Necessary Existent and Creator encapsulates all divine attributes and the world's contingency.
