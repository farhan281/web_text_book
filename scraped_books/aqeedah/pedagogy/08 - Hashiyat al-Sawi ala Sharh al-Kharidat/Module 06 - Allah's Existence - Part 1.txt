Proof of the World's Temporality and God's Necessary Existence
Proof of world's temporality and God's necessary existence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the classical kalam proof that the world is temporally originated (hadith) because change occurs in it, and that existence is a necessary attribute of God because every effect points to an effecter.

Core Idea
Change proves the world is created; every effect demands a necessary Cause.

This passage from Sanusi's Kharida presents the foundational argument for the temporality of the world. It uses the observable fact of change to establish that the world is not eternal, and then moves to show that existence is an essential attribute of the Creator. The reasoning is concise but powerful, forming the basis of Islamic cosmological arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the compact style: each phrase carries a logical step.
2
Identify the two main claims
The passage makes two assertions: (1) The world is temporally originated (hadith) because change occurs in it. (2) Existence (wujud) is a necessary attribute of God, because every effect points to an effecter.
These are two parts of a single cosmological argument.
3
Analyse the proof for temporality
Focus on the first claim. The evidence is 'change occurs in it' (qama bihi al-taghayyur). Why does change imply temporality? Because anything that changes cannot be eternal (qadim); it must have a beginning.
Change is observable in the world – bodies move, transform, decay.
4
Analyse the proof for necessary existence
Now examine the second claim. The text says 'every effect points to an effecter' (kullu atharin yahdi ila mu'aththirin). Since the world is an effect (being temporal and dependent), it requires a cause that must exist necessarily.
This is the classic cosmological argument from contingency.
5
Consider the logical flow and avoid misunderstanding
Trace the full argument: change → temporality → dependency → cause → necessary existence. Note that the proof is not circular; it proceeds stepwise from observation to conclusion.
Common misunderstanding is to see it as circular; careful reading shows otherwise.

Commentary
This passage presents the classical kalam proof for the temporality of the world. It states that the world (everything other than God) is hadith (temporally originated) and muftaqir (dependent). The proof is the presence of change (taghayyur) in the world. Whatever changes is temporally originated. It then defines hadith as existence after non-existence, and its opposite is qidam (pre-eternity). Then it asserts that existence (wujud) is a necessary attribute of God, because every effect (athar) points to an effecter (mu'aththir). This is a concise cosmological argument.

The Status of the Name 'Allah': Common Noun or Proper Noun?
Al-Sawi presents al-Baydawi's claim, refutes it, then raises and partially answers an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the debate over whether 'Allah' is a common noun or a proper noun, and why the shahada requires it to be a proper noun for tawheed.

Core Idea
'Allah' must be a proper noun for the shahada to establish tawheed.

The nature of the name 'Allah' has been debated among theologians. Is it a common noun describing an attribute, or a proper noun referring to a specific being? This passage presents arguments and counter-arguments, highlighting the theological stakes for the declaration of faith.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's argumentative flow come through before you interpret.
Notice that the passage contains a claim, refutation, objection, and partial answer.
2
Understand al-Baydawi's claim
Al-Baydawi claims 'Allah' is a common noun (kulli) meaning 'the one truly worshipped'. Since only the Creator fits this, it becomes an attribute.
This makes 'Allah' an attribute rather than a proper name.
3
Examine the refutation using the shahada
The author refutes that if 'Allah' were common, 'la ilaha illa Allah' would not restrict His essence as a particular being, thus failing to achieve tawheed. The Lawgiver intended it as tawheed, so it must be a proper noun.
This is the key theological argument against al-Baydawi.
4
Consider the objection and partial answer
An objection is raised: a proper noun must be coined for a specific person known to the coiner, but Allah's essence without attributes is inconceivable to humans. The answer from al-Shihab begins: if the coiner is Allah, He knows His essence; if human, the human need only conceive the named thing in some way. The answer is cut off.
The argument is incomplete, but the core idea is that no impossibility arises.

Commentary
This passage presents an argument and counter-argument regarding the nature of the name 'Allah'. It begins with al-Baydawi's claim that 'Allah' is a common noun (kulli) meaning 'the one truly worshipped', and because only the Creator fits this description, it is actually an attribute. The author then refutes this: if it were common, the shahada 'la ilaha illa Allah' would not achieve tawheed (monotheism), because it would not pin down the essence as a particular being. The Lawgiver intended it as tawheed, so it cannot be common. Then an objection from al-Sayyid 'Isa al-Safawi is raised: a proper noun must be coined for a specific person known to the coiner, but Allah's essence without attributes is inconceivable to humans, so how can humans coin a proper noun for it? The answer is cut off, but the point is that there is no impossibility.

The Philosophers' Theory of Emanation
Detailed exposition of the philosophers' emanation scheme.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' theory of emanation, including the generation of the first intellect and the subsequent chain of spheres and intellects.

Core Idea
The philosophers imagined creation as an eternal cascade of intellects and spheres.

The philosophers attempt to explain the origin of the universe through a theory of emanation, where from the First Intellect, spheres and intellects emanate in a chain. This passage details that process, culminating in ten intellects and nine spheres. It is important to understand this theory as a precursor to the Sunni refutation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the starting point
The passage begins by defining doubt (shakk) as hesitation and identifies it as the first intellect. Notice how the first intellect is described as originated in itself but eternal by its cause.
This sets the foundation for the entire emanation chain.
2
Trace the emanation process
Follow how from the first intellect, considering different aspects, arises a second sphere (the Footstool) and a third intellect. Then continue observing the pattern: each subsequent intellect gives rise to a sphere and the next intellect.
The pattern repeats until nine spheres and ten intellects are formed.
3
Identify the final elements
Note the description of the tenth intellect as the 'bestowing intellect' (al-ʿaql al-fayyāḍ) and the claim that spheres and intellects are originated in essence but eternal in time, coexisting with their cause.
This claim is crucial for the subsequent refutation.
