The Rational Argument against Obligating al-Aṣlaḥ on God
Al-Sawi states the Mu'tazilite doctrine, its proof, and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite argument that God must do what is most beneficial (al-aṣlaḥ) and why the Ash'ari response rejects this as a violation of divine free will.

Core Idea
God's actions are not subject to rational obligation; He freely chooses what is best.

The Mu'tazilites held that reason compels God to always do what is most beneficial for His creatures, otherwise His actions would imply imperfection. Al-Sawi presents this claim and its strongest proof—that abandoning the most beneficial would entail absurdity—then points out its fatal flaw: it negates God's unilateral choice and rests on a defective philosophy. This lesson explores the rational foundation of the debate.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazilite claim
Read the first part of the passage carefully. Note how the author defines the Mu'tazilite doctrine: that 'al-aṣlaḥ' means God must produce the most beneficial to the point of being unable to abandon it.
The phrase 'لزوم صدور الأصلح عنه' is key.
2
Identify the strongest proof
Find where the author states the Mu'tazilite proof: that abandoning al-aṣlaḥ would entail absurdities like foolishness, ignorance, frivolity, or stinginess.
These four absurdities are enumerated.
3
Understand the refutation
Notice how the author dismisses the argument: it rejects the principle of choice (ikhtiyār) and is based on a flawed philosophy. Reflect on why imposing rational necessity on God is problematic.
The footnote explains 'rational obligation' (al-wujūb al-ʿaqlī) – what cannot be absent in the mind.

Commentary
This passage is an argument refuting the Mu'tazilite doctrine that it is rationally obligatory (wājib ʿaqlī) for God to do what is most beneficial (al-aṣlaḥ) for His creatures. The author begins by stating the Mu'tazilite claim: the meaning of al-aṣlaḥ is that God must necessarily produce the most beneficial, to the point that He cannot abandon it; otherwise obligation has no meaning. Their strongest proof is that abandoning the most beneficial would entail something impossible in God, such as foolishness, ignorance, frivolity, or stinginess. The author then notes that this clearly rejects the principle of choice (ikhtiyār) and is based on a philosophy that is manifestly flawed. Next, the famous story of al-Ash'ari and al-Jubba'i is presented as a reductio ad absurdum. Al-Ash'ari poses a case of three brothers: one obedient, one disobedient, and one who dies as a child. Al-Jubba'i gives the standard Mu'tazilite answer: the first is rewarded, the second punished, and the third neither. Al-Ash'ari then asks what the third brother would say: if he complains that he was not kept alive to obey and earn reward, al-Jubba'i responds that God knew he would disobey if he grew up, so death was more beneficial. Al-Ash'ari then turns to the second brother: if he complains that he was not killed as a child to avoid sin and punishment, what would God say? Al-Jubba'i is speechless. This shows the inconsistency: the same reasoning that justifies the third's death would also justify the second's death, but that would lead to contradictory judgments. Al-Ash'ari's reply about the donkey stopping is a polite way of saying the argument exposed a flaw. The conclusion: al-Ash'ari abandoned Mu'tazilism and devoted himself to refuting it, establishing Sunni doctrine. The two footnotes (qawluhu) explain that 'necessary occurrence' means rational obligation (al-wujūb al-ʿaqlī) which cannot be absent in the mind, and 'manifest in defect' means flaw.

The Ash'ari-Jubba'i Story: A Reductio ad Absurdum
Al-Sawi narrates the famous debate between al-Ash'ari and al-Jubba'i on al-aṣlaḥ.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari used the example of three brothers to expose the inconsistency of the Mu'tazilite doctrine of al-aṣlaḥ.

Core Idea
The story of the three brothers demonstrates that the Mu'tazilite principle of al-aṣlaḥ leads to contradictory and absurd conclusions.

The most famous episode in the Ash'ari-Mu'tazili debate is the fictional dialogue between Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari and his teacher Abu Hashim al-Jubba'i. By posing a simple case of three brothers with different fates, al-Ash'ari forces al-Jubba'i into a corner, showing that the same reasoning that justifies the death of a child also would exonerate the sinner, which the Mu'tazila themselves reject. This story illustrates the dialectical refutation of wujūb al-aṣlaḥ.

Guided Path
1
Read the story completely
Read the Arabic passage once without pausing. Then read the translation. Pay attention to the dialogue structure.
Notice how al-Ash'ari sets up the scenario with three brothers.
2
Trace the logical trap
Follow al-Ash'ari's two questions. The first question leads to al-Jubba'i's answer based on God's foreknowledge. The second question applies the same logic to the disobedient brother.
The second question creates a contradiction: the same reasoning cannot justify both outcomes.
3
Understand the conclusion
Al-Jubba'i's silence and his outburst show the argument's power. Reflect on why al-Ash'ari abandoned Mu'tazilism after this.
The story marks a turning point in Islamic theology.

Affirming the Beatific Vision of Allah
Al-Sawi affirms the vision of Allah and explains its nature as complete unveiling by sight

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni doctrine of the beatific vision as a religious obligation and its nature as a complete unveiling by sight that does not entail physicality.

Core Idea
The believers will see Allah on the Day of Resurrection—a real but non-encompassing vision.

This passage from al-Sawi's hashia affirms the reality of seeing Allah in the afterlife. It clarifies that this belief is a religious obligation, defines the vision as complete unveiling by sight, and distinguishes it from knowledge. The commentary also explains the spiritual fatherhood of the Prophet and the non-corporeal nature of the vision.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal affirmation and explanation of the Sunni belief that the believers will see Allah on the Day of Resurrection. The author begins by stating the ruling: it is an obligation (wājib) in Islam to affirm this vision with certainty. The phrase 'the father through whom we exited from darkness to light' refers to the Prophet Muhammad, who is the spiritual father of the believers, as indicated by Quran 33:6. The vision is defined as 'complete unveiling by sight' (al-inkishāf al-tāmm bil-baṣar), meaning that the believers will see Allah with their eyes in the Hereafter, but not in a physical or encompassing manner. The marginal notes clarify: the obligation here is religious (sharʿī), not rational—meaning that belief is rewarded and denial punished. The relationship of the Prophet to believers is like a root to branches, united by Islam; the Prophet is exalted above being a physical body. Finally, the nature of the vision is compared to knowledge: unveiling by sight is more perfect than unveiling by knowledge, though neither encompasses Allah. Ibn al-ʿArabī is quoted to say that the vision strengthens the knowledge of God acquired in this world, as seeing is more powerful than hearing. The passage thus defends the reality of ru'ya while upholding divine transcendence.

The Prophetic Permission for Poetic Defense and Condemnation of Deniers
Ibn al-Munir's poetic condemnation of ru'ya deniers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the Ash'ari tradition allows using poetry to defend theological truths, and that denying the vision of Allah is a serious error.

Core Idea
Poetry can be a weapon for truth, and denying ru'ya places one in peril.

The passage begins by justifying the use of poetry in theological polemics, citing the Prophet's permission to Hassan ibn Thabit. Then it launches a sharp condemnation of those who deny the vision of Allah in the Hereafter, warning them of severe consequences.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening justification for poetry
Read the lines where Ibn al-Munir cites the Prophet's permission for Hassan ibn Thabit to engage in satire. Reflect on why this precedent is invoked.
The Prophet's permission establishes that poetry can be used in defense of truth.
2
Identify the warning against deniers
In Ibn al-Munir's verses, note how he describes those who deny ru'ya: they disbelieve in Allah's promise and are on the brink of hellfire.
3
Reflect on the seriousness of denying ru'ya
Consider how the poet equates denial of ru'ya with disbelief in Allah's promise. This shows the high stakes of this theological issue.
This connects to the learning objective.

Discourse on the vision of Allah, the Exalted. Ibn al-Munir said: When he moved to satire, the Prophet (peace be upon him) permitted Hassan (ibn Thabit) to do so, and we follow his example, saying: A group disbelieved in the vision of their Lord—this is the promise of Allah, which He does not break—and they called themselves the saved ones; no, if they are not in the blazing fire, they are on the brink. Abu Hayyan said: Ignorantly, the heart of the nation of Ahmad (the Prophet) and those with insight were likened to hobbled donkeys. Loss is incumbent upon you, so look fairly at the verse of al-A'raf (7:143), for it is just. O denier of the servants' sight of their Lord, Allah has established it by the text of His Book—for those in Paradise, without modality (bi-lā kayf). Reason permits it with the light of knowledge, and its proof is apparent to those with insight, and the just-minded have acknowledged it. It is the analogy (qiyas) on the existence of our God, and all agree that it is by the principle of bi-lā kayf. So decisively affirm permissibility, and do not be obstinate and approve the speech of the foolish. Inform about it where the analogy is consistent, and relieve your heart from the trouble of sophistry. Or leave both (reason and revelation) and follow desire, and you will be led to destructive pitfalls. You will be counted in this world among its intelligent as the dumbest of the foolish, like hobbled donkeys. And by this will be the requital of your like, destruction, but with sharpened swords. This is my creed, I do not incline to another, and it is correct and not adorned (with false embellishment). The composer of these verses said: This is what Allah has opened (to me), and whoever has a stronger answer to it, let him bring it, and he will have the reward.

Commentary
This passage presents a poetic defense of the Ash'ari doctrine of the vision of Allah (ru'ya) in the Hereafter, citing verses from Ibn al-Munir and Abu Hayyan. The author begins by establishing the permissibility of using poetry in defense of truth, citing the Prophet's permission for Hassan ibn Thabit in satire. Then, Ibn al-Munir's verses condemn those who deny ru'ya, calling them disbelievers in the promise of Allah. He warns that such deniers, even if they claim to be saved, are either in hellfire or on its brink. Abu Hayyan's verses follow, criticizing those who ignorantly liken the Muslim community to hobbled donkeys for denying ru'ya. He appeals to the Quranic verse (7:143) as a just source, where Moses asks for vision and is shown the mountain. The poet then addresses the denier directly: Allah has affirmed vision by His Book, for believers in Paradise, without modality (bi-lā kayf). Reason also permits it through intuitive knowledge (nur al-ma'rifa), and the proof is clear to the insightful. The proof is a rational analogy (qiyas) from the existence of God: since we believe in God without seeing Him now, we can analogically affirm that He can be seen in the afterlife, without modality. The poet urges decisively accepting permissibility, avoiding obstinacy and the speech of fools. He advises the reader to rest from the toil of sophistry (safsafa). The passage ends with the poet stating that this is his creed, and he challenges anyone with a stronger argument to bring it. The tone is confident and combative, defending the orthodox Ash'ari position.

Quranic and Rational Proofs for the Vision of Allah
Abu Hayyan's poetic argument for ru'ya using Quran and reason.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the permissibility of seeing Allah in the Hereafter is established by both the Quran (verse 7:143) and rational analogy (qiyas) on the existence of God, without modality (bi-lā kayf).

Core Idea
Allah affirms vision in the Quran, and reason confirms it through analogy.

The poet Abu Hayyan takes up the defense, citing the verse of al-A'raf (where Moses seeks vision) as a just proof. He then presents a rational argument: just as we affirm God's existence without seeing Him now, we can affirm that He can be seen in Paradise, without modality (bi-lā kayf).

Guided Path
1
Read Abu Hayyan's critique of deniers
Read the lines where Abu Hayyan criticizes those who deny ru'ya, calling them like hobbled donkeys. Notice the strong language.
2
Identify the Quranic proof from verse 7:143
The poet cites 'آية الأعراف' (the verse of al-A'raf) as a just proof. Recall that this verse describes Moses asking to see Allah and being shown the mountain. This is taken as evidence that vision is possible.
The verse does not explicitly prove ru'ya in the Hereafter, but is used as a basis.
3
Understand the rational analogy (qiyas)
The poet says 'وهو القياس على وجود إلهنا' (it is the analogy on the existence of our God). This means: just as we affirm God's existence without seeing Him now, we can affirm that He can be seen in the afterlife, by analogy.
The analogy is conditional on 'bi-lā kayf' (without modality) to avoid anthropomorphism.
4
Note the condition of bi-lā kayf
The poet emphasizes that the vision is 'ممن في جنة من غير كيف' (for those in Paradise, without modality). This safeguards God's transcendence.
Bi-lā kayf is essential to the Ash'ari position.

Decisive Affirmation and the Creed of the Poet
Concluding exhortation on ru'ya and statement of creed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the Ash'ari position on ru'ya is a matter of certainty, and that one must avoid sophistry and obstinacy, adhering to the clear proofs.

Core Idea
Affirm ru'ya decisively, or risk being led to destruction.

The poet concludes by urging the reader to accept the permissibility of ru'ya without hesitation, warning against following desire and sophistry. He states his own creed firmly and issues a challenge to those who disagree, inviting stronger arguments if any exist.

Guided Path

Read the call to decisive affirmation

Understand the warning against sophistry and desire
The poet warns against 'تعنت' (obstinacy) and 'السفسفة' (sophistry), and says that following desire leads to destruction ('المهاوي المتلفة').
This contrasts the clear proofs with destructive paths.
3
Note the poet's creed and challenge
Read the final lines: 'هذا اعتقادي لا أميل لغيره' (This is my creed, I do not incline to another). Then he challenges anyone with a stronger argument to bring it.
This shows confidence and openness to discourse.

Proof of the Necessity of Trustworthiness for Messengers
Al-Sawi proves that messengers must be trustworthy using a conditional syllogism and interprets verses about forgiveness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical proof that messengers must be trustworthy and how apparent Quranic verses implying sin are interpreted.

Core Idea
Trustworthiness is necessary for messengers; otherwise, forbidden acts would become obedience—a logical impossibility.

This passage presents a rational proof for one of the essential attributes of messengers: trustworthiness (al-amāna). Using a conditional syllogism, the author demonstrates that if messengers could betray God, the very concept of obedience would be contradicted. The passage also addresses Quranic verses that might seem to suggest otherwise, offering three interpretations that preserve the messengers' infallibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and its translation
Read the complete Arabic text once to get a feel for its structure. Then read the literal English translation to ensure you grasp the meaning.
2
Identify the logical syllogism
The author uses an exceptive syllogism (qiyās istithnā'ī). Find the conditional premise and the conclusion. Notice how the argument is structured.
The syllogism is explicitly explained at the end of the passage.
3
Examine the three interpretations of the verses
The passage offers three ways to understand verses like 'That Allah may forgive you your sin' (48:2). List each interpretation and see how they protect the doctrine of trustworthiness.
These are necessary because some might object that the Prophet had sins.
4
Reflect on the importance of this proof
Ask yourself: Why is trustworthiness a necessary attribute? How does this proof strengthen our understanding of prophethood?
This connects to the broader belief in the prophets' infallibility (ʿiṣma).

Commentary
This passage is a proof and explanation regarding the necessity of trustworthiness (al-amāna) for messengers. The author first states the heading: 'What is obligatory regarding the messengers.' Then he gives the logical proof: If it were permissible for messengers to betray God by a forbidden or disliked act, then that act would become obedience—a contradiction. The proof is presented in the form of an exceptive syllogism (qiyās istithnā'ī). The author explains that the syllogism has a conditional conjunctive premise (sharṭiyya muttaṣila) and an implicit exceptive premise. By excepting the contradictory of the consequent, we obtain the contradictory of the antecedent. The structured argument: If they committed treachery (khayāna), then that forbidden or disliked act would become obedience; but it is impossible for an act that is forbidden or disliked to become commanded obedience; therefore, treachery cannot occur. Hence, trustworthiness is necessary. The author then addresses verses that might imply a lack of trustworthiness: 'That Allah may forgive you your sin' and 'We removed your burden.' He gives three interpretations: (1) They refer to the sins and burdens of the Prophet's nation. (2) They refer to the Prophet's own sin on the hypothetical assumption it occurred—meaning, if any sin had occurred, it would be forgiven. (3) 'Burden' refers to the weight of revelation, which was heavy on him, and God lightened it. Finally, the author clarifies the logical structure explicitly for the reader.

Self-Sufficiency Entails Perfect Attributes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how God's absolute self-sufficiency logically entails a chain of attributes including self-subsistence, being unlike creation, freedom from deficiencies, and necessary attributes like hearing, sight, and speech.

God's self-sufficiency logically requires all perfect attributes.

This passage from al-Sawi traces the logical implications of God's absolute self-sufficiency (al-ghina al-mutlaq). Starting from the fact that God is free from all need, the author shows what must follow: that He is unlike contingent beings, that He is self-subsistent, and that He is free from deficiencies. Then he shows how this freedom necessarily entails positive attributes like hearing, sight, and speech, as well as freedom from purposes and the non-necessity of actions. This chain of reasoning demonstrates the coherence of the Islamic concept of God, where one attribute necessarily implies others.

Definition of Qada and Qadar in Al-Ajhuri's Poem
Al-Ajhuri's poem and commentary defining qada and qadar with school differences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definitions of qada and qadar according to the Ash'ari and Maturidi schools as presented in Al-Ajhuri's poem.

Core Idea
Qada is the eternal decree, qadar is the temporal creation of things in accordance with it.

This lesson presents the foundational definitions of divine decree (qada) and predestination (qadar) from a classical theological poem. Al-Ajhuri's concise lines capture the nuanced difference between God's eternal willing and His bringing things into existence. The commentary unpacks these terms and highlights the key disagreement between Ash'aris and Maturidis on whether qadar is created or eternal.

Guided Path
1
Read the poem and translation
Read the Arabic poem and its English translation carefully. Notice the rhyme and the two different definitions offered for qada.
Pay attention to the word 'قضاؤه' (his decree) and 'الإيجاد' (bringing into existence).
2
Identify the two definitions of qada
The poem offers two definitions: qada as God's will attached from pre-eternity, and an alternative as knowledge with attachment. Both are eternal.
The commentary clarifies that some scholars define the first as knowledge.
3
Compare Ash'ari and Maturidi views on qadar
The commentary states that qadar is an attribute of action: created (حادثة) according to Ash'aris, eternal (قديمة) according to Maturidis because it is takwin (bringing into existence).
This is a key theological difference between the two schools.
4
Understand the practical implication
Every destined thing must occur as decreed, so patience and submission are required. This connects theology to practice.
Reflect on the phrase 'فيجب إذن الصبر والتسليم' (so patience and submission are necessary).

Commentary
This passage begins with a heading 'The principles of the path leading to God' and then discusses the definition of al-qadr as a property of action. The commentator al-Ajhuri has composed a poem in rajaz meter to clarify the definitions of qada and qadar according to the Ash'ari and Maturidi schools. The poem states that the will of God is attached in pre-eternity, and that is qada, while qadar is the bringing into existence of things in a specific manner. Some scholars define the first (qada) as knowledge with attachment in pre-eternity, and qadar as bringing into existence in accordance with that knowledge. The commentary explains that every destined thing must occur exactly as foreordained, so patience and submission are obligatory. Then it notes that the Maturidis consider qada to be eternal, while the Ash'aris consider qadar to be created, because it is the act of bringing into existence (takwin). The commentary then explains linguistic points: the word 'علا' (exalted) is used as a past tense verb indicating transcendence, creating a play on words (jinas). It also notes an omission of the conjunction waw in the matn, implying the original phrase included 'and decreed.' The passage concludes with a warning that if one does not patiently accept God's decrees, he will lose both worlds and find no helper.
