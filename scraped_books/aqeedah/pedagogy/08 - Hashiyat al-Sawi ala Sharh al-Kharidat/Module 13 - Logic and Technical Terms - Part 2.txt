Kasb and Iktisab: Human Acquisition vs. Divine Creation
Defines kasb and iktisab as human acquisition distinct from divine creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari distinction between divine creation (ijad/ikhtira') and human acquisition (kasb/iktisab), and why human actions are attributed to us only as acquisition.

Core Idea
Humans acquire actions; God alone creates them.

One of the most subtle topics in Islamic theology is the relationship between divine power and human action. The Ash'ari school solves this by distinguishing between creation, which belongs only to God, and acquisition, which is the human role. In this passage, the author establishes this distinction clearly.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
Read the Arabic text once slowly. Then read the English translation. Notice the contrast between 'إيجاد واختراع' and 'كسب واكتساب'.
The passage is concise but dense. Take your time.
2
Identify the two categories
Find where the passage defines divine creation (attached to eternal power) and where it defines human acquisition (attached to temporal power). Note the key terms: القدرة القديمة and القدرة الحادثة.
These two categories are the heart of the distinction.
3
Understand the key distinction
The passage says human actions are attributed to us only as kasb, not as creation. Why? Because creation is exclusively God's act of bringing something from non-existence. Human power only 'accompanies' the action through choice and intention.
This preserves both God's sole creative power and human moral responsibility.
4
Reflect on the theological significance
Ask yourself: Why does this matter? The answer is that it solves the problem of predestination versus free will without compromising divine omnipotence.
This distinction is a cornerstone of Ash'ari theology.

Commentary
This passage defines the key Ash'ari terms kasb and iktisab, which denote human acquisition of actions as opposed to divine creation. It begins by stating the central claim: actions are attributed to humans only as acquisition, not as creation. The clarification explains why: God's power creates existence from nothing, which is called ijad/ikhtira'. Human power attaches to voluntary actions but only as acquisition — we have choice and intention, but the actual bringing into existence is God's act. Thus kasb/iktisab signifies the human contribution of intention and effort without any creative power.

Milla, Din, and Sharia: Identical in Essence
Al-Sawi defines milla, dīn, and sharīʿa and states the ruling on disbelief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between milla, dīn, and sharīʿa and how they relate to the ruling on disbelief in Islam.

Core Idea
Milla, din, and sharia are three perspectives on one reality: Islamic law.

In this passage, al-Sawi clarifies three key terms that appear frequently in Islamic theological discourse: milla, dīn, and sharīʿa. Although often used interchangeably, each highlights a different aspect of divine legislation. Understanding this distinction is crucial for grasping why certain statements are considered disbelief (kufr) according to the consensus of the Muslim community.

Defining 'Opposite' as Linguistic Incompatibility
Al-Sawi defines 'ḍidd' as linguistic opposite, meaning absolute incompatibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that 'ḍidd' in this context means absolute incompatibility, including both existential and non-existential opposites, and why it is used in discussing divine attributes.

Core Idea
In kalam, 'opposite' means any incompatibility, not just technical opposites like black and white.

Al-Sawi begins by clarifying the sense in which 'opposite' (ḍidd) is used when discussing what is impossible for Allah. He distinguishes between the linguistic opposite, which is any incompatibility, and the technical opposite that will be discussed later. This distinction is crucial because the attributes of Allah are beyond simple binary opposites.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of linguistic opposite
Read the Arabic sentence defining 'ḍidd' as absolute incompatibility. Notice the phrase 'mā yunāfī' (whatever is incompatible).
The term 'linguistic opposite' here is broad.

Contrast with technical opposite
Al-Sawi contrasts 'ḍidd luġhawī' with 'ḍidd iṣṭilāḥī'. The technical opposite is a narrower category. Understand why the broader sense is needed for divine attributes.

This includes both existential and non-existential incompatibilities.

Commentary
This passage provides a definition and classification. It first defines what is meant by 'ḍidd' (opposite) in the context of the impossible attributes of Allah. It is the linguistic opposite, meaning absolute incompatibility, whether the incompatible thing is existential (like white) or non-existential (like blindness). So the intended meaning is that anything incompatible with the mandatory attributes is impossible for Allah, not just the technical opposite (like the relation between white and black). The passage then enumerates the four types of opposition (tanāfuʿ) according to the logicians: (1) contradiction of contradictories (like existence and non-existence), (2) opposites proper (like white and black), (3) privation and possession (like sight and blindness in a subject capable of sight), and (4) relatives (like fatherhood and sonship, where each requires the other for conception). The commentary adds two notes: first, that the phrase 'everything that is incompatible' includes any type of incompatibility, whether it is truly an opposite, a contradictory, or something equal or more specific. Second, it contrasts the logicians' fourfold division with the jurists' (uṣūliyyūn) twofold division, where only contradictories and opposites are recognized, with privation and possession subsumed under contradictories and relatives under opposites. Finally, the passage explains the logical basis for the fourfold enumeration: considering whether the two opposed items are existential or not, and if existential, whether they are interdependent in conception (relatives) or not (opposites); if one is existential and one non-existential, it is privation if the non-existential is a lack in a suitable subject, otherwise it is contradiction. Examples clarify each case.

His statement: (So the one who infers the existence of fire... etc.) is an orderly redistribution and arrangement. His statement: (meaning: ignorance) he indicated by that that what is meant by blindness is spiritual blindness, which is the blunting of insight. His statement: (that supplication benefits) i.e., for what has descended and what has not descended. His statement: (according to the people of truth) i.e., they are Ahl al-Sunna wa al-Jama'a. Commentary
This passage defines the three levels of yaqin (certainty) and uses them to affirm the benefit of du'a (supplication) against the Mu'tazila. It begins by distinguishing three modes of knowing something: mere observation from a distance ('ilm al-yaqin), direct sight ('ayn al-yaqin), and intimate experience (haqq al-yaqin). The example of fire illustrates this: inferring fire from smoke is not like seeing it from afar, and seeing it is not like mixing with it and knowing its fuel. The commentator then explains that the phrase 'al-muzil li-l-'ama' (the remover of blindness) refers to ignorance (jahl), i.e., spiritual blindness. The author's statement indicates that du'a is beneficial, a point on which there is no doubt among Ahl al-Haqq (i.e., Ahl al-Sunna), and the Qur'an and Sunna are filled with it. This is contrary to the Mu'tazila, who deny the benefit of du'a, arguing that whatever Allah has decreed will happen regardless. They interpret the du'a mentioned in the Qur'an as merely worship, not a means of altering decree. The passage then cites two Qur'anic verses to illustrate the higher levels of yaqin. The commentator clarifies that the example of fire is a well-ordered analogy (laf wa nashr murattab). He also explains that the du'a benefits both for what has been decreed and what has not, and notes that Ahl al-Sunna are the people of truth.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Du'a cannot change what Allah has decreed, so it is useless.
Correction: Du'a is itself decreed by Allah and is a means by which His decree is realized; therefore it is beneficial.

Key Takeaway
The three levels of certainty illustrate the depth of knowledge, and du'a is an integral part of divine decree, not an attempt to change it.
