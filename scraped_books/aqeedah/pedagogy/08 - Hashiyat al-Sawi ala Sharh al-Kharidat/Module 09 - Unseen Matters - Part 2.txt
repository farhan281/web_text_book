Details of the Balance and Weighing of Deeds
Al-Sawi provides details about the Balance's nature and the weighing process.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the Balance is one for all nations, its description with two pans, how deeds are weighed, and why the deeds of disbelievers are also weighed.

Core Idea
The single Balance has luminous and dark pans; deeds of believers and disbelievers are weighed differently.

After establishing the reality of the Balance, Al-Sawi provides further details: there is only one scale for all nations (the plural in the Qur'an is for glorification), it has two pans (luminous for good deeds, dark for evil), and even the deeds of disbelievers are weighed for specific purposes. This lesson unpacks these specifics, showing the comprehensive nature of divine justice.

Guided Path
1
Affirm the oneness of the Balance
Read from 'والصَّحيحُ أنّه ميزان واحد' to 'للتعظيم'. Notice that the plural 'موازين' is interpreted as glorification, not multiple scales.
This opposes the view that there are many scales for different deeds.
2
Understand the physical description
Read 'وإنَّ خِفَّة الموزون وثقله على صورته في الدُّنيا' and the commentary on two pans: one luminous for good, one dark for evil. Light deeds float up, heavy deeds fall.
This literal description underscores the physical reality of the scale.
3
Examine the weighing of disbelievers' deeds
Read the final part from 'وإنَّ الكفَّار توزن أعمالهم'. Identify that their deeds are weighed to increase punishment for sins and to lighten punishment for good deeds (like freeing slaves), but not for salvation from disbelief.
This shows divine justice: even disbelievers benefit from good deeds in this world's terms.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal exposition of the Islamic belief in al-Mizan (the Balance) on Judgment Day, combining definition, proof, refutation of opposing views, and detailed explanation. The author begins by stating the subject: 'Faith in the Balance.' He then defines it as a scale placed before the Sirat (the bridge) by which the deeds of servants are weighed. The proof is given from both the Qur'an (multiple verses) and the Sunnah, whose hadiths have reached tawatur (mass transmission). He notes that it is 'reported' (ward), meaning it is established. Next, he addresses the position of the Mu'tazila, who deny the Balance or reinterpret it. He quotes a comment: 'Contrary to the Mu'tazila' and explains that they say it does not exist (some deny it outright) or that it does not exist now but will exist at the time of need. This division shows their inconsistent stance. The author then asserts the Sunni position: carrying the texts upon their literal meaning (al-hamlu 'ala al-haqiqah) is possible, so it is obligatory to believe in the Balance as a real entity, even if we do not know the exact nature (haqiqah) of its substance. He condemns the Mu'tazila interpretation as 'complete justice' (tamām al-'adl) as 'obstinacy and arrogance' (inād wa mukābarah), because when a literal meaning is possible, resorting to metaphorical interpretation (ta'wīl) is unnecessary and forced. He then clarifies that the correct view is that it is one scale for all nations and all deeds; the plural form in the Qur'anic verse 'We shall set up the scales' is for glorification, not to indicate multiple scales. Further details are given: the lightness and heaviness of the deeds correspond to their form in this world (i.e., the actual nature of the deeds). The deeds of disbelievers are also weighed, but for a different purpose: their evil deeds (other than disbelief) are weighed to increase their punishment, while their good deeds that do not require intention (like freeing slaves, endowments, maintaining family ties) are weighed to reduce punishment for non-disbelief (but not for salvation from the punishment of disbelief itself). The author also provides a physical description: the scale has two pans, one luminous for good deeds and one dark for evil deeds, and light things float up while heavy ones fall. This confirms the literal nature of the Balance.
