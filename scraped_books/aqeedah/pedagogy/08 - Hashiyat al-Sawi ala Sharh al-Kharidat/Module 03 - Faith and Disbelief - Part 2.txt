Commentary
This passage provides a detailed analysis of the first testimony, 'La ilaha illallah'. The author begins by situating this testimony within the broader context of the rulings of theology, prophethood, and heard matters, stating that all these are encapsulated in two sentences, the first being 'La ilaha illallah'. He defines 'ilah' as the one worshipped with right (al-ma'bud bi-haqq), so the full meaning is 'no worshipped with right except Allah'. The grammatical discussion follows: the word 'Allah' may be in the accusative (nasb) or nominative (raf'), but the preferred is the nominative, as per Ibn Malik's Alfiyyah. Then the passage addresses the nature of the exception (istithna) in this statement. It is classified as 'general made specific' (al-amm al-makhsus), where the generality is intended in the wording but not in the meaning. Thus, the exception is continuous (muttasil) because the word 'Allah' is included in the general negation but is excluded in meaning. The author emphasizes that this is a case of 'generality of negation' (umum al-salab) and not 'negation of generality' (salab al-umum), otherwise the exception would be discontinuous (munqati'), which is incorrect. Next, the accurate meaning of 'la ma'bud bi-haqq' is explained: what is negated is any being worshipped with right other than Allah, both in the believer's mind and in objective reality. This negation does not apply to the disbeliever's mind, where false deities are considered worthy. The believer's statement reports his inner conviction and reality, ignoring the disbelievers' false beliefs. Finally, the dropping of the nunation on 'ma'bud' is noted as a stylistic harmony with 'ilah'; otherwise it would be in the accusative due to resemblance to a construct phrase. The passage thus clarifies both the linguistic and theological nuances of the first testimony.

Commentary

Finally, the passage provides a list of hadiths that extol the virtue of the shahada. Each hadith highlights a different benefit: it is the best remembrance, it should be increased before death, it secures one from the Fire, it renews faith, it is the key to the heavens, it leads to a raised status on the Day of Judgment with a luminous face, and if said sincerely, it opens the doors of heaven and reaches the Throne, provided major sins are avoided. The author notes that these hadiths are why the Sufi masters chose the shahada as their primary dhikr in the spiritual path to Allah.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of shukr (thankfulness to God). The author defines shukr as using every blessing God has given (intellect, hearing, sight, tongue, etc.) for the purpose for which it was created. He then explains that the word 'shakuran' in the original text means 'much thankful', and shukr reduces to three elements: (1) belief in the heart that all blessings come from God alone, (2) service with the limbs by performing all acts of obedience (obligatory and recommended), and (3) speech with the tongue, such as saying 'la ilaha illa Allah' and other remembrances. The commentary then links each part of the definition to its category: 'that he believes' refers to belief in the heart, 'he speaks with his tongue' refers to speech, and 'he works with his limbs' refers to service. The passage then discusses the endless nature of shukr: one must even be thankful for the ability to be thankful, and for success in repentance. This leads to the conclusion that true complete shukr is impossible, as the Prophet indicated in his supplication. Because it is the path of the truthful (prophets and major saints), few attain it, as God says in the Quran. The author includes a poetic example illustrating the infinite cycle of gratitude, and concludes with the famous hadith of the Prophet expressing his gratitude.

Commentary
This passage presents a specific supplication (du'a) and its commentary. The supplication asks Allah to seal one's deeds, states, and life with good, so that death occurs in the best state of tawhid, with longing and desire for Allah. It asks that Allah take the soul directly without intermediary angels, replace sins with good deeds, and help at times of difficulty. It concludes with a verse from the Qur'an (Q3:53) asking to be written among the witnesses. The commentator then explains the phrase 'يا رحيم' as addressing Allah as 'O Most Compassionate', and notes that it alludes to the hadith that the merciful are shown mercy by the Most Merciful, and that showing mercy to those on earth results in mercy from heaven. He further explains that 'واقبض أرواحنا بيدك' means not seeing the angel of death, indicating reliance on Allah directly. 'عند العثرات' refers to times of hardship. He also notes another hadith that when the servant says 'يا أرحم الراحمين', Allah responds acknowledging His mercy and inviting him to ask. Finally, he comments on the phrase 'يرحمكم من في السماء' suggesting the possibility that 'من' (who) refers to Allah, but the text cuts off. The lesson emphasizes the importance of supplication for a good end and the virtue of calling upon Allah by His compassionate attributes.
