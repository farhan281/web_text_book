The Worldly Benefit of Creed
Benefit of creed for worldly actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why creed is necessary for the correctness and success of all actions, both religious and worldly.

Core Idea
Creed is the foundation of all successful action.

This lesson explores the first part of Shaykh al-Hashimi's answer to a question about the value of studying creed. He explains that creed is not only a religious requirement but a practical necessity for any meaningful endeavor, providing the motivation and unity of purpose needed for success.

Guided Path
1
Read the question and initial answer
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice that the answer begins by stating the benefit in three dimensions: legally, customarily, and rationally.
2
Distinguish creed from tawhid
The answer distinguishes between creed (a condition for starting an action) and tawhid (a condition for its success). Understand this key distinction.
Tawhid here means unity of purpose and leadership, not just theological monotheism.
3
Reflect on the examples
The author gives concrete examples: ships, cars, airplanes, etc. Consider why unity of goal and leadership is essential for any collective endeavor.
These examples show the universal applicability of the principle.
4
Connect to your own experience
Think of a personal or observed situation where lack of unity led to failure. How does this align with the author's claim?

To proceed:
My master, the scholar, the knower of Allah, Muhammad al-Hashimi (may Allah have mercy on him and be pleased with him) was asked about the fruit that the reader of the science of Islamic creed reaps and what is the calamity that befalls him from not reading it?

Thus, the safety of a ship, car, airplane, kingdom, school, factory, town, or house is not possible without unity of goal and leadership, nor can an action succeed whose leaders and goals are multiple.
As for the calamity that befalls the one lacking creed in something by which he seeks to obtain benefit or repel harm: it is perpetual confusion, regret, disappointment, hesitation, and whispers; he is unable to commence an action with determination and resolve, and if he commences, he is unable to persevere in it.

Commentary
This passage is a question-and-answer exchange. The questioner asks Shaykh Muhammad al-Hashimi about the benefit (thamara) of studying Islamic creed and the harm (wabāl) of neglecting it. The answer proceeds in two parts.

First, the benefit: The shaykh states that according to scholars and all rational people, the worldly benefit of creed is the correctness (ṣiḥḥa) and success (najāḥ) of actions—legally (sharʿan), customarily (ʿādatan), and rationally (ʿaqlan). He then explains that creed, whether true or false, is a condition (sharṭ) for beginning any action. Monotheism (tawḥīd) is a condition for the success of that action. Creed is a strong motivator, but monotheism ensures unified direction. He illustrates this with concrete examples: ships, cars, airplanes, kingdoms, schools, factories, towns, and houses all require unity of goal and leadership to function properly. Multiplicity of leaders and goals leads to failure.

Second, the harm: The one lacking creed suffers perpetual confusion (ḥayra), regret (ḥasra), disappointment (khaība), hesitation (taraddud), and whispers (wasāwis). Such a person cannot begin any action with determination and resolve, and if he does begin, he cannot persevere. Thus, creed is presented as essential not only for religious acts but for all meaningful human endeavor.

The Harm of Neglecting Creed
Harm of lacking creed.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Without creed, life is perpetual confusion and failure.

This lesson addresses the second part of Shaykh al-Hashimi's answer, which describes the calamity of being without a correct creed. He lists the psychological harms that prevent a person from starting or persisting in any action with determination.

Guided Path
1
Read the harm section carefully
Read the Arabic passage and translation. Note that it begins with 'As for the calamity...' and describes both internal states and practical inability.
2
Identify the specific harms
The passage lists five harms: perpetual confusion, regret, disappointment, hesitation, and whispers. Understand each term.
These are psychological and spiritual ailments.
3
Understand the practical consequence
The harm is not just internal; it prevents a person from beginning actions with determination and, if started, from persevering.
This connects to the earlier point about creed being a motivator.
4
Contrast with the benefit
Compare this description to the benefit described in the previous lesson. How does lacking creed lead to the opposite of correctness and success?

The Relative Pronoun in Praise: Grammar and Theology
Al-Sawi explains the opening phrase, analyzes 'al-ladhi', and answers an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the grammatical and theological justification for using the relative pronoun 'al-ladhi' to describe Allah in opening supplications.

Core Idea
The relative pronoun in praise is valid when interpreted as derived.

Al-Sawi begins by deferring the discussion of basmala and hamdala to later, then praises the opening phrase 'al-ladhi nawwara...' as a clever beginning. He then delves into the grammatical nature of 'al-ladhi', revealing a subtle debate among scholars. This lesson unpacks that debate and shows how a grammatical objection is resolved through interpretation (ta'wil).

Guided Path
1
Understand the context of the opening phrase
Notice that al-Sawi first sets aside the explanation of basmala and hamdala for later, before focusing on the relative pronoun phrase. This shows his systematic approach.
2
Analyze the definition of barā‘at maṭla‘
Al-Sawi praises the phrase as a good opening. Understand that barā‘at maṭla‘ means the author hints at his main objective from the very beginning.
This is a rhetorical device, not purely grammatical.
3
Examine the grammatical nature of 'al-ladhi'
Al-Sawi presents the classification of the relative pronoun as partitive (juz’ī) or universal (kullī) according to different scholars. Compare the views of al-‘Aḍud and al-Sayyid with that of al-Sa‘d.
This is a technical grammatical debate.
4
Resolve the objection regarding derived vs. non-derived terms
The objection claims that since 'al-ladhi' is non-derived (jāmid), it cannot be a descriptive term (na‘t). The answer is that it is interpreted (mu’awwal) as a derived term, making it valid.
This shows how grammatical rules can be applied flexibly in theological contexts.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from al-Sawi on the opening words of al-Dardir's Sharh al-Kharida. First, al-Sawi notes that the explanation of the basmala and hamdala will be provided later when the commentator addresses them. Then he focuses on the phrase 'al-ladhi nawwara qulūbanā...' (the One who illuminated our hearts). He praises it as a good opening (ḥusn iftitāḥ) and an excellent beginning (barā‘at maṭla‘), meaning the author hints at his purpose from the start. Next, he analyzes the relative pronoun 'al-ladhi': grammatically, it is a relative noun (ism mawṣūl). According to al-‘Aḍud and al-Sayyid al-Sharīf, it is partitive (juz’ī) in both its form (waḍ‘an) and usage (isti‘mālan). However, al-Sa‘d al-Taftāzānī holds that it is universal (kullī) in form but partitive in usage. The relative pronoun is used to enable the speaker to describe known objects (ma‘ārif) by clauses. Al-Sawi then states the rule that the clausal part (the relative clause) should be known in its relation to the addressee. Here, 'al-ladhi' is an attribute of Allah due to its connection (as in the Quranic occurrence). It is used for praise and to convey the purpose: that Allah alone deserves praise and His favors necessitate praise. An objection arises: a descriptive term (na‘t) must be derived (mushtaqq), but 'al-ladhi' is non-derived (jāmid), so it cannot be used as a descriptive term. The answer is that it is interpreted (mu’awwal) as a derived term, meaning 'the One described as being light...' etc. The footnotes clarify: (1) The 'clause connected' means the clause is the connection (ṣila). (2) A Quranic example: 'Praise be to God who sent down to His servant the Book' (18:1).

Derived Terms Indicate Causation
Commentary on derived term and objection-answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the use of a derived term (al-mustaq) in a theological statement indicates causation, and how the objection that this restricts praise is answered.

Core Idea
Derived terms show causation, not limitation.

In this passage, the commentator unpacks a subtle grammatical point about the phrase 'al-ḥamdu lillāh.' He explains that the use of a derived term indicates that the attribute from which it is derived is the cause of the judgment. This triggers an objection—doesn't this restrict praise to that attribute? The answer preserves the full scope of praise while explaining the role of the specific attribute.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage carefully, then read the literal translation. Notice that the passage begins mid-sentence, continuing from a previous discussion.
The word 'الشيخ' refers to the author of the original text, al-Kharida.
2
Identify the grammatical claim
The commentator states that attaching a judgment to a derived term indicates causation (ʿilliyya). Understand this grammatical principle.
3
Understand the objection
The objector argues that using a derived term would restrict praise to that specific attribute, whereas Allah deserves praise for His essence and all attributes.
4
See the answer
The commentator clarifies that the attribute 'enlightening' is not the cause of deserving praise, but the cause of the Shaykh's informing us that Allah deserves all praise.
This preserves the universal praise of Allah.
5
Reflect on the lesson
Consider how grammatical analysis helps clarify theological statements. The derived term points to a reason for the statement, not a restriction of the attribute.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer discussion about how to interpret the phrase 'Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim'. The commentator mentions that Hafid al-Sa'd (the grandson of al-Sa'd) proposed that the phrase is an allegorical metaphor (isti'ara tamthiliyya), where the entire scene of God's generosity is likened to a king's generosity.
