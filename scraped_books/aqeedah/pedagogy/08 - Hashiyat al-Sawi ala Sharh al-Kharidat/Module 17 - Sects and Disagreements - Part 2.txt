Unpacking the Poem: Envy as the Root of Spiritual Fraud
Al-Sawi provides a grammatical and thematic tafsir of Ibn al-Farid's verse, focusing on envy as the driving force.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the grammatical and thematic explanation of Ibn al-Farid's line, particularly the role of envy in leading people astray.

Core Idea
Envy of true spiritual masters leads some to imitate their outward appearance without inner transformation.

After the general critique, al-Sawi provides a detailed word-by-word commentary on the line of poetry he quoted. This deep dive explains how each phrase connects grammatically and what it means conceptually. The key point is that the people referred to in the poem preferred immediate worldly pleasures (blindness) over guidance due to their envy of the true people of the path. They copied their external appearance but did not perform their inner work.

Guided Path
1
Read the word-by-word explanation
Read the Arabic and translation of the commentary carefully. Notice how al-Sawi links each phrase to the rest of the sentence.
The explanation is technical – focus on the meaning of each key term.
2
Identify the grammatical links
Al-Sawi clarifies that 'concerning my sect' is connected to 'they went astray' and that 'when they preferred' means 'they loved the transient'. Understand these connections.
Grammar here serves to reinforce the meaning.
3
Grasp the root cause: envy
The passage emphasizes that the motivation for choosing blindness over guidance is envy. Note how envy operates: they envy the states and ranks of the true people of the path.
Envy leads them to imitate outwardly but not truly change.
4
Reflect on the outcome
The final phrase 'They adorned themselves with their appearance outwardly, but did not work like their work' sums up the tragedy. What is the lesson for sincere seekers?
This distinction between form and substance is crucial.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a poem by Ibn al-Farid, where al-Sawi explains the meaning and draws a contrast between true and false spiritual seekers. The author begins by stating that the fundamentals of the path to Allah are not what these people practice—rather, they have regressed because they followed their whims and Satan. He then quotes the poem: the poet says that concerning his own way, when people preferred spiritual blindness over guidance out of envy, they went astray. After the citation, al-Sawi elaborates that such individuals demand honor and charity as if it were their right, and they threaten to expose others if not given. They pretend to be people of spiritual states, but their real behavior is corrupt. The true way of the poor (fuqara) is exactly the opposite: humility, brokenness, love of obscurity, chastity, asceticism, scrupulousness, altruism, and trust in Allah. These false ones are the worst of people, consuming wealth falsely, claiming high ranks while being in low depths, and they have multiplied in this age. Then al-Sawi gives a word-by-word commentary of the poem line: 'concerning my sect' is connected to 'they went astray'; 'when they preferred' means they loved the transient over the eternal; 'over guidance' means instead of it; 'out of envy' is for the purpose of showing their envy of the true people of the path, so they imitated their outward appearance without doing their inner work. This lesson is a sharp critique of hypocrisy in spiritual claims.

The Benefit of Supplication and Refutation of Mu'tazila
The commentator states the benefit of supplication and refutes the Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sunni theologians affirm the benefit of supplication despite predestination, and how they refute the Mu'tazila objection.

Core Idea
Supplication is a divinely ordained cause that brings benefit; it does not contradict divine decree.

The commentator explains a phrase from the matn that implies the benefit of du'a. He then contrasts the Sunni position with the Mu'tazila denial. This lesson will unpack that argument, showing how the Quran and Sunnah support the efficacy of prayer, and why the Mu'tazila rationalist objection fails.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it, then read the translation carefully. Notice the flow from explaining 'blindness' to introducing the Mu'tazila disagreement.
2
Identify the two opposing views
Find where the commentator states the position of Ahl al-Haqq and where he mentions the Mu'tazila. Note the evidence each side uses.
3
Understand the Mu'tazila reasoning
The Mu'tazila argue that since Allah's decree is fixed, supplication is unnecessary. Ask yourself: does this contradict the Sunni view that du'a is beneficial? The commentator's implicit answer is no, because du'a is itself part of the decree.
The passage cuts off, but the commentary clarifies the refutation.

Commentary
This passage comments on the phrase 'المزيل للعمى' (the remover of blindness), interpreting 'blindness' as ignorance. The commentator then notes that this implies supplication (دعاء) is beneficial, which is a certainty among Ahl al-Haqq (the people of truth, i.e., Ahl al-Sunna). He supports this by stating that the Quran is filled with supplication and the Sunnah contains more than can be counted. Then he mentions that the Mu'tazila hold the opposite view: they deny the permissibility of supplication, arguing that since everything is predestined by Allah, supplication is unnecessary and ineffective. The commentator's phrase 'ويجب' (and it is necessary) suggests that supplication is obligatory or required, but the sentence is cut off. The passage thus presents the Sunni position affirming the benefit of dua against the rationalist Mu'tazila objection based on predestination.
