Commentary
This passage is a linguistic derivation of the phrase 'الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ' (praise be to God). Al-Sawi explains that the original form was a complete verbal sentence: 'أَحْمَدُ حَمْدَ اللهِ' (I praise a praising for God). The verb is then dropped because the verbal noun (ḥamd) indicates the action. Thus it becomes 'حَمْدَ اللهِ' (with ḥamd in the accusative as a verbal noun). Then the case ending is changed from accusative (naṣb) to nominative (rafʿ) to indicate permanence and continuity (thubūt wa dawām), leading to 'حَمْدُ اللهِ'. The Ruling and Meaning of Knowing Allah
The commentator interprets 'ma'rifa' and cites Quranic verses about Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the knowledge of Allah required in the ruling refers to knowledge of His necessary existence, and how Quranic verses illustrate His attributes.

Core Idea
Knowing Allah means knowing Him as the Necessary Being, as defined by reason and revelation.

The commentator begins by clarifying the meaning of 'ma'rifa' in the context of the obligation to know Allah. He explains that it is not mere linguistic knowledge but a specific theological understanding of Allah's necessary existence. He then provides Quranic verses that describe Allah's essence, speech, actions, attributes, and self, to ground this knowledge in revelation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation to get a sense of the commentator's main points.
Pay attention to the grammatical note about 'an'.
2
Identify the definition of ma'rifa
Find where the commentator defines ma'rifa as 'your knowledge of the necessary (al-wajib)'.
This is the core theological point.
3
Examine the Quranic verses
Notice the five categories: essence (Ikhlas), speech (Be), actions (every day), attributes (First/Last/etc.), and self (nothing like Him).
Each verse addresses a different aspect.
4
Reflect on the purpose
Understand that these verses together show that Allah is known through revelation and reason, and that He is incomparable.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a line from the matn (the original text) regarding the ruling of knowing Allah. The commentator begins by stating that the matn interpreted 'ma'rifa' (knowledge) in a specific sense suited to the context. The matn then gives a grammatical analysis of the word 'an' (that) in the phrase, explaining that despite being nominative, it is treated as a subordinating particle. An example is provided: 'you hear about al-Mu'aydi is better than seeing him,' which is a proverb meaning that hearing about something can be better than seeing it, but here it is applied to the concept that knowing Allah is knowing the necessary (al-wajib). Then several Quranic verses are cited to illustrate different aspects of Allah: Surah al-Ikhlas for His essence, verses about His speech, actions, attributes (First, Last, Manifest, Hidden), and the negation of likeness. The commentator then explains three expressions from the matn: First, the phrase 'and because we are not obligated by that' is analyzed as a conjunction of cause to effect, though the commentator suggests it might be the opposite, an effect to a cause. Second, the phrase 'except that the meaning' is explained: what follows the explicative 'ay' (i.e.) is an appositive (badal) that must be interpreted as a verbal noun to match the preceding explicit verbal noun. Third, the example 'you hear' is parsed as a nominal sentence with a subject and predicate, confirming that the subject must be a noun.

Grammatical Analysis of Commentary Expressions
The commentator explains three grammatical points from the matn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the grammatical analysis of three specific expressions from the matn regarding cause-effect relationships and appositive structures.

Core Idea
The commentator's linguistic precision reveals deeper theological meanings through grammatical analysis.

In this lesson, we examine the commentator's detailed grammatical analysis of three phrases from the original text. He discusses whether a conjunction is cause-to-effect or effect-to-cause, the nature of the explicative 'ay' and appositive, and the parsing of a nominal sentence.

Guided Path
1
Read the three expressions
Read the Arabic and translation for each of the three explained phrases.
They are: (1) 'ولعدم تكليفنا بذلك', (2) 'إِلَّا أَنَّ المعنى', (3) 'تسمع'.
2
Understand the cause-effect discussion
Analyze the first expression: is the conjunction from cause to effect or effect to cause? The commentator presents both views.
3
Grasp the appositive rule
Learn that after the explicative 'ay', what follows must be an appositive (عطف بيان) that is a verbal noun to match the preceding explicit verbal noun.
4
Note the nominal sentence parsing
See how 'تسمع' is parsed as a subject (مبتدأ) and 'خير' as its predicate (خبر), confirming the subject must be a noun.

The Path to Knowing God: The Temporality of the World
Al-Sawi introduces the proof of the world's temporality and explains the method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that for al-Sawi, the path to knowledge of God is through establishing the temporality of the world, and you will grasp the methodological shift from rational judgments to applied proof.

Core Idea
The temporality of the world is the essential proof for knowing God.

Having established the categories of rational judgment and the obligation to know God, al-Sawi now introduces the specific path that leads to that knowledge: proving that the world is temporally originated (hadith). This passage marks a transition from general principles to the first concrete argument in kalām. It also clarifies key terms like 'path,' 'proof,' and the nature of relational (i‘tibārī) matters.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the translation. Let the text introduce the new topic.
2
Identify the main claim
Find where al-Sawi states that the path to knowing God is the temporality of the world. This is the central thesis.
3
Understand the methodological shift
Notice that al-Sawi moves from discussing rational judgments to presenting a specific proof. This shows the applied nature of kalām.
The divisions of rational judgment were preparatory.
4
Note the glosses on key terms
Examine how al-Sawi explains 'knowledge' as assent (taṣdīq) and the 'path' as a metaphor for proof. These clarifications are essential.
The metaphor of the path is a declared simile.
5
Reflect on the role of i‘tibārī matters
Consider why al-Sawi mentions that states are relational mental considerations. This ties to the nature of the proof.

Commentary
This passage begins a new section titled 'Chapter on explaining that the world is temporally originated' (Fasl fi bayan anna al-‘alam hadith). The author states that after finishing the explanation of the divisions of rational judgment and the obligation of knowing God upon every legally responsible person, he proceeds to explain the path that leads to knowledge of God, which is the temporality of the world. He then quotes the base text: 'Then after you have known that it is obligatory upon every legally responsible person...' and notes that the verb 'i‘laman' has the light nun of emphasis. The author explains that the verb 'knowledge' is given the meaning of 'assent' (taṣdīq), making it transitive with the bā’ in the phrase 'that this world – with all its parts.' He then glosses the phrase 'for it is not described by existence or non-existence' as referring to a state described by affirmation (thubut) rather than existence or non-existence; and the truth is that such things are relational matters (umūr i‘tibāriyya) with no external reality, only mental considerations. Next, he glosses 'he began explaining the path that leads' as referring to the proof and evidence, likening it to a sensory path metaphorically because each conveys to the intended goal. Finally, he notes that 'then after you have known' refers back to an earlier statement about the legal obligation upon the responsible person, and the phrase 'and he embedded in knowledge...' is an answer to a question about the material (incomplete).

The World as a Sign and Refutation of Sophists
Al-Sawi defines the world as a sign and affirms fixed realities against sophists.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the world is called a sign, how it indicates the existence of its Maker, and that the realities of things are fixed, contrary to the Sophists.

Core Idea
The world is a sign pointing to God, and its realities are fixed and knowable.

Al-Sawi begins this section by reminding us that proving the world is temporally originated is the path to knowing God. He then explains why the world is called 'a sign' and uses the demonstrative pronoun 'this' to show that the realities of things are fixed. Finally, he rebukes the Sophists who deny this fixedness.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the main claim
Read the full Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that the purpose is to prove the world's temporal origination as a path to knowing God.
Focus on the phrase 'الطريق الموصل' – the path that leads.
2
Understand why the world is called a sign
Al-Sawi states that the world is named 'عالم' because it is a sign (علامة) of the existence of its Maker. Reflect on how creation points to a creator.
The word 'عالم' is derived from 'علم' but here it is linked to 'علامة'.
3
See the argument from the demonstrative pronoun
The use of 'هذا' in 'هذا العالم' indicates that the realities of things are fixed and knowledge of them is achieved. This sets up the refutation of the Sophists.
Al-Sawi explains that the demonstrative points to something present, implying objective reality.
4
Recognize the refutation of the Sophists
Al-Sawi affirms that the fixedness of realities is accepted by all religious communities except the Sophists, who are divided into three groups. Understand that this passage defends epistemological objectivity.
The text breaks off before naming the three groups, but the stance is clear.

The Dual Purpose of the Poet's Line: Supplication and Completion
Al-Sawi comments on the dual purpose of a line from the poem.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the dual purpose of the poet's line: it is both a supplication for piety and a grammatical completion of the verse.

Core Idea
The poet's line is both a prayer and a structural completion.

This passage from al-Sawi's hashiah explains a key line in the opening poem. The line that says 'for the one who seeks to know God's attributes' is actually more than a statement of purpose—it is a supplication to Allah. Al-Sawi breaks down the grammar to show the poet intended two things at once.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the English translation. Notice the flow of the commentary.
The passage is short but dense.
2
Identify the two intended purposes
The hashiah explicitly says the poet intended two things: supplication (الدعاء) and completion (التكملة). Find these in the text.
Supplication is for the seeker to achieve piety.
3
Understand the grammatical analysis
The word 'تكملة' is in the accusative case, coordinated with 'الدعاء' as the object of 'قصد'. This shows both are part of the poet's intent.
Grammatical coordination (عطف) links the two purposes.
4
Relate to the overall work
The line's dual function reminds us that the pursuit of knowledge of God's attributes is tied to prayer for piety, blending theology and devotion.

لمن حاول معرفة صفات الله تعالى، وتكملة البيت، كأنَّه قال : اللهم ؛ اجعله محصّلاً للتّقوى. قوله : (لمن حاول معرفة صفات الله تعالى أي زاولها واشتغل بها . : قوله : ( وتكملة البيت بالنَّصب عطفاً على الدُّعاء؛ أي: فقصد بها أمرين : الدُّعاء والتكملة .
For the one who seeks to know God's attributes, and the completion of the verse, as if he said: 'O Allah, make him an achiever of piety.' His statement: 'For the one who seeks to know God's attributes' i.e., he undertakes it and occupies himself with it. His statement: 'And the completion of the verse' in the accusative, coordinated as a conjunction with 'the supplication'; i.e., so he intended by it two things: the supplication and the completion.

Commentary
This passage is a brief commentary on a line of the poem that states the purpose of the work: it is for the one who seeks to know God's attributes. The hashiah explains that the line includes both a supplication (دعاء) to God to make the seeker achieve piety (تقوى) and a grammatical completion of the verse structure. The expression 'تكملة البيت' (completion of the verse) is parsed as an accusative object coordinated with 'الدُّعاء', indicating that the poet aimed at two things: offering a supplication and completing the poetic meter of the line.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think 'تكملة البيت' is a separate phrase unrelated to the supplication.
Correction: It is syntactically linked to the supplication as a coordinated object, showing both are part of the poet's single intent.
