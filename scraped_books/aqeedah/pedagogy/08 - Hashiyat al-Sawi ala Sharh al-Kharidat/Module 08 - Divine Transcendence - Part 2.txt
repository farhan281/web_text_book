The Mu'tazila's Argument Against Vision of Allah and Its Refutation
Al-Sawi presents the Mu'tazila's strongest argument (shubhat al-muqabala) and refutes it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila's strongest argument (shubhat al-muqabala) against the vision of Allah and how the Ash'ari response refutes it by redefining ru'ya as a special perception and rejecting analogical reasoning.

Core Idea
Just as we know Allah without spatial constraints, we can see Him without them.

The Mu'tazila argued that seeing Allah would imply physical limitations, contradicting His transcendence. In this passage, Al-Sawi presents their strongest argument—the argument of facing (muqabala)—and refutes it by clarifying that ru'ya is a type of perception created by Allah, not bound by ordinary physical conditions. This refutation also challenges the method of qiyas al-gha'ib 'ala al-shahid.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Let the structure of argument and refutation emerge naturally.
Notice the logical flow from 'if seen' to a series of impossible consequences.
2
Identify the Mu'tazila's chain of consequences
The argument lists five necessary consequences of vision: facing, direction/space, rays/distance, substance/accident, whole/part. Each is meant to show contradiction with Allah's nature.
The chain leads to the conclusion that vision is impossible.
3
Examine the Ash'ari response
The response redefines ru'ya as a special perception that Allah creates without physical prerequisites. It also rejects the analogy from worldly vision.
The example of knowledge shows that perception can exist without location.
4
Reflect on the disagreement
Consider why the Mu'tazila insisted on this argument and how the Ash'ari reply preserves both divine transcendence and the possibility of vision in the afterlife.

Commentary
This passage is a combination of the Mu'tazila's objection to the vision of Allah and its refutation. It begins by stating that the Mu'tazila disagree with Ahl al-Sunna on this issue, deeming vision impossible. They cling to specious arguments, the strongest of which is the argument of facing (muqabala). The argument is presented: if Allah were seen, He would have to be facing the seer, implying direction and space, requiring a connection of rays, proper distance, and the seen would be either a substance or accident, and either whole (necessitating finitude) or part (necessitating division). Since all these consequences are impossible for Allah, the premise of vision is also impossible. Then the answer is given: vision is a type of perception that Allah creates whenever and wherever He wills, so these physical constraints do not apply. The method of qiyas al-gha'ib 'ala al-shahid (analogizing the unseen to the witnessed) is invalid, as shown by the example of knowledge (ilm): we know Allah without spatial constraints, similarly vision can occur without such constraints. Finally, it is mentioned that the Prophet (peace be upon him) confirmed this in many hadiths.

Refuting the Mu'tazila's Proof from 'La Tudrikuhu'
Al-Sawi presents the Mu'tazila's textual proof and the Sunni response distinguishing idrak from ru'ya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sunni scholars refute the Mu'tazila's claim that the verse 'La tudrikuhu al-absar' disproves the possibility of seeing Allah.

Core Idea
The verse denies comprehensive perception, not sight itself.

The Mu'tazila argue that the Qur'anic verse 'Vision does not perceive Him' proves Allah cannot be seen, even in the Hereafter. But Sunni scholars point out a crucial distinction: the Arabic word 'tudrikuhu' means 'comprehend' or 'encompass', not simply 'see'. This lesson unpacks that rebuttal and explains why it resolves the apparent contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazila's Argument
Start by reading the first part of the passage, where Al-Sawi presents the Mu'tazila's claim: they say vision of Allah is impossible in both worlds, and some even consider it kufr. Their strongest proof is the verse 'La tudrikuhu al-absar'.
Notice that they interpret 'tudrikuhu' as 'sees'.
2
Understand the Sunni Response
Now read the answer: 'la tudrikuhu' means 'does not comprehend', not 'does not see'. The verse is about comprehensive perception, not the act of seeing. Sights cannot fully encompass Allah, just as intellects cannot.
This preserves the possibility of seeing Allah without full comprehension.
3
Reflect on the Broader Implication
Consider why the Mu'tazila made this mistake: they clung to physical habits or philosophical inclinations. This shows how a subtle linguistic point can save correct theology.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the phrase 'fa-ahaluha' and 'mutamassikin bi-shubah' from the base text. It explains that the Mu'tazila said vision is impossible in both worlds, and some even consider it kufr. They have both rational and transmitted proofs; the strongest rational was already given, and the strongest transmitted is the verse 'La tudrikuhu al-absar'. The Mu'tazila argue that this verse is in praise of Allah, implying that being seen would be a deficiency. The response is that 'la tudrikuhu' means 'does not encompass/comprehend' (la tuhitu bihi), as the verse says 'tudrikuhu' not 'tarahu', so it is about comprehension, not sight. Therefore, sights do not encompass Allah, just as intellects do not. The passage concludes that the Mu'tazila have deviated due to clinging to physical habits or philosophical inclinations.

The Hadith on Vision of Allah in the Resurrection Grounds
Al-Sawi cites a hadith and interprets it to support ru'yah for believers before Paradise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a hadith cited by al-Sawi supports the occurrence of ru'yah (vision of Allah) for believers in the resurrection grounds and how the Salaf interpret the verse about 'uncovering the shin.'

Core Idea
The vision of Allah occurs not only in Paradise but also in the resurrection grounds for believers.

Al-Sawi cites a detailed hadith describing events on the Day of Judgment to support the view that believers will see Allah in the resurrection grounds before entering Paradise. This hadith addresses the testing of believers and hypocrites through successive manifestations. Understanding this hadith clarifies an important point in Islamic eschatology: the vision of Allah (ru'yah) is granted to believers even before Paradise.

Guided Path
1
Read the full hadith narrative
Begin by reading the entire hadith as presented in the Arabic passage. Notice the sequence of events: the call, the destruction of false worshippers, the remaining community, the tests with angels, and the final manifestation of Allah.
The hadith is lengthy but forms a coherent story.
2
Identify the test for believers and hypocrites
Focus on how the believers recognize the true Lord despite the angelic impostors. The hypocrites fail to prostrate when Allah manifests.
This highlights a key difference between sincere believers and hypocrites.
3
Connect to the Quranic verse
Al-Sawi links the hadith to the verse 'Yawma yukshafu ʿan sāq' (The Day the shin shall be uncovered). Note how he explains the Salaf's metaphorical interpretation.
This connection reinforces the hadith's authenticity and interpretation.
4
Understand the doctrinal conclusion
End by affirming that the Sunnah indicates ru'yah occurs in the resurrection grounds for believers, which is the correct view according to al-Sawi.
This is the main takeaway: ru'yah is not limited to Paradise.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a line from the main text that states the Sunnah implies the occurrence of ru'yah (vision of Allah) for believers in the resurrection grounds (ʿaraṣāt al-qiyāma) as well. The commentator, al-Sawi, begins by explaining this line: he cites a lengthy hadith describing events of the Day of Judgment. In this hadith, a call is made for each nation to follow its object of worship; the worshippers of the sun and other false deities are thrown into the Fire with their objects, except those whom Allah has protected, such as those who worshipped Jesus, Mary, or Ali (these are mentioned as examples of those who were worshipped improperly). Then the hadith continues: the believing community remains with its hypocrites. They refuse to move until they see their object of worship. Two different angels appear in succession, each claiming to be the Lord, but the believers recognize them as created beings because they are confined in space. Finally, Allah manifests, and the believers prostrate. The hypocrites attempt to prostrate but fail, and a voice separates the criminals. Al-Sawi then connects this to the Quranic verse 'Yawma yukshafu ʿan sāq' (the Day the shin is uncovered) and notes that the Salaf interpret 'kashf al-sāq' as the removal of the veil, not a literal shin. After explaining the hadith, al-Sawi concludes by affirming that ru'yah indeed occurs in the resurrection grounds for believers, and this is the correct view.

Disbelievers Do Not See Allah on the Day of Judgment
Al-Sawi refutes the claim that disbelievers see Allah, quoting the verse of hijab.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Quranic proof that disbelievers are veiled from Allah on the Day of Judgment and why the presence of hypocrites does not imply they see Him.

Core Idea
The Quranic veil is decisive: disbelievers are veiled and do not see Allah.

Some have claimed that even disbelievers, particularly hypocrites, will see Allah on the Day of Judgment. Al-Sawi addresses this claim head-on by quoting a powerful Quranic verse and explaining why the physical crowding of hypocrites with believers does not mean they share in the vision. This lesson clarifies the doctrine.

1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the objection 'some say' and the refutation that follows.
The passage is dense; take your time.

Al-Sawi quotes the verse 'Kalla innahum 'an rabbihim yawma'idhin lamahjubun'. Understand that this is the main evidence that disbelievers are veiled.
The verse is from Surah al-Mutaffifin (83:15).
3
Analyze the counterargument
The objector might say that hypocrites are present among believers, so they should see. Al-Sawi responds that presence does not cause vision; their actions are mere imitation.
The key term is 'taqlid' (imitation).
4
Reflect on the lesson

Commentary

Commentary
