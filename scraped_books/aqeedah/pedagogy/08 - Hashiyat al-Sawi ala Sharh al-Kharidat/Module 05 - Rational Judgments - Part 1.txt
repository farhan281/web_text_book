The Three Rational Judgments
Al-Dardir enumerates the three rational judgments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three fundamental categories of rational judgment that form the basis of kalam.

Core Idea
Every rational judgment falls into one of three categories: necessary, impossible, or possible.

In this passage, the author introduces the foundational classification that underpins all theological reasoning. Before delving into the attributes of God, one must grasp these three categories—obligation, impossibility, and permissibility—which the mind uses to assess any proposition. The poet urges the reader to grasp this deeply.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic line and its translation. Identify the three terms for the rational judgments: al-wujub, al-istihala, al-jawaz.
The passage is short but dense; take time to note each term.
2
Understand each category
Reflect on what each category means: al-wujub (obligation/necessity) means something the mind deems must be; al-istihala (impossibility) means something the mind deems cannot be; al-jawaz (permissibility) means something the mind deems may or may not be.
These are rational, not legal, categories.
3
Ask why this matters
Consider why the author emphasizes 'without fail' and 'so understand the pleasure of ambitions.' This knowledge is the gateway to all further theological discussion.
Mastering these categories is essential for proper understanding of God's attributes.

Definitions of the Three Rational Judgments
Al-Dardir defines the three rational judgments: necessary, impossible, and possible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definitions of the three rational judgments (wajib, mustahil, ja'iz) and how they are distinguished by their acceptance of existence and non-existence.

Core Idea
The three rational judgments are the basic building blocks of theological reasoning about possibility and necessity.

This lesson introduces the foundational categories that every student of kalam must know: the rationally necessary, impossible, and possible. These definitions are not abstract; they directly apply to our understanding of Allah's attributes and the nature of creation. Let's read the poet's concise definitions.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then read the literal translation. Pay attention to the key terms for each category.
The passage is a concise poetic definition, so read carefully.
2
Identify the three terms
Identify which lines define al-wajib al-'aqli, al-mustahil, and al-ja'iz. Notice the key phrases for each: 'lam yaqbal al-intifa' (does not accept non-existence), 'lam yaqbal al-thubut' (does not accept existence), and 'qabilun lil-intifa'i wa lil-thubuti' (accepts both).
These phrases are the core of the definitions.
3
Understand the logical opposites
Notice that wajib and mustahil are opposites: one cannot not exist, the other cannot exist. Ja'iz is the middle ground that can either exist or not exist.
This logical structure is key to kalam reasoning.
4
Apply to theology
Reflect on how these categories apply: God's existence is wajib, the existence of a partner for God is mustahil, and the creation of the world is ja'iz.
This shows the practical importance of these definitions.

Commentary
This passage provides the definitions of the three rational judgments. The poet defines al-wajib al-'aqli (the rationally necessary) as that which cannot allow non-existence (al-intifa') in its essence; it must exist. Finally, al-ja'iz (the rationally possible) is defined as that which can accept both non-existence and existence; it is neutral. These definitions are crucial for kalam arguments about God's attributes: for God, existence is wajib, non-existence is mustahil, and creation of the world is ja'iz.

The proof is simple but powerful: we observe change in the world, and change is incompatible with eternity. Let's see how the poem lays this out.

Negative and Positive Attributes of the Three Judgments
Al-Sawi defines the first two judgments as negative attributes and the third as positive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand which of the three rational judgments are considered negative attributes and which is positive, and the basis for this classification.

Core Idea
Wujub and istihalah negate, while ja'iz affirms possibility.

Al-Sawi clarifies the semantic nature of the three rational judgments introduced earlier. While all three describe realities about existence, they do so in different ways: some negate a state, others affirm it. This lesson unpacks that distinction.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage on attributes
Read the first two glosses in Arabic and their translation. Observe how Al-Sawi defines the attributes of wujub and istihalah as negative, and ja'iz as positive.
The first gloss establishes the subject being discussed.
2
Understand the concept of negative attribute
A negative attribute (صفة سلبية) indicates the absence of something. Here, wujub means non-acceptance of non-existence, so it negates non-existence. Similarly, istihalah negates existence. Ja'iz, however, affirms both possibility of existence and non-existence, making it positive.
Positive here is relational (اعتبارية).
3
Reflect on why this classification matters
Why does Al-Sawi take the trouble to classify these as negative or positive? Because it affects how we reason about Allah's attributes. Negative attributes describe what Allah is not, positive attributes describe what He is.
This will be built upon in later lessons.

The Ordering of the Three Judgments in Kalam
Al-Sawi provides two complementary explanations for the order of the three judgments in the list.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the reasoning behind the order in which the three rational judgments are presented, including considerations of nobility, opposition, and simplicity.

Core Idea
The order of judgments reflects nobility, quick recall of opposites, and priority of simple over compound.

After defining the attributes of the three judgments, Al-Sawi turns to explain why they were presented in the sequence of wujub, then istihalah, then ja'iz. The order is not arbitrary but follows logical and pedagogical principles. This lesson explores those principles.

Read the passage on ordering

The given order is explained by two factors: first, istihalah is the opposite (ضد) of wujub, and opposites come to mind quickly when mentioning the first. Defending the Threefold Division: Looseness in Expression
Al-Sawi presents an objection against the division of rational judgment and answers with a concession of looseness in expression

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the division of rational judgment into wajib, mustahil, and ja'iz is defended as a loose expression, not a strict logical division.

Core Idea
Rational judgment is simple, so its threefold division is a loose convention, not rigorous analysis.

Before we can use the three categories of rational judgment confidently, we must face a subtle objection: how can a simple mental act be divided into three? Al-Sawi presents this criticism and then defends the traditional usage by conceding that the division is a looseness in expression. This lesson unpacks that exchange.

The Three Rational Judgments as Metaphorical Divisions
Al-Sawi explains that the three rational judgments are not literal divisions of judgment itself.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the three rational judgments are called 'divisions of judgment' only metaphorically, and how they actually characterize the object of judgment.

Core Idea
The three rational judgments describe the thing judged, not the act of judging.

Al-Sawi addresses a subtle point: the three rational judgments are often called 'divisions of judgment,' but judgment itself is a simple act. He explains that this is a metaphorical expression, and what is actually divided are the qualities of the thing being affirmed or denied. Understanding this nuance is crucial for correct theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then its literal translation. Notice the key terms: مسامحة (looseness), تجوز (metaphor), and the three categories.
The passage is a commentary, so it assumes you know the basic classification.
2
Identify the problem and solution
Al-Sawi states there is looseness in earlier phrasing. Find the problem: how can the three be divisions of judgment? Then see his solution: they are divisions by metaphor.
3
Study the example
Al-Sawi gives the example 'God is powerful.' The judgment is affirming power; that affirmation is not one of the three, but its necessity is. Understand how the three apply to the quality, not the act.
4
Learn the three definitions
At the end, Al-Sawi defines each of the three: necessary (cannot not be), impossible (cannot be), possible (can be or not be). Internalize these as qualities of the thing judged.
These definitions are the foundation of rational theology.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the classification of rational judgments into necessary (wājib), impossible (mumtaniʿ), and possible (jāʾiz). The commentator, al-Sāwī, addresses a previous objection: how can these three be called 'divisions of the judgment' (aqsām al-ḥukm) when judgment itself is a simple act of the mind? He first admits that the phrasing of earlier scholars contains a looseness (musāmaḥa). Then he clarifies the intended meaning (al-murād): every rational judgment (ḥukm ʿaqlī) concerning affirmation (ithbāt) or negation (nafy) is necessarily characterized (ittiṣāf) by exactly one of these three.
