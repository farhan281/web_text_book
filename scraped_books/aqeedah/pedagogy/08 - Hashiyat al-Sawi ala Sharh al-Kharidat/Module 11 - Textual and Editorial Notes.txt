Grammatical Analysis of 'Muhammad' as Badal or Bayan
Sanusi analyzes 'Muhammad' as badal or 'ataf bayan with objection and answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the grammatical distinction between badal and 'ataf bayan, and how the objection regarding the discarded nature of mubdal minhu is resolved.

Core Idea
A badal syntactically discards its predecessor, but not its meaning.

Sanusi presents a grammatical analysis of the word 'Muhammad' in an opening phrase, debating whether it functions as a badal or 'ataf bayan. This is not just a technical quibble—it affects how we understand the verb's agency. In this passage, he raises and resolves a key objection about the discarding of the substituted word.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice the compact style and the flow from stated possibilities to objection and answer.
2
Understand the two possibilities
Identify that Sanusi considers 'Muhammad' as either a badal or an 'ataf bayan for 'sayyid', both used for praise like an adjective.
3
Recognize the objection
The objection claims that if it were a badal, 'sayyid' would be discarded, but it is not. Ask yourself: What does 'discarded' mean here?
The rule is that the mubdal minhu is in the ruling of being discarded.
4
Study the answer
The answer distinguishes between syntactic and semantic discarding. The mubdal minhu is discarded only in terms of the operative agent (al-'amal), not in meaning.
In badal, the operative agent acts on the badal directly, skipping the mubdal minhu.

If you say: It is objected to it being a badal by their statement that the mubdal minhu (the substituted-from) is considered as discarded, whereas here it is not the case. And it is answered: By that their statement 'the mubdal minhu is considered as discarded' refers to the aspect of the operative agent (al-'amal), because the operative agent in the badal is different from the operative agent in the mubdal minhu, unlike the other appositional terms.

Commentary
This passage is a grammatical analysis of the word 'Muhammad' in the opening phrase. The commentator presents two possibilities: it is either a badal (substitute) or an 'ataf bayan (explanatory apposition) of 'sayyid', used for praise similarly to an adjective. An objection is then raised: if it were a badal, the mubdal minhu (the word 'sayyid') would be considered discarded (in terms of meaning), but here 'sayyid' is not discarded—it retains its significance. The answer clarifies that the rule 'the mubdal minhu is like discarded' applies only to the operative agent (the word governing the badal), not to the meaning. In a badal, the governing word acts on the badal directly, not on the mubdal minhu, so the mubdal minhu is ignored syntactically. This differs from other appositional terms (like adjectives or conjunctive nouns) where the governing word acts on both. Thus, 'Muhammad' can be a badal without discarding the significance of 'sayyid'.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the author's introductory blessings, defining key terms and providing supporting hadiths. The commentator (Al-Sawi) explains each phrase of the blessing formula step by step. First, he defines 'المؤيَّد' as 'the strengthened' from the root 'التأييد' meaning strengthening. Then he defines 'المعجزات' (miracles) as a plural of 'معجزة', which is a supernatural event occurring at the hand of a claimant to prophethood, accompanied by a challenge, noting that a full discussion will come later. Next, 'الباهرة' means 'the overwhelming' of the opponent. Turning to 'وعلى آله', he states that 'الآل' means all followers, so 'الأصحاب' (Companions) are a specific subset included in the general term; thus the phrase 'الأصحاب' is a specification after generality. He then explains why the author used the phrase 'أولي المناقب' as an attribute of the Companions, citing the hadith where the Prophet taught the Companions how to send blessings upon him, saying 'O God, send blessings upon Muhammad and upon his family (آل).' This hadith was recorded by Al-Bukhari and Muslim. The text then quotes a hadith from Anas ibn Malik about the reward for saying this salawat: if one says it while standing, he is forgiven before sitting; if sitting, before standing. Next, a linguistic note: 'آل' is a collective noun with no singular from its own root; its singular is understood from meaning. Then he explains the word 'أصحابه': it is an irregular plural of 'صحب' (not 'صاحب') because the regular plural of the form 'فَعْل' (with fatha and sukun) on 'أفعال' requires the medial letter to be a weak letter (like 'سيف' becomes 'أسياف'), whereas 'صحب' has a sound medial. Thus it is not the plural of 'صاحب' (which would be 'أصحاب' anyway, but that is a different pattern). The word 'صحب' is a triliteral noun like 'باب' giving 'أبواب'. Then 'أولي' means 'possessors', 'المناقب' are virtues (opposite of defects), and 'الفاخرة' means magnificent, things to boast about in both worlds. Finally, he notes that God mentioned their merits in many Quranic verses and the Prophet praised them in many hadiths, thus emphasizing the high status of the Companions.

The Reward for Sending Blessings and the Nature of 'آل'

Sending blessings brings immediate forgiveness, and 'آل' has no singular from its own root.

Commentary
This passage is a grammatical commentary on the phrase 'لا سيّما رفيقه في الغار' (especially his companion in the cave) from the original text praising Abu Bakr al-Siddiq. The commentator begins by explaining the previous phrase 'كعدل' as a simile (تشبيه) where the verbal noun is interpreted as the agent noun (اسم الفاعل). He notes that the word 'المطهرين' (the purified ones) appears with a ya' in the manuscripts, but Arabic grammar requires a waw because it is a predicate. Then he discusses the conjunction of the Companions to the Family (آل) as a conjunction of the specific to the general (عطف الخاص على العام) to indicate their greater honor. The main focus is the construction 'لا سيّما'. The commentator explains that the author omitted the waw before 'لا سيّما', and this omission is either permissible or due to poetic necessity. He cites his teacher al-Amir, who quotes Tha‘lab as stating that dropping the waw is an error, but others allow it. If the waw is retained, there are three views: it is parenthetical (اعتراضية), making the following sentence an attribute; circumstantial (حالية) in the accusative; or resumptive (استئنافية) with no syntactic position. Then the commentator analyzes 'لا' as a generic negator (نافية للجنس) that operates like 'إن', taking the noun in the accusative and the predicate in the nominative. He addresses an objection: can 'سي' be raised (رفع) if 'لا' is treated like 'ليس'? He answers no, because the intended meaning is the negation of the entire genus of similarity, not a partial negation that allows affirmation of others. Then he discusses the obligatory deletion of the predicate of 'لا سيما', noting that some argue that 'ما' itself can be the predicate when the noun after it is raised. Finally, he mentions that the origin of 'سي' is 'سوي' (with waw), as evidenced by its conjugations like 'تساويا' and the dual 'سيان'. The passage ends with a footnote reference to Sharh al-Amir.

This passage is a grammatical analysis of the construction 'lā siyyamā' as it appears in the commentary on the Companions. The answer is that 'sī' is like 'mithl' in being deeply indefinite; thus adding it to a definite noun does not make it definite.
