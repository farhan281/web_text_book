The Ten Etiquettes of the Sufi Path
Al-Ṣāwī enumerates ten etiquettes for the Sufi seeker and glosses some.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the ten etiquettes that a Sufi seeker must observe to attain perfection, including their spiritual significance and practical applications.

Core Idea
The ten etiquettes are a roadmap to spiritual perfection through purity, humility, and self-accountability.

Al-Ṣāwī presents a concise yet profound list of ten etiquettes that form the backbone of Sufi training. These etiquettes cover external practices, inner states, and social conduct, all aimed at refining the soul and drawing closer to Allah. In this lesson, we will explore each etiquette and its rationale, as explained by the commentator.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the numbered list and the commentary glosses at the end.
2
Identify the ten etiquettes
Find each of the ten items in the list. Observe how each is introduced with 'ومنها' or numbered. Notice the brief explanation given for some.
The commentary expands on items 1, 5, and 9.
3
Examine the commentary glosses
Read the four glosses at the end. They clarify specific points: sleeping on purity, visiting the righteous, and speaking gnostic terminology.
These glosses are from al-Ṣāwī himself.
4
Reflect on the rationale behind each etiquette
For each etiquette, ask: why is this important for spiritual growth? How does it combat pride, attachment, or negligence?
The commentary helps, but also think about the underlying wisdom.
5
Connect to your own practice
Consider how these etiquettes apply to your own spiritual journey. Which ones resonate most? Which are most challenging?
The goal is not just knowledge but transformation.

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration of ten etiquettes (ādāb) of the Sufi path (ṭā'ifa) that lead to perfection (kamāl). It begins with the introductory phrase 'Among the etiquettes of this group by which perfection is attained.' Then the list proceeds: (1) Maintaining ritual purity and sleeping in that state; the commentator specifies that purity includes even the minor purification (wuḍū) for one who is in a state of major impurity (junub). (2) Not exposing the severe private parts in seclusion out of modesty from Allah and the angels. (3) Respecting the elderly and showing compassion to the young, orphans, and the poor, indeed to all creatures. (4) Good manners toward the people of knowledge, especially those who serve the Sacred Law (sharī'a) and the shaykhs of the path, as they are the heirs of the prophets. (5) Not visiting any of the righteous (al-Ṣāliḥīn) while one is under spiritual training (tarbiya) before attaining perfection, for fear that one might see a miracle (karāma) or virtue in someone else that was not seen in one's own shaykh, leading to the belief that the shaykh is deficient and thus the disciple loses his spiritual support (madd). The commentator adds that this applies to both living and dead righteous, and requires the shaykh's permission. (6) Thinking ill of oneself and well of others, so that one sees everyone else as better than oneself. (7) Not seeking personal revenge in any matter. (8) Constantly viewing one's worship as infiltrated by ostentation (riyā') and evil thoughts (khawāṭir radī'a), such that it deserves punishment except for Allah's gracious forgiveness; thus one seeks forgiveness both for the worship itself and for the act of seeking forgiveness. (9) Not speaking the language of the gnostics (al-‘ārifīn) about separation (farq) and union (jam‘), subsistence (baqā') and annihilation (fanā'), until one is perfected; even for the perfect, it is better to avoid such speech unless there is a need like teaching, as the commentator notes. (10) Holding oneself to account (muḥāsaba) for wrong actions—forbidden, disliked, and excess permissible acts—as well as for the inner thoughts (khawāṭir nafsāniyya and shaytāniyya) that occur, and seeking forgiveness from them. The passage then includes four glosses from al-Ṣāwī explaining the introductory phrase, the meaning of 'sleeping upon purity,' the scope of visiting the righteous, and the exception for speaking gnostic terminology.

Differentiating Nafsānī from Shayṭānī Inner Thoughts
Al-Ṣāwī explains the difference between nafsānī and shayṭānī khawāṭir and how to suppress the first.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between nafsānī and shayṭānī inner thoughts and the specific remedy for the nafsānī thought.

Core Idea
Not every bad thought is from Shayṭān; the nafs persistently insists on sin, requiring dhikr and guidance.

Al-Ṣāwī distinguishes two types of inner thoughts that challenge the spiritual seeker. The first originates from the lower self and is marked by persistent insistence, like a child nagging. The remedy lies in constant dhikr, reflecting on consequences, and turning to the shaykh.

Guided Path

The shayṭānī is not elaborated; the contrast is key.
3
Understand the child analogy

This analogy shows the inner pressure of the nafs.
4

These are practical steps for the spiritual seeker.
5

Commentary
This passage provides a brief but significant distinction between two types of inner thoughts (khawāṭir) that afflict the spiritual seeker. The first type is the nafsānī thought, which originates from the lower self (nafs). It is characterized by persistent insistence on sin or desire (shahwa). The author uses a vivid analogy: like a child who stubbornly nags his mother until she gives him what he wants. The remedy for this type is threefold: (1) constant engagement in dhikr (remembrance of Allah), (2) reflecting upon and explaining the dire consequences of acting on that thought, and (3) turning toward the shaykh for guidance and support. The second type, shayṭānī thought, is not described here, but the contrast is drawn to highlight the distinct nature of the nafsānī thought and the necessity of specific countermeasures.

The commentator unpacks each phrase, explaining the grammar, semantics, and theological significance. This lesson explores the rationale behind the author's choice of words and the commentator's clarifications.

This passage is the conclusion of the book, where the commentator explains the author's closing statements. The Meaning of Closing Prayers and Colophon
Al-Sawi explains closing prayers and records completion date.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the significance of the closing prayers in classical Islamic texts and al-Sawi's explanation of the phrase 'رضي الله عنهم وعنا'.

Core Idea
Closing prayers are not mere formality but carry deep theological meaning about God's mercy and emulation of the Quran.

This lesson examines the concluding section of al-Sawi's commentary. He not only records the completion date but also explains the meaning of the prayers he writes, showing that even the closing lines of a scholarly work are carefully chosen for their spiritual and methodological significance.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the literal translation to grasp the overall meaning.
Notice that the passage includes a Quranic verse, prayers, a colophon, and a commentary.
2
Understand al-Sawi's interpretation of 'رضي الله عنهم'
Al-Sawi explains that the preposition 'عن' here means 'passing beyond', so the phrase means God's anger passes beyond them and us. This is a precise theological interpretation.
Compare this with the common understanding of 'رضي' as simply being pleased.
3
Reflect on the author's choice of closing words
Al-Sawi notes that the author concluded with the same words as Surah al-Saffat. This was done as an act of following (iqtidā') and seeking blessing (tabarruk).
This shows the importance of emulating Quranic language in Islamic scholarship.

Commentary
This passage contains the closing of the book and the author's commentary. It begins with a Quranic verse (al-Hashr 59:9) praising the Companions' selflessness. Then the author writes a prayer: 'God be pleased with them and with us', followed by 'Praise be to God... Amen, and peace be upon the messengers.' Then the colophon records the date of completion: Jumada al-Ula 1177 AH. After that, al-Sawi comments on the phrase 'رضي الله عنهم وعنا بهم'. He explains that the preposition 'عن' here means 'passing beyond' (mujāwazah), so the meaning is that God's anger passes beyond them (i.e., He is not angry with them) and us, due to our love for them and emulation of them. Finally, he notes that the author closed his book with the same words that God closed Sūrat al-Ṣāffāt (i.e., 'سبحان ربك رب العزة عما يصفون وسلام على المرسلين والحمد لله رب العالمين' – here shortened to 'وسلام على المرسلين والحمد لله رب العالمين') as an act of following (iqtidā’) and seeking blessing (tabarruk).

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think the phrase 'رضي الله عنهم' simply means 'God is pleased with them', without appreciating the nuance of the preposition 'عن'.
Correction: Al-Sawi explains that 'عن' here indicates 'passing beyond', so the intended meaning is that God's anger passes beyond them (i.e., He is not angry with them), which is a more precise interpretation.

Key Takeaway
Al-Sawi's closing prayers reveal his theological precision and his method of emulating the Quran in his writings.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Al-Sawi carefully analyzed the meaning of a simple prayer he included in his book. When you recite common Islamic phrases like 'رضي الله عنهم' or 'بارك الله فيك', do you ever stop to consider their precise meaning? What would change in your heart if you did?
?
The author deliberately ended his work by emulating the Quran's conclusion. In your daily life, do you consciously imitate the Quran's language and spirit, or does your speech and behavior follow convenience? What specific step can you take today to align more closely with the Quran?
?
Al-Sawi sought blessing (tabarruk) by connecting his writing to a Quranic verse. How often do you intentionally seek Allah's blessing in your ordinary tasks—studying, working, even resting? If you were to treat every activity as an opportunity for tabarruk, what would feel different?
