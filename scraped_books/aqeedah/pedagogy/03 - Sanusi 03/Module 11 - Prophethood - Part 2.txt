The Rational Proof of Prophetic Infallibility
Sanusi provides a rational proof for prophetic infallibility using divine knowledge and the command to follow.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational argument that prophets cannot sin, based on God's knowledge and command to follow them.

Core Idea
God's knowledge and command make prophetic sinlessness a logical necessity.

In this lesson, Sanusi presents a deeper rational proof. He argues that since God chose prophets and His knowledge is perfect, their truthfulness is guaranteed. Moreover, because God commands us to follow them, their actions must be entirely pleasing to Him. This double argument firmly establishes prophetic infallibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the rational argument
Carefully read the Arabic and translation of this proof.
Focus on the logical flow.
2
Analyze the first premise
Understand that God's choice and His encompassing knowledge necessitate that prophets are truthful.
God's knowledge is perfect and cannot be mistaken.
3
Analyze the second premise
Note that God's command to follow them requires their actions to be fully in line with His pleasure.
4
Combine the premises
See how both premises together lead to the conclusion of prophetic infallibility.
The two arguments are complementary.

And from it is taken: the obligation of truthfulness of the messengers, peace be upon them, and the impossibility of lying upon them, and otherwise they would not be trustworthy messengers to our Lord, the Knower of hidden things, the Mighty and Sublime. And the impossibility of performing all forbidden actions; because they, peace be upon them, were sent to teach the creation by their words, actions, and silences, so it is necessary that in all of them there be no violation of the command of our Lord, the Mighty and Sublime, who chose them over all of creation and entrusted them with the secret of His revelation(1). There is no doubt that the attribution of 'the messenger' to 'God' Almighty necessitates that He, Mighty and Sublime, chose him for the message just as He chose his brother messengers for that. And you have known that His knowledge, praise be to Him, encompasses what has no end, and that ignorance and what is in its meaning is impossible for God. So it follows that His confirmation of them corresponds to what He knew of them of truthfulness and trustworthiness, so it is impossible that they be in reality contrary to what He knew of them. And He commanded following them, peace be upon them, in their words and actions(2), so it is necessary that all of them be in accordance with what pleases our Lord, Mighty and Sublime. And that is what is sought.

Commentary
This passage is a derivation (istinbat) from the testimony of faith. It begins by deducing the necessary attributes of prophets: they must be truthful, lying is impossible for them, and they cannot commit any forbidden act. The reason given is that if they could lie or sin, they would not be trustworthy messengers from God, Who knows all hidden things. Moreover, they were sent to teach humanity by their words, actions, and even their silences; therefore, none of these can contradict God's commands. The author then provides a deeper rational proof: since God chose them for the message and His knowledge encompasses all, and ignorance is impossible for God, His confirmation of them (i.e., calling them messengers) must correspond to their actual state of truthfulness and trustworthiness. Thus, it is impossible for them to be other than what God knows them to be. Additionally, God commands us to follow them, so their actions must be in full accord with His pleasure.

Human Attributes Permissible for Prophets
Sanusi derives the permissibility of non-diminishing human attributes for prophets.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why human attributes that do not diminish prophetic rank are permissible for prophets, and how this is derived from the testimony of faith.

Core Idea
Prophets can experience human hardships because such trials elevate, not diminish, their rank.

This lesson continues exploring what the testimony of faith entails about prophets. Having already established that prophets must be truthful and cannot sin, we now consider whether ordinary human experiences like illness or hunger are permitted for them. Sanusi argues that such attributes are not only permissible but can actually increase their status when borne patiently.

Sanusi deduces permissibility of human attributes for prophets and concludes shahada contains all required beliefs.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Prophets are human, but the shahada holds the entire creed in a few words.

Sanusi continues to draw lessons from the two testimonies. He first deduces that prophets are allowed to have human attributes that do not detract from their lofty status. Then he marvels at how the brief words of the shahada contain the entire creed. This passage highlights both the humanity of prophets and the profundity of the declaration of faith.

Miracles as the Greatest Evidence of Prophethood
The author argues that miracles are the greatest proof of prophecy and are created by Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why miracles are considered the greatest proof of a prophet's truthfulness and why they must be created solely by Allah.

Core Idea
Miracles prove prophethood because prophets cannot even ward off their own hardships.

In this passage, the author presents a powerful argument for why miracles are the strongest evidence of prophethood. He explains that these extraordinary events are not performed by the prophets themselves but are created directly by Allah to confirm their mission. The reasoning is simple: if prophets had the power to perform such wonders, they would certainly use that power to protect themselves from everyday hardships—yet they do not. This shows that miracles are divine signs, not human abilities.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of the spiritual stages of dhikr (remembrance of God) and the indispensability of mentioning the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) after the shahada. The author first describes the state of the remembrancer after negating all things other than God (la ilaha): the heart is purified, and the person enters the divine presence uttering 'illa Allah' in a state of absolute despair of anything beside God. Then, after the heart is illuminated by the truth of tawhid, the author explains that benefiting from this light requires adherence to the Sharia, which in turn requires constant remembrance of the one who brought the Sharia — the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). Therefore, the remembrancer must follow 'la ilaha illa Allah' with 'Muhammad Rasul Allah' to safeguard the light of tawhid within the fortress of the Sharia. Finally, the author generalizes this principle: in every dhikr of God, the believer should not neglect the mention of the Prophet, whether by sending blessings upon him, affirming his prophethood, or similar acts of veneration. The Prophet is described as 'the greatest door of God' (bab Allah al-a'zam) and the guide to God; neglecting him leads to failure and deprivation. The passage concludes with a rhetorical question emphasizing the necessity of remembering the guide to reach God.
