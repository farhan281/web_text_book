The overall portrait is of a scholar who combined asceticism, knowledge, spiritual authority, and compassion, earning him unparalleled respect among his contemporaries.

Yes; the fire of jealousy and envy ignited in the hearts of some scholars of his time, and when they saw that the creeds of al-Sanusi were written for success and educational progress, it soon subsided; out of mercy for the Imam and for them beforehand. The historian al-Hadiki said in his 'Tabaqat': 'When he authored some of his creeds, many of the scholars of his time denied it and spoke inappropriately, so he became very distressed. So he awoke and his sadness had gone, and he became strong against the deniers, and they were silenced from then on, and then they acknowledged his virtue.' Perhaps the overwhelming desire that carried our Imam al-Sanusi to the platform of leadership in the science of kalam and tawhid was his good opinion of this science and the purity of his intention in learning and teaching it. You know this from his statement (may Allah have mercy on him): 'There is no science of the outward sciences that yields knowledge of Him Almighty and watchfulness of Him except tawhid, and through it the understanding of all sciences opens, and to the extent of one's knowledge of it, his fear of Him Almighty increases.' So success and support coincided with him, and he rose in the ranks of this science until he became its head, and his works in the science of tawhid were pointed at with fingers for excellence, until it was said about them: 'His creeds are sufficient therein, the Small one', nothing of the creeds equals it as he indicated, and it was especially marked. And the shaykh Abu Muhammad 'Abd Allah al-Waryajli had vowed upon himself that the 'Small Creed of al-Sanusi' would never leave him, and he placed it in his pocket despite his great rank and immense fairness. He said: I entered Paradise and saw Ibrahim al-Khalil (peace be upon him) teaching boys the 'Creed of al-Sanusi', and they were studying it on tablets, reciting it aloud.'

Commentary
This passage is a biographical narrative compiled by a later author to illustrate the acceptance, opposition, and miraculous confirmation of Imam al-Sanusi's creedal works. It begins with a quote from Sanusi's own commentary 'al-Minhaj al-Sadid' describing the unique style of the poetic creed (manzuma) by Abu al-'Abbas al-Jaza'iri. The quote praises the poem for combining pleasing verse with rigorous rational proofs for beliefs, and for adding Sufi exhortations to awaken the soul, following the method of the Qur'an and the Sunnah. This is presented as an ideal method of teaching both the elite and the common. The narrative then turns to the opposition Sanusi faced from jealous scholars of his time. Despite initial success, opposition arose, but it subsided by divine mercy. The historian al-Hadiki reports a story: when Sanusi authored some of his creeds, many scholars denied him and spoke ill, causing him distress. He then saw a dream of 'Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him) threatening him for fearing people. After this, his sadness left, he became strong against the deniers, and they were silenced and eventually acknowledged his virtue. The author then suggests that Sanusi's success in kalam and tawhid was due to his good opinion of the science and pure intention. He quotes Sanusi's own statement that no outward science yields knowledge of Allah and watchfulness except tawhid, and that through tawhid understanding of all sciences opens and fear of Allah increases. This indicates Sanusi's foundational view. As a result, he rose to leadership in the field, his works on tawhid were praised, and they became unmatched. One shaykh vowed to always carry Sanusi's small creed in his pocket, showing its high value. Finally, a student of Sanusi reports a dream in which a deceased righteous relative saw Prophet Ibrahim (peace be upon him) teaching the 'Creed of al-Sanusi' to children in Paradise, indicating divine acceptance of the creed.
