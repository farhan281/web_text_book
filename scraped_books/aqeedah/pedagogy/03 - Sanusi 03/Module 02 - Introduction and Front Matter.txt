The Value of Knowledge and Sincerity in Usul al-Din
Editorial preface on the value of knowledge and sincerity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the science of usul al-din is considered foundational for guiding people toward righteousness and sincerity.

Core Idea
Knowledge and sincerity are the twin foundations of true faith.

This editorial introduction opens with a powerful quote about respecting people of honor, knowledge, and age. The editor uses this to highlight the importance of usul al-din (foundations of religion) as a science that cultivates truthfulness and aligns inner and outer states. Let us explore how this preface sets the stage for studying Islamic creed.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening quote and editor's commentary
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how the editor first presents a saying about respect, then comments on its fairness, and finally connects it to usul al-din.
The quote is attributed to Ziyad ibn Abihi, a historical figure known for strict justice.
2
Identify the three categories of respect
Find the three things the quote advises: honoring people of honor (sharaf), venerating people of knowledge (ilm), and respecting elders (sinn). Reflect on why these are emphasized.
These categories reflect a universal order: social rank, knowledge, and age.
3
Understand the importance of usul al-din
The editor states that usul al-din helps guide people to their right paths. Consider how a science of creed (aqidah) can promote truthfulness and sincerity.
Usul al-din grounds beliefs firmly, preventing hypocrisy and discord.
4
Connect knowledge with sincerity
The passage links outward truthfulness (sincerity) with inward soundness. Reflect on how proper knowledge of usul al-din can align one's inner and outer self.
This is a key theme in Islamic theology: iman must be genuine.

Commentary
This passage is an editorial introduction that opens with a quoted saying attributed to someone (likely Ziyad ibn Abihi, as implied). The quote advises three things: honoring people of honor (dhuwi al-sharaf), venerating people of knowledge (dhuwi al-'ilm), and respecting elders (dhuwi al-asnan). The editor then comments that even if Ziyad was unjust in his actions, he was fair in this saying. The editor then emphasizes the importance of the science of usul al-din (foundations of religion) in guiding people, connecting it to sincerity and inner purity. The passage concludes with a note about the publisher's efforts in reviving the Sanusi 'Aqidah series. The quote and commentary together establish the value of knowledge and proper social hierarchies, setting the stage for a work on Islamic creed.

Why Sharh al-Aqida al-Sughra is Special
Editorial introduction praising Sanusi's work and quoting his own words.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Imam al-Sanusi's Sharh al-Aqida al-Sughra holds a special place among his works and how it uniquely anchors Islamic creed in the shahada.

Core Idea
This small book is a unique gift that encapsulates all doctrines of tawhid in the simple phrase of the shahada.

Before diving into the theological arguments of the text, it is valuable to appreciate the book's own origin story as shared by its editor. In this introduction, we learn that this concise commentary was loved by the Imam himself, who considered it a divine blessing that summarizes all Sunni creed in the foundational formula of Islam: there is no god but Allah, Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the editorial introduction
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice the tone of praise and tenderness in describing Sanusi's work.
This passage is from the editor, not from Sanusi's own teaching.
2
Understand why Sanusi loved this book
The editor compares Sanusi's works to pious sons; this commentary is the most dutiful. Look for the tenderness in the final lines and the supplication for its readers.
3
Discover the unique method
Sanusi's innovation was grounding all Sunni doctrines in the shahada ('La ilaha illa Allah Muhammad Rasul Allah') in an unprecedented way. This made the book stand out.
This method is considered his great opening (fath).
4
Reflect on the promise of reward
Sanusi promises that whoever memorizes this creed can 'frolic in Paradise'. This shows his high regard for the book's spiritual benefit.

Commentary
This passage is an editorial introduction that praises Imam al-Sanusi's 'Sharh al-Aqida al-Sughra' and quotes the Imam's own words about his book. The editor begins by comparing al-Sanusi's works to dutiful sons, and states that this commentary is the most dutiful and beloved to him. This is evident from the tenderness that appears in the final lines, where al-Sanusi offers a heartfelt supplication for anyone who takes this book to heart, acts on its recommendations to constantly remember Allah and repeat the word of tawhid. The editor then notes that al-Sanusi's unique achievement was to ground all Sunni doctrines in the single formula 'there is no god but Allah, Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah' in an unprecedented way. This brought him spiritual openings (fath) and joy. The Imam declares that whoever memorizes this creed may 'frolic in Paradise' as a reward. Next, al-Sanusi himself describes the book as a divine gift: though small in size, it contains abundant knowledge and all the doctrines of tawhid. He claims no equivalent exists and it surpasses major works. Finally, al-Sanusi praises his own commentary (Sharh al-Aqida al-Sughra) as a concise explanation that completes the objective, removes obscurities, and leads to the 'alchemy of happiness' and 'elixir of salvation.'

Praise and Widespread Acceptance of the Sanusi Creed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the historical reception and widespread acclaim of Imam al-Sanusi's creed, as evidenced by dreams, scholarly practices, and poetic praise.

Core Idea
The Sanusi creed was universally embraced across centuries, not just a text but a living tradition.

This passage offers a collection of testimonies—from dreams of Paradise to scholarly vows and poetic praise—showing how the Sanusi creed became a cornerstone of Islamic theological education. The author presents these as 'glad tidings' of the creed's merit, establishing its value for the student of knowledge.
