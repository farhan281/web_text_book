Al-Hamd and Al-Shukr: Definitions and Distinction
Sanusi defines al-hamd and al-shukr and explains their mutual relationship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise definitions of al-hamd and al-shukr in Islamic theology and how they differ in scope and means.

Core Idea
Praise is for perfection, gratitude is for favors; they overlap but are not identical.

Sanusi begins by clarifying two fundamental terms in Islamic theology: al-hamd (praise) and al-shukr (gratitude). Though often used interchangeably, they have distinct definitions and applications. This passage establishes their precise meanings and explains how they intersect.

Guided Path
1
Read the definitions
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how Sanusi defines al-hamd first, then al-shukr.
The definitions are precise; pay attention to the key terms.
2
Examine the refinement in al-hamd
Sanusi explains why he says 'in speech' instead of 'by the tongue'. This is to include both eternal (divine) and temporal (human) praise.
This distinction is subtle but important for theological precision.
3
Contrast the two definitions
Note that al-hamd is praise for any beautiful attribute (perfection or beneficence), while al-shukr is praise in response to a favor, and can be expressed by tongue, heart, or limbs.
4
Understand the relationship
Sanusi says they have 'generality and particularity in one respect'. Learn the two dimensions: hamd is broader in object (all perfection), shukr is broader in locus (multiple modes).

Classification of Cause, Condition, and Preventer
Sanusi classifies cause, condition, and preventer by their logical effects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinct logical roles of cause, condition, and preventer in Islamic legal theory, and how each affects the existence or non-existence of a ruling.

Core Idea
Cause works both ways; condition only stops; preventer only blocks existence.

This passage from Sanusi's 'Umm al-Barahin' concludes his discussion of three key concepts that govern how legal rulings are affected by external factors. Understanding these categories is essential for grasping how scholars reason about obligations and prohibitions. Sanusi defines each term precisely through their logical effects on the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Read the definitions and examples carefully.
The passage defines cause, condition, and preventer in a logical framework. Pay attention to how each term's existence or non-existence affects the ruling. The examples (year for zakat, menstruation for prayer) illustrate the abstract definitions.
Notice that the condition's non-existence always negates, but its existence doesn't guarantee.
2
Identify the logical pattern for each term.
The passage summarizes: cause affects both sides, condition only non-existence side, preventer only existence side. Try to map these patterns onto the examples.
This logical structure is foundational for kalam discussions on causality.
3
Reflect on why this distinction matters.
Consider how this classification helps avoid confusion about when a ruling is obligatory or not. The preventer, for example, does not automatically make the ruling exist when absent.
The full treatment belongs to usul al-fiqh, as Sanusi mentions.

The Fourfold Classification of Knowable Things
Sanusi classifies known things into four exhaustive categories based on logical criteria.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourfold classification of knowable things in kalam logic and how each category is defined by the criteria of coexistence and removal.

Core Idea
All pairs of knowable things fall into exactly one of four categories based on their ability to coexist and be removed.

This passage from Sanusi presents a fundamental logical tool in kalam: the exhaustive classification of any two known things. By applying two simple criteria—whether they can coexist and whether they can both be absent—theologians can precisely categorize relationships. This classification underpins many arguments about divine attributes and creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Carefully read the Arabic passage together with the English translation. Notice how Sanusi introduces the categories and then explains each with examples.
The passage begins by noting that contradictories and correlatives are included under opposites in a broader sense.
2
Identify the two core criteria
Look for the two criteria Sanusi uses: possibility of coexistence (الاجتماع) and possibility of removal (الارتفاع). Understand that 'removal' means both can be absent simultaneously.
These criteria are the basis for the entire classification.
3
Work through the four divisions
Follow the logical tree: first check if coexistence is possible; if yes, they are 'different'. If not, check if removal is possible; if no, they are 'contradictories'. If removal is possible, then check if they differ in essence; if yes, they are 'opposites'; if no, they are 'likes'.
Each division is accompanied by an example: speech and sitting (different), existence and non-existence (contradictories), motion and rest (opposites), two instances of whiteness (likes).
4
Test your understanding with examples
Try classifying new pairs such as heat and cold, or knowledge and ignorance. Check which criteria apply and see if you can place them correctly.
Heat and cold are opposites; knowledge and ignorance are contradictories.

The two contradictories and the two correlatives are included in the two opposites. And because of this they say: The known things are confined to four divisions: the two likes, the two opposites, the two different, and the two contradictories. Because if two known things can possibly coexist, they are the two different; and if with that their removal is not possible, they are the two contradictories; and if with that their removal is possible, then either they differ in essence or not: the first are the two opposites, and the second are the two likes. Commentary
This passage defines the logical classification of known pairs into four exhaustive categories. The author begins by stating that contradictories (النقيضين) and correlatives (المتضايفين) are subsumed under opposites (الضدين) in a broader sense. Then he presents the standard fourfold division: likes (المثلين), opposites (الضدين), different (الخلافين), and contradictories (النقيضين). The criteria are two: possibility of coexistence (اجتماع) and possibility of removal (ارتفاع). If two things can coexist, they are 'different' (e.g., speech and sitting). If they cannot coexist and also cannot both be absent simultaneously, they are 'contradictories' (e.g., existence and non-existence). If they cannot coexist but can both be absent (i.e., there is a middle state), then if they differ in essence they are 'opposites' (e.g., motion and rest), and if they are identical in essence they are 'likes' (e.g., two instances of whiteness). The author then clarifies each division with examples. Note the careful distinction: likes and opposites both allow removal, but likes are identical in quiddity while opposites are not. The classification is exhaustive for all pairs of known things.

Proof That Two Likenesses Cannot Coexist
Sanusi gives a proof that two likes cannot coexist.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's reductio ad absurdum proof that two like accidents cannot coexist in the same substance.

Core Idea
Two likes cannot coexist because that would force two opposites to coexist.

Sanusi now presents a proof from his companions (Ash'ari scholars) that two like accidents cannot occupy the same subject. The argument is a clever reductio: assuming two likes can coexist leads to an impossible conclusion—the coexistence of opposites. As you read, notice how the proof relies on the established principle that a substrate that can receive a thing can also receive its opposite.

This concurrence and connection of the originated power with those actions without any effect on its part is what is termed in technical language and in the Law as 'acquisition' (al-kasb wa al-iktisāb), and on its basis actions are attributed to the servant, as His saying the Exalted: 'To it is what it has earned and against it is what it has acquired' (Qur'an 2:286). Commentary
This passage is a definition and argumentative proof of the Ash'ari doctrine of kasb (acquisition), followed by refutations of two extreme sects and a conclusion affirming the middle path of Ahl al-Sunna. The author begins by explaining that God, by His pure choice, creates a power in humans concurrent with certain actions, and this concurrence is a condition for moral obligation. This relationship between the created power and the action, where the power has no effect at all, is technically called 'kasb' or 'iktisab'. Because of this, actions are attributed to the servant, as in the Qur'anic verse 'to it is what it has earned...'. However, invention and bringing into existence are exclusive to God alone. Next, the author distinguishes two states: when God creates in the servant a power concurrent with the action, the servant is called 'mukhtar' (voluntary agent); when God creates the action without that concurrent power, the servant is 'majbur' (compelled), like an involuntary tremble. The sign of the presence of concurrent power is that the action becomes easy according to custom, both to do and to leave. The sign of compulsion is difficulty. Everyone perceives the difference between these two states, and the Law affirms both: obligation applies only in the first state, as the verse 'God does not obligate any soul except its capacity.' The author then draws two conclusions: First, the Jabriyya are refuted—they claim all actions are equal with no concurrent power; they are innovators and contradict both law and reason. Second, the Qadariyya are refuted—they claim that the created power has an effect in actions according to the servant's will; they are innovators who associate partners with God. The correct doctrine of Ahl al-Sunna lies between these two corrupt extremes. The footnotes provide additional clarifications: e.g., power to create is God's most specific attribute; rational compulsion (denying creatorship of the servant) is actually true and does not harm free will; the Jabriyya addressed are those who affirm obligation; the Qadariyya are called 'Magians of this nation' based on a hadith; and the word 'taḥaqqaqa' is read as passive or imperative from the editor's commentary.

Refuting Extremes: Jabriyya and Qadariyya
Sanusi refutes Jabriyya and Qadariyya and affirms the Sunni middle path.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi refutes both the Jabriyya and Qadariyya and establishes the middle path of Ahl al-Sunna.

Core Idea
Ahl al-Sunna avoids jabr and qadr by affirming God's sole creation and the servant's acquisition.

Having established the doctrine of kasb, Sanusi now critiques the two extreme positions. He declares the Jabriyya innovators who deny any concurrent power, and the Qadariyya as 'Magians of this nation' who claim the servant's power has effect. The correct position lies between them.

Understanding Compound Ignorance (al-Jahl al-Murakkab)
Sanusi defines compound ignorance and gives examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of compound ignorance and why it is considered a severe intellectual affliction.

Core Idea
Compound ignorance is ignorance of one's own ignorance.

Sanusi now turns to a particularly dangerous form of ignorance that afflicts many: compound ignorance. Unlike simple ignorance, which is merely lacking knowledge, compound ignorance is a condition where a person not only believes something false but is also unaware of their error. This passage defines it and gives striking examples from the philosophers.

Commentary
This passage defines compound ignorance (al-jahl al-murakkab). Sanusi explains that it consists of two layers: first, believing something contrary to reality (simple ignorance), and second, being ignorant of that ignorance (additional ignorance). It is called 'compound' because it combines two ignorances. He gives examples: the philosophers' belief in the influence of celestial spheres and their belief in the eternity of the world, both great ignorances, and they are unaware of their ignorance, thinking they are correct. The passage ends abruptly, possibly referencing the Quranic verse 'they think that they are on guidance' (2:?)

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think compound ignorance simply means having many false beliefs.
Correction: Compound ignorance specifically involves being unaware of one's own ignorance, not just having multiple false beliefs.
