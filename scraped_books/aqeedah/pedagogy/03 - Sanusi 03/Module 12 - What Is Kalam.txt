The Shahada: The Essence of All Saving Beliefs
Sanusi defines uluhiyyah and applies it to the shahada.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the shahada (La ilaha illa Allah) summarizes all saving beliefs through the meaning of uluhiyyah as Allah's absolute independence and creation's total dependence.

Core Idea
The shahada is not just a formula but the theological essence of all saving beliefs.

Sanusi has been explaining the saving beliefs that constitute proper faith. Now he shows that all these beliefs are concentrated in the declaration of faith itself. The meaning of 'uluhiyyah' (divinity) is key: it is defined by two complementary truths—Allah's complete independence and everything else's complete dependence on Him. This passage unpacks that definition to reveal the depth of the shahada.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage slowly, then the literal translation. Notice how the author connects the shahada to the entire body of saving beliefs.
The passage is cut off at the end; we focus on what is given.
2
Identify the definition of uluhiyyah
The passage states that uluhiyyah means two things: Allah's independence (istighna') from all else, and all else's dependence (iftiqar) on Him. Find and note these two terms.
These two pillars define the core of tawhid.
3
Understand the meaning of the shahada
Sanusi then explains that 'La ilaha illa Allah' means: none is independent from everything other than Him, and everything besides Him depends upon Him, except Allah. This is a direct application of the definition.
4
Reflect on the implication
Ask yourself: How does this definition of uluhiyyah become a foundation for all other beliefs? For example, why would belief in Allah's power, knowledge, etc., flow from His independence?
This prepares you for the continuation of the text.

Commentary
This passage explains that all the saving beliefs (al-aqa'id al-munjiya) are summarized in the shahada (declaration of faith). The meaning of uluhiyyah (divinity) is defined as two things: 1) independence (istighna') of Allah from everything else, and 2) dependence (iftiqar) of everything else upon Allah. Thus the phrase 'La ilaha illa Allah' means that no entity possesses independence from all others except Allah, and everything besides Him is dependent upon Him. The passage begins to unpack the implications of Allah's independence, but is cut off at the end of the page. This shows how the core of Islamic theology is encapsulated in the very formula of tawhid.

The Meaning of Uluhiyyah and Attributes of Self-Sufficiency
Sanusi defines uluhiyyah and derives attributes from self-sufficiency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of uluhiyyah as comprising Allah's self-sufficiency and creation's dependence, and the six attributes that self-sufficiency necessitates.

Core Idea
Allah's self-sufficiency is the root of His necessary attributes.

Sanusi begins by showing that the testimony 'La ilaha illa Allah' encapsulates all creedal meanings. He then defines the essence of divinity (uluhiyyah) as two complementary aspects: Allah's absolute self-sufficiency (istighna') and the total dependence of everything else upon Him. From the first aspect, he derives six essential attributes that logically follow from self-sufficiency.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of uluhiyyah
Notice that uluhiyyah is defined by two relational concepts: istighna' (self-sufficiency) and iftiqar (dependence). The shahada expresses that only Allah has these two qualities.
2
List the six attributes from istighna'
Read the list of six attributes: existence, eternity, perpetuity, opposition to originated things, self-subsistence, and transcendence above deficiencies. Ponder how each follows from self-sufficiency.
These are the so-called 'necessary attributes' – they cannot be absent without implying need.
3
Include hearing, sight, speech
Sanusi adds that hearing, sight, and speech are also necessitated because lacking them would mean need for an originator, a substratum, or a deficiency-repeller.
This shows the interconnectedness of divine attributes.
4
Reflect on the logic
Ask yourself: why would the absence of an attribute imply need? How does self-sufficiency guarantee these attributes?

Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof section. The author begins by stating that all the previously discussed creedal meanings (from earlier lessons) are encompassed by the two testimonies (al-shahadatayn). He then defines uluhiyyah (divinity) as consisting of two components: (1) istighna' (self-sufficiency) of Allah from all others, and (2) iftiqar (dependence) of all others upon Him. Both corollaries are supported by the rhetorical question: 'How could that be when He is the Self-Sufficient?' The passage is logically structured: definition, then attribute derivation, then corollaries.

Corollaries of Divine Self-Sufficiency: No Purposes or Obligations
Sanusi derives corollaries about no purposes and no obligations from self-sufficiency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Allah's self-sufficiency entails that He has no purposes in His actions and that nothing is obligatory upon Him from possible things.

Core Idea
If Allah had purposes or obligations, He would be dependent, contradicting His self-sufficiency.

Having established that self-sufficiency necessitates certain attributes, Sanusi now draws two important corollaries. First, Allah is free from purposes (aghrad) in His actions and judgments, because having a purpose would imply need for that purpose. Second, nothing from among possible things (mumkinat) is obligatory upon Him to do or leave, because that would imply need for perfection through that act. Both conclusions rest on the same foundational principle: Allah is al-Ghani, the Self-Sufficient.

Guided Path
1
Read the corollaries carefully
The passage presents two corollaries derived from istighna': (a) Allah has no purposes in His actions, (b) Nothing is obligatory upon Him from possible things. Note the repeated rhetorical question at the end.
2
Analyze the first corollary
Why does having a purpose imply need? Because a purpose is something external that fulfills a goal. If Allah had a purpose, He would be depending on that purpose to be achieved. But He is self-sufficient, so He cannot have purposes.
This relates to divine transcendence above motives.
3
Analyze the second corollary
Why is nothing obligatory upon Allah? Because obligation would mean He needs to perform that act to attain perfection. But He is already perfect and self-sufficient. Hence, reward, punishment, or any possible thing cannot be obligatory upon Him.
This is a key Ash'ari principle: all actions of Allah are voluntary, not required.
4
Reflect on the unifying principle
Both corollaries use the same argument: if x were the case, Allah would be dependent, but He is self-sufficient. So x cannot be. How does this shape Islamic theology?

Commentary
This passage presents a grammatical argument about the famous declaration of faith 'لا إله إلا الله' specifically whether the word 'الله' after 'إلا' is a predicate (خبر) of the negative 'لا' or a substitute (بدل). Nazir al-Jaysh favors the predicate view and then lists three objections raised against it and begins to answer them. For the second objection, he explains that the exception here is 'مفرغ' (dispersed), meaning the thing excepted from is not mentioned explicitly but estimated for meaning. In such cases, the excepted item can be a predicate. He gives the example 'ما زيد إلا قائم' to show that it is accepted. The answer to the third objection is cut off in this passage.

And if you wish, you may say in the meaning of 'Ilah': He is the one independent of everything else, and everything other than Him is dependent on Him, and it is more apparent than the first meaning and closer to it, and it is the foundation for it, because no one deserves to be worshipped – i.e., everything humbles itself to Him – except one who is independent of everything else, and everything other than Him is dependent on Him. So it became clear that the second expression is better than the first, and by it the inclusion of the beliefs of faith under this honorable word becomes clear, and the believer's breast expands for the outpouring of the lights of gnosis, and he is on the shore of salvation and security from every misstep that occurred in the meaning of this noble word, and the strong and the weak enter the meadow of this honorable word; he frolics in its flowers, and stroll in the Salsabil of its rivers, and harvest from the fruits of its gnoses, and hears from the chirping of the birds of its guidance what is written for him, and for this we chose in the foundation of creed the interpretation by it of this honorable word.
Source pages p. 277

Commentary
This passage defines the word 'Ilah' (إله) and explains why one interpretation is preferred for the foundation of Islamic creed (عقيدة).

Sanusi begins by stating that among all possible divisions or meanings, only 'Ilah' as a universal term is valid. He then gives the first definition: 'Ilah' means 'the one truly worshipped' (المعبود بحق), and the name is a proper noun for the specific individual who exists as such. The meaning of 'لا إله إلا الله' on this view is: no one deserves servitude except the unique individual who is the Creator.

Then Sanusi offers a second definition: 'Ilah' is 'the one independent of everything else, while everything else is dependent on Him' (المستغني عن كل ما سواه، والمفتقر إليه كل ما عداه). He argues that this second meaning is more apparent (أظهر) and closer (أقرب) to the truth, and it is actually the foundation (أصل) of the first definition. The reasoning is that worship is only deserved by one who possesses absolute self-sufficiency and upon whom all others depend.

Sanusi then declares the second expression superior. He lists several benefits: it clarifies how all beliefs of faith are included under this word, expands the believer's heart for divine knowledge, provides safety from errors in understanding, and makes the meaning accessible to both the strong and weak in understanding. The imagery of a garden with flowers, rivers, fruits, and birds illustrates the richness of this interpretation.

Finally, Sanusi states that for these reasons, he has chosen this second interpretation as the foundation of the creed (أصل العقيدة).

The Kalima's Implication of the Three Rational Judgments
Sanusi shows the kalima implies the three rational judgments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how 'La ilaha illa Allah' implicitly contains the three rational categories about Allah, and what kind of implication is involved.

Core Idea
The shahada implies the three rational judgments—necessary, impossible, possible—about Allah.

Sanusi now brings his discussion to a close by showing that the very words of the Islamic declaration of faith contain, in a subtle way, the entire framework of rational theology. The kalima is not just a formula of belief; it implies the three necessary categories that every mukallaf must know. But this implication is not a direct logical one—it works through entailments.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the literal translation. Let the author's conclusion settle.
2
Identify the three categories
The passage names three divisions: wajib (necessary), mustahil (impossible), and ja'iz (possible). Notice how they are presented as what must be known about Allah.
These categories are the foundation of all attributes of Allah.
3
Understand the type of implication
Sanusi clarifies that 'tadammun' here is linguistic, not logical. This means the kalima conveys these categories as part of its meaning, not as a logical deduction.
Linguistic implication is about the word's meaning; logical implication is about necessary consequences.
4
See how they are established
The categories are established as 'lawazim' (entailments), not by direct correspondence or inclusion. This means they are necessary consequences of the kalima.
This distinction helps avoid overstating what the kalima directly says.
5
Connect to prior discussion
Realize that this is a concluding summary. The entire earlier discussion on rational judgments is now tied back to the shahada itself.
