Learning Objective

Core Idea
In divine action, knowledge guides will, and will directs power.

This passage from Sanusi's work presents a concise yet crucial principle in Ash'ari theology: the three divine attributes of power, will, and knowledge do not operate simultaneously but in a specific causal order. Understanding this hierarchy is key to grasping how God's attributes relate to His actions and the creation of possible things.

Why Divine Power and Will Do Not Attach to the Necessary and Impossible
Sanusi proves the non-attachment of power and will to necessary and impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why divine power and will logically cannot apply to the necessary and impossible, and why this is not a deficiency.

Core Idea
Divine power and will only attach to possible things; logical impossibility is not impotence.

Sanusi presents a logical proof that the attributes of power and will, being effective, can only produce effects that are possible—i.e., those that admit non-existence and existence. The necessary being (God) cannot be an effect because it never had non-existence, and the impossible cannot be an effect because it can never exist. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this core theological principle.

Guided Path
1
Understand the key terms
Familiarize yourself with the Arabic terms: power (قدرة), will (إرادة), necessary (واجب), impossible (مستحيل), effect (أثر).
2
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to get the overall argument.
3
Follow the two-part proof
Notice how Sanusi treats the necessary and impossible separately, each leading to an absurdity (tahsil al-hasil and qalb al-haqiqa).
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Understand that the non-attachment is not a deficiency; rather, attachment would lead to greater absurdities.

And what does not accept existence at all, like the impossible, also cannot be an effect for them; otherwise, it would entail the inversion of reality (qalb al-ḥaqīqa), by making the impossible return as exactly the possible. So there is no deficiency at all in the non-attachment of the eternal power and will to the necessary and impossible. Commentary
This passage is a proof (dalil) explaining why divine power and will do not extend to the necessary (al-wajib) and the impossible (al-mustahil). The argument proceeds as follows: (1) Power and will are effective attributes (sifatan mu'athiratani) – they produce an effect. (2) An effect must exist after non-existence (min lazim al-athar an yujada ba'da 'adam). (3) The necessary (e.g., God) cannot be preceded by non-existence; therefore it cannot be an effect. If we assumed it could, that would be 'tahsīl al-hāsil' – obtaining what is already obtained, a logical absurdity. (4) The impossible cannot receive existence at all; it cannot be an effect. If we assumed it could, that would be 'qalb al-haqiqa' – inversion of reality, making the impossible into the possible. (5) Therefore, the non-attachment of power and will to these categories is not a deficiency; on the contrary, if they were attached, deficiency would arise because it would imply absurd consequences like the ability to annihilate God or to make a false god. So God's power is perfect; its scope is limited to possible things by logical necessity, not by incapability.

Thus the attributes of meanings are the causes (ʿilal) and the qualitative attributes are their necessary concomitants (lāzima). The term 'maʿnawiyya' comes from 'maʿna' (meaning) with the relative ya', and the waw replaces the alif in 'maʿna'. Finally, Sanusi lists the seven: being powerful, willing, knowing, living, hearing, seeing, and speaking.

Proof of God's Remaining (Baqa')
Sanusi concludes the proof that God's remaining is as necessary as His pre-eternity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof for the necessity of God's remaining based on the impossibility of two equal possibilities without a preponderator.

Core Idea
God's remaining is as necessary as His pre-eternity, proven by a single logical step.

Sanusi concludes his proof for the attribute of baqa' (remaining) with a concise argument. He ties it directly to the earlier proof for qidam (pre-eternity), showing that the same logic applies to God's eternal future existence. This passage demonstrates how kalam uses the principle of the preponderator to link two essential divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Note the rhetorical question and the concluding phrase.
The passage is a single argument, not a list.
2
Understand the key terms
Identify the terms 'بقاء' (baqa'), 'قدم' (qidam), and 'فاعل مرجح' (preponderating agent).
These are central to the argument.
3
Follow the logical structure
The argument: if two equally possible outcomes exist without a preponderator, it's impossible. But we already proved God's pre-eternity is necessary. Therefore, His remaining must also be necessary.
4
Ask: why is this proof valid?
Consider that if God could cease to exist, then He would be contingent in the future, which contradicts the impossibility of equal possibilities without a preponderator.
This connection is what Sanusi uses.

Source Text

مقابله المساوي له في القبولِ مِنْ غيرِ فاعل مرجح ، كيف وقد سبق قريباً بالبرهان القاطع وجوب قدمِهِ جَلَّ وعزَّ ؟! فإذاً ؛ يجبُ بقاؤُهُ تبارك وتعالى كما وجب قدمه
Its counterpart equal to it in acceptance without a preponderating agent – how [could that be] when we have already proven by decisive proof the necessity of His pre-eternity, may He be exalted and glorified?! Therefore, His remaining, blessed and exalted, is necessary as His pre-eternity was necessary.

Commentary
This passage is a short argument that concludes the proof for the attribute of baqa' (remaining). The argument runs: if there were two equal possibilities without a preponderator, that would be impossible. But we have already proven that God is necessarily pre-eternal (qadim). Therefore, He cannot be contingent; He must also be eternal in the future (baqi). The conclusion is stated directly: His remaining is necessary just as His pre-eternity was necessary.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think that proving qidam automatically proves baqa' without additional reasoning.
Correction: This passage shows that baqa' requires a separate argument based on the impossibility of equality without a preponderator, not a direct consequence of qidam.

Key Takeaway
God's remaining (baqa') is a necessary attribute proven by the same principle of impossibility of equal possibilities without a preponderator that underlies the proof for His pre-eternity.
