The Impossibility of Qualifying Attributes
Proof that attributes cannot be further qualified, leading to God's independence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why an attribute cannot be further qualified by another attribute and how this establishes that God is a self-subsistent essence, not an attribute of another.

Core Idea
God is the subject of attributes, not an attribute itself.

Sanusi argues that if an attribute could accept another attribute, it would lead to an infinite regress. Since God is necessarily described by multiple attributes, He cannot be an attribute of something else—He is the independent essence. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage carefully
Read the Arabic text once and then the translation. Notice how Sanusi starts with a classification of attributes and then builds his proof.
2
Understand the premise about acceptance
Sanusi says that if an attribute accepted another, it could never be free of it or its opposite, leading to an infinite chain. Grasp this conditional reasoning.
This is the core of the proof.
3
See the conclusion and its application to God
Sanusi concludes that attributes cannot be further qualified by positive attributes, and then applies this to God: God has attributes but is not an attribute for another.
4
Reflect on the significance
Ask yourself: If God were an attribute of something else, what would that imply? How does this proof protect God's transcendence?

وأما برهانُ وجوبِ استغنائِهِ جلَّ وعزَّ عن المخصص؛ أي: الفاعل: فهو أنه لو احتاجَ إلى الفاعل لكانَ حادثاً، وذلك محال؛ لما عرفت بالبرهان القاطعِ مِنْ وجوبِ قدمِهِ تعالى وبقائِهِ.
فتبيَّن بهذين البرهانينِ: وجوب الغنى المطلق لمولانا جل وعلا عن كل ما سواه، وهو معنى قيامِهِ جَلَّ وعزَّ بنفسِهِ.
Essential (nāfsiyya) because essential and privative (salbiyya) qualities are possessed by essences (dhawāt) and attributes (maʿānī); for if an attribute (ṣifa) accepted another attribute, it would necessarily never be free of it, or of its like, or of its opposite, and the same would follow in the other attribute that inhered in it, and so on; because the acceptance is essential (nafsī), so there must be unification among similars, and that is impossible; due to what necessarily follows from it of infinite regress (tasalsul) and the entry of infinite attributes into existence, and that is impossible.
Therefore, the attribute does not accept being qualified by a positive attribute other than the essential one inherent in it; I mean: attributes of meaning (ṣifāt al-maʿānī) and attributional (maʿnawiyya). And our Master, Mighty and Exalted, the decisive proof has demonstrated the necessity of His being qualified by attributes of meaning and attributional attributes, so it follows that He is a high essence described by exalted attributes, and He is not in Himself an attribute for another—exalted is He above that, a great exaltation.
As for the proof of the necessity of His, Mighty and Exalted, independence from the specificator (al-mukhaṣṣiṣ), i.e., the agent: it is that if He needed an agent, He would be temporal (ḥādith), and that is impossible; because you have known by the decisive proof the necessity of His, Exalted, pre-eternality (qidam) and permanence (baqāʾ).
Thus it is made clear by these two proofs: the necessity of absolute independence (al-ghinā al-muṭlaq) for our Master, Mighty and Exalted, from everything other than Him, and that is the meaning of His, Mighty and Exalted, being self-subsistent (qiyāmuhu bi-nafsihi).

Commentary
This passage presents two proofs for God's absolute self-sufficiency and concludes with their combined implication. The passage is a continuous argument: first, a proof that attributes cannot be further qualified, establishing that God is not an attribute of another; second, a proof that God cannot need a creator, establishing His self-subsistence.

The author begins with a statement: 'Essential (nāfsiyya) because essential and privative (salbiyya) qualities are possessed by essences and attributes.' This refers to the fact that essential and privative attributes are directly inherent in essences or other attributes. He then argues that if an attribute were to accept another attribute, it would lead to an infinite regress or an impossible combination. Specifically, if an attribute A accepted attribute B, then A could never be free of B or its opposite, and then the second attribute B would also have to be qualified by another, ad infinitum. This regress is impossible because it would entail an infinite number of attributes existing, which is absurd.

From this, the author concludes: 'Therefore, the attribute does not accept being qualified by a positive attribute other than the essential one inherent in it.' That is, attributes cannot be further described by positive attributes like the attributes of meaning (ṣifāt al-maʿānī) and attributional attributes (ṣifāt maʿnawiyya). Then he applies this to God: since decisive proof shows that God must be described by attributes of meaning and attributional attributes, it follows that He is a high essence described by exalted attributes. Furthermore, He is not in Himself an attribute for another being, meaning He is not a quality of something else—He is the independent subject of attributes, not a predicate. The author exclaims 'exalted is He above that' to emphasize God's transcendence.

The second proof begins with 'As for the proof of the necessity of His independence from the specificator (al-mukhaṣṣiṣ)'. This proof argues that if God needed a creator (a specificator or agent), then He would be temporal (ḥādith), which contradicts His necessary pre-eternality (qidam) and permanence (baqāʾ). Since we already know by decisive proof that God is eternal and permanent, He cannot depend on any agent for His existence.

The conclusion brings both proofs together: 'Thus it is made clear by these two proofs: the necessity of absolute independence for our Master from everything other than Him, and that is the meaning of His being self-subsistent (qiyāmuhu bi-nafsihi).' The first proof shows God is not an attribute of another; the second shows He does not need an external cause. Together they establish that God is absolutely independent and self-subsistent.

Correction: Sanusi shows that if attributes could be qualified, it would lead to an infinite regress, which is impossible. Therefore, God is the ultimate subject, not an attribute.

God's Independence from Any Creator
Proof that God cannot need a creator, concluding His absolute independence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that God cannot need an external creator, and that this establishes His absolute self-sufficiency.

Core Idea
If God needed a creator, He would be temporal—a contradiction.

The second proof is simpler but equally decisive: if God depended on an agent for existence, He would be contingent and temporal. Since we already know God is eternal and permanent, such dependence is impossible. This lesson explains that reasoning and its conclusion.

1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic and translation. Note how Sanusi states the proof concisely.
2
Understand the conditional
Sanusi says: if God needed a specificator (agent), He would be temporal. Accept this conditional; why is being temporal impossible for God?
Because God is necessarily eternal and permanent.
3
Connect to the prior proof
The conclusion says 'Thus it is made clear by these two proofs...'. See how both proofs together establish absolute independence.
4
Reflect on self-subsistence
The ultimate meaning is God's qiyām bi-nafs (self-subsistence). Think about what it means for something to exist without a cause.

The Dependency Chain of Divine Attributes
Sanusi proves the necessity of four attributes via dependency chain.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Allah's power, will, knowledge, and life are necessary for any contingent event to exist.

Core Idea
Every event depends on four essential divine attributes.

Sanusi begins by establishing a logical dependency among the divine attributes. He argues that without each of these four attributes, no event could occur. This passage lays the foundation for proving that Allah must possess them eternally.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage aloud if you can, then read the English translation. Notice the flow of the argument.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the dependency chain
Trace the reasoning: power depends on will, will depends on knowledge, and all three depend on life. Understand why each link is necessary.
Think about why life is a condition for the other attributes.
3
Analyze the conclusion
The author concludes that if any attribute is absent, no event can exist. Reflect on why this proves the necessity of these attributes.
This is a general proof for all contingent events.

Proof of the necessity of power, will, knowledge, and life: For if any of them were absent, then none of the contingent events would exist. It has already been presented to you that the effect of eternal power depends on His will, may He be exalted, for that effect, and His will for that effect depends on knowledge of it, and the description with power, will, and knowledge depends on His description with life, since it is a condition for them, and the existence of the conditioned without its condition is impossible. Therefore, the existence of any event whatsoever depends on the description of its agent with these four attributes. So if any of them were absent, none of the contingent events would exist, due to his incapability then. And by this it is made clear: the necessity of His being described, may He be exalted, with these attributes in pre-eternity; for if they were originated, the origination of them would depend on His being described, may He be exalted, with their likes before them, then we transfer the discussion to their likes, leading to infinite regress, which is impossible. Thus the existence of those attributes on this assumption would be impossible, and that leads to the absurd.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof (burhan) for the necessity of Allah's attributes of power (qudra), will (irada), knowledge ('ilm), and life (hayat). The argument proceeds in two stages. First, it establishes that these four attributes are necessary for the existence of any contingent event. The author explains that the effect of eternal power depends on divine will; will depends on knowledge; and power, will, and knowledge each depend on life as a condition. Since a conditioned thing cannot exist without its condition, any event requires its agent to possess these four attributes. Consequently, if any of these attributes were absent, no event could exist, as the agent would be incapable. Second, the author argues that these attributes must be eternal (azaliyya). If they were originated (haditha), then their origination would itself require that Allah already be described with similar attributes prior to them, leading to an infinite regress (tasalsul), which is impossible. Therefore, their existence as originated is impossible, and they must be eternal. This proof simultaneously establishes the existence, eternity, and universal applicability of these attributes, as noted by commentators.

The Eternity of Divine Attributes
Sanusi proves the eternity of four attributes via infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Allah's attributes of power, will, knowledge, and life must be eternal, not created.

Core Idea
If attributes were created, an infinite regress would follow, which is impossible.

After establishing the necessity of the four attributes, Sanusi now addresses their temporal status. He argues that these attributes must be eternal, because if they were originated, their origination would require prior similar attributes, leading to an absurd infinite regress. This lesson unpacks that crucial argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the eternity argument
Read the Arabic and translation for the second part. Notice the conditional reasoning: 'if they were originated... then infinite regress... impossible'.
Focus on the logical structure: assumption → consequence → impossibility.
2
Understand the infinite regress argument
The author says that if these attributes came into existence at some point, then before that they must have been absent, and their coming into existence would itself require prior attributes (like power, will, knowledge) to create them. This creates an endless chain.
Infinite regress (tasalsul) is considered impossible in Islamic theology.
3
Connect to the conclusion
Since infinite regress is impossible, the assumption that attributes are originated is false. Therefore, they must be eternal.
This argument is a standard way to prove the eternity of divine attributes.
