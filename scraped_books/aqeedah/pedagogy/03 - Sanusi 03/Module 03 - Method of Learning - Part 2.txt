Grammatical Rules of Exception (Istithna')
Grammar of exception with mushakala and sentence types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the grammatical rules governing the exception particle 'illa' in Arabic, including the conditions for preference between nominative and accusative and the distinction between complete and incomplete sentences.

Core Idea
'Illa' in complete sentences extracts; in incomplete sentences, it affirms the negated ruling.

This passage from Sanusi's commentary establishes the grammatical foundation needed to analyze the Shahada. Before addressing the theological implications, we must grasp how 'illa' functions in different sentence structures. The author explains the rules of exception (istithna') and the role of congruence (mushakala) in determining case endings.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the two types of sentences
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice that the author discusses complete and incomplete sentences separately.
The passage is technical; take your time.
2
Understand the concept of mushakala
Mushakala (congruence) affects whether accusative or nominative is preferred. The author gives examples where this happens.
If there is congruence, the two forms become equal.
3
Distinguish complete and incomplete sentences
In complete sentences, 'illa' extracts the exception from a prior full meaning. In incomplete sentences, something must be estimated before 'illa' to make extraction possible.
4
See why estimation (taqdir) is needed
When the sentence before 'illa' is incomplete, an element must be estimated to avoid a gap in meaning. This estimation corrects the meaning.
Estimation is only for meaning, not for grammar.
5
Reflect on the ruling after 'illa'
In incomplete sentences, the objective is to affirm the negated ruling for what comes after 'illa', not to perform exception.
This becomes crucial for the Shahada.

Commentary
This passage is a grammatical and theological analysis of the Shahada 'La ilaha illa Allah' with a specific focus on the function of the word 'illa' (except). It begins by discussing a general rule of preferences (tarjih) in exception constructions due to congruence (mushakala), and then moves to the specific application to the Shahada.

The author first explains that in normal exception sentences, when the construction is complete (tam), 'illa' is used to extract the exception from the preceding statement. For example, 'Qama al-qawmu illa Zaydan' means the people stood except Zayd. This works because the sentence before 'illa' is complete and conveys a meaning from which Zayd is excluded. Similarly, in negative sentences like 'Ma qama al-qawmu illa Zaydan', the exception also extracts Zayd from the negation, resulting in exclusivity.

However, the Shahada 'La ilaha illa Allah' is not a complete sentence before 'illa' because 'ilaha' is indefinite and the predicate is omitted. According to the rules of grammar, if the sentence before 'illa' is incomplete, an estimated element must be supplied to make it complete so that exception can occur. But the author argues that if 'illa' is treated as purely for exception, the intended meaning of the Shahada—negating divinity from everything and affirming it only for Allah—would not be achieved. This is because in a complete sentence with an estimated predicate, the ruling after 'illa' (negation in positive sentences, affirmation in negative ones) is not universally agreed upon; some grammarians hold that what comes after 'illa' is not spoken about at all.

An objector asks: if that is the case, how can 'La ilaha illa Allah' be tawhid? The author responds (qultu) that the construction is tawhid according to customary understanding ('urf), and because all rational people agree that divinity belongs to Allah alone; disbelief only arises by adding another deity. Therefore, the simple negation of other gods is sufficient for tawhid, regardless of the grammatical technicalities. The passage concludes by noting that Nazir al-Jaysh will continue the discussion based on his research.

In summary, the passage guards against over-rigid grammatical analysis that might undermine the straightforward theological meaning of the Shahada, emphasizing that customary usage and the universal agreement on tawhid are sufficient.

Implication for Tawhid: Objection and Answer
Objection and resolution regarding tawhid from exception grammar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the grammatical analysis of 'La ilaha illa Allah' leads to an objection against it being tawhid, and how the author resolves this objection through the concept of custom ('urf) and universal agreement.

Core Idea
The Shahada is tawhid by custom and consensus, not by strict grammatical exception rules.

Having established the grammar of exception, the author now applies it to the Shahada. An objector argues that if 'illa' is used purely for exception, the desired meaning of tawhid is not achieved. The author responds by appealing to customary usage and the universal agreement on Allah's exclusive divinity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two objectives of the Shahada
The author states the Shahada has two goals: negating divinity from everything and affirming it for Allah. Read the passage to see this.
These objectives are from earlier discussion.
2
Understand the objection
If 'illa' is purely for exception, the speech before it must be complete. In the Shahada, it is incomplete, so an estimated predicate is needed. Then the ruling after 'illa' is not universally agreed upon; some say it is left unspoken. Thus, tawhid would not be achieved.
The objection is based on grammatical technicalities.
3
See the author's response
The author responds that the Shahada is tawhid according to custom ('urf) and because all rational people agree that divinity belongs to Allah alone. Disbelief is only by associating partners.
This overrides the grammatical objection.
4
Reflect on the use of custom
The author prioritizes customary understanding over strict grammatical rules when the meaning is clear to all.
This shows a balance between language and theology.

The True Cause of Misguidance: Blind Imitation
Sanusi explains that innovators are deceived by habits and outward texts, and their reliance is blind imitation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sanusi identifies blind imitation of habits and outward texts as the primary cause of misguidance for innovators.

Core Idea
The innovators' greatest error is blind imitation, not reasoned reflection guided by revelation.

Sanusi diagnoses the root cause of theological deviation. He points to two deceptive sources: the habitual patterns of the world and the surface meanings of scripture. The real problem is that innovators rely on uncritical imitation instead of the pure rational insights illuminated by divine revelation. This lesson unpacks that diagnosis.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the two deceptive elements
Identify the two things that deceive innovators: the habits (customs) and the outward (apparent) meanings of the Qur'an and Sunnah.
Habits here refer to the regular patterns of the created world.
2
Understand the charge of blind imitation
Sanusi says the innovators' greatest reliance is blind imitation (taqlid) of things that cannot validly be imitated.
This includes habits and other things, not just texts.
3
Contrast with the proper method
Note that innovators abandoned pure rational considerations that are illuminated by the Book and Sunnah.

Commentary
This passage explains the proper practice of dhikr (remembrance of Allah). The author states that the one who remembers Allah should say after the declaration 'There is no god but Allah' the testimony 'Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah', in order to preserve the light of tawhid by placing it within the protection of the Sharia. The author then generalizes: in every act of dhikr, the believer should not neglect to mention the Prophet, either by sending blessings on him immediately after, or by affirming his message along with blessings, or any other form of showing reverence and adherence to him. The reason is that the Prophet is described as 'the greatest gate of Allah' (al-bāb al-aʿẓam), meaning that all good in this world and the Hereafter is only attained through connection with him. Consequently, whoever neglects to mention the Prophet and to cling to his Sharia will not achieve his objective; rather, he will be thrown into a 'prison of separation' (sijn al-qaṭīʿa), deprived of the goods of both worlds. The author reinforces this by stating that the Prophet is the guide (dalīl) of all creation to Allah. How then can anyone reach Allah while forgetting to mention his guide? This rhetorical question underscores the absolute necessity of always associating the Prophet with tawhid in one's remembrance.
