The Doctrine of Acquisition (al-Kasb) and Human Power
Sanusi states that human power has no causal effect; actions are created by God directly.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari doctrine of kasb: that human power has no causal effect, and actions are created directly by God, with human power merely accompanying them by divine custom.

Core Idea
Human power has no causal effect; God alone creates all actions.

In this passage, Sanusi builds on the earlier proof of God's sole creative agency to clarify the controversial doctrine of acquisition (al-kasb). He directly addresses a common concern: if God creates everything, what role do we have? The answer is that our power (qudrah) accompanies the action but does not cause it. This lesson unpacks the precise wording and logic of Sanusi's conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text once, noting the key terms: أثر, قدرة, اختيارية, عادة. Then read the translation to confirm your understanding.
The passage ends mid-sentence; the intended meaning is clear from context.
2
Identify the key claim
Find the sentence that states 'there is no effect for our power.' Notice the list of voluntary actions that follow.
3
Understand the role of human power
Sanusi says our power is a created accident that 'accompanies' voluntary actions. What does 'accompanies' mean? How does it relate to God's custom (al-ʿādah)?
The phrase 'at the presence of that power—not by it' is crucial.
4
Connect to the doctrine of kasb
Reflect on why this position is called 'acquisition' (kasb). Human power 'acquires' the action in the sense of being concomitant, not causal.
This distinguishes Ash'ari theology from Mu'tazilite and Qadari views.

وَبِهَذَا تَعْرِفُ : أَنْ لَا أَثَرَ لِقُدْرَتِنَا فِي شَيْءٍ مِنْ أَفْعَالِنَا الِاخْتِيَارِيَّةِ ؛ كَحَرَكَاتِنَا وَسَكَنَاتِنَا ، وَقِيَامِنَا وَقُعُودِنَا وَمَشِينَا وَنَحْوِ ذَلِكَ ، بَلْ جَمِيعُ ذَلِكَ مَخْلُوقٌ لِمَوْلَانَا جَلَّ وَعَزَّ بِلَا وَاسِطَةٍ ، وَقُدْرَتُنَا أَيْضاً مِثْلُ ذَلِكَ ؛ عَرَضٌ مَخْلُوقٌ لِمَوْلَانَا جَلَّ وَعَزَّ ، تُقَارِنُ تِلْكَ الْأَفْعَالَ الِاخْتِيَارِيَّةَ وَتَتَعَلَّقُ بِهَا مِنْ غَيْرِ تَأْثِيرٍ لَهَا فِي شَيْءٍ مِنْ ذَلِكَ أَصْلاً ، وَإِنَّمَا أَجْرَى اللهُ تَعَالَى الْعَادَةَ أَنْ يَخْلُقَ عِنْدَ تِلْكَ الْقُدْرَةِ - لَا بِهَا ـ مَا شَاءَ مِنَ
And by this you know that there is no effect for our power in anything of our voluntary actions, like our movements and our stillnesses, our standing, our sitting, our walking, and the like of that. Rather, all of that is created for our Master, may He be glorified and exalted, without mediation. And our power also is like that: an accident created for our Master, may He be glorified and exalted, which accompanies those voluntary actions and is connected to them, without any influence for them in anything of that at all. Rather, God, exalted, has only made it His custom to create at the presence of that power—not by it—what He wills of...

Commentary
This passage is a theological discussion on the doctrine of acquisition (al-kasb). Building on the previous proof of God's sole agency, the author concludes that human power has no causal effect (athar) in any of our voluntary actions—such as movements, stillness, standing, sitting, walking, etc. All these actions are created directly by God without any intermediary. Our power (qudratunā) itself is also a created accident (ʿaraḍ) that God creates. This power accompanies the voluntary actions and is related to them, but it exerts no influence whatsoever. The only role of this power is that God, by His custom (ʿādah), creates the action at the moment when the power is present, not through the power as a cause. The text cuts off mid-sentence at the bottom of the page, but the intended meaning is clear: the human power is merely a concomitant, not a cause.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some believe that if human power has no causal effect, then humans cannot be held accountable for their actions.
Correction: Ash'ari theology holds that humans 'acquire' (kasb) their actions through the power that God creates in them, so actions are attributed to humans even though God creates them. This preserves human responsibility without ascribing independent causal power.

Checkpoint
According to this passage, what role does human power (qudratunā) play in our voluntary actions?
A) It causes the actions to occur.
B) It has no effect; actions are created by God directly.
C) It creates the actions through God's permission.
D) It is the primary cause, and God merely enables it.

Key Takeaway
Human power is a created accident that accompanies voluntary actions by God's custom, but it has no causal effect; God alone creates all actions.

?
The Ash'ari doctrine says your actions are yours because God connects your power to them, not because you cause them. How does this reshape your understanding of personal responsibility? The Middle Path: Refuting Jabriyya and Qadariyya
Sanusi refutes Jabriyya and Qadariyya and affirms Ahl al-Sunna's middle position.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sanusi refutes both Jabriyya and Qadariyya and how Ahl al-Sunna's doctrine of kasb (acquisition) is the rational middle path.

Core Idea
Human power cannot create; truth lies between denying and overstating its role.

Sanusi uses a clear rational principle to expose two extremes. The Jabriyya deny any human role in actions, while the Qadariyya grant creative power to humans. The Sunni position emerges as the only coherent and balanced view, beautifully illustrated by the analogy of pure milk emerging from between dung and blood.

Guided Path
1
Understand the rational principle
Read the opening sentence carefully: 'According to reason... it is not within its capacity to invent anything at all.' This is the foundation for all that follows.
Notice Sanusi bases his argument on reason (al-ʿaql), not just transmitted texts.
2
Identify the refutation of Jabriyya
Find where Sanusi describes the Jabriyya's error: they deny any power accompanying actions, making all actions equal. See how he condemns them as innovators and simpletons.
The Jabriyya's denial contradicts the evident distinction between voluntary and involuntary acts.
3
Identify the refutation of Qadariyya
Find where Sanusi refutes the Qadariyya: they claim the created power (al-qudra al-ḥāditha) effectively produces actions, which is a form of shirk. Note the strong language: 'Magians of this community'.
The Qadariyya overstate human power, attributing creative capacity to the slave.
4
Appreciate the middle path of Ahl al-Sunna
Read how Sanusi presents the Sunni doctrine using the milk analogy. Understand that actions are created by Allah but acquired by humans through their power. This is the doctrine of kasb.
The analogy shows that the truth emerges pure between two corrupt extremes.

بحسب العقل وما في نفس الأمر فليس في وسعها - أي في طاقتها - اختراع شيء ما. وبهذا تعرف: بطلان مذهب الجبرية القائلين باستواء الأفعال كلها، وأنه لا قدرة تقارن شيئاً منها عموماً، ولا شك أنهم في هذه المقالة مبتدعة بله يكذبهم الشرع والعقل، وبطلان مذهب القدرية مجوس هذه الأمة القائلين بتأثير القدرة الحادثة في الأفعال على حسب إرادة العبد، ولا شك أنهم مبتدعة أشركوا مع الله تعالى غيره، وتحقق مذهب أهل السنة بين هذين المذهبين الفاسدين، فهو قد خرج من بين فرث ودم لبناً خالصاً سائغاً للشاربين؛ بين قوم أفرطوا وهم الجبرية، وقوم فرطوا وهم القدرية.
According to reason and what is in the matter itself, it is not within its capacity—i.e., its ability—to invent anything at all. And by this you know: the invalidity of the doctrine of the Jabriyya, who say that all actions are equal and that there is no power accompanying any of them at all, and there is no doubt that they are innovators, simpletons, whom both divine law and reason refute; and the invalidity of the doctrine of the Qadariyya, the Magians of this community, who say that the originated power has an effect in actions according to the slave's will, and there is no doubt that they are innovators who associated partners with Allah, may He be exalted, other than Him. And the doctrine of Ahl al-Sunna is established between these two corrupt doctrines, for it has come forth from between dung and blood as pure milk, palatable to the drinkers; between a people who exceeded due limits, namely the Jabriyya, and a people who fell short, namely the Qadariyya.
Source pages p. 221

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of two extreme sects regarding human actions. It begins by establishing a rational principle: human power (al-qudra al-haditha) cannot create or invent any effect; it is merely a capacity. From this principle, Sanusi deduces the invalidity of the Jabriyya, who deny that human power has any role at all, making all actions equal in attribution to Allah, and thus deny the distinction between voluntary and involuntary acts. He notes that both divine law and reason refute them. Then he refutes the Qadariyya, who claim that the created human power effectively produces actions according to the slave's will, which is akin to shirk by attributing creative power to other than Allah. The correct position of Ahl al-Sunna is presented as a middle path: actions are created by Allah but acquired by humans through their power. Sanusi uses a striking analogy: just as pure milk emerges from between dung and blood without being affected by them, so the truth of Ahl al-Sunna emerges between the excess of the Jabriyya (who deny human power entirely) and the deficiency of the Qadariyya (who overstate it). Thus the passage establishes the distinctive Sunni doctrine of kasb (acquisition).
