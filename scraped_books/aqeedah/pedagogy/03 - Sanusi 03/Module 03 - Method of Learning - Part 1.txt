The Importance of the Science of Usul al-Din
Sanusi praises the science of usul al-din and explains its benefit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the practical and spiritual benefits of studying the science of usul al-din, as described by Sanusi.

Core Idea
The science of usul al-din awakens truthfulness and guides people to their right paths.

Sanusi opens his treatise by declaring that the science of the principles of religion is among the most important sciences. He does not simply assert its value theoretically; he immediately ties it to a concrete effect: guiding people toward truth and sincerity. This passage establishes the practical and spiritual purpose of the entire work.

3

Trace the argument: the science → guides people → awakens truthfulness → eliminates hypocrisy and discord. This shows that theological knowledge has direct moral consequences.
Sanusi is teaching that correct belief leads to correct action.

Thus, the science of usul al-din is not merely theoretical but has practical spiritual and social benefits: it cultivates sincerity and harmony among people.

He then begins to enumerate the necessary attributes of Allah, stating that twenty attributes are obligatory for Him, which will be explained in subsequent lessons.

Wad'i Rulings: Cause and Condition
Sanusi defines wad'i rulings and explains cause and condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definitions of cause and condition as wad'i rulings and the crucial qualification 'with consideration of its essence'.

Core Idea
A cause is defined by its essence, not by whether it always produces its effect.

Sanusi introduces the wad'i (declaratory) rulings, explaining that they consist of cause, condition, and impediment set by the Lawgiver. He provides a precise definition of 'cause' with a crucial qualification that prevents misunderstanding when effects are blocked. He then begins to define 'condition' as that which is necessary but not sufficient.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the structure: first the general definition of wad'i rulings, then the definition of cause with its important qualification, then the beginning of the definition of condition.
2
Understand the definition of cause

The example of the zenith clarifies this.

Note that a condition is defined by its absence necessitating non-existence, but its presence does not guarantee existence. This contrasts with cause.
4

Compare cause and condition: both involve existence and non-existence, but cause links existence to existence and non-existence to non-existence, while condition only links non-existence to non-existence.
This distinction is foundational in Islamic legal theory.

The Objection of Delaying Investigation and Its Answer
Sanusi answers the objection about granting time for investigation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi resolves the tension between the obligation of investigation before faith and the practical demand for immediate response, and why indefinite delay is not permitted.

Core Idea
Faith cannot be postponed by indefinite investigation — proof is presented now.

Having established that investigation (nazar) is obligatory before faith, a natural objection arises: what if someone called to faith asks for time to investigate? Sanusi confronts this objection head-on, rejecting the option of forcing faith without knowledge as absurd, and instead provides a practical response that requires immediate investigation or listening to a proof, with the sword as a last resort for stubborn refusal.

Guided Path
1
Read the Objection and the Three Options
Begin by reading the hypothetical objection posed to Sanusi's position, which suggests three possible ways to handle a person who asks for time to investigate.
Notice the precision of the objection — it forces a choice between contradicting one's own principle or setting arbitrary limits.
2
Understand Why Faith Before Knowledge is Weak
Sanusi first refutes the idea that faith could be required before knowledge. He argues that this would lead to absurd consequences like equating a true prophet with a false one, and allowing someone to later return from faith after discovering falsehood.
This step shows the logical flaw in forcing belief without understanding.
3
See the Proper Handling of the Investigation Request
When someone asks for time to investigate, Sanusi distinguishes two cases: if they know how to investigate, they must do it immediately; if not, they must listen to a brief proof presented in the same session.
The key is that no extra time is granted — the investigation or listening happens right away.
4
Recognize the Consequence of Refusal
If after this brief presentation the person refuses to believe, their stubbornness is proven, and the obligation is to use force (the sword) until they believe or die.
This clarifies the serious nature of rejecting clear proof.

Commentary
This passage is a grammatical analysis of the Shahada ('لا إله إلا الله محمد رسول الله'). It begins by discussing an extension of the Shahada—adding 'وحده لا شريك له'—and notes that the predicate can be either nominative (more preponderant) or accusative (less preponderant).
