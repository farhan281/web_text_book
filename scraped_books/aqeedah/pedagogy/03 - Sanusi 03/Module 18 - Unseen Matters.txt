Refuting the Mu'tazila on Divine Speech and the Internal Speech Analogy
Sanusi refutes the Mu'tazila and clarifies the correct use of the internal speech analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the analogy with human internal speech is used to refute the Mu'tazila, and how it does not imply any similarity in essence between God's speech and human speech.

Core Idea
The analogy only shows a shared negative attribute—both are not letters/sounds—not any likeness in reality.

Sanusi here addresses a common misunderstanding about a key argument in kalam. When scholars compare God's speech to human internal speech, some mistakenly think this implies a likeness. Sanusi clarifies that the analogy is only a logical refutation, not a claim of similarity, and warns against the error of tashbih.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation to grasp the overall argument.
Notice the rhetorical question and strong warning at the end.
2
Identify the Mu'tazilite position
The Mu'tazila claim that speech is confined to letters and sounds. How does Sanusi refute this?
Look for the phrase 'انحصار الكلام في الحروف والأصوات'.
3
Understand the warning against tashbih
Sanusi emphasizes that the analogy does not imply any likeness in essence. He explains why such a likeness is impossible.
He mentions temporal priority, posteriority, and accidental nature of human internal speech.
4
Grasp the true purpose of the analogy
The analogy is used as a refutation (naqd): internal speech is real speech without letters/sounds, so God's speech can be too. The only commonality is a negative attribute.
The term 'الصفة السلبية' (negative attribute) is key.

What is obligatory for His essence and His attributes, and what is found in the books of the theologians of using the analogy of internal speech in the witnessed realm when they refute the Mu'tazila who say that speech is confined to letters and sounds... Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite position that speech is limited to letters and sounds, and a clarification of the correct use of the analogy to human internal speech (kalam nafsi). The author begins by stating that the subject of this discussion concerns what is obligatory for God's essence and attributes. He then addresses the fact that in books of kalam, scholars use the example of internal speech in the witnessed world (i.e., human internal speech) when refuting the Mu'tazila. The author warns that this analogy should not be misunderstood as implying a likeness (tashbih) between God's speech and human internal speech in essence (kunh). God is absolutely transcendent and has no partner in His essence, attributes, or acts. The author then poses a rhetorical question: how could anyone imagine that God's speech is similar to human internal speech, given that human internal speech is accidental (arad haditha) and involves temporal sequence, ordering, and cessation, depending on its expression in verbal speech? Anyone who imagines such a similarity places himself in the same category as the Hashwiyya and other innovators who claim that God's speech consists of letters and sounds. The author then clarifies the true purpose of the analogy: it is a refutation (naqd) of the Mu'tazila. The scholars say to them: your restriction of speech to letters and sounds is refuted by the existence of internal speech, which is truly speech yet is not composed of letters or sounds. If this is established for human beings, then God's speech is also speech without letters or sounds. The only commonality between the two is a negative attribute (sifa salbiyya): both are not letters or sounds. However, their realities are utterly different (mubayana li-l-mubayana). The author concludes with a strong warning: many have slipped here because they lacked divine illumination.

Two Benefits of Studying the Lives of the Prophets

Core Idea
Studying prophetic lives detaches you from the world's allure.

The author explains that reflecting on the lives of the prophets yields two profound benefits: it consoles the heart when it loses worldly things, and it opens the eyes to how little this world matters to Allah. Through Prophetic hadith, we learn to view the world as a carcass—something to pass through, not cling to.

Guided Path

Identify the two benefits

Explore the supporting hadith
Read each hadith cited in the passage and consider how it reinforces either tasalli or tanabbuh. The hadith about the world being a carcass illustrates the prophets' detachment, while the stranger/traveler hadith models the attitude of a believer.
The third hadith about the mosquito wing emphasizes the world's insignificance even for a disbeliever.

Three narrations on the power and merit of 'La ilaha illa Allah'.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The testimony of faith holds such weight that even Paradise itself proclaims it.

The profound virtue of 'La ilaha illa Allah' is highlighted through three narrations from Ibn al-Jawhari and a Companion. These stories illustrate how the phrase is celebrated in Paradise, its power to shake a wedding, and the forgiveness it brings when uttered sincerely with exaltation. This lesson explores these narrations to deepen our appreciation for the foundation of our faith.

Consider how this motivates us to say the phrase often with presence of heart.

وقد ذكر الشيخ أبو عبد الله بن أسعد اليافعي اليمني الشافعي في كتابه الإرشاد والتطريز في فضل ذكر الله تعالى وتلاوة كتابه العزيز عن الشيخ أبي زيد القرطبي أنه قال سمعت في بعض الآثار أن من قال لا إله إلا الله ألف مرة كانت فداءه من النار فعملت على ذلك رجاء بركة الوعد أعمالاً ادخرتها لنفسي وعملت منها لأهلي وكان إذ ذاك يبيت معنا شاب كان يقال أنه يكاشف في بعض الأوقات بالجنة والنار وكان في قلبي منه شيء فاتفق أن استدعانا بعض الإخوان إلى منزله فنحن نتناول الطعام والشاب معنا إذ صاح صيحة منكرة واجتمع في نفسه وهو يقول يا عم هذه أمي في النار وهو يصيح بصياح عظيم لا يشك من سمعه أنه عن أمر
And Sheikh Abu Abdullah ibn As'ad al-Yafi'i al-Yamani al-Shafi'i mentioned in his book al-Irshad wa al-Tatriz fi Fadl Dhikr Allah Ta'ala wa Tilawat Kitabihi al-Aziz from Sheikh Abi Zaid al-Qurtubi that he said: I heard in some traditions that whoever says 'La ilaha illa Allah' a thousand times, it becomes his ransom from the Fire. So I acted upon that, hoping for the blessing of the promise, storing up deeds for myself and doing deeds for my family. At that time, there was a youth staying with us who was said to have spiritual unveiling of Paradise and Hell at some times, and I had some doubt about him. It so happened that some brothers invited us to his home. We were eating food with the youth present when he screamed a dreadful scream and gathered himself [shrank into himself] and said: 'O uncle, this is my mother in the Fire!' and he was screaming with a great scream such that no one who heard it could doubt that it was from a real matter.

Commentary
This passage narrates a story from al-Yafi'i quoting al-Qurtubi. The narrator heard a tradition that saying 'la ilaha illa Allah' 1,000 times ransoms from Hell. He began doing so, hoping to benefit himself and his family. There was a youth who was said to have spiritual unveiling (kashf) of Paradise and Hell. The narrator was skeptical. One day at a meal, the youth suddenly screamed and cried out that his mother was in Hellfire. The vivid description shows the reality of the unseen and the importance of sincere dhikr. The story ends abruptly, but the point is made.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The story proves that the mother is definitely in Hell and that the youth's vision is infallible.
Correction: The story describes the youth's vision and the narrator's reaction; it emphasizes the gravity of the afterlife and the power of dhikr. It does not definitively state the mother's final fate, nor does it claim the youth's kashf is always accurate. Rather, it shows that such experiences can be real and impact the observer.
