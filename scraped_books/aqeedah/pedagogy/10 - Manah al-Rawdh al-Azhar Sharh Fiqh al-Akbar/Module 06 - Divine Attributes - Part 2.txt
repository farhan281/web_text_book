The Eternity of God's Attributes and the Act-Object Distinction
Enumeration of five eternal divine attributes and the act-object distinction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why all of God's attributes are eternal and how His eternal act differs from the created object.

Core Idea
God's attributes are eternal; His act is uncreated, but its object is created.

This passage from the Fiqh al-Akbar establishes a foundational principle of Sunni theology: that God's attributes—knowledge, power, speech, creative act, and act—are eternal, not acquired at the moment of creation. It also clarifies a subtle distinction between God's act (fi'l) and the object of that act (maf'ul), affirming that the act is uncreated while the object is created. Understanding this distinction helps prevent misconceptions about God's relationship with creation.

This is a key theological point.

Commentary
This passage defines and explains the eternity of God's attributes and the distinction between God's act (fi'l) and the object of that act (maf'ul). It begins by stating that God has never ceased to be knowing, powerful, speaking, creating, and acting by His eternal attributes—knowledge, power, speech, creative act, and act—each of which is an attribute in pre-eternity (azal). The commentary then elaborates on each phrase: 'knowing by His knowledge' means His knowledge is an eternal attribute, not a later acquisition that would imply prior ignorance; what is pre-eternal cannot cease. Similarly, 'powerful by His power' means His power is eternal, not contingent. 'Speaking by His speech' refers to the essential, internal speech (kalam nafsi), which is eternal. 'Creator by His act of creating' and 'agent by His act' mean that when He creates or acts, He does so by His eternal attribute of creation and act, not by a new, contingent attribute; He does not acquire knowledge, power, creation, or act at the moment of creation. The passage then clarifies that the agent is Allah alone, without partner, and that His act is eternal, while the object (the created thing) is contingent, occurring when His eternal act attaches to it. It emphatically denies that the act could be created, as that would imply multiple creators. The author notes that this is an indication from the Great Imam (Abu Hanifa) that if God's act were created, it would necessitate a second creator, contradicting the fact that Allah is the Creator of all things.

The Ash'ari View on Takwin and Divine Acts
Presents and evaluates the Ash'ari view on takwin with Hanafi support.

Core Idea
Takwin is power at work — no separate attribute needed.

This passage presents a key debate in Islamic theology: is the attribute of bringing into existence (takwin) distinct from divine power, or is it simply power in relation to its objects? The Ash'ari school argues the latter, and the commentator Ibn al-Humam shows that even Hanafi sources, including Abu Hanifa himself, support this view. Let us examine the arguments closely.

Commentary
This passage presents and evaluates the Ash'ari position on the attribute of takwin (bringing into existence) and the nature of divine acts. It begins by stating the Ash'ari view: the attribute of takwin is not distinct from power (qudra) but is power considered with a specific attachment (ta'alluq) to its object. For example, 'creating' (takhliq) is simply power attached to the created thing, and similarly 'providing' (tarziq) is power attached to provision. The Ash'aris further assert that the 'attributes of acts' (sifat al-af'al)—like creating, providing, etc.—are contingent (haditha) because they consist of the attachments of power, and attachments are contingent events. The commentator then cites Ibn al-Humam's response: the Hanafi characterization of takwin as attributes indicating effect does not refute the Ash'ari view nor require that these attributes be entirely separate from attached power and will. In fact, Ibn al-Humam claims that Abu Hanifa's own statements, as transmitted by al-Tahawi, support the Ash'ari understanding. The Tahawi quote emphasizes that Allah's names like 'Creator' are eternal and not acquired at the moment of creation; He deserved the name 'Creator' before any creation because He is 'over all things powerful.' The commentator explains that this justification ties the name 'Creator' to God's eternal power over creation, thus affirming that the name is eternal even without a created object. This, he concludes, is exactly what the Ash'aris hold. The passage ends with a cautionary note: implied understandings (mafhum) should not contradict explicit textual statements (mantuq). This suggests a methodological principle for interpreting the sources.

The passage concludes that this understanding matches the Ash'ari position, and that the implicit meaning (taken from Abu Hanifa's words) does not contradict the explicit wording of the Hanafi masters.

Commentary
