Why Neither Iqrar nor Tasdiq Alone Suffices
Sanusi proves by Quranic evidence that both iqrar and tasdiq are required.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Quranic proof that both iqrar and tasdiq are necessary for iman, using the examples of the hypocrites and the People of the Book.

Core Idea
Each component alone fails: hypocrites lack tasdiq, People of the Book lack proper iqrar.

Sanusi now quotes Imam al-A'zam to reinforce that neither iqrar alone nor tasdiq alone is sufficient. He provides two Quranic examples: the hypocrites who only declare verbally but are denounced as liars, and the People of the Book who know the truth inwardly but do not properly acknowledge the prophethood of Muhammad. This lesson shows why both elements are essential for true faith.

Guided Path
1
Read the quote from Imam al-A'zam
Read the Arabic and translation of the quote from 'Al-Wasiyya'. Notice how the Imam states the definition and then gives the hypothethical: if iqrar alone were iman, all hypocrites would be believers.
2
Examine the first Quranic proof: hypocrites
The author cites Quran 63:1, where Allah calls the hypocrites liars in their claim of faith because they lack tasdiq. This proves that iqrar without tasdiq is not iman.
The verse: 'And Allah bears witness that the hypocrites are certainly liars.'
3
Examine the second Quranic proof: People of the Book
The author cites Quran 2:146, where the People of the Book recognize Muhammad as they recognize their own sons, yet they are not believers because they did not properly acknowledge his universal prophethood.
Their knowledge (tasdiq) alone does not benefit them because their iqrar was incomplete.
4
Reflect on the implication
Understand that true iman requires both verbal declaration and heartfelt conviction. Neither can stand alone.

And faith (al-iman) is acknowledgement (al-iqrar) i.e. with the tongue, by verification, and inner conviction (al-tasdiq) i.e. with the heart, according to divine success. And the precedence of acknowledgement is to indicate that it is first in the position of manifestation, although inner conviction is the one that is started with in the case of consideration, and because the Lawgiver sufficed with mere acknowledgement and did not differentiate in the ruling between the pious and the hypocrite, nor between the righteous and the wicked. And Imam al-A'zam said in his book "Al-Wasiyya": Faith is acknowledgement with the tongue and inner conviction with the heart. Acknowledgement alone does not constitute faith; because if it were faith, all the hypocrites would be believers. Likewise, knowledge (al-ma'rifa) alone i.e. mere inner conviction does not constitute faith; because if it were faith, all the People of the Book would be believers. Allah Almighty said concerning the hypocrites: "And Allah bears witness that the hypocrites are certainly liars" [Qur'an 63:1], i.e. in their claim of faith while they have no inner conviction. And Allah Almighty said concerning the People of the Book: "Those whom We gave the Book recognize him as they recognize their own sons" [Qur'an 2:146]. End. And the meaning is that mere knowledge of the People of the Book about Allah and His Messenger does not benefit them since they did not acknowledge the prophethood of Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) and his message to them and to all creation, for they claimed that he (peace and blessings be upon him) was sent only to the Arabs specifically, so their acknowledgement in this way is not pure. Then inner conviction is an essential pillar in itself (rukn hasan li'aynihi) that cannot possibly fall away in any circumstance, unlike acknowledgement, for it is a condition (shart) or part (shatr) and a pillar for something else (rukn hasan li-ghayrihi). For this reason it falls away in the case of coercion and when excuses occur. This is because the tongue is the interpreter of the heart, so it is an indicator of inner conviction both in existence and absence. If one substitutes it with something else at a time when one is able to express it, he becomes a disbeliever. But if his ability to express it ceases due to coercion, he does not become a disbeliever, because the cause of fear for his life is an apparent indication of the persistence of inner conviction in his heart and that what drives him to this substitution is his need to repel harm from himself, not a change of belief in his case, as indicated by the saying of Allah Almighty: "Whoever disbelieves in Allah after his belief – except for one who is coerced while his heart is at rest with faith – but whoever opens their breast to disbelief, upon them is wrath from Allah and for them is a great punishment" [Qur'an 16:106]. As for his substituting it at a time when he is able, it is an indication of his belief change, so it becomes the pillar of faith both in existence and non-existence, as Shams al-A'immah al-Sarakhsi stated. However, the author of "Al-'Umdah" – and he is Abu al-Barakat Abdullah ibn Ahmad ibn Mahmud al-Nasafi (may Allah have mercy on him) – stated that acknowledgement is a condition for the application of rulings, which is the choice of the Ash'aris, and Abu Mansur al-Maturidi also held this. Then, in the omission of the object of belief in the words of Imam al-A'zam, there is an indication that summary faith (al-iman al-ijmali) is sufficient in the intended purpose. The verification is that faith is inner conviction in the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) with the heart in all that is known by necessity to have come from him from Allah, summarily, and that it is sufficient to discharge the obligation of faith, and its rank is not lower than detailed faith (al-iman al-tafsili). Thus in "Sharh al-'Aqa'id", except that the better thing to say is: summarily if it is considered.

Commentary
This passage defines faith (iman) and explains the roles of its two components: iqrar (verbal declaration) and tasdiq (inner conviction). The author begins by stating that iqrar is with the tongue and tasdiq with the heart, noting that iqrar is mentioned first to show it is the initial outward manifestation, though tasdiq is the starting point in consideration. The Lawgiver (the Prophet) accepted mere iqrar in rulings, not distinguishing between the pious and the hypocrite in outward judgment. The author then quotes Imam al-A'zam from his work 'Al-Wasiyya': iman is both iqrar and tasdiq; neither alone suffices. He supports this with Quranic evidence: the hypocrites are liars because they lack tasdiq, and the People of the Book know the truth but lack proper iqrar (they did not acknowledge Muhammad's prophet hood universally). The author then explains that tasdiq is an essential pillar that never falls away, while iqrar is a conditional pillar that drops under coercion. He elaborates: the tongue indicates what is in the heart; if one is able to express it but substitutes it, he becomes a disbeliever; if coerced, the fear shows tasdiq remains. He cites Quran 16:106. He then mentions scholarly views: Shams al-A'immah al-Sarakhsi considered iqrar as an integral part of faith, while Abu al-Barakat al-Nasafi and the Ash'aris considered it a condition for worldly rulings. He concludes that summary faith (tasdiq in all that the Prophet brought, without details) is sufficient and not inferior to detailed faith.

The Distinct Roles of Tasdiq and Iqrar in Faith
Sanusi distinguishes between tasdiq as essential and iqrar as conditional, with the coercion exception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that tasdiq is an essential pillar that never drops, while iqrar is a conditional pillar that can be excused under coercion, and the reasoning behind this distinction.

Core Idea
Inner conviction is indispensable; verbal declaration is conditional and drops under duress.

Sanusi now explains the precise theological roles of tasdiq and iqrar. Tasdiq is described as an 'essential pillar' (rukn hasan li'aynihi) that never falls away, while iqrar is a 'condition' or 'pillar for something else' (rukn hasan li-ghayrihi) that can be dropped under coercion. This distinction is supported by the natural role of the tongue as the interpreter of the heart and by Quranic evidence from Surah al-Nahl. The lesson concludes with a mention of scholarly differences on the exact nature of iqrar.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two categories of pillars
Read the description of tasdiq as a 'rukn hasan li'aynihi' and iqrar as a 'shart' or 'rukn hasan li-ghayrihi'. Grasp the difference: tasdiq is essential in itself; iqrar is conditional and can be excused.
The term 'hasan' here means complete or perfect in its category.
2
Learn the coercion exception
The author explains that if a person is coerced into denying faith verbally but maintains inner conviction, he does not become a disbeliever. The tongue's statement under duress does not reflect the heart.
Quran 16:106 is cited as evidence: 'except for one who is coerced while his heart is at rest with faith.'
3
Note the scholarly divergence
Some scholars, like al-Sarakhsi, consider iqrar as part of the essence of faith, while others like al-Nasafi and the Ash'aris consider it a condition for worldly rulings. Both views are mentioned.
This divergence does not affect the core position that tasdiq is always required.
4
Reflect on the wisdom
This shows that Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity; He judges the heart. The conditional nature of iqrar reflects mercy in times of duress.

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory commentary on the statement 'Believers are equal in faith and tawhid.' The author begins by affirming that believers are equal in the essence of faith (its origin) and in tawhid itself, yet these admit increase and decrease in terms of strength and weakness in the ranks of certainty (iqan). He then elaborates on the consequences: obedience and worship are the fruit of faith and certainty, illuminating the heart with the light of gnosis; disobedience, by contrast, blackens the heart and weakens love of God, and persistent sin can lead to disbelief itself, as small sins lead to major ones and major sins to disbelief. The author then unpacks the phrase 'the believers are equal' by explaining that they are equal in the foundation of faith and tawhid, not in their degrees. He uses an analogy: disbelief is like blindness, faith like sight. All who see share the reality of sight, but they differ in its strength and weakness—some have weak sight, some see only thick lines without glasses, some see very close or far. Similarly, believers share the essence of faith but differ in its strength. For this reason, Imam Muhammad (likely Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybani) is reported to have disliked saying 'my faith is like the faith of Gabriel' and instead said 'I believe in what Gabriel believed in.' Likewise, one should not claim equality with the prophets or even the greatest Companions like Abu Bakr and Umar. The variation in the light of the word of tawhid (la ilaha illa Allah) in hearts is incalculable; the author gives a vivid simile: some hearts have light like the sun, some like the moon, some like a brilliant planet, some like a great torch, and some like a weak lamp. He supports this with two hadiths: one stating that certain actions are 'the weakest of faith,' and another that 'the strong believer is more beloved to God than the weak believer.' He concludes by noting that strength includes both outward, practical strength and inner, knowledge-based strength, and that this variation follows the pattern of these lights.
