Believers Will See Allah in Paradise without Likening or Modality
Affirms beatific vision with restrictions and scriptural proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni doctrine of beatific vision: that believers will see Allah in Paradise with their physical eyes, but in a manner completely unlike any created vision, free from likeness, modality, quantity, and distance.

Core Idea
The beatific vision is real but utterly transcendent: we see Allah without knowing how.

This passage from the sharh establishes one of the most discussed doctrines in Sunni kalam: that the believers will see Allah in Paradise. The author immediately qualifies this vision with a series of negations to safeguard divine transcendence. He then provides scriptural evidence from hadith and Quran, and finally clarifies the relationship between vision and comprehension.

Guided Path
1
Read the doctrinal statement
Focus on the first sentence and note all the restrictions placed on the vision: without likeness, modality, quantity, distance, crowding.
These restrictions are meant to avoid anthropomorphism while affirming the reality of vision.
2
Examine the hadith evidence
Identify the two hadiths cited: the short one about no crowding/harm, and the longer one from Muslim about the veil being lifted.
The longer hadith is explicitly linked to the Quranic verse about 'the best reward and more'.
3
Understand the commentary on negations
The author explains the terms 'without likeness', 'without modality', etc., and adds that there is no distance, connection, separation, indwelling, or union.
This refutes those who claim union with God (ittihad).
4
Grasp the principle of affirmation and negation
The author states a key methodological principle: we affirm what revelation establishes and negate what reason declares Allah transcendent of.
This is the balanced Sunni approach: tathbit and tanzih together.
5
Analyze the Quranic proof from al-An'am 103
Note how the verse 'Eyesight does not comprehend Him' is used to show that vision (ru'yah) is possible while comprehensive perception (idrak) is not.
This refutes those who claim the verse prohibits any vision.

Commentary
This passage is a combined statement of doctrine and proof for the beatific vision (ru'yat Allah). It begins by affirming that believers will see Allah in Paradise with their physical eyes, but immediately qualifies this: 'without likeness (bila tashbih), without modality (bila kayfiyya), without quantity (bila kamiyya), and there is no distance between Him and His creation.' This is the standard Sunni position that the vision is real but utterly unlike any creaturely vision. The author then supports this with the well-known hadith reported by twenty-one Companions: 'You will not be crowded in seeing Him' (or 'you will not be harmed'), which is in the Sahihs. The longer hadith from Muslim describes the joy of looking at Allah's Face, and the author clarifies that the 'increase' (ziyada) in Surah Yunus 26 refers to this vision. The commentary then stresses that the vision is 'accompanied by transcendence (tanzih) not enclosed in likeness,' rejecting any modality, quantity, direction, connection, separation, indwelling, or union, which are creeds of the existentialists (wujudiyya). The key principle: we affirm what revelation establishes and negate what reason demands transcendence from, citing the verse 'Eyesight does not comprehend Him' (al-An'am 103) to show that comprehension (idrak) is more specific than vision (ru'yah). Thus the vision is affirmed without the limitations of creaturely sight.

Ambiguity in Description Does Not Diminish Certainty
Principle and quotation on ambiguous attributes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how ambiguous descriptions of divine attributes do not undermine the certainty of core truths established by revelation.

Core Idea
Ambiguity in how something happens does not cast doubt on the fact that it happens.

The Qur'an contains verses that describe Allah's attributes in ways that are clear in their affirmation of the reality but unclear in the modality. Some might think this uncertainty makes the belief itself uncertain. In this passage, the author explains that such ambiguity regarding descriptions that reason cannot accommodate does not harm our knowledge of the original truth that aligns with revelation. Imam Abu Hanifa then summarizes the creed for the vision of Allah in the afterlife.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage with care
Read the Arabic passage and its translation once to get the flow. Notice that there are two parts: a principle and a quotation.
2
Understand the principle
The author states that ambiguity in a description that reason prevents does not impair knowledge of the original that conforms to transmission. Identify what 'description that reason prevents' means — any creaturely modality.
This is the key tool for handling mutashabih attributes.
3
Examine the quotation from Imam Abu Hanifa
Read his statement about the meeting with Allah in Paradise. Notice the affirmation (haqq) and the negations (without how, likeness, direction). This is a model for affirming core truth while denying modality.
4
Reflect on the misconception
Consider the wrong belief that ambiguity makes the belief uncertain. The passage shows that the core truth remains certain because it is established by clear revelation; only the modality is ambiguous.
This is a crucial principle in Sunni theology.

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory principle that addresses a potential objection: if the verse about vision is ambiguous (mutashabih) regarding direction, quantity, and modality, how can we be certain of the vision itself? The author states that such ambiguity in the description that reason prevents (i.e., any creaturely modality) does not impair (la yadqah) the knowledge of the original (al-asl) that conforms to the transmitted proof (al-naql). This succinctly summarizes the creed: affirmation of the reality (haqq) with negation of modality, anthropomorphism, and spatial direction.

Vision of Allah is Additional to Knowledge
Proves that vision is superadded to knowledge using analogy and scripture.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the vision of Allah is considered an additional perfection beyond mere knowledge, and how proofs from reason and revelation support this.

Core Idea
Vision is not just knowledge; it is a higher, complete unveiling.

The author now addresses a crucial distinction: vision (ru'yah) is not merely an intensification of knowledge but an entirely different mode of perception. He uses the simple analogy of looking at the moon to show that direct sight gives a completeness that intellectual knowledge alone does not. This lesson unpacks that argument and its scriptural supports.

Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic and English to get the overall argument.

Understand the moon analogy

Commentary
This passage is a proof that the vision of Allah (ru'yah) is an additional perfection beyond knowledge ('ilm). The author argues that vision involves a complete unveiling (inkishaf tamm) through the physical sight, but purified of facing, direction, and form. It is therefore something extra (za'id) over the attribute of knowledge. To illustrate, he uses the example of seeing the moon: when you look at the moon, it is unveiled to you; when you close your eyes, you still have knowledge of it, but the unveiling during sight is more complete. This is the meaning of the hadith 'The report is not like the eyewitness' — knowledge from report is less perfect than direct vision. He then cites Prophet Abraham's request for reassurance (Surah al-Baqarah 260) to show that even after knowledge, a higher rank (ain al-yaqin) exists. Prophet Moses' request to see Allah (Surah al-A'raf 143) also demonstrates that vision is a higher level. The conclusion (al-hasil) is that the vision of Allah is miraculous (khariq li'l-ada), not requiring facing, as the Prophet could see behind his back. And just as Allah sees us without facing (by consensus), so vision is a special relation (nisba khassa) between seer and seen, not necessarily requiring physical confrontation.

Commentary
This passage defines and explains the terms 'qurb' (nearness) and 'bu'd' (distance) as applied to Allah. It is a doctrinal clarification. First, it negates that nearness or distance is physical or spatial (length of distance), nor is it about worldly honor or humiliation. Rather, the obedient believer is 'near' to Allah without a 'how' (bila kayf), and the disobedient is 'far' without a 'how'. The commentary expands: the nearness is spiritual, not spatial, citing a hadith about the generous and miser, and the verse 'My mercy is near to the doers of good'. It clarifies that these terms are not to be interpreted as honor or humiliation in the human sense because that would be tahrif or ta'wil inappropriate for ordinary believers; Imam Abu Hanifa classified them as mutashabihat (ambiguous). Then the commentary returns to the original statement: 'the obedient is near without how' means without tashbih, and the disobedient is far with the description of tanzih (transcendence). It also says that nearness, distance, and 'iqbal' (facing) are used for the one who supplicates (munaji), i.e., the servant humbling himself before Allah, as in the verse 'Prostrate and draw near'. Finally, the passage adds a historical note: the Salaf (early generations) unanimously refrained from ta'wil (interpretation) of the attribute 'yad' (hand), and al-Ash'ari followed them, but for other attributes there was difference between ta'wil and tafwid (consigning the meaning to Allah).

The Equality and Double Virtue of Quranic Verses
Classification of Quranic verses by virtue of mention and mentioned

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that all Quranic verses are equal in virtue as divine speech, but some have an additional virtue due to the excellence of what they mention, such as Ayat al-Kursi.

Core Idea
All Quranic verses are equal in speech; some are doubly blessed by their subject matter.

This passage from a classical creedal work clarifies a subtle point about the Quran's verses. While all verses are equally the speech of Allah and share the same fundamental virtue, some verses carry an extra dimension of excellence because of the content they describe. The author uses Ayat al-Kursi as the prime example, showing how it combines the virtue of mentioning Allah with the virtue of the glorious attributes it contains.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation

The passage is concise; each sentence carries doctrinal weight.
2
Identify the two categories of verses
The passage divides verses into two groups: those with only the virtue of the mention (like verses describing disbelievers), and those with both virtues (like Ayat al-Kursi). Find the criteria for this division.
The division is based on the content – what is being mentioned.
3
Understand the double virtue of Ayat al-Kursi
Ayat al-Kursi mentions the majesty, greatness, and attributes of Allah. Thus it has both the virtue of the act of mentioning (which all verses share) and the virtue of the subject mentioned (Allah's glory). Trace how the text explains this.
This is the key insight: the subject of the verse can add a layer of excellence.
4
Contrast with verses about disbelievers
Verses that describe disbelievers only have the virtue of the mention, because the subject (the disbelievers) has no virtue. This contrast clarifies the principle.
The virtue of the mention is always present; the extra virtue depends on the subject.

This balances reverence for the entire Quran with the special status of certain verses.
