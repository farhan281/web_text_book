The Great Imam and al-Fiqh al-Akbar. There is disagreement regarding al-Fiqh al-Akbar: is it the book of the Great Imam or not? Our master Shams al-Milla wa al-Din wa al-Birr al-Taqi al-'Imadi mentioned that these two books are by Abu Hanifa, and a large group of shaykhs concurred on that. The author of the book 'Miftah al-Sa'ada wa Misbah al-Siyada' said: 'What has been said about al-Fiqh al-Akbar that it is not by Imam Abu Hanifa is from the fabrications of the Mu'tazila, claiming that Abu Hanifa was on their school. The truth is that al-Fiqh al-Akbar is by Imam Abu Hanifa. Hence we see the preference for attributing this book to Abu Hanifa.'

Commentary
This passage presents historical evidence to affirm that the book al-Fiqh al-Akbar is genuinely authored by Imam Abu Hanifa. The author acknowledges a disagreement but states the correct position. He then cites several authorities: 'Abd al-Qahir al-Baghdadi states Abu Hanifa was the first theologian among jurists and wrote the book against the Qadariyya. Abu al-Muzaffar al-Isfarayini provides a reliable chain of transmission. Al-Zabidi and Ibn al-Nadim also confirm the attribution. Al-Kardari refutes the Mu'tazili claim that Abu Hanifa had no kalam works, arguing that his views in these books match his known school. Another scholar, Shams al-Milla, and the author of Miftah al-Sa'ada further support the authenticity, labeling the denial as Mu'tazili fabrication. Thus, the passage seeks to establish the reliable attribution of al-Fiqh al-Akbar to Abu Hanifa.

Core Idea
The community's pledge and lack of alternative claims confirm Abu Bakr's rightful caliphate.

Abu Hanifa's Debate on Human Actions
Abu Hanifa debates a Mu'tazili and refutes the view that humans create actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Abu Hanifa refuted the Mu'tazili claim that humans create their own actions using a simple linguistic argument.

Core Idea
Humans cannot create their own actions because they cannot change the articulation of sounds.

Abu Hanifa engages a Mu'tazili in a debate and uses a clever demonstration to show that humans are not the creators of their actions. The passage presents the argument and its conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Read the story in Arabic
Read the Arabic narrative carefully. Notice the dialogue: Abu Hanifa asks the Mu'tazili to say 'ba' and 'dal', then challenges him to produce 'ba' from the mouth position of 'dal'.
2
Understand the Mu'tazili position
The Mu'tazili believed that humans create their own actions. This debate demonstrates the impossibility of that view by using a simple physical action (speaking).
3
Analyze the logical refutation
The challenge shows that if humans were truly creators, they could control every aspect of their actions, including the physical mechanism of sound production. Their inability proves they are not the creators.
This is a reductio ad absurdum argument.
4
Reflect on the outcome
The Mu'tazili was silenced (inqata'a). This indicates that the argument was decisive.
The story is attributed to Abu Hanifa and is found in al-Mu'taqad al-Muntaqad.
