Miracles, Wonders, and the Friends of Allah
Defines mu'jiza and karama and describes the wali.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definitions of mu'jiza and karama, the key distinction of tahaddi (challenge), and the qualities of a wali (saint).

Core Idea
A miracle challenges; a wonder confirms; the saint lives in obedience.

This lesson introduces two types of extraordinary events in Islamic theology: the miracle (mu'jiza) granted to prophets as proof, and the wonder (karama) granted to saints as honor. Understanding their relationship helps clarify how divine power works through human agents. The passage also outlines the characteristics of a true friend of Allah (wali).

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the author first defines mu'jiza, then karama, then describes the wali.
2
Distinguish mu'jiza and karama
Identify the key factor that separates them: tahaddi (challenge). Mu'jiza is accompanied by a claim of prophethood; karama is not.
The author gives examples: reviving a dead person vs. the Dajjal's deceptive sign.
3
Understand the role of the wali
Note that karama is for the wali and serves as a sign of the prophet's truth. The follower's wonder reflects the followed one.
4
Reflect on the wali's description
The passage lists qualities: knowledge of Allah and His attributes, consistent obedience, avoidance of sins, and turning away from worldly pleasures and heedlessness.
Consider how these qualities relate to the concept of sainthood.

Commentary
This passage defines two key terms: mu'jiza (miracle) and karama (wonder/grace) and explains the difference between them. The author first defines mu'jiza: it is an extraordinary event (kharq lil-'ada) that occurs in accordance with a challenge (tahaddi), which is the claim of prophethood. Examples: reviving the dead or annihilating a mountain. He then excludes what is not extraordinary, like the daily sunrise. The extraordinary event can also occur on the opposite side: a false prophet's claim is disproven by an extraordinary event that exposes his falsehood, like the Dajjal's miracles that are actually deceptions. Then he defines karama: it is also extraordinary but not accompanied by a challenge. It is a grace for the wali (saint) and a sign of the prophet's truthfulness because the wonder of the follower is attributed to the followed one. Finally, he describes the wali: a person who knows Allah and His attributes to the extent possible, consistently performs acts of obedience, avoids sins, and turns away from indulgence in pleasures, desires, heedlessness, and lusts.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Karamat confirm the truth of prophethood but are not exclusive to prophets or Imams.

This passage presents concrete examples of karamat from early Islamic history accepted by Ahl al-Sunna. It then contrasts the Sunni position with two opposing views: the Mu'tazila denied these miracles because they did not witness them, while the Shia restricted them exclusively to the twelve Imams. The examples reinforce the Sunni belief that God can grant miracles to righteous saints as a sign of their sincerity and the truth of the Prophet's message.

Imam Abu Hanifa on Karamat
States Imam Abu Hanifa's agreement with the majority on karamat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Abu Hanifa's position on the nature of karamat (wonders) and their distinction from prophetic miracles (mu'jizat).

Core Idea
The only difference between a miracle and a wonder is the presence of a challenge (tahaddi).

Imam Abu Hanifa joins the majority of scholars in affirming that extraordinary events can occur to saints, just as they can to prophets. The critical difference lies in the intention of proving prophethood. This lesson examines that position and a notable dissent from al-Qushayri.

Contrast how al-Qushayri excludes certain events from karamat.
3
Understand 'tahaddi'
The term 'tahaddi' refers to the challenge to produce something similar. In miracles, it serves as proof of prophethood; in karamat, there is no such challenge.
4
Reflect on the disagreement
Consider why al-Qushayri might have made exceptions. This is a matter of theological interpretation within Sunnism.
The text does not evaluate the positions, only reports them.

Refutation of the Claim That Karamat Are Irhas or Miracles
Refutes the claim that karamat are irhas or miracles of prophets.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Sunni position on karamat does not conflict with prophetic miracles and how the same event can be considered a miracle for a prophet and a wonder for a saint.

Core Idea
Karamat are not irhas or miracles; they are permissible extraordinary events for saints.

Some have claimed that the extraordinary events associated with Mary and the companion of Solomon are actually irhas (forerunners) for Jesus' prophethood or miracles of Zechariah or Solomon. This lesson presents the classical Sunni refutation of that claim, showing that these events do not require a prophetic claim and that calling them miracles for a prophet does not undermine their nature as karamat.

2

Consider why the author says it does not harm the position even if the event is called an irhas or miracle for a prophet.
3
Understand the conclusion on miracles vs. karamat
The author distinguishes between the reality of a miracle (must be accompanied by a challenge) and the relative consideration: the same event can be a miracle for the prophet (because it confirms his truth) and a wonder for the saint.
This shows how karamat are not in conflict with prophetic miracles.

The reality of a miracle is that it is accompanied by a challenge, whereas for a saint it is called a 'special miracle' (karama).
