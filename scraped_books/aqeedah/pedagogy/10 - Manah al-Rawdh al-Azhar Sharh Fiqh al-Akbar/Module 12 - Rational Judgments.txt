Mental Existence and the Nature of Knowledge
Al-Maturidi's definition of knowledge and the denial of mental existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Maturidi position on mental existence and knowledge as a state of disclosure rather than a mental form.

Core Idea
Knowledge is disclosure, not a mental form indwelling the mind.

The early kalam imams denied that the thing itself exists in the mind; only its phantom does. Imam al-Razi interpreted this as a denial that mental forms constitute knowledge. Al-Maturidi clarifies that knowledge is the state of disclosure (al-hal al-injilai'i), not the obtained image. This lesson unpacks this foundational epistemological stance.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on mental existence
Read the Arabic text from 'أقول: وهذا هو عندي...' to '...أساطين العلم والعرفان'. Focus on the distinction between the thing and its phantom.
The author uses 'شبحه' (its phantom) to refer to the mental representation.
2
Identify al-Maturidi's definition of knowledge
Find the phrase 'الحالة الإنجلائية' and contrast it with 'الصورة الحاصلة'. Understand why al-Maturidi rejects the obtained form.
The author sides with the investigators rather than the Peripatetics.
3
Understand the implication for epistemology
Reflect on why denying meanings in minds is considered unintelligible. The author defends the reality of mental content while rejecting its identification with knowledge.
This sets the stage for discussing the Quran's modes.

I say: And this is, in my view, the reality of the denial of our theologian imams of mental existence, i.e., that a thing is not in the mind but its phantom; and Imam al-Razi interpreted it as denial of its being knowledge. The apparent meaning of 'I say' is also true, for knowledge with us, as stated by the Imam of the Sunna, the guide of guidance, Abu Mansur al-Maturidi, may God sanctify his secret, is the state of disclosure, not the obtained form. And the investigators among the philosophers and Sayyid Zahid and Bahr al-Ulum among the later ones are upon this, although the majority of the ignorant Peripatetics are upon the opinion of the form. So this is the intention of our companions. Then later ones went to where they went. Otherwise, the denial of the subsistence of meanings in minds is not intelligible from an intelligent person, let alone those pillars of knowledge and gnosis. But the true and sincere creed of our Salaf is that these four modes are all loci of existence of the Mighty Quran truly and rightfully, and scenes of witnessing the Noble Furqan truly and sincerely. For the Quran, which is an eternal attribute of the presence of Might, His Majesty, subsisting eternally and forever in His generous Essence, impossible to separate from Him, and neither it nor other than it, neither creator nor created, it is identically the recited on our tongues, the heard in our ears, the written in our lines, the memorized in our hearts. And praise be to God, Lord of the worlds. It is not another Quran indicating the Quran, no! Rather, all of them are its manifestations, and it is the manifested in them truly, without separating from the Divine Essence or connecting with any contingent thing, or having indwelling in it, or the hem of its foot being touched by any contingency of those garments, or being affected by change of clothes, the changer of the clothed, multiplicity by multiplicity of manifestations, as I said: and I said: a sun beyond the comprehension of bats. So upon you is faith, not extremism.

Commentary
This passage is an extended commentary on mental existence and the Quran's relationship to the four modes. The author begins with 'I say' (aqulu) explaining that the denial of mental existence by the early kalam imams means that the thing itself is not in the mind; only its phantom is. Imam al-Razi interpreted this as a denial that the mental form constitutes knowledge. The author then affirms this, stating that knowledge according to Imam al-Maturidi is the state of disclosure (al-halat al-injilai'iyya), not the obtained form (al-sura al-hasila). This view is held by the investigators among the philosophers and later scholars like Sayyid Zahid and Bahr al-Ulum, while the ignorant Peripatetics hold the view of forms. This, he says, is the intended meaning of the Maturidi school. Later theologians deviated. But the true Salafi creed is that all four modes are loci of existence for the Quran itself. The Quran is an eternal attribute of God, subsisting in His essence, inseparable. It is neither other than God nor created; it is identically what is recited, heard, written, and memorized. These modes are not separate entities indicating the Quran; rather, they are its manifestations (tajalliyat) without separation from God, without connection with accidents, without indwelling (hulul), and without being affected by change. The author ends with a poetic line comparing the Quran to a sun beyond bats' comprehension, urging faith (iman) and avoiding extremism (ilhad).

The Quran's Uncreated Presence in Four Modes
Affirmation of the Quran's uncreated nature and its presence in four modes as manifestations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Salafi creed that the uncreated Quran is identically present in four modes: recitation, hearing, writing, and memorization.

Core Idea
The Quran is one eternal attribute, manifested in four modes without division or change.

After clarifying knowledge, the author turns to the Quran. He affirms the true Salafi creed that the Quran—an eternal, uncreated attribute of Allah—exists in four modes: recited, heard, written, and memorized. These are not separate entities but manifestations (tajalliyat) of the same Quran. This lesson unpacks this creed and its implications.

Understand the uncreated attribute

Reflect on manifestations without change

Persuasive or Certain? The Status of the Mutual Hindrance Argument
Discussion of whether the argument is persuasive or demonstrative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between a persuasive (iqnāʿī) and a certain (qaṭʿī) argument in kalam, and why scholars differed on the status of the mutual hindrance proof.

Core Idea
The mutual hindrance argument is either persuasive or certain—and which view you hold has serious theological implications.

The Quranic argument from mutual hindrance ('if there were other gods, they would dominate each other') appears straightforward, but scholars debated its logical force. Is it merely a persuasive rhetorical argument, or does it constitute a certain proof for tawḥīd? This passage presents two camps and hints at the high stakes of the debate.

The passage is dense with technical terms. Don't worry if you don't catch everything on the first read.

The verifiers' view is presented as the stronger, more accepted position.
3
Understand the criteria for certain proofs
Reflect on why al-Taftazani considers the argument merely persuasive (based on customary connection) while the verifiers consider it certain.
This touches on deeper issues in kalam methodology about what constitutes a necessary vs. customary connection.

Commentary
This passage discusses whether the proof from the verse 'If there were gods besides Allah...' is a certain proof or merely persuasive. Al-Taftazani considered it a persuasive argument (ḥujja iqnā‘iyya) because the necessary connection between multiple gods and corruption is based on customary experience, not logical necessity. He supports this by referring to the verse 'they would have dominated each other.' However, the 'verifiers' (muḥaqqiqūn) such as al-Ghazali, Ibn al-Humam, and al-Baydawi did not accept this and considered the argument to be certain (qaṭ‘iyy). They even claimed that whoever says it is merely persuasive becomes a disbeliever. The commentator then begins a technical note about the particle 'law' (if) in the verse, explaining that it is not used in its usual grammatical sense of negation of the past result due to negation of the condition. This is left incomplete at the end of the page.

Proof of Divine Oneness through the Mutual Hindrance Argument
The author explains the proof of tawheed from the verse of tawheed using the mutual hindrance argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical proof of tawheed known as burhān al-tamānuʿ and how it refutes the possibility of multiple necessary beings.

Core Idea
The mere possibility of disagreement between two gods proves both cannot be divine.

This lesson delves into one of the most powerful rational proofs for the oneness of God, derived from the Qur’anic verse 21:22. The argument shows that if there were two gods, the possibility of them willing opposite things leads to an absurdity, thus establishing that the Creator must be one. We will unpack the logic step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage first, then the English translation. Notice how the author begins with the conclusion and then provides the proof.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the core structure of the argument
Find the conditional statement: if two gods exist, then mutual hindrance is possible. Understand why this possibility is crucial.
3
Examine the two logical outcomes
The argument considers two scenarios when two gods will opposite things: either both occur (impossible) or neither occurs (implies impotence of one god). Trace why both lead to a contradiction.
This is the heart of the proof.
4
Reflect on the objection and its refutation
The author addresses the objection that the two gods could always agree. Understand why the mere possibility of disagreement is sufficient to break the claim of necessity.
This shows the strength of the argument.

The Permissibility of Obligating the Impossible
Al-Ghazali's proof that Allah may obligate the impossible, refuting the Mu'tazilites.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Allah can command the impossible, as shown by the Qur'anic supplication and the case of Abu Jahl.

In this lesson, we examine a key argument from Imam al-Ghazali defending the Ash'ari position that Allah has the authority to obligate His servants beyond their capacity. This refutes the Mu'tazilite view that such obligation is rationally impossible.
