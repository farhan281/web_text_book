The Nature of Ability as a Created Quality at the Time of Action
Defines ability as a created attribute at the moment of acquisition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that ability (istitāʿah) is a quality created by Allah at the moment of the servant's intention to act, and that the servant is responsible for using it correctly.

Core Idea
Ability is created at the moment of intention, not pre-existing.

This passage delves into the nature of human ability (istitāʿah) within the framework of acquisition (kasb). It clarifies that ability is not a permanent trait but a quality Allah creates simultaneously with the servant's intention to act. The servant's intention determines which ability is created, and he is held accountable for its use. Additionally, the passage distinguishes between this created ability and another meaning of ability as soundness of means.

Pay attention to the phrase 'عند اكتساب الفعل' (at the time of acquiring the action).
2

Understand the servant's responsibility
The passage explains that the servant, by his intention, 'wastes' the ability to do good and thus deserves blame. Reflect on how this relates to human accountability.
This is a key point in the Ash'ari doctrine of acquisition.

Power Suitable for Opposites
Abu Hanifa's view that power is suitable for opposites and its implications for human responsibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Abu Hanifa's doctrine that a single power can be directed to opposites, and how this grounds human responsibility.

Core Idea
One power, one capacity, but multiple ways to use it.

This passage from the commentary on Fiqh al-Akbar delves into one of Abu Hanifa's distinctive positions on divine decree and human action. Before we can discuss what is possible or impossible for humans to do, we must first understand the nature of the power (qudra) that God creates in them. Abu Hanifa holds that this power is one and the same, suitable for both good and evil acts.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on power
Read the Arabic passage and its translation focusing on the first part about power being suitable for opposites. Notice the key phrase 'هي بعينها' (it is the very same).
The word 'تعلق' (attachment) is crucial.
2
Understand the concept of ta'alluq
The power itself does not change; only its attachment to an action differs. This means the same power can attach to faith or disbelief.
3
Apply to the disbeliever
The disbeliever has the power to believe but used it for disbelief. Therefore, he is blameworthy because he wasted his capacity.
This shows human responsibility is preserved.

مع أن القدرة صالحة للضدين عند أبي حنيفة رحمه الله، حتى أن القدرة المصروفة إلى الكفر هي بعينها القدرة التي تصرف إلى الإيمان، لا اختلاف إلا في التعلق وهو لا يوجب الاختلاف في نفس القدرة، فالكافر قادر على الإيمان المكلف به إلا أنه صرف قدرته إلى الكفر وضيع باختياره صرفها إلى الإيمان، فاستحق الذم والعقاب من هذا الباب، وأما ما يمتنع بالغير بناء على أن الله تعالى علم خلافه أو أراد خلافه، كإيمان الكافر وطاعة العاصي، فلا نزاع في وقوع التكليف به لكونه مقدور المكلف بالنظر إلى نفسه، فليس التكليف به تكليفا بما ليس في وسع البشر نظرا إلى ذاته، ومن قال إنه تكليف بما ليس في الوسع فقد نظر إلى ما عرض له من تعلق علمه تعالى وإرادته سبحانه بخلافه، وبالجملة لو لم يكلف العبد به لم يكن تارك المأمور عاصيا، فلذا عد مثل إيمان الكافر وطاعة الفاسق من قبيل المحال بناء على تعلق علمه وإرادته بخلافه، وهو عندنا من قبيل ما لا يطاق بناءً على صحة تعلق القدرة الحادثة في نفسه، وإن لم يوجد عقيبه، وهذا نزاع لفظي عند أرباب التحقيق، والله ولي التوفيق.
Although power (al-qudra) is suitable for opposites according to Abu Hanifa, may Allah have mercy on him, such that the power directed to disbelief is the very power that is directed to faith; there is no difference except in the attachment (ta'alluq), and that does not necessitate difference in the power itself. So the disbeliever is capable of the faith that is obligated upon him, except that he directed his power to disbelief and wasted by his choice the directing of it to faith, so he deserves blame and punishment from this door. As for that which is impossible due to something else (al-mumtani' bi al-ghayr) based on that Allah Most High knew otherwise or willed otherwise, like the faith of the disbeliever and the obedience of the sinner, there is no dispute about the occurrence of obligation with it because it is within the power of the obligated person with respect to itself. So the obligation with it is not an obligation with what is not within the capacity of humans with respect to its essence. And whoever says that it is an obligation with what is not within capacity has considered what has accrued to it from the attachment of His knowledge and will to its opposite. In short, if the servant were not obligated with it, then the one who leaves the commanded act would not be disobedient. Therefore, such things like the faith of the disbeliever and the obedience of the sinner are counted as impossible (muhal) based on the attachment of His knowledge and will to its opposite, and in our view they are from the category of what is not possible (la yutaq) based on the validity of the attachment of the temporal power in itself, even if it does not occur afterwards. This is a verbal dispute among the investigators. And Allah is the Granter of success.

Commentary
This passage presents Abu Hanifa's doctrine that power (qudra) is one and the same for both good and evil actions. The difference is only in the direction (ta'alluq). The disbeliever had the power to believe but used it for disbelief. Then it discusses the concept of 'impossible due to another' (mumtani' bi al-ghayr) — something that is possible in itself but becomes impossible because God's knowledge or will attaches to its opposite. The author argues that such things are still possible in themselves and thus obligation is valid. He concludes that the dispute over whether such obligation is 'obligation with what is not within capacity' is merely verbal.

Sins Other Than Shirk Are Subject to God's Will
Commentator explains fate of sins (excluding shirk/kufr) under God's will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that for a believer who dies with sins other than shirk and kufr without repenting, God may punish or forgive them, but they will ultimately enter Paradise.

Core Idea
Believers’ sins (excluding shirk/kufr) are under God’s will, never eternal.

This lesson addresses a crucial question in Sunni theology: what happens to a believer who dies with minor or major sins, but without repenting? The text clarifies that such sins do not lead to eternal punishment. Rather, they are subject to God’s will—He may punish temporarily or forgive—and the believer will eventually dwell in Paradise.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal clarification on the fate of Muslim sinners. It begins with the core statement: sins other than shirk and kufr, for which a person dies without repenting while still a believer, are subject to God's will. God may punish them in the Fire temporarily or forgive them entirely, but ultimately the person will not remain in Hell forever. The commentator then unpacks each element. 'Sins' refers to all disobedience, excluding specifically shirk (associating partners with Allah) and kufr (disbelief). Repentance is not required for this category to be under God's will; the person dies as a believer but unrepentant. 'In the will of Allah' means under the attachment of His will: He may punish with justice to the degree deserved, or forgive by His bounty, even if intercession is presented. Crucially, such a person will never be eternally punished in Hellfire; they will eventually enter Paradise and abide there forever. This is a key point in Sunni theology distinguishing major sins (excluding shirk/kufr) from the fate of the sinful believer.

Then know that the Great Imam, may Allah have mercy on him, authored 'al-Fiqh al-Akbar' during his life and 'al-Wasiyya' at the time of death, and I have mentioned their wordings completely. Among the elegant stories narrated is that Abu Hanifa, may Allah be pleased with him, debated a Mu'tazili and said to him: 'Say "ba".' He said: 'Ba.' Then he said to him: 'Say "dal".' He said: 'Dal.' He said: 'If you are the creator of your actions, then bring out the ba from the place of articulation (makhraj) of the dal' — or as he said — so the Mu'tazili was confounded.

Commentary
This passage is an anecdote illustrating a rational proof against the Mu'tazili doctrine that humans create their own actions. The author first notes that Abu Hanifa composed two works: al-Fiqh al-Akbar during his life and al-Wasiyya at the time of his death, and he has reproduced their contents in full. He then relates an interesting story: Abu Hanifa debated a Mu'tazili (who believed humans are the creators of their actions). Abu Hanifa asked him to pronounce the letter 'ba' (bilabial), which the Mu'tazili did. Then he asked him to pronounce 'dal' (alveolar), which he also did. Abu Hanifa then challenged: 'If you are the creator of your actions, then bring out the ba from the makhraj (place of articulation) of the dal' — meaning, change the inherent property of the letter. Since the Mu'tazili could not alter the natural mechanism of speech, he was confounded and defeated. This argument demonstrates that the action of speech is created by Allah, not by the speaker, thereby refuting human autonomy.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Humans create their own actions independently of Allah.
Correction: The inability to alter the inherent properties of one's actions (such as the makhraj of letters) shows that actions are not self-created but created by Allah.

Key Takeaway
The fixed properties of human actions, such as the makhraj of letters, demonstrate that they are created by Allah, not by the human agent.
