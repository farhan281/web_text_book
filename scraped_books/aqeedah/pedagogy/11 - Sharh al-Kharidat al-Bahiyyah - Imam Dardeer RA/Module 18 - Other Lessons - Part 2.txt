The Spiritual Practice of Hunger and Its Effect on Deeds
Dardeer explains the spiritual practice of hunger and its connection to deed quality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the spiritual discipline of voluntary hunger, its connection to fasting, and how the quality of food influences the quality of one's deeds.

Core Idea
What you eat shapes what you do — even in spiritual practice.

Imam Dardeer presents hunger as a chosen practice, not a compulsory one. He defines its minimal form and explains that its difficulty is eased by frequent fasting. Then he draws a profound link: our actions are the fruit of what we consume. This lesson explores that link.

Guided Path

Halal here is defined as food whose origin is unknown.
2

Fasting is presented as the 'bridle' that helps travelers control their desires. See how fasting assists in adopting the practice of hunger.
3
Learn the food-deed connection
The author explicitly states that actions are the fruit of what is eaten. Identify the three categories of food (haram, halal, mutashabih) and their corresponding effects on deeds.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of the sixth spiritual practice: hunger (al-ju'). The author describes hunger as a chosen practice (ikhtiyar), not a compulsory one. Its minimal form is to eat no more than one light meal per day and night from halal food. 'Halal' here is defined as food whose origin is unknown (i.e., one is uncertain about its permissibility, so it is ruled as halal by default). At the beginning, this practice is difficult and requires much fasting. Fasting acts as a 'bridle' for the travelers on the path, restraining the lower self. The author then draws a direct causal link between food and actions: actions are the fruit of what one eats. Haram food produces only evil and prohibited deeds; pure halal produces only good deeds; and ambiguous (mutashabih) food produces mixed deeds tainted by showing off, self-admiration, and evil thoughts.

The beginner's dhikr is 'La ilaha illa Allah' alone, reserved for the end in group recitation.
