Defining al-Qadim: The Eternal
Dardeer defines al-Qadim and negates origination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the meaning of al-Qadim (the Eternal) in Sunni kalam and why it entails that Allah cannot be originated or a locus of originated things.

Core Idea
Al-Qadim means Allah is eternal without beginning, and nothing else coexists with Him.

In this passage, Imam Dardeer begins to define the essential attribute of qidam (eternity). He starts by negating what Allah must not be—originated or a locus of originated things—before affirming His positive attribute as the Eternal. This approach of negation then affirmation is typical of kalam reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the structure: first a negation (what Allah is not), then an affirmation (what He is).
The parenthetical is likely a gloss explaining the negation.
2
Identify the two parts
Identify the negative part ('أن يكون حادثاً ...') and the positive part ('القديم ...'). Understand that al-Qadim is defined by both negating temporal attributes and affirming eternal ones.
3

Existence as a Self-Attribute (Ṣifa Nafsiyya)
Proof that existence is a necessary attribute of God and is called ṣifa nafsiyya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why existence is considered a necessary self-attribute of God and how the argument from effect to cause supports this.

Core Idea
Every effect points to a cause; thus existence is inseparable from God's essence.

In this passage, Imam Dardeer begins his discussion of divine attributes by establishing the first necessary attribute: existence. He provides a simple yet profound proof based on the principle that every effect requires an efficient cause. This not only affirms God's existence but also introduces the concept of a 'self-attribute' (ṣifa nafsiyya), an attribute that is not separate from the essence. The passage then prepares us for the upcoming negative attributes.

3

The passage then announces that after this self-attribute come five negative attributes (sifāt salbiyya) which will be enumerated next.

Why God Must Have Attributes
Proof of divine attributes via reductio, then praise and transcendence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical proof that God must possess attributes and the description of His transcendence beyond all imperfections.

Core Idea
Denying attributes to God leads to absurdity; affirming them leads to glorification.

This passage from al-Kharidat al-Bahiyyah presents a concise yet powerful argument: if God lacked attributes, He would be temporal, which is impossible. The argument then moves to a sublime description of God's names and His transcendence (tanzih). Read closely to see how logical necessity and devotional praise are woven together.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to understand the flow of argument.
Notice the conditional 'لو لم يكن' at the beginning.
2
Understand the reductio argument
The passage argues that if God were not described with attributes, He would be temporal. Since temporal origination is impossible for God, the antecedent must be false. The impossibility is further supported by leading to infinite regress and circularity.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum: denying attributes leads to an impossibility.
3
Analyze the positive and negative descriptions
The passage lists beautiful names (e.g., al-Jalīl, al-Jamīl) that affirm perfection, then negates imperfections like indwelling, direction, connection, separation, and foolishness. Reflect on how both affirmation and negation are needed to describe God properly.
The names are not arbitrary; they follow from the logical necessity of attributes.

Core Idea
God's seven essential attributes are eternal and inseparable from His essence.

Imam al-Dardīr now turns to the attributes that are not merely negative or relational but positive meanings subsisting in God's essence. These seven attributes—knowledge, power, will, life, speech, hearing, and sight—are the core of Sunni divine attribute theology. Existence is Identical to Essence: The Ash'ari Proof

Core Idea
Existence and essence are one; if separate, reason breaks down.

Imam al-Ash'ari presents a tight logical argument that existence must be identical to the essence. This dilemma forces us to see that a separate existence would lead to absurdities. We will walk through each branch and its conclusion.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof of the negative attribute of pre-eternity (al-qidam). The author begins with a line from the doctrinal poem: 'And they are: pre-eternity by essence, so know, and everlastingness, self-subsistence, you have attained piety.' Then he explains that the first negative attribute is pre-eternity by essence (al-qidam al-dhātī), meaning God is eternal in His own right, not by an eternal cause. He distinguishes this from what philosophers call 'pre-eternity by another' (qidam bi-l-ghayr), which he says is impossible because a decisive proof shows nothing is eternal by another. He then defines pre-eternity as the negation of beginning-ness (salb al-awwaliyya), meaning God has no beginning to His existence. He provides a rational proof: if God were not eternal, He would be originated (ḥādith), and then He would need an originator. That originator would also be originated by another, and due to similarity, this would lead to circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul), both of which are impossible. The footnotes add further clarification. The first footnote contrasts essential pre-eternity with essential origination, noting that time-related concepts do not apply to God. The second footnote explains that 'qidam' for Allah means negation of beginning or prior non-existence, while for others it can have various meanings like temporal pre-eternity or relative pre-eternity. The third footnote mentions the textual proof from the Quran 'He is the First' and explains that this means God has no beginning, not that He is first in a temporal sequence. It also notes that the term 'al-Qadīm' is permissible to use due to consensus and usage.

Definition and Proof of the Attribute of Pre-existence (al-Qidam)
Dardeer defines al-qidam as negation of beginning and provides its rational proof by reductio ad absurdum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the meaning of Allah's attribute of pre-existence (al-qidam) as the negation of beginning, its rational proof, and the impossibility of circularity and infinite regress.

Core Idea
Allah's pre-existence means He has no beginning, and denying it leads to absurdity.

Before diving into the details, it is essential to grasp the precise definition of al-qidam as the negation of beginning. Dardeer then provides a powerful logical proof that establishes this attribute by showing that the alternative leads to impossible consequences like circularity or infinite chains. This lesson unpacks both the definition and the proof.

Commentary
This passage defines the first negative attribute, al-qidam (pre-existence or eternity), and provides its rational proof. The author begins by stating that the attributes being discussed are the negative attributes, and the first is al-qidam bi al-dhat (essential pre-existence). He clarifies that this means Allah is eternal by His essence, not by an external cause. He then distinguishes this from the philosophers' concept of pre-existence by another, which is impossible because nothing besides Allah and His attributes can be eternal. The meaning of qidam is defined as the negation of beginning (salb al-awwaliyya): Allah has no beginning to His existence. The proof follows: if Allah were not eternal, He would be originated (hadith). An originated being needs an originator (muhdith). That originator would also be originated, due to similarity (tamathul) between them. This leads either to circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul), both of which are impossible. Circularity would require two things to be both prior and posterior to each other, which is contradictory. Infinite regress would imply an infinite chain of originated deities, each incapable and dependent, which cannot produce the world. The impossibility of these consequences proves that the original assumption (non-eternity) is false, so qidam is established. The passage also includes footnotes elaborating the meaning of qidam and the transmitted proof from the Quranic verse 'He is the First'.

Two cannot become one without ceasing to be two.

Definition of Unity in Acts (Wahdaniyya al-Fi'l)
Dardeer defines divine unity in acts and denies effective power to others.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what unity in acts (waḥdāniyya al-fiʿl) means and why it entails that only Allah has effective power.

Core Idea
Only Allah truly acts; all others are incapable.

Dardeer now turns to the third dimension of divine unity: unity in acts. This principle clarifies that all effective power belongs solely to Allah, and every creature is inherently powerless. The passage dismantles any notion of independent secondary causality.
