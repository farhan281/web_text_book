And among them: that he should always see his worship as having defectiveness from ostentation and evil thoughts, and that such worship deserves punishment were it not for God's forgiveness, so he asks forgiveness for his worship and for his seeking forgiveness. And among them: that he should not speak the speech of the knowers (‘ārifīn) concerning separation (farq) and union (jam‘), and subsistence (baqā’) and annihilation (fanā’), until he becomes perfect; indeed, it is preferable even for the perfect one to leave that except for a necessary need. And among them: self-accounting (muḥāsaba) for what he has committed of forbidden things, disliked things, and excess of permissible things, and for what has occurred in his soul of psychological and satanic thoughts, and seeking forgiveness from them. And the difference between the psychological thought and the satanic thought is that the first comes with insistence on sin or lust, like a child who insists on his mother until she gives him what he wants; so it must be suppressed by constant remembrance (dhikr), explaining the consequence of this matter, and turning to the shaykh. The second comes without insistence; rather it commands sin and makes it appear attractive. If the person obeys, fine; otherwise it moves to another, because its purpose is misguidance in any form, not a particular sin.

Commentary
This passage enumerates the essential etiquettes (ādāb) of the Sufi path that lead to perfection. The first set deals with physical and moral conduct: maintaining ritual purity even while sleeping, covering one's private parts even in solitude out of modesty before God and angels, respecting elders and showing compassion to all creatures, and especially showing proper etiquette toward scholars, particularly the guardians of the Law and the shaykhs of the path, as they are the inheritors of prophets. The second set addresses spiritual discipline: a disciple should not visit other righteous people during his training lest he see in them a virtue absent in his own shaykh, which could cause him to lose the spiritual flow (madad) from the shaykh. One should have a low opinion of oneself and think well of others, never defend oneself, and always consider one's worship as tainted by ostentation and evil thoughts, thus requiring constant repentance—even from one's repentance. One should not speak about advanced concepts like farq (separation) and jam‘ (union), baqā’ (subsistence) and fanā’ (annihilation) until fully perfected, and even then it is better to refrain unless necessary. Finally, one must practice self-accounting (muḥāsaba) for all actions, including forbidden, disliked, and excessive permissible acts, and for psychological and satanic thoughts. A key distinction is given: psychological thoughts pressure relentlessly like a demanding child, requiring suppression through dhikr, reflection, and turning to the shaykh; satanic thoughts are whimsical, tempting different sins, aiming simply at misguidance.

Conditions for the Acceptance of Du'a
Enumeration of conditions for du'a acceptance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key conditions that make a supplication acceptable to Allah, including avoiding impossibility, adopting humility, choosing virtuous times, and not constraining Allah's will.

Core Idea
Du'a must be possible, humble, timely, and unconstraining.

When we call upon Allah, our du'a must meet certain conditions to be proper and acceptable. In this passage, the author outlines the essential criteria: it must not be impossible by reason, law, or custom; it should be offered with humility and at blessed times; and it must not impose a specific demand on Allah except in dire need.
