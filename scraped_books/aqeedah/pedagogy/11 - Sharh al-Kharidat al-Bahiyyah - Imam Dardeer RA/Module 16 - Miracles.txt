The Miracles of the Spider and Dove in the Cave
Dardeer narrates the story of the cave, the pursuit, and the miracles of the spider web and dove eggs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the miraculous protection of the Prophet (ﷺ) and Abu Bakr (رضي الله عنه) in the Cave of Thawr through the spider web and dove eggs, and how these miracles affirm prophethood.

Core Idea
Allah uses the smallest creatures to protect His prophets.

The Hijra was a pivotal moment in Islamic history, filled with danger and divine intervention. In this passage, Imam Dardeer recounts the extraordinary events that unfolded when the Prophet (ﷺ) and Abu Bakr took refuge in the Cave of Thawr. The story demonstrates how Allah used the simplest of creatures to shield His beloved Prophet from the eyes of the disbelievers.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage slowly, then refer to the translation. Notice the detailed description of the cave and the pursuit.
The Arabic includes poetic quotations from al-Burda.
2
Identify the main elements of the story
List the key events: the pursuit, the cave entrance, the reassurance of the Prophet, the divine blinding, the spider web and dove eggs, and the polytheists' conclusion.
3
Reflect on the miraculous nature
Consider why the spider web and dove eggs are considered miracles. What would have happened if they weren't there? How did they prevent the disbelievers from looking closer?
The polytheists used the undisturbed state as evidence that no one had entered.
4
Connect to prophethood
Think about how this event serves as a proof of prophethood. The protection was not coincidental but divinely ordained.
Allah blinded both sight and insight.

And the cave: an opening in the top of Mount Thawr, about an hour's distance from Mecca. The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) and Abu Bakr entered it when they left as emigrants from Mecca to Medina. The polytheists went in pursuit of them and tracked their footsteps until they came to the cave, and the tracks stopped. So they began searching until one of them said: 'Look at the cave.' They said: 'There is no one in the cave' – and if they had looked the slightest look they would have seen them. Then distress intensified upon Abu Bakr, may Allah be pleased with him, out of fear for the Messenger of Allah, and he said: 'If they looked under their feet they would see us.' So the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) said: 'Do not grieve; indeed Allah is with us.' So Allah blinded their sight from them, just as He blinded their insight. It is said: When they entered the cave, Allah sent two doves, and they laid eggs at the mouth of the cave, and a spider wove a web over it, until one of them said: 'What is the matter with you concerning the cave? Indeed, the spider has pitched a tent over it, and the dove has laid eggs at its mouth!' Meaning that it is not possible for them to have entered the cave in this state, and it is not possible for the weaving and the eggs to have occurred after their entry. And to that the author of al-Burda alluded, saying: 'And what the cave contained of goodness and generosity, and every eye of the disbelievers was blinded about it. So the truthful one (as-Sidq) in the cave and as-Siddiq did not leave, while they said: There is no one (arim) in the cave. They thought of the dove and of the spider upon the best of creation, that it did not weave and did not lay eggs.' His saying 'as-Sidq' means the possessor of truthfulness, and he is the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him). And his saying 'lam yarima' means they did not depart and did not separate from it. And the meaning of 'arim' is 'one' (aḥad).

Commentary
This passage recounts the well-known story of the cave (ghār) during the Hijra. Dardeer describes the cave's location on Mount Thawr, about an hour from Mecca. The Prophet and Abu Bakr entered it while emigrating to Medina. The polytheists followed their tracks until they reached the cave, where the tracks ceased. The search party debated but ultimately concluded the cave was empty, though had they looked closely they would have seen the two. Abu Bakr became extremely anxious for the Prophet's safety, but the Prophet reassured him with the Quranic verse 'لا تَحْزَنْ إِنَّ اللهَ مَعَنَا' (Do not grieve; indeed Allah is with us). Allah then blinded the disbelievers' physical sight as He had blinded their spiritual insight. Dardeer then mentions the miracles: Allah sent two doves that laid eggs at the cave's mouth and a spider that wove a web across it. This led the polytheists to conclude no one could have entered recently. The poet of al-Burda (Imam al-Busiri) alluded to this, saying: 'And what the cave contained of goodness and generosity, and every eye of the disbelievers was blinded about it. So the truthful one (as-Sidq) in the cave and as-Siddiq did not leave, while they said: There is no one (arim) in the cave. They thought of the dove and of the spider upon the best of creation, that it did not weave and did not lay eggs.' Dardeer then explains the poetic terms: 'as-Sidq' refers to the Prophet (the truthful one), 'lam yarima' means they did not leave, and 'arim' means 'one' (aḥad). The miracle thus served as divine protection and a sign of the Prophet's prophethood.

The Three Conditions of a True Miracle
Dardeer defines the miracle and explains its three conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise Islamic definition of a miracle (mu'jizah) and the three conditions that distinguish it from other supernatural events.

Core Idea
A miracle is a supernatural event tied to a prophetic challenge, requiring the absence of any counteraction.

Imam Dardeer begins by defining the miracle and immediately clarifies its three essential conditions. Without these conditions, a supernatural event might be mistaken for a saintly marvel, a prophetic sign, or even sorcery. This lesson unpacks the definition and explains how each condition safeguards the unique role of miracles in proving prophethood.

Guided Path
1
Understanding the definition
Read the Arabic definition carefully. Notice the phrase 'khariq lil-'ada' and the three qualifiers: coupled with challenge, absence of opposition.
The definition is compressed; each word carries weight.
2
Analyzing each condition
Identify each condition and its purpose: what does it exclude? The commentary will help you see the logic.
3
Applying the conditions to examples
Consider the examples given: Ibrahim's fire, karamat, irhasat, sorcery, and trickery. See how each condition applies.
This solidifies the concept.

And the miracle: an affair that breaks the usual course of events, coupled with the challenge (tahaddi) with absence of opposition. Included in our saying 'an affair' is action and omission, like the non-burning of fire for Ibrahim, peace be upon him. And our saying 'breaks...etc.' is a safeguard against adhering to habitual occurrences. And our saying 'coupled with the challenge' i.e., the claim of prophethood, is a safeguard against the marvels (karamat) of saints, and the foundational signs (irhasat) which precede the missions of prophets as a foundation for them. And our saying 'with absence of opposition' is a safeguard against sorcery (sihr) and trickery (sha'badha). The exclusion of sorcery by this condition is based on the statement that sorcery breaks the usual course, but the truth is that it is among ordinary matters except that its causes are hidden. As for trickery — also called sha'badha — it is agility of the hand and taking like sorcery, showing a thing other than its reality in the eye's view.

Commentary
This passage defines the miracle (mu'jizah) and specifies its three conditions. The first condition: it is a supernatural event (khariq lil-'ada). The second: it is accompanied by a challenge (tahaddi), meaning the prophet's claim of prophethood. This excludes karamat (saints' marvels) and irhasat (pre-prophethood signs). The third: it is not contested (la mu'arada), which excludes sorcery and trickery. The passage then clarifies that sorcery is actually not supernatural but has hidden causes, and trickery is mere sleight of hand. Each condition is explained with an example: the non-burning of Ibrahim's fire illustrates 'affair' as action; the challenge ensures the event is tied to a prophetic claim; and the absence of opposition distinguishes true miracles from deceptive acts.
