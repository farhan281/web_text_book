Introduction to Sam'iyyat: Belief in the Unseen
Opening and closing lines establishing Sam'iyyat and its authority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what Sam'iyyat are and why it is obligatory for every Muslim to believe in the matters of the afterlife and unseen that are conveyed by revelation.

Core Idea
Sam'iyyat are beliefs that come only from revelation and are as certain as necessary knowledge.

The third section of the creed deals with matters that cannot be known by reason alone—only through divine revelation. These are called Sam'iyyat (hearing-based matters). The poet begins by stating that belief in these is necessary, and concludes by affirming that everything the Prophet brought becomes like necessary knowledge. This lesson introduces the category and its foundational principle.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening line
Read the first line 'القسم الثالث: السمعيات' and its translation. Notice that the author classifies this as the third section of Aqeedah.
This establishes the context for the rest of the beliefs.
2
Understand the obligation
Focus on the phrase 'ويلزم الإيمان' (it is necessary to believe). Ask yourself: why must we believe these things? How do we come to know them?
3
Read the final lines
Read the last two lines about everything the Prophet brought becoming like necessary knowledge (الضروري). This explains the epistemic status of Sam'iyyat.
4
Reflect on the overall message
Consider how the opening and closing lines frame the entire list. The poet is saying that these beliefs are not optional but are as certain as basic logical truths.

Ru'ya in the Afterlife: Paradise and the Resurrection
Imam Dardeer discusses the locations of ru'ya in the afterlife.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two occasions when believers see Allah in the afterlife and the majority view that disbelievers do not see Him.

Core Idea
Believers see Allah in Paradise and at the Resurrection, but disbelievers remain veiled.

This passage from Imam Dardeer's commentary addresses the question of when and where the beatific vision (ru'ya) occurs in the afterlife. He affirms that believers will see Allah in the Garden of Eternity, and also on the Plain of Resurrection based on the Sunnah. A minority opinion suggesting disbelievers might see Allah as torment is mentioned but rejected by the majority. The passage clarifies the Sunni position that only believers enjoy this vision.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two locations
Read the passage and highlight where ru'ya occurs: Jannat al-Khuld and arsat al-qiyama.
Note that 'arsa' means an open courtyard.
2
Understand the majority view
Pay attention to footnote (7) which states the majority consensus that disbelievers do not see Allah.
This corrects the minority view mentioned in the main text.
3
Consider the attributes of majesty
Notice the phrase 'sifat al-jalal' – seeing Allah in His majestic attributes is possible for all who see Him.
This concept will be developed in later lessons.

Seeing Allah in Dreams: Heart-Vision Not Eye-Vision
Imam Dardeer discusses dream-vision as heart-vision.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that true dream-visions of Allah are heart-based spiritual experiences, not physical sight, and that the righteous have experienced this without compromising Allah's transcendence.

Core Idea
Dream vision of Allah is heart-vision, not eye-vision.

The possibility of seeing Allah in dreams is a delicate topic. The majority of Sunni scholars affirm it for the righteous, but they insist it is a vision of the heart, not the eyes. This passage by Imam Dardeer establishes that distinction and provides important context to protect against misunderstanding and false claims.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to absorb its flow. Then read the literal translation. Notice the footnote markers—they contain further scholarly discussion.
2
Identify the author's core claim
Look for the phrase 'a type of witnessing that occurs with the heart, not with the eye.' This is the central thesis: dream-vision is spiritual, not physical.
3
Note the majority view and supporting story
The footnote mentions that most scholars permit it, citing consensus and the anecdote of Imam Ahmad seeing Allah in a dream ninety-nine times. This reinforces the legitimacy of such visions for the righteous.
4
Understand the warning about false visions
The footnote also warns: if the seen one appears with created attributes or commands contrary to Sharia, it is the devil. This safeguards against deception.
5
Reflect on the theological significance
Consider why the author stresses 'bi al-qalb la bi al-ayn'. This protects Allah's transcendence—He is not seen with physical eyes in this world, even in dreams.

This passage defines the third division of theological topics — the sam‘iyyāt (matters known only through revelation) — and focuses on the necessity of belief in the Reckoning (al-ḥisāb). The author begins by stating that the topics of this discipline are three: divine matters, prophecies, and sam‘iyyāt. Having already covered the first two, he now addresses the third. He quotes the poetic line: 'Belief in the Reckoning, the Gathering, the Punishment, and the Reward is necessary,' then provides commentary.

He explains that 'it is necessary' means it is obligatory for the legally responsible (mukallafīn). 'Belief' means affirmation (taṣdīq). The Reckoning (al-ḥisāb) is defined linguistically as counting, and terminologically as God stopping His servants in the Gathering-place (al-maḥshar) to call them to account for their deeds — whether acts, words, or beliefs — in detail. This detailed accounting occurs by God speaking to them with eternal speech (kalām qadīm) that is not composed of letters or sound, thereby affirming the Ash‘arī doctrine that God’s speech is not created and not composed of letters and sounds. The hearing of this speech may occur either by God removing the veil so they hear it directly, or by a created sound that God brings into existence to indicate the speech. The speaker may be angels only, or God together with angels.

The author then describes the varied nature of the reckoning: it can be easy or difficult, secret or public, based on favor or justice, according to each person’s deeds. God forgives whom He wills and punishes whom He wills. The reckoning applies to both believers and disbelievers, humans and jinn, after they receive their books (scriptures of deeds). He cites the Qur’anic verse (84:7-9) about the one who receives his book in his right hand and is given an easy reckoning.

The easiest form of reckoning is when God alone accounts with the person, without any human, jinn, or angel knowing. God says: 'These are your sins — I have forgiven them; these are your good deeds — I have multiplied them.' This reckoning does not apply to the infallible (ma‘ṣūmīn, i.e., prophets). Furthermore, seventy thousand people are exempted from being brought to account altogether, the best of whom is Abu Bakr al-Ṣiddīq, and they enter Paradise without any reckoning, as reported in a ḥadīth. Finally, the author notes that this community (the Muslim umma), despite being the last, will precede others in the reckoning and other matters on the Day of Judgment.

Angels: Existence and Nature
Dardeer states the obligation to believe in angels and their infallibility, then defines an angel.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the obligation to believe in angels, their infallibility, and the classical definition of an angel.

Core Idea
Angels are infallible, luminous beings created to obey Allah.

This passage opens the section on belief in angels by establishing three foundational points: that belief in their existence is obligatory, that they are infallible (ma'sum), and what an angel is. Dardeer anchors his teaching in a Qur'anic verse affirming their perfect obedience, then provides a precise definition that distinguishes angels from other beings.

Guided Path
1
Read the verse on obedience
Begin by reading verse 66:6 cited by Dardeer. Notice how it establishes the angels' complete submission to Allah's commands. This is the basis for their infallibility.
2
Learn the definition of an angel
Dardeer defines an angel as a subtle, spiritual, luminous body capable of beautiful transformations. Understand each attribute: laṭif (subtle), rūḥānī (spiritual), nūrānī (luminous).
This definition is crucial to distinguish angels from jinn and humans.
3
Understand the two levels of belief
The passage introduces a distinction: general belief (ijmālī) in angels we know in general, and detailed belief (tafṣīlī) in those we know specifically. This lesson focuses on the general obligation.
Detailed belief will be covered in the next lesson.

Commentary
This passage addresses the obligation to believe in angels, their infallibility, and the categories of belief. It begins by stating the obligation to believe in the existence of angels and their 'isma (infallibility), citing Qur'an 66:6 which states they do not disobey Allah. It then provides a definition of an angel (malak): a subtle (laṭif), spiritual (rūḥānī), luminous (nūrānī) body with the power of beautiful transformations. The next part distinguishes two levels of obligatory belief: general belief in those known generally, and detailed belief in those known in detail. Detailed belief is further divided: by individual (bi-l-shakhṣ), listing specific angels like Jibril, Israfil, Mika'il, 'Azra'il, Munkar, Nakir, Ridwan, and Malik; and by type (bi-l-naw'), giving examples such as the bearers of the Throne, assistants of 'Azra'il, the guardian angels (ḥafaza) who protect humans, citing Qur'an 13:11, and the recording angels (kataba) who write all deeds of every accountable person, with a note about when they leave. It mentions the well-known view that there are two recording angels named Raqib and 'Atid, referencing Surah Qaf.
