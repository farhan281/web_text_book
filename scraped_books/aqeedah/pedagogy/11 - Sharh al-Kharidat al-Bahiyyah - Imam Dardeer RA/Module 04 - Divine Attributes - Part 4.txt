The Scriptural Proof for Ru'ya from Moses' Request
Presents a sam'iyy proof for the possibility of ru'ya based on Moses' request and answers an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the scriptural proof for the possibility of seeing Allah based on Moses' request, and how the Ash'ari response refutes the Mu'tazili objection.

Core Idea
Moses' request proves ru'ya is possible because prophets never ask for the impossible.

This lesson examines a key scriptural (sam'iyy) proof for the permissibility of seeing Allah: the request of Prophet Moses as recorded in the Qur'an. It shows why the request itself implies that ru'ya is possible, and then addresses a major objection often raised by those who deny it.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its English translation carefully. This will give you the full argument in the author's own words.
Notice how the passage is structured: a proof statement, then an objection, then a response.
2
Understand the core proof
The proof has two parts: (1) Moses, as a prophet, would not ask for something impossible, so ru'ya must be possible. (2) Allah conditioned ru'ya on the possible event of the mountain settling, and a condition on a possible makes the conditioned possible.
3
Examine the objection and answer
The Mu'tazila object that Moses asked to teach his people that ru'ya is impossible, and that the condition (settling while moving) is impossible. The answer shows these interpretations are forced and irrational.
Pay attention to how the answer refutes both parts of the objection without introducing new evidence.

It has also been argued for permissibility by a scriptural proof (sam'iyy): that Moses, peace be upon him, asked for it [i.e., seeing Allah] by His saying exalted: 'My Lord, show me [Yourself] that I may look upon You.' For if it were not permissible, he would not have asked for it; otherwise, his request would be either ignorance of the rulings of divinity, or foolishness or frivolity in seeking the impossible, and the prophets are free of all of that. And that Allah exalted conditioned it upon something possible – namely, the settling of the mountain – and that which is conditioned upon the possible is possible, since the meaning of conditioning is informing of the occurrence of the conditioned thing upon the establishment of the condition, and the impossible does not occur under any possible circumstances. So if it were not possible, then falsehood in His exalted information would follow, and that is impossible. And it has been said that Moses' question was not to obtain what he sought, but rather to teach his people that it is impossible when they said to him: 'We will not believe you until we see Allah', and we do not concede that the condition is possible; rather it is the settling of the mountain while it is moving, which is impossible?! Its answer: all of that is contrary to the apparent [meaning], so there is no reason to interpret it thus. For if his people were believers, his statement 'It is impossible' would suffice them; otherwise, they would not believe him in Allah's ruling of impossibility, so the question is frivolous in any case. And the settling while moving is possible in that stillness may occur instead of motion; it is only the combination of motion and stillness that is impossible.

Commentary
This passage presents a scriptural proof (dalil sam'iyy) for the possibility (jawaz) of seeing Allah (ru'ya) based on the request of Moses in the Qur'an. The argument follows a logical sequence: (1) If ru'ya were impossible, Moses, being a prophet, would not have asked for it—since prophets are free from ignorance, foolishness, or frivolity in asking for the impossible. (2) Allah conditioned the response on a possible event (the mountain settling), and a condition attached to a possible thing must itself be possible; otherwise, God's word would be false. The objection from the Mu'tazila claims Moses' question was to teach his people that ru'ya is impossible, and that the condition (mountain settling while moving) is impossible. The answer refutes both: the interpretation is forced and against the apparent meaning; if the people were believers, Moses' assertion of impossibility would suffice, so the question would be pointless; and settling while moving is possible because stillness can replace motion—only the simultaneous combination of motion and stillness is impossible. Thus the original proof stands.

The Transmitted Proofs for Seeing Allah
Cites Qur'an, hadith, and early consensus for ru'ya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Qur'anic and hadith evidence for the believers' vision of Allah and the early consensus on this matter.

Core Idea
Seeing Allah is affirmed by Quran, hadith, and early consensus.

This lesson examines the transmitted proofs (dalil al-naql) for the reality of ru'ya. The author presents the key evidence from the Qur'an and Sunnah, and emphasizes that the early community agreed upon this without metaphorical interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation to grasp the overall argument.
Notice the emphasis on consensus before innovations.
2
Identify the two categories of evidence
The passage cites a Qur'anic verse and a hadith. Find each and note how they are introduced.
The verse is from Surah al-Qiyamah, the hadith is about seeing the moon.
3
Reflect on the role of consensus
The author states the umma agreed on the literal meaning before innovations arose. Ask yourself why this consensus matters.
Consensus is a third pillar of evidence here.

And there has come for it—i.e., for the occurrence of vision for the believers—the transmitted proof (dalil al-naql) from the Book and the Sunna, and the community (umma) has agreed upon that before the appearance of innovations, by keeping the texts as they appear without interpretation, and everything that is so, affirmation of it is obligatory. As for the Book, it is His exalted saying: 'Faces on that day are radiant, looking at their Lord.' And as for the Sunna, it is many hadiths, among them his saying: 'You will see your Lord as you see the moon on the night of the full moon,' and it is a well-known hadith. And the Mu'tazila opposed that, declaring it impossible, clinging to doubts, the strongest of which is the doubt of confrontation, and it was answered that the meaning of 'does not perceive Him' is 'does not encompass Him', for He said 'does not perceive' and did not say 'does not see', so eyesights do not encompass Him just as minds do not encompass Him.

Commentary
This passage presents the transmitted (naqli) evidence for the reality of ru'ya for the believers. The author states that the consensus of the early community (before heretical innovations) was to take the relevant texts in their apparent meaning (zahir) without metaphorical interpretation (ta'wil), and that such texts must be affirmed with certainty. Two key proofs are cited: (1) Qur'an 75:22-23, 'وجوه يومئذ ناضرة * إلى ربها ناظرة' (Faces that day are radiant, looking at their Lord). (2) The well-known hadith that believers will see their Lord as clearly as the full moon. The Mu'tazila rejected ru'ya and relied on several doubts; the strongest is the 'shubhat al-muqabala' (the doubt of confrontation) from the verse 'لا تدركه الأبصار' (Sight does not perceive Him) (6:103). They argued that if Allah could be seen, He would be encompassed by sight, which would imply deficiency. The response distinguishes between 'idrak' (encompassment) and 'ru'ya' (seeing): the verse negates encompassment, not vision itself, just as the mind cannot fully comprehend Allah but can know Him. Thus the proof from the Qur'an and hadith is preserved, and the Mu'tazila objection is refuted.

Attributes Derived from Divine Independence
Dardeer derives attributes from God's independence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how God's absolute self-sufficiency necessitates His essential attributes: existence, pre-eternity, perpetuity, dissimilarity to creation, self-subsistence, and transcendence above deficiencies, including hearing, sight, and speech.

Core Idea
God's independence directly entails His core attributes without dependence on anything.

This lesson explores the chain of reasoning that begins with God's oneness and self-sufficiency. Imam Dardeer shows how from God's independence we derive the foundational attributes that distinguish Him from all creation. The passage is a logical unfolding of necessary truths.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the first part of the passage (up to 'كيف وهو الغني بالإطلاق عن كل ما سواه') to grasp the logical flow from independence to attributes.
Notice the if-then structure: independence necessitates existence, pre-eternity, etc.
2
Identify the attributes from independence
List the attributes that follow from God's independence: existence, pre-eternity, perpetuity, dissimilarity to originated things, self-subsistence, transcendence, hearing, sight, speech, and negation of purposes and inherent power in creatures.
These are all necessary for a self-sufficient being.
3
Understand the reasoning
For each attribute, ask: why must God have it if He is independent? For example, if He were like created things, He would depend on others.
This shows the method of kalam: deriving attributes from a foundational concept.
4
Reflect on the negation of deficiencies
Notice that transcendence leads to positive attributes like hearing and sight, because lacking them would be a deficiency implying dependence.

He then states that the tajalli of actions precedes that of attributes and names. If the wayfarer remains steadfast, he ascends; if not, he falls into heresy and abandons the path.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think that all types of tajalli can occur simultaneously or in any order.
Correction: The author explicitly states that the tajalli of actions must come first, and the wayfarer must pass through it before the higher manifestations.
