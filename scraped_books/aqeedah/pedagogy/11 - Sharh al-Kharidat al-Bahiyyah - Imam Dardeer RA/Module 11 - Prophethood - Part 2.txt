Dardeer cites Quran 40:78 to argue against fixing the number of prophets.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Quran itself leaves the count open, so we must follow suit.

The author begins by establishing a principle: it is better not to fix the number of prophets. He grounds this directly in the Quran, showing that Allah Himself chose not to enumerate all prophets. Then he highlights a logical risk: any fixed number could wrongly include or exclude a true prophet.

Ahad Hadith Do Not Yield Certainty in Belief
Dardeer explains that prophetic numbers from ahad hadith are not certain.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ahad hadith only give probability, and belief requires certainty.

He explains that these narrations are ahad, meaning they have limited chains of transmission, and thus do not produce certainty (qat'). In matters of belief, probability (zann) is not acceptable.

Ahad hadith are not definitively proven.

The author states that such narrations do not yield certainty (qat') and that conjecture (zann) is not considered in matters of belief.
This is a key principle in Islamic theology: belief must be based on certainty.
4
Apply to the issue
Since the hadith only gives probability, we cannot base a firm belief about the number of prophets on it. Therefore, the earlier recommendation to avoid specifying stands.
