The poet explains that God creates actions, grants success, abandons, and that the servant has a kasb that is not effectual.

God creates every action; our responsibility lies in our acquisition, not creation.

He affirms that God is the sole creator of all actions, including those of the servant, and then introduces the divine attributes of tawfiq (granting success) and khidhlan (abandonment). Commentary

Second, the ultimate states of sa'ada (bliss) and shaqawa (wretchedness) are determined by God in pre-eternity and do not change. Third, the servant has a limited kasb (acquisition) – his action is acquired by him but created by God, and he is responsible for it, but this kasb does not have an effect (la yu'akkir) in creation; it is merely a locus of divine power.

Human Agency: Between Compulsion and Free Will
Lines 49-50a: rejecting compulsion and absolute free will, affirming grace and justice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari perspective that human action is neither compelled nor absolutely free, and that divine reward and punishment are based on pure grace and justice.

Core Idea
Human action is acquired, not forced and not independent.

Before we dive into the details of divine justice, the poem first clarifies the nature of human agency. It rejects two extremes: that humans are completely compelled (Jabri) or that they have absolute free will (Qadari). Instead, it affirms a middle path where actions are acquired by human choice yet created by Allah. This sets the stage for understanding why punishment and reward are just and gracious.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rejected extremes
Read the first line and note the two positions being rejected: compulsion (jabr) and absolute free will (ikhtiyār). Understand that the Ash'ari view is a middle path.
The term 'majbūr' means compelled; 'ikhtiyār' here means complete independent choice.
2
Analyze the second line: reward and punishment
The poem then connects human action to divine justice: if Allah rewards, it is by pure grace (faḍl); if He punishes, it is by pure justice (ʿadl). Consider why reward is described as grace and punishment as justice.
Note that 'yubnā' might be read as 'yuthīb' (He rewards) in some interpretations.
3
Reflect on the implications for human responsibility
Ask yourself how this view preserves human accountability without making humans independent of Allah. The commentary explains that actions are 'acquired' (kasb) by humans but created by Allah.
This is a key Ash'ari doctrine that avoids both Jabri fatalism and Qadari libertarianism.

(۲) المراد بالعبد في قول المصنف : وخالق لعبده كلُّ مخلوق يصدر عنه الفعل، عاقلا كان أو غير عاقل، ومن النَّاس من قصر العبد على المُكلّف، أن بعض الأدلة التي ذكرها العلماء في هذه المسألة لا تجري في غير فعل المكلف. و«ما» في قول المصنف: «وما عمل مصدرية تسبك ما بعدها بمصدر ، وتقدير العبارة: (والله خالق لعبده ولعمل عبده) ولا خلاف في أنه خالق لعبده، وإنما ذكره للتمهيد لذكر ما بعده ، وللتأسي بقوله تعالى: ﴿وَاللَّهُ خَلَقَكُمْ وَمَا تَعْمَلُونَ) [الصافات: ٩٦].

أحدهما : بيان هل المُوجِدُ للفعل المنسوب إلى العبد هو قدرة الله تعالى أم قدرة العبد؟ وثانيهما: بيان هل للعبد فيه كَسْبٌ أو لا ؟ وقد ذكر المصنف الفرع الأول هنا، وسيأتي قريباً كلامه عن الفرع الثاني عند قوله : وعندنا للعبد كسب». وخلاصة القول في هذه المسألة : أنَّ مذهب أهلِ السُّنَّة أنَّ أفعال العباد الاختيارية واقعة بقدرة الله تعالى وحدها، وليس لقدرة العباد تأثير فيها ، بل الله تعالى أجرى عادته بأن يوجد في العبد قدرة واختيارًا ، فإذا لم يكن هناك مانع أوجد فيه فعله المذكور مقارنا لهذه القدرة وهذا الاختيار الذين أوجدهما الله تعالى فيه، فيكون فعل العبد - على هذا ـ مخلوقا الله تعالى إبداعاً وإحداثًا ، ومَكْسوبًا للعبد، والمراد بكسبه : مقارنة وجود الفعل لقدرته واختياره، من غير أن يكون هناك تأثير منه، أو مدخل في وجوده سوى كونه محلا، وهذا مذهب الشَّيْخ أبي الحسن الأشعري، وسيأتي إيضاح معنى الكشب في الكلام على الفرع الثاني من فرعي المسألة .

The issue of the creation of the acts of the servants. The obligation of His oneness (1), and the comprehensiveness of His knowledge of all known things, and His power and will over all possible things. He said: And when the obligation of His exclusivity in creation and bringing into existence is established, (then the Creator) means: God Almighty, not other than Him, is the Creator (of His servant) (2) intended meaning: every created thing that issues from him. (1) He said: (from the obligation of His oneness) is an explanation of what preceded. For if you say: Indeed, oneness has been explained by the commentator as that which has no likeness in His essence and attributes, and the Mu'tazila agree to that, so there is no meaning to the inference. The answer: The inference is not based on that particular meaning, but rather with consideration of the generality of the attachment of power and the impossibility of the conjunction of two effective agents on one effect. (2) The intended meaning of 'servant' in the author's statement: And the Creator of his servant is every created thing from which the act issues, whether rational or not. Some people restrict 'servant' to the morally responsible, because some of the proofs mentioned by the scholars in this issue do not apply to the act of the non-responsible. And 'ma' in the author's statement: 'and what they do' is a verbal noun that combines what follows into an infinitive, and the estimation of the phrase is: (And God is the Creator of his servant and the act of his servant). There is no disagreement that He is the Creator of his servant; He only mentioned it as a prelude to what follows, and following His statement: {And God created you and what you do} [Surah as-Saffat: 96]. This issue has two branches: One: the clarification of whether the creator of the act attributed to the servant is the power of God Almighty or the power of the servant? And the second: the clarification of whether the servant has acquisition (kasb) in it or not? The author has mentioned the first branch here, and his speech about the second branch will come soon at his statement: 'And according to us, the servant has acquisition.' And the summary of the statement in this issue: The school of Ahl al-Sunna is that the voluntary acts of the servants occur by the power of God Almighty alone, and the power of the servants has no effect in them. Rather, God Almighty has established His custom by creating in the servant power and choice, and if there is no obstacle, He creates in him the mentioned act concurrent with this power and this choice which God Almighty has created in him. Thus, the act of the servant - upon this - is created by God Almighty by invention and origination, and acquired by the servant. And the intended meaning of his acquisition (kasb) is: the concurrence of the existence of the act with his power and choice, without there being any effect from him or any entry in its existence other than being a locus. This is the school of Shaykh Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari, and the clarification of the meaning of 'kasb' will come in the discussion of the second branch of the issue. And most of the Mu'tazila said: The acts of the servants occur by their power alone, independently without compulsion, but by choice. And a group of theologians held that they occur by both powers together. This group differs among themselves: some of them say that the acts of the servants occur by the combination of the two powers - the power of God Almighty and the power of the servant - such that both powers attach to the act itself. This is the school of al-Ustadh from Ahl al-Sunna and al-Najjar from the Mu'tazila; according to them, the conjunction of two effective agents on one effect is not impossible. And some of them say that the acts of the servants occur by both powers together, such that the power of God attaches to the essence of the act, and the power of the servant attaches to the description of the act such as being obedience or disobedience and other descriptions that do not describe His acts, as in slapping the orphan either as discipline or harm: the slapping itself occurs by the power of God Almighty and His influence; as for its being considered obedience if he intended discipline, and disobedience if he intended harm, that occurs by the power of the servant and his influence. And the philosophers, followed by Imam al-Haramayn, held that the voluntary acts of the servants occur in the manner of obligation and the impossibility of failure, by a power that God Almighty creates in the servant when the conditions are fulfilled and the obstacles are removed.

Commentary

The Sunni Position on Human Actions
Summarizes the Sunni (Ash'ari) position and the meaning of kasb.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni (Ash'ari) position that God alone creates voluntary acts and the meaning of 'kasb' (acquisition) as concurrent power and choice without causal effect.

Core Idea
God creates the act; the servant acquires it through concurrent power and choice.

Having defined the issue and its branches, this lesson presents the core Sunni position as articulated by al-Ash'ari. It explains that voluntary acts are created solely by God, while the servant's power and choice are created by God and concurrent with the act, without any causal role. This is the doctrine of 'kasb'.

Guided Path
1
Read the Sunni position statement
Focus on the sentence that begins 'wa khulasatu al-qawl...' and understand the key claim: acts occur by God's power alone.
Note the phrase 'waqiat bi-qudrati Allahi ta'ala wahdaha'.
2
Understand how the servant's power fits
The passage says God creates in the servant power and choice, and the act occurs concurrent with these. That is 'kasb'.
Kasb is not causation but concomitance.
3
Note the Ash'ari attribution
The position is attributed to Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari, and the author promises further clarification later.
This is the orthodox Sunni position.
4
Contrast with other views (preview)
Mentally note that this denies the Mu'tazili view of independent human power and prepares for the next lesson.

Core Idea

The Necessitarian View of Philosophers and Imam al-Haramayn
States the necessitarian view of philosophers and Imam al-Haramayn on human acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the necessitarian position that human voluntary acts occur necessarily by a power created by God when conditions are met, leaving no room for genuine human choice.

Core Idea
Human actions occur necessarily by divine power, with no real human causality.

After presenting the combinational views, the passage turns to the position of the philosophers, followed by Imam al-Haramayn. They hold that voluntary human acts occur by way of necessity and impossibility of non-occurrence, by a power that God creates in the servant when conditions are present and impediments absent. This view effectively denies any genuine human contribution, setting up the need for the Sunni doctrine of kasb.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proponents
The passage mentions 'the philosophers' and then 'Imam al-Haramayn' (al-Juwayni) as holding this view.
Imam al-Haramayn is a major Ash'ari theologian.
2
Understand the mechanism
They say that acts occur by way of necessity and impossibility of non-occurrence. This means that when God creates power in the servant under the right conditions, the act must happen; it cannot be otherwise.
This is a deterministic position.
3
Note the conditions
The power is created 'when the conditions are realized and the impediments are absent.' This implies that human action is completely dependent on divine creation of power.
This view minimizes human agency.
4
Contrast with the combinational views
Unlike the previous views that gave some role to human power, this view holds that the human power is merely instrumental and does not truly cause the act.
This is considered a weaker position by later Sunnis.

Logical Clarification: Negation and Universality
Footnote clarifies the logical rule of negation and universality, showing the intended meaning is universal negation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical distinction between 'negation of universality' and 'universal negation' and how it applies to the phrase 'not everything he does is by choice'.

Core Idea
When negation precedes a universal, it may still mean universal negation if context supports it.

The previous lesson established that the servant does not create any action. However, the phrase 'laysa kullan yaf'alu ikhtiyaran' (not everything he does is by choice) could be misinterpreted according to standard logical rules to imply that some actions are created by the servant. This lesson delves into the footnote's explanation: why this phrase actually means universal negation, and how we know this from the context. We'll learn about the probabilistic nature of the logical rule.

Guided Path
1
Understand the logical rule
The footnote explains that if a negation tool (like 'laysa') precedes a universal tool (like 'kull'), the usual meaning is 'negation of universality' (salb al-'umum). This means the negation applies to the aggregate, allowing some individuals to be excluded. For example, 'Not every student performs his duties' implies some students do perform.
This is the standard logical rule.
2
See the apparent problem
Applying this rule to 'laysa kullan yaf'alu ikhtiyaran' would yield: 'Not every action the servant does is by choice' – implying some actions are created by the servant. This contradicts the intended Ash'ari belief.
This would be a doctrinal error.
3
Learn the resolution
The footnote states the rule is 'probabilistic' (aghlabiyya). Sometimes, even when negation precedes a universal, the intended meaning is 'universal negation' ('umum al-salb) – i.e., every individual is negated. The context, especially the earlier statement 'wa lam yakun mu'thiran' (and he is not an influencer), confirms that the servant has no creative influence. Thus, the phrase means he creates no action at all.
The Quranic verse about Allah not loving every conceited boaster is given as an analogous example.

The apparent meaning of this expression according to the rules of logic is corrupt; because the rule is that if a negation tool like 'la' or 'laysa' or 'ma' and a universal tool like 'kull' or 'jami'' or 'ammah' come together, and the negation tool precedes the universal tool, it indicates negation of universality, i.e., the negation applies to the aggregate as a whole, which does not preclude some individuals being outside the ruling. If you say 'Every Muslim does not neglect his duty', that means every individual Muslim does not neglect his duty. An analogous example is the verse: 'And Allah does not love every conceited boaster' (Quran 57:23). Commentary
This passage refutes the Mu'tazila who claim that the servant creates his own actions.
