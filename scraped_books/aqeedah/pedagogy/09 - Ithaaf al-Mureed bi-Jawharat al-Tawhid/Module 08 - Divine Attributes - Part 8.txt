The Comprehensive Meaning of the Two Testimonies
Al-Sharnubi defines 'wa jami' ma'na' and explains the inclusion of all beliefs in the two testimonies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the phrase 'wa jami' ma'na' refers to the intended meaning that the two testimonies of Islam encompass all obligatory beliefs concerning divinity and prophethood in terms of obligation, possibility, and impossibility.

Core Idea
The two testimonies are the comprehensive foundation of all Islamic creed.

Al-Sharnubi begins by unpacking the phrase 'wa jami' ma'na' from the poem. He explains that it refers to the meaning that has been established—the totality of beliefs that every Muslim must hold. This lesson clarifies what that meaning is and how the two testimonies relate to Islam itself.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that Al-Sharnubi uses a very compressed style here.
2
Understand the definition of 'wa jami' ma'na'
Identify that the phrase refers to the intended meaning which is the sum of all obligatory beliefs. Note that these beliefs are categorized by three rational judgments: wajib, jaiz, mustahil.
3
Examine the three relationships to Islam
The passage gives three ways the two testimonies relate to Islam: as the greatest part, as essential for attainment, or as indicators. Reflect on why these are important.
Each relationship emphasizes the centrality of the testimonies.

Commentary
This passage defines the phrase 'wa jami' ma'na' from the poem and explains how the two testimonies encompass all obligatory beliefs. It begins by stating that the phrase means the intended meaning of the word that has been established in a fixed place, and that this meaning refers to all the creeds necessarily believed by law, which pertain to divinity and prophethood in terms of obligation (wujub), possibility (jawaz), and impossibility (istihalah). Then it explains the relationship of the two testimonies to Islam, giving three possibilities: they are the greatest part, or without them Islam is not attained, or they indicate Islam. Then it focuses on the first testimony: it establishes divinity for Allah and negates it from all else. It then defines the reality of divinity as necessity of existence and self-eternity, which entails independence from all others and dependence of all others on Him. From that follow other necessary attributes like permanence, being different from contingents, self-subsistence, transcendence from deficiencies, and freedom from obligation. The author then explains that the dependence of contingents necessitates Allah's life, power, will, knowledge, oneness, and non-effect of others. Once these are obligatory (wajib), their opposites become impossible (mustahil), and whatever else is possible (jaiz). Thus the first testimony contains all three rational judgments (wajib, mustahil, jaiz) regarding Allah.

The First Testimony and the Three Rational Judgments
Al-Sharnubi shows how the first testimony encompasses the three rational judgments regarding Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the first testimony (لا إله إلا الله) establishes divinity and necessarily leads to the three rational judgments of obligation, impossibility, and possibility regarding Allah.

Core Idea
The first testimony logically entails all necessary divine attributes and their opposites.

In the second part of the passage, Al-Sharnubi demonstrates the rich implications of the first testimony. He shows that by affirming divinity for Allah and negating it from all else, the testimony logically implies a chain of necessary attributes, their impossible opposites, and all possibilities in between. This lesson traces that logical derivation.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Pay attention to the logical connectives like 'فإن', 'وكما', 'ومتى'.
2
Identify the starting point: divinity affirmed and negated
The first testimony establishes divinity for Allah and negates it from everything else. That is the foundation.
3
Follow the entailment chain
From the reality of divinity (wajib al-wujud) follow necessary attributes like independence, life, power, etc. Notice how each step is derived from the previous.
This is a crucial logical argument.
4
Conclude with the three rational judgments
Once the necessary attributes are established, their opposites become impossible, and all else is possible. Thus the testimony contains wajib, mustahil, and jaiz regarding Allah.

The Consensus on Prophet Muhammad's Superiority
States the consensus that Prophet Muhammad is the best of creation and commands acceptance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the superiority of Prophet Muhammad over all creation is a matter of consensus (ijma') and why it is impermissible to dispute it.

Core Idea
The Prophet's superiority is not debatable—it is a settled consensus.

In this passage, the author establishes a foundational belief of Ahl al-Sunnah: that Prophet Muhammad is the best of all creatures. He presents this as a matter of consensus (ijma'), and commands the reader to accept it without dispute. Let's examine the text and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic carefully, then the English translation. Notice the author's commanding tone.
2
Identify key terms: إجماع and شقاق
These terms are central. Understanding 'ijma' as a source of authority is crucial.
The footnote explains the etymology of shiqaq.
3
Reflect on the command to turn away from dispute
Why does the author emphasize avoiding dispute? Because the matter is settled by consensus.
This connects to the broader principle of respecting ijma.
4
Apply: Why is breaking consensus not permissible?
The passage says directly: because it is not permissible to break consensus. This is a foundational principle in Islamic theology.
Consensus is a binding proof.

Ranking of Virtue: Prophets and Angels
Explains the ranking of virtue after Prophet Muhammad: prophets then angels, with details on variation and belief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic hierarchy of virtue after Prophet Muhammad, including the positions of prophets and angels, the variations among prophets, and the basis of their ranking.

Core Idea
After Muhammad, the most virtuous are the prophets, then the angels.

This lesson clarifies the order of virtue among Allah's creation. Starting from our Prophet Muhammad, the text explains how the prophets follow him, and then the angels follow the prophets. It also touches on how believers should approach the ranking of specific individuals based on revelation, avoiding speculation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the overall hierarchy
The passage begins by stating that the prophets follow Prophet Muhammad in virtue, and after them come the angels. Grasp this basic structure before diving into details.
2
Explore variation among prophets
Notice that prophets vary in their closeness to Muhammad. The text highlights the Ul al-Azm messengers as superior to other messengers, and messengers superior to non-messenger prophets.
The ranking among prophets is based on revelation, not speculation.
3
Learn the rule of belief in ranking
The passage emphasizes that we must believe in superiority according to what has been reported, and we must not rush to designate superiority where no specific revelation has come. This is why the author left some ambiguity.
This principle prevents theological error.
4
Angels rank after prophets
After the prophets, the rank of angels follows. In general, angels are above non-prophet humans, but in detail, only the chief angels (like Jibril, Mikail, Israfil, Azrael) directly follow the prophets.
Even non-messenger angels are superior to saintly humans.

The Divine Permission to Forgive Sins Other than Shirk
Al-Laqani discusses the permissibility and wisdom of forgiving sins other than shirk.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the theological basis for why Allah may forgive sins other than shirk, and the wisdom behind making forgiveness conditional on divine will.

Core Idea
Forgiveness is Allah's right, but His mercy outweighs punishment for sins, not for disbelief.

This lesson explores a crucial distinction in Islamic theology: why Allah can forgive any sin except shirk. Al-Laqani lays out a rational and scriptural argument that forgiveness of sins is a beneficent act with no harm, supported by multiple Quranic verses, and explains the wisdom behind differentiating sins from disbelief. Let's examine his reasoning and the evidence he provides.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of pardon
Read the first part where Al-Laqani defines 'al-‘afw' as dropping a punishment due to Allah. Notice that he argues it is good because it benefits the servant without harming anyone.
This sets the moral framework for forgiveness.
2
Examine the Quranic evidence
Three verses are cited. Identify each and how they build the case: (1) Allah accepts repentance and pardons, (2) Allah forgives all sins, (3) Allah does not forgive shirk but forgives what is less to whom He wills.
The third verse is key—it explicitly excludes shirk.
3
Clarify what forgiveness means
Note that forgiveness involves both waiving punishment and concealing the sin after accountability.
4
Understand the wisdom behind the distinction
Al-Laqani gives two reasons: sins involve fear and hope, and they arise from momentary passion, whereas disbelief is a permanent doctrine whose sanctity cannot be lifted.
This rationalizes why shirk is unforgivable without repentance.
5
Reflect on the implications
Consider how this balances divine justice and mercy, and why it encourages repentance while maintaining the gravity of disbelief.

The Ash'ari View: Existence Is the Reality of a Thing
Al-Shanwani defines the Ash'ari position on existence and the non-existent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari doctrine that existence is not a separate attribute added to the essence, and the consequent status of the non-existent.

Core Idea
For the Ash'aris, existence is the very reality of a thing, not a quality like redness.

This passage from al-Shanwani's commentary clarifies the Ash'ari position on existence, distinguishing it from the Mu'tazilite view. Al-Shanwani explains that existence is not an additional attribute that inheres in a subject; rather, what exists in the external is the essence itself qualified by existence. This leads to the conclusion that the non-existent has no reality in the external world.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the English translation. Note the key terms: 'sam'iyyat', 'wujud', 'mahiyya', 'ma'dum'.
The passage is dense; take your time with each phrase.
2
Identify the central claim
Find the statement that existence is 'the very reality' (ayn al-haqiqa) and not additional to quiddity. Understand the contrast with the example of redness.
The redness analogy shows existence is not a quality like color.
3
Understand the consequence
See how the Ash'ari view leads to the conclusion that the non-existent is nothing in the external—it only becomes real through existence.
This refutes the Mu'tazilite view that the non-existent has some reality.

Consequently, the non‑existent is nothing in the external world—it has no reality or fixity; it only becomes real when it comes into existence. This refutes the Mu'tazilite view that the non‑existent is a thing with some reality.

Preserving the Rational Soul and Retaliation
Defines preservation of rational soul and its legal implications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Islamic law protects the rational soul and how retaliation (qisas) upholds this protection.

Core Idea
The rational soul is inviolable; qisas deters its violation.

This lesson examines a brief but foundational passage from the Itḥāf al-Murīd. The commentator specifies that the universal of 'preserving the soul' refers specifically to the rational soul, which distinguishes humans from animals. This distinction grounds the legal prohibition of killing and maiming without right, and it explains why the law of retaliation (qisas) was legislated. As you read, consider how a single attribute—rationality—carries profound legal consequences.
