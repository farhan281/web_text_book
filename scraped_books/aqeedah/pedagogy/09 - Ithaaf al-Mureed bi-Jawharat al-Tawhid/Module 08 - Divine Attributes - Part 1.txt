This passage is a definition and classification of the theological position regarding the relationship between the divine name (الاسم) and the named (المسمى) essence. Grammatical Analysis of 'Wajib Lahu al-Wujud'
Grammatical analysis of the phrase and debate on predicate/subject.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the grammatical structure of the phrase 'fa-wajib lahu al-wujud' and the debate over whether it involves a fronted predicate or an indefinite subject.

Core Idea
Grammar clarifies theology: the fronted predicate emphasizes God's existence.

The commentator now analyzes the grammar of the author's line, specifically the function of the particle 'fa' (fa' al-fasiha) and the order of words. He explains why 'wajib' is best considered a fronted predicate (khabar muqaddam) and refutes an alternative view that treats it as an indefinite subject. This lesson focuses on the grammatical reasoning that supports the intended theological emphasis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the 'fa' and its function
The commentator explains that the 'fa' in 'fa-wajib lahu' is 'fa' al-fasiha, which reveals an implied condition (e.g., 'if you want to know... then...'). This sets up the grammatical analysis.
The 'fa' links the statement to an implied conditional, making the sentence rhetorical.
2
Analyze the first grammatical view
The commentator states the first and correct view: 'wajib' is a fronted predicate (khabar muqaddam), and 'al-wujud' is a delayed subject (mubtada' mu'akhkhar). The fronting is for emphasis (ihtimam), not for exclusivity.
This view maintains the standard Arabic order of subject-predicate but emphasizes the predicate.
3
Examine the alternative view
Some scholars considered 'wajib' to be an indefinite subject (mubtada') with the prepositional phrase 'lahu' attached to it, and 'al-wujud' as the predicate. They justified it by saying the unknown (obligation) is judged by the known (existence).
This view violates a grammatical rule: a definite noun cannot be predicated from an indefinite.
4
Understand the refutation
The commentator rejects the alternative view because it contradicts the practice of the Arabs: it is not transmitted that they predicate a definite from an indefinite. Therefore, the first view (fronted predicate) is preferable.
The lesson concludes that the first view is both grammatically sound and theologically appropriate.

Al-Razi's Definition of Existence
Al-Razi defines existence as a necessary uncaused state of the essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's definition of existence as a 'state' and how it excludes other types of attributes through its precise phrasing.

Core Idea
Existence is a state inseparable from the essence, not caused by any cause.

Al-Razi offers a precise definition of existence that distinguishes it from other attributes. The definition is built around the concept of a 'hal' (state) that necessarily belongs to the essence for its entire duration, uncaused. Understanding this definition clarifies how existence relates to the essence and what it is not.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition
Read al-Razi's definition: 'The state necessarily belonging to the essence as long as the essence is not caused by a cause.' Notice the three key components: 'state,' 'necessarily belonging,' and 'not caused.'
The Arabic uses 'hal' which is a technical term.
2
Analyze each part
Examine the explanation of each phrase: 'necessarily belonging' means inseparable; 'as long as the essence' means for the entire duration; 'not caused' rules out any external cause. See how these components exclude attributes of meanings and negative attributes.
3
Understand the exclusions
The definition excludes attributes of meanings (sifat al-ma'ani) because they are not states; negative attributes (sifat salbiyya) are also excluded. Entitative attributes (sifat ma'nawiyya) are excluded either by the 'as long as' clause or the 'not caused' clause.
The exclusion of entitative attributes is twofold: either because their permanence depends on the permanence of their meanings, or because they are caused.
4
Reflect on the terminology
Note that calling something a 'hal' is a terminological choice. There is no dispute over terms; what matters is the conceptual distinction.

Regarding meaning, there is a disagreement among theologians: some say it is a self-attribute (nafsiyya) like existence, some say it is a negative attribute (salbiyya) like dissimilarity, and some say it is an attribute of meaning (ma'ani) like knowledge. The author states the preponderant view is that qidam is a negative attribute, and he explains the meaning: it negates that God's existence is preceded by non-existence. He then mentions that some scholars (including al-Ash'ari according to al-Malawi) consider it a self-attribute, but he refutes this: if it were a self-attribute, the essence would not be conceivable without it, yet the essence is conceivable as existing without the qidam label; thus it is not a self-attribute. Even weaker is the view of Ibn Kullab that it is an attribute of meaning subsisting in the essence.
