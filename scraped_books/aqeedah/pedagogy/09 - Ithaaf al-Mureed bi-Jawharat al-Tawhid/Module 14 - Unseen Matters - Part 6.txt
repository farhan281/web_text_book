The Prophet's Basin and the Faithful
Explanation of the basin verse and the covenant

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the nature of the Prophet's basin, the covenant of faith, and why faith in the basin is obligatory based on mutawatir hadith.

Core Idea
The Prophet's basin is a reward for those who keep their covenant of faith.

In this lesson, we explore the first part of the commentary on verse 112, which describes the Prophet's basin and the believers who drink from it. The commentator explains the covenant taken from all humanity and emphasizes that only the faithful of this ummah will drink from our Prophet's basin, as confirmed by abundant hadiths.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the translation to grasp the commentator's explanation.
The passage includes footnotes that clarify points.
2
Identify the covenant and its significance
The covenant is the primordial pledge to believe in Allah, the Last Day, and follow His messengers. Those who die keeping it are faithful.
3
Understand why the basin is specific to this ummah
The commentator notes that each prophet has a basin, but our Prophet's basin is singled out because it is confirmed by mutawatir hadiths.
This counters a potential misunderstanding.
4
Recognize the role of mutawatir hadith
Belief in the basin is obligatory because the narrations reach the level of tawatur. This is a key principle in kalam.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on verse 112 of the poem, dealing with the prophetic basin (al-hawd). The commentator begins by stating the section on the heard matters and notes the obligation to believe in the basin's existence. He then explains the verse: 'A people obtain drink from it who were faithful with their covenant.' The covenant is the one taken from all humanity in pre-eternity, and those who uphold it are the believers of this ummah. The commentator clarifies that although the description could apply to previous nations, hadiths indicate that only the believers of the Prophet Muhammad will drink from his basin. This is because each prophet has his own basin, but our Prophet's basin is specifically mentioned due to the abundant mutawatir hadiths. Then the phrase 'say: He is driven away, whoever transgressed' is explained: those who are repelled are those who changed and altered the covenant of Islam. The commentator lists categories of such people: apostates, innovators (like Kharijites, Rawafid, Mu'tazila), oppressive rulers, open sinners, and those of deviation. A distinction is made: the apostate is eternally punished, while the sinner is under God's will, possibly pardoned or punished.

The Driven Away: Apostates, Innovators, and Sinners
Classification of those driven away from the basin

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the categories of people who are driven away from the Prophet's basin and the distinction between apostasy and sin in terms of punishment.

Core Idea
Those who break the covenant are driven away, but the punishment differs between apostates and sinners.

In this lesson, we continue the commentary and learn about those who will be repelled from the basin. The commentator lists apostates, innovators, oppressors, and open sinners, and clarifies that apostates face eternal punishment while sinners are under God's will.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the list of categories and the final distinction.
The passage ends with a note about the will of Allah.
2
List the categories of the driven away
From the passage, identify each group mentioned: apostates, innovators (Kharijites, Rawafid, Mu'tazila), oppressive rulers, open sinners, and people of deviation.
Note that innovators are described as 'more severely repelled'.
3
Understand the distinction in punishment
The commentator distinguishes between those who change through apostasy and those who change through sins. Apostates are eternally punished, while sinners are under Allah's will.
This shows that not all driven away are equal in punishment.

The Major Intercession of Prophet Muhammad: Obligatory Belief and Definition
Definition of intercession and explanation of three obligatory beliefs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that accepting the major intercession of Prophet Muhammad is an obligatory belief based on revelation (samiyyat), its linguistic and technical definition, and the three underlying obligations implied in the passage.

Core Idea
Intercession is not a rational necessity but a revealed obligation.

This lesson explores a fundamental article of faith in Sunni Islam: the major intercession of the Prophet Muhammad. The passage establishes that belief in this intercession is obligatory by revelation (samiyyat), not by reason, and defines intercession in both linguistic and technical senses. Additionally, the text hints at three essential obligations that must be affirmed regarding intercession.

Guided Path
1
Understand the source of obligation
The passage declares intercession as 'obligatory by hearing' meaning it is a revealed truth, not a rational necessity. Recognize that this is a sam'iyyat matter.
The term 'sami'an' indicates reliance on revelation.
2
Define key terms
Learn the linguistic meaning of shafa'ah as 'al-waseelah wa at-talab' (the means and the request) and the technical definition as 'asking for good for others.' Also distinguish between 'mushaffa'' (with fatha) meaning the one whose intercession is accepted, and the variant with kasra meaning the one who accepts intercession.
This distinction clarifies who is interceding and who is accepting.
3
Identify the three implied obligations
The commentator indicates that the verse points to three obligatory beliefs: that intercession is true, that it is specifically for the Prophet Muhammad, and that it is a grant from Allah not prevented by any condition.
These obligations are implicit in the phrase 'mushaffa' Muhammad' and the context.

Commentary
This passage is a theological commentary on the nature of martyrs' souls after death, specifically addressing the well-known hadith reports that the souls of martyrs are in the crops of green birds or in the cavities of birds. The commentator begins by explaining the poet's line about the martyr's provision (rizq) from God: the martyr is described as being provided with desired bliss from the gardens (al-jannāt). Then he addresses the problematic interpretation of the hadith: if the souls are literally inside birds, how can they experience bliss? He offers three figurative interpretations to resolve this: First, the preposition 'fī' (in) is interpreted as 'on' and the birds themselves are the means of transport — the souls ride the birds. Second, the birds' cavities are like transparent, spacious howdahs (litters) that do not confine the soul. Third, the souls are like birds in the speed of traversing long distances; the reference to 'cavities' is metaphorical for swift movement. He explicitly negates two literal readings: that the souls have wings and fly, or that they re-enter other bodies (i.e., reincarnation). The purpose of these interpretations, he states, is to avoid the false doctrine of metempsychosis (tanāsukh), the belief that souls transmigrate into new bodies after death, which is incompatible with Islamic teachings on the resurrection.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and refutation combined. It opens with 'The Issue of Provision', specifying the correct placement of the words 'al-makruh' and 'al-muharram' in the poem. Al-Bajuri first defines 'al-makruh' as what God or His messenger prohibits with a non-emphatic prohibition, explaining that this includes both explicit prohibitions (by literal indication) and implicit ones (derived from commands). He then defines 'al-muharram' more expansively: anything explicitly forbidden by God or His messenger, or by consensus, or by clear analogy, or that carries a fixed punishment, discretionary punishment, or a severe threat not interpreted metaphorically. The definition covers both hidden harms (like usury) and obvious harms (like poison and wine). The footnotes by al-Shanwani provide scriptural evidence and distinctions, including the debate on fees for teaching the Qur'an. The passage concludes by stating that this refutes the Mu'tazila, who deny that the forbidden can be provision because they believe in rational good and evil — that is, they hold that reason alone can determine moral value, and since the forbidden is evil, it cannot be from God's provision. The Ash'ari position affirms that all things, including the forbidden, are provision from God, as provision is defined as what benefits the body or sustains it, regardless of legal permissibility.
