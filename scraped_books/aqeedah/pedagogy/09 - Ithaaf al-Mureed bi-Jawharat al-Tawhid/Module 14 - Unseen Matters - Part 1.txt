Seeing Allah in the Afterlife
Sanusi affirms that believers will see Allah in the afterlife with physical eyes but without modality or limitation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ashʿari doctrine of the beatific vision: that believers will see Allah in Paradise with their physical eyes, but without modality, direction, or limitation.

Core Idea
Believers will see Allah in the afterlife — truly, but without 'how'.

The poem now turns to one of the most important matters of the afterlife: the vision of Allah by the believers. Sanusi affirms that this vision will be a real sight with the eyes, yet completely free from the limitations of physical vision in this world. Let's examine his precise wording.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without overanalyzing. Then read the literal translation. Notice the key affirmations and restrictions.
The passage is only two lines, but packed with meaning.
2
Understand 'ru'ya bi-l-abṣār'
This phrase means a sensory vision with the eyes, not a metaphorical or intellectual sight. The Ashʿaris affirm this literally against the Muʿtazila.
3
Grasp the two restrictions
Bilā kayf (without modality) means we do not ask 'how' it happens. Lā inḥiṣār (without limitation) means Allah is not confined to a place, direction, or finite form.
These restrictions safeguard tanzih (transcendence).
4
Consider the term 'jā'iz'
The vision is possible (jā'iz) because it is attached to a revealed promise. It is not rationally necessary, but it is established through scripture (samʿiyyāt).
This counters the Muʿtazila claim that vision is rationally impossible.
5
Reflect on the second line
The second line mentions that this vision is for the believers in the afterlife, and possibly for the chosen one (the Prophet) in this world, as in the Miʿrāj.
Some commentators interpret 'al-mukhtār' as the Prophet.

Commentary
This passage discusses the beatific vision (ru’yat Allāh) in the afterlife. It affirms that Allah will be seen by the eyes (abṣār) of the believers in Paradise, but without modality (kayf) and without limitation (inḥiṣār). This is the Ashʿari position against the Muʿtazila who denied the possibility of seeing Allah. The second line is somewhat corrupt but likely states that this vision is established for the believers in the afterlife, and perhaps for the chosen (al-mukhtār) in this world (as in the case of Prophet Muhammad on the night of Miʿrāj) or that it is a matter of jā’iz (possible) that has been revealed.

?

Guardian Angels and Self-Accounting
Two verses on guardian angels, recording angels, and self-accounting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic beliefs about guardian and recording angels and the importance of self-accounting.

Core Idea
Every person is accompanied by angels who record deeds and must hold themselves accountable.

The poem begins by affirming that every person has appointed angels who guard and record their deeds without negligence. This establishes a foundation of divine oversight. The following verse then calls the believer to take heed by actively accounting for their own soul and reducing worldly hopes.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic verses
Read verses 85-86 in Arabic carefully. Notice the terms 'ḥāfiẓūn' and 'kātibūn' for the angels, and the command 'ḥāsibi al-nafs'.
These verses set the tone for personal responsibility.
2
Understand the function of the angels
The first verse states that there are guardians (ḥāfiẓūn) who protect, and writers (kātibūn) who record deeds. They are chosen and never neglect.
This emphasizes constant divine vigilance.
3
Reflect on self-accounting
The second verse commands 'fa-ḥāsibi al-nafs' (so account yourself) and 'qallili al-āmāl' (reduce hope). This is a practical directive to evaluate one's actions and detach from worldly illusions.
Self-accounting is a key spiritual practice.

Action, Striving, and Death
Four verses on action, striving, death, and the angel of death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the importance of fulfilling God's commands even forgetfully, the value of striving, and the obligatory belief in death and the angel of death.

Core Idea
Strive in obedience and accept death as an appointed moment, for effort leads to attainment.

These verses guide the believer in practical effort and correct belief about death. They encourage doing any of God's commands even if forgetful, and highlight that striving may lead to success. Then they affirm the obligatory belief in death and the angel of death, emphasizing that every killed person dies exactly by his appointed lifespan.

This teaches persistence and hope in effort.

Grasp the creed about death

This affirms predestination and the angel's role.

The Soul and the Coccyx Bone
Two verses on the soul's annihilation and the coccyx bone.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic discussion on whether the soul perishes at the trumpet blast and the role of the coccyx bone as the basis for resurrection.

Core Idea
The soul's survival after the trumpet blast is a point of scholarly disagreement, but the coccyx bone remains as the seed for resurrection.

The final two verses address eschatological details: disagreement among scholars about the annihilation of the soul at the first trumpet blast, with al-Subki preferring its survival, and the nature of the coccyx bone which al-Muzani argued decays, unlike the soul. These show the precision of theological debates on resurrection.

The coccyx is said to be the origin from which the body is resurrected.

Specification of 'Everything Perishes'
Verse 93 on the specification of 'Everything perishes'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how scholars have specified the general Quranic statement 'Everything is perishing' and why it is important to follow their specification.

Core Idea
Everything perishes save His Face, but scholars specified its application.

This verse from al-Jawhara addresses a key Quranic verse (Q28:88) that everything perishes except Allah's Face. The poet notes that scholars have specified (khaṣṣaṣū) the generality of this verse, meaning they identified what is exempted. It is a reminder that apparent general statements in revelation may have specific applications known to the scholars.

Commentary
These verses cover more eschatological and theological issues. Verse 93 refers to the Quranic verse 'Everything is perishing except His Face' (Q28:88), which scholars have specified (khaṣṣaṣū) to exclude certain things; one should seek what they specified. Verse 94 advises not to delve into the nature of the spirit (rūḥ) because no clear text from the Lawgiver (al-Shāri') has come about its detailed nature, although some information exists. Verse 95 reports Imam Malik's view that the spirit is a form (ṣūra) like the body, and that the transmitted text is sufficient. This lesson explains a key exception to the general rule of accountability. The hadith identifies six types of individuals who, due to physical or mental conditions or living in a period without prophets, are not held accountable. Al-Shanwānī's commentary clarifies each category and resolves a common misconception about the term 'akmah'.

Core Idea
God's justice tests those who never heard the message in an intelligible form.

This lesson examines a hadith narrated by Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani that describes how Allah will test a specific group of people on the Day of Judgment. These are individuals who never received the prophetic message in a clear form due to various circumstances. The narration lists several categories and outlines the procedure of their test. Understanding this hadith helps us appreciate the comprehensiveness of divine justice.

Ahl al-Fatrah: The Correct Ruling of Salvation
Al-Amir concludes that ahl al-fatrah are saved, refuting contrary views.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the correct Islamic ruling on the people of the fatrah and the rationale behind their salvation, including the distinction between obligation and compulsion in the Hereafter.

Core Idea
All ahl al-fatrah are saved by divine mercy, regardless of their actions.

Al-Amir addresses a delicate theological question: what happens to people who never received a prophetic message? He navigates between conflicting reports and established principles to affirm that they are saved. This lesson unpacks his argument, distinguishing between obligation and compulsion in the Hereafter.

Guided Path
1
Understand the term ahl al-fatrah
Learn who the ahl al-fatrah are—people who lived in periods without a prophet—and why their fate is debated.
2
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage slowly, then follow with the translation to grasp al-Amir's flow.
Notice the technical terms: taklif, qahr wa jabr.
3
Grasp the key distinction
Focus on why the command to enter the fire is not a binding obligation (taklif) but an act of compulsion (qahr wa jabr). This is central to the argument.
Obligation only applies in this world; the Hereafter is for recompense.
4
Examine the handling of conflicting reports
See how al-Amir deals with reports that some ahl al-fatrah might be punished—he categorizes them as weak (ahad) or exceptional.
This shows the principle that certain (qat'i) evidence takes precedence.
5
Reflect on the conclusion and its implications
Understand that the truth (al-haqq) is that all ahl al-fatrah are saved, and imagine how this reflects on divine mercy and justice.

Commentary
This passage is a theological argument by al-Amir concerning the fate of ahl al-fatrah (people who never received a prophetic message). He first comments on the hadith of the fire test, clarifying that the fire could be Hell or something else, and the duration of punishment (for those who fail) is uncertain. He emphasizes that the command to enter the fire is not a binding obligation (taklif) because there is no obligation in the Hereafter; it is an act of divine compulsion (qahr wa jabr). He cites al-Malawi's gloss to explain that Allah's anger on that day is unprecedented and He is not questioned about His actions. Al-Amir then states that Ibn Hajar's interpretation (possibly implying that some ahl al-fatrah may be punished) is contrary to the more correct view (al-asahh). The truth (al-haqq) is that all ahl al-fatrah are saved (najun), and the imams have even said this applies if they had changed and worshipped idols. Any reports of punishment for some are either weak solitary hadiths that cannot override certain knowledge, or refer to special cases known only to Allah.

Answering Quranic Objections on Acquired Knowledge
Objection-and-answer with four interpretative responses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how scholars interpret Quranic verses that seem to imply Allah acquires knowledge, and you will know the four classic responses that preserve Allah's eternal knowledge.

Core Idea
Quranic verses about Allah 'knowing' after events are metaphorical, not literal.

The Quran contains verses that, taken literally, suggest Allah learns new things from events. This contradicts the doctrine that Allah's knowledge is eternal and unchanging. In this passage, the author presents the objection and then gives four scholarly answers that explain why the apparent meaning is not intended. Understanding these answers is essential for correct interpretation of these verses.

Understand the theological problem
Reflect on why the apparent meaning of these verses contradicts the Islamic doctrine of Allah's eternal, unchanging knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. The objection is that many Quranic verses appear to imply that Allah acquires knowledge through events, which would contradict the impossibility of acquired knowledge. The objector cites several verses: from Surat al-Kahf (12), al-Baqara (143), Aal Imran (142), al-Kahf (7), Muhammad (31), al-Anfal (66), and Saba' (21). The answer is that the apparent meaning is not intended. The author then presents four scholarly responses (ajwiba) other than what the commentator previously mentioned. Just as a king says 'We conquered' while his army conquered, so Allah attributes to Himself the actions of His chosen ones. Second response (al-thānī): The verse means that the event occurs and becomes part of our knowledge; Allah's eternal knowledge of the event's occurrence is attached to it in eternity, not as a result of the event. Third response (al-thālith): The word 'knowledge' is metaphorical (majāz); the real intended meaning is that people become distinguished by their inner states, so that believers can know whom to befriend. Fourth response (al-rābiʿ): 'Knowledge' is a metaphor for 'seeing'; in Arabic, 'knowledge' and 'seeing' are used interchangeably, as seen in the verse 'Have you not seen how your Lord did?' Thus, these verses do not imply that Allah acquires new knowledge.

Linguistic and Conditional Arguments for Ru'ya
Ahl al-Sunnah's second and third proofs: linguistic negation and conditional attachment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two additional proofs from the verse of Musa: the linguistic principle of negation and the conditional attachment to a permissible thing.

Core Idea
Negation proves possibility; condition proves possibility.

Ahl al-Sunnah offer two more sophisticated arguments from the same verse. First, they analyse the grammar of negation: when Arabs negate something, they imply it could happen. Second, they note that Allah attached ru'ya to a condition that is intrinsically possible, showing ru'ya itself is possible. This lesson explores these arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the second proof about negation
Understand the linguistic principle: the Arabs only negate what is possible to affirm. Allah's negation 'lan taranī' therefore implies ru'ya is possible.
The example of the mountain not speaking is given to illustrate.
2
Read the third proof about the condition
Allah says 'if the mountain remains, you will see Me.' The condition is possible, so the result (ru'ya) is possible.
Contrast with the Mu'tazila claim that the condition is impossible due to foreknowledge.
3
Reflect on the refutation
How do these two proofs together refute the Mu'tazila reliance on the same verse?
The verse actually supports Ahl al-Sunnah.

Refuting the Mu'tazilite Scriptural Objection to Ru'ya
Sanusi answers the Mu'tazilite scriptural objection from the Quranic verse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi refutes the Mu'tazilite scriptural objection to seeing Allah by showing that the Quranic verse is in the context of praise and that other scriptural requests were due to obstinacy, not impossibility.

Core Idea
The Quranic 'No vision can grasp Him' is praise, not absolute negation.

Sanusi explains that this verse is presented in a context of praise, not as a categorical denial. The heading: 'Obligation of believing in the goodness of his generation and his Companions, then the Followers.' 
75 - And his Companions, his Companions are the best of generations, so listen, then a follower, then a follower of those who followed. 
Then he pointed to another obligatory ruling of belief by his saying 'and his Companions' (peace be upon him), meaning: every individual among the Companions who believed in him and accompanied him even briefly. And what is meant is whoever was a Companion in reality, whether knowledge of his companionship reached us or not. ('Good') of the people of the later generations, meaning: the best of them and the most rewarded, because they gave shelter and supported. As for their superiority over the earlier generations other than the prophets, there is no debate about it; due to His statement: 'Certainly Allah was pleased with the believers' [al-Fath:18] and 'And the foremost, the first' [al-Tawba:100], and due to the hadith: 'Indeed Allah chose my Companions over the worlds except the prophets and messengers.' And it is not hidden that the rank of one who accompanied him (peace be upon him) and fought with him or was killed under his banner is higher than one who did not accompany him or did not attend a battle with him, and than one who spoke with him briefly or walked with him a little or saw him from a distance or in childhood, even though the honor of companionship is attained for all. Commentary
This passage is a classification and proof passage. It begins with a heading: it is obligatory to believe in the superiority of the Prophet's generation (the Companions) and then the Followers. The verse (line 75) states 'his Companions are the best of generations.' The commentary explains that 'his Companions' means every individual Companion regardless of length of companionship. 'Good of the later generations' means they are the best and most rewarded because they gave shelter and support. Their superiority over earlier non-prophet generations is proven by Quranic verses (pleasure of Allah on believers, and the foremost) and a hadith that Allah chose the Companions over all except prophets. The passage then clarifies that there are degrees among Companions: those who accompanied, fought, or were martyred are higher than those with brief or distant interaction, though all share the honor of companionship. The best of the Companions is deferred to a later statement.

The Best of the Companions: The Caliphs
Declares the best Companions are the caliphs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the best of the Companions are those who were entrusted with the caliphate, specifically the Rightly Guided Caliphs.

Core Idea
The best Companions are the caliphs, led by Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali.

This lesson focuses on a concise yet powerful statement from the author about the hierarchy among the Companions. It establishes that the highest rank belongs to those who succeeded the Prophet in leading the community, a key tenet of Sunni belief.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage in Arabic
Read the short Arabic sentence: 'Qism al-nubuwwat. Wa-khayruhum man wulliya al-khilafah.' Notice the section heading.
The phrase 'Qism al-nubuwwat' signals a new section.
2
Understand the meaning
Translate it: 'And the best of them is the one who was entrusted with the caliphate.' Reflect on who 'them' refers to—the Prophets or the Companions? The context makes it clear: it refers to the Companions.
3
Identify the key point
The author asserts that the best Companions are those who became caliphs. This includes Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali, and this order reflects their virtue.
This is a foundational Sunni belief about the superiority of the four caliphs.

Section of prophethoods. And the best of them is the one who was entrusted with the caliphate. And al-qarn (the generation): people of one close time period, who share in some of the intended matters. And it was called a 'qarn' because it connects a nation to a nation and a world to a world. Then it was made a name for the time or its people. So the qarn of the Companions of Allah is the duration of his Companions from the mission to the last of them who died, which is one hundred and twenty years, or it is the Companions themselves. And the qarn of the Followers: from year one hundred to about seventy. And the qarn of the Followers of the Followers: completed to about the two hundred and twenties. And Allah knows best. And His saying: 'So listen' is a completion.

Commentary
This passage is both a doctrinal statement and a lexical definition. It begins by stating that the best of the Companions is the one who was given the caliphate, referring to the Rightly Guided Caliphs. Then it defines the term 'qarn' (generation) as a group of people from a close time period who share common characteristics. The reason it is called 'qarn' is because it connects one nation and world to another. Over time, it became used for either the time period or the people of that time. It then specifies the durations: the generation of the Companions spans from the Prophet's mission until the death of the last Companion, about 120 years. Alternatively, it may refer to the Companions themselves. The generation of the Followers is from around year 100 AH to about 170 AH, and the generation of the Followers of the Followers extends to around 220 AH. The passage ends with a note that the word 'fastami' in the poem is a completion.
