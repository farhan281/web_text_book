The Story of the Three Brothers: The Debate That Changed al-Ash'ari
Sanusi narrates the debate between al-Ash'ari and al-Jubba'i over the three brothers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the parable of the three brothers exposed the fatal flaw in Mu'tazilite theology and led to al-Ash'ari's conversion.

Core Idea
The dilemma of the three brothers shattered Mu'tazilite coherence.

After establishing the rational objection, Sanusi narrates the famous debate between Abu Hashim al-Jubba'i and his student Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari. This story is not just historical; it illustrates the logical failure of the Mu'tazilite position and marks the birth of Ash'ari theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the story carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to understand the scenario of three brothers and their respective fates. Then read the translation.
Note that the story is a concrete illustration of the abstract objection.
2
Understand al-Jubba'i's answer
Al-Jubba'i gives the fate of each brother according to Mu'tazilite principles: the pious believer to Paradise, the sinful to Hell, and the child to an intermediate state.
This reflects their doctrine of al-manzila bayn al-manzilatayn.
3
Follow al-Ash'ari's first question
Al-Ash'ari asks why the child was not kept alive to obey and enter Paradise. Al-Jubba'i answers by citing God's foreknowledge that the child would sin if he lived.
4
See the turning point: the second question
Al-Ash'ari then asks about the disobedient brother: if God knew he would sin, why was he not killed as a child? Al-Jubba'i is silenced.
This is the moment that exposed the inconsistency.
5
Reflect on the significance
This story is not just a clever argument; it caused al-Ash'ari to abandon Mu'tazilism and start his own school.
It shows the power of logical reasoning in theology.

The fifth aspect: that from their doctrine it follows that God Most High did not do what was obligatory upon Him with everyone who lived safely until reaching puberty and then disbelieved or apostatized after Islam, because causing his death in childhood or taking away his intellect would have been more beneficial (aslah) for him, yet He did not do that. Al-Ash'ari was one of al-Jubba'i's students, and the shaykh was teaching this topic one day in his class. Commentary
This passage contains the fifth objection (al-wajh al-khamis) against the Mu'tazilite doctrine of al-aslah, followed by the famous story of the three brothers. The argument proceeds as follows: The Mu'tazila claim that God must do what is most beneficial for His servants. But then why does God let certain people reach maturity only to disbelieve or apostatize? Killing them as children would have been more beneficial. If the Mu'tazila respond that the true benefit is the taklif (religious obligation) that leads to eternal bliss, then God has not done what is most beneficial for those He killed as children or made insane, because they were deprived of that opportunity. If the Mu'tazila counter that God knew they would cause harm if they lived, so He killed them for their benefit, then the objection is: why did God not kill all notorious tyrants and misleaders like Pharaoh, Haman, Mazdak, and Zoroaster as children? This exposes the inconsistency. The author then narrates the well-known debate between Abu Hashim al-Jubba'i, the Mu'tazilite scholar, and his student Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari. Al-Ash'ari posed the case of three brothers: one died obedient as an adult, one died disobedient as an adult, and one died as a child. According to Mu'tazilite principles, the child is in an intermediate state (al-manzila bayn al-manzilatayn) — neither rewarded nor punished. Al-Ash'ari then raised a dilemma: the child could ask why he was not kept alive to obey and enter Paradise. Al-Jubba'i's answer (that God knew he would sin) is then turned back against him when al-Ash'ari asks about the disobedient brother: if God knew he would sin, why not kill him as a child to save him from hell? Al-Jubba'i was unable to answer. This debate is said to be the turning point where al-Ash'ari abandoned Mu'tazilism and began refuting it.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Qadariyya sects and an affirmation of the Ahl al-Sunna position on divine decree. The author begins by introducing a sect that denied God's eternal determination and claimed that His knowledge is renewed at the moment of occurrence. They are called 'Qadariyya' despite denying qadr, because they exaggerated in denial. Then a second sect, the Mu'tazila, affirmed God's eternal knowledge but claimed that the slave creates his own actions. Both are refuted: the first by labeling their belief as disbelief (kufr) and noting its extinction; the second is already refuted earlier in the text. The commentator then clarifies that Ash'aris and Maturidis agree on the meaning of qadr despite differences in expression. Finally, the Ahl al-Sunna position is summarized: it is obligatory to believe that God knew all actions from eternity and created them according to that knowledge. The proof is the hadith of Jibril where belief in qada and qadr is explicitly mentioned as part of iman.

The Shahadatayn Encompass All Obligatory Beliefs
Explains how the shahadatayn contain all obligatory beliefs about God and prophets.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the two testimonies of Islam encapsulate all obligatory beliefs about God and the prophets, and how these beliefs are categorized into necessary, possible, and impossible.

Core Idea
The shahada contains all essential beliefs about God and prophets in three rational categories.

This passage from the commentary unpacks a dense line of poetry to reveal a profound truth about the shahadatayn. It shows that the first testimony affirms God's divinity, and together they cover every obligatory belief regarding God and His messengers, organized into the three rational judgments of necessity, possibility, and impossibility. Let's explore how this works.

The beliefs are divided into wajib (necessary), ja'iz (possible), and mustahil (impossible). These are applied to both God and prophets. For example, it is necessary for God to exist, possible for prophets to have miracles, impossible for God to have a partner.

Divine Decree and the Status of Evil Actions
Sanusi explains that all actions are decreed, but evil is not pleasing to Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni position that all actions are decreed by Allah, but evil deeds are not with His pleasure.

Core Idea
All actions occur by Allah's decree, but He does not approve of evil.

The final segment of this passage addresses the sensitive topic of predestination and moral evil. Sanusi affirms that all actions of creatures are by Allah's decree, power, will, and volition, but then qualifies that evil actions are not with His pleasure, command, or love.

Guided Path
1
Read the affirmation and qualification

2
Understand the comprehensive decree
All actions, good and evil, fall under Allah's decree and will.
3
Understand the distinction
Evil actions are not with Allah's approval or love, even though they occur by His will. This preserves divine justice.
4
Reflect on the importance
This prevents fatalism and avoids attributing evil to Allah directly.
