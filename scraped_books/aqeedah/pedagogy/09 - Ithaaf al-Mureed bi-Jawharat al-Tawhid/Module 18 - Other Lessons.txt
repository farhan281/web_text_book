Commands to Good and Prohibition of Evil Traits
Poet commands good and prohibits specific evil traits.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the specific good and evil traits that the poet commands and prohibits, and the importance of acting upon them.

Core Idea
The poet commands good and lists specific blameworthy traits to avoid.

In these two lines, the poet gives a concise ethical instruction: command what is good and avoid specific evil traits. He lists several blameworthy qualities that believers must guard against. This passage serves as a practical moral guide.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to understand the basic meaning.
Notice that the passage has two parts: a command and a prohibition.
2
Identify the command and prohibition
The passage begins with a command to enjoin good (عرف) and prohibits slander, backbiting, and all blameworthy traits.
3
Recognize the listed blameworthy traits
The poet gives specific examples: self-admiration, pride, envy, argumentation, and disputation. Make sure you can identify each one.
Consider why these traits are harmful.

Defining Gratitude and Its Three Forms
Definition of shukr and its three categories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the terminological definition of gratitude (shukr) and its three categories as established in classical Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Gratitude is any action—heart, tongue, or limbs—that magnifies the Benefactor.

Before discussing specific acts like prayer upon the Prophet, the commentator establishes the broader concept of gratitude (shukr). This definition is foundational because it shows that true thankfulness is not limited to words but encompasses belief, speech, and physical actions. Let's examine this comprehensive definition.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that the heading mentions prayer upon the Prophet, but the body provides a definition of shukr.
The heading is context; the core is the definition.
2
Identify the three categories
The passage lists three types of action that constitute gratitude: belief of the heart, speech of the tongue, and action of the limbs. Find each in the text.
These are the three forms of shukr.
3
Understand the condition of gratitude
The definition requires that the action indicates magnification (ta'dhim) of the Benefactor due to His being a benefactor. This distinguishes true gratitude from mere outward show.
The intention matters.
4
Reflect on the misconception
Some may think this passage defines prayer (salat) upon the Prophet, but it actually defines shukr. Use the commentary to clarify.
This is a common misunderstanding.

This sets the foundation that salat and salam are specific forms of this broader concept of gratitude.

Worldly and Otherworldly Rulings for the Muqallid
Sanusi states the agreed worldly rulings and the otherworldly ruling for the muqallid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the agreed-upon worldly rulings for the muqallid and his ultimate otherworldly fate according to Ahl al-Sunnah.

Core Idea
The muqallid is treated as a Muslim in this world and saved in the next, even if his faith is imperfect.

This lesson explains the two sets of rulings for the imitator (muqallid) in matters of faith. The scholars agree that in this world, he is treated as a Muslim in all legal matters. Regarding the Hereafter, the investigators of Ahl al-Sunnah hold that he will not be eternally punished and will eventually enter Paradise.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Begin by reading the full Arabic passage from the source, then its literal translation. Notice the concise style and the two distinct parts: worldly and otherworldly rulings.
2
Identify the worldly rulings
The passage lists specific worldly rulings that apply to the muqallid by agreement. List them: he may marry, lead prayer, his slaughtered meat is eaten, he inherits and is inherited from, he receives spoils, and is buried in Muslim cemeteries.
These rulings show that he is considered a Muslim in all legal matters of this world.
3
Understand the otherworldly ruling and its proof
According to the investigators of Ahl al-Sunnah, the muqallid will not be eternally punished in the Fire, nor punished for disbelief; he will eventually be saved. Note the proofs from Qur'an (4:94) and hadith (whoever prays our prayer...). Also note that he is still disobedient for abandoning reflection.
The proofs emphasize the outward signs of Islam, not internal certainty.

The statement that the disagreement about the faith of the imitator is verbal. The worldly rulings are agreed upon: he is married, leads prayer, his slaughtered meat is eaten, Muslims inherit from him and he inherits from them, he receives a share [of spoils] and is buried in their cemeteries. As for the otherworldly rulings according to the investigators of Ahl al-Sunnah: he is not eternally in the Fire if he enters it, nor is he punished therein for disbelief, and his ultimate end is salvation and Paradise, due to His saying {And do not say to one who offers you peace, 'You are not a believer'} [Qur'an 4:94] and his saying: 'Whoever prays our prayer, enters our mosque, and faces our qibla is a Muslim.' But he is disobedient by abandoning reflection. And otherwise, meaning if the imitator does not have certainty in his belief from what another informed him of in the aforementioned manner, that belief does not suffice for the validity of his Islam and its rulings being applied to him, because he remains in al-ḍayr, i.e., in the harm of doubt contrary to faith, and has not escaped it. This is not part of the disagreement at all, because they agree on the invalidity of his faith. The disagreement about the faith of the imitator is only with regard to the rulings of the Hereafter and what is with Allah. As for the rulings of this world, sufficient faith therein is merely verbal confession; whoever confesses has Islamic rulings applied to him in this world and is not judged as a disbeliever, unless an act indicating his disbelief is associated with it, such as prostrating to an idol.

Commentary
This passage discusses the rulings for the muqallid (imitator) in faith. It begins by stating that the disagreement about his faith is verbal (lafẓī). The worldly (dunyawī) rulings are unanimously agreed: he is considered a Muslim in all legal matters—marriage, leading prayer, eating his slaughtered meat, inheritance, receiving spoils, and burial in Muslim cemeteries. Regarding the otherworldly (ukhrāwī) rulings, according to the muḥaqqiqūn (investigators) of Ahl al-Sunnah, he will not be eternally punished in the Fire if he enters it, nor will he be punished for disbelief; his ultimate fate is salvation and Paradise. This is supported by Qur'an 4:94, which forbids calling a person who greets you a non-believer, and a hadith stating that one who prays facing the qibla is a Muslim. However, such a person is disobedient for abandoning reflection (naẓar). The passage then clarifies that those who follow Ash'ari or Maturidi schools are not engaging in blameworthy imitation (taqlīd madhmūm) in creedal matters, because they only accept the ruling after understanding its evidence from the original sources, akin to someone who consults an astronomer about the moon's position and then verifies it himself. The passage then distinguishes: if the muqallid does not have certainty (jazm) in the way described, his faith is invalid and he is not considered a Muslim in the afterlife; this is not a matter of disagreement. The disagreement among scholars only pertains to the otherworldly rulings, whereas for worldly rulings, mere verbal confession (iqrār) is sufficient unless accompanied by an act of disbelief like prostrating to an idol.

The Condition of Certainty in the Muqallid's Faith
Sanusi explains the necessity of certainty for the muqallid's otherworldly faith and clarifies the scope of disagreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the imitator's faith requires certainty (jazm) for the Hereafter and how this differs from blameworthy taqlid, and the distinction between worldly and otherworldly rulings.

Core Idea
Certainty based on evidence saves the muqallid from blameworthy imitation, while doubt invalidates his faith in the afterlife.

This lesson delves into the requirement of certainty (jazm) for the faith of the muqallid to be valid in the Hereafter. It contrasts the acceptable following of Ash'ari/Maturidi schools with blameworthy taqlid, and concludes by distinguishing the rulings of this world and the next.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the second part of the source passage carefully in Arabic and English. Look for the analogy of the astronomer and the condition for valid faith.
2
Analyze the astronomer analogy
The passage compares a follower of Ash'ari/Maturidi to someone who asks an astronomer about the moon's position, then verifies it himself. This shows that true faith requires personal verification and certainty, not blind imitation.
Blameworthy taqlid is rejected; acceptable following involves understanding the evidence.
3
Find the condition for valid faith
The passage states that if the muqallid does not have certainty (jazm) in his belief from the information provided, his faith is invalid and he remains in doubt (ḍayr). This is not a matter of disagreement; all agree his faith is invalid.
Certainty is the key for otherworldly acceptance.
4
Distinguish worldly and otherworldly rulings
The passage concludes that the disagreement is only about otherworldly rulings. For worldly rulings, mere verbal confession (iqrār) is sufficient, unless accompanied by an act of disbelief.
This clarifies the scope of the earlier discussion.

The First Obligation: Knowledge
States the first obligation and the existence of disagreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the first obligation upon every accountable person is to acquire certain knowledge, and that scholars have disagreed about the precise nature of this obligation.

Core Idea
Certain knowledge is the first duty of every accountable person.

The text now turns to a foundational question: what is the very first thing Allah requires of a responsible human being? The author does not begin with a practical act like prayer, but with knowledge (ma'rifa). Before any act can be performed, its necessity and correctness must be known with certainty. This lesson presents that claim and signals that there is disagreement among scholars on this very point.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then its English translation. Notice the imperative 'wa-jzim' (be certain). This is a direct command to the student.
The passage is cut off mid-sentence; the discussion likely continues.
2
Understand 'jizm' as firm certainty
The commentary explains 'wa-jzim' as believing with a firm belief that no hesitation affects. Knowledge must be free from doubt.
This is the opposite of blind acceptance or probabilistic opinion.

This passage from Jawharat al-Tawhid succinctly lists them and explains why religion is paramount.

Conditions for Commanding Good and Forbidding Evil
Al-Sanusi enumerates three conditions for commanding good and forbidding evil and states their legal consequences.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Commanding good is not unconditional; it requires knowledge, safety from greater harm, and likely effectiveness.

The duty of enjoining good and forbidding evil is often assumed to be obligatory in all circumstances. However, classical scholars have laid out conditions that limit this obligation. This passage from Ibn Abi Zayd al-Qayrawani's Risala, as commented upon by al-Sanusi, clarifies three essential conditions: knowledge, safety from greater harm, and effectiveness. Understanding these conditions prevents misuse of this important duty.

Guided Path

4
