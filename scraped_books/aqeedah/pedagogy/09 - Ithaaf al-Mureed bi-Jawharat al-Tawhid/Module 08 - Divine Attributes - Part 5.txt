Proofs Against Adding the Attribute of Perception
Commentator refutes perception as an extra attribute with three rational proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three rational arguments used to reject perception as an additional attribute of Allah.

Core Idea
Perception entails physical connection; since that is impossible for Allah, perception cannot be His attribute.

The commentator presents a concise case against considering perception (al-idrāk) as an attribute distinct from knowledge, hearing, and sight. The argument rests on three independent but complementary rational proofs: the entailment of physical connection, the sufficiency of divine knowledge, and the invalidity of the opposite claim. This passage sharpens our understanding of how kalam reasoning applies logical entailment to divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the full passage
Read the Arabic passage once to get the overall flow. Then read the translation to ensure you understand the literal meaning.
The passage uses technical terms like al-lāzim and al-malzūm.
2
Identify the three arguments
The passage contains three distinct proofs against adding perception. Find each one and note how they are connected.
Look for the keywords: 'li-mā anna' (first), 'wa-li-anna' (second), and the refutation beginning with 'wa-daʿwā' (third).
3
Analyze the entailment argument
Focus on the first argument: perception and connection are rationally inseparable. Since connection is impossible for Allah, perception must also be impossible.
This uses the principle that impossibility of the necessary (lāzim) implies impossibility of the necessitating (malzūm).
4
Understand the sufficiency argument
The second argument: Allah's knowledge already encompasses all objects of perception, so no separate attribute is needed, especially without revelatory or evidentiary support.
This is a 'principle of parsimony' argument within kalam.
5
Evaluate the refutation of opposites
The third argument refutes the opponent's claim that without perception Allah would have its opposites. Knowledge itself precludes those opposites.
The opponent's false dichotomy is dismantled by appealing to the attribute of knowledge that Allah necessarily possesses.

Commentary
This passage presents three rational arguments against establishing perception (al-idrāk) as an extra attribute beyond knowledge, hearing, and sight. First, there is a rational entailment (talāzum ʿaqliyy) between perception and physical connection to its objects (e.g. touch, smell). Since connection is impossible for God, the attribute which necessitates it must also be impossible. Second, God's knowledge already encompasses all objects of perception, so no additional attribute is needed—especially since no revelation or divine act indicates one. Third, the opponent's claim that without perception God would have its opposites (like ignorance) is invalid, because God's knowledge already precludes those opposites. The passage concludes that God is necessarily described with knowledge (or with the attributes known from revelation), not with a separate perception attribute.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When rational and scriptural proofs conflict, suspension is a reasoned theological stance.

This passage discusses the attribute of perception (al-idrāk) and why some theologians refrain from affirming or denying it. It traces the disagreement back to a methodological difference in proving attributes. By reading this, you'll grasp the reasoning behind the suspension position.

Presents Mu'tazilite doubt and gives Ash'ari answer.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Attributes are neither other nor identical—avoiding both temporal change and multiple pre-eternals.

The Mu'tazilites posed a powerful dilemma: if God's attributes are real, they must be either temporal or pre-eternal, both leading to unacceptable conclusions. The Ash'ari answer, derived from al-Sanusi's poem, is that the attributes are neither other than the essence nor identical to it. This lesson unpacks the doubt and its resolution.

The example of Christians shows the severity.

The attributes are neither other than the essence nor identical to it. This resolves the dilemma because attributes are not separate pre-eternals nor temporal accidents.

The Mu'tazilite Dilemma on Divine Attributes
Presents the Mu'tazilite dilemma that attributes must be either originated or eternal, both problematic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite argument that if attributes are additional to God's essence, they must be either originated (implying change and pre-eternal imperfection) or eternal (implying multiple eternal beings, which is disbelief).

Core Idea
Attributes cannot be additional without falling into an impossible dilemma.

The Mu'tazilites sought to deny real attributes by presenting a logical dilemma. This lesson examines their two-horned argument, which forced a choice between originated attributes (leading to change in God) and eternal attributes (leading to polytheism).

Guided Path
1
Understand the context
The Mu'tazilites are arguing against the Ash'arite view that attributes are real and additional to God's essence. Their objection is a logical dilemma.
2
Read the objection passage

The text presents two horns: attributes are either originated (haditha) or eternal (qadima).
3
Examine the first horn
If attributes are originated, then God's essence would be a locus for originated things (hawadith), implying change. Also, in pre-eternity, God would lack these attributes.
This contradicts divine immutability and perfection.
4
Examine the second horn
If attributes are eternal, then there would be multiple eternal entities, leading to shirk (associating partners with God). The Mu'tazilites cite the Christian belief in two eternal persons as a precedent.
Consensus of Muslims considers multiplicity of eternals as disbelief.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
The Mu'tazilites conclude that real additional attributes cannot exist. This sets the stage for the Ash'arite response.
The response is covered in the next lesson.

Commentary
This passage defines and classifies divine attributes based on whether they have 'talluq' (relation/connection) to an object beyond themselves. It begins by stating the criterion: an attribute that requires an additional matter (like power requiring an object of power) has talluq; one that does not (like life) does not. (4) Hearing, sight, and perception relate only to existent things (necessary and possible that are actual). The Fourfold Classification of Divine Attributes by Talluq
Classification of ṣifāt al-maʿānī into four sections based on talluq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four categories of the attributes of meanings based on the scope and mode of their talluq.

Core Idea
Attributes of meanings vary in what they relate to and how they relate.

After establishing the criterion, the author now classifies the attributes of meanings into four distinct categories based on whether they have talluq, and if so, to what objects and in what manner. This classification reveals the precise relationships between divine attributes and their objects.

Guided Path
1
Read the classification passage
Read the Arabic passage that describes the four sections, and the English translation.
2
Identify each section
Note the four sections: life (no talluq), knowledge/speech (all rational judgments), power/will (possible only), hearing/sight/perception (existent only).
3
Note the mode of talluq
For each section, note how the talluq is described: disclosure, indication, creation/annihilation, specification.
4
Understand the note on obligation
The passage ends by noting that knowledge of talluqs is not obligatory for all, as it is subtle.

ومعنى تعلّق علمه تعالى بالمستحيل علمه تعالى باستِحَالتِه ، وأنَّه لو تُصُوِّرَ وقوعه لَزِمَهُ مِن الفساد كذا. واعلم أنَّ تعلقات القدرة والإرادة والعلم مترتبة عند أهل الحقِّ ؛ فتعلق القدرة تابع لتعلَّق الإرادة، وتعلُّق الإرادة تابع لتعلق العلم ؛ فلا يُوجِدُ تعالى أو يُعْدِمُ مِن الممكناتِ إِلَّا ما أراد إيجاده أو إعدامه منها ، ولا يُريد منها إِلَّا ما علم أنَّه يكون ؛ فما علم أنَّه يكون من الممكنات أَرَادَه ، وما علم أنه لا يكون لم يُرد كَونَهُ ؛ فعندنا إيمانُ أبي جهل مأمور به غير مراد له تعالى ؛ لِعِلْمه عَدَمَ وقوعِه ، وكُفْرُهُ منهي عنه ، وهو واقع بإرادته تعالى وقدرته ؛ لعلمه وقوعَهُ .
And the meaning of the attachment of His knowledge, exalted is He, to the impossible is His knowledge of its impossibility, and that if its occurrence were imagined, corruption of such-and-such would necessarily follow from it. And know that the attachments of power, will, and knowledge are ordered according to the people of truth: the attachment of power follows the attachment of will, and the attachment of will follows the attachment of knowledge. So He, exalted, does not bring into existence or annihilate from among possibilities except what He wills to bring into existence or annihilate from them, and He does not will from them except what He knows will be. So what He knows will be from among possibilities He wills, and what He knows will not be He does not will its existence. Thus with us the faith of Abu Jahl is commanded but not willed by Him, exalted, because of His knowledge of its non-occurrence, and his disbelief is prohibited, and it occurs by His will and power, because of His knowledge of its occurrence.

Commentary
This passage is a two-part explanation. First, it defines the meaning of God's knowledge of the impossible: it is His knowledge that the impossible cannot occur and that if it were supposed to occur, corruption would follow. Second, it explains the ordering of the three attributes of power, will, and knowledge according to Ahl al-Haqq (the people of truth). The ordering is such that the attachment of power (i.e., what is brought into existence) is dependent on the attachment of will, and the attachment of will is dependent on the attachment of knowledge. Therefore, God only creates or annihilates possibilities that He wills, and He only wills what He knows will happen. This leads to the example of Abu Jahl: his faith is commanded by God (since belief is obligatory) but God does not will it because He knows it will not happen; his disbelief is prohibited but God wills it and brings it about because He knows it will happen. This illustrates the distinction between divine command and divine will, a key point in Sunni theology.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some may think that God cannot know the impossible because it is not real.
Correction: God's knowledge encompasses both what is real and what is impossible; He knows the impossibility and its hypothetical consequences.

Key Takeaway
God's knowledge of the impossible is His knowledge that it cannot happen and that it would cause corruption if it did.
