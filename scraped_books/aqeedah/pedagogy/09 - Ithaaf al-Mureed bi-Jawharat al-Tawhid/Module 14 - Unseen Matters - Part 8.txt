Affirming the Major Portents of the Hour
Author affirms five specific major portents of the Hour as true.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the five major eschatological signs (ashrat al-sa'a) that Sunni Muslims affirm as true, as listed by the author.

Core Idea
The major portents of the Hour are true and must be believed.

In this passage, the author explicitly affirms the truth of several major signs that will precede the Day of Judgment. These include the Dajjal, Gog and Magog, the descent of Jesus, the sun rising from the west, and the beast of the earth. Let's read the text and see how these are presented.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic text once, then read the English translation. Notice the concise style — each sign is simply listed without elaboration.
The word 'حق' (true) at the beginning and end emphasizes their certainty.
2
Identify each sign mentioned
List the five signs as they appear: the Dajjal, Gog and Magog, the descent of Jesus, the sun rising from the west, and the beast of the earth.
3
Understand the theological import
Recognize that affirming these signs as true is a requirement of Sunni creed. The passage does not explain them further; belief itself is sufficient.
These are major signs; the text does not mention minor signs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that evil actions are not attributed to God's pleasure, command, or love.

Core Idea
Evil is not from God's will in the sense of approval.

Sanusi begins with a crucial clarification: while God creates all actions, evil acts are not by His pleasure or command. This upholds divine transcendence and justice. Let's examine this foundational creedal point.

2
Understand the triple negation
The passage denies three attributes: pleasure, command, and love. Ask why each is denied for evil acts.
This prevents attributing evil to God's will in a moral sense.

Connect to divine transcendence
Reflect on how this protects God from being associated with evil. This is a key Sunni position.

In this lesson, we examine the commentator's explanation of the poet's heartfelt supplication. The poet does not merely hope for any answer, but for a decisive proof—one that is sound, accepted, and religiously valid. The commentator clarifies the nuance of the request and its comprehensive scope.
