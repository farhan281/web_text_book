The Many Miracles of the Prophet, Especially the Quran
One verse listing the Prophet's miracles, focusing on the Quran.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the Prophet Muhammad had numerous miracles, with the Quran being the greatest intellectual miracle that challenges humanity.

Core Idea
The Quran is the ultimate miracle of the Prophet, a divine speech that defies human imitation.

This concise verse from the poem lists the Prophet's miracles, emphasizing that they are many and brilliant. Among them, the Quran stands out as the speech of Allah, a miracle that challenges all of humanity to produce its like. This lesson unpacks the significance of this statement and what it means for our understanding of prophethood.

Guided Path
1
Read the verse carefully
Read the Arabic verse once. Notice its concise structure: it first mentions the Prophet's miracles in general, then singles out one specific miracle.
The verse uses the word 'ghurar' which means brilliant or outstanding.
2
Identify the two parts of the verse
The verse has two parts: a general statement about many miracles, and a specific mention of the Quran as a miracle for all of humanity. Understand the relationship between the two.
The phrase 'minhā' (among them) shows that the Quran is one example of many miracles.
3
Reflect on the nature of the Quran as a miracle
The Quran is described as 'mu'jiz al-bashar' — a miracle for mankind. Think about why this is significant: it is an intellectual miracle, not a physical one, and it remains a challenge for all times.
The Quran's inimitability (i'jāz) is its miraculous nature.

The verse emphasizes the Qur'an's inimitability as the central proof of prophethood.

Scope of Miracles: The Condition of 'Matter' (أمر)
Sanusi defines the miracle and elaborates the first condition: that it be a 'matter'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first condition of a miracle: that it must be a 'matter' (أمر) encompassing words, actions, and other phenomena, and why this excludes ordinary events.

Core Idea
A miracle must be a 'matter'—words, acts, or beyond natural laws.

The definition of a miracle includes seven conditions. The first condition is that it be a 'matter' (أمر), which includes all types of miraculous phenomena: speech (like the Quran), actions (like Moses' staff), and other extraordinary events (like the fire not burning Abraham). This condition ensures that miracles are not limited to one category.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition

2
Understand 'أمر'
See how 'أمر' includes words, actions, and other supernatural events. Reflect on examples.
3
Distinguish from ordinary
Realize that 'أمر' alone does not make something a miracle; it is just one condition.
The next lessons will add further conditions.

Commentary

'Ma' adam al-mu'aradah' (without opposition) excludes magic and sleight of hand, which can be opposed or imitated. The passage collectively establishes that a true miracle is a divine act that cannot be imitated and is a sign of prophethood.

The Extraordinary Nature of Miracles: Condition of Kharq al-'Adah
Explains the condition that the miracle must be extraordinary (kharq al-'adah).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second condition of a miracle: that it must be extraordinary (kharq al-'adah), meaning it violates the normal course of nature, and how this distinguishes miracles from ordinary events.

Core Idea
A miracle must break the norm—it cannot be a usual occurrence.

The second condition is that the miracle must be 'kharq al-'adah' (extraordinary). This means it violates the habitual pattern of nature. The passage explains that 'breaking the norm' means going against the usual ruling (hukm) of nature. This condition excludes anything that happens normally or within the bounds of natural laws.

Appreciate the exclusivity

Only events that truly break the norm qualify.

The Challenge Condition: Miracles as Proof of Prophethood
Explains the condition that a miracle must be accompanied by a challenge, excluding karamat and false claims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third condition: that a miracle must be accompanied by a challenge (tahaddi), which distinguishes it from saintly miracles (karamat) and prevents false claims.

Core Idea
A miracle is tied to a prophetic claim—it challenges others to produce its like.

The third condition is that the miracle must be 'accompanied by a challenge' (muqrun bi al-tahaddi). This means the prophet or claimant explicitly challenges others to produce something similar. This condition excludes karamat (marvels of saints) and irhasat (preparatory signs), as those are not tied to a prophetic claim. It also prevents a false prophet from using a past miracle as his own proof.

The text explicitly mentions karamat al-awliya'.
3
Prevent false claims
See how this condition blocks liars from using past miracles as evidence for their own prophethood.
The text gives an example of a righteous person from the past.

Inimitable Miracles: Condition of Non-Opposition
Explains the condition that a miracle must be without opposition, excluding magic and trickery.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the final condition: that a miracle must be without opposition (adam al-mu'aradah), meaning it cannot be imitated or countered, which excludes magic and sleight of hand.

Core Idea
A true miracle cannot be opposed or imitated—unlike magic and tricks.

The seventh condition is that the miracle must occur 'without opposition' (ma' adam al-mu'aradah). This means that no one can produce the like or counteract it. This final condition excludes magic and sleight of hand (sihr and shu'badhah), because those can be learned and opposed by others. A true miracle is divinely protected from imitation.

This lesson introduces one of the key topics in Ilm al-Kalam: the nature of miraculous events. The author provides a rigorous definition of karama that separates it from prophetic miracles, preparatory signs, and deceptive wonders. By carefully analysing each condition, we will see how theologians safeguard the concept of sainthood from confusion.

The definition of karama and its limits in the breaking of customs. And karama: a matter that breaks the custom, not accompanied by a claim to prophethood, nor is it a precursor to it, appearing at the hand of a slave who is outwardly righteous, committed to following a prophet whose law he is charged with, accompanied by correct belief and righteous action, whether he knows of it or not. Commentary
This passage defines karama and distinguishes it from other types of supernatural events. The definition is given: karama is a miracle (something that breaks the usual natural law) that is not accompanied by a claim to prophethood (thereby excluding the prophetic miracle mu'jiza), nor is it a precursor to prophethood (excluding irhas, a miracle that precedes prophethood). It appears at the hand of a righteous slave who is committed to following a prophet. It must be accompanied by correct belief and righteous action. Then the commentator systematically explains each part of the definition: by saying 'a matter that breaks custom' (khariq lil-'ada), it includes all similar events; by 'not accompanied by a claim to prophethood', it excludes the prophetic miracle (mu'jiza); by 'not a precursor', it excludes irhas; by 'appearing at the hand of a righteous slave', it excludes what is called ma'una (assistance) that may appear at the hands of ordinary people (not necessarily saints); by 'committed to following a prophet', it excludes ihana (disgrace), which are miracles that happen to liars like Musaylima to confirm their lie; and by 'accompanied by correct belief and action', it excludes istidraj (gradual enticement), which are miracles that happen to sinners as a test. Thus the definition precisely identifies the concept of karama as distinct from other types of extraordinary events.

Proof of the Possibility and Occurrence of Karamat
Definition and two proofs for karamat: rational and scriptural.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational and scriptural proofs used by Sunni scholars to establish the possibility and actual occurrence of karamat (miracles of saints).

Core Idea
Karamat are rationally possible and historically attested through Quranic and post-Quranic examples.

Sanusi now addresses a common theological question: Can saints perform miracles? He begins by defining karamah as a breaking of customary laws, then provides a twofold proof. First, a rational argument that such events are possible because they entail no logical contradiction. Second, a scriptural argument citing Quranic stories and later examples. This lesson walks through both proofs, showing how they defend the reality of karamat against skeptics.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition
Read the opening clause: karamah is defined as a breaking of customs (kharq al-'adat). This sets the scope of the discussion.
Karamat are not supernatural in the sense of violating nature, but extraordinary events that God brings about.
2
Follow the rational proof
Identify the logical steps: (1) The extraordinary act is possible in itself. (2) Whatever is possible can be brought into existence by divine power. (3) No impossibility follows from supposing its occurrence. Therefore, it is permissible.
3
Examine the scriptural proof
List the Quranic examples: Mary, Companions of the Cave, Asif. Then note the appeal to later karamat of the Companions and Followers. This establishes actual occurrence.
The scriptural proof is cumulative; each example strengthens the case.
4
Observe the closing distinction
The final sentence distinguishes sainthood (wilayah) from prophethood: sainthood is not acquired, implying that karamat are divine gifts, not earned.
This answers a possible objection: if one could earn sainthood, karamat might be seen as earned, but they are purely from God.

The passage concludes by noting that sainthood is not acquired through effort like prophethood, implying it is a divine gift.

The Distinction Between Karamat and Prophetic Miracles
Refutes confusion between karamat and prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why saintly miracles (karamat) do not cause confusion with prophethood, as they are distinguished by the presence of a prophetic claim and challenge (tahaddi).

Core Idea
Karamat do not confuse because prophets alone claim prophethood and issue a challenge.

Some theologians argued that if saints can perform extraordinary acts, people would mistake them for prophets. The author refutes this by highlighting the unique feature of prophetic miracles: the prophet claims prophethood and issues a challenge. This lesson unpacks that refutation and clarifies the essential difference between mu'jizah and karamah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opposing view
Read the passage and note who denied karamat and their reasoning: they feared confusion between prophets and saints.
The opponents include al-Ustadh, al-Halimi, and most Mu'tazila.
2
Understand the author's command
The author says 'cast away his speech'—a strong rejection of the objection.
3
Grasp the key distinction
Focus on the difference: mu'jizah involves a claim of prophethood and a challenge (tahaddi), while karamah does not.
This is the core of the refutation.

Commentary
This passage refutes the objection that karamat would cause confusion between prophets and saints. The author identifies the deniers: al-Ustadh (Abu Ishaq al-Isfarayini) and Abu 'Abd Allah al-Halimi among the Ahl al-Sunnah, and most Mu'tazila. The author responds by commanding to reject this view. Therefore, the presence of a claim and challenge identifies the true prophet, and karamat remain distinct.
