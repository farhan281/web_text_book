Author explains his purpose and a student's criticism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Laqqani wrote this commentary and how a student's criticism prompted him to expand his earlier summary.

Core Idea
A critic's words can motivate deeper scholarship.

This lesson introduces the author al-Laqqani and the story behind the writing of Ithaaf al-Mureed. A student's criticism of a shorter work led him to compose a fuller commentary, showing how even criticism can drive beneficial knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the author's self-identification
Notice the author's humble description of himself as 'the humble, lowly, and mortal servant' and his invocation for forgiveness. This sets a tone of humility and dependence on Allah.
The author, 'Abd al-Salam al-Laqqani, is a Maliki scholar.
2
Understand the earlier summary work
The author explains that he had previously summarized his father's commentary ('Umdat al-Murid') on the creedal poem 'Jawharat al-Tawhid' into a short work called 'Irshad al-Murid'. This contained the chosen Sunni doctrines without addition.
The father, Ibrahim al-Laqqani, wrote the original poem and commentary.
3
Identify the student's criticism
A student from Takrur (a region in North Africa) obtained the summary and expressed something indicating weak resolve and distant aspiration. The author quotes a poetic line: 'Be a man whose foot is in the earth and the peak of his resolve is in the Pleiades.'
The criticism likely was that the summary was too brief or lacked depth.
4
Recognize the author's response
The author hastened to address the student's concern by writing a fuller commentary, citing the hadith: 'The one who guides to good is like the doer of it.' This explains his motivation for composing Ithaaf al-Mureed.
The hadith wisdom: guiding others to knowledge is rewarded like performing the good deed.
5
Reflect on the lesson
Consider how this passage shows the author's humility, responsiveness to criticism, and commitment to spreading sound creed. His intention is pedagogical and sincere.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of the author's introduction. It begins with the title phrase 'With the jewels of the unique pearls' (which likely refers to the book's title or a dedicatory phrase, with the footnote explaining 'jewels' as necklaces and 'unique pearls' as precious independent gems). Then the author identifies himself humbly as 'Abd al-Salam ibn Ibrahim al-Maliki al-Laqqani, invoking Allah's forgiveness. He states his prior work: he had summarized his father's commentary (the 'Umdat al-Murid') on his father's creedal poem 'Jawharat al-Tawhid' into a short work called 'Irshad al-Murid', which contained the sound Sunni creed without excess. After publishing it, a student from the Takrur region (a place in North Africa) criticized it, showing weak resolve and distant aspiration. The author wishes the student had considered the poetic advice: 'Be a man whose foot is on the ground but whose ambition reaches the Pleiades' (i.e., have lofty aims despite humble beginnings). Consequently, the author hastened to address the student's concern by redirecting his attention, citing the hadith that 'the one who guides to good is like the doer of it.' The author thus explains his motivation for writing the present commentary: to provide a more thorough explanation to help the critic and others.

He is Imam Zayn al-Din Abu Yahya Zakariyya ibn Muhammad ibn Zakariyya al-Ansari al-Khazraji al-Misri by birth, the Shafi'i Ash'ari, Shaykh of the Scholars of Islam, Judge of Judges. He was born in the year 824 AH in a village called 'Sanika' in Egypt, and grew up in his village as an orphan in poverty. His righteous mother took care of him and entrusted him to a righteous shaykh who sponsored him, so he memorized the Qur'an and 'Umdat al-Ahkam. Then he traveled to Cairo and joined al-Azhar in the year 841 AH, enduring poverty and deprivation. He said, as in Tabaqat al-Sha'rani: 'I came from the countryside as a youth, and I did not occupy myself with any worldly matter, nor did I attach my heart to anyone among creation. I often went hungry in the mosque, so at night I would go to the ablution place and elsewhere, and wash and eat the watermelon rinds I found, content with that. I remained thus for years. Then Allah appointed for me a person from among the saints of Allah, who worked in the mills sifting wheat, and he would check on me and buy for me what I needed of food, drink, clothing, and books.' Among his teachers: al-Hafiz Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani, 'Alam al-Din al-Bulqini, al-Kafiji, and many others. It is reported that they numbered one hundred and fifty shaykhs. Among his students: al-Sha'rani, who accompanied him for twenty years, Shihab al-Din 'Umaira al-Barlisi, Imam Shams al-Din al-Ramli and his father al-Shihab al-Ramli, Imam al-Shihab Ahmad ibn Hajar al-Haytami, al-Allama al-Khatib al-Shirbini, and countless others. Among his works: Ihkam al-Dilala 'ala Tahrir Sharh al-Risala, a commentary on al-Risala al-Qushayriyya in Sufism; Tuhfat al-Bari bi-Sharh Sahih al-Bukhari; al-Hudud al-Aniqa wa al-Ta'rifat al-Daqiqa; a gloss on the commentary of al-Mahalli on Jam' al-Jawami'; al-Muttala' Sharh Isaghuji in logic; Fath al-Ilah al-Majid bi-Idah Sharh al-'Aqa'id; and among his great juridical works: Asna al-Matalib fi Sharh Rawd al-Talib. His works nearly reached one hundred. His death: he lived to the age of 102 years; Shaykh al-Islam Zakariyya died in the year 926 AH, may Allah have mercy on him and elevate his rank. He was buried in Cairo near the grave of the Imam of the world, Imam al-Shafi'i.

(2) He is the verifying imam Jalāl al-Dīn Abū ʿAbd Allāh Muḥammad ibn Aḥmad ibn Muḥammad al-Anṣārī, of al-Maḥallah origin, al-Qāhirī, al-Shāfiʿī al-Ashʿarī. He was a profoundly learned jurist, logician, theologian, legal theorist, and exegete. The reason for his being named al-Maḥallī is his attribution to al-Maḥallah al-Kubrā in the Gharbiyyah district of Cairo. He was born in Cairo in the year 791 AH, and grew up in a house of knowledge and righteousness; his father Abū al-ʿAbbās Aḥmad acquired learning from Imam al-Bulqīnī.
Among his teachers: al-Ḥāfiẓ Walī al-Dīn al-ʿIrāqī, Shams al-Dīn al-Barmāwī, al-Ḥāfiẓ Ibn Ḥajar; he benefited greatly in the rational sciences from his study under the distinguished scholar ʿAlāʾ al-Dīn al-Bukhārī, and many others. His students: al-Ḥāfiẓ al-Sakhāwī, al-Ḥāfiẓ al-Suyūṭī, al-Ḥāfiẓ Ibn Qaṭlūbughā al-Ḥanafī, Ibn Abī Sharīf al-Maqdisī, al-Sharīf al-Samhūdī, and many others. His qualities: He was a scholar, a verifier, a contemplative, extremely intelligent, a sign of understanding, sound of mind, to the extent that some of his contemporaries said of him: 'His mind pierces diamonds.' Imam al-Maḥallī said of himself: 'My understanding does not accept error.' He was severe in the truth, showing no favoritism, and feared not the blame of any blamer for the sake of God. He earned his living through the wheat trade, and was chaste in himself. Ibn al-ʿImād described him as 'the Taftāzānī of the Arabs.' His works: He authored books that are refined, extremely concise and thoroughly revised, towards which journeys are undertaken; the imams turned to them and accepted them. Among them: 'al-Badr al-Ṭāliʿ fī Ḥall Jamʿ al-Jawāmiʿ'; 'al-Anwār al-Muḍiyyah fī Madḥ Khayr al-Bariyyah', which is a commentary on al-Burdah; 'Kanz al-Rāghibīn Sharḥ Minhāj al-Ṭālibīn'; among his logical works: a commentary on al-Shamsiyyah which he did not complete; among his famous works is the Qur'anic exegesis known as 'Tafsīr al-Jalālayn', which he began from the beginning of Sūrat al-Kahf to the end of the Qur'an, then he died and it was completed by his student Imam al-Suyūṭī. His death: He passed away at the beginning of the year 864 AH. May God Most High have mercy on him and elevate his station.

Biography of Abu Muhammad al-Juwayni
A biographical sketch of Abu Muhammad al-Juwayni

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the life, education, and contributions of the Shafi'i scholar Abu Muhammad al-Juwayni, father of Imam al-Haramayn.

Core Idea
A scholar's journey from Juwayn to mastery of the Shafi'i school.

This lesson introduces you to Abu Muhammad al-Juwayni, a towering figure in Shafi'i jurisprudence. His biography illustrates the traditional path of seeking knowledge: studying under multiple teachers, traveling to centers of learning, and producing works that shaped the madhhab. The passage details his birth, teachers, students, works, and character.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the translation. Familiarize yourself with the names and flow of the biography.
Notice the structure: birth, teachers, students, works, praise, death.
2
Identify key stages in his education
Trace his educational journey: first in Juwayn, then Nishapur, then Merv. Note the names and specialties of his teachers.
He studied under Abu Bakr al-Qaffal al-Marwazi, a major Shafi'i authority.
3
Note his students and works
List his prominent students, especially his son Imam al-Haramayn. Recognize his authored works like 'Al-Silsila' and 'Al-Tabsira'.
His students became influential scholars themselves.
4
Reflect on his character
Read the descriptions from al-Subki and al-Dhahabi. Consider his dedication to seeking knowledge and his piety.
His qunut supplication shows his zeal for learning.

عصره، ولد في جُويْن من نواحي نيسابور ، ونشأ في بيت علم وأدب ، لم تذكر كتب التراجم سنة ولادته ، لكن يغلب على الظن أنه ولد ما بين (۳۸۰ - ۳۹۰ هـ) تقريبا، والله أعلم. من شيوخه: درس على أبي يعقوب الأبيوردي في جوين ، ثم سافر إلى نيسابور وتفقه على أبي الطيب الصعلوكي ، ثم ارتحل إلى مرو قاصدا شيخ الخراسانيين العلامة الكبير أبو بكر القفال المروزي ؛ فلازمه حتى تخرج به مذهبا وخلافا وطريقة ، فأتقن المذهب وطريقة شيخه . من تلامذته: ابنه أبو المعالي إمام الحرمين عبد الملك ، وأبو بكر محمد الصفار مفتي نيسابور ، وأبو نصر الطوسي، وغيرهم الكثير . من مؤلفاته: «السلسلة» في معرفة القولين والوجهين وله التبصرة في ترتيب أبواب للتميز بين الاحتياط والوسوسة» وله كتاب التعليقة وغيرها من المؤلفات. وقال السبكي عنه: له المعرفة التامة بالفقه والأصول، والنحو والتفسير ومن صفاته : أنه كان حريصا على طلب العلم ، قال إمام الحرمين: كان والدي يقول في دعاء القنوت: اللهم لا تعقنا عن العلم بعائق ، ولا تمنعنا عنه بمانع . قال عنه الذهبي : أبو محمد شيخ الشافعية ، كان فقيها مدققا محققا نحويا مفسرا ، كان مجتهدا في العبادة مهيبا بين التلامذة، صاحب جد ووقار، وهو صاحب وجه في المذهب. وفاته توفي له سنة ٤٣٨ هـ ، وقيل : سنة ٤٣٤ هـ .
Of his era, he was born in Juwayn in the districts of Nishapur, and grew up in a house of knowledge and literature. The biographical books do not mention his birth year, but it is most likely that he was born between approximately 380-390 AH, and Allah knows best. Among his teachers: He studied under Abu Ya'qub al-Abiwardi in Juwayn, then traveled to Nishapur and studied fiqh under Abu al-Tayyib al-Sa'luqi, then journeyed to Merv intending to study under the shaykh of the Khorasanis, the great scholar Abu Bakr al-Qaffal al-Marwazi; he stayed with him until he graduated in madhhab, differing opinions, and methodology, mastering the madhhab and the method of his shaykh. Among his students: His son Abu al-Ma'ali Imam al-Haramayn 'Abd al-Malik, Abu Bakr Muhammad al-Saffar the mufti of Nishapur, Abu Nasr al-Tusi, and many others. Among his works: 'Al-Silsila' on knowledge of the two opinions and two aspects, and he has 'Al-Tabsira' on ordering chapters for distinguishing between caution and obsession, and he has the book 'Al-Ta'liqa' and other works. Al-Subki said about him: He has complete knowledge of fiqh, usul, grammar, and tafsir. Among his qualities: He was diligent in seeking knowledge. Imam al-Haramayn said: My father used to say in the Qunut supplication: 'O Allah, do not hinder us from knowledge by an obstacle, nor prevent us from it by a barrier.' Al-Dhahabi said about him: Abu Muhammad, shaykh of the Shafi'is, was a precise, thorough jurist, grammarian, exegete, diligent in worship, awe-inspiring among students, dignified and serious, and he is a recognized authority in the madhhab. His death: He died in the year 438 AH, and it is said 434 AH.

Commentary
This passage is a biography of the Shafi'i scholar Abu Muhammad al-Juwayni, the father of Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni. It begins by mentioning his birthplace in Juwayn, near Nishapur, and his upbringing in a scholarly home. His birth year is estimated between 380-390 AH. The text then lists his teachers, showing his educational journey: first under Abu Ya'qub al-Abiwardi in Juwayn, then Abu al-Tayyib al-Sa'luqi in Nishapur, and finally the prominent Khorasani scholar Abu Bakr al-Qaffal al-Marwazi in Merv, whom he studied with until he mastered the Shafi'i madhhab and its methodology. His students include his own son, the famous Imam al-Haramayn, as well as other notable figures like Abu Bakr al-Saffar and Abu Nasr al-Tusi. His authored works are mentioned, including 'Al-Silsila', 'Al-Tabsira', and 'Al-Ta'liqa'. The passage then quotes al-Subki praising his comprehensive knowledge of fiqh, usul, grammar, and tafsir. It describes his personal qualities: his zeal for learning, as illustrated by a prayer his son narrated, and his meticulousness and piety. Al-Dhahabi's description sums up his stature: he was a precise jurist, grammarian, exegete, and a dignified teacher. Finally, his death date is given, with the primary year 438 AH and an alternate 434 AH.

The Life and Works of Imam al-Jujri
Biographical note on al-Jujri

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the life, teachers, and scholarly contributions of Imam Muhammad ibn Abd al-Mun'im al-Jujri.

Core Idea
Al-Jujri: a scholar who learned from the greats and taught while his teachers were still alive.

This lesson introduces the commentator of our text, al-Jujri. We learn about his name, birth, education, teachers, and his own works. Understanding his background gives context to his commentary, which is part of the source text we study.

Guided Path
1
Read the biography carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then read the translation. Notice the details about his name, birth, and early education.
2
Identify his teachers
The passage lists several notable teachers. Note their names and think about their significance in Islamic scholarship.
Teachers include Ibn Hajar, al-Sakhawi, al-Mahalli, and al-Munawi.
3
Note his principal works
The passage mentions three commentaries. Identify each one and note their full titles.
Works include Tashil al-Masalik, a commentary on al-Irshad, and a commentary on Shudhur al-Dhahab.
4
Reflect on his teaching status
Al-Jujri taught during his teachers' lifetimes, even receiving their students. This shows his high scholarly standing.

His saying: (al-Jujri) with two jeems correctly, and in one copy instead of the second a ha'. Al-Amir. He is Muhammad ibn Abd al-Mun'im ibn Muhammad al-Jujri al-Qahiri al-Shafi'i, born in the year (821 AH) in Jujar, then he traveled to Cairo and memorized the Qur'an, and al-Minhaj al-Far'i and al-Asli, and Alfiyyat Ibn Malik, and among his teachers: al-Hafiz Ibn Hajar, and al-Shihab al-Sakhawi, and al-Jalal al-Mahalli, and al-Munawi and others. He died in the year: (889 AH).

Commentary
This passage is a biographical note identifying the commentator al-Jujri. It begins by clarifying the correct spelling of his name: with two jeems, though some copies replace the second jeem with a ha. Then it gives his full name: Muhammad ibn Abd al-Mun'im ibn Muhammad al-Jujri al-Qahiri al-Shafi'i. It mentions his birth year (821 AH) and his birthplace (Jujar). It describes his education: he moved to Cairo, memorized the Qur'an, studied the Minhaj al-Far'i and al-Asli, and Alfiyyat Ibn Malik. It lists his notable teachers, including Ibn Hajar, al-Sakhawi, al-Jalal al-Mahalli, and al-Munawi. It notes his expertise in recitation and issuing fatwas, to the extent that his teachers sent students to him. It lists his major works: a commentary on 'Umdat al-Salik (Tashil al-Masalik), a commentary on al-Irshad, and a commentary on Shudhur al-Dhahab. It concludes with his death year (889 AH).
