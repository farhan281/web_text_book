The Ruling of Apostasy: Execution and Its Scope
Al-Laqqani clarifies the ruling of apostasy and extends it to deniers of ijmāʿ and permitters of the forbidden.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the ruling of apostasy (execution if unrepentant), why it is not a hadd penalty, and how it applies also to those who deny ijmāʿ or permit the forbidden.

Core Idea
The punishment for apostasy is execution for kufr itself—not a fixed hadd penalty—and extends to denying consensus or permitting the unlawful.

After defining apostasy, al-Laqqani clarifies the nature of its punishment: the execution is not a hadd penalty (a fixed expiatory punishment) but rather a punishment for the crime of kufr itself. He then extends the same ruling to two additional cases: one who denies something established by consensus (ijmāʿ) and one who permits something clearly prohibited like zina. This lesson examines these three aspects of the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Read the continuation of the first couplet
The line 'ليس حَد' is explained: the execution is not a hadd penalty. Understand the distinction.
2
Read the second couplet and its commentary
The second couplet extends the ruling to those who deny ijmāʿ or permit the forbidden like zina. The commentary says they are treated the same.
3
Synthesize the scope
Notice that the ruling covers three types: denier of what is necessarily known, denier of ijmāʿ, and one who permits the haram.
All are executed if they do not repent.
4
Reflect on the purpose
Think about why the author distinguishes from hadd punishments. This shows the severity of apostasy as a foundational violation.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and ruling for apostasy (ridda). It begins with a couplet from the poem: 'And whoever denies something necessarily known from our religion is killed as a disbeliever, not as a hadd penalty.' The commentary then explains that any legally responsible person (mukallaf) who denies a matter that is known by necessity to be from Islam – such as the obligation of prayer and fasting, or the prohibition of zina and wine – commits kufr by that denial. The punishment is execution if he does not repent, because his denial implies rejection of the Prophet's report. Then the passage extends the same ruling to two additional cases: anyone who denies something established by ijmāʿ (consensus), or who permits something clearly forbidden like zina, receives the same ruling as the denier of what is necessarily known. The concluding line reiterates that such a person is treated as a disbeliever and executed.
