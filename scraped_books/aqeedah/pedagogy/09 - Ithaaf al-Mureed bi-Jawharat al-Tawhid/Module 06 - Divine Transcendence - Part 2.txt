Refuting the Mu'tazilites with the Example of Children's Pain
Verse and commentary refuting Mu'tazilite obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the example of children's pain refutes the Mu'tazilite doctrine of obligation (al-aṣlaḥ).

Core Idea
Children's pointless suffering proves God is not obligated to do what is best.

The Mu'tazilites claimed that God must always do what is most beneficial for His creatures. This passage challenges that claim with a simple observation: God inflicts pain on children and animals who derive no benefit from it. The poet uses this as a decisive refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice the rhetorical question format.
The passage is one line of poetry followed by a commentary.
2
Understand the Mu'tazilite claim
Recall that the Mu'tazilites believed God is obligated to do what is most beneficial (al-aṣlaḥ) for humans. This passage targets that belief.
3
Grasp the logical force of the example
Ask yourself: If God must always do what is best, why does He cause pain to beings who gain nothing from it? The example shows that the Mu'tazilite claim is false.
The phrase 'so beware of punishment' warns against persisting in this error.

Commentary
This passage presents a concrete example refuting the Mu'tazilite doctrine of obligation (al-aṣlaḥ). The poem line asks rhetorically: 'Did they not see His causing pain to children and the like? So beware of punishment!' The commentary explains that this is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite claim. It specifies that the 'they' refers to the Mu'tazilites themselves. The 'children' are those who have not reached puberty, and 'the like' includes animals and the weak (al-ʿajazah). The point is that God inflicts painful ailments on beings who derive no benefit from that suffering. If God were obligated to do what is most beneficial, He would not do such things. Therefore, the Mu'tazilite position is false. The poem ends with a warning: 'So beware of punishment' meaning that the Mu'tazilites should fear God's punishment for their erroneous belief.

The Three Aspects of Discussing the Vision of God
Author outlines three aspects of ru'yah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three aspects under which the vision of God is discussed: rational possibility, scriptural proof, and whether it occurs in this world or the Hereafter.

Core Idea
The vision of God is rationally possible and proven by revelation.

The author introduces the issue of seeing God in the Hereafter, stating that it is rationally possible and established by revelation. He then outlines three key aspects that need to be examined: reason, scripture, and timing. This lesson presents that outline and the foundational poetic line.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the poetic line and the three questions that follow.
The poetic line summarizes the Sunni position: seen with eyes, without how or limitation.
2
Identify the three aspects
Find the three questions the author lists. They form the structure of the entire discussion on ru'yah.
3
Understand the role of reason
Note that the first aspect is about rational possibility – whether the intellect can accept that God is seen. This sets up the debate with the Mu'tazila.
Reason alone does not deem it impossible or necessary; it is possible.
4
Connect to the poetic line
The line 'but without how and without limitation' indicates that the vision is not like physical sight – it is beyond human comprehension.

The Mu'tazilite Objection to the Vision of God
Author presents Mu'tazilite rational objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite argument that the vision of God is rationally impossible and the Sunni position that it is possible.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila deny vision because it requires direction, but Ahl al-Sunnah affirm it without conditions.

After outlining the three aspects, the author addresses the first: rational possibility. He presents the Mu'tazilite claim that reason judges vision of God impossible, and explains their doubt based on physical vision requiring direction and facing. This lesson unpacks that argument and the Sunni consensus against it.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation focusing on the Mu'tazilite position and their proof.
2
Identify the two opposing camps
The Mu'tazila say reason deems vision impossible; Ahl al-Sunnah say it is possible. Find these statements.
3
Analyze the Mu'tazilite proof
They argue: vision requires the seen object to be in front and the observer to turn the pupil; but Allah is not a body and has no direction; therefore vision is impossible.
This is a syllogism: major premise (vision needs direction), minor premise (Allah has no direction), conclusion (no vision).
4
Understand the Sunni response
The text states that Ahl al-Sunnah unanimously agree that vision is rationally possible. The implication is that the Mu'tazilite analogy is invalid because Allah's vision is not like physical sight.

Commentary
This passage presents Ahl al-Sunnah's rational response to the Mu'tazila's claim that seeing Allah (ru'ya) requires the seen object to be in a particular direction, facing the seer, etc. The author first states that Ahl al-Sunnah do not accept this premise. Instead, they define ru'ya as a power (quwwa) that Allah places in His servant at His will, without necessitating any physical conditions like facing, direction, or space. They affirm that Allah is not a body and has no direction; therefore, concepts like confronting or turning the pupil are impossible for Him. Yet, ru'ya can still occur as an uncovering (inkishaf) similar to seeing the full moon, based on authentic hadiths. Then the author mentions al-Zamakhshari's harsh poetic attack on Ahl al-Sunnah, accusing them of following whims and likening Allah to creation, while labeling themselves as sunna. The author then presents a rebuttal poem from al-Sayyid al-Bulaydi, who defends Ahl al-Sunnah by asserting they rely on verses, not nonsense, and that the denial of ru'ya leads to deprivation. The response emphasizes that the vision of Allah in the Hereafter is without modality (bila kayf) and without depiction (ghayr irtisam). This passage is a polemical refutation defending the Ahl al-Sunnah position on the nature of ru'ya against Mu'tazila and Zamakhshari's criticism.

Commentary
This passage refutes the Mu'tazila argument that seeing Allah is impossible, using the story of Musa (Moses) and the verse 'Lan tarani' (You will not see Me). The author first describes the Mu'tazila position, including al-Zamakhshari's grammatical claim that 'lan' indicates permanent negation. Then Ahl al-Sunnah respond with three arguments: (1) Musa asked to see Allah, and a prophet would not ask for something impossible. (2) Linguistically, negation only occurs where the possibility of affirmation exists; negating a possible thing is meaningful, but negating an impossible thing is absurd. Since Allah said 'Lan tarani', the negation presupposes that vision is possible in itself. (3) The verse conditions vision on the mountain settling, which is a possible event; conditioning on a possible thing indicates the conditioned thing is also possible. The Mu'tazila objection that the mountain's settling is impossible because Allah knows it won't happen does not make it inherently impossible. The passage then addresses another proof: the verse 'La tudrikuhu al-absar' (Vision comprehends Him not). The Mu'tazila argue that perception (idrak) by sight is vision, so it negates vision. Ahl al-Sunnah answer that idrak is a specific kind of vision involving encompassing, not mere vision; negating the specific does not negate the general. This argument is presented logically: negation of the more specific does not imply negation of the more general.

Three Arguments for the Permissibility of Seeing Allah
Enumerates three arguments from the verse 'Lan tarani' for the permissibility of seeing Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three arguments Ahl al-Sunnah derive from the verse 'Lan tarani' to prove that seeing Allah is possible.

Core Idea
Musa's request, negation's nature, and conditional phrasing all show vision is possible.

Ahl al-Sunnah argue that the same verse 'Lan tarani' used by the Mu'tazila actually proves that seeing Allah is possible. They present three logical and linguistic arguments: Musa (a prophet) would not ask for something impossible; negating something implies it could exist; and conditioning vision on the possible event of the mountain settling shows that vision itself is possible.

Musa asked to see Allah. As a prophet, he knew what is permissible. This shows vision is possible.
3
Analyze the second argument
Linguistic convention: negation (nafy) only occurs where affirmation is possible. Since Allah negated vision, it implies vision is possible.
Example: 'The mountain did not speak' is absurd because speech is impossible for a mountain.

Analyze the third argument
Vision was conditioned on the mountain settling, which is a possible event. Conditioning on a possible thing indicates the conditioned is also possible.
The Mu'tazila objection that Allah knew it wouldn't happen does not change intrinsic possibility.

Commentary
This passage refutes the Mu'tazila argument that seeing Allah is impossible, using the story of Musa (Moses) and the verse 'Lan tarani' (You will not see Me). The author first describes the Mu'tazila position, including al-Zamakhshari's grammatical claim that 'lan' indicates permanent negation. Then Ahl al-Sunnah respond with three arguments: (1) Musa asked to see Allah, and a prophet would not ask for something impossible. (2) Linguistically, negation only occurs where the possibility of affirmation exists; negating a possible thing is meaningful, but negating an impossible thing is absurd. Since Allah said 'Lan tarani', the negation presupposes that vision is possible in itself. (3) The verse conditions vision on the mountain settling, which is a possible event; conditioning on a possible thing indicates the conditioned thing is also possible. The Mu'tazila objection that the mountain's settling is impossible because Allah knows it won't happen does not make it inherently impossible. The passage then addresses another proof: the verse 'La tudrikuhu al-absar' (Vision comprehends Him not). The Mu'tazila argue that perception (idrak) by sight is vision, so it negates vision. Ahl al-Sunnah answer that idrak is a specific kind of vision involving encompassing, not mere vision; negating the specific does not negate the general. This argument is presented logically: negation of the more specific does not imply negation of the more general.

Negating encompassing vision does not negate simple vision.

Commentary
This passage presents a third issue: whether the proof for seeing Allah in revelation is specific to the afterlife or also applies to this world. The author states that Ahl al-Sunnah differed on this. One group holds that all revelatory proofs for ru'ya are confined to the afterlife; thus verses like 'La tudrikuhu al-absar' and 'Lan tarani' refer to this world, while other proofs establish vision in the afterlife. The majority, however, hold that revelation indicates the possibility of seeing Allah in this world for whomever Allah wills, citing the Prophet's Mi'raj (ascension). This group asserts that the Prophet saw his Lord with his physical eyes in their natural place, not transferred to the heart. They also say that the Prophet saw his Lord repeatedly during the negotiations for prayer reduction. This view is attributed to many Companions, including Ibn Abbas. In contrast, Aisha and Muawiya held that the Prophet's vision during the Mi'raj was a dream (ru'ya manam), not wakeful. The author notes that they based this on ijtihad. Some people support their view by citing the verse about 'the vision' (al-ru'ya) in Surah al-Isra', arguing that the word ru'ya (with alif maqsura) means dream vision, whereas wakeful vision is expressed as ru'ya with a ta (رؤية). The passage ends with this linguistic point, setting the stage for a refutation that will follow.

Learning Objective

Commentary
This passage refutes the claim that the Prophet's vision during the Night Journey (Isra) was merely a dream. The author presents three arguments (wujuh). The author illustrates this with the extreme example of someone claiming to fly and speak with angels in a dream—nobody would be shocked. Yet the Prophet’s report of the Isra caused mockery, apostasy, and Abu Bakr’s famous testimony. Therefore the vision must have been wakeful, not a dream. The sequence follows the author's method: first historical context, then linguistic evidence, then practical argument from the effect.

This lesson tackles a foundational principle of Sunni metaphysics: the objective existence of the essences of things.
