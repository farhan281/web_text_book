Refuting al-ʿInādiyya: The Denial of Existence
Definition, argument, and refutation of al-ʿInādiyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the beliefs of al-ʿInādiyya sect and how Ahl al-Sunna refuted their denial of existence.

Core Idea
Al-ʿInādiyya claim nothing exists; Ahl al-Sunna affirm existence and refute their atomism-based argument.

The first sophist sect, al-ʿInādiyya, takes an extreme position by denying all existence. They argue that the division of atoms leads to absurdity, thus nothing exists. In this lesson, we examine their claim, their proof from atomism, and the response from Ahl al-Sunna, including a famous story of Imam Abu Hanifa.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of al-ʿInādiyya
Understand their core denial of existence—they claim all things are illusions and fantasies.
Note the etymology: 'īnād' means obstinacy.
2
Examine their argument from atomism
They present a dilemma: either the atom divides into finite equal parts or infinite parts, both supposedly impossible.
3
Learn the Ahl al-Sunna response
Ahl al-Sunna choose the finite division alternative and reject the proofs against the atom as specious.
They assert the existence of the indivisible atom.
4
Reflect on the Imam Abu Hanifa story
The story shows a practical refutation: a sophist's mule disappears, and the Imam uses the sophist's own logic against him.
The sophist recants and the mule is returned.

An explanation of the sects of the Sophists, the people of deceptive wisdom. And his intention is to refute the three sects of the Sophists: al-ʿInādiyya: those who deny the realities of things, and claim that they are illusions and fantasies; they categorically assert that there is no existent at all. al-Sūfisṭāʾiyya: with dammah on the sīn, then sukūn on the wāw, then fatḥah on the fāʾ, then sukūn on the sīn, a relative noun to Sūf Asṭā with the hamzah cut; Sūf is a name for knowledge, and Asṭā is a name for embellished speech; its meaning in the language of the Greeks is wisdom whose exterior is adorned and whose interior is corrupt; they are a people among the Greek philosophers who delved into asceticism until they produced nonsense. al-Shanwānī. al-ʿInādiyya: with kasrah on the ʿayn, a relative noun to ʿinād (obstinacy), because they obstinately opposed the rational people regarding the affirmation of the Necessary and the contingent, and claimed that the established realities are fantasies like the perceptions of a sleeping person. al-Shanwānī. They categorically asserted that there is no existent at all, meaning neither mentally nor externally. They said: we affirm with certainty that there is no verification of a relation of one thing to another in reality. And they argued against Ahl al-Sunna that what they say about it being existent is either that they say it is divided into a limited boundary and equal parts, or that they say it is divided into infinite parts; and both are invalid. As for the first, because of the proofs established for the negation of the indivisible atom; the second is invalid because it necessitates equality of bodies, and that a small stone equals a mountain; and whatever leads to falsehood—which is the affirmation of existents—is false. And Ahl al-Sunna replied to them: we choose the first alternative; and as for your saying that it is not valid because of the proofs established for the negation of the indivisible atom, we do not concede that they are proofs; rather they are specious arguments. It is related that a Sophist came to Imam Abū Ḥanīfah to debate him while he was on a mule; the Imam ordered one of his students to take the mule. When the Sophist came out, he did not find the mule, so he sought it and asked about it. The Imam said to him: 'Your mule had no reality, so do not seek it.' So he retracted his belief, and the mule was returned to him. al-Shanwānī. The existent and the fixed are synonymous, thus the judgment would be nullified in the author's statement: 'and fixed in the external, the existent', because the condition of judgment—which is the change of the predicate and subject in concept—is absent. So the commentator answered by denying the absence of the condition for the judgment, by explaining the difference in concept between the subject and the predicate: because the subject, which is 'the existent', is considered with the belief in its affirmation, while the predicate, which is 'fixed in the external', is what is in reality. al-Shanwānī. And al-ʿIndiyya: those who deny the affirmation of the realities of things in themselves, and their being established as they are observed; they claim that they are dependent on opinion and belief. subservience to opinion and belief is an explanatory conjunction; if they believe that a stone is fire, it becomes fire; if they believe that a human is a stone or fire, he becomes a stone or fire. And they argued for that by saying that the bilious person finds something sweet as bitter if he believes it to be so. And it was objected: that the bitterness of something sweet only arises from an accidental matter, which is the agitation of bile, because it is from the perspective of belief; indeed you see that if the predominance of bile goes away, the bitterness goes away, unlike if the belief goes away without the predominance going away, the bitterness remains. (al-Shanwānī) The upshot is that al-ʿIndiyya are those who say: the consideration is according to what is with us and our belief. And al-Lā Adariyya: those who deny knowledge of the affirmation of a thing or its non-affirmation; they claim that they have no knowledge of any reality; and they are a people of disbelievers. al-Lā Adariyya: with fatḥah on the hamzah after the two lāms, then sukūn on the dāl, kasrah on the rāʾ, and tashdīd on the yāʾ, a relative noun to 'lā adrī' (I do not know), because they claim that they do not perceive realities. The reason for including them in the text: because their judgment of the affirmation of the realities of existents is a branch of the knowledge of their affirmation; so it cannot be said that the author left them out of the text. Al-Saʿd explicitly addressed them when he said: 'and knowledge of them is verified.' al-Shanwānī. [They deny] the affirmation of a thing or its non-affirmation, i.e., its non-existence; they said: we do not know its affirmation nor its non-existence. Their sophism in that is that since falsity has entered upon judgment regarding sensory matters by considering al-ʿIndiyya, and regarding intellectual matters with respect to al-ʿInādiyya, then suspension of judgment became necessary; so they say: we do not know affirmation or non-existence. If one were to say to one of them: 'Are you alive or dead?', he would say: 'I do not know.' al-Shanwānī. And they are a people of disbelievers, i.e., the three Sophist sects. al-Shanwānī.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and refutation of the three Sophist sects. The commentator begins by stating the intention to refute the three Sophist sects. The first is al-ʿInādiyya, who deny the realities of things and assert that nothing exists at all. A footnote explains the etymology of 'Sufistā'iyya' and 'Inādiyya', and then gives a detailed argument: the Sophists argued that the atom cannot be divided into either finite or infinite parts, both leading to absurdities. The Ahl al-Sunna respond by choosing the first alternative and rejecting that the proofs against the atom are valid, calling them specious. A story about Imam Abu Hanifa is told to illustrate the refutation. Then there is a short note on the words 'existent' and 'fixed' being synonymous, addressing a potential objection about the wording of the matn. The second sect is al-ʿIndiyya, who deny the fixed realities of things and claim that things are dependent on belief. The footnote gives an example: if they believe a stone is fire, it becomes fire. They argue from the experience of a bilious person tasting sweet as bitter. The objection is that the bitterness arises from an accidental cause (bile), not from belief alone, as shown by the fact that removing the bile removes the bitterness. The third sect is al-Lā Adariyya, who deny knowledge of whether anything is affirmed or not, claiming they have no knowledge of any reality. The footnote explains that they are included in the text because their judgment is a branch of knowledge, and al-Sa'd explicitly addressed them. Their sophism is that since doubt has entered sensory and intellectual judgments, they suspend judgment. The passage concludes that all three sects are disbelievers.

Refuting al-ʿIndiyya: Reality Depends on Belief
Definition, example, and refutation of al-ʿIndiyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the belief of al-ʿIndiyya that realities depend on belief, and how it is refuted by the example of the bilious person.

Core Idea
Al-ʿIndiyya claim that belief creates reality; reason shows that accidental causes, not belief, produce changes.

The second sophist sect, al-ʿIndiyya, asserts that things have no fixed reality but are subject to belief. They argue from the experience of a bilious person tasting sweet as bitter. This lesson examines their claim, their evidence, and the rational refutation that distinguishes accidental causes from belief.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of al-ʿIndiyya
They say that the realities of things are not fixed in themselves but are dependent on one's belief (al-ʿind wa al-iʿtiqād).
2
Examine their example of the bilious person
They argue that if a bilious person believes something sweet is bitter, it becomes bitter for them.
3
Learn the objection and refutation
The objection is that the bitterness arises from an accidental cause (bile), not from the belief itself. When the bile goes away, the bitterness disappears.
If belief alone were the cause, removing belief would remove bitterness even if bile remains.

Refuting al-Lā Adariyya: The Skeptics
Definition, sophism, and ruling of disbelief for al-Lā Adariyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the skeptical position of al-Lā Adariyya and why their suspension of judgment is refuted as disbelief.

Core Idea
Al-Lā Adariyya claim we cannot know anything; Islam affirms knowledge of realities.

The third sophist sect, al-Lā Adariyya, advocates complete skepticism, denying knowledge of any reality. They argue that since both sensory and intellectual judgments have been doubted, one must suspend judgment. This lesson explains their position, why they are included in the text, and why they are considered disbelievers.

They argue that since the senses can be deceived (as al-ʿIndiyya show) and reason can be faulty (as al-ʿInādiyya show), we must suspend all judgment.
They use the existence of other sophist sects as evidence for doubt.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle of Sunni methodology: the necessity of following the righteous predecessors and avoiding innovation. The text explains that faith, action, and intention are only complete when aligned with the Sunnah, which is defined by the Quran, Hadith, consensus, and clear analogy. Understanding this helps prevent both extreme innovation and neglect of sound scholarly tradition.

Commentary
This passage is a combined verse and commentary that defines the obligation to follow the righteous Salaf (predecessors) and to avoid blameworthy innovations (bidʿa). It opens with the heading 'Obligation of following the Salaf and avoiding innovations' and then quotes verse 139 of the poem: 'So follow the righteous among those who preceded, and avoid innovation from those who came later.' The commentary first explains the clause 'follow the righteous among those who preceded': it means to imitate them in beliefs, words, and deeds because of their strict adherence to the truth. The proof is the hadith commanding adherence to the Prophet's Sunnah and the Sunnah of the rightly-guided caliphs, with the metaphor of 'biting with molars' indicating firmness. The righteous are defined as those who fulfill the rights of Allah and the rights of people. Then the commentary explains 'avoid the blameworthy innovation from those who came later': i.e., those who came after the elite Companions and their scholars. The command to follow the Companions is understood as applying to their scholars, not all individuals. The author then provides a logical sequence: he first commanded following the Salaf and then commanded avoiding innovation because faith is perfected only through action, action through intention, and all three through conformity to the Sunnah. Finally, he defines Sunnah as everything that agrees with the Quran, Hadith, consensus (ijmāʿ), or clear analogy (qiyās jalī); whatever falls outside these sources is blameworthy innovation.
