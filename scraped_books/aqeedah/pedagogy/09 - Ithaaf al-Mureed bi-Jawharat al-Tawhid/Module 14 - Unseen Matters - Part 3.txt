Commentary
This passage discusses the controversial issue of the survival of the 'coccyx bone' (عجب الذنب) after death, which is relevant to the bodily resurrection. It begins by citing the well-known hadith in Bukhari and Muslim stating that every part of the human body decays except this bone, and from it the body is recreated on the Day of Resurrection. The bone is described as small, like a mustard seed, located at the base of the spine, and is unique to humans. The commentator notes that the comparison (of the bone to the spirit) is not restricted to the time of the blast. Then the objection of Imam al-Muzani is presented: he held that everything, including this bone, must be annihilated completely, based on the Quranic verse 'Everyone upon it is perishing' (ar-Rahman 26), arguing that the annihilation of the whole (all created beings) necessitates the annihilation of every part. The commentator then explains how al-Muzani's followers reconciled his view with the hadith: they said that God first annihilates the human body by the earth, and when only the coccyx remains, He annihilates it by a different means (not by earth), just as the Angel of Death is made to die without any other angel. This interpretation does not contradict the hadith that says the earth does not eat the bone, because that hadith only denies annihilation by earth, not annihilation by other means. Ibn Qutayba agreed with al-Muzani, stating that this bone is merely the last part of the body to decay. However, the commentator states that the stronger opinion (الأقوى في النظر) is that the bone never decays at all, based on the apparent meaning of the hadith. Its preservation is considered a devotional matter (تعبدي), though some scholars have suggested a wisdom behind it: that it serves as a sign for the angels when they are tasked with resurrecting each individual with the exact same material substance they had in the world, preventing confusion.

Specifying the General Statement 'Everything is Perishing'
Commentary explains specification of the general verse and presents two interpretive approaches.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how scholars reconcile the verse 'Everything is perishing' with the survival of the spirit and tailbone, and the two main interpretive approaches: specification (takhsīṣ) and reinterpretation (ma'nā al-imkān).

Core Idea
The Qur'anic 'everything perishes' is not absolute—scholars specify or reinterpret it to fit revealed truths.

This lesson addresses a key question: how can the verse 'Everything is perishing' (Qaṣaṣ 88) be reconciled with the belief that the spirit and tailbone endure? The poem and commentary present two scholarly responses: specification (takhṣīṣ) and reinterpretation of 'perishing' as 'liable to perish.' You will learn the definitions of general and specific, and see how later scholars offered an alternative that avoids specification.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the problem

The problem is an apparent contradiction between the general verse and specific hadith.

The author explains that scholars specify the verse's generality, restricting it to exclude certain things. Learn the definitions of 'general' (al-ʿāmm) and 'specification' (al-takhṣīṣ).
Specification restricts a general term to some of its individuals.

This avoids the need to specify the verse.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the poetic line 'And every thing is perishing; they have specified its generality...' The author first states that since the view of the survival of the spirit and the tailbone is preponderant, a response is given to the verse 'Everything is perishing except His Face' (Qasas 88) which apparently implies that everything except God perishes. The author explains that scholars have specified (takhsis) the generality of this verse, i.e., they limited its comprehensive scope so that it does not include things like the spirit, the tailbone, the Preserved Tablet, Paradise, Hell, etc. He defines specification as restricting the general to some of its individuals, and defines the general (al-'amm) as an expression that comprehensively includes everything qualified for it without enumeration (ghayr hasr). He then says that the poet (al-Laqqani) followed the response of a group including Ibn 'Abbas. Finally, he mentions the view of later verifying scholars (muhaqqiqun) that there is no exception or specification needed; rather, the meaning of 'perishing' in the verse is 'liable to perish' or 'possible to perish' due to its contingency and need, which is also the meaning of 'vanishing' (fanin). This view avoids the need for specification because the verse does not assert actual annihilation of everything, but rather its possibility.

Commentary
This passage deals with the belief in the questioning in the grave (su'al al-qabr), which is part of the sam'iyyat (matters known through revelation). The author states that belief in the questioning in the grave, its bliss, and its punishment is obligatory, just like the belief in the resurrection (ba'th al-hashr). The explanation clarifies that the questioning is by Munkar and Nakir, two angels who question the deceased after burial. The obligation is based on revelation (sam'an), not reason. The term 'Munkar and Nakir' is explained: their names derive from the fact that the deceased does not recognize them. They have a unique form unlike any creation. The passage also addresses the scope: the questioning applies to the entire Ummah of Muhammad (s) – believers, hypocrites, and disbelievers – as held by the majority. It cites hadiths to support this. There is a minority view by Ibn Abd al-Barr that disbelievers are not questioned, but the majority opinion is that they are. Ibn al-Qayyim adds that every nation is questioned by its own prophet. The passage also notes that the questioning in the grave is about beliefs (aqa'id) only.

Commentary
This passage is a lexical and theological explanation of the names of the two angels of the grave, Munkar and Nakir. The commentator explains that 'Munkar' is derived from 'ankara' (to deny), and it can be read with fatha or kasra on the kaf, both being possible as a passive participle (one who is denied) or an active participle (one who denies). 'Nakir' follows the pattern of 'fa'eel' and can mean 'one who denies' or 'one who is unknown'. The key point is that these names are given because the deceased does not recognize them and has never seen any form like theirs. The author (Ibn `Ashir) adds that their appearance is entirely unique: they do not resemble any creature known to humans—not humans, angels, birds, beasts, or insects. They are a 'badī' (innovative) creation, unlike anything seen. This unfamiliarity adds to the terror of the questioning, as the soul encounters beings beyond its experience.

Ba'th is revival; hashr is gathering—two distinct phases of resurrection.

In this passage, Imam Sanusi clarifies the terminology of resurrection. He distinguishes between ba'th (raising the dead) and hashr (gathering them for judgment). Understanding these terms is essential for grasping the later discussion about the nature of the resurrected body.

وشبه في الوجوب قوله: (كبعث الحشر) أي: كوجوب بعث الله جميع العباد وإعادتهم بعد إحيائهم بجميع أجزائهم الأصلية، وهي التي من شأنها البقاء من أول العمر إلى آخره، وسوقهم إلى محشرهم لفصل القضاء.

وقد فرق الإمام السنوسي بين البعث والحشر فقال: إن البعث عبارة عن إحياء الموتى وإخراجهم من قبورهم، والحشر: سوقهم جميعا إلى الموقف. فقوله: كوجوب بعث، أي: إحياء، والإضافة لأمته، أي: بعث الله الناس للحشر.

وقوله: (الأصلية) إشارة لرد شبهة من طرف المنكرين. قالوا لو أكل إنسان آخر وصار غذاء له ومن أجزاء بدنه، فالأجزاء المأكولة إما أن تعاد في بدن الأكل أو بدن المأكول، وأيا ما كان لا يكون أحدهما معادا بتمامه، على أنه لا أولوية لجعلها جزءا من بدن أحدهما دون الآخر، ولا سبيل إلى جعلها جزءا من كل منهما. وأيضا إذا كان الآكل كافرا والمأكول مؤمنا يلزم تنعيم الأجزاء العاصية أو تعذيب الأجزاء المطيعة. والجواب: أن الحشر للأجزاء الأصلية لا الحاصلة بالتغذية؛ فالمعاد من كل من الآكل والمأكول الأجزاء الأصلية الحاصلة في أول الفطرة من غير لزوم فساد. فإن قيل: يجوز أن تصير تلك الأجزاء الغذائية الأصلية في المأكول ذلك لا نطفة وأجزاء أصلية لبدن آخر ويعود المحذور. قلنا: المحذور إنما هو في وقوع إمكانه؛ فالله تعالى قادر أن يحفظها من أن تصير جزءا لبدن آخر فضلا عن أن تصير جزءا أصليا. اهـ.
And He likened in obligation His saying: (like the resurrection of the gathering) meaning: like the obligation of God raising all servants and returning them after their revival with all their original parts (ajzā'ihim al-aṣliyya), which are those that are meant to persist from the beginning of life to its end, and driving them to their gathering place for the passing of judgment. And Imam Sanusi distinguished between ba'th and hashr, saying: ba'th is reviving the dead and bringing them out of their graves, and hashr is driving them all to the standing place. So His saying: like the obligation of raising, i.e., reviving, and the addition [of God] to His community, i.e., God raising people for the gathering. And His saying: (original) is a reference to refuting a doubt from the deniers. They said: if one person eats another and it becomes nourishment for him and part of his body, then the eaten parts are either returned in the body of the eater or the body of the eaten, and in either case one of them is not fully returned, with no priority to make them part of one body over the other, and there is no way to make them part of both. Also if the eater is a disbeliever and the eaten is a believer, it would necessitate giving bliss to the disobedient parts or punishing the obedient parts. The answer: the gathering is for the original parts, not those acquired through nutrition; so the restored person from each of the eater and the eaten are the original parts present at the first creation without corruption. If it is said: it is possible that those nutritional parts become original parts for another body and the problem returns. We say: the problem is only in the occurrence of its possibility; God is able to preserve them from becoming part of another body, let alone becoming an original part.

Commentary
This passage first clarifies that the obligation of resurrection is similar to the obligation of the grave events. It then defines ba'th as reviving the dead and bringing them out of graves, and hashr as driving them to the standing place. The main point is that resurrection involves only the original parts (al-ajza' al-asliyya) that persist from birth to death, not parts acquired through nutrition. This serves to refute the objection of the deniers that if a person eats another, the body parts would be shared. The objection argues that if a disbeliever eats a believer, the same parts would have to be either punished or rewarded, leading to contradiction. The answer is that the resurrection is only for the original parts present at the first creation; God is able to preserve those original parts from being assimilated into another body. Even if it is possible that nutritional parts become original for another, God can prevent that. The passage also notes that this refutes the charge of reincarnation (tanasukh).
