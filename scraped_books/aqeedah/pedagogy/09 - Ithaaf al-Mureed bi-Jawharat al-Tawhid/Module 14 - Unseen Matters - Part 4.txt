The Original Parts Condition and Refutation of the Doubt
Sanusi refutes the doubt about cannibalism by specifying original parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the condition that resurrection involves only original parts and how this refutes the doubt about shared body parts from cannibalism.

Core Idea
Only original parts are resurrected; acquired parts do not create contradiction.

A common objection to bodily resurrection involves the case of cannibalism: if one person eats another, whose body do the parts belong to? Imam Sanusi refutes this by clarifying that resurrection applies only to the original parts present from birth. This lesson examines the doubt and the theological response.

Guided Path
1
Understand the doubt
Read the doubt presented by the deniers. They argue about shared body parts and contradictory rewards or punishments.
Notice the two aspects: the physical sharing and the ethical problem.
2
Learn Sanusi's answer
Read Sanusi's response: resurrection is for the original parts only, not the acquired ones.
This solves both aspects.
3
Consider the counter-objection
A possible counter is raised: what if acquired parts become original for another? Sanusi responds that God can prevent that.
Reflect on the reliance on divine power.

Commentary
This passage first clarifies that the obligation of resurrection is similar to the obligation of the grave events. It then defines ba'th as reviving the dead and bringing them out of graves, and hashr as driving them to the standing place. The main point is that resurrection involves only the original parts (al-ajza' al-asliyya) that persist from birth to death, not parts acquired through nutrition. This serves to refute the objection of the deniers that if a person eats another, the body parts would be shared. The objection argues that if a disbeliever eats a believer, the same parts would have to be either punished or rewarded, leading to contradiction. The answer is that the resurrection is only for the original parts present at the first creation; God is able to preserve those original parts from being assimilated into another body. Even if it is possible that nutritional parts become original for another, God can prevent that. The passage also notes that this refutes the charge of reincarnation (tanasukh).

Resurrection and the Original Parts
Sanusi defines ba'th and hashr and explains the condition of original parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that resurrection in Islam applies only to the original parts that persist from birth, not to parts acquired through nutrition, and how this answers the cannibal objection.

Core Idea
Only original parts are resurrected; nutrition does not create a problem for bodily resurrection.

The passage clarifies two key eschatological terms—ba'th (resurrection) and hashr (gathering)—and addresses a classic objection: if a cannibal eats another person, how can both be resurrected fully? Sanusi explains the crucial qualification that resurrection concerns only the original parts present at the beginning of life, not those acquired through nutrition. This allows a coherent response to the objection while preserving the doctrine of bodily resurrection.

The answer is that resurrection is for the original parts, not those acquired through nutrition. God preserves those original parts from being permanently absorbed. Also note the clarification about parts like nails and saliva not being resurrected.

Commentary
This passage defines the resurrection (ba'th) and gathering (hashr) and explains the crucial condition of original parts (al-ajza' al-asliyya). The author first states the revealed belief: all people will be returned to life after death with all their original parts — those that persist from the beginning of life to the end — and driven to the place of judgment. The word order in the text is noted as inverted (قلب), with the proper order being 'their resurrection after their return'. The specification of 'original parts' is a response to an objection raised by deniers: the cannibal problem. The objection and answer are detailed: if a cannibal eats another human, how can both be resurrected completely? The answer is that resurrection applies only to the original parts present at the beginning of creation, not those acquired through nutrition. A further objection is considered: what if the original parts of the eaten person become part of the eater's body and then original for him? The answer is that God preserves those parts from becoming original for another. Then the passage clarifies what counts as original (parts existing at the soul's blowing, including the foreskin, but excluding nails and saliva). This is a definition combined with a refutation, following the author's argumentative order, preserving the nuance of body, soul, and divine power.

Refuting the Reincarnation Objection
The author refutes the claim that resurrecting from original parts implies reincarnation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author defends resurrection against the charge of reincarnation by distinguishing between a body created from original parts and a completely new body.

Core Idea
Resurrection from original parts is not reincarnation; it is a verbal dispute.

This passage addresses a common objection against bodily resurrection: that rebuilding a body from its original matter is tantamount to reincarnation. The author, using a commentary on 'Aqa'id al-Nasafi, clarifies the precise condition that would make it reincarnation and why the Islamic view avoids it.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Start by reading the objection in the source. Notice how it cites hadith descriptions to argue that the resurrected body differs from the earthly one, implying reincarnation.
The objection uses physical differences as evidence.
2
Identify the author's key distinction
The author responds by stating that reincarnation would only follow if the second body is NOT created from the original parts of the first. This is the critical condition that separates resurrection from reincarnation.
The word 'original parts' (أجزاء أصلية) is central.
3
Understand the conclusion
If someone still calls it reincarnation, it becomes a verbal dispute. The proofs for resurrection apply regardless of the name.
The author emphasizes substance over terminology.

Commentary
This is an objection-and-answer passage defending the resurrection of original parts from the charge of reincarnation (tanasukh). The objection states that if the resurrected body is made from original parts of the first body, it is a different body, thus implying reincarnation – citing hadiths about physical differences in the afterlife. The answer clarifies that reincarnation would only follow if the second body were NOT created from the first body's original parts. If one still calls it reincarnation, it is a mere verbal dispute. There is no proof that returning the soul to such a body is impossible; the proofs for resurrection apply whether one calls it reincarnation or not. The passage preserves the nuance that the identity of the body is tied to its original matter.

The Scriptural and Consensual Proof of Resurrection

By the end of this lesson, you will understand that resurrection is a revealed truth, not a rational necessity, established by the Book, Sunnah, and consensus.

Core Idea
Resurrection is a truth revealed by the Lawgiver, made certain through revelation.

This lesson examines how classical Sunni theology grounds belief in resurrection. The author asserts that resurrection is a truth firmly established by divine revelation and the consensus of the early community. You will see why this belief is not a matter of rational proof but of trusting the Lawgiver's report.

Commentary
This passage presents the proof for resurrection as a matter of faith. It states that belief in resurrection and bodily gathering is a truth established by three sources: the Quran, the Sunnah, and the consensus (ijma) of the Salaf. It further asserts that resurrection is among the possibilities (mumkinat) that the Lawgiver has informed us about; and whatever the Lawgiver informs about is true and corresponds to reality. Two Quranic verses are cited as evidence: 'Who will give life to bones when they are decayed?' (Yasin 78) and 'As We began the first creation We will repeat it' (al-Anbiya 104). The footnote by al-Amir criticizes the phrasing of the argument, noting that it appears to take the claim itself as evidence, but the point remains that the proof is ultimately the divine report. The passage is a concise statement of the epistemological basis for belief in resurrection.

Resurrection of All Creatures: Proofs from Hadith and Reason
The author presents two opinions on who is resurrected, favoring the inclusion of all.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the evidence from the Quran and Sunnah that all creatures, including animals, will be resurrected, and how the objection about moral responsibility is answered.

Core Idea
All creatures will be gathered on the Day of Resurrection for Divine justice, whether morally responsible or not.

This lesson explores a key aspect of Islamic eschatology: the scope of resurrection. While many assume only humans are resurrected, the Prophet ﷺ taught that even animals will be gathered. We will examine the two scholarly opinions and the evidence that supports the majority view.

This shows that resurrection is not only for punishment but also for establishing justice.

The Meaning of 'Easier' in Divine Resurrection
The author clarifies that divine reuse is called easier relative to creatures' abilities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the act of repeating creation is called 'easier' for Allah and how this relates to the recipient's preparedness, not a difference in divine power.

Core Idea
Divine reuse is called easier not because God's power varies, but because of the recipient's readiness.

The Quran describes the repetition of creation as 'easier' upon Allah, which raises a theological question: if Allah's power is eternal and all acts are equal for Him, how can one act be easier? This passage from al-Maqasid provides a precise answer by distinguishing between ease from the agent's side and ease from the recipient's side. We'll explore this distinction and its implications for understanding divine attributes.

Commentary
This passage addresses an objection regarding the verse 'and it is easier upon Him' (al-Rum 27). The objection asks: how can the act of repetition be easier for God, given that His power is eternal and all objects of power are equal before it? The answer distinguishes two aspects: ease can be from the agent's side (by increase in conditions of agency) or from the recipient's side (by increase in preparedness to receive). Here it is from the recipient's side; from the aspect of God's power, all acts are equal. A common interpretation is that the comparative form (af'ala al-tafdil) is not used in its usual sense but rather indicates that, according to human custom and experience, repeating something is easier than beginning it. Al-Baydawi clarifies that this is relative to human capacity and analogy. The pronoun 'it' in 'easier upon Him' is said to refer to 'creation' (al-khalq). The passage ends with a call to reflect, indicating the subtlety of the issue.

Proof of Resurrection and the Dispute on Who Is Gathered
Sanusi proves resurrection by divine information and presents the dispute on universality of gathering.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that resurrection is proven by divine information and that there is a dispute on whether all creatures or only accountable humans are gathered.

Core Idea
Resurrection is true by Allah's report; all beings will be gathered for justice.

In this passage, Sanusi presents the proof for resurrection and then addresses a scholarly dispute on the universality of the gathering. He shows that resurrection is a possible matter that Allah has informed us about, and that the Lawgiver's report is necessarily true. He then explains that the majority of scholars, including al-Nawawi, hold that all creatures—including animals—will be gathered for judgment, while a minority limit gathering to those who will be recompensed. He supports the majority view with a hadith about animals being judged.

Commentary
This passage presents the proof for the resurrection and gathering of bodies, and then addresses a dispute about who is gathered. The author begins by stating that belief in resurrection and gathering is a truth established by three sources: the Book (Qur'an), the Sunnah, and consensus. He explains that resurrection is among the possible things that the Lawgiver (i.e., Allah through revelation) has informed about; anything that has been informed about by the Lawgiver is necessarily true, and the information corresponds to reality. He then cites two Qur'anic verses as evidence: the first from Surah Ya-Sin where the disbeliever asks who can give life to decayed bones, and the second from Surah al-Anbiya' where Allah says 'as We began the first creation, We will repeat it.' The argument is that just as Allah created the first time, He can recreate. The author then presents a dispute: the majority of verifiers, including al-Nawawi, hold that all people — including animals — will be gathered for judgment, with no distinction between those who are morally accountable and others (like animals). A minority group holds that only those who will be recompensed (i.e., humans accountable for deeds) will be gathered. A footnote (1) by al-Amir critiques the phrasing of the proof, noting a potential circularity: the claim that resurrection is true is supported by the fact that it is in the Book, etc., but that is essentially the same as saying the Lawgiver informed about it — the evidence and the claim seem to overlap. Another footnote (3) by al-Shanwani explains the view of the majority, citing a hadith from Sahih Muslim about animals being gathered for retaliation, and addresses the objection that non-human animals are not subject to religious law, answering that their punishment is for justice among themselves, not for sin.

Ruling on Miscarriage in Resurrection
Sanusi states the ruling on miscarriage: if spirit was blown, it is resurrected; otherwise like inanimate matter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the criterion for resurrection of a miscarriage based on whether the spirit was blown into it.

Core Idea
Resurrection requires a spirit; miscarriages without spirit are not resurrected.

This lesson examines a specific case in eschatology: the fate of miscarriages. Imam Sanusi clarifies that the determining factor is whether the fetus received the spirit (at 120 days). If so, it is resurrected and enters Paradise; otherwise, it is treated as inanimate matter and turns to dust. This ruling illustrates how kalam applies principles to particular instances.

This connects to the broader Islamic belief that souls are resurrected.

This shows that resurrection applies only to beings that had a spirit, and that the gathering of inanimate matter is only for the purpose of annihilation.

Commentary
This passage defines the two key terms in the verse line—'sayyi'at' (evil deeds) and 'hasanat' (good deeds)—and explains the rule of recompense for each in the Divine reckoning. 

First, the author defines 'sayyi'at': they are actions for which the doer is blameworthy according to the Sacred Law. The intended meaning is those deeds that the servant actually performed ('ḥaqīqatan') or is legally considered to have performed ('ḥukman'), such as when they are imposed on a wrongdoer due to his oppression of others and the depletion of his own good deeds. These may be minor or major sins. Their recompense with God is 'by the like' — that is, if God chooses to punish them, the punishment is exactly equivalent to the sin. However, God may pardon them, unless the sin is disbelief. They are called 'sayyi'ah' because the doer will be grieved when facing the recompense. 

Second, the author defines 'hasanat': they are actions for which the doer is praised according to the Law, because the good appearance of their owner is pleasing to see. The intended meaning is the accepted, original good deeds that the servant actually performed or deeds that are in the same ruling (e.g., charity given on one's behalf). This excludes good deeds that were taken in exchange for wrongs (i.e., taken from the wrongdoer). As for their recompense, God multiplies them for this Muslim nation, increasing their reward to the equivalent or more, without any upper limit. This multiplication is 'by grace' — a gift from God without any obligation or compulsion upon Him. 

The passage thus clarifies the fundamental asymmetry: sins are punished by the like, but good deeds are multiplied by God's free generosity.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal exposition on the ruling of expiation of minor sins by avoiding major sins. It begins by defining expiation (kaffārāt) and forgiveness (ghufr). The author explains that forgiveness is either covering the sin through repentance or through divine pardon and removal of its consequence. The first opinion, held by the leading theologians (aʼimmat al-kalām), is that expiation is not definitively obligatory but is permissible and strongly hoped for. They argue that if avoiding major sins were certain to absolve one of all minor sins, then the person would be in the position of one who is certain he faces no consequences, which would undermine the fabric of Islamic law. They interpret the verse 'If you avoid the major sins... We will expiate from you your misdeeds' (al-Nisa 31) as conditional upon God's will, similar to the verse 'But He forgives what is less than that for whom He wills' (al-Nisa 48). Thus, it is a promise subject to divine will, not a guarantee.

The second opinion, held by some jurists, hadith scholars, and the Muʿtazila, is that avoidance of major sins definitely expiates minor sins, so much so that punishment for them is impossible. They appeal to the apparent meaning of the same verse and consider this view more famous. The author then clarifies that the underlying issue is whether punishment for minor sins is permissible in the first place. He declares the first opinion (the theologians' view) to be the truth.

Finally, the author qualifies the discussion: forgiveness is tied to performing obligatory duties. He cites a hadith stating that performing the five prayers, fasting Ramadan, and avoiding the seven major sins result in the opening of eight gates of Paradise. Another version says that prayers, Jumuʿah, and Ramadan expiate between them when major sins are avoided. This shows that even with avoidance of major sins, one must still fulfill obligations for full forgiveness.

Commentary

It begins by specifying that angels and prophets are not included among those who receive these books, likely because they are not from the obligated ones in the same sense as humans and jinn.
