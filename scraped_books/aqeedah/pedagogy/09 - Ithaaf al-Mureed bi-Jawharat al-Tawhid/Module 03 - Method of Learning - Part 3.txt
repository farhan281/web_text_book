Core Idea
'Fai'tamid' is both a command to hold firm and a signal that the creed is complete.

In this brief passage, the commentator explains the final word of the creedal section. The command 'So rely' serves a dual purpose: it urges the reader to firmly adopt the Sunni creed, and it also marks the end of the theological discussion. Let's explore this layered meaning.

Commentary
This passage is an editorial/commentarial note. It begins with the section heading "Section of Sam'iyyat" (سمعيات). Then it provides a phrase: "so that he may attribute thereby the honor of knowledge to himself and the baseness of ignorance to others" — this appears to be a gloss on the author's intention or a moral lesson from the preceding creedal discourse. All Good from the Salaf, All Evil from Innovation
Explaining the line: all good from Salaf, all evil from innovation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that all good comes from following the pious predecessors (Salaf) and all evil from religious innovation, and the specific role of the four imams in this framework.

Core Idea
Following the Salaf brings every good; innovation brings every evil.

This lesson examines a famous principle from Islamic theology: that every good is in following the Salaf, and every evil is in innovation. Through the commentary on the poem of Jawharat al-Tawhid, we explore how this statement is argued, with special emphasis on the four Sunni imams. By understanding this, you will grasp the foundational Sunni methodology of adhering to the path of the early generations.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation carefully. Notice the two-part structure: the first hemistich about good and the second about evil.
2
Identify the two key claims
The passage states that all good is due to following the Salaf, and all evil is due to innovation. Identify who the Salaf are: prophets, Companions, Followers, and especially the four imams.
The four imams are mentioned as those whose schools cannot be abandoned by consensus.
3
Analyze the reasoning for the command
The commentator explains that the divine command to adopt good morals is reasoned by the first claim—all good comes from following the Salaf. The second claim serves as the reason for an implied prohibition against being like the wicked.
The command is 'Be as the best of creation used to be'; the prohibition is 'Do not be as the wicked were.'
4
Recognize the implied prohibition and the four imams' status
Understand that the second part of the line provides the basis for forbidding innovation. Note the special consensus that it is forbidden to depart from the schools of the four imams, highlighting their authority.

All good is in following the Salaf; all evil is in religious innovation.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed explanatory commentary on the couplet 'فَكُلُّ خَيْرٍ فِي اتِّبَاعِ مِن سَلَفْ وكلُّ شَرٌّ فِي ابْتِدَاعِ مَنْ خَلَفْ'. The author first interprets 'كل خير' (all good) as being attained through following the Salaf, specifying the Salaf as the prophets, companions, tabi'in, and especially the four leading imams (Abu Hanifa, Malik, Shafi'i, Ahmad) upon whose schools there is consensus that it is forbidden to deviate. Then he interprets 'وكل شر' (and all evil) as a cause for an implicit prohibition to adopt the evil characters and actions of the evil ones, which are the result of innovation (ibtida') of the later generations. He then defines 'bid'ah' (innovation) as anything introduced in religion contrary to the command of the Lawgiver (the Prophet) and His evidence, both specific and general. The definition is conditional: any act of worship or practice that has no basis in the Shari'ah is considered blameworthy innovation. The passage emphasizes that all religious good lies in adhering to the path of the Salaf, and all evil stems from introducing new practices in religion.
