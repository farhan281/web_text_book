Two Positions on Increase and Decrease of Faith
The author presents two scholarly positions on iman's increase/decrease and states his preference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two scholarly positions regarding whether faith increases and decreases, and which position the author adopts.

Core Idea
Faith's increase and decrease is a classic debate between Ash'aris and Hanafis.

This passage introduces a foundational issue in Islamic theology: does iman (faith) increase and decrease? Scholars have differed, and the author presents the two main views along with his own preference. As we read, pay attention to the groups involved and the reasoning behind each position.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the translation. Get a sense of the author's classification.
2
Identify the two madhhabs
Find the two positions listed: one that affirms increase and decrease, and one that denies it. Note which groups adhere to each.
The first includes Ash'aris, Mu'tazila, Ahl al-Hadith, and al-Shafi'i. The second includes Abu Hanifa and his followers, including Imam al-Haramayn.
3
Note the author's preference
The author clearly chooses the first madhhab. Observe that he says this holds whether iman is defined as tasdiq alone, actions alone, or both with verbal confession.
This shows the principle is independent of the definition of iman.
4
Consider the significance
Reflect on why this issue matters. It affects how we understand the stability of faith and the relationship between belief and actions.

Observational Evidence for the Variability of Faith
Shows that under one definition of faith, increase/decrease is self-evident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how direct observation and common experience prove that faith increases and decreases when faith is defined to include acts and confession.

Core Idea
When faith includes actions, its increase is obvious from daily life.

Sanusi begins by clarifying his position: he upholds the increase and decrease of faith regardless of how it is defined. He then shows that if faith is taken to include acts and verbal confession, the matter is so obvious it needs no proof—just look at how people differ in obedience. Even the same person varies over time.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening passage
Read the Arabic passage carefully. Notice that Sanusi states his position clearly and then distinguishes between two possible meanings of faith.
Pay attention to the phrase 'على أي معنى من المعاني حملنا الإيمانَ'.
2
Understand the first definition
If faith means acts alone or acts plus affirmation and confession, then the evidence is observational. Identify why no further proof is needed.
The author says 'المشاهدة والأخبار المتواترة' are enough.
3
See the implication for individuals
Consider the example of the same person having more obedience at one time and less at another. This shows his faith varies over time.
This is a concrete, everyday example.
4
Reflect on the strength of this argument
Ask yourself: why does the author consider this so obvious? Because it is based on direct observation, which is the most basic form of evidence.
This sets the stage for the more complex arguments that follow in the next lesson.

This passage addresses the second topic: whether the dispute about the increase and decrease of faith is real or verbal. Acceptance of iman's increase and decrease and clarification of the dispute about it. The increase of iman and its occurrence (by what increases obedience) meaning: due to the increase of obedience (of the human) which is: doing what is commanded and avoiding what is prohibited (and its decrease) meaning: iman in itself, not restricted to a particular subject; so it does not apply to prophets and angels, for it is not permissible for their iman to decrease. (By its decrease) means obedience by consensus. This is the madhhab of the majority of Ash'aris (١). Al-Bukhari said: I met more than a thousand men of scholars in various regions and I did not see anyone disagreeing that iman is statement and deed, and it increases and decreases (٢). End. And the Hanafis are among those who say that iman is tasdiq alone, they hold that iman does not increase nor decrease. And upon this, the true investigation that we should adopt is to consider the dispute in some of its aspects to be verbal: that is, between the Hanafis who say that iman does not increase or decrease and that iman is only tasdiq, and the muhaddithun who say that iman increases and decreases and that it is tasdiq, iqrar, and deed, and the Mu'tazila who say that iman is acts of obedience and it increases and decreases, and this is very clear, for we do not deny that the obedience of some people is greater than the obedience of others, and when obedience is part of the reality of iman, it necessarily follows that we conclude that it increases and decreases. As for the dispute between the Ash'aris and the Hanafis concerning the increase and decrease of iman, it cannot be verbal; for they all hold that the reality of iman is tasdiq alone, and acts of obedience are from the perfection of iman according to all of them as well. This, by reference to the original doctrines of the two groups regarding iman, and by reference to the evidence of the two groups and its presentation in the manner that we clarified to you recently, makes it clear that the focus of argumentation for the two groups is: establishing whether tasdiq, whose locus is the heart, accepts increase and decrease or does not accept them. From all this, the reality of this issue becomes clear to you in a manner worthy of acceptance, and Allah is the Grantor of success. Muhi.

Commentary
This passage classifies the different positions on whether iman (faith) increases and decreases. It first states the general acceptance that iman increases and decreases, defining increase as due to increase in obedience and decrease as due to decrease in obedience, but notes that this does not apply to prophets and angels since their iman cannot decrease. Then it attributes this view to the majority of Ash'aris and cites al-Bukhari's statement that the early scholars agreed that iman is statement and deed and increases and decreases. Then it introduces the Hanafi position that iman is only tasdiq (inner conviction) and does not increase or decrease. The passage then analyzes the nature of the dispute: the disagreement between the Hanafis and the muhaddithun (traditionists) and the Mu'tazila is considered verbal in some respects, because both sides acknowledge that actual obedience varies; the real issue is whether obedience is part of the reality of iman. However, the dispute between the Ash'aris and the Hanafis is not verbal because both define iman as tasdiq alone and consider actions as part of its perfection. The key difference lies in whether the tasdiq in the heart itself admits of increase and decrease. The passage concludes that the investigation centers on this point.

Proofs That Faith Increases and Decreases
The author presents rational and transmitted proofs that faith increases and decreases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational and transmitted proofs used by the majority of Muslim theologians to argue that faith (iman) increases and decreases.

Core Idea
Faith varies: if it didn't, prophets and sinners would be equal.

Muslim scholars debate whether faith is static or dynamic. The majority (including Ash'aris) hold that faith increases and decreases. In this passage, the author presents their key proofs from reason and scripture. Let's examine each argument carefully.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of proof
Notice the author divides the proofs into rational ('aql) and transmitted (naql). This is a common approach in kalam.
The rational proof is a conditional argument.
2
Analyze the rational argument
Work through the conditional: if faith didn't vary, then common sinners would equal prophets. Since that's false, faith must vary.
Look for the terms 'lazim' and 'malzum'.
3
Examine the transmitted evidence
The author cites Quran, hadith, and a companion saying. Each shows that faith can increase or be superior.
Note the argument that whatever increases can also decrease.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The proof is complete: faith admits both increase and decrease.
This opposes the Murji'ah view that faith is fixed.

Protesters. The proofs of the majority for the acceptance of faith of increase and decrease are upon that by reason and transmission: As for reason, then because if the reality of faith did not differ, then the faith of common individuals of the community – rather those immersed in sin and disobedience – would be equal to the faith of the prophets and angels, peace be upon them, and the consequent (al-lāzim) is false, so likewise the antecedent (al-malzūm). Commentary
This passage presents the proofs of the majority (the Ashʿarīs and others) that faith (īmān) increases and decreases. The author opens with the heading 'Protesters' (muḥtajjīn), indicating that these are the arguments of those who hold this position. The proofs are divided into rational (ʿaql) and transmitted (naql). The rational proof is a conditional argument: If faith did not differ in reality, then the faith of ordinary sinful believers would equal that of prophets and angels. This consequent is false (because prophets have superior faith), so the antecedent (that faith does not differ) is also false. Therefore, faith must be variable. The transmitted proof cites multiple texts: the Qurʾānic verse from Sūrat al-Anfāl showing that recitation increases faith, a ḥadīth where Ibn ʿUmar asks about increase and decrease and the Prophet affirms it, and another ḥadīth comparing Abū Bakr’s faith to that of the whole community. The author concludes by noting that whatever can increase can also decrease, completing the argument.

Faith Does Not Increase or Decrease: The Hanafi View
The author presents the opposing view that faith is fixed and their response to majority proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that faith is a single, non-variable act of definitive assent according to Abū Ḥanīfah, and their reinterpretation of textual proofs about increase.

Core Idea
Abū Ḥanīfah: faith is definitive assent – it either exists or not; actions do not change its essence.

In this passage, the author presents the position of Imam Abū Ḥanīfah and many theologians who argue that faith does not increase or decrease. They define faith as a kind of certainty that cannot vary, and they reinterpret the apparent increase mentioned in the Qurʾān and ḥadīth as an increase in the objects of belief, not in the quality of faith itself.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the core claim
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice the phrase 'faith does not increase nor decrease' – this is the central thesis.
The opening 'Wa qīla' signals an opposing opinion.
2
Understand the definition of faith as definitive assent
Focus on their definition: faith is 'al-taṣdīq al-bāligh ḥadd al-jazm wa al-idhʿān' – assent reaching the level of certainty and submission. This kind of inner state cannot admit of degrees; you either have it or you don't.
Compare with the majority view which includes actions.
3
See how they reinterpret 'increase' in texts
Read their response to the first party. They argue that the increase mentioned in verses refers to an increase in what is believed (i.e., new rulings revealed progressively), not in the strength of faith itself.
This preserves the unity of faith while accounting for scriptural language.

And it was said, i.e., a group of scholars, the greatest of them being Imam Abū Ḥanīfah and his companions and many of the theologians: faith does not increase nor decrease, because it is a name for assent (taṣdīq) that has reached the limit of certainty (jzm) and submission (idhʿān), and in this what was mentioned cannot be conceived. The Companions had believed in general, and the Sharia had not been completed, and the rulings were descending little by little, so they believed in every new thing from them.

Commentary
This passage presents the argument of those who deny that faith increases or decreases, led by Abū Ḥanīfah and many theologians. The author introduces it with 'And it was said' (wa qīla). Their core claim is that faith is defined as definitive assent (al-taṣdīq al-bāligh ḥadd al-jazm wa al-idhʿān) – a wholehearted certainty that does not admit of degrees. Therefore, when a person adds a good deed or commits a sin, his inner assent remains unchanged; it is not affected by external actions. The apparent variation in faith would only occur if faith were defined as acts of obedience, which do vary in quantity. Then they respond to the earlier majority’s textual proofs. They argue that the increase mentioned in verses and ḥadīth refers to an increase in the objects of belief (mā yuʾmanu bihī), not in the quality of faith itself. For the Companions, they had believed in the basic principles, but as new revelation came down with new rulings, they believed in each new thing as it was revealed. Thus the 'increase' is in the number of propositions believed, not in the strength of the same belief. This preserves the definition of faith as a single, non-variable act of assent.

The Fourth Condition for Repentance: Human Rights
States the additional condition for repentance involving others' rights and the dispute over its proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the additional condition required for repentance when a sin involves another person's rights, and the dispute between Sunnis and Mu'tazila over the proof of its obligation.

Core Idea
Repentance for sins against others requires returning rights or seeking forgiveness.

When we sin against Allah alone, the three general pillars of repentance suffice. But when we wrong another person, a fourth condition is necessary: we must make amends or obtain their forgiveness. This passage from Jawharat al-Tawhid explains this condition and highlights the theological debate over whether the obligation is proven by revelation or reason alone.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the literal translation. Notice how the text distinguishes sins between servant and God from those involving a human right.
The passage ends abruptly; the rest is assumed to continue on the next page.
2
Identify the fourth condition
Find the phrase that states the fourth condition: 'radd al-ẓulāma' (restoring the injustice) or 'taḥṣīl al-barā'a' (obtaining acquittal). This is the key addition.
3
Understand the dispute over proof
Note that both sides agree repentance for such sins is obligatory (wājib 'ayn). The disagreement is on the source of this obligation: revelation (sam') for Sunnis versus reason ('aql) for Mu'tazila.
The Sunni proof is the Quranic verse 24:31.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider why this condition matters: it shows that Islamic repentance is not just a private matter but involves restoring justice to others. The dispute also touches on the role of reason in knowing moral obligations.

Commentary
This passage discusses a special case of repentance: when the sin involves the rights of another person. For such sins, in addition to the three general pillars, a fourth condition is required: restoring the wrong (radd al-ẓulāma) to its owner or obtaining forgiveness from him. The text then states that there is consensus on the obligation of repentance being a personal duty (wājib 'ayn); the only disagreement is regarding the proof (dalīl) that establishes this obligation. The position of Ahl al-Sunna is that the proof is revelation (sam'), citing the Quranic verse commanding repentance. The Mu'tazila, on the other hand, argue that reason alone suffices to know the obligation. The passage ends with a fragment that was cut off at the page break.

Defining Apostasy: Denying What Is Known by Necessity
Al-Laqqani defines apostasy as denying what is necessarily known from religion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Laqqani's definition of apostasy as denying what is known by necessity from religion and the meaning of 'known by necessity.'

Core Idea
Apostasy is defined by denying what every Muslim knows is from Islam.

Al-Laqqani begins by stating the definition of apostasy in a concise poetic line. The commentary unpacks what 'known by necessity' means, explaining that it covers matters so clear that no Muslim doubts them, like the obligation of prayer and the prohibition of zina. This lesson establishes the conceptual foundation for understanding the legal ruling that follows.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening couplet
Read the Arabic line and translation. Notice that it defines apostasy as denying something 'necessarily known' from the religion.
The term 'ma'lum bil-darura' is key.
2
Understand the explanation
The commentary explains the lines and gives examples: obligation of prayer, fasting, prohibition of zina and wine.
These are matters that no one raised as a Muslim can deny.
3
Define 'known by necessity'
The commentary defines this as something known by both scholars and common Muslims without any doubt. It is effectively treated as a necessary truth.
4
Reflect on the implication
Ask yourself: why does this definition matter? It determines who is considered an apostate.
This connects to the punishment in the next lesson.

بيان أسباب الكفر وحدّ الرّدة

وَمَنْ لِمَعْلُومٍ ضرورةٌ جَحَدْ مِنْ دِينِنَا يُقْتَلُ كُفْرًا لَيْسَ حَدْ

(وَمَنْ لِمَعْلُومٍ ضرورةً جَحَدٌ * مِن دِينِنَا)، أي: وكلُّ مكلَّف جحد أمرًا معلومًا كونه من الدين بالضرورة؛ كوجوب الصَّلاة والصّوم وحُرمة الزنا والخمر ونحوها؛ فإنَّه يكفر بذلك، و(يُقْتَلُ كُفْرًا) إنْ لم يتب؛ لأَنَّ جَحْدَهُ ذلك المعلومَ مُستلزم لتكذيب النبي [؟] في إخباره عنه أَنَّه مِن الدِّين، والمعلوم بهذا المعنى: هو ما يَعرِفُ نسبته إلى الدِّين خواص المسلمينَ وعوامهم من غير قبول للتشكيك، فالتحق بالضّروريّات.

(لَيْسَ حَدْ) أي: ليس قتله حدًّا وكفَّارةً لجُرْمِه، كما في سائر الحُدود.

حكمُ مَن أنكر المُجمع عليه أو الإجماع

وَمِثْلُ هَذَا مَنْ نَفَى لِمُجْمَعِ أَوْ اسْتَبَاحَ كَالزِّنَا فَلْتَسْمَعِ

(وَمِثْلُ هَذَا) أي: مثلُ كُفْرِ جاحِدِ هذا المعلومِ مِن الدين بالضرورة وقتله.
Statement of the causes of disbelief and the ruling of apostasy. 'And whoever denies something necessarily known from our religion is killed as a disbeliever, not as a hadd penalty.' (And whoever denies something necessarily known from our religion) meaning: every morally responsible person who denies a matter that is known to be part of the religion necessarily – like the obligation of prayer and fasting, and the prohibition of zina and wine and similar – indeed he disbelieves by that, and (is killed as a disbeliever) if he does not repent; because his denial of that known matter necessitates that he has denied the Prophet [؟] in his informing that it is from the religion. And the known in this sense: is what the elite and common Muslims know its relation to the religion without acceptance of doubt. So it joins the necessary matters. (Not a hadd) meaning: his killing is not a hadd penalty and expiation for his crime, as in other hudud. The ruling of one who denies what is agreed upon or ijmāʿ. 'And similar to this is one who denies something agreed upon or permits like zina – so listen.' (And similar to this) meaning: like the disbelief of the denier of this matter known from the religion by necessity and his killing.
Source pages p. 599

Commentary
This passage is a definition and ruling for apostasy (ridda). It begins with a couplet from the poem: 'And whoever denies something necessarily known from our religion is killed as a disbeliever, not as a hadd penalty.' The commentary then explains that any legally responsible person (mukallaf) who denies a matter that is known by necessity to be from Islam – such as the obligation of prayer and fasting, or the prohibition of zina and wine – commits kufr by that denial. The punishment is execution if he does not repent, because his denial implies rejection of the Prophet's report. The author defines 'known by necessity' (ma'lum bil-darura) as something that both scholars and ordinary Muslims recognize as being from the religion without any doubt, so it is effectively treated as a necessary truth even if originally theoretical. The next line clarifies that this execution is not a hadd penalty (a fixed punishment that expiates the sin), but rather a punishment for kufr itself. Then the passage extends the same ruling to two additional cases: anyone who denies something established by ijmāʿ (consensus), or who permits something clearly forbidden like zina, receives the same ruling as the denier of what is necessarily known. The concluding line reiterates that such a person is treated as a disbeliever and executed.
