The Three Types of Agents: Choice, Compulsion, and Nature
Classification of agents into three types and affirmation of Allah's free will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three categories of agents in Islamic theology and why Ahl al-Sunna affirm only the agent by choice for Allah.

Core Idea
Allah acts by free choice, not by necessity or nature.

In this passage, al-Shanwānī concludes his discussion on divine agency by distinguishing three types of agents. This classification is crucial for understanding the Ashʿarī position on God's will and causality. Read on to see how each type is defined and which one applies to Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the passage begins with 'wal-ḥāṣil' indicating a conclusion.
2
Identify the three types of agents
Find the three divisions: agent by choice, agent by compulsion, and agent by nature. Observe how each is defined.
The definitions are distinct: choice involves will, compulsion is necessity, nature requires conditions.
3
Note the doctrinal position
Al-Shanwānī states that Ahl al-Sunna affirm only the agent by choice for Allah, except some non-Arabs (al-aʿājim) who claim Allah acts by compulsion in His attributes.
This is a key Ashʿarī refutation of necessitarian views.
4
Reflect on the implications
Consider why this matters: affirming free will for Allah upholds His sovereignty and rejects deterministic causality in divine action.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of agents (fāʿil) into three types, presented as the conclusion of the discussion on the impossibility of Allah being a cause or a nature. It is a doctrinal clarification that reinforces the Ashʿarī position on divine agency. The commentator al-Shanwānī begins by stating that the result (al-ḥāṣil) is that the agent divides into three categories. The first is the agent by choice (fāʿil bi-l-ikhtiyār): this is the one who acts freely—if he wills, he does, and if he wills, he refrains. The commentator emphasizes that Ahl al-Sunna (the Ashʿarīs and Māturīdīs) affirm only this type of agency for Allah, with the exception of some among the non-Arabs (al-aʿājim, likely referring to certain philosophers or theologians) who claim that Allah acts by compulsion (taʿlīl) in His attributes, meaning that His attributes necessitate action without choice. The second type is the agent by compulsion (fāʿil bi-l-taʿlīl), also called the agent by necessity (fāʿil bi-l-iḍṭirār). This agent acts necessarily without the possibility of refraining, and does not require the absence of obstacles nor the presence of conditions—it is pure deterministic causality, like the example given earlier of the finger moving the ring in the philosophers' view. The third type is the agent by nature (fāʿil bi-l-ṭabʿ), which acts without choice but depends on the presence of conditions and absence of obstacles, like fire burning when there is contact and no wetness. The passage thus clearly distinguishes between these modes of agency and affirms that Allah, being the volitional Creator, belongs only to the first category. This has profound implications for understanding divine causality, free will, and the rejection of philosophical determinism.

The Third Proof: Necessity of the Preponderator
Argument that human independence leads to infinite regress, requiring God as preponderator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third rational proof that human acts require a preponderator, and that the ultimate preponderator must be God to avoid infinite regress.

Core Idea
Human independence leads to infinite regress; only God can be the ultimate preponderator.

In this lesson, we examine the third rational proof from the Sanusi tradition. It argues that if humans were truly independent creators of their acts, their choices would require an endless chain of preponderators. This proof shows why the ultimate cause of all actions must be God.

Guided Path
1
Understand the premise
If humans independently create acts, they must be able to both do and leave the act. Read the passage carefully to see this condition.
This premise sets up the need for a preponderator.
2
Recognize the need for a preponderator
Since doing and leaving are equally possible, a preponderator is required to tip the balance toward doing. Identify this step in the text.
3
Examine the two possibilities
The preponderator could come from the human's own choice or from God. Notice how the author presents these options.
This is a crucial fork in the argument.
4
Identify the infinite regress
If the preponderator comes from human choice, that choice itself is an act needing another preponderator, leading to an endless chain. Trace this logic in the passage.
Infinite regress is the absurd consequence.
5
Conclude with God as the preponderator
The only way to avoid infinite regress is that the preponderator comes from God. This is the desired conclusion.
This establishes God's ultimate causality.

And if the existence of a preponderator is necessary, then this preponderator is either from the servant by his choice, which would entail infinite regress because we would move to the emanation of this preponderator from him, and so on; or the preponderator is from God, which is what is required.

Commentary
This passage presents the third rational proof, which uses the principle that a contingent act needs a preponderator. If humans independently create their acts, they must be able to both do and leave the act (otherwise they wouldn't have full power). Their actually doing it, given that both doing and leaving are equally possible, requires a preponderator to tip the balance toward doing. If that preponderator comes from the human's own choice, then that choice itself is another act requiring a preponderator, leading to an infinite regress. Therefore, the ultimate preponderator must be God. The proof shows that independent human power leads to absurdity; thus, human acts are ultimately determined by God's preponderating will.

The Two Meanings of 'Amal and the Notes on 'Every Created Thing'
Sanusi defines 'amal with two meanings and clarifies ontological status.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two meanings of 'amal — the masdari (conceptual) and the resultant (existential) — and why only the latter is created by Allah.

Core Idea
Action is both a concept and a concrete reality; only the concrete is created.

Sanusi begins with two clarifying notes about Quranic phrases before defining 'amal. He then introduces the crucial distinction: 'amal can refer to the conceptual attachment of power (masdari meaning) or the actual movements and rest (resultant meaning). This distinction is foundational for the Ash'ari theory of kasb and the debate with the Mu'tazila.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening notes
Read the first two notes regarding the interpretation of 'every created thing' and 'ma' in the Quran. Understand the different possibilities.
The notes are by al-Shanwani.
2
Identify the two meanings of 'amal
Find where Sanusi states that 'amal is used homonymously for two meanings. Note the definitions.
The first meaning is conceptual, the second existential.
3
Understand the ontological status
See that the masdari meaning is not created because it is conceptual, while the resultant meaning is created by Allah.
This is a key Ash'ari position.

(1) His saying: 'every created thing' meaning contrary to some who said: what is meant is only the morally responsible servant, because some Quranic proofs do not apply to other than his action. al-Shanwani. (2) His saying: 'and what you do' — it is possible that 'ma' is a masdariyya, and the estimation is 'and Creator of His servant and his action', and this is preferable because there is no deletion on this probability and it is the original basis. Know that 'amal is used homonymously for the masdari meaning, which is the attachment of power to existence, and for the meaning resulting from the masdar, which are movements and states of rest. The first is a conceptual matter, and it is not created by Allah, because power does not attach to conceptual matters. The second is existential, and it is created by Allah, and it is what the morally responsible is obligated with in reality. So the maksub (acquired thing) for the servant is the meaning resulting from the masdar, which is movements and states of rest. As for kasb (acquisition), it is the masdari meaning which is the expression of the attachment of power to movements and rest. For example, if a person strikes another with a sword and cuts his hand: the maksub is the cutting which is the movement of his hand, and the kasb is the attachment of his power to that cutting. As for the effect of the cutting, it is neither maksub nor kasb, but the morally responsible is held responsible for it and required to pay retaliation or blood-money because it arises from his maksub. And it is possible that 'ma' is a relative pronoun, and 'ta'malun' is the relative clause, and the resumptive pronoun is deleted due to the presence of the condition of its being accusative by a verb, and the estimation is 'and Creator of that which you do', and that which is done includes the resultant meaning, which is movements and rest, and also the effect. The first is the point of dispute between us and the Mu'tazila: it is created by Allah in our view, and created by the servant in their view. The conclusion is that the masdari meaning is not an effect and not created by anyone, neither by Allah nor by the servant. The effect is an effect and created by Allah by consensus. The disagreement is only concerning the resultant meaning, and it is called 'ma'mul' (effected). And what was said in the text is also said concerning His saying: 'And Allah created you and what you do' (as-Saffat, 96). al-Shanwani.

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed definition of 'amal (action) and clarifies the Ash'ari doctrine of kasb. It begins with two notes: first, that 'every created thing' in certain verses is not limited to the morally responsible servant; second, that 'ma' in 'wa ma ta'malun' could be a masdariyya (making the phrase mean 'your doing'). Then it explains that 'amal has two meanings: the masdari (verbal noun) meaning, which is the conceptual attachment of power to existence, and the resultant meaning, which is the concrete movements and rests. The former is not created because power does not attach to conceptual matters; the latter is created by Allah. The servant's kasb (acquisition) is the masdari meaning (attachment of his power), while the maksub (acquired thing) is the resultant movement. An example of striking with a sword distinguishes the cutting (maksub), the attachment of power (kasb), and the effect (like amputation) which is neither but imposes legal liability. The passage then addresses a verse, noting that the dispute with the Mu'tazila is over the resultant meaning: Ash'aris say it is created by Allah, Mu'tazila say by the servant. The masdari meaning is not created by anyone, and the effect is created by Allah by consensus.

Kasb and Maksub: The Example of Striking
Sanusi illustrates kasb and maksub with the example of striking.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between kasb (acquisition) and maksub (acquired thing) through the example of a person striking with a sword.

Core Idea
The servant's kasb is the attachment of power; the maksub is the movement that results.

After establishing the two meanings of 'amal, Sanusi applies them to the key Ash'ari concept of kasb. He clarifies that the maksub is the concrete movement (the resultant meaning), while kasb is the conceptual attachment of power (the masdari meaning). The vivid example of a sword strike shows the distinction and also introduces the effect (athar), which is neither kasb nor maksub but imposes legal liability.

Read the example

Map kasb and maksub

This aligns with the earlier definition.

Understand the effect's role

Section of Divine Matters. He began from actions ... the refutation of the claim that anything affects by its nature or by a power in it (1), but rather Allah Almighty, according to the course of custom, creates that effect at the time of it, not through it, like covering at the time of wearing (2), and satiation at the time of drinking, and burning at the time of contacting fire. (1) His saying: 'it affects by its nature or by a power in it' i.e. by ability. The summary is that the sects are four: The first says that things affect by their nature like water and fire – they are disbelievers. The second says: they do not affect by their nature, but by a power Allah deposited in them – they are the Mu'tazila. The third says: they do not affect by their nature nor by a power Allah deposited in them, but by rational necessity – these are the ignorant about the reality of the customary judgment, and perhaps this leads to disbelief, because they make every customary thing rational, and deny the extraordinary, and from that is reviving after death, leading to denial of the resurrection. The speaker is not judged a disbeliever unless he believes this necessary consequence. The fourth sect says: the effecter is Allah Almighty, and the concomitance is customary. Al-Shanwani. (2) His saying: 'like covering at the time of wearing' i.e. the veil obtained when wearing a garment. Al-Shanwani.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation and classification. It begins by stating the refuted claim: that anything in existence affects independently by its own nature or by an inherent power. The author asserts the correct Ash‘ari position: Allah alone creates the effect at the time of the cause, not through the cause, and this is according to regular custom. Examples are given: covering when wearing a garment, satiation when drinking, burning when touching fire. The author then, in the footnote, expands this into a four-fold classification of views on causality. The first group holds that things act by their nature (e.g., water wets, fire burns) – they are disbelievers. The second group (Mu‘tazila) holds that things do not act by nature but by a power Allah has placed in them. The third group denies both nature and inherent power but claims rational necessity between cause and effect; they are ignorant of the nature of custom and risk falling into denial of miracles and resurrection, though not automatically deemed disbelievers unless they accept the necessary consequence. The fourth group (Ahl al-Sunna) holds that the only true agent is Allah, and causal concomitance is merely customary. The passage then glosses the example of covering at the time of wearing as the veil that occurs when wearing a garment.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Ash'aris deny all causality entirely; they believe there is no connection between causes and effects.
Correction: Ash'aris affirm that Allah creates effects in conjunction with causes according to His custom, but they deny that causes have any inherent power to produce effects. The connection is customary, not necessary.

Key Takeaway
The Ash‘ari position denies inherent causal power in creatures and attributes all effect to Allah, with cause-effect relationships being customary conjunctions.
