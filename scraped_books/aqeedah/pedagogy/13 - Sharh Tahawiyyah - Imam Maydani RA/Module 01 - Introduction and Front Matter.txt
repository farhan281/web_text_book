Critical Review of Previous Editions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the importance of textual criticism in preserving classical texts and how errors can propagate through successive editions.

Core Idea
Textual errors, even small ones, can change the meaning of a scholarly work.

Before diving into the content of the commentary, the editor provides a candid assessment of the available printed editions. This critical review is not mere bibliography; it alerts the reader to common pitfalls in manuscript-based scholarship. Understanding these issues will sharpen your awareness when using any classical text.

The book was printed in three editions:

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Maydani's motivation for writing his commentary on the Tahawiyyah creed, his reverence for the creed and its author, and the structure of a classical scholarly introduction.

Core Idea
Humility and hope drive a scholar to serve the creed of Ahl al-Sunnah.

This lesson opens Imam Maydani's commentary on the Tahawiyyah creed. His humility and hope to be among the followers of Abu Hanifa shine through this introduction.

Commentary
This passage is the author's introduction to his commentary on the Tahawiyyah creed. It begins with the basmalah, a Qur'anic verse, and a lengthy praise of Allah (hamd) describing His attributes of decree, uniqueness, transcendence, judgment, and forgiveness. Then follows salawat upon the Prophet Muhammad, his family, and his companions, ending with a phrase about tawhid. After 'amma ba'd (to proceed), the author identifies himself as Abdul Ghani al-Ghunayni al-Maydani, hoping for mercy. He states that the science of tawhid is the foundation of faith and the noblest science, but only when derived from the Qur'an, Sunnah, and consensus of the upright. He praises the Tahawiyyah creed as one of the greatest works, despite its small size, because it covers all fundamentals explicitly or implicitly. It is the creed of Abu Hanifa, the Imam of Imams, who spoke only by revelation. The author then explains that he wrote this commentary because he found no existing commentary despite many requests. He expresses deep humility, saying he is unworthy and incapable, yet he hopes to be among the followers of Abu Hanifa and to be with him in the Hereafter under the banner of the Prophet. The introduction concludes with a prayer for the Prophet.

Commentary
This passage is a taqriz (commendation) written by Sheikh 'Abd al-Rahman al-Kuzbari (d. 1362 AH) in praise of the commentary on al-'Aqida al-Tahawiyya by 'Abd al-Ghani al-Ghanimi al-Maydani. The taqriz begins with the basmala and a lengthy praise of Allah and blessings upon the Prophet, emphasizing that Allah has preserved the Muslims from innovations. The author then states that he has examined this commentary and found it to be unique and a strong fortress for correct beliefs. He praises the commentator, listing his qualities and his lineage, and asserts that the commentary contains no criticism but rather transmits the doctrines of the imams of the religion reliably. He notes that the author has attributed every saying to its source and maintained scholarly trustworthiness. The commendation concludes with a recognition of the commentator's vast knowledge, intelligence, and piety, and expresses hope for his lofty station in the afterlife. Misconception & Correction
Misconception: This introduction is part of the original Tahawiyyah.
Correction: It is actually a preface written by the editor Hasan ibn Ibrahim al-Bitar.

Checkpoint
Why was this commentary on the Tahawiyyah written according to the introduction?
A) Because the Tahawiyyah was obscure and needed clarification
B) Because no previous commentary fully unlocked its symbols and extracted its treasures
C) Because Imam Tahawi requested it
D) Because the science of tawhid was neglected

Key Takeaway
The Tahawiyyah is a foundational treatise on tawhid, and this commentary was written to unlock its meanings and provide proofs, filling a gap in the literature.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
The editor says the science of tawhid is the greatest because 'upon it turns the rulings' and it is 'the means to success in the abode of peace.' In your daily life, do you actually treat your understanding of tawhid as the foundation upon which every action and every intention turns? Or have you separated your knowledge of tawhid from your everyday choices, like a theory you hold but rarely consult? What is one habit you will change this week to make tawhid the actual pivot of your actions?
?
The editor praises al-Maydani for fulfilling the rights of his teachers with complete etiquette. When you seek knowledge, do you approach your teachers with the same reverence and humility, recognizing that their guidance is a light from Allah, or do you take their teachings for granted, assuming you can learn on your own? Write down one concrete way you will show gratitude to a teacher this month.
?
This commentary was written to 'unlock the symbols and extract the treasures' of the Tahawiyyah. Are you content to leave the treasures of tawhid buried in the pages of books you never read, or will you commit to digging into these texts with a commentary that opens them for you? What is the next step you will take to access the knowledge this book offers?
