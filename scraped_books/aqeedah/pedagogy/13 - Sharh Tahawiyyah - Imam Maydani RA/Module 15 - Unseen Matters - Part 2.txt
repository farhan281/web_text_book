Comprehensive Recording by the Noble Scribes
Commentary on the comprehensiveness of the recording angels.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the recording angels record all deeds, including unintentional utterances and sighs, and that this includes disbelievers.

Core Idea
Not even a sigh is missed by the scribes.

This lesson examines the Tahawiyyah's belief in the noble scribes. We see that their recording is comprehensive, covering every action and utterance, intentional or not, and that this applies to all people, believers and disbelievers alike.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on scribes
Read the Arabic and translation carefully to understand the author's description of the angels as 'preservers' who neglect nothing.
Notice the phrase 'لا يهملون من شأنهم شيئاً'.
2
Identify Imam Malik's argument
See how Imam Malik uses the generality of verse 50:18 to conclude that even a groan is recorded.
The verse: 'ما يلفظ من قول إلا لديه رقيب عتيد'.
3
Recognize inclusion of disbelievers
The passage states that the disbeliever is included because his breaths and deeds are recorded for or against him.
This shows the recording is universal.
4
Reflect on implications
Consider how this knowledge affects one's awareness of accountability.

We believe in the angels, the noble scribes, and that Allah has made them preservers of the deeds of the servants, both what is for them and what is against them; meaning they do not neglect anything of their affairs that they have done intentionally, inadvertently, or forgetfully, in health or sickness. Imam Malik said: They write down everything from the servant, even his groaning in his sickness; arguing from the generality of the verse, which is His statement: 'He does not utter a word except that there is a watcher ready' [Surah Qaf verse 18]; and then this includes the disbelieving servant because his breaths and deeds are recorded for him or against him. Al-Nawawi said: The correct view upon which the verifiers agree, nay, some have transmitted consensus on it, is that if a disbeliever does beautiful actions like charity and maintaining family ties, then becomes Muslim and dies upon Islam, the reward for his action is written for him. As for the claim that it contradicts the principles, it is not accepted. I said: And its criterion is as some have said: if it does not depend on intention. And some mentioned that the 'mu'aqqibat' (guardian angels) in His statement: 'For him are angels succeeding before him and behind him, guarding him by the command of Allah' [Surah Ra'd verse 11] are other than the scribes, without dispute. And it is narrated from Uthman that he asked the Prophet, peace be upon him, how many angels are over a human, and he mentioned twenty angels. Al-Mahdawi said: And al-Abi mentioned that it is preserved from Ibn Atiyya that every human is entrusted from the time he becomes a drop in the womb until his death with four hundred angels. Thus mentioned by al-Laqqani. And upon this, in the words of the author there are two issues, and the apparent meaning of the reports is that the recording is real, and the knowledge of the instrument is referred to Allah the Exalted.

Commentary
This passage is a continuous commentary on the Tahawiyyah's statement of belief in the noble scribes (al-kirām al-kātibīn). The author explains that these angels are made 'preservers' (hāfiẓīn) of human deeds, meaning they neglect nothing—whether done intentionally, inadvertently, forgetfully, in health or illness. He then cites Imam Mālik, who argued from the Qur'anic verse (50:18) that even a groan in sickness is recorded, because the verse states that every utterance has a watcher. This generality includes the disbeliever, whose breaths and deeds are recorded for or against him. Next, al-Nawawī is quoted to address a specific ruling: if a disbeliever performs good deeds sincerely prior to Islam and then converts and dies as a Muslim, the reward for those deeds is written for him. The author (al-Maydānī) interjects with 'qultu' and provides a criterion: the deed must not require intention (niyya) to be valid, as good deeds like charity are intrinsically rewarded. He then addresses a separate point: the 'mu'aqqibāt' (guardian angels) mentioned in Surah Ra'd (13:11) are distinct from the recording scribes, as they guard by Allah's command, not record. Finally, a report from 'Uthmān states the Prophet mentioned twenty angels over a human, but al-Mahdawī relates through al-Ābī and Ibn 'Aṭiyya that each human is assigned 400 angels from the time of being a drop in the womb until death. The lesson concludes by noting that the author's words contain two issues, and the apparent meaning of the transmitted reports is that the recording is real (ḥaqīqī), while the exact nature of the instrument ('ilm al-āla) is known to Allah.

The Angel of Death and the Meaning of 'Alamin
Defines Angel of Death and explains 'alamin with rational proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the role of the Angel of Death and the comprehensive meaning of 'alamin in Islamic theology.

Core Idea
'Alamin is everything besides Allah, pointing to His existence.

This lesson begins with an affirmation of faith in the Angel of Death, who is appointed by Allah to take souls at their appointed times. Then the commentator explains the term 'alamin, which is often misunderstood as merely 'worlds'. He defines it as everything other than Allah and provides a rational argument that all contingent beings point to the Necessarily Existent.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice the two parts: first the belief in the Angel of Death, then the definition of 'alamin and its proof.
The passage links faith in the unseen with a rational argument about creation.
2
Understand the Angel of Death's role
Identify who the Angel of Death is and what his specific task is according to the text. He is appointed by Allah to take souls of all the 'worlds' at the end of their terms.
3
Analyze the term 'alamin
The commentator defines 'alamin as a plural of 'alam, meaning a sign or instrument by which one knows. Here it refers to everything that indicates the Maker, i.e., everything besides Allah.
This includes both substances (jawahir) and accidents (a'rad).
4
Reflect on the rational proof
The passage provides a rational argument: all possible things (mumkin) need an effectant that is self-necessarily existent (wajib li-dhatihi). This shows that the existence of creation points to Allah.
This is a classic kalam argument for the existence of God.

Commentary
This passage begins with the heading affirming belief in the Angel of Death, grave punishment, and questioning. The commentator then explains the belief in the Angel of Death (malak al-mawt) who is appointed specifically by Allah to take the souls of all the worlds (al-'alamin) when their terms end. The term 'alamin is then defined: it is the plural of 'alam, which originally means a sign or instrument by which one knows, like a seal. Here it is predominantly used for that by which the Maker (al-Sani') is known. Thus, 'alamin includes everything other than Allah from substances (jawahir) and accidents (a'rad). The commentator gives a rational argument: because these entities are possible (mumkin) and in need of an effectant, and that effectant must be self-necessarily existent (wajib li-dhatihi), they indicate His existence.

Commentary
This passage is a combination of a directive and a legal discussion. First, the author states the key conditions for a supplication to be answered: sincerity of intention (ṣidq al-niyya), purity of inner self (khuṣūṣ al-ṭawiyya), and presence of heart (ḥuḍūr al-qalb). He supports this with a prophetic hadith quoting the Prophet ﷺ: 'Call upon Allah while being certain of the response, and know that Allah does not answer the supplication from a heedless heart.' This establishes that mere verbal invocation is insufficient; the heart must be engaged and certain.

Then the author moves to a scholastic disagreement (ikhtilāf al-mashāyikh): Is it permissible to say that the supplication of disbelievers (duʿāʾ al-kāfirīn) is answered? The majority (al-jumhūr) forbade saying so. Their evidence includes the Quranic verse in Surah al-Ra'd (13:14) that the supplication of disbelievers is only in error (ḍalāl). They reason that a disbeliever does not truly supplicate Allah because he does not know Him; even if he acknowledges His existence, he describes Him with inappropriate attributes, thus negating his acknowledgment. As for the hadith that the supplication of the oppressed, even if he is a disbeliever, is answered—they interpret it as referring to ingratitude for blessings (kufrān al-niʿma), not disbelief in God.

Conversely, some scholars allowed saying that a disbeliever's supplication may be answered. They cite the story of Iblis in Surah al-Hijr (15:36-37), where Iblis asked for respite and Allah granted it, calling that a response (ijāba). Among those who held this view were Abu al-Qasim al-Hakim and Abu Nasr al-Dabusi. The author then notes that al-Sadr al-Shahid stated that the fatwa follows this permissive view, as recorded in Sharh al-Aqa'id. Thus, the passage presents both sides but indicates that the preferred legal opinion is the permissive one.

Commentary
This passage provides the methodological principle for handling reports of disputes among the Companions. The author begins by stating that we remain silent about what happened between them, because those events were the result of their ijtihad (independent reasoning). He then quotes Ibn Daqiq al-'Id's creed: reports about the Companions' conflicts are either false and should be ignored, or authentic but subject to favorable interpretation. The reason is that Allah's prior praise of them (in the Quran) establishes their virtue, and later reports (al-kalam al-lahiq) that seem negative are open to interpretation. The author then cites a hadith from Sahih Muslim prohibiting cursing the Companions and extolling their immense merit: even spending gold like Mount Uhud cannot match the reward of a Companion's moment with the Prophet. A saying of Ibn 'Abbas reinforces this: the Companions' brief company with the Prophet surpasses forty years of worship by later Muslims. In the final sentence, the author makes a strong doctrinal statement: love for the Companions is a part of religion (din), faith (iman), and righteousness (ihsan), while hatred for them is disbelief (kufr), dissension (shiqaq), hypocrisy (nifaq), and transgression (tughyan). This is because the Prophet himself testified to their goodness, so loving them follows from loving him, and hating them follows from hating him.
