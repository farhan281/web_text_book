Commentary
This passage is an editor's introduction describing the methodology and improvements made in preparing this edition of Sharh al-Tahawiyyah. The editor lists several key points: firstly, he added a comprehensive biography of the author at the beginning. Fourthly, he explained obscure terms and concepts from various sciences and heretical sects. He emphasizes that he did not alter the original text arbitrarily, only adding or correcting when necessary for clarity, based on likely scribal or editorial errors. He then humbly requests the reader's indulgence for any shortcomings, acknowledging that this edition is a modest effort rather than a full critical edition. The Baa in the Basmala: Instrument or Dependence?
The commentator refutes the instrumental interpretation of the baa in the basmala.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the grammatical and theological reasoning behind the interpretation of the baa in the basmala, and why the instrumental interpretation is rejected.

Core Idea
The baa in the basmala signifies dependence and connection, not instrumentality.

The basmala is the opening phrase of every chapter of the Qur'an except one, and its grammatical structure has deep theological implications. In this passage, the commentator addresses a common objection that the baa in 'bismillah' should indicate an instrument, like a tool used for an action. By drawing on the concept of seeking help (isti'anah) and the preference for connection (mulabasah), he shows how grammar serves tawhid.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the English translation. Notice the technical terms: الاستعانة, الآلة, الملابسة.
The passage is dense; read slowly.
2
Identify the objection
Find where the objector claims that seeking help (isti'anah) only applies to instruments, like in 'I cut with the adze'.
The objection is introduced by 'بعضهم' (some people).
3
Understand the refutation
The commentator refutes by stating that permission was given to seek help directly through Allah and His names, and that the baa for isti'anah signifies complete dependence on Allah for all actions.
This highlights tawhid af'al (unity of acts).
4
Examine the preference for 'connection'
Al-Kashshaf favors the interpretation of mulabasah (connection) over instrumentality because it brings blessing by connecting the action to Allah's name.
Mulabasah means 'adherence' or 'connection'.
5
Reflect on theological significance
Consider how this grammatical analysis reinforces tawhid: all actions depend on Allah, and the basmala expresses that dependence.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and methodological discussion. It begins by stating the Prophet's approach: he required only tasdiq (affirmation) from the Arabs, without distinguishing between taqlidi (imitative) or burhani (demonstrative) faith. Then it classifies people into three types: 1) al-Jafi al-ghaliz (the coarse, stubborn person) who is rigid upon imitation and falsehood — for him, proof is useless; only force works. 2) al-Shakkun (the doubters) who have some intelligence but cannot grasp demonstrative proofs — they must be treated gently with persuasive (khatabi) speech based on customary premises, because demonstrative proofs would confuse them, like bats cannot see sunlight. 3) al-Qatn (the intelligent but obstinate) who is not convinced by rhetorical speech — he must be confronted with decisive rational proofs. The author then states that the obligation to believe in God and tawhid applies to all. The Prophet used both types of proof: with the polytheists, he used rhetorical proofs based on accepted customary matters, since they could not grasp demonstrative proofs. The Quran contains both: the demonstrative proofs are for the learned, conveyed by ishara (allusion), as al-Razi explained; the rhetorical proofs are for the common people, conveyed by ibara (explicit expression). The verse 'No moist or dry thing...' indicates the Quran contains all knowledge. The specific example of the verse 'If there were in them gods besides Allah, they would have been corrupted' contains both: by expression it gives the rhetorical proof that if multiple gods existed, the heavens and earth would fall into disorder, which is a premise acceptable to common people.

The author uses the verse 'If there had been in them gods besides Allah, they would both have been corrupted' to illustrate a rhetorical proof, showing that its strength lies in customary necessity rather than absolute logical necessity.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of the two types of proofs found in the Quran: demonstrative certain proofs (adilla burhāniyya qaṭ'iyya) and rhetorical proofs (adilla khiṭābiyya). The author then gives an example: the verse 'If there had been in them gods besides Allah, they would both have been corrupted' (21:22) encompasses both proofs. The rhetorical proof, indicated by expression, is the necessary corruption of the heavens and earth due to the departure from the observed order if there were multiple gods. He supports this by quoting al-Razi, who said that Allah treats the possible as actual based on outward appearance. This illustrates that the rhetorical proof is not a certain demonstrative proof but a persuasive argument based on the usual course of events.

The author then clarifies the true certain rational proof indicated by allusion: it is the proof of mutual impediment (burhān al-tamānu') which is agreed upon by the theologians (mutakallimūn). This proof entails that if there were two gods, a single object of power would have to be between them, leading to either both gods being impotent or one of them being impotent — both are rationally impossible. This is the real demonstrative proof, not the rhetorical argument from the verse that merely points out corruption of the observed order. The author stresses that mutual impediment (tamānu') can be either demonstrative (burhānī) or rhetorical (khiṭābī). One should not confuse them. The corruption indicated by allusion is the logical impossibility of two gods acting on the same object, while the corruption indicated by expression is the observed disorder. These are two different things. The author concludes by warning against drawing incorrect implications, such as negating the possibility of agreement based on the rhetorical proof, since that would be a misunderstanding.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and description of the nature of the true religion, Islam. The author begins by stating that the religion of Allah is one, the same for all realms and all creatures (angels, prophets, believers), and that this religion is Islam. He supports this with three Qur'anic verses: 'Indeed, the religion with Allah is Islam', 'Whoever seeks a religion other than Islam, it will never be accepted from him', and 'I have approved for you Islam as a religion'. These verses establish that Islam is the only religion accepted by Allah; any other is rejected. The author then explains that this religion is characterized by being a middle path (mutawassit) between various pairs of extremes. First, it is between ghuluww (extremism, exceeding limits) and taqseer (negligence, falling short). To support this, he cites two narrations: one from Al-Hasan al-Basri via al-Hakim al-Tirmidhi, and another from Bakr al-Muzani, both stating that the religion was placed 'below extremism and above negligence'. The narration adds that the enemy (Satan or misguidance) calls to both negligence and extremism, both leading to Hell. Then the author lists other pairs: between tashbih (anthropomorphism) and ta'til (divestment of attributes), between jabr (compulsion) and qadr (free will), and between amn (feeling secure from Allah's plan) and ya's (despair of Allah's mercy). Thus, Islam's correct path avoids all these extremes, maintaining a balanced, moderate position.
