Maturidi rejects this, saying hearing without sound is impossible. For him, Musa heard a sound that indicated Allah's internal speech, and this is why Musa is called al-Kalim (the one spoken to directly).

Commentary
This passage defines two types of ability (istitāʿa) in Ashʿarī theology and answers an objection. The author begins by distinguishing the ability that necessitates action—like tawfīq (divine facilitation)—which cannot be attributed to the created and occurs with the action. The second type is ability in the sense of health, capacity, capability, and soundness of limbs, which precedes the action and is the basis for divine address (khiṭāb) and obligation (taklīf). He quotes Qurʾān 2:286 as proof that obligation is based on capacity (wusʿ), not on the power that necessitates action. Then he elaborates on the first type: the reality of power (ḥaqīqat al-qudra) is an accident (ʿaraḍ) created by Allah in the living being at the moment of intending an action, after the means are sound. It is either a cause or a condition for the action, with the majority deeming it a condition. If one intends good, Allah creates the power for good; if evil, He creates power for evil. The human is then the one who uses or wastes that power, deserving reward or punishment. This explains why Allah blames disbelievers for not being able to hear: they lacked the created power to do good because they intended evil. Since ability is an accident, it cannot subsist and thus must be simultaneous with the action. The objection from opponents is that obligations are before the action, e.g., a disbeliever is obligated to believe now, or a person must pray after time enters, so ability must exist before the action, otherwise it would be obligating the incapable. The author answers that the ability before the action is not the reality of power but rather the capacity and soundness of means (wusʿ wa salāma). Thus, power (qudra) has two senses: (1) the reality of power, simultaneous with the act, and (2) capacity, which precedes the act and is the basis for obligation. He reaffirms the proof from the Qurʾānic verse. This passage clarifies the Ashʿarī position that actual effective ability is created at the moment of action, while obligation rests on the more general ability of health and means.

Commentary
This passage is a quote from Ibrahim al-Kurani's treatise, arguing for a sophisticated tawhid of actions that simultaneously affirms human acquisition (kasb) as having real effect, but only by God's permission and not independently. He begins by establishing that only God has existence by essence; all else exists through Him and is dependent. Therefore, any perfection, including power, belongs to God alone. The act is through God, and what is through God is in a sense God (i.e., a manifestation of His power). He cites Ibn al-Humann and concludes that there is no need to interpret kasb as mere relation without effect; one can affirm both divine agency and human acquisition as effect by permission.
