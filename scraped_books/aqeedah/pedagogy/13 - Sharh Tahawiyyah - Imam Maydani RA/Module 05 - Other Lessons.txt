Gratitude for Knowledge: A Lesson from al-Shadhili
Al-Shadhili's quote on gratitude for knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Shadhili's perspective on gratitude for divine favor in the context of knowledge acquisition.

Core Idea
True knowledge leads to gratitude, not arrogance.

This lesson presents a brief but profound statement by the great Sufi master al-Shadhili. He reminds us that the purpose of studying others' works is not merely to accumulate information, but to recognize the unique blessing Allah has granted us and to respond with thankfulness. This perspective transforms how we approach learning.

Guided Path
1
Read the quote carefully
Read the Arabic quote and its translation. Notice the word 'gratitude' (الشكر) and the phrase 'to know what Allah has bestowed upon us'.
The quote is from al-Shadhili, a renowned Sufi master.
2
Reflect on the attitude of gratitude
Consider what al-Shadhili means by looking at others' speech to recognize Allah's favor. How does this differ from studying to gain knowledge alone?
Gratitude here is not just verbal but a recognition of divine favor.
3
Consider the biographical context
Read the brief biography of al-Shadhili. Note his background in religious sciences and his turn to Sufism.
This context shows that his statement comes from a place of deep learning and spiritual insight.

The Correct Meaning of the Request for Reassurance
Interprets the story's request and refutes misconceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the correct interpretation of the king's request to see the dead raised, distinguishing it from mistaken interpretations and recognizing it as a yearning for direct witnessing after certainty through inference.

Core Idea
Certainty does not end the soul's yearning for direct observation.

This passage tackles a subtle theological point: the story of Prophet Ibrahim asking to see how Allah brings the dead to life. Some mistakenly think this implies doubt. The author clarifies that Ibrahim already had certainty through inference but yearned for direct witnessing, just as one certain of a beautiful city still longs to see it.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then the literal translation. Get a feel for the author's style and the flow of his argument.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the refuted interpretation
The author begins by rejecting an interpretation that the request was to reassure the heart about Jibril. Why does he say a little contemplation negates it?
Think about the context of the story.
3
Understand the two alternative views
The author mentions two other interpretations: (1) increase of tranquility, (2) acquiring certainty through an intuitive path. He critiques the second as not benefiting the dispute.
Notice how he dismisses these quickly.
4
Grasp the correct summary
The author gives his own summary: certainty from inference leads to a desire to witness the modality. He uses the analogy of Damascus to make this vivid.
The analogy is key to understanding.

الملك ليطمئن قلبه بأنه جبريل، والتأمل اليسير ينفيه، وقيل: زيادة الاطمئنان، ويرجع الكلام في معنى زيادته ويجيء فيه ما تقدم، وقيل: طلب حصول القطع بالإحياء بطريق آخر وهو البديهي - سبب وقوع الإحساس به وهو حسن ولا يفيد في محل النزاع لأحد الفريقين، وحاصله أنه لما قطع بذلك عن موجبه اشتاق إلى مشاهدة كيفية هذا الأمر العجيب الذي جزم بثبوته؛ كمن قطع بوجود دمشق وما فيها من أجنة يانعة وأنهار جارية فنازعته نفسه في رؤيتها والابتهاج بمشاهدتها؛ فإنها لا تسكن ولا تطمئن حتى يحصل مناها وكذا شأنها في كل مطلوب مع العلم بوجوده؛ فليس تلك المنازعة والتطلب ليحصل القطع بوجود دمشق؛ إذ الفرض ثبوته اهـ
The king (wanted) to reassure his heart that it was Jibril, but a little contemplation negates this. And it was said: (it is about) the increase of tranquility. And the discussion returns to the meaning of its increase and what preceded comes into it. And it was said: (it was) a request for obtaining certainty about the revival through another path, which is the intuitive one - a cause for experiencing it, and it is good but does not benefit in the point of dispute for either of the two parties. And the summary is that when he was certain of that from its necessitator, he yearned to witness the modality of this wondrous matter which he was certain of its existence; like one who is certain of the existence of Damascus and what is in it of ripe fruits and flowing rivers, then his soul contends with him to see it and delight in witnessing it; for it does not settle nor rest until it obtains its desire. And similarly is the case in every sought-after thing together with knowledge of its existence. So that contention and seeking is not to obtain certainty of the existence of Damascus, because its existence is assumed. End.

Commentary
This passage is an interpretative commentary on the story of a king (understood as Prophet Ibrahim, peace be upon him) who asked to see the dead raised. The author begins by refuting the interpretation that the king wanted to reassure his heart that the angel was Jibril, arguing that a little contemplation negates this. He then presents two other interpretations. The first is that the request was for an increase in tranquility. The second is that it was a request to obtain certainty about the revival through an intuitive path, which is good but does not benefit the dispute. The author then gives the summary: since the king was already certain through inference (from a necessitator), he yearned to witness the modality itself, just as someone certain of Damascus's existence yearns to see it despite already being certain. The contention is not to obtain certainty but to satisfy the soul's desire for direct witnessing.

Praying Behind Any Muslim from Ahl al-Qibla
Explanation of the ruling on praying behind any Muslim from Ahl al-Qibla.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the ruling on praying behind any Muslim, including the sinful and innovators, and the exceptions when it becomes impermissible.

Core Idea
You may pray behind any Muslim, but dislike applies for sin and innovation unless it reaches disbelief.

The Ahl al-Sunna hold that prayer is valid behind any Muslim who faces the Qibla, regardless of righteousness or sin. This teaching is based on direct prophetic command and the practice of early scholars. However, dislike applies when the person is openly sinful or an innovator, and it becomes impermissible if the person's state reaches disbelief.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once and then the literal translation to understand the main ruling and its conditions.
Pay attention to the terms 'bar', 'fajir', and 'ahl al-qibla'.
2
Identify the evidence
Notice the two pieces of evidence: the hadith 'Pray behind every righteous and sinful person' and the practice of early scholars praying behind sinners and innovators without objection.
3
Understand the dislike and exception
The passage explains that any reported prohibition from the Salaf is interpreted as dislike (karaha), not impermissibility. However, if the sin or innovation reaches the level of disbelief, prayer behind that person is not permissible.
Dislike is a recommended avoidance, not a prohibition.
4
Reflect on the ruling's implications
Consider how this ruling promotes unity while acknowledging degrees of sin. It allows Muslims to pray together while discouraging following sinful leaders.

ونرى الصلاة) جائزة (خلف كل بر) مهتد (وفاجر) معتد حيث كان من أهل القبلة)؛ لقوله : صَلُّوا خَلْفَ كُلِّ بَر وَفَاجِره (١)، ولأن علماء الأمة كانوا يصلون خلف الفسقة وأهل الأهواء والبدعة من غير نكير، وما نقل عن بعض السلف من المنع عن الصلاة خلف المبتدع فمحمول على الكراهية؛ إذ لا كلام في كراهة الصلاة خلف الفاسق والمبتدع، وهذا إذا لم يؤد الفسق أو البدعة إلى حد الكفر وإلا فلا كلام في عدم جواز الصلاة خلفه. كذا في شرح العقائد (۳).
And we hold prayer (to be permissible) behind every righteous (person) and every sinful (person), as long as he is from the people of the Qibla; due to his saying (peace be upon him): 'Pray behind every righteous and sinful person', and because the scholars of the community used to pray behind the sinful and people of desires and innovation without disapproval, and what has been reported from some of the predecessors about forbidding prayer behind an innovator is interpreted as dislike; for there is no doubt about the dislike of prayer behind a sinful and innovator, and this is if the sin or innovation does not reach the level of disbelief; otherwise, there is no question about the impermissibility of praying behind him. Likewise in Sharh al-Aqa'id.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn statement 'ونرى الصلاة خلف كل بر وفاجر من أهل القبلة'. The commentator explains that prayer is permissible behind any Muslim, whether righteous or sinful, as long as he is from the people of the Qibla (i.e., a Muslim who faces the Qibla). He cites the hadith 'Pray behind every righteous and sinful person' as evidence. He also notes that the scholars (ulama) of the ummah used to pray behind sinful people and people of innovation without any objection. The reports from some of the Salaf forbidding prayer behind an innovator are interpreted as a recommendation of dislike (karaha), not a prohibition. The exception is when the sin or innovation reaches the level of disbelief; in that case, it is not permissible to pray behind such a person. He references Sharh al-Aqa'id for this ruling.

?

Have you ever judged someone's act as kufr without proper knowledge? This is a serious matter. Reflect on your own tendency to label others. Are you quick to declare someone a disbeliever? Enumerates the duties of the imam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the specific duties entrusted to the Imam (leader) as outlined in classical Sunni theology.

Core Idea
The Imam's duties ensure social order, worship, and justice.

The text first enumerates the practical responsibilities of the Imam. These are not mere suggestions but essential functions for the community's well-being. Understanding these duties clarifies the scope of leadership in Islam.

This helps see the comprehensive role of the Imam.

Commentary
This passage defines the duties and conditions of the imam (leader of the Muslim community). It begins by enumerating the imam's responsibilities: suppressing those who cause disorder (overpowering factions, thieves, highway robbers), establishing communal worship (Friday and Eid prayers), adjudicating disputes, accepting legal testimonies, arranging marriages for orphans with no guardians, and distributing war spoils. However, he must be a capable administrator, able to execute laws and protect the state, and ensure justice. أقول: وأنا أقول بما قال هؤلاء الأئمة وأعتقد ما يعتقدونه وأؤمن بما يؤمنون به وأشهد بما يشهدون به، وأشهد الله تعالى على ذلك وكفى بالله شهيدا، على ذلك نحيا وعلى ذلك نموت وعلى ذلك نبعث إن شاء الله من الآمنين. وأسألك يا إلهي إذا نزلتُ قبري وخلوت بوزري وأسلمني أهلي في غربتي أن تؤنس وحشتي وتوسع حفرتي وتكتب على ناصية مصيبتي في لوح صحيفتي بقلم عفوك: (الْيَوْمَ يَغْفِرُ اللَّهُ لَكُمْ وَهُوَ أَرْحَمُ الرَّحِمِينَ ) [سورة يوسف آية: 92]، وإذا جمعت رفاني وحشرتني يوم ميقاتي فنشرت صحيفة سيئاتي وحسناتي انظر إلى عملي فما كان حسنا فاصرفه في أمر أوليائك وما كان من قبيح فيل به إلى ساحل عتقانك، ثم إذا أوقف عبدك بين يديك ولم يبق إلا الافتقار إليك واعتماده عليك فقس بين غناك وفقره وبين عزك وذله ثم افعل به ما أنت أهله إنك أهل التقوى وأهل المغفرة، وهذه وسيلتي إليك، تطفلا عليك، وصل وسلم على سيدنا محمد فإنه أقرب من يتوسل به إليك، والمأمول منك القبول. وقد وافق تمام تبييضها في وقت الضحوة النهارية مع تمام بياض دمشقنا المحمية، التي تكفل لها ولأهلها رب البرية من الدولة الجائرة البغية المصرية نهار الأربعاء لست ليال خلت من أول الأشهر المحرمية سنة ست وخمسين ومائتين وألف هجرية بخط جامعها أفقر البرية إلى عفو ربه ذي الذات العلية، عبد الغني.
I say: And I say what these imams said, and I believe what they believe, and I have faith in what they have faith in, and I bear witness to what they bear witness to, and I call Allah Almighty as witness to that, and sufficient is Allah as a witness. Upon that we live, upon that we die, and upon that we will be resurrected, if Allah wills, among the secure. And I ask You, O my God, when I descend into my grave and am alone with my burden and my family abandons me in my alienation, that You comfort my loneliness and widen my grave and write on the forefront of my calamity in the tablet of my scroll with the pen of Your forgiveness: 'Today Allah forgives you, and He is the most merciful of the merciful' [Surah Yusuf, verse 92]. And when You gather my remains and muster me on the day of my appointed time and spread out the scroll of my evil deeds and good deeds, look at my deeds: whatever is good, divert it to the cause of Your saints, and whatever is bad, send it to the shore of Your emancipation. Then when You stop Your servant before You and nothing remains except neediness to You and dependence on You, then measure between Your riches and his poverty, and between Your might and his humility, then do with him what is worthy of You. Indeed, You are worthy of piety and worthy of forgiveness. And this is my means to You, importuning upon You. And send blessings and peace upon our master Muhammad, for he is the nearest by whom one seeks intercession to You, and what is hoped from You is acceptance. And the completion of its fair copy coincided with the time of the forenoon light with the complete whiteness of our protected Damascus, which the Lord of creation has guaranteed for it and its people from the oppressive, tyrannical Egyptian state, on Wednesday, six nights having passed from the beginning of the month of Muharram in the year 1256 Hijri, written by its compiler, the most poor of creation to the pardon of his Lord, the Possessor of the high essence, Abdul Ghani.

Commentary
This passage is a personal appendix by the commentator, Imam al-Maydani, in which he affirms his adherence to the creed of the imams and offers an extended personal supplication. It begins with 'I say: And I say what these imams said...' showing his complete agreement with the preceding creed. He then calls Allah as witness and declares that they live, die, and will be resurrected upon this belief, seeking to be among the secure. The supplication that follows is deeply personal: he asks Allah to be with him in the grave when he is alone with his sins and abandoned by family, asking for comfort and expansion of the grave, and that Allah write forgiveness on his scroll using the verse from Surah Yusuf. He then asks that on the Day of Judgment, after the scrolls are spread, Allah divert his good deeds to the cause of the saints and send his bad deeds to the shore of emancipation, and finally that Allah treat him with what is worthy of Allah's mercy and forgiveness, acknowledging his complete dependence. He sends blessings on the Prophet Muhammad as the best means of intercession. The colophon then states that the fair copy of this commentary was completed in Damascus on Wednesday, six days into Muharram 1256 AH, praising Allah's protection from the oppressive Egyptian state, and signed by the compiler Abdul Ghani (al-Maydani).
