No Rational Obstacle to Servant's Causal Power
Author argues no rational obstacle to servant's causal power and addresses objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why reason does not prohibit attributing real causal power to the servant, and how the author reconciles this with divine omnipotence and revelation.

Core Idea
Reason allows servant's power; revelation alone reserves creation to God.

In this passage, the author builds on his earlier refutation of rational proofs against human agency. He argues that once those proofs are set aside, there is no rational impediment to the servant having genuine causal power. He then addresses a potential objection concerning the uniqueness of divine creation, and finally notes that it is revelation, not reason, that assigns all creation exclusively to God.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the full translation. Let the author's own argument come through.
Notice that the author uses a hypothetical scenario to illustrate his point.
2
Identify the main claim
The author states that after refuting the opponents' rational proofs, no rational barrier remains. This is the core claim.
The claim is: there is no rational obstacle to the servant having real effect.
3
Follow the hypothetical scenario
The author constructs a scenario: God teaches good and evil, creates power, obligates, and promises reward/punishment. Understand why this does not imply divine deficiency.
The analogy with God giving us knowledge is key.
4
Understand the response to the objection
The objection is that creation is exclusive to God, unlike knowledge. The author responds that God is not compelled and acts by choice, and the wisdom is soundness of obligation.
Note that the author acknowledges the distinction but argues it does not invalidate the point.
5
Distinguish reason from revelation
The author concludes that while reason allows the servant's effect, revelation attributes all creation to God. This is the final balance.
This shows the harmony between rational theology and revealed texts.

Commentary
This passage is a rational argument defending the possibility of real causal power in the servant without detracting from divine omnipotence. The author begins by stating that since the opponent's rational proofs have been refuted, there is no rational barrier left. He then presents a hypothetical scenario: God could teach a rational servant good and evil, create in him a power enabling both action and restraint, and then oblige him and promise reward and punishment based on that empowerment. This would not imply any deficiency in divinity because it merely gives the servant power over some of God's objects of power, analogous to God giving us knowledge of some of His objects of knowledge. The author anticipates an objection: knowledge and creation are different (creation is exclusive to God). He responds that this difference does not nullify the point, because God is not compelled or coerced; He acts by choice in a small matter incomparable to His total power. The wisdom behind this is to make obligation sound and command/prohibition meaningful. Nevertheless, God's attribution as Creator is not severed because the servant's act exists only through God's enablement. Finally, he notes that the revealed law (samʿ) does, however, attribute all creation exclusively to God, cutting off attribution to servants. This is an acknowledgment that reason does not forbid the servant's effect, but revelation asserts exclusive creation by God.

Refuting the Accusation of Jabr
Refutation of Ash'ari accusation of jabr and rational foundation for human responsibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the author argues that reason does not preclude attributing voluntary acts to servants and how he refutes the Ash'ari accusation of jabr.

Core Idea
Reason itself does not forbid human responsibility—only revelation does, and that is not compulsion.

The Ash'aris claim that the author's position implies jabr—that humans are compelled under the illusion of choice. The author first refutes this by showing that rational proofs against voluntary acts are invalid. He then sets the stage: there is no rational obstacle to God empowering a servant and holding him accountable.

Guided Path
1
Read the Ash'ari accusation
Start by reading the first line where the Ash'ari claim is stated: that the author's view leads to jabr. Notice how the author immediately frames this as a misunderstanding.
The accusation is that humans are 'compelled in the form of one who chooses'.
2
Follow the rational argument
The author presents a conditional: if God teaches, empowers, and obligates, no deficiency arises. Trace the analogy with divine knowledge to understand how God can share power without imperfection.
Compare with God giving us knowledge—He does not lose His own knowledge.
3
Examine the objection and reply
Note the potential objection that creation (khalq) is different from knowledge. The author dismisses it as not detrimental. Understand why: God is not forced or overcome.
The key is that God acts by choice and the empowerment is a favor.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained refutation of the Ash'ari claim that the author's view leads to jabr (absolute compulsion), and an exposition of how human responsibility is preserved through the concept of 'azm (firm intention). The author begins by stating the Ash'ari accusation: that their position ultimately reduces to jabr, making man a compelled being under the illusion of choice. He then reminds that he has already shown that the Ash'ari rational proofs against attributing voluntary acts to servants are invalid. Hence, there is no rational obstacle to affirming that God can empower a servant and then hold him accountable. Using a simile, he argues that just as God informs us of some of His knowledge without deficiency, so too can He empower us over some of His power-possibles without deficiency. A potential objection—that creation is exclusive to God while knowledge is shared—is raised and dismissed as not detrimental. The author clarifies that God is not forced or overcome in this arrangement; He acts by choice, and the wisdom behind empowerment is the validity of moral obligation (taklīf). Importantly, all acts remain related to God through origination (ījād). Revelation, however, cuts off the attribution of origination from servants. To avoid pure jabr and uphold obligation, a specification (takhṣīṣ) is necessary. This specification does not require attributing all acts to the servant's origination; it suffices to say that the servant's power touches only his firm intention. All prerequisites of action—movement, inclination, call, choice—are created by God and not within the servant's power. The servant's power lies in his determined intention (al-'azm al-muṣammam) that arises after God creates those inner states. When the servant forms this intention, God creates the external act. The act is then attributed to God as a movement and to the servant as a specific human act (e.g., adultery). God creates these inner states to manifest what He already knows—obedience or opposition. The author refutes the notion that God's foreknowledge compels the act; knowledge has no causal influence. Likewise, the creation of these inner processes does not compel the servant, because God has empowered him to intend or refrain based on his inclination. Real-life examples of doing what one dislikes due to fear or modesty show that compulsion is not necessary. Thus, the firm intention, created by God but acquired by the servant's power, is the basis for valid obligation, reward, punishment, praise, and blame. Pure jabr and the invalidity of obligation are thereby negated. All other countless acts and abstentions are exclusively created by God without the mediation of the servant's originated power. The passage ends with an acknowledgment that even this good intention rarely occurs without divine facilitation (tawfīq), but it is still attributed to the servant as his own act.

Specification Through Firm Intention
Explanation of specification (takhsis) through firm intention and validation of obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the concept of specification (takhsis) through the firm intention ('azm) and how it preserves human responsibility without attributing origination of acts to the servant.

Core Idea
Human power is real but limited: it consists of the firm intention to act, while the act itself is created by God.

Having established that reason permits human responsibility, the author now explains the precise mechanism that avoids both pure jabr and the invalidity of obligation. He introduces specification (takhsis): a limited role for human power in the firm intention. All prerequisites of action are created by God, but the servant's intention is his own act, making him morally accountable.

Guided Path
1
Read the wisdom and necessity of specification
Read from 'إذ كان سبحانه غير مُلحاً' to 'وجب التخصيص'. Understand that obligation is sound only because of specification, which is necessary due to revelation attributing all origination to God.
Specification is a middle way between pure jabr and full human origination.
2
Identify the locus of human power
The author states: 'إنما محل قدرته عزمه'. Note that all inner states (inclination, call, choice) are created by God. Only the firm intention after those is the place of human power.
Contrast with the view that humans create their own actions independently.
3
Follow the process of act creation
When the servant forms the intention, God creates the act. The act is attributed to God as a movement and to the servant as a specific moral act (e.g., adultery). See how this avoids jabr and preserves accountability.
God's foreknowledge and creation of inner states do not compel because they are not causal.
4
Reflect on the sufficiency of intention
The author concludes that this one thing—the firm intention—suffices for valid obligation, reward, punishment, blame, and praise. All other acts are directly created by God without human power's mediation.
Even good intention requires divine facilitation (tawfiq), but it is still attributed to the servant.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained refutation of the Ash'ari claim that the author's view leads to jabr (absolute compulsion), and an exposition of how human responsibility is preserved through the concept of 'azm (firm intention). The author begins by stating the Ash'ari accusation: that their position ultimately reduces to jabr, making man a compelled being under the illusion of choice. He then reminds that he has already shown that the Ash'ari rational proofs against attributing voluntary acts to servants are invalid. Hence, there is no rational obstacle to affirming that God can empower a servant and then hold him accountable. Using a simile, he argues that just as God informs us of some of His knowledge without deficiency, so too can He empower us over some of His power-possibles without deficiency. A potential objection—that creation is exclusive to God while knowledge is shared—is raised and dismissed as not detrimental. The author clarifies that God is not forced or overcome in this arrangement; He acts by choice, and the wisdom behind empowerment is the validity of moral obligation (taklīf). Importantly, all acts remain related to God through origination (ījād). Revelation, however, cuts off the attribution of origination from servants. To avoid pure jabr and uphold obligation, a specification (takhṣīṣ) is necessary. This specification does not require attributing all acts to the servant's origination; it suffices to say that the servant's power touches only his firm intention. All prerequisites of action—movement, inclination, call, choice—are created by God and not within the servant's power. The servant's power lies in his determined intention (al-'azm al-muṣammam) that arises after God creates those inner states. When the servant forms this intention, God creates the external act. The act is then attributed to God as a movement and to the servant as a specific human act (e.g., adultery). God creates these inner states to manifest what He already knows—obedience or opposition. The author refutes the notion that God's foreknowledge compels the act; knowledge has no causal influence. Likewise, the creation of these inner processes does not compel the servant, because God has empowered him to intend or refrain based on his inclination. Real-life examples of doing what one dislikes due to fear or modesty show that compulsion is not necessary. Thus, the firm intention, created by God but acquired by the servant's power, is the basis for valid obligation, reward, punishment, praise, and blame. Pure jabr and the invalidity of obligation are thereby negated. All other countless acts and abstentions are exclusively created by God without the mediation of the servant's originated power. The passage ends with an acknowledgment that even this good intention rarely occurs without divine facilitation (tawfīq), but it is still attributed to the servant as his own act.

Commentary

Commentary
This passage is a lengthy quotation from Ibrahim al-Kurani explaining the unity of actions (Tawhid al-Af'al) and the concept of kasb. The author introduces it as an elaboration. Al-Kurani starts with a fundamental tawhid principle: only Allah exists in essence; all else exists only through Him. Therefore, any perfection, including human capacity, is from Allah. He references Ibn al-Humam who argued that there is no need to specify those verses. Types of Impossibility and the Scope of Divine Charge
Maydani distinguishes types of impossibility and states the agreed-upon scope of divine charge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between impossibility in itself (ممتنع لذاته) and impossibility due to external factors (ممتنع بالغير), and how this distinction affects what Allah charges a soul with.

Core Idea
Impossibility in itself is never charged, but what is impossible by divine decree is still within human power.

This lesson begins by unpacking the phrase 'Allah does not charge a soul except what it can bear.' Maydani first clarifies that charging with something impossible in itself, like combining two opposites, never occurs. However, charging with something that becomes impossible only due to divine foreknowledge or will—like the faith of a disbeliever—is a different category, and there is no disagreement that such a charge can occur because, considered in itself, it is within the person's capacity.

Reflect on the human capacity

This passage is a theological explanation of the statement 'And He did not charge them except with what they can bear' from the Tahawiyya creed. Agreed-Upon Scope and the Mu'tazila-Ash'ari Dispute
Maydani presents the Quranic proof for non-charging beyond capacity and the dispute over its permissibility.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Charging beyond capacity never occurs, but whether it is permissible is disputed based on rational ugliness vs. divine sovereignty.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

However, he then provides a solution: this reasoning only holds if the thing is possible in itself without any external impediment. وَالاسْتِطَاعَةُ الَّتِي يَجِبُ بِهَا الفِعْلُ مِنْ نَحْوِ التَّوْفِيقِ الَّذِي لَا يَجُوزُ أَنْ يُوصَفَ المَخْلُوقُ بِهَا تَكُونُ مَعَ الفِعْلِ، وَأَمَّا الاسْتِطَاعَةُ مِنَ الصَّحَّةِ وَالوُسْع وَالتَّمَكُنِ وَسَلَامَةِ الآلاتِ فَهِيَ قَبْلَ الفِعْلِ وَبِهَا يَتَعَلَّقُ الخِطَابُ، وَهُوَ كَمَا قَالَ اللهُ تَعَالَى: ﴿لَا يُكَلِّفُ اللَّهُ نَفْسًا إِلَّا وُسْعَهَا﴾ [سورة البقرة آية: ٢٨٦].
And the ability (istita'ah) by which the action becomes obligatory, such as the divine success (tawfiq) which it is not permissible to attribute to the created being, is concurrent with the action. As for the ability of health, capacity, capability, and soundness of limbs, it is before the action, and it is to this that the command (khitab) is attached, as Allah Almighty said: 'Allah does not charge a soul except [according to] what He has given it.'
Source pages p. 222

Commentary
This passage defines and classifies two types of ability (istita'ah) used in kalam discussions on human action and divine command. The first type is the ability that makes the action obligatory, such as divine success (tawfiq) – a direct grant from Allah that cannot be attributed to the created being as a possessed quality. This ability is simultaneous with the action, meaning it is given exactly when the act occurs. The second type is the ability of health, capacity, capability, and soundness of limbs. This is the natural, physical capacity that exists before the action, and it is to this ability that the divine command (khitab) is addressed, as evidenced by the Quranic verse (2:286) that Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity (wus'). Thus, the command is based on the pre-existing ability, not the supernatural tawfiq.

Actions of Servants: Creation and Acquisition
Statement on creation and acquisition of actions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that human actions are created by Allah and acquired by the servant, and how this relates to divine charge and the meaning of 'la hawla wa la quwwata illa billah'.

Core Idea
Allah creates, humans acquire; no might or power except through Him.

This lesson addresses a key theological question: What is the relationship between divine power and human action? The classical Sunni position balances divine creation of all actions with human responsibility through acquisition (kasb). This passage from Imam Tahawi's creed explains that, and then unpacks the famous phrase 'There is no might nor power except by Allah.'

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the English translation. Note the key terms 'khalaq' (creation) and 'kasb' (acquisition).
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Understand the principle of action
The passage states that actions are created by Allah but also involve acquisition from the servant. Ask yourself: What does 'acquisition' mean here?
Acquisition means the servant voluntarily engages with the act that Allah creates.
3
Learn about divine charge and capacity
Notice that Allah only charges servants with what they can do, and they can only do what He charges. This establishes a perfect match between command and ability.
4
Study the interpretation of 'la hawla...'
The phrase is interpreted as two parts: no device to avoid sin except by Allah's help, and no power to obey except by Allah's success. Reflect on how this shows total reliance on Allah.
This interpretation is a summary of the earlier principle.

وَأَفْعَالُ العِبَادِ هِيَ بِخَلْقِ الله تَعَالَى وَكَسْبٍ مِنَ العِبَادِ، وَلَمْ يُكَلِّفُهُمْ إِلَّا مَا يُطِيقُونَهُ وَلَا يُطِيقُونَ إِلَّا مَا كَلَّفَهُمْ، وَهُوَ حَاصِلُ تَفْسِيرِ قَوْلِ: لَا حَوْلَ وَلَا قُوَّةَ إِلَّا بِاللَّهِ، تَقُولُ: لَا حِيلَةَ وَلَا حَرَكَةَ لِأَحَدٍ عَنْ مَعْصِيَةِ اللهِ إِلَّا بِمَعُونَةِ اللهِ، وَلَا قُوَّةَ لِأَحَدٍ عَلَى إِقَامَةِ طَاعَةٍ وَالثَّبَاتِ عَلَيْهَا إِلَّا بِتَوْفِيقِ الله.
And the actions of servants are by the creation of Allah and acquisition (kasb) from the servant. And He does not charge them except with what they are capable of, and they are not capable except of what He charged them. And that is the summary of the interpretation of the saying: 'There is no might nor power except by Allah.' You say: There is no device and no movement for anyone from disobeying Allah except by Allah's help, and no power for anyone to perform obedience and remain steadfast upon it except by Allah's success (tawfiq).
Source pages p. 222

Commentary
This passage addresses the theological issue of human action. It affirms that actions of servants are created by Allah but also involve acquisition (kasb) from the servant, a term from Ash'ari kalam meaning the human's voluntary engagement with the created act. The servant's capacity matches the divine command: Allah does not command beyond what they can do, and they cannot do beyond what He commands. The interpretation of 'There is no might nor power except by Allah' is then given: there is no device (hila) or movement (haraka) to avoid sin except by Allah's help, and no power to perform obedience and remain steadfast except by Allah's tawfiq. This shows total reliance on Allah.
