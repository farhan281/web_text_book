Mutawatir News: Definition and Necessary Knowledge
Definition of mutawatir and proof of its necessary knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of mutawatir news and the rational basis for its generation of necessary knowledge without reasoning.

Core Idea
Mutawatir news yields direct, undeniable knowledge without reasoning.

Sanusi now turns to a foundational category of transmitted knowledge: mutawatir news. He defines it precisely and shows why it produces certain knowledge without needing deduction. This passage establishes the epistemic bedrock for accepting reports about the unseen.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition carefully
Read the Arabic definition of mutawatir news. Note the condition that collusion on falsehood is rationally impossible.
The term 'tawāṭu’' (collusion) is key to the definition.
2
Examine the proof
Identify the proof that mutawatir gives knowledge: we find ourselves knowing about Mecca and Baghdad through reports alone.
This is an appeal to immediate experience.
3
Identify the two matters
Sanusi explicitly enumerates two points about mutawatir. Find them and see how they reinforce each other.
The first matter is about causation, the second about the nature of the knowledge.
4
Reflect on necessary knowledge
Consider why the knowledge is called necessary: it is obtained even by children who do not reason.
This distinguishes it from acquired (kasbī) knowledge.

Commentary
This passage defines mutawatir news and explains its epistemic status. It first defines mutawatir as news transmitted by a group whose collusion on falsehood is rationally impossible, and its result is certain knowledge (al-‘ilm bi lā shubhah). It then states that mutawatir produces necessary (darūrī) knowledge, giving examples: knowledge of past kings and distant cities like Mecca and Baghdad. This knowledge arises by necessity from the reports themselves, not through reasoning. The passage further clarifies two points: first, that mutawatir causes knowledge necessarily, as seen in the immediate knowledge of the existence of these cities; second, that the resulting knowledge is necessary because it is obtained even by those who do not engage in reasoning, such as children. Thus, it is not a product of deduction but a direct cognition.

Objection to Mutawatir Based on Probability and Its Answer
Objection and answer on mutawatir probability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection that probability cannot yield certainty is answered using the rope analogy.

Core Idea
Adding probabilities can yield certainty when they cohere, like strands of a rope.

This passage presents a key objection against the reliability of mutawatir reports: that individual reports only give probability, and stacking probabilities cannot produce certainty. The answer uses a powerful analogy to show that combination creates something new. Let's follow the argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the translation to grasp the overall argument.
Notice the specific examples of Christians and Jews.
2
Identify the objection and answer
The passage is clearly divided into an objection (starting with 'فإن قيل') and an answer ('قلنا'). Pinpoint where each begins.
3
Analyze the two sub-arguments of the objection
The objection has two parts: (1) probability piling doesn't give certainty, (2) possibility of lying in each implies possibility in the whole. Understand each.
The first is about certainty, the second about collective falsehood.
4
Examine the analogical answer
The answer uses the analogy of a rope made of weak hairs. Compare the structure: many weak strands combine to produce strength. Relate this to reports: many probable reports combine to produce certainty.
This is the key: combination produces a new quality.
5
Reflect on the significance
Ask yourself: what does this analogy imply about the nature of tawatur? Why is it a valid refutation?
The answer does not deny the premises; it shows a flaw in the reasoning.

The answer responds by analogy: just as a rope made of many thin hairs gains strength from combination, so multiple reports can produce certainty even if each is merely probable. The collective recurrence transcends the individual limitations.

Objection to Uniformity of Necessary Knowledge and Its Answer
Objection that necessary knowledge is uniform and its answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that necessary knowledge can vary in strength due to factors like habit and practice, and that denial of mutawatir knowledge is due to obstinacy.

Core Idea
Necessary knowledge is not uniform; it varies by human factors like habit and attention.

This passage addresses an objection that necessary knowledge must be uniform and admits no gradation. The objector points to varying certainty in different truths and to sects that reject mutawatir. The answer refutes the premise and explains that variation arises from familiarity, practice, and mental focus, while outright denial is mere obstinacy.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objector argues that necessary knowledge should be uniform without gradation, yet our experience shows variation. For example, knowledge that one is half of two is stronger than knowledge of Alexander's existence. Additionally, groups like the Sumaniyya and Brahmins deny that mutawatir gives knowledge.
This challenges the reliability of mutawatir reports.
2
Analyze the answer
The author denies the premise that necessary knowledge is uniform. He explains that types of necessary knowledge can vary due to differences in familiarity, habit, practice, bringing to mind, and mental representation of the boundaries of judgments.
3
Consider the rebuttal of denial
The author attributes the denial of mutawatir by some rational people to obstinacy and stubbornness, akin to the Sophists who reject all necessary truths. Thus, such denial does not undermine the necessity of mutawatir knowledge.
This shows that the objector's evidence is flawed.

Thus, the variation does not undermine the necessity of knowledge from mutawatir.

The Scope of Reports That Yield Knowledge
The author responds to an objection about the types of reports that yield knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the author's response to an objection about the types of reports that yield knowledge, and how reports from God, angels, or consensus relate to the two main categories.

Core Idea
Not every certain report is a source of necessary knowledge by itself.

The discussion on knowledge-producing reports continues with an objection: why limit knowledge-yielding reports to just two types? The objector lists several other candidates. The author clarifies what is meant by 'report' in this context, showing that reports from God, angels, or consensus ultimately fall under the same categories or require additional proofs.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
The objector claims that true reports yielding knowledge are not only the two types already discussed; they could include reports from God, angels, consensus, or reports with contextual clues.
Notice the examples given: report of God, angel, ahl al-ijma', and a report about Zayd's arrival.
2
Understand the author's main answer
The author replies that 'report' here means a report that yields knowledge for all people simply by being a report, without considering external clues or proofs.
This definition excludes reports that rely on circumstantial evidence.
3
Examine the handling of divine and angelic reports
Reports from God or angels only benefit people when they reach them through the Prophet, so they take the ruling of prophetic reports.
Thus they are not an independent category.
4
See how consensus reports are treated
The report of ahl al-ijma' is considered like mutawatir in ruling, though some argue it only yields knowledge after considering proofs that consensus is a proof.
The author points out that even prophetic reports are inferential in this sense.

Meaning of Hadith and the Philosophers' Interpretation
Defines hadith as preceded by non-existence and contrasts philosophers' view

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise definition of 'muhdath' in Sunni theology and how it differs from the philosophers' usage of 'huduth'.

Core Idea
Philosophers and theologians use the same word 'huduth' with different meanings.

This passage introduces the foundational term 'muhdath' (temporally originated) as defined by Sunni theologians. It then contrasts this with the philosophers' claim that the heavens and elements are pre-eternal, and explains that when philosophers say everything other than Allah is 'hadith', they mean something else entirely. Understanding this distinction is crucial to avoid confusion in later theological discussions.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of muhdath
The passage begins by defining 'muhdath' as that which is brought from non-existence to existence. Note the phrase 'مخرج من العدم إلى الوجود'.
This is the standard Ash'ari definition.
2
Examine the philosophers' view
The philosophers hold that the heavens and elements are pre-eternal (qadim) in their matter, form, and shape as species. They never lack a form at any time.
This contradicts the Sunni view that everything other than Allah is created in time.
3
Grasp the equivocation on huduth
Notice that philosophers still use the term 'huduth' for everything other than Allah, but they mean 'need for another' (ihtiyaj ila al-ghayr), not 'being preceded by non-existence'. This is a key difference.
Be careful not to assume agreement when the same term is used.

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'muhdath' (temporally originated) as that which is brought from non-existence into existence, i.e., it was non-existent and then came to be. This distinction is crucial because it shows that the philosophers do not affirm that the world is created in time ex nihilo; they merely acknowledge ontological dependency. The passage thus guards against equivocation on the term 'hadith'.

The Objection of Musa's Request and Its Refutation
Author refutes the objection that the story of Musa negates the possibility of ru'ya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author defends the possibility of ru'ya by answering the strongest objection based on the story of Moses.

Core Idea
Conditional possibility does not imply impossibility.

This passage addresses the most powerful objection against the possibility of seeing Allah—the story of Moses asking to see Him. The author carefully unpacks the objection and shows why it fails to prove impossibility. By analyzing his response, you will see how careful reasoning about conditional statements and context resolves apparent scriptural conflicts.

Guided Path

The vision (ru'ya) is conditioned upon the mountain's stability. The author asserts this is possible because the condition itself is possible.
This is the foundation of the argument.
2
Present the objection
The strongest objection is that Moses asked for ru'ya to teach his people, implying impossibility, and that the condition is impossible (mountain stability while moving).
Two parts: purpose of request and impossibility of condition.

The objections fail to negate the possibility of ru'ya, so the original argument stands.
The author's reasoning is consistent with scripture and logic.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer argument defending the possibility of seeing Allah in the afterlife. The author first states that the vision of Allah is made conditional upon the mountain's stability in the Quranic verse (Quran 7:143). He argues that this conditional is possible because the condition (stability of the mountain) is not inherently impossible. The dependent (the vision) being conditional upon a possible thing makes it also possible, because the meaning of a conditional statement is simply that the dependent occurs when the condition occurs, and impossibility does not arise from such a conditional. Then he presents the strongest objection: Moses' request was actually for his people who demanded to see Allah plainly (Quran 2:55); Moses already knew it was impossible, so he asked to teach them its impossibility. Additionally, the objector denies that the condition (the mountain's stability) is possible at the moment of its being made to appear? Actually the objection says: we do not concede that the condition (al-mu'allaq 'alayh) is possible; rather, it is the mountain's stability while it is moving, and that is impossible (because the mountain was made to quake). The author answers that both of these objections depart from the apparent meaning of the text, and there is no necessity to adopt such departures. Regarding the first, if the people were believers, Moses' word would suffice; if disbelievers, they wouldn't believe him, so the request would be futile in either case. Regarding the second, stability while moving is not necessarily impossible; it could be that stillness replaces motion, and only the simultaneous existence of motion and stillness is impossible. Thus the objection fails.

Author denies conditions for Allah's vision and refutes rational objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the conditions required for physical vision do not apply to Allah, and how the rational objection based on necessity is answered in Sunni theology.

Core Idea
Allah is seen without place, direction, or distance; vision is by His creation, not by conditions.

Sanusi has affirmed that believers will see Allah in the Hereafter. Now he addresses the objections that arise from applying the conditions of physical sight to Allah. The first set of objections is rational: they assume that vision requires certain conditions like place, direction, and distance. The author denies these conditions and refutes the analogy used to support them. Then he answers the objection that if vision were possible, it would be necessary when conditions are met.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of both rational and scriptural objections against the vision of Allah (ru'ya). The author first states the correct doctrine: Allah is seen (in the Hereafter) not within a place, not in a direction, not by facing, not by connection of a ray of light, and not by a distance between the seer and Allah. The rationalist objection that vision requires these conditions is invalid because it is based on analogizing the unseen (gha'ib) to the witnessed (shahid), which is a false analogy. Then he addresses a rational argument: some argue that if ru'ya were possible, then when all conditions (sound sense, etc.) are present, vision would be necessary; otherwise it would be possible for mountains to exist before us unseen, which is absurd. The author replies that we do not concede that necessity: vision in our doctrine is by Allah's direct creation (khalq), so it does not become obligatory simply by the presence of conditions. Finally, he addresses the scriptural objection from Quran 6:103, 'Vision comprehends Him not.' The author answers by making two concessions: first, that the word 'absar' (vision) is for generality (istighraq), giving universal negation (no vision comprehends Him) rather than negation of universality (it does not mean that He is never seen). Second, that 'idrak' (comprehension) here means vision in an absolute sense, not vision that encompasses all aspects of the object. Even with these concessions, the verse does not indicate that vision is impossible at all times and in all states (i.e., it could be possible in the Hereafter). Thus the objection fails.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni position that Allah may forgive any sin (except shirk) for whomever He wills, with or without repentance, and how the two Mu'tazila objections are refuted.

Core Idea
Allah's mercy is vast; He may forgive any sin without repentance as He wills.

This passage addresses a critical difference between Sunni and Mu'tazila theology: the scope of divine forgiveness. The author states that Allah may forgive any sin (except shirk) for whomever He wills, even without repentance. The Mu'tazila restricted forgiveness, arguing that threats must be fulfilled and that leniency encourages sin. The passage defends the Sunni view by refuting these arguments.

Commentary
This passage addresses the scope of Allah's forgiveness for sins other than shirk. The author states the Sunni position: Allah forgives minor and major sins for whomever He wills, whether the sinner repents or not. This is contrary to the Mu'tazila, who restrict forgiveness to minor sins automatically and to major sins only if accompanied by repentance. The Mu'tazila support their view with two arguments. First, they cite verses and hadiths that threaten punishment for disobedient people; they claim that these threats must be carried out, otherwise they would be false. The author responds that even if these texts are general, they indicate that punishment will occur (in some cases), not that it is obligatory in every instance; moreover, there are many texts promising forgiveness, so the forgiven sinner is excluded from the general threats. The author also refutes the claim that breaking a threat is an act of generosity; this would imply that Allah changes His word, which contradicts the Quranic verse 'My word is not changed.' The second Mu'tazila argument is that if sinners know they might be forgiven without repentance, they would be encouraged to sin and others would follow, undermining the purpose of sending prophets. The author answers that the mere possibility of forgiveness does not lead to such certainty; rather, the terrifying nature of the threats makes people fear punishment, which acts as a sufficient deterrent. Finally, the author adds that punishment for minor sins is permissible even if the perpetrator avoids major sins – a statement affirming divine justice and sovereignty.

Second Mu'tazila Objection and Its Refutation
Sanusi presents the second Mu'tazila argument and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second Mu'tazila objection that forgiveness encourages sin and why the mere possibility of pardon does not undermine deterrent threats.

Core Idea
Forgiveness possibilities do not cancel the deterrent power of threatening texts.

The Mu'tazila raised a second objection: if God can forgive without punishment, sinners will be emboldened. This seems to contradict the wisdom of sending prophets. Sanusi responds by showing that the possibility of forgiveness does not lead to certainty of impunity, and the Qur'anic threats remain preponderant. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Commentary
This passage presents the second Mu'tazila argument against forgiveness without punishment. The argument states that if sinners know they will not be punished, they will be emboldened to sin, and others will be encouraged, which contradicts the purpose of sending prophets. The response clarifies that the mere possibility of pardon does not lead to a definite belief that punishment will not occur. On the contrary, the many threatening texts in revelation, which include severe intimidation, make punishment more likely for each individual, and this itself serves as a sufficient deterrent. Thus, even though forgiveness is possible, the overwhelming evidence of threat still prevents people from taking sin lightly. The author uses the word 'kayfa' (how so?) to emphasize the logical flaw in the Mu'tazila claim.
