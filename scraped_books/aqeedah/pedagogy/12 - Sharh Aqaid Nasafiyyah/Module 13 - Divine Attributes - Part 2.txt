Core Idea

This lesson presents a foundational doctrine of Sunni Islam: the Quran is not created but is the uncreated speech of Allah. Sanusi explains the precise wording used by the orthodox scholars to safeguard against misunderstandings, especially the error of the Hanabila who considered the letters and sounds of the Quran eternal. Proof for Eternal Inner Speech (Kalam Nafsi)
Sanusi presents the proof for eternal inner speech and refutes opposing views.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the proof that Allah's speech is an eternal attribute (kalam nafsi) distinct from created verbal forms.

Core Idea
Allah's speech is an eternal inner attribute, not created letters or sounds.

Sanusi now turns to a critical dispute: is Allah's speech eternal or created? He establishes that the real issue is affirming or denying inner speech (kalam nafsi). He then presents a concise proof based on consensus and the impossibility of created accidents in Allah, and answers two opposing views.

Guided Path
1
Identify the real point of disagreement
Read how Sanusi frames the dispute: it is about affirming or denying inner speech, not about the temporality of letters and sounds.
Both sides agree that the Arabic Quran is created; the disagreement is on the attribute behind it.
2
Follow the core proof
The proof: Allah is called 'speaking' by consensus and revelation; this must mean He has an attribute of speech; created speech cannot subsist in Him; therefore, the speech must be eternal inner speech.
This is a deductive argument based on what can or cannot be attributed to Allah.
3
Analyze the response to the Hanabila argument
The opponents point to the Quran's created features (composition, revelation, Arabic). Sanusi says this argument only refutes the Hanabila, not him, because he agrees the composition is created.
This shows the importance of precision: the discussion is about the meaning, not the form.
4
Understand the refutation of the Mu'tazila view
The Mu'tazila say Allah is 'speaking' by creating sounds. Sanusi refutes by analogy: the one who moves is the one in whom motion resides, not the one who creates it. Likewise, the speaker is the one in whom speech resides, not the one who creates it.
This clarifies that 'speaker' refers to a subject of the attribute, not an agent of creation.

The author then presents four absurd consequences of identifying takwin with mukawwan: (1) the mukawwan would be a creator of itself, hence eternal and independent of Allah; (2) the Creator would have no effective attachment to the world, only priority and power, which does not make Him a creator; (3) Allah would not be the bringer into existence of things, since the meaning of 'bringer into existence' requires that takwin subsist in Him, but if takwin is identical to the effect, it cannot subsist in Him; (4) it would be correct to say that the creator of blackness is black and that the stone is the creator of blackness, because both would share the same attribute. All these absurdities show that the judgment that act and effect are different is necessary.

Commentary
This passage proves that Allah is not obligated to do what is most beneficial (al-aslah) for His servants, a doctrine held by the Mu'tazila. The author begins with the thesis: it is not obligatory upon Allah to do what is more beneficial for the servant. He then argues by reductio ad absurdum: if it were obligatory, then many impossible consequences would follow. First, Allah would not have created the disbelieving poor person who suffers in both worlds, because that would contradict His doing the most beneficial. Second, Allah would have no grace upon servants, because His acts would be mere payment of a debt, not generosity. Third, the grace upon the Prophet would not be greater than upon Abu Jahl, if each received maximum benefit. Fourth, asking for divine protection and relief would be meaningless, because whatever Allah does not do for each person would be harmful and must be avoided. Fifth, nothing would remain in Allah's power concerning the interests of servants, since He would have already done all that is obligatory. The author then declares that the consequences of this Mu'tazilite principle are manifestly absurd. He diagnoses the cause: their deficient understanding of divine matters and their habit of analogizing Allah to creatures. The Mu'tazila cling to the idea that leaving the more beneficial would be stingy and foolish. The author answers that Allah has the right to withhold what He wills; His generosity, wisdom, and knowledge of outcomes are proven by decisive proofs, and His actions are pure justice and wisdom. Finally, he questions whether 'obligation' can even be conceived regarding Allah, since there is no authority above Him to impose it.

The Absurd Consequences of Obligating the Best for the Servant
Taftazani enumerates five absurd consequences of the Mu'tazilite principle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Mu'tazilite principle that God must do what is best for every servant leads to absurd conclusions that contradict clear theological truths.

Core Idea
If God were obligated to give the best, no one would be disbeliever, poor, or tormented.

Taftazani begins his refutation by taking the Mu'tazilite principle at face value and showing where it leads. If God is truly obligated to do what is best for each servant, then many undeniable realities—like the existence of disbelievers, the need for supplication, and the distinction between Prophet and Abu Jahl—become impossible. This lesson walks through each consequence to see why the principle collapses under its own weight.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get the overall argument. Notice the repeated 'otherwise' clauses that introduce each consequence.
The passage uses conditional reasoning: if X (obligation), then Y (absurd result).

Identify each of the five consequences that would follow if God were obligated to give the best. Write them down in your own words.
The consequences are: no disbelievers/poor, no favor from God, Prophet and Abu Jahl equal, supplication meaningless, God's power exhausted.
3
Reflect on why each is impossible
For each consequence, ask yourself: Is this actually true? Why can't it be? This reveals the principle's falsity.
For example, disbelievers exist, so the principle cannot hold.
