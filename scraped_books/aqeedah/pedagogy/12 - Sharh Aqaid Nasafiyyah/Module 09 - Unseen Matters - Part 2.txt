Punishment for Minor Sins and the Mu'tazila View
Sanusi discusses punishment for minor sins and refutes the Mu'tazila who exempt those avoiding major sins.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni position on punishment for minor sins, the Mu'tazila exception for those avoiding major sins, and the decisive response that 'absolute major sin' refers specifically to kufr.

Core Idea
Punishment for minor sins is permissible; the promise of expiation is tied to avoiding kufr, not all major sins.

In this passage, Sanusi presents the Sunni theological position that Allah may punish for minor sins regardless of whether one avoids major sins. He then addresses the Mu'tazila claim that avoiding major sins makes punishment for minor sins impossible, citing textual evidence. The response clarifies that the 'absolute major sin' is kufr, thus the promise of expiation is specific to avoiding disbelief, not other sins.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Sunni position on minor sins
Read the first part of the passage where Sanusi states that punishment for a minor sin is permissible whether or not one avoids major sins, and note the supporting verses.
Notice the use of سَوَاء (whether) indicating no condition.
2
Identify the Mu'tazila objection
Read the Mu'tazila view: if one avoids major sins, punishment for minor sins is not permissible. Distinguish between rational impossibility and textual impossibility as they frame it.
They say it is not rationally impossible but does not occur due to textual evidence.
3
Analyze the Sunni response
Read the response that 'absolute major sin' (الكبيرة المطلقة) is kufr, and understand the linguistic principle about collective vs. distributive meaning.
The example 'ركب القوم دوابهم' illustrates that each person rode his own mount.

Commentary
This passage presents the ruling on punishment for minor sins and the Mu'tazila exception. The author states that it is permissible for Allah to punish for a minor sin regardless of whether the sinner avoids major sins, citing two verses: one shows forgiveness is for whom He wills, implying some sins may not be forgiven, and the other indicates that all sins are enumerated for recompense. Then a group of Mu'tazila argues that if one avoids major sins, punishment for minor sins cannot occur, not because it is rationally impossible, but because textual evidence shows it does not happen; they cite the verse that promises expiation of misdeeds for those who avoid major sins. The response is that the 'absolute major sin' is disbelief (kufr), because it is the peak of sin. The explanation uses the linguistic principle that when a collective is addressed, the rule may apply to each individual distributively—for example, 'the people rode their mounts' means each person rode his own mount. Thus the verse about avoiding major sins applies to kufr, not to all major sins, so avoiding other major sins does not guarantee expiation. The Arabic example at the end is partially garbled in the OCR.

Affirming Intercession and Its Proofs
Taftazani affirms intercession and provides proofs from Quran and hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni doctrine of intercession for major sinners and the scriptural proofs that support it, including the clarification of a key logical nuance.

Core Idea
Intercession for major sinners is proven by widespread reports and Quranic context.

Taftazani begins by stating the established position: intercession is granted by prophets and righteous people on behalf of major sinners. This builds on the earlier principle that forgiveness is possible even without intercession. He then presents Quranic and hadith evidence, carefully addressing a potential misunderstanding about logical implication.

Guided Path
1
Read the affirmation
Read the first sentence noting that intercession is established for major sinners against the Mu'tazila, based on widespread reports.
The author is building on a prior principle: forgiveness is permissible without intercession, so with intercession it is even more fitting.
2
Examine the Quranic proof
Look at the two Quranic verses cited: one commanding the Prophet to seek forgiveness for believers, and the other denying intercession's benefit for disbelievers. Understand how the denial implies benefit for believers.
The author argues that denying intercession for disbelievers would be pointless if intercession did not benefit believers; context proves the point.
3
Clarify the logical nuance
Note the author's clarification: this argument does not rely on the concept of opposite implication (mafhūm al-mukhālafa). It is based on contextual appropriateness, not a universal logical rule.
This avoids a common objection from those who reject opposite implication.
4
Confirm hadith evidence
Read the prophetic hadith 'My intercession is for those who commit major sins from my community' and note that such hadiths are mutawātir in meaning.
This provides strong, multi-chain evidence.

And intercession is established for the messengers and the righteous on behalf of those who commit major sins, according to widespread reports (mustafīḍ min al-akhbār), contrary to the Mu'tazila. This is based on what preceded regarding the permissibility of pardon and forgiveness without intercession, so by intercession it is even more so. According to them: since it is not permissible, it does not occur. Our proof: the saying of the Most High: 'And ask forgiveness for your sin and for the believing men and believing women' [Muhammad 19], and His saying: 'So the intercession of intercessors will not benefit them' [al-Muddaththir 48]. For the style of this speech indicates the establishment of intercession in general. Otherwise, there would be no meaning to denying its benefit for the disbelievers when the intent is to make their condition abhorrent and to realize their misery, because such a context requires that they be characterized by what is specific to them, not by what is common to them and others. And it is not intended that the attachment of the judgment to the disbeliever indicates its negation from others, such that it might be objected that it only constitutes an argument for those who hold the concept of opposite implication (mafhūm al-mukhālafa). And his saying, peace be upon him: 'My intercession is for those who commit major sins from my community' – and it is well-known; rather, the hadiths on the topic of intercession are mutawātir in meaning. The Mu'tazila argued with verses such as His saying: 'And fear a Day when no soul will avail another at all, nor will intercession be accepted from it' [al-Baqara 48], and His saying: 'For the wrongdoers there is no friend and no intercessor to be obeyed' [Ghafir 18]. The answer: after conceding their generality over persons, times, and circumstances – it is necessary to specify them to the disbelievers in order to reconcile the evidences. Since the principle of pardon and intercession is established by definitive proofs from the Book, the Sunna, and consensus, the Mu'tazila said: pardon for minor sins absolutely, and for major sins after repentance, and intercession for increase in reward. Both are corrupt. As for the first: because the repentant person and the committer of a minor sin who avoids major sins – according to them they do not deserve punishment, so pardon has no meaning. As for the second: because the texts indicating intercession are in the sense of seeking pardon for the offense.

Refuting Mu'tazila Objections and Corrupt Views
Taftazani refutes Mu'tazila objections and their two views on pardon and intercession.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila objections to intercession and Taftazani's response, including the necessity of specifying general verses, and why their two views on pardon and intercession are corrupt.

Core Idea
General verses denying intercession are specified to disbelievers; Mu'tazila views on pardon contradict their own principles.

The Mu'tazila attempted to reject intercession by citing verses that seem to deny it universally. Taftazani responds by specifying those verses to disbelievers in order to harmonize all evidence. He then refutes two additional Mu'tazila positions: that pardon applies only to minor sins or after repentance, and that intercession is only for increasing reward. Both are shown to be inconsistent with the texts and the Mu'tazila's own reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazila objections
Read the verses the Mu'tazila use to argue against intercession: al-Baqara 48 and Ghafir 18.
These verses seem to deny interception absolutely.
2
Understand the response
Taftazani concedes the verses are general but argues they must be specified to disbelievers to reconcile with the definitive proofs for intercession.
This is a standard principle of usul: general texts can be specified by other evidence.
3
Examine the two corrupt views
Read the Mu'tazila two views: (1) pardon only for minor sins or major after repentance, (2) intercession only for increase of reward. Understand why each is corrupt.
4
Refute the first view
The author points out that according to Mu'tazila, the repentant person and one who avoids major sins do not deserve punishment, so there is no need for pardon.
Thus their concept of pardon becomes meaningless.
5
Refute the second view
The author states that the texts about intercession refer to seeking pardon for offenses, not just increasing reward.
This aligns with the Sunni understanding.

Believers Who Commit Major Sins Will Not Remain in Hell Forever
Taftazani proves that sinful believers are not eternally in Hell and refutes Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Harut and Marut were sinless angels who taught magic as a divine trial, and sin lies in practicing magic.

The story of Harut and Marut often raises questions: Did these angels commit sins? Why were they punished? The author clarifies that the most correct view is that they are indeed angels, and they did not commit disbelief or major sin. This lesson explains the distinction between teaching magic and believing in it.
