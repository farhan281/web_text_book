Definition of the World and Its Created Nature
Defines 'alam as everything other than Allah and states it is created in all parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise theological definition of 'the world' (al-alam) as everything other than Allah, and why its status as created (muhdath) in all parts is foundational for proving the existence of the Creator.

Core Idea
The world is the sum of all created existents, excluding Allah and His attributes.

This passage from the 'Aqaid al-Nasafiyyah establishes the essential definition of the world (al-alam) in Islamic theology. Before proving that the world is created and requires a Creator, the author first clarifies what 'world' means. The definition includes all existents other than Allah, whether bodies or accidents, plants or animals, and explicitly excludes the divine attributes to avoid any confusion between Creator and creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the opening statement: 'The world with all its parts is originated (muhdath).' This is the core claim.
2
Understand 'alam'
The passage defines 'alam' as everything other than Allah. Reflect on the examples given: bodies, accidents, plants, animals. These are all things through which the Maker is known.
3
Examine the exclusion
Notice that Allah's attributes are explicitly excluded. Why? Because they are neither other than the Essence nor identical to it. This nuanced position prevents associating creation with the Creator's attributes.
4
Grasp the scope
The world includes all parts: the heavens and their contents, the earth and everything on it. This comprehensive scope sets the stage for the argument that the entire cosmos is created.
This connects to the next lesson on the proofs of creation.

العالم بجميع أجزائه محدث (والعالم) أي ما سوى الله تعالى من الموجودات مما يعلم به الصانع. يقال عالم الأجسام وعالم الأعراض وعالم النبات وعالم الحيوان إلى غير ذلك، فتخرج صفات الله تعالى، لأنها ليست غير الذات كما أنها ليست عينها . بجميع أجزائه من السماوات وما فيها والأرض وما عليها .

Commentary
This passage provides the definition of 'the world' (al-alam) in Islamic theology. It states that the world in all its parts is originated (muhdath), meaning created in time. It defines the world as everything other than Allah among existing things (al-mawjudat), through which the Creator is known. Examples include the world of bodies, accidents, plants, animals, etc. The attributes of Allah are excluded from this definition because they are neither other than the Essence nor identical to it, according to the Ash'ari position of attributes being neither identical nor separate. The passage concludes by emphasizing the world includes all parts from the heavens and their contents to the earth and everything on it. This sets the stage for the argument that the world is created and therefore requires a Creator.

The World and Its Createdness
Al-Nasafi defines the world and asserts its temporality, refuting the philosophers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of the world in Islamic theology and why it is temporally created, distinguishing the Islamic view from that of the philosophers.

Core Idea
The world is everything besides Allah, created from nothing.

Al-Nasafi begins by defining the world and establishing its temporality. This foundational claim directly opposes the philosophers who assert the world's pre-eternity. Understanding this clarifies the Sunni position on creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation to grasp the overall argument.
Notice how Al-Nasafi defines the world before making his claim.
2
Understand the definition of 'world'
Identify what is included and excluded from the definition of 'ālam—everything except Allah and His attributes.
The attributes are excluded because they are neither other than nor identical to the Essence.
3
Grasp the meaning of 'temporally originated'
Understand that ḥādith means brought from non-existence to existence, i.e., it was non-existent and then came to be.
This is the key point of divergence from the philosophers.
4
Contrast with the philosophers' view
Learn how the philosophers claim pre-eternity of the heavens and elements, and how they reinterpret ḥudūth as dependence rather than creation from nothing.
This distinction is crucial for avoiding confusion.

Yes, they asserted the occurrence of everything besides Allah, but in the sense of need for another, not in the sense of the priority of non-existence over it.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and assertion of a core theological doctrine: the world is temporally originated (ḥādith). It defines 'the world' (al-ʿālam) as everything besides Allah among existents by which the Maker is known, including bodies, accidents, plants, animals, etc. The divine attributes are excluded because they are neither other than the Essence nor identical with it. The world's temporality means it was brought from non-existence to existence—it was non-existent and then came to be. This refutes the philosophers (falāsifa) who claim the pre-eternity (qidam) of the heavens and elements in their matters and forms, meaning that they have never been devoid of any form at all. Although the philosophers do speak of 'occurrence' (ḥudūth) for everything besides Allah, they mean it in the sense of dependence on another (iḥtiyāj ilā al-ghayr), not in the sense of non-existence preceding existence. The passage clearly distinguishes the Islamic position from the philosophical one.

Defining the Indivisible Part and the Philosophers' Rejection
Sanusi defines the indivisible part and presents philosophical opposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's definition of the indivisible part (al-juzʾ alladhī lā yatajazzaʾ) and why the philosophers deny its existence, proposing instead hylē and form.

Core Idea
The indivisible part is a non-composite entity, but philosophers reject it in favor of hylē and form.

Sanusi begins by defining the atom (jawhar) as a non-composite entity that cannot be divided in any way. He clarifies that the category of non-composite includes more than just atoms, and then presents the philosophers' alternative view that bodies are composed of hylē and form. This passage sets up the central debate over the existence of indivisible parts.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic definition
Read the Arabic passage from 'أو غير مركب كالجوهر' to 'وتركب الجسم إنما هو من الهيولى والصورة'. Notice the key terms: جزئ لا يتجزأ (indivisible part) and جوهر فرد (individual atom).
The author deliberately avoids the word جوهرة to prevent an objection.
2
Identify the non-composite entities
The passage lists four things that must be refuted to establish the atom: hylē (prime matter), form, immaterial intellects, and souls. Count them and note their role in the philosophical system.
3
Understand the philosophers' view
The philosophers deny the individual atom; they claim bodies are composed of hylē and form. Ask yourself: why would they reject such a straightforward definition?
This is the starting point of the argument.

Commentary
This passage covers the definition of the indivisible part (al-juzʾ alladhī lā yatajazzaʾ) and various proofs for its existence. It begins by stating that a self-subsistent entity can also be non-composite, like the atom (jawhar), which is an entity that cannot be divided in any way. The author notes that the term 'jawhara' (which can also mean jewel) was avoided to prevent objections. Then he clarifies that the category of non-composite includes not only atoms but also hylē, forms, immaterial intellects and souls, according to the philosophers. The philosophers (especially the Peripatetics) deny the existence of the indivisible atom; they hold that bodies are composed of hylē (prime matter) and form. The author then presents the strongest proof for the atom: a true sphere touching a true plane must do so at a single indivisible point; if it touched at two points, those points would form a line, contradicting the sphericity. Next, he presents two famous arguments from the teachers (mashāyikh): (1) If everything were infinitely divisible, there would be no difference in size between a mustard seed and a mountain because both would have infinite parts; size differences depend on finite parts. (2) The cohesion of a body's parts is not essential; God can separate them until only an indivisible part remains. If separation is possible, God can do it; if not, then the part is already indivisible. However, the author declares all these proofs weak. The sphere proof only proves a point (nuqṭa), not an indivisible part, because the point's inherence does not imply the indivisibility of the substance it inheres in. As for the two arguments from the mashāyikh, the philosophers (Muʿtazila? Actually the opponents here are the philosophers who deny atoms) do not claim that bodies are actually composed of infinite parts; rather they say bodies are potentially divisible infinitely, and size is determined by continuous quantity, not by number of parts. Therefore, separation is also possible indefinitely, without requiring an indivisible part.

Proofs for the Atom and Their Weaknesses
Sanusi presents and refutes three proofs for the indivisible part.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three proofs for the indivisible part (sphere on plane, mustard seed, and separation) and Sanusi's refutation of each.

Core Idea
All proofs for the atom fail: one proves only a point, the others assume actual infinite division.

Sanusi presents the strongest proof (sphere on plane) and two famous proofs from the teachers (mustard seed and separation). Then he systematically refutes each, showing that they do not necessitate the indivisible part. This lesson explores the arguments and their weaknesses.

Guided Path
1
Read the three proofs
Read the Arabic passage from 'وأقوى أدلة إثبات الجزء' to 'والكل ضعيف'. Identify the three proofs: sphere on plane, mustard seed and mountain, and separation by divine power.
Notice that Sanusi calls the first proof 'the strongest'.
2
Understand the refutation of the sphere proof
The proof only proves a point (nuqṭa), not an indivisible part. A point inheres in a subject without making it indivisible.
3
Understand the refutation of the other two proofs
The philosophers do not claim bodies are actually composed of infinite parts; they claim potential infinite divisibility. Therefore, the mustard seed and separation arguments do not apply.
This refutation relies on the distinction between actual and potential division.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

In this passage, the author addresses a sophisticated objection: perhaps eternality does not require a specific state, and an infinite series of motions could be eternal as a whole. If two gods could exist, they could disagree, leading to impossibility — so only One can exist.

The Proof of Mutual Hindrance is one of the most powerful arguments in Islamic theology for tawheed. It shows that the concept of a necessary being cannot apply to more than one essence, because that would lead to logical impossibility. The proof is airtight: it shows that the concept of multiple necessary beings is self-contradictory.

وهذا تحقيق ما يقال إن وجود العالم إن لم يتعلق بذات الله تعالى أو صفة من صفاته لزم تعطيل الصانع واستغناء تحقق الحوادث عن الموجد وهو محال. وإن تعلق فإما أن يستلزم ذلك قدم ما يتعلق وجوده به فيلزم قدم العالم، وهو باطل، أو لا، فليكن التكوين أيضا قديما، مع حدوث المكون المتعلق به. وما يقال من أن القول بتعلق وجود المكون بالتكوين قول بحدوثه إذ القديم ما لا يتعلق وجوده بالغير، والحادث ما يتعلق وجوده به ففيه نظر، لأن هذا معنى القديم والحادث بالذات، على ما يقول به الفلاسفة. وأما عند المتكلمين، فالحادث ما يكون لوجوده بداية، أي يكون مسبوقا بالعدم، والقديم بخلافه. ومجرد تعلق وجوده بالغير لا يستلزم الحدوث بهذا المعنى، لجواز أن يكون محتاجا إلى الغير صادرا عنه دائما بدوامه، كما ذهب إليه الفلاسفة فيما ادعوا قدمه من الممكنات كالهيولى مثلا. نعم إذا أثبتنا صدور العالم عن الصانع بالاختيار دون الإيجاب بدليل لا يتوقف على حدوث العالم، كان القول بتعلق وجوده بتكوين الله تعالى قولا بحدوثه. ومن هنا يقال التنصيص على كل جزء من أجزاء العالم إشارة إلى الرد على من زعم قدم بعض الأجزاء كالهيولى. وإلا فهم إنما يقولون بقدمها بمعنى عدم المسبوقية بالعدم، لا معنى عدم تكونه بالغير.
And this is a verification of what is said: that if the existence of the world were not dependent on the essence of God or on one of His attributes, it would necessitate the suspension (ta'til) of the Maker and the independence of the occurrence of events from the Originator, which is absurd. And if it is dependent, then either that necessitates the eternity of that on which its existence depends, so the eternity of the world follows – which is false – or not; then let takwin also be eternal, while the made thing dependent on it is temporal. And what is said – that the statement that the existence of the made depends on takwin is a statement of its temporality, because the eternal is that whose existence does not depend on another, and the temporal is that whose existence depends on another – is subject to consideration (fihi nazar), because this is the meaning of eternal and temporal in essence (bi al-dhat), according to what the philosophers say. As for the mutakallimun, the temporal is that whose existence has a beginning, i.e., it is preceded by non-existence; and the eternal is the opposite. And mere dependence of its existence on another does not imply temporality in this sense, because it is possible that it be in need of another, emanating from it always, perpetually, as the philosophers hold regarding what they claim to be eternal among possibles (mumkinat), like hylic matter (hayula) for example. Yes, if we establish the emanation of the world from the Maker by choice (ikhtiyar) rather than necessity (ijab), by a proof that does not depend on the world's temporality, then the statement that its existence depends on the takwin of God is a statement of its temporality. And from here it is said: the specification of every part of the world is an indication of the refutation of those who claimed the eternity of some parts, like hylic matter. Otherwise, they only claim its eternity in the sense of not being preceded by non-existence, not in the sense of not being originated by another.

Commentary
This passage is a complex argument about the world's dependence on God and the definitions of 'eternal' (qadim) and 'temporal' (hadith). The author begins with a conditional: the world's existence must depend on God (essence or attribute). If not, God would be inactive. If it does depend, either that dependence makes the depended-upon eternal (so the world would be eternal) or not. The objection claims that if the world's existence depends on takwin, that entails the world is temporal, because dependence on another is the definition of temporality. The author refutes this: that definition is from the philosophers (falasifa). The mutakallimun define temporal as having a beginning (preceded by non-existence). Dependence on another does not necessarily imply a beginning; something could be eternally dependent, as the philosophers claim for eternal materia. However, if we prove that the world emanates from God by free choice (ikhtiyar) – not necessary emanation – then dependence on takwin implies temporality. Finally, specifying each part of the world refutes those who claim some parts (like hylic matter) are eternal; they only mean 'not preceded by non-existence', not 'not caused by another'.

Key Takeaway
Proving creation by choice (ikhtiyar) is the key to establishing the world's temporality, and the specification of each part refutes the claim that any part is eternal in the sense of being uncaused.
