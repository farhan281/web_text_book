Refuting the Tautology Objection
Refutes the objection that affirming realities is a tautology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the statement that realities are established is not a tautology, but rather an affirmation of extra-mental existence.

Core Idea
Affirming realities affirms existence, not tautology.

This passage addresses a philosophical objection: if we say 'realities are established,' is that not just repeating 'the established is established'? The author refutes this by clarifying that the statement is about the correspondence of our mental concepts to external reality. He also introduces the idea that a thing can be considered under different aspects, making the judgment informative in some contexts and tautological in others.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the first part where the objector claims that affirming realities is tautological. Note the example given.
The objection is a common philosophical challenge.
2
Understand the refutation's core point
The author explains that the judgment affirms that our conceptualized realities (human, horse, etc.) have actual existence in themselves. This is not a tautology but a factual statement.
He compares it to saying 'the Necessary Existent is existent'.
3
Learn the concept of i'tibarat (aspects)
The same thing can be considered from different aspects, leading to informative or tautological judgments. The example of a human as body vs. rational animal illustrates this.
This concept is key to resolving the objection.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Judging realities is only futile if you ignore the aspect from which you judge.

This lesson addresses a common philosophical objection: if realities are fixed, then saying they are established is just a tautology. The author shows that such judgments are actually informative because they affirm that our concepts correspond to real entities. He illustrates this with examples from ordinary categories.

Proof Against the Sophists: Direct and Argumentative
Two proofs: direct and by implication against sophists.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two proofs (direct and by implication) that Islamic theologians use to refute the sophists' absolute denial of reality.

Core Idea
The sophist's denial of all reality is self-defeating: either it fails, or denial itself becomes a reality.

The author presents two complementary arguments against the sophists who deny all reality. The first argument appeals to our immediate certainty about things known through observation and demonstration. The second argument turns the sophist's own denial into a proof of reality. This lesson explains both proofs and their scope.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the two distinct parts signaled by 'لنا تحقيقا' and 'وإلزاما'.
2
Analyze the direct proof
The first proof (التحقيق) appeals to necessary certainty from observation and demonstration. Ask yourself: why does the author claim we are 'necessarily certain'?
3
Analyze the argument by implication
The second proof (الإلزام) considers the sophist's denial. Work through the two possibilities: if denial is realized, it becomes a reality; if not, reality is already established.
4
Understand the scope of the argument

This passage presents two proofs against the sophists. Therefore, absolute denial is false. The author notes that this argument only works against the first group (العنادية), who deny all reality outright.

Sensory Errors Do Not Undermine Certainty
Objection and answer regarding sensory errors and self-evident truths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author defends the reliability of necessary knowledge despite sensory errors and disagreements.

Core Idea
Sensory errors are limited to specific causes; when absent, certainty holds.

This lesson presents a common objection against the foundation of knowledge: that even necessary knowledge (like sensory and self-evident truths) can be mistaken or disputed. The author responds by distinguishing between errors caused by particular conditions and the certainty that remains when those conditions are absent.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
The objectors claim that necessary knowledge includes sense perception, which can err, and self-evident truths, which can be disputed. This seems to undermine all knowledge.
Notice the examples: squint-eyed seeing double, jaundiced tasting bitter.
2
Identify the author's response
The author distinguishes: sensory errors occur only due to specific causes, so when those causes are absent, certainty remains. Similarly, disputes over self-evident matters are due to lack of familiarity or obscurity, not a flaw in the truths themselves.
The author also addresses theoretical knowledge as a branch of necessary knowledge.
3
Reflect on the implication
This defense preserves the foundation of necessary knowledge. Without it, no theoretical knowledge could be built.

First, 'necessary' can be contrasted with 'acquired' (الاكتسابي): in this sense, necessary knowledge is that which the creature cannot attain by his own power; it is directly given by God. Second, 'necessary' can be contrasted with 'inferential' (الاستدلالي): in this sense, necessary knowledge is that which occurs without any thought or examination of evidence. Consequently, some scholars classify sensory knowledge as acquired (because it involves intentional engagement with causes), while others classify it as necessary (because it does not involve inference). This dual usage explains why there is no contradiction in the statement of the author of al-Bidāyah, who first divides originated knowledge into necessary (God-given without human acquisition) and acquired (God-given through human acquisition, whose causes are senses, truthful report, and rational consideration). He then further divides the product of rational consideration into necessary (obtained at first glance without reflection, e.g., whole greater than part) and inferential (requiring reflection, e.g., inferring fire from smoke). The passage ends by mentioning inspiration (الإلهام), defined as the casting of a meaning into the heart by way of emanation (بطريق الفيض), likely as another mode of knowledge not covered by the previous classifications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the proof of correspondence (burhan al-tatbiq) demonstrates the impossibility of an actual infinite regress of contingent causes.

Core Idea
An actual infinite chain of effects is impossible because a shifted copy would be equal, which is absurd.

This lesson introduces a classical logical argument known as the proof of correspondence. By aligning two hypothetical series of causes, the proof shows that an actual infinite regress leads to a contradiction. The passage also clarifies why objections based on imaginary series or divine attributes do not apply.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Let the author's own wording introduce the proof.
Notice the technical term 'برهان التطبيق'.
2
Understand the setup
The proof assumes two series: one from the last effect to infinity, and another shifted by one effect (starting from the cause before the last) to infinity.
The shift creates a deficiency in the second series.

Follow the logical reasoning
If every member of the first series has a counterpart in the second, then the deficient series would equal the longer one—impossible. Commentary
This passage presents the first rational proof for the possibility of seeing Allah. The author, al-Nasafi, states that the people of truth (Ahl al-Haqq) have two proofs: rational and scriptural. He begins the rational proof by observing that we are certain of seeing physical objects and accidents; we can distinguish between bodies and between accidents by sight. For a common judgment (the judgment that something is visible) there must be a common cause. This cause can only be existence (wujud), origination (huduth), or contingency (imkan), with no fourth possibility. He defines origination as existence after non-existence, and contingency as the lack of necessity of either existence or non-existence. Non-existence cannot be a cause, so the cause must be existence. Existence is shared between the Creator and His creatures, so it is valid that He may be seen from the aspect that the cause of validity—existence—is realized. Its impossibility depends on whether some property of the possible is a necessary condition or some property of the Necessary is a preventer. He then extends the argument: it is also valid that sounds, tastes, and smells be seen, but they are not seen because Allah did not normally create the sight for them, not because they are impossible to see. After one sight, we may or may not be able to analyze it into substances and accidents. Thus the object of sight is the thing having some identity, i.e., existence, and its sharing is necessary. والله لا يغفر أن يشرك به بإجماع المسلمين، لكنهم اختلفوا في أنه هل يجوز عقلا أم لا؟ فذهب بعضهم إلى أنه يجوز عقلا، وإنما : علم عدمه بدليل السمع، وبعضهم إلى أنه يمتنع عقلا لان قضية الحكمة : التفرقة بين المسئ والمحسن والكفر نهاية في الجناية لا يحتمل الإباحة، ورفع الحرمة أصلا، فلا يحتمل العفو ورفع الغرامة. وأيضا : الكافر يعتقده كفرا حقا، ولا يطلب له عفوا أو مغفرة فلم يكن العفو عنه حكمة. وأيضا : هو اعتقاد الأبد، فيوجب جزاء الأبد، وهذا بخلاف سائر الذنوب.
And Allah does not forgive that partners be associated with Him, by consensus of the Muslims, but they differed as to whether it is rationally permissible or not. So some of them held that it is rationally permissible, but knowledge of its non-occurrence is only known through revelation (sam'), and others held that it is rationally impossible, because the requirement of wisdom is discrimination between the evildoer and the doer of good, and disbelief is the ultimate in transgression; it does not admit of permissibility and the removal of inviolability at all, so it does not admit of forgiveness and removal of penalty. Also: the disbeliever truly believes his disbelief, and he does not ask for pardon or forgiveness for it, so forgiveness for him would not be wise. Also: it is an eternal conviction, so it necessitates eternal punishment, and this is unlike other sins.

Commentary
This passage discusses the theological question of whether it is rationally possible for Allah to forgive the sin of shirk (associating partners with Him). It begins by stating the consensus that Allah does not forgive shirk (based on Quran 4:48), but then notes a difference of opinion among scholars regarding the rational justification for this. One group holds that it is rationally permissible for Allah to forgive shirk, but we know from revelation that He will not do so. The other group argues that it is rationally impossible for shirk to be forgiven, and they offer three arguments: (1) Divine wisdom requires that there be a distinction between the evildoer and the good-doer; disbelief is the ultimate crime, so it cannot be simply condoned or forgiven without undermining wisdom. (2) The disbeliever actually believes his disbelief to be true and does not even ask for forgiveness, so forgiving someone who does not repent would not be wise. (3) Disbelief is an eternal conviction (i.e., the disbeliever persists in it until death), so it deserves eternal punishment, unlike other sins which are temporary. The passage thus summarizes a key intra-Muslim debate about the relationship between divine attributes (wisdom, justice) and the possibility of forgiveness for the greatest sin.
