What Is a Miracle? Definition and Purpose
Definition and necessity of miracles for prophethood authentication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of a miracle and why it is necessary for establishing a prophet's truthfulness.

Core Idea
A miracle breaks custom to prove prophecy, not magic.

Before we explore how miracles produce knowledge, we must first understand what a miracle is and why it is needed. The author gives a precise definition: a miracle is an act contrary to custom performed by a claimant to prophethood in a challenge that others cannot match. Without miracles, we could not distinguish a true prophet from a liar.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of a miracle
The passage defines a miracle (mu'jizah) as an event that breaks custom, appears on the hand of a claimant to prophethood, and challenges deniers who cannot produce its like. Notice the three conditions: (1) khilaf al-'adah, (2) 'ala yad mudda'i al-nubuwwa, (3) 'inda tahaddi al-munkirin.
The word 'mu'jizah' comes from 'ajz' (inability) – it leaves others incapable.
2
Understand the purpose of miracles
The passage states that without miracles, we would not be obligated to accept the prophet's word, nor could we tell the truthful from the false. Miracles serve as God's authentication mechanism for prophethood.

Commentary
This passage defines a miracle (mu'jizah) and explains how it provides certain knowledge of a prophet's truthfulness. The author begins by stating that God supports prophets with miracles that break customary patterns of nature (nawqidat lil-'adat). He defines a miracle as an event that occurs contrary to custom at the hand of a claimant to prophethood, in a context of challenge to disbelievers who are unable to produce anything similar. The purpose of miracles is to make acceptance of the prophet's claim obligatory and to distinguish the truthful from the liar. When a miracle appears, certainty about the prophet's truthfulness arises through the 'way of custom' (tariq jary al-'adah). This means that God creates in the observer a certain knowledge immediately after the miracle, even though it is logically possible that God does not create that knowledge. The author provides an analogy: if someone in a gathering claims to be a king's messenger and asks the king to break his habit by rising from his seat three times, and the king does so, the audience gains necessary customary knowledge of the claimant's truthfulness. This knowledge is certain even though deception is possible in itself. The author clarifies that intrinsic possibility (al-imkan al-dhati), meaning logical permissibility, does not preclude certain knowledge. For example, we know with certainty that Mount Uhud has not turned into gold, although it is logically possible. Similarly, the miracle gives certain knowledge through custom, which is one of the sources of certain knowledge, like sensory perception. The author then addresses potential objections: the possibility that the miracle comes from other than God, that it is not meant for authentication, or that it supports a liar. These possibilities do not detract from the certain knowledge produced, just as the logical possibility that fire might lack heat does not diminish our sensory certainty of its heat.

How Miracles Provide Certain Knowledge
Epistemology of miracles: certainty through custom and response to objections

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how miracles produce certain knowledge of a prophet's truthfulness through God's custom, and why logical possibilities do not undermine this certainty.

Core Idea
God's custom, not logical necessity, makes miracles a source of certainty.

Now that we know what a miracle is, the author explains its epistemic power. When a miracle occurs, observers gain certain knowledge of the prophet's truthfulness not by logical proof but through God's established custom (jary al-'adah). The author provides a vivid analogy with a king's unusual action and addresses objections that logical possibilities might weaken this certainty.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the mechanism of knowledge
Read how certainty (jazzm) is obtained 'bi tariq jary al-'adah' – by the way of the running of custom. This means God directly creates knowledge in the observer's mind when a miracle occurs, even though it is logically possible that He does not.
This is not a logical proof but a divine, customary cause-and-effect.
2
Analyze the king analogy
The example of the king rising three times shows how a break in ordinary behavior compels observers to believe the messenger. Notice the parallel: the king's action is a 'custom-break' analogous to the miracle.
3
Understand intrinsic possibility vs certain knowledge
The author argues that 'al-imkan al-dhati' (intrinsic possibility) does not prevent certain knowledge. We know Mount Uhud is not gold even though it is logically possible. Similarly, the logical possibility of a miracle being false does not negate the certainty produced by custom.
4
See how objections are dismissed
The passage lists three possibilities: miracle from other than God, not for authentication, or authenticating a liar. These are rejected as no more damaging than the logical possibility that fire might not be hot.

How Miracles Produce Certainty Through God's Custom
The author explains how miracles produce certainty by customary divine action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how miracles yield certain knowledge of a prophet’s truthfulness through customary divine action, and why logical possibilities do not undermine that certainty.

Core Idea
Miracles generate certainty by God's custom, not by logical necessity.

In this passage, the author explains a foundational principle in proving prophethood: miracles (mu'jizāt) produce certain knowledge (jazm) of the prophet's truthfulness, but not through logical necessity. Instead, God has established a custom (al-'āda) of creating knowledge in the observer upon witnessing a miracle. The author carefully distinguishes between what is logically possible and what is certain, using analogies to show that customary certainty is robust against speculative doubts.

Commentary
This passage is a proof that miracles (mu'jizat) produce certainty (jazm) about the truthfulness of a prophet through God's custom (al-'āda), not through logical necessity. The author argues that when a miracle occurs, God creates knowledge of the prophet's sincerity in the observer's mind as a customary occurrence, even though logically God could choose not to create that knowledge. He illustrates with an analogy: a man claims to be a king's messenger and asks the king to break his habit; when the king complies, the observers acquire necessary customary knowledge of the man's truthfulness, even though the king could have acted otherwise. Similarly, intrinsic possibility (imkān dhātī) – meaning that something is rationally permissible – does not prevent certain knowledge; we are certain Mount Uhud is not gold, though it could be. The author stresses that this certainty is not undermined by speculative possibilities (e.g., the miracle being from someone else, or being used to support a liar) because customary knowledge, like sensory knowledge, remains certain despite logical possibilities. The example of fire's heat: we are certain fire is hot, even though God could deprive it of heat without contradiction.

The Common Element in Tawatur: Miracles and Their Details
The author defines the common element as the appearance of miracles and explains the condition of tawatur.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that tawatur (mass transmission) applies to the core event (the miracle) even if individual details are transmitted through ahad (single) chains.

Core Idea
Tawatur concerns the common element, not every detail.

This passage clarifies a key principle in the study of miracles: how can a miracle be known with certainty (mutawatir) when the stories about it come through individual reports? The author explains that what is mutawatir is the core fact—the occurrence of the miracle—while the surrounding details may be ahad. This distinction resolves a potential confusion about the reliability of historical reports.

Proof for the Reality of Saintly Miracles (Karamat)
Taftazani provides evidence from tawatur and the Quran for the occurrence of karamat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Taftazani proves the reality of saintly miracles (karamat) through transmitted reports and Quranic testimony.

Core Idea
Mutawatir reports and Quranic miracles establish karamat beyond doubt.

Before this passage, the possibility of karamat was established. Now Taftazani provides evidence that karamat actually occur. He appeals to two types of proof: the massive transmission of reports from the Companions and later saints, and explicit Quranic accounts of miracles granted to righteous non-prophets. Once occurrence is proven, the question of possibility becomes moot.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice the two sources of evidence Taftazani presents.
The passage is concise; each sentence carries weight.
2
Understand tawatur as a proof
Taftazani first appeals to mutawatir (massively transmitted) reports from the Companions and later generations. Such transmission makes denial impossible, especially for the common occurrence, even if details are reported via solitary chains.
Tawatur is a key concept in Islamic epistemology for establishing historical facts.
3
Examine the Quranic evidence
The second proof is from the Quran itself: the examples of Maryam receiving provision in the sanctuary and the companion of Solomon bringing the throne of Bilqis in an instant. These are clear karamat.
These events are explicitly mentioned in the Quran, making them undeniable for Muslims.
4
Grasp the logical conclusion
After establishing that karamat have occurred, Taftazani states there is no need to separately prove their possibility. Occurrence automatically entails possibility.
This is a sound logical step: if something happened, it is possible.
5
Reflect on the strength of the proof
Ask yourself why this combination of tawatur and Quranic testimony is convincing. Consider how it addresses skeptics who might rely on reason alone.
The proof is grounded in transmitted knowledge, which is foundational in Islamic theology.

Saintly Karamah as Proof of Prophethood
Refutes Mu'tazila objection by reclassifying saintly karamah as prophetic mu'jizah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the karamah of a saint is considered a mu'jizah for the prophet, thereby refuting the Mu'tazila objection that saintly miracles cause confusion with prophetic miracles.

Core Idea
A saint's karamah confirms the prophet's truth, not challenges it.

The Mu'tazila argued that if saints could perform miracles, prophetic miracles would become indistinguishable, causing confusion. The author provides a clear refutation: the saint's miraculous deed is only possible because of his obedience to a prophet, and thus it becomes a miracle (mu'jizah) for that prophet. In this lesson, we'll unpack this argument and understand the relationship between saintly karamah and prophetic mu'jizah.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the objection
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Note the Mu'tazila objection that saintly miracles could be confused with prophetic miracles.
The objection is stated at the beginning: 'if the appearance of extraordinary phenomena from the saints were permissible, it would be confused with the miracle'.
2
Understand the author's answer
The author responds by saying that the saint's karamah is actually a mu'jizah for the prophet. This reclassification resolves the confusion because the prophet remains the source of the miracle.
The key phrase: 'that is... a miracle (mu'jizah) for the Messenger to whom this karamah appeared'.
3
Learn the condition for karamah
Notice that the saint must be obedient to the prophet and not claim independence. If the saint claimed prophethood, the event would not be a karamah.
The text says: 'if this saint claimed independence by himself and lack of following, he would not be a saint, and that would not appear at his hand'.
4
Grasp the conclusion
The passage concludes that every extraordinary event is a mu'jizah relative to the prophet and a karamah relative to the saint, because the saint has no prophetic claim.
Final sentence: 'So every karamah of a saint is a miracle (mu'jizah) for the prophet of this saint'.

Commentary
This passage refutes the Mu'tazila objection against karamat (miracles of saints). The objection is that if saints could perform miracles, then prophetic miracles (mu'jizat) would be indistinct, causing confusion. The saint's miraculous deed confirms the truth of his prophet, because the saint's piety and obedience to the prophet are preconditions. The saint does not claim prophethood; therefore, his karamah is not a rival but a testament to the prophet's message. The conclusion summarizes: the extraordinary event, when related to a prophet, is a mu'jizah regardless of who manifests it; when related to a saint, it is a karamah because the saint has no prophetic claim.
