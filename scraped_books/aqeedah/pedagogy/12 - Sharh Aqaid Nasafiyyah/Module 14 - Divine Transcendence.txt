Allah is Not an Accident: Two Rational Arguments
Al-Nasafi negates that Allah is an accident and provides two rational arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Al-Nasafi negates that Allah is an accident and the two rational arguments supporting this negation.

Core Idea
An accident depends on a substrate; Allah is self-subsistent, so He cannot be an accident.

Al-Nasafi begins the section on negative attributes by stating that Allah is transcendent above certain qualities. The very first attribute negated is that Allah is an accident ('arad). This lesson examines the two concise rational arguments he provides to demonstrate why this negation is necessary.

Allah is Not an Accident: Two Proofs
Sanusi gives two proofs that Allah is not an accident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Allah cannot be an accident based on the two rational arguments: dependence on a substrate and impossibility of persistence.

Core Idea
Allah cannot be an accident because accidents are contingent and cannot persist without infinite regress.

Sanusi opens with the declaration that Allah is not an accident, then gives two concise proofs. The first appeals to the definition of an accident as dependent on a substrate, making it contingent, whereas Allah is necessary. The second shows that persistence would require an extra attribute leading to an infinite chain. This lesson unpacks these foundational arguments.

Contingency is a key concept in kalam.
3

This argument relies on the impossibility of tasalsul (infinite regress).

Reflect on the conclusion
Both arguments together establish that Allah, who is necessary and eternal, cannot be an accident.
These proofs are foundational for divine transcendence.

He is not an accident (1) because it does not subsist by itself but depends on a substrate that supports it, thus it is contingent; and because its persistence is impossible, otherwise persistence would be a meaning subsisting in it, necessitating the subsisting of a meaning in a meaning, which is impossible. For the subsisting of an accident in a thing means that its location is dependent on the location of that thing, and an accident has no location in itself to enable something else to be located dependently. This is based on the view that the persistence of a thing is an extra meaning over its existence, and that subsisting means dependence in location. But the truth is that persistence is the continuation of existence and its non-removal. Its reality is existence with respect to relation to the second moment of time. The meaning of our saying 'it existed and did not persist' is that it occurred and its existence did not continue, so it was not established in the second moment. And that 'subsisting' means characterizing relation (al-ikhtiṣāṣ al-bāʿith) as in the attributes of the Creator, and that the renewal of bodies at every moment and the observation of their persistence by renewal of similars is not more improbable than that in accidents. Indeed, their argument—meaning the philosophers—regarding the subsistence of an accident in an accident by the speed and slowness of motion is not complete. For here there is not something that is motion and another thing that is speed or slowness. Rather, here there is a particular motion which is called fast relative to some motions and slow relative to others. Hence it is clear that speed and slowness are not two different types of motion, for real types do not differ by relations.
(1) Al-ʿaraḍ in logic: that which subsists in another, like whiteness, length, and shortness, and it is the opposite of substance.

Commentary
This passage is a proof that Allah is not an accident (ʻaraḍ). The author gives two main arguments: (1) an accident does not subsist by itself but requires a substrate, making it contingent, whereas Allah is necessary; (2) an accident cannot persist (baqā') because if it did, persistence would be an additional meaning subsisting in it, leading to an impossible chain of meanings in meanings. The author then corrects a common misunderstanding: the true meaning of persistence is continuation of existence, not an extra attribute, and subsisting (qiyām) means a characterizing relation, not spatial dependency. He then refutes the philosophers' objection that accidents inhere in accidents (e.g., speed in motion) by analyzing that speed and slowness are not separate accidents but relative descriptions of motion itself. Thus the objection falls. The footnote defines ʻaraḍ as that which subsists in another, like whiteness, as opposed to substance.

Refuting the Philosophers: Accidents and Relativity
Sanusi corrects definitions of persistence and subsistence, and refutes the philosophers' speed/slowness argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the correct definitions of persistence and subsistence, and why the philosophers' argument about speed and slowness fails to prove that accidents inhere in accidents.

Core Idea
Persistence is continuation, not an extra attribute; speed and slowness are relative, not separate accidents.

After establishing that Allah is not an accident, Sanusi addresses two further issues: the nature of persistence and subsistence, and the philosophers' objection that accidents can inhere in accidents (e.g., speed in motion). He corrects misunderstandings and shows that the philosophers' example is invalid because speed and slowness are not real species of motion but relative descriptions.

Guided Path

He rejects the idea that persistence is an extra attribute.
2
Understand the refutation of philosophers
The philosophers claim that speed and slowness are accidents inhering in motion. Sanusi argues that speed and slowness are not separate entities but relational labels applied to the same motion.
This refutes the claim that an accident can subsist in another accident.
3
Reflect on the implication
Since speed and slowness are not distinct types, the objection fails. This preserves the principle that accidents do not inhere in accidents.
This maintains the consistency of the earlier proof.

Proves Allah is neither a body nor a substance, discussing different definitions of substance.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Allah cannot be described as a body or a substance, including the rational proofs and the role of revelation in determining divine attributes.

Core Idea
Allah is beyond being a body or a substance, as both imply temporality and limitation.

This passage from the Sharh Aqaid al-Nasafiyyah addresses a key point of Islamic theology: affirming Allah's transcendence beyond physical categories. It provides rational and scriptural reasons to negate two common misconceptions: that Allah is a body or a substance. The discussion also clarifies different definitions of 'substance' to prevent misunderstanding.

Commentary
This passage negates that Allah is a body (jism) or a substance (jawhar). For body, the proof is that it is composite and occupies space (mutaḥayyiz), which are signs of temporality (ḥudūth). For substance, the author distinguishes two usages: (1) In the Ashʻarī view (ʻindanā), substance means the indivisible part (al-juz' alladhī lā yatajazzā), which is located and part of a body—this cannot apply to Allah. (2) Among philosophers (al-falāsifa), substance means an existent not in a subject, either abstract or located, but they still consider it a division of the possible (al-mumkin), so it is contingent. If by 'substance' and 'body' one means the self-subsistent existent not in a subject (i.e., a general description), the author says it is still impermissible to apply them to Allah because revelation does not use them, and the common understanding (tabādur al-fahm) would associate them with composition and location, which are inapplicable to Allah. Thus the negation is both rational and based on adherence to revealed terminology.

Divine Transcendence: Negating Corporeal Attributes
Denying that Allah has form, limit, number, parts, composition, or finiteness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the six negative attributes that assert Allah's transcendence over all characteristics of created bodies, and why each negation follows from the principle of divine necessity (wujub).

Core Idea
Allah is free from all attributes of bodies because He is absolutely necessary.

This passage from the Sharh al-'Aqa'id al-Nasafiyyah presents a series of negations that purify the concept of Allah from any resemblance to creation. By denying that Allah has form, limit, number, parts, composition, or finiteness, the theologian establishes that Allah is not a body and not subject to the limitations of created things. The reasoning is grounded in the fundamental principle of wujub (necessary existence).

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation

Notice the concise style; each negation is followed by a brief reason.

List out the six terms: mutasawwar, mahdud, ma'dud, mutabaghghid, mutaqazzi', murakkab, mutanahi. Understand their meanings.
The terms are in the order: form, limit, number, parts, divisibility, composition, finiteness.
3

For each negation, note the reason given. For example, form is a property of bodies; limit implies boundary; number implies quantity; parts and composition imply neediness; finiteness is for magnitudes.
The final negation ties together all others: they are all attributes of bodies.
4
Connect to the principle of wujub
The passage states that composition implies neediness, incompatible with wujub (necessary existence). Reflect that all six negations ultimately flow from Allah's absolute self-sufficiency.
Wujub is the key concept: necessity of existence excludes any potentiality or need.

Tanzih is grounded in negating necessary existence, not in word definitions.

The author, Imam al-Nasafi, establishes a foundational principle: the transcendence of Allah from body, direction, and composition is not based on how earlier scholars defined terms like 'accident' or 'substance'. Rather, it rests on the rational judgment that such attributes would negate Allah's necessary existence (wujub al-wujud). In this passage, he explains why the path of the masters (mashayikh) is weak and why the correct basis is the negation of contingency and temporality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the opponent argues from apparent texts and rational deduction for Allah's direction and body, and the two authentic Sunni responses: tafwid (delegation) and ta'wil (interpretation).

Core Idea
Apparent texts about Allah must be understood through definitive proofs of transcendence, not taken literally.

This lesson presents a key dialectical exchange in kalam: the opponent's use of literal texts and rational deduction to argue for Allah's spatiality, and the Sunni theologian's response. The response offers two authentic methods—tafwid and ta'wil—which safeguard Allah's transcendence while honoring the revealed texts. Understanding this argument is crucial for grasping how Sunni theology reconciles scripture with reason.
