Allah Creates All Actions of Servants
Affirmation of divine creation of all human acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni doctrine that Allah is the sole creator of all human actions, both good and evil, and how this differs from the Mu'tazilite view.

Core Idea
Allah creates every action; humans acquire them.

This passage establishes a cornerstone of Sunni theology: nothing in creation, including human deeds, comes into existence except by Allah's creative power. It directly counters the Mu'tazilite claim that humans independently create their own actions. Let's examine the text to grasp this essential distinction.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then its literal translation. Notice the two pairs of opposites: disbelief/faith, obedience/disobedience.
2
Identify the key claim
The passage asserts that Allah is the Creator (khāliq) of all servant actions. Highlight this central affirmation.
3
Understand the rejected view
The text explicitly rejects the Mu'tazilite position. Ask: Why is it important to deny that humans create their own actions?
The Mu'tazila argued for human free will by asserting independent creation.
4
Reflect on the nuance
Consider the common misunderstanding: does this make humans puppets? The commentary hints at the concept of kasb (acquisition). Read the commentary for clarification.
This nuance will be developed in later lessons.

It explicitly rejects the Mu'tazilite view that humans create their own actions. This is a concise declaration of the Sunni position on divine predestination and human acquisition (kasb).

The Rational Proof for Allah's Creation of Actions
Rational proof: if man created his actions, he would know their details; walking example disproves it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational argument that if humans created their own actions, they would have detailed knowledge of every subtle component, but they do not—proving the Creator is Allah.

Core Idea
If you don't know every detail of your action, you didn't create it.

This lesson unpacks the powerful rational proof presented by the People of Truth against the Mu'tazilite claim that humans create their own actions. Through the simple example of walking, we see that a person is unaware of countless microscopic movements and pauses, which disproves the idea that they are the creator. This argument extends to all bodily actions, affirming that Allah alone creates.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the rational proof
Read the Arabic text and translation focusing on the logical structure: a conditional premise, an observation, and a conclusion.
Pay close attention to the phrase 'لو كان خالقا لأفعاله لكان عالما بتفاصيلها'.
2
Understand the conditional premise
The argument assumes that if a person creates an action by power and choice, they must have full knowledge of all its details. Identify this assumption and why it is reasonable.
This is the key logical link: creation with free will requires knowledge of what is being created.
3
Examine the walking example
Consider walking: it includes many small pauses and variations in speed that escape awareness. The walker does not know these details, not due to forgetfulness but because they truly lack such knowledge.
This disproof is direct: the consequent (knowing details) is false, so the antecedent (man creating his actions) is false.
4
Extend to other actions
The argument applies even more strongly to complex limb movements like grasping and striking, where the micro-movements are even less known. Reflect on the implications for all human actions.
This reinforces the Sunni position that Allah is the sole creator of all actions.

And Allah, exalted, is the creator of all actions of servants, from disbelief and faith, obedience and disobedience, not as the Mu'tazila claimed that the servant is the creator of his actions. Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal proclamation and refutation of the Mu'tazilite view that man creates his own actions. The author first states the Sunni position that Allah is the creator of all actions, including disbelief and faith. He then provides historical context: early Mu'tazilites avoided calling the servant a 'creator', using terms like 'producer' and 'inventor', but later al-Jubba'i and his followers, equating creation solely with bringing into existence, felt free to use 'creator'. The author presents two proofs from Ahl al-Haqq. The first proof is rational: if a person created his own actions, he would necessarily have full knowledge of their details, because creating by power and will requires that. Yet a person walking is unaware of the countless subtle pauses and variations in speed, and if asked, would not know them. This lack of awareness extends to the movements of limbs and muscles, which are even more hidden—thus the premise that man creates his actions is false. The second proof is scriptural: the verse 'Allah created you and what you do' (37:96). The author explains that 'ما' can be interpreted as a masdariyya (making the meaning 'your action') or as a mawṣūla (meaning 'your works'). In either case, the created thing is the action itself, not the act of creating. The author clarifies that by 'action' we mean the observable movements and rests, not the verbal noun of 'doing'. The footnote notes that this second proof is a transmitted (naqlī) proof, relying on revelation, unlike the first rational proof.

The Scriptural Proof: Quran 37:96
Scriptural proof: Quran 37:96 and linguistic analysis affirming Allah creates actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the verse 'Allah created you and what you do' is interpreted to affirm that Allah creates human actions, and the distinction between the act of creating and the created action.

Core Idea
Allah explicitly says He created your actions, not just the objects you act on.

This lesson examines the second proof from the People of Truth: a direct scriptural evidence from the Quran. The author explains the verse's linguistic nuances, showing that whether 'mā' is interpreted as masdariyya or mawsūla, the verse proves that Allah creates the actions themselves. The lesson also clarifies that by 'action' we mean the observable movements, not the abstract concept of 'doing'.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the scriptural proof
Read the Arabic text and translation, paying attention to the verse and the different interpretations of 'mā'.
Note the translation of the verse: 'And Allah created you and what you do'.
2
Interpret 'mā' as masdariyya
When 'mā' is masdariyya, the phrase means 'your action'. Understand why this interpretation avoids omitting a pronoun and directly refers to the action as created.
This is the preferred interpretation in the author's explanation.
3
Interpret 'mā' as mawsūla
When 'mā' is mawsūla, the phrase means 'your works'. This also includes the actions themselves as created entities.
Both interpretations support the Sunni position.
4
Clarify the meaning of 'action'
The author distinguishes between the verbal noun (creating) and the object (the created movement). 'Action' here refers to what is observed, like movements and rests.
This avoids the Mu'tazilite confusion that only the act of creating is from Allah, while the action is from man.
5
Compare with rational proof
Note that this proof is naqli (transmitted) not aqli (rational). It complements the first proof.

Divine Measuring (Taqdir) and the Answer to Compulsion
Defines taqdir and answers the compulsion objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of taqdir as divine determination of every creature’s limits and the Islamic response to the objection that this entails compulsion.

Core Idea
Divine will does not compel human action; it works through human choice.

This passage from the Aqida al-Nasafiyya defines the concept of taqdir (divine measuring) and immediately addresses a common objection: if everything is determined by God, are humans compelled? The author clarifies that taqdir means God’s determination of each creature’s specific limits, including moral, temporal, and consequential aspects. He then argues that this requires divine power and will, but not coercion, because God’s will is realized through human choice.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of taqdir
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Focus on the opening phrase 'وتقديره وهو تحديد كل مخلوق بحده' and the enumeration that follows.
Notice the four categories: good/evil, benefit/harm, time/place, reward/punishment.
2
Understand the objector's argument
The objector claims that if taqdir determines everything, then disbelievers and sinners are compelled, making moral obligation invalid. Find this in the passage starting with 'فإن قيل'.
The objection is based on a misunderstanding of how divine will relates to human actions.
3
Learn the answer: 'bi-ikhtiyarihima'
The answer is that God willed disbelief and sin with their choice (bi-ikhtiyarihima), so there is no compulsion. Reflect on how this preserves human responsibility.
The key phrase is 'باختيارهما'—emphasizing that human choice is the vehicle for divine will.
4
Connect to the broader lesson
Consider why this distinction matters for Islamic theology: it upholds both divine sovereignty and human accountability.

Commentary
This passage defines al-taqdir (divine measuring) and then addresses an objection about compulsion. Taqdir is the determination of each creature's specific limits, including moral qualities (good/evil), benefit/harm, temporal and spatial dimensions, and the ensuing reward and punishment. The purpose is to show that everything falls under God's will and power. The passage then notes that this requires divine power and will, and there is no coercion. An objector argues that if God measures and creates everything, then the disbeliever is compelled, making moral obligation impossible. The answer is that God willed from them disbelief and sin by their own choice (bi-ikhtiyarihima), so they are not compelled. The author emphasizes that divine will does not negate human choice.

Command Does Not Necessarily Entail Will
Refutation of Mu'tazila equation of command with will

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God's command does not imply His will, and how the example of a master commanding a servant illustrates this separation.

Core Idea
Command and will are distinct; a thing can be commanded but not willed.

The Mu'tazila argued that God's command to believe must mean He wills belief from the disbeliever, and His prohibition of disbelief means He does not will it. But this conflates two separate divine attributes. In this lesson, we examine the Sunni response: command and will are not necessarily linked. The author provides a clear logical counterexample and a human analogy to demonstrate that a command can be issued without the commander actually willing the commanded act. This distinction is crucial for understanding divine decree and human responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazila claim

Notice how this leads them to conclude that the disbeliever's faith is willed but his disbelief is not.
2
Identify the Sunni counter-argument
The author counters with two possibilities: a thing may be commanded but not willed, and a thing may be prohibited but still willed. This breaks the supposed necessary connection.
The author grounds this in divine wisdom and the fact that God is not questioned about what He does.
3
Analyze the example of the master
The author gives an analogy: a master commands his servant to do something, not because he wants the act itself, but to expose the servant's disobedience to others. This shows command without will.
Consider how this mirrors God's command to the disbeliever: He commands faith to establish proof, but may not will it from that particular individual.
4
Reflect on the theological significance
Understand that this distinction preserves God's justice and wisdom. It explains why some are commanded to believe yet remain disbelievers without attributing injustice to God.
This lesson is foundational for the Sunni doctrine of qadar (divine decree) and human accountability.

Kasb: The Ash'ari Solution to Divine Omnipotence and Human Action
Objection and response via kasb with two-aspect solution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the problem of a single act falling under two powers and how the Ash'ari doctrine of kasb resolves it by distinguishing creation (khalq) from acquisition (kasb).

Core Idea
A single act can be created by God and acquired by the servant from different aspects.

This passage presents a powerful objection against human free will: if God alone creates all acts, how can a servant be said to act by choice? The author acknowledges the force of this objection but, guided by revelation and necessity, proposes the doctrine of kasb. As you read, track how he navigates between the proof of divine creation and the undeniable reality of human choice.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the objection carefully: it says that being an agent means originating (ijad) the act, but God alone originates, and a single act cannot fall under two independent powers.
This is a logical argument against human agency.
2
Acknowledge the strength
Notice that the author does not dismiss the objection; he calls it strong and solid. This shows intellectual honesty.
3
See the two grounds
The author mentions two things: proof (burhan) that God is Creator, and necessity (darura) that human power and will have an input in some acts. Identify these two as the basis for the solution.
Proof comes from revelation/reason; necessity comes from everyday experience.
4
Learn the kasb solution
The solution: God creates all things, and the servant 'acquires' (kasb). Acquisition means the servant directs his power and will toward the act; God then brings the act into existence (khalq).
This preserves divine omnipotence while giving humans meaningful choice.
5
Reflect on the two aspects
The same act is maqdur (subject of power) for both God and the servant, but from different aspects: God from the aspect of creation, the servant from acquisition. This is the key to the resolution.
The author says this meaning is necessary even if we cannot fully articulate it.

Presents a proof from Quran against permissibility and resolves it using external prevention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the proof from Quran that burdening beyond capacity is not permissible, and the Ash'ari resolution distinguishing intrinsic possibility from external impossibility.

Core Idea
A possible in itself may become impossible due to an external factor.

After establishing the consensus and the dispute, the text presents a proof that burdening beyond capacity is impossible: if it were permissible, supposing it occurs would lead to an impossible consequence (falsifying God's speech). But then a supplement and resolution follow, showing that impossibility can arise from an external cause, using the example of the world's non-existence after creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof and supplement
Read the Arabic and translation for this passage. It begins with 'وقد يستدل' and continues to the end.
The proof is a conditional argument (if permissible then no impossible consequence, but actually an impossible consequence follows).
2
Understand the proof structure
The proof argues: if burdening beyond capacity were permissible, then supposing it occurs would not entail an impossibility. But if it occurred, God's speech ('لا يكلف الله نفسا إلا وسعها') would be false, which is impossible. Therefore, the presupposition (that it is permissible) is impossible.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Grasp the supplement about divine knowledge
The supplement extends the same reasoning: everything that Allah knows will not occur cannot occur, because if it occurred, His knowledge would be false. So all possible events that are known not to happen are impossible in occurrence.
This applies to all objects of Allah's knowledge, will, and choice.
4
Learn the resolution via external prevention
The resolution denies that every possible in itself, when supposed to occur, entails no impossibility. That holds only if no external impossibility attaches. The example: the world's non-existence is possible in itself, but supposing it occurs after creation would entail the failure of effect from complete cause, which is impossible. So the impossibility is due to an external factor (الامتناع بالغير).
This refutes the initial claim that the proof's consequent is impossible.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal argument about whether Allah can burden a person beyond their capacity. The verse about believers praying not to be burdened refers to unbearable accidents, not obligations. Therefore, the antecedent (permissibility) is impossible. The author then adds a supplement clarifying that this applies to anything whose non-occurrence is known by Allah. For example, the non-existence of the world is possible in itself, but if it occurred after Allah created it, it would entail the failure of effect from complete cause, which is impossible. So the possible in itself does not entail impossibility from its own essence, but from an external consideration it may.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and refutation of the Mu'tazila theory of generated acts (tawallud). It begins by giving examples: pain after beating and breaking after shattering, and similar events like death after killing. These are topics of dispute: does the servant have any role in them? The correct view (Ash'ari) is that all such things are created directly by Allah, because only Allah is the Creator and all contingent things depend on Him without any intermediary. The Mu'tazila attribute some acts to other than Allah, distinguishing between direct agency (mubasharah) where an act proceeds without another act, and generation (tawallud) where an act necessitates another act (e.g., hand movement necessitates key movement). Ijtihad: Error and Correctness and the Chosen View
Discusses the ruling on the mujtahid's error and correctness and presents the preferred view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the debate about whether every mujtahid is correct in ijtihad matters, and the chosen view that there is a predetermined ruling with speculative evidence.

Core Idea
In ijtihad, there is one correct ruling, but the evidence is speculative.

This passage tackles a classic debate in Islamic legal theory: does every mujtahid arrive at the correct ruling, or is there a single correct ruling that some may miss? The author presents the competing views and then offers the preferred position. By the end, you'll see how the evidence (qati' vs dhanni) determines correctness.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the structure: statement of disagreement, basis, verification, and chosen view.
2
Identify the two opinions and their basis
The passage first states that a mujtahid may err or be correct. Then it mentions the view of some Ash'aris and Mu'tazila that every mujtahid in subsidiary matters without qati' is correct. The disagreement is rooted in whether Allah has a specific ruling in every incident or His ruling is what the mujtahid concludes.
3
Analyze the verification (tahqiq) and chosen view
The author breaks down the possibilities: either Allah has no specific ruling before ijtihad, or He does. If He does, then either there is no evidence, or there is evidence (qati' or dhanni). The chosen view is that the ruling is specific and there is dhanni evidence. The mujtahid is correct if he finds that evidence, wrong if he misses it.
This is the key point: correctness depends on finding the speculative evidence, not on the opinion itself.
