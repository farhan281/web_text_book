Rulings on Istihlal of Intercourse During Menstruation and Liwat with Wife
The author cites Sarakhsi and Muhammad on istihlal of intercourse during menstruation and liwat with one's wife.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the differing opinions within the Hanafi school on whether deeming certain forbidden acts permissible constitutes disbelief.

Core Idea
Not every istihlal of a forbidden act is automatically kufr.

This short passage presents two specific cases from Hanafi jurisprudence concerning acts whose prohibition is established in the Qur'an and Sunnah. Imam al-Sarakhsī held that one who deems intercourse with a menstruating wife permissible becomes a disbeliever, while Muḥammad al-Shaybānī disagreed, and this latter view is declared correct. Similarly, deeming sodomy with one's wife permissible does not result in disbelief, according to the most sound opinion.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the passage presents two separate rulings.
2
Identify the two opinions on istihlal of intercourse during menstruation
Find the view of Imam al-Sarakhsī and the view of Muḥammad al-Shaybānī. Note which one is declared correct.
The phrase 'وهو الصحيح' signals the preferred view.
3
Consider the ruling on liwat with one's wife
The passage states that istihlal of this act does not make one a disbeliever according to the most sound opinion. Reflect on why this might be.
4
Reflect on the underlying principle
Ask yourself why these acts are treated differently from other acts of istihlal. The commentary will help you understand the role of definitive versus probable proofs.
This connects to the broader theme of takfir and ijtihad.

Commentary
This passage is a citation of two different opinions within the Hanafī school regarding istiḥlāl of certain acts. Imam al-Sarakhsī, in his work Kitāb al-Ḥayḍ (Book of Menstruation), held that istiḥlāl of sexual intercourse with a menstruating wife is kufr, because the prohibition is established by definitive texts. However, in al-Nawādir, Muḥammad al-Shaybānī (the famous student of Abū Ḥanīfa) held that it is not kufr, and the author of the present work (al-Taftāzānī) declares this latter view to be the correct one (ṣaḥīḥ). Regarding istiḥlāl of sodomy (liwāṭ) with one's own wife, the author states that according to the most sound opinion (al-aṣaḥḥ), it is not kufr. This lesson presents a nuanced legal discussion where even some acts that are clearly forbidden are not necessarily grounds for takfīr if the person simply deems them permissible, perhaps due to differences in the definitiveness of the proof or the nature of the prohibition.

Verbal Offenses Against Allah
Enumeration of verbal acts that constitute kufr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that specific verbal expressions showing contempt for Allah, His names, commands, promise, threat, or the prophets are considered kufr.

Core Idea
Contempt in words is kufr, even without inner disbelief.

This lesson examines the first set of actions from the passage: verbal expressions that directly show contempt for Allah, His attributes, or the prophets. These are clear examples of how outward speech can constitute kufr, regardless of the speaker's inner conviction.

1

These are all verbal or mental acts.

The common element is contempt (istikhfāf).
3
Reflect on the scope
The list is not exhaustive; it illustrates a category. Reflect on how this principle extends to similar actions.
The author ends with 'to other such branches'.

And whoever describes Allah with what is not befitting (mā lā yalīq) or mocks (akhḍara) any of His names or any of His commands, or denies His promise (waʿd) and threat (waʿīd), becomes an unbeliever (kāfir). Likewise, if he wishes that there were no prophet from among the prophets with the intent of belittlement (istikhfāf) or enmity (ʿadāwa). Likewise, if he laughs with an expression of approval (ʿalā wajh al-riḍā) at someone who speaks unbelief. Likewise, if he sits in a high place with a group around him asking him questions, making him laugh, and hitting him with pillows—they all become unbelievers. Likewise, if he commands a man to disbelieve in Allah (yakfur bi-Allāh) or resolves to command unbelief. Likewise, if he issues a fatwā to a woman to become an unbeliever in order to separate from her husband. Likewise, if he says, 'In the name of Allah' (bismillāh) when drinking wine or committing fornication. Likewise, if he prays facing other than the qibla or without purification deliberately, he becomes an unbeliever, even if that prayer coincides with the qibla. Likewise, if he utters a word of unbelief out of belittlement (istikhfāf) not conviction (iʿtiqād), and other such branches (furūʿ).

Commentary
This passage is a list of specific actions and words that constitute kufr, even if not accompanied by inner conviction of unbelief, because they show contempt for sacred matters. The author enumerates several cases: describing Allah in an unbecoming manner (mā lā yalīq), mocking any of Allah's names or commands, or denying His promise and threat (al-waʿd wa al-waʿīd). Next, wishing that no prophet existed with the intent of belittlement or enmity is kufr. Laughing approvingly at someone who speaks kufr is also kufr, as it indicates agreement. Sitting in an elevated place with people asking questions, making him laugh, and hitting him with pillows is a scene of mockery, and all participants become kuffār. Commanding someone to disbelieve in Allah, or resolving to do so, is kufr. Issuing a fatwā to a woman to become an unbeliever so she can separate from her husband (a corrupt legal trick) is kufr. Saying 'Bismillāh' when drinking wine or committing fornication is kufr, as it treats the forbidden act as if it were permissible and invokes Allah's name. Praying deliberately facing away from the qibla or without ablution is kufr, even if the prayer itself happens to be valid in form. Finally, uttering a word of unbelief out of belittlement (istikhfāf) rather than conviction is still kufr, because the contempt itself is the basis of unbelief. The passage ends by noting that there are many other such branches (furūʿ) derived from these principles.

The Principle of Wisdom and Istihlal
Begins with a principle and then discusses istihlal rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that departing from divine wisdom is kufr and that considering a forbidden act lawful (istihlal) can be kufr, with a valid difference of opinion among Hanafi scholars.

Core Idea
Going against wisdom is kufr, but not every sin is – only 'considering it lawful' makes it so.

The passage opens with a profound principle: religions align with wisdom, so desiring otherwise is disbelief. It then addresses a specific case: is it kufr to consider intercourse with a menstruating wife lawful? Imam al-Sarakhsi says yes, but Muhammad ibn al-Hasan says no, and his view is preferred. This lesson clarifies the role of istihlal and the scope of scholarly disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Read the general principle
Begin with the opening sentence: 'Religions are in accordance with wisdom. And whoever wants to depart from wisdom...' Understand that kufr here is defined as wanting Allah to judge unwisely.
This principle underlines that kufr is a rejection of divine wisdom, not just a sin.
2
Examine the istihlal cases
Read the rulings on considering lawful intercourse with a menstruating wife. Note the disagreement: al-Sarakhsi says kufr, Muhammad ibn al-Hasan says not. The text states the latter is correct.
The key is istihlal – the act of considering something lawful. Without it, the sin itself is not kufr.
3
Consider sodomy with one's wife
Read the next ruling: istihlal of sodomy with one's wife is not kufr according to the most sound opinion. This shows that not all istihlal is treated equally.
The ruling indicates nuances based on the nature of the act and scholarly consensus.

And in summary: knowledge of the unseen is a matter that Allah alone is unique in; there is no way to it for the servants except by notification from Him, Exalted, and inspiration by way of a miracle or a grace or guidance to inference by signs in what is possible for that. And for this reason it is mentioned in the fatwas that the saying of a person when seeing a halo of the moon: 'There will be rain' — claiming knowledge of the unseen without a sign — is kufr. And Allah knows best.
Source pages p. 153

Commentary
This passage states the ruling and definition of a soothsayer (kahin). It begins by affirming that believing a soothsayer in his reports about the unseen is kufr (disbelief), citing the Prophet's hadith that whoever does so has disbelieved in what was revealed to Muhammad. Then it defines the soothsayer as one who claims to know future events and secrets of the unseen. It describes two types among the Arabs: those who claim contact with jinn or familiar spirits, and those who claim deduction by a special understanding. It then equates the astrologer (munajjim) who claims knowledge of future events to the soothsayer. The passage concludes with the general principle: knowledge of the unseen belongs exclusively to Allah; humans can only attain it through divine notification (i.e., revelation, miracle, or inspiration) or through inference from signs when possible. Therefore, declaring rain from a lunar halo without a valid sign is kufr.

Is a Disbeliever's Prayer Answered?
Presents scholarly dispute on answering disbeliever's prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key arguments for and against the idea that a disbeliever's prayer is answered, and how a hadith about the oppressed is interpreted by the majority.

Core Idea
The majority say no; a minority say yes, citing Iblis' example.

This passage introduces a nuanced theological debate: can we say that a disbeliever's prayer is ever answered? The majority of scholars firmly reject this based on Qur'anic verses and the nature of true supplication. However, a minority point to the story of Iblis as evidence that even a disbeliever's request can be granted. Let us explore both sides and the reasoning behind each.

The scholars have differed on whether it is permissible to say that the prayer of a disbeliever is answered. The majority prohibited it, because of His saying: 'And the prayer of the disbelievers is only in error' [Ghafir 40:50], and because he does not call upon God, since he does not know Him, and because even if he acknowledges Him, since he describes Him with what is unfitting, he has contradicted his acknowledgement. Commentary
This passage presents a scholarly dispute (ikhtilaf) regarding whether it is permissible to say that a disbeliever's prayer is answered. The majority of scholars (al-jumhur) prohibit this, citing three arguments: first, Quran 40:50 which states that the prayer of disbelievers is only in error; second, that the disbeliever does not truly call upon God because he does not know Him; third, that even if the disbeliever acknowledges God, his improper descriptions of God contradict that acknowledgment. Regarding the hadith that the oppressed person's prayer is answered even if he is a disbeliever, the majority interpret this as referring to someone who is ungrateful for blessings (kufran al-ni'mah) rather than a theological disbeliever. On the other hand, some scholars allow it, citing the Quranic story of Iblis who asked for respite and God granted it, which is considered an answer to his prayer. The scholars who hold this view include Abu al-Qasim al-Hakim al-Samarqandi, Abu al-Nasr al-Dabusi, and al-Sadr al-Shahid, and the passage notes that the fatwa is given according to this latter view.
