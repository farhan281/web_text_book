Refuting the Mu'tazilite Arguments: Wisdom and the Meaning of Obligation
Taftazani critiques the Mu'tazilite reasoning and redefines obligation on God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Taftazani refutes the Mu'tazilite claim that leaving the best implies stinginess, and why obligation on God is fundamentally different from obligation on humans.

Core Idea
Obligation on God is not like obligation on humans; it does not imply blame or inability.

After showing the absurd consequences, Taftazani diagnoses the root error: the Mu'tazila rely on an insufficient analogy of the unseen to the witnessed. Their main objection is that God's leaving what is best would be stinginess or folly. Taftazani answers that God's generosity and wisdom are proven, and that He has the right to prevent. He then questions the very meaning of obligation on God, clarifying that it cannot mean blame for leaving nor inability to act, as that would undermine divine free will.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Mu'tazilite argument
Find where Taftazani states the Mu'tazilite claim: that leaving the best would be stinginess or folly. Write it down.
The phrase is 'أن ترك الأصلح يكون بخلا وسفها'.
2
Understand Taftazani's answer
Read the response: prevention is the right of the preventer, and God's attributes of generosity, wisdom, and kindness are proven by decisive proofs. His actions are pure justice and wisdom.
God's withholding is not stinginess because He is not obliged to give; He has the right to give or withhold based on wisdom.
3
Analyze the meaning of obligation
Taftazani then asks: what does it mean for something to be obligatory on God? He rejects two meanings: that its neglect deserves blame (anthropopathic) and that He cannot abandon it (deterministic).
The second meaning would negate free will (ikhtiyār) and lean toward philosophy.
4
Reflect on the overall refutation
Consider how this critique complements the earlier reductio. The Mu'tazilite position is both logically absurd and conceptually flawed.

Is It Rationally Possible for God to Forgive Shirk?
The author presents the rational debate over forgiving shirk.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational debate about whether God could forgive shirk, and why revelation settles the matter.

Core Idea
Reason alone leaves room for debate; revelation confirms that shirk is unforgivable.

This passage presents a subtle theological issue: although Muslims agree that God does not forgive shirk (associating partners with Him), scholars have differed on whether it is even rationally possible for Him to do so. The author outlines two competing views and their reasoning, setting the stage for understanding how kalam balances reason and revelation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once through, then read the literal translation. Notice the flow: consensus → disagreement → two positions with arguments.
2
Identify the two positions
Find the two sides: those who say rational possibility but revelation says otherwise, and those who say rational impossibility due to wisdom and the disbeliever's attitude.
3
Examine the arguments for rational impossibility
Focus on the two reasons given: (1) wisdom requires distinguishing evil-doer from good-doer, and kufr is the ultimate crime; (2) the disbeliever does not seek forgiveness, so forgiving would not be wise.
4
Reflect on the role of revelation
Note that both sides accept the revealed ruling that shirk is not forgiven. The debate is only about what pure reason could permit before revelation intervenes.
This highlights the kalam principle that reason may speculate but revelation provides certainty.

Commentary
This passage discusses the issue of God forgiving shirk (associating partners with Him). It begins by affirming that God does not forgive shirk by consensus of Muslims. However, the scholars differed on a finer point: Is it rationally possible (ja'iz aqlan) for God to forgive shirk, or is it rationally impossible? Some hold that it is rationally possible, but we know from revelation (sam') that He will not. Others hold that it is rationally impossible, and they give two reasons: First, wisdom (hikma) requires distinguishing between the evil-doer and the good-doer; kufr is the ultimate crime that cannot be made permissible or have its prohibition removed, so forgiveness (lifting the punishment) is also impossible. Second, the disbeliever himself believes his kufr is true (he doesn't see it as wrong), so he does not seek forgiveness; therefore forgiving him would not be an act of wisdom—punishing him is just.

Sanusi states the Sunni position on forgiveness and refutes the first Mu'tazila argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ahl al-Sunna position on forgiveness of sins and how they refute the first Mu'tazila argument from threat verses.

Core Idea
Allah's forgiveness is not bound by repentance or sin severity; threats indicate occurrence, not obligation.

In this passage, Sanusi affirms that Allah forgives all sins except shirk as He wills, even without repentance. This counters the Mu'tazila who limit forgiveness based on sin type and repentance. The first Mu'tazila objection uses threat verses; Sanusi responds by clarifying the logical force of those texts.

Infallibility is not a condition for leadership; ordinary probity is sufficient.

The question of whether the Imam must be infallible (maʿṣūm) is a key point of difference between Sunni and Shīʿa theology. In this passage, the author presents two concise arguments to show that infallibility is not a prerequisite, then tackles a Quranic objection head-on by clarifying what 'wrongdoer' really means. This passage argues that the imam is not required to be infallible (ma'sum). The True Nature of Infallibility (ʿIṣma)
Author defines ʿiṣma as divine grace that does not remove free will or trial, and refutes the inherent property view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ashʿarī-Māturīdī definition of ʿiṣma as a divine grace that preserves free will, and why it does not remove trial or make a person inherently sinless.

Core Idea
ʿIṣma is a divine grace, not an inherent property; it preserves free will and trial.

After refuting the necessity of the Imam's infallibility, the author turns to defining what infallibility (ʿiṣma) actually means in Sunni theology. This definition is crucial because it prevents misunderstanding: ʿiṣma does not negate human choice or remove the purpose of trial. The author also refutes a rival view that treats ʿiṣma as a fixed personal trait.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of ʿiṣma
Start with the phrase 'وحقيقة العصمة' and read until 'مع بقاء قدرته واختياره'. Notice that the definition emphasizes that Allah does not create sin in the servant, while the servant retains power and choice.
This shows that ʿiṣma is an act of Allah, not a quality of the person.
2
Understand the 'grace' (luṭf) explanation
Read the next sentence explaining that ʿiṣma is a grace that inclines one to good and deters from evil, yet choice remains. This preserves the reality of trial (ibtilāʾ).
Trial requires genuine choice; this is the key to Sunni theology of human responsibility.
3
See the consequence and refutation
Read the quote from Abu Mansur al-Maturidi: 'Infallibility does not remove trial.' Then read the final sentence refuting those who say ʿiṣma is an inherent property of the person.
This refutation denies any notion of metaphysical sinlessness.

Commentary
This passage argues that the imam is not required to be infallible (ma'sum). The author gives two reasons: first, the example of Abu Bakr, whose imamate is accepted without certainty of his infallibility. Second, the principle that the burden of proof lies on those who assert a condition; absence of evidence suffices for denial. The author then presents an objection from the opponent citing the Quranic verse 'And My covenant does not include the wrongdoers' (2:124), arguing that a non-infallible person is a wrongdoer and thus cannot receive the covenant of imamate. The author answers by denying the premise: a wrongdoer is defined as one who commits a sin that removes his moral probity without repentance, so a non-infallible person is not automatically a wrongdoer. Then the author defines infallibility: it is that Allah does not create sin in the servant while preserving his power and choice. This is a divine grace (lutf) that inclines one to good and deters from evil, but does not negate free will, thus preserving the purpose of trial. The author quotes Abu Mansur al-Maturidi: 'Infallibility does not remove trial.' Finally, he refutes the view that infallibility is an inherent property of the person.
