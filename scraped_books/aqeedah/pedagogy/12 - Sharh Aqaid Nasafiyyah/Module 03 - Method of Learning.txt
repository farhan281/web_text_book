Commentary
This passage extols the virtue of the science of Usul al-Din. It begins by declaring it the noblest and clearest in proof (ahshar al-‘ulum mahajjan wa awdahuha hujjah). The reason: it unveils the covers of divinity (kashif ‘an astar al-uluhiyyah), looks upon the secrets of lordship (mutṭali‘ ‘ala asrar al-rububiyyah), and distinguishes the true prophet from the false claimant (al-fariq bayn al-nabi wa al-mutanabbi). Because of this, studying it is the best occupation, and discussing it is better than idle talk. It is the means for the fortunate (al-su‘ada’) to approach the Supreme Assembly (the angels) and Paradise. Whoever holds fast to it is guided, and whoever turns away perishes.

Four Proofs That the Mujtahid May Err
The author presents four proofs that the mujtahid may err.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four evidence-based proofs that the mujtahid can err, drawn from the Qur'an, hadith, the nature of qiyas, and logical consistency.

Core Idea
Four proofs from revelation and reason demonstrate that ijtihad is not infallible.

The author now provides four proofs to establish that the mujtahid may indeed err, contrary to those who claim ijtihad always hits the truth. These proofs are drawn from the Qur'an, hadith, the nature of qiyas, and the logical impossibility of contradictory rulings. Each proof reinforces the earlier principle that error is possible and the mujtahid is excused.

Guided Path
1
Examine the Qur'anic proof from Surah al-Anbiya
Read the verse about Solomon and David. Understand that if both prophets' ijtihad were equally correct, specifying Solomon would be meaningless.
The pronoun in فَفَهَّمْنَاهَا refers to the judgment or fatwa.
2
Study the hadith and historical proofs
Multiple hadiths indicate fluctuating correctness and reward. Companions corrected each other, showing error is possible.
The hadiths are mutawatir in meaning.
3
Understand the proof from qiyas (analogy)
Qiyas is a revealer (muzhir) not an establisher (muthbit). The truth in textual matters is singular, so only one view can be correct.
This proof relies on the consensus that truth in nass is one.
4
Consider the logical proof from contradictory attributes
If all mujtahids were correct, the same action could be both prohibited and permitted, which is impossible.
This is a reductio ad absurdum argument.

There is no disagreement on this position that the mistaken one is not sinful; rather the disagreement is whether he is mistaken from the beginning to the end, i.e., with respect to both the proof and the ruling—and this is the view of some of the shaykhs, and it is the choice of Shaykh Abu Mansur—or only at the end, i.e., with respect to the ruling, where he erred in it, even if he was correct in the proof, having established it properly, gathering its conditions and pillars, and thus he brought what he was required to do of considerations, and he is not obligated in ijtihad to produce a decisive proof whose implication is necessarily true.
