Learning Objective

The order of mention does not prove superiority when the context is refuting exaggerated claims.

This lesson examines the fourth objection raised against the Ash'ari position that angels are superior to prophets. The objector cites Quran 4:172, where Jesus and angels are mentioned together, and argues from Arabic linguistic convention that the lower is mentioned first. The answer, however, reveals the verse's context: it refutes Christian claims of Jesus' divinity, and the 'progression' refers to specific qualities, not absolute honor.

Understand why this argument does not prove angels are superior to Jesus or prophets. The verse's purpose and the specific meaning of progression prevent that inference.

الرابع: قوله تعالى ﴿وَلَنْ يَسْتَنْكِفَ الْمَسِيحُ أَنْ يَكُونَ عَبْدًا لِلَّهِ وَلَا الْمَلَائِكَةُ الْمُقَرَّبُونَ﴾ [النساء: 172] فإن أهل اللسان يفهمون من ذلك أفضلية الملائكة من عيسى عليه السلام إذ القياس في مثله: الترقى من الأدنى إلى الأعلى. يقال: لا يستنكف من هذا الأمر الوزير ولا السلطان ولا يقال: السلطان ولا الوزير. ثم لا قائل بالفضل بين عيسى عليه السلام وغيره من الأنبياء. والجواب: أن النصارى استعظموا المسيح بحيث يرتفع من أن يكون عبدًا من عباد الله، بل ينبغي أن يكون ابنًا له سبحانه لأنه مجرد لا أب له. وكان قادرًا على إبراء الأكمه والأبرص ويحيي الموتى بخلاف سائر عباد الله من بني آدم. فرد عليهم بأنه لا يستنكف من ذلك المسيح، ولا من هو أعلى منه في هذا المعنى، وهم الملائكة الذين لا أب لهم ولا أم. ويقدرون بإذن الله تعالى على أفعال أقوى وأعجب من إبراء الأكمه والأبرص وإحياء الموتى. فالترقى والعلو إنما هو في أمر التجرد وإظهار الآثار القوية لا في مطلق الشرف والكمال. فلا دلالة على أفضلية الملائكة.
The fourth: His statement the Exalted: 'And the Messiah will not disdain to be a servant of Allah, nor the angels brought near' [4:172]. For the people of language understand from that the superiority of angels over Jesus, peace be upon him, because the analogy in such a case is progression from lower to higher. It is said: 'the minister does not disdain this matter, nor the king,' but it is not said: 'the king nor the minister.' Then no one claims superiority between Jesus, peace be upon him, and other prophets. The answer: That the Christians magnified the Messiah, to the extent that he is raised above being a servant of Allah, rather he should be a son of Him - exalted is He - because he is pure (without father). And he was able to heal the blind and leprous and revive the dead unlike other servants of Allah from the sons of Adam. So He replied to them that the Messiah does not disdain that, nor those who are higher than him in this sense, and they are the angels who have no father or mother. And they are able, by Allah's permission, the Exalted, to perform acts stronger and more wondrous than healing the blind and leprous and reviving the dead. So the progression and elevation is only in the matter of being free from physical origin and manifestation of powerful effects, not in absolute honor and perfection. So there is no indication of the superiority of angels.

Commentary
This passage contains the fourth objection and its answer. The objector uses the verse where both Jesus and angels are mentioned in a negative construction, arguing that Arabic usage places the lower item first. Since Jesus is mentioned first, angels are considered higher. The answer explains the context: the verse was revealed to refute Christian claims that Jesus is divine. The verse emphasizes that even Jesus, who has unique qualities (no father, miracles), does not disdain servitude to God, and neither do the angels, who are even more detached from physical origins (no parents) and capable of greater miracles. Thus, the 'progression' is about these specific qualities, not absolute honor. Hence, the verse does not prove angels are superior in every respect.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Because Jesus is mentioned before angels in Quran 4:172, angels are absolutely superior to Jesus and all prophets.
Correction: The verse's order does not indicate absolute superiority; it reflects the context of refuting Christian claims. The 'progression' refers to specific qualities like freedom from physical parents and ability to perform mighty acts, not overall honor.
