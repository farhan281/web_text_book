Al-Suyuti's Definition of Usul al-Din
Al-Suyuti defines Usul al-Din concisely.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Suyuti's concise definition of Usul al-Din as the science of what must be believed.

Core Idea
Usul al-Din is the science of obligatory belief.

Al-Suyuti, the great polymath, offers a succinct definition: Usul al-Din is the science that investigates what must be believed. This lesson unpacks that definition and its implications for the scope of Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage

The word 'يج' is likely 'يجب' meaning 'must'.
2

This word is the key to the definition's precision.
3
Compare with other definitions
Hold this definition in mind as you encounter al-Zuhayli and al-Sha'rani in subsequent lessons.
Each scholar emphasizes a different facet.

مقدمة عن
علم أصول الدين - التوحيد - علم الكلام
عرف السيوطى علم أصول الدين بأنه : علم يبحث عما يج اعتقاده .
ويقول الأستاذ الدكتور محمد الزحيلي في كتابه ( تعریف بالعلوم الشرعية ) : سماه العلماء: علم أصول الدين لأنه يبحث في أركان الدين ... وأعظم مبادئه وأول أهدافه وغاياته هو الإيمان، وإن بقية أحكام الدين فروع له ومبنية عليهما .
وقال الإمام الشعراني في كتاب الدرر المنثورة :-
وأما زبدة علوم أصول الدين فيرجع كله إلى معرفة الله تعالى وصفاته وأسمائه، والإيمان بجيمع ما جاءت به الكتب الإلهية والأحاديث النبوية من أخبار البرزخ والمعاد ومواقف القيامة وغير ذلك من الأمور المغيبة عنا . وهذا كله معروف ومقرر عند كل مسلم مخالط لأهل الإسلام ولو لم يفصح هو عن التعبير عن ذلك على طريقة المتكلمين . ومعظم ما فيه ( باب معرفة الله تعالى) إذ هو بحر لا يدرك قراره تحقق بها غرق إلى الأبد ولا يخاف عليه سلب بعد ذلك قال ومن تعالى :
ألا إِنَّ أَوْلِيَاءَ اللهِ لا خَوْفٌ عَلَيْهِمْ وَلا هم يحزنون ﴾ [يونس : ٦٢]
In the name of Allah, the Mighty, the Protectress? (problematic basmalah). Introduction to the Science of the Foundations of Religion (Uṣūl al-Dīn) – Tawḥīd – Kalām. Al-Suyūṭī defined the science of Uṣūl al-Dīn as: a science that investigates what [must/should] be believed. And Dr. Muḥammad al-Zuḥaylī says in his book 'Taʻrīf bi al-ʻUlūm al-Sharʻiyyah': The scholars named it the science of Uṣūl al-Dīn because it investigates the pillars of religion... and the greatest of its principles and the first of its objectives and goals is faith (īmān), and the remaining rulings of religion are branches of it and built upon them. And Imām al-Shaʻrānī said in his book 'al-Durar al-Manthūrah': The essence of the sciences of Uṣūl al-Dīn all reduces to knowledge of Allah the Exalted, His attributes and names, and belief in everything brought by the divine books and prophetic hadiths concerning the reports of the barzakh, the final return, the stations of the resurrection, and other matters hidden from us. And all this is known and established for every Muslim who mixes with the people of Islam, even if he does not express it in the manner of the mutakallimūn (theologians). And most of what is in it is 'the chapter of knowledge of Allah the Exalted', for it is a sea whose depth cannot be reached; whoever realizes this drowns forever and has no fear of being stripped thereafter. And he said, and from the Exalted: 'Behold, verily the friends of Allah – no fear shall be upon them, nor shall they grieve' [Yūnus: 62].
Source pages p. 7

Commentary
This passage is a compilation of three definitions of the science of Uṣūl al-Dīn (also called Tawḥīd or Kalām) from three different scholars. First, al-Suyūṭī defines it as 'a science that investigates what [must/should] be believed.' The word 'يج' is unclear in the OCR but likely means 'must be believed' (yajibu). Second, Dr. Muḥammad al-Zuḥaylī explains that scholars named it Uṣūl al-Dīn because it examines the pillars of religion. Its greatest principle and primary goal is faith (īmān); all other religious rulings are branches of it and built upon it. Third, Imām al-Shaʻrānī states that the essence of these sciences is knowledge of Allah, His attributes and names, and belief in all that the divine books and prophetic hadiths convey about the unseen, such as the barzakh (intermediate state), the final return, the stations of the resurrection, etc. He notes that this knowledge is familiar to every ordinary Muslim who mixes with Muslims, even if they do not express it in the technical language of the mutakallimūn (theologians). He adds that most of this science revolves around the chapter of knowledge of Allah, which is like a fathomless sea; whoever truly comprehends it is drowned in it (i.e., is overwhelmed by its vastness) and then loses all fear after that. He then quotes the Qur'anic verse about the awliyā' (friends of Allah) having no fear or grief, possibly to illustrate the outcome of such knowledge.

Al-Zuhayli's Emphasis on Faith as the Foundation
Al-Zuhayli explains the rationale for the name Usul al-Din.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Zuhayli's explanation that faith (iman) is the root principle of Usul al-Din, with all other rulings as branches.

Core Idea
Faith is the root; all other religious rulings are branches.

Dr. Muhammad al-Zuhayli explains why this science is called Usul al-Din: it investigates the pillars of religion, with faith as its greatest principle. This lesson explores the foundational role of faith in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read al-Zuhayli's text and translation. Note the terms 'arkan al-din' and 'iman'.
Al-Zuhayli is a contemporary scholar, so the language is modern.
2
Identify the main claim
Al-Zuhayli states that faith is the greatest principle and the first objective. The rest of religion is branches built upon it.
This establishes iman as the foundation of the entire religion.
3
Reflect on the implication
If faith is the root, then Usul al-Din, as the study of that root, is the most fundamental science.
This elevates the discipline above all other Islamic sciences.

Usul al-Din is essentially knowledge of Allah and the unseen, not just for theologians.

Imam al-Sha'rani presents a comprehensive definition: the core of Usul al-Din is knowledge of Allah, His names and attributes, and belief in all that revelation brings. This lesson unpacks his vision and its democratic accessibility.

Usul al-Din is the science of what must be believed; all other rulings depend on faith.

This lesson introduces the foundational science of Islamic theology, Usul al-Din. Al-Suyuti and al-Zuhayli offer complementary definitions that highlight its focus on obligatory beliefs and the pillars of religion. Understanding these definitions sets the stage for the entire subject, emphasizing that faith is the root from which all other religious knowledge grows.

3
Reflect on the role of faith

Commentary
This passage is a classification and historical introduction by Taftazani. He first divides Islamic rulings (al-ahkam al-shar'iyyah) into two categories: (1) those related to how actions are performed, called subsidiary (far'iyyah) and practical ('amaliyyah); (2) those related to belief, called fundamental (asliyyah) and doctrinal (i'tiqadiyyah). Legal iman centers on heart affirmation (tasdiq) of the Prophet's message; verbal confession is normally required but not an indispensable pillar.

The Non-Existent Is Not a Thing
Defines the status of the non-existent and refutes Mu'tazila

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the non-existent is not a thing in the sense of being real, and why the Mu'tazila position is a real ontological claim, not merely a semantic dispute.

Core Idea
The non-existent has no reality; it is merely not.

This lesson addresses a foundational kalam debate about the status of non-existence. The author clarifies that 'thing' when meaning real existence does not apply to the non-existent, and contrasts this with the Mu'tazila who give possible non-existents some reality. The passage also touches on linguistic usage, but the key is the ontological ruling.

The first meaning is the ontological one; the second is linguistic.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and refutation regarding the status of the non-existent. It explains the statement 'the non-existent is not a thing' by considering two possible meanings. First, if 'thing' means something established and realized in external reality, then this is a necessary judgment that no one denied except the Mu'tazila, who hold that possible non-existents have some sort of external reality. Second, if 'thing' is taken as a linguistic term that can apply to both existent and non-existent or to anything knowable or reportable, then it becomes a lexical discussion—one must rely on transmitted usage. The author emphasizes that the first interpretation is the one accepted by the verifiers, making the non-existent truly nothing in reality.
