The View that Attributes are Necessary by Essence: Presentation and Critique
Taftazani presents and critiques the claim that all qadim are wajib by essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that God's attributes are necessary in themselves and Taftazani's critique that this violates tawḥīd.

Core Idea
Claiming attributes are necessary by essence conflicts with the oneness of God.

This passage from Taftazani's commentary presents a controversial view from later scholars like Ḥamīd al-Dīn al-Ḍarīr, who argued that both God and His attributes are necessary in themselves. However, Taftazani finds this claim deeply problematic and critiques it on the grounds of tawḥīd and logical consistency. Let's examine the argument and its refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the compressed style and the flow from claim to objection to critique.
2
Identify the core argument and objection
Identify the claim that both God and attributes are necessary in themselves. Then find the proof given, and the subsequent objection and response.
The proof is that if not necessary, they would be contingent and need a cause.
3
Understand Taftazani's critique
Taftazani points out two problems: multiplicity of necessary beings contradicts tawhid, and the alternative (attributes as possible) conflicts with their own principle that every possible is created.
Consider how this critique defends orthodox Sunni theology.

Commentary
This passage presents and critiques a controversial view. The author reports that some later scholars, including Imam Ḥamīd al-Dīn al-Ḍarīr, explicitly state that both God and His attributes are necessary in themselves (wājib al-wujūd li-dhātihī). They argue that everything eternal is necessary in itself, otherwise it would be contingent and need a cause, making it created. Then an objection is raised: if attributes are necessary, they would be enduring (bāqiya), and endurance is a meaning (an attribute) subsisting in another meaning, which is problematic. Their answer is that each attribute endures by an endurance that is identical to itself. The author finds this extremely problematic because claiming multiple necessary beings is against tawḥīd. Also, if they say attributes are possible (contingent), that contradicts their claim that every possible is created (which would make attributes created). Finally, the author mentions an alternative: if they claim attributes are eternal in time (qadīma bi-l-zamān) meaning not preceded by non-existence, this does not contradict essential createdness (meaning they depend on God's essence). This last part is cut off but implies a potential solution.

Al-Qadim and Al-Wajib: Distinction and Extension
Al-Nasafi defines qadim and distinguishes it from wajib in concept and scope.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between al-qadim and al-wajib in both concept and extension, and why al-qadim is more inclusive by applying to the divine attributes.

Core Idea
Al-qadim is broader than al-wajib—it applies to Allah's attributes, while al-wajib does not.

In this passage, al-Nasafi clarifies the relationship between two central kalam terms: al-qadim (the eternal) and al-wajib (the necessary). While some treat them as synonyms, the author shows they differ in meaning and scope. Understanding this distinction is essential for later discussions on the divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through.
Notice the concise style.
2
Identify the definition of qadim
Al-Nasafi defines qadim implicitly from wajib: no beginning of existence. Note the conditional statement: if it were originated, it would need a cause.
3
Understand the synonymy claim and its rejection
Some said wajib and qadim are synonyms. Al-Nasafi rejects this because the concepts are distinct. Reflect on why concepts can differ even if they apply to the same subject.
4
Grasp the extension difference
Al-Nasafi explains that in terms of truth-value, qadim is more general: it applies to the attributes, while wajib does not. Multiple eternal attributes are possible, but not multiple eternal essences.
This is a key distinction for the later arguments about attributes.

The Later View: Attributes as Necessary by Essence
Al-Nasafi presents and critiques the view that attributes are necessary by essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that the divine attributes are necessary by essence, the objection that this leads to a meaning subsisting in a meaning, and the response to that objection.

Core Idea
Later scholars argued that all eternal beings are necessary by essence, including the attributes, but this raises the problem of meanings subsisting in meanings.

Some later scholars, like Imam Hamid al-Din al-Darir, took a strong position: not only God but also His attributes are necessary by essence. They based this on the principle that whatever is eternal cannot be contingent. This lesson presents their argument, an objection about everlastingness, and their attempted response.

2

The key premise is that everything eternal must be necessary.
3
Analyze the objection

4
Examine the response
The response: each attribute's everlastingness is identical to that attribute itself.
5
Note the author's critique

The Philosophers' Division of Eternity and Origination
Al-Nasafi critiques the philosophers' division of eternity and origination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' distinction between essential and temporal eternity and origination, and why it is problematic for kalam.

Core Idea
The philosophers divided eternity and origination into essential and temporal, a view that undermines key kalam principles.

This, he explains, is exactly the philosophers' division of eternity and origination, which rejects many established kalam principles. He promises further investigation.

An objection that negating both otherness and identity is contradictory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical objection that the Ash'ari position of negating both otherness and identity of the divine attributes with the essence appears to combine contradictories and thus seems logically impossible.

Core Idea
Negating both otherness and identity is contradictory if no middle ground exists.

The classical theological debate over divine attributes often hinges on logical consistency. Author responds by defining otherness and identity, allowing an intermediary state.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Ash'ari scholars define 'otherness' and 'identity' to reconcile the distinction between God's essence and attributes without separation.

Core Idea
Otherness means separable; identity means identical; God's attributes are neither.

In response to the objection that attributes must be either identical to or entirely distinct from the essence, the author clarifies that 'otherness' and 'identity' have technical definitions that allow a middle ground. This passage lays out those definitions and shows how they apply to divine attributes.

Read the definition of otherness
The passage states that 'otherness' means two existents such that one can be conceived existing without the other, i.e., separation is possible. Notice the condition of conceivability.
This is a technical definition, not ordinary usage.
