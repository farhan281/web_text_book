The Necessary Attributes of Prophets
Stanza listing necessary prophetic attributes and their opposites.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four necessary attributes of prophets (trustworthiness, truthfulness, delivering the message, and intelligence), their opposites being impossible, their shared human traits, and that their sending is a grace and mercy from Allah.

Core Idea
Prophets are divinely protected messengers with essential perfections.

This passage from the matn concisely lists the attributes that all prophets must possess. It establishes their moral and intellectual perfection while affirming their human nature, and it grounds their mission in divine grace. Let's explore each element carefully.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic stanza once to appreciate its poetic form. Then read the English translation to understand the literal meaning.
The Arabic is a rhymed verse (rajaz), which aids memorization.
2
Identify the four necessary attributes
The passage commands to describe all messengers with four attributes: trustworthiness (amana), truthfulness (sidq), delivering (tabligh), and intelligence (fatana). Notice they are qualities that ensure the message is conveyed correctly.
3
Understand the impossible opposites and possible human traits
The opposites of these attributes are impossible for prophets—they cannot be treacherous, dishonest, conceal the message, or be foolish. However, prophets share ordinary human traits like eating, which are merely possible (ja'iz) for them.
This balances their elevated status with their humanity.
4
Reflect on the sending as grace and mercy
The passage concludes that sending prophets is a favor (tafaddul) and mercy (rahma) from Allah to all worlds. This emphasizes that prophethood is not an obligation on Allah but a gift.

First line: authority of prophetic rulings

Core Idea
Prophetic rulings are certain, not probable.

This lesson examines the first line of the stanzas, which establishes the epistemological status of prophetic teachings. Imam Laqqani asserts that every ruling from the Prophet is not merely opinion but reaches the level of necessary knowledge, meaning it is indubitably true. This is a foundational principle in Sunni creed regarding the authority of the Sunnah.

This distinguishes prophetic rulings from mere scholarly opinions.

The Obligatory Attributes of Messengers and Their Infallibility
Beginning of the section on prophethood, enumerating necessary attributes and explaining infallibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four necessary attributes of messengers and the concept of prophetic infallibility as defined by the commentator, including its scope and one apparent difficulty.

Core Idea
Prophets possess four essential attributes and are protected from sin, yet may perform disliked acts for divine purposes.

This lesson introduces the second major section of Islamic theology: prophethood. The author lists the essential qualities every messenger must possess, and the commentator explains the crucial concept of 'isma (infallibility), clarifying its meaning and addressing a potential objection from the Quran.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the four attributes
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the four attributes listed in the couplet: trustworthiness, truthfulness, conveyance, and intelligence.
The commentator explains that 'trustworthiness' includes infallibility.
2
Understand the definition of 'isma
Focus on the commentator's definition: Allah's preservation of the prophets' inward and outward from anything prohibited, even if the prohibition is one of dislike.
This definition is broader than just avoiding major sins.
3
Consider the exception for disliked acts
The commentator notes that prophets may perform disliked acts (makruh) for legislative purposes, making those acts acts of closeness to Allah.
The example from al-Maraqi illustrates this principle.
4
Reflect on the verse interpretation
The commentator addresses the apparent contradiction of Quran 48:2 ('forgive your sins') by offering two interpretations: 'security' or concealment of the sin itself for prophets, while for the ummah it is concealment of punishment.
This shows how apparent difficulties are resolved within the doctrine of infallibility.

The obligatory [section]: the chapter on prophethood. Since occurrence is possible by reason, and indeed the proof of revelation has come concerning it. And describe all the messengers with trustworthiness, truthfulness, conveyance, and intelligence. And here ends the discourse on what the poet, may Allah have mercy on him, intended in the section on divine matters, and he followed it with the second section, which is prophethood, and he said: 'And describe all the messengers' with the sukun of the sin linguistically, i.e., it is obligatory upon you, O accountable person, to believe that all the messengers of Allah, peace be upon them, are characterized by (trustworthiness) and infallibility: which is Allah's preservation of their inward and outward from being stained by anything prohibited, even if it is a prohibition of dislike. Indeed, they may do the disliked act for legislative purposes, so it becomes for them an act of closeness. Sayyidi Abdullah al-Alawi said, may Allah have mercy on him, in al-Maraqi (1): 'And perhaps he does the disliked act, clarifying that it is for disassociation, so it becomes in his side an act of closeness, like the prohibition of drinking from the mouth of the waterskin.' Commentary
This passage begins a new section on prophethood (al-nubuwwat) after completing the section on divinity (al-ilahiyyat). The couplet states two things: first, that the occurrence (of prophethood or miracles) is rationally possible and has been proven by revelation; second, the obligation to describe all messengers with four attributes: amana (trustworthiness), sidq (truthfulness), tabligh (conveyance of the message), and fatana (intelligence). The commentator then explains that 'describing all messengers' means it is obligatory for every accountable person to believe that all messengers are characterized by amana and isma (infallibility). He defines isma as Allah's preservation of their inward and outward from committing anything prohibited, even if the prohibition is one of dislike (karaha). He notes that prophets may perform disliked acts (makruh) for the purpose of legislation (tashri'), making those acts acts of closeness (qurba) for them. He quotes a poem from Sayyidi Abdullah al-Alawi from al-Maraqi to illustrate that doing a disliked act to clarify its permissibility becomes an act of closeness, like the prohibition of drinking from the mouth of a waterskin. Finally, he addresses a potential objection from the Quranic verse 'that Allah may forgive you your sins, past and future' (Q. 48:2). He explains that this verse should not be taken literally for prophets; instead, it means either 'security' (amana) or that 'forgive' is derived from 'ghufr' meaning concealment (satr). This concealment for prophets is the protection from the sin itself, while for the ummah it is the concealment of punishment. Thus, the verse does not imply actual sin for prophets.

Commentary
This passage establishes the necessity of believing in the preeminence of Prophet Muhammad over all other prophets and that he is the final prophet. It then outlines a hierarchy: after him are the rest of the messengers (anbiya?), then the prophets (which here seems to mean the same? Actually it says 'the prophets are the heads of the angels' meaning prophets are superior to angels, without specifying which angels). Then it ranks the companions of the Prophet in order of virtue: Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, Ali, then the remaining ten promised Paradise (the 'ashara mubashshara), then those who fought at Badr, then the Prophet's household, then the rest of the companions, then the tabi'un, and then the tabi' al-tabi'in. This hierarchy reflects the well-known Sunni position on the superiority of the Rightly Guided Caliphs and the virtue of the early Muslims.

The Hierarchy of the Companions and Successors
Lists the Companions and early generations in order of virtue.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni ranking of the Companions and early Muslim generations in virtue.

Core Idea
The Companions have a specific order of virtue, beginning with the first four caliphs.

After establishing the superiority of prophets, the Kharida turns to the Companions of the Prophet. This passage lists them in order of merit, from the Rightly Guided Caliphs to the later generations, reflecting the Sunni consensus on the hierarchy of the early Muslim community.

Guided Path
1
Read the list carefully
Read the Arabic list from 'fa ash-hab al-nabi' to the end. Note the sequence: Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, Ali, then others.
2
Identify the groups
The list includes the ten promised Paradise, the Badriyun (those who fought at Badr), the Prophet's household, the rest of the Companions, the Tabi'un, and Tabi' al-Tabi'in.
3
Understand the principle
This hierarchy is based on precedence in Islam and service. It defends the Sunni position on the caliphate.
