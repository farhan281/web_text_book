The Five Negative Attributes of Allah
Sanusi enumerates the five negative attributes: qidam, baqa, qiyam bi nafsihi, mukhalafa, wahdaniyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the five negative attributes (sifat salbiyya) that negate from Allah what is not befitting, and their significance in affirming Allah's transcendence.

Core Idea
Five negations protect Allah's transcendence: pre-eternity, permanence, self-subsistence, dissimilarity, and oneness.

This passage from al-Bahja al-Saniyya introduces the five negative attributes of Allah. These attributes are not positive descriptions but negations of imperfections. Understanding them is crucial for tawhid, as they safeguard the concept of Allah's absolute perfection and uniqueness.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic poem
Read the Arabic lines carefully, noticing the rhythm and the sequence of attributes. Then read the literal translation.
The poem is written in rajaz meter, a common teaching format.
2
Identify the five attributes
Locate each of the five negative attributes in the poem: qidam, baqa, qiyam bi nafsihi, mukhalafa, and wahdaniyya. Understand that they negate flaws from Allah.
The word 'salbiyya' means negative or privative.
3
Understand the concluding line
The final line states that effective causation (ta'thir) is only for Allah. Reflect on how this connects to the five attributes, especially wahdaniyya (oneness).
This line reinforces tawhid and rejects any other independent cause.
4
Reflect on the meaning of 'tilt al-tuqa'
The phrase 'these are piety' indicates that believing in these attributes is an act of piety. Consider why negating imperfections from Allah is a form of worship.
Piety (tuqa) here refers to safeguarding one's faith by affirming Allah's transcendence.

The Seven Attributes of Ma'ani
Sanusi lists the seven essential attributes of Allah: knowledge, life, power, will, speech, hearing, sight.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the seven essential attributes of Allah (the 'ma'ani') and the distinction between His will and His command.

Core Idea
Seven eternal attributes define Allah's essence; His will is not identical to His command.

Sanusi now turns from the general classification of attributes to the specific list of seven that are called 'attributes of ma'ani.' These are real, eternal qualities that subsist in Allah's essence. The passage also contains an important clarification: Allah's will (irada) is not the same as His command (amr), a distinction that avoids theological confusion.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic poem

The poem uses rhyme and meter, so read it aloud if possible.
2

Understand the qasd-amr distinction
The line 'الْقَصْدُ غَيْرُ الأمرِ' tells us that purpose (qasd) is distinct from command (amr). This clarifies that Allah's will may not always correspond to what He commands.
This prevents confusion about divine decree and command.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The poem ends by affirming that Allah is al-fa'il (the Agent) and al-mukhtar (the Free Chooser), grounding the attributes in His active freedom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the meaning of the self-attribute (nafsiyya) and be able to list and explain the five negative attributes of Allah.

Core Idea
The self-attribute is Allah's existence itself; the five negative attributes negate all imperfection.

In this passage, the author introduces the concept of the self-attribute (ṣifa nafsiyya), which is simply Allah's necessary existence. He then enumerates five negative attributes (ṣifāt salbiyya) that negate any possible imperfection from Allah. Understanding these categories is foundational for Islamic theology.

These are called 'negative' because they deny imperfections.

They are not positive attributes added to the essence.

Distinction between will and command

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ashʿarī distinction between God's will (irāda) and His command (amr), and why they can be opposite.

Core Idea
God may will something He does not command, and vice versa.

This lesson tackles a subtle theological point: the difference between what God wills (irāda) and what He commands (amr). The author clarifies that 'intention' (qaṣd) is distinct from 'command', resolving potential tensions between divine decrees and ethical commands.

Guided Path
1
Read the second part of the poem
Read the lines: 'And everything that is happening is what He willed. And if He may have commanded its opposite, then intention is different from command, so abandon dispute.'
2
Understand the statement
Everything that occurs is by God's will. But His command may be the opposite of what He wills (e.g., He commands good but wills trial).
This shows will and command are not identical.
3
Grasp the Ashʿarī position
God's command is His revealed law (sharʿ), while His will is His creative decree (qadāʾ). They can differ because God may decree hardship while commanding obedience.
This solves the problem of evil and divine commands.
4
Reflect on 'intention' (qaṣd)
The author uses 'qaṣd' (intention) to mean the same as will? Actually qaṣd is often synonymous with irāda. The point is that God's intention (what He wants to happen) is different from what He commands humans to do.
This is a key Ashʿarī point to reconcile predestination with obligation.

The Attribute of Distinctness from Others (Mukhālif lil-Ghayr)
Mashāt defines mukhālif lil-ghayr and proves it by showing that similarity leads to absurd consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the meaning of God's attribute of being distinct from all others and the proof that similarity would lead to impossibility.

Core Idea
God is utterly unlike any creation; similarity would imply contingency and lead to an infinite regress.

The fourth negative attribute is mukhālif lil-ghayr, meaning that God is distinct from everything else. Mashāt gives a logical proof: if something were similar to God, it would also need an originator, leading to an infinite chain or a vicious circle, both impossible. This shows that the world must be originated and God is unique.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition and the proof

The proof uses the concept of tamāthul (similarity) and its consequences.
2
Follow the logical argument
The argument: if something were similar to God, it would also need an originator; then that originator would need another, leading to infinite regress or circle—both impossible. Therefore, similarity is impossible, and God is distinct.
The impossibility of the conclusion proves the premise false.
3
Understand the conclusion
The conclusion is that the world cannot be similar to God and must be originated (ḥādith). This reinforces God's uniqueness.

Al-Baqa: The Eternal Endlessness of Allah
Defines al-baqa and provides its rational proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the negative attribute of al-baqa and its rational proof based on Allah's eternality.

Core Idea
If Allah is eternal without beginning, He must also be eternal without end.

In this lesson, we explore the second negative attribute: al-baqa, which negates any end to Allah's existence. The author provides a concise rational argument: if something is eternal, its nonexistence is impossible. This attribute complements al-qidam and completes our understanding of Allah's timeless existence.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of al-Baqa
Read the Arabic definition carefully: 'al-baqa is the negation of otherness (al-akhariyyah), meaning no end to His existence.' Note that it is a negative attribute.
Contrast with positive attributes like knowledge or power.
2
Understand the proof from Qidam
The proof states that whatever is eternal (qadim) cannot cease to exist, because if it could, it would need a preponderant and would be contingent. This establishes baqa as necessary.
The logic is: if possible, it would require a cause, contradicting qidam.
3
Reflect on the necessity of Baqa
Consider why baqa must be a real distinct attribute: it is the logical consequence of qidam. Allah's existence has no temporal boundaries.

The Fourth Negative Attribute: Mukhalafah lil-Ghayr
Defines mukhalafah lil-ghayr and gives a rational proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the meaning and rational proof of the attribute 'Mukhalafah lil-Ghayr' (God's difference from all contingent things).

Core Idea
If God were like His creation, He would need a creator — so He must be utterly different.

This lesson introduces the fourth negative attribute: Allah's complete difference from all contingent beings. Al-Kharida and its commentator Sanusi argue that affirming similarity would lead to impossibility, so transcendence is necessary. We will explore the meaning of this attribute and its logical foundation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the English translation to get a sense of the argument.
The passage combines definition and proof in a concise style.
2
Understand the definition of mukhalafah
Notice what the attribute negates: God is not a body, accident, moving, stationary, etc. He is different in essence, attributes, and actions.
This is a negative attribute — it removes similarity without affirming a positive quality.
3
Follow the rational proof
The proof is a reductio: if God were similar to contingent things, He would also be contingent and dependent, leading to circularity or infinite regress — both impossible. Therefore, similarity is impossible.
The argument assumes the principle that similar things share the same ontological status.
4
Reflect on the implication
Reflect on why this attribute is necessary to believe. It protects tawhid by affirming that nothing resembles Allah in any way.
This connects to the broader Islamic creed of transcendence (tanzih).

Why Huduth is Impossible: Tasalsul and Dawr
Sanusi proves impossibility of huduth through tasalsul and dawr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational arguments against the possibility of huduth for God, namely that it leads to tasalsul (infinite regress) or dawr (circularity), both of which are impossible.

Core Idea
Huduth is impossible because it would lead to infinite regress or circular dependency.

After establishing that the absence of negative attributes would entail huduth, Sanusi explains why huduth itself is impossible: it would either set off an infinite chain of causes (tasalsul) or a circular dependency (dawr). This lesson unpacks these two rational arguments, which are foundational in Islamic theology.

Read the explanation of huduth's impossibility

Understand tasalsul (infinite regress)

This argument shows that an infinite chain of causes is absurd.
3
Understand dawr (circularity)
Dawr is the dependence of something on that which depends on it. If the chain does not continue infinitely, it would loop, also impossible.
Circularity leads to logical contradiction.

Commentary
