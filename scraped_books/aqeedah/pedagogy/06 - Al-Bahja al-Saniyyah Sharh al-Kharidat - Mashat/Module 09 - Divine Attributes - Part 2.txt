Hearing and Sight: Divine Attributes of Disclosure
Definition of hearing and sight as eternal attributes of disclosure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that hearing and sight are two eternal divine attributes that provide a unique and real disclosure of all existents, distinct from each other and from the attribute of knowledge.

Core Idea
Hearing and sight are real, eternal attributes of Allah, each providing a unique disclosure distinct from knowledge.

In this passage, the author introduces the sixth and seventh attributes: hearing and sight. He emphasizes that these are not merely metaphors but real, eternal attributes that fully disclose all existents in a way that differs in reality from disclosure through knowledge. Understanding this distinction is crucial for a correct conception of Allah's attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic text and its translation. Notice how the author presents hearing and sight as the sixth and seventh attributes.
Pay attention to the phrase 'ينكشف بهما جميع الموجودات انكشافا تاما' – 'by which all existents are fully disclosed.'
2
Understand the definition
The passage defines hearing and sight as eternal attributes. Reflect on what it means for an attribute to be eternal and 'fully disclosing' all existents.
Contrast this with the temporary or partial disclosure we experience.
3
Identify the key distinction
The passage states that disclosure through hearing and sight differs 'in reality' from disclosure through knowledge, and that each of the two differs from the other. Ask yourself why this distinction matters.
This guards against reducing hearing and sight to mere knowledge or conflating them.
4
Reflect on the threefold difference
Note that the passage affirms three distinct modes of divine disclosure: through knowledge, through hearing, and through sight. Consider how each contributes to Allah's complete awareness.
This understanding supports the doctrine that each attribute has a unique reality.

The Two Meanings of Divine Speech: Sensory and Psychical
Explanation of the homonymous use of 'kalam Allah' for sensory and psychical speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between sensory and psychical divine speech, and why the eternal attribute of speech is indivisible and uncreated.

Core Idea
Divine speech has two meanings: sensory (letters/sounds) and psychical (eternal attribute).

The term 'kalam Allah' is often misunderstood as univocal. In this passage, the commentator clarifies that it is used homonymously for two distinct realities: the eternal psychical speech that is an attribute of God, and the sensory speech that consists of letters and sounds. Understanding this distinction is crucial for avoiding theological errors about God's speech.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation once to get the overall flow. Pay attention to the definitions of sensory and psychical speech.
2
Identify the two meanings
Locate where the passage defines 'sensory speech' (kalam hissi) and 'psychical speech' (kalam nafsi). Note their characteristics.
Sensory speech involves letters/sounds; psychical speech is an eternal attribute.
3
Understand the homonymy
The passage states that 'kalam Allah' is used for both meanings. Ask yourself why the same term can refer to two different things without ambiguity in context.
This is called ishtirak (homonymy) and is resolved by convention (urf).
4
Explore the implications
Reflect on why psychical speech cannot be divided or described with time-related attributes. How does this affect the understanding of divine speech in scriptures and prophethood?
The passage connects this to the division of speech into command, prohibition, etc., which applies only to sensory speech.
5
Address the misconception
Consider the common misunderstanding that God's speech to Moses was a created sound. The passage clarifies it was psychical speech according to the majority of Ash'aris and some Maturidis.
This guards against anthropomorphism and createdness of the divine attribute.

Know that 'the speech of God' is used homonymously for the sensory and the psychical, which is the eternal attribute. So it is a conventional reality in each. The sensory is what is by letter and sound, and its denotation is part of the denotation of the eternal psychical speech, which is subsistent in His essence. The psychical is what is not by letter or sound, nor described with precedence or delay, nor division, nor beginning nor end, and it is eternal, not created. Commentary
This passage is a detailed clarification of the term 'kalam Allah' (divine speech). It states that the term is used homonymously (bi'l-ishtirak) for sensory (hissi) and psychical (nafsi) speech. The psychical speech is the eternal attribute itself, not composed of letters or sounds, and it is not subject to division, precedence, or delay. The sensory speech consists of letters and sounds and denotes part of the meanings of the psychical speech. The scriptures (heavenly books) indicate some of those meanings in detail, but only God encompasses all. The psychical speech includes all necessary, impossible, and possible things in detail. The passage also discusses the communication (taklim) to Moses on the mountain, which, according to Ash'aris and some Maturidis, was through psychical speech. Finally, the division of speech into command, prohibition, news, etc., applies to the sensory speech's denotations, not to the eternal attribute itself, which is indivisible.

Allah's speech, hearing, and sight are coeternal, non-sensory attributes that perfectly disclose all existence.

This lesson examines the divine attributes of speech, hearing, and sight as presented by the commentator. These are among the essential attributes of Allah, and the passage clarifies their eternal nature and their functional distinction from one another and from divine knowledge.
