Refutation of Natural Causality and Inherent Power
Sanusi refutes those who attribute effects to nature, cause, or inherent power, declaring it kufr or bid'a.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sunni theologians reject belief in independent natural causation and inherent powers, and how such views lead to logical impossibilities like infinite regress or circular reasoning.

Core Idea
Only Allah is the true agent; attributing effects to nature or inherent power is kufr or bid'a.

In this lesson, we examine a poetic passage from Sanusi's al-Kharida that refutes false doctrines about causality. The author clearly distinguishes between what constitutes kufr (disbelief) and what constitutes bid'a (innovation). He then provides a rational argument: if creatures had independent causal power, it would lead to impossible consequences like infinite regress (tasalsul) or circularity (dawr).

The passage is from a didactic poem, so each line delivers a self-contained point.

These rulings are based on the principle that only Allah has true causal efficacy.
3
Understand the argument against inherent power
Read the last two lines: if one claims an inherent power, it must itself be temporally originated (hadith), leading to tasalsul and dawr. Ask why these are impossible.
Tasalsul (infinite regress) and dawr (circularity) are both logically invalid in kalam.

Negation of Natural Causality: Allah Alone Creates All Effects
Commentator explains Ash'ari kasb doctrine and denies causal efficacy to created things.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari doctrine that all effects are directly created by Allah alone, and that created things have no independent causal power.

Core Idea
Created things have no real effect; Allah alone creates all outcomes.

This passage from the commentary on Al-Kharida explains a central Ash'ari principle: the denial of natural causality. The commentator clarifies that when we say 'fire burns' or 'food satiates,' we do not mean that these things cause the effect by their own power, but rather that Allah creates the effect at the moment of contact. The passage uses the concept of kasb to distinguish human responsibility from divine creation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the meaning of kasb
The passage begins by quoting 'You used to do' and explains human actions are in terms of acquisition (kasb/iktisab), not creation (ijad/ikhtira'). Understand this key distinction: humans acquire actions, Allah creates them.
Kasb is a core Ash'ari concept that reconciles divine omnipotence with human accountability.
2
Note the denial of causal efficacy
The commentator then extends the same principle to all ordinary phenomena: fire, food, water, stars, knife. They have no effect by nature, by causal power, or by a deposited power.
This denial includes three categories: nature (tab'), causal power ('illa), and deposited power (quwwa mawda'a).
3
Recognize the exclusive divine agency
The passage concludes that all effect belongs to Allah alone, by pure choice, upon the existence of these things. This affirms that Allah is the sole agent.
The Quranic verse about Prophet Ibrahim and the fire serves as proof.
4
Reflect on the Quranic proof
The commentator cites the verse 'We said, O fire, be cool and safe' to demonstrate that even fire's burning can be suspended by Allah's command.

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory note on the Ash'ari doctrine of kasb (acquisition) and the negation of natural causality. The commentator begins by quoting part of a Quranic verse 'You used to do' (كنتم تعملون) and explains that human actions are attributed to servants only from the perspective of acquisition (kasb and iktisab), not from the perspective of origination (ijad) or invention (ikhtira'). This is a fundamental distinction in Ash'ari theology: Allah is the sole creator of actions, while humans acquire them. The commentator then extends this principle to all ordinary phenomena: fire does not burn by its nature (tab'), food does not satiate by itself, water does not quench thirst or cause plants to grow, stars do not ripen fruit, knives do not cut. He explicitly denies that these things have any effect—neither by nature, nor by an inherent causal power ('illa), nor by a power that Allah has deposited (quwwa mawda'a) in them. Instead, the effect belongs entirely to Allah alone, by His sheer choice (bi-mahd ikhtiyarihi) at the moment these things exist. This is supported by the verse in which Allah commanded the fire to be cool and safe for Prophet Ibrahim. The passage thus guards against any form of belief that created entities have independent causal efficacy.

A faa'ida distinguishing tab' and 'illa effects in terms of conditionality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the effect of nature (tab') is conditional and the effect of a cause ('illa) is unconditional, and why this distinction matters for understanding kalam beliefs about causality.

Core Idea
Nature works only when conditions are met; a cause works unconditionally.

This short 'benefit' passage clarifies a key conceptual distinction between two types of supposed causal efficacy that were mentioned earlier as erroneous beliefs. Before we understand why Ash'aris reject both, we need to grasp how they differ according to their advocates. The passage explains that nature (tab') requires conditions and absence of obstacles, while a cause ('illa) does not—it necessitates its effect immediately. Let's read the passage and see the examples.

1
Read the passage and translation

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive classification of theological positions on the nature of causality.
