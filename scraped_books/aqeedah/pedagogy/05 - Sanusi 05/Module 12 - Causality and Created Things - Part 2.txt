The Five Positions on Created Power
Sanusi enumerates four false positions on created power and affirms the first.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the five positions on the effect of created power and why only the first is correct according to Sanusi.

Core Idea
Created power has a dependent effect; only Allah creates independently.

Sanusi now addresses a key dispute: Does the power Allah creates in humans actually affect their actions? Various sects and scholars have answered differently. In this passage, he enumerates five positions, declares four false, and affirms the Ash'ari middle path of acquisition (kasb). He also expresses astonishment that some respected scholars were attributed with erroneous views.

Guided Path
1
Read the enumeration of positions
Read the Arabic passage and identify each of the five positions on created power. Note who each is attributed to: the second to Imam al-Haramayn, the third to al-Qadi, the fourth to the Jabriyya, the fifth to the Qadariyya.
The first position is not explicitly described here but is the Ash'ari view affirmed later.
2
Notice the author's evaluation
The author clearly states that all positions except the first are false. Underline the sentence: 'وهذه الأقوال كلها باطلة ما عدا الأول'.
3
Consider the amazement
Sanusi expresses amazement that the second position was attributed to Imam al-Haramayn, given his own writings support the Ash'ari doctrine. Ask: Why would Sanusi find this contradictory?
This highlights the importance of verifying attributions and not relying on isolated reports.
4
Connect to the theory of kasb
Reflect on how the correct position aligns with the Ash'ari theory of acquisition: the servant's power is created by Allah and has an effect that is dependent, not independent.
This resolves the tension between divine omnipotence and human responsibility.

Proof of Concurrent Temporally Originated Power
Sanusi proves concurrent temporally originated power and explains its basis in accident nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the kalam argument for the existence of a temporally originated power concurrent with its effect, based on the necessary distinction between forced and voluntary movement, and why this concurrence is grounded in the nature of accidents.

Core Idea
The distinction between forced and voluntary movement proves that power appears at the time of action.

Sanusi begins a concise refutation of the Jabriyya determinists by demonstrating that voluntary movement necessarily implies a power that is present at the time of the action. He then clarifies that this power is an accident, and its concurrence with the effect follows from the transient nature of accidents. This passage lays the foundation for understanding human agency within Sunni kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice the two distinct parts: the proof from movement distinction and the explanation about accidents.
The passage is dense; take it slowly.
2
Identify the proof
Find the key phrase 'الفرق الضروري بين حركة الاضطرار وحركة الاختيار' and understand how this distinction leads to affirming concurrent powers.
3
Understand the nature of the power
Sanusi states that concurrence is not because it is a power, but because it is an accident. Explore why accidents have the ruling of non-existence.
Accidents do not persist, so they must be concurrent with their effect.
4
Apply to refutation
Consider how this argument refutes the Jabriyya who deny human power entirely.

Commentary
This passage begins a refutation of the Jabriyya (determinists) by affirming the existence of a temporally originated power (qudra haditha) that is concurrent with its effect. The proof is based on the necessary distinction between involuntary and voluntary movement. Since voluntary movement implies a power that is present at the time of the action, unlike forced movement where no such power is present. Sanusi then notes that the concurrence of the power with its effect is the position of Imam al-Haramayn and many other Sunni imams. He clarifies that this property of concurrence is not due to the power being a power per se, but because it is an accident (arad). Accidents, by their nature, are characterized by non-existence (they do not subsist for two consecutive moments), so they must be concurrent with their effect since they cannot precede it.

Refutation of the Doctrine of Tawallud (Generation of Effects)
Sanusi refutes tawallud by logical deduction and lists its absurdities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the doctrine of tawallud (generation of effects) is rationally impossible according to Sanusi, and what logical contradictions it entails.

Core Idea
Created power cannot produce effects outside its own locus—tawallud is absurd.

In this passage, Sanusi builds on his earlier proof that a created power cannot affect its own locus. He now extends that conclusion to show that it cannot generate effects in another locus through an intermediary. This refutes the Mu'tazilite doctrine of tawallud, which they used to explain secondary causation. Sanusi highlights several logical contradictions that follow from their view.

Guided Path
1
Recall the earlier conclusion
Start by remembering that created power cannot affect its own locus. This is the foundation for the refutation.
2
Identify the doctrine of tawallud
Recognize that the Qadariyya claim effects can be generated through an intermediary, called tawallud. Examples: throwing a stone, striking with a sword.
The examples show effects that seem produced indirectly.
3
List the absurdities
Sanusi states that tawallud leads to contradictions: an effect between two agents, an act without an agent, or an agent without will and knowledge.
Contemplate why each of these is impossible.
4
Consider the majority consensus
The majority of scholars reject generation even for natural processes like satiety from eating. This further weakens the doctrine.
This shows the doctrine is not universally accepted.
5
Reflect on the overall argument
Connect the conditional premise, the examples, the absurdities, and the consensus to see the complete refutation.
Sanusi's argument is deductive and cumulative.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation (ibṭāl) of the doctrine of tawallud (generation of effects), which was held by the Mu'tazila and Qadariyya. The argument begins with a conditional: if it is established that a created power (al-qudrah al-ḥādithah) cannot produce an effect in its own locus (maḥall), then it logically follows that it cannot produce an effect outside its locus through something it has power over (maqdūr). Sanusi gives examples such as throwing a stone or striking with a sword, where the movement of the hand (the locus of power) is followed by the stone's movement or the sword's cutting, which are outside the locus. The Mu'tazila call this secondary effect 'tawallud'. Sanusi then condemns them as the 'Magians of this community' for attributing causal efficacy to creatures. He adds that their doctrine entails multiple absurdities: an effect between two agents, an act without an agent, or an agent without will and knowledge of the effect—all of which are impossible. He further notes that the majority of scholars reject generation in cases like satiety from eating, which undermines the theory. The refutation concludes by invoking divine assistance.

The Ash'ari View on Created Power
Sanusi defines the Ash'ari view on created power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari view that created power has no causal effect and is merely concurrent with its object, like knowledge.

Core Idea
Ash'ari: created power has no effect; it is like knowledge.

Sanusi begins by stating the position of Ahl al-Haqq (the Ash'aris) on the nature of created power. This view denies that human power has any causal role in bringing about actions—rather, it is simply a concurrent attribute with no effect. This is a foundational principle of Ash'ari occasionalism.

Guided Path
1
Read the Ash'ari passage
Read the Arabic text for the Ash'ari position carefully. Notice the key phrase 'لا تأثير لها في شيء من الممكنات' (has no effect on anything among contingent things).
This is the core claim.
2
Analyze the analogy with knowledge
Sanusi compares the relation of power to its object to the relation of knowledge to its object. Knowledge does not affect its object; it simply corresponds to it. Similarly, created power does not cause its maqdūr.
This analogy emphasizes the lack of causal influence.
3
Understand the locus restriction
The passage adds that created power is only related to what occurs in its own locus (maḥall). What is outside the locus has no relation to power at all.
This further limits the scope of human power.

Commentary
This passage begins by stating the Ash'ari position (madhhab ahl al-ḥaqq) on created power (al-qudra al-ḥāditha). According to the Ash'aris, created power has no effect (lā ta'thīr lahā) on any contingent thing (mumkināt). Instead, it is related to its object of power (maqdūr) in a relationship that does not involve causing that object to exist. The author compares this to knowledge: knowledge relates to its object without affecting it. So created power is merely concurrent with the act, not causing it. The one exception: created power is only related to what occurs in its own locus (maḥall), not to things outside that locus. For example, the power in a hand is related to the hand's motion, but not to the effect of that motion on another object. The author then contrasts the Mu'tazilite view: they hold that the servant is the inventor (mukhtari') of his actions. While they agree that created power directly relates only to what is in its locus, they claim that the act in the locus is a cause (sabab) that brings about an effect (musabbab) outside the locus. Both the cause and the effect are considered under the servant's power: the cause directly, the effect indirectly by means of performing the cause. The author adds a nuance: the Mu'tazila only claim tawallud (generation) in the case of theoretical knowledge (al-ʿilm al-naẓarī). For them, theoretical reflection (al-naẓar) generates knowledge within the same locus (the mind), not outside it. This is their sole exception to the principle that generated effects occur outside the locus. Thus the passage lays out the key point of dispute: for Ash'aris, only God creates all actions; for Mu'tazila, humans create their own acts via causality.

The Mu'tazilite View on Created Power and Causality
Sanusi describes the Mu'tazilite theory of causality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite view that humans create their actions and that the act in the locus is a cause for effects outside the locus, with the exception of theoretical knowledge.

Core Idea
Mu'tazila: humans invent actions; cause and effect are under human power.

After presenting the Ash'ari position, Sanusi turns to the Mu'tazilite view. The Mu'tazila agree that power directly acts only in its locus, but they argue that the act in the locus is a cause that produces effects outside. They also maintain that only theoretical knowledge is generated (tawallud) within the same locus.

This establishes human agency.

Identify the cause-effect structure
The Mu'tazila hold that the act in the locus is a cause (sabab) for something outside the locus (musabbab). Both are under human power: the cause directly, the effect indirectly by bringing about the cause.
This is the core of their causal theory.
3
Examine the exception for theoretical knowledge
The passage notes that the Mu'tazila only acknowledge tawallud (generation) in the case of theoretical knowledge: reflection (naẓar) generates knowledge in the same locus (the mind). This is their sole exception to the principle that generated effects occur outside the locus.
This nuance distinguishes their view on knowledge from other effects.

Definition and Refutation of Tawallud
Sanusi defines, traces origin, and refutes tawallud

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite doctrine of tawallud and why Sanusi refutes it based on the impossibility of a single effect having two agents.

Core Idea
A single effect cannot come from two distinct agents.

Sanusi begins by defining tawallud, the Mu'tazilite theory of generated causation. He traces its roots to the philosophers' concept of natural causes and then refutes it using a logical argument: one effect cannot have two causes.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of tawallud
Read the Arabic definition carefully: 'إيجاد حادث بواسطة مقدور بالقدرة الحادثة'. Notice what constitutes a generating cause and its effect.
The definition involves a created power producing an effect through a cause.
2
Understand the philosophical origin
The passage explains that the Mu'tazila took this idea from the philosophers' natural causes, which can be impeded, unlike rational causes. See how the Mu'tazila adapted it and changed terminology.
3
Grasp the refutation argument
Sanusi argues that a single effect cannot be produced by two agents: the generating cause and the servant's power. This shows the doctrine is logically incoherent.
This is the key argument: one effect, one agent.

The reality of tawallud (generation) according to them is: bringing into existence an originated thing by means of an object of power through created power. And this doctrine, they only took it from the doctrine of the philosophers (al-falāsifa) concerning natural causes (al-asbāb al-ṭabī'iyya); for they claimed that nature (al-ṭabī'a) affects its effect (maf'ūl) as long as no impediment (māni') prevents it, and it is not with them like rational causes (al-'ilal al-'aqliyya) that necessitate the judgments (al-aḥkām) by their essences (dhawātihā), since it is not permissible for an impediment to prevent them. So al-Mu'tazila took that and named it tawallud, and they did not make the ruling of the generating cause (al-sabab al-muwallid) like the rational cause, due to the possibility that generation may be prevented by an impediment. Then they changed the expression so that their source would not appear; so they said: it is the act of the agent of the cause! And this, when investigated, has no result; because one effect (al-athar al-wāḥid) cannot be established for two agents (mu'aththirayn); therefore, from the necessity of the cause's effect in it is the impossibility of power's effect in it. And the saying of the one who says: 'It affects it by means of the cause' amounts in the conclusion to saying that it is the act of its cause, just as the Creator, according to them, is the act of the servant while the servant is the inventor of his act, and his act was not an act of God Almighty, but they forbid its attribution to God.

Commentary
This passage defines and critiques the Mu'tazilite concept of tawallud (generation). It begins with the definition: 'The reality of tawallud according to them is: bringing into existence an originated thing by means of an object of power through created power.' In other words, a human performs an act (the cause) and as a result, another event (the effect) is generated—e.g., motion of the hand generates motion of a key. The author then traces the origin: the Mu'tazila took this idea from the philosophers (falāsifa) who believed in natural causes. The philosophers held that nature (ṭabī'a) produces its effect unless an impediment (māniʿ) prevents it—unlike rational causes which necessarily produce their effects and cannot be impeded. The Mu'tazila adopted this model, calling it tawallud, and allowed that the generation might be blocked by an impediment. To hide their borrowing, they changed terminology, saying: 'It is the act of the agent of the cause'—meaning the servant is the agent of the cause, and the effect is the act of that agent by means of the cause. The author refutes this: when examined, the claim has no substance. The key argument: a single effect (al-athar al-wāḥid) cannot be produced by two distinct agents. If the cause (e.g., the hand's motion) truly produces the effect, then the servant's power (qudra) cannot also produce it. If the effect is attributed to the servant's power, it cannot also be attributed to the cause. So the Mu'tazilite position amounts to saying the effect is the act of its cause, not of the servant's power directly. Moreover, they claim the servant is the inventor of his act, yet they deny that God creates the act—but they also forbid attributing the act to God. The passage thus concludes that the doctrine is incoherent: an effect must have a single agent, and the Mu'tazila cannot consistently maintain both that the servant invents his act and that the act is generated by a cause.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and refutation of the Mu'tazili doctrine of tawallud (generation). The author (Sanusi) first gives the technical definition: tawallud is causing an event indirectly through an object of power (maqdūr) using a created power. He then traces the origin of this doctrine to the Greek philosophical theory of natural causes, where nature produces effects unless impeded. The Mu'tazila adopted this and called it tawallud, but they did not treat the generative cause as necessary, allowing for prevention. He then critiques this: a single effect cannot have two agents (the cause and the power). If the cause influences it, then the servant's power cannot influence it. Constant conjunction does not prove causation – it is merely God's custom.

Sanusi now examines one of the Mu'tazila's key arguments for the reality of generated acts (tawallud). Refuting the Mu'tazila on Tawallud with Counterexamples and Inconsistency
Sanusi refutes the Mu'tazila by listing counterexamples and rebutting their excuse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi refutes the Mu'tazila on tawallud by pointing out their own inconsistent denials of generation and by exposing the absurd consequences of their partial acceptance.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila's own examples undercut their theory of generated acts.

Sanusi continues his critique of the Mu'tazila's theory of tawallud (generated acts). He now employs a powerful refutation: he lists cases where the Mu'tazila themselves admit that no generation occurs despite apparent causal connections. This inconsistency, when combined with the absurd implication of partial generation, leaves their position untenable. Additionally, he anticipates and refutes their possible excuse of non-constancy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the counterexamples
Read the list of phenomena where the Mu'tazila deny generation: satiety, thirst-quenching, illness, heat from friction, etc. Notice that these occur according to motives and intentions, yet the Mu'tazila agree no generation happens.
2
Understand the partial admission
Some Mu'tazila admit generation in satiety and heat. Note how Sanusi uses this to force an absurd conclusion: if one generated act is a body (fire from flint), then by similarity all bodies would be generated, which is impossible.
3
Examine the latent fire objection
The Mu'tazila might claim fire was latent in the flint. Sanusi dismisses this as absurd because there is no fire before striking, as seen in the marakh tree example.
This shows the generated thing is not latent but appears anew.
4
Refute the constancy excuse
The Mu'tazila might say they deny generation in the first list because the association is not constant. Sanusi retorts that the same non-constancy applies to their own examples (throwing, wounding), so their criterion fails.
This undermines their entire theory.

Sanusi retorts that the same lack of constancy applies to the examples they do claim as generated, like throwing and wounding; sometimes a thrower fails to hit, etc. So their criterion fails.

The Condition of Regularity in Generation and Its Counterexamples
Sanusi refutes the condition of regularity by citing counterexamples from Mu'tazilite doctrine.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the Mu'tazilite condition of regularity by demonstrating that their own examples of generation also lack regularity.

Core Idea
Irregularity is universal—it cuts both ways.

In this passage, Sanusi addresses a key objection from the Mu'tazilites: that generation (tawallud) only occurs when the cause regularly produces the effect. He turns their own criterion against them by pointing out that the very examples they accept as generated—throwing, wounding, lifting—also fail to be regular. This reveals an inconsistency in their position and defends the Ash'ari view that generation is not tied to empirical regularity.

Each example shows the effect is not constant.

Mu'tazilite Views on Lifting Heavy Objects
Mu'tazilite views on whether lateral movement suffices for lifting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite debate on whether the same movement can both move an object sideways and lift it, and the empirical reasoning used by Abu Hashim to argue for additional movements.

Core Idea
Lifting requires extra movements beyond lateral motion, says Abu Hashim.

In this lesson, we explore a specific debate within Mu'tazilite kalām about the nature of movement. While early Mu'tazilites believed that the same leaning (iʿtimād) that moves an object sideways also lifts it, Abu Hashim argued that we must add extra movements for lifting. His reasoning is based on what we actually observe: a person might move an object sideways but be unable to lift it. This passage shows how Mu'tazilites used empirical experience to ground their theory of generation.

1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then read the literal translation. Notice that the text presents two contrasting views on the same issue.

Look for the early Mu'tazilites' view and Abu Hashim's view. The early ones say the same leaning lifts; Abu Hashim says additional movements are needed.

Analyze Abu Hashim's argument

This is a key methodological point for Mu'tazilites.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite theory of tawallud (generated acts). Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite theory of tawallud (generated acts). The author begins by stating that the true doctrine (al-madhab al-ḥaqq) is to reject the entire principle of tawallud and to attribute all possible things (mumkināt) directly to Allah from the outset. Then, for the sake of argument (jadalan), the author hypothetically concedes the principle of tawallud to show that even then the Mu'tazilite views are self-contradictory. First, the older Mu'tazilite view is nullified by the argument of Abū Hāshim, and Abū Hāshim's own view is nullified because it entails the conjunction of two similars (ijtimā' al-mithlayn)—he requires an increase of movements, which is impossible. Requiring an additional movement in the upward direction beyond that which already moves it is a condition for something that can be realized without that condition, thus contradicting the nature of a condition. Next, the author discusses the second issue: a group carrying a heavy object where each individual could carry it alone. The question put to 'Abbād al-Ṣaymarī is whether the part specific to each carrier is determinate or indeterminate. Both options lead to absurdity: an indeterminate part cannot be raised, and a determinate part has no basis for specification since all parts are equal. Commentary
This passage is a series of objections and responses concerning the theory of vision based on rays emanating from the eye. The opponents initially claimed that only objects directly contacted by rays are visible. The refutation points out that accidents like motion, rest, conjunction, and separation (al-akwan) and colors are seen, but rays cannot contact them because rays are bodies and accidents cannot touch bodies. The opponents then refined their theory: the visible is either what the ray contacts or what inheres in what the ray contacts. The refutation counters that if that were true, tastes and smells (which inhere in bodies) would be visible. The opponents further restrict to things that 'admit visibility,' but the refutation notes that distant objects are seen without their color, showing the theory still fails. The passage ends with an incomplete question about a body. The commentary follows the author's order: first the objection about accidents, then the opponent's answer, then the objection about tastes/smells, then the opponent's further restriction, then the counterpoint about distant objects and colors, and final partial question.
