God's Transcendence: Five Negations
Sanusi enumerates negations asserting God's transcendence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the five negations that establish God's absolute difference from created things and why each negation is necessary.

Core Idea
God must be declared free from all attributes of contingent things.

Sanusi now moves from affirming God's power and will to establishing His transcendence. He lists five specific negations that safeguard God's uniqueness and prevent any similarity to created beings. This passage is a cornerstone of tanzih, showing that any hint of corporeality or spatial limitation would undermine God's divinity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the translation. Notice the five negations Sanusi mentions and the logical consequence he draws.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the five negations
List the five things Sanusi says God must be exalted above: being a body, subsisting in a body, being adjacent to a body, being in a direction relative to a body, and being depicted in imagination.
These are all attributes of contingent things.
3
Understand the argument
Sanusi argues that any of these would imply similarity to contingent things, leading to the absurd consequence that what applies to them (origination) would apply to God, undermining His pre-eternity and divinity.
The argument is a reductio ad absurdum.

If God were similar to them, then the same necessities that apply to contingent things (like origination) would apply to Him, undermining His pre-eternity, persistence, and all divine attributes.

The Attribute of Being Unlike Created Things
Sanusi proves God must be transcendent above all created attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the proof that God is unlike all created things and how any form of similarity would contradict His divinity.

Core Idea
God's transcendence is proven by the impossibility of any similarity to creation.

Sanusi has already shown that God must be eternal and not originated. Now he extends that reasoning to show God is completely unlike everything He created. This passage lists five specific forms of similarity that are impossible for God and explains why denying similarity is essential to His Lordship.

Thus mukhālafah is not just a negative attribute but a positive necessity.

Proof that the Creator Cannot Be a Nature or Necessary Cause
Sanusi argues that the Creator cannot be a nature or necessary cause and refutes the objection of impediment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's argument that attributing a delay to an impediment leads to impossibility, thereby proving that the Creator cannot be a nature or necessitating cause.

Core Idea
A nature that acts by necessity cannot delay its effect without an infinite regress.

Sanusi has already established that the Creator cannot be subject to temporal attributes. Now he extends this reasoning to show that the Creator cannot be a 'nature' or a 'necessitating cause' that acts by blind necessity. Would a delay in the effect of such a nature be possible? Sanusi anticipates an objection and refutes it with a powerful argument that leads to either the non-existence of the Eternal or an infinite regress.

Guided Path
1
Understand the previous conclusion
Sanusi begins by stating a conclusion from earlier: that something cannot be characterized by a temporal attribute or an accident in place of its permissible opposites, because that would imply either the attribute is eternal or the non-existence of the Creator continues—both impossible.
This sets the foundation for the new argument.
2
Follow the deduction about the Creator
From that impossibility, Sanusi directly concludes that the Creator cannot be a nature or a necessitating cause. Notice the logical link: a nature or necessitating cause would require temporal attributes, which have been ruled out.
This is a crucial step in Islamic theology regarding divine causality.
3
Analyze the objection and refutation
Sanusi presents an objection: perhaps the delay in the effect of a nature is due to an impediment or missing condition. Then he refutes it by showing that the same argument would apply to that impediment or condition, leading to either the non-existence of the Eternal (God would not be always the Creator) or an infinite regress, both of which are impossible.
This refutation strengthens the original conclusion.

Commentary
This passage is a proof and an objection-and-answer. Sanusi first concludes from a previous argument that the Creator cannot be characterized by a temporal attribute or an accident in place of their permissible opposites, otherwise either the attribute would be eternal or the non-existence of the Creator would continue—both impossible. Then he presents an objection: perhaps the delay in the effect of a nature is due to an impediment or lack of a condition. He refutes this by showing that if one posits an impediment or condition for the delay, the same argument applies to that impediment or condition, leading to either the non-existence of the eternal (i.e., God would not have always been the Creator) or an infinite regress of impediments and conditions. Thus, the objection fails.

Why God Has No Obligations, Injustice, or Purposes
Sanusi proves impossibility of obligation, injustice, and purpose for God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational arguments proving that obligation, injustice, and purpose in action are impossible for Allah, affirming His absolute sovereignty and perfection.

Core Idea
God is above obligation, injustice, and purpose—He acts from absolute freedom.

In this passage, Sanusi makes a crucial move in establishing divine transcendence: he shows that Allah cannot be subject to obligation (wujub) or injustice (zulm), and therefore His actions cannot be for any purpose (gharad). These claims are not arbitrary; they are grounded in rational proofs that protect tawhid from anthropomorphic limitations. Let’s explore how he builds his case.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the concise style; each clause carries a proof.
2
Identify the two impossibilities
The passage first states that obligation and injustice are impossible for God. Find the reasons given for each impossibility.
Obligation would require disobedience; injustice would require acting improperly.
3
Understand the purpose argument
Sanusi deduces that God's actions cannot be for a purpose. Follow his reasoning: if there were a purpose, it would either oblige God or make Him subjugated—both impossible.
The Quranic verse (28:68) is cited to reinforce the point.
4
Examine the additional argument
The passage ends with a separate argument about the eternality of purpose and action. Understand why this leads to a contradiction.
This refers to an earlier discussion (referenced as 'it has already passed').
5
Reflect on the implications
Consider why these concepts are essential for a proper understanding of God's attributes and actions.
They safeguard God's transcendence and absolute independence.

Commentary
This passage is a proof that obligation (wujub) and injustice (zulm) are impossible for God. It first argues: obligation would require that some possible things disobey God, which is impossible since nothing disobeys Him; injustice would mean acting improperly, which is impossible due to divine perfection. From this it deduces that God's actions cannot be for a purpose (gharad). Reasoning: if there were a purpose, it would either oblige God to act (so He would be under obligation) or He would not be the true cause, making Him subjugated. Both are impossible. Then cites Quran 28:68 to affirm God's absolute freedom in creation and choice. Finally, it adds another argument: if the purpose were eternal, the action would be eternal, which is false (and the discussion of that point is said to have already passed).

God's Transcendence and the Nature of Reward and Punishment
Sanusi describes God's transcendence and the non-purposeful nature of reward and punishment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that God's attributes are perfect and unchanged, and that reward and punishment are solely by His will without any obligation or need.

Core Idea
God's actions are perfect and do not affect His essence; reward is pure favor, punishment is pure justice.

Sanusi here summarizes key principles of Islamic theology regarding God's transcendence. He affirms that God's power, knowledge, and will are perfect and unchangeable, and that He existed alone before creation. Then he explains the nature of reward and punishment, emphasizing that they are purely by God's will without any external cause. This passage helps the student understand that God is in no need of creation and acts freely.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing, then read the English translation. Notice the flow from divine attributes to reward and punishment.
The passage is dense but each sentence builds on the previous.
2
Identify the three attributes
The passage starts by mentioning God's power, knowledge, and will. Notice that these are described as 'bāhir' (manifest) and that no perfection or deficiency attaches to His essence from them.
3
Grasp the concept of immutability
The phrase 'God existed with nothing, and He is now as He was' affirms God's eternal existence and absolute changelessness. This is a core Ash‘arī belief.
4
Analyze reward and punishment
Reward is given purely by God's favor, not due to any inclination or obligation. Punishment is just, not vengeful. Both are signs of His sovereignty and the submission of all contingents to His will.
Note the negations: 'lā limaylin ilayhi' and 'lā lishifā'i ghayz'.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Each event occurs as it should, according to God's knowledge and will, without adding any perfection or deficiency to Him. This reinforces His absolute self-sufficiency.

Sanusi's Refutation of Nature as Cause of the Self
Sanusi refutes the claim that nature (tab') is present in the existence of the self.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi uses a reductio ad absurdum to refute the claim that nature (ṭabʿ) is the cause of the self's existence, based on the opponents' own principle that a uniform nature produces a uniform spherical shape.

Core Idea
If nature caused your self, you would be a sphere.

This passage presents a crisp logical refutation. Sanusi quotes a statement: 'there is no nature in the existence of your self, otherwise you would be spherical.' He then explains that this is a necessary consequence (ilzām) based on the opponents' own belief that a nature equal from all aspects necessitates a spherical shape in composite things. The argument shows that since the self is not spherical, it cannot be caused by nature — and by extension, not by any cause (ʿilla). We will walk through the logic step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation. Note that the passage is a refutation (ilzām). The author quotes a statement and then unpacks the reasoning.
The key is to see how the opponents' own principle is turned against them.
2
Understand the opponents' principle
The opponents believe that a nature that is uniform in all respects produces a shape that is uniform in all respects, which is spherical. They use this to explain the celestial sphere.
This principle is the foundation of the reductio.
3
Apply the principle to the self
If the self were caused by a uniform nature, it would be spherical. But it is not. Therefore, nature is not the cause. The argument concludes that if nature is negated, cause (ʿilla) is even more soundly negated.
The term 'cause' here refers to a more deterministic cause, nature being a weaker cause.

Commentary
This passage defines the concept of ittihad (union) and provides a logical proof of its impossibility for all beings, whether eternal or temporal. Commentary
This passage is a proof for divine transcendence (tanzih). It begins by restating the impossibility of Allah being described with attributes that would entail likeness to temporal beings. Then it quotes a line of poetry expressing bewilderment in understanding. The commentary then explains the author's statement: 'Because all of that necessitates His likeness to temporal things.' Defining Divine Oneness as Negation of Division and Peer
Sanusi defines oneness as negation of division and peer, then summarizes as negation of two quantities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the core definition of God's oneness as the negation of division and peer, summarized by the negation of continuous and discontinuous quantity.

Core Idea
God's oneness means He cannot be divided and has no rival in divinity.

Sanusi begins by stating the meaning of God being 'one' (wāḥid). After the preliminary classification of unity types, he now defines it as a double negation: God does not accept division, and there is no peer for Him in divinity. He then condenses this into the negation of two kinds of quantity—continuous (length, width, depth) and discontinuous (number). This sets the foundational vocabulary for the rest of the discussion on tawḥīd.

The word 'نفي' appears twice – pay attention to what is denied.

These are technical terms: continuous quantity and discontinuous quantity.

Reflect on the linkage
Ask yourself: how does negating division and negating a peer correspond to negating the two quantities? This connection will be clarified in the commentary.
Think of continuous quantity as internal division (parts) and discontinuous quantity as external peers (multiple individuals of the same kind).

Commentary
This passage defines God's oneness (waḥdāniyya) after the preliminary classification of types of unity. It is a definition passage. Consequently, God is one in His essence (dhāt)—not composed of parts; one in His attributes—no one shares His attributes; and one in His actions—no partner, opponent, or vizier. The author then clarifies two misconceptions: God's unity is not like the indivisibility of a very small particle, for that would make Him a composed atomic substance; nor is it like the simplicity of an abstract meaning, for such meanings are attributes dependent on a subject, whereas God is self-subsistent. Both are impossible for God, as earlier proofs established.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

The Three Demands of Tawhid and the Dilemma of Two Gods
Sanusi enumerates the three demands of tawhid and begins the proof for the second demand with a logical dilemma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the overall structure of Sanusi's argument for the impossibility of a second god, including the three demands of tawhid and the logical dilemma that forms the core proof.

Core Idea
If two gods existed, their wills would either conflict or agree—both are impossible.

Sanusi begins by outlining the three foundational demands of tawhid, then focuses on the second: proving that no partner can exist in divinity. This lesson presents the opening of that proof, where he sets up a logical dilemma: if there were two gods, their universally related wills would either differ or agree regarding every possible act. Both alternatives are later shown to be impossible, establishing the impossibility of multiple gods.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three demands
Read the passage carefully and note the three demands listed by Sanusi. The first demand (unity of essence) was already covered; the second (denial of a partner) is the focus here; the third (dissimilarity to creation) is mentioned for context.
The three demands are: 1) oneness of essence, 2) denial of a peer in divinity and exclusive creation, 3) dissimilarity to all originated things.
2
Understand the logical implication
Sanusi argues that if there were two gods, their wills (and powers) would necessarily relate to every possible thing. When two wills relate to the same act, they must either agree or differ. This is the 'implication' (ملازمة) of his proof.
The necessity of universal relation of God's will and power was established earlier.
3
Recognize the two alternatives
The two alternatives are: they differ (اختلاف) or they agree (اتفاق). Both are claimed to be impossible, and the proof proceeds to demonstrate each.
These two alternatives form the horns of the dilemma.
4
See the overall proof structure
The proof is a classic kalam argument: if there were two gods, it leads to either contradiction (divergence) or subordination (agreement), both of which are impossible. Therefore, two gods cannot exist.
This structure is used in many theological proofs for divine unity.

Commentary
This passage is a proof (برهان) for the second of three demands concerning the oneness of Allah. The author first outlines the three demands: (1) proof of the unity of the essence (no composition or division), (2) denial of a partner or peer in divinity and in exclusive creation, and (3) the oneness meaning dissimilarity to all originated things. He notes that the first demand was already covered earlier. He then focuses on the second demand. The proof is a classic kalam argument: if there were two gods, their wills would either differ (disagree) or agree. Both possibilities are shown to be impossible, so the existence of two gods is impossible. The author first demonstrates the impossibility of disagreement: if two gods will opposite things (e.g., one wills existence of a substance, the other wills non-existence), then either both wills are ineffective (leading to impotence of both) or one is ineffective (leading to impotence of one). He then elaborates three specific impossibilities that arise if both wills are suspended: (a) impotence of both gods, (b) the impossible situation of a locus being empty of both contradictories, and (c) the paradoxical occurrence of prevention without an impediment. Next, he addresses the scenario where the wills agree but one will is specifically related (i.e., not universal). This leads to the impossibility that one god's will would not be universally related, which contradicts the necessary attribute of God. The argument is structured as a logical dilemma, thoroughly dismantling any possibility of multiple gods.

Impossibility of Disagreement Between Two Gods
Sanusi gives two reasons why disagreement between two gods is impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two logical arguments that prove disagreement between two gods is impossible.

Core Idea
Two gods cannot disagree without logical contradiction.

This short but dense passage from Sanusi presents two powerful arguments that rule out the possibility of disagreement between two gods. Using concrete examples like motion and rest, Sanusi shows that any scenario of conflicting wills leads to absurdity. The arguments underscore that a second god would either be powerless or the very nature of possibility would be violated.

Commentary
This passage provides two arguments demonstrating the impossibility of two gods disagreeing. The first argument uses the example of motion and rest: if two gods exist, and one wills motion specifically for some entity, then the other willing rest for the same entity becomes impossible because rest would then be prevented by the first will, contradicting its possibility—this inverts the nature of possibilities. The second argument concerns a 'preventer' (mani'): if one god's will attaches to the opposite of what the other wills, then the preventer (e.g., the god willing motion preventing rest) would be attributed with a ruling (the prevention) that does not properly reside in it, which is absurd. Both cases show that disagreement leads to logical contradictions, hence impossible.

Impossibility of Possible Agreement Between Two Gods
Sanusi argues that possible agreement between two gods implies possible disagreement, which is impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why possible agreement between two gods is impossible because it logically entails possible disagreement, which is already proven impossible.

Core Idea
Possible agreement between two gods would imply possible disagreement, which is impossible.

Sanusi continues his argument against multiple gods by examining a scenario where two gods could potentially agree. He shows that if their agreement is merely possible, then disagreement must also be possible. Since disagreement has already been shown impossible, the scenario collapses. This lesson walks through that logical deduction.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the English translation. Let Sanusi's own words set the stage.
Notice the condensed theological style.
2
Identify the logical structure
The argument is a conditional proof: if agreement is possible (antecedent), then disagreement is possible (consequent). Identify these two parts.
The passage explicitly states the implication.
3
Understand why the consequent is false
Recall that earlier arguments proved disagreement between two gods is impossible. Since the consequent is false, the antecedent (possible agreement) must also be false.
This is a classic modus tollens argument.

Commentary
This passage discusses the second type of agreement between two gods: possible agreement (ittifaq ja'iz). Sanusi explains that if their agreement is not necessary but merely possible, then by logical correlation, disagreement would also be possible (since possibility is symmetrical between opposites). However, disagreement has already been proven impossible through multiple arguments (as shown earlier). Since the consequent (disagreement being possible) is false, the antecedent (agreement being possible) must also be false. Therefore, possible agreement is impossible. This argument is straightforward: possible agreement entails possible disagreement, but disagreement is impossible, so possible agreement cannot stand.
