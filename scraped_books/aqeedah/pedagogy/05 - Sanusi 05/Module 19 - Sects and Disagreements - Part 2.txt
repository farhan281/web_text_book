Clarifying 'Choice-Based Motion' and the Refutation of the Jabriyya
Sanusi clarifies the meaning of 'choice-based motion' and defends his terminology against criticism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the exact wording of 'choice-based motion' is not essential for refuting the Jabriyya and how a single particular difference suffices to contradict their universal claim.

Core Idea
A single particular difference refutes a universal claim of no difference.

Sanusi addresses a terminological nuance in his Creed. He explains that his phrase 'choice-based motion' means a motion to which choice attaches, not that every acquired act is chosen. He then defends his wording by showing that any expression indicating a particular difference between acts is enough to refute the Jabriyya, who claim no difference exists at all.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition
Read Sanusi's definition of 'choice-based motion' and note his clarification that an acquired act may occur without conscious choice, such as when one is absent-minded.
Key: the motion is one that is suitable for choice, not always chosen.
2
Note the grammatical point
Observe the alternative readings of 'wa hiya hal' (accusative as circumstantial or nominative as predicate) and how this reflects the author's attention to textual precision.
3
Trace the defense strategy
Sanusi admits that using 'acquisition-based motion' would be better, yet his phrasing from Imam al-Haramayn is still valid. The refutation of the Jabriyya does not depend on exact wording.
4
Grasp the logical argument
The Jabriyya claimed a universal negative: no difference between acts. Proving even one particular difference (between some acts) contradicts that. This is a valid logical refutation: a universal negative is contradicted by a particular affirmative.
Universal negative vs particular affirmative.

Refuting al-Jabriyyah with Logical Contradiction
Author refutes jabriyyah using logical contradiction of universal and particular propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the logical principle of universal negative vs. particular affirmative is used to refute the Jabriyyah claim of no distinction in actions.

Core Idea
A universal negative is contradicted by a single particular affirmative.

This passage shows that even simple logical principles can dismantle major theological errors. The Jabriyyah claimed that all human actions are identical with no room for moral distinction. The author points out that if we can find even one example of a distinction, the entire claim collapses. This is the power of contradiction in logic.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context of the passage
Learn that the passage is answering a critique of a definition of aqidah from al-Irshad, and the author shows that any definition can refute the Jabriyyah.
The objection is not the main focus; the logical refutation is.
2
Identify the Jabriyyah claim
The Jabriyyah claim that there is no distinction between actions at all—a universal negative statement.
A universal negative says 'no actions are distinguished'.
3
Recognize the counter-example
The author asserts that there is a distinction between some actions—a particular affirmative. This directly contradicts the universal negative.
Even one example of distinction disproves the claim.
4
Apply the logical principle
In Aristotelian logic, a universal negative (E) is contradicted by a particular affirmative (I). They cannot both be true.
This is a standard rule of opposition.

Two Refutations of Jabriyya: Denial of the Self-Evident and Contradiction of Revelation
Sanusi explains the invalidity of Jabriyya via two arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two main arguments against Jabriyya: that it denies self-evident necessary knowledge and that it contradicts the Sharia's system of obligation.

Core Idea
Jabriyya is refuted by both reason and revelation.

This passage from Sanusi's commentary unpacks the conclusion that the doctrine of Jabr (determinism) is invalid. After establishing the existence of acquired power and its limited role, Sanusi now shows that Jabriyya not only violates a necessary distinction everyone knows intuitively, but also undermines the very structure of Islamic law.

Read the Arabic and translation

The first is about a self-evident distinction; the second about religious obligation.
3
Reflect on the severity

The Sharia removes obligation from impossible acts and obliges only feasible ones—this presupposes human capability.

Mu'tazilite Disagreement on Tawallud
Objection to claimed Mu'tazilite consensus on tawallud by citing exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Mu'tazilite theory of tawallud is challenged by internal disagreements, and why claiming consensus is problematic.

Core Idea
Even within the same school, scholars disagreed on whether generated acts belong to the agent or to God.

This lesson presents an objection to the idea that all Mu'tazilites agreed on the nature of generated acts (tawallud). The questioner cites two prominent Mu'tazilites, al-Nazzam and Hafs al-Qird, who held nuanced positions that break the supposed consensus. Examining their views reveals internal diversity and challenges the coherence of the Mu'tazilite stance.

Guided Path
1
Understand the claim of consensus
Imam al-Haramayn is reported to have said that the Mu'tazilites agreed that the generated act is the act of the agent of the cause.
This claim sets up the objection.
2
Examine al-Nazzam's view
Al-Nazzam attributed generated events to God's creation of natures and properties in bodies, not to human agents.
He did not say they are the act of the agent.
3
Examine Hafs al-Qird's distinction
Hafs al-Qird differentiated: acts that depend on the causer's choice (e.g., cutting) are the agent's act; those that don't (e.g., falling) are not.
This splits the category of generated acts.
4
Reflect on the implication
These examples show internal division, undermining the claim of unanimous agreement among the Mu'tazilites.
The objection is left open, unresolved in this passage.

Commentary
This passage presents and refutes the first of three schools on the execution of divine threat. Sanusi begins by stating that the first school, that of the Batiniyya (an esoteric sect), claims that Quranic threats of punishment are merely for intimidation and will not actually occur. He then examines their three arguments. First, they cite Q. 39:16, but Sanusi points out that the intimidation mentioned there is worldly, while the threatened punishment will occur in the Hereafter. Second, they argue from God's mercy: a merciful God would not punish a weak creature. Sanusi dismisses this as based on rationalist reasoning (التحسين العقلي), which is false. Third, they argue from divine predetermination: since all actions are by God's will and creation, humans have no role, so punishment would be unjust. Sanusi labels this as forbidden inquiry into qadar (divine decree) and cites a hadith commanding silence when qadar is discussed. He concludes by affirming God's absolute freedom and our limited knowledge, thereby rejecting the Batiniyya's position.

Sanusi explains the method of establishing tafḍīl and presents the disagreement on its certainty.

Learning Objective

Tafḍīl is based on transmission, not analogy, and its certainty is debated.

The issue of preferring some Companions over others is a delicate matter. Scholars first clarified how such preference should be established. Is it by analogy or by transmitted texts?
