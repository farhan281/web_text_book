Contingent Properties Prove Divine Will
Sanusi refutes preponderance without preponderator and doctrines of nature/cause, proving divine will from contingent bodily properties.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the specific size and quality of the body prove that the Creator has choice (will) and why this refutes the doctrines of nature and necessary cause.

Core Idea
Specific bodily properties prove the Creator's free choice.

Sanusi presents a powerful argument from the contingency of our physical characteristics. By observing that our body has a specific size and quality among many possibilities, we can deduce that its Creator must have chosen this particular form. This reasoning demolishes the idea that things happen by mere nature or necessary cause.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the refuted doctrines
The passage begins by invalidating two false doctrines: preponderance without a preponderator, and ascribing creation to nature or necessary cause. Notice these are stated as headings.
These are the views that Sanusi will refute with his argument.
2
Observe the contingency of bodily properties
Sanusi points out that your body could have had a different specific quantity and quality. This is a key observation: it is not necessary that it be exactly as it is.
Contingency means it could have been otherwise.
3
Understand the conclusion: divine choice
From this contingency, Sanusi concludes that the Maker has choice (اختيار) in specifying your essence with one of the possibilities. This is the core proof of divine will.
The specification implies a choosing agent.
4
Reflect on the refutation
This argument directly undermines the idea of preponderance without a preponderator (that something could occur without a cause) and the doctrines of nature or cause (that properties arise necessarily).

Proof of God's Pre-eternity (Qidam)
Sanusi proves God's pre-eternity and refutes temporal origination through logical contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's rational proof that God must be pre-eternal (qadīm) and why temporal origination leads to logical absurdities.

Core Idea
If God were not pre-eternal, we would face impossible infinite regress or circularity.

Sanusi now turns to a specific divine attribute: pre-eternity (qidam). He argues that the Maker of the universe cannot have come into existence after non-existence, because that would require a regress that never ends or a vicious circle. This brief but powerful proof establishes a foundational attribute of God.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the literal translation to understand the key terms: qadīm, tasalsul, dawr.
2
Understand the definition of pre-eternal
Sanusi defines qadīm as 'not preceded by non-existence'. Grasp that this is a precise logical meaning, not a vague 'old'.
He contrasts it with temporal origination (being preceded by non-existence).
3
Follow the contrapositive argument
The proof argues: if not pre-eternal, then needs a maker. That maker leads to either infinite regress or circularity, both impossible.
Identify the two branches: the original maker is either not an effect of the current maker, or it is.
4
Understand why each option is impossible
Infinite regress: a chain without beginning cannot exist. Circularity: a thing cannot be prior to itself.
These are basic rational axioms in kalam.
5
Appreciate the conclusion
Since every alternative is impossible, the original assumption (that God is not pre-eternal) is false. Therefore God must be pre-eternal.
This establishes qidam as a necessary attribute.

Both are impossible, as infinite regress implies an infinite chain with no beginning, while circularity makes a single thing prior to and preceded by itself. Therefore, the Maker must be pre-eternal.

Sanusi proves God's permanence and deduces the impossibility of perishing for the eternal.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Eternal existence cannot be followed by non-existence.

Sanusi now turns to another essential divine attribute: permanence (baqā'). He argues that God's existence is such that non-existence can never attach to it. Using a concise proof by contradiction, he shows that the Eternal cannot perish. Refuting the Ta'lil Objection: Concomitance Not Causality

Learning Objective

Dependency in attributes is concomitance, not efficient causality.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the eternity of God's attributes and a refutation of objections. It begins with a chapter heading. The author states that all of God's attributes must be eternal. The proof: if any attribute were originated, then the essence of God would either never be free from it (if it is always present) or from its opposite (if it is absent at some point, then its opposite would have to be present, which is also originated). The sign of origination is the occurrence of non-existence after existence. Since the eternal cannot cease to exist, it cannot have an originated attribute. Furthermore, any essence that cannot be realized without an originated thing must itself be originated. This is analogous to the proof for the origination of the world. Then an objection is raised: this reasoning depends on the principle that a substrate capable of a quality is never free from it or its opposite; but perhaps it can be free from both and then acquire them. The answer: if the substrate could be free from both while having capacity, then it could be free from all attributes it can receive, because the capacity is uniform (being a self-attribute). That would lead to the absurdity that a contingent being could be free from all its essential attributes. Moreover, for originated beings, they cannot be free from accidents (akwān) by necessity. For God, He must be qualified by what His actions indicate, i.e., knowledge, power, will. If these were originated, they would depend on other attributes, leading to circularity or infinite regress. Therefore, the attributes must be eternal. From this, it follows that non-existence is impossible for them, hence change is impossible both in essence and attributes. The author then enumerates consequences: God's knowledge cannot be acquired or subject to forgetfulness; His power needs no instrument; His will is without purpose; His attributes are without body parts or speech in letters and sounds, etc., because all such would imply change and origination.

Core Idea

Sanusi presents a second, concise argument against attributing purposes to God's actions. He sets up a clear either-or scenario: the purpose is either eternal or originated. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God's actions cannot have a purpose and why assigning a purpose leads to impossible consequences such as infinite regress or attributing change to Allah.

Core Idea
God's actions need no purpose; any supposed purpose leads to absurdities.

Sanusi begins by refuting the claim that divine actions require a purpose. He presents a proof that if God's actions had a purpose, it would either benefit God (impossible, as it would imply change in His essence) or benefit the action itself (also impossible, as it would lead to circular reasoning or infinite regress). This argument establishes a key Ash'ari tenet: Allah is absolutely free from need and His acts are not motivated by external purposes.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof of infinite regress
Read the first part of the passage where Sanusi argues that if an action is temporally originated and requires a purpose, then that purpose would also require another purpose, leading to an infinite chain of purposes—which is impossible.
This argument is a standard Ash'ari refutation of the idea that God acts for reasons.
2
Examine the two possibilities for purpose
Sanusi then analyzes the two possibilities for what the purpose could be: a benefit returning to God, or a benefit returning to His action. Identify why each is impossible.
3
Understand why both are impossible
The first possibility would necessitate change (hawadith) in Allah's essence, which is impossible. The second would either render God obligated to do what is best (which is not obligatory), or imply that God cannot directly benefit the servant without an action, or lead to circular reasoning or infinite regress.
This demonstrates that any attempt to assign a purpose to divine actions fails.

A proof of its temporal origination: or it is originated and thus stands in need of a purpose, then likewise and it would regress infinitely, leading to an infinite series of originated events, and its falsehood has already been mentioned. Also: the purpose is either a benefit that returns to Him, or to His action. The first is impossible because it necessitates the description of His exalted essence with accidental occurrences; and the second is impossible because there is no obligation to consider what is good and what is best, and because He is able to convey that benefit to the servant, for example, without an intermediary, and because it would entail the cause of something by itself or an infinite regress. (This is because) we would transfer the discussion to that same benefit. They said: If there is no purpose, then the action is foolishness. We say: Foolishness by convention is that which is done with ignorance of consequences, or preferring immediate pleasure, so that the foolish person does what harms him or causes his destruction while he is unaware. And how far is this from the action of the Exalted One, who is above the renewal of perfection or deficiency, from whose knowledge nothing at all escapes, in secret or in public?! And if you have understood, from what has been mentioned, the lack of preference of some actions over others with respect to Him, the Exalted, then you have understood the ignorance of one who trespasses upon the unseen and thinks that the intellect, without a revealed law, can attain to the perception of good and evil with respect to Him, the Mighty and Majestic. Moreover, even if that were conceded to them as a dialectical point, the intellect could not be certain of anything because of the contradictory aspects [lit. faces] of consideration in that matter. Thus, we do not know the obligation of faith nor the prohibition of disbelief except after the coming of the revealed law.

Commentary
This passage is a complex argument defending the Ash'ari position that God's actions are not motivated by purposes (aghrāḍ) and that the human intellect without revelation cannot determine moral value. The passage begins with a proof of the impossibility of assigning a purpose to God's action: if the action were temporally originated, it would require a purpose, leading to an infinite regress of purposes, which is false. Then it analyzes the two possibilities for the purpose—either a benefit returning to God or to His action. Both are impossible: the first because it would necessitate change in God's essence; the second because God is not obliged to do what is most beneficial, He can achieve the benefit without mediation, and it would cause circular reasoning or regress. The objectors then argue that an action without purpose is foolish (safah). The answer redefines safah as acting with ignorance of consequences or preference for immediate pleasure—qualities impossible for God given His perfect knowledge and lack of change. The conclusion is that since God has no preference for one action over another, the intellect cannot independently know good and evil, so we need revelation to know the obligation of faith and the prohibition of disbelief.

Redefining Foolishness and the Need for Revelation
Sanusi redefines foolishness and argues that revelation is necessary for moral knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the conventional definition of foolishness (safah) and why it does not apply to Allah, leading to the conclusion that the intellect alone cannot know moral values without revelation.

Core Idea
True foolishness requires ignorance; God is all-knowing, so His actions are never foolish.

After refuting the claim that God's actions need a purpose, Sanusi addresses the objection that purposeless action is foolish. He defines safah in human terms—acting with ignorance of consequences or preference for immediate pleasure—and shows that these qualities are impossible for God. This leads to a powerful conclusion: since God has no preference among actions, the human intellect cannot independently determine good and evil; we need the revealed law (shar‘) to know the obligations of faith and prohibitions of disbelief.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Read the objection that an action without purpose is foolish, and Sanusi's redefinition of foolishness.
The objection is based on a common human intuition, but Sanusi shifts the definition.
2
Analyze the definition of safah
Notice that safah is defined conventionally as acting with ignorance of consequences or preferring immediate pleasure. Understand why this definition cannot apply to Allah.
Allah has perfect knowledge and is not subject to change or pleasure-seeking.
3
Connect to the role of revelation
Sanusi concludes that since Allah has no preference among actions, the intellect cannot determine moral value without revelation. Understand why this follows from the previous arguments.
This is a foundational Ash'ari principle: moral values are known only through shari‘ah.
