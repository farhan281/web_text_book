The First Proof: Knowledge Entails Truthfulness
Sanusi presents the first proof for God's truthfulness with an objection and answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first kalam proof for God's truthfulness, its objection, and how the distinction between inner and verbal speech defends the proof.

Core Idea
For Allah, knowledge and inner speech are one, making lying impossible.

Sanusi begins the proofs for God's truthfulness by presenting an argument that connects knowledge to truthful speech. The proof relies on the concept of inner speech (كلام نفسي) that always matches what one knows. But an objection from human experience challenges this link. In this lesson, you will see how the answer preserves the proof by distinguishing human verbal lies from the indivisible nature of divine knowledge and speech.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof carefully
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how the proof defines truthful speech as inner speech that corresponds to knowledge, and then applies this to Allah's perfect knowledge.
The proof is attributed to al-Ustadh and al-Imam (al-Ash'ari and al-Baqillani).
2
Identify the objection
Find the objection: humans can know something and still lie about it. This seems to break the link between knowledge and truthfulness.
The objection uses the observed behavior of humans to argue against the universal claim.
3
Analyze the answer
Study how the answer distinguishes between verbal speech (كلام لفظي) and inner speech (كلام نفسي). Humans lie verbally, but their inner speech always matches their conviction. For Allah, there is no composition (تركيب) to allow a split, and no whisperings.
The key point is that the proof concerns inner speech, not mere verbal utterances.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Consider how this argument upholds the impossibility of lying for Allah. The answer shows that the objection is based on a misunderstanding of the locus of truthfulness.
This proof is foundational for establishing God's attribute of truthfulness.

Second and Third Proofs: Necessity and Perfection
Sanusi enumerates two proofs for truthfulness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two additional rational proofs for God's truthfulness: one based on the necessity of actions, and one based on the perfection of attributes.

Core Idea
God's truthfulness is proven by necessity and perfection.

Sanusi presents two more concise proofs for God's truthfulness. The second proof uses the logic of necessity: if lying were possible for Allah, it would become necessary, contradicting His truthfulness. The third proof uses perfection: truthfulness is a perfection, and Allah cannot be deficient. These arguments reinforce the impossibility of lying for Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once carefully, then read the full literal translation. Notice how each proof is introduced separately.
The passage contains two distinct proofs marked by 'الثاني' and 'الثالث'.
2
Understand the second proof's logic
Trace the logic: if lying were possible (jā'iz), it would become necessary (wājib) because Allah does not do merely permissible things. This would then prevent truthfulness, leading to contradiction.
The footnote clarifies that 'jā'iz' here applies only to actions, not to essence or attributes.
3
Understand the third proof's logic
The proof builds on Allah's speech being established. Truthfulness is a perfection, falsehood a deficiency. Since Allah cannot have deficiency, He must be truthful.
This proof is simpler and relies on the concept of perfection.
4
Reflect on complementarity
Consider how the two proofs approach the same conclusion from different angles: one from the impossibility of falsehood leading to contradiction, the other from the necessity of perfection.
5
Respond to common misunderstanding
Some might think the second proof says Allah cannot do anything permissible. Read the footnote and understand its nuance.

The third proof: Allah has speech, and truthfulness is a perfection; its opposite (falsehood) is a deficiency; deficiency upon Allah is impossible, so He must be truthful. The footnote clarifies that 'جائز' (permissible) refers to actions, not essence or attributes; Allah's actions are not all necessary, but the argument here is about the impossibility of attributing falsehood to Him.

Objection and Answer on God's Truthfulness
Refutes an objection that lying can coexist with knowledge, distinguishing internal from external speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection that knowledgeable people can lie is refuted by distinguishing internal from external speech, and why this distinction does not apply to God.

Core Idea
Lying is only in external speech, not in internal knowledge, and God's simplicity prevents any composition.

This passage addresses a powerful objection against the argument that God's knowledge necessitates His truthfulness. The objector points out that we often see people who know the truth yet lie. The answer resolves this by drawing a crucial distinction between internal and external speech, which also highlights the divine attribute of simplicity.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Begin by reading the objection presented at the start of the passage. The objector claims that since knowledgeable people sometimes lie, knowledge does not entail truthfulness. Understand the challenge this poses to the argument that God must be truthful.
The objection is based on empirical observation of human behavior.
2
Analyze the answer's distinction
The answer distinguishes between internal speech (kalām nafsī) and external verbal speech (kalām lafẓī). Internal speech always matches one's conviction, while lying occurs only in external speech. Recognize why this preserves the connection between knowledge and internal truthfulness.
Internal speech is the mental assent or conviction; external speech is the actual utterance.

Apply the distinction to God
The answer then explains why this distinction does not apply to God: God has no composition, so He cannot have knowledge and truth in one 'part' and lying in another. Also, whisperings and temporal estimations are impossible for God. Understand the theological significance.
God's simplicity (tajarrud) means no division into parts or loci.

Reflect on the overall argument

God's speech must be truthful because lying is a deficiency, and deficiency is impossible for Him.

This lesson presents one of the classical kalam proofs for the truthfulness of God's speech. It begins with the already established attribute of divine speech and then uses the principle of perfection to derive truthfulness. The argument is concise but powerful, showing how rational deduction from divine attributes works.

Commentary
This passage defines the proper approach to interpreting the Quranic attribute of istiwā (establishing above the Throne). It begins by stating that the literal meaning of physical settling is impossible for Allah. It then lists four acceptable interpretations among many. The fourth interpretation, noted with a footnote, suggests that 'istawā' refers to a created entity. The author then declares the apparent way as that of the Salaf (early Muslims): to leave the choice open among the permissible interpretations without specifying one, while affirming Allah's transcendence. Specifying a particular interpretation without evidence is called an innovation (bid'a) and audacity. However, specifying with evidence (e.g., linguistic analysis) is permissible, as imams did. The passage concludes by asserting that non-specification is the sound Sunni position, agreed upon by Ahl al-Haqq.
