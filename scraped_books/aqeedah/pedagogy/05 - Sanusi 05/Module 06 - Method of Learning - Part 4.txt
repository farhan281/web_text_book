Foundational Definitions: Usul, Ahkam, and the Primary Sources
Sanusi defines usul, ahkam, hukm, and the first two sources.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key terminology of usul al-fiqh: what is meant by usul, ahkam, and hukm, and the definitions of the Book and Sunnah as two of the four sources.

Core Idea
Every legal ruling rests on a foundation: the four sources are the only valid proofs.

This lesson unpacks the opening framework of Sanusi's work. Before listing the four sources, he clarifies the meaning of 'foundations' and 'rulings', and introduces the technical classification of Allah's address. Then he names the Book and Sunnah as the first two sources of Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Understand the terms usul and ahkam
Recognize that 'usul' means proofs and 'ahkam' means legal rulings. This sets the stage for the discussion.
Usul are the sources; ahkam are the derived rules.
2
Grasp the definition of hukm
Hukm is Allah's address relating to actions. It is divided into three categories: requirement (iqtida'), choice (takhyir), and stipulation (wad'). Each category includes specific sub-rulings.
Requirement includes obligation, prohibition, recommendation, and dislike; choice is permissibility; stipulation relates to cause, condition, or obstacle.
3
Learn the four sources enumerated
The passage states that the proofs for establishing rulings are limited to four, though only three are detailed here. The first is the Book (Quran).
The fourth source (Qiyas) is mentioned elsewhere.
4
Examine the definitions of the Book and Sunnah
The Book is the Qur'an revealed to Prophet Muhammad. The Sunnah consists of his non-recited sayings, actions, and approvals.
Sunnah is confined to these three categories.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the earlier statement about the 'foundations of rulings.' It first clarifies that 'usul' means proofs (adilla) and 'ahkam' means legal rulings. It then defines 'hukm' as Allah's address related to the actions of the legally responsible, through requirement (iqtida'), choice (takhyir), or stipulation (wad'). It provides a detailed definition of ijma': 'the agreement of the mujtahids of the nation in an age upon a matter.' The Definition of Ijma': Consensus of the Mujtahids
Sanusi defines ijma' and gives two additional conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of ijma' as agreement of all mujtahids in an age, and the two additional conditions some scholars add regarding temporal continuity and the absence of a prior settled disagreement.

Core Idea
Ijma' requires complete agreement of all mujtahids — prior disagreement or incomplete tenure can invalidate it.

Having established the Book and Sunnah, Sanusi now turns to the third source: ijma'. He provides a core definition and then presents two variant definitions that add specific conditions. The footnotes from al-Manjur and al-Zarkashi explain the reasoning behind these additions.

Guided Path
1
Read the core definition of ijma'
The basic definition: agreement of the mujtahids of the Muslim community in an age upon a matter.
Mujtahids are qualified legal scholars.
2
Understand the first addition
Some scholars add that the consensus must last until the end of that age, because it is not considered a binding proof before that.
This addition ensures the definition excludes temporary agreements.
3
Understand the second addition
Others add that the consensus must not be preceded by a settled disagreement from a living or deceased mujtahid.
A settled disagreement that became established prevents later consensus on one view.
4
Review the scholarly justifications
Al-Manjur explains the first addition to make the definition comprehensive and exclusive. Al-Zarkashi explains the second addition, clarifying that only a 'settled' disagreement blocks consensus; unresolved disagreements can later be resolved, as with the Companions on fighting apostates.
The footnotes provide important nuance.

The Five Rulings and Legal Setting
Sanusi defines the five rulings and explains legal setting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the five categories of Islamic legal rulings (obligation, prohibition, recommendation, dislike, permissibility) and the concept of 'setting' (al-wad') as cause, condition, or impediment.

Core Idea
Islamic law classifies all acts into five values and treats some as causes, conditions, or impediments.

Before explaining the sources of law, Sanusi clarifies the types of rulings these sources establish. He divides rulings into those that require action, offer a choice, or set a condition for other rulings. This lesson unpacks these categories and their logical structure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five rulings
Read the passage and note the five rulings: obligation, prohibition, recommendation, dislike, and permissibility. These cover every possible act.
The first four fall under 'requirement' (iqtida), while the last is 'choice' (takhyir).
2
Understand requirement vs choice
Requirement includes obligation, prohibition, recommendation, and dislike. Choice is simply permissibility. Notice the difference in how they are derived.
3
Grasp the concept of setting
Setting (wad') means a ruling that makes something a cause, condition, or impediment for another ruling. Understand each: cause (e.g., time for prayer), condition (e.g., purity), impediment (e.g., menstruation).
These are not direct rulings but affect how rulings apply.

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration and definition. It first clarifies the categories of legal rulings (the five rulings) by explaining the terms 'requirement' (including obligation, prohibition, recommendation, dislike), 'choice' (permissibility), and 'setting' (cause, condition, impediment). Then it states that the evidence for these rulings is confined to four sources. The first source is the Book (Quran), defined as the revealed Qur'an. The second is the Sunnah, defined as the non-recited words, actions, and approvals of the Prophet. The third is Ijma' (consensus), defined as the agreement of the mujtahids of the Muslim community in any era upon a matter. The author also notes two additional conditions that some scholars add: (1) that the consensus continues until the end of that era, and (2) that the consensus is not preceded by an established disagreement from a living or deceased mujtahid. The fourth source is Qiyas (analogy), defined as equating a branch case to an original case due to a shared effective cause. The author explains that Qiyas is only considered from qualified mujtahid imams because it requires broad premises and is prone to error.

The Four Sources of Islamic Law
Sanusi enumerates and defines the four sources of Islamic law.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four primary sources of Islamic law as defined by Sanusi: the Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas, along with conditions for their validity.

Core Idea
All Islamic rulings derive from four sources: Quran, Sunnah, consensus of scholars, and analogical reasoning.

Having established the categories of rulings, Sanusi now presents the evidence from which these rulings are derived. He enumerates four sources, explaining what each means and, in the case of Ijma and Qiyas, qualifying them with important conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four sources
Read the passage and list the four sources: the Book (Quran), Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas. Notice the order.
These are the only valid evidences according to Sanusi.
2
Understand the definition of each source
For each source, note the definition: Quran is the revealed book, Sunnah is the Prophet's non-recited sayings/actions/approvals, Ijma is agreement of mujtahids in an era, Qiyas is equating a branch to an original due to same cause.
The Sunnah is limited to what is not recited (i.e., not Quran).
3
Note the additional conditions for Ijma
Some scholars add conditions: either that consensus continues until the end of the era, or that it is not preceded by an established disagreement from a mujtahid. Understand both views.
These conditions reflect different schools of thought on what constitutes valid consensus.
4
Appreciate the caution about Qiyas
Sanusi emphasizes that not all analogies are valid; only those from qualified mujtahid imams are considered, due to the complexity of premises and potential error.
This highlights the need for expertise in deriving rulings via analogy.

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration and definition. It first clarifies the categories of legal rulings (the five rulings) by explaining the terms 'requirement' (including obligation, prohibition, recommendation, dislike), 'choice' (permissibility), and 'setting' (cause, condition, impediment). Then it states that the evidence for these rulings is confined to four sources. The first source is the Book (Quran), defined as the revealed Qur'an. The second is the Sunnah, defined as the non-recited words, actions, and approvals of the Prophet. The third is Ijma' (consensus), defined as the agreement of the mujtahids of the Muslim community in any era upon a matter. The author also notes two additional conditions that some scholars add: (1) that the consensus continues until the end of that era, and (2) that the consensus is not preceded by an established disagreement from a living or deceased mujtahid. The fourth source is Qiyas (analogy), defined as equating a branch case to an original case due to a shared effective cause. The author explains that Qiyas is only considered from qualified mujtahid imams because it requires broad premises and is prone to error.

Usul al-Fiqh and the Sunni Position on Legal Rulings
Sanusi defines usul al-fiqh and states the Sunni position against the Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of usul al-fiqh and the Sunni position that legal rulings are derived from revelation or reason based on revelation, not from pure reason alone.

Core Idea
Legal rulings come from revelation or reason derived from it, not from pure reason.

Sanusi begins by defining usul al-fiqh as the science of the evidences and derivation of rulings. He then clarifies the Sunni doctrine against the Mu'tazila, who rely on pure reason. This lesson establishes the foundational principle that revelation is the primary source, and reason functions only within its framework.

Understanding Qiyas and the Sunni Position on Legal Rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of qiyas (analogy) in usul al-fiqh and the Sunni principle that legal rulings are derived from revelation or reason derived from it, not from pure reason.

Core Idea
Legal rulings come from revelation and reasoned derivation, not from human reason alone.

This passage from a classical commentary on Sanusi’s creed explains a key methodological principle of Ahl al-Sunnah: that legal rulings are not established by pure reason but by revelation or reason based on revelation. It also defines qiyas, a central tool of juristic reasoning, and restricts its valid use to qualified mujtahid imams. Let’s unpack the text carefully.

Following the Salaf: Abandoning Innovation in Creed and Practice
Commentary on abandoning innovations and adhering to the way of the salaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why following the righteous salaf requires abandoning innovations that have no basis in Shari'ah, and how such innovations affect both beliefs and outward actions.

Core Idea
Innovations without Shari'ah basis must be abandoned; they corrupt either creed or practice.

The commentator unpacks the matn's phrase 'following the righteous salaf' to show that it is not just a slogan but a command to avoid all innovations lacking Shari'ah support. He then clarifies that such innovations can arise in beliefs—like those of the Mu'tazila—or in outward practices common in later times. This lesson explains the principle and its application.

The commentator gives specific examples for each category.

Reflect on reliance upon Allah
The passage ends with 'There is no power and no strength except with Allah,' showing that avoiding bid'ah requires divine assistance.
This emphasizes the need for tawfiq in following the salaf.
