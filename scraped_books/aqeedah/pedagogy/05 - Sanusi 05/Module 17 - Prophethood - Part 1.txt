The Necessity of Prophethood
Sanusi explains the necessity of sending messengers and the insufficiency of intellect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the sending of messengers is necessary and why the intellect alone is insufficient for knowing moral obligations.

Core Idea
Without prophets, human reason cannot know right from wrong.

Sanusi begins a new chapter on prophethood by establishing that the sending of messengers is not only possible but necessary. He explains that while reason can know God's existence, it cannot determine moral obligations without divine revelation. This lesson explores why prophethood is among the contingent things we must believe in.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on prophethood
Read the Arabic passage slowly. Notice how Sanusi states that sending messengers is among the contingent things (jā'izāt) that must be believed.
The passage is concise; each word matters.
2
Understand the limitation of intellect
Sanusi says the intellect does not perceive obedience or disobedience without a revealed law. Reflect on why this is significant.
This is the key reason for the necessity of prophethood.
3
Ask: why must we believe in this?
Consider the implication: if intellect alone cannot know moral obligations, then without prophets, humans would have no guidance. This makes belief in prophethood mandatory.
This connects to the broader creed.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of prophethood and miracles. It begins with a new chapter: 'On Prophethoods'. Sanusi states that the sending of messengers is among the contingent possibilities (jā'izāt) that must be believed. The reason given is that the intellect alone cannot know obedience, disobedience, or intermediate matters without a revealed law. Then, under the heading 'Discussion on the Miracle', he explains that God supports prophets with miracles (mu'jiza) that prove their truthfulness. The definition of a miracle is given as an act of Allah that is extraordinary (khāriq li-l-'āda), concurrent with the claim of prophethood, issued as a challenge before its occurrence, and is not contradicted (i.e., it incapacitates anyone who wants to oppose it from producing the like). Then Sanusi explains the first condition ('act of Allah'): it excludes the eternal (al-qadīm) because that is not an act of Allah, so it cannot be a miracle. It includes acts to which temporal power (al-qudra al-ḥāditha) is attached, e.g., the Prophet's recitation of the Quran – that is a miracle for him but not for others, because others merely narrate it and did not receive it from the angel. It also includes acts to which temporal power is not attached, like reviving the dead.

The Definition and Conditions of a Miracle
Sanusi defines miracles and explains the first condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise definition of a miracle (mu'jiza) and its conditions, especially the first condition that it is an act of Allah.

Core Idea
A miracle is an extraordinary act of God that proves a prophet's truth.

Having established the necessity of prophethood, Sanusi now explains how God supports prophets with miracles. He provides a precise definition with several conditions, and then elaborates on the first condition—that a miracle must be an act of Allah—distinguishing it from eternal matters and explaining examples like the Quran recitation.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of a miracle
Read the Arabic definition carefully. Note each condition: act of Allah, extraordinary, concurrent with claim, challenge before occurrence, not contradicted, incapacitates opposition.
This is the core definition of mu'jiza.
2
Identify the six conditions
List the six conditions from the definition. They are: فعل الله, خارق للعادة, مقارن لدعوى الرسالة, متحدى به قبل وقوعه, غير مكذب, يعجز من يبغي معارضته عن الإتيان بمثله.
The last condition is sometimes considered part of the definition.
3
Understand the first condition in detail
Sanusi explains that 'act of Allah' excludes eternal things and includes acts with temporal power (like Quran recitation) and without (like reviving the dead).
This clarifies that miracles are divine acts, not human.
4
Apply the first condition to the Quran example
The Prophet's recitation of the Quran is a miracle for him because he received it from the angel; others only narrate it.
This shows how the condition works in practice.

Introduction to Prophethood and the Necessity of Revelation
Sanusi transitions to the chapter on prophethood, establishing revelation as the source of obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that moral and legal obligations are known only through divine revelation, and that the sending of prophets is a necessary belief to convey that revelation.

Core Idea
Reason alone cannot know obedience or disobedience; revelation is required.

In this brief but pivotal passage, Sanusi wraps up a preceding argument and opens a new chapter on prophethood. He makes two critical points: all knowledge of obligation and prohibition comes from revelation, not reason; and sending messengers is an essential part of what we must believe about Allah. This lesson unpacks these foundational ideas.

3
Understand the content of prophethood
Note that sending messengers is listed among the 'possible things' that must be believed. The messengers convey Allah's commands, prohibitions, permissions, and prescriptive speech.
4
Reflect on the role of reason
The passage ends by asserting that reason alone cannot perceive obedience, disobedience, or intermediate categories. This justifies the need for revelation and prophets.

The greatest miracle is intellectual, not sensory.

Objection and Answer: Challenge by Inaction
Sanusi addresses an objection about challenging by not acting and provides an answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the definition of miracle accommodates prophecies of non-action.

Core Idea
A miracle can be a predicted non-event if the knowledge of it is from Allah.

This lesson presents an objection to the standard definition of miracle as an act of Allah. The objector points out that prophets sometimes challenge by promising that something will not happen. The answer clarifies that the miracle lies in the prophetic knowledge and its fulfillment, which is itself an act of Allah. Some scholars even added a phrase to the definition to cover such cases.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the objection introduced by 'فإن قلت' (if you say). Note the examples: Prophet Muhammad's statement 'My Lord has protected me' and Noah's challenge in the Quran. The objection argues that a miracle can be a challenge by non-action.
The key phrase is 'تحدي بعدم الفعل' (challenge by inaction).
2
Grasp the answer
Read the answer starting with 'فالجواب'. The miracle is identified as the prophet's knowledge and his informing of the outcome in accordance with what appeared. This knowledge is itself an act of Allah, created for the prophet.
The miracle is not the non-action per se but the divine knowledge and its realization.
3
Note the modification
Some scholars accepted the objection and added to the definition of miracle the phrase 'or what takes its place' (أو ما يقوم مقامه) to explicitly include cases of non-action.
This shows the flexibility of theological definitions in response to valid objections.

Defending Miracles: Possibility Does Not Imply Actuality
Sanusi defends the reliability of miracles against the objection that miracles could appear on liars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi defends the evidential value of miracles by distinguishing logical possibility from actual occurrence, and why a miracle on a liar does not undermine certainty.

Core Idea
Possibility does not imply actuality; a miracle on a liar is possible but never actual.

Sanusi addresses a critical objection about miracles: if a miracle can appear on a false prophet, how can we trust any miracle? This passage provides a precise rational defense by clarifying what 'possibility' truly means, and why the mere possibility of a miracle on a liar does not undermine the certainty of a true prophet's miracle.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that Sanusi begins by mentioning Allah’s protection against sorcery, then raises a hypothetical objection about miracles on liars.
Pay attention to the logical flow from the objection to the response.
2
Identify the objection and response
The objection: if a miracle can appear on a liar, then no miracle proves truthfulness. Sanusi’s response: such a miracle would not produce knowledge; also, possibility does not imply actuality.
The key phrase is 'لا يلزم من جواز الشيء وقوعه'.
3
Understand the meaning of 'جواز'
Sanusi defines 'جواز' (permissibility) as: if a thing were to occur, no impossibility would follow from it in its essence. This is logical possibility, not actual occurrence.
Contrast this with 'وقوع' (actual occurrence).
4
Apply the principle to the miracle argument
Even if a miracle on a liar is logically possible, it does not happen in reality. The actual miracle of a true prophet remains certain, because possibility does not entail occurrence.
The example of the world’s non-existence illustrates this perfectly.

This lesson unpacks his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path

Read the passage and note what Sanusi explicitly denies vs. what he allows. He first says he does not deny that Allah may grant special knowledge, then lists four specific claims he rejects.

Sanusi uses an analogy with the afterlife: if God can create a more perfect perception of Himself in the Hereafter, then the intellect cannot deem it impossible for Him to create a similar perception in the heart now.
This argument establishes the logical possibility, not actual occurrence.

Sanusi notes that such matters are subjective (wijdān) and that he only knows his own state. He cannot know others' states without a truthful report, which has not been established.
He also questions whether Sufi claims pertain to the Essence or to deeper knowledge of attributes.
4
Reflect on the final verse
The passage ends with the Prophet's prayer for increase in knowledge (Quran 20:114), implying that even the most knowledgeable should seek more, leaving room for further divine gifts.
This reinforces the open-ended nature of divine knowledge.

Commentary
This passage discusses the scope and limits of special divine knowledge. The author first states that he does not deny that God can grant specific knowledge to a servant, as He taught al-Khiḍr (Quran 18:65). However, he objects to four specific claims: (1) claiming an immediate vision of God (ruʾyah ʿājilah) in this life, (2) claiming advancement over the rank of prophethood, (3) claiming participation in prophethood itself, and (4) claiming encompassing knowledge (ʿilm iḥāṭah) of God. 

The author then presents an argument for the possibility of such perception: Since God will create a perception of Himself in the afterlife that is more complete than our current knowledge (which is merely knowing the effect from its cause), the intellect cannot deem it impossible for God to create a similar perception in the heart in this world. The clarity of that perception would be comparable to actual vision. Therefore, the correct position is to affirm the logical possibility of such perception.

He then shifts to epistemological limits: such matters are subjective (wijdān), and God's favor is infinite. The author admits he only knows his own state; he can only know the state of others by truthful customary report (inbāʾ ṣādiq fī al-ʿādah), which has not been established. Regarding the Sufis' claims, he says they have not been distinguished enough to know whether the perceived object relates to God's Essence (al-dhāt) or to a deeper understanding of His attributes and names. The author questions how we can be certain that God did not bestow some knowledge on any saint or prophet beyond what we ourselves know. He ends by quoting the Prophet's prayer for increase in knowledge (Quran 20:114), implying that even the most knowledgeable should seek more. This demonstrates a balanced approach: affirming the possibility while rejecting unverified claims.

When the prophet shows a miracle indicating that he is His messenger, and he establishes his truthfulness by His confirming him... then his truthfulness is established. Then when he informs that there is no god but Him, and no creator other than Him... then divine unity is established. This statement is transmitted from Abu Hashim. An objection is raised against it: that we do not concede that knowledge of the validity of prophethood does not depend on that. Its explanation: that the one who says that he is a messenger, when he claims prophethood and produces a supernatural event (al-khariq) as proof of his truthfulness... the existence of the supernatural event does not indicate his truthfulness unless it is verified that this act which he brought cannot be done by anyone other than his sender; so that his act matches his challenge, and his request takes the place of his saying: 'You have spoken truthfully.' So if we have no knowledge negating the agency of another... then it is not known that it is His act. Commentary
This passage presents an objection to the view attributed to Abu Hashim that one can know the truthfulness of a prophet and thereby know divine unity without prior knowledge of God's oneness. The author first states the view: when a prophet shows a miracle, his truthfulness is proven, and then when he announces that there is no god but God, divine unity is proven. The objection is then raised: we do not concede that knowledge of prophethood is independent of divine unity. The explanation follows: for a miracle (al-khariq) to prove the prophet's truthfulness, we must be certain that the act is beyond the power of any agent other than the one who sent him. This is because the miracle functions as a verbal confirmation: the prophet's request for a miracle is like saying 'I am truthful,' and God's performing it is like saying 'You are truthful.' But if we do not know that no other agent can perform it, we cannot attribute it to God. Therefore, establishing that the miracle is solely God's act requires first establishing that God is one and unique in His power (al-wahdaniyya). Thus, knowledge of prophethood depends on prior knowledge of tawhid.

Prophethood Requires Tawhid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the establishment of prophethood rationally depends on knowledge of God's oneness (tawhid).

Core Idea
Without tawhid, no miracle can be attributed to a divine sender.

Sanusi concludes his argument by showing that even the proof of prophethood—through miracles—requires prior knowledge of tawhid. In this passage, he explains why identifying the agent of a miracle is impossible if we do not already know that God is one. This deepens our understanding of the logical priority of tawhid over all other theological truths.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification. Sanusi begins by defining prophethood as the specific quality of hearing revelation from Allah, either through an angel or directly. If the recipient is commanded to convey that revelation, then it becomes a message (رسالة). Based on this, he distinguishes two types: a messenger (رسول) is one who receives revelation and is commanded to convey it; a prophet (نبي) is one who receives revelation but is not commanded to convey it. Thus, the messenger is more specific (أخص) absolutely: every messenger is a prophet, but not every prophet is a messenger. Then Sanusi reports three other opinions: (1) the two terms are synonymous; (2) they are general and specific in some respect—the overlap occurs in a human messenger, while a prophet exclusively receives revelation without being commanded to convey, and a messenger exclusively can be an angel sent to others; (3) they are completely disparate—messengers are those with books and laws, while prophets judge by previous scriptures. Sanusi then points out a difficulty with the third opinion using the Qur'anic verse 'وكان رسولاً نبياً' (He was a messenger, a prophet), which indicates that both titles can combine in one person, contradicting the claim of complete disparity. This passage thus lays out the technical definitions and the scholarly debate on the relationship.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on Sanusi's definition of a miracle (mu'jiza). It begins by explaining the condition that a miracle must be 'extraordinary' (khāriq lil-‘ādah), i.e., breaking the normal course of events. The commentator gives examples of acts that are challenged to be produced (like beating and killing) and clarifies that the term 'extraordinary' excludes ordinary events, because ordinary events can be performed by both a truthful person and a liar. Among ordinary events are magic and similar phenomena, even if their causes are rare—some mistakenly consider magic extraordinary, but it has a specific natural cause. Also ordinary are the inherent properties of objects, like a magnet attracting iron. The reason for requiring the act to be extraordinary is that without it, the miraculous nature (i‘jāz) cannot be established; moreover, a miracle serves as a divine attestation, and ordinary occurrences cannot serve that purpose because they are not exclusive to prophets. It is also agreed that the exact type of extraordinary act need not be specified in advance.

The commentator then addresses an objection from the Barāhima (Indian theologians who denied prophethood). They argue that rare natural phenomena, like magnetism to someone unfamiliar, can be mistaken for miracles. They ask: what assurance do we have that a claimant to prophethood has not simply mastered a rare science or discovered a secret of nature that appears miraculous to the ignorant? The answer is twofold: First, we only accept as miraculous acts that are clearly beyond any human artifice or natural science, such as reviving the dead, turning a stick into a snake, or healing the blind and leprous without any means. These are categorically different from what can be achieved by talismans or tricks. Second, God's established custom regarding His prophets and saints is that He protects them from any suspicion of being associated with such arts; for example, a prophet may be brought up in a remote valley far from sorcerers, or be unlettered, so that it is impossible to attribute his miracles to human learning.

Saints, who are followers, do not fall into that category. Moreover, if a miracle appears on another prophet, it does not impair the original prophet's miracle by scholarly consensus. Thus the objection is invalid.

Definition of Karamat and Its Conditions
Sanusi defines karamat with exclusions and explains irhas and tahaddi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise definition of karamat (wonder of a saint) and how it is distinguished from miracles, magic, istidraj, and irhas.

Core Idea
Karamat is a wonder worked on a righteous non-prophet, not challenged beforehand.

In Islamic theology, karamat are extraordinary events granted to saints. But not every extraordinary event is a karamat. This lesson presents Sanusi's definition and explains each condition, showing how karamat differs from miracles of prophets, deceptive acts of the wretched, and signs preceding prophethood. By understanding these distinctions, you will avoid common confusions.

This passage provides a formal definition of karamat (wonder of a saint) and then explains each condition.
