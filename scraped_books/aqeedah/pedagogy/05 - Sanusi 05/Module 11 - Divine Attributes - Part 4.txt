Negative Attributes: Definition and Nuances
Sanusi defines negative attributes and discusses permissible negations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of negative attributes in Islamic theology, including the verified view and the concept of permissible negations.

Core Idea
Negative attributes are the negation of what cannot be said about God.

Sanusi begins by clarifying what negative attributes are. He first reports a common definition, then presents a more precise, verified one. He also introduces the subtle idea that some negations may be 'permissible' in God's right, which leads to the concept of temporality in certain divine acts like pardon.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage carefully focusing on the definition and examples. Notice the distinction between the common and verified definitions.
The ellipsis '... إلى آخرِهِ' indicates an omission; the core meaning is clear.
2
Identify the two definitions
Find the common definition ('قالوا: إنها عبارة عن كل ما يمتنع...') and the verified definition ('والتحقيق: أنها عبارة عن نفي كل ما يمتنع'). Understand the difference.
The verified view refines the common one: it is not simply 'what is impossible to attribute', but the 'negation of all that is impossible'.
3
Understand permissible negations
Note that some negations are not absolute but permissible (جائزة). The example of God's pardon after an offense shows that these are expressed as temporal (حادثة).
This nuance prevents misunderstanding that all negative attributes are mere negations.

The Third Objection: Ruling Without Attribute
Sanusi presents the third objection on the return of a ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical problem of attributing a ruling to an essence that does not have the corresponding attribute subsisting in it, and why Sanusi considers this arbitrary.

Core Idea
Arbitrary attribution undermines the consistency of the Ash'ari doctrine.

Sanusi continues his critique of the Ash'ari theory of will by presenting a logical impossibility: if the attribute of will does not subsist in the essence, how can the ruling 'being willing' be attributed to that essence? He argues this is an arbitrary distinction not observed in other attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic and translation of the third objection. Note the structure: it begins with a general statement, then a clarification, then a refutation.
2
Understand the logical structure
The objection says it's impossible for the ruling of an attribute (being willing) to return to an essence that does not have the attribute (will) subsisting in it, unless the ruling is specific to that essence. The Ash'aris deny specificity.
Specificity would mean only that essence can receive the ruling.

Sanusi calls this 'arbitrary' (تحكم) and gives a counterexample: many attributes subsist in things without their rulings applying elsewhere.
E.g., the attribute of blackness subsists in a body, but its ruling (being black) doesn't apply to other bodies.

The Infinite Regress Argument Against an Originated Will
Sanusi presents the infinite regress argument against an originated divine will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why an originated divine will leads to an infinite regress, and how this argument is used in Islamic theology to affirm the eternity of God's will.

Core Idea
An originated will needs a cause just like any other created thing, leading to an endless chain.

This lesson presents a classical kalam argument against the idea that God's will is created (hadith). If God's will were created, it would require specifiers for its existence and time, each needing another will, ad infinitum. This is a key proof for the eternality of God's attributes.

1
Read the Arabic passage carefully

Understand the logical chain
The argument is: an originated will is a contingent thing that needs specification → specification requires another will → that will, if also originated, requires another → infinite regress. Ask yourself why specification requires a will.
In kalam, every contingent thing needs a cause for its specification.

Reflect on why infinite regress is considered impossible in Islamic theology, and how this argument supports the doctrine of God's eternal attributes.
Infinite regress would entail an endless chain of causes, which is seen as a logical absurdity.

Attributes are not the essence itself; if they were, God would be contradictory.

Sanusi now turns to prove a foundational point: the divine attributes (such as power, will, knowledge) are distinct from God's essence. Commentary
This passage is a proof (burhān) that the divine attributes (ṣifāt) are distinct from the essence (dhāt). It begins by stating what it aims to prove: if the seven attributes belong to the essence without additional meanings subsisting in it, then the essence itself would be those attributes. The author then demonstrates the entailment using a logical principle: sharing in a more specific essential property necessitates sharing in a more general essential property. For example, sharing in 'rationality' necessitates sharing in 'animality', because rationality is a specific type of animality. Applied to God: if the essence has the specific property of knowledge (i.e., encompassing all objects of knowledge) and power (i.e., bringing about possibles), and if these properties are not from distinct attributes, then the essence itself must be those properties—thus the essence would be knowledge and power. The author notes that this argument is even stronger for the Muʿtazila, who hold the entailment as a causal relation, whereas the Ashʿarīs see it as mere concomitance. The passage concludes that according to both views, the essence possessing these properties must be identical with them if no distinct attributes exist.

The First Absurd Consequence: Contrariety Contradiction
Sanusi enumerates the first impossible consequence: the essence would be both a contrary and not a contrary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why identifying the essence with knowledge would force a contradiction between being a contrary and not being a contrary.

Core Idea
If the essence were knowledge, it would simultaneously be a contrary and not a contrary.

Sanusi begins his refutation by showing that if the essence were identical with an attribute like knowledge, it would have to be both a contrary of something (e.g., ignorance) and not a contrary, because essences themselves are not subject to contrariety. This contradiction proves the impossibility of identity.

Guided Path
1
Read the first consequence passage
Read the Arabic and translation for the first absurd consequence. Notice how Sanusi sets up the contradiction: the essence as knowledge would be contrary to ignorance, but as essence it cannot be contrary.
Contrariety (taḍād) is a property of accidental meanings, not of essences.
2
Understand the contradiction
The essence would have to be both a contrary (because it is knowledge) and not a contrary (because it is an essence). This is impossible, so the identity cannot hold.
3
Apply the same logic to other attributes
Sanusi says the same applies to power, will, and other attributes. Try to reason through one other attribute to see the pattern.
This shows the argument is general, not limited to knowledge.

Sanusi presents the second consequence: the substrate would both exist and not exist.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why identifying the essence with an attribute would require both the existence and non-existence of a substrate.

Core Idea
If the essence were identical with an attribute, the substrate would have to both exist and not exist.

The second impossible consequence arises from the nature of attributes requiring a substrate and the essence not requiring one. If they are identical, the same thing would both require and not require a substrate, leading to another contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Read the second consequence passage
Read the Arabic and translation for the second consequence. Identify the two requirements: attributes need a substrate (maḥall) to exist in, while the essence does not.
Substrate means something that supports the attribute, like a body supporting color.
2
Understand the contradiction
If essence = attribute, then the same thing would require a substrate (because it is an attribute) and not require a substrate (because it is an essence). Both cannot be true.
This is a direct logical impossibility.
3
Connect to the broader argument
This shows that the identity hypothesis leads to a necessary existence and non-existence of the same thing, which is absurd.

Commentary
This passage is the refutation of the consequent that the essence would be identical with the attributes. Sanusi presents three impossible consequences (lawāzim) that would follow from such identity, proving that identity cannot hold. The first consequence: the essence would simultaneously be a contrary of something (e.g., knowledge is contrary to ignorance) and not be a contrary (because essences are not subject to contrariety). This is a contradiction. The second consequence: a meaning (like knowledge) requires a substrate (maḥall) in which it subsists, while the essence requires no substrate. If they are identical, then the substrate would both exist and not exist for the same thing—another contradiction. The third consequence: the multiple existences of the essence and the attributes would have to become one existence. But union of two things is impossible, as previously proven: either both realities are annihilated, both remain, or one is annihilated—all absurd. Thus, the original identity is impossible, proving that attributes are distinct from the essence.

Commentary
This passage is a proof by necessary consequences (lawāzim) that the divine essence (al-dhāt) cannot be identical with the attributes (al-ṣifāt). The argument proceeds by enumerating three absurd consequences that would follow if essence and attributes were the same, and then gives a general proof against the very possibility of union (ittiḥād) between distinct things.

Sanusi first states the first necessary consequence: If the essence were identical to an attribute like knowledge, it would both have to be opposite (against ignorance) and not opposite (because essences are not opposed), which is contradictory. He asks the reader to apply the same reasoning to other attributes like power and will.

The second consequence: The meaning (al-maʿnā) — here a mental consideration referring to an attribute — requires a subject (maḥall) in which it inheres, while the essence requires no subject. If they were the same, both the existence and non-existence of a subject would be necessitated, which is impossible.

The third consequence: If the essence were identical to the attributes, then their existences would become one existence. This leads to the general problem of union (ittiḥād).

Sanusi then invokes a previously established proof that union of two things is impossible. He analyzes the concept: If two things become one, either both realities (ḥaqīqatān) are annihilated, both remain, or one remains and the other is annihilated. Each case is absurd: annihilation of both yields a third thing, not the union; both remaining together yields two, not one; and one remaining with the other annihilated violates the nature of union, which requires each to exist without multiplicity.

Furthermore, if the attributes were united with the essence, contradictory properties would coexist in one thing (e.g., some attributes have effect, some not; some are opposite to what others are not), which is impossible.

Sanusi concludes that union of a thing with another is absolutely inconceivable (mā lā yuʿqalu muṭlaqan).

Finally, he points back to his own earlier poem (qawl) where he summarized each of the three consequences in condensed phrases.

The Three Impossibilities of Union
Sanusi enumerates three divisions of union and proves each invalid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three possible divisions of union between two realities and why each one is logically invalid.

Core Idea
Union is impossible because every way it could happen leads to a contradiction.

Sanusi employs a powerful logical tool: exhaustive division (taqsīm). He breaks down the concept of union into every conceivable scenario and demonstrates that each one is self-contradictory. This forces the conclusion that union itself is rationally impossible. Let's examine these three scenarios and see why they fail.

Union implies a single entity, not a pair.

Consider 'one exists without the other.' Union requires each to be realized in a way without multiplicity, so one cannot simply disappear while the other remains. This too is contradictory.
Union implies mutual presence, not absence of one.

Commentary
This passage is a proof by elimination (taqsīm) against the possibility of union (ittiḥād) between two distinct realities. The author first states that all divisions of union are invalid, so union itself is invalid because it must fall under one of those divisions. The author concludes that union is absolutely unintelligible. He then refers to three key necessary properties he mentioned in his poetic lines: (a) being opposite and not opposite, (b) entailing and not entailing a locus, and (c) two or more existences becoming one.

Commentary
This passage defines and proves the impossibility of the famous problem of 'sawad halawa' (blackness-sweetness). The author begins by stating that the entire discussion about preventing the combination of characteristics of two attributes for one thing is based on this problem. He then explains the disagreement among rational thinkers: can two distinct accident-properties exist in one essence, such as blackness and sweetness? The correct position, which he affirms, is that this is impossible, and this ruling is extended to the eternal attributes (i.e., the divine attributes). The proof of the verifiers (al-muhaqqiqun) is given: if two characteristics like blackness and sweetness combine in one subject, then that subject would both oppose and not oppose the same thing—because blackness opposes whiteness but not sweetness, while sweetness does not oppose whiteness. This contradictory conjunction is impossible. After this, the author quotes al-muqtarih (the proposer) who clarifies a condition: this problem only applies to those who affirm the theory of states (ahwal). For those who deny states and hold that the most specific description of a thing is its very existence, combining two characteristics would mean that two existences become one, which is also impossible. This reasoning is then applied to the eternal attributes: if power and knowledge were both attributes of God, then He would both oppose ignorance (since power opposes ignorance) and not oppose it (since knowledge does not oppose ignorance), which is absurd; or the two existences would collapse into one, which is also absurd. Thus, the impossibility of combining distinct attribute-characteristics in one essence is firmly established.

Refuting the Deniers of Attributes: Ta'lil as Talazum
Sanusi defends divine attributes by redefining ta'lil as talazum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ash'ari theologians defend divine attributes by redefining ta'lil as necessary concomitance (talazum), thereby preserving God's necessity and avoiding any implication of contingency.

Core Idea
Ta'lil in divine attributes means necessary concomitance, not efficient causation.

This passage presents a key Ash'ari response to those who deny real divine attributes. The objectors argue that attributes would make God dependent and thus contingent. The Ash'ari response redefines the very notion of 'causation' (ta'lil) in the divine context, explaining it as necessary concomitance (talazum) rather than efficient causation. By this move, the Ash'aris preserve both the reality of attributes and God's absolute necessity.

Commentary
This passage presents and refutes an objection against affirming real divine attributes (sifat). The objectors argue that if attributes exist, they would necessitate 'ta'lil' (making the Necessary Being caused), which would imply that God is possible/contingent – a clear contradiction. Sanusi explains their reasoning: if attributes exist, they would be real entities distinct from God's essence, so God's existence would derive from them, making Him dependent and thus contingent. The Ash'ari response, given by 'our imams', is to deny the conditional argument (man' al-istithnā'iyya). They do so by redefining 'ta'lil' when applied to God. In the context of divine attributes – specifically on the view that affirms 'ahwal' (states of being qualified by attribute, e.g., 'knowledgeability' as distinct from knowledge itself) – 'ta'lil' does not mean efficient causation (إفادة الثبوت) but rather 'talazum' (necessary concomitance). For example, God's knowledge necessarily accompanies His state of being-knowing (al-'ālimiyya), but that does not mean knowledge causes that state to exist; rather, they are two necessary entities that always coexist, like substance and accident among contingent beings (though without temporal priority). This understanding avoids any implication of dependence or temporality. Sanusi then notes that he alluded to this in his earlier statement 'قلنا: معنى التعليل هنا' (We said: the meaning of ta'lil here). He further explains that the phrase 'here' refers to the divine context, indicating that Ash'ari scholars differ on the meaning of ta'lil in the observable world (al-shāhid), but in the divine context it is unanimously understood as talazum. The final parenthetical note clarifies that on the contrary view (denying states), no talazum would exist because there is no distinction between the attribute and the state.

Commentary
This passage presents the philosophers' argument against divine attributes and the beginning of its refutation. The philosophers argue that if attributes existed, they would be in need (muftaqira) of the essence, either because an attribute cannot subsist by itself or because some attributes are conditions for others (like life is a condition for power). This need contradicts the necessary nature of God, who is absolutely self-sufficient. The answer denies the entailment (mulazama): need in the sense of receiving existence from another would imply origination (hadith), but the attributes are not originated; they are themselves necessary of existence. The passage ends with a distinction about another meaning of need (inseparability), which is not completed in this window.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some may think that the refutation accepts the premise that attributes need the essence, but merely argues that this need does not contradict necessity.
Correction: Sanusi does not accept that attributes are in need in the sense of receiving existence; he denies that the philosophers' notion of need applies. Attributes are necessary of existence, so the type of need that would imply originatedness is absent.

Key Takeaway
Sanusi refutes the philosophers by denying that the attributes' dependence on the essence implies deficiency, because that dependence is not a need for existence.
