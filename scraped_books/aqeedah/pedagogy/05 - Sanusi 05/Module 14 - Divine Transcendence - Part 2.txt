Two Gods Agreeing? Composition and Dependency Paradox
Sanusi presents two arguments against two gods necessarily agreeing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the necessary agreement of two gods leads to absurd consequences of composition and contradictory dependency, reinforcing the impossibility of multiple necessary beings.

Core Idea
Necessary agreement of two gods implies each is composed and events become both needy and independent.

Sanusi presents two powerful arguments against the possibility of two gods necessarily agreeing. The first shows that agreement implies each god possesses only parts of attributes, leading to composition—an impossibility for the divine. The second reveals a deeper contradiction: if events are independent of each god separately, they are simultaneously in need and not in need of them. This passage strengthens the tawhid argument by exposing logical inconsistencies.

Guided Path
1
Understand the first argument: composition from parts
Read how necessary agreement forces each god to have only a part of knowledge, power, and will, implying composition (tarkib). Then note that composition of the deity is impossible, especially from separate parts.
Sanusi emphasizes that even composition from connected parts is impossible, so separate parts are even more absurd.
2
Understand the second argument: contradictory dependency
Examine the consequence that events would be both in need of each god and independent of each god simultaneously—a contradiction. This is derived from the necessary independence of events from each god regarding the other.
This argument is stronger because it does not rely on the inverse of a proof.
3
Compare the two arguments and note the role of 'aks al-dalil
Realize that the first argument might be rebutted as appealing to the inverse of a proof, but Sanusi claims he has established it in a way that avoids this. The second argument avoids this issue entirely.
The footnote explains 'aks al-dalil: absence of the proof does not entail absence of the proven.

Commentary
This passage presents two arguments against the possibility of two gods necessarily agreeing (ittifaq wajib). First, if they necessarily agreed, each would have only a part of knowledge, power, and will, implying composition (tarkib). Since composition from two connected parts is already impossible for the deity, composition from separate parts is even more absurd. Second, if events were necessarily independent of each god—meaning each could create events independently of the other—then events would paradoxically be in need of both gods (since both are required for the agreement) and independent of each simultaneously. This contradiction is stronger than the first because the first might be rebutted as merely appealing to the inverse of a proof (arguing from absence of composition to impossibility of agreement), but the author notes he has already established that argument in a way that avoids that objection. The footnote explains the concept of 'aks al-dalil (inverse of the proof).

Impossibility of Contingent Agreement Between Two Gods
Sanusi argues against the possibility of contingent agreement between two gods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why contingent agreement between two gods is impossible, because it entails the possibility of disagreement, which leads to incapability.

Core Idea
Contingent agreement is impossible because it opens the door to disagreement.

Sanusi now examines the second type of agreement: contingent agreement. He argues that if two gods could agree contingently, they could also disagree, since agreement and disagreement are opposites. But disagreement has already been proven impossible, so contingent agreement must also be impossible.

3

4

Sanusi offers a clearer formulation: 'Whenever their agreement is possible, they must accept incapability.'

Commentary
This passage presents two arguments against the possibility of two gods agreeing contingently (ittifaq ja'iz) and any agreement at all. First, if their agreement is possible, then their disagreement is also possible, because two opposites (agreement and disagreement) are such that if one is possible, the other must be possible. However, disagreement has already been proven impossible (as it leads to incapability and other absurdities). Therefore, agreement being possible is itself impossible. The author restates this argument syllogistically: if A then B, but B is false, so A is false. He notes that this formulation aligns better with the creed (al-'aqida). A marginal note adds that if two agreeing gods were imagined, they would be incapable. Second, he argues that any agreement (whether necessary or contingent) necessarily entails incapability because a single act cannot be divided between two agents; they would conflict, leading to both or one being incapable. Incapability is impossible for God because it contradicts power (qudra). If incapability were eternal (qadim), it would be impossible to cease, so God would never be able to do anything. If it were temporal (hadith), then its opposite (power) would be eternal and cannot cease to exist, so incapability could not exist. Furthermore, it is impossible for God to have a temporal attribute. The footnote clarifies that the opposite of incapability is power, which is eternal.

Core Idea
Any agreement between two gods makes them incapable because a single act cannot be shared.

Commentary

The objection argues that the world could be divided between two gods, so mutual hindrance (tamānuʿ) would not occur. Second Answer: Analysis of Two Possible Divisions
Sanusi analyzes the two possible divisions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why dividing the world between two gods fails whether the domains are similar (both substances) or different (substances vs accidents), due to the principles of similarity and inseparability.

Core Idea
Substances and accidents are inseparable; true division is impossible.

Sanusi provides a more detailed second answer to the objection. He examines two possibilities for the division: both parts consisting of substances, or one part substances and the other accidents. He shows that in either case, conflict (tamānuʿ) necessarily occurs, demonstrating the impossibility of multiple gods.

Guided Path
1
Examine the case of similar types
If both parts are substances, due to similarity, power over one substance implies power over all substances, leading to conflict.
This is based on the principle of tamāthul (similarity).
2
Examine the case of different types
If one part is substances and the other accidents, this is impossible because substances and accidents are inseparable; power over one requires power over the other, and conflict remains.
This is based on talāzum (inseparability).
3
Understand why conflict persists
Even if division were possible, when one god wants a substance and the other does not want its accident, conflict arises because the accident cannot exist without the substance.
This final point seals the argument.

Refutation of the Mu'tazilite Argument on Evil
Ibn al-Tilmisani refutes the Mu'tazilite claim that evil cannot be from Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ibn al-Tilmisani refutes the Mu'tazilite claim that evil cannot be from Allah by showing that moral categories are relative and based on divine law, and that Allah's names are tawqifiyya.

Core Idea
Good and evil are relative, not intrinsic; Allah creates everything by His power.

The Mu'tazilites argued that if Allah did evil, He would be called evil—a contradiction to His perfection. Ibn al-Tilmisani presents the response of Ahl al-Sunna, showing that actions are attributed to Allah based on their need for a creator, not their moral value. This lesson walks through the key points of that refutation, clarifying how Allah's actions are all good from His perspective.

2
Follow the Sunni Responses

These responses dismantle the Mu'tazilite premise step by step.
3
Reflect on the Conclusion
Consider the final point that Allah is called 'Creator of everything' but not specific evil things because names are revealed. This upholds divine perfection while affirming creative power.
The conclusion preserves Allah's transcendence and sovereignty.

First, they attribute actions to Allah based on their origination and need for a determiner (mukhaṣṣiṣ), not on their moral quality. Good and evil are relative (iḍāfiyān), not intrinsic to actions. For example, killing a person is evil for his relatives but good for his enemies. Second, when analyzed according to the Sharī'a, 'good' (ḥasan) means what the Law commands to do, and 'evil' (qabīḥ) means what it prohibits. These categories apply only to human actions. From Allah's perspective, all actions are ḥasan because He has the right to do anything possible, and He is praised for all perfection. Thus the Mu'tazilite analogy fails. Finally, the argument that calling Allah the doer of evil would require calling Him 'evil' is invalid because Allah's names are tawqīfiyya (based on revealed texts, not human reasoning). He is called 'Creator of everything,' but not specific evil things like apes and swine, even though He creates them.

The Necessity of Tawhid for Knowing the Sender
Sanusi argues tawhid is prerequisite for knowing the prophet's sender.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why knowledge of tawhid is necessary to identify the sender of a prophet and avoid circular reasoning.

Core Idea
Without tawhid, the miracle cannot be attributed to a specific sender, leading to circularity.

After refuting dualism, al-Sanusi argues that even miracles cannot prove prophethood unless one already knows tawhid. Otherwise, you don't know who performed the miracle, so you can't know the sender, and you need the sender to know the prophet. This creates a vicious circle.

Guided Path
1
Understand Ibn al-Tilmisani's point
Read the quote from Ibn al-Tilmisani: no rational person affirms two agents with universal influence. Those who affirm a creator other than God do not give that other universal efficacy.
This sets up the rational basis for tawhid, independent of revelation.
2
Apply to the miracle of the prophet
Sanusi then applies this: without knowing tawhid, we cannot attribute any act—including miracles—to a specific agent. The miracle at the prophet's hand could be from any supposed god.
The miracle's agent is ambiguous unless we already know there is only one God.
3
Recognize the circularity
If the sender is unknown, the prophet cannot be known (since the prophet is known through his sender). But if we try to know the sender through the prophet, we get circular reasoning (dawr). This shows tawhid must be known first.
Circularity is a logical impossibility; thus tawhid is a prerequisite.

Refutation of the Objection to Multiple Gods
Sanusi refutes an objection by showing logical consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why limiting the number of gods to only actually existing possible things fails to avoid an infinite regress and leads to contradictions.

Core Idea
Limiting gods to actual possible things still leads to an impossible infinite.

The objector tries to salvage the idea of multiple gods by restricting them to only those possible things that will actually exist. Sanusi shows that this restriction causes two fatal problems: it turns non-existent possible things into impossibilities, and even actual existents are endless in succession, leading to an infinite number of co-eternal gods which is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic and translation of the objector's proposal. Understand that they attempt to limit gods to only actual possible things to avoid infinity.
The objector assumes that possible things not decreed to exist are irrelevant.
2
Identify Sanusi's first argument
Notice that Sanusi says limiting gods to actual existents causes an 'inversion of realities': non-existent possible things would become impossible because they lack a potential creator.
This is a logical contradiction: things considered possible would become impossible.
3
Identify Sanusi's second argument
Even actual existents are endless in time (e.g., heaven and hell). Thus there would be infinite gods in sequence, but for eternal gods they must be simultaneous, which is impossible.
The type of infinity (simultaneous vs. successive) is key.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Consider why the objection fails decisively: it leads to an impossible simultaneous infinite of co-eternal beings.
This reinforces the proof of God's oneness.

This would avoid an infinite number because only actual existents are counted. The objector claims this escapes the earlier reasoning. Sanusi responds with two points. First, limiting the number of gods only to actual existents leads to an 'inversion of realities': possible things that do not exist would become impossible, because they would lack a potential creator, yet they are still considered possible. This is contradictory. Second, even the set of actual existents is infinite in the sense of non-interruption (e.g., successive rewards and punishments are endless in time), though not simultaneous. Thus a god for each would still result in an infinite number of gods. However, this infinity would be simultaneous, which is impossible for beings that are all eternal (pre-eternal). Because an infinite number of co-eternal beings cannot exist together; they would have to be successive, but that would contradict their pre-eternity. Hence the objection fails.

Commentary
This passage is a proof by dilemma against the claim that human power (al-qudra al-ḥāditha) can independently create acts. The author, al-Sanusi, assumes a premise from the opponent and shows it leads to absurdities. He structures the argument as follows: either the effect (ta'thir) is an essential attribute of the power itself, or it is not. If it is essential, then when the power relates to an act, two impossible outcomes follow: either (a) the power does not effect the act, meaning its essential attribute is negated (since it fails to produce its natural effect), while God is the true creator, or (b) the power does effect the act, but then it must overcome God's own creative power, which is impossible because God is all-powerful. Both are impossible. Then he addresses an attempted objection: someone might say that the power's effect is always in accordance with God's will. But al-Sanusi responds that if the effect is an essential attribute, it cannot depend on any external condition—it would necessarily occur whenever the power relates to an act, leading to the conflict mentioned. Next, he considers the second branch: if the effect is not an essential attribute but rather something acquired through another attribute, then an infinite regress arises because that attribute would itself need another attribute to produce its effect, and so on. This regress is impossible. Thus the original position is refuted.

The Mu'tazilite Argument from 'Vision Perceives Him Not'
Sanusi presents the Mu'tazilite argument from Qur'an 6:103.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Mu'tazilites used Qur'an 6:103 to deny the vision of God and their two distinct purposes for doing so.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazilites weaponized a verse against vision by defining vision as perception.

The Mu'tazilites frequently invoked the Qur'anic verse 'Vision perceives Him not' to oppose the Ash'ari doctrine that believers will see God in the Hereafter. Sanusi, quoting Ibn al-Tilimsani, explains that they used this verse in two ways: to counter the proof texts for vision, and to assert the absolute impossibility of seeing God. In this lesson, we will examine the logic of their argument, which hinges on equating vision with perception and claiming the verse's generality.

Guided Path

The minor premise is that vision equals perception; the major is that perception is universally negated.
2
Understand the second purpose
The second purpose treats the verse as self-praise, so negating perception is a perfection. Affirming it would be a deficiency, impossible for God.
This argument aims to show that vision itself is impossible, not just denied.
3
Reflect on the strength of the argument
Consider why the Mu'tazilites thought the verse was decisive. They relied on the definite plural 'al-absar' indicating absolute generality, extending to all times and individuals.

Al-Juwayni's Critique of the Ru'ya Proof
Al-Juwayni critiques the proof by analogous reasoning leading to absurdity.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If existence causes createdness, then God would be created—an absurdity that refutes the proof.

Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni, a master of kalam, presents a powerful objection to the proof for seeing Allah. He uses the example of substance and accident both being created, and argues that the common property of createdness must have a shared cause. Distinguishing Intellectual Attributes from General Ones
Sanusi defends that equal rulings cannot have different causes, refuting a counterexample.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why intellectual attributes (like knowing and powerful) cannot be caused by different realities, and why the example of color-ness does not apply to them.

Core Idea
Intellectual attributes are defined by their correlates, so different causes would change their very meaning.

Sanusi addresses a clever objection: if a common attribute like 'color-ness' can be caused by various specific colors, why can't intellectual attributes be caused differently? His response reveals a key difference between real-world properties and the rational judgments that define Allah's attributes. Let's see why the analogy fails.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The opponent concedes that a common attribute may exist but claims that equal rulings can still have different causes. He offers the example of 'color-ness' (al-lawniyyah) which is common to all colors yet caused by each specific color.
This is a counterexample aimed at breaking the rule that different causes cannot produce identical rulings.
2
Identify the key distinction
Sanusi distinguishes intellectual attributes (al-ahkam al-`aqliyyah) like 'being knowing' (al-`alimiyyah) from sensory generalities like color-ness. Unlike color-ness, intellectual attributes are not defined by themselves but by their correlates (knowledge and power).
This distinction is the heart of the answer.
3
Apply the distinction
If we explained 'being knowing' by a reality contrary to knowledge, its very intelligibility would be inverted. This is impossible. In contrast, color-ness is simply the general property of having a color, and its specific causes do not change its meaning.
The opponent's example actually reinforces Sanusi's point: the specific (al-akhass) cannot be a cause for the general (al-a`amm) in the case of intellectual attributes.
4
Realize the conclusion
Sanusi concedes that specific colors cause color-ness, but he argues that this is a case where the specific is a cause for the general, which is precisely what is denied for intellectual attributes. Thus the objection fails.

He then elaborates on the rays theory: rays are luminous particles that separate from the eye and attach to the seen object, requiring the object to be facing the seer and at a moderate distance. When the base meets a smooth surface like a mirror, it reflects back, allowing the seer to see themselves. They also claim that one does not see the inside of the eyelid due to excessive proximity. The specific assignment of these perceptions to particular organs is merely by the rule of habit ('āda). Likewise, the condition that the perceived object be in a direction and at a moderate distance is also merely habitual. Since habit can be broken (inkhirāq al-'āda), Allah can create perception of something very near, very far, or even without direction, just as He creates knowledge of things without the knower being in a direction relative to them. Thus, the innovators' argument fails because it mistakenly assumes vision depends on physical rays, whereas in reality perception is directly created by Allah and can occur without such conditions.

Commentary
This passage is a composite argument proving that God does not act for a purpose (gharad). It begins with a preliminary argument: if God had a purpose, it would be either a benefit returning to Himself (impossible, as it would entail His essence being subject to originated things) or a benefit returning to His act (impossible, because God is not obliged to choose the most beneficial for creatures; He can benefit them directly without an act; and it would either be a tautology or lead to infinite regress if we ask about the purpose of that benefit). The author then explains that once we know all actions are equally possible for God and He is free (mukhtar) with no obligation, it follows that He cannot have a purpose that drives Him. He then presents two formal proofs from his creed (al-'Aqida): the first proof argues that if an act had a purpose, that act would be obligatory (wajib) on God, meaning He could not omit it, because the very definition of a purpose is that it contains a wisdom that rationally impels Him, such that omitting it would be a deficiency. But this would imply God is compelled (maqhur) and not free, contradicting His essential freedom (ikhtiyar) and the Quranic verse (28:68). The second proof argues that the purpose must be either eternal (qadim) or originated (hadith). If eternal, the act would be eternal as well, which is false. If originated, then that originated purpose would itself need another originated purpose, leading to an infinite regress of originated things (tasalsul), which is impossible. Hence, God has no purpose in any of His acts.

The Impossibility of Purpose in Allah's Actions
Sanusi presents the third proof against attributing purpose to Allah's actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument that refutes attributing any purpose to Allah's actions, based on the impossibility of benefit returning to Allah or to creation without leading to contradiction or infinite regress.

Core Idea
Attributing purpose to Allah leads to logical dead ends: either temporal accidents or infinite regress.

In this passage, Sanusi presents a crisp logical proof that Allah's actions cannot be driven by any purpose or benefit, whether for Himself or for creation. By carefully analyzing each possibility, he shows that both lead to irrational conclusions, thereby affirming divine transcendence. This argument is a cornerstone of Ash'ari theology, emphasizing that God's actions are free from external motives.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the division of purposes
Read the passage and identify the two categories of purpose Sanusi presents: a benefit returning to Allah Himself or a benefit returning to His creation.
Notice that he begins with 'الثالثُ : الغرضُ' indicating this is the third point in a series.
2

Understand why a purpose benefiting Allah Himself is impossible: it would necessitate describing His essence with temporal occurrences (hawadith) and imply that He is deficient without His acts.
This aligns with the principle that Allah is free from temporal accidents and is perfect in His essence.
3
Refutation of purpose benefiting creation
Learn why a purpose benefiting creation is also impossible: Allah is not obligated to observe what is beneficial for creation, and He can directly grant benefit or remove harm without any mediating act.

Follow the regress argument
Trace Sanusi's additional argument: if we insist the benefit itself is the purpose, asking 'why this benefit?' leads either to circular reasoning (the benefit being its own purpose) or to an infinite regress of purposes.
This shows the futility of trying to ground a divine purpose in any created benefit.
5
Conclude the argument
Synthesize the logical conclusion: since both categories are false and any alternative leads to regress, Allah's actions are entirely free from purpose or motive.
This establishes the Ash'ari position of divine transcendence beyond anthropomorphic motivations.

Refuting the Foolishness Argument
Sanusi refutes a Mu'tazilite argument about purpose in divine actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi refutes the Mu'tazilite claim that divine acts without purpose would be foolish, by redefining foolishness in human terms and denying its application to God.

Core Idea
Foolishness requires ignorance; God's perfect knowledge makes it impossible.

The Mu'tazilites argued that if God acts without purpose, His actions would be foolish, contradicting His wisdom. Sanusi responds by clarifying the definition of foolishness, showing it depends on ignorance and short-sightedness—qualities incompatible with divine transcendence. This passage reveals a key method of refutation: exposing anthropomorphic assumptions in theological objections.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Mu'tazilite objection
Read the first part of the passage where the Mu'tazilite argument is presented: no purpose implies foolishness, which is impossible for a wise God.
Note the logical structure: if no purpose, then foolish; but God is wise, so purpose must exist.
2
Examine Sanusi's redefinition of foolishness
Focus on Sanusi's definition of foolishness (safah): it involves ignorance of consequences or preference for immediate pleasure, leading to harm.
This definition is key to denying the implication.
3
See how the redefinition denies the implication
Sanusi argues that since God has perfect knowledge and is free from ignorance and base desires, the definition of foolishness cannot apply to Him. Thus, the logical implication (no purpose → foolish) is denied.
This is a classic example of man' al-luzum (denial of entailment).
4
Reflect on divine transcendence
Consider how this refutation upholds divine transcendence (tanzih) by removing anthropomorphic concepts from discussions about God's actions and rulings.
The Mu'tazilite argument assumed a human-like framework for divine action.

Statement of the rules of ta'wil (interpretation), and when it should be applied. As for that whose apparent meaning is impossible, like {He established Himself upon the Throne} (20:5), we turn it away from its apparent meaning by consensus. Then if it has one valid interpretation, it must be taken upon that; otherwise, tafwid (delegation) together with tanzih (transcendence) is obligatory, and that is the madhhab of the early scholars, contrary to Imam al-Haramayn. When he mentioned that what reason deems possible, if revelation informs of its occurrence, must be believed in its apparent meaning, and its ta'wil is not permissible, and attempting its ta'wil is an innovation... he mentioned that what revelation informs of, and its apparent meaning is impossible according to reason, then we turn it away from its impossible apparent meaning, because we know with certainty that revelation does not inform of the occurrence of what cannot occur. If we were to deny reason in this, and act upon the apparent meaning of the impossible transmitted text, that would lead to the demolition of the transmitted text as well, because reason is the basis for establishing prophethood from which the soundness of transmission derives; so from discrediting reason necessarily follows discrediting transmission. Then after turning the word away from its impossible apparent meaning: if it has no valid interpretation other than one, then it must be taken upon that, because there is no other; and that is like His saying: {And He is with you wherever you are} (57:4) — it is impossible to take it upon its apparent meaning of essential companionship, and there remains after that only taking it upon companionship by knowledge and care, and its like: {Except He is the fourth of them} (58:7), and similar things that are many. And if after that it has interpretations, each of which is valid, then is one specified to remove confusion from the common people – which is the madhhab of Imam al-Haramayn – or is the matter delegated to Allah Almighty to avoid arbitrary judgment – which is the madhhab of the early scholars?

Commentary
This passage explains the rules of ta'wil (interpretation) when the apparent meaning of a revealed text is impossible according to reason. The author begins by stating that when the apparent meaning is impossible (like 'establishing Himself on the Throne' which suggests physical location), all scholars agree that we must turn away from that apparent meaning. Then, after turning away, if there is only one valid interpretation, it must be adopted; otherwise, tafwid (delegating the meaning to Allah) along with tanzih (affirming transcendence) is obligatory. This is the position of the early scholars, contrary to Imam al-Haramayn. The author then provides the logical reason for this: we know with certainty that revelation cannot report something impossible, and if we were to reject reason in this, we would also destroy transmission because reason is the foundation for confirming prophethood. After giving the general principle, he gives examples: verses about God's 'withness' (ma'iyya) cannot mean literal spatial companionship, so they must be interpreted as knowledge and care. Then he raises the case of multiple valid interpretations: should one be specified to remove confusion (Imam al-Haramayn's view) or should the matter be delegated to Allah (the early scholars' view)? The passage ends with this question, leaving the two positions presented.
