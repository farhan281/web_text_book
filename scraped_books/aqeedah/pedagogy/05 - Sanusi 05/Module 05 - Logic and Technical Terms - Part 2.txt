The Necessary Chain: Definition and Possibility
Definition of the necessary chain with an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what the necessary chain is and why it does not entail impossibility, using the examples of eternal bliss and torment.

Core Idea
Endless future chains are possible, unlike impossible infinite regress.

The concept of the necessary chain (السلسلة اللازمة) often causes confusion because people assume any endless chain is impossible. In this passage, the author clarifies that the necessary chain is a chain of order that can continue indefinitely into the future, just like the eternal rewards and punishments of the afterlife. This distinction is key to many theological debates.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to understand what the author means by 'the necessary chain' and 'the chain of order'.
Note that the author uses the term 'chain of order' to describe a sequence where each obstacle is followed by another.
2
Consider the objection and response
The author mentions that some might think this chain leads to impossibility. Identify why he says that and how he responds using the examples of Paradise and Hell.
The key is that impossibility only applies to regress that has no end, but future chains can be endless.
3
Reflect on the examples
Contemplate how the bliss of Paradise and the torment of Hell are endless but not impossible. This illustrates that not all endless sequences are problematic.
This connects to the broader theological context of the vision of God (ru'ya).

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of the concept of the necessary chain (al-silsila al-lāzima) and three objections related to the vision of God (ru'ya). The author begins by defining the necessary chain as a chain of order (silsilat al-tartīb) where after every obstacle there exists another obstacle. He argues that if the necessary chain were like that, it would lead to an impossibility only if it implied an infinite regress with no end, but in fact it can be possible if it refers to future events that are endless, like the eternal bliss of Paradise and torment of Hell. Then the author presents three objections: The second objection (it seems the first was not mentioned here) is that vision itself cannot be seen (i.e., a vision cannot be the object of another vision), and its proof relies on infinite regress. The author responds that this is refuted if one admits that existence is what makes a thing visible, implying that a vision exists and thus could be seen. The third objection is that a person cannot see his own vision (i.e., the mental image of his own sight), but can possibly see the vision of another. The proponent argues that seeing another's vision does not lead to infinite regress because the second locus (the vision of another) can be annihilated by God along with obstacles. The author refutes this by pointing out that if one allows seeing the obstacle (i.e., the intermediate vision), then the same infinite regress applies when the vision of another is not seen; and if one does not allow seeing obstacles, then that cuts off the regress in both cases. The author then concludes that the correct position, given the premise that existence makes things visible, is that of Shaykh al-Ash'ari with the addition of al-Qadi's response.

Refutation of Objections on Vision of God
Presentation and refutation of two objections about vision.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two objections related to the vision of God (ru'ya) — that vision itself cannot be seen and that one cannot see one's own vision — and how the author refutes them using the premise that existence is the cause of visibility.

Core Idea
Existence makes things visible; thus vision can be seen and regress can be avoided.

After clarifying the necessary chain, the author addresses two objections that attempt to use infinite regress to deny the possibility of seeing God. The first objection claims that vision itself cannot be seen, and the second that one cannot see one's own vision. The author responds by invoking the principle that existence (الوجود) is what makes something visible, and by analyzing the cases of seeing one's own vision versus another's.

Guided Path
1
Read the second objection and its refutation
The second objection says that vision (الرؤية) cannot itself be seen, based on infinite regress. The author refutes it by stating that if existence is the cause of visibility, then vision, which exists, can be seen.
This refutation relies on the premise that existence is the cause of visibility (المصحح للرؤية).
2
Read the third objection and its refutation
The third objection says a person cannot see his own vision but can see another's. The author refutes it by analyzing two scenarios: if obstacles can be seen, regress follows; if not, regress is cut off. Either way, the objection fails.
The author notes the weakness of this objection and references al-Qadi's correction of al-Ash'ari's view.
3
Understand the conclusion
The author concludes that the correct position is that of Shaykh al-Ash'ari combined with al-Qadi's response, given that existence is the cause of visibility.
This concludes the discussion on vision.

Defending the Two-Fold Division of Kalam: Objection and Response
Defense of the two-fold division of kalam with objection and answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical defense of the two-fold division of kalam into report and request, and why attempts to add a third category lead to absurdity.

Core Idea
The two-fold division of speech is not arbitrary but logically necessary.

In this passage, Sanusi presents a sophisticated objection-and-answer argument defending the reduction of all types of kalam to just two: khabar (report) and talab (request). An opponent claims that a third category is possible, but the response shows that such a move leads to logical contradiction. The passage illustrates how rational consistency demands the two-fold division.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim
The passage begins by restating that all divisions of kalam reduce to report and request. Understand this foundational claim before engaging with the objection.
The types include inquiry, promise, and threat, all considered subtypes of report.
2
Follow the objection
Some masters object that the division is not necessarily limited to two; one could posit a third category that relates to the two like they relate to each other. Grasp the logical symmetry they propose.
3
Understand the counter-objection
The defender responds by applying the same logic to divine attributes: the opponent could similarly posit a meaning outside knowledge and power, leading to an infinite regress. See how the objection backfires.
This is a dialectical move showing the opponent's argument is too strong.
4
Grasp the specific absurdity
The core argument: if a single meaning were both report and request, it would both contradict and not contradict prohibition—an impossibility. Recognize this as a reductio ad absurdum.
Command contradicts prohibition; report does not. So a single entity cannot have both properties.
5
See the conclusion
The passage concludes that to avoid this logical absurdity, some scholars adopted multiplicity in divine attributes (multiple eternal speech attributes). Notice how this reinforces the two-fold division.
This shows the practical theological implication of rejecting the division.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection-and-answer argument defending the view that all types of speech (kalam) reduce to two categories: khabar (report) and talab (request). The author begins by summarizing the premise: istikhbar, promise, and threat all revert to khabar, so the overall division is only khabar and talab. Some masters then defend this position against the claim that the division is incomplete. They argue that just as it is valid to reduce all types to two, it is equally valid in reason to posit a third type that is included under them analogously. The opponent counters that the same could be said for attributes like knowledge and power: one could imagine a meaning that relates to both, leading to an infinite regress. Then a specific objection is raised: if there were a single meaning that is both khabar and talab, it would both contradict prohibition (since khabar does not contradict, but command does) and not contradict – an impossibility. The response is that this same impossibility would apply to the opponent's own analogy of knowledge and power. The passage concludes that to avoid this absurdity, some scholars adopted multiplicity in the divine attributes (i.e., multiple eternal attributes of speech), fleeing the logical consequence. The reasoning is tightly dialectical, showing that the two-fold division is logically consistent and that objections against it backfire.

Definition of Unity and Its Divisions
Sanusi defines unity quoting two authorities and classifies it into real and relative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of unity (al-wahda) according to al-Baydawi and al-Juwayni, and the distinction between real unity (al-wahid al-haqiqi) and relative unity (al-wahid al-idafi).

Core Idea
Unity has two forms: absolute indivisibility (God) and relative indivisibility (created things).

Before diving into the proofs of God's oneness, Sanusi establishes the conceptual ground by defining what 'unity' means. He draws on two major authorities, al-Baydawi and al-Juwayni, to show that unity is not a single concept but has a real and a relative sense. This distinction is crucial because it allows us to speak of God's unique oneness while still using the same word for unity in other contexts.

Guided Path
1
Read the preliminary note
Sanusi introduces the chapter on oneness and states the need for a preliminary on the meaning and divisions of unity. Read this opening to understand the purpose of the passage.
2
Study al-Baydawi's definition
Al-Baydawi defines unity as a thing being indivisible into parts sharing the same essence. Notice how this definition covers two types: real unity (no division at all) and relative unity (divisible into unequal parts).
Real unity applies only to Allah; relative unity applies to composite things like the human body.
3
Understand what is excluded
Al-Baydawi's definition excludes things divisible into equal parts, like a group of identical drops. This clarifies that relative unity requires parts that are different in essence.
4
Consider al-Juwayni's definition
Al-Juwayni gives a simpler definition: 'the one is that which is not divisible.' This emphasizes indivisibility as the core meaning of unity.
Al-Juwayni's definition is more general and does not distinguish between real and relative.

This passage classifies the concept of 'the one' (al-wāḥid) into seven logical types, a standard kalam taxonomy used to analyze unity. The author begins by listing two preliminary subdivisions: the one by individual (al-wāḥid bi-l-shakhṣ) is subdivided into one by continuity (ittiṣāl) and one by aggregation (ijtimāʿ), also called one by composition (tarkīb) and one by connection (irtibāṭ). The one by accident (al-wāḥid bi-l-ʿaraḍ) is subdivided into one by predicate (maḥmūl) and one by subject (mawḍūʿ). He then states these are seven divisions and explains the basis of division. The first major division is whether the one is indivisible in any respect (the true one) or not. The second major division (the divisible one) is further divided based on predicability: if it cannot be predicated of many, it is the one by individual; if it can be predicated of many, it is one in one respect and many in another, requiring two different respects. The respect of unity can be the essence itself (one by species), a part of the essence (either common to many realities as genus or specific as differentia), or external to the essence (one by accident, which is either by predicate or subject). Finally, the one by individual that is divisible is split: if its divisions are similar, it is one by continuity (e.g., magnitude or a simple body receiving division through magnitude); if divisions are different, it is one by aggregation (e.g., a body with different organs). This logical framework is foundational for discussing God's oneness, which negates all forms of composition and multiplicity.

This passage is an exposition and defense of a specific theory of kasb (acquisition) attributed to al-Qadi Abu Bakr al-Baqillani, as adopted by al-Shahrastani. The argument proceeds as follows:

1. The passage begins by clarifying the nature of the 'most specific description' (akhaṣṣ waṣf) of an act. It states that the accidental event/al-ready state (al-ḥāditha) affects this description, not the act's existence. Al-Qadi holds that this most specific description is a 'state' (ḥāl), while the 'Master' (al-Ustādh, i.e., Abu Ishaq al-Isfara'ini) denies states and says it is an 'aspect' (wajh) and a 'consideration' (i‘tibār).
2. Al-Shahrastani chose al-Qadi's view and differentiated between the two aspects of invention (ikhtirā‘) and acquisition (kasb). The key idea: motion qua motion is the act of God, involving existence and invention. From this follows God's complete knowledge of it and that God is not described by it as an inherent attribute—so we do not say He is moving by it. The same motion, in its particularity (e.g., being prayer, theft), is attributed to the servant. The servant's power (qudra) only affects this aspect, not the existence. The servant's essence becomes the locus of this act and its acquisition, so he is described as moving, still, praying, etc.
3. Moral and legal evaluation depends on this specific aspect. If the act conforms to a command, it is obedience; if it violates a prohibition, it is disobedience. This aspect is what the obligation (taklīf) addresses, what reward and punishment are for, not the existence per se.

The Mu‘tazila hold that things have realities in non-existence, and God only brings them into existence, not affecting their specific natures. The obligation is not about existence but about these specific natures. However, they claim that the servant's power is limited to the act of existence? The passage says: the Mu‘tazila say that what the obligation is directed to is not within the servant's power, and what is within his power is not what the obligation is directed to. This is a criticism: they separate the object of obligation from what the servant can do. Al-Qadi's view avoids this by making the specific quality both the object of obligation and what the servant's power acts upon.
5. Finally, Ibn al-Tilmisani adds a note: although al-Shahrastani's account escapes the standard Ash‘ari problem (that obligation becomes impossible: 'Do, O you who have no act!') because it gives the servant's power some effect, it is still weak. The weakness lies in al-Qadi's principle that all possible things are equally attributable to God; specification of some to God is arbitrary, which undermines the distinction. But the passage ends by stating that this leads to the possibility (imkān) of the acts.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition and defense of a specific theory of kasb (acquisition) attributed to al-Qadi Abu Bakr al-Baqillani, as adopted by al-Shahrastani. The argument proceeds as follows:

1. The passage begins by clarifying the nature of the 'most specific description' (akhaṣṣ waṣf) of an act. It states that the accidental event/al-ready state (al-ḥāditha) affects this description, not the act's existence. Al-Qadi holds that this most specific description is a 'state' (ḥāl), while the 'Master' (al-Ustādh, i.e., Abu Ishaq al-Isfara'ini) denies states and says it is an 'aspect' (wajh) and a 'consideration' (i‘tibār).
2. Al-Shahrastani chose al-Qadi's view and differentiated between the two aspects of invention (ikhtirā‘) and acquisition (kasb). The key idea: motion qua motion is the act of God, involving existence and invention. From this follows God's complete knowledge of it and that God is not described by it as an inherent attribute—so we do not say He is moving by it. The same motion, in its particularity (e.g., being prayer, theft), is attributed to the servant. The servant's power (qudra) only affects this aspect, not the existence. The servant's essence becomes the locus of this act and its acquisition, so he is described as moving, still, praying, etc.
3. Moral and legal evaluation depends on this specific aspect. If the act conforms to a command, it is obedience; if it violates a prohibition, it is disobedience. This aspect is what the obligation (taklīf) addresses, what reward and punishment are for, not the existence per se.
4. al-Shahrastani then compares this view favorably to the Mu‘tazila position. The Mu‘tazila hold that things have realities in non-existence, and God only brings them into existence, not affecting their specific natures. The obligation is not about existence but about these specific natures. However, they claim that the servant's power is limited to the act of existence? The passage says: the Mu‘tazila say that what the obligation is directed to is not within the servant's power, and what is within his power is not what the obligation is directed to. This is a criticism: they separate the object of obligation from what the servant can do. Al-Qadi's view avoids this by making the specific quality both the object of obligation and what the servant's power acts upon.
5. Finally, Ibn al-Tilmisani adds a note: although al-Shahrastani's account escapes the standard Ash‘ari problem (that obligation becomes impossible: 'Do, O you who have no act!') because it gives the servant's power some effect, it is still weak. The weakness lies in al-Qadi's principle that all possible things are equally attributable to God; specification of some to God is arbitrary, which undermines the distinction. But the passage ends by stating that this leads to the possibility (imkān) of the acts.

Kasb as the Object of Legal Obligation
Sanusi defines kasb as the act concomitant with acquired power and explains why it is the object of obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why kasb—the act performed with acquired power—is the proper object of divine obligation, and how all acts are ultimately Allah's creation with conventional indications.

Core Idea
Kasb is the act concomitant with acquired power, and obligation is by divine convention, not by our power.

In this passage, Sanusi unpacks the phrase 'and it is the object of legal obligation' from his earlier definition of kasb. He clarifies that kasb—the existence of the predetermined act together with the acquired power—is what the Law obligates. He then explains that acts devoid of acquired power, like involuntary tremors, are graciously exempted, and that the connection between acts and reward is purely conventional, not rational.

Commentary
This passage explains the phrase 'And it is the object of legal obligation' (وهو مُتعلَّقُ التكليف الشرعي). The author clarifies that 'it' refers to kasb, which is defined as the existence of the predetermined act (maqdūr) together with the acquired power (qudra ḥāditha). This is what the Law obligates. The reason is that if the act occurs without acquired power—like an involuntary tremor (حركة الارتعاش)—Allah has graciously exempted such acts from obligation, both in terms of commanding and forbidding (negation and affirmation). However, if Allah had reversed the obligation (i.e., obligated involuntary acts and exempted voluntary ones), or obligated all acts, it would have been equally good, since there is no effect of the servant's power in any of them. Rather, all acts are created by Allah. The Law has set them, when accompanied by accidents (a‘rāḍ) like powers and wills, as indicators (amārāt) for reward and punishment. The author then draws an analogy: just as it is valid for Allah to make one of His acts, when accompanied by another act of His, an indicator for what He wills of reward or punishment, it is also valid for Him to make it devoid of that other act, or to make another act in its place an indicator, because the indication in such matters is conventional (ja‘liyya), not rational. This underscores that the connection between acts and reward/punishment is by divine convention, not by any inherent effect of the servant's power.

The First Aspect of Looseness in the Term 'Miracle'
Sanusi explains the first aspect of looseness in the term 'mu'jizah' via two cases and an analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the term 'mu'jizah' (miracle) is used metaphorically to mean 'absence of power' rather than literal incapacity, and how this parallels the loose usage of 'ignorance'.

Core Idea
A 'miracle' does not literally incapacitate opponents; it signals the absence of power to imitate.

Balāgha is the sum of fluent word choice and eloquent arrangement.

Ibn al-Tilmisānī provides concise definitions for four central terms of Arabic rhetoric. These definitions form the foundation for understanding how the Quran achieves its unmatched eloquence. In this lesson, we examine his classification and the relationships between the terms.

[على بيان معنى الفصاحة، والجزالة، والنظم، والبلاغة] قالَ ابنُ التِّلِمْساني: ( الفصاحةُ: عبارة عن دلالة اللفظ على المعنى بشرطِ إيضاح الغرض منه، والجزالة: عبارة عن دلالته على معناه بشرطِ قلَّةِ حروفِهِ وتناسب مخارجها، والنظم عبارة عن الأسلوب الخاص في ترتيب الأقوالِ بعضها مع بعض، ثم الحسْنُ فيهِ بحسَبِ تناسب الكلماتِ في مواردِها، وذلك أنواع وأصناف، ومجموع الجزالة والنظم هو البلاغة ) قلتُ: المشهور بين البيانيين: أنَّ الفصاحةَ تُوصَفُ بها الكلمة والكلام والمتكلم: فمعناها في الكلمةِ: أنْ تكونَ خالصةً مِنْ تنافرِ الحروفِ؛ احترازاً من نحو قوله: [من الطويل] غَدَائِرُهُ مُسْتَشْزَرَاتِ إِلَى الْعُلَا
[On explaining the meaning of eloquence (al-faṣāḥa), fluency (al-jazāla), composition (al-naẓm), and rhetoric (al-balāgha)] Ibn al-Tilmisānī said: 'Eloquence (al-faṣāḥa) is the expression of the word indicating the meaning with the condition of clarifying the intended purpose from it; fluency (al-jazāla) is its indication of its meaning with the condition of fewness of its letters and the harmony of their points of articulation; composition (al-naẓm) is the specific style in arranging statements with each other, and then the beauty in it is according to the proportion of words in their contexts; and there are types and kinds of that; and the sum of fluency and composition is rhetoric (al-balāgha).' I said: The well-known among the rhetoricians is that eloquence is used to describe the word, the speech, and the speaker: its meaning in the word is that it be free from discordance of letters; as a precaution against such as his saying [al-ṭawīl]: 'Ghada'iruhu mustashzarāti ilā al-'ulā' (His locks are raised high...)
Source pages p. 588

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification of four key rhetorical terms used in discussing the Quran's inimitability. Ibn al-Tilmisānī first gives his definitions: faṣāḥa is the word's clear indication of meaning that also clarifies the speaker's intent; jazāla is the word's indication with few letters and harmonious articulation; naẓm is the specific arrangement of words in a discourse, with beauty arising from the proportional placement of words; and balāgha is the combination of jazāla and naẓm. After citing this, the author (al-Sanūsī or the commentator) adds his own clarification: the standard view among rhetoricians (bayāniyyūn) is that faṣāḥa applies to the word, the sentence (kalām), and the speaker. For the word specifically, faṣāḥa means it is free from discordant letters (tanāfur al-ḥurūf), and he gives an example from the famous line of Imru' al-Qais where the word 'mustashzarāt' has discordant letters. This comment refines the definition by grounding it in the established technical usage of the rhetorical tradition.

Understanding Balāgha: Eloquence in Context
Sanusi defines balāgha and illustrates it with three situational examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that balāgha (rhetorical eloquence) requires both fasāḥa (linguistic fluency) and conformity to the situation (muqtaḍā al-ḥāl).

Core Idea
Balāgha is eloquence that fits the context, not just fancy words.

This passage from Sanusi's Sharḥ al-Sanūsiyya introduces the concept of balāgha, a key term in Arabic rhetoric. While fasāḥa ensures the speech is clear and flowing, balāgha adds the crucial requirement that the speech must suit the situation and the audience. The author illustrates this with three communication scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition
Understand that balāgha applies to speech and speaker, and its meaning in speech is being eloquent and conforming to the situation.
Notice the phrase 'jarīyan 'alā mā yaqtaḍīhi al-ḥāl'
2
Examine the three examples
Each example describes a different audience: a denier, an uninformed person, and a doubtful person. Note the corresponding level of emphasis.
Emphasis increases with the strength of denial or doubt.
3
Reflect on the key insight
Balāgha is not just about beautiful language but about effective communication tailored to context.

وأما البلاغة : فلا يُوصَفُ بها إلا الكلام والمتكلم . أما معناها في الكلام : فهو أن يكون فصيحاً، جارياً على ما يقتضيه الحالُ ؛ أي : السبب الذي ورد الكلام لأجله ؛ كالكلام الوارد لدفع إنكار مُنكِرٍ ؛ فإنَّهُ يناسبُهُ أنْ يُؤكَّد بحسب مراتب الإنكار . والوارد لإفادة خالي الذهنِ مِنَ الحُكْم يناسبُهُ أَنْ يُلقى إليه الكلام غير مُؤكَّد . والوارد لإفادة مَنْ هو مُشعر بالحكمِ شَاكٌ فِيهِ يُستحسَنُ أَنْ يُؤكَّدَ .
And as for balāgha: it is not described except for speech and the speaker. As for its meaning in speech: it is that it be eloquent, running according to what the situation requires; that is, the reason for which the speech was uttered. For example, speech that comes to repel the denial of a denier: it is appropriate that it be emphasized according to the degrees of denial. And speech that comes to inform one whose mind is free of the judgment: it is appropriate that the speech be delivered to him unemphasized. And speech that comes to inform one who has a sense of the judgment but is doubtful about it: it is recommended that it be emphasized.
Source pages p. 590

Commentary
This passage introduces balāgha (rhetoric) and restricts its description to speech and the speaker. It then defines balāgha in speech: it must be eloquent (fasīḥ) and also conform to the situation (muqtaḍā al-ḥāl), meaning the reason for which the speech is produced. Three examples illustrate degrees of emphasis: for repelling a denier, emphasis is needed according to the strength of the denial; for informing someone with no prior knowledge, no emphasis is appropriate; for someone who is aware but doubtful, moderate emphasis is recommended. Thus balāgha adds contextual appropriateness to eloquence.

Misconception: Some think balāgha is simply eloquent or ornate speech, regardless of context.
Correction: Balāgha specifically requires that speech conform to the situation (muqtaḍā al-ḥāl) and the needs of the audience, as shown by the three examples.

Balagha: The Faculty of Eloquence and Its Distinction from Fasaha
Sanusi defines balagha as a faculty and distinguishes it from fasaha.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's definition of balagha as a faculty, its relationship to fasaha, and the two extremes of eloquence.

Core Idea
Eloquence is a higher, more specific quality than fluency.

Sanusi begins by defining balagha (eloquence) as a faculty that enables a speaker to produce eloquent speech. He then distinguishes it from fasaha (fluency), showing that while every eloquent speaker is fluent, the reverse is not true. This passage also introduces the two extremes of eloquence, from the miraculous to the animal-like.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then its translation. Let the definitions sink in.
2
Identify the definition
Notice that balagha is defined as a faculty (malaka) enabling eloquent expression. This is the core.
3
Understand the relationship
Sanusi says balagha is more specific than fasaha. Every eloquent person is fluent, but not vice versa. Grasp this hierarchy.
4
Explore the two extremes
Find the highest (i'jaz) and lowest (near animal sounds) extremes. Note that between them are countless degrees.
The highest is judged by taste (dhawq).

Source Text

وأما معناها في المُتكلّم : فهي مَلَكَةٌ يَقتدِرُ بها على التعبير بكلام بليغ . فعُلِمَ مِنْ هذا : أنَّ البلاغة أخصُّ مِنَ الفصاحة ، فكلُّ بليغ فصيح ، وليس كل فصيح بليغاً ، وقد تُطلَقُ إحداهما على الأخرى توسعاً . وللبلاغة طرفان : أعلى ؛ وهو الإعجاز ، والمحكّم فيه الذوق ، وأدنى ؛ وهو ما إذا بُدِّلَ عن حالِهِ التحق عندَ البلغاء بأصواتِ الحيوانات ، وبينهما مراتب لا تكاد تنحصر
As for its meaning in relation to the speaker: it is a faculty by which one is able to express oneself with eloquent speech. So it is known from this that balagha is more specific than fasaha: every eloquent (baligh) speaker is fluent (fasih), but not every fluent speaker is eloquent. Sometimes one is used in place of the other by extension. Balagha has two extremes: the highest, which is i‘jāz (inimitability), and its arbiter is taste; and the lowest, which is that if it were changed from its state, it would, in the view of the eloquent, join the sounds of animals. Between them are ranks that are almost endless.
Source pages p. 591

Commentary
This passage defines balagha (eloquence) in terms of a speaker's faculty and clarifies its relationship to fasaha (fluency). It begins by stating that balagha is a faculty (malaka) enabling one to produce eloquent speech. From this definition, it deduces that balagha is more specific (akhaṣṣ) than fasaha: every eloquent person is fluent, but not every fluent person is eloquent. This distinction is important because the two terms are sometimes used interchangeably out of broadening. Then it describes the two extremes (ṭarafān) of balagha: the highest degree is i‘jāz, which is the miraculous inimitability of the Qur’an, judged by taste (dhawq); the lowest degree is speech that, if altered, would resemble animal sounds. Between them lie countless intermediate levels.
