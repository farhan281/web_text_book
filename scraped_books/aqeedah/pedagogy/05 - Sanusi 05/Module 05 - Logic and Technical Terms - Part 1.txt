Sanusi’s Doctrine on Karamat and Their Nearness to Prophetic Miracles
Sanusi affirms karamat and compares them to prophetic miracles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi’s affirmation of saintly miracles (karamat) and why he considers them nearly on par with prophetic miracles.

Core Idea
Karamat almost join the miracles of prophets; denial stems from lack of personal experience.

Sanusi begins by stating that his position on karamat aligns with the mainstream Sunni verifiers. He then quotes his own work, Sharh al-Aqida al-Wusta, where he makes a striking comparison: the miracles of saints are almost as manifest as those of prophets. He also explains that the denial of such miracles is expected from innovators, as they have never witnessed them.

Guided Path
1
Understand Sanusi's alignment with Ahl al-Sunna
Sanusi explicitly states that his view on karamat is that of the generality of verifiers from Ahl al-Sunna. This establishes orthodoxy.
2
Analyze the comparison between karamat and mu'jizat
Sanusi says karamat 'almost join' (takadu talhaq) prophetic miracles. Notice the careful language: not equal but nearly so.
This underscores the extraordinary nature of karamat.
3
Reflect on the reason for denial
Deniers are mainly innovators who lack personal experience; their denial is based on ignorance, not evidence.
Sanusi implies that witnessing leads to certainty.

Sanusi’s Critique of Hasty Takfir Regarding Saintly Miracles
Sanusi criticizes the takfir of those who accept saintly bilocation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sanusi criticizes a Sunni jurist who declared takfir on those who believe in the possibility of saintly miracles like Ibrahim ibn Adham's bilocation.

Core Idea
Declaring disbelief over the possibility of saintly miracles is an excessive and unwarranted act.

After affirming karamat, Sanusi turns to criticize a specific incident where some Sunni jurists declared that believing in the possibility of events like Ibrahim ibn Adham being in two places at once constitutes disbelief. Sanusi finds this astonishing, implying that such takfir is hasty and unbefitting of true scholarship.

Guided Path
1
Understand the incident
Sanusi references a story about Ibrahim ibn Adham being seen in Basra and Mecca on the same day. Some jurists said that believing this is possible is kufr.
This is a specific case of a saintly miracle (bilocation).
2
Analyze Sanusi’s reaction
Sanusi calls this 'the wonder' (al-ajab), expressing amazement that Sunni jurists would resort to takfir over a matter of possibility.
Sanusi himself holds that such events are possible and are karamat.

Sanusi’s Humility Before the Station of the Awliya
Sanusi humbly acknowledges the saints' superior spiritual state.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will appreciate Sanusi’s humility in acknowledging the superior spiritual station of the awliya and his own limitations.

Core Idea
The saints attain direct vision; we are only on the shore of wish, gathering from their ocean.

Sanusi concludes by quoting himself from a discussion of the conditions of sainthood. He humbly admits that compared to the awliya, he and most people are merely on the 'shore of wish', scooping from the ocean of tawhid. He recognizes that the saints' level of direct vision (al-ayan) is far above the level of rational proof (al-burhan) that most possess.

Contrast between al-ayan and al-burhan
Sanusi contrasts the saints' direct vision (al-ayan) with the demonstrative knowledge (al-burhan) that most people have. He admits the saints are superior.
This shows Sanusi's epistemological humility.

Classification of Rational Proof and Definition of Burhān
Sanusi classifies rational proofs into five types and defines burhān as composed of yaqīniyyāt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the five types of rational proof and why only burhān yields certainty through its certain premises.

Core Idea
Burhān is the only rational proof that delivers certainty, built entirely from certain premises.

Before diving into theological arguments, Sanusi establishes a crucial logical framework: not all rational proofs are equal. He classifies proofs by their material (premises) and distinguishes burhān as the only demonstrative proof composed entirely of certain premises. This lesson introduces that classification and the beginning of the types of certain knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the passage cuts off mid-sentence; the full classification continues in the next lesson.
2
Identify the five types of rational proof
The passage lists five types: burhān, jadal, khaṭābah, shiʿr, and mughālaṭah. Note that only burhān is described as 'decisive' and is composed of certain premises.
3
Understand burhān's unique status
Burhān is defined as a proof whose premises are all certain (yaqīniyyāt). This is what sets it apart from the other four types, which may use probabilities, accepted opinions, or even false premises.
The certain premises themselves are six types; the passage begins listing them with awwaliyyāt (first principles).

Commentary
This passage begins a new section on the divisions of rational proof (aqsām al-ḥujjah al-ʿaqliyyah). Sanusi explains the phrase 'decisive proofs' (al-barāhīn al-qāṭiʿah) from his matn. He notes that al-barāhīn is the plural of burhān, which is one division of rational proof. Proof (al-ḥujjah) is first divided by its matter (māddah) into two: rational (ʿaqliyyah) and transmitted (naqliyyah). Rational proof then has five subtypes: burhān (demonstration), jadal (dialectic), khaṭābah (rhetoric), shiʿr (poetry), and mughālaṭah (sophistry). Burhān is defined as that which is composed of premises that are all certain (yaqīniyyah). The certain premises themselves are six types. He begins to list them, starting with awwaliyyāt (first principles), also called badīhiyyāt (self-evident truths), which are known immediately by the intellect upon merely conceiving the terms, such as 'the whole is greater than the part.' The text cuts off at the example, but the classification is clear.

Consequently, they mistakenly attribute benefit or harm to things that do not truly have such effects. This leads to shirk (associating partners with Allah), as they affirm intermediaries (وسائط) between God and creation, ascribe causal efficacy to what has no real effect, and place trust in beings that possess no power or control. Sanusi concludes that all contingent things (الممكنات) are mere illusions (خيالات) that — through their very state of dependence — call out: 'The goal is ahead; we are but a test, so do not disbelieve' (alluding to Quran 2:102).

Commentary

Why does the opponent's objection involve circular reasoning (musādara)?
A) The opponent assumes speech is only letters and sounds, which is exactly what is in dispute.
B) The poetic line is anachronistic.
C) The emphatic in the verse does not prevent metaphor.
D) The Ahl al-Sunna have no proof.

Key Takeaway
The definitive rational proof (burhān qaṭ'ī) that Allah's speech is not limited to letters and sounds precedes and validates the scriptural inference, protecting it from dialectical objections based on poetry.
