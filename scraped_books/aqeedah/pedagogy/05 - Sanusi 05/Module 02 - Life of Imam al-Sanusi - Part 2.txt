Sanusi's Forgiveness, Charity, and Asceticism
Describes Sanusi's forgiveness, charity, and ascetic practices.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi combined forgiveness, generosity, and ascetic withdrawal as expressions of his spiritual state.

Core Idea
Forgiveness and charity are the fruit of a heart detached from this world.

This lesson follows Sanusi's final illness, where he forgave a wrongdoer, and then explores his charitable habits and ascetic lifestyle. His words and actions reinforce a life oriented toward the Hereafter.

Guided Path
1
Read the forgiveness scene
Read the account of the scholar who visited him during illness. Notice how Sanusi forgave him despite the wrong, and the scholar's later grief.
This shows Sanusi's magnanimity.
2
Reflect on his charity
He gave charity and commanded his family to do so, especially in hard times. His saying links charity to love of Paradise.
The phrase 'especially during high prices' emphasizes extra virtue.
3
Examine his asceticism
He preferred seclusion, visited ruins for reflection, wept inwardly, and disciplined his sleep and fasting to the point of affecting his face.
His interior was filled with tawhid, his exterior with zuhd.

His main preaching to people was about fear of the majestic Lord and watching Him, and mentioning the states of the afterlife, and along with this he would preach to everyone according to what suited their state, and rarely would you see him except that he was moving his lips in remembrance of God Almighty. And among the forms of his compassion and fear of his Lord, Glorious is He: that a wolf passed by him being chased by a hunter and his dogs, so they caught it and then slaughtered it. When he reached it and saw it thrown on the ground, he wept and said: 'There is no god but Allah, where is the soul that was flowing in it?' And he used to say: 'A person should walk gently and look ahead so that he does not kill an animal on the ground. And if he saw someone hitting an animal violently, he would become upset and say to the beater: "Be gentle, O blessed one!"' And he forbade the teachers of children from beating the boys, and said: 'God Almighty has one hundred mercies, there is no hope in them except for one who is characterized by mercy for all of creation and has compassion on them.' And some scholars visited him during his terminal illness, and he had wronged him, so he asked him for forgiveness, so he forgave him and prayed for good for him. And when the Imam died, this scholar wept over him and was very grieved and said: 'The world has lost him.' And he used to give charity and command his family to give charity, especially in times of hardship and hunger, and he said: 'Whoever loves Paradise, let him give much charity, especially during high prices.' And he preferred seclusion, and visited ruined places for reflection, and said: 'Where are its inhabitants? And how do they enjoy themselves?' And he said: 'How many a laugher among people while his heart weeps out of fear of his Lord, that is the state of the knowers (al-ʻārifīn).' And his state with this world was the state of a prisoner, so his sleep was little and his fasting was long, so he would sleep at the beginning of the night and stay awake the rest of it, with the commitment to fast for a year if he returned to sleep whenever he woke up, and this had affected his face. And what was said about him was true: 'His interior is the realities of tawḥīd, his exterior is asceticism and stripping away, and his speech is guidance for every seeker.' And with all this, he was extremely shy, to the point that he would write intercessions for the sultans out of shyness from the one who asked him for that. So his maternal brother, Shaykh ʻAlī al-Tālūtī, once blamed him for that, and the Imam said to him: 'By God, O my brother, overpowering shyness prevents me from it, and I cannot say "I will not write". If shyness enters its companion into the Fire, then I will enter it.'

Commentary
This passage is a biographical account of Imam Sanusi’s character and piety. It is not a doctrinal argument but a description of his personal virtues. The text proceeds by narrating several aspects of his life: 1) His preaching: He focused on fear of God and the afterlife, tailoring his exhortation to each person, and was constantly engaged in dhikr. 2) His compassion: He wept over a slaughtered wolf, lamenting the loss of its soul, and advised walking gently to avoid harming animals, rebuking those who beat animals. 3) His care for children: He forbade teachers from beating children, stating that God’s mercy is only for those who show mercy to all creatures. 4) His forgiveness: A scholar who had wronged him visited him during his final illness; Sanusi forgave him and prayed for him, and the scholar later grieved deeply at his death. 5) His charity: He gave charity especially during hardship, promising Paradise for those who give charity during high prices. 6) His asceticism: He preferred solitude, visited ruins for reflection, and wept inwardly while outwardly appearing happy. When his brother criticized this, he defended himself by saying that if shyness leads to hell, he would accept it rather than overcome his nature. The passage does not contain any theological argument but presents a model of Islamic piety combining fear, mercy, asceticism, and humility.

Learning Objective

Even a saint's shyness can be overwhelming, yet it remains a virtue when not sinful.

This shows his sincerity and inability to overcome his nature.

Reflect on the lesson
Consider the balance between shyness and duty. Sanusi's actions were not sinful, but his brother saw a potential issue. This shows the complexity of human character.

His daily states and some of his character traits—may Allah have mercy on him: He fasted day by day like the fast of our master David (upon our prophet and him be prayer and peace), and broke his fast with little food, and would not search on his day of breaking fast for what he would eat. He would perhaps remain three days or more without eating or drinking. If food was brought to him, he ate; otherwise, he remained as he was. And perhaps they would ask him after most of the day had passed: 'Are you breaking fast?' He would say: 'Neither breaking fast nor fasting.' So it would be said to him: 'Why do you not inform us of your breaking fast?' And he would smile. And he would perhaps joke with some of his companions, and you would find none better than him at that time, and he would not raise his voice, but rather moderate it. He would shake hands with people and would not prevent kissing his hand. He did not wear a specific garment by which he was known, but his garment was what people were accustomed to. He disliked speech after Fajr and ʿAṣr. He would delay in his prayer by the opening takbīr after the iqāma, and would not say the takbīr except after a while. After the Fajr prayer, he would read his litanies (awrād) in his mosque, then proceed to teaching knowledge until the customary breakfast time. Then he would stand for an hour with the people at the door of his house, then enter and pray Ḍuḥā the length of reading ten ḥizb, then occupy himself with reading (muṭālaʿa) throughout the day. After noon (zawāl), he would go out for his retreats (khalawāt) until sunset, and sometimes he would pray Ẓuhr with the people, then sit praying supererogatory prayers and reciting until ʿAṣr, then occupy himself with his litany (wird) until sunset. When he prayed Maghrib, he would pray six rakʿas, and remain in his mosque until he prayed ʿIshāʾ, and would recite whatever was easy.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical description of Imam al-Sanūsī's daily schedule and character habits. It begins with his fasting practice: he fasted every other day like the prophet David, ate little, and sometimes went three days or more without food or drink, eating only if food was offered. He would not announce his fast; when asked, he gave ambiguous answers. He was pleasant in joking, moderate in voice, allowed hand-kissing, and wore ordinary clothes. He disliked talking after Fajr and ʿAṣr. In his prayers, he would delay the opening takbīr after the iqāma. His daily routine: after Fajr he read his awrād (litanies), then taught until breakfast, then greeted people briefly, then prayed Ḍuḥā reciting ten ḥizb (sections of Quran), then studied (muṭālaʿa) for the rest of the day. After noon he went to his retreats until sunset; sometimes he prayed Ẓuhr with the congregation and then spent time in supererogatory prayer and recitation until ʿAṣr, then focused on his wird (prescribed litany) until sunset. After Maghrib he prayed six rakʿas and stayed in the mosque until ʿIshāʾ, reciting whatever was easy. The passage presents a picture of disciplined, ascetic life devoted to worship, teaching, and seclusion.

The final picture of his life:
Let us ask Shaykh Muhammad ibn Umar al-Mallali, the student of Imam al-Sanusi, to draw for us this picture as he tells us about the moments of meeting and departure from this transient world to the everlasting abode. He, may Allah have mercy on him, said:
He, may Allah be pleased with him, was in the last part of his life very withdrawn from people; hardly anyone could cheer him up as had been his habit before that. Going out to the mosque for teaching and prayer was difficult for him, and he would not go out on some days except out of shyness of the people who were waiting for him in the mosque for prayer. When he, may Allah be pleased with him, felt the pain of the illness from which he died, he stopped going to the mosque. The people heard of his illness and began coming to the mosque and not finding him, so their hearts changed due to the loss of the shaykh and not seeing him. The shaykh was informed of that, so he began to force himself to go out to the mosque for prayer for the sake of the people. When they saw him, they rejoiced and were delighted by his coming out and seeing him.
Then one day he went out and came to the door of the mosque, wanting to ascend into it, but he could not (1). He said: 'How can I go up to the mosque, O Lord?' or as he said. Then he intended to return to his house, but it occurred to him, fearing that he would cause sadness to the people by his return, so he forced himself to ascend to the mosque and led the people in the 'Asr prayer on Friday. He did not complete the prayer except with great difficulty, and this was the last prayer he prayed.
He returned to his house and remained until the morning of the next day, Saturday. His wife brought food near him, and he said to her: 'I cannot [eat] anything. He said to her: 'I cannot [eat] anything (1).' She said to him: 'What is the matter with you?' He said to her: 'I have fallen short.' Then he lost consciousness, and he remained in that state the whole day.
Then his wife spoke to him and said: 'What has taken away your consciousness?' or close to this. He said to her: 'Indeed, the angels ascended with me to the lowest heaven, and I heard a speaker saying to me: 'Leave what you are upon, for your term has drawn near.' Then he said: 'I cannot explain to you the rest of what I saw,' or as he said.
His wife said to him: 'And what were you commanded to leave?' He said to her: 'I have left that prison; I will never take anything from it again.'
Then he kept to his bed from then until he died. The duration of his illness was ten days. Every hour his illness grew stronger, his pain multiplied, his strength and movement weakened, and his tongue became heavy. Yet despite that, he was of sound mind; he did not groan nor did he ...[possibly 'complain'?].
(1) Al-Mallali mentioned in (al-Mawahib al-Qudsiyya, folio 13) that the effort reached him to the point that he was unable to perform tayammum and the pillars of prayer, so he said: 'May Allah have mercy on Abu Hanifa, who declared them to be dropped,' meaning the prayer.

Umar's Knowledge and Spiritual Unveiling
Author demonstrates Umar's superior knowledge through hadith and kashf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the sources that demonstrate Umar ibn al-Khattab's extraordinary knowledge and the concept of spiritual unveiling (kashf).

Core Idea
Umar's knowledge was divinely inspired, surpassing ordinary learning.

This lesson explores the profound knowledge of Umar ibn al-Khattab as attested by Ali ibn Abi Talib, Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib, and a prophetic dream. The passage also introduces the concept of kashf (unveiling), where Umar's pure mind received knowledge directly from Allah. By examining these testimonies, we gain insight into the spiritual stature of one of Islam's greatest figures.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the literal translation to understand the flow.
Notice the three distinct testimonies and the final description of Umar.
2
Identify the three proofs
Identify the statements from Ali, Sa'id, and the hadith. Understand how each supports Umar's superior knowledge.
The dream interpretation is key—milk symbolizing knowledge.
3
Understand kashf (unveiling)
Reflect on the term 'mukkashaf' and how Umar's pure mind could inscribe knowledge without external evidence.
Kashf is a form of divine inspiration beyond ordinary learning.

Commentary
This passage establishes the extraordinary knowledge of Umar ibn al-Khattab. It begins by quoting Ali's testimony that Umar was 'the most knowledgeable of us about Allah' at his death. Then Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib confirms his unparalleled knowledge. The author then cites a hadith from the Sahih (Bukhari and Muslim): the Prophet dreamed he was drinking milk until satiation came from his fingernails, and he gave the leftover to Umar; the Prophet interpreted this dream as 'knowledge.' Thus Umar received prophetic knowledge symbolically. Finally, the author describes Umar as 'mukkashaf' (one granted unveiling), meaning his mind was so pure that whatever he thought would become manifest, even without external evidence. This emphasizes that Umar's knowledge was divinely inspired.

Umar's Exceptional Knowledge and Unshakeable Certainty
Anecdotal demonstration of Umar's intuitive knowledge and certainty.

Umar's knowledge of Allah was so deep it became part of his very being.

This passage from a classical text celebrates the unique spiritual stature of Umar ibn al-Khattab. Through anecdotes and prophetic testimonies, it reveals how his intellect was elevated to the level of unveiling (kashf), making theoretical knowledge intuitive and certainty absolute. As we read, we will see how Umar's trust in his God-given intellect allowed him to face even the angels of the grave with complete confidence.

Commentary
This passage is a panegyric demonstration of the extraordinary knowledge of Allah possessed by the Companion Umar ibn al-Khattab (رضي الله عنه). The author begins by recounting a story where Umar astutely deduced the inheritance shares of a woman's brother, showing his immediate and accurate knowledge without deliberation. The author comments that such instances are countless, and they illustrate a 'holy perception' that made difficult theoretical sciences intuitively necessary for him. The author then invites the reader to consider: if Umar could perceive matters that have abundant textual evidence so effortlessly, what must his knowledge of Allah be like? This is the main theme: Umar's marifa (knowledge of Allah) was of the highest order. The author then cites that Umar himself said at the death of the Prophet that he died 'the most knowledgeable of us of Allah,' and Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib affirmed that he never saw anyone more knowledgeable than Umar. The author then references a prophetic dream in which milk symbolized knowledge, and the Prophet gave the leftover to Umar, indicating his superior share of knowledge. Next, the author states that Umar was a mukashaf (one endowed with unveiling): whatever he estimated in his mind would occur, as if manifested in the pure mirror of his mind, without need for evidence or indication. This is the point about kashf (unveiling) and intuitive knowledge. The author then asks rhetorically: if this was the case for worldly matters, how much more for knowledge of the Creator, Whose signs are clear in all beings? The author then presents the famous incident about the questioning in the grave: when the Prophet informed Umar about the trial of the grave and the angels Munkar and Nakir, Umar asked if his intellect ('aql) would be with him. When the Prophet affirmed, Umar said he would be sufficient for them. The Prophet then declared, 'Indeed, Umar is certain.' The author emphasizes Umar's trust in his intellect and his lack of fear despite the formidable situation. This shows his knowledge had reached the level of haqq al-yaqin (truth of certainty) or even 'ayn al-yaqin (eye of certainty). The author marvels that such a statement could only come from one whose knowledge of Allah was so integrated into his being that everything else ceased to matter. Finally, the author adds a related point about Uthman: the Prophet said the angels of heaven are shy before him, and it is narrated that Uthman would not raise his head to heaven out of shyness from Allah. This serves as further evidence of the profound knowledge and spiritual state of the Companions.

Core Idea
The Sahaba's knowledge and imamate are proven by clear Qur'anic and prophetic testimony.

Sanusi now turns to proof texts to demonstrate the exalted status of the Companions. He presents a series of hadith and a Qur'anic verse that together show their certainty, their superiority not by outward acts but by inner faith, and their role as guides for all believers. This passage establishes that the Companions were not ordinary Muslims but divinely appointed leaders with the highest level of ijtihad.

Commentary
This passage is a proof text in which Sanusi establishes the high rank and knowledge of the Companions (Sahaba) through textual evidence. He begins by describing the fruit of spiritual watchfulness as perfect knowledge and certainty, to the point of seeing reality directly. He then cites three prophetic hadiths: first, that Abu Bakr's certainty is so complete that even if the veil of the unseen were removed, it would increase him not; second, that Abu Bakr's superiority is not by outward acts but by a deep-seated inner faith; third, that Umar's virtues are so vast they cannot be enumerated, and he is a 'good deed' among Abu Bakr's good deeds. Sanusi then asks rhetorically how one can count the good qualities of the Companions. وهذا أوان الفراغ مِنْ هذا التعليق المباركِ إِنْ شاءَ الله تعالى ، فنسأله سبحانَهُ أَنْ يَختم لنا بالإيمان والإسلام ، واتباع السنة ، والمغفرة لجميع ذنوبنا بلا محنة في الدنيا ولا في الآخرة ، وأنْ يبوثنا معَ الآباء والأمَّهاتِ والإخوة والذريَّةِ والأحبَّةِ مِنْ أعالي الفردوس المنازل الفاخرة ، وأنْ يُسهل الفهم على كلِّ مَنْ تعاطى هذا الشرحَ أو أصله ، ويختم له بخواتم السعداء ، ويشرح صدره ، ويُزَكِّيَ في الدنيا والآخرة فعله وقولَهُ ، آمين آمين آمين يا رب العالمين . والصلاة والسلام على سيّد الأولين والآخرينَ ، ورضي الله تعالى عن آلِهِ وصحبهِ ومَنْ تبعهم بإحسان إلى يوم الدين ، وسلام على جميع النبيين والمرسلين وآخر دعوانا أن اسم مدرب العالمين ولاحول ولا قوة إلا بالله العلی العظيم
And this is the time of completion of this blessed commentary, if Allah wills. So we ask Him—glorified is He—to conclude for us with faith and Islam, following the Sunna, forgiveness for all our sins without trial in this world or the Hereafter; and to settle us with fathers, mothers, brothers, offspring, and beloved ones in the lofty abodes of Paradise; and to facilitate understanding for everyone who engages with this commentary or its original, and to seal him with the seals of the fortunate, and to expand his breast, and to purify in this world and the Hereafter his deed and his saying. Amen, amen, amen, O Lord of the worlds. And prayers and peace be upon the master of the first and the last, and may Allah be pleased with his family, his companions, and those who follow them in excellence until the Day of Judgment. And peace be upon all the prophets and messengers. And the conclusion of our call is that [?] the name of the trainer of the worlds, and there is no power nor strength except by Allah, the Most High, the Magnificent.

Commentary
This passage is the author's closing supplication and colophon for his commentary. He thanks Allah for completion and asks for a good end, forgiveness, and to be gathered with loved ones in Paradise. He also prays for those who use his work, that they gain understanding and righteousness. He ends with salutations on the Prophet, his family, companions, and all prophets, and a standard closing phrase. The specific phrase 'اسم مدرب العالمين' appears to be a corruption of the usual 'الحمد لله رب العالمين' (Praise be to Allah, Lord of the worlds). This passage shows the author's humility and traditional closing formulas.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Scholarly works often end with the author boasting about their achievement.
Correction: Instead, Sanusi ends with humble supplication, acknowledging Allah's grace and seeking forgiveness and guidance for himself and his readers.
