Conclusion: Faulty Scale of Reason and Necessity of Prophethood
Sanusi concludes the argument and transitions to the necessity of prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's conclusion that rational judgment (tahsin wa taqbih) is a faulty scale and that the search for prophethood is necessary.

Core Idea
Reason's scale for judging actions is faulty; truth depends on revelation, making prophethood necessary.

Sanusi concludes his argument by declaring that using reason to assess God's rulings is like using a defective scale. The correct approach is to rely on revelation. This realization makes it obligatory to investigate prophethood and its conditions, which is the subject of the next chapter.

1
Read the conclusion
Read the passage where Sanusi declares the scale of reason faulty and truth suspended on revelation.
2
Understand the imagery
Note the simile of a faulty scale and the phrase 'disgraced and regretful'.
This emphasizes the failure of rationalism.
3
Connect to prophethood
See how this conclusion naturally leads to the necessity of investigating prophethood.

As for the invalidity of its return to the slave immediately: because he only obtains in the immediate term mere fatigue. And as for the invalidity of its return to him in the hereafter: because reason has no scope before revelation in any matter of the afterlife by consensus. And as for the invalidity of its return to the Lord, Exalted: because of His being exalted and sublime above that a perfection should be renewed for Him; rather, He is perfect in His essence and eternal attributes, and independent of creation and their actions. So this aspect of rational consideration repels the obligation of gratitude and opposes the aspect that made it obligatory according to them—which is the perception that He, Exalted, is a bestower of favors. If they say: We do not grant that there is no benefit in gratitude before revelation; rather, there is a benefit for the slave—namely, security from the punishment that might possibly be established on the occasion of turning away from gratitude. We say: Similarly, it is possible that one may be punished in performing gratitude from two aspects. The first: that he has fatigued therein the self that is owned by God, Exalted, and acted therein without His permission. So he becomes like someone who thanks a king who has conveyed a favor to him by fatiguing the king's slaves in performing its thanks without his permission. There is no doubt that he has exposed himself by thanking the king in this manner to punishment. The second: that one whom a generous king of extreme generosity gives a small morsel, for example of barley bread, while he has from the treasuries of types of foods and kinds of wealth that which has no end, and it does not diminish by what he gives from it; then that needy poor person starts mentioning the king and praising him in gatherings for giving that barley morsel—he deserves punishment, because of his mockery of the king and belittling of his status when he praises him for something insignificant in his sight. And there is no doubt that all the favors of this world and the next, in comparison to the greatness of the power of God, Exalted, and the vastness of His kingdom and His majesty, are as nothing. So it has become clear to you by this: that the entry of reason into seeking God's rulings about actions with the scale of assigning goodness and ugliness (tahsin wa taqbih) is an entry with a faulty scale, by which its possessor returns disgraced and regretful. So the truth is: that [judgment] is suspended on revelation, and the darkness in knowing it is [dispelled] by hearing. So the search for prophethood (nubuwwa) and the verification of the conditions of the message become obligatory, and that is the chapter we will now begin.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained argument refuting the rational obligation of gratitude (shukr) to God, thereby demonstrating the insufficiency of rational judgment (tahsin wa taqbih) and establishing the necessity of prophethood (nubuwwa). The author proceeds in a structured argumentative order. First, he states the invalidity of gratitude returning to the slave immediately (only fatigue), in the hereafter (reason has no jurisdiction before revelation), and to God (who cannot acquire new perfection and is self-sufficient). Thus, rational consideration opposes the obligation derived from perceiving God as a benefactor. Then he addresses an objection: the objector claims there is a benefit—security from possible punishment. The author answers by showing that punishment is equally possible on two grounds: first, one fatigues limbs that belong to God without permission, analogous to thanking a king by exhausting his slaves; second, praising God for a trivial favor given His infinite generosity is like praising a king for a barley morsel when he owns endless treasures—an insult deserving punishment. The conclusion is that rational judgment with the scale of goodness and ugliness is a faulty scale; the truth is to rely on revelation (shar'), which makes it necessary to investigate prophethood and its conditions—the subject of the next chapter.

The Parable of the King and the Necessity of Revelation
Sanusi uses a parable to prove reason alone cannot determine moral duty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi's parable demonstrates that reason alone is insufficient for determining moral obligation, and why revelation is necessary.

Core Idea
Reason without revelation is like praising a gift while ignoring the Giver's infinite majesty.

Sanusi presents a vivid parable to show the limitations of human intellect in judging divine commands. Before we can understand the role of reason in theology, we must see why it cannot serve as the ultimate judge of good and evil. This passage prepares the ground for the study of prophethood.

Guided Path
1
Read the parable carefully
Read the story of the king and the poor person. Notice the king's immense generosity and the smallness of the gift.
2
Identify the analogy
The king represents Allah, and the gifts represent all blessings. The poor person's praise for the small gift mirrors our tendency to rely on reason alone.
3
Recognize the moral
Praising the king for a trivial gift while ignoring his vast treasures is mockery. Analogously, using reason alone to judge divine commands is flawed.
This is the key turning point.
4
Apply the lesson to theology
Reason's entry into moral judgment via taḥsīn and taqbīḥ is a faulty scale. The correct scale is revelation (samʿ).
5
Understand the implication
Since moral obligation depends on revelation, we must investigate prophethood and the conditions of the messenger.
This leads to the next chapter.

Commentary
This passage is an argument by analogy (tamthīl) demonstrating the inadequacy of reason alone for determining moral obligation. Sanusi presents the example of a generous king who gives a poor person a tiny piece of barley bread, while possessing infinite treasures. If the poor man then publicly praises the king only for that small gift, he would be mocking the king by treating a trivial gift as if it were great. The moral is clear: all worldly and otherworldly blessings from Allah are as nothing compared to His majesty. Therefore, when a person tries to use his intellect to judge which actions are good or bad (taḥsīn and taqbīḥ) in order to derive divine commands, he is using a faulty scale. He cannot properly assess the weight of Allah's favors. The correct position is that knowledge of moral obligation depends exclusively on revelation (samʿ). Consequently, one must investigate prophethood and the conditions of the messenger, which is the topic of the next chapter.

Two Refutations on Delaying Miracles
Sanusi refutes a claim using two arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two arguments Sanusi uses to refute the claim that delaying miracles is impermissible, and why the second is a concessive argument that does not accept the opponent's principle.

Core Idea
Miracles may be delayed for wise reasons known to Allah.

Sanusi addresses a common objection: that a prophet must perform a miracle immediately upon claiming prophethood. He dismantles this objection using two distinct arguments, the first striking at its foundation, the second granting the premise but showing it fails. This passage is a model of theological refutation: one argument destroys the opponent's basis, the other defeats them on their own terms.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the two refutations
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that Sanusi presents two clear 'ways' (وجهين) of refuting the claim. Mark the first and second arguments.
The first argument is against the principle of husn-qubh; the second is a concessive argument.
2
Understand the first refutation
The first refutation invalidates the very foundation of the opponent's claim: the principle that actions are inherently good or bad (al-taḥsīn wa al-taqbīḥ) and that God must consider benefit. Sanusi says this has already been refuted earlier.
This is a direct attack on the Mu'tazilite framework.
3
Understand the second refutation
The second refutation assumes (for the sake of argument) that the principle is valid, but argues that delaying miracles may still be beneficial because of envy and rivalry among people. Allah knows that some people will only accept after the prophet's death.
This is a concessive argument: it does not accept the principle, but shows it does not lead to the opponent's conclusion.
4
Reflect on the concession
Notice that the second argument does not endorse the principle of husn-qubh. It simply shows that even if one accepts it, the opponent's claim that miracles must be immediate does not follow. This is a powerful rhetorical strategy.
Many students mistakenly think Sanusi accepts the principle in the second argument.

Commentary
This passage presents two refutations of an opposing claim (likely that it is obligatory to perform a miracle immediately upon claiming prophethood, and delaying it is impossible). The author first refutes by invalidating the very foundation of the argument, namely the Mu'tazilite principle of objective good and evil (al-taḥsīn wa al-taqbīḥ) and the obligation to consider what is most beneficial (al-aṣlaḥ). He refers to a previous discussion where this was already refuted. Then, even if one concedes that principle (second way), the author argues that it is not necessary that benefit lies in immediate miracles; indeed, divine knowledge may deem delaying better because envy and rivalry would prevent acceptance during the prophet's life, but after his death those obstacles may vanish. Thus, postponement may be beneficial for some people, so the claim of impossibility collapses.

Three Views on the Indication of Miracles
Sanusi enumerates and explains three views on miracle indication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three scholarly views on how miracles indicate the truthfulness of a prophet, and note how two of them reduce to a rational basis.

Core Idea
Miracles indicate truth either by rational necessity or by custom.

How do we know that a miracle proves a prophet's truth? Sanusi presents three views from the scholars. He notes that two views reduce to a single rational foundation, while the third relies on customary indication. This lesson lays the groundwork for understanding the proof that miracles cannot come from liars.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and its translation to get the flow of the argument. Notice the classification of views.
The passage uses technical terms like 'عقلي' and 'عادي'.
2
Identify the three views
Locate the three views: first two (rational) and third (customary). Note how Sanusi says the first two reduce to one statement.
The first two views both hold that indication is rational, differing only in explanation.
3
Understand the third view
The third view analogizes miracle indication to circumstantial evidence of emotions. Also note its connection to muwaḍa‘a (conventional assignment) and divine speech.
The phrase 'كَدَلالة قرائنِ الأحوالِ' is key.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation supporting the possibility of the first meaning of i'ada, which is recreating substances from nothing after their annihilation. The argument is based on the nature of substances (jawhar) and accidents ('arad): they are inherently receptive to both existence and non-existence. This receptivity is essential, not accidental, otherwise it would lead to infinite regress. Since their essence does not change after non-existence, they retain this receptivity. The proof is that if they were not receptive to non-existence, they would be eternal and necessary, which contradicts their known temporality (huduth). Conversely, if they were not receptive to existence, they would be impossible, which contradicts observable reality.

The Rational Possibility of Grave Events and Accepting Scriptural Apparent Meanings
Sanusi clarifies the rational permissibility of reviving parts of the dead and the rule of accepting scriptural apparent meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the events of the grave are rationally possible and why we must accept the transmitted texts about them without forced interpretation.

Core Idea
Reason does not forbid what revelation affirms about the grave.

Sanusi addresses a key issue in the samiyyat: the events of punishment and questioning in the grave. Some might think these are rationally impossible, but the author explains that there is no rational impediment, and since revelation has reported them, we must accept them literally. Let's read his argument.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal clarification by the author (likely Sanusi or his commentator) regarding the mechanism of punishment in the grave. He begins by stating that there is no rational objection (لا مانع في العقل) to God reviving only some parts of the dead person (e.g., the part that hears or speaks) and giving him enough intellect and understanding to comprehend and answer the questions of the two angels. Although we humans do not hear the angels' speech, the dead can perceive them. Similarly, it is possible for the dead to hear the greeting of someone who passes by their grave. All these are rationally possible, and since they are attested in transmitted texts (السمع), we must believe in their literal meaning (ظاهره) without recourse to metaphorical interpretation (تأويل). The passage concludes by affirming God's absolute power over all things, which underlies the possibility of such events. The author is countering those who deny these phenomena on rational grounds.
