The Superiority of Knowledge Over Action
Sanusi argues that knowledge is superior to action using comparisons and examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sanusi considers a sinful scholar superior to an obedient ignorant person, and how knowledge is the foundation of valid action.

Core Idea
A little knowledge with action is better than much action without it.

Sanusi tackles a common question: is a pious but ignorant worshipper better than a scholar who sometimes sins? He argues strongly for the superiority of the scholar, because knowledge is the basis of valid actions. The passage uses examples and analogies to drive the point home, and also defends the honor of genuine scholars against critics.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Notice the heading 'فضل العلم على العمل' which sets the topic.
The passage is dense; read it slowly.
2
Identify the key comparisons
Sanusi compares the sinful scholar (العالم المخالف) with the conforming blind follower (المقلد الموافق). He also compares a little action with knowledge to much action without it, and gives the example of Christian monks.
Pay attention to the logical reasoning: because the majority say the muqallid's faith is invalid, his actions are worthless.
3
Reflect on the broader argument
Consider why Sanusi brings up the merits of true scholars and the problem of impostors. This reinforces that knowledge is the true criterion for evaluating deeds.
The passage warns against judging scholars by those who only imitate them.

Commentary
This passage begins with a heading 'The superiority of knowledge over action'. It then argues that a scholar who sins with his limbs is better than a muqallid who outwardly obeys, because the majority of scholars deem the muqallid's faith invalid, so his actions are worthless. Even a little action with knowledge is better than much action without it; in fact, actions without knowledge have no effect. The author gives the example of Christian monks who practice extreme asceticism but gain no benefit in the Hereafter because they lack true knowledge. He then argues that if we counted the merits of true scholars—their teaching, authorship, and jihad against falsehood—the deeds of all common Muslims would be negligible in comparison. The reason some people criticize scholars is that they see pretenders and not the true scholars. The passage defends the honor of scholars and warns against judging them by the faults of impostors.

Commentary

If any knowledge remains, it is used for worldly gain and proximity to oppressors. Only a few are protected by God's grace. The author concludes that this is the time warned about in hadiths, feared by the righteous predecessors, and now we have reached it in a state of extreme weakness.

The author explains that Sufi ecstatic utterances are expressions of fana', not belief in union.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand how deliberate Sufi ecstatic utterances are understood as involuntary expressions during states of spiritual annihilation (fana'), not as doctrinal claims.

Core Idea
Sufi ecstatic sayings are involuntary outbursts during fana', not doctrinal beliefs.

This passage addresses a controversial topic: why some Sufis utter phrases that sound like claiming divinity (e.g., 'I am the Truth'). The author presents the explanation given by knowledgeable Sufi scholars, which centers on the concept of fana' and the distinction between states of spiritual intoxication and sobriety.

Commentary

However, when the Sufi returns to normal consciousness (sahw), he does not repeat such statements. Thus, these utterances are excused as products of the state, not as doctrinal claims.

Sanusi critiques inductive reasoning and introduces the Sufi path to knowledge.

Learning Objective

Induction cannot prove a universal, but spiritual discipline can unlock direct knowledge.

Sanusi presents an objection to a proof that relied on induction to claim that all human knowledge is limited. He shows that induction can never cover all cases, so the claim fails. Then he introduces the Sufi perspective, which asserts that through spiritual discipline (riyada) after correct belief and practice, one can attain knowledge beyond ordinary means. This passage illustrates the limits of empirical reasoning and the role of spiritual experience in Islamic epistemology.

Commentary
This passage presents a criticism of a proof that relied on induction. The author states that someone (probably the opponent) claimed, based on his survey, that no human from Adam to the last person has knowledge beyond what he listed. The author dismisses this claim as obviously false because inductive reasoning cannot cover all humans. Even if the opponent only reports what he found in his sample, it does not prove that all humans are limited to that. Furthermore, the Sufi claim contradicts this: they assert that through spiritual discipline (riyada), after correcting one's creed, fulfilling religious obligations, and eating lawful food, one can attain knowledge (presumably direct experiential knowledge of God) through seclusion and isolation. This alternative undermines the opponent's claim that human knowledge is confined to what he enumerated.

Refuting the Condition of a Specific Direction for Vision
Sanusi refutes the condition of a specific direction (jiha) in vision.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's logical refutation of the claim that vision requires a specific direction, and why this is a crucial step in arguments about seeing Allah.

Core Idea
Vision is not confined to one direction—we see all directions at once.

Sanusi now addresses a physical objection: some argue that vision requires a specific direction (jiha). He constructs a concise logical proof to show that this condition is false. By recognizing that we see in all six directions simultaneously, the condition collapses. This prepares the ground for further arguments about the possibility of seeing Allah without spatial constraints.
