Commentary
This passage is a combined introduction and enumeration. It first establishes the extraordinary nature of the Prophet being unlettered, then defines the key terms 'aya' (sign) and 'mu'jiza' (miracle), and finally lists the various scholarly opinions on the precise nature of the Quran's miraculousness (i'jaz).

The passage begins by describing the benefits of the Prophet's mission: establishing proofs, refuting opponents, and spiritual purification. It emphasizes that all this was accomplished through an unlettered prophet who never studied a book nor associated with scholars, a fact known necessarily and confirmed by Quran 29:48.

Next, it states that the Prophet had many signs and miracles, but the greatest is the Qur'an, on which Muslims unanimously agree. However, they differed on the specific aspect that makes it miraculous. The author then enumerates six opinions:

1. Some Mu'tazila: The miraculousness is in its unique style (uslub) and composition (nazm).
2. Some others: Only in its eloquence (fasaha) and fluency (jazala).
3. Imam al-Haramayn and al-Qadi: In the combination of these qualities.
4. Some, including Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari and al-Nazzam from the Mu'tazila: In the 'sarf' or turning away—the Arabs were capable of producing similar speech but were prevented from doing so after the Prophet's mission.
5. Some: In its lack of contradiction and internal consistency.
6. Some: In its reports of the unseen.
7. Some: In its agreement with rational principles.

The passage thus presents a range of views without deciding in favor of one, leaving the matter open.

Commentary
This passage classifies the various opinions (aqwal) on the nature of the Quran's inimitability (i'jaz), then selects the best opinion, and finally refutes the theory of sarfa (prevention). The author (Sanusi) presents eight opinions in sequence. First: the view of the Qadi 'Abd al-Jabbar and Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni that i'jaz lies in the 'totality' (bi al-majmu'), meaning the combination of eloquence and unique style. Second: the opinion of some (including a position attributed to al-Ash'ari and al-Nazzam) that i'jaz is by 'turning away' (sarfa)—i.e., Allah prevented the Arabs from producing a similar text even though they were capable. The author notes that al-Nazzam claimed the Arabs could do so before the Prophet's mission but were then prevented. Third: i'jaz lies in the entire Quran's coherence—absence of contradiction and mutual confirmation of verses. Fourth: i'jaz in predicting the unseen. Fifth: i'jaz in agreement with reason. Sixth: the opinion of some later scholars that i'jaz is the Quran's pre-eternality. Seventh: that i'jaz is its being an expression of the eternal divine speech. After listing, the author declares the best opinion to be that of the Qadi and Imam al-Haramayn: i'jaz is the combination of eloquence (jasala/fasaha) and a unique style (uslub khas) that is beyond human capacity. He argues that the Prophet challenged with a single surah, which contains both aspects. To produce a true imitation, one must match both qualities. He gives examples: a poet who produces a eloquent poem cannot be opposed by a prose work even if eloquent, nor by a poem that matches meter but lacks eloquence. True opposition requires both. He cites Musaylima's laughable attempts as proof. Finally, he refutes the sarfa theory with two arguments: (1) if Allah had prevented the Arabs, this prevention would have been widely reported, but no such reports exist; (2) if i'jaz were merely prevention, lower eloquence would be more appropriate for showing the miracle, but the Quran is unanimously acknowledged to be at the highest level of eloquence, which contradicts the sarfa theory.

The author agrees this is a great proof that it is from God, but notes the challenge was not made on that basis. The second opinion says the miracle is the Quran's prophecies about the unseen. Again, this is true but not the basis of the challenge, since not every surah contains such prophecies, yet the challenge applies to any surah. The third opinion says the miracle is its harmony with rational truth. The author accepts this as a description but insists the challenge did not occur on that basis. The fourth opinion says the Quran is miraculous because it is eternal (qadim). The author refutes this: if by 'eternal' one means the meaning (i.e., the eternal speech), then a miracle must be an act of Allah, and the eternal speech is not an act; if one means the physical words and letters, they are clearly contingent (haditha). The same refutation applies to the claim that the miracle is its being an expression of the eternal speech: the expression itself is not miraculous. Thus, all four views are either insufficient or invalid as the basis of the challenge.
