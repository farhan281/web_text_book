Two Subgroups and Their Belief on Speech
Sanusi presents the two subgroups of the Hashwiyya and their common belief in eternal letters and sounds.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two subgroups within the Hashwiyya regarding God's form and their shared belief that God's speech consists of eternal letters and sounds.

Core Idea
The Hashwiyya split on whether God has human form, but agree that His speech is eternal letters spoken in various languages.

After describing the Hashwiyya's general anthropomorphism, Sanusi divides them into two groups: one that asserts God has a human shape, and another that says He is located in a direction without form. Despite this difference, they both agree that God's speech is eternal and composed of letters and sounds, spoken in different languages.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the two subgroups
Find the description of the first group (human form) and the second group (direction without form).
2
Note the comparison to Jews
Sanusi says the first group equals the Jews in belief. Reflect on why.
3
Observe the common ground on speech
Both groups agree that God's speech is eternal letters and sounds, spoken in various languages.
4
Consider the difference in articulation
The first group says He speaks with articulation points; the second says without.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Hashwiyya sect's beliefs about God's attributes, especially His speech. The author begins by stating the Hashwiyya's claim that God's speech, which subsists in His essence, is composed of letters and sounds, yet it is eternal and beginningless. He immediately denounces this as extreme misguidance and deep ignorance. He then contrasts them with other heretical sects who might have deceptive doubts but at least do not immediately contradict necessary rational principles, whereas the Hashwiyya disregard the necessities of the intellect entirely. He then describes their specific beliefs: they believe God is a body, sitting on the Throne in contact and stability, moving and descending every Friday. He divides them into two groups: one group holds that God has a human form, likening them to the Jews; the other group asserts that God is located in a direction but without shape or limbs. Both groups agree that God's speech is eternal and consists of letters and sounds which God speaks in various languages. The first group says He speaks without using the points of articulation, while the second group (who believe He has a human form) says He speaks through those points. The author then points out the absurdity of attributing volition to an eternal being, since volition implies temporal events, and questions how God could will to speak or be silent if His speech is eternal. The passage ends with a rhetorical question exposing the contradiction in their belief.

Absurdity of Volition in Eternal Speech
Sanusi critiques the Hashwiyya's attribution of volition and silence to eternal speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi exposes the logical contradiction in the Hashwiyya's claim that God's eternal speech can be subject to His will, silence, and arrangement.

Core Idea
Attributing volition and silence to an eternal being's speech is rationally impossible.

Sanusi continues his refutation by pointing out the incoherence of the Hashwiyya's position. If God's speech is eternal, how can He will to speak or be silent? How can He arrange eternal letters in different languages? These questions reveal the absurdity of their claims.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note the two claims
First, the letters are eternal. Second, God arranges them by His will and speaks in various languages.
2
Reflect on Sanusi's rhetorical question
He asks how volition can enter upon the eternal. Volition implies change, but eternal is changeless.
This is the core logical problem.
3
Consider the claim of silence
They say God speaks and is silent at will. But if speech is eternal, silence would mean cessation of the eternal.
4
Conclude the contradiction
The Hashwiyya's beliefs are internally contradictory, showing their lack of rationality.

The Anthropomorphists' Belief about God's Body
Sanusi describes the mujassimah's belief in a corporeal God and divides them into two groups.

Refuting the Tawkid Objection and the Role of Decisive Proof

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Tawkid alone doesn't block metaphor; the real defense is prior decisive proof about speech.

In this passage, Sanusi addresses a sharp objection: if tawkid can occur with metaphor in poetry, why should it prevent metaphor in the Qur'an? He distinguishes the type of tawkid and the presence of metaphorical indicators. Distinguish between literal and conceptual tawkid
Sanusi distinguishes between conceptual emphasis (al-tawkid al-ma'nawi) and verbal emphasis. He argues that only conceptual emphasis prevents metaphor in the attribution (nisba), but the dispute is about the source of speech, not the predicate.

Sanusi explains that the poetic line is a derivative metaphor (isti'ara tabi'iyya) and has an explicit indicator (qarina) of metaphor, unlike the Qur'anic verse. Therefore, the analogy fails.

A further objection: Sanusi's answer seems circular because the opponent denies the premise. Sanusi counters that the Sunni proof is based on a prior decisive proof (burhan qat'i) that speech is not confined to letters and sounds, making the verse literal evidence.
This shows that the dispute ultimately rests on foundational epistemological commitments.

Defending the Unity of Divine Knowledge
Sanusi answers an objection about unity of divine knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi resolves the apparent contradiction between the unity of God's knowledge and His knowledge of past, present, and future.

Core Idea
God's knowledge is timeless; time describes the object, not the knowledge.

A powerful objection threatens the Islamic doctrine of divine knowledge: if God's knowledge is one, how can it encompass both future and present states, which seem logically different? In this passage, Sanusi answers by clarifying that time is not a container for God's knowledge but a relation to the known object. This distinction preserves both the unity and the comprehensiveness of divine knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the objection (beginning with 'فإن قيل') and the answer. Notice the dilemma it poses: knowledge of the future entails non-existence, knowledge of the present entails existence.
The objection assumes that if knowledge is one, it cannot attach to contradictory states.
2
Identify Sanusi's key move
Sanusi answers that God knows each thing in relation to its specified time and place, and that the relation to time is an attribute of the knowledge, not a temporal container.
The phrase 'فالإضافة إلى الزمان صفة للعلم، لا ظرف للعلم' is the crux.

Understand the refutation of the error
The passage ends by noting that the source of the error is in how we speak about the known object. Realise that human language describes knowledge in temporal terms, but this is a limitation of expression, not of God's attribute.
4
Reflect on the common misunderstanding
The common misunderstanding is that unity of knowledge cannot accommodate different temporal states. Sanusi shows that the relation to time is in the object, not the knower.

Then the answer: The Creator the Exalted, in His eternity, knows the existence of a thing relative to its specified time just as He knows it relative to its specified place, and He knows that it is non-existent before its existence, and if it is something that does not persist, He knows its non-existence after its existence. Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer (fa-in qīla... fa-l-jawāb) defending the unity of God's knowledge. The objection raises a dilemma: If God's knowledge is one, how can it simultaneously know future events (what will be) and present events (what is)? These seem to be different because future knowledge implies non-existence of the object, while present knowledge implies its existence. If the knowledge is the same, one of the two attachments would contradict the actual state of the object. Sanusi's answer (from Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi or similar source) is that God in His eternity (azal) knows the existence of a thing relative to its specified time, just as He knows it relative to its specified place. He knows that it is non-existent before its time, and if it perishes, He knows its non-existence after. So God's knowledge is not 'contained' (mazrūf) in time; time is not a container for knowledge. Instead, the relation to time is a description (ṣifa) of the knowledge—that is, the knowledge itself is timeless, but it includes the aspect of temporal relation as part of its content. Thus God's knowledge does not change with time; it is eternal, and the temporal qualification belongs to the known object (al-maʿlūm), not the knowledge itself. The confusion arises from mistaking the way we describe God's knowledge (in terms of past/present/future) for a feature of the knowledge itself.

Learning Objective

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory and argumentative passage about the objects of the divine attributes of hearing (sam'), sight (basar), and perception (idrak). Scholarly Disagreement on Universality of Perception
Commentator details the Sunni disagreement on the universality of perceptions other than sight.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the disagreement between early Sunni scholars (Ibn Kullab and al-Qalanisi) and Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari over whether divine perceptions other than sight are universal, including the specific issue of hearing Allah's speech.

Core Idea
Al-Ash'ari held all perceptions are universal; Ibn Kullab restricted universality to sight only.

After establishing the general principle, the commentator presents a key historical debate. The early scholars of the Sunnah agreed that sight relates to everything, but they differed on whether other perceptions (like hearing and touch) also do. The commentator sides with Imam al-Ash'ari, who argued for universal perception, and contrasts this with the view of Ibn Kullab that hearing cannot perceive Allah's eternal speech.
