Why Opposition Must Be Impossible for a Miracle to Be Specific
Sanusi explains why the miracle must be impossible to oppose to establish its specificity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the impossibility of opposition is a necessary condition for a miracle to be specific to the prophet, and why supernatural events before or without a challenge do not prove prophethood.

Core Idea
A miracle's uniqueness proves the prophet's truthfulness, not the challenge itself.

Sanusi explains a subtle but crucial point: the impossibility of opposing a miracle is not primarily for the sake of the challenge (tahaddi). Instead, it is to establish that the miracle is uniquely from the prophet (ikhtisas). Without this specificity, the miracle could be attributed to anyone, and it would not prove prophethood. This passage clarifies why certain supernatural events—like those before the claim or unchallenged ones—do not qualify as miracles.

Similarly, if a supernatural event occurs before the claim of prophethood (like irhas), it does not prove specificity because all claimants are equal in that case. The same applies to a supernatural event that occurs after the claim but was never challenged with. Without the impossibility of opposition, the event cannot be established as the prophet's exclusive miracle.

The Condition of Correspondence in Opposing Miracles
Sanusi distinguishes the condition for opposing specified vs. unspecified miracles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between specified and unspecified miracles and whether correspondence is required when opposing each.

Core Idea
For specified miracles, correspondence is required; for unspecified, opinions differ.

Sanusi clarifies a nuanced point about miracles: if someone claims a specific miracle, an opponent must match it exactly. But if the miracle is not specified, the requirement of correspondence is disputed among scholars, with the stronger view being that it is not required. This lesson unpacks that distinction.

Commentary
This passage addresses the condition of correspondence (mumathala) when someone tries to oppose a miracle. The author distinguishes two cases. Core Idea
Miracles after death confirm past prophethood, not new address.

Al-Qadi raises a logical puzzle: if prophethood means receiving divine address, and that ends at death, how can a posthumous miracle prove prophethood? Sanusi's answer shows that the miracle serves as evidence for the prophet's past claim, not for a present address. This lesson unpacks the objection and its elegant resolution.

Sanusi rebuts al-Qadi's objection that delayed miracles waste the mission's benefit.

Learning Objective

Commentary
This passage is a response to a Mu'tazila objection against the Ash'ari doctrine of miracles. The Mu'tazila argue that since Ash'aris hold that Allah can lead people astray without regard to benefit, He could create miracles (khawariq al-'adat) at the hands of false prophets to mislead people. The author presents three answers based on different views of how miracles prove a prophet's truthfulness.

First, the passage begins by affirming that for the past, we know by induction that Allah's custom is to expose false claimants. For the future, we have certain knowledge that Muhammad (saw) is the final prophet, so any later claim is invalid, and any miracle performed by a false claimant is irrelevant.

Then the Mu'tazila objection is stated: according to Ash'ari doctrine, Allah can lead astray whom He wills, so nothing prevents Him from creating miracles to support false prophets.

Answer one (rational proof view): It is permissible for Allah to lead astray, but not through a miracle, because a miracle is intrinsically linked to truth. Just as blackness and whiteness cannot coexist, leading astray and a miracle cannot coexist. Moreover, leading astray through evidence would require turning the evidence into doubt—which is impossible.

Answer two (conventional proof view): Allah can lead astray, but not by creating a contradictory statement. A miracle functions like a verbal affirmation from Allah. Since Allah cannot break His promise, He cannot create a miracle to support a lie.

Answer three (customary proof view): The miracle itself produces certain knowledge of the prophet's truth. Once that certain knowledge is attained, the possibility of falsehood is excluded. The mere logical possibility that the miracle could have been used to mislead does not undermine actual certain knowledge. This is like our knowledge of our own existence: we are certain of it while acknowledging that non-existence is logically possible. Logical possibility does not imply actual doubt.

The final paragraph appears to be an editorial gloss clarifying the phrase 'in the case of the truthful one' from earlier, but the text is cut off.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

In this passage, Sanusi addresses Jewish interlocutors who accept Moses' miracles but reject Muhammad and Jesus. He presents a logical dilemma: either miracles indicate truthfulness or they do not. If they do, then the same standard applies to all prophets with miracles. This argument showcases the universal rational principle underlying prophetic proof.

Proofs for prophethood are either rational or transmitted; the Qur'an's eloquence is a rational proof.
