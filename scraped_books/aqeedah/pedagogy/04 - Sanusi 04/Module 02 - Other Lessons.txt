Warning against Arrogance and Call to Sincerity of Heart
Sanusi's preface warns against arrogance and emphasizes sincerity of the heart.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the spiritual dangers of arrogance and the importance of sincerity of the heart as the only cure.

Core Idea
Arrogance is the root of disbelief; sincerity of heart is its only cure.

In his preface, Sanusi delivers a passionate warning against the destructive power of arrogance, which blinds the heart to truth. He identifies three deadly diseases—arrogance, stubbornness, and envy—and prescribes the antidote of heartfelt sincerity. This passage sets the spiritual tone for the entire work, calling readers to inner purification.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the translation to grasp the full message.
Notice the author's emotional tone—this is a warning, not a calm lecture.
2
Identify the three diseases
Find where the author lists the malignant diseases that overcome the 'people of stubbornness'. List them in your mind.
These diseases are arrogance, blind stubbornness, and envy/hatred of scholars.
3
Understand the antidote of sincerity
The author declares sincerity (sidq) as the only cure. Note that he distinguishes between verbal sincerity and sincerity of the heart.
4
Examine the evidence
Read the Quranic verses and the hadith about pride. Reflect on how they reinforce the message that arrogance is fatal to faith.
The hadith: 'No one enters Paradise with an atom's weight of pride in his heart.'
5
Contrast the two hearts
Compare the arrogant heart (hard, refusing to listen) with the believer's heart (gentle, soft, seeking). Ask yourself which description fits you.
This is the ultimate lesson: humility and openness to truth are essential.

Commentary
This passage is the author's introduction or preface to his work. He begins with the traditional praise of God and blessings on the Prophet, establishing a reverent tone. Then he directly addresses 'people of stubbornness' (أهل العناد), warning them that they are destroying themselves by rushing into ruin and blinding themselves to obvious truths. He identifies three spiritual diseases that have overtaken them: arrogance (الكبر), blind stubbornness (عناد أعمى), and envy and hatred of scholars. These are described as malignant diseases with no cure except the antidote of sincerity (الصدق). He supports this with the well-known hadith of the three men trapped in a cave who were saved by their sincerity. The author then warns the reader not to misunderstand sincerity as mere verbal honesty; the sincerity meant here is sincerity of the heart (صدق الجنان). Verbal sincerity can be mere effort, but the heart's sincerity is beyond falsehood. He asks: what is sincerity of the hearts? It is that the heart does not become arrogant and hardened, like stone. An arrogant heart refuses to listen to truth. He cites several Quranic verses describing the disbelievers' refusal to listen: they plug their ears, cover themselves, and persist in arrogance. Even if they hear outwardly, they do not understand inwardly. Their hearts are hollow, devoid of good. He then quotes the famous hadith that no one with an atom's weight of pride in his heart will enter Paradise. He concludes that the only thing that prevented the disbelievers from faith was the malignant tree of arrogance, which produced stubbornness, envy, and hatred of knowledge and scholars. He contrasts this with the heart of a believer, which is gentle and soft, always seeking. The passage is a powerful exhortation to humility and inner sincerity, emphasizing that the root of disbelief is arrogance of the heart.

Reward and Punishment Have No Rational Cause
Sanusi states that reward and punishment have no rational cause, only conventional signs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why reward and punishment are not rationally necessary but are divine conventions signalled by acts of obedience and disobedience.

Core Idea
Reward and punishment are not automatic; they are entirely Allah's choice.

In this passage, Imam Sanusi asserts a foundational Ash‘arī principle: that divine reward and punishment are not causally linked to human actions by rational necessity. Rather, acts of obedience and disobedience are merely signs that Allah, by His will, has chosen to associate with specific outcomes. This lesson unpacks that key tenet.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once and then the translation. Pay attention to the opening negation: 'no cause rationally'.
Notice that the passage is a concise doctrinal statement.
2
Understand the role of obedience and disobedience
The passage says obedience and disobedience are created signs (amāratān makhlūqatān) that indicate reward and punishment by law (shar‘an). They are not causes.
3
Consider the hypothetical reversal
Sanusi imagines Allah reversing the indication or granting reward/punishment without any sign. He says that would still be good from Him.
This emphasizes Allah's absolute sovereignty.
4
Connect to the Quranic verse
The passage ends with the verse 'He is not questioned about what He does' (Qur'an 21:23), reinforcing that Allah's actions are beyond human questioning.
This verse is a key proof text in Ash‘arī theology.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal statement about the nature of reward and punishment in Sunni theology. The author begins by asserting that reward and punishment have no rational cause (la sabab lahumā ‘aqlan). This means that Allah is not obligated by any rational necessity to reward the obedient or punish the disobedient; He does so by His will and wisdom. Instead, acts of obedience and disobedience are created signs (amāratān makhlūqatān) that Allah creates directly without any assisting intermediary from the servant. These signs indicate by divine law (shar‘an) what Allah has chosen of reward or punishment. The author then considers a hypothetical reversal: if Allah reversed the indications (e.g., rewarding disobedience) or bestowed reward or punishment without any preceding sign, it would still be good and fitting from Him because He is sovereign. He quotes the Quranic verse “He is not questioned about what He does” (lā yus’alu ‘ammā yaf‘al) to emphasize Allah’s absolute authority. This passage establishes that divine justice is not constrained by human conceptions of causality; rather, Allah’s actions are inherently just and good.

The State of the Times and the Obligation to Withdraw
The author quotes scholars lamenting the rarity of the righteous and advises withdrawal in tribulation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the moral decline of the times as described by medieval scholars and the recommended response of withdrawal and supplication.

Core Idea
In times of widespread tribulation, the only path to salvation is to withdraw from people and cling to Allah.

This passage presents a heartfelt lament about the rarity of righteous people and the abundance of fools. Drawing on the words of earlier scholars, the author paints a picture of escalating spiritual decay. It concludes with a clear directive: after acquiring necessary knowledge, one must withdraw from society, stay at home, and fervently supplicate Allah for preservation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to grasp the overall tone and content. Notice the progression from critique to prescription.
2
Identify the scholarly quotations
The passage quotes Ibn Rizq, Ibn al-Hajj, and Ibn Abi Jamra. Note how each successive quote amplifies the sense of decline and rarity of the righteous.
Ibn Abi Jamra's quote includes a hadith that provides a glimmer of hope.
3
Understand the author's conclusion
The author applies the earlier scholars' observations to his own time (end of the ninth century) and declares that the only way for salvation is to withdraw completely from people.
The prescription includes staying at home, weeping over oneself, and supplicating like a drowning person.

Commentary
This passage is an exhortation and warning about the moral decline of the times. Refuting the Claim of Lies in the Quran: Explaining Al-A'raf 7:11
Sanusi refutes the claim that a Quranic verse contains a lie.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi refutes the objection that a Quranic verse contains a lie by using metaphorical interpretation and an alternative literal reading.

Core Idea
A Qurʾānic verse that seems to contain a lie is actually a metaphor honoring humanity.

The critics of Islam sometimes claim that the Qurʾān contains contradictions or false statements. One such allegation targets verse 7:11, where Allah says He created 'you,' formed 'you,' then commanded the angels to prostrate to Adam. Critics argue that the command to prostrate came after creating Adam, not after creating all humans. In this passage, Sanusi provides two cogent refutations, showing that the verse is perfectly sound and that the objection stems from misunderstanding metaphor.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The critic claims the verse implies a lie because the sequence of events is wrong: 'We created you, then formed you, then said to the angels: prostrate to Adam.' The objection assumes 'you' refers to all humans, but the command to prostrate was given after Adam's creation, not after ours.
This doubt is based on a literal reading that ignores context and possible interpretations.
2
Examine the first refutation: metaphor
Sanusi explains that 'you' refers to Adam, our father. Since we are his descendants, God's creation of Adam is attributed to us metaphorically, as when one says 'I planted this crop' meaning the original seed.
This metaphorical usage (majaz) is common in Arabic and everyday speech.
3
Examine the second refutation: literal interpretation
An alternative reading is that the creation and formation of all humans occurred before the command to prostrate, when Adam's offspring were extracted from him as particles (dharr). Thus the verse is literally true without any metaphor.
This interpretation relies on the hadith about the extraction of the human seed from Adam.
4
Appreciate the response's strength
Both refutations show that the verse is sound. The objection arises from ignorance of metaphorical language and of the broader Islamic tradition.

Refuting the Claim that the Quran Contains Poetry
Sanusi cites Quranic examples that appear to match poetic meters, then refutes the claim that the Quran is poetry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's refutation of the claim that the Quran contains poetry, including the conditions for something to be considered poetry and the principle of taghlīb.

Core Idea
Matching a poetic meter does not make something poetry; intention and context are decisive.

People sometimes point to Quranic verses that appear to fit poetic meters to argue that the Quran is poetry. Sanusi addresses this objection by first citing such examples, then systematically refuting them. He explains that mere metrical agreement is insufficient—the speaker must intend poetry, and often rhyme is required. He also notes that many of the cited examples have alterations, and even if one concedes, the principle of taghlīb (domination) ensures the Quran is not classified as poetry.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the examples Sanusi gives and then his refutation. Pay attention to the key conditions he sets for something to be poetry.
The passage uses technical terms like وزْن and تقفية.
2
Identify the conditions for poetry
Extract from the refutation what Sanusi says is necessary for something to be considered poetry: intentional use of meter, and according to some, rhyme. Understand why mere metrical agreement is not enough.
3
Understand the taghlīb argument
Reflect on the principle of taghlīb (domination) and how it applies here: the overwhelming majority of the Quran is not poetry, so isolated instances do not change its nature.
This is a powerful argument that prevents exceptional cases from overturning the general rule.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. Sanusi first cites examples from the Quran that appear to match poetic meters (mudāriʿ, muqtaḍab, mujtathth, mutaqārib) to show the objector's claim. Then he gives the answer: the mere presence of such meters does not make the speech poetry. For it to be poetry, the meter must be intentionally intended by the speaker (the Prophet ﷺ) and, according to some, must include rhyme. Furthermore, many of the cited examples have some alteration (i.e., they do not perfectly match the meter), and even if one concedes that they are poetry, the principle of taghlīb (domination) applies—i.e., the overwhelming majority of the Quran is clear prose, so isolated instances do not change its classification. The argument shows that the Quran is not poetry and that the objection is weak.

Key Takeaway

The State of Corruption Before Islam
Sanusi enumerates the corruptions of various nations before Islam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the extent of moral and religious corruption among different nations prior to the prophethood of Muhammad (s).

Core Idea
Before Islam, the world was engulfed in diverse forms of misguidance and corruption.

Sanusi begins by painting a vivid picture of the spiritual and moral decay that had taken hold across the globe. From Arab idolatry to Jewish scriptural tampering and Christian trinitarianism, he shows that all nations had strayed far from the truth. This sets the stage for understanding why divine guidance was urgently needed.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage listing corrupt nations
Read the Arabic passage once to get an overview of the various nations and their corruptions described by Sanusi.
Notice the vivid language used to describe each group.
2
Identify specific corruptions
Go through each nation mentioned (Arabs, Persians, Turks, Indians, Jews, Christians) and note the specific corrupt practice described for each.
Make a mental or written list to keep track.
3
Reflect on the universality of corruption
Consider that Sanusi includes all major known nations and concludes that all sects were in misguidance.
This universality emphasizes the need for a universal divine message.

Commentary
This passage is an extended argument for the necessity of prophethood, focusing on the state of the world before the advent of Prophet Muhammad (sallallahu alayhi wasallam). The author, likely Sanusi, begins by describing the corruption and deviation that pervaded all nations: the Arabs were engaged in idolatry, female infanticide, and attributing daughters to Allah; the Persians practiced fire-worship and permitted incest; the Turks were destructive; the Indians worshipped cows and stones; the Jews altered scriptures and believed in divine corporeality; the Christians adopted trinitarianism and worshipped Jesus. The author then states that when corruption reached its peak, Allah, as His custom, sent a renewer of religion—and from historical necessity, it is known that this was none other than Muhammad (sallallahu alayhi wasallam). The passage concludes by listing the achievements of the Prophet: he corrected corruption, distinguished truth, established the religion firmly, illuminated hearts, and spread blessings. A footnote clarifies that renewal can occur through prophets or through later renewers (mujaddids), citing a hadith from Abu Dawud.

The Divine Custom of Sending Renewers and the Mission of Muhammad
Sanusi explains Allah's custom of sending a renewer at the peak of corruption, and identifies Muhammad as that renewer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Allah's custom of sending renewers when corruption reaches its peak, and how Muhammad (s) fulfilled that role.

Core Idea
When corruption becomes extreme, Allah sends a renewer, and Muhammad is that renewer.

Having described the depth of corruption, Sanusi now introduces the divine principle: when people reach the height of corruption, Allah out of His grace sends a renewer. He then argues that historically, only Muhammad (s) can claim to have been that renewer, based on the transformation he brought. This establishes the necessity of his prophethood and message.

A footnote clarifies that renewal can occur through prophets or through later renewers (mujaddids), citing a hadith from Abu Dawud.

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration and explanation of the four conditions required for a wali (friend of God) according to Ibn Dahhaq. The passage is structured as a list with four numbered conditions, each accompanied by detailed justification.

First condition: The wali must be knowledgeable in the principles of religion (usul al-din) so that he can distinguish between the Creator and creation, and between a true prophet and a false claimant. This knowledge is foundational for recognizing God and His messengers.

Second condition: The wali must be learned in the rulings of Sharia, both through transmission (naql) and understanding (fahm). This enables him to rely on his own reasoning (ijtihad) and not need to imitate others in legal matters, just as he is independent in matters of tawhid. Ibn Dahhaq emphasizes that if God were to take away all knowledge from other scholars, the wali would still possess it and could reestablish the entire religion. The term 'wali' implies one who supports the religion of God, which is impossible for someone who does not comprehensively know the religion, its principles, and its branches.

Third condition: The wali must exhibit praiseworthy character (khuluq mahmud) as indicated by both the Law and reason. The Law aspect is scrupulousness (wara') by avoiding prohibitions and performing all obligations. The reason aspect flows from knowledge of usul al-din: knowing that the world is temporal leads to detachment from worldly things; knowing God's oneness leads to sincerity in all actions; knowing divine decree produces contentment (rida)—accepting what God decrees without fear or hope for worldly outcomes. These three cognitive fruits result in kindness and forgiveness toward others, because the wali recognizes that humans cannot truly benefit or harm.

Fourth condition: The wali is perpetually in a state of fear (khawf), never feeling secure. This is because he does not know whether he is predestined for felicity or misery. He sees that misery comes from disobedience, so he fears sin and practices wara'. He also fears that his good deeds might be nullified by their opposites (e.g., doubt, ingratitude, hypocrisy, or rights owed to others). Thus, his fear is constant and multifaceted, covering both commission of sins and loss of virtues.

First condition: The wali must be knowledgeable in the principles of religion (usul al-din) so that he can distinguish between the Creator and creation, and between a true prophet and a false claimant. This knowledge is foundational for recognizing God and His messengers.

Second condition: The wali must be learned in the rulings of Sharia, both through transmission (naql) and understanding (fahm). This enables him to rely on his own reasoning (ijtihad) and not need to imitate others in legal matters, just as he is independent in matters of tawhid. Ibn Dahhaq emphasizes that if God were to take away all knowledge from other scholars, the wali would still possess it and could reestablish the entire religion. The term 'wali' implies one who supports the religion of God, which is impossible for someone who does not comprehensively know the religion, its principles, and its branches.

Third condition: The wali must exhibit praiseworthy character (khuluq mahmud) as indicated by both the Law and reason. The Law aspect is scrupulousness (wara') by avoiding prohibitions and performing all obligations. The reason aspect flows from knowledge of usul al-din: knowing that the world is temporal leads to detachment from worldly things; knowing God's oneness leads to sincerity in all actions; knowing divine decree produces contentment (rida)—accepting what God decrees without fear or hope for worldly outcomes. These three cognitive fruits result in kindness and forgiveness toward others, because the wali recognizes that humans cannot truly benefit or harm.

Fourth condition: The wali is perpetually in a state of fear (khawf), never feeling secure. This is because he does not know whether he is predestined for felicity or misery. He sees that misery comes from disobedience, so he fears sin and practices wara'. He also fears that his good deeds might be nullified by their opposites (e.g., doubt, ingratitude, hypocrisy, or rights owed to others). Thus, his fear is constant and multifaceted, covering both commission of sins and loss of virtues.

ولا خفاءَ أنَّ التحصُّنَ مِنَ المفاسد في هذا الزمانِ الذي فاضَ فيهِ على البسيطة كلّها عُباب الفتنِ ، وعمَّتْها سحائب المخالفاتِ.. إنَّما يكون للمرء بعد تحصيلِهِ ما يحتاجُ إليهِ في أصل الدين وفرعه ؛ بملازمة الخلوة ، والاعتزال عن الناس جملةً ، وإخمالِ الذَّكرِ جداً ، ومجانبة أنواع الرئاسة وأصحابها وأسبابها جملةً وتفصيلاً ، فمَنْ صبرَ على ذلك يسير هذه اللحظةِ مِنَ العمرِ .. أفضى بفضلِ اللهِ تعالى بنفْسِ الموتِ إلى منتهى الراحة ، وغاية السرور الكثيرِ الدائم أبد الآبادِ والعاقل الموفَّقُ في هذا الزمانِ مَنْ جعلَ أنيسَهُ في خلوتِهِ ذكرَ مولاه جل وعزَّ ، وتلاوة كتابه العزيز ، والنظر في جوامع كَلِم نبيِّهِ عليهِ الصلاة والسلام ، ونزَّهَ عقلَهُ وطَرْفَهُ في رياض تلك المعاني وعجائب تلكَ الأزهارِ ؛ فإِنَّ في ذلكَ مِنَ اللَّذَّاتِ مَعَ السلامةِ مِنْ كُلٌّ شرِّ .. ما لا يعلمُ قدرَهُ إلا الله تعالى ، الذي يمنحُهُ لَمَنْ شاءَ بمحضِ فضله .
اللهم ؛ امنحْ لنا ذلكَ وأكثرَ منهُ بمخضِ فضلك في عافية بلا محنة ، واصرف عنا كل شاغل يصرفنا عنه يا ذا الجلال والإكرام ، يا أرحم الراحمين .
لأن الشهرة سبب كل بلية ، ولذا ورد : خُص بالبلاء من عرفته الناس يعني .وقال بعض العارفين : الظهور يقصم الظهور ، وقد قال عليه الصلاة والسلام : كفى بالمرءِ مِنَ الشَّرِّ أن يشار إليهِ بالأصابع ؛ إنْ خيراً فهو مزلةٌ إلا مَنْ رحم الله ، وإنْ شرّاً فهو شرٌّ .
Seeking protection from the trials of the end times. And it is no secret that seeking protection from evils in this time, in which the flood of tribulations has overflowed the whole earth, and the clouds of violations have covered it... is only possible for a person after he has acquired what he needs in the foundations of religion and its branches, by adhering to seclusion, withdrawing from people entirely, and greatly effacing his mention, and avoiding all types of leadership, its people, and its causes, completely and in detail. Whoever is patient with that, a little of this moment of life, will reach, by the grace of Allah Almighty, with the pangs of death, the ultimate comfort and the utmost everlasting great joy. The successful intelligent person in this time is the one who makes his companion in his seclusion the remembrance of his Lord, Mighty and Majestic, and the recitation of His Noble Book, and contemplating the comprehensive sayings of His Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, and purifies his intellect and his gaze in the meadows of those meanings and the wonders of those flowers. For in that are pleasures, along with safety from every evil... the measure of which none knows except Allah Almighty, Who grants it to whomever He wills by His pure grace. O Allah, grant us that and more of it by Your pure grace in wellbeing without trial, and divert from us every distraction that diverts us from You, O Possessor of Majesty and Honor, O Most Merciful of the merciful. For fame is the cause of every calamity, and thus it is reported: 'The one whom people know is singled out for tribulation, meaning...' And some gnostics said: 'Prominence breaks the back.' And indeed the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, said: 'Sufficient as evil for a person is that he is pointed at with fingers; if he is good, it is a trial, except for whom Allah has mercy, and if he is evil, it is evil.'

Commentary
This passage is a spiritual exhortation on seeking protection from the widespread trials of the end times. The author first states that protection can only be achieved after one has obtained necessary knowledge of religion, by means of: 1) adhering to seclusion, 2) withdrawing from people entirely, 3) greatly effacing one's mention (avoiding fame), and 4) avoiding all types of leadership, its people, and its causes. Whoever endures this temporary difficulty will, by God's grace, attain ultimate comfort and eternal joy at death. The wise person is one who in his seclusion companions remembrance of God, Quran recitation, and contemplation of the Prophet's sayings, and who purifies his intellect and gaze upon spiritual meanings. The pleasure and safety in this are incalculable. The passage then includes a prayer for God to grant this and to remove distractions. Finally, a proof for avoiding fame is given: fame is the cause of every calamity, as supported by a hadith that being pointed at is sufficient evil—if good, it is a trial; if evil, it is evil. A gnostic saying is also quoted: prominence breaks the back.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some may think that being famous for goodness is always praiseworthy and free from harm.
Correction: The hadith clarifies that even if one is good, being pointed at is a trial (mazilla) unless Allah has mercy. Fame brings risks of pride and distraction.

Key Takeaway
The wise person fills his seclusion with divine remembrance and scripture, and recognizes that fame is a dangerous trial that corrupts spiritual safety.
