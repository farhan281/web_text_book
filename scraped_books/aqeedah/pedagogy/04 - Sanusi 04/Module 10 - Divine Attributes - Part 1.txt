Divine Mercy and the Sincere Seeker
Exposition on divine mercy and the seeker's disposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Imam's exposition on how Allah's mercy guides the sincere seeker and deceives the obstinate.

Core Idea
Sincere intention attracts divine guidance; obstinacy invites deprivation.

The Imam begins with a profound insight into divine mercy and justice. He relates from Ibn Dahaq that the seeker of truth with sincere intention, free from hatred of scholars, pride, and envy, will be granted the jewel of faith. Conversely, the obstinate one is denied guidance. This principle is captured in a concise statement that forms the basis for understanding how spiritual success depends on one's inner disposition.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on divine mercy
Read the Arabic passage about the mercy to the truthful and deception to the deceivers. Notice the contrast between the two groups.
The passage is attributed to Imam Ibn Dahaq.
2
Identify the key condition
Find the conditions that lead to guidance: sincere intention, not hating scholars, no pride or envy.
3
Understand the principle
The Imam summarizes with a sentence: 'No one listened with the intention of seeking guidance except that he was guided...' Reflect on how this applies to learning.
This is a universal spiritual law.

The Danger of the Mutasannina (False Sunnis)
Warning against false claimants to Sunni identity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Imam's warning about those who falsely claim to be Sunni and attribute corrupt doctrines to the Salaf.

Core Idea
False affiliation to Ahl al-Sunna is a dangerous deception.

The Imam turns to a pressing issue of his time: people who outwardly claim to follow Ahl al-Sunna but actually propagate corrupt beliefs, staining the reputation of true scholars. He specifically warns about the 'mutasannina' – those who pretend to be Sunnis. They have no real share of the sunna except 'chatter of the tongue' and falsely attribute anthropomorphic views to Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal, misleading others into thinking they follow him in creed as they do in legal rulings.

Read the passage on mutasannina

2
Identify the corrupt practice
The Imam says the Hashwiyya stained some Sunni imams with their corrupt doctrine. Find how they misrepresented Ahmad ibn Hanbal.
They attributed anthropomorphism to him because they followed him in fiqh.
3
Reflect on the harm
Consider why this is dangerous: it misleads people about true Sunni creed and slanders a respected imam.
The Imam emphasizes that they have no real connection to the Salaf.

Unifying Sharia and Haqiqa Against Mu'tazila
Emphasis on integration of law and spiritual reality in Sunni theology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Imam's assertion that knowing the madhhab of Ahl al-Haqq integrates sharia and haqiqa, as exemplified in refuting Mu'tazila on human actions and rational ethics.

Core Idea
True knowledge of Sunni creed unifies law and spiritual reality.

The Imam concludes with a crucial methodological point: the madhhab of Ahl al-Haqq (the people of truth) is not merely legal or spiritual—it synthesizes both. He demonstrates this through his refutation of Mu'tazilite doctrines on the creation of human actions and rational good and evil. This shows that authentic Sunni theology does not separate external observance (sharia) from inner reality (haqiqa) but integrates them seamlessly.

Read the passage on sharia and haqiqa

2
Understand the unity
The Imam says that the true madhhab unifies sharia and haqiqa. Think about how theological reasoning and legal compliance come together.
3
Examine the example
The refutation of the Mu'tazila on khalq af'al al-'ibad and tahsin wa taqbih 'aqli is the case study. See how the Imam uses rational arguments rooted in revelation.
This shows that haqiqa is not esoteric but the inner truth of sharia.

Sanusi's Defense of Karamat and Critique of Deniers
Editorial summary of Sanusi's stance on karamat and a takfir ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's argument that denying the possibility of karamat (saintly miracles) contradicts Allah's absolute power, and his criticism of those who label belief in specific karamat as kufr.

Core Idea
Denying karamat limits Allah's power; overpolicing them risks takfir.

Sanusi tackles a sensitive issue: the miracles of saints (karamat). Some deny them entirely, while others go so far as to declare belief in a particular karamat as disbelief. In this passage, Sanusi censures both extremes, affirming Allah's unlimited power and defending the integrity of the awliya.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First read the Arabic passage and its literal English translation. Notice how Sanusi frames his criticism: he first dismisses innovators who deny karamat, then expresses greater surprise at some Sunni jurists.
The passage includes a direct quote from Sanusi.
2
Identify Sanusi's two critiques
Sanusi criticizes two groups: (1) deniers of karamat from the people of desires and innovations, and (2) some fuqaha of Ahl al-Sunnah who declared belief in a specific karamat as kufr. Understand why he calls the second group more surprising.
3
Analyze the underlying principle
The core principle is that Allah's power is limitless. Denying karamat implicitly restricts what Allah can do through His saints. The takfir of those who believe in a karamat is even worse—it wrongly excludes believers from the fold of Islam.
This connects to the lesson's objective.
4
Reflect on the story of Ibrahim ibn Adham
The specific incident involves Ibrahim ibn Adham being seen simultaneously in Basra and Mecca on the same day. Consider why some jurists found this impossible and why Sanusi defends its permissibility.
Sanusi argues that such a miracle is possible by Allah's power.

Commentary
This passage is an editorial summary of Sanusi's positions. It first notes that Sanusi criticizes those who transmit unauthenticated statements from Ahl al-Sunnah scholars. Then it mentions that Sanusi expanded on the matter of karamat (miracles of saints), emphasizing that Allah's power encompasses allowing such miracles at the hands of saints (awliya). Sanusi then criticizes those who find such miracles too astonishing for saints. He quotes Sanusi as saying that while he is not surprised at the deniers of karamat (who are people of desires and innovations), he is surprised at some fuqaha of Ahl al-Sunnah. These fuqaha, based on a story about Ibrahim ibn Adham appearing simultaneously in Basra and Mecca on the same day, declared that whoever believes such a miracle is possible is a disbeliever. The editor thus highlights Sanusi's defense of the permissibility of karamat and his rejection of those who label belief in them as kufr.

Thus it is proven by this that the Maker of the world is willing and choosing, not a cause nor a nature.

Commentary
This passage is a proof and refutation. Sanusi begins by stating that the Maker must be willing (murid), otherwise the world would remain non-existent forever. Then he considers two alternatives: if God were a cause ('illa) or a nature (tabi'a), the world would be eternal, which is false. Then he presents and refutes two objections: (1) that the Maker is a nature but a pre-eternal obstacle (mani') prevented creation; (2) that the Maker is a nature but a condition (shart) was missing. Both are refuted because the obstacle or condition would also be pre-eternal, leading to an infinite regress or impossibility. Therefore, the Maker is willing, not a cause or nature.

God's Sole Creation of All Blessings
Sanusi emphasizes God's sole origination of all blessings as a favor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that God alone creates all blessings as a favor from His generosity, not because of any merit in creation.

Core Idea
God's blessings are pure gifts, not earned.

This opening passage of Sanusi's text sets the stage by acknowledging the infinite nature of God's blessings and His exclusive role as their Creator. Before discussing the Prophet's intercession, Sanusi establishes the foundational principle that all good comes from God alone, as a pure favor.

Guided Path

Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the translation. Notice the rhetorical flow from the inability to count blessings to the affirmation that all blessings come from God as a favor.
2
Identify three key points
The passage makes three points: (1) All blessings cannot be enumerated by mind, tongue, or fingers. (2) All blessings (worldly and otherworldly) are created by God alone. (3) These blessings are a favor (فضلاً) not deserved by anything except His generosity.
The phrase 'فضلاً منه' is crucial – it means 'as a favor from Him,' emphasizing that blessings are not owed.
3
Reflect on the rhetorical question
The passage ends with a rhetorical question: 'Is there a second with Him in delivering them?' The implied answer is no – God has no partner in bestowing blessings.
This is a strong affirmation of tawheed in the context of divine provision.

Commentary
This is an explanatory and refutational passage. Sanusi addresses the question of whether the name 'Allah' is derived (مشتق) or a proper name (علم). He states the truth (الحق) that it is a proper name with no derivation, and all claims of derivation are rejected. He then mentions the closest derivation view: from 'alaha' meaning to settle, citing a poetic example, but this interpretation only yields a meaning of transcendence. He then connects this to the kalima: because 'Allah' is a proper name for the essence, 'la ilaha illa Allah' becomes a word of tawhid, affirming no deity but that One. Next, he presents an opposing view that 'Allah' is an attribute (صفة) and not a name, with arguments: names indicate the named, but Allah is not perceived; proper names imply pointing, impossible for Allah. Sanusi then reports al-Zamakhshari's refutation: you use 'ilah' as a description (e.g., 'a great ilah') but you do not describe the word 'ilah' itself (you don't say 'thing ilah'), so it functions as a noun, not an attribute. If it were an attribute, it would modify something else. Also, attributes need a subject; 'Allah' itself is the subject. Thus it is a proper name for the essence.

Why 'Al-'Alamin' is Plural: Linguistic Precision
Sanusi explains the linguistic reason for the plural 'al-'alamin' and its grammatical justification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the linguistic reason why the Quran uses the plural form 'al-'alamin' instead of the singular 'al-'alam'.

Core Idea
The plural 'al-'alamin' is necessary to express that Allah’s lordship encompasses all genera of creation without exception.

This lesson continues the commentary on 'Rabb al-'Alamin' by focusing on the word 'al-'alamin'. Sanusi explains that the singular 'alam' refers to each genus individually, so using the plural avoids the misunderstanding that only one world is meant.

This is a precise linguistic choice in the Quran.

The Meaning of 'Radiya Allahu Anhu' as Supplication
Sanusi analyzes the phrase 'Radiya Allahu anhu' and whether it is an attribute of action or essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sanusi interprets the supplication 'Radiya Allahu anhu' as an attribute of action rather than an attribute of essence.

Core Idea
Supplication points to a future event, so the attribute must be an action, not an eternal essence.

The common phrase 'Radiya Allahu anhu' (May Allah be pleased with him) is often said as a supplication for the Companions. But what does it mean theologically? Is Allah's pleasure an eternal attribute or an action that occurs in time? Sanusi clarifies this distinction in a short but precise argument. Let's unpack it.

Sanusi says that supplication is for a future matter not yet existent. This is the reason he favors the first interpretation. Why does that matter?
Attributes of essence are eternal, so they cannot be the object of a supplication.

Many assume 'Radiya Allahu anhu' always describes an eternal attribute. But as a supplication, it must refer to a future act of bestowal.
This distinction is important for understanding divine attributes in general.

Commentary
This passage explains the theological import of the common phrase 'Radiya Allahu 'anhu' (May Allah be pleased with him). Sanusi first states that when said as a supplication (duʿāʾ), it is a statement expressing a wish. He then analyzes the divine attribute of being pleased (riḍāʾ): it can be either an attribute of action (ṣifat fiʿl) meaning the actual bestowal of favor, or an attribute of essence (ṣifat dhāt) meaning the eternal will to bestow favor. In the context of supplication for the Companions, the first interpretation is more appropriate because supplication is directed toward a future event that has not yet occurred in the present. If it were an attribute of essence, it would be eternal and not subject to supplication. The passage ends mid-argument, likely continuing on a missing page.
