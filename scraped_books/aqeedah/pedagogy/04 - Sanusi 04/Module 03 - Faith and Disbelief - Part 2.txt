Partial Repentance is Valid
Sanusi affirms partial repentance against Abu Hashim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ash'ari scholars hold that repentance from some sins while persisting in others is valid, contrary to the Mu'tazili view of Abu Hashim.

Core Idea
You can repent from one sin while still struggling with another.

This short but significant passage settles a key question about repentance: must it be comprehensive? Sanusi presents the Ash'ari position that repentance can be partial, using a clear analogy from obligatory and evil acts. Read on to see the reasoning and the opposing view.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Notice the explicit disagreement with Abu Hashim and the analogy introduced by 'our companions'.
The passage is compact: a claim, a refutation, and an analogy.
2
Understand the analogy
The analogy compares repenting from some sins to performing one obligatory act while neglecting another. Just as one can do a good deed despite failing at another, one can abandon an evil deed while still committing another.
The key is that motives for each act are independent.

The point is that the motives for each sin are independent, and genuine regret can target a specific sin even if other sins remain.

General Repentance and Detailed Requirements
Explains sufficiency of general repentance and details requirements for sins against God and people

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that general repentance suffices for all sins, but sins involving the rights of Allah or others may require additional concrete actions beyond remorse.

Core Idea
Repentance repairs your relationship with Allah; for wrongs against people, you must also repair the harm.

This passage from Sanusi addresses a key practical question: what exactly does repentance require? While some scholars demand detailed remorse for each sin, Sanusi affirms that general repentance is sufficient. However, when a sin involves the rights of another person, additional steps like restitution or apology become necessary—separate obligations that complement but are not part of repentance itself.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
First read the Arabic passage and its translation to get an overview of the argument. Notice the structure: general principle, then details for sins against God and people, then verification.
The passage includes quotes from Taftazani and Imam al-Haramayn.
2
Understand the general principle
Sanusi states that general repentance (al-ijmāl) is sufficient for all sins, even if known in detail, contrary to some Mu'tazila who require detailed remorse. This is a lenient view that simplifies repentance.
The principle applies whether the sin is against God or people.
3
Examine the requirements for sins against God
For pure rights of God (e.g., fleeing battle, neglecting commanding good), remorse may suffice. But some sins require extra actions like surrendering for punishment (drinking wine) or paying missed obligations (zakat, prayer).
The extra actions are obligations alongside repentance, not part of it.
4
Examine the requirements for sins against people
For oppression (usurpation, murder), restitution or surrender for retaliation is needed. For misguidance, guidance. For harm (backbiting), apology. Backbiting doesn't require detailing unless the victim heard a worse version.
Imam al-Haramayn distinguishes between murder (remorse suffices for God, but refusal to surrender is a new sin) and usurpation (remorse invalid while holding property).

Contrary to some Mu'tazila that detailed regret is necessary for what one knows. Al-Taftazani said: They said: Then if the sin is purely concerning the right of Allah the Exalted, regret may suffice, as in committing flight from battle and abandoning commanding good, and it may need an additional matter, such as surrendering oneself for the prescribed punishment in drinking, and surrendering what was obligated in abandoning zakat, and similarly in abandoning prayer. Commentary
This passage is a discussion on the conditions for valid repentance. It begins by contrasting the view of some Mu'tazila who require detailed regret for every sin. Al-Taftazani is then cited, explaining that if a sin is purely against Allah's rights (e.g., fleeing battle, abandoning commanding good, or skipping prayer), regret may suffice, but sometimes an additional action is needed, like surrendering for punishment. If the sin involves rights of people, then alongside regret, one must either deliver the right or its substitute (if oppression), guide the person (if misguidance), or apologize (if harm, like backbiting). However, detailing the backbiting is not required unless the victim heard it in a worse form. Then the author gives the verification: this extra requirement is a separate obligation outside repentance. He quotes Imam al-Haramayn, who distinguishes murder from usurpation: in murder, if the killer regrets but does not surrender for retaliation, his repentance to Allah is valid, but withholding retaliation is a new sin needing separate repentance. In usurpation, repentance is invalid without returning the stolen item because one cannot genuinely regret while still holding it. Thus, the key point is that repentance from sins against humans often requires restoring rights, while sins against Allah may only require regret plus any necessary acts of worship.

Repentance from Sins Against Humans: Restoring Rights
Sanusi enumerates the requirements for repentance regarding sins against human rights with a verification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the additional requirements for repentance when sins involve the rights of others, and the distinction between murder and usurpation.

Core Idea
Sins against humans require regret plus restoring rights, guidance, or apology—and the obligation is separate from repentance.

Sanusi now turns to sins that involve the rights of other people. He explains that in addition to regret, one must fulfill the affected person's rights: deliver what is owed, guide if misled, or apologize if harmed. He concludes with a verification by Imam al-Haramayn clarifying that these extra actions are separate obligations, and distinguishing between murder and usurpation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage for sins against humans
Read the Arabic and translation. Note the three categories: oppression (requires delivering right or substitute), misguidance (requires guiding), and harm (requires apology).
Also notice the special case of backbiting.
2
Understand the verification
The passage then states that this extra requirement is a separate obligation outside repentance. Imam al-Haramayn's quote illustrates the distinction between murder and usurpation.
In murder, regret can be valid without surrendering for retaliation, but usurpation requires returning the property.
3
Apply the principle
Reflect on why the distinction exists: in usurpation, the sin continues as long as one holds the property, while in murder, the act is past.
This helps understand the nature of ongoing sins.
