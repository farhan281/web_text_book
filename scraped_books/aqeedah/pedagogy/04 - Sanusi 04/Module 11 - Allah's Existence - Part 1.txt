The World's Composition and the Three Possibilities
Sanusi defines the world and presents the tripartite division for an eternal body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the kalam definition of the world and the three logical possibilities for an eternal body.

Core Idea
Every body must be moving, at rest, or neither—but none works for an eternal body.

Sanusi now moves from rational foundations to the first major proof: the world's temporal origination. He defines the world as everything besides Allah and explains that all bodies are accompanied by accidents like motion and rest. This sets up a tripartite division that will challenge any claim of eternal existence for a body.

Notice the phrase 'لو كان جسم من أجرام العالم... لم يخلُ' which introduces the division.
2
Understand the definition of the world
Sanusi defines the world as everything other than Allah, and says it consists of bodies (ajram) to which accidents (a'rad) like motion and rest inhere.
This definition is crucial for the proof.
3
Identify the three possibilities
The passage presents three possibilities for an eternal body: moving, at rest, or neither moving nor at rest. Note that these are presented as exhaustive.
The impossibility of all three will prove temporal origination.

Commentary
This passage presents the classical kalam argument (burhan al-tamānu‘ or similar) for the temporal origination of the world. The author first establishes that the world (al-‘ālam) is everything other than Allah, and it consists of bodies (ajrām) that are necessarily accompanied by accidents (a‘rād) such as motion and rest.

The argument proceeds as follows:
- Suppose a particular body (e.g., the sky or the earth) were eternal (existed in pre-eternity, al-azal).
- That eternal body would have to be either: (1) moving, (2) at rest, or (3) neither moving nor at rest. These are the only possibilities.
- Sanusi argues that all three are impossible for an eternal body:
   - The third case (neither moving nor at rest) is clearly impossible because a body must either be in a particular place (fixed) or moving from it; such a neutral state is inconceivable for a body.
   - The second case (eternally at rest) is also impossible, because if it were eternally at rest, it would be incapable of ever becoming moving (change from rest to motion would be impossible).
- The first case (eternally moving) is not explicitly discussed here, but the implication is that it too is impossible because motion is an accident that cannot be eternal (as per other kalam arguments).
- Therefore, since all three options are impossible, no body can be eternal. Hence, the world (composed of bodies) must have a temporal beginning (ḥudūth).

The passage ends mid-sentence with the start of the explanation for the second case. The complete argument would also refute eternal motion, but the provided text stops here. The proof relies on the principle that bodies cannot exist without accidents, and accidents are temporal, so bodies must be temporal.

Impossibility of the Third and Second Divisions
Sanusi explains the impossibility of the third division (neither moving nor rest) and begins explaining the impossibility of the second (eternal rest).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why an eternal body cannot be neither moving nor at rest, and why it cannot be eternally at rest.

Core Idea
An eternal body cannot be neither moving nor at rest, and if eternally at rest it could never move.

Having established that an eternal body must be either moving, at rest, or neither, Sanusi now refutes two of these possibilities. He first shows that 'neither moving nor at rest' is inconceivable for any body. Then he begins to explain why eternal rest is also impossible, because it would prevent the body from ever acquiring motion. This completes the proof that bodies cannot be eternal.

2
Read the start of the explanation for the second division
Sanusi argues that if a body were eternally at rest, it would never be able to become moving. This is because a change from eternal rest to motion would require a cause, but the eternal state would be immutable.
The argument assumes that an eternal attribute cannot change.
3
Connect to the overall proof
Realize that these refutations, together with the refutation of eternal motion (not in this passage), show that no body can be eternal, thus the world is temporally originated.
This lesson covers the third and part of the second division; the first division will be handled separately.

Proof of Creation: The Impossibility of Eternal Rest
Sanusi presents the second case impossibility for an eternal body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi refutes the possibility of a body being eternally at rest, and why this refutation is key to proving the world's origination.

Core Idea
An eternal body at rest could never begin to move, proving it cannot be eternal.

In this lesson, we begin Sanusi's proof for the creation of the world. He first establishes that every worldly thing is a body with accidents. He then argues that if any body existed eternally, it must be either moving, at rest, or neither. He quickly dismisses the third option, then focuses on the second: eternal rest. Through a careful argument, he shows that an eternally resting body would be impossible because it could never start moving, contradicting our observation that motion does occur.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the three cases
Read the Arabic passage once through. Notice how Sanusi lists three possible states for an eternal body: moving, resting, or neither. Then read the translation to ensure understanding.
The three cases are exhaustive – no other states are possible for a body.
2
Focus on the second case: eternal rest
The second case is that the body is eternally at rest. Sanusi argues that if it were eternally at rest, it could never move; because the eternal cannot cease to exist. But we observe motion, so rest cannot be eternal.
This argument uses the principle that what is eternal cannot admit of non-existence.
3
Understand the role of similarity (tamāthul)
Sanusi claims that because we observe motion in some bodies, and all bodies are similar in nature, motion is possible for every body. This refutes the assumption that any body is eternally at rest.
The similarity of bodies is taken as evident – all bodies share the same essence with respect to motion and rest.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
After showing the impossibility of the second case, reflect on how this fits into the overall proof: if a body cannot be eternally at rest, it cannot be eternal. We still have to consider the first case (eternal motion) in the next lesson.

Chapter on the origination of the world and establishing the conclusive proof upon it. Since you have known this: the first thing you begin with from investigation is the investigation into the origination of the world; and it is everything other than God, Blessed and Most High. So if you investigate it, you find all of it to be bodies (ajrām) to which accidents (a‘rāḍ) inhere, of motion (ḥarakah), rest (sukūn), and others. And the three divisions are impossible for the body in pre-eternity; thus the existence of the body in pre-eternity is impossible; because its existence stripped of those three divisions cannot be conceived.

As for the explanation of the impossibility of the third division: it is obvious; because a body cannot be conceived in pre-eternity nor in what never ceases ... not fixed in a place nor moving from it.

As for the explanation of the impossibility of the second division – namely the body being at rest in pre-eternity – its aspect is: that if it were so, it would never accept that it could move at all; because its rest on this assumption is eternal (qadīm), and the eternal does not accept non-existence; because if it accepted non-existence, its existence would need a specific (mukhaṣṣiṣ) on account of its contingency then, so it would be originated (muḥdath), and it was assumed eternal, so this is an inconceivable contradiction. And the evidence that rest accepts non-existence is our observation of motion in some bodies, and that necessitates the possibility of motion upon all bodies, due to their similarity (tamāthulihā).

As for the explanation of the impossibility of the first division – namely the body being in motion in pre-eternity – its aspect is that which you have now understood regarding the impossibility of the second division, and this division is increased by another aspect of impossibility: that the reality of motion cannot be conceived as eternal; because it is the transfer from one place to another, so it is thus only accidental (ṭāri’ah) upon the body, and it is necessary that its existence be preceded by the being in the place from which it transfers. And the eternal cannot be conceived as accidental, nor can anything else precede its existence.

So there has come out for you by this conclusive proof: that the entire world is originated; from its Throne to its carpet; it cannot be conceived in the mind that any part of it is eternal.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof (burhān) for the origination (ḥudūth) of the world. It is a classification of possible states for an eternal body leading to impossibility. The author begins by defining the world as everything other than God and stating that all worldly things are bodies (ajrām) possessing accidents (a‘rāḍ) like motion and rest. The proof then proceeds as a reductio ad absurdum: assume some body (e.g., the sky or earth) existed in pre-eternity (al-azal). Such a body cannot be devoid of three possibilities: it is either moving, at rest, or neither. The author then argues each case is impossible. The third case (neither moving nor at rest) is dismissed as inconceivable: a body must either be fixed in a place or moving from it. The second case (eternal rest) is impossible because if the body had always been at rest, it could never begin to move; the eternal (qadīm) cannot cease to be (lā yaqbal al-‘adam). If it were possible for it to cease, then its existence would require a specific cause (mukhaṣṣiṣ), making it originated (muḥdath), contradicting its assumed eternity. Moreover, we observe motion in some bodies, and all bodies are similar (tamāthul), so motion is possible for any body; thus the assumption of eternal rest is invalid. The first case (eternal motion) is impossible for the same reason (if eternal, it could never cease; but motion is observed to cease), plus an additional argument: motion is essentially a transition, which is accidental (ṭāri’) and requires a prior state (the body’s being in the starting place). The eternal cannot be accidental or preceded by anything. Therefore, no body can be eternal; hence the entire world—from the Throne to the carpet—is originated. The proof is presented as conclusive (qaṭ‘ī).

The Impossibility of Eternal Motion and the Conclusion
Sanusi refutes the first case (eternal motion) and concludes the proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why eternal motion is impossible for a body and how the entire proof establishes that the world is originated.

Core Idea
Eternal motion is impossible because motion is an accident that must be preceded by a state.

In this lesson, we complete Sanusi's proof by examining the first case: that a body could be eternally in motion. Sanusi argues that motion is essentially a transition from one place to another, which implies it is accidental and preceded by a prior state. The eternal cannot be accidental or preceded. Therefore, the first case is impossible as well. This leaves no possibility for any body to be eternal, concluding that the entire world—from Throne to carpet—is originated.

Guided Path

Commentary
This passage presents a proof (burhān) for the origination (ḥudūth) of the world. It is a classification of possible states for an eternal body leading to impossibility. The author begins by defining the world as everything other than God and stating that all worldly things are bodies (ajrām) possessing accidents (a‘rāḍ) like motion and rest. The proof then proceeds as a reductio ad absurdum: assume some body (e.g., the sky or earth) existed in pre-eternity (al-azal). Such a body cannot be devoid of three possibilities: it is either moving, at rest, or neither. The author then argues each case is impossible. The third case (neither moving nor at rest) is dismissed as inconceivable: a body must either be fixed in a place or moving from it. The second case (eternal rest) is impossible because if the body had always been at rest, it could never begin to move; the eternal (qadīm) cannot cease to be (lā yaqbal al-‘adam). If it were possible for it to cease, then its existence would require a specific cause (mukhaṣṣiṣ), making it originated (muḥdath), contradicting its assumed eternity. Moreover, we observe motion in some bodies, and all bodies are similar (tamāthul), so motion is possible for any body; thus the assumption of eternal rest is invalid. The first case (eternal motion) is impossible for the same reason (if eternal, it could never cease; but motion is observed to cease), plus an additional argument: motion is essentially a transition, which is accidental (ṭāri’) and requires a prior state (the body’s being in the starting place). The eternal cannot be accidental or preceded by anything. Therefore, no body can be eternal; hence the entire world—from the Throne to the carpet—is originated. The proof is presented as conclusive (qaṭ‘ī).

Proof of God's Existence from the Origination of the World
Sanusi argues for a Creator from the origination of the world.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the kalām argument that the originated world must have a Creator, and why the emergence of existence from non-existence requires a free agent.

Core Idea
The world's transition from non-existence to existence demands a Creator.

This lesson presents the classical proof of God's existence based on the origination (ḥudūth) of the world. Sanusi argues that since the world was preceded by non-existence, it cannot bring itself into being; it requires an originator. He then addresses a key objection about the equality of existence and non-existence, showing that non-existence is more original, making existence an outweighed state that cannot arise without a cause.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation

2
Identify the core argument
The passage argues: the world is originated (it was preceded by non-existence). For it to come into existence, it must have an originator, because a thing cannot bring itself from non-existence to existence without a cause.

Sanusi discusses that existence and non-existence are either equal relative to the world, or non-existence is more appropriate. In either case, existence is either equal or outweighed. Since preponderance of an equal or outweigh without a cause is impossible, a cause must exist.
The cause is the Creator (Allah).

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the existence of God based on the contingency of the world. Sanusi begins by stating the premise: the world is originated (ḥādith) after its non-existence was certain. From this, he argues that it necessarily has an originator (muḥdith), because it is rationally inconceivable for something to move from non-existence to existence without a cause. Proof of the World's Temporality from the Impossibility of Eternal Attributes
Sanusi proves the world's temporality via the impossibility of eternal attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument that the world must be temporal because its necessary attributes are changeable and cannot be eternal.

Core Idea
If the world's necessary attributes cannot be eternal, the world itself cannot be eternal.

Sanusi presents a concise proof for the temporality of the world (ḥudūth al-ʿālam). He argues that certain attributes, such as motion and rest, are necessary for bodies but are inherently changeable. Since change is impossible for the eternal, these attributes cannot be eternal, and consequently the bodies that possess them cannot be eternal either. This passage is a classic kalām argument that establishes the temporal nature of the entire world.

Sanusi proves God's pre-eternity and divides the necessary into two types.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If God were temporal, an infinite regress would occur — but regress is impossible.

Theologians must establish that God is pre-eternal before discussing His attributes. Sanusi presents a concise proof: assuming God's temporal origination leads to an impossible infinite chain or vicious circle. Therefore, He must be pre-eternal. The passage then introduces a crucial distinction between self-evident and theoretical necessary truths that underpins subsequent reasoning.

The Proof of the World's Temporality
Sanusi presents the proof of the world's temporality via the impossibility of a pre-eternal body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first kalām proof for the temporality of the world, based on the impossibility of a body being pre-eternal.

Core Idea
A pre-eternal body cannot be subject to change, but all bodies are—so the world must have a beginning.

Sanusi now guides you into the first substantive proof of Islamic theology: the temporality (hudūth) of the world. This proof uses the observation that everything other than God consists of bodies and their accidents, which are always changing. By supposing a body were eternal, we find it leads to an impossibility, proving the world must have been created.

Guided Path
1
Understand the subject: the world
Note that Sanusi defines 'the world' as everything other than Allah. This is the scope of the proof.
2
Identify the structure of bodies
Observe that he states all worldly things are bodies (ajrām) that have accidents (aʿrāḍ) like motion and rest.
3
Trace the impossibility argument
Follow the conditional: if a body were pre-eternal, it must be either moving, resting, or neither. Each option is impossible for a pre-eternal entity, so no body can be eternal.
This is the classic kalām argument.

Commentary
This passage presents the first argument for the temporality (hudūth) of the world. The author first identifies the subject of inquiry: the world, defined as everything other than God. He then notes that observation shows the world consists of bodies (ajrām) that are subject to accidents (aʿrāḍ) like motion and rest. The proof is a conditional argument: suppose a body (e.g., the sky or earth) existed in pre-eternity (al-azal). Such a body would necessarily be either in motion, at rest, or neither (i.e., static but not in motion or rest). However, each of these three states is impossible for a pre-eternal entity: motion and rest are accidents that change, and a pre-eternal being cannot be subject to change; the third option (neither moving nor resting) is also impossible because a body must be characterized by one of these two accidents. Therefore, the premise that a body is pre-eternal leads to impossibility, so the world cannot be pre-eternal—it must have come into existence (ḥadatha). This is the classic kalām proof for the createdness of the universe.

Commentary
This passage presents the systematic proof for the origination (ḥudūth) of the world, which is a foundational argument in Kalām for establishing the existence of a Creator. The author first defines the world as 'everything other than God' and states that it is composed of bodies (ajrām) that have accidents (a‘rāḍ) like motion and rest. He then formulates the proof: Suppose a specific body, e.g., the heaven or earth, existed in pre-eternity (al-azal). That body would have to be either (1) moving, (2) at rest, or (3) neither moving nor at rest. He then demonstrates that all three are impossible. The third division is impossible because a body without motion or rest is inconceivable—it must be either in a place (rest) or moving from it. The second division (eternal rest) is impossible because if rest were eternal, it could never accept motion; but we observe motion in bodies, proving that rest is not eternal. Moreover, if eternal rest were to cease, it would require a specifier, making it originated—but eternity precludes origination. The first division (eternal motion) is impossible for the same reason as the second, with the additional point that motion is by definition a transition from one locus to another, which is inherently temporal and cannot be eternal. Since all three possibilities are impossible, the assumption that the body existed in pre-eternity is impossible; therefore the body must be originated (ḥādith). This argument, often called the argument from the temporal origination of accidents (ḥudūth al-a‘rāḍ), establishes that the world is not eternal and therefore needs a Creator.

Refuting the Possibility of an Eternal Body
Sanusi refutes each of the three possible states for an eternal body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why each of the three states (eternal motion, eternal rest, or neither) is impossible for a body in pre-eternity.

Core Idea
Neither eternal rest nor eternal motion can exist; the third option is inconceivable.

In this lesson, Sanusi provides detailed refutations for each of the three divisions. He shows that the third is logically impossible, the second leads to a contradiction with observed motion, and the first is impossible because motion is inherently temporal.

Read the refutation of the third division

Study the refutation of eternal rest

This refutation uses observation and logical contradiction.
3
Study the refutation of eternal motion
Learn why eternal motion is impossible: motion is transition from locus to locus, which is inherently temporal and cannot be eternal.
Sanusi also notes that the same reasoning from rest applies.

The Foundation: Inseparable Things and the Necessity of Origination
Sanusi introduces the conclusive proof and its core inference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure of Sanusi's conclusive proof that the entire world is originated, based on the inference from one inseparable thing to another.

Core Idea
The proof infers the origination of the world from the origination of its inseparable properties.

Sanusi begins by establishing a necessary condition: for something to come into existence, it must be preceded by its transition from another state, and the eternal cannot be temporal. He then declares that this conclusive proof shows the whole world to be originated. The proof's essence is an inference from the origination of one of two inseparable things to the origination of the other.

Commentary
This passage presents the conclusive proof (al-burhān al-qaṭʿī) for the temporal origination (ḥudūth) of the entire world. The author begins by stating a necessary condition: for something to come into existence, it must be preceded by its existence in the space from which it moves, and the eternal cannot be temporal nor preceded by another. He then declares that this proof establishes that the whole world, from the Throne to the earth, is originated; nothing in it can be eternal. He then exhorts the reader to know that understanding the world's origination is a foundational principle for all other beliefs and a key to subsequent benefits. The proof itself, he says, is conclusive and accessible. Its summary is an inference from the origination of one of two inseparable things (al-mutalāzimān) to the origination of the other. The world consists entirely of bodies (ajrām) that are inconceivable apart from motion or rest. Since motion and rest cannot be eternal (azalī) — as proven earlier by the example of the impossible — it necessarily follows that bodies, which cannot exist without them, cannot be eternal. If a body were eternal, it would have to be neither moving nor still at some instant, which is inconceivable. The author then analyzes the states of a body: it can only be in motion, rest, or neither (the absence of both). All three are impossible for a body in pre-eternity (al-azal); moreover, the third state (absence of both) is also impossible in future eternity (mā lā yazāl). Therefore, bodily existence absolutely is impossible in pre-eternity. Since the entire world is composed of bodies and their accident-like attributes, the whole world cannot subsist in pre-eternity, so it must be originated. This is the desired conclusion (al-maṭlūb).

The author states there is disagreement: some say it is necessary (ḍarūrī), others say it is theoretical (naẓarī). The truth, he says, is that it is theoretical but so close (qarīb) that it does not require extensive reflection (taʾammul). This is why even children, who have not learned formal reasoning, can grasp it. Because of its nearness, Fakhr al-Dīn al-Rāzī mistakenly claimed it is necessary. The author refutes Rāzī's further claim that this knowledge is also present in animals by instinct (ṭibāʿ al-bahāʾim). He gives the example of an animal that is frightened by the sound of a stick, which Rāzī might have cited as evidence that animals know that a sound requires a cause. The author explains that this fear is due to associative learning (ulfa), not rational deduction. The animal has previously experienced pain from being hit with a stick, and so it associates the sound with pain, not from any rational inference that every event has a cause. Attributing intellectual reasoning to animals, he says, is one of the most astonishing claims one can hear.

Proof of the Necessity of a Creator

Learning Objective

Sanusi presents a concise logical proof for the necessity of a Creator. The argument hinges on the concepts of preponderance (rajah) and preponderated (marjuh). Learning Objective
