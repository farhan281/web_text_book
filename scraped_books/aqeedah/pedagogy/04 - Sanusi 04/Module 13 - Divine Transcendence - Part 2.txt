The Reason for Mentioning the Throne
Sanusi explains the reason for specifying the Throne.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Throne is specifically mentioned in the istiwa' verse, despite all creation being equally dependent on Allah.

Core Idea
The Throne is mentioned as the greatest creation to show that even the mightiest is utterly dependent on Allah.

After explaining the meaning of istiwa', Sanusi discusses why the Throne is singled out. He argues that because it is the greatest created thing, specifying it highlights the total dependence of all creation. Let's explore his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the relevant passage
Read the second part of the source passage where Sanusi explains the reason for mentioning the Throne.
Look for the analogy of the ring thrown in a vast land.
2
Understand the problem
Recognize that one might mistakenly think that such a great creation as the Throne is self-sufficient.
This misunderstanding is corrected.
3
Grasp the conclusion
The passage concludes that if the Throne, the greatest creation, is in utmost need, then all lesser creations are even more dependent.
Thus the Throne's mention serves as a powerful lesson.

Commentary
This passage presents Imam al-Haramayn's ta'wil (interpretive explanation) of the Qur'anic attribute 'istiwa' (establishing/rising over) in the verse about the Throne. The author begins by stating that Imam al-Haramayn interpreted 'istiwa' as 'istila'' (domination) through qahr (subjugation) and tadbir (management). This interpretation removes any notion of physical movement, location, or inherent quality from the Throne; all its states occur only through Allah's will and creation. Next, the passage explains why the Throne is specifically mentioned in the verse, despite all creation being equally dependent on Allah. The reason is that the Throne is the greatest created entity; all other creations are like a small ring in a vast desert compared to it. One might mistakenly think that such a powerful thing is self-sufficient. Thus, Allah specifies it to show that even the greatest creation is utterly dependent, unable to manage its own affairs, and therefore all lesser creations are even more so.

Iblis's Error: Attributing Effect to Self
Sanusi presents the Sunni denial of other effective agents and then narrates Iblis's error.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fundamental error of Iblis—attributing his own actions and excellence to himself—and the Sunni rejection of any effective agent other than God.

Core Idea
Iblis's sin was not just disobedience but attributing his own actions to himself.

Sanusi begins by restating the Sunni position: no other effective agent exists besides God. He then uses Iblis as a case study to show how the false belief in one's own efficacy leads to arrogance and rebellion. This lesson examines the two hidden ignorances in Iblis that caused his downfall.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Sunni framework
Recognize that Ahl al-Sunnah affirm no effective agent but God; read the opening lines of the passage.
The key term 'مؤثر' (effective agent) is central.
2
Identify Iblis's two ignorances
Note the two 'جهالتين' (ignorances) in Iblis: (1) considering himself superior due to origin, (2) seeing himself as the doer of his own worship.
3
See how these led to his statements
Read how Iblis openly declared 'I am better than him' and accused God of injustice.
His words reflect the two ignorances.

Commentary
This passage is a theological argument contrasting the erroneous belief of Iblis (Satan) with the correct belief of the angels, as an illustration of the Sunni creed regarding divine action and human agency. The author begins by restating the position of Ahl al-Sunnah: they disavow any inherent power or strength (حول وقوة) and entrust all affairs to God, seeing no partner with Him and no other effective agent (مؤثر) in any effect, whether by nature or by a property placed in it. This sets the framework for understanding the error of Iblis. Iblis had two hidden ignorances (جهالتين) in his inner self: first, he considered himself superior because he was created from fire while Adam was from dust; second, he saw himself as the doer (فاعلاً) of his own worship and excellence. When commanded to prostrate to Adam, he became arrogant, magnified his own origin and worship, and even attributed injustice (جور) to God for commanding him to humble himself before a being with no prior worship. He openly declared his defiance using Qur'anic verses. The author then contrasts Iblis with the noble angels. They were created from great light and had worshipped God for thousands of years before Iblis existed. Yet when commanded to prostrate, they hastened to obey, taking pleasure in obedience, fearing God, and hoping for His reward. They did not rely on their noble origin (light), recognizing that it and other things are equal in that they are created. Their distinction (اختصاص) is purely by God's grace (فضل), not by their nature. They fled from any sense of entitlement, realizing that even their worship is God's act without intermediary (بلا واسطة). Thus they saw themselves as having no effect (تأثير) whatsoever, being equal to Adam in not bringing worship into existence. God praised them for this correct belief and obedience, emphasizing in the verse 'So the angels prostrated, all of them together' with multiple words of generalization to dispel any wonder that all of them prostrated despite their vast numbers. The passage ends with a supplication and the author's hope that God bestows the honor of uprightness and is just to whom He wills.

The Angels' Correct Creed: All Actions Are from God

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the angels' recognition that their worship and excellence are purely from God's grace, not from their own nature or effort.

Core Idea
The angels saw no effect from themselves; even their worship was God's act.

After contrasting Iblis, Sanusi presents the noble angels as the embodiment of correct belief. Despite their high rank and long worship, they attributed all to God and obeyed immediately. This lesson explores their humility and doctrinal correctness.

3

They recognized that their distinction and worship are purely by God's grace, not by their own doing.

Commentary
This passage is a theological argument contrasting the erroneous belief of Iblis (Satan) with the correct belief of the angels, as an illustration of the Sunni creed regarding divine action and human agency. The author begins by restating the position of Ahl al-Sunnah: they disavow any inherent power or strength (حول وقوة) and entrust all affairs to God, seeing no partner with Him and no other effective agent (مؤثر) in any effect, whether by nature or by a property placed in it. This sets the framework for understanding the error of Iblis. Iblis had two hidden ignorances (جهالتين) in his inner self: first, he considered himself superior because he was created from fire while Adam was from dust; second, he saw himself as the doer (فاعلاً) of his own worship and excellence. When commanded to prostrate to Adam, he became arrogant, magnified his own origin and worship, and even attributed injustice (جور) to God for commanding him to humble himself before a being with no prior worship. He openly declared his defiance using Qur'anic verses. The author then contrasts Iblis with the noble angels. They were created from great light and had worshipped God for thousands of years before Iblis existed. Yet when commanded to prostrate, they hastened to obey, taking pleasure in obedience, fearing God, and hoping for His reward. They did not rely on their noble origin (light), recognizing that it and other things are equal in that they are created. Their distinction (اختصاص) is purely by God's grace (فضل), not by their nature. They fled from any sense of entitlement, realizing that even their worship is God's act without intermediary (بلا واسطة). Thus they saw themselves as having no effect (تأثير) whatsoever, being equal to Adam in not bringing worship into existence. God praised them for this correct belief and obedience, emphasizing in the verse 'So the angels prostrated, all of them together' with multiple words of generalization to dispel any wonder that all of them prostrated despite their vast numbers. The passage ends with a supplication and the author's hope that God bestows the honor of uprightness and is just to whom He wills.

Sanusi presents a reductio proof against temporal attributes in pre-eternity.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof for God's unity, known as the argument from mutual hindrance (tamānuʿ). The proof proceeds: 1) If two gods exist, each must have complete and independent power over all possibles. 2) For any possible act, both powers would pertain to it. 3) If they agree on an act (e.g., creating an indivisible atom), three possibilities arise: (a) The act occurs from both together – impossible because it would either split the indivisible (if differences exist) or multiply the same effect (if identical). (b) The act occurs from one only – requires a preference without a preferrer, impossible, and both are equally incapable. 4) If they disagree, one wills existence, the other non-existence, resulting in mutual hindrance and no act occurs. Thus, both would be incapable, contradicting divinity. An objection suggests cooperation: the two gods together produce the effect without each acting independently. The answer refutes this: cooperation implies each alone is insufficient, hence deficient, which is inconsistent with divine perfection. The footnote explains that the ability of a deity is determined by essence, and possibles are equally related to both, so no preference exists. This solidifies the impossibility of two gods.

The Impossibility of Two Gods Creating an Indivisible Atom
Sanusi presents a disjunctive argument against two gods creating a single atom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why it is logically impossible for two gods to jointly create a single indivisible atom, given the constraints of divine power and the nature of indivisible substance.

Core Idea
Two creators for one atom leads to either division of the indivisible or duplication of the existent.

In this passage, Sanusi deploys a disjunctive argument to rule out any scenario in which two divine beings could cooperate to produce a single atom. He first considers two broad possibilities: either both create the atom together, or only one does. Each path is shown to entail an absurdity. This establishes a foundational argument for divine unity in action.

Guided Path
1
Read the disjunctive argument
Read the first part of the passage where Sanusi presents the two main possibilities (the atom from both together, or from one alone). Note how he breaks each into sub-possibilities.
The argument is a 'proof by cases'.
2
Examine the first case: both together
Notice the two subcases: either each god creates a different part (leading to division of the indivisible) or they create the same existent (leading to 'obtaining the already obtained'). Both are absurd.
These are the two impossible outcomes under the first possibility.
3
Examine the second case: one alone
Understand why preponderance without a preponderator is impossible when both gods are equal in power. The equality assumption forces either both be capable or both incapable; if one acts, the other must be incapable, violating equality.
The concept of 'murajjiḥ' (preponderator) is crucial.
4
Summarize the disjunctive proof
Reflect on how these two possibilities exhaust the logical space, and both lead to impossibility. Therefore, the initial assumption of two gods creating an atom is false.

Commentary
This passage presents a classical kalam refutation of the possibility of two gods (or divine beings) creating a single indivisible atom (jawhar fard). The argument is a disjunctive proof that covers all possibilities and shows each leads to an absurdity. First, the author considers two initial possibilities: either both gods create the atom together (معاً), or only one of them creates it. For the first case (both together), two sub-possibilities arise: either each god creates a part of the atom, leading to division of what is indivisible, or each creates the very same existent, which is 'obtaining what has already been obtained' (تحصيل الحاصل), i.e., an effect already actualized by the other. Both are impossible. For the second case (one god alone), the problem is that since the two gods are assumed equal in power (متساويان), there is no preponderator (مرجح) to prefer one over the other to be the sole creator; otherwise, the other would be incapable in the very aspect the first is capable, which contradicts equality. The author then presents an objection: the critic proposes a third possibility not covered—that the two gods together, as a collective agent, create the atom, not each individually. In this scenario, each has insufficient power alone, but combined they succeed, like two people lifting a heavy object together. The author answers by analyzing the nature of the proposed 'weak power' (قدرة ضعيفة) of each god. When they act together, either each one's power has some effect (تأثير) on the atom or it has no effect at all. If each has some effect, then either the effects are identical (leading again to تحصيل الحاصل) or they are distinct (leading to the indivisible atom having two effects, which requires it to be divisible—absurd). If neither has any effect in the combined state, then the aggregate of two non-effective attributes is also non-effective, analogous to adding two bits of knowledge that remain inert. Thus the third possibility collapses into the same impossibilities. The argument thus exhaustively demonstrates the logical necessity of divine unity (tawhid) by showing that multiple creators cannot coherently produce even a single atom.

Answering the Objection of Cooperative Agency
Sanusi answers the objection of cooperative agency by analyzing the effect of each power.

Learning Objective

Cooperative agency still forces either identity or division of effects.

Having presented the initial disjunctive proof, Sanusi anticipates an objection: what if the two gods are not each independently powerful but rather cooperate like two people lifting a heavy object? In this response, he analyzes the nature of each god's power and demonstrates that even this scenario collapses into the same absurdities.

Same problems as before.

This refutes the cooperative model.
5
Conclude the refutation
All possibilities lead to absurdity, so the objection fails. The proof of divine unity is complete.

Commentary
This passage presents a classical kalam refutation of the possibility of two gods (or divine beings) creating a single indivisible atom (jawhar fard). The argument is a disjunctive proof that covers all possibilities and shows each leads to an absurdity. First, the author considers two initial possibilities: either both gods create the atom together (معاً), or only one of them creates it. For the first case (both together), two sub-possibilities arise: either each god creates a part of the atom, leading to division of what is indivisible, or each creates the very same existent, which is 'obtaining what has already been obtained' (تحصيل الحاصل), i.e., an effect already actualized by the other. Both are impossible. For the second case (one god alone), the problem is that since the two gods are assumed equal in power (متساويان), there is no preponderator (مرجح) to prefer one over the other to be the sole creator; otherwise, the other would be incapable in the very aspect the first is capable, which contradicts equality. The author then presents an objection: the critic proposes a third possibility not covered—that the two gods together, as a collective agent, create the atom, not each individually. In this scenario, each has insufficient power alone, but combined they succeed, like two people lifting a heavy object together. The author answers by analyzing the nature of the proposed 'weak power' (قدرة ضعيفة) of each god. When they act together, either each one's power has some effect (تأثير) on the atom or it has no effect at all. If each has some effect, then either the effects are identical (leading again to تحصيل الحاصل) or they are distinct (leading to the indivisible atom having two effects, which requires it to be divisible—absurd). If neither has any effect in the combined state, then the aggregate of two non-effective attributes is also non-effective, analogous to adding two bits of knowledge that remain inert. Thus the third possibility collapses into the same impossibilities. The argument thus exhaustively demonstrates the logical necessity of divine unity (tawhid) by showing that multiple creators cannot coherently produce even a single atom.

The Proof of al-Tamānu' for God's Uniqueness in Actions
Sanusi presents the full proof of al-tamānu' for God's exclusive creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the proof of al-tamānu' (mutual hinderance) and how it demonstrates that God alone is the exclusive creator of all events.

Core Idea
No two independent causes can produce one effect; only God's power is truly effective.

Sanusi now deploys a powerful rational argument known as the proof of mutual hinderance (al-tamānu') to demonstrate that God's uniqueness extends not only to His essence but also to His actions. This proof shows that if any being other than God had genuine causal influence, contradictions would arise. In this lesson, you will trace the logical steps of this proof and see why God must be the sole creator.

Guided Path
1
Understand the aim of the proof
Sanusi states that the same proof used to negate a second god also proves God's sole creative power. This proof targets the idea that anything else can have independent causal influence.
The aim is to show that no creature can be a true cause alongside God.
2
First scenario: Two powers on the same effect
Assume a created being has influence over an effect that is also within God's power. Then the effect's occurrence leads to three impossible consequences: from both (one effect, two independent causes), from one (preponderance without a preponderator), or from neither (the event creates itself).
The impossibility of one effect from two independent causes is key.
3
Second scenario: Opposite effects
Assume the two powers aim for incompatible outcomes (e.g., motion vs. stillness). Then three possibilities arise: both occur (impossible due to contradiction), neither occurs (impossible: body devoid of both and God is impotent), or one occurs (if the other's occurs, God is disabled; if God's occurs, again preponderance without a preponderator).
This scenario covers cases where the powers are directed to mutually exclusive objects.
4
Draw the conclusion
Both scenarios lead to absurdities if a second cause is admitted. Therefore, the attribute of influence and creation is exclusive to God alone.
The proof solidifies tawhid in actions.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on Sanusi's proof that God alone creates and on the classification of created acts. The commentator begins by presenting a hypothetical scenario: if two powers (one divine, one created) are assumed to be attached to contradictory effects on the same body at the same time (movement vs. stillness), then several impossible outcomes follow. First, if both effects occur, that would necessitate the conjunction of two opposites (movement and stillness), which is impossible. Second, if neither occurs, that would imply that God is unable to produce His desired effect and that the body is devoid of both movement and stillness, which is also impossible. Third, if only one occurs, then if it is the created power's effect, that would imply God is incapacitated; if it is God's effect, then that would be a preference without a preferrer, and also the possibility of the eternal power being incapacitated because it was assumed equal to the created power with respect to that effect. The only valid conclusion is that the created power cannot be truly effective; rather, the attribute of effecting and creating belongs exclusively to God. The commentator then explains the result: since the proof establishes God's exclusive creation, the powers created in creatures have absolutely no effect on any action, whether movement, stillness, or otherwise. All actions, along with the essences and powers of creatures, are directly created by God. The essences are merely vessels for God's power. The commentator then classifies beings into two types: (1) compelled (majburah) beings, where God creates the action (e.g., movement) without creating an accompanying power, such as in involuntary tremors; (2) chosen (mukhtarah) beings, where God creates the action along with an accident called power, which accompanies the action without having any effect—it only provides the creature with a sense of ease and ability, leading to the designation 'chosen'. Finally, the commentator notes that for either category, God may grant consciousness of the created accidents or may cause sleep or heedlessness, emphasizing that God does as He wills and is not questioned.
