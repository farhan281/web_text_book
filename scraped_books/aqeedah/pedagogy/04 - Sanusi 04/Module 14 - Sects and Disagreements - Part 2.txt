Sanusi's First Counterargument: Why Not Kill All Disbelievers as Children?
Sanusi presents the Mu'tazila claim and then refutes it with a reductio ad absurdum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first counterargument Sanusi uses against the Mu'tazila claim of al-aslah, showing that it leads to an absurd implication regarding disbelievers.

Core Idea
If killing children to prevent disbelief is beneficial, why didn't God kill all disbelievers as children?

Sanusi refutes the Mu'tazila doctrine that God must always do what is most beneficial for His servants. He begins by presenting their claim and then immediately turns it against them with a devastating reductio ad absurdum. In this lesson, we examine the first counterargument involving Pharaoh, Haman, and other notorious disbelievers.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Mu'tazila claim
Read the first part where the Mu'tazila state that causing children to die before puberty is most beneficial because God knows they would disbelieve if they lived.
They argue that death saves them from the harm of obligation.
2
Identify the counterargument
Sanusi then asks: if that logic holds, why didn't God cause all known disbelievers like Pharaoh to die as children? That would have been more beneficial.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Reflect on the implication
Notice that the Mu'tazila would have to accept that such a death is better, yet they themselves do not experience it. Their principle leads to a contradiction.

Abu Ali vs. Abu Hashim on Ihbat
Taftazani reports the disagreement on ihbat between Abu Ali and Abu Hashim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two competing theories of nullification (ihbat) held by Abu Ali al-Jubba'i and his son Abu Hashim.

Core Idea
Nullification of lesser good deeds can turn them into punishment, according to Abu Ali; Abu Hashim adds that an equal portion of greater deeds is also nullified.

This passage dives into a technical dispute within the Mu'tazila school about how good and bad deeds interact. The concept of ihbat (nullification) is central to their soteriology. Taftazani reports the positions of Abu Ali and his son Abu Hashim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scholars and their disagreement
Abu Ali (al-Jubba'i) and Abu Hashim (his son) had conflicting views on how nullification works.
This is a nuanced theological technicality.
2
Understand Abu Ali's view
Abu Ali held that only the lesser amount of reward or punishment is nullified, and nothing of the greater is affected. The nullified amount becomes its opposite (reward becomes punishment, etc.).
This is called 'pure ihbat' (al-ihbat al-mahd).
3
Understand Abu Hashim's view
Abu Hashim held that the lesser is nullified, and an equal amount from the greater is also nullified. He gave an example with 100 parts punishment and 1000 parts reward.
Here, 100 parts from the reward would be nullified alongside the 100 parts punishment.
4
Consider the implications
These different mechanisms lead to different outcomes for the believer's ultimate fate.
Think about the severity of punishment or reward remaining.

Key Takeaway
Cross-madhhab hisba is permissible; a Hanafi may enjoin a Shafi'i on matters prohibited in the Hanafi school, and a Shafi'i may enjoin a Hanafi on matters prohibited in the Shafi'i school.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Would you ever correct a fellow Muslim for something that is not wrong in their madhhab but is in yours, even if it might strain the relationship? Are you willing to risk that for the sake of what you believe is right?
?
If someone from a different school corrected you on a matter you follow your own scholars on, would your first reaction be defensiveness or sincere reflection? What does that reveal about your heart?
?
The examples in this lesson require real courage to enact—could you, right now, name one issue where you would act as a Hanafi or Shafi'i toward someone from another school? If not, what is stopping you?
