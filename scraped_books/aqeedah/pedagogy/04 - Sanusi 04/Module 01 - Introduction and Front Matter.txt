Commentary
This passage is the author's preface (بين يدي الكتاب) to the work. It begins with the basmalah, praise of God, and salutation upon the Prophet Muhammad, his family, and companions, followed by an address to the reader. The author then turns to a sharp admonition against the people of obstinacy (أهل العناد) who, despite clear truths before them, persist in arrogance and blind opposition. He describes the spiritual diseases that afflict them: pride, obstinate defiance, envy, and hatred of scholars. The only cure for these malignant diseases is truthfulness (الصدق). He supports this with the well-known hadith of the three men trapped in the cave who were saved by their truthfulness in supplication. The final sentence asserts that God does not withhold guidance from a heart that is sincerely burning with the pain of separation (presumably from truth or God). This preface sets a moral and spiritual tone, urging sincerity and humility as prerequisites for the theological discussions to follow.

Commentary
This passage is a bibliographic and biographical discussion. It begins by quoting al-Malali, who lists the works of Imam al-Sanusi, including answers to questions, sermons, letters, talismans, and copies of others' works. Then it addresses works whose attribution to al-Sanusi is uncertain. The first is 'Haqa'iq al-Sanusi' (also 'al-Haqa'iq fi al-Ta'rifat'). The author clarifies that most of the definitions in this treatise are from al-Sanusi, but he is not its author; rather, it was compiled by his teacher al-Tha'alabi from al-Sanusi's 'Sharh al-Sughra'. Some mistakenly attributed it to al-Sanusi because the material is his. The author notes variation in manuscript copies. Next, the author discusses 'Tawhid Ahl al-'Irfan', which al-Baghdadi mentioned is not an independent work but a commentary on 'Umm al-Barahin', re-listed to prevent confusion. Then 'al-'Iqd al-Farid' is a commentary on 'Lamiat al-Jaza'iri'; the author expresses surprise that al-Baghdadi gave the correct title but also said it is a commentary on 'al-Jaza'iriyya' (which are the same). Finally, 'Nusrat al-Faqir' is a long letter mentioned by al-Malali, with surviving manuscripts. The passage thus clarifies the authorship and nature of several works attributed to al-Sanusi.

Commentary
This passage is a preface in which Sanusi explains his motivation for writing this creed and his pedagogical approach. He begins by stating his method: he limited himself to the wording without excess, aiming to drive the reader's soul to benefit through brevity. He then laments that most people of his time find truth heavy and incline to Satan's deceptions. Satan cuts off their path by making them think they are perfect in belief and that blind following (taqlid) is the ultimate knowledge. Sanusi cites poetry by Ibn al-Mubarak condemning corrupt kings and scholars as the source of religious corruption. He then describes his own reaction: a blind jealousy and foolish compassion for common Muslims and many fiqh students, seeing corruption in their beliefs and neglect of proofs of tawhid. So he began teaching this creed and others, gently clarifying truth with proofs for everyone, regardless of social status, aiming to save them from evil belief and the danger of taqlid. He continued this work, which enraged Iblis, until the devil attacked with his forces, bringing ignorant followers who are 'composite ignorant' (jahil murakkab). The passage shows Sanusi's concern for laypeople, his critique of blind following, and his method of using rational proofs. However, the passage ends mid-sentence, suggesting continuation.

The Purpose and Foundation of This Work
Sanusi opens the preface with praise, states the purpose of the work, and defines praise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the purpose of Sanusi's book: to guide the responsible person from imitation (taqlid) to correct rational consideration (nazar), and the importance of beginning with praise.

Core Idea
Sanusi's goal: replace disputed imitation with agreed-upon rational consideration.

Sanusi begins his theological primer with an opening that is both reverent and systematic. He invokes divine praise, sets forth the precise aim of his work, and roots his method in a prophetic tradition. This lesson unpacks those foundational elements.

Guided Path

Read the passage and translation

The passage is the formal opening of the book.
2
Identify the book's purpose
Locate the sentence that begins with 'فهذه جمل مختصرة'. This states the aim: to bring the responsible person from imitation (التقليد) to correct consideration (النظر الصحيح).

The hadith underscores the importance of starting with praise.

Examine the definition given: praise is verbal commendation of the praised one for something beautiful.
This definition sets the meaning for the rest of the work.

Commentary
This passage is the formal opening of the book. Sanusi begins with the basmala (implicitly, he says الحمد لله) and prayers, then states the purpose of the work: to provide concise summaries that will lead the legally responsible person from imitation (taqlid) to correct consideration (nazar). He notes that imitation is controversial regarding the validity of faith, whereas nazar is agreed upon. This highlights the importance of rational reflection in attaining certainty. He then explains why he started with praise, citing a hadith, and defines praise as verbal commendation.
