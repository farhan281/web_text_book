Learning Objective

Core Idea
Karamat of saints are almost like miracles of prophets; denying them is innovation.

Imam Sanusi, a prominent Ash'ari theologian, affirms the reality of karamat (miracles of saints) as part of Sunni doctrine. This passage presents the core evidence for prophethood in Islamic theology: miracles (mu'jizat) are extraordinary events that cannot be achieved by any human means, such as magic or medicine. The author argues that their uniqueness to prophets proves divine support.

Sanusi defines mu'jiza, explains its etymology, and the nature of i'jāz.

Core Idea
Miracles are divine signs that establish the truth of prophethood by demonstrating human inability.

Sanusi explains that Allah supports truthful prophets with clear proofs of their truthfulness, which remove all doubt except for those destined for punishment. These proofs are called mu'jizāt (miracles), a term rooted in the concept of human incapacity. In this passage, he defines the word, traces its linguistic derivation, and clarifies the meaning of i'jāz.

Commentary
This passage explains the nature of miracles (mu'jiza). The context is the author's commentary on a line from the creed about Allah supporting prophets. The author explains that because the claim of prophethood can be made by both truthful and false claimants, Allah out of His grace supports the truthful with something that proves their truthfulness so clearly that only those destined for punishment doubt it. This supporting evidence is called mu'jiza in the terminology of the theologians (mutakallimūn). The word mu'jiza is derived from 'ajz (inability), the opposite of power (qudra). The real meaning of i'jāz is the affirmation of inability; the term was originally a figure of speech (metaphor) to signify manifestation of inability, then it was used for the act (the miracle) that customarily causes inability (because people are unable to match it). The tā' at the end of mu'jiza indicates that the word has been transferred from an adjective (mu'jiz, meaning 'making incapable') to a noun (mu'jiza, meaning 'miracle'). Some say the tā' is for exaggeration, similar to the tā' in 'alāma (sign).

The Definition of Miracle and Key Objections

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of a miracle in Islamic theology and the key objections raised against it, particularly the need for a challenge (tahaddi) and the status of preparatory miracles (irhas).

Core Idea
Miracles require a challenge; preparatory events are classed as miracles only by analogy.

In this passage, the author presents the definition of a miracle (mu'jizah) and then addresses two objections. The first objection concerns events like the cloud shading the Prophet before his mission, which lack an explicit challenge. The second objection involves miracles that appear after the challenge. The author then explains how preparatory miracles (irhas) are classified, showing the nuanced theology behind extraordinary events.

Commentary
This passage presents definitions of miracle (المعجزة) and addresses objections to them. It begins by quoting a definition: 'the action by which is intended affirmation' – likely a previous definition. Then it cites another definition from 'some of the companions': it is a matter intended to manifest the truthfulness of the claimant to prophethood. Then the author raises two objections. The second objection states that people count as miracles events that precede prophethood and are not accompanied by a challenge (التحدي), like the cloud shading the Prophet before his mission or stones greeting him. The Imām (likely al-Ashʿarī or another authority) stipulated that a miracle must be accompanied by a challenge, so this definition is not comprehensive. The third objection says that a miracle can be delayed after the challenge, e.g., the prophet says his miracle will appear on a certain day and it appears later; this also corrupts the definition. Then the text moves to an answer: the challenge creates the link between the claim and the miracle; without an explicit challenge, a sign is not a miracle. It suffices that the prophet indicates the sign as proof, without needing to say 'no one can bring its like'. Consequently, the miracles of past prophets are not miracles for others. Then the answer to the second objection: counting preparatory miracles (الإرهاصات) as miracles is by way of analogy (تغليب). The verifiers say that if extraordinary events appear from the prophet before his mission, and they are widespread and he was expected to be a prophet, they are irhaas (foundation for mission); otherwise they are a pure karaamah (divine favor). If such events appear from a righteous person other than the prophet, they are either irhaas or karaamah depending on whether his prophethood was expected. Otherwise it is pure irhaas, like the light on the forehead of ʿAbdullāh (the Prophet's father). This shows the nuanced classification of miracles and related extraordinary events.

Commentary
This passage presents an extended analogy to explain how a miracle (mu'jiza) indicates the truthfulness (sidq) of a prophet (rasul). The author first states that the preceding discussion includes all conditions of the miracle and clarifies its aspect of indication, then amplifies it with an example. The example is attributed to the imams of the school: a person appears before a great assembly in the court of a king who has hidden himself from view. The person asks the assembly if they know why the king has gathered them, and then delivers a dire warning: the king commands and forbids certain things, and informs them of an imminent horror that only those who immediately prepare and give full attention can escape. The person claims that the king has sent him to convey this message. He then argues that his speech is delivered in the king's awareness and oversight, and though the king is hidden from them, he is not hidden from seeing and hearing them. The king has power to punish or reward. The person further appeals to his own lifelong reputation for truthfulness, even in trivial matters, and asks rhetorically how, after reaching full maturity, he could dare to lie about the king in his very presence. The analogy parallels the situation of a prophet: the prophet comes with a miracle (the equivalent of the king's unseen knowledge and power), the people cannot see God, but the miracle is performed in God's awareness and serves as evidence that the prophet is a true messenger. The prophet's known truthfulness and the impossibility of deception in such a context force rational acceptance of his message. The passage thus demonstrates the logical necessity of accepting a prophet who performs a genuine miracle.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of the necessary conditions for a miracle to serve as proof of prophethood, followed by an identification of two errors that lead to denial of miracles. Sanusi first argues that the miracle itself does not require an analogy from ordinary experience because the proof is immediate when the conditions are met. He illustrates with a hypothetical scenario where a prophet reminds the people of God's power and knowledge, then calls upon God to revive bones, which then become a speaking person. Sanusi asserts that after establishing these 'pillars'—knowledge of God's oneness and attributes—no one would doubt the prophet's truthfulness. He then explains that deniers of prophecies are ignorant of these pillars. He identifies two specific errors: first, denying that the miracle is an act of God by not believing in a free-acting Creator, instead attributing the world to necessary causation through intellects, souls, spheres, and natures (as do philosophers and naturalists). Second, denying that the act is a real breach of custom, claiming it could be achieved through tricks or esoteric knowledge. Sanusi then contrasts this with the one who is guided to the truth: such a person knows from his established tawhid that the challenging act is God's act, knows the prophet's claim (by witnessing or tawatur), knows the act is beyond tricks, and knows it occurs in accordance with the prophet's claim as God's response. This person gains necessary knowledge (ilm daruri) of the prophet's truthfulness without needing any further example. The passage thus presents the epistemological foundation of miracle-based proof of prophethood.

Majority View on the Miracle of the Quran
Sanusi states the majority view that the Quran's miracle lies in its supreme eloquence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the majority view on the aspect of the Quran's inimitability and how it is supported by different groups.

Core Idea
The Quran's miracle is primarily its unmatched eloquence, recognized by experts and common people alike.

After establishing that the Quran is a miracle by consensus, scholars debated the specific feature that makes it inimitable. Sanusi presents the majority (jumhūr) position: the Quran's supreme eloquence, which is attested by natural intuition, scholarly expertise, and universal recognition of human incapacity to rival it.

Guided Path
1

Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that Sanusi first mentions consensus, then presents the majority view.
2
Identify the three groups
The passage presents three groups who recognize the Quran's eloquence: the eloquent Arabs by natural intuition, the scholars of rhetoric by their expertise, and the common people by witnessing everyone's inability to rival it.
Each group has a different mode of recognition, but all agree on the miracle.
3
Understand the role of additional features
The passage lists other remarkable features (predictions, divine knowledge, etc.) but states that the primary miracle is eloquence according to the majority.
This guards against the misconception that the majority view denies these features.

The Ṣarfah Theory: How God Prevented Imitation of the Quran
Sanusi presents the view that the Quran's miracle is divine prevention of competition (al-ṣarfah).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the ṣarfah theory, its proponents, and the nuanced explanation of divine prevention according to al-Murtaḍā.

Core Idea
The Quran's miracle lies not in its content but in God's active prevention of any rival composition.

While most Muslim scholars held that the Quran's inimitability is intrinsic, a minority proposed that God actively prevented anyone from producing a similar text. This view, known as al-ṣarfah, was championed by figures like al-Naẓẓām and al-Murtaḍā. Their reasoning raises important questions about divine power and human ability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proponents and definition
Read the opening lines to see who held this view and how ṣarfah is defined as divine prevention of opposition.
Note that al-Naẓẓām was a key figure among the Muʿtazila.
2
Understand the three mechanisms
The passage lists three ways God could prevent competition: removing power, removing claims, or removing necessary knowledge. Compare them.
Al-Murtaḍā preferred the third mechanism.
3
Focus on al-Murtaḍā's preferred view
Read the verification section explaining that the Arabs had the required knowledge, but God removed it when they attempted to imitate the Quran.
This addresses why no successful imitation emerged despite ability.

Normally, having both enables one to produce an imitation, but God prevented it by removing that knowledge.

Critique of the Ṣarfah Theory
Sanusi critiques the ṣarfah theory by pointing out that it contradicts known reactions of the Arabs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sanusi's critique of the ṣarfah theory uses the historical reaction of the Arabs as a decisive refutation.

Core Idea
The Arabs' amazement at the Quran disproves the ṣarfah theory.

The ṣarfah theory claimed that the Quran is miraculous because God diverted the Arabs' attention away from producing similar speech, not because they lacked the ability. Sanusi offers a sharp objection: if the theory were true, why were the Arabs so amazed by the Quran? Their astonishment reveals that they recognized its superiority and were unable to match it. This lesson unpacks that logical argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the full English translation. Note that it begins with 'Wa fīhi naẓar' — a phrase signaling a critical objection.
2
Identify the logical structure
The passage uses a conditional: 'if it were so, then...' followed by two consequences. Identify the 'if' clause and the two 'then' clauses.
The structure is: hypothesis → consequence1 (no amazement) → consequence2 (prior production).
3
Understand the first consequence
If the ṣarfah theory were true, the Arabs would not have been amazed by the Quran. But historically they were amazed. Therefore, the theory contradicts known facts.
Amazement (istighrāb and taʿajjub) indicates perceived superiority.
4
Understand the second consequence
If they had the ability (as ṣarfah assumes), they would have produced something similar before the challenge. They did not, so the theory fails.
This reinforces that the miracle lies in the Quran itself, not in a divine distraction.

Commentary
This passage presents a critical objection (wa fīhi naẓar) to the ṣarfah theory. The author argues that if the theory were true, i.e., if the Arabs originally had the knowledge but God removed it when they tried, then they would not have expressed astonishment at the Quran's composition and eloquence. Their amazement shows that they recognized its superiority and lacked the ability. Moreover, prior to the Prophet's challenge, they would have produced something similar if they had the ability, but they didn't. This counterargument is concise but powerful: the very fact that the Arabs were impressed by the Quran's eloquence indicates that they did not possess the same level of eloquence, contradicting the ṣarfah assumption that they had it.

First Proof of the Ṣarfah Proponents
Sanusi begins the first argument for the ṣarfah theory based on ability to produce simple phrases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first argument used by proponents of the ṣarfah theory of inimitability.

Core Idea
If Arabs could match Quranic phrases, why couldn't they match a whole surah?

Sanusi presents the first of several arguments advanced by those who hold that the Quran's inimitability results from divine prevention (ṣarfah). This argument relies on the premise that the eloquent Arabs could certainly produce short phrases similar to those in the Quran, and therefore should have been able to produce a complete surah—yet they did not. The failure points to an external hindrance.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then read the translation. Notice that it is the first of several proofs and that it is based on a certainty about Arab capabilities.
The passage is cut off mid-sentence; the argument continues in the next lesson.
2
Identify the premise
The key premise is that we are certain (naqṭaʿu) that the eloquent Arabs were able to speak (qādirīn ʿalā al-takallum) similar to the individual words (mufradāt) and short compounds (murakkabāt qaṣīra) of the surah.
Examples given are 'al-ḥamdu lillāh' and 'rabbi l-ʿālamīn'.
3
Follow the reasoning
If they could produce such short phrases, then logically they should have been able to produce something similar to the entire surah (fa-yakūnūna qādirīn ʿalā al-ītiyān bi-mithlihi). The argument implies that their failure to do so shows an external prevention.
4
Recognize the conclusion
The conclusion, though not fully stated in this passage, is that since they could produce parts but not the whole, something must have prevented them — that is the ṣarfah (divine turning away).
This argument is the first of several aspects (wujūh) listed by Sanusi.

The Doctrine of Sarfah and Its Critique
Sanusi states the Mu'tazili doctrine of sarfah and critiques it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazili doctrine of sarfah (divine prevention) as an explanation for the Quran's inimitability, along with Sanusi's critical objection.

Core Idea
The Quran's inimitability: a divine miracle, not a literary challenge.

This lesson presents a controversial theory among early Islamic theologians regarding the nature of the Quran's miraculous character. Some argued that the Quran's inimitability was not intrinsic but due to Allah preventing others from matching it. Sanusi states this theory and then offers a critical objection rooted in the historical response of the Arabs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the doctrine and its proponents
Read the passage to find who held the doctrine of sarfah and what the term means. Note the names al-Nazzam, many Mu'tazila, and al-Murtada of the Shia.
Sarfah literally means 'turning away'.
2
Understand the modes of prevention
The passage lists three ways Allah could prevent challengers: removing their power, claims, or knowledge. Focus on the third mode and its two sub-versions.
The preferred version is removal after acquisition.
3
Follow the verification and critique
Read Sanusi's elaboration (tahqiq) and then his objection (fihi nazar). Ask yourself: why does the historical fact of Arab astonishment weaken the sarfah theory?
The objection points to timing: amazement occurred before the challenge.

Commentary
This passage presents the doctrine of al-sarfah and then critiques it. First, Sanusi states the position of al-Nazzam and many Mu'tazila, along with al-Murtada of the Shia: that the Quran's miraculous inimitability (i'jaz) is due to Allah 'turning away' (sarfah) the challengers from producing its like, even though they were capable. He then explains the three modes of this turning away: Allah could remove their power, or remove their claims/aspirations, or remove the knowledge required to compose such speech. Under the last mode, two possibilities are given: either the knowledge was never acquired, or it was acquired but then removed. The chosen view according to al-Murtada is the removal after acquisition. The verification (tahqiq) follows: the Arabs originally had knowledge of the Quran's composition and knew how to compose similar speech, but whenever they tried, Allah removed that knowledge. Then Sanusi raises an objection (fihi nazar): if this were true, the Arabs would not have been amazed by the Quran's composition, nor would their eloquent ones have been astonished at its eloquence, and some similar speech would have occurred from them before the Prophet's challenge. This objection undermines the sarfah theory by pointing out the historical reality of their wonder.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer unit. The author first presents the objection (shubha) raised by opponents: they claim that the Qur'an contains poetry in every meter, despite the Qur'an itself denying that it is poetry (Yasin:69). To support their claim, they cite numerous Qur'anic verses and match each with a classical Arabic poetic meter (al-tawīl, al-madīd, al-basīṭ, al-wāfir, al-kāmil, al-hazaj, al-rajaz, al-ramal, al-sarīʿ, al-munsariḥ, al-khafīf, al-muḍāriʿ, al-muqtaḍab, al-mujtathth, al-mutaqārib). The author then gives the refutation (al-radd). He argues that merely having the same metrical pattern (wazn) does not make a phrase poetry. For something to be called poetry, the metrical arrangement must be intentionally composed as poetry by the speaker. Moreover, some scholars require rhyme (taqfiya) as well. Furthermore, even the examples cited involve alterations and are not perfect matches. Finally, even if one concedes the presence of poetic meter, the principle of taghlīb (predominance) applies: the overwhelming majority of the Qur'an is plain prose (nathr), so occasional coincidences do not classify the whole as poetry. The passage concludes by noting that this discussion pertains to the first type of Qur'anic miracle (iʿjāz), and the author has covered three stations regarding it.

منها : خرورُ الأوثان سُجَّداً ليلةَ ولادتِهِ (۲) ، واستنارَ البيتُ عندَ خروجه ، وعظُمَ النور حتى إنَّ أُمَّهُ صلَّى اللهُ عليهِ وسلَّمَ أبصرَتْ حينئذ قصور بصرى مِنْ أَرضِ الشامِ (۳) ، وسمعت أصواتاً عظيمةً ، ودنَتْ منها نجوم السماء ، حتى إنَّها لتكادُ أنْ تمسَّها ، ثم سمعوا هاتفاً يهتفُ على جبلِ الحَجُونِ بقولِهِ (٤) : فَأُقْسِمُ مَا أُنْثَى مِنَ النَّاسِ أَنْجَبَتْ وَلَا وَلَدَتْ أُنْثَىٰ مِنَ النَّاسِ وَاحِدَهُ كَمَا وَلَدَتْ زُهْرِيَّةٌ ذَاتُ مَفْخَرٍ مُجَنَّبَةٌ لَوْمَ الْقَبَائِلِ مَاجِدَهُ وهتف هاتف آخر على جبل أبي قبيس يقول (٥) : [ من السريع] يَا سَاكِنِي الْبَطْحَاءِ لا تَغْلَطُوا وَمَيَّزُوا الأَمْرَ بِعَقْلٍ مَضِي أُخْتُ بَنِي زُهْرَةَ مِنْ سِرِّكُمْ فِي غَابِرِ الدَّهْرِ وَعِنْدَ الْبَدِي وَاحِدَةٌ مِنْكُمْ فَهَاتُوا لَنَا فِيمَا مَضَى لِلنَّاسِ أَوْ مَا بَقِي وَاحِدَةٌ مِنْ خَيْرِكُمْ مِثْلَهَا جَنِينُهَا مِثْلُ النَّبِيِّ التَّقِـي وارتعدَ عند ولادتِهِ صلَّى اللهُ عليهِ وسَلَّمَ إِيوانُ كسرى أَنوشَرْوانَ بنِ قباد بن فيروز ، وسقطَتْ مِنْ قصرِهِ أربع عشرة شرافة (١) ، وكتب إليهِ صاحب اليمن يخبرُهُ بأنَّ بحيرة ساوة غاضَتْ تلك الليلةَ ، وكتب إليهِ صاحبُ فارس يخبرُهُ بأنَّ بيوت النيران التي يعبدونها قد خمدَتْ تلك الليلةَ ، ولم تكن خمدَتْ قبلَ ذلكَ بألفِ سنةٍ ، وكتب إليه صاحبُ الشامِ يخبرُهُ بأنَّ وادي السماوة انقطعَتْ جَرْيتُهُ تلك الليلة(۲)
ثم أخبرَهُ المُوبَذانُ - ومعناه : القاضي والمفتي بلغتهم - أنَّه رأى إبلاً صعاباً ، تقود خيلاً عِراباً ، فانتشرَتْ في بلادهم .
فأرسل كسرى عبد المسيح الغساني إلى سطيح الكاهن - وكانَ مِنْ أخواله - يستخبرُهُ علم ذلكَ ، وكانَ سطيح جسداً ملقئ لا جوارح له ، ووجهه في صدرِهِ ، لم يكن له رأس ولا عنقٌ ، لا يقدر على الجلوس إلا إذا غضبَ انتفخَ ، وقد قيلَ لَهُ : أَنَّى لك هذا العلم ؟ فقال : لي صاحب مِنَ الجِنِّ استمعَ أخبار السماءِ مِنْ طُورِ سيناءَ حِينَ كلَّمَ اللهُ
The third category: matters outside the two categories. Among them: the falling down of idols prostrating on the night of his birth, and the house became illuminated when he came out, and the light became great such that his mother, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, then saw the palaces of Busra from the land of Syria, and she heard great voices, and the stars of the sky drew near to her, to the extent that they almost touched her. Then they heard a caller calling out on the mountain of al-Hajun with his saying: 'I swear, no female among humans has given birth, and no female among humans has borne one like what Zuhriyya, possessed of pride, protected from the blame of the tribes, noble, has borne.' And another caller called out on the mountain of Abu Qubays saying: 'O inhabitants of al-Batha, do not err, and distinguish the matter with a penetrating mind. Sister of Banu Zuhra, from your secret in the rest of time and at the beginning. One of you, so bring us one like her in what has passed for people or what remains. One of the best of you like her; her fetus is like the pious Prophet.' And at his birth, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, the arch of Kisra Anushirwan bin Qubad bin Firuz shook, and fourteen battlements fell from his palace. And the ruler of Yemen wrote to him informing him that the lake of Sawa had dried up that night, and the ruler of Persia wrote to him informing him that the fire-temples which they worshipped had been extinguished that night, and they had not been extinguished before that for a thousand years. And the ruler of Syria wrote to him informing him that the valley of al-Samawa had had its flow cut off that night. Then al-Mubadhan – meaning the judge and mufti in their language – informed him that he saw difficult camels leading Arab horses, and they spread throughout their lands. So Kisra sent Abd al-Masih al-Ghassani to Satih the soothsayer – and he was from his maternal uncles – to inquire from him the knowledge of that. And Satih was a body cast down, having no limbs, his face in his chest; he had no head nor neck, he could not sit except that when he became angry he would swell. And it was said to him: 'How do you have this knowledge?' He said: 'I have a companion from the jinn who listens to the news of the heaven from Mount Sinai when Allah spoke.'

Commentary
This passage enumerates the third category of miraculous events (mu'jizat) accompanying the Prophet's birth, which are extraordinary occurrences beyond the usual two categories (likely the Qur'an and other demonstrated miracles). The author lists several specific events: the prostration of idols, the illumination of the house, the mother seeing distant palaces, hearing voices, and the appearance of celestial phenomena. Then follows the narration of the shaking of Kisra's palace and the fall of battlements, along with reports from various rulers about the drying of Lake Sawa, the extinguishing of the fire-temples, and the drying of a river. The passage concludes with Kisra consulting the soothsayer Satih, who explains his knowledge comes from a jinn companion. This story serves to confirm the extraordinary nature of the Prophet's birth and his prophethood, showing that even the natural and supernatural worlds responded to his arrival.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some may think these events are necessary proofs of prophethood.
Correction: They are additional confirmatory signs, not independent proofs.
