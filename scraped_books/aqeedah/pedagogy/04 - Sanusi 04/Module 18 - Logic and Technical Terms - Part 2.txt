Fard Kifaya and the Duties of the Muhtasib
Sanusi defines commanding good as a collective duty and details the muhtasib's scope.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that commanding good and forbidding evil is a collective duty, and the muhtasib's scope of action depends on whether the matter pertains to Allah's rights, individual rights, or public welfare.

Core Idea
Commanding good is a collective duty; the muhtasib's role is defined by the nature of the right.

Sanusi explains that the obligation of commanding good is not on every individual singly, but a collective duty. When some qualified persons undertake it, the rest are absolved. However, if someone is officially appointed as muhtasib, it becomes his personal duty. Sanusi then outlines the muhtasib's jurisdiction, dividing matters into three categories: those concerning Allah's rights, those concerning individual rights, and those concerning public welfare. Each category has a different procedural rule.

Guided Path
1
Understand collective obligation
Read the first part where Sanusi describes the ruling as fard kifaya. Note that it is obligatory on all but drops if some do it.
Contrast this with individual obligation (fard 'ayn).
2
Identify the muhtasib's appointment
When a person is appointed, it becomes individually obligatory on him. This appointed person is called muhtasib.
3
Distinguish the three categories of duties
Sanusi divides his duties: rights of Allah (no complaint needed), non-general individual rights (complaint needed), and general public interests (act absolutely).
Look for the keywords: بحث وتجسس, استعداه صاحب الحق, على الإطلاق.
4
Note specific actions to forbid
The passage lists changes in worship forms, additions to adhan, unqualified teachers/jurists, and obstructing judges.
These illustrate the muhtasib's authority.

Then it is a collective obligation. If in every region someone capable undertakes it, the obligation falls from the rest, even though it is obligatory upon all; for such is the nature of a collective obligation: it is obligatory upon all, but drops with the action of some. Yes, if someone is appointed for this, it becomes specifically incumbent upon him; and he is called 'muhtasib' in common parlance. He acts (yahtasib) in matters pertaining to the rights of Allah without investigation and spying, and in matters pertaining to the rights of individuals that are not general, such as the procrastination of a wealthy debtor and a neighbor's transgression on a neighbor's wall — he acts if the aggrieved party complains. And in matters that are general, such as the disruption of the town's water supply, the destruction of its walls, and the neglect of its people to care for needy wayfarers when there is no money in the public treasury — he acts and commands absolutely. And he forbids those who change the forms of worship, such as speaking loudly in a silent prayer and vice versa, and those who add to the adhan, and those who take charge of issuing fatwas, teaching, and preaching without being qualified, and against judges if they obstruct [justice].

Commentary
This passage covers several related points. First, the nature of the obligation: it is a collective duty (farḍ kifāya) — if some qualified individuals in each region undertake it, the obligation is lifted from the rest. However, if a person is officially appointed (manṣūb) for this role, it becomes an individual obligation upon him; such a person is called muḥtasib. Second, the muḥtasib's scope is divided into three categories: (a) matters pertaining to the rights of Allah (e.g., acts of worship), where he can act without prior complaint or investigation; (b) matters of individual rights that are not widespread, such as a wealthy debtor delaying payment or a neighbor's encroachment — these require a complaint from the aggrieved party; (c) matters affecting the public interest, such as the town's water supply, the fortification wall, and care for needy travelers — in these, the muḥtasib acts and commands on his own authority. Finally, the passage lists specific actions the muḥtasib must oppose: altering the prescribed forms of prayer, adding innovations to the adhan, unqualified persons giving fatwas or teaching, and judges who obstruct justice. (The last item is incomplete in the manuscript.)

Graded Severity in Commanding Good and Forbidding Evil
Sanusi details the graduated response in hisbah based on the sin's severity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the principle of graduated response in hisbah and how the severity of the sin determines the appropriate level of intervention.

Core Idea
The severity of the sin dictates the force of the response in hisbah.

Sanusi explains that commanding good and forbidding evil is not limited to obligatory and prohibited acts; it applies to all levels of sin. He then illustrates the principle of gradual escalation through concrete examples from Hanafi jurisprudence, showing how the response increases with the gravity of the sin.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to grasp the overall message. Notice how the author moves from a general principle to specific examples.
The initial part may seem fragmented; it continues from a previous discussion.
2
Identify the general principle
The passage states that hisbah is not confined to wajib and haram. Understand that it encompasses all levels of sin, including makruh and recommended acts.
3
Examine the graduated scale
Look at the three examples: knee, thigh, private parts. Each has a different recommended response: gentle reproach, harsh reproach, striking, and finally killing if persisted.
The escalation is based on the severity of the exposure.
4
Reflect on the methodology
Consider why the response is gradual: it aims to correct the evil with minimal force while ensuring the objective is achieved. This reflects a balanced approach to maintaining public morality.
This prevents unnecessary violence while upholding the obligation.

... or they fall short in examining the disputes, and upon the imams of frequented mosques if they lengthen the prayer. And by this it is known that commanding good and forbidding evil is not confined to the obligatory and prohibited. And one should carry out the hisbah with gentleness and calm, progressing gradually from the milder to the harsher according to the state of the evil. That was mentioned in 'al-Muhit' of the Hanafiyyah: that whoever sees another with his knee uncovered: he should reproach him gently, and not contend with him if he persists; regarding the thighs: he should reproach him harshly, and not strike him if he persists; regarding the private parts: he should strike him, and if he persists, kill him (1).

Commentary
This passage is a legal classification of the levels of enforcement in commanding good and forbidding evil (hisbah). It begins with a general principle: the obligation is not limited to the obligatory and forbidden—i.e., it extends to recommended and disliked acts as well. The author then cites from the Hanafi work 'al-Muhit' a graduated scale: for a minor exposure (knee), gentle reproach and no fighting if he persists; for a greater exposure (thigh), harsh reproach but no hitting; for the private parts, striking, and if he persists, killing. The reasoning is that the severity of the response matches the gravity of the sin. The passage emphasizes a methodology of gradual escalation while maintaining the goal of eradicating evil.
