Transformation of Objects by the Prophet's Blessing
Sanusi enumerates two miracles of transformation by the Prophet's blessing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two specific miracles of transformation performed through the Prophet's blessing and their significance as proofs of prophethood.

Core Idea
The Prophet's blessing literally transformed objects and people, proving his prophethood.

This brief passage from Sanusi's work presents two vivid examples of the Prophet Muhammad's blessing (baraka) affecting physical objects. The first involves an ugly person becoming beautiful by wiping with the Prophet's ablution water or hand. The second is a piece of firewood turning into a sword that lasted for years. These miracles are presented as literal historical events that testify to the Prophet's special rank.

Sanusi uses these to show that the Prophet's baraka (blessing) directly affects physical objects, demonstrating his prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise definition of sihr (magic) according to Sanusi, including its four essential components and how it differs from miracles.

Core Idea
Sihr is an extraordinary act from an evil soul, learned and performed through specific actions.

Sanusi now turns to a topic often confused with miracles: sihr (magic). While both involve extraordinary events, the source and purpose differ fundamentally. This lesson unpacks his concise definition, which identifies four key elements that distinguish sihr from divine acts.

Guided Path

Read the Arabic definition carefully
Start by reading the Arabic sentence slowly. Notice its compact structure: it begins by announcing the topic, then gives the definition with four parts joined by 'min' and 'bi'.
The definition is one sentence, but each prepositional phrase introduces a distinct component.

Identify the four components
Break the definition into its parts: (1) it is an extraordinary matter (khariq lil-'adah), (2) from an evil, wicked soul (nafs sharīra khabītha), (3) by undertaking specific actions (a'māl makhsūsa), (4) which can be learned and taught (ta'allum wa ta'līm).

Contrast with miracles

The 'evil soul' element is the key differentiator.
4
Reflect on learnability
Note that sihr is said to involve learning and teaching. This means it is not innate but can be acquired—making it different from innate gifts or divine grants.
This also implies that sihr is a real, knowable art, not mere superstition.
