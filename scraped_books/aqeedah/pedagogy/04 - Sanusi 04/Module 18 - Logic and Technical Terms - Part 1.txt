Distinguishing Praise and Thanksgiving
Sanusi defines hamd and shukr and compares them in specificity and generality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between hamd and shukr in terms of their loci and objects, and how they relate to intrinsic and transitive virtues.

Core Idea
Hamd is praise with the tongue for any virtue; shukr is gratitude for blessings expressed by tongue, heart, or limbs.

Sanusi begins by clarifying two central terms of Islamic theology: praise (hamd) and thanksgiving (shukr). While often used interchangeably in everyday language, he shows how they differ in scope. This distinction is essential for understanding acts of worship and how we respond to Allah's attributes and blessings.

Guided Path
1
Read the definitions of hamd and shukr
Read the Arabic definitions carefully. Note that hamd is defined as praise with speech, and shukr as praise in return for blessings, with or without speech.
The author considers one definition of hamd better because it includes both eternal and temporal praise.
2
Compare hamd and shukr
Focus on the sentence that compares them. Hamd is more specific in place (only tongue) but more general in object (any virtue). Shukr is the opposite: more general in place but more specific in object (only blessings).
Think of examples: praising Allah for His attributes (hamd) vs. thanking Him for a meal (shukr).
3
Learn the terms fada'il and fawadil
These terms classify the objects of praise. Fada'il are intrinsic virtues (e.g., knowledge), fawadil are transitive benefits (e.g., generosity). This helps understand why hamd is more general in object.

Poetic Proof for Generality of Thanksgiving

Core Idea
A poet's words can be evidence even without explicitly saying 'shukr'.

After learning about the definition of shukr, Sanusi presents a proof from poetic tradition. This passage demonstrates how the imams used a verse to argue that shukr can be expressed by hand, tongue, and heart. It also shows how an apparent objection is resolved through logical inference.

The True Meaning of Shukr and Its Opposite Kufran
Sanusi defines shukr linguistically and explains its opposite, noting the deficiency of heart-only gratitude.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Gratitude is spreading blessings; ingratitude is hiding them.

Sanusi opens a key theological term: shukr (gratitude). He defines it linguistically as spreading and manifesting a blessing, and its opposite kufran as concealment. This passage clarifies that heartfelt gratitude alone is insufficient—it must be expressed outwardly, or it becomes a form of concealment. However, the tongue's praise only has value if it originates from the heart.

Two Meanings of the Definite Article in 'Al-Hamd'
Sanusi analyzes two meanings of the definite article in 'al-hamd'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two possible grammatical functions of the definite article in 'al-hamd' and their shared theological conclusion that all praise belongs exclusively to Allah.

Core Idea
The definite article in 'al-hamd' reveals that all praise, in every form, belongs to Allah alone.

Sanusi delves into the opening word of the Fatiha, 'al-hamd', to uncover its precise meaning. The definite article here is not trivial; it carries two possible interpretations, each leading to the same profound truth: all praise is ultimately Allah's. This lesson unpacks these interpretations and their implications.

The author then lists four logical possibilities for the flow of praise: Allah praising Himself by Himself, Himself for His action, His action for Himself, or His action for another action. In all cases, the praise is ultimately attributed to Allah. The second possibility is that the definite article indicates the essence (al-haqiqa), meaning the specific nature of praise is uniquely His. Both interpretations lead to the same conclusion: all praise is exclusively Allah's.

Suspension on the Additional Entity and Its Proof of Temporality
Sanusi discusses the additional entity and the soundest position of suspension.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sanusi recommends suspension (waqf) regarding the existence of an additional entity beyond atoms and accidents, and how he provides a conditional proof for its temporality.

Core Idea
Suspension of judgment is the soundest stance when proofs for both sides are weak.

Sanusi addresses a debated question among theologians and philosophers: Is there an entity beyond the material world of atoms and their attributes? After noting that the proofs confining the world to atoms are weak, he argues that the correct position is to suspend judgment—neither affirming nor denying. He then offers a conditional proof that if such an entity exists, it must be temporally originated, based on the uniqueness and eternality of God.

Guided Path
1

This sets the stage for why a middle position might be needed.
2

Sanusi says the mutakallimūn's proofs for confining the world are weak, and the philosophers' proofs for the additional entity are invalid. Neither side is conclusive.
3

The truth is to refrain (yūqaf) from affirming or denying the additional entity definitively. This is the position of suspension (waqf).
Waqf here does not mean doubt about God, but about this specific entity.
4
Follow the conditional proof
Sanusi argues that if the entity exists, it cannot be God (by proof of unity); therefore, by Sunnah and consensus, it must be temporally originated. This proof does not rely on prior knowledge of the entity.
The proof is conditional on the existence of the entity, not on its affirmation.

Sanusi identifies a deep-rooted psychological obstacle — the false imagination (al-wahm al-kādhb) that cannot conceive of a non-bodily existent. This passage shows how this flawed assumption corrupts theological reasoning, driving some to liken God to creation and others to deny Him entirely. The Created Power: Non-Effect and Limited Scope
Sanusi clarifies the non-effective nature of the created power and its limited scope, refuting misconceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the created power cannot have any causal effect, how it resembles knowledge, and why it relates only to some possibles.

Core Idea
The created power is like knowledge: it relates but does not create.

Having defined kasb, Sanusi now clarifies the nature of the created power. He shows that the correct understanding, based on the Sunna and consensus of the Salaf, is that this power has no effect—just as knowledge has no effect on its object. He also notes that this power is not universal; it only relates to certain acts. This lesson unpacks these important clarifications.

Guided Path
1
Understand the need for clarification
Sanusi explains why clarification is necessary: rational proof shows God creates all things, yet religious texts attribute actions to humans. Many people misunderstand kasb to imply that the created power has effect.
This sets up the problem of apparent conflict between reason and revelation.
2
Contrast correct view with misunderstanding
Sanusi states that the correct view, according to the Sunna and Salaf, is that kasb is accompaniment without any effect. He rejects the notion that the created power has any influence.
He uses strong language: 'لا يصح غيره' – no other view is valid.
3
Grasp the analogy with knowledge
Sanusi compares the created power to knowledge: just as knowledge of a thing does not bring it into existence, power over a thing does not create it. Both are attributes that relate but do not effect.
This analogy clarifies how an attribute can be real without being causal.
4
Note the scope limitation
Finally, Sanusi states that the created power is not related to all possible things, but only to some. It is limited in scope.
This prevents overgeneralization and emphasizes divine control over which acts are within human capacity.

Hisba Across Madhhabs: A Hanafi-Shafi'i Example
Example of cross-madhhab hisba from al-Muhit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the duty of hisba applies between followers of different madhhabs, using a specific example from the Hanafi legal tradition.

Core Idea
Hisba can cross madhhab lines on clear rulings, not on pure ijtihad.

This lesson presents a concrete example from the Hanafi compendium 'al-Muhit' that illustrates the scope of hisba across madhhabs. The passage shows that a Hanafi may object to a Shafi'i on certain matters, and a Shafi'i may object to a Hanafi on others, as long as the issue is considered clearly wrong by the objector's own school. This clarifies that the duty of commanding good and forbidding evil is not confined to one's own madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that it names two parties: a Hanafi objecting to a Shafi'i, and a Shafi'i objecting to a Hanafi.
The passage is a single sentence from 'al-Muhit al-Hanafiyya'.
2
Identify the two directions of hisba
List the specific matters for which each madhhab may object. For the Hanafi: eating hyena and omitting basmala. For the Shafi'i: drinking muthallath and marriage without a guardian.
3
Reflect on the underlying principle
Consider why these matters are singled out. The key is that each school considers its own ruling on these issues to be clear and definitive, not open to ijtihad. Hence hisba applies across madhhabs.
This shows that hisba is not limited to one's own school but applies whenever the wrong is clear in the objector's view.

Commentary
This passage presents a practical example from the Hanafi legal tradition. It cites the work 'Muhīṭ al-Ḥanafiyya' to illustrate the scope of hisba between followers of different madhhabs. A Hanafi may object to a Shafiʿi on matters where the Hanafi considers the ruling clear, such as eating hyena (which Hanafis permit but Shafiʿis forbid) and omitting the basmala intentionally (which Hanafis consider valid but Shafiʿis invalid). Conversely, a Shafiʿi may object to a Hanafi on matters like drinking 'muthallath' (a wine-like beverage) and marriage without a guardian (which Shafiʿis require). This shows that the duty of commanding good and forbidding evil applies across madhhabs when the matter is considered clearly wrong by one's own school, but not in matters of pure ijtihad.

Duty to Forbid Evil Does Not Require Personal Perfection
Sanusi explains that the duty to forbid evil remains even if one commits it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the obligation to enjoin good and forbid evil remains binding even on those who commit the same sin, because the duties of personal avoidance and commanding others are separate and independent.

Core Idea
Your sin does not cancel your duty to correct others.

Many people assume that only those who are blameless have the right to advise others. But Sanusi clarifies that this is not the case. In this passage, he explains why a person who commits a sin is still obligated to forbid the same sin in others, because the duties of abstaining and forbidding are separate.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the literal meaning. Then read the translation carefully. Notice the key terms and the flow of the argument.
The passage is short but packed with a clear logical point.
2
Identify the misconception addressed
The passage directly refutes the idea that only the pious can command good and forbid evil. Underline the phrase 'not specific to one who is pious'.
This misconception is common even today.
3
Analyze the reasoning: two distinct duties
Focus on the phrase 'two distinct obligations'. Why does the author say they are distinct? Because failing to fulfill one (i.e., committing the sin) does not release you from the other (forbidding it).
This is the core logical move of the passage.

ثم لا يختص وجوب الأمر بالمعروف والنهي عن المنكر بمَنْ يكون ورعاً لا يرتكب مثلَهُ ، بل مَنْ رأى مُنكَراً وهو مرتكب مثله .. فعليه أن ينهى عنه ؛ لأنَّ تركه للمنكَرِ ونهيه فرضانِ متميزانِ ، ليسَ لَمَنْ تركَ أحدهما أن يترك الآخر (١) . (۱)
Then the obligation of commanding good and forbidding evil is not specific to one who is pious and does not commit the like of it. Rather, whoever sees an evil while himself committing its like, he must forbid it, because his abandoning of the evil and his forbidding it are two distinct obligations; it is not for one who abandons one of them to abandon the other.
Source pages p. 562

Commentary
This passage addresses a common objection: that only those who are perfectly virtuous have the right to command good and forbid evil. The author refutes this by stating that the obligation does not depend on the personal piety of the one commanding. Even if a person is guilty of the same sin he sees, he must still forbid it. The reason given is that abstaining from evil and forbidding it are two separate duties (farḍān mutamayyizān). Failing to fulfill one does not justify abandoning the other. Therefore, a sinner is still obligated to enjoin good and forbid evil.
