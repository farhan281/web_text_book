The Proof of God's Pre-Eternity and Everlastingness
Sanusi proves that the Creator must be eternal and necessarily everlasting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Creator must be both pre-eternal (no beginning) and necessarily everlasting (no end), based on rational arguments that show these attributes are necessary to avoid logical contradictions.

Core Idea
The Creator cannot have a beginning or an end; both would lead to logical absurdities.

In this passage, Sanusi presents the classical kalam argument for God's pre-eternity and everlastingness. He shows that if God had a beginning, He would need a creator, leading to absurdities like infinite regress or circularity. Similarly, if God could cease to exist, He would be contingent, contradicting His necessary existence. We will unpack this proof step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Note the two main claims: pre-eternity (qidam) and everlastingness (baqa').
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Understand the proof for pre-eternity
Sanusi argues that if the Creator had a beginning, He would need an originator, leading to either infinite regress or circular reasoning—both impossible. Therefore, He must be pre-eternal.
This is a reductio ad absurdum argument.
3
Understand the proof for everlastingness
Sanusi then argues that if the Creator could cease to exist, His existence would be contingent, requiring a cause, making Him originated—contradicting His pre-eternity. So He must be necessarily everlasting.
This uses the definition of necessary vs. contingent.

The argument follows the standard kalam method of showing that the absence of these attributes leads to logical impossibilities.

Proof of God's Independence from a Specifier
Sanusi proves God's independence from a specifier using pre-eternity and permanence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God cannot depend on any specifier or agent for His existence, based on the necessity of His pre-eternity and permanence.

Core Idea
God's pre-eternity and permanence logically exclude any need for a specifier.

Sanusi has defined self-subsistence (al-qiyām bi al-nafs) as independence from a place and a specifier. Having already established God's pre-eternity and permanence, he now uses those attributes to prove that God cannot possibly need a specifier. This argument is crucial because it rules out the idea that God's existence could be caused or specified by anything else.

The Eternity of Divine Power
Sanusi gives two proofs for the eternity of divine power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God's power must be eternal and not temporal, and how the impossibility of eternal impotence and infinite regress support this.

Core Idea
God's power is eternal because its opposite would be eternal, and a temporal power would require an endless chain.

After establishing that God is powerful with a distinct attribute, Sanusi now proves that this attribute must be eternal. He gives two arguments: if power were temporal, its opposite (impotence) would be eternal, which contradicts the observed existence of creatures; also, a temporal power would need another power to bring it into existence, leading to an infinite regress. This lesson examines these proofs.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic and English carefully. Note the two separate arguments separated by 'وأيضاً'.
The first argument uses the principle that the eternal does not cease.
2
Understand the first argument (from opposite)
If power were temporal, then before its existence, impotence would be eternal. Since the eternal cannot cease, impotence would never cease, meaning God would never be powerful, but creation shows He is powerful.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Understand the second argument (infinite regress)
If power were temporal, it would need another power to create it, leading to an infinite chain of powers, which is impossible.

The Proof from Comparing Past Events (Flood Argument)
Sanusi presents a clever proof using sets of past events to show an infinite past is impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how comparing two sets of past events demonstrates that the past cannot be infinite.

Core Idea
The flood argument shows an infinite past leads to contradiction.

Sanusi presents a clever logical proof that the past must be finite. By comparing the set of events up to the flood with the set up to now, he shows that an infinite past would require the larger set to be equal to a proper subset, which is impossible. This proof is a classic example of reasoning about infinite sets in kalam.

Guided Path
1
Understand the setup
Identify the two sets: Set A = past events up to the flood, Set B = past events up to now. Note that Set B includes Set A plus extra events.
The flood is an arbitrary example; any point would work.
2
Align the ends
Place the end of both sets at the present moment. Then consider the other ends (the beginnings). There are two possibilities: either the beginnings match or they don't.
The comparison is between the two starting points once the ends are aligned.
3
Analyze the two possibilities
If no difference appears at the start, then Set A and Set B have the same size, which is impossible because Set B is larger. If a difference appears, then Set A must have a beginning, making it finite, and Set B is finite as well because it adds only a finite amount.
Both possibilities lead to the conclusion that the past is finite.
4
Draw the conclusion
Since the past must be finite, any assumption of an eternal or temporal impediment (like an infinite chain of events) is impossible.
This proves that the world must have a beginning.

Commentary
This passage is a proof by contradiction refuting the hypothetical possibility of two weak deities cooperating to create a single indivisible atom (jawhar fard). He then analyzes the two logical possibilities for the weak power of each god upon meeting to create: either each has some effect, or neither has any effect.

Therefore, the combined power of the two gods has no effect, meaning each god is individually incapable (ʿājiz). But incapability contradicts divinity, as established earlier in the section on God's attributes. Thus, this hypothesis leads to affirming two incapable beings—which is absurd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument from difference (tamānuʿ) that proves the impossibility of two gods, based on the necessity of mutual incapacity when they will opposites.

Core Idea
Two gods willing opposites leads to both being incapable—contradicting divinity.

Sanusi presents a powerful logical argument known as the 'argument from mutual prevention' (tamānuʿ). By considering a concrete scenario where two supposed gods will opposite actions for the same body, he demonstrates that any outcome leads to incapacity in at least one, and by the principle of similitude, both. This argument is foundational in proving the oneness of God.

Since the attribute of divinity requires omnipotence, any being with that attribute must be able to act on everything. If there are two such beings, each must have universal power, which leads to conflict in any single thing. The tamānuʿ argument shows that they cannot both exercise universal power, so each is incapable of some things, contradicting divinity. Therefore, each is generally incapable of anything at all, eternally. The author clarifies that the earlier discussion of agreement and disagreement was only to expose this necessary general incapacity; it is not that they become incapable only in those specific scenarios. The final conclusion is that the supposition of two gods necessarily entails their incapacity and denial of divinity, and consequently, nothing of the world would exist (since creation requires omnipotence).

Knowing God: Obligation and the Role of Reason
Ibn Dihaq argues knowledge of God is obligatory and distinguishes necessary vs. acquired knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that knowledge of God's existence is rationally necessary and that further divine attributes require sound reflection on proofs.

Core Idea
Knowledge of God starts with reason, but faith deepens through reflection.

This passage from Ibn Dihaq's commentary establishes why we can know God. It argues that God obligates us to know Him, and such knowledge must be possible. The author then distinguishes between necessary knowledge of God's existence and acquired knowledge through reflection, setting the stage for proper theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation to grasp the argument that knowledge of God is an obligation and thus possible.
Notice the use of the Quranic verse as support.
2
Identify the two types of knowledge
The passage distinguishes between knowledge of God's existence, which is innate to reason, and knowledge of further attributes, which requires reflection.
3
Understand the necessity of reflection
The author says that people differ on matters that require sound reflection on decisive proofs. This shows that reason alone is not enough for all knowledge of God.
4
Reflect on the obligation
Consider why impossible knowledge would be an unjust burden. This connects to divine justice.

Commentary
This passage is an excerpt from Ibn Dihaq's commentary on al-Irshad, presenting a kalam argument for the possibility of knowing God and identifying sources of error. It begins by stating that we are commanded to know God; if such knowledge were impossible, it would be an impossible obligation, which God does not impose. Knowledge of God is twofold: necessary knowledge of His existence (innate to reason) and acquired knowledge of further attributes through sound reflection on decisive proofs. The author then states that error among the ignorant arises from three ways, but only two are given in this excerpt. The first way is misunderstanding the manner of creation: atheists (mulḥida) think creation issues from God as a necessary effect from a cause, which leads to either the effect being eternal (like its cause) or the cause being temporal (like its effect). The correct view is that creation is by choice (ikhtiyār) and power (iqtidār). The second way is the mistaken notion that a being cannot exist if it is neither inside nor outside the world, leading to anthropomorphism (tajsīm). The passage thus defends divine transcendence and the necessity of creation being by will, not necessary emanation.

Avoiding Error in Understanding God's Action and Nature
Ibn Dihaq explains two theological errors: necessary emanation and anthropomorphism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two common errors in theology: believing that creation is a necessary emanation from God, and anthropomorphizing God due to a misunderstanding of His relation to the world.

Core Idea
God creates by choice, not necessity; He is beyond inside and outside.

In this continuation, Ibn Dihaq identifies two ways people go wrong in understanding God. The first is thinking that creation issues from God necessarily, like an effect from a cause. The second is imagining that a being must be either inside or outside the world, leading to anthropomorphism. Both errors are corrected by proper understanding of divine transcendence and will.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first error
Read about the atheists' view that creation is a necessary effect from God as a cause.
This is called 'necessary emanation'.
2
Understand the contradiction
If creation is necessary, either the world would be eternal (like God) or God would be temporal (like the world), both unacceptable.
3
The correct view
The world's issuance from God is by choice and power, preserving God's eternity and world's temporality.
4
Identify the second error
The mistaken notion that a being must be either inside or outside the world.
This leads to anthropomorphism (tajsīm).
5
The correct view
God is transcendent, not confined by space. He is neither inside nor outside.

Two Types of Knowledge of God
Sanusi classifies knowledge of God into two types and introduces three errors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between knowledge of God's existence (attained by necessary reason) and deeper knowledge requiring rational proof.

Core Idea
Knowing that God exists is universal; knowing what He is requires proof.

Sanusi begins by affirming that knowledge of God comes in two levels: a basic certainty shared by all rational people, and a deeper understanding that demands evidence. This foundational distinction sets the stage for critiquing those who err in their understanding of God.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening classification
Start by reading Sanusi's statement that knowledge of God is of two types. Notice how he immediately associates the first type with the necessity of reason.
The phrase 'ضرورة العقل' (necessity of reason) is a key term.
2
Identify the first type
The first type is knowledge of God's existence. Sanusi claims this is known by the necessity of reason, meaning no rational person genuinely denies it. Reflect on why he says 'ما رُبيَ في العقلاء' (no one raised among the rational) denies God.
3
Identify the second type
The second type is knowledge that requires additional reflection (النظر) and correct proofs (الأدلة القاطعة). This includes understanding God's attributes and actions beyond mere existence.
This sets up the need for kalam arguments.
4
Note the transition to errors
Sanusi then introduces the three ways of error among those ignorant of the truth. This bridges to the detailed critique in the following lessons.
The classification of errors shows why deeper knowledge is necessary.

Commentary
This passage enumerates and critiques errors in the knowledge of God. It begins by distinguishing two types: knowledge of God's existence (attained by necessary reason, as no rational person denies a Creator) and deeper knowledge requiring proof. Sanusi then classifies the errors of those ignorant of truth into three ways. The first error is that of atheists who view creation as necessary emanation from God, which implies either eternity of the world or temporality of God—both contradictions. The correct view is that creation issues from God by choice and power. The second error is anthropomorphism, stemming from the false assumption that a being cannot exist without location, leading to attributing bodily parts to God. They cling to scriptural terms (hand, face) without understanding their linguistic usage and neglect the proof of dependence that demonstrates creation's temporality. Had they reflected, they would see such attributes entail temporality and incapacity. The third error is polytheism and multiplicity of gods, which Sanusi notes has four types (though not fully enumerated here). Throughout, Sanusi emphasizes that proper knowledge of God requires inferential proof, not mere verbal acknowledgment.

First Error: Atheists and Emanation
Sanusi critiques the atheistic view of necessary emanation and presents the correct view of voluntary creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the atheistic view that creation emanates necessarily from God is contradictory, and why the correct view is creation by choice.

Core Idea
Creation cannot be a necessary emanation; it must be voluntary.

Sanusi now details the first of three errors: the atheists' claim that the world proceeded from God as an effect from a cause. He argues this leads to absurd consequences and contrasts it with the correct understanding of voluntary creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the first error description
Begin with 'وخرج الغلط...' and read how Sanusi presents the error of the atheists (الملحدة). Note their claim: creation issues from God like a necessary effect.
The term 'الملحدة' refers to those who deviate from correct belief, often implying atheists or materialists.
2
Analyze the contradiction
Sanusi points out that if creation is a necessary effect of God, then either the effect is eternal (if cause is eternal) or the cause is temporal (if effect is temporal). Both are untenable. Work through this logic.
This is a classic kalam argument against emanationism.
3
Identify the correct view
Once one understands that the world is temporal (hadith) and God is eternal (qadim), the only coherent position is that creation occurs by choice and power (ikhtiyar wa iqtidar).
This affirms God's free will and omnipotence.

Commentary
This passage enumerates and critiques errors in the knowledge of God. It begins by distinguishing two types: knowledge of God's existence (attained by necessary reason, as no rational person denies a Creator) and deeper knowledge requiring proof. Sanusi then classifies the errors of those ignorant of truth into three ways. The first error is that of atheists who view creation as necessary emanation from God, which implies either eternity of the world or temporality of God—both contradictions. The correct view is that creation issues from God by choice and power. The second error is anthropomorphism, stemming from the false assumption that a being cannot exist without location, leading to attributing bodily parts to God. They cling to scriptural terms (hand, face) without understanding their linguistic usage and neglect the proof of dependence that demonstrates creation's temporality. Had they reflected, they would see such attributes entail temporality and incapacity. The third error is polytheism and multiplicity of gods, which Sanusi notes has four types (though not fully enumerated here). Throughout, Sanusi emphasizes that proper knowledge of God requires inferential proof, not mere verbal acknowledgment.

Second and Third Errors: Anthropomorphism and Polytheism
Sanusi critiques anthropomorphism and polytheism as errors rooted in ignorance of proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why anthropomorphism (attributing bodily parts to God) is an error and how the proof of dependence refutes it, and that polytheism is the third error.

Core Idea
Although God's hand, face, etc., are mentioned in scripture, they cannot be taken literally; the proof of dependence shows God is not like creation.

Sanusi now addresses two further errors: those who anthropomorphize God based on literal readings of scripture, and those who fall into polytheism. He emphasizes that proper inference from the proof of dependence (dalīl al-iftiqār) corrects these mistakes.

Guided Path
1
Read the second error
Start with 'والطريق الثاني...' and note how Sanusi describes the error of anthropomorphism. They claim a being cannot exist without being inside or outside the world, so they attribute location to God.
This is a philosophical argument against transcendent existence.
2
Understand the scriptural misuse
Sanusi criticizes them for clinging to literal words (hand, face, eye) without understanding their linguistic meanings or using the proof of dependence.
The proof of dependence shows all contingent things require a cause; God is not contingent.
3
Reflect on the proof of dependence
If they had used this proof, they would realize that attributing bodily features would make God temporal and incapable like other creatures.
This is a key kalam argument for tanzih (transcendence).
4
Note the third error: polytheism
Sanusi briefly mentions the third error as polytheism and multiplicity of gods, stating it has four types. This concludes the classification.
The passage does not elaborate on the four types, leaving them for further study.

Prophets' Miracles: Beyond Magic and Trickery
Sanusi argues that prophets' miracles are genuine and distinct from magic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof that prophets' miracles are genuine divine acts, distinct from any human trickery, as argued by Imam al-Sanusi.

Core Idea
Prophets' miracles are necessarily divine, not magic, because enemies could not expose any trickery.

How can we be certain that the extraordinary acts of prophets are truly from God and not mere magic or trickery? Imam al-Sanusi presents a compelling rational argument that the miracles of the prophets are beyond any human capability and definitively prove their truthfulness. This lesson will walk through the key points of this argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the translation. Notice how Sanusi builds his argument step by step.
The passage begins with a rhetorical question to engage the reader.
2
Understand the core argument
Sanusi argues that prophets performed extraordinary acts that cannot be attained by any trick, magic, or deep knowledge. If they could be, it would be impossible for prophets alone to perform them throughout history.
The examples given include reviving the dead and splitting the sea.
3
Note the evidence from the Prophet's illiteracy
Sanusi cites Quran 29:48, which states that the Prophet Muhammad was not literate before revelation. This removes the possibility that the Quran came from human learning.
This is a specific proof for the Prophet's truthfulness.
4
Consider the role of enemies in verification
Sanusi points out that enemies and jealous people would eagerly search for any trick or flaw. Their failure to expose anything is strong evidence that the miracles are genuine.
Custom (ʿāda) makes it impossible for such a thing to remain hidden.
5
Arrive at the conclusion
The conclusion is that the truthfulness of all messengers is known by necessity (ḍarūra) to every person whom God grants success. This is not just inference but direct compelling knowledge.
The phrase 'by necessity' means it is self-evident to those who reflect.

Source pages p. 448

Commentary
This passage is a rational proof that the miracles (mu'jizat) performed by the prophets are genuinely divine and cannot be imitated by magic or human skill. The author begins by stating that God supported the prophets with extraordinary events that are definitively known to be unattainable by any trick of magic or deep knowledge in fields like medicine. Examples include reviving the dead and parting the sea. The argument is that if such acts could be achieved by trickery, it would be impossible for them to be exclusively performed by prophets throughout history. Additionally, it is a matter of necessary knowledge that the prophets were completely removed from the sciences that might produce such things. The author then cites Qur'an 29:48, which confirms that the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) was not literate, further removing any suspicion of human origin. The proof continues by noting that both allies and opponents acknowledge this. Furthermore, the enemies of the prophets, driven by jealousy and hatred, would eagerly investigate and expose any trickery; the fact that they could not find any evidence of fraud is itself proof of authenticity. The conclusion is that the truthfulness of all messengers is known by necessity (darura) to every person whom God grants success. This is not a mere inference but a direct, compelling knowledge.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some people think that prophets' miracles could be mere magic or advanced human trickery.
Correction: Sanusi argues that miracles are definitively beyond any trick, as shown by the uniqueness of prophets in performing them, their ignorance of such arts, and the inability of enemies to expose any fraud.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
After reading that the truthfulness of the messengers is known necessarily to every successful person, ask yourself: Do you know it as a necessity, or is it just a belief you hold? What might be blocking you from that certainty? Examine your heart and commit to removing any doubt by studying the proofs more deeply.
?
The passage says enemies and the envious scrutinized the prophets but could find no fault. Is your own scrutiny of the prophets and their message led by a desire for truth or by something else? If you have ever doubted, have you honestly investigated the evidence as the enemies did? Make a firm intention today to search for truth with sincerity, not with a preconceived rejection.
?
The Qur'anic verse about the unlettered Prophet Muhammad (29:48) is presented as a proof that the Quran is not from human sources. Does this remove any lingering doubt you have about the divine origin of the Quran? If not, what more do you need to be convinced? Be honest with yourself and take a step towards certainty by reflecting on the miracle of the Quran in your own life.
