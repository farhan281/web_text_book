Sanusi enumerates the three rational judgments and defines each with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three categories of rational judgment that underpin all discussions in kalam.

Core Idea
Every theological question hinges on whether something is necessary, impossible, or possible.

Sanusi begins by establishing the foundational categories of rational judgment. Before we can reason about God or creation, we must know the three possible ways the mind can assess any proposition: necessary, impossible, or possible. This short passage lays the groundwork for every argument that follows in the study of kalam.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and definition of the three rational judgments. Sanusi states that rational judgments are confined to three types: necessity (wujub), impossibility (istihala), and possibility (jawaz). He then defines each. The necessary (wajib) is that whose non-existence is inconceivable to the mind; for example, a substance (jawhar) necessarily occupies space (tahayyuz). In kalam, substances always have location, so their occupation of space is necessary. The impossible (mustahil) is that whose existence is inconceivable; for example, a substance being devoid of both motion and rest is impossible because a substance must have either motion or rest. The possible (ja'iz) is that whose existence and non-existence are both conceivable; for example, the death of one of us today or tomorrow – it is neither necessary nor impossible, but possible. Sanusi emphasizes that these three categories are the pivot (madar) of all kalam discussions, meaning every theological question will involve determining whether something is necessary, impossible, or possible with respect to God or creation.

The Three Rational Judgments
Sanusi enumerates the three rational judgments with definitions and examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three categories of rational judgment in kalam and how they form the foundation of theological reasoning.

Core Idea
Rational judgments are three: necessary, impossible, and contingent.

Before diving into theological arguments, a kalam student must first grasp the basic categories of rational judgment. Imam Sanusi opens his discussion with a clear classification: every rational claim falls into one of three types. These three categories are the building blocks of all subsequent proofs about God and the world.

Commentary
This passage classifies the rational judgments into three fundamental categories that underlie all discussions in 'ilm al-kalam. The author begins by stating that the rational judgment is limited to three types: obligation, impossibility, and possibility. These three are the central pivot of kalam topics.

1. The necessary (al-wajib): Defined as that whose non-existence is inconceivable by the intellect. An example given is the occupation of space (al-tahayyuz) for a substance. That is, it is rationally necessary that any substance occupies space; we cannot conceive a substance without spatial extension.

2. The impossible (al-mustahil): Defined as that whose existence is inconceivable by the intellect. The example is a body being devoid of both movement and rest. It is rationally impossible for a body to have neither motion nor rest; it must be either moving or at rest per se.

3. The contingent (al-ja'iz): Defined as that whose existence and non-existence are both rationally sound. The example is the death of a particular person today or tomorrow; both possibilities are rationally acceptable.

These three judgments are the basic categories used in kalam to describe God (wajib al-wujud), attributes of God (wajib), and the world (ja'iz, sometimes mustahil).

Definition and Proof of the Permissible in God's Acts
Sanusi defines the permissible regarding God and proves non-obligation of benefit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of the permissible (al-ja'iz) regarding God's actions and the proof that God is not obliged to observe benefit or what is most beneficial.

Core Idea
God is not obliged to create the best possible world; His actions are free and gracious.

In this passage, Sanusi introduces the third rational category—the permissible (al-ja'iz)—as it applies to God's own acts. He clarifies that nothing is obligatory upon God, and then provides a concise argument: if God were bound to create what is most beneficial, then trials, commands, and prohibitions would not exist. This sets the stage for understanding divine freedom and grace.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the compact style: Sanusi packs both definition and proof into a single sentence.
2
Identify the definition of al-ja'iz
Find the phrase starting with 'وأما الجائز'. The definition is: every act of God such that nothing is obligatory upon Him, nor is He required to observe benefit or the most beneficial.
3
Understand the proof
The proof is a conditional: 'otherwise (if He were obliged) there would have occurred no trial... nor any command or prohibition.' Since trials and commands do exist, the obligation must be false.
This is a proof by contradiction (burhan al-tamanu').
4
Reflect on the theological implication
Consider why this matters: it upholds God's transcendence and freedom. He is not compelled by any external consideration; His actions are pure grace.
This refutes the Mu'tazilite doctrine that God must create the best (al-aslah).

Commentary
This passage defines the concept of 'al-ja'iz' (the permissible) with regards to God's actions. Sanusi begins by stating that he is explaining the proof that God is not obliged to observe what is beneficial (salah) or most beneficial (aslah) for creatures. Hence the obligation is impossible, and God's actions are purely by His choice and grace.

The Syllogistic Proof of Shukr
Sanusi answers an objection by producing a meaningful inference from the three modes of gratitude.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi uses a syllogism in the first figure to prove that using the heart, tongue, and limbs for gratitude is linguistically called shukr.

Core Idea
Shukr is not just a word; it's a logical conclusion from evidence.

Sanusi has established that shukr (gratitude) encompasses three modes: heart, tongue, and limbs. Now he answers an objection by demonstrating that calling these three actions 'shukr' is not arbitrary but follows from a valid syllogism. This passage presents a subtle logical inference that every student of kalam should memorize, as it grounds a linguistic claim in rational proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the translation. Notice that the author is giving an answer (يجاب) to an objection, and that he calls the inference 'meaningful' (معنوي) and 'subtle' (ظريف).
The passage is short but dense. Take your time.
2
Identify the syllogism's parts
Break the argument into its premises and conclusion. The author mentions two premises: (1) every customary recompense for a blessing is linguistically shukr, and (2) using the three (heart, tongue, limbs) is a customary recompense. The conclusion is: using the three is linguistically shukr.
This structure is known as the 'first figure' of a syllogism.
3
Reflect on why it is 'subtle'
The author calls this inference 'subtle' (ظريف) and urges memorization. Reflect on why it is clever: it transforms a linguistic observation into a logical necessity. Understanding this helps appreciate how kalam uses logic to defend doctrine.

The Circularity Argument Against Taqlid
Sanusi refutes the claim that taqlid is valid from the hadith of faith by demonstrating a circular dependency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why taqlid (imitative belief) cannot be a valid basis for the fundamental articles of faith, because it leads to a logical circularity that prevents knowledge from being established.

Core Idea
Taqlid in faith creates a vicious circle: you need to know God before you can trust the Prophet.

Some argue that the hadith defining faith by certain traits supports the validity of taqlid. However, upon closer examination, this argument collapses into a logical fallacy. Imam Sanusi shows that if we rely on taqlid for the truths of faith, we would have to imitate the Prophet himself—but imitating the Prophet presupposes that we have already proven his prophethood, which itself depends on knowing God and His attributes. This creates a circular dependency, proving that taqlid is impossible for fundamental beliefs.

The Three Rational Judgments: Wajib, Mustahil, Ja'iz
Sanusi defines the three rational judgments and their centrality to kalām.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definitions of the three rational judgments (wajib, mustahil, ja'iz) and why they are the foundational principles of kalam.

Core Idea
All kalam arguments hinge on three rational judgments: necessary, impossible, and possible.

Before diving into proofs about Allah and creation, Sanusi insists we master the basic conceptual tools. Here he lays out the three rational judgments that underpin every discussion in kalam. Understanding these definitions is the first step to sound theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation. Notice how Sanusi first announces the three categories, then defines each with an example, then explains their importance.
The examples are crucial—they ground abstract definitions in concrete physical reality.
2
Identify the three categories
Find the three terms: wājib, mustaḥīl, jāʾiz. Note their definitions: wājib is that whose non-existence cannot be conceived; mustaḥīl is that whose existence cannot be conceived; jāʾiz is that whose existence and non-existence are both conceivable.
Contrast 'cannot be conceived' with 'cannot happen physically'—this is a rational, not empirical, judgment.
3
Study the examples
Sanusi gives examples: for wājib, a substance occupying space; for mustaḥīl, a substance devoid of motion and rest; for jāʾiz, a person's death today or tomorrow. Explain why each example fits its category.
These examples use the physical world to clarify abstract concepts.
4
Understand why these are mabādiʾ
Sanusi states that these three judgments are principles of kalam because the mutakallim's goal is to affirm or negate one of them, or affirm something branching from them. Reflect on how every theological claim involves one of these judgments.
For example, 'Allah exists' is wājib; 'Allah is unjust' is mustaḥīl; 'I will be saved' is jāʾiz.
5
Avoid the common misconception
The common error is to confuse 'necessary' (wājib) with 'always physically inevitable.' But Sanusi defines it intellectually: what cannot be rationally denied. Note the difference.
For example, the laws of physics are not wājib in this sense—they could be otherwise rationally.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and division of the rational judgment 'necessary' (wājib). It takes as its starting point a definition of wājib: 'that whose non-existence is inconceivable in the intellect'. The author then clarifies that 'inconceivable' means the intellect does not perceive its non-existence. This non-perception can occur in two ways. First, necessarily (ḍarūrī): the intellect immediately sees that the judgment is necessary without any reflection. The example given is a substance's occupation of space (taḥayyuz al-jawhar), meaning it takes up an amount of void. The necessity of this attribute for a substance is instinctively known. Second, through theoretical reasoning (naẓarī): the intellect needs to reflect to reach the judgment. The example is the pre-eternity (qidam) of God. One reasons: if God were originated (ḥādith), He would need an originator, leading to an infinite regress (tasalsul) or a circle (dawr), both impossible; therefore origination is impossible for Him, so pre-eternity is necessary, as there is no intermediate state. Thus wājib is divided into two kinds: necessary (ḍarūrī) and theoretical (naẓarī). The author then states that the same division applies to the other two rational judgments: impossible (mumtaniʿ) and contingent (jāʾiz). This sets up the next lesson.

Commentary
This passage enumerates the four foundational principles (arkān) upon which the conclusive proof for the world's origination rests. Thus the four pillars ensure that the logical structure of the proof is sound: bodies have an inseparable accident, that accident is originated, so bodies must be originated as well, provided that an infinite regress of originated accidents is impossible.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The proof for the world's temporality rests on four pillars, starting with establishing accidents in bodies.

Sanusi begins by outlining the foundational pillars of the famous argument for the temporal origination of the world. The Fourth Pillar: Refuting Infinite Regress
Sanusi explains the necessity of the fourth pillar and refutes the objection of an infinite regress of accidents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the fourth pillar—the impossibility of an infinite regress of events—is necessary to complete the proof for the world's temporal origination.

Core Idea
Even if accidents are temporal, an infinite regress of them would allow a body to be eternal—so the fourth pillar blocks this.

After establishing the first three pillars, Sanusi introduces the opponent's objection: accidents might be temporally originated but have no beginning. This would allow the world to be eternal. The fourth pillar, the impossibility of an infinite regress of events, is needed to refute this. This lesson explains that objection and the reasoning behind the fourth pillar.

2
Understand the opponent's objection
The opponent agrees that accidents are temporally originated but argues they may have no beginning—i.e., an infinite series. If so, the body could be eternal while always having accidents.
This objection threatens the entire proof, because the third pillar alone doesn't guarantee that the series of accidents has a starting point.
3
See how the fourth pillar resolves it
Sanusi uses the example of the celestial sphere: if its motions have no beginning, its temporal origination does not follow. Only by establishing that an infinite regress is impossible can we conclude that the series must have a first event, thus forcing the body to be temporal.
The fourth pillar is the principle 'impossibility of events without a beginning' (استحالة حوادث لا أوَّلَ لها).

Four Sub-Principles for the Second Principle

Core Idea
Four refutations are needed to prove accidents are created.

Proof of the Second and Third Principles: Accidents Cannot Subsist by Themselves or Transfer
Sanusi proves accidents cannot be self-subsistent or transfer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational arguments proving that accidents cannot be self-subsistent nor transfer between substances.

Core Idea
Accidents cannot stand alone or move; their very definition forbids it.

Sanusi now proves two key principles about accidents: they cannot subsist by themselves, and they cannot transfer from one substance to another. These proofs rely on the very definition of an accident and the absurdity of infinite regress. The arguments are concise but foundational for kalam metaphysics.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Begin by reading the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the literal translation to grasp the flow of the argument.
Notice the compact style of Sanusi; each clause carries weight.
2
Identify the two arguments
The passage contains two distinct proofs: a definitional argument (reversal of essence) and a regress argument (tasalsul). Find where each begins.
The definitional argument applies to both self-subsistence and transference; the regress argument specifically targets transference.

This shows that accidents depend on substances for their very definition.

If an accident like motion transferred, then that transference would itself be an accident requiring another transference, leading to an infinite chain. This is impossible.
Regress is avoided by denying the initial transference.

The proof of temporality does not require refuting every possible state of accidents.

Sanusi here addresses a potential objection to the standard proof for the temporality of bodies. The objection claims that the proof relies on refuting three specific states of accidents. Sanusi explains that the core proof is self-sufficient and that the refutation of those states, while perhaps helpful, is not logically required. He then clarifies a key phrase that might have been misunderstood.

The key distinction is between the accident's own existence and the state of the body.

Thus, refuting the three states (subsistence in itself, transmigration, quiescence/manifestation) is not essential for the proof. If someone insists it is, Sanusi notes that the creed already provides guidance on those issues.
