Authority of the Ruler in Armed Conflict
Sanusi explains ruler's authority in armed conflict and quotes Imam al-Haramayn on knowledge levels.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why authority to wage armed conflict in commanding good and forbidding evil rests with the ruler, and how Imam al-Haramayn distinguishes between general and specific knowledge in this context.

Core Idea
Armed conflict in enjoining good is the ruler's domain, not the individual's.

This passage introduces a critical limit on the duty of commanding good and forbidding evil: when it escalates to armed confrontation, authority transfers to the ruler to avoid civil strife. Imam al-Haramayn's quote then clarifies who may enjoin or forbid based on the nature of the ruling—general knowledge or specific ijtihad—and forbids mujtahids from rebuking each other on valid disagreements.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation to grasp the flow from the general rule to the detailed quote.
2
Understand the ruler's authority
Note the condition: when it comes to setting up fighting and drawing weapons, it is tied to the ruler to prevent fitna. This is the main point of the first part.
Fitna means civil strife or chaos.
3
Distinguish general vs. specific knowledge
Imam al-Haramayn distinguishes between rulings that are equally perceptible to public and elite (where everyone may enjoin/forbid) versus those requiring ijtihad (where only mujtahids may act). Identify these two categories.
4
Learn about mujtahid disagreement
The quote further states that a mujtahid should not rebuke another mujtahid on a matter of disagreement, because each is correct according to the Ash'ari view.
This underscores tolerance in ijtihad.

But if the matter reaches the point of setting up fighting and drawing weapons, it is tied to the ruler, out of caution against fitna. Thus Imam al-Haramayn mentioned it, and he said: 'If a legal ruling is equally perceptible to the general public and the elite, then the general public and others have the right to enjoin good and forbid evil concerning it. But if its perception is specific to ijtihad, then the general public have no command or prohibition in it; rather the matter is referred to the people of ijtihad. Moreover, a mujtahid may not object with rebuke and reprimand to another mujtahid in a matter of disagreement, because every mujtahid in subsidiary matters is correct according to us, and whoever says that the correct opinion is one, then it is not determined in his view.'

Commentary
This passage introduces a crucial qualification: when the action of enjoining good and forbidding evil escalates to the point of armed confrontation (nasb al-qital) and drawing weapons (shahr al-silah), authority reverts to the ruler (sultan) to avoid fitna (civil strife). The author then quotes Imam al-Haramayn, who distinguishes between rulings that are generally known (like basic obligations) – in which both commoners and specialists may act – and rulings that require ijtihad. In the latter, only qualified mujtahids may command or forbid. Furthermore, one mujtahid may not rebuke another on a point of legitimate disagreement, as each is correct in his ijtihad according to the Ash'ari position (that every mujtahid is correct in furu'). Even those who hold that there is only one correct opinion cannot determine it definitively.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That individuals can take up arms against rulers in the name of commanding good.
Correction: The passage explicitly states that when it comes to armed conflict, authority is tied to the ruler to avoid fitna.

Key Takeaway
Armed conflict in commanding good is the ruler's duty, and only qualified scholars may enjoin or forbid on matters requiring ijtihad, with mutual respect among them.
