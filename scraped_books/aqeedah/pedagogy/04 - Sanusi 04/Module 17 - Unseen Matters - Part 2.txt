And the meaning of nadam (regret) is: sadness and pain over what one did, and wishing it had not been done. And another condition is sometimes added to tawba: it is the resolve (ʿazm) to abandon recidivism in the future. Commentary
This passage first defines the meaning of nadam (regret) as sadness and wishing the act had not occurred. Then it introduces an additional condition sometimes proposed: the resolve (ʿazm) not to repeat the sin in the future. An objection is raised: this condition is impossible for a person who may not think of the sin again (due to death, insanity, etc.) or who becomes unable to commit it (e.g., loss of speech for slander). The answer given is that the resolve is conditional upon the possibility of thinking of it and having the ability. If ability is removed, no resolve is required. This is supported by Imām al-Ḥaramayn's words. Then the author provides the verification (taḥqīq): the mention of resolve is only for explanation and affirmation, not as a separate condition. Genuine regret for the sin's ugliness necessarily implies a resolve not to repeat it (if one could). Finally, a warning: common people use 'tawba' to mean just saying they will stop sinning, but that is not actual repentance unless accompanied by heartfelt regret, evidenced by prolonged sorrow and tears.

Discussion on commanding good and forbidding evil. What is attached to the chapter of repentance and resembles it in deterring the commission of sin and neglect of the obligatory: commanding good and forbidding evil. Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof section for the duty of commanding good and forbidding evil. It begins by stating that this topic is attached to the chapter on repentance because it similarly deters sin and neglect of duty. The author defines al-ma'ruf as the obligatory (wajib) and al-munkar as the forbidden (haram). He asserts that commanding the good and forbidding the evil, in these senses, are two obligations that do not depend on the appearance of an Imam, contrary to the Rafida (a Shi'a group) who claim that this duty requires an infallible Imam. Then he provides proofs: the Qur'an (two verses: 3:104 and 31:17) and the Sunnah (two hadiths: one direct command and a warning that if Muslims abandon this duty, Allah will send evil rulers and then the supplications of the righteous will not be answered). The passage thus establishes the obligation and its basis in primary sources.
