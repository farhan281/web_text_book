The Reality of Karamat in Sunni Doctrine
Sanusi affirms the reality of saintly miracles and refutes those who deny them.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's affirmation of karamat (saintly miracles) as a mainstream Sunni doctrine, his comparison to prophetic miracles, and his critique of both innovators and some Sunni jurists who deny specific karamat.

Core Idea
Karamat are real and almost match prophetic miracles; denying them is innovation.

In this passage from his Sharh al-'Aqida al-Wusta, Imam al-Sanusi sets the record straight on the doctrine of karamat. He affirms their reality as part of Sunni orthodoxy, equating their manifest power nearly to that of prophetic miracles. Importantly, he also takes to task both those who deny karamat outright and some Sunni jurists who overstep by declaring apostasy for believing in specific saintly feats.

He says karamat 'almost join' the miracles of prophets.
3
Analyze the critique of jurists
Observe how Sanusi distinguishes between innovators who deny karamat and some Sunni jurists who go too far by declaring apostasy. He finds the latter's position 'wonderful' in a negative sense.
Reflect on why this critique is important for maintaining balance in Sunni theology.

Reflect on the humble closing

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal exposition on the reality of karamat (extraordinary acts granted to saints). Sanusi begins by stating that his position on karamat is that of the majority of verifiers from Ahl al-Sunna: they affirm karamat. He notes that he has discussed this in his works. He then quotes from his own Sharh al-'Aqida al-Wusta, where he says that the karamat of awliya, in their manifestation, almost equal the miracles of prophets. Denying them is characteristic of innovators who lack direct experience. He then directs criticism at some Sunni jurists who, concerning the story of Ibrahim ibn Adham being in two places at once, said that believing that is possible leads to kufr. Sanusi finds this extreme. Finally, he adds a humble comment about the high station of awliya, acknowledging that they are at a level beyond mere rational proof, and we can only wish to reach that level. The passage defends the reality of karamat against both external and internal detractors.

The Linguistic and Technical Meaning of al-ʿĀlam
Sanusi defines 'al-ʿālam' linguistically and its use by theologians.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the linguistic definition of 'al-ʿālam' as a species or genus with distinguishing signs, how mutakallimūn extended it to every created thing, and why this term points to Allah.

Core Idea
Every created thing bears a sign pointing to its Creator.

This lesson unpacks Sanusi's definition of 'al-ʿālam' (the world) and its theological significance. You will see how the term moves from a linguistic description of species and genera to a technical term for all originated things, each bearing clear signs of its contingency and distinction from the Creator.

Commentary
This passage defines and explains the term 'al-ʿālam' (the world) and its indication of Allah. The author begins by stating the topic: discourse on the world in terms of its naming and its indication of Allah, Lord of the worlds. He first clarifies the root of 'tarbiya' (upbringing), which involves transferring something from one state to another toward a goal; then it was used for the owner and reformer because upbringing is often necessary for them. This connects to the word 'rabb' (Lord) in the phrase 'Rabb al-ʿālamīn' (Lord of the worlds). Next, Sanusi explains that 'al-ʿālamūn' is an irregular sound plural of 'al-ʿālam'. He then gives the linguistic definition of 'al-ʿālam' as every species or genus that has a distinguishing sign ('alāma) by which it is distinguished from other originated ('ḥāditha') species and genera. Examples: world of humans, birds, horses (species); world of animals, bodies, growths (genera). The suitability of the name lies in the distinguishing features (fuṣūl and khaṣāʾiṣ) that make them known. Then he states that the mutakallimūn (theologians) transferred this term to every originated thing (ḥādith). The reason: every originated thing has signs that distinguish it from its Creator, the Eternal Lord, so that it is never confused with Him. This is supported by the verse 'Say: Name them' (Qur'an 13:33), challenging the polytheists to describe their alleged partners—whether they possess any quality fit for divinity. A second possible suitability: every originated thing provides knowledge to the contemplator of what is necessary for the Great Lord and negates from Him characteristics of created things. This is supported by Qur'an 3:190 and 7:185, which call for reflection on creation as signs. Thus, the world's very name indicates the Creator and refutes polytheism. The author presents both possibilities (from 'alāma and from 'ilm) without deciding, but both lead to the same conclusion: the world points to its Maker.

Definitions of Certainty, Knowledge, and Other Cognitive States
Sanusi classifies cognitive states using an arithmetic proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Islamic theologians classify cognitive states, and why genuine knowledge requires certainty derived from proof or necessity, not mere acceptance from authority.

Core Idea
Knowledge is certain judgment conforming to reality, derived from proof; not all firm beliefs qualify.

In this passage, Sanusi uses a concrete arithmetic example to illustrate the spectrum of human cognition, from genuine knowledge down to compound ignorance. By tracing how certainty arises from necessary premises, he establishes the criteria that distinguish knowledge from belief, opinion, doubt, and error. This foundational classification underpins all later theological reasoning.

Guided Path

The example involves adding 4 fourteen times to get 40, then determining what 1/4 of 1/10 of 40 is.
2
Identify the criteria for knowledge
Look for the definition: certainty conforming to reality, obtained by a proof. Compare with the case of taqlid (blind acceptance) to see why the latter is not knowledge.
Knowledge requires personal rational derivation.
3
Map the other cognitive states
List opinion (zann), illusion (wahm), doubt (shakk), and compound ignorance (jahl murakkab). Note how they differ in certainty and correspondence to reality.
Compound ignorance is certainty in falsehood.
4
Reflect on a common misconception
Why isn't correct belief (i'tiqad sahih) considered knowledge? This highlights the importance of personal rational verification.
Knowledge must be based on proof or necessity, not authority.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed epistemological classification using an arithmetic example. The author begins with a concrete arithmetic scenario: placing the number 4 on a board and adding another 4 fourteen times yields 40, giving the necessary knowledge that 4 is a tenth of 40. However, this knowledge is not immediate; it arises after observing the division of 40 into ten equal parts of 4. From this, the author shows how two necessary premises—one initially obvious (1 is a quarter of 4) and one derived (a quarter of 4 is a quarter of a tenth of 40)—combine to produce a certain conclusion that the price paid for a commodity is 1 dirham. This conclusion, if reached through a proof (burhan) composed of these premises, yields knowledge (ma'rifa/'ilm), defined as certain judgment (jism) conforming to reality and derived from proof. The author then contrasts this with other cognitive states: if one accepts the same conclusion blindly (taqlid) from a reliable authority, it is correct belief (i'tiqad sahih) but not knowledge because it lacks personal rational grounding. If one has a preponderant but not certain inclination, it is opinion (zann); the weaker inclination is illusion (wahm); if probabilities are equal, it is doubt (shakk). Finally, if one is certain of a falsehood due to sophistry or blind following, it is compound ignorance (jahl murakkab) or corrupt belief (i'tiqad fasid). The passage highlights the criteria for genuine knowledge: certainty, correspondence to reality, and derivation from a necessary or proof-based foundation.

The grammatical attachment of a phrase can protect core theological truths.

The Meaning of 'No Influencer with Him'
