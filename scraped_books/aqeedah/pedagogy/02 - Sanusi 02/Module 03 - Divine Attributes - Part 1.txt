Refuting Belief that God Has Dimensions and Change
Sanusi refutes divine dimensions and change using Abraham's verse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the author considers belief in divine dimensions and change as ignorance, and how Abraham's verse provides a logical proof for divine transcendence.

Core Idea
Change implies temporality, and temporality cannot be true of God.

In this passage, Sanusi refutes two false beliefs about God: that He has dimensions and that He changes. He invokes Abraham's famous declaration 'I do not love those that set' to construct a logical argument: anything that changes cannot be eternal, and so cannot be God. Let's examine the proof step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it, then read the translation. Let the author's own words come through before you interpret.
Notice the concise logical structure.
2
Identify the two false beliefs
The passage begins by naming two false beliefs: that God has dimensions (ab'aad) and that He is subject to change (tabaddul wa taghayyur). Identify them and note how the author labels those who hold them as ignorant.
These are common anthropomorphic conceptions.
3
Trace the logical argument
Follow the proof from the Quranic verse to the conclusion: everything that sets (afil) is changing (mutaghayyir); everything changing is not eternal (qadim); therefore it cannot be a god (ilah). Ask yourself how each step connects.
This is a syllogism: afil→mutaghayyir, mutaghayyir→la qadim, so la ilah.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of two false beliefs about God: that He has dimensions (ab'aad) that encompass His Essence, and that He is subject to change (tabaddul wa taghayyur). The author declares such a person ignorant (jahil) of the Quranic verse in which Abraham says, 'I do not love those that set (afilin).' The argument is then stated: everything that 'sets' (afal) is changing (mutaghayyir), and everything that changes is not eternal (qadim). Therefore, such a changing thing cannot be a god (ilah). The author uses this concise logical proof to uphold divine transcendence (tanzih) and refute anthropomorphic conceptions.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think that the verse 'I do not love those that set' applies only to celestial bodies, and does not provide a general logical proof.
Correction: The author uses the verse to derive a universal principle: setting implies change, change implies non-eternality, and non-eternality negates divinity. Thus the argument applies to anything that changes, not just stars.

Key Takeaway
The argument that change implies non-eternality proves that God cannot change, for any changing thing cannot be eternal and thus not divine.

The Necessary Attributes of God
Sanusi enumerates the necessary attributes of God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the categories of necessary attributes: the essential attribute, attributes of negation, attributes of meanings, and mental attributes.

Core Idea
God's necessary attributes define His unique perfection.

Sanusi begins his classification of divine attributes by listing those that are rationally necessary for God. These attributes are not arbitrary; they are required by the fact that God is the Necessary Being. This lesson presents the first category: the attributes that must be affirmed of God, including both negative and positive descriptions.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on necessary attributes
Carefully read the Arabic text and its translation to familiarize yourself with the list.
2
Identify the two main subcategories
Notice that the passage first mentions 'the essential attribute and the attributes of negation', then 'the attributes of meanings and the mental attributes'. These are two groups.
The first group describes God's essence and what He is not; the second describes His perfections.
3
Reflect on the meaning of each attribute
Consider why each attribute (e.g., necessary of existence, pre-eternality, power) is necessarily true of God.
Think about what would be missing if any of these were not affirmed.

The Impossible Attributes of God
Sanusi enumerates the impossible attributes of God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the attributes that are logically impossible for God, which are the opposites of the necessary attributes.

Core Idea
What cannot be said about God is as important as what must be said.

After establishing what God must be, Sanusi now clarifies what God cannot be. These impossible attributes serve as boundary markers, ensuring that we do not attribute to God any imperfection or limitation. This lesson covers the negations of the necessary attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on impossible attributes
Read the Arabic and translation to see the list of impossible attributes.
2
Notice the correspondence
Compare these impossible attributes to the necessary attributes from the previous lesson. Each impossible attribute is the opposite of a necessary one.
For example, 'nonexistence' opposes 'necessary of existence', 'origination' opposes 'pre-eternality', etc.
3
Understand the logical necessity
Reflect on why attributing these to God would be contradictory to His being the Necessary Being.
If God could be described by any of these, He would not be God.

The Possible Attributes of God's Actions
Sanusi states the possible attribute regarding God's actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that God has the freedom to do or leave any possible action, and that this does not imply arbitrariness.

Core Idea
God's actions are not compelled; He has absolute freedom.

The final category in Sanusi's classification is the possible attributes, which apply to God's actions. Unlike the necessary and impossible attributes, these are not fixed descriptions of God's essence but pertain to what He may choose to do. This lesson clarifies the scope of God's creative freedom.

Guided Path
1
Read the short passage on possible attributes
Read the Arabic and translation. Note that it says 'to do every possible thing or to leave it'.
This is brief but significant.
2
Understand the meaning of 'possible'
Here, 'possible' means logically possible, not contradictory. God can choose to bring about any logically possible state of affairs.
This distinguishes God's actions from necessary or impossible ones.
3
Reflect on the theological implications
This attribute emphasizes God's complete freedom and sovereignty. He is not compelled to act in any particular way.
It also implies that the world is not necessary but contingent on God's will.

The Necessity of God's Eternity and Permanence
Sanusi proves God's eternity and permanence by showing the impossibility of His origination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God must be both eternal (no beginning) and permanent (no end) on the basis of rational proof.

Core Idea
If God were not eternal, He would be impossible, not just imperfect.

This short but powerful passage from Sanusi establishes a foundational truth about Allah: He must be eternal and permanent. The argument is twofold: first, if He were originated, He would need an agent and suffer the same incapability as other creatures; second, and more decisively, His origination would lead to circularity or infinite regress, which are impossible. This lesson unpacks the logic and shows why the attributes of qidam and baqa' are rationally necessary.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic text and its English translation. Notice the concise, logical style: the argument is presented as a conditional chain.
Pay attention to the word 'otherwise' (وإلا) which signals the counterfactual.
2
Understand the first consequence: need for an agent
Sanusi argues that if God were not eternal, He would need an agent (fāʿil) to bring Him into existence, making Him originated (ḥādith). An originated being is inherently incapable (ʿajz) like all other creatures.
This already shows that a non‑eternal God would be imperfect, but Sanusi goes further.
3
Grasp the second, stronger consequence: impossibility
Even worse, assuming God's origination leads to logical absurdities: either circularity (dawr) where existence precedes itself, or infinite regress (tasalsul) of causes, both rationally impossible. Hence His existence would be impossible altogether.
This is the core of the proof: origination is not just a flaw, it is a logical contradiction for the First Cause.
4
Connect to the attributes of qidam and baqā'
The argument establishes both pre‑eternity (qidam) and permanence (baqā'). If God had a beginning but no end, the same regress would apply to His continuation. Thus both are necessary.
Sanusi explicitly mentions both attributes in the opening sentence.

Commentary

2. Among all prophets, the Prophet Muhammad received the greatest share, and his message is characterized by ease and the removal of burdens, as supported by Quranic verses. The author also mentions that those who received this specific nurture through the Prophet comprise two-thirds of the people of Paradise. Finally, he connects the opening phrase 'Rabb al-'Alamin' to the general tarbiyyah, and the mention of the Prophet in the Fatiha (or in the context) to the specific tarbiyyah.

Clarification: The Proof That God Must Be a Self Described with Attributes
Sanusi clarifies the proof with a logical illustration and explains why a quality cannot accept another quality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the clarified proof that if God were not knowing by Himself, He would be a quality, but qualities cannot possess the necessary attributes, so He must be a self.

Core Idea
If God were a quality, He could not be described with moral attributes, so He must be a self.

Sanusi now clarifies his proof with a logical illustration. He considers the supposition that God is not knowing by Himself—meaning He is not a self described with attributes. If that were so, He would be a quality subsisting in a locus. But then He could not be described with the attributes of meanings and moral attributes, yet such description is necessary. Therefore, the supposition is false, and we confirm that God is indeed a self described with those attributes. This clarification reinforces the central claim that God is not a quality but a self with attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the clarification passage
Read the Arabic passage starting from 'Wa-tawḍīḥ al-burhān' to the end. Then read the translation carefully.
This is a logical argument (conditional proof).
2
Identify the hypothesis and its implications
The argument begins with 'If the Exalted were not knowing by Himself' (i.e., not a self described with attributes). This leads to 'He would be a quality subsisting in a locus.' Then trace the implication that such a quality cannot be described with attributes of meanings and moral attributes.
Note the term 'صفات المعاني' (attributes of meanings) and 'صفات المعنوية' (moral attributes).
3
Understand the falsification
Since it is necessary for God to be described with those attributes, the consequent (being a quality) is false. Therefore, the antecedent (not being a self) is also false, establishing the desired conclusion: God is a self described with those attributes.
This is a modus tollens argument: if P then Q, not Q, therefore not P.
4

The passage ends with an explanation of why a quality cannot accept another quality, using the idea of infinite regress. This supports the impossibility of a quality being receptive to existential attributes.

Four Divisions of Things Regarding Locus and Agent
Al-Bayli classifies things into four categories based on need for a locus and an agent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Bayli classifies all things into four categories based on their need for a locus (mahall) and an agent (fa'il), and how this classification differentiates God's essence, His attributes, and contingent beings.

Core Idea
All things are divided into four categories based on need for a locus and an agent.

This passage presents a foundational classification in Islamic theology. Al-Bayli uses two simple criteria—whether something requires a substrate in which to exist (mahall) and whether it requires an efficient cause to bring it into being (fa'il)—to generate a complete taxonomy of all things. By understanding these four categories, we gain clarity on the unique nature of God and the dependence of all else.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the four categories are enumerated.
2
Identify the two criteria
The classification uses two criteria: need for a locus (mahall) and need for an agent (fa'il). Understand what each means.
A locus is a substrate; an agent is a cause.
3
List and label the four divisions
Write down the four categories: (1) needs neither, (2) needs both, (3) needs locus only, (4) needs agent only. Then match each with its example.
4
Reflect on the theological significance
Notice that only God's essence falls into the first category; His attributes need a locus (His essence) but not an agent. Accidents need both. This shows God's self-sufficiency.
This classification prevents mistakes like thinking God's attributes are created.

(۲) قال العلامة البيلي في " تقييداته على شرح صغرى الصغرى » ( ق ۱۱ ) : ) واعلم : أن الأشياء بالنسبة للمحلَّ والفاعل على أربعة أقسام : أحدها : ما لا يحتاج إليهما ؛ وهي ذات الله عز وجل ، وثانيها : ما يحتاج إليهما معاً صفات الحوادث ، وثالثها : ما يحتاج إلى محل ولا يحتاج إلى فاعل ؛ كصفاته جل وعز ، ورابعها : ما يحتاج إلى فاعل دون محل ؛ كذوات وهي الحوادث ) .
(2) The scholar al-Bayli said in his annotations on Sharh Sughra al-Sughra (folio 11): (Know that things with respect to locus (mahall) and agent (fa'il) are of four divisions: The first: what does not need either of them—that is the essence (dhat) of God Almighty. The second: what needs both of them—the attributes of accidents (sifat al-hawadith). The third: what needs a locus but does not need an agent—like His attributes (sifatihi) [i.e., God's attributes]. The fourth: what needs an agent but not a locus—like essences that are accidents (dhawat al-hawadith).)

Commentary
This passage is a classification (taqsim) by al-Bayli. He divides all things (al-ashya') with respect to two considerations: need for a locus (mahall, i.e., a substrate in which they inhere) and need for an agent (fa'il, i.e., an efficient cause). The four categories are: (1) that which needs neither a locus nor an agent—this is the essence of God, which is absolutely self-sufficient; (2) that which needs both—these are the attributes of accidents, which inhere in a locus and are brought into existence by an agent; (3) that which needs a locus but not an agent—these are God's attributes, which inhere in His essence but are not created; (4) that which needs an agent but not a locus—these are the essences of accidents, i.e., the substances that are accidents themselves (such as color), which are brought into existence but do not inhere in another accident.

God's Oneness: Definition and Proof
Sanusi affirms absolute oneness of God, defining it and proving it by impossibility of a peer or partner.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic doctrine of God's oneness as absolute uniqueness without any peer, and the rational proof that negates any equal or partner in essence, attributes, or actions.

Core Idea
God's oneness means He has no like in essence, attributes, or action.

This lesson focuses on one of the essential attributes of God: oneness (wahdaniyyah). Sanusi defines it precisely and provides a logical proof that God must be unique without any equal or partner. The passage also clarifies what oneness does not mean, distinguishing it from types of unity that imply similarity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic text once, then the full English translation. Notice the structure: main text by Sanusi followed by a gloss by al-Bayli.
The gloss provides important clarifications on types of unity.
2
Understand the definition of wahdaniyyah
Sanusi defines wahdaniyyah as three negations: no like in essence, no like in attributes, and no effective agent with Him in any action.
3
Trace the proof against an equal
The proof works by contradiction: if there were an equal or partner, God would need a specifier to distinguish Himself, which implies need and thus contingency (huduth) and incapacity, contradicting necessary existence.
Follow the logical steps carefully.
4
Examine the three invalid types of unity
Al-Bayli explains three types of unity that are impossible for God: unity of genus, species, and person. Understand why each implies a likeness.
These are distinguished from wahdaniyyah.
5
Reflect on theological significance
Consider why this attribute is foundational: without absolute uniqueness, God would not be the Necessary Being.
This refutes polytheism and anthropomorphism.

And Oneness (al-wahdaniyyah) is necessarily true of Him, the Exalted (1); meaning: there is no like (mithl) of Him in His essence, nor in any of His attributes, nor any effective agent (mu'aththir) with Him in any of the actions; for if there were with Him a like or an effective agent, He would not be Necessary Existence; because He would then need someone to specify (yukhassis) Him with what distinguishes Him from what resembles Him generally or specifically, and that entails temporality (huduth) and incapacity ('ajz) regarding every possible thing. Commentary
This passage affirms the attribute of oneness (wahdaniyyah) for God and defines it in three negations: no equal in essence, no equal in attributes, and no partner in actions. The proof is by contradiction: if there were an equal or partner, God would not be Necessary Existence, because He would require a 'specifier' (mukhassis) to differentiate Him from the other, implying need and thus contingency (huduth) and incapacity. The gloss by al-Bayli defines the term 'wahdaniyyah' linguistically and distinguishes it from three invalid types of unity: unity of genus (e.g., sharing in animality), unity of species (e.g., sharing in humanity), and unity of person (individual identity). These types entail likeness, which is impossible for God as He has no genus or species.

The Meaning and Proof of Divine Oneness (Wihdāniyya)
Sanusi defines wihdāniyya and proves its necessity via a conditional argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that wihdāniyya means God has no like in essence or attributes and no co-influencer, and the rational proof that if there were a like or influencer, God would not be necessary of existence.

Core Idea
God's oneness means He has no like or partner — any like would make Him contingent.

Sanusi begins this lesson by stating that divine oneness (wihdāniyya) is necessary for God. He then clarifies what oneness truly means: it is not merely numerical unity, but the absence of any like in essence or attributes, and the absence of any co-influencer in actions. To prove this, he offers a concise rational argument that any such like or influencer would imply contingency and limitation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Pay attention to the definition of wihdāniyya and the conditional proof that follows. Notice that Sanusi starts with a statement of necessity, then defines, then gives a proof.
2
Identify the two components of the proof
The proof has two parts: (1) if there were a like or influencer, God would need a specifier, and (2) needing a specifier implies contingency and incapability, which contradict necessary existence. Find these in the passage.
3
Reflect on the implication
Ask yourself: Why would a like or influencer require a specifier? How does this argument establish that God is unique in essence, attributes, and actions? The commentary will explain.

Al-Baylī on Divine Oneness and the Three Types of Unity
Al-Baylī defines wihdāniyya and explains three types of unity impossible for God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the linguistic structure of the term 'wihdāniyya' and why three types of created unity cannot apply to Allah.

Core Idea
God's oneness is unique—no genus, no species, no numerical sharing.

Al-Baylī begins by breaking down the word 'wihdāniyya' itself, showing it is an intensive relative form. He then distinguishes Allah's oneness from three kinds of unity found in creation. This passage clarifies that divine oneness is absolute uniqueness, with no partner or equal in any category.

Guided Path
1
Examine the word 'wihdāniyya'
Read the morphological analysis: the yā' for relation, extra alif, intensifying nūn, and feminine tā'. This form emphasizes uniqueness.
Notice that the term itself is constructed to stress that Allah's oneness is not ordinary.
2
Identify the three types of unity
Find and understand the definitions: unity of genus (sharing in a broader category), unity of species (sharing in a specific kind), and unity of individual (numerical oneness).
Examples are given: genus—man and animal; species—Zayd and Amr; individual—a single person.
3
Understand why they are impossible for God
Read the conclusion: Allah has no genus or species; thus none of these unities can apply to Him. His oneness is incomparable.
This refines our understanding of tawḥīd beyond simply saying 'Allah is one'.

Why Wihdāniyya Is Not Redundant
Al-Baylī answers an objection that wihdāniyya is redundant with God's dissimilarity to creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the attribute of wihdāniyya (oneness) is not redundant with God's dissimilarity to creation.

Core Idea
Dissimilarity does not rule out another eternal being; wihdāniyya does.

An objector might think that since God is already described as different from created things, stating that He is one is unnecessary. Al-Baylī explains why this is not so: being different from creation does not automatically mean there cannot be another eternal being. The attribute of wihdāniyya is needed to explicitly deny any co-eternal being.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the objection: 'If you say: the attribute of wihdāniyya is unnecessary because He is different in His essence, attributes, and actions from contingents, so what is the benefit of stating it?' Notice the logic: dissimilarity is already affirmed, so why mention oneness?
2
Understand the answer
Read Al-Baylī's answer: 'It does not follow from His being different from contingents... that He is one, because it is possible that there is another eternal being.' Grasp the key point: dissimilarity to creation does not logically exclude another eternal being who is also dissimilar to creation.
3
Identify the logical gap
Ask yourself: What does dissimilarity actually cover? It covers difference from created things. But another eternal being would also be different from creation. So dissimilarity alone does not prevent multiple eternal beings. Wihdāniyya fills this gap by explicitly denying any 'similar eternal being'.
The phrase 'قديم مماثل' (similar eternal being) is key.
4
Test your understanding with the checkpoint
Answer the multiple-choice question to check if you see why wihdāniyya is needed.

Commentary
This passage is a proof and refutation. The author begins by positing a hypothetical: if a contingent (created) power could effect origination and annihilation (bringing things into existence and destroying them) like God's power, it would be similar to God's power. But if it were similar, it would itself be contingent (haditha), because it would need a 'specificator' (mukhassis) that distinguishes it from other powers—specifying its essence, its relation, and its scope. The contingency of the attribute necessitates the contingency of the entity it qualifies (the power's possessor), which would then imply the contingency of the divine essence itself—utterly impossible. Next, an objection is raised: the origination and annihilation attributed to some created powers (like heat causing burning) are not from the power's own reality but are accidental (aradi) to it, granted by God. Thus, these powers act according to God's will and knowledge, not their own, and no similarity remains. The argument then questions whether this second attribute's necessitation of effect is essential (dhati) or accidental—if essential, no further cause; if accidental, the regress continues. The Argument for Universal Divine Power and Will
Sanusi argues by contradiction for universal divine power and will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why divine power and will must extend to all possible things, and the three impossible consequences if they were restricted.

Core Idea
If divine power could be limited, it would be no power at all.

Sanusi now delivers a decisive proof that Allah's power and will cannot be restricted to some things but must encompass all possible existence. He uses a reductio ad absurdum: assuming restriction leads to logical impossibility, thereby affirming universality. This argument solidifies the attribute of absolute divine sovereignty.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem
Consider what it means for divine power to be 'restricted' to only some possible things. Why would anyone think that? Sanusi assumes this for the sake of argument.
This is a hypothetical assumption, not a real belief.
2
Follow the first consequence
If power and will were restricted, then inability (ajz) would become general: the creator would be incapable of creating some things, and since all possibles are equally in need of a creator, He would be incapable of all.
Additionally, the restricted powers themselves would require a specifier, making them created.
3
Identify the second and third consequences
Second: the powers themselves would be created because they would need a creator to specify them. Third: mutual hindrance (tamānuʿ) would arise between the restricted powers and the powers that are attached to what they are not attached to.
Each consequence is impossible for the divine essence.
4
Connect to the bigger picture
Since restriction leads to impossibility, the opposite must be true: divine power and will are universal over all possible things. This preserves divine perfection and unity.
This argument is a cornerstone of Ashʿarī theology.

The Necessity of Universal Divine Knowledge
Sanusi asserts the necessity of divine knowledge's universality and provides a rational proof from the impossibility of specification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God's knowledge must encompass all things—necessary, possible, impossible—and how limitation would imply temporal origination.

Core Idea
Partial knowledge needs a cause; God's knowledge is uncaused and universal.

Sanusi begins by stating that God's knowledge must cover every category of existence because any restriction would require an external cause, leading to temporal origination which is impossible for God. This foundational argument establishes divine omniscience as a rational necessity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. Notice the strong assertion that knowledge must encompass all three categories.
The categories are: wājib (necessary), jā'iz (possible), mustaḥīl (impossible).
2
Identify the key argument
The passage uses 'specification to some' (ikhtiṣāṣ bil-ba‘ḍ) as the problem. Why does specification entail hudūth?
Because specification requires a cause (fā‘il) to choose it, and that cause would make the attribute originated.
3
Understand the implication
If the attribute (knowledge) is originated, then its subject (God) would also be originated, which is impossible.
This is based on the principle that an attribute cannot be separated from its subject or its opposites.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Therefore, God's knowledge must be all-encompassing, without any specification.

Commentary
This passage is a multi-part argument and refutation regarding the divine attribute of knowledge. Sanusi begins by stating the necessity ('wujub') of God's knowledge encompassing all things—necessary, possible, and impossible—because specification to some would require a cause, implying temporality (huduth). He then notes that every contingent event points to the four demands (existence, necessity, possibility, etc.) as in power and will. He explains why the proof of existence of this attribute was not given in the earlier creedal text: it was already implied in the phrase 'upon His knowledge'. The proof of necessity is derived from the universality of relation: if specification causes temporality, how much more so would outright contingency? He then presents a footnote (by al-Bayli) defining knowledge as an eternal attribute of encompassing without prior concealment. Next, he derives negation of multiplicity from the same proof, as multiplicity requires a determiner. He clarifies that 'origination of attribute necessitates origination of subject' leads to circularity or infinite regress. He adds that hiddenness of some parts implies hiddenness of all. Finally, he discusses the refutation of the possibility of opposites to knowledge, arguing that they must be originated because they are opposite to originated knowledge, and if they come after knowledge their origination is clear. The passage concludes with a prayer for divine success.
