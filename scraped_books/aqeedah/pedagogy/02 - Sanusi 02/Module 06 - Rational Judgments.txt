The Necessity of Rational Proof and Condemnation of Blind Imitation
The author asserts the need for rational proof in beliefs and warns against blind imitation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why rational proof is necessary for foundational beliefs and the ruling on blind imitation (taqlid) in matters of faith.

Core Idea
Blind imitation in faith is blameworthy; rational proof is essential for true belief.

This passage from Imam Sanusi's creed establishes a fundamental principle: certain beliefs cannot be known without rational demonstration. The author warns that simply hearing revelation or reciting verses does not constitute proof; one must reflect and grasp the rational aspect. He then condemns those who imitate blindly, even if their belief happens to be correct, and explains the juristic ruling on such imitation.

The verses support the need for reflection.

And know, my brother, that among beliefs there are those that cannot do without rational proof; so the person addressed must perceive the aspect of obligation, impossibility, or possibility in it, even if he hears the speech of God and His authentic Sunnah... that does not suffice him by mere hearing, rather he must reflect on the aspect of the proof, otherwise he would be among those whom the Exalted Majesty has condemned with such as His saying: 'Then do they not reflect upon the Quran, or are there locks upon their hearts?' [Muhammad 24], and His saying: 'Indeed, the worst of creatures in the sight of God are the deaf and dumb who do not reason' [Al-Anfal 22]. And the jurists have ruled his salvation because his imitation coincidentally corresponded to the truth, on condition of his certainty and his not abandoning his belief if his imitated one abandons it, and they have said that his sin is great because he resembles those who found their fathers astray, so they follow in their footsteps, hastening.

Commentary
This passage is an argument for the necessity of rational proof in certain beliefs. The author begins by stating that some beliefs cannot be known without rational proof; the person must understand the aspect of wujub (obligation), istihala (impossibility), or jawaz (possibility) in that belief. Mere hearing of revelation is insufficient—one must reflect (tafakkur) on the proof. He cites two Quranic verses condemning those who do not reflect or use reason. He then gives an example: someone who thinks that memorizing the verse 'If there were other gods...' suffices for the proof of tawhid, without grasping the rational impossibility of multiple gods, is a muqallid (blind imitator). Such a person is compared to any imitator of false beliefs. The jurists (fuqaha) rule that such a person is saved only if his imitation happens to coincide with the truth and he is certain and consistent, but he commits a grave sin (ma'siya) because he resembles those who blindly follow their ancestors (as in Quranic condemnation). The passage thus establishes that taqlid in foundational beliefs is blameworthy and that rational proof is essential.

Definition of Wajib and Mustahil in Rational Judgments
Sanusi defines wajib and mustahil with their subdivisions and states the obligation to know them.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the obligation to know the three rational judgments (wajib, mustahil, ja'iz) regarding Allah and the messengers, and the definition of wajib and mustahil with their subdivisions into self-evident and theoretical.

Core Idea
The rational judgments are defined by what the mind can or cannot conceive.

This lesson begins Sanusi's core subject: what every Muslim must know about Allah and the prophets. He first states the obligation to know the three rational judgments—necessary, impossible, and permissible—then defines the first two. Understanding these definitions is essential because they form the foundation for all subsequent theological arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the obligation
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that Sanusi first states that every accountable person must know three things about Allah and the prophets: the necessary, the impossible, and the permissible.
2
Understand the definition of wajib
Sanusi defines wajib as that whose non-existence cannot be conceived. Look at his two subtypes: dharuri (self-evident, no reflection needed) and nadhari (theoretical, requires reflection). The examples help clarify.
3
Understand the definition of mustahil
Mustahil is defined as that whose existence cannot be conceived. It also divides into dharuri and nadhari. The passage gives the first example and begins the second.
Note the parallel structure with wajib.
4
Reflect on the importance of these definitions
These definitions are the building blocks of kalam. They provide a clear rational framework for discussing what can and cannot be true about Allah.

Commentary
This passage begins with the author's customary praise and prayer, then immediately states the foundational obligation: every legally responsible person (mukallaf) must know what is wajib, mustahil, and ja'iz regarding God and regarding the prophets. This establishes the subject matter of the entire treatise. The author then provides a section heading for the definitions of the three rational judgments. He defines al-wajib (the necessary) as that whose non-existence cannot be conceived by the mind. He divides it into two types: dharuri (self-evident), which requires no reflection, such as the truth that one is half of two; and nadhari (theoretical), which requires reflection, such as the truth that one is half of a sixth of twelve. Note that the example for nadhari requires a simple calculation, but is not immediately obvious. He then begins the definition of al-mustahil (the impossible) as that whose existence cannot be conceived by the mind. He similarly divides it into dharuri and nadhari, but the text cuts off at 'or after reflection' on this page. The definition is structurally parallel to that of wajib: just as wajib is inconceivable non-existence, mustahil is inconceivable existence.

Sanusi enumerates the three rational judgments and defines each with examples.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three rational judgments (obligatory, impossible, possible) and their subdivision into necessary and theoretical knowledge.

Core Idea
The three rational judgments are the grammar of all theological reasoning.

Sanusi begins by stating the foundational knowledge every Muslim must acquire: understanding what is obligatory, impossible, and possible for Allah and the prophets. He then provides clear definitions and examples, showing that some truths are self-evident while others require reflection.

Commentary

The examples are simple arithmetic or everyday observations, illustrating that these categories are foundational for reasoning about God and prophets.

Definition of the Contingent (al-Ja'iz)
Sanusi defines the contingent and gives examples with and without reflection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of the contingent (al-ja'iz) in kalam, its two categories based on reflection, and the meaning of intellectual validity.

Core Idea
The contingent is what the intellect finds equally acceptable for existence or non-existence.

Before we can discuss what must be true about Allah, we need a clear vocabulary for classifying things according to reason. One of the most basic categories is the contingent — that which can either exist or not exist in the intellect. Sanusi defines it precisely and shows how it can be known with or without reflection.

Guided Path

3

Identify the two categories: without reflection (bila ta'mmul) and after reflection (ba'da al-ta'mmul). Examine the examples given for each.
The example of a body being white does not require deep thought, while wishing for death requires reflection.

The Obligation to Seek Proof: Ruling on the Blind Follower
Sanusi states the ruling on one whose belief is correct but based on taqlid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a person whose belief is correct but based on mere blind following (taqlīd) must still seek rational proof to attain genuine knowledge, according to the stronger opinion.

Core Idea

The Permissible for God: Actions, Not Essence
Sanusi defines the permissible for God and distinguishes attributes of action from essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what it means for something to be 'permissible' (ja'iz) with respect to God, and why permissibility applies only to His attributes of action, not to His essence or essential attributes.

Core Idea
God's permissibility is about what He can do, not what He is.

In this passage, Sanusi defines a key term in Islamic theology: al-ja'iz (the permissible) as it relates to God. He clarifies that while God's power and will encompass all possible things, permissibility does not touch His essence or essential attributes, which are necessarily existent. Instead, it refers to the actualization of contingent things through His power and will. This distinction is foundational for understanding divine attributes.

Why God is Not Obligated to Reward the Obedient
Sanusi argues that God's reward of the obedient is rationally contingent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why rewarding the obedient is a contingent (jāʾiz) matter for God and not a rational obligation.

Core Idea
No one has a claim over God; obedience itself is His creation.

Sanusi continues his discussion of rational judgments by addressing a common question: Does God owe rewards to those who obey Him? In this passage, he explains why reward is merely possible and never mandatory. The reasoning is rooted in God's perfection and the nature of human actions.

Guided Path

The passage is concise; each phrase carries weight.
2
Identify the three reasons
Sanusi provides three distinct reasons why reward is not obligatory. List them: (1) no one has a right over God, (2) God gains no benefit from obedience, (3) obedience is God's creation and the servant only acquires it.
These reasons are linked by 'إذ' and 'أيضاً'.

This relates to the Ashʿarī theory of acquisition.

Reward of the Obedient is Not Obligatory but Permissible
Sanusi states that rewarding the obedient is not obligatory but permissible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sanusi holds that rewarding the obedient is not rationally obligatory upon God, but is among the permissible acts of divine grace.

Core Idea
God's reward is a gift, not a debt.

This lesson examines a foundational principle in Sunni theology: that no human act creates an obligation upon God. While we might assume that obedience deserves reward, Sanusi argues that reward is purely a matter of divine permission. The passage uses two rational arguments to show that reward cannot be obligatory.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Get familiar with Sanusi's concise statement and the two reasons. Notice the key Arabic terms: واجب, جائز, اكتساب, اتصاف.
The passage is short but dense.
2
Identify the two reasons
The passage gives two arguments: no one has a right against God, and obedience is God's creation (the servant has no causal effect). Understand each.
3
Reflect on the theological implication
Consider why this matters: it protects God's absolute sovereignty and emphasizes that reward is purely by grace, not obligation.
This connects to broader Ash'ari theology.

Source Text

لا يجب على الله تعالى إثابةُ المطيع عقلاً. وكذا مِنَ الجائزاتِ: إثابةُ اللهِ تعالى المطيع؛ إذ لا حق لأحدٍ عليه تعالى؛ إذ لا نفع له تعالى بطاعة أحد، وأيضاً: فالطاعة خلق له تبارك وتعالى، وليس للعبد فيها إلا الاكتساب والاتصاف، ولا أثر له فيها أصلاً.
It is not obligatory upon God, exalted, to reward the obedient, rationally. And likewise among the permissible things: rewarding of God, exalted, for the obedient; for there is no right for anyone against Him, exalted; for there is no benefit for Him, exalted, in the obedience of anyone. Also: obedience is a creation of His, blessed and exalted, and the servant has in it only acquisition (iktisāb) and description (ittiṣāf), and he has no effect in it at all.

Commentary
This passage makes a doctrinal statement: rewarding the obedient is not rationally obligatory upon God, but is among the permissible things (jā'izāt). The author gives two reasons. First, no one has any right against God, and God derives no benefit from anyone's obedience. Second, obedience is God's creation; the servant only acquires (iktisāb) and is described (ittiṣāf) by it, and has no causal effect in bringing it about. Therefore, reward is purely by God's grace, not by obligation.
