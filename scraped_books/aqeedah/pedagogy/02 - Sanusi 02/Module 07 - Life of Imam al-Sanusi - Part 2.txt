The last panel of his life
Let us call upon the Shaykh Muhammad ibn Umar al-Mallali, the student of Imam al-Sanusi, to draw for us this panel as he tells us about the hours of meeting and departure from this transitory world to the everlasting abode. He (may Allah have mercy on him) said:
Allah ( he was, may Allah be pleased with him, hardly ever cheerful with anyone as was his custom before that, and it was difficult for him to go out to the mosque for teaching and prayer, and he would not go out to it on some days except out of shyness of the people who were waiting for him in the mosque for prayer. In his later life he was very withdrawn from people.
When he (may Allah be pleased with him) felt the pain of the illness from which he died, he stopped going to the mosque. The people heard of his illness and would come to the mosque but not find him, and their hearts changed from the loss of the Shaykh and not seeing him. So the Shaykh was informed of that, and he began to force himself to go out to the mosque for prayer for the sake of the people. When they saw him they rejoiced and were delighted by his coming out and seeing him.
Then one day he went out and came to the door of the mosque, and wanted to ascend to it, but could not. He said: 'How can I go up to the mosque, O Lord?' or something like that. He intended to return to his house, but he feared that by returning he would bring grief to the people. So he forced himself to ascend to the mosque and led the people in the afternoon prayer on Friday, and he did not complete the prayer except with difficulty of breath. This was the last prayer he prayed.
Then he returned to his house and remained until the morning of Saturday the next day. His wife brought food near him, and he said to her: 'I can manage nothing, nothing(1).' She said to him: 'What is the matter with you?' He said to her: 'I have fallen behind.' Then he lost consciousness and remained in that state all day long.
Then his wife spoke to him and said: 'What made you lose consciousness?' or something close to this. He said to her: 'The angels ascended with me to the lowest heaven, and I heard a speaker saying to me: Leave what you are doing, for your term has drawn near.' Then he said: 'I cannot explain to you the rest of what I saw,' or something like that.
His wife said to him: 'What were you commanded to leave?' He said to her: 'I have left that prison (imprisoning that thing); I will never take anything from it again.'
Then he remained bedridden from then until he died. The duration of his illness was ten days, and every hour his illness increased, his pain intensified, his strength and movement weakened, and his tongue became heavy. Yet with all that he was of sound mind, neither groaning nor moaning.
(1) Al-Mallali mentioned in 'al-Mawahib al-Qudsiyya' (folio 13) that his hardship reached such an extent that he was unable to perform tayammum and the pillars of prayer, so he said: 'May Allah have mercy on Abu Hanifa, for he ruled that they drop' – meaning prayer.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical narrative of Imam Sanusi's final days, as recounted by his student Muhammad ibn Umar al-Mallali. It begins with a description of Sanusi's increasing withdrawal from people in his later years. He found it difficult to go to the mosque for teaching and prayer, and sometimes only went out of shyness before those waiting. When he fell ill, he stopped going to the mosque, causing distress to the community. To spare them grief, he forced himself to lead the Friday afternoon prayer, his last prayer, which he could barely complete. After returning home, he collapsed and lost consciousness. When revived, he told his wife that angels had taken him to the lowest heaven, where a voice commanded him to leave his current state, as his death was near. He also mentioned having left 'that prison' (likely a practice or possession) permanently. The narrative notes his illness lasted ten days, during which his suffering increased but he remained conscious without complaint. A footnote adds that in his extreme weakness, he could not perform tayammum or prayer actions, and he invoked Abu Hanifa's ruling that such duties are dropped by those unable to perform them.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical and methodological introduction describing Imam Sanusi's innovative graded series of kalam textbooks. It begins by stating that his series is distinguished by its progressive structure: starting from a very concise summary (mu'htasar al-mukhtasar) in brief texts, moving to a summary in larger texts and concise commentaries, then to an intermediate level, and finally to a comprehensive exposition. Next, it notes that this graded approach was not entirely new—scholars like al-Ghazali and al-Taftazani had similar multi-level works—but Sanusi's innovation was his inspired foresight: he anticipated that his series would become central to future teaching, and indeed it attracted scholarly attention in his own lifetime. The passage then explains that Sanusi designed his series with all types of students in mind: from the weakest and dullest, to the mediocre, to the intelligent but easily distracted, to the naturally successful who need only a hint. This understanding drove him to create a kalam school whose curriculum spans from the most basic level (children in kuttab and common craftspeople, even women concerned with domestic duties) up to the advanced investigating scholar. The series thus ranges from 'Sughra Sughra al-Sughra' (the smallest of the small) to 'Sharh al-'Aqida al-Kubra' (the commentary on the Greater Creed), guiding the reader from blind imitation to the highest verification. The author then highlights Sanusi's reputation in kalam, quoting Sheikh Abu 'Imran al-Aghdawi who said he never saw anyone 'sift' this science like Sanusi. The passage concludes by noting the immediate success of the Sanusiyyat: students flocked to them, some of his teachers extracted a book called 'Haqa'iq al-Sanusi', and manuscript catalogs show an enormous impact through commentaries and glosses, especially on the 'Sughra' and its commentary, which received extraordinary attention. The final sentence, though cut off, restates that the wonderful aspect of Sanusi's methodology in the Sanusiyyat is the arrangement of levels. A footnote cites the source of the quote.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical reason why the Prophet is described as sayyid (master) before mawla (supporter) in Sunni creed.

Core Idea

In this passage, Sanusi explains that the order of these titles is not arbitrary but follows a logical sequence of action: one first seeks refuge from a sayyid, and then receives support from a mawla. Understanding this distinction deepens our appreciation of the Prophet's role as both refuge and helper.
