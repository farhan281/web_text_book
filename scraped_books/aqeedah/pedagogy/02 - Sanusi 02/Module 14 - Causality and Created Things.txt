Knowledge Is Created by God, Not by the Proof
Sanusi denies causality of proof and affirms divine creation of knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that in Ash'ari theology, knowledge obtained through demonstration is directly created by God, not caused by the proof or human effort, and you will be able to distinguish this view from the philosophical and Mu'tazilite positions.

Core Idea
God alone creates knowledge; demonstration is merely the occasion, not the cause.

Sanusi here addresses a crucial point: after we have used a proof to arrive at knowledge, we must not attribute that knowledge to the proof itself. Rather, we must affirm that God creates both the understanding of the proof and the understanding of its conclusion. This passage safeguards the principle of divine sovereignty over all acts of cognition, rejecting any notion of secondary causality in the realm of knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the English translation. Let the author's words establish the framework.
2
Identify the two rejected theories
Look for the phrases 'لا بطريق التعليل' and 'لا بطريق التولد'. Understand how the philosophers and Mu'tazilites explain knowledge differently.
The philosophers attribute knowledge to causation (ta'lil), the Mu'tazilites to generation (tawallud) from human action.
3
Understand the positive Ash'ari position
Focus on the statement that knowledge comes 'بمحض خلق الله فضلاً منه' and that God creates both understanding of the evidence and the thing evidenced. No other agent shares in this.
The word 'فضل' emphasizes that knowledge is a grace from God, not earned.
4
Reflect on implications
Consider why this distinction matters: if God alone creates knowledge, then human reasoning is a context but not a cause, preserving divine omnipotence.

Commentary
This passage specifies the theological framework for how knowledge (ma'rifa) is attained through demonstration. After one obtains knowledge through a proof, he must affirm that this knowledge was created directly by God as a grace (fadl), and that the demonstration itself, as well as the person's own thought and investigation, have no causal effect (athar) in producing that knowledge. The author explicitly rejects two alternative views: (1) the philosophers' theory of causation (ta'lil), where the proof causes knowledge; and (2) the Mu'tazilites' theory of generation (tawallud), where human action generates knowledge. Instead, the true position is that God alone creates both the understanding of the evidence (dalil) and the understanding of the thing evidenced (madlul) in the mind of the seeker, with no partner. This upholds absolute divine sovereignty over all acts of cognition.

Knowledge Created by God, Not by Reasoning
Sanusi explains the divine creation of knowledge and rejects causal theories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that in Ash'ari theology, knowledge obtained through reasoning is directly created by God, with no causal role for the reasoning process itself.

Core Idea
Knowledge is God's direct creation, not caused by proof or thought.

Sanusi now clarifies the proper theological understanding of how knowledge arises when a person reasons. He insists that the proof and the person's effort have no effect; rather, God creates both the understanding of the proof and the conclusion. This passage rejects two false views: the philosophers' causation and the Mu'tazilites' generation.

Commentary
This passage continues the ruling on the muqallid and explains the proper theological account of how knowledge arises after reasoning. The author explicitly rejects two erroneous views: the philosophers' theory of causation (taʿlīl), which holds that the proof produces knowledge as a natural effect, and the Muʿtazilite theory of generation (tawallud), which holds that human acts generate other acts or effects. Instead, the only real agent is God, who creates both the understanding of the proof and the understanding of the thing proven as a consequence. This underscores Ashʿarī occasionalism: all effects are directly from God, and secondary causes are merely occasions.

Knowledge After Proof: Customary or Rational Necessity?
Sanusi records the Ashʿarī and al-Juwaynī disagreement on the nature of knowledge after proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between customary necessity (lāzim ʿādatan) and rational necessity (lāzim ʿaqlan) in the context of knowledge after proof, and why al-Ash'ari's view is considered more apparent.

Core Idea
Al-Ash'ari: God's pattern is customary, not logically binding.

Sanusi now turns to a key debate among Ash'ari theologians: When God creates knowledge of a proof, does He necessarily create knowledge of the conclusion? This question touches on divine causality and whether God's actions are bound by rational necessity. In this passage, two positions are presented, and Sanusi gives his preferred view.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice the structure: a question, two opinions, and an author's preference.
2
Identify the two types of necessity
Look for the terms 'lāzim ʿādatan' (customarily necessary) and 'lāzim ʿaqlan' (rationally necessary). Understand the analogies: satiety after eating vs. accident with a body.
3
Note the condition of no impediment
The passage specifies 'without the occurrence of a specific or general impediment'. This sets the context for the disagreement.
4
Compare the two views
Al-Ash'ari says it is customarily necessary, so it is possible for knowledge to be delayed. Al-Juwaynī says it is rationally necessary, so it cannot be delayed. Contrast what each view implies about God's freedom.
5
Reflect on Sanusi's conclusion
Sanusi states that al-Ash'ari's view is 'aẓhar' (more apparent). Consider why he might favor the view that preserves God's customary pattern without logical compulsion.

And our imams differed: Is God's creation of knowledge of the thing proven (al-madlūl) after His creation of knowledge of the proof, without the occurrence of a specific impediment (āfa khāṣṣa) or a general impediment (āfa ʿāmma), a customary (ʿāda) necessary consequence, like satiety after eating, or a rational (ʿaqlan) necessary consequence, like an accident with a body, for example? So Shaykh al-Ashʿarī (may God be pleased with him) said: It is a customary necessary consequence, so it is possible for it to be delayed (takhalluf). And Imām al-Ḥaramayn [al-Juwaynī] said: It is a rational necessary consequence, so it is not possible for it to be delayed. And the more apparent is what al-Ashʿarī said, and God knows best.

Commentary
This passage presents a theological disagreement among the Ashʿarī imams concerning the relationship between the creation of knowledge of the proof and the creation of knowledge of the thing proven. Specifically, after God creates understanding of the proof, does He then necessarily create knowledge of the conclusion? The author notes that this question applies when no impediment (āfa) is present, whether specific (like doubt or ignorance) or general (like death or unconsciousness). Two views are given: Al-Ashʿarī holds that the sequence is 'customarily necessary' (lāzim ʿādatan), meaning it is God's habitual pattern but He could, in principle, withhold the knowledge, as in the case of satiety normally following eating but sometimes not. Imām al-Ḥaramayn (al-Juwaynī) holds that it is 'rationally necessary' (lāzim ʿaqlan), meaning it is inconceivable for God to create knowledge of the proof without also creating knowledge of the conclusion, like an accident cannot exist without a body. Sanusi then states that the more apparent (aẓhar) opinion is that of al-Ashʿarī, thus favoring the occasionalist view that God's customary patterns are not logically binding.

Sanusi's Refutation of Eternal World and Essential Causation
Sanusi refutes objections to divine agency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi uses the diversity and temporal delay of the world to prove that it is created by a free agent, not by a necessary cause or nature.

Core Idea
Only a free agent can explain the world's variety and temporal beginning.

In this passage, Sanusi addresses two major objections against the creation of the world: that it could be eternal, or that it could be caused by a necessary essence or nature. He shows that these alternatives are impossible because they would require uniformity and simultaneity. The observable world, however, is full of diversity and temporal delay, which can only be explained by a free agent who chooses to create differently at different times.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the opening objection carefully. Note that the objector suggests two alternatives to divine choice: the world could be eternal, or its preponderance could be by causation (ta‘līl) or nature (ṭab‘).
These are classical philosophical objections from those who deny a personal creator.
2
Analyze Sanusi's response
Sanusi argues that if the world were caused by a single cause or nature, its effects would be uniform and simultaneous. This is a logical necessity. Then he points to the observable diversity and temporal delay to contradict this.
Focus on the key terms: ‘illa (cause), ṭabī‘a (nature), athar (effect).
3
Connect to the conclusion
The conclusion is that all bodies must be created, because they all share the attribute of neediness for an agent. This refutes both the eternity of the world and natural/essential causation.
See how observation (mushāhada) is used as evidence.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer (su’āl wa-jawāb) refuting two possibilities: that the world could be eternal (qadīm) or that its preponderance could be by causation (‘illa) or nature (ṭab‘) rather than divine choice (ikhtiyār). Sanusi presents the objection: What prevents this? The answer is that if it were so, there would be no variation in magnitudes and attributes and no temporal delay from eternity, because a single cause or nature produces uniform and simultaneous effects. But observation (mushāhada) shows immense variation and temporal occurrence (ta’akhkhur), so the world cannot be caused by a single nature or essence. The delay of many bodies from eternity proves they are not produced by a necessary cause. Since all bodies share the property of being contingent (needing an agent), they must all be created. This refutes both the eternity of the world and natural/essential causation.

Refutation of Natural Causality with Conditions
Sanusi rebuts the claim that natural causes need conditions and may be delayed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the delay of the world cannot be explained by natural causes that require conditions and the absence of obstacles, and how Sanusi demonstrates the impossibility of such a scenario.

Core Idea
If nature's effect depends on conditions and no obstacles, delay would be permanent, not temporary.

The objector tries to salvage natural causality by insisting that nature only produces its effect when conditions are present and obstacles are absent. This would allow for a delay in the world's creation. Sanusi responds by showing that if the obstacle or missing condition is eternal, it would never be removed, so the world would never come to exist. This refutation strengthens the argument that the world must be created directly by a free agent, Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the objector's concession and their claim that nature's effect is conditional.
The objector concedes that delay disproves necessary cause but not natural cause.
2
Understand the objection's logic
The objector says: Nature only acts when conditions are met and obstacles removed. So the world's delay could be due to an eternal obstacle or missing condition.
3
Analyze Sanusi's first response: eternal obstacle
If an obstacle existed eternally, it would be eternal and cannot cease to exist. Therefore, it would permanently prevent the world from existing.
This leads to the conclusion that the world would never exist, which contradicts observed existence.
4
Analyze Sanusi's second response: missing condition
If a condition was absent eternally, it would never come to be because its existence would require removing an eternal obstacle or an infinite regress of conditions, both impossible.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Since both possibilities are impossible, the delay cannot be explained by natural causes. Therefore, the world must be created by a free agent.
This refutes the objector's attempt to preserve natural causality.

If it is said: There is no doubt that the delay of bodies and their attributes from eternity decisively indicates that their creation is not by way of cause; for a rational cause cannot be separated from its effect. But as for the delay indicating that creation is not by way of nature, it may not be granted; because it is established that the effect of nature according to those who affirm it among the innovators is not necessary in every case, but rather its impression follows it only when conditions are fulfilled and obstacles are removed. On this basis, the delay of the worlds from eternity is due to the existence of an obstacle preventing them in eternity, or the absence of a condition there.
The answer: That if there were an obstacle preventing the worlds from existing in eternity, it would never cease to exist; because whatever is proven eternal is impossible to cease. Then it would follow that nothing of the worlds would ever exist. And if the condition for the existence of the worlds was absent in eternity, that condition would never come to exist; so nothing of the worlds would ever exist either; because the existence of that condition in the future depends on the removal of an eternal obstacle or an infinite regress of conditions, both of which are impossible.
Source pages p. 144

Commentary
This passage contains a further objection and its refutation. The objector concedes that delay disproves necessary causal production (because a cause cannot be separated from its effect), but argues that it does not disprove natural production, because according to those who believe in natures (like some innovators), nature's effect is not always necessary; it only occurs when conditions are present and obstacles absent. So the delay of the worlds could be due to an obstacle in eternity or a missing condition. Sanusi answers: If there were an eternal obstacle, it would be eternal and thus cannot cease, so nothing would ever come into existence. If a condition was absent in eternity, that condition itself would never come to be (since its existence would require removing an eternal obstacle or an infinite regress of conditions, both impossible). Therefore, the delay cannot be explained by obstacles or conditions, so the world must be created by a free agent.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think obstacles or missing conditions could indefinitely delay a natural effect, but eventually the effect would occur when the obstacle is removed or condition appears.
Correction: Sanusi shows that if the obstacle or missing condition is eternal, it would be permanent and prevent the effect forever, so the world would never exist.

Checkpoint
According to Sanusi's answer, why cannot an eternal obstacle explain the delay of the world?

C) Because obstacles only affect physical things, not eternal ones

Key Takeaway
The delay of the world cannot be explained by natural causes with conditions, because any eternal condition or obstacle would be permanent and prevent existence forever.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Sanusi argues that any eternal obstacle would be permanent and prevent existence forever. Is there an eternal obstacle in your life—a doubt, a sin, a fear—that you have accepted as unchanging? What if that obstacle is not eternal but created by you, and God can remove it today if you turn to Him?
?
If you believe that God creates all things directly, does your heart truly rely on Him for every small outcome, or do you still trust in 'natural' processes as if they operate independently? Where in your life do you act as if conditions and obstacles are the real determinants?
?
Sanusi says that a missing condition in eternity would never come to exist by itself. Is there a condition you are waiting for before you fully commit to God—like perfect circumstances, more knowledge, or a sign? What if that condition never comes, and God is calling you to act now without it?
