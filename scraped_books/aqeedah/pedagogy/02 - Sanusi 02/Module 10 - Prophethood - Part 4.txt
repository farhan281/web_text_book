Critique of Improper Comparisons Among Prophets
Sanusi refutes improper comparisons and answers a defense.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's critique of comparing prophets in a way that implies belittlement, and his response to the defense of such comparisons.

Core Idea
Comparing prophets can inadvertently belittle them; we cannot presume to know what superiority requires.

Sanusi, a master of Ash'ari theology, turns his attention to a common scholarly practice that he finds burdensome: the tendency to compare prophets by highlighting the greatness of Prophet Muhammad at the expense of others. He gives a concrete example and then addresses the objection that such comparisons are justified by the Prophet's superiority. This passage challenges us to think carefully about how we honor all prophets.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic text once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Let Sanusi's tone and argument come through.
Notice that Sanusi says 'I have always found it burdensome'.
2
Identify the problematic method
Find the two examples Sanusi gives of how scholars compare prophets. Understand why he considers this method degrading.
The comparison of water gushing from a rock vs. from fingers is a key example.
3
Examine the objection and answer
Read the final part where someone argues that such comparisons are required by Prophet Muhammad's superiority. How does Sanusi respond?
Sanusi's answer is sharp: 'From where do you have that?'

Commentary
This passage presents Sanusi's critique of a common scholarly practice: comparing prophets in a way that diminishes the status of other prophets to elevate Prophet Muhammad. He finds this practice burdensome (أستثقل). He gives an example: the miracle of water gushing from a rock for Moses versus water from between the Prophet Muhammad's fingers. Scholars claim the latter is greater because fingers are not accustomed to water, while rocks are. Sanusi notes that a contemporary poet composed a poem balancing Prophet Muhammad's miracles against all other prophets' miracles. Sanusi judges that the poet did well in the derivation (استنباط) but ill because it implies belittlement (الغض والانحطاط) of other prophets. He then presents an objection: some might argue that such comparisons are required by Prophet Muhammad's superiority. Sanusi's answer is sharp: from where do you know that? The actual requirements of his superiority are unknown to us. This underscores that we cannot presume to know how superiority manifests.

What would change in your daily speech if you took this seriously?

The Proper Source of the Prophet's Superiority
Sanusi criticizes a poet's comparison of miracles and answers an objection about the Prophet's superiority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sanusi criticizes the poet's comparison of miracles and how we should derive the Prophet's superiority exclusively from revelation, not from our own reasoning.

Core Idea
We know the Prophet's superiority only from his own report, not by comparing miracles.

Sanusi addresses a contemporary poet who composed a poem matching the miracles of all prophets with those of Prophet Muhammad. While praising the derivation, he warns that this approach risks belittling other prophets. He then responds to the objection that this is required by the Prophet's superiority, clarifying that we cannot know the details of that superiority by our own reasoning—only through what the Prophet himself informed us.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the poet's action
Read the Arabic passage carefully. Notice that Sanusi first describes the poet's composition and gives a dual assessment: he did well in derivation but poorly in what is understood from it.
The poet attempted to match all miracles of previous prophets with those of Prophet Muhammad.
2
Examine the objection and Sanusi's response
The objector claims that such matching is required by the Prophet's superiority. Sanusi challenges: 'From where do you have that?' This is a crucial turning point.
3
Understand the epistemological basis
Sanusi explains that we do not know the details of the Prophet's superiority from ourselves. We only know it from his own reports, which are either general or specific statements like 'I have been given such and such.'
Our knowledge is limited to what the Prophet told us.
4
Grasp the consequence of overstepping
If we go beyond the revealed reports and try to deduce superiority by comparing miracles or attributes, we fall into ill-adab (bad manners) and an inextricable difficulty. The correct path is to confine ourselves to what the Prophet informed, as the imams did.
This is the key warning of the passage.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer argument combined with a critique of an improper method. The author first criticizes a contemporary poet who composed a poem matching all the miracles of previous prophets with those of Prophet Muhammad. The poet's method of deriving parallels is praised, but the implication of belittling previous prophets is condemned. This leads to a potential objection: someone might say that such matching is required by the Prophet's superiority. The author answers sharply: 'From where do you have that?' He explains that we do not know the details of what the Prophet's superiority entails from our own reasoning. We only know it from the Prophet himself, and even then only general matters or specific explicit statements. Therefore, we must confine ourselves to what he reported and not go beyond to speculate about why he is superior based on comparing miracles or attributes. Doing so would lead to 'ill-adab' (improper conduct) with the Prophet's loved ones (i.e., previous prophets) and falling into an inextricable difficulty. The author concludes that the correct path, as followed by the imams, is to avoid such comparisons and accept the superiority based solely on divine report.

The Danger of Independent Reasoning About Prophetic Superiority
Sanusi contrasts proper reliance on prophetic report with dangerous independent reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why we must not independently reason about the reasons for a prophet's superiority but must rely on what the Prophet informed us.

Core Idea
Prophetic superiority is known only through divine report, not human reasoning.

Sanusi begins by clarifying the proper epistemological approach to the question of prophetic superiority. He warns that attempting to deduce the causes of a prophet's excellence through our own observation of his attributes leads to ill-adab. Instead, we must accept what the Prophet himself has told us and stop there.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how Sanusi presents two scenarios: one safe and one dangerous.
The passage is a single conditional argument.
2
Identify the two scenarios
Find the 'if' and 'otherwise' parts. What is the safe approach? What leads to ill-adab?
3
Reflect on the role of revelation
Consider why Sanusi insists that we cannot know superiority from ourselves. What does this teach about the limits of human reason in theology?

Commentary
This passage is a theological and ethical argument about the proper attitude towards the superiority of prophets, particularly the Prophet Muhammad. The author, Sanusi, warns against attempting to deduce or rationalize the reasons for a prophet's superiority by examining his attributes or signs independently, as this leads to ill-adab (lack of proper respect) towards the prophets and ultimately towards God.

The author begins by stating that we cannot know from ourselves the comprehensive realities or details of prophetic superiority; we only know them from the Prophet himself. From him, we know general matters whose true nature only God knows, and some specific matters like his statements about what he was given. If we simply believe in his superiority based on his own report and restrict ourselves to that, without trying to seek out causes through our own observation of his signs, attributes, and states, we are correct and safe from ill-adab. Otherwise, ill-adab is inevitable.

Sanusi then uses a metaphor: those who engage in such independent reasoning are not like one who demolishes a palace to build a city or vice versa, but like one who demolishes both. The explanation is that the superior (al-afḍal) does not like to be favored by something that God did not make the cause of his superiority, nor does he like for the excellent one to be lowered in rank. The inferior (al-mafḍūl) also does not like for his inferiority to be attributed to a cause God did not make, nor does he like to be distinguished from the superior. All prophets are messengers of God, and their dislike of these things is for God's right, not for themselves. Thus, ill-adab with them becomes ill-adab with God.

Finally, Sanusi connects this to the issue of the names of prophets: the names by which God named them should not be compared in terms of which is more honorable, from the perspective of God's naming. As for when a person names himself, he should only choose the name 'servant' (ʿabd), as the Prophet chose to be a prophet-servant rather than a prophet-king.

Commentary
This passage is a theological and ethical argument about the proper attitude towards the superiority of prophets, particularly the Prophet Muhammad. قلنا : إنَّما يصح الفخرُ بها إنْ صَحَّ مِنْ حيثُ كونُها مِنَّةٌ مِنَ اللهِ تعالى عليه ، فإن صح الفخرُ بها مِنْ هذا الوجه.. فلِمَ لا يصح افتخاره بالسيادة ، وهي أيضاً مِنَّةٌ مِنَ اللهِ تعالى عليهِ ؟!
فالظاهرُ : أَنَّهُ عليهِ الصلاة والسلامُ نفى التفاخر النفي المطلق ولم يَخُصَّ ذلك بسيادة ولا غيرها ، كما قال صلَّى اللهُ عليهِ وسلَّمَ : ( أَنَا سَيْدُ وَلَدِ آدَمَ وَلا فَخْرَ ، وَأَنَا حَامِلُ لِوَاءِ الْحَمْدِ يَوْمَ الْقِيَامَةِ وَلا فَخْرَ، وَأَنَا أَوَّلُ مَنْ تَنْشَقُ عَنْهُ الْأَرْضُ وَلَا فَخْرَ ، وَأَنَا أَوَّلُ شَافِعِ وَأَوَّلُ مُشَفّع وَلا فَخْرَ، وَأَنَا أَوَّلُ مَنْ يُحَرِّكُ حِلَقَ الْجَنَّةِ فَأَدْخُلُهَا مَعَ فُقَرَاءِ الْمُؤْمِنِينَ وَلا فَخْرَ ، وَأَنَا أَكْرَمُ الأَوَّلِينَ وَالْآخِرِينَ وَلا فَخْرَ )
فبانَ لكَ بهذا كلّهِ : أنَّ إطلاق الأولوية والأشرفية في بعض الأسماء دونَ بعض مِنْ غيرِ نظر إلى ما ذكرناه مِنْ تسمية الله تعالى وتسمية غيرِه.. قصور في النظرِ ( انتهى بلفظه ، وقليل منه بالمعنى )
And the statement of the one who said about the meaning of the hadith: 'Pride is only for me in servitude' is speech I do not understand; because the relationship of servitude to him and to others is one relationship. If it is said: 'He meant thereby the servitude which is his state and station.' We say: 'Pride in it is only valid if it is correct from the aspect of it being a favor from God upon him. If pride in it is valid from this aspect, then why is his pride in mastership not valid, and it also is a favor from God upon him?' So the apparent meaning is that he, peace and blessings be upon him, denied boasting absolutely and did not single out mastership or anything else, as he said, peace and blessings be upon him: 'I am the master of the children of Adam, and no pride; I am the bearer of the banner of praise on the Day of Resurrection, and no pride; I am the first from whom the earth will split open, and no pride; I am the first intercessor and the first whose intercession is accepted, and no pride; I am the first to move the rings of Paradise and enter it with the poor believers, and no pride; I am the most honorable of the first and the last, and no pride.' So it became clear to you by all this that applying priority and nobility to some names without consideration of what we mentioned of naming God and naming others is a shortcoming in insight. (Ended with his wording, and a little with meaning.)

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an interpretation of a hadith in which the Prophet is reported to have said 'I am the master of the children of Adam, and I am not boasting.' Some people claimed that the Prophet meant that his only pride is in his servitude to God. The author rejects this interpretation as unintelligible, because the attribute of servitude is relative and applies equally to the Prophet and to others. If it is argued that what is meant is a particular state of servitude that is unique to the Prophet, the author responds that if pride in that state is permissible because it is a grace from God, then pride in his mastership (sayyid) should also be permissible, as that too is a grace from God. The author then asserts that the apparent meaning of the hadith is that the Prophet denied all forms of boasting absolutely, not just in certain titles. This is supported by multiple hadiths where the Prophet enumerates his honors—being the master of all humans, the bearer of the banner of praise, the first to be resurrected, the first intercessor, the first to enter Paradise—and after each he says 'and no pride.' The author concludes that applying priority and honor to some names (like 'sayyid') without considering the distinction between divine names and human names is a deficiency in insight. The passage thus clarifies the correct understanding of the Prophet's humility and rejection of any form of self-praise.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The Prophet denied pride only in his servitude, meaning he was proud of his mastership.
Correction: Sanusi shows that the Prophet denied all boasting absolutely, including in his mastership and other honors, as demonstrated by the hadiths where he says 'no pride' after each honor.

Checkpoint
What is the main point the author makes about the Prophet's statement 'I am the master of the children of Adam, and no pride'?
A) The Prophet was proud of his servitude.
B) The Prophet denied all forms of boasting absolutely, not just in specific honors.
C) The Prophet only denied pride in his mastership, not in other honors.
D) The Prophet's statement is ambiguous and cannot be understood.

Key Takeaway
The Prophet's denial of boasting is absolute; no honor, including mastership, justifies pride.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you achieve something significant—whether in your career, education, or even religious practice—do you feel a sense of pride that separates you from others? How does the Prophet's absolute denial of pride challenge the way you view your own accomplishments? Will you take a concrete step this week to acknowledge a success without any trace of self-congratulation?
?
Think of a time you silently considered yourself 'better' than another believer because of your knowledge, piety, or status. How does Sanusi's argument that servitude is equal for all press on that feeling? Are you willing to ask God tonight to purify your heart from that hidden pride?
?
The Prophet mentioned his unique honors but immediately denied boasting about them. What honors do you hold onto—perhaps your lineage, your role in the community, or your sacrifices for Islam—that you secretly feel give you a right to be recognized? Will you surrender that right today and seek only God's pleasure?
