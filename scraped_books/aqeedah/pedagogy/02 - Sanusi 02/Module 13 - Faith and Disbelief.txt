Certainty Alone Is Not Enough: Conjecture, Doubt, and False Certainty
Sanusi rules out conjecture, doubt, misgiving, and incorrect certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that valid faith requires both certainty (jazzm) and correctness (muṭābaqa), and that conjecture, doubt, misgiving, or mistaken certainty are insufficient by scholarly consensus.

Core Idea
Certainty alone is not enough—it must be correct certainty.

Sanusi now addresses a crucial refinement: what kind of knowledge suffices for the obligations of faith? He rules out not only weak forms like conjecture and doubt but also confidently held false beliefs. This passage establishes that saving faith requires both the quality of certainty and the truth of its object.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Let the author's two-part structure become clear.
Notice the consensus language (bi-ijmāʿ) at the end of each part.
2
Identify the two scenarios ruled out
The passage first rules out conjecture, doubt, and misgiving. Then it rules out certainty that does not match reality. Underline both conditions.
The second scenario uses the example of disbelievers who are certain of falsehood.
3
Understand the consensus argument
Sanusi uses 'by consensus' (bi-ijmāʿ) to show that this ruling is not his personal opinion but the agreed position of the scholars.
Consensus gives the ruling authority beyond individual reasoning.
4
Reflect on the implication for faith
Ask yourself: Why is it not enough to just feel certain? What does this teach us about the nature of true faith?
Faith is not just psychological conviction; it must correspond to objective truth.

If the legally responsible person does not obtain certainty (jazzm) concerning these three matters regarding God Most High and regarding His messengers, peace and blessings be upon them, but rather only obtains conjecture (dhann), doubt (shakk), or misgiving (wahm), that will not suffice him, by consensus. And if he obtains certainty, but it does not conform to what is actually the case—like the certainty of the Jews, Christians, and all the disbelievers in the disbeliefs they were certain about—that will not suffice him, nor will he be excused for it, by consensus.

Commentary
This passage clarifies that mere certainty is not enough; it must be correct certainty. The author first states that if a person only has conjecture (dhann), doubt (shakk), or misgiving (wahm) regarding the required beliefs about God and His messengers, that is insufficient by scholarly consensus. Then he adds that even if they have certainty (jazzm), but it does not match reality—as in the example of Jews, Christians, and other disbelievers who hold false beliefs with certainty—such certainty is also insufficient and they are not excused, again by consensus. This underscores that valid faith requires both the quality of certainty and the correctness of its object.

Is Knowledge Itself Faith or Its Prerequisite?
Sanusi contrasts two kalam positions on the relation between knowledge and faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two kalam views on whether knowledge of God and His messengers is identical to faith or merely a prerequisite that leads to inner assent.

Core Idea
Faith is not just knowledge; it is inner assent that follows knowledge.

In this passage, Sanusi addresses a key question in kalam: does the knowledge we gain through rational proofs about God and His messengers constitute faith itself, or is faith something else that comes after? The Ash'ari school equates knowledge with faith, while al-Bāqillānī distinguishes knowledge as a necessary condition for the inner speech of faith. Let's explore both positions.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the two positions presented.
2
Identify the two schools
Find the two views: the Ash'ari view (that knowledge is faith) and the Qadi's view (that knowledge is a necessary condition, and faith is inner speech).
The Qadi is Abu Bakr al-Baqillani.
3
Reflect on the linguistic argument
Notice that some imams preferred the Qadi's view because it aligns with the Arabic meaning of iman as 'assent'.
Think about why linguistic appropriateness matters in theology.

ثمَّ المعرفة بهذهِ الثلاثةِ في حقٌّ الله تعالى وفي حقٌّ رسلِهِ عليهم الصلاة والسلام : هل هي نفس الإيمان الذي كُلفنا بهِ ؛ وهو مذهبُ الأشعري ، أو ملزومةٌ للإيمان، فيكون الإيمان هو حديث النفس التابع لتلك المعرفةِ ؛ وهو مذهب القاضي ، وصحَّحَهُ بعضُ الأئمةِ ؛ لأنَّهُ أنسبُ لمعنى الإيمانِ لغةً ، وبالله تعالى التوفيق
Then, the knowledge (maʿrifa) of these three things with respect to God Almighty and with respect to His messengers (peace and blessings be upon them): Is it the very faith (īmān) that we are obligated to have? — That is the school of al-Ashʿarī. Or is it a necessary condition (malzūma) for faith, so that faith is the inner speech (ḥadīth al-nafs) that follows that knowledge? That is the school of al-Qāḍī [Abū Bakr al-Bāqillānī], and some imams deemed it correct because it is more appropriate to the linguistic meaning of faith (īmān). And with God is success.
Source pages p. 131

Commentary
This passage raises the question of whether the knowledge (maʿrifa) of the three fundamental matters concerning God and His messengers—i.e., the knowledge that results from the rational proofs discussed earlier—is identical to the faith (īmān) that humans are commanded to have, or whether it is something separate that is a prerequisite. The text presents two views: 1) The school of al-Ashʿarī holds that this knowledge is itself faith; i.e., faith is essentially knowledge. 2) The school of al-Qāḍī Abū Bakr al-Bāqillānī holds that knowledge is a 'necessary condition' (malzūma) for faith, and that faith is actually 'inner speech' (ḥadīth al-nafs) which follows that knowledge. Some later imams preferred al-Qāḍī's view because it aligns better with the linguistic meaning of īmān as 'assent' or 'acquiescence' (taṣdīq). The author does not decisively choose but notes the latter was corrected by some. (A footnote on the page corrects 'malzūma' to 'lāzima' [necessary concomitant], indicating that knowledge is a concomitant of faith, not a cause.) Sanusi concludes with 'And with God is success', leaving the issue open but indicating the linguistic reasoning for the Qāḍī's view.

Key Takeaway

Knowledge and Faith: Identity or Concomitance?
Sanusi presents the debate on maʿrifa and īmān with a terminological correction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two main positions on the relationship between knowledge of God and faith, and the correct terminology regarding causality.

Core Idea
Knowledge of God is a necessary result of faith, not its cause.

The relationship between knowledge (maʿrifa) and faith (īmān) is a subtle but important point in Islamic theology. In this passage, Sanusi presents two competing views and then corrects a common terminological error. Understanding this distinction helps clarify the nature of faith and its prerequisites.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice the technical terms معرفة and إيمان.
The passage includes a footnote with a crucial correction.
2
Identify the two views
Locate al-Ash'ari's view (knowledge = faith) and al-Qāḍī's view (knowledge is the source, faith is inner speech). Ask yourself what difference this makes.
3
Understand the correction
Read the footnote carefully. It corrects the terminology: knowledge is a necessary concomitant (lāzima) of faith, not its source (malzūma). Reflect on the Quranic evidence given.
This corrects a logical misunderstanding.
4
Apply to the broader context
Consider how this relates to the definition of faith and its opposites. The passage also mentions general infirmities that negate perception.

هل المعرفة بالمذكورات هي الإيمان نفسه أو لازمةٌ عنه؟ ثمَّ المعرفة بهذهِ الثلاثةِ في حقٌّ الله تعالى وفي حقٌّ رسلِهِ عليهم الصلاة والسلام: هل هي نفس الإيمان الذي كُلفنا بهِ؛ وهو مذهبُ الأشعري، أو ملزومةٌ للإيمان (1)، فيكون الإيمان هو حديث النفس التابع لتلك المعرفةِ؛ وهو مذهب القاضي، وصحَّحَهُ بعضُ الأئمةِ؛ لأنَّهُ أنسبُ لمعنى الإيمانِ لغةً، وبالله تعالى التوفيق. ويضادُّهُ، والآفة العامة: كالموت والإغماء والنوم والجنون؛ مما ينافي الإدراك للمطلوب. (1) عبَّرَ في (الوسطى) (ص150) بلفظ: (لازمة) لا (ملزومة)، وهو الصواب، وفي هامش (و): (صوابه: أو لازمة؛ إذ المعرفة المذكورة لازمة للإيمان، لا ملزومةٌ له؛ لما أنَّ الإيمان بمعنى التصديق والإذعان إذا وجد لزمته تلك المعرفةُ، ولا يلزم من حصول تلك المعرفة فقط الإيمان، ألا ترى إلى قوله تعالى: ﴿ وَجَحَدُوا بِهَا وَاسْتَيْقَنَتْهَا أَنفُسُهُمْ ﴾ [النمل: 14]، فإن المعرفة وجدت فيهم ولم يلزمها الإيمان، ولو كانت المعرفة ملزومة . . لاستحال تخلفُ الإيمان حينئذ؛ لأن تخلف اللازم عن ملزومه محال عقلاً).
Is knowledge of the aforementioned [three judgments] itself faith or a concomitant of it? Then knowledge of these three concerning Allah Almighty and concerning His messengers, peace be upon them: is it the very faith with which we are charged? That is the doctrine of al-Ash'ari. Or is it that which has faith as a concomitant (malzūma) (1), so that faith is the inner speech following that knowledge? That is the doctrine of al-Qāḍī, and some imams deemed it correct because it is more suitable to the linguistic meaning of faith. And with Allah is success. And it is opposed by general infirmities such as death, unconsciousness, sleep, and madness, which negate perception of the required [knowledge]. (1) He expressed in al-Wusṭā (p. 150) with the term (lāzima) not (malzūma), and that is correct. And in the margin of (w): (Its correction is: or a concomitant (lāzima), for the aforementioned knowledge is a concomitant of faith, not that which has faith as a concomitant, because faith in the sense of affirmation (taṣdīq) and submission, when it exists, that knowledge necessarily accompanies it, but from the mere presence of that knowledge, faith does not necessarily follow. Do you not see the saying of the Exalted: 'And they rejected them, while their souls were convinced of them' [Quran 27:14]? For knowledge existed in them but faith did not follow it. If knowledge were that which has faith as a concomitant (malzūma), then faith would be impossible to be absent when it is present, because the absence of the concomitant (lāzim) from its source (malzūm) is intellectually impossible.)
Source pages p. 131

Commentary
This passage addresses the relationship between maʿrifa (knowledge of the three rational judgments concerning God and His messengers) and īmān (faith). Two views are presented: (1) Al-Ash'ari's view that this knowledge is itself the faith that is required. (2) Al-Qāḍī's view (probably al-Bāqillānī) that this knowledge is the source (malzūma) from which faith necessarily arises, with faith being the inner speech (ḥadīth al-nafs) that follows that knowledge. The author notes that some imams corrected this view as more linguistically appropriate. The footnotes correct the terminology: the knowledge is a concomitant (lāzima) of faith, not its source (malzūma). Proof: faith (as affirmation and submission) necessitates knowledge, but knowledge does not necessitate faith, as shown by the Quranic verse where the disbelievers had certain knowledge yet did not have faith. The passage also briefly mentions what opposes faith: general infirmities (death, unconsciousness, sleep, madness) that negate the perception of the required knowledge.
