Refutation of Divine Corporeality and Change
Sanusi refutes corporeality and change in Allah using Ibrahim's verse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi refutes the attribution of dimensions and change to Allah using a logical argument derived from the Quranic verse of Ibrahim.

Core Idea
Change implies temporality; what is temporal cannot be God.

Sanusi presents a decisive refutation of those who ascribe physical dimensions or change to Allah. He draws on the Quranic account of Prophet Ibrahim, who rejects the worship of celestial bodies because they set—and whatever sets is changeable, hence not eternal. This short argument encapsulates a foundational principle of Islamic theology: Allah is beyond all change and imperfection.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once and then its translation. Notice the strong refutation tone and the logical flow.
The passage builds from a false belief to a Quranic proof to a syllogism.
2
Identify the two false beliefs
The passage refutes two ideas: that Allah has dimensions (ab'ad) and that He undergoes change (tabaddul/taghayyur). Note how they are linked.
Both imply limitation and temporality.
3
Analyze the Quranic citation
The verse from Ibrahim (AS) 'I do not love those that set' is used as the starting point. What does 'setting' imply about change?
Setting is a type of change—a celestial body moves from visibility to invisibility.
4
Trace the logical syllogism
The argument: Everything that sets (aafil) is changeable (mutaghayyir). Everything changeable is not eternal (qadim). Therefore, nothing that sets can be God. Apply this to Allah: He does not set or change.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum against corporeal conceptions of God.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of those who attribute physical dimensions (ab'ad) or attributes of change (tabaddul, taghayyur) to Allah. The author argues that such a person is ignorant of the Quranic verse where Prophet Ibrahim says 'I do not like those that set' (Al-An'am:76), referring to the setting of celestial bodies. The logical argument is: everything that sets (aafil) is changeable (mutaghayyir). Everything changeable cannot be eternal (qadim). Therefore, it cannot be God (ilah). The argument uses a syllogism: major premise—everything changeable is not eternal; minor premise—everything that sets is changeable; conclusion—the setting celestial bodies are not eternal and hence not God. Applied to God: one cannot attribute change to Him, as change implies contingency and thus being created, contradicting His eternality.

Refuting Eternal or Causal Origin – The Necessity of a Free Creator
Sanusi refutes the possibility of an eternal or causally necessitated world.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the world cannot be eternal or produced by a necessary cause, and how its observed variation and temporal sequence prove it must be created by a free agent.

Core Idea
A single necessary cause cannot produce variety and delay – only free choice can.

This lesson addresses a critical objection: what if the world is eternal or produced by a necessary cause rather than God's free will? Sanusi presents a powerful argument that such views are contradicted by the obvious diversity and temporal sequence we observe in the world. Let's see how he uses this simple observation to refute both ideas.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Start by reading the objection in Arabic and English. Notice the two possibilities it raises: the world could be eternal (no creator) or caused by nature (a necessary cause rather than free choice).
The objector is questioning why free will is necessary.
2
Identify Sanusi's key reasoning
Sanusi argues that if the world were eternal or produced by a single necessary cause, its quantities and attributes would be uniform and simultaneous with the cause. This is a logical principle: one cause produces one uniform effect.
3
Apply the reasoning to observed reality
Now consider the world as we see it: are its quantities and attributes uniform? No – they differ infinitely. Are they all simultaneous? No – many are temporally delayed. This contradicts what would be expected from a single necessary cause.
Think of examples: different shapes, sizes, times of appearance.
4
Draw the conclusion
Since the world shows diversity and temporal delay, it cannot be eternal or from a single necessary cause. Therefore, it must be created by a free agent who chooses among possibilities.
This is a classic kalam argument for God's free will.

The Attribute of Being Unlike Created Things (Mukhalafat al-Hawadith)
Sanusi defines and proves the attribute of Allah being unlike all contingent things.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof for why Allah must be unlike all contingent things in essence and attributes.

Core Idea
If Allah were like contingent things, He would be contingent — impossible for the Eternal.

Having established that Allah is eternal and everlasting, Sanusi now turns to a necessary consequence: His incomparability. This passage shows that any resemblance to creation would force contingency, which is impossible. The proof proceeds by analyzing what would follow if Allah were similar to contingent things, both in essence and attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read and translate the passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the formal structure: a statement of obligation, a conditional (otherwise), an explanation, and a detailed proof.
2
Identify the proof structure
The proof is a conditional argument: if Allah were similar to contingent things, He would be contingent. But He is eternal, so similarity is impossible. Trace how Sanusi splits this into two parts: essence and attributes.
3
Study the list of impossible attributes
Sanusi lists corporeality, accidentality, dimensions, directions, etc. Reflect on why each of these entails contingency or temporality.
These are necessary concomitants of contingent things.
4
Understand the attribute-based proof
If Allah's attributes resembled contingent attributes, they would be contingent. Since His essence cannot be without attributes, the essence would then be contingent as well. This is a key subtlety.
The principle: whatever necessarily accompanies a contingent thing is itself contingent.
5
Grasp the conclusion
The premise of similarity leads to an impossibility (contingency for the Eternal). Therefore, the premise is false, and the opposite — dissimilarity — is established.

The attribute of being unlike created things belongs to Him, Glorified be He. And it is necessary that the Exalted be different in His essence and His attributes from everything other than Him of contingent things, otherwise He would be contingent like them. Meaning: When it was established by decisive proof previously the necessity of eternality and everlastingness for the Exalted, it followed that His exalted essence and His elevated attributes are not of the genus of contingent things. Therefore, corporeality, accidentality, and every necessary concomitant of them that entails contingency are impossible for His essence and His attributes, such as dimensions, directions, times, proximity and distance by space, smallness and bigness, contact, motion, and rest. For if His exalted essence or His elevated attributes were characterized by resemblance to contingent things, it would follow that He is contingent. As for the necessity of His contingency in the resemblance of His essence to contingent things, it is apparent. And as for the necessity of His contingency in the resemblance of His attributes to contingent things: because when it then follows that His attributes would be contingent, and the essence cannot be devoid of attributes, it follows that the essence would be contingent like its attributes; because whatever necessarily accompanies a contingent thing is necessarily contingent. This is the meaning of our saying: 'otherwise He would be contingent like them'; i.e., if He were not different but rather similar, and if He were similar to them, He would be contingent like them; because whatever is established for one of two similars is established for the other, and His contingency is impossible due to what preceded of the necessity of His eternality and everlastingness. Thus, its necessary condition is invalidated—namely, His being similar—and the desired conclusion is established—namely, His being unlike contingent things.

Commentary
This passage defines and proves the attribute of mukhalafat al-hawadith (being unlike created things). It begins by stating the obligation: Allah must be different in His essence and attributes from all contingent things. The reason given is that if He were similar, He would be contingent like them. The explanation (يعني) expands: since it has been decisively proven that Allah is eternal and everlasting, His essence and attributes cannot be of the same genus as contingent things. Therefore, it is impossible for Him to possess corporeality, accidentality, or any necessary concomitants that entail contingency, such as dimensions, directions, times, spatial proximity/distance, smallness/largeness, contact, motion, and rest. If His essence or attributes were to resemble contingent things, He would be contingent. The proof is then split into two parts: (1) The contingency resulting from similitude of essence is obvious. (2) The contingency resulting from similitude of attributes: if His attributes were contingent (because they resemble contingent attributes), then His essence would also necessarily be contingent because the essence cannot be devoid of attributes, and whatever is necessarily concomitant with a contingent thing is itself contingent. This is the meaning of the statement 'otherwise He would be contingent like them.' The conclusion is that if He were not different but similar, He would be contingent, which is impossible given His necessary existence. Therefore, the premise (that He is similar) is false, and the desired conclusion (that He is unlike contingent things) is established.

Two False Conclusions from Corrupt Analogies: Anthropomorphism and Atheism
Sanusi identifies two false conclusions from corrupt analogies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a single flawed analogy can lead to two opposite theological errors: anthropomorphism and atheism.

Core Idea
A faulty analogy can lead to both extreme anthropomorphism and outright atheism.

This passage from Sanusi warns against drawing conclusions about Allah based on imperfect analogies with created things. He shows that the same corrupt reasoning can lead to two opposite errors: some end up attributing a body to God, while others deny His existence altogether. Understanding this helps us appreciate the importance of sound analogical reasoning in theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation to understand the two errors.
Notice the common premise about agents in the contingent world.
2
Identify the common premise
Both errors start from the observation that agents in the created world are bodies.
3
Trace the two conclusions
See how one group concludes God is a body, the other denies God because a body cannot create.
These are opposite conclusions from the same premise.
4
Reflect on the warning
Understand why using a valid commonality (jami') is crucial in analogy.
Without it, reasoning becomes corrupt.

First, the anthropomorphists (mujassima) concluded that God is a body because every agent in the contingent world is a body. Second, another group, based on the same premise, concluded that no agent exists because if God were a body He could not create, so they denied God's existence altogether (ta'til). These are two extremes resulting from the same flawed reasoning.

The Principle of Comprehensiveness in Interpreting Forgiveness
Sanusi introduces the principle and linguistic interpretation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi interprets the verse 'li-yaghfira laka Allahu' using the principle of 'taking the ends to indicate comprehensiveness' and the linguistic meaning of forgiveness as covering.

Core Idea
Forgiveness in the verse means covering, not pardoning actual sin.

Sanusi approaches a potentially problematic verse that seems to ascribe sin to the Prophet. He applies a sophisticated hermeneutic principle: the mention of extremes implies totality. This allows him to maintain the Prophet's sinlessness while affirming God's comprehensive covering of any imperfection.

Guided Path
1
Read the verse and identify the issue
Begin by reading the verse from Surah Al-Fath: 'That Allah may forgive you what has passed of your sin and what will come.' Notice that it appears to attribute sin to the Prophet.
This verse is often questioned by critics of prophethood.
2
Understand the principle of taking ends for comprehensiveness
Sanusi explains that the verse is a case of 'taking the ends to indicate comprehensiveness', like saying 'I read the Quran from its beginning to its end'. The mention of 'what has passed' and 'what will come' covers all sins.
This is a common Arabic rhetorical device.

Sanusi then clarifies that 'maghfirah' here means 'sitr' (covering) in the linguistic sense, not the technical term for pardoning sin. He also reads 'min' as 'an' (away from), so the covering is from the causes and effects of sin, not the sin itself.
This preserves the Prophet's infallibility.

And their saying: 'With Your praise' they mean: we declare You free from imperfection while being praisers of You; i.e., praising You with every perfection in every state; so the bā' is for accompaniment. Or we declare freedom from imperfection because of the grace of Your success that necessitates Your praise and thanks, not through any power from us; so the bā' here is causal, and it is an expression of the effect instead of the cause, because praise (al-ḥamd) meaning gratitude is an effect of blessings. And it is possible that the meaning is: we declare freedom from imperfection by virtue of Your praise itself; i.e., praising You with every perfection, because praising with perfection is a declaration of freedom from its opposite; so the bā' is instrumental, and Allah Almighty knows best. And their saying: 'And we sanctify for You' they mean, and Allah Almighty knows best: we sanctify ourselves; i.e., purify them from every evil thought for You; i.e., for Your pleasure and independence from everything besides You. And it is possible that the meaning is: You purify our hearts for Your service and worship; because service and worship are only valid with a heart pure from all impurities. As for His answer, glorified and exalted, to them with His saying: 'Indeed, I know what you do not know': its meaning, and Allah Almighty knows best: I, even if I place on earth one who will cause corruption and shed blood, yet I know in that of wisdoms and interests that occur by pure choice, not by necessity and obligation, that which you cannot encompass with knowledge. And the remainder of the meanings in the verse belong to exegesis, and with Allah Almighty is success.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the angels' statement 'We declare Your praise' (biḥamdika) and 'We sanctify for You' (wa nuqaddisu laka) from Surah Al-Baqarah, followed by the meaning of Allah's response. The author first analyzes the phrase 'biḥamdika'. He gives three potential interpretations for the bā': 1) accompaniment (li-l-muṣāḥabah): they declare God's transcendence while praising Him in every state; 2) causality (sababiyyah): they declare transcendence because of the grace of God's tawfīq which necessitates praise; 3) instrumentality (ālah): they declare transcendence by the very act of praising Him, since praising with perfection itself negates imperfection. For 'wa nuqaddisu laka', he gives two readings: a) they sanctify themselves (purify from evil thoughts) for God's sake; b) God purifies their hearts for His service. Finally, he explains Allah's reply 'I know what you do not know' as indicating that divine wisdom and interests occur through pure choice (ikhtiyār), not necessitation (luzūm), and that human knowledge cannot encompass them. The author then defers further meanings to tafsīr.

Misconception & Correction

Correction: Sanusi shows that their words contain principles about divine transcendence, the nature of praise, and the role of free will in divine actions.
