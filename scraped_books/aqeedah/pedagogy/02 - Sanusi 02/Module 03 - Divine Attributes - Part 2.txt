Proofs for the Existence and Necessity of Divine Knowledge
Sanusi presents a proof for the existence of divine knowledge from the dependency of contingent events on will, and a proof for its necessity from the universality of its relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi proves the existence and necessity of divine knowledge through its relation to will and the principle of universality.

Core Idea
Every contingent thing depends on both will and knowledge; limitation in knowledge implies origination.

Sanusi now provides a two-fold proof: first, he shows that every originated thing points to the same four demands as power and will, establishing that knowledge is a necessary attribute. Second, he argues that the proof of necessity is derived from the universality of its relation—if specification causes origination, then outright possibility would also cause origination.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage. Notice how Sanusi connects this proof to earlier discussions on power and will.
He mentions that every originated thing indicates 'the four demands' (al-maṭālib al-arba‘a).
2
Understand the dependency on will
Sanusi says that just as existence depends on will, it also depends on knowledge, because intending a specific possible without knowledge is impossible.
This is the first proof: knowledge is necessary for any intentional act.
3
Examine the proof of necessity
The proof of necessity comes from the universality of relation: if specification (i.e., limiting knowledge to some things) would make it originated, then how much more would it be originated if it were merely possible from the beginning?
This is a fortiori argument.
4
Reflect on the implication
Thus, God's knowledge must be necessary and universal, not contingent or limited.

Commentary
This passage is a multi-part argument and refutation regarding the divine attribute of knowledge. Sanusi begins by stating the necessity ('wujub') of God's knowledge encompassing all things—necessary, possible, and impossible—because specification to some would require a cause, implying temporality (huduth). He then notes that every contingent event points to the four demands (existence, necessity, possibility, etc.) as in power and will. He explains why the proof of existence of this attribute was not given in the earlier creedal text: it was already implied in the phrase 'upon His knowledge'. The proof of necessity is derived from the universality of relation: if specification causes temporality, how much more so would outright contingency? He then presents a footnote (by al-Bayli) defining knowledge as an eternal attribute of encompassing without prior concealment. Next, he derives negation of multiplicity from the same proof, as multiplicity requires a determiner. He clarifies that 'origination of attribute necessitates origination of subject' leads to circularity or infinite regress. He adds that hiddenness of some parts implies hiddenness of all. Finally, he discusses the refutation of the possibility of opposites to knowledge, arguing that they must be originated because they are opposite to originated knowledge, and if they come after knowledge their origination is clear. The passage concludes with a prayer for divine success.

Refuting Objections: Multiplicity and Opposites in Divine Knowledge
Sanusi refutes objections that divine knowledge could be multiple or subject to opposites, using arguments from origination and logical consistency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi refutes the possibilities of multiplicity in divine knowledge and of opposites arising to it, using arguments from origination and circularity.

Core Idea
Multiplicity and opposites in divine knowledge would lead to temporal origination or logical contradictions.

Sanusi now addresses potential objections. If someone claims that divine knowledge could be multiple or that its opposites (ignorance) could exist, he shows that both are impossible because they entail hudūth (origination), which is incompatible with God's eternity. He also uses the argument from hiddenness to reinforce the universality of knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic. Sanusi draws from the same proof used for universality to negate multiplicity and other issues.
He references earlier discussions on power and will.
2
Understand the argument against multiplicity
If knowledge were multiple (e.g., many knowledges), the specific number would require a determiner, leading to origination.
He also mentions that hiddenness of some parts implies hiddenness of all, so knowledge must be perfectly manifest.
3
Understand the argument against opposites
The opposites of knowledge (ignorance) can only be originated, because if they are opposite to originated knowledge, they must be originated. Alternatively, if they come after knowledge, their origination is obvious.
Eternal things cannot cease; so if opposites were eternal, they would have to have always existed, which is impossible.
4
Conclude the refutation
Thus, divine knowledge is single, eternal, and without opposite.
