The Short Answer on Miracles and Speech
Sanusi summarizes the rebuttal to an objection about miracles and speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's concise rebuttal to the objection that miracles are identical to speech, and how he distinguishes indication from identity.

Core Idea
Miracles indicate by convention, not by being speech itself.

In this passage, Sanusi delivers a crisp refutation of a subtle theological objection. Some scholars thought that if miracles indicate divine truth like speech, then they must be speech. Sanusi shows that indication is not the same as identity: a sign points to something without being it. This short answer, he claims, settles the matter completely.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice the steps Sanusi takes: analogy, possible objection, and resolution.
The passage is short but packed.
2
Identify the objection
The objection is not stated explicitly but is implied: if miracles indicate like speech, they must be identical to speech. See how Sanusi counters by distinguishing indication from identity.
3
Appreciate the conclusion
Sanusi calls his answer the utmost verification and says it leaves no dust. Reflect on why he is so confident.
This shows the clarity of the argument.

تكلَّمَ بتصديقِ مَنْ ظهرَتْ على يدِهِ ؛ وذلك كما تقولُ : الإشارة تدلُّ وضعاً على ما يدلُّ عليه القولُ ، وهل المشير متكلم أو أبكم ؟ محتمل ، ليس في الإشارة ما يدلُّ على شيءٍ مِنْ ذلك ، وهي في نفسها الكلام بلا فرق ، سواء كان المشير مُتكلّماً أو أبكم وهذا غايةُ التحقيقِ في جواب السؤالِ ، وإِنْ كانَ قدِ استهولَهُ وعظَمَهُ كَثيرٌ مِنَ الأئِمَّةِ ، وهذا الجواب القصيرُ المُحقَّقُ لم يترك عليه غباراً ، والله تعالى أعلم ، وبه التوفيق .
He spoke with the attestation of the one on whose hand it appeared; and that is like you say: the sign indicates by convention what speech indicates, but is the signifier speaking or mute? It is possible; there is nothing in the sign that indicates any of that, and it is in itself speech with no difference, whether the signifier is speaking or mute. And this is the utmost verification in answering the question, even though many of the imams considered it serious and great. And this short verified answer leaves no dust upon it. And Allah knows best, and to Him is success.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an objection. It begins by stating that the indication of a miracle is like the indication of a sign: both indicate by convention. The objection might be that if the miracle is analogous to speech, then the one performing the miracle would have to be a speaker, but the passage explains that the sign itself does not indicate whether the signifier is speaking or mute; it is simply speech. So the answer is that both are mere indication, not that the miracle is actual speech. The author then notes that this short answer is sufficient and satisfies the question, despite some scholars thinking the question is difficult. He concludes with praise to Allah.

The Shared Indication of Speech and Miracles
Explaining that dalaala is shared between kalam and mu'jiza.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that miracles and divine speech share the attribute of indication (dalaala) but are not the same, and there is no necessary connection between them beyond that.

Core Idea
Miracles indicate like speech, but they are not speech.

This note clarifies a common confusion: are miracles a form of divine speech? The commentator explains that while both are dalaala (indication) by convention, the miracle is not speech itself. Understanding this distinction is key to proper kalam reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation. Notice that the commentator begins by defining how speech indicates a meaning, and then draws a parallel with miracles.
Pay close attention to the phrase 'مشاركة لصفة الكلام' (shares in the attribute of speech).
2
Identify the shared concept
Identify that both speech and miracles indicate a meaning in the heart (of listener/beholder) through convention. This shared concept is dalaala (indication).
3
Understand the distinction
Note that the commentator explicitly says the miracle is not itself speech. They only share indication, just as speech shares indication with a sign (الإشارة). There is no necessary connection (لا تلازم) between them.
This refutes any claim that miracles are a type of divine speech.

الكلام عند سماعه سماعَ إدراك يدلُّ على معنى يقع في قلب السامع ، ودلالة المعجزة من حيث الوضع دالة على معنى يقع أيضاً في قلب المشاهد لها ، فهي من حيث الدلالة مشاركة لصفة الكلام ، لا أنها في نفسها كلام ، وعليه : فلا أصلاً ؛ إذ لا تلازم بين المعجزة من حيث الدلالة وبين الكلام ، بل هما مشتركان في مفهوم الدلالة كاشتراك الكلام مع الإشارة ، ولا يعرف قيمة هنا وحرَّره وبيَّنه بوجازةٍ أحسن بيان . . إلا من طول دور ما لخصه الإمام السنوسي النظر في مبسوطات كتب الكلام
Speech, when heard with a hearing of comprehension, indicates a meaning that falls into the heart of the listener. And the indication of the miracle, from the perspective of convention, indicates a meaning that also falls into the heart of the beholder. So it, from the perspective of indication, shares in the attribute of speech, not that it is itself speech. And upon that, there is no foundation [؟]; for there is no necessary connection between the miracle from the perspective of indication and speech; rather, they are shared in the concept of indication like the sharing of speech with the sign. And one does not know the value here, and he refined it and explained it with conciseness in the best explanation, except from a long [؟] what the Imam al-Sanusi summarized in the consideration of the extended works of kalam.
Source pages p. 177

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory note on the concept of dalaala (indication). It explains that speech (kalam) when heard with comprehension indicates a meaning in the heart of the listener. Similarly, a miracle (mu'jiza) indicates a meaning in the heart of the beholder by convention. Therefore, the miracle shares with speech only in the aspect of indication, not in being speech itself. There is no necessary connection between the two beyond that shared concept. The note praises the Imam al-Sanusi for summarizing this clearly.
