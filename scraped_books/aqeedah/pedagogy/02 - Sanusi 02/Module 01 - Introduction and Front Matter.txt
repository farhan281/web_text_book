Word about the book Sharh Sughra al-Sughra

There is no doubt that 'al-Sanusiyya al-Sughra' is the core of the blessed 'Sanusiyyat'. It does not have that length which would trouble a student of knowledge. So what compelled Imam al-Sanusi to abbreviate it with 'Sughra al-Sughra' that is in our hands? Between

Indeed, whoever stands on the reason for that knows the utmost care of Imam al-Sanusi in making the creed of Ahl al-Sunna understood, and his exertion of effort in diversifying its formulation and size to achieve this noble goal. So 'Sughra al-Sughra' was authored for the father of the historian of Imam al-Sanusi's biography, Shaykh al-Mallali. He said in 'al-Mawahib al-Qudsiyya': (He composed it for my father - may Allah Almighty preserve him from every affliction and calamity, and grant him the highest rank. And that is because my father, when he read on the Shaykh (may Allah be pleased with him) his 'Aqidat al-Sughra' and completed it with commentary more than once... he saw that studying and memorizing it had become heavy upon him due to his old age and many worries. So he asked the Shaykh (may Allah be pleased with him) to make for him a creed smaller than 'al-Sughra' such that he could study and memorize it. So he composed this creed for him and wrote it in his own handwriting.)

And he (may Allah be pleased with him) pointed out in it fine points and bright gems that he did not mention in the previous creeds.((1))

Then when Imam al-Sanusi saw the spread of 'Sughra al-Sughra', praise be to Allah, and the appearance of some inquiries about its phrases... he set about - according to his custom in authorship - to explain it. So came 'Sharh Sughra al-Sughra' a precious commentary that a student of deep knowledge would not dispense with. His student al-Mallali described it saying: (In it are wonderful benefits and strange subtleties.((2))

And among what you will see in 'Sharh Sughra al-Sughra':
- Ease of expression, clarity of evidence, and avoiding as much as possible the technical terms of kalam. This is an approach consistent with its original 'Sughra al-Sughra' or 'al-Aqida al-Mukhtasara'.
- Glorification of the noble prophetic station by writing long pages at its beginning. Its opening is peace, and its seal is musk and fragrance. And we do not exaggerate if we say that nearly a quarter of the book was for achieving this creedal objective that many writers and researchers in beliefs may neglect.
- Showing some gentleness in the issue of taqlid (imitation in faith).
- Its excellent editing that a specialized student of knowledge cannot do without...

Commentary
This passage is an editorial introduction that provides background on the work 'Sharh Sughra al-Sughra' and outlines its features. It begins by praising the 'Sanusiyya al-Sughra' as the core of the Sanusi creeds and notes that it is not overly long. It then explains the motivation for writing an even shorter creed: the father of al-Mallali, a student of Sanusi, found the Sughra too burdensome due to his old age and preoccupations, so he requested a shorter version. Sanusi complied by writing 'Sughra al-Sughra' and included in it fine points not present in his earlier creeds. After the short creed spread and questions arose about its phrasing, Sanusi wrote a commentary on it. The passage then lists the features of this commentary: (1) ease of expression and clarity of evidence, avoiding kalam terminology to stay consistent with the original brief creed; (2) glorification of the Prophet by dedicating roughly a quarter of the book to this topic, with the opening being salutations and the closing being praise; (3) a gentle treatment of the issue of taqlid; and (4) an excellent editing that a specialized student cannot ignore. The passage highlights Sanusi's pedagogical concern and the unique approach of this commentary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Sanusi wrote his creedal summary and commentary, and the pedagogical goal of moving from blind imitation to certain knowledge.

Core Idea
Al-Sanusi wrote to rescue believers from ignorance and blind imitation through clear rational proofs.

He tells us that he first wrote a short creedal summary, and now he is writing a commentary to remove any confusion. The goal is not just to state beliefs, but to ground them in understanding so that the believer is no longer blindly following but has certain knowledge. This sets the tone for the entire work.

الحمدُ للهِ الذي مَنَّ علينا بالإيمان والإسلام ، وهدانا بنبينا ومولانا محمد عليه الصلاةُ والسلامُ ، فبيَّنَ للناس معرفة مولانا العظيم على وجه التمامِ ، وبلغ لهم عنِ اللهِ تعالى الحلال والحرام وسائر الأحكامِ ، وخُص صلَّى اللهُ عليهِ وسلَّمَ في جميع ذلك بجوامع الكلام ، وتيسير المعاني للإعمال والإفهام

Author's Introduction. In the name of God, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. The shaykh, the jurist, the imam, the learned, Abu Abdullah Muhammad ibn Yusuf al-Sanusi al-Hasani, may God have mercy upon him and forgive him and his parents, amen, said: Praise be to God who has bestowed upon us faith and Islam, and guided us through our Prophet and master Muhammad, upon him be blessings and peace, who explained to people the knowledge of our Great Master in perfect manner, and conveyed to them from God Almighty the lawful and the unlawful and all rulings, and he, may God bless him and grant him peace, was distinguished in all that by comprehensive speech, and facilitation of meanings for action and understanding. To proceed: I have written a concise summary concerning what is obligatory for the legally responsible person to believe concerning God Almighty and concerning His messengers, in a way that the legally responsible person emerges thereby from the darkness of ignorance and blind imitation. So I intended to follow it with a concise commentary that unveils their meanings from every obscurity and complexity. And I ask God Almighty to benefit through it; indeed He is the Guardian of success and guidance. (1) Recorded in (A) as 'for action and understanding' and in (H) alone as 'for notification' instead of 'for action and understanding'.
Source pages p. 102

Commentary
This passage is the author's introduction, a formal opening in which al-Sanusi states his purpose for writing this commentary. It begins with the basmala and the author's full name and lineage, followed by a prayer for himself and his parents. He then praises God for the gifts of faith and Islam, and for sending the Prophet Muhammad who conveyed religious knowledge and laws with clarity and comprehensiveness. The phrase 'جوامع الكلام' refers to the Prophet's gift of concise speech. After the 'Amma ba'd' transition, al-Sanusi reveals his project: he previously wrote a concise summary of what every mukallaf (legally accountable person) must believe about God and His messengers. That summary was designed to rescue the believer from ignorance and taqlid (blind imitation). Now, he intends to write a short commentary to clarify any obscurities or complexities in that summary. He concludes by asking God for success and benefit. The footnote records a textual variant in the phrase 'للأعمال والإفهام' (for action and understanding) which appears as 'للإعلام' (for notification) in some manuscripts.

Praise of the Prophet and His Ummah
Sanusi praises the Prophet's mission and its effects on the ummah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will appreciate how Sanusi eulogizes the Prophet's mission and the unique spiritual and numerical favors bestowed upon his ummah.

Core Idea
The Prophet's mission brought divine knowledge and produced spiritual elites; his ummah fills two-thirds of Paradise.

This passage is a devotional eulogy rather than a theological argument. Sanusi celebrates the transformative impact of the Prophet's coming, from dispelling ignorance to granting treasures of divine knowledge. He then highlights the exceptional status of this ummah, which is preserved in faith and will populate the majority of Paradise. Read it as a meditation on divine grace.

Guided Path
1
Read the full passage
Read the Arabic passage once slowly, then the English translation. Let the poetic rhythm and devotional tone sink in before analyzing.
This passage is not a proof but a praise; treat it as such.
2
Identify the effects of the Prophet's mission
Notice how Sanusi describes the removal of darkness, spread of faith, and the outpouring of divine treasures. List the key blessings mentioned.
These include knowledge, wisdom, secret jewels, and spiritual elites.
3
Examine the spiritual hierarchy
Look at the terms for the spiritual elites: poles, pegs, leaders, righteous, substitutes. Reflect on their role in sustaining faith across generations.
These are standard Sufi categories but here they indicate the abundance of saints.
4
Reflect on the ummah's status
Consider the promise that the ummah will be preserved until the Day of Judgment and that its members will constitute two-thirds of Paradise. This is a great honor.
The hadith about 120 rows is cited to support this.

Commentary
This passage is a eulogy of the Prophet Muhammad's mission and its blessings upon the believers. It begins by invoking divine favor and describes how the Prophet's advent opened doors of joy and removed sorrows. Then it details how through his mission, darkness of disbelief and ignorance was dispelled, and faith in Allah, His messengers, His books, and His angels spread. It highlights that Allah granted treasures of divine knowledge (al-maʿārif al-rabbāniyya) and infused sciences (al-ʿulūm al-laduniyya) through the Prophet, and that from each generation emerged spiritual elites such as the poles (al-aqṭāb), pegs (al-awtād), leaders (al-nuqabāʾ), righteous (al-akhyār), and substitutes (al-abdāl). The earth became filled with tawḥīd and praise of Allah's messengers, angels, and books. The passage then focuses on the Prophet's community (umma), stating that it will persist until the Day of Judgment, protected in faith despite changing dynasties and trials. It says their light—both spiritual and physical—increased to the extent that they nearly become prophets, and their numbers are so vast that they constitute two-thirds of the inhabitants of Paradise, as signified by the hadith that the rows of Paradise are 120 rows meaning the umma will occupy a large portion.
