The Referent of 'This' and the Seven Possibilities for Book Naming
The commentator explains the referent of 'this' and discusses al-Jurjani's seven possibilities for naming books.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what the word 'this' refers to in the poem's first line and why the commentator argues it is the words in the mind, not the physical text, and you will learn al-Jurjani's seven possibilities for what a book's name denotes.

Core Idea
A book's name refers to words in the mind indicating meanings, not the written text.

This passage from the commentary on the opening line of the urjuzah tackles a subtle question: what does the word 'this' point to? Is it the spoken words, the written text, or the meanings? The commentator draws on al-Jurjani's analysis to clarify that it is the words in the mind, considered along with their meanings. Understanding this sets the stage for grasping how theological texts are named and referred to.

1
Understand the referent of 'this'

Notice the waw is for isti'naf (resumption).

Words cease to exist letter by letter upon utterance.
3
Examine al-Jurjani's seven possibilities

The possibilities are enumerated in the Arabic passage.

And this is a urjuzah (didactic poem) which I have named 'Jawharat al-Tawhid' (The Jewel of Tawhid) and which I have refined. His saying: (and this) – the waw is for isti'naf (resumption), and what is referred to by 'this' is the words present in the mind, in consideration of their indication of specific meanings, whether the introduction (khutbah) preceded the composition or came after it. Commentary
This passage discusses the referent of the word "هذه" (this) in the line "وَهَذِهِ أُرْجُوزَةٌ..." The commentator first explains that the waw is for resumption. The referent is the words present in the mind, in consideration of their indication of specific meanings, regardless of whether the introduction preceded or followed the composition. He refutes the opinion that if the introduction preceded, the referent is words in the mind, but if it followed, the referent is external words; he argues that spoken words are accidents that cease to exist immediately upon utterance, so they cannot be external. Then he reports that al-Sayyid al-Jurjani, in his discussion of the naming of books and biographies, presented seven possibilities for what a book's name refers to: words only, meanings only, inscriptions only, words and meanings, words and inscriptions, meanings and inscriptions, or all three. Al-Jurjani chose words in consideration of their indication of meanings. The commentator then discusses whether this chosen possibility is included among the seven or is an eighth; the apparent view is that it is one of the seven, but it is restricted by the consideration of meanings. He also notes that some have claimed the chosen is meanings present in the mind, but that is against the well-known opinion. The reason the other possibilities are rejected is that meanings are not independent; they depend on words, so they cannot be the referent or part of the referent. Therefore four possibilities are invalid: meanings alone, meanings with words, meanings with inscriptions, and meanings with both words and inscriptions. Thus those possibilities become invalid.

Two Objections to the Demonstrative Reference
Two objections to the demonstrative referent: summary-detail mismatch and particular-universal mismatch.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two objections raised against the claim that the demonstrative 'hādhi' refers to mental words, and the reasoning behind them.

Core Idea
The objections highlight a mismatch between summary and detail, and between particular and universal.

In this passage, the author explains why the demonstrative 'hādhi' in 'hādhi urjūzah' must refer to the words in the author's mind. However, two objections are raised against this: one concerning the nature of mental words as summarized versus the detailed poem, and the other concerning particular versus universal reference. Let's examine these objections closely.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context
The author has already determined that the demonstrative refers to mental words by eliminating other possibilities. Read the opening lines to see which possibilities were invalidated.
The inscriptions are not always available, so they cannot be the referent.
2
Identify the first objection
The first objection states that mental words are summarized (mujmal), but 'urjūzah' is a detailed poem (mufaṣṣal). This creates a mismatch between subject and predicate.
Note the terms mujmal and mufaṣṣal.
3
Identify the second objection
The second objection states that the referent is particular to the author's mind, but 'urjūzah' is a universal term applicable to any such poem. Thus, it cannot refer to a particular instance.
This involves the universal-particular distinction.
4
Reflect on the significance
Consider why these objections matter for the coherence of the demonstrative reference. They challenge the straightforward interpretation.
The answer will address these in the next lesson.

Commentary
This passage addresses two objections to the earlier claim that the demonstrative 'hādhi' in 'hādhi urjūzah' refers to the words in the author's mind. The first objection argues that mental words are summarized (mujmal), whereas 'urjūzah' denotes a detailed (mufaṣṣal) poem, so there is no correspondence. The second objection notes that the referent is particular (juz'ī) to the author's mind, but 'urjūzah' is a universal term applying to any such poem. Shaykh 'Abd al-Salām answers by assuming two omitted words (taqdīr muḍāfayn): either 'mufaṣṣal' or 'naw'' (type). The author then analyzes the answer: if mental content is only summarized, the implied 'mufaṣṣal' solves the first objection but not the second, so a second implied word is needed; but if the mind can contain both summarized and detailed (the preferred view), no implied word is needed. Moreover, 'urjūzah' might itself refer to the summarized form, making the issue moot. If we accept that difference matters, then the second implied word (type) is better. Finally, the author notes that using a demonstrative for mental words is metaphorical (majāz).

Answering the Objections: The Role of Taqdīr Muḍāf
Shaykh 'Abd al-Salām's answer using omitted words, and its critique.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Shaykh 'Abd al-Salām answers the two objections through assuming omitted words (taqdīr muḍāf) and the subsequent analysis of this solution.

Core Idea
The answer requires assuming omitted words or rethinking the nature of mental content.

In this passage, the author presents Shaykh 'Abd al-Salām's answer to the two objections. The answer involves assuming two omitted words (taqdīr muḍāfayn) to reconcile the mismatches. The author then analyzes this answer, offering a more nuanced view that may not require any assumption at all. Let's explore this resolution.

1
Understand the answer

This is taqdīr muḍāf, a common technique in Arabic grammar.

Analyze the basis of the answer
The answer assumes that mental content is only summarized, and that book names are genus-nouns (ʿilm al-jins). Consider what this implies.
If mental content is only summarized, then adding 'mufaṣṣal' solves the first objection.

Explore the author's preferred view

This simplifies the solution.
4
Consider the alternative approach
If we concede that the difference matters, then it's better to assume the omitted word in the second objection only, i.e., 'summary of an urjūzah'.
The analogy of removing a sock before reaching the river illustrates premature resolution.

Interpreting 'Mufassal' in the Opening Verse
Al-Bajuri analyzes two interpretations of 'Mufassal' to resolve objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two interpretations of the word 'Mufassal' in the opening verse and how they address different objections.

The answer further explains that in usul al-fiqh, actions of children and animals are legally irrelevant, and mubah is defined as that which has no sin in doing or leaving, so it is only negated where its existence is valid. The footnote then defines legal ruling as God's speech concerning the mukallaf's action, including mental acts like belief, and distinguishes it from customary and rational rulings.

Core Idea

A common criticism of Ash'ari theology is that it relies on revelation to know God, but then needs God to validate revelation—a circularity. Rulings on Denying Miracles Based on Certainty
Author classifies miracles by certainty and assigns ruling: kufr, fisq, or ta'zeer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how scholars classify miracles according to the certainty of their transmission and the corresponding legal consequences for denying them.

Core Idea
Denying a miracle is not always disbelief; it depends on how the miracle is known.

The author of the Jawharat al-Tawhid now turns to a practical application of the principles of certainty. Not all miracles are transmitted with the same level of authority, so the ruling on denying them varies accordingly. In this brief but important passage, he lays out a three-tier classification that balances theological rigour with fairness.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that the author uses a conditional structure: 'if this, then that' for each category.

Commentary
This passage provides a classification of miracles based on the level of certainty in their transmission, with corresponding legal rulings. The author begins by stating the rule for miracles that are known by definitive proof (ma'lum bil-qat') and transmitted via mass transmission (tawatur), like the Qur'an. Denying such a miracle constitutes disbelief (kufr) without exception. Then he addresses miracles that are not at that level: if they are well-known (mashhur) among the community, like the miracle of water flowing from the Prophet's fingers, then denying them is considered corruption (fisq), not disbelief. Finally, for miracles that are less famous but still authenticated by a sound (sahih) or good (hasan) chain of narration, the denier receives a discretionary punishment (ta'zeer). This gradation demonstrates the nuanced approach of the scholars in assessing reactions to narrated miracles.
