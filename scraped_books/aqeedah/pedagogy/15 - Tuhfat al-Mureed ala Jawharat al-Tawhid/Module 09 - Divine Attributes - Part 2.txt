The Seven Modalities of Power and the Ash'ari View
Enumeration of seven power relationships and Ash'ari view on annihilation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the seven modalities through which divine power relates to possible things, and the Ash'ari position that annihilation occurs indirectly.

Core Idea
Divine power relates to creation and annihilation through seven distinct modalities.

This passage from the commentary on al-Jawhara provides a detailed classification of how God's power (qudra) engages with possible beings. The author distinguishes three types of relationships: eternal suitability, grasp, and temporal actuality. He also notes the unique Ash'ari view on annihilation, which sets it apart from direct causation. Let's work through the text to master these distinctions.

Guided Path
1

Read the Arabic text once through, then follow the translation. Notice the technical terms for each type of relationship.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the three categories
The author divides the relationships into three kinds: one eternal potential, three 'grasp', and three 'temporal actual'. Find the descriptions for each.
The 'grasp' and 'temporal actual' each have three sub-items.
3
Understand the Ash'ari view
Pay attention to the last part where al-Ash'ari's opinion is cited: power does not directly annihilate but removes sustenance. Compare this to the earlier classification.
This is a key theological nuance.

Commentary
This passage elaborates on the ways in which the divine power (qudra) relates to possible things. The commentator starts by clarifying that the phrase 'by which every possible thing is brought into existence and annihilated' refers to the eternal potential relationship (al-talluq al-saluhi al-qadeem), also called the eternal suitability. This means that power, from eternity, is suitable for creating and annihilating things at the appropriate times. Then he distinguishes three types of 'grasp' relationships (talluq al-qabdah): power relates to our non-existence before existence (keeping us in nonexistence), to the continuation of existence after it is given, and to the continuation of nonexistence after annihilation. In these three, the possible is held in the grasp of God's power, subject to His will. Next, three 'actual temporal' relationships (talluq tanjizi) are listed: power relates to our actual creation after prior nonexistence, to our actual annihilation after existence, and to our actual recreation at the resurrection. Each of these is a temporal actuality. Thus the total is seven relationships: one eternal potential, three grasp, and three actual. The commentator notes that eternal nonexistence is not subject to power because it is necessary (i.e., God's own nonexistence is impossible). Finally, he reports that al-Ash'ari held a different view: power does not directly cause annihilation; rather, when God wills a thing to cease, He removes the 'supplies' that sustain it, and it ceases by itself, like a lamp that goes out when oil is cut.

The Seven Relations of Divine Power

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the seven types of relations (ta'alluqat) of qudra: the primordial aptitude, the three grasp relations, and the three actualized relations, along with the special case of eternal non-existence and al-Ash'ari's view on annihilation.

Core Idea
Divine power relates to creation and annihilation in seven distinct ways.

This passage from the commentary on Jawharat al-Tawhid examines the different ways in which Allah's power (qudra) relates to possible things. It distinguishes between a primordial suitability that exists prior to any actual creation, a 'grasp' relation that holds possibilities in check, and an actualized relation that brings about existence or non-existence at a specific time. The text also addresses the special case of eternal non-existence and presents al-Ash'ari's unique perspective on how annihilation occurs.

True agency belongs to Allah's essence; power is only a cause.

The phrase 'by it' in the definition of qudra is carefully chosen. It points to a crucial theological distinction: the real effect comes from Allah's essence, not from power as an independent agent. This lesson unpacks that distinction and explains the prohibition against misleading phrases that might suggest otherwise.

Commentary
This passage discusses the relations (ta‘alluqāt) of divine will. It distinguishes three concepts: (1) the eternal decisive relation (al-ta‘alluq al-tanjīzī al-qadīm) – God's eternal specification of a thing with the attributes it will have when created; (2) the eternal potential relation (al-ṣulūḥī al-qadīm) – the will's eternal suitability to specify either existence or non-existence, while actually specifying one; (3) the temporal decisive relation (al-tanjīzī al-ḥādith) – a view by some scholars that at the moment of creation God gives a new specification distinct from the eternal one. The commentator then presents the 'verified position' that the temporal decisive is merely a manifestation of the eternal decisive, not an independent relation. He includes a critique from another source arguing that if the temporal specification is different, it must be independent, so the verification is flawed; the true position is to deny any temporal specification entirely, as the eternal decisive is sufficient. The passage ends with a supporting reference from al-Sharqāwī's gloss.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed explanation of the different types of attachment (ta'alluq) of the divine will to possible things. The passage concludes with support from another commentary that some scholars explicitly denied the temporal determinate attachment as redundant. The key point is that the divine will attaches to possible things in a way that preserves both God's eternal will and the actual coming into existence of things.

The author refutes the potential attachment by arguing that non-attachment to something that is not yet knowable is not ignorance, just as non-attachment of power to the impossible is not weakness. Then he concludes that the correct view is that there is only one attachment: the eternal actual one.

Commentary
This passage is an extended explanation of why Allah's knowledge cannot be called 'acquired' (مكتسب). The author begins by clarifying the line from the matn: it is forbidden in both law and reason to apply the term 'acquired' to Allah's knowledge. He warns against the misconception that the prohibition is only on the wording while the meaning is acceptable; rather, it is the belief itself that is impossible. On both definitions, Allah's knowledge cannot be acquired because it would entail originated accidents (قيام الحوادث) in His essence and prior ignorance—both impossible. The author then addresses a Quranic verse (18:12) that appears to attribute acquisition to Allah. He cites two interpretations: either it means manifesting a correlate of His eternal knowledge, or the verb is read as 'nu‘limu' (We make known). He rejects a third interpretation (anthropomorphic projection) as likely spurious. Finally, an objection is raised that the verse implies causation (تعليل) for Allah's action, which is not allowed. The reply is that the lām in the verse indicates consequence (العاقبة) and benefit (الفائدة), not causation. Thus the verse does not imply that Allah's knowledge is acquired or that His action is caused.

The insertion of the word 'validity' is important because life does not necessitate actual knowledge, as seen in the case of a mad person who is alive but lacks knowledge. The definition of temporal life says it is a quality that entails the reception of sensation and voluntary movement, an accident that makes the body capable of sensation and voluntary motion, as opposed to forced motion. Finally, the proof for God's life is presented: since God has power, will, and knowledge, and every being with these attributes must have life, God necessarily has life.
