The Definition of Ja'iz in God’s Right and the Tautology Objection
Al-Ajhari defines ja'iz and handles tautology objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Ajhari defines ja'iz and defends it against the charge of tautology through grammatical analysis and the distinction between essence and action.

Core Idea
A good definition is never redundant—it specifies scope.

When the poet defines 'possible in God's right' as 'that which is possible for us to create or annihilate,' a critic might object that this merely says 'the possible is possible.' But by analyzing the grammar—especially the two tamyīz words—the commentator shows that the definition actually concerns actions, not essences, and thereby gains its utility. This lesson unpacks that grammatical insight and the first two rounds of objection and answer.

Guided Path
1
Read the verse and translation
Read the Arabic verse line and its literal translation without analyzing yet. Let the wording sink in.
Notice the words 'ījādan' and 'i‘dāman'.
2
Examine the grammatical analysis
The commentator explains that 'jā'iz' is a fronted predicate, 'mā amkanā' is a delayed subject, and 'ījādan' and 'i‘dāman' are tamyīz (discriminatives) that specify the actions. This transforms the meaning from 'the possible is possible' to 'bringing into existence and annihilating the possible are possible in God's right.'
The tamyīz is crucial here.
3
Follow the objection and first answer
The objection claims tautology. The first answer says the tamyīz removes the tautology by making the subject actions. Then a second objection arises: if both doing and leaving are possible, the tautology returns. The second answer invokes the strong verbal difference between words for 'doing' and 'leaving' versus 'possible' and 'contingent.'
This shows the layered logical defense.

44. And possible in His right is that which is possible for us, in terms of bringing into existence and annihilating, like His giving wealth. His statement: (And possible in His right ... etc.) (2) When he finished speaking about the necessary and the impossible, he began to speak about the possible, which is the second of the three divisions in summary, and he only postponed it in detail due to the length of the discussion earlier. And (possible) is a predicate preceding, and (that which is possible for us) is a subject delayed. The alif of (amkana) is for absoluteness. And (bringing into existence) and (annihilating) are two discriminatives transformed from the genitive which was the subject originally. The estimation: And the bringing into existence of that which is possible and its annihilation, each of them is possible in His right. For if it is said: This information has no benefit, because the possible is the contingent, and the contingent is the possible, so it is as if he said: The possible is possible or the contingent is contingent. The answer is that the discriminative, namely (bringing into existence and annihilating), removes its lack of benefit, because it is a discriminative transformed from the genitive which was the subject originally, and the estimation is: The bringing into existence of the contingent and its annihilation, each of them is possible in His right as mentioned, and the commentator indicated this by his saying: i.e., the doing of every contingent and its leaving, for he estimated that taken from his saying: bringing into existence and annihilating, otherwise there is no need for estimation with the discriminative. And an objection was made: the doing and leaving, each of them is contingent, so the difficulty returns. And the answer was: The strong verbal difference is sufficient, since perhaps one might imagine that the attribute of doing or leaving is necessity, unlike the possible and the contingent, for their difference is not strong. And the original difficulty is repelled by the fact that the subject is the contingent in itself, and the predicate is the possible in His right, so it is restricted by being in His right, so it is as if he said: The contingent in itself is possible in His right, contrary to the Mu'tazila in their saying that some contingents are necessary upon Him, for they said: It is obligatory to do what is good and best (al-salāh wa al-aslah), and contrary to the Barāhima in their saying that sending messengers is impossible, while it is among contingent things. And this is a significant benefit, conveyed by al-'Allāma al-Amir and al-'Allāma al-Shanwānī. His statement: Like His giving wealth is an example of doing the contingent, and an example of leaving it is not giving it to him. And 'al-rizq' with fatḥa on the rā' is a verbal noun; but with kasra it is a noun for what is provided. The pronoun refers to God, and the genitive in (rizqihi) is from the verbal noun's annexation to its agent, and the first object is omitted, and (al-ghinā) is its second object. The estimation: Like God's giving the servant wealth (al-ghinā), which with kasra and shortening is the opposite of poverty, so it is abundance of wealth. But with kasra and lengthening, it is the chanting of poetry. And with lengthening and fatḥa, it is benefit. As for with fatḥa and shortening, it has not been heard.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed definition and defence of the concept of ja'iz (possible/permissible) in relation to God. It begins by citing the verse (line 44 of the poem): 'And possible in His right is that which is possible for us, in terms of bringing into existence and annihilating, like His giving wealth.' The commentator then explains the grammar: 'jā’iz' is a predicate in front, and 'mā amkanā' is a delayed subject; the alif of 'amkanā' indicates absoluteness; 'ījādan' and 'i‘dāman' are two discriminatives (tamyīz) transformed from the annexed phrase that was originally the subject. The intended meaning is: bringing into existence and annihilating each possible thing are both possible in God's right. The commentator then raises an objection: this statement seems tautological because 'jā’iz' and 'mumkin' are synonyms, so it is like saying 'the possible is possible'. The answer is that the discriminatives ('ījādan' and 'i‘dāman') prevent tautology by specifying that the subject is the action (doing or leaving) of the contingent, not the contingent itself. The commentator further explains that the strong verbal difference between 'doing' and 'leaving' is enough to clarify the benefit. He then refutes the original difficulty by stating that the subject is 'the contingent in itself' and the predicate is 'possible in His right', so it affirms contingency in essence and permissibility in relation to God, thus denying any necessity or impossibility. This refutes two groups: the Mu'tazila, who claim that some acts (like what is good and best) are obligatory upon God, and the Barāhima (a group of Indian Brahmins), who claim that sending messengers is impossible. Both claims are false because they contradict the principle that all contingents are possible in God's right. The passage ends with an explanation of the example 'like His giving wealth', clarifying the word 'rizq' and 'ghinā' in various forms. This lesson shows the careful logical work in defining a kalam term and defending its usefulness.

The Benefit of Defining Ja'iz: Refuting Mu'tazila and Barāhima
Al-Ajhari defends the utility of the ja'iz definition by refuting Mu'tazila and Barāhima.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the definition of ja'iz serves to refute the Mu'tazila claim of obligatory acts and the Barāhima claim of impossible acts, thereby establishing God's absolute freedom.

Core Idea
Defining ja'iz protects divine omnipotence from unwarranted restrictions.

After establishing the definition, the commentator reveals its ultimate benefit: it contradicts erroneous schools that impose necessity or impossibility on certain actions of God. The Mu'tazila argue that God must do what is best (al-salāh wa al-aslah), while the Barāhima claim sending messengers is impossible. The definition affirms that God is free to act or not, and nothing is obligatory or impossible in His right.

These are historical theological opponents.
3
Reflect on the benefit
The passage explicitly calls this a 'significant benefit' because it shows how a precise definition can correct false beliefs.
4
Examine the example

Distinguishing the Possible from the Permissible for God
The commentator distinguishes possible from permissible and refutes two sects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the crucial distinction between 'mumkin' (possible in itself) and 'ja'iz' (permissible for God) and how this refutes the Mu'tazila and Barahima.

Core Idea
What is possible in itself is not automatically permissible; only God's permission makes it so.

This passage addresses a subtle but important distinction: just because something is logically possible (mumkin) does not mean it is automatically permissible (ja'iz) for God to do. The commentator clarifies that in the creedal statement, the predicate is qualified by 'fi haqqihi ta'ala'—meaning God's permission is the deciding factor. This understanding refutes two groups: the Mu'tazila who claim God must do what is best, and the Barahima who claim God cannot send messengers.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to identify the potential confusion about ja'iz and mumkin. Then read the translation.
Notice how the commentator first states the confusion, then resolves it.
2
Understand the distinction
The commentator explains that 'mumkin' describes a thing's essence, while 'ja'iz' is a ruling about God's action regarding it. The predicate is restricted to God's right.
Key: 'al-mumkin fi dhatihi ja'iz fi haqqihi ta'ala'.
3
Recognize the two refutations
The passage explicitly refutes the Mu'tazila (obligation of al-salah wa al-aslah) and the Barahima (impossibility of sending messengers). Understand why each group's claim contradicts the distinction.
Both groups impose obligation or impossibility on God, which is denied by the ja'iz characterization.

And the scholars' saying that the absoluteness is explained by something preceding or following is a majority matter, as stated by some investigators. Some gnostics said: from the subtlety of God's granting the proof to a person at the time of the question is His saying, Exalted: 'O man, what deceived you concerning your generous Lord?' [al-Infitar 6], for He inspires him with the proof to say: 'Your generosity deceived me, O my Lord.'
Footnote (1): His saying: (i.e., that by which we argue in answer, etc.) This is apparent regarding the question that occurs in this world, because the questioner in this world may ask for evidence of the answer. As for the answer to the question that occurs in the Hereafter, its need for evidence to indicate it is not apparent, because it has not been reported that the angels ask the deceased after his answer for evidence to confirm his answer; rather, when God grants him success and he answers them, they turn away from him and say to him: 'Sleep like the sleep of a bride.' So the apparent meaning is to interpret 'al-hujja' as the answer itself. This is what appears. End of al-Ajhuri.
Footnote (2): His saying: ('min lutf') the expression of al-Amir is 'min latif'. End of al-Ajhuri.

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory commentary on two phrases from the poem: 'when the question absolutely' and 'our proof'. The commentator first explains that 'when the question absolutely' means the question that comes to us from another in an absolute sense, encompassing this world, the grave, and the Hereafter. He notes that the principle that absoluteness is explained by a preceding or following context is only a general rule, not absolute. Then he explains 'our proof' as a correct legal argument that cannot be criticized or rejected. He mentions a subtle point: God's granting of the proof is seen in the verse 'O man, what deceived you concerning your generous Lord?', where the servant is inspired to answer that it was God's generosity that deceived him. The first footnote further clarifies that the interpretation of 'hujja' as an argument applies primarily to this-worldly questioning; in the Hereafter, the 'hujja' is actually the answer itself, not a separate proof. The second footnote corrects an expression: the word 'lutf' is better read as 'latif' according to al-Amir.
