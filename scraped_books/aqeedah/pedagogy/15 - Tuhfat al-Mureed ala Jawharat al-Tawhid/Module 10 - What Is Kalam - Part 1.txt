Core Idea

Before diving into theological arguments, we must clarify what we mean by 'name' ('ism') and 'named' ('musamma'). This might seem like a dry terminological issue, but it has real consequences for how we discuss the attributes of Allah. The Four Types of Hamd and the Inclusion of 'Bil-Lisan'
The author enumerates the four types of hamd and discusses the scope of the definition.

Core Idea
Praise has four types; the definition of hamd must include eternal praise.

This passage from the Jawharat al-Tawhid commentary classifies hamd into four types and addresses a subtle definitional issue. Some scholars restrict hamd to verbal praise, but the author shows how that would exclude divine praise. He resolves it by invoking a well-known figurative usage. Let's examine his reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and a methodological discussion. The author first lists the four possible types of hamd based on whether the praiser and the praised are eternal (qadim) or contingent (hadith). He further defends the use of figurative language in definitions, stating that the objection against figurative usage applies only when the figure is not well-known, and here it is common.

The Definition of Hamd and Its Distinction from Madh
Definition of hamd and distinction from madh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise definition of hamd as praise for a voluntary good act, how it differs from madh, and why the voluntary nature is attached to the cause of praise, not the attribute used.

Core Idea
Hamd is praise for voluntary good; madh is for involuntary qualities.

The text begins by clarifying the phrase 'upon the voluntary good' in the definition of hamd. It explains that the goodness of the act is based on the praiser's belief, even if the act is not legally good. Then it distinguishes hamd from madh by the voluntary nature of the praised attribute. Finally, it addresses a nuance: the voluntary condition applies to the cause of praise, not the attribute with which one praises.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and clarification. The author explains the phrase 'upon the voluntary good' in the definition of hamd. He emphasizes that the goodness of the action is according to the praiser's belief, even if it is not legally good (e.g., plundering). The restriction 'voluntary' excludes praise of involuntary qualities, which is called madh, not hamd. He gives the example: one says 'madhtu al-lu'lu'ata' (I praised the pearl) for its beauty, but not 'hamadtuha'. He then cites al-Zamakhshari who claims hamd and madh are synonymous, but the author's own position distinguishes them. The key point is that 'voluntary' is a restriction on the mahmud 'alayhi (what is praised for), not on the mahmud bihi (what is praised with). This means the cause of praise must be a voluntary act, but the attribute used to praise can be involuntary. Example: if Zaid does a voluntary favor (honoring you), and you say 'Zaid is good' (an involuntary attribute), it is still hamd because the cause is voluntary.

The Qualifier 'Upon Glorification and Magnification'

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Genuine praise requires sincere glorification, not just the form.

In defining hamd, the author adds a crucial qualifier: praise must be 'upon the manner of glorification and magnification.' This prevents mere verbal praise from being considered hamd if it is actually intended as mockery. The angels' sarcastic remark to Abu Jahl in the Quran illustrates this exclusion perfectly.

Core Idea
Imam al-Haramayn defined the intellect as knowledge—either conceptual or affirmative—of the three categories.

Imam al-Haramayn's definition of the intellect as knowledge of the necessary, impossible, and possible is foundational in Ash'ari theology. This lesson unpacks two interpretations: one emphasizing conceptual grasp, the other emphasizing axiomatic belief. The source passage explains both.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then read the literal translation. Notice that the passage presents two distinct interpretations marked by 'qīl' (it is said).
The passage is a footnote expanding on an earlier reference.
2
Understand the first interpretation
The first interpretation equates intellect with conceptualization (taṣawwur) of the three categories: necessary, impossible, and possible. Focus on the definitions given for each category.
Conceptualization is a mental grasp of meaning without judgment.
3
Understand the second interpretation
The second interpretation equates intellect with affirmation (taṣdīq) of necessary propositions that exemplify the three categories. The examples (one half of two, fire hot, opposites cannot coexist) illustrate each category.
Affirmation involves assent to a proposition as true.
4
Compare and reflect
Consider the difference between mere conceptual understanding and affirmed belief. Ask: Which interpretation seems closer to our everyday notion of 'intellect'?
Both interpretations agree that intellect is knowledge of the three categories, but differ in the type of knowledge.

Source Text

قوله : (لأن إمام الحرمين ... إلى آخره قيل : المراد بالمعرفة التي جعلها إمام الحرمين نفس العقل تصور المفاهيم الثلاثة : بأن يتصور أن الواجب ما لا يقبل العدم، وأن المستحيل ما لا يقبل الوجود، وأن الجائز ما يصحُ وُجوده وعدمه . وقيل : المراد بتلك المعرفة: التصديق ببعض الضروريات من الأقسام الثلاثة، كأن يصدق بأن الواحد نصف الاثنين، وبأن النار حارة، وأن النقيضين لا يجتمعان : فكون الواحد نصف الاثنين واجب ضرورةً، وثبوت الحرارة للنار جائز ضرورة، واجتماع النقيضين مستحيل ضرورةً، فالتصديق بذلك وما شابهه هو العقل بناءً على هذا القول، يُؤخذ ذلك من حاشية الشرقاوي على الهدهدي». انتهى أجهوري
His saying: (Because Imam al-Haramayn ... to the end) It is said: What is meant by the knowledge that Imam al-Haramayn made identical to the intellect is the conceptualization (taṣawwur) of the three concepts: that one conceptualizes that the necessary is that which does not admit non-existence, that the impossible is that which does not admit existence, and that the possible is that whose existence and non-existence are sound. And it is said: What is meant by that knowledge is affirmation (taṣdīq) of some necessary things from the three categories, like one's affirming that one is half of two, that fire is hot, and that two opposites cannot coexist: for one being half of two is necessarily true, the heat of fire is necessarily possible, and the coexistence of opposites is necessarily impossible. So affirmation of that and the like is the intellect according to this second opinion. That is taken from the gloss of al-Sharqawi on al-Hadadi. End of al-Ajhuri.

Commentary
This passage is a footnote that expands on the earlier mention of Imam al-Haramayn's view. It presents two interpretations of what Imam al-Haramayn meant by saying that knowledge (ma'rifa) of the three categories is the intellect itself. The first interpretation (al-qīl) is that this knowledge is mere conceptualization (taṣawwur): mentally grasping the definitions of necessary, impossible, and possible. The second interpretation is that it is affirmative belief (taṣdīq) in certain necessary propositions that exemplify these categories: for instance, that one is half of two is necessarily true (wājib), that fire is hot is necessarily possible (jā'iz bi al-ḍarūra), and that opposites cannot coexist is necessarily impossible (mustaḥīl bi al-ḍarūra). According to this second view, the intellect is the set of such axiomatic affirmations. The commentator cites al-Sharqawi's gloss as the source.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Assuming that 'intellect' here means the faculty of reason rather than the actual knowledge of first principles.
Correction: Imam al-Haramayn identifies the intellect with the knowledge itself—either conceptual or affirmative—of the three categories, not the faculty that acquires such knowledge.

Key Takeaway
Imam al-Haramayn defines the intellect as knowledge of the three necessary categories—either as conceptual understanding or as affirmed axiomatic beliefs.
