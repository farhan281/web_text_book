The Absolute Scope of Questioning and the Meaning of Proof
Al-Bajuri explains the absolute scope of questioning and the nature of the proof sought.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three realms of questioning (this world, grave, Hereafter) and what 'proof' (حجة) means in this supplication, including why it is needed only in this world.

Core Idea
The supplication for proof covers all times and places, but the need for a distinct proof is primarily in this world.

The commentator now addresses two key phrases: 'عند السؤال مطلقا' (at the questioning absolutely) and 'حجتنا' (our proof). He clarifies that absolute questioning includes this world, the grave, and the Day of Resurrection. Then he defines the proof as a sound legal argument. Through a story from the gnostics and a footnote, he further explains that in the grave, the proof is the answer itself, not an additional evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three realms of questioning
Read the passage on 'عند السؤال مطلقا' and identify the three realms: this world, the grave, and the Hereafter.
The commentary explicitly lists them: في الدنيا وفي القبر وفي القيامة.
2
Understand the definition of proof
Note the definition of 'حجتنا' as a sound, legally acceptable argument in response to a question.
3
Examine the story and footnote
Read the story about divine kindness where God inspires the answer 'غرني كرمك', and the footnote clarifying that in the grave the answer itself is the proof.
The footnote distinguishes between this world and the Hereafter.

End of al-Ujhuri. (3) We did not find it with this wording. Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a verse (142) of the poem. The commentator, al-Bajuri, provides a detailed grammatical and theological explanation. He begins by analyzing the word 'هذا' (this) as either a subject with an omitted predicate or an object of an omitted verb, marking a transition (تخلص) to a new topic. Then he explains 'أرجو الله' using the imperfect tense to indicate continuous hope that renews with changing circumstances. The verb 'يمنحنا' (grant us) takes two objects: 'نا' (us) and 'حجتنا' (our proof). The commentator discusses whether the plural pronoun 'نا' refers to the community of Muslims or to the author himself (as a majestic plural). He prefers the former based on the hadith about gathering in supplication, but also allows the latter as showing God's favor. He cites the Quranic verse 'وأما بنعمة ربك فحدث'. Then he clarifies 'عند السؤال مطلقا' (at the questioning absolutely) meaning in the three realms: this world, the grave, and the Hereafter. He notes that the absolute qualification here is not explained by context, contrary to the usual rule. Next, he explains 'حجتنا' as the argument used to answer the question, a sound shari proof. He relates a story that some gnostics found God's kindness in the verse 'ما غرك بربك الكريم' because it inspires the answer 'غرني كرمك يا رب'. The commentary then includes three footnotes: first, a clarification that the proof is only needed in this world, not in the grave; second, a correction of a phrase; third, a verification of the hadith about prayer not being rejected between two prayers. Footnote (3) attributes the hadith to Abu al-Darda' as a mawquf report. The passage follows the order of the verse line by line.

The Closing Prayers: Structure and Purpose
Commentary explains the purpose of the final prayers and the hadith about supplication between two prayers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rhetorical function of 'ثُمَّ' in the closing verse and the author's intention in placing prayers at both ends of his work as a means of securing divine acceptance.

Core Idea
The closing prayers aim to secure acceptance of the entire work.

In the final verses of the poem, the author offers prayers and peace upon the Prophet, his family, and his followers. But why does he repeat the prayers at both the beginning and the end? The commentary reveals a subtle rhetorical shift and a hopeful strategy: the prayers serve as a frame that, by divine generosity, ensures acceptance of all the theology contained within.

A hadith is cited: 'The supplication between two prayers upon me is not rejected.' This is used as an analogy—if supplication between two prayers is accepted, then surely the content between two such prayers is also accepted.
The hadith strengthens the author's hopeful framing.

The Virtue of Prayers upon the Prophet at the Beginning and End of a Work
Commentary on repeating salawat at bookends for acceptance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the author placed prayers and peace upon the Prophet at both the start and end of his book, and the proper intention behind such closing formulas.

Core Idea
Prayers upon the Prophet at both ends of a work invite acceptance of the whole work.

In this passage, the commentator explains a subtle yet powerful reason for beginning and ending a book with salawat upon the Prophet. The author does not do this merely out of convention, but with a deep hope that God will accept the entire work between these prayers. The commentator also warns against showing off by using such formulas merely to announce completion.

1
Read the poem lines and explanation

2
Understand the logic of acceptance

Learn the lesson on intention

Guidance on intention when closing a work with salawat and 'God knows best'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the proper intention behind placing closing formulas like salawat and 'God knows best' at the end of a written work, and why mere notification is considered disliked.

Core Idea
Intent matters: closing formulas are for spiritual gain, not for announcing the end.

When authors end their works with prayers upon the Prophet and the phrase 'God knows best', there is a subtle but important point of intention. The commentator explains that these should not be used merely to signal the work's completion. Instead, the author's heart must be set on the virtue of these dhikrs. This lesson unpacks that guidance and the parallel warning for the common closing phrase 'Wallāhu a'lam' (God knows best).

الخاتمة
نحو التأليف واعلم أنه إذا أورد الإنسان الصلاة والسلام في آخر عمله لا ينبغي أن يُريد بهما الإعلام بإتمامه، بل ينبغي له ألا يقصد إلا تحصيل فضيلتهما، وإلا وقع في الكراهة، وكذا قولهم : (والله أعلم عند التمام فينبغي ألا يقصدوا بذلك الإعلام بالانتهاء، بل ينبغي أن يَقْصِدُوا به تفويض العلم إليه تعالى .
The Conclusion
Regarding authorship: Know that if a person offers prayer and peace at the end of his work, he should not intend by them to announce its completion; rather, he should intend only to attain their virtue. Otherwise, he falls into dislike. Similarly, their saying: 'And God knows best' upon completion: they should not intend thereby to announce the ending, but rather intend to entrust knowledge to Him, Exalted.

Commentary
This short passage is a concluding instruction on proper intention. The commentator advises that when an author ends his work with prayers upon the Prophet and the phrase 'God knows best', he should not aim to inform the reader that the work is finished. Instead, his sole purpose should be to seek the spiritual benefit and virtue of these formulas. If he intends mere notification, it becomes disliked (makruh). The instruction applies to both the prayer/peace formula and the common closing phrase 'God knows best'.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: It is permissible to use closing formulas like salawat and 'God knows best' simply to mark the end of the work.
Correction: The commentator clarifies that such an intention turns the act into a disliked (makruh) practice; instead, one must intend the spiritual virtue or to entrust knowledge to Allah.

Key Takeaway
