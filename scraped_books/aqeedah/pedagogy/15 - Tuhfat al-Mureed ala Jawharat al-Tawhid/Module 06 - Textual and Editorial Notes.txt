Rulings of Basmala and Hamdala for Various Acts
Enumerates categories of acts not started with basmala/hamdala and their rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the five categories of acts that are excluded from the sunna of beginning with basmala or hamdala, and the rulings that apply to each.

Core Idea
Not every action begins with bismillah—the Lawgiver has specified exceptions.

The sunna of starting actions with basmala or hamdala is well-known, but there are important exceptions. This passage from the Tuhfat al-Murid explains five categories of acts that are not started with basmala or hamdala, ranging from trivial matters to acts with their own prescribed beginnings.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic text once to get a feel for its flow. Then read the literal translation. Pay attention to the structure: it lists five exclusions.
Notice the repeated word 'خرج' (excluded) which marks each new category.
2
Identify the five excluded categories
Find each of the five categories mentioned: trivial matters, inherently prohibited, inherently disliked, pure dhikr, and acts with a prescribed beginning other than basmala/hamdala.
The passage also contrasts 'inherent' vs 'external' in rulings for prohibited and disliked acts.
3
Understand the rulings for each category
For each category, note whether basmala/hamdala is sunna, prohibited, disliked, or simply not prescribed. Compare the rulings for inherently vs externally prohibited/disliked acts.
The ruling changes when the prohibition is due to an external factor rather than the act itself.
4
Reflect on the wisdom of these exceptions
Consider why the Lawgiver made these exceptions. How does this preserve the dignity of sacred phrases and the integrity of acts of worship?

غير البسملة والحمدلة (۱) ، فخرجت سفاسف الأمور كلبس النعل والبصاق والمخاط، فلا تسن البسملة ولا الحمدلة عليها، وخرج المحرَّمُ لذاته كالزنا، والمكروه لذاته كالنظر لفرج زوجته بلا حاجة (٢) ، فتحرم على الأول، وتكره على الثاني، بخلاف المحرَّم لعارض كالوضوء بماء مغصوب والمكروه لعارض كأكل البصل، فلا تحرم على الأول، ولا تكره على الثاني، وخرج الذكر المحض كـ (لا إله إلا الله)، فلا تسنُ التسمية عليه، بخلاف غير المحض كالقرآن؛ لاشتماله على غير الذكر؛ كالأخبار والمواعظ، وخرج ما جعلَ الشارع له مبدأ غير البسملة والحمدلة؛ كالصلاة، فلا يبدأ بالبسملة ولا بالحمدلة، بل بالتكبير مثلاً .
Other than basmala and hamdala (1), so trivial matters like putting on a sandal, spitting, and mucus are excluded; so basmala and hamdala are not sunna for them. And what is inherently prohibited, like adultery, and what is inherently disliked, like looking at one's wife's genitals without need (2), are excluded; so it is prohibited for the first, and disliked for the second. Unlike what is prohibited due to an external factor, like performing wudu with usurped water, and what is disliked due to an external factor, like eating onions; so it is not prohibited for the first, nor disliked for the second. And pure remembrance like 'La ilaha illa Allah' is excluded; so saying the tasmiya is not sunna for it. Unlike impure remembrance like the Qur'an, because it includes other than remembrance, such as narratives and exhortations. And that for which the Lawgiver has prescribed a beginning other than basmala and hamdala, like prayer, is excluded; so it is not begun with basmala nor with hamdala, but rather with the takbir, for example.

Commentary
This passage classifies the types of actions that are excluded from the sunna of beginning with basmala or hamdala. It lists five categories: 1) Trivial matters (safasif) like putting on a sandal, spitting, or mucus – these are not started with basmala/hamdala. 2) Acts inherently prohibited (muharram li-dhatihi) like adultery – saying basmala/hamdala for them is forbidden. 3) Acts inherently disliked (makruh li-dhatihi) like looking at one's wife's genitals without need – saying them is disliked. This is contrasted with acts prohibited or disliked due to an external factor (li-‘arad), where the ruling differs. 4) Pure remembrance (dhikr mahd) like the shahada – basmala is not sunna for it, unlike the Qur'an which contains non-dhikr elements. 5) Acts with a prescribed beginning other than basmala/hamdala, like prayer – they begin with takbir, not basmala. The passage thus clarifies the scope of the sunna of basmala and hamdala.

Why Did the Author Begin the Poem with Basmala?
Defends beginning the poem with basmala by distinguishing types of poetry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic ruling on beginning didactic poetry with basmala and how the author refutes the objection.

Core Idea
Didactic poetry is exempt from the prohibition of starting with basmala.

The author anticipates a potential criticism: since his work is in verse, and scholars have said poetry should not begin with basmala, why did he do so? In this passage, he provides a clear distinction that resolves the issue. Let's see his reasoning.

Commentary
This passage addresses an objection: since the work is in verse (poetry), and scholars say poetry should not begin with basmala, why did the author begin with basmala? The answer distinguishes types of poetry. Only prohibited poetry (like lampooning lawful persons) and disliked poetry (like vague amorous verse) are not begun with basmala. Didactic poetry on sciences, like this poem, is begun with basmala by consensus. The author did not include it in the poetic line itself, unlike al-Shatibi who openly mentions 'bismillah' in his verse, because that would be contrary to what is preferable (khilaf al-awla).

The word 'thumma' and the order of salam before salah
Commentator explains 'thumma' and justifies salam before salah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the different interpretations of the word 'thumma' in the poem and the justification for placing salam before salah due to poetic necessity.

Core Idea
Poetic necessity can override conventional order without altering doctrinal rank.

In this passage, the commentator unpacks the word 'thumma' and explains why the poet placed 'salam' before 'salah' despite the customary order. This lesson will guide you through the linguistic analysis and the theological reasoning behind the apparent reversal.

Guided Path
1
Interpret the word 'thumma'
Read the passage and note the two possible functions of 'thumma': resumptive or conjunctive. Understand the subtypes when conjunctive.
The commentator provides a detailed breakdown of the possibilities.
2
Understand the meaning of 'salam' as a greeting
Learn why 'salam' is interpreted as a supreme greeting rather than mere safety, and how this relates to the Prophet's station.
The commentator contrasts two interpretations and prefers 'greeting'.
3
Address the objection about the order
Consider the objection from the Quranic verse and see how the commentator resolves it with poetic necessity and the particle 'ma'a'.
The commentator provides a clever linguistic indication of the correct rank.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the phrase 'ثُمَّ سَلَامُ اللهِ' from the poem. The author first addresses the word 'ثُمَّ' (thumma), noting it can be interpreted either as a resumptive particle (الاستئناف) starting a new sentence, or as a conjunction (العطف). If it is a conjunction, it could indicate either sequential mention (الترتيب الذكري) or sequential rank (الترتيب الرتبي). The latter is justified because the rank of matters related to the created (the Prophet) is lower and delayed relative to matters related to the Creator (the Basmala and Praise). Thus, placing 'salam' before 'salah' in the poem is not a chronological order but a hierarchical one. Next, the commentator explains 'سلام الله' (peace of Allah) as a greeting (تحية) befitting Allah, not mere safety (أمن). This interpretation is preferred because 'أمن' might suggest the Prophet could be in need of safety, contradicting his status. However, the Prophet does fear Allah out of awe, as he said: 'إني لأخوفكم من الله'. The passage then addresses an objection: why does the author put 'salam' before 'salah' when the custom and the Quranic verse (33:56) order prayer first? The answer is poetic necessity (ضرورة النظم), but the author cleverly acknowledged the correct order by using 'مع' (with) for prayer, indicating that prayer is the primary one, like saying 'the minister with the sultan'. Thus, the order in the poem is for rhyme, not doctrine.

Understanding the Variant 'Ara' and the Meaning of 'Din'
Commentator explains line meaning and variant reading

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the linguistic meaning of 'din' and the implications of the variant reading 'ara' in the poem line.

Core Idea
Religion includes both true and false beliefs; a variant reading changes the meaning.

In this lesson, we examine a line from the poem and its commentary. The commentator explains that the word 'din' applies to any practiced belief system, even idolatry, and discusses a variant reading that affects the line's meaning. Understanding these textual notes deepens our grasp of the author's intent.

Understand the context and the 'waw' clause

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a line of the poem that begins with 'wa qad khala'. The commentator explains that the wa is a circumstantial clause, indicating the situation at the time of the sending of the Prophet. The line implies that the state of idol worship among the Arabs is called a 'din' (religion). The commentator affirms this, explaining that 'din' linguistically means any system that one practices as a religion, whether true or false. He cites Quran 3:85 to show that the term 'din' is applied to both Islam and other religions. Then he notes a variant reading in some manuscripts: 'ara' instead of 'khala'. He examines the verb 'ara', noting that it can come from two different roots: 'ara ya'ru' meaning 'to befall' (with an example from a poem), and 'ariya ya'ra' meaning 'to be empty' (like khala). The appropriate meaning in context is the latter ('became empty'), not the former. He suggests that the variant might be a metathesis (qalb) where the original 'ariya (with kasra) was changed to 'ara (with fatha). Finally, he identifies the poet of the quoted line as Abu Sakhr al-Hudhali and refers to the work Khizanat al-Adab.

Reinterpreting 'Perishing' as Potentiality
The author presents the view of later verifiers that 'halik' means 'liable to perish'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the alternative view of later verifiers (muhaqqiqun) who reinterpret the word 'halik' to mean 'liable to perish' rather than 'actually perishing', thus avoiding the need for specification.

Core Idea
The word 'perishing' can mean 'liable to perish', making the verse logically consistent without exceptions.

While many scholars specify the verse 'Everything is perishing' to exclude certain entities, a group of later verifiers offered a different solution. They argued that the word 'halik' does not mean 'actually perishing' but rather 'liable to perish', thereby preserving the verse's generality without needing specification. This lesson examines that perspective.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem
Recall the verse 'Everything is perishing' and the apparent conflict with hadith about survival.
This sets the stage for the alternative solution.
2
Learn the later verifiers' view
They reject both exemption and specification, offering a reinterpretation of 'halik'.
This is a key point: no exceptions needed.
3
Examine the meaning of 'halik'
The word 'halik' means 'liable to perish' (qabil li-l-halak), not necessarily actual perishing. Similarly, 'fan' means 'fading' or 'liable to fade'.
This linguistic reinterpretation resolves the contradiction.
4
Reflect on the implication
The verse now means everything is subject to perishing, but not that it is currently perishing, allowing for continued existence of certain entities.
The verse remains general without needing specification.

Commentary
This passage addresses the apparent contradiction between the Quranic verse 'Everything is perishing except His Face' (28:88) and the hadith that affirm the survival of certain things like the soul and the coccyx. The author states that the answer is to specify (takhsis) the general meaning of the verse, restricting it to things other than those exempted by hadith. He lists the exempted items: the soul, the coccyx, bodies of prophets and martyrs, the Throne, the Footstool, Paradise, Hell, and the Houri. Al-Suyuti's poem enumerates eight: Throne, Footstool, Hell, Paradise, coccyx, souls, the Preserved Tablet, and the Pen. The author explains that this makes the verse a case of 'general specified' (al-amm al-makhsus), where the general word is limited to some of its referents. However, he also presents the view of later verifiers (muhaqqiqun) who deny specification and instead interpret 'perishing' (halik) as 'liable to perish' (qabil li-l-halak), not as actual perishing, thus resolving the apparent conflict without specification. This second view allows the verse to remain general without needing exceptions.

The Poet's Supplication: Grammar and Theology of Hope
Commentary on the poet's supplication and the plural pronoun interpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the grammatical and theological nuances in the poet's supplication for proof, especially the use of the plural pronoun.

Core Idea
The poet's 'we' in supplication reflects divine favor, not arrogance.

The commentator unpacks the poet's line, revealing layers of meaning. From the grammar of 'this' to the continuous hope expressed by the imperfect tense, to the verb's two objects, and finally the profound debate over the pronoun 'us'—whether it refers to the community or is a plural of majesty. This lesson guides you through each layer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the grammatical analysis of 'هذا'

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the poet's line: 'هَذَا وَأَرْجُو اللهَ أَنْ يَمْنَحَنا عِنْدَ السُّؤَالِ مُطلَقاً حُجَّتَنَا'. The commentator analyzes each part. First, the word 'هذا' can be a subject or object, meaning 'this is my request' or 'ask for this'. This is a transition (takhallus) from the previous themes. Second, the verb 'أَرْجُو' is in the imperfect tense, indicating a continuous and renewed hope according to changing conditions. Third, the verb 'يَمْنَحَنا' means 'to grant us' and takes two objects: 'us' (the supplicants) and 'our proof' (hujjatana). The commentator then discusses the interpretation of the plural pronoun 'نا': it is better to take it as referring to the community of Muslims or people of knowledge, based on the hadith encouraging collective supplication. Alternatively, it could be a plural of majesty where the poet refers to himself alone, to manifest the greatness bestowed upon him by Allah in enabling him to seek knowledge and pray. The commentator cites the Quranic verse 'And as for the blessing of your Lord, narrate it' to support that this is an expression of gratitude, not pride. He concludes that this does not contradict the humility required in supplication, because acknowledging one's divine gifts is not arrogance.
