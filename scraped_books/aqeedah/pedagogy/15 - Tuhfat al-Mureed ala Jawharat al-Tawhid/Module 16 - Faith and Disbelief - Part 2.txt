The Faith of Prophets and Angels
Commentator explains faith of prophets and angels with proofs and objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the faith of prophets only increases and never decreases, while the well-known view is that angels' faith is innate and does not change, though some scholars hold it increases.

Core Idea
Prophets' faith only grows; angels' faith is innate and unchanging.

The commentator now specifies that the increase and decrease of faith applies only to ordinary believers. Prophets' faith only increases because the perfect can always receive more perfection. Angels, by contrast, have innate (jibilli) faith that does not vary. This lesson examines the proofs and objections related to these special cases.

Guided Path
1
Understand the rule for prophets
Read the text about prophets: 'فمحله في غير إيمان الأنبياء والملائكة. وأما إيمان الأنبياء فيزيد ; لأن الكامل يقبلُ الكمال، ولا ينقص'. Then consider the objection about the Mi'raj and the answer.
2
Examine the proof from Ibrahim
The commentator uses the story of Ibrahim (AS) asking to see the revival of the dead. It was not due to doubt but to seek tranquility. This supports increase of faith. How does this relate?
The text quotes Quran 2:260 and explains it.
3
Understand the hadith about doubt
The hadith 'We are more deserving of doubt than Ibrahim' is interpreted as either about the ummah or as humility. Why is this important?
It removes any implication of doubt in prophets.
4
Analyze the angels' faith
The well-known view is that angels' faith does not change because it is innate. But al-Ajhuri says it increases. Which one is presented as the main view?
The text says 'وهو المشهور' for the view that it does not increase or decrease.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the lines of the poem that deal with the increase and decrease of faith (iman). The commentator first explains that according to Ahl al-Sunnah, actions are part of the perfection of faith, so faith increases with increased obedience and decreases with decreased obedience. He clarifies that the majority of Ash'aris preponderate the view that faith increases, and this is what the poem indicates. Then he explains that the increase is caused by increased obedience (the 'ba' is causative, 'ma' is masdariyya). He then notes that while the general rule is that faith increases and decreases with deeds, Allah may also increase or decrease it by His sheer will without any external cause. Next, the commentator specifies that this increase and decrease applies to the faith of ordinary believers, not to prophets or angels. Prophets' faith only increases, because the perfect accepts more perfection, and it never decreases. He addresses an objection: the prophets experience great unveilings that might suggest a change in their faith. The answer is that this does not entail actual variation in the substance of their faith. He supports this with the example of Ibrahim (Abraham) asking to see how Allah revives the dead, which was not due to doubt but to seek tranquility of heart. He explains the hadith 'We are more deserving of doubt than Ibrahim' as either referring to the state of the ummah or as an expression of humility from the Prophet. Finally, he discusses the faith of angels: the well-known view (from Ibn al-Qayyim) is that it neither increases nor decreases because it is innate (jibilli) and what is innate does not vary. However, a minority view (from al-Ajhuri) holds that angels' faith does increase, like that of prophets.

Commentary

Transmitted Proofs for Increase and Decrease of Iman
The author cites scriptural texts for increase and addresses an objection about decrease with a supplementary premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the transmitted proofs (Quran and hadith) demonstrate that iman increases and decreases, and how the supplementary premise addresses the objection regarding the prophets' iman.

Core Idea
Iman increases and decreases; supplementary premise proves decrease, except for prophets due to infallibility.

The author now presents the scriptural evidence for the increase of iman, then addresses a potential gap: the texts only mention increase. A logical supplement is introduced to infer decrease, but this raises an objection regarding prophets. Let's examine how he resolves this.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the scriptural proofs
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Note the Quranic verses and hadiths cited. They all speak about iman increasing, but do they mention decrease?
Observe that the first hadith (Ibn 'Umar) explicitly mentions both increase and decrease.
2
Notice the gap: only increase is explicit
The author points out that the verses and the second hadith (Abu Bakr) only indicate increase, not decrease. This is a key observation – the transmitted proofs alone do not directly prove decrease.
3
Understand the supplementary premise
To prove decrease, the author adds a logical premise: 'Everything that accepts increase accepts decrease.' This is a rational principle. Understand why this completes the proof.
This premise is not from scripture but from reason.
4
Consider the objection and answer
An objection is raised: if everything that increases also decreases, then prophets' iman could decrease. How does the author answer? He says prophets are excluded because of their constant infallibility that prevents decrease.
Think about why infallibility ('isma) is a valid exception.

Al-Ghazali on Increase in Tranquility of Faith
Al-Ghazali distinguishes three meanings of 'iman' and explains increase in tranquility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Ghazali's argument that demonstrative belief does not increase in certainty but can increase in tranquility.

Core Idea
Certainty is all-or-nothing; only the heart's tranquility deepens.

Imam al-Ghazali, in his Iqtisad, offers a nuanced perspective on the increase of faith. He argues that if faith means demonstrative certainty, it cannot increase because true certainty is one level. However, the soul's tranquility towards that certainty can grow with repeated evidences. This lesson explores that distinction.

Guided Path
1
Read al-Ghazali's passage
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice he lists three meanings of 'iman' and then focuses on demonstrative belief.
2
Distinguish certainty from tranquility
Al-Ghazali says certainty is indivisible: either you have it completely or you don't. But tranquility of the soul in that certainty can vary.
Tranquility (ṭumaʾnīna) is a psychological calmness.
3
Reflect on the role of evidence
Repeated evidence does not increase certainty itself but increases the heart's attachment and ease with the truth.
This is how 'increase' can be applied to demonstrative belief.

Discrete Quantity in Actions and Refutation of Mu'tazila
Defines and negates discrete quantity; refutes Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the concept of the discrete quantity in actions and how it is negated by the oneness of actions, thereby refuting the Mu'tazila claim that humans create their own actions.

Core Idea
The discrete quantity in actions is the false idea that someone other than God brings actions into existence.

Sanusi introduces a subtle but critical distinction: actions are attributed to humans only in the mode of acquisition (kasb) and choice, not creation. This passage clarifies the concept of the discrete quantity—the erroneous notion that anyone other than Allah can originate an act. It then uses this principle to refute the Mu'tazila who claim that humans create their own voluntary actions.

Commentary

Commentary
This passage is a classification. The commentator divides riya' (showing off) into two types: jali (conspicuous) and khafi (hidden).
