The Name 'Allah': A Personal Proper Name for the Necessary Existent
The author defines 'Allah' as a proper name for the Necessary Existent and clarifies its unique status.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the unique nature of the name 'Allah' as a personal proper name, not a description or derivative, and its implications for theology and supplication.

Core Idea
'Allah' is a personal proper name, not a description or a derivative.

This passage from the commentary on Jawharat al-Tawhid defines the most fundamental name in Islam. The author clarifies that 'Allah' is a personal proper name (علم شخصي) for the Self that is Necessarily Existent, distinguishing it from descriptive titles. Understanding this distinction is crucial for avoiding theological errors about Allah's identity.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and its translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then the translation. Let the author's argument unfold naturally.
Notice the key terms: علم شخصي and علم بالغلبة.
2
Identify the main claim about 'Allah'
The author states that 'Allah' is a proper name (علم) for the Self that is Necessarily Existent. Ask yourself why this is important.
This claim reframes the name as a direct identifier, not a description.
3
Understand the specification vs. part distinction
The phrase 'الواجب الوجود' (the Necessarily Existent) is a specification of the named, not part of the meaning. If it were part, the name would be a universal term.
4
Reflect on the 'personal proper name' concept
A personal proper name indicates a specific entity in external existence without physical particularizers. Understand why physical attributes are impossible for Allah.
This refutes the idea of 'predominant name' (علم بالغلبة).
5
Consider the practical implications
The name 'Allah' is considered the greatest name, but conditions like lawful sustenance affect supplication. Think about how this connects to the earlier theological discussion.

And it is the greatest name of Allah according to the majority, and al-Nawawi chose that it is al-Hayy al-Qayyum. Commentary
This passage defines the word 'Allah' as a proper name (علم) for the Divine Self (الذات) that is Necessarily Existent (الواجب الوجود) and deserving of all praise. The author explains that the description 'the Necessarily Existent' is merely a specification (تعيين) of the named entity, not part of the meaning of the name itself; if it were part of the meaning, the name would be a universal term applicable to any necessary being. Instead, 'Allah' is a 'personal proper name' (علم شخصي), indicating a specific entity in external existence (في الخارج), not in the sense of having physical particularizers (مشخصات) like height or whiteness, as that is impossible for Allah. This understanding shows that it is not a 'predominant name' (علم بالغلبة) that originally applied to something else, contrary to some claims. The passage then states that 'Allah' is the greatest name of Allah according to the majority of scholars, though al-Nawawi preferred 'al-Hayy al-Qayyum'. The failure of supplication when using this name is due to missing conditions, especially lawful sustenance.

The Ignorance of Common People and the Need for Intellectual Reflection
Al-Yusi relates a story of two women showing theological ignorance, illustrating the need for intellectual reflection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how anecdotes of common ignorance demonstrate the necessity of systematic theological reflection (naẓar) to avoid falling into explicit disbelief.

Core Idea
Unchecked natural disposition can lead to kufr; only disciplined reflection saves belief.

Al-Yusi relates a childhood memory of two women discussing God's forgiveness. One woman's casual statement reveals a shocking theological error: implying that God was created. This anecdote is not just a quaint story—it is a proof that the human fitra, without guidance and reflection, can stray into explicit disbelief. The commentator uses this and similar examples to argue that intellectual reflection (naẓar) is an obligation for everyone, not just scholars.

The passage shows that fitra alone is not enough. Only through deliberate intellectual reflection can one avoid such errors.

Proof from the Self's Changing Attributes
The author explains the imperative 'look to yourself' and derives a proof for a creator from the changing attributes of the self.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the changing attributes of one's own self provide a rational proof for the existence of a necessary Creator.

Core Idea
Your changing self proves you need an unchanging Maker.

This lesson explores a profound argument found in the Quran and elaborated by scholars: the proof from the self. By examining our own attributes—how they appear and disappear—we can reason to the existence of a Creator who is necessary and unchanging. We will study the precise explanations of key terms and the logical structure of the argument.

Guided Path
1
Understand the phrase 'look to yourself'
Recognize that 'to yourself' means 'in your essence', focusing on the root meaning and the distinction between essence and soul.
The author clarifies that we cannot know the soul directly, so we look at its states.
2
Identify the changing attributes of the self
The passage lists many attributes like hearing, sight, knowledge, pleasure, pain, etc. Notice that these all change from existence to non-existence.
These are inseparable from the self.
3
Grasp the logical inference
Since the attributes are originated and the self cannot exist without them, the self must also be originated. Every originated thing needs a Maker.
This is the core of the proof.
4
See the Quranic support and conclusion
The verse from Surah al-Dhariyat is quoted to emphasize that the signs are within us, encouraging reflection. The author summarises the argument in a clear syllogism.
The conclusion is that the Maker is wise, all-knowing, all-powerful.

Commentary
This passage explains the phrase 'to yourself' (ila nafsika). The author clarifies that 'to' here means 'in', and 'yourself' means your essence (dhat), not the spirit, as we have no access to the spirit. The point is to consider the states (ahwal) of the essence, which include sensory attributes like hearing, sight, speech, dimensions, colors, and also internal states like knowledge, ignorance, faith, disbelief, pleasure, pain, etc. All these states are constantly changing from nonexistence to existence and vice versa, proving they are originated (haditha). Since these attributes are inseparable from the essence, the essence itself must be originated (because the necessary attendant of an originated thing is also originated). This proves the need for a wise Creator who is necessary of existence, all-knowing, all-powerful, and all-willing. The author then summarizes the argument in syllogistic form: (1) My essence is characterized by originated attributes. (2) Everything characterized by originated attributes is originated. (3) Everything originated must have a wise Maker with these attributes. The verse from Surah al-Dhariyat is cited to support this, meaning that in ourselves are signs, so we should not neglect reflection.

Your changing states reveal your contingency and point to your Creator.

The Qur'an commands us to look to ourselves, but what does that mean? The author of our text clarifies that this reflection is not on the soul, but on the essence (dhat) and its ever-changing states. These states—such as your hearing, sight, emotions, and colors—are contingent, and studying them leads to a profound proof for the existence of God.

The Proof of Divine Oneness: Concurrence and Prevention
Presents the proof of divine oneness through concurrence and prevention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical proof that establishes the oneness of God by showing that any plurality of gods leads to contradiction and impossibility.

Core Idea
Two gods cannot create the world—they either agree or disagree, and both paths lead to impossibility.

The author now presents the central proof for tawḥīd, building on the previous discussion of divine attributes. This proof uses the famous argument of 'concurrence and prevention' (al-tawarud wa al-tamānu'). By carefully analyzing what would happen if there were two gods, the proof shows that only one God can exist. As you read, notice the precise logical steps and the two branches of the argument.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of the proof
Read the opening lines where the author clarifies what is being proved: oneness of essence and attributes, i.e., no equal to God. Then follow the initial conditional: if there were two gods, the world would not exist.
The proof relies on observable existence of the world.
2
Follow the agreement scenario
The author considers the case where two gods agree on a common will. Three sub-possibilities are examined and each is shown impossible: acting together, acting in sequence, or dividing the work.
This is called 'burhan al-tawarud' because the two gods 'concur' on the same object.
3
Follow the disagreement scenario
Now consider the case where the two gods differ—one wants existence, the other annihilation. Neither can both wills be fulfilled (contradiction) nor can one will alone succeed (the other would be powerless).
This leads to impossibility as well.

Conclude the proof
Since both agreement and disagreement lead to absurdity, the original assumption of multiple gods is false. Therefore, oneness of God is established.
The proof is generalizable to any number of gods beyond two.

Notice the careful use of hypothetical reasoning and the rejection of each possibility by showing it leads to a contradiction or logical flaw (e.g., 'tahsil al-hasil', 'ijtima' mu'aththirayn').

Commentary
This passage provides the rational proof of God's oneness (tawhid). The author, al-Ujhuri, begins by distinguishing two senses of 'continuous quantity in actions'. First, multiplicity of actions is affirmed because God performs many actions. Second, participation of others in His actions is negated by the oneness of actions. Then he presents the core proof: if there were two gods, the world would not exist, but the world does exist, so there cannot be two gods. This is a standard kalam argument often called the 'argument from mutual hindrance' or 'burhan al-tamana'. The author then explains the argument in detail by considering two scenarios: the two supposed gods either agree or disagree. In the case of agreement, he lists three possibilities for how they might cooperate: simultaneous creation, sequential creation, or creation of parts. All are impossible due to logical problems (two agents on one effect, tahsil al-hasil, incapacity due to blocking). This is called 'burhan al-tawarud'. In the case of disagreement, where one wants to create and the other to destroy, either both wills are executed (impossible as contradictory), or only one is executed. But if only one is executed, the other is incapable, and since they are assumed equal, the other would also be incapable. So the one whose will prevails is the true god. This is 'burhan al-tamana'. The author cites Ibn Rushd to reinforce that the successful will indicates divinity. The conclusion is that the existence of the world necessitates one God.

The indivisible atom is the smallest real part, and all bodies are made of it, so the world is created.

In this passage, the commentator explains the key term 'jawhar fard' (indivisible atom) and its property of being originated (hadith). By showing that all bodies are composed of these atoms, the author argues that the entire world is temporally created, establishing a cornerstone of Islamic kalam against the philosophers' eternalist views.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of the indivisible atom
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. Notice that the atom cannot be divided in any real sense—cutting, breaking, estimation, or supposition. The author stresses that this indivisibility is in accordance with reality.
The intellect can imagine dividing further, but that is impossible in reality.
2
Understand the meaning of 'hadith'
The atom is described as 'hadith', meaning it is preceded by non-existence. The author explicitly defines 'hadith' as that which was not then came to be.
3
Trace the logical chain to the world's temporality
Since all bodies are composed of these originated atoms, each body is originated. The entire world, being a collection of bodies, is therefore temporally created. This is the Muslim position.
This argument directly refutes the philosophers who claim the world is eternal.

His statement: (And the indivisible atom [jawhar fard] is originated [hadith]) with the sukun of the thā' for the necessity of meter, meaning: the indivisible atom is the part that does not divide, such that it does not accept division at all—neither by cutting, nor by breaking, nor by estimation [wahm], nor by supposition [farḍ] in accordance with reality; otherwise, the intellect may suppose the impossible. And the meaning of its being originated is that it is preceded by non-existence, because the only meaning of 'originated' is that which is preceded by non-existence. And all bodies are composed of it, so they are originated, and the entire world in all its parts is originated. This is the doctrine of the Muslims. And the philosophers said: All bodies are composed of hyle [hayūlā]—that is, matter, like clay for a pitcher—and of form [ṣūra]; which according to them is a substance subsisting in another, like the 'pitcher-ness' subsisting in the clay, and a mediator [wasīṭa] between them. And what is meant by 'mediator' in logic is the third possibility; for example, our saying: Zayd is either standing or in a squatting position, there is a third possibility, which is that he is sitting. So what is meant is not mediation [tawassuṭ] but rather simply the third state. And according to us, it (i.e., the form) is an accident, not a substance. And His statement: (According to us it is not denied), meaning: among us, the community of Muslims, its existence and establishment in reality is not denied, because God is able to separate bodies such that no part remains on another. His purpose in that is to refute the philosophers who deny the indivisible atom. And on the disagreement concerning its existence and non-existence depends the assertion of the temporality [ḥudūth] of the world or its eternity [qidam]. And when you know that, you know that this issue should be known and given attention, so be attentive.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof: it defines the indivisible atom (jawhar fard) as the part that cannot be divided in any real sense, and then argues that it is 'originated' (hadith), i.e., preceded by non-existence. Since all bodies are composed of such atoms, all bodies are originated, and thus the entire world is temporally created. This is the Muslim position. The passage then contrasts with the philosophers' view that bodies are composed of hyle (matter) and form (soul), with a mediator. The author clarifies that in logic, 'mediator' means a third possibility, not an actual intermediary. He then states that according to Ahl al-Sunnah, form is an accident, not a substance. The saying 'according to us it is not denied' means that Muslims affirm the reality of the indivisible atom, because God has power to completely disintegrate bodies. This refutes the philosophers who deny the atom. The whole debate has direct bearing on whether the world is temporal or eternal. The author concludes by emphasizing the importance of this issue.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Muslim position on the indivisible atom refutes the philosophers' eternalist materialism.

Understand the philosophers' two-component theory

2
Grasp the logical meaning of 'wasita'

3
See the Muslim critique of form
According to Muslims, form is an accident, not a substance. This counters the philosophers' view.
4
Recognize the consequence: temporality vs. eternity
