Commentary
This passage discusses the meaning of the letter ba in basmala and the etymology of the word 'ism'. First, two possible meanings of the ba are presented: accompaniment (musahaba) for blessing, or seeking help (isti‘ana). Both are permissible, but the preferable (awla) is accompaniment, because seeking help uses the ba of instrumentality, which could imply that God's name is used as a means, potentially implying ill-literary toward God. A counterargument is that seeking help by the name is like seeking help by the essence, but the author notes that the suspicion of ill-literary remains. Then the passage mentions an allusive (ishari) interpretation: the ba signifies that all existence is through God and will be through God, indicating divine attributes of perfection and transcendence above imperfection. Finally, the etymology of 'ism' is given: Basrans derive it from sumuww (highness), Kufans from wasama (to mark/sign). The text breaks off mid-sentence at 'because'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two linguistic derivations of the word 'ism' (name) according to the Basran and Kufi schools, and why the Kufi derivation is specifically from a verb.

Core Idea
The word 'ism' is either from 'highness' or 'marking' — both reveal something about naming.

Before diving into theological arguments, it helps to know the linguistic roots of key terms. Scholars have debated whether the word 'ism' (name) comes from the root meaning 'highness' (sumuww) or 'marking' (wasm). This passage presents both views and critiques a common simplification.

2
Identify the two derivations

The Basrans focus on the name's elevation over the named; the Kufis insist on derivation from verbs.
3
Understand the Kufi principle
The Kufis derive all nouns from verbs. That's why they say 'ism' comes from the past tense verb wasama (he marked), not from the noun wasm (mark).
This principle is key to their entire theory of derivation.
4
Note the criticism
The commentator says some scholars' statement that it's from wasm (mark) is 'too lenient' because it overlooks the verb-root requirement.
5
Summarize the meaning
Conclude: 'ism' essentially means 'that which indicates the named entity'.
Both derivations ultimately agree on the function.

Ism differs from musamma unless ism means the referent.

In this passage, the commentator defines hamd linguistically and gives examples to distinguish praise in return for a blessing from praise without such context. He then uses the term 'kalam' to ensure the definition covers both God's eternal praise and human praise, leading to four categories. This opening is foundational for understanding the terminology used throughout the text.

Commentary
This passage provides the linguistic definition of 'hamd'. The author defines it as verbal praise (thana' bi al-kalam) of a voluntary beautiful action in a way that shows veneration and glorification, whether or not it is in response to a blessing. He gives examples to illustrate both cases. He then clarifies the meaning of 'thana' (praise) and its opposite 'natha' (blame). He addresses a potential objection that definitions should be free from metaphor, noting that well-known metaphors are acceptable.

The Linguistic and Technical Definition of a Companion
Defines ṣaḥābī linguistically and technically, with key conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise definition of a companion (ṣaḥābī) according to Sunni scholarship, including its linguistic basis and the conditions for being considered a companion.

Core Idea
A companion is anyone who met the Prophet believing, even if they never saw or narrated from him.

This passage from Tuhfat al-Murīd clarifies who qualifies as a companion of the Prophet (ṣaḥābī). The author begins by explaining the linguistic nature of the word 'ṣuḥb' and then gives a technical definition that is broader than common assumptions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic nature of 'ṣuḥb'
Notice that 'ṣuḥb' is a collective noun, not a plural of 'ṣāḥib'. This affects how we understand the term.
The singular 'ṣāḥib' exists but the collective form is used.
2
Learn the linguistic meaning
The root meaning is 'long association' (ṭālat ʿishratuka bihī). This sets the stage for the technical definition.
3

A companion is one who met the Prophet after his mission while believing in him, in a customary meeting place (anywhere on earth).
Meeting includes both seeing and not seeing.
4

Seeing the Prophet, narrating from him, or even visually distinguishing him are not required according to the sound view.
5

This will be expanded in the next lesson.

Apostasy and Its Effect on Companionship
Explains the effect of apostasy on companionship with examples and madhhab differences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how apostasy affects the status of companionship, including its complete negation if death occurs in apostasy, and the return of companionship upon reversion to Islam according to different schools.

Core Idea
Apostasy after companionship cancels it if death occurs in that state; returning to faith restores it but without reward.

After defining the companion, the author addresses a crucial situation: what happens if a companion apostatizes? This passage explains the ruling using examples and notes differences between the Shāfiʿī and Mālikī schools regarding the return of the companion's status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling on apostasy
If a companion apostatizes and dies an apostate, he is not a companion at all. Example: ʿAbd Allāh b. Khaṭal.

2
Understand the case of returning to faith
If the apostate returns to Islam (like ʿAbd Allāh b. Abī Sarḥ), companionship returns, but without reward according to the Shāfiʿīs.
The reward is lost, but the title and certain rulings apply.
3
Note the Mālikī position
It is famously said that companionship does not return among Mālikīs, but their books express hesitation, allowing referral to the Shāfiʿī view.
4
Learn the practical outcome
The Shāfiʿī view that companionship returns is adopted for the sake of designation (tasmiya) and suitability (kafāʾa) in marriage.
This has legal implications for marriage to a companion's daughter.
