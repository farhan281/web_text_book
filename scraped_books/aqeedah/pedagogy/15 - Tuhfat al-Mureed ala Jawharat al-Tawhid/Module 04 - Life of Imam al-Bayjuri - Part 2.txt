Commentary
This passage introduces two major sections of authority in Sunni Islam: the two imams of tawhid (al-Ash'ari and al-Maturidi) as the doctrinal heads, and the imam of the Sufis, al-Junayd. It begins by citing the heading 'The Two Imams of Tawhid' and simply names al-Ash'ari and al-Maturidi. Then it turns to the phrase 'kadha Abu al-Qasim' from the poem, explaining that it refers to al-Junayd. The author notes that the poet could have been clearer by saying 'their Junayd also'. Next, it discusses the juristic disagreement over using the kunya 'Abu al-Qasim' because it is the kunya of the Prophet. Imam al-Shafi'i forbids it absolutely, while the other three imams allow it after the Prophet's death. Al-Junayd adopted the view of Abu Thawr. Three spiritual sayings of al-Junayd follow, emphasizing exclusive following of the Prophet, the gravity of turning away from Allah, and the overwhelming nature of divine generosity. The passage then narrates a story in which Iblis tries to deceive al-Junayd by serving him, but al-Junayd sees through him and uses him as a punishment. A footnote explains the term 'dakheel' (extraneous) and interprets the possible meanings of the story: either Iblis intended to make al-Junayd self-admire, or al-Junayd detected falsehood in Iblis's deeds.

Commentary
This passage is a historical narrative recounting the events immediately following the Prophet's death. It begins with Abu Bakr's statement to the gathered crowd emphasizing the necessity of a leader, and the people's affirmation. Then it describes the consultation between the Muhajirun and Ansar regarding the caliphate, with the Ansar initially suggesting two amirs. Umar argues for Abu Bakr's precedence by citing Quranic verses about the Prophet's companion in the cave. The narrative then describes the Prophet's washing and shrouding, including the disagreement over whether to remove his clothes, and the miraculous voices. The burial location is resolved by Abu Bakr's hadith, and the Prophet is buried in Aisha's house. This account serves as background for the subsequent discussion of imamate.

The Colophon of Tuhfat al-Mureed
The copyist records the completion date and prays for forgiveness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the components and significance of a manuscript colophon, particularly as recorded by Ibrahim al-Bijuri.

Core Idea
A colophon preserves the humility of the copyist and the date of transmission.

This lesson examines the concluding note of the manuscript, known as a colophon. Written by the copyist Ibrahim al-Bijuri, it records the precise date of completion and includes humble prayers, offering us a glimpse into the tradition of manuscript copying.

Commentary
This passage is the colophon of the manuscript, providing details about its completion. It states that the copying was completed on a blessed Thursday night, during the early part of the month of Ṣafar in the year 1234 AH (approximately 1818-1819 CE). The copyist is identified as Ibrāhīm al-Bījūrī, who humbly describes himself as having shortcomings and asks for forgiveness for himself, his parents, and all Muslims. The colophon concludes with a blessing upon the Prophet Muḥammad, his family, and companions, emphasizing remembrance.
