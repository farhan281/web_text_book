Renewing Repentance and the Disagreement on Its Acceptance
The commentator explains renewing repentance and the disagreement on its acceptance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the obligation to renew repentance for repeated sins and the scholarly disagreement on whether repentance is accepted with certainty or probability.

Core Idea
Repentance must be renewed for each sin, and scholars differ on the certainty of its acceptance.

The poem line 126 addresses a crucial aspect of repentance: when a person falls into sin again after repenting, they must renew their repentance. The commentator then explains the difference between persistence and occasional sin, and presents two views on whether Allah accepts repentance definitively or only probably. This lesson unpacks these points.

Guided Path
1
Read the poem line and its explanation
Read the Arabic and translation, focusing on the necessity of renewing repentance for a sin committed again.
Notice the poet's use of 'لَكِنْ يُجَدِّدْ تَوْبَةٌ'.
2
Understand the key distinction
Identify that only persistence in sins harms, not the act of sinning if followed by repentance, as supported by Quran and hadith.
3
Explore the disagreement on acceptance
Compare Al-Ash'ari's definitive acceptance view with Imam al-Haramayn and al-Qadi's probable acceptance view, noting their proofs.
The disagreement is about sins other than disbelief.
4
Note the exception for disbelief
Recognize that repentance from disbelief is accepted definitively by consensus, based on Quran 8:38.

126 - 'But repentance should be renewed for what one committed, and regarding its acceptance, their opinion has differed.' And his saying: ('But repentance should be renewed for what one committed') with sukoon on the dal because it is rajaz; i.e., it is obligatory upon him to renew repentance for the sin which he committed a second time. So only persistence in sins harms, unlike the case where whenever he falls into a sin he repents from it. Allah the Exalted said: 'Indeed Allah loves those who repent' [Al-Baqarah 2:222]; they are those who whenever they sin they repent. And in the hadith: 'The one who repents from sin is like one who has no sin.' And his saying: ('And regarding its acceptance, their opinion has differed') i.e., regarding the acceptance of repentance, the opinion of the scholars has differed. Our Imam Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari said that it is accepted definitively by a definitive proof, as is indicated by His saying: 'And He is the One who accepts repentance from His servants' [Ash-Shura 42:25], and supplication for its acceptance due to lack of confidence in its conditions. And Imam al-Haramayn and al-Qadi said that it is accepted probabilistically by a probable proof, but it is close to definitive, because it is possible that the meaning of His saying: 'And He is the One who accepts repentance from His servants' is that He accepts it if He wills. This disagreement applies to other than the repentance of the disbeliever; as for it, it is accepted definitively by a definitive proof unanimously, due to His saying: 'Say to those who disbelieve, if they desist, what has passed will be forgiven for them' [Al-Anfal 8:38].

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of line 126 of the poem. It first explains the necessity of renewing repentance for a sin that one commits again. It emphasizes that only persistence harms, not the repetition per se if one repents each time, quoting Quran and hadith. Then it addresses the disagreement on whether repentance (tawba) is accepted with certainty or only probably. Al-Ash'ari holds definitive acceptance, while Imam al-Haramayn and al-Qadi hold probability close to certainty. The passage then clarifies that this disagreement is only about the repentance of others; the repentance of a disbeliever (from kufr) is accepted definitively by consensus, citing the verse about forgiveness for those who desist from disbelief. This sets up the next discussion about the exact definition of a disbeliever's repentance.

This lesson examines a nuanced question within the topic of repentance (tawba): When a disbeliever converts to Islam, is that act itself sufficient as repentance from disbelief, or must he also feel regret (nadam) specifically for his past kufr? The commentator presents two positions, illustrating the depth of scholastic discussion on the nature of tawba.

The Ranking of the Six Essentials
Presents the ranking of the six essentials with reasoning and debates.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the ranking of the six essential matters (religion, life, lineage, intellect, wealth, honor) according to their importance in Islamic law, including the reasoning and minor debates.

Core Idea
Religion sits at the top; everything else serves to protect it.

In this passage, the commentator explains the hierarchy of the six essentials that Islamic law aims to preserve. The ranking begins with religion, followed by life, lineage, intellect, wealth, and honor—with nuanced debates about the order of intellect and lineage, and wealth and honor. Al-Sanusi offers his own opinion on the latter.

Guided Path
1
Read the ranking order
Read the opening sentences that list the six essentials in order from most emphasized to least. Note the justification given for each step.
The order is: religion, life, lineage, intellect, wealth, and honor.
2
Understand the lineage vs. intellect debate
Notice that some scholars place intellect before lineage. The commentator argues that lineage comes first because adultery is more severely prohibited than drinking wine.
3
Examine the wealth vs. honor debate
Read Al-Sanusi's argument that the punishment for taking wealth (theft, highway robbery) is greater than that for attacking honor (slander), so wealth should come first, contrary to some scholars.
There is also a special case when honor affects lineage.

Commentary
This passage ranks the six essentials according to their importance and explains how each is preserved by specific legal punishments. It begins by asserting that religion is the most emphasized because preserving all other essentials serves as a means to preserve religion. Next is life, since killing someone is next to disbelief. Then lineage, then intellect; some scholars place intellect before lineage, but the commentator says the first order (lineage then intellect) is better because adultery is more severely prohibited than drinking wine. Then wealth, and honor is at the same level unless an attack on honor disrupts lineage, in which case it rises to the level of lineage; there is also a disagreement about whether honor should precede wealth, but al-Sanusi argues that the punishment for taking wealth (theft, highway robbery) is greater than that for attacking honor (slander). The passage then comments on each term in the poem: deen (religion) means Allah's legislation; its preservation involves fighting disbelievers and apostates. Nafs (life) includes all rational beings even potentially; its preservation involves qisas. Mal (wealth) includes all lawfully ownable property; its preservation includes the hadd for theft and highway robbery. Nasab (lineage) is the parent-child bond; its preservation includes the hadd for adultery. `Aql (intellect) is preserved by the hadd for drinking wine and compensation for its loss. `Ird (honor) is a relative attribute; its preservation includes the hadd for false accusation of a chaste person and discretionary punishment for others. The passage concludes that all these must be preserved, and the most emphasized are known; the poet did not order them by emphasis only due to poetic constraints.

Is Ghibah a Major or Minor Sin?
Presents debate on sin rank and the relied-upon view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the scholarly disagreement on whether ghibah is a major (kabirah) or minor (saghirah) sin and the relied-upon distinction based on the status of the person backbitten.

Core Idea
The gravity of backbiting depends on who is backbitten.

After establishing the prohibition, the author addresses a scholarly debate: is ghibah a major or minor sin? Some scholars consider it always major, others say minor due to its prevalence. The relied-upon view distinguishes based on the victim's religious standing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the different positions
Read the passage and list the opinions: al-Qurtubi says major, others say minor, Ibn Hajar distinguishes.
2
Understand the reasoning
Notice why some consider it minor: widespread affliction makes it seem less severe. The author critiques this reasoning.
The author says 'في التعليل نظر' meaning the reasoning is questionable.
3
Focus on the relied-upon view
Ibn Hajar al-Haytami's view: ghibah of a scholar or Quran carrier is major; of others, minor. This is called 'al-mu'tamad'.
This is the view the author seems to endorse.

Duties of the Listener and Subtle Backbiting

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the seven stages of the soul (nafs) along with a cautionary note about teaching. The commentator begins by explaining the poet's plea for deliverance from the plots of his own nafs, which it calls a kind of shaytan in its plotting. He then quotes a poetic couplet to emphasize the danger of the nafs, which is more wicked than seventy devils. The commentator lists seven types of nafs: (1) al-ammara – the soul that commands evil; (2) al-lawwama – the soul that blames its owner after sin; (3) al-mulhama – the soul inspired with its wickedness and piety through striving; (4) al-mutma'inna – the soul tranquil with noble character; (5) al-radiyya – the soul content with Allah as Lord; (6) al-mardiyya – the soul upon which Allah bestows pleasure and forgiveness; (7) al-kamila – the soul that has perfections as its nature and continues to progress. He then states that after achieving perfection, one may only take up the role of spiritual guide with explicit permission. Unfortunately, few people awaken from their heedlessness and seek guidance sincerely. The wise person must strive diligently to walk the path of guidance.

The Consequence of Inclining to Shaytan, Nafs, and Hawa
The commentator explains that anyone who inclines to these three has gone astray.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that inclining to shaytan, the nafs, and hawa leads to straying from right guidance, and why these three are identified as the source of every trial.

Core Idea
Inclining to shaytan, nafs, or hawa causes departure from right guidance.

This short passage from the commentary explains a critical warning: whoever inclines toward shaytan, the ego, or desire has gone astray. These three are identified as the source of all trials. The commentator explains that this is the reason for the poet's plea for deliverance from them.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the literal translation to get a clear sense of the meaning.
Notice the phrase 'هَوْلاء' (these) – it points to something previously mentioned.
2
Identify 'these three'
Recall from the previous lesson that 'these three' refer to al-shaytan al-rajim, the nafs (especially the ammara), and al-hawa. This passage builds on that context.
The commentator explicitly says 'أحد هذه الثلاثة' (any of these three).
3
Understand 'ghawa' and its opposite
The word 'غوى' (strayed) contrasts with 'الرشد' (right guidance) and 'الاستقامة' (uprightness). Understand that inclining to these adversaries leads to loss of spiritual direction.
4
Notice the causal link
The passage states that these three are 'منشأ كل فتنة' (the source of every trial). This explains why inclining to them results in straying: they are the roots of all misguidance.
This is presented as a 'تعليل' (cause) for the poet's earlier statement.
5
Reflect on the warning
Consider why this warning precedes the plea for salvation. Recognizing the danger is the first step to seeking deliverance.
The passage ends by connecting to the poet's next line: 'ثم التي هي الخلاص' (then the salvation).
