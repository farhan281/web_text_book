The Four Categories of Things and the Theory of States
Classifies things into four categories based on the theory of ahwal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourfold classification of things according to the theory of ahwal and the position of the verifying scholars on the existence of states.

Core Idea
Things are either existents, non-existents, states, or relational matters—but the verifying scholars deny the reality of states.

This lesson presents a classification of all things into four categories based on the theory of states (ahwal), which some theologians affirm. However, the author notes that the chosen position among verifying scholars is that states do not exist. Let's explore the four categories as outlined in the passage.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note the four categories
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. Identify the four numbered categories and their brief definitions.
2
Understand the position on states
Notice that the author begins by stating the chosen opinion: there is no state. Then he presents the classification for those who affirm states. This sets up a dialectic.
3
Consider the intermediate nature of states
States are described as having subsistence but not reaching the level of existents (seen) nor the level of non-existents (pure nothing). Reflect on this middle ground.

The Fourfold Classification of Things According to the View Affirming States
Bajuri enumerates the four categories of things on the view affirming states.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four categories of things as defined by those who affirm the existence of states (aḥwāl), and the two subtypes of considerative matters.

Core Idea
Things are not simply existent or non-existent; states and considerative matters bridge the gap.

This passage from al-Bajuri's commentary presents a precise classification of all possible things according to the theological school that affirms the reality of states (aḥwāl). Beyond the basic binary of existent and non-existent, the author introduces two intermediate categories: states and considerative matters. Understanding this taxonomy is essential for grasping later arguments about divine attributes and their relationship to Allah's essence.

Guided Path
1

Identify the four main categories

The first two are opposites, the third is intermediate.

Break down considerative matters

The Grammatical and Rhetorical Structure of the Divine Names
Explication of grammatical structure and rhetorical device ihtibak.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the grammatical parsing of the line and the rhetorical device of ihtibak that links the names and attributes of Allah.

Core Idea
Ihtibak elegantly ties the greatness of Allah's names to the pre-eternity of His attributes.

This lesson unpacks a classic line from the poem on divine names. The author not only explains the grammar but also reveals a sophisticated rhetorical figure that emphasizes the inseparable connection between the names of Allah and His attributes. We will see how the omitted words are supplied by parallel clauses, creating ihtibak.

Guided Path
1
Understand the grammar
Identify the subject, predicate, and parenthetical clause in the first parsing. Note that 'His names' is the subject, 'the great' is an adjective, and 'pre-eternal' is the predicate.
The second clause 'thus the attributes of His Essence' is parsed as a parenthetical with fronted predicate.
2
Recognize the omission pattern
Notice how the commentator offers an alternative parsing based on omission: the predicate of the first clause is omitted and indicated by the later 'pre-eternal', and the adjective 'great' is omitted from the second clause and supplied by the first.
This creates a symmetry where each clause supplies what the other lacks.
3
Appreciate ihtibak
Understand that this omission pattern is the rhetorical device called ihtibak (interweaving). It emphasizes the parallelism and unity between the names and attributes, and the comparison is for emphasis.
The device is not redundancy; it is a deliberate rhetorical strengthening.

Commentary
This passage is a grammatical and rhetorical analysis of a line from the poem declaring the names of Allah are great and pre-eternal, and so are the attributes of His essence. The author first explains the lexical meaning: 'names' is the plural of ism, referring to names that indicate the essence alone (like Allah and the Persian Khuday) or names that indicate an attribute (like al-Ālim and al-Qādir). Then he parses the sentence: 'His names' is the subject, 'the great' is an adjective, and 'pre-eternal' is the predicate. The subsequent clause 'thus the attributes of His Essence' is parsed with 'thus' as a fronted predicate and 'attributes' as a delayed subject, making the entire clause parenthetical, with the comparison being in pre-eternity. The commentator offers an alternative parsing based on omission: the predicate of the first clause is omitted and indicated by the later 'pre-eternal', and the adjective 'great' is omitted from the second clause and indicated by its presence in the first. This creates the rhetorical figure of ihtibak (interweaving), where what is omitted from one is supplied by the other, emphasizing the parallelism. The description 'great' is further defined as majestic and sanctified, meaning purified from being used for other than Allah or from improper interpretation, and its greatness is a matter of consensus.

Defining Name and Attribute in Theology
Definitions of theological 'name' and 'attribute'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how theologians define 'name' and 'attribute' specifically in the context of Allah's names and attributes.

Core Idea
Names point to essence; attributes point to added meanings.

Before diving into whether divine names are tawqifiyyah, we need a clear definition of what we mean by 'name' and 'attribute' in theology. This lesson establishes the precise theological usage, distinguishing it from everyday linguistic categories like nouns vs. verbs.

Guided Path

Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the literal translation. Notice the nuance: 'name' here means what indicates the essence, not the grammatical noun.

3
Understand the exclusion
The passage explicitly rules out two other meanings of 'ism': the grammatical (noun vs verb/particle) and the onomastic (kunya vs laqab). Why is this important?
This prevents confusion with non-theological uses of the term.
4
Reflect on the theological significance
Why define name as indicating essence? This sets the stage for discussing whether names are knowable by reason or only by revelation.
The definition impacts the whole debate on tawqifiyyah.

Commentary
This passage defines the terms 'ism' (name) and 'sifa' (attribute) as they are used in the context of the previous discussion on whether they are tawqifiyyah. The commentator notes that in the line of poetry being explained, the word 'asmā'uhu' (His names) has had its first hamza dropped for the sake of meter. He then clarifies that what is meant by 'names' here are those that correspond to attributes, not the linguistic categories of noun, verb, or particle. The key definitions: a 'name' is something that indicates the essence itself (e.g., the proper name 'Allah'), while an 'attribute' is something that indicates a meaning that subsists in the essence but is additional to it (e.g., 'knowledge', 'power'). Finally, the commentator emphasizes that the term 'name' here does not refer to the grammatical category (opposed to verb and particle), nor does it refer to teknonymy (kunya) or nicknames (laqab). Thus, the discussion is specifically about divine names and attributes.

Core Idea
In kalam, 'opposite' of Allah's attributes includes both existing and non-existing contradictions.

This passage clarifies a crucial distinction in Islamic theology: when we say that something is impossible for Allah, we often describe it as the 'opposite' of His attributes. The commentator explains exactly what 'opposite' means here—and why it isn't the same as the philosophical definition. You'll also learn two ways to understand the phrase 'في حقه'.

The Grammatical Rule and the Exception of the Shahada
Sanusi refutes the application of the grammatical rule to the shahada.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the shahada cannot follow the usual grammatical rule that negation + universal yields 'salb al-'umum', and why it is specifically 'umum al-salb'.

Core Idea
The shahada defies the usual linguistic rule because applying that rule would nullify tawhid.

The formula 'La ilaha illa Allah' appears to follow the pattern 'negation + universal noun (ilaha)'. According to Arabic grammar, when the negation tool precedes the universal tool (like 'kull'), the statement is usually 'salb al-'umum' (negation of the universal). But the author argues that applying this rule to the shahada would destroy its meaning. This lesson examines that grammatical nuance and why the shahada is an exception.

If it were 'salb al-'umum', it would mean 'there is no god who encompasses all divinity' or 'the category of divinity is not universal'—which doesn't negate false gods. That would not convey tawhid.
4

The rule is a general rule (ghalibiyya), not absolute. The shahada is an exception due to its unique meaning.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ash'ari theologians define 'thing' (shay') exclusively as the existent and how this refutes the Mu'tazilite notion of non-existent things having reality.

Core Idea
For Sunnis, 'thing' = existent; non-existents are not things.

This passage from the commentary on Jawharat al-Tawhid establishes a foundational terminological principle: the word 'thing' (shay') in technical theological discourse is synonymous with 'existent' (mawjud). The author clarifies that this definition excludes all non-existent entities—whether possible or impossible—and then contrasts this with the Mu'tazilite view that non-existents are 'things' with a fixed reality. Understanding this distinction is crucial for grasping later discussions on divine attributes and creation.

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'thing' (shay') within the Ash'ari theological framework. The commentator begins by stating that according to Ahl al-Haqq (the people of truth, i.e., the Ash'aris and others), 'thing' is identical with 'existent' (mawjud). He explains that an entity considered in itself, in terms of its actualization (tahaqquq), is called 'thing', and considered in terms of its actualization in the external world is called 'existent'. Thus 'thing' and 'existent' are coextensive (mutasawiyan ma sadaqan): whenever 'thing' applies, 'existent' applies, and vice versa. Therefore everything that is a thing is existent, and every existent is a thing. The non-existent (ma'dum) is not a thing, whether it is a possible non-existent (mumkin) or an impossible one (mumtani'), because things have no established reality (thubut) in themselves before their existence. This contrasts with the Mu'tazilite view, which holds that non-existent things are 'things' because they have a fixed reality in themselves before existence, merely concealed like a garment in a chest. Consequently, the Mu'tazila believe that realities are not created by a maker, and divine power only brings them forth from concealment. The Sunnis affirm that things are created by a maker, and power is connected to their existence because they had no prior established reality. This is the technical (istilahi) definition; lexically, 'thing' can refer to anything, existent or non-existent. The commentator then includes a footnote from the commentator (likely al-Ajhuri) explaining that according to some, 'thing' and 'existent' are conceptually distinct (mutaghayiran mafhuman) but coextensive; according to others they are synonymous (mutaradifan). The author's statement can accommodate both, though it leans towards synonymy. He also summarizes Zakariyya al-Ansari's view: for Sunnis, 'thing' is the existent; for Mu'tazila, it is what has mental or external actualization.

Commentary
This passage states two doctrinal positions: (1) the existence of a thing is identical to its essence (not an additional attribute), and (2) the indivisible atom (الجوهر الفرد) is created. The commentator explains the first point by quoting al-Ash'ari and his followers, who hold that the existence of something is exactly its reality. This opposes Imam al-Razi, who asserts that existence is a 'state' (الحال) that inheres in the essence and is not caused. The footnote clarifies that for al-Ash'ari, existence is not an extra attribute in the external world; it is a mental consideration (أمر اعتباري) equivalent to actuality. We can conceive an essence without some attributes (like knowledge), but we cannot conceive an essence without existence. The passage also touches on the theory of 'states' (الأحوال) held by al-Baqillani and al-Juwayni, who maintain that states are fixed in themselves, neither existent nor non-existent, but the majority of the Ash'aris reject this view. The indivisible atom is affirmed as created, which is the Ash'ari position on atomism. The commentary also includes a remark that the earlier definition of 'existent' as 'fixed externally' is not the author's intended meaning; rather, it is a refutation of the Sophists, affirming the reality of concepts like 'human', 'animal', etc. The text is cut off at the end, but the core teaching is clear: existence is identical to essence, and atoms are created.

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'hawa' (desire) and distinguishes it from 'hawa'' (air). The commentator explains that 'hawa' (with qasr, short vowel) refers to the soul's inclination toward what it desires, even if that leads to destruction. In its unqualified use, it usually implies inclination contrary to the truth, as in the Quranic verse 'And do not follow hawa'. However, it can also be used for inclination to the truth, as shown in the hadith where Aisha says that Allah hastens to fulfill the Prophet's desire. The term 'hawa' is so called because it causes its follower to fall (yahwi) into the Fire. Then the commentator contrasts it with 'al-hawa'' (with madd, long vowel), which means the wind between heaven and earth that sails ships. A poetic evidence is quoted in which a poet speaks of both being combined in him: the long one (hawa') causes him to fall short of his hopes, and the short one (hawa) leads to his death and shrouding. The poem illustrates the destructive effect of desire and the softness of wind that leads to laziness. The explanation of the poem makes clear the moral: trusting desire leads to ruin.

Meanings of the Prophet's Family, Progeny, and Followers
Al-Bijuri defines three terms and argues for the universality of the prayer's recipients.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise meanings of the terms for the Prophet's family, progeny, and followers as used in the prayer supplication, and why the author emphasizes generality over exclusivity.

Core Idea
The prayer for the Prophet includes all who follow his path, encompassing believers past and future.

In this passage, al-Bijuri unpacks three key phrases from the poem—'his family', 'his progeny', and 'a follower of his path'. He clarifies that the prayer covers not only the immediate household but every believer who follows the Prophet's way, even if sinful. He then addresses a potential misunderstanding about the phrase 'from his nation', ensuring that its scope is universal, not limited to a specific era.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on three phrases from the opening of the poem: 'his family' (Ālihī), 'his progeny' ('Itratihi), and 'a follower of his path' (Tābi‘ li-Nahjihi). The author first defines 'Ālihī' as those on whom eternal prayer and peace are sent, noting he previously discussed the family. For 'Itratihi', he explains it as the Prophet's household, offering alternative opinions: his wives or his offspring and close kin. For 'Tābi‘ li-Nahjihi', he expands that it includes any follower of the Prophet's way, even if only by faith—thus including sinful believers. He notes the purpose of this generality is to encompass all in the supplication, as it is superior. Next, he analyzes the phrase 'from his nation' (min Ummatihi), stating it refers to the 'nation of response' (those who accept his call). He emphasizes that this restriction is for clarification, not to exclude genuine followers who might not seem part of his nation, because any follower of his law is necessarily from his nation due to the universality of his mission. He then addresses a potential objection: could someone follow his law without being from his nation, like Jesus when he descends? The author answers that at that time Jesus will be from his nation. The benefit is to affirm generality, preventing the mistaken idea that only the first three centuries are meant, drawing an analogy with Quran 6:38. He concludes the commentary, offering supplications for acceptance and forgiveness.
