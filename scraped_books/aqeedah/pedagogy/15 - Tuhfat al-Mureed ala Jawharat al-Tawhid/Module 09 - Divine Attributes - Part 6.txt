Refuting the Mu'tazila: Vision Without Modality
Refutation of Mu'tazila rational objection to ru'ya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila rational objection against seeing Allah and the Ash'ari response that vision is a special power not requiring direction or space.

Core Idea
Vision of Allah is possible without physical constraints; it is a power He creates in the seer.

The Mu'tazila argued that seeing Allah is impossible because it would require Him to be in a direction and location—attributes impossible for the Creator. The author responds by affirming vision 'without modality' (bila kayf) and denying that such conditions are necessary. Let's unpack this crucial refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazila objection and the Ash'ari answer
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. Identify the Mu'tazila claim and the two parts of the answer: denial of necessity and the positive statement that vision is a power.
The answer distinguishes between customary necessity ('ada) and rational necessity ('aql).
2
Understand the meaning of 'bila kayf'
The qualification 'but without modality' means that the seen object (Allah) is not characterized by any modality of contingent things, such as direction or space.
This is a key Ash'ari concept to preserve divine transcendence.
3
Grasp the distinction between custom and reason
The answer states that direction and facing are required by custom ('ada) in this world, not by reason ('aql). Therefore, they are not necessary for a divinely empowered vision.
This undermines the Mu'tazila claim of rational impossibility.

Commentary
This passage is a theological clarification and refutation. It begins by establishing two distinct issues concerning ru'ya: (1) its permissibility (jawāz) – is it possible? and (2) its occurrence (wuqū‘) – did it happen or will it happen? The author notes that the primary focus of the text (al-Jawhara) is the first station. Then, he explains the author's phrase 'with the eyes': this literally means vision with the pupils, but three opinions exist: (i) only with the pupils; (ii) with the entire face, based on the verse; (iii) with every body part, as reported from Abu Yazid al-Bistami. The author then addresses the crucial qualification 'but without modality' (lākin bi-lā kayf). This is to prevent the misunderstanding that Allah is seen with a modality like physical beings. The vision is real but not subject to conditions like direction, location, etc. The main purpose is to refute the Mu'tazila rational argument that vision of Allah is impossible because it would require direction and spatial location (jiha and hayyiz), which are attributes of contingent beings. The answer is that the necessity of facing and location is denied; vision is a power Allah creates in the seer, not dependent on such conditions. The Mu'tazila claim of necessity is rejected because it is not a rational necessity but only a customary one (in this world). Thus, the doctrine of ru'ya without modality is preserved.

Seeing Allah in Paradise and on the Day of Judgment
Author discusses ru'ya for awliya in Jannah and on the Day of Judgment with hadith evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two contexts in which believers see Allah in the afterlife: the continuous vision of the special ones in Paradise and the vision on the Day of Judgment, along with the evidence from hadith and the interpretive approach to 'uncovering the shin'.

Core Idea
Seeing Allah is the ultimate reward, occurring for the righteous in Paradise daily and for all believers at the Resurrection.

The author now addresses the beatific vision (ru'ya) as a reward for the righteous. He first describes the special state of the awliya who see Allah continuously in Paradise, then presents the hadith evidence for the vision on the Day of Judgment, and finally explains the meaning of the Quranic expression 'uncovering the shin.' This passage combines a spiritual insight with a scriptural proof and a creedal clarification.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage about the special ones
Notice the quote from Abu Yazid al-Bistami emphasizing how the awliya would complain about Paradise if veiled from Allah.
2
Understand the vision on the Day of Judgment
Follow the hadith sequence: the nations gather, the Muslims wait for their Lord, initial unrecognized manifestation, then the recognized manifestation and prostration.
3
Interpret 'uncovering the shin'
Learn that later scholars (khalaf) interpret it as removal of the veil, while the early predecessors (salaf) delegate the meaning to Allah.
4
Reflect on the creedal significance
Recognize that ru'ya is affirmed for believers, and that the hypocrite sees but does not prostrate, indicating his punishment.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of the occurrence of seeing Allah (ru'ya) in the afterlife, both for the special servants (khawass) in Paradise and for all believers on the Day of Judgment. The author first mentions that the special ones (awliya) see Allah every morning and evening in Paradise, and some of them are continuously in a state of witnessing (shuhud). He quotes Abu Yazid al-Bistami, who says that if these special ones were veiled from seeing Allah for even an hour, they would cry out for relief from Paradise and its pleasures just as the people of Hell cry out for relief from the Fire. Then the author moves to the courtyards of the Resurrection (arasat al-qiyamah), specifically the standing (al-mawqif). He states that the correct view (al-sahih) is that ru'ya occurs there as well, because there is evidence from the Sunnah. He then cites the well-known hadith in which, on the Day of Judgment, the nations are called to follow their objects of worship. This nation (the Muslims) says that they will wait for their Lord. Then Allah appears to them initially in a form they do not recognize, and they seek refuge from Him. Then He manifests Himself (yatajalla) in a way appropriate to that station and 'uncovers the shin' (kashf 'an al-saq). At that point, the believers see Him as they know Him (i.e., according to their belief), and they prostrate, except the hypocrite. The author then explains that the verse 'The Day when the shin shall be uncovered' (Quran 68:42) by the later scholars (khalaf) is interpreted as the removal of the veil, while the early predecessors (salaf) delegated the meaning to Allah. He directs the reader to the commentators of Sahih al-Bukhari for further details.

The Prophet's Vision of Allah in this World: Affirmation and Principle
Author affirms the Prophet's vision in this world and addresses the Aisha-Ibn Abbas disagreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the majority of scholars affirmed the Prophet's vision of Allah with his physical eyes during the Night Journey, and that the principle 'the affirmer outweighs the denier' resolves the disagreement between Aisha and Ibn Abbas.

Core Idea
The Prophet saw Allah with his eyes; Ibn Abbas's affirmation takes precedence over Aisha's denial.

Having established that seeing Allah is rationally possible, the author now turns to its actual occurrence. He affirms that the Prophet saw his Lord during the Night Journey, and addresses a key disagreement among the companions. The passage provides a crucial principle for weighing conflicting reports.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the transition
The author moves from discussing possibility to actuality. Notice the phrase 'هذا تخلص من بحث إلى بحث آخر'.
2
Understand the majority view
The Prophet saw Allah with his physical eyes (بعيني رأسه) according to most scholars, contrary to the claim that his eyesight was transferred to his heart.

Learn the principle of affirmer over denier
Ibn Abbas affirmed the vision, Aisha denied it. The rule that the affirmer (مثبت) is preferred over the denier (نافي) settles the matter, as stated by Ma'mar ibn Rashid.

The Inner Wisdom of Moses' Repetitions during the Isra'
Author explains the wisdom behind Moses' repeated returns to the Prophet during the Isra'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the inner and outer wisdom behind Moses' repeated returns to the Prophet during the Isra', as explained by Ibn Wafa.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed derivation of the beliefs (العقائد) that follow necessarily from the first testimony. The author takes the two implications of the testimony—self-sufficiency (الاستغناء) and dependence (الافتقار)—and extracts from each the necessary, impossible, and possible attributes of Allah.

The author notes this is based on the doctrine of states (الأحوال), a nuanced position in kalam regarding the ontological status of attributes. Adding 23 from self-sufficiency and 18 from dependence gives 41 beliefs: 20 necessary (11+9), 20 impossible (11+9), and 1 possible. The author concludes that the first testimony thus contains the three divisions of rational judgment (الحكم العقلي) regarding Allah.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed derivation of the beliefs (العقائد) that follow necessarily from the first testimony. The author takes the two implications of the testimony—self-sufficiency (الاستغناء) and dependence (الافتقار)—and extracts from each the necessary, impossible, and possible attributes of Allah.

First, from self-sufficiency: Allah must be necessary in existence, pre-eternal, everlasting, unlike created things, self-subsisting, and transcendent. Additionally, He must have the attributes of hearing, sight, and speech (and their concomitants of being Hearing, Seeing, Speaking). The author notes this is based on the doctrine of states (الأحوال), a nuanced position in kalam regarding the ontological status of attributes. The proof is given: if these attributes were not necessary, Allah would be in need of something else to give them to Him. Thus, these eleven attributes are necessary (واجبة), and their opposites are impossible (مستحيلة), giving another eleven. Finally, from self-sufficiency also follows the negation of any necessity for Allah to do or refrain from any possible act—He is free from compulsion—which is one possible (جائز) belief. Total: 11+11+1=23 beliefs from self-sufficiency.

Second, from dependence: Allah must have life, power, will, and knowledge (and their concomitants of being Living, Powerful, Willing, Knowing), plus oneness (الوحدانية). That gives 9 necessary attributes. Their opposites are impossible, so 9 impossible. Total from dependence: 18 beliefs.

Adding 23 from self-sufficiency and 18 from dependence gives 41 beliefs: 20 necessary (11+9), 20 impossible (11+9), and 1 possible. The author concludes that the first testimony thus contains the three divisions of rational judgment (الحكم العقلي) regarding Allah.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Prophethood is a pure gift from Allah, not earned by human effort.

The poem line 'Bal dhālika faḍlu llāh' asserts that prophethood is not a matter of human merit but a grant from Allah. Commentary
This is a detailed commentary on the line 'Bal dhālika faḍlu llāh yuʾtīhi li-man yashāʾu jalla llāhu wāhibu l-minan'. The commentator begins by identifying the rhetorical function: it is an abrupt transition (iḍrāb intiqālī) that corrects or shifts, not invalidates the previous statement. The demonstrative 'dhālika' refers to prophethood previously mentioned. He defines faḍl as giving without any recompense, a quality exclusive to Allah. He notes an implied deletion: the meaning is that the mentioned prophethood is 'an effect of Allah's favor'. Alternatively, 'dhālika' could refer to the selection for prophethood itself, which is a part of Allah's favor. Then he explains 'yuʾtīhi li-man yashāʾu' as past tense (ā'tāhu) even though the verb is in the present tense—this use of the present is to evoke the wondrous nature. The first verb refers to the giving of prophethood which has ceased because Muhammad is the seal; the second refers to the eternal will, which is ongoing. The pronoun in 'yuʾtīhi' refers to al-faḍl in the sense of 'the bestower', not the favor itself, thus a case of istikhdām (one word used in two different senses). Then 'jalla llāhu' means He is exalted above anything being obtained contrary to His will. Then 'wāhibu l-minan' is explained as 'giver of gifts without compensation'. The commentator addresses a potential logical redundancy (taḥṣīl al-ḥāṣil): if it is already established that He gives, saying 'He gives gifts' seems redundant. He argues this is not a metaphor; rather, the phrase specifies that He gives gifts by this particular giving, just as in the hadith 'man qatala qatīlan fa-lahu salabuhu' is not a metaphor because the killing referred to is a specific one. The commentator then discusses the definite article 'al' in 'al-minan': whether it indicates a specific covenant (al-ʿahd) meaning the complete type like prophethood, or generality (al-istighrāq). He prefers generality. Finally, he raises an important theological point: Allah's names are tawqīfiyya (determined by revelation). The name 'al-Wāhib' is not among the established names (only 'al-Wahhāb' is). So how can the author use it? He offers two solutions: either the author follows the view that the mere occurrence of the root suffices, or the view that any term indicating perfection can be used even if not explicitly reported. Alternatively, if 'al-Wāhib' is indeed reported (as some claim from Ibn Ḥajar's commentary), then there is no issue at all.

Commentary
This is a detailed commentary on the line 'Bal dhālika faḍlu llāh yuʾtīhi li-man yashāʾu jalla llāhu wāhibu l-minan'. The commentator begins by identifying the rhetorical function: it is an abrupt transition (iḍrāb intiqālī) that corrects or shifts, not invalidates the previous statement. The demonstrative 'dhālika' refers to prophethood previously mentioned. He defines faḍl as giving without any recompense, a quality exclusive to Allah. He notes an implied deletion: the meaning is that the mentioned prophethood is 'an effect of Allah's favor'. Alternatively, 'dhālika' could refer to the selection for prophethood itself, which is a part of Allah's favor. Then he explains 'yuʾtīhi li-man yashāʾu' as past tense (ā'tāhu) even though the verb is in the present tense—this use of the present is to evoke the wondrous nature. The first verb refers to the giving of prophethood which has ceased because Muhammad is the seal; the second refers to the eternal will, which is ongoing. The pronoun in 'yuʾtīhi' refers to al-faḍl in the sense of 'the bestower', not the favor itself, thus a case of istikhdām (one word used in two different senses). Then 'jalla llāhu' means He is exalted above anything being obtained contrary to His will. Then 'wāhibu l-minan' is explained as 'giver of gifts without compensation'. The commentator addresses a potential logical redundancy (taḥṣīl al-ḥāṣil): if it is already established that He gives, saying 'He gives gifts' seems redundant. He argues this is not a metaphor; rather, the phrase specifies that He gives gifts by this particular giving, just as in the hadith 'man qatala qatīlan fa-lahu salabuhu' is not a metaphor because the killing referred to is a specific one. The commentator then discusses the definite article 'al' in 'al-minan': whether it indicates a specific covenant (al-ʿahd) meaning the complete type like prophethood, or generality (al-istighrāq). He prefers generality. Finally, he raises an important theological point: Allah's names are tawqīfiyya (determined by revelation). The name 'al-Wāhib' is not among the established names (only 'al-Wahhāb' is). So how can the author use it? He offers two solutions: either the author follows the view that the mere occurrence of the root suffices, or the view that any term indicating perfection can be used even if not explicitly reported. Alternatively, if 'al-Wāhib' is indeed reported (as some claim from Ibn Ḥajar's commentary), then there is no issue at all.

Core Idea
Allah's names are tawqīfiyya, but 'al-Wāhib' can be justified by occurrence of the root or by a reported variant.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of a line from the poem concerning the divine recompense for deeds. The author begins by stating the line: 'And evil deeds with Him are by the like, and good deeds are multiplied by grace.' He then comments on each part. First, he explains that evil deeds are punished by a like punishment if God chooses to punish them, but He may forgive them unless they are unbelief, in which case the doer is eternally in the Fire. He defines 'evil deed' as any act for which the doer is blameworthy according to the Law, minor or major. It is called 'evil deed' because the doer will be distressed when confronted with it on the Day of Judgment. The author clarifies that the evil deeds meant are those actually committed by the servant or those placed upon him due to a wrong he did to another, after his own good deeds are exhausted. In that case, the oppressor's good deeds are given to the oppressed, and if they are exhausted, the oppressed's evil deeds are placed on the oppressor, then the oppressor is thrown into the Fire. Next, he explains the second part: 'good deeds are multiplied by grace,' meaning that God multiplies them by His grace, not out of obligation. He defines 'good deed' as any act for which the doer is praised according to the Law, called so because of the beauty of its doer's face when seeing it on the Day of Judgment. The author then details the types of good deeds that are subject to multiplication. The intended good deeds are the 'accepted, original' ones actually performed by the servant or those in their ruling, such as charity given by another on one's behalf. Excluded are: (a) rejected deeds due to hypocrisy—these have no reward; (b) original deeds that are themselves the result of multiplication—they are not multiplied again; (c) good deeds merely intended—they are recorded as one without multiplication, similarly if one resolves on a sin then abandons it. Also excluded are good deeds taken in exchange for a wrong, i.e., when the oppressed takes good deeds from the oppressor—these are not multiplied. Multiplication is a distinctive feature of this Muslim community; other communities had their good deeds rewarded with a single good deed. The minimum multiplication is tenfold, and it can increase to seventy, seven hundred, or more without limit. The variation in multiplication depends on the sincerity and good intention accompanying the good deed.

قوله : (الدَّائِمُ) أي : كلُّ منهما، ويحتمل أن يكون صفة للسلام، ويكون المصنف حذف من الصلاة نظيره والتقدير ثم الصلاة الدائمة والسلام الدائم ؛ فيكون في كلامه الأول لدلالة الثاني، وإن كان خلاف الغالب ؛ وهو الحذف من الثاني لدلالة الأول. ولا يخفى أن الدوام باعتبار فضلهما وثمرتهما لا باعتبار لفظهما ؛ لأنهما عَرَضان ينقضيان بمجرد النطق بهما .
قوله : ( عَلَى نَبِيِّ) أي : كائنان على نبي .
وقوله : (دَأْبُهُ المَرَاحِمُ) جملةٌ من مبتدإ وخبرٍ صفةٌ لـ (نبي)؛ أي: على نبي موصوف بأن دأبه المراحم . ومعنى الدأب العادة والمراحم جمع مرحمة بمعنى الرحمة، فالمعنى: عادته المستمرة الرحمة للعالمين. ففيه تلميح لقوله تعالى: ﴿وَمَا أَرْسَلْنَاكَ إِلَّا رَحْمَةً لِلْعَالَمِينَ [الأنبياء: ۱۰۷] .
وقوله : (مُحَمَّدٍ) بدل من (نبي) أو عطف بيان عليه زاده الله تشريفاً وتكريماً لديه، وإنما ترك الناظم وصفه بالسيادة لضرورة النظم، وإلا فيستحبُّ وصفه بالسيادة استعمالاً للأدب، كما قاله الجلال المحلي في الصلاة وغيرها. وأما حديث: لا تُسيّدوني في صلاتكم فقال السيوطي: لا أصل له
وروى أبو داود (١٤٨١) من حديث فضالة ابن عبيد عن النبي : إذا صلى أحدكم فليبدأ بتحميد ربه والثناء عليه، ثم يُصلي على النبي ، ثم يدعو بما شاء». قال أبو سليمان الداراني رحمه الله : من أراد أن يسأل الله حاجةٌ فليبدأ بالصلاة على النبي ، ثم يسأله حاجته، ثم يختم بالصلاة على النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم ، فإن الله يقبل الصلاتين، وهو أكرم من أن يدع ما بينهما .
(۱) قوله : (ولا يخفى ... إلخ) لا حاجة إلى هذا ؛ لأن الصلاة في كلام المصنف بمعنى الرحمة، والسلام في كلامه بمعنى التحية، وهما موصوفان بالدوام، ومعنى كلام المصنف : ثم الرحمة والتحية الدائمتان على نبي. انتهى أفاده الأمير. انتهى أجهوري.
His saying: (the perpetual) means: each of them (i.e., prayer and peace). It is possible that it is an adjective for peace, and that the author omitted its counterpart from prayer, the estimation being: then the perpetual prayer and the perpetual peace. So in his statement, the first indicates the second, although it is contrary to the usual pattern, which is omission from the second indicated by the first. And it is not hidden that perpetuity is with respect to their virtue and reward, not with respect to their utterance, because they are accidents that pass away upon mere pronunciation.
His saying: (upon a Prophet) means: both are upon a Prophet.
His saying: (whose habit is mercies) is a sentence of subject and predicate that is an adjective for 'Prophet'; i.e., upon a Prophet described by the fact that his habit is mercies. The meaning of 'da'b' is habit, and 'al-marahim' is the plural of 'marhama' meaning mercy. So the meaning: his constant habit is mercy for the worlds. In it is a hint to His saying, Exalted: 'And We have not sent you except as a mercy for the worlds' [al-Anbiya: 107].
His saying: (Muhammad) is a substitute for 'Prophet' or a clarifying conjunction. May God honor and venerate him. The author only omitted describing him with 'sayyid' due to poetic necessity; otherwise, it is recommended to describe him with 'sayyid' as an expression of respect, as al-Jalal al-Mahalli said concerning prayer and other matters. As for the hadith: 'Do not call me sayyid in your prayer,' al-Suyuti said: it has no basis.
And Abu Dawud narrated (1481) from the hadith of Fadala ibn 'Ubayd from the Prophet: 'When one of you prays, let him begin with the praise of his Lord and exaltation of Him, then send prayers upon the Prophet, then supplicate as he wishes.' Abu Sulayman al-Darani said: Whoever wants to ask God for a need, let him begin with prayer upon the Prophet, then ask his need, then conclude with prayer upon the Prophet, for God accepts the two prayers and is too generous to abandon what is between them.
Footnote (1): His saying: ('And it is not hidden...' etc.) There is no need for this, because 'prayer' in the author's speech means mercy, and 'peace' in his speech means greeting, and both are described as perpetual. The meaning of the author's speech is: then the perpetual mercy and greeting upon a Prophet. End of al-Amir. End of al-Ajhuri.

Commentary
This passage provides detailed grammatical and theological analysis of the poem's description. First, the word 'al-da'im' (the perpetual) is explained as applying to both prayer and peace; it is possible that it is an adjective for 'peace' alone with the adjective for 'prayer' implied. The perpetuity is not in the utterance itself (since words pass away) but in their virtue and reward. Then the phrase 'upon a Prophet' is straightforward. The description 'whose habit is mercies' is a nominal sentence acting as an adjective: the Prophet's constant habit is mercy for all worlds, hinting at the Quranic verse 'mercy for the worlds'. The name 'Muhammad' is grammatically a substitute or clarifying conjunction. The commentator notes that the poet omitted the title 'sayyid' (master) due to poetic meter, but it is recommended to use it out of respect; the hadith that forbids saying 'sayyid' in prayer is unfounded according to al-Suyuti. Finally, a hadith from Abu Dawud is quoted to show the virtue of beginning and ending supplication with prayer upon the Prophet, and Abu Sulayman al-Darani emphasizes that God accepts such prayers. The footnote simplifies: prayer means mercy, peace means greeting, both perpetual on the Prophet.
