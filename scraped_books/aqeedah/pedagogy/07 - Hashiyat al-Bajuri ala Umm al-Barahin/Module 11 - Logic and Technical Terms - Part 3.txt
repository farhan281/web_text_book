The Cause for Remembrance
Commentator identifies a causal relationship between seeing wonders and the command to remember.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why seeing divine wonders is given as the cause for the command to frequently remember them.

Core Idea
Observation of divine acts motivates increased remembrance.

Sanusi commands the intelligent person to increase in mentioning divine secrets and wonders. The commentator identifies the causal link: 'for he sees for them...' explains why one should remember them often. This lesson explores how one thing leads to another in theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the gloss
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice how the commentator explicitly labels a phrase as a cause (ʿillah).
2
Identify the cause and effect
What is the cause (ʿillah) and what is the effect (maʿlūl)? The effect is the command to increase remembrance.
3
Reflect on the implication
Why does seeing wonders compel the intelligent person to remember them frequently? How does this connect to tafakkur (contemplation)?
This shows the interplay between knowledge and action.

لا امتزاج مماشةٍ كامتزاج چشم بآخر، فاندفع ما يقال: إن الامتزاج من خواص الأجسام كامتزاج الماء بالعسل.
(قوله: فَإِنَّهُ يَرَى لَهَا ... إلخ) عِلَّةٌ لقوله: (فعلى العاقل أن يكثر من ذكرها ... إلخ).
(قوله: مَا لَا يَدْخُلُ تَحْتَ حَصْرِ أَي تحت عَدَدٍ محصورٍ، وهذا كناية عن المبالغة في الكثرة.
There is no mixing of juxtaposition like the mixing of one eye with another, so what is said is repelled: that mixing is a property of bodies like the mixing of water with honey. (His statement: 'for he sees for them... etc.') is a cause for his statement: 'so it is upon the intelligent person to increase in mentioning them... etc.'. (His statement: 'what does not enter under enumeration') i.e. under a limited number, and this is a metaphor for exaggeration in abundance.
Source pages p. 225

Commentary
This passage contains three separate explanatory comments on Sanusi's text. The first comment refutes the claim that mixing (imtizāj) is a property of bodies. The author distinguishes between a true mixing of substances (like water and honey) and a mere juxtaposition (mumāshshah) without interpenetration, using the mixing of one eye with another as an example—a phenomenon that does not involve bodily mixture. Thus, the argument that mixing is inherently bodily is rejected. The second comment explains that the phrase 'for he sees for them' is a causal connection (ʿillah) to the earlier imperative that the intelligent person should frequently remember those secrets and wonders. The third comment clarifies the phrase 'what does not enter under enumeration' (ḥaṣr), meaning it is beyond counting, and that this is a metaphorical expression (kināyah) for abundant blessings.

The Metaphor of Uncountability
Commentator explains uncountability as a metaphorical expression for exaggeration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the phrase 'what does not enter under enumeration' is a metaphorical expression (kināyah) for exaggeration in abundance, not a literal statement about count.

Core Idea
Uncountability is a metaphor for vastness, not a mathematical claim.

Sanusi describes divine blessings as 'what does not enter under enumeration'. The commentator clarifies that 'enumeration' here means a limited number, and this phrasing is a metaphorical device (kināyah) to emphasize excessive abundance. This lesson unpacks how Arabic rhetoric enriches theological expression.

Guided Path
1
Read the gloss
Read the Arabic and translation. Note the definition of 'ḥaṣr' as a limited number and the mention of 'kināyah'.
2
Understand kināyah
Kināyah is a rhetorical device where something is alluded to indirectly. Here, 'not limited by number' alludes to vast abundance.
This avoids implying that divine blessings can be counted.
3
Apply the concept
Think of other examples of kināyah in religious texts where exaggeration conveys meaning.
For instance, 'rivers of milk' in Paradise.

Defining Tawfiq: Divine Success as Creation of Obedience
Definition of tawfiq and refutation of al-Ash'ari's definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise definition of tawfiq (divine success) and why al-Ash'ari's definition is rejected.

Core Idea
True divine success is not mere capability but the actual creation of obedience.

The commentator begins by clarifying that success comes only from Allah, then defines tawfiq linguistically and technically. He argues that the best definition is that of Imam al-Haramayn—creating obedience—rather than al-Ash'ari’s definition of creating ability. This passage sharpens our understanding of divine grace and agency.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Start by reading the Arabic and translation to get the overall flow.
Notice the emphasis on restriction ('with Allah').
2
Identify the two definitions
Locate Imam al-Haramayn's definition of tawfiq as 'creation of obedience' and al-Ash'ari's as 'creation of ability to obey'.
3
Understand the objection
See why al-Ash'ari's definition is problematic: it would apply to disbelievers, who have ability but are not successful.
4
Grasp the rebuttal
The term 'ability' here refers to the concomitant accident (al-ʿaraḍ al-muqārin) that accompanies obedience, not mere capacity.
This resolves the objection because disbelievers lack that concomitant accident.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
No additional qualifier is needed; the definition is complete without adding 'facilitation of the path of good'.
The commentator invites reflection: 'So reflect.'

(قوله: وَبِاللهِ التَّوْفِيقُ أي لا بغيره فتقديمُ الجار والمجرور لإفادة الحصر. والتوفيق لغةً: التأليف بين شيئين فأكثر. وشرعاً : خَلْقُ الطاعة في العبد. كذا عَرَّفَه إمامُ الحَرَمَيْنِ، وهو أولى من تعريف الأشعري له: بأنه خَلْقُ قُدْرَةِ الطاعة في العبد؛ لأن خَلْقَ القدرة على الطاعة موجود في الكافر مع أنه غيرُ مُوَفِّقٍ. ودُفعَ ذلك: بأنه ليس المراد بالقدرة: سلامة الآلات حتى يَردَ ما ذُكِرَ، بل المراد بها: العَرَضُ المقارِنُ للطاعة، وذلك غير موجود في الكافر لعدم وجود الطاعة منه، وبهذا كُلَّه تَعْلَمُ أنه لا حاجة لزيادة بعضهم وتسهيل سبيل الخير إليه لإخراج الكافر. فتأمل.
(قوله: الْعَرَضُ الْمُقَارِنُ) وهو القوة المصاحبة للطاعة، ومعلوم أن القوة لا تكون إلا بسلامة الآلات
(His statement: 'And with Allah is success (tawfīq)') i.e., not with other than Him, so the fronting of the preposition and its object is for the purpose of restriction. Tawfīq linguistically is the bringing together of two or more things. In sacred law: it is the creation of obedience in the servant. Thus Imam al-Ḥaramayn defined it, and it is more appropriate than al-Ashʿarī’s definition: that it is the creation of the ability (qudra) of obedience in the servant; because the creation of ability to obey exists in the disbeliever despite that he is not successful (muwaffaq). And that was rebutted: that what is meant by 'ability' is not the soundness of faculties such that what was mentioned would be problematic; rather what is meant by it is the accident (ʿaraḍ) concomitant (muqārin) with obedience, and that does not exist in the disbeliever due to the non-existence of obedience from him. And by all this you know that there is no need for some people to add 'and facilitation of the path of good to him' to exclude the disbeliever. So reflect. (His statement: 'the concomitant accident (al-ʿaraḍ al-muqārin)') and it is the power accompanying obedience, and it is known that power is only with soundness of faculties.
Source pages p. 225

Commentary
This passage defines the term tawfīq (divine success) and defends a specific understanding of it. The commentator first notes that the phrase 'with Allah is success' implies restriction: success comes only from Allah. Then he gives the linguistic meaning of tawfīq as 'bringing together', and the technical (sharʿī) meaning as 'creating obedience in the servant', as defined by Imam al-Ḥaramayn. He argues this definition is superior to that of al-Ashʿarī, who defined tawfīq as 'creating the ability (qudra) to obey'. The objection is that creating the ability to obey exists even in a disbeliever (who has the capacity but does not obey), so that definition would make the disbeliever successful, which is incorrect. The response is that 'ability' here does not mean mere capacity or sound faculties; rather, it refers to the accident (ʿaraḍ) that accompanies obedience—a power specifically coinciding with the act of obedience. Since the disbeliever never performs obedience, that concomitant accident does not exist in him. Therefore, al-Ashʿarī’s definition is flawed, and there is no need to add extra qualifiers like 'facilitation of the path of good' to exclude the disbeliever. The marginal note by al-Ajhuri clarifies that this 'concomitant accident' is the power accompanying obedience, which requires sound faculties.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think that having the ability to obey is sufficient for divine success (tawfiq).
Correction: True tawfiq is the creation of obedience itself, not just the capacity. The ability that counts is the concomitant accident that accompanies the act of obedience, which disbelievers lack.
