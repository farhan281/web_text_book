The Syllogism Against Created Influence
Al-Bajuri explains the syllogism against created influence with its two premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical syllogism that refutes the idea that creatures can influence by a power God placed in them, and how it confirms God's independence from all mediums.

Core Idea
No creature can influence even with God-given power—God needs no medium.

Al-Bajuri unpacks a tight syllogism embedded in Sanusi's text. It may look simple, but it cuts to the heart of Islamic causality: if creatures could influence by a God-given power, God would become dependent on a medium. Since dependence is impossible for God, such influence is impossible. This lesson walks through the logic step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two premises
Read the passage and locate the major premise (kubra) and minor premise (sughra). Notice how Al-Bajuri extracts them from the text.
The major is a conditional; the minor denies its consequent.
2
Understand the consequence
The major says: if a creature influences by God-given power, then God becomes dependent on a medium. Ask: why would that be a problem?
3
See the minor premise's proof
Al-Bajuri indicates that the minor premise (God's dependence is false) is proven by the earlier discussion of God's self-sufficiency. Look at how that connects.
4
Check your understanding
Do you see why this syllogism refutes the idea of independent secondary causation? Consider what would happen if the conclusion were false.

Commentary
This passage provides a logical proof (qiyas) and a doctrinal derivation. First, Al-Bajuri explains the syllogism implicit in Sanusi's text: The major premise (kubra) is that if a creature influences by a God-given power, then God would become dependent on a medium for some actions. The minor premise (sughra) is that God being dependent on a medium is false (bāṭil). The conclusion is that such influence is impossible. Al-Bajuri notes that Sanusi's statement 'because you have known...' provides the justification for the minor premise. Then, Al-Bajuri turns to the phrase 'the inclusion (tadammun) of the statement: there is no god except Allah'. He clarifies that it is the meaning of the shahada, not the wording, that entails the three divisions (aqsām thalātha). This inclusion (tadammun) is not the linguistic signification of part of the meaning, but rather a logical entailment. Specifically, from God's self-sufficiency (istighnā') eleven necessary attributes are derived: existence, pre-eternity, perpetuity, otherness from originated things, self-subsistence, hearing, sight, speech, and their consequences. From the dependence of all else (iftiqār) on God, the remaining attributes (like life, will, power) are derived. When these attributes are necessary, their opposites become impossible. Additionally, from the permissible (jā'iz) aspect of the shahada, it follows that God is not obligated to do anything possible. Al-Bajuri concludes with 'So reflect' (fa-tadabbar), inviting the reader to contemplate the comprehensiveness of tawhid.

Three Divisions of Tawhid from the Shahada
Al-Bajuri explains the three divisions subsumed under the shahada and their corresponding attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the meaning of 'There is no god except Allah' entails three divisions—necessary, impossible, and permissible—and how specific divine attributes are derived from each.

Core Idea
The shahada logically yields three categories: what God must be, cannot be, and may do.

Al-Bajuri explains that the shahada's meaning, not its wording, includes the three divisions of tawhid. From God's self-sufficiency (istighna') come eleven necessary attributes; from the dependence of all else (iftiqar) come the rest; and from the permissible aspect comes that God is not obligated to do anything. This lesson unpacks that elegant derivation.

Guided Path
1
Understand 'inclusion' (tadammun)
Al-Bajuri clarifies that it is the meaning of the shahada, not the word itself, that entails the three divisions. This is a logical implication, not a linguistic part-whole relation.
Do not confuse with dalalat al-tadammun in language.
2
Identify the three divisions
The three divisions are: 1) what is necessary (wajib) for God, 2) what is impossible (mustahil) for God, and 3) what is permissible (ja'iz) regarding God. Locate how each is derived.
3
Map attributes to each division
Note that eleven attributes (existence, pre-eternity, etc.) come under God's self-sufficiency; the remaining attributes (life, will, power) come under the dependence of creation; and the permissible aspect yields that God is not obligated to create anything.
The opposites of necessary attributes become impossible.
4
Reflect on the comprehensiveness
Al-Bajuri ends with 'fa-tadabbar' (so reflect). Think about how the entire creed of attributes is encapsulated in the simple statement of tawhid.

Learning Objective
