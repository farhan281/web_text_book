The First Six Necessary Divine Attributes
Sanusi lists the first six necessary divine attributes with brief definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first six of the twenty attributes that reason necessarily affirms for God, as enumerated by Sanusi.

Core Idea
The first six attributes establish God's existence, eternality, and absolute uniqueness.

Sanusi begins his enumeration of the twenty attributes that reason alone shows must belong to God. In this passage, he lists the first six, which establish God's existence, eternity, self-subsistence, and absolute uniqueness. Understanding these is the starting point for all later theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice the concise listing style and the brief definitions embedded within the sentence.
The passage ends with a question mark in the OCR; it likely continues on the next page.
2
Identify each attribute
List the six attributes mentioned: existence (al-wujud), eternity without beginning (al-qidam), perpetuity (al-baqa'), being unlike originated things (mukhalafatu al-hawadith), self-subsistence (qiyamuhu bi-nafsihi), and oneness (al-wahdaniyyah).
3
Understand the definitions
Sanusi gives short clarifications for self-subsistence (no need for location or specifier) and oneness (no second in essence, attributes, or actions). Reflect on what these imply about God.
These definitions are crucial for avoiding misconceptions.
4
Reflect on rational necessity
These attributes are not derived from scripture alone but from rational necessity. Ask yourself: why must God have these attributes?
This is a key principle in kalam – reason independently affirms these.
5
Connect to the larger list
Keep in mind that this is only the beginning; the full set of twenty attributes continues on the following pages. These first six form the foundation.

Commentary
This passage begins the enumeration of the twenty attributes that are rationally necessary for God. Sanusi states that among the things wajib (necessary) for God are twenty attributes, and he lists the first few: 1) Existence (al-wujud) – that God exists. 2) Eternity without beginning (al-qidam) – God has no beginning. 3) Perpetuity (al-baqa’) – He has no end. 4) Being unlike originated things (mukhalafatuhu lil-hawadith) – God is not similar to any created being. 5) Self-subsistence (qiyamuhu bi-nafsihi) – He does not need a location or a specifier (i.e., He does not depend on anything else for His existence). 6) Oneness (al-wahdaniyyah) – He is one in essence, attributes, and actions; there is no second to Him in any respect. The Twenty Attributes and the Nafsiyya Attribute
Sanusi enumerates the twenty attributes and classifies the first six, highlighting existence as nafsiyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the twenty necessary attributes of Allah and the classification of the first six, with a focus on existence as the nafsiyya (essential) attribute.

Core Idea
Existence is the only essential attribute; the other five are relational.

Sanusi begins by listing the twenty attributes incumbent upon Allah. He then singles out the first attribute—existence—as nafsiyya, meaning it is not a positive attribute added to the essence but rather the essence itself. This lesson orients you to the full list and clarifies the unique nature of the essential attribute.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice the flow from the general list to the specific classification.
The passage lists all twenty attributes in the opening sentence.
2
Identify the six attributes
Sanusi says 'فهذِهِ سِتُّ صِفَاتٍ' (so these are six attributes). Identify which six he means: existence, pre-eternity, everlastingness, unlike created things, self-subsistence, and oneness.
Existence is the first and is nafsiyya; the other five are often called salbiyya (negative).
3
Understand nafsiyya
Existence is called nafsiyya because it is the essence itself, not an added attribute. Contrast with the five that follow.
The commentary explains that nafsiyya means not a positive existential attribute.
4
Reflect on the remaining attributes
The passage implies that the twenty include more than these six; the next lessons will cover the other fourteen.

Among what is obligatory for our Lord, majestic and exalted, are twenty attributes: existence, pre-eternity, everlastingness, His unlike created things, His self-subsistence, meaning He does not need a locus nor a specifyer, and oneness, meaning there is no second to Him in His essence nor in His attributes nor in His actions. Commentary
This passage classifies and enumerates the twenty necessary attributes (sifat wajiba) of Allah. Sanusi first lists all twenty: existence, pre-eternity, everlastingness, being unlike created things, self-subsistence, oneness, then the seven attributes of meanings (power, will, knowledge, life, hearing, sight, speech), and finally the seven meaning-related attributes (being powerful, willing, knowing, living, hearing, seeing, speaking). He then explains the grouping: the first attribute, existence, is called nafsiyya (essential) because it is not a positive existential attribute added to the essence; the next five are not classified here but are often called salbiyya (negative/relational). The seven attributes of meanings (sifat al-ma'ani) are real existential attributes that inhere in the essence, each with its own object of relation (ta'allaq). The seven meaning-related attributes (sifat ma'nawiyya) are the ascriptions that necessarily follow from possessing the seven attributes of meanings; they are not additional existents but the state of being described by those attributes.

The Seven Attributes of Meanings
Sanusi enumerates the seven attributes of meanings with their specific objects of relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the seven attributes of meanings (sifat al-ma'ani) and how each relates to its object (ta'allaq).

Core Idea
The seven attributes of meanings are real existential attributes, each with a specific scope.

After listing the first six attributes, Sanusi proceeds to the seven attributes called 'sifat al-ma'ani' (attributes of meanings). These are real, positive attributes that inhere in Allah's essence. Each has a distinct object of relation (ta'allaq) that defines what it encompasses. This lesson walks through each one and its scope.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation. Note the term 'صفات المعاني' and the list that follows.
These attributes are distinct from the first six.
2
Enumerate the seven
Identify each: power, will, knowledge, life, hearing, sight, and speech.
Each has a unique ta'allaq.
3
Understand the ta'allaq
Power and will pertain to all possible things; knowledge to all categories; life to nothing; hearing and sight to all existents; speech to the same as knowledge, but not a letter or sound.
The ta'allaq shows the scope of each attribute.
4
Reflect on speech
Speech is defined negatively: not a letter or sound. This distinguishes it from human speech.

The Seven Meaning-Related Attributes
Sanusi enumerates the seven meaning-related attributes as necessary concomitants of the attributes of meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the seven meaning-related attributes (sifat ma'nawiyya) and how they are necessary concomitants of the attributes of meanings.

Core Idea
Meaning-related attributes are not additional existences but necessary ascriptions.

Sanusi completes his classification by introducing the seven meaning-related attributes (sifat ma'nawiyya). These are not additional existential attributes; rather, they are the necessary descriptions that follow from possessing the seven attributes of meanings. This lesson clarifies their nature and relationship to the previous group.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation. Note the term 'صفات معنوية' and the list.
These are the last seven of the twenty.
2
Enumerate the seven
They are: powerful, willing, knowing, living, hearing, seeing, speaking. Each corresponds to one of the seven attributes of meanings.
These are not additional attributes but the state of being described by the first seven.
3
Understand 'inseparable'
Sanusi says they are 'ملازمة للسبع الأولى' (inseparable from the first seven). This means they necessarily exist because the attributes of meanings exist.
They are not separate existents; they are concomitants.
4
Distinguish from ma'ani
The attributes of meanings are existential attributes in the essence; the meaning-related attributes are the ascriptions that result from them.
For example, if Allah has power (attribute of meaning), He is powerful (meaning-related).

Third, it negates inability (ʿajz) regarding any possible thing, and negates that He could bring about something while disliking it—i.e., without willing it—or while in a state of drowsiness, heedlessness, unawareness, or by nature—all of which are deficiencies. Fourth, it negates ignorance (jahl) of any known thing. Fifth, it negates literal death, deafness, blindness, and muteness. Then it notes that the opposites of the intrinsic attributes (ṣifāt maʿnawiyya) are obvious from these negations. Finally, it states the single possible attribute: God may do or leave any contingent act—there is no necessity or impossibility for Him regarding created actions.

Sanusi now turns to proving that God is self-subsistent (qiyām bi-nafsihi), meaning He does not depend on a substrate or a cause for His existence. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's rational proof that God's actions concerning possible things are permissible (ja'iz), neither necessary nor impossible.

Core Idea
Possible things remain possible only by God's free choice, not by compulsion.

Sanusi now turns to what God must do—or not do—regarding created things. He argues that reason alone cannot force God to act in any particular way toward the world of possibles. The following passage presents a crisp logical demonstration that God's actions are purely permissible, preserving both His freedom and the nature of possibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then its English translation. Notice the logical structure: 'if... then...' and the conclusion 'not intelligible.'
The passage is short but dense; take your time.
2
Identify the three rational categories
The passage mentions necessary (wajib), impossible (mustahil), and permissible (ja'iz). Understand how each relates to God's actions.
3
Trace the argument's logic
Sanusi argues: If any possible action were necessary or impossible for God, that possible would change its nature. But that is not rationally conceivable. Therefore, God's actions are merely permissible.
This is a proof by contradiction.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider why this matters: it defends God's absolute freedom and the coherence of the category 'possible' in kalam.

Commentary
This passage proves that God's actions regarding possible things (mumkinat) are neither necessary (wajib) nor impossible (mustahil) but permissible (ja'iz). The argument: If any possible action were rationally necessary for God, or rationally impossible, then that possible thing would change its nature to necessary or impossible. But possible things are defined as being able to exist or not exist. Changing their nature is not rationally conceivable. Therefore, God's actions toward possibles are merely permissible, meaning He may do them or leave them without constraint.

Attributes Derived from Allah's Self-Sufficiency
Sanusi enumerates attributes derived from istighna and derives further negations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the principle of Allah's absolute self-sufficiency (istighna) logically entails His essential attributes, negates any purpose in His actions, and precludes any obligation upon Him.

Core Idea
Allah's self-sufficiency is the key that unlocks His attributes and transcends all need.

In this passage, Sanusi applies the first pillar of uluhiyya—Allah's absolute independence from all else—to derive a set of essential attributes. He also draws important conclusions: that Allah has no purposes behind His actions, and that nothing can be morally obligatory upon Him. Understanding this logical derivation is central to the Islamic conception of God's perfection.

1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic text slowly, then refer to the literal translation. Notice how Sanusi begins with 'As for His self-sufficiency...' and then lists attributes.
The Arabic uses 'yūjibu' (necessitates) which is a key logical term.
2
Identify the six attributes derived from istighna
List the six attributes: existence, pre-eternity, everlastingness, dissimilarity to created things, self-subsistence, and freedom from deficiencies. Understand why each is necessary given self-sufficiency.
These are the six essential attributes common in Ash'ari theology.

Examine the inclusion of hearing, sight, and speech
Sanusi says these are included in the above. See the reasoning: if they were not necessary, Allah would need an originator or locus, contradicting self-sufficiency.

Understand the negation of purposes

5
Grasp the conclusion: no obligation upon Allah
Finally, Sanusi argues that no act of contingent things is obligatory upon Allah, because that would imply need for perfection. This is a key Ash'ari principle.
This guards against the Mu'tazilite idea that God must do the optimal.

Commentary
This passage derives the essential divine attributes from the first pillar of uluhiyya, istighna’ (self-sufficiency). Sanusi explains that Allah's absolute independence logically necessitates six attributes: existence (wujūd), pre-eternity (qidam), everlastingness (baqā’), dissimilarity to created things (mukhālafah li-l-ḥawādith), self-subsistence (qiyām bi-l-nafs), and transcendence above imperfections (tanzīh ‘an al-naqā’iṣ). Then he adds that included in these is the necessity of the attributes of hearing, sight, and speech, because if these were not necessary, Allah would be dependent on an external cause or a locus, contradicting His self-sufficiency. He further derives that Allah is free from any purpose or aim (gharaḍ) in His actions, since having a purpose would imply need. Finally, he concludes that no act is morally obligatory upon Allah, because obligation would imply that He needs something to perfect Himself, which contradicts His absolute wealth. This passage establishes key points in Sunni theology: that Allah has essential attributes and that He is above any compulsion or need.

Deriving Four Attributes from Dependence
Sanusi derives the divine attributes of life, power, will, and knowledge from the fact that all else depends on Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi logically derives four essential divine attributes (life, power, will, knowledge) from the fact that all creation depends on Allah.

Core Idea
Dependence of creation demands that Allah be living, powerful, willing, and knowing.

Sanusi now turns to the second fundamental observation about Allah's relationship to creation: absolute dependence. From this single fact, he extracts four necessary attributes. The argument is tight: if even one of these attributes were missing, nothing contingent could exist, which would contradict the universal dependence we observe. Let's examine the logical chain.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Begin by reading the Arabic text carefully, then its literal translation. Notice the cause-and-effect structure: 'wa ammā ... fa-huwa yūjibu ...'. This is a concise logical proof.
The phrase 'kayfa wa huwa alladhī yaftaqiru ilayhi kullu mā siwāhu' is a rhetorical question reinforcing the conclusion.
2
Identify the four attributes and the logical connection
The passage directly states that dependence (iftiqār) necessitates four attributes: life (al-ḥayāh), universal power (ʿumūm al-qudrah), will (al-irādah), and knowledge (al-ʿilm). Try to see each as a necessary condition for creating and sustaining contingent beings.
3
Understand the hypothetical proof (condition contrary to fact)
Sanusi provides a conditional proof: if any of these attributes were absent, it would be impossible for any contingent thing (ḥawādith) to exist. Since contingent things do exist, the attributes must be present. This is a foundational argument in kalām for divine attributes.
This argument assumes that creation's dependence is evident. Sanusi is building on the earlier observation that everything other than Allah is contingent.

This is not a mere linguistic point: it determines how we derive knowledge of Allah's attributes from His names. The passage explains that 'name' includes both names that indicate the Essence alone, like 'Allah', and those that indicate an attribute, like 'al-Knowing'.

The annotations of the scholar Muhammad al-Anbabi (His saying: 'that it is other than the named') — the summary is that most Ash'aris said: the name is identical to the named, evidenced by His saying Most High: 'Glorify the name of your Lord' (Surah al-A'la, 1), and 'You worship not besides Him except names' (Surah Yusuf, 40), and the saying of Labid al-'Amiri addressing his daughters regarding lamentation over him: 'So stand and say by that which you know / And do not scratch a face nor tear hair / Until the year, then the name of peace be upon you both / And whoever weeps a full year has excused himself.' Al-Sa'd said in Sharh al-Maqasid: In inference by the two verses there is an admission of difference (al-mughāyara) since it is said: glorification and worship are for the essences, not the names — noting that glorification is valid for the name itself, meaning its sanctification from what contradicts veneration, as in al-Baydawi, and worship is attached to it outwardly for the purpose of indicating that these deities are non-existent in the presence of divinity, so they are as if names without named entities, and the word 'name' in the line of poetry is superfluous (muqḥam) indicating that it is not a real greeting, since they are not safe after it. And it may be said: There is no admission of difference in the inference by the two verses, because their saying: glorification and worship are for the essences, not the names, is based on the claim of the speaker that there are named entities and names.

Commentary
This passage is a theological debate over whether the term 'name' (ism) is identical to the named (musamma) or distinct. It begins with al-Ajhuri's gloss confirming that the verification is that 'name' refers to the names of God, which are other than the essence. Then al-Anbabi notes that most Ash'aris hold that 'name' is identical to the named, citing scriptural proofs: two Quranic verses and a line from the pre-Islamic poet Labid. Sa'd al-Din al-Taftazani (al-Sa'd) in his Sharh al-Maqasid critiques these proofs, arguing that they actually imply difference. For the first verse, glorification of the name of the Lord could be for the name itself, but it could also be interpreted as glorification of the named. For the second verse, worship of 'names' without named entities indicates they are mere names without realities. Labid's line uses 'name' superfluously to show the greeting is not real. The passage ends by suggesting that these criticisms themselves rely on the assumption that there are distinct named entities and names, which is the point under debate.

The Scope of Negating States
Al-Ajhuri on the scope of negated states.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the negation of states in Islamic theology specifically targets 'effect-determined' states, while existence and similar non-effect-determined conditions are affirmed but classified as 'amr i'tibari' (considerational matters).

Core Idea
Negating states does not deny all third entities; only effect-determined states are rejected.

In theological debates about divine attributes, the concept of 'states' (ahwal) is controversial. Al-Ajhuri clarifies that those who negate states only reject a particular kind—those that are effect-determined. Existence itself, whether eternal or temporal, is not considered a state but an 'amr i'tibari', as also affirmed by Sa'd al-Taftazani. This passage sets the precise scope of the negation.

Counting Attributes After Negating States

The Answer: Dispensation and the Exception of Existence
