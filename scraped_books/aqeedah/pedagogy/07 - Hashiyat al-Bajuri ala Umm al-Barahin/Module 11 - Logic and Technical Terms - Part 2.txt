Four Categories of Existents: Place and Specifier
Commentator classifies existents into four categories regarding need for place and specifier.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Muslim theologians classify existents into four categories based on their need for a place (mahall) and a specifier (mukhasis), and how this classification highlights the uniqueness of Allah and His attributes.

Core Idea
Existents are either self-subsistent, contingent, or attributes — only Allah is self-sufficient.

In Islamic theology, understanding the nature of existence is foundational. This passage from Sanusi's Umm al-Barahin provides a crisp classification of all existents into four kinds based on two criteria: whether they require a locus (mahall) and whether they require a specifier (mukhasis). By working through this classification, you will see how theologians distinguish the Creator from creation and how divine attributes fit into the picture.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two criteria
Learn what 'mahall' (place/locus) and 'mukhasis' (specifier) mean. These are the two factors used to categorize existents.
Mahall refers to the substrate in which something inheres, like body for accidents. Mukhasis is the agent that specifies a thing's existence.
2
Read the passage and translate
Read the Arabic passage slowly. Then read the English translation. Notice the four categories enumerated by the author.
The passage uses 'qism' for each category.
3
Examine each category
Go through each of the four categories: 1) no need for either (God's essence), 2) need both (accidents), 3) need specifier but not place (essences of contingents), 4) need place but not specifier (God's attributes).
Category 4 might be surprising: God's attributes subsist in His essence but do not need a specifier.
4
Understand the theological point
Reflect on why this classification matters. It establishes God's self-sufficiency and the eternal nature of His attributes, distinguishing them from created attributes.
This guards against the misconception that God's attributes are created or separate entities.

Commentary
This passage provides a fourfold classification of existents based on their relationship to two concepts: 'mahall' (place/locus) and 'mukhasis' (specifier). The author states that existents fall into four categories. First, that which does not depend on either: this is the essence of God, who is self-subsistent and uncaused. Second, that which depends on both: the accidents of contingent beings, which inhere in a substance and require a cause for their specification. Third, that which depends on a specifier but not on a place: the essences of contingent beings (substances), which are not in a subject but need a cause to bring them into existence. Fourth, that which subsists in a place (i.e., inheres in an essence) but does not need a specifier: the attributes of God, which inhere in His essence but are eternal and not caused by an external specifier. This classification underscores the unique ontological status of God and His attributes.

Comment on the Insufficiency of Sanusi's Wording
Al-Bajuri critiques Sanusi's definition and expands it to cover both discrete and continuous quantity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sanusi's definition of wahdaniyya is considered incomplete and how Al-Bajuri extends it to cover both discrete and continuous quantity.

Core Idea
Sanusi's wording only negates discrete quantity; Al-Bajuri shows it can include continuous as well.

Al-Bajuri, in his commentary, points out a subtle limitation in Sanusi's definition of wahdaniyya (divine uniqueness). The definition only directly negates the existence of a separate second entity, but does it also negate the possibility of combination or union? This passage shows how the wording can be reinterpreted to include both types of quantity, thereby fully defending tawhid.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Problem
Read the passage and note al-Bajuri's critique that Sanusi's wording primarily negates discrete quantity (kamm munfasil) but appears to miss continuous quantity (kamm muttasil).
Discrete quantity refers to separate individuals, continuous quantity refers to connected parts.
2
See the Fix
Observe how al-Bajuri reinterprets 'no second' to mean 'no second either by conjunction or by separation,' thus including both types of quantity.
This reinterpretation makes the definition comprehensive.
3
Grasp the Summary
Understand that the six quantities (discrete and continuous for essence, attributes, and actions) are all negated by wahdaniyya, and note the special case of the sixth quantity.
The sixth quantity (continuous in actions) is negated only if pictured as participation.

Commentary
This passage is a critical commentary by al-Bajuri on the phrasing of Sanusi in the matn. Sanusi defined wahdaniyya as 'no second to Him in His essence, attributes, and actions.' It does not obviously negate 'continuous quantity'—the idea of union or combination. However, al-Bajuri then shows that the wording can be extended to include the negation of continuous quantity by reinterpreting 'no second' to mean no second either in separation (discrete) or in conjunction (continuous). He then summarizes that there are six kinds of quantities (presumably three categories—essence, attributes, actions—each with both discrete and continuous), and all are negated by wahdaniyya. The sixth quantity (which is the continuous in actions) is only negated if it is pictured as participation (i.e., another sharing in the same action), but not if it is pictured as a multiplicity of distinct acts.

Core Idea
Negating the second is the key to oneness; all other numbers follow.

Sanusi's definition of Allah's oneness using 'no second to Him' may seem to define 'the One' rather than 'oneness.' This lesson examines the objection and the subtle response, showing why this wording is both precise and comprehensive.

Distinguishing Idafat al-Bayan and Idafa Bayaniyya in Sifat al-Ma'ani
Sanusi distinguishes idafa lil-bayan from idafa bayaniyya in defining sifat al-ma'ani.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between the two types of idafa used in defining sifat al-ma'ani and why this distinction matters for theological terminology.

Core Idea
The precise meaning of 'sifat al-ma'ani' hinges on the correct type of idafa.

In defining the technical term 'sifat al-ma'ani', Sanusi clarifies which type of annexation (idafa) is used. He distinguishes two types: idafa lil-bayan and idafa bayaniyya, each with its own logical relationship between the annexed terms. Understanding this difference is essential for grasping the precise meaning of the compound term.

The first has 'بإطلاق' (unrestricted), the second has 'من وجه' (from one aspect).

The correct view is that they are different; the synonymity view is rejected.

Extreme Difference as Mutual Exclusion
Al-Ajhuri glosses 'غاية الخلاف' in contrariety definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that 'غاية الخلاف' means mutual exclusion (التنافي) and how this qualification distinguishes true contraries from mere differences.

Core Idea
Extreme difference means mutual exclusion, not any difference.

Al-Sanusi defines contrariety using the phrase 'غاية الخلاف'. Al-Ajhuri clarifies that this refers to mutual exclusion, not extreme difference in degree. For example, standing and laughing can coexist, so they are not true opposites.

Al-Bajuri unpacks Sanusi's argument for the permissibility of human accidents in prophets using a formal syllogism.
