The Rule for 'Yūjib' vs 'Yu'khadh'
Al-Bajuri explains the rule distinguishing 'yūjib' and 'yu'khadh'.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
'Yūjib' signals a necessary belief; 'yu'khadh' signals a permissible belief.

Al-Bajuri highlights a subtle but important pattern in Sanusi's wording: sometimes he says 'this necessitates (yūjib)' and other times 'it is taken (yu'khadh)'. He explains this is intentional—the choice of verb corresponds to whether the belief is rational obligatory (wājib) or merely permissible (jā'iz). This insight reveals how kalam authors use language as a teaching tool.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The closing phrases are mere formalities without deeper meaning.
Correction: The author shows that they carry profound significance: 'salam' is magnified and Quranic, and 'al-hamdu lillah' signals acceptance and connects to Paradise.

Checkpoint
According to the author, why is concluding with 'al-hamdu lillah' a sign of acceptance?
A) Because it is a short phrase.
B) Because it is the last supplication of the believers in Paradise.
C) Because it is a command from God.
D) Because the Prophet always said it.

Key Takeaway
Concluding a book with 'peace' and 'praise' reflects Quranic influence and signals the acceptance of the author's work.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
You just learned that the author ended his book with 'al-hamdu lillah' as a sign that his supplication was accepted. When you finish your daily prayers or recitation, do you end with genuine praise that reflects hope for acceptance? What would change if you truly believed that your closing words determined the fate of your deeds?
?
The author borrowed from the Qur'an to magnify his closing. Do your own words, especially when you conclude something important, reflect Quranic language? If not, what is one phrase from the Qur'an you could insert into your daily conversations to remind yourself of Allah's presence?
?
The word 'salam' is used here with great weight. In your life, when you say 'assalamu alaikum', do you mean it as a weighty prayer for peace and safety, or is it a habit? How can you restore its original magnificence starting today?
