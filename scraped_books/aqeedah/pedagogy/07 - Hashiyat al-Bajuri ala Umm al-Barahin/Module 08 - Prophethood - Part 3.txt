Human Imperfections in Prophets Elevate Their Status
Bajuri explains why human imperfections increase prophetic status, focusing on the intention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how human imperfections in prophets, rather than detracting from their status, actually increase it when accompanied by noble intentions.

Core Idea
Prophets' human experiences elevate their rank because they transform into acts of obedience.

This passage from Bajuri's commentary addresses a potential concern: if prophets are perfect, how can they experience human imperfections like marriage, illness, or hardship? Bajuri explains that these very experiences become a means of increasing their station when they are undertaken with the right intentions. He identifies three such intentions: setting legislation, strengthening for worship, and practicing patience.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation, and notice the logical flow.
2
Identify the key claim
Bajuri clarifies that the lack of detraction does not automatically increase status; rather, it is the actual occurrence with intention that does.
3
Understand the three intentions
See how marriage, fasting, and illness can be motivated by legislation, strength, or patience.
The 'original' likely refers to the original text's mention of fasting.
4
Reflect on the broader lesson
This shows that kalam considers the intention behind actions, not just the action itself.

(قوله: بَلْ ذَاكَ مِمَّا يَزِيدُ... إلخ) أي: لأنه إما أن يقارِنَهُ قَصْدُ التشريع كما في النكاح، أو قصدُ التقوّي على العبادة كما في الأصل، أو طَاعَةُ الصَّبر كما في المرض ونحوه واختلف هل الثوابُ على المصائب أو على الصبر عليها؟ فذهب العز بن عبد السلام في طائفة إلى الثاني؛ لأن الثوابَ إنما يكون على صنع العبد، والمصائب لا صُنْعَ له فيها. وذهب الجمهورُ إلى الأول لقوله تعالى: ﴿ذَلِكَ بِأَنَّهُمْ لا يُصِيبُهُمْ ظَمَا وَلَا نَصَبٌ وَلَا مَخْمَصَةٌ إلى أن قال: إِلَّا كُتِبَ لَهُم بِهِ عَمَلٌ صَالِحٌ﴾ [التوبة:120]، ولخبر مُسْلِمٍ عن عَائِشَةَ مرفوعاً: «مَا مِنْ مُسْلِمٍ يُشَاكُ بِشَوْكَةٍ فَمَا فَوْقَهَا إِلَّا كُتِبَ لَهُ بِهَا دَرَجَةً، وَمُحِيَتْ عَنْهُ بِهَا خَطِيئَةٌ». واعلم أن الصبر على ثلاثة أقسام أحدُها الصبرُ على العبادات ومشاقها. وثانيها: الصبر على المصائب وحرارتها وثالثها الصبر على الشَّهوات ولذاتها. قال الضُّحَّاكُ: من مَرَّ في سوق فرأى ما يشتهيه ولا يَقْدِرُ عليه، فصَبَرَ واحتسب؛ كان خيراً من ألف دينار يُنْفِقُهَا كلها في سبيل الله. وقال أبو سليمان الرازي: تَنفُسُ فقير دون شهوة لا يَقْدِرُ عليها أفضل من عبادةِ غنيّ ألفَ عام. حاشية العلامة أحمد الأجهوري . (His saying: 'Since that does not detract... etc.') is a justification for the permissibility of human attributes. In some copies: (Because that does not detract). The meaning of 'does not detract' is that it does not impugn nor diminish. And since the lack of detraction does not necessitate an increase in the loftiness of their station, he turned from that by saying: (Rather, that is among the things that increase it). The demonstrative pronoun refers to permissibility, but what is intended by it is actual permissibility; because that which increases it is actual occurrence, not merely the permissibility of occurrence. (His saying: 'Rather, that is among the things that increase it... etc.') means: because it is either accompanied by the intention of legislation, as in marriage; or the intention of strengthening oneself for worship, as in the original; or the obedience of patience, as in illness and the like. And there is disagreement: Is the reward for the calamities or for the patience upon them? Al-'Izz ibn 'Abd al-Salam and a group held the second view, because reward is only upon the action of the servant, and calamities are not his action. The majority held the first view due to His statement, the Exalted: 'That is because they are not stricken with thirst, nor fatigue, nor hunger... until He said: except that it is written for them as a righteous deed' [Quran 9:120], and the report of Muslim from 'A'isha, raised: 'There is no Muslim who is pricked by a thorn or what is above it except that it is written for him thereby a degree, and there is erased from him thereby a sin.' And know that patience is of three types: one is patience in acts of worship and their hardships; the second is patience in calamities and their heat; the third is patience in desires and their pleasures. Al-Dahhak said: Whoever passes through a market and sees something he desires and is not able to obtain it, and he is patient and seeks reward, it is better than a thousand dinars which he spends entirely in the way of Allah. And Abu Sulayman al-Razi said: The breathing of a poor man without desire which he is not able to obtain is better than the worship of a rich man for a thousand years. Gloss of the scholar Ahmad al-Ajhuri: (His saying: 'or the obedience of patience') is coordinated with (taqwa). (His saying: 'patience in desires') means: in their absence.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on Sanusi's text regarding the permissibility of human attributes in prophets and their effects. Bajuri begins by explaining that the statement 'since that does not detract' is a justification for allowing human imperfections in prophets. He notes that some copies read 'because that does not detract,' meaning it does not impugn or diminish their status. However, the fact that it does not detract does not automatically mean it increases their status. That is why Sanusi then says 'rather, that is among the things that increase it.' Bajuri clarifies that the demonstrative 'that' refers to the permissibility of such occurrences, but more specifically to actual occurrence, because it is the actual happening, not just the possibility, that increases their rank. He then explains the possible intentions behind such occurrences: either the intention of establishing a legislative example (as in marriage), or strengthening oneself for worship (as in the original, perhaps referring to fasting), or the act of patience in obedience (as in illness). This leads to a discussion on whether reward is for the calamities themselves or for the patience shown. Al-'Izz ibn 'Abd al-Salam held that reward is for patience because the servant has no role in the calamity, while the majority held that reward is for the calamity based on Quranic verses and hadith. Then Bajuri classifies patience into three types: patience in worship, patience in calamities, and patience in refraining from desires. He supports this with quotes from al-Dahhak and Abu Sulayman al-Razi, emphasizing the virtue of patience. The marginal gloss by al-Ajhuri clarifies that 'obedience of patience' is coordinated with 'taqwa' and that 'patience in desires' means patience in their absence.

The Three Types of Patience and the Debate on Reward
Bajuri presents a debate on reward and then classifies patience into three types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three categories of patience and the scholarly debate on whether reward is for the calamity itself or for the patience shown.

Core Idea
Patience takes three forms: in worship, in calamities, and in refraining from desires.

After discussing how prophets' human experiences increase their status when accompanied by patience, Bajuri delves into the concept of patience itself. He presents a famous debate between al-'Izz ibn 'Abd al-Salam and the majority regarding the basis of reward in calamities. He then categorizes patience into three types, each corresponding to a different arena of life, and supports this with sayings of early scholars emphasizing the virtue of patience in refraining from desires.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the reward debate
Identify the two positions and the evidence provided.
2
Understand the classification
Note the three types of patience and their definitions.
3
Reflect on the quotes
Consider the statements of al-Dahhak and Abu Sulayman al-Razi on the virtue of patience in desires.
4
Connect back to prophets
Recognize that prophets exemplify all three types of patience.
