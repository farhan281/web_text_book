Sanusi states the religious obligation to know the three judgments for God and the prophets.

Learning Objective

Knowing Allah's attributes is not optional—it's a personal obligation.

This passage makes clear that theology is not just for scholars; every accountable person must know what is necessary, impossible, and possible regarding Allah and His messengers. As you read, notice how he emphasizes the universal scope of this duty.

Commentary
This passage states the practical ruling of the previous definition. Sanusi asserts that it is a legal obligation (wajib shar‘an) upon every mukallaf (a person of sound mind who has reached maturity) to know the three rational categories—necessary, impossible, and possible—as they apply to God. That is, a person must know what attributes are necessary for God (e.g., existence), what are impossible (e.g., non-existence), and what are possible (e.g., creating or not creating). Furthermore, the same obligation applies to the prophets: one must know what is necessary for them (e.g., truthfulness), what is impossible (e.g., lying), and what is possible (e.g., normal human actions like eating). This establishes that theology is not merely academic but a personal religious duty.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the matn of Umm al-Barahin about when the basmalah is not to be said (the third type), then raises an objection about an apparent contradiction between two hadiths—one commanding basmalah at the beginning of every action, and another commanding hamdalah (al-hamdu lillah) instead—and provides an answer by distinguishing two types of beginning. The author first states that basmalah is not sought (i.e., not recited) on the third type of action. This third type must satisfy two conditions: it must not be 'pure remembrance' (dhikr mahd), meaning it is either not remembrance at all or is impure remembrance (i.e., not exclusively devotional), and the Lawgiver must not have prescribed for it a specific beginning other than basmalah or hamdalah. An example is the prayer, for which the Lawgiver prescribed takbir as its beginning, not basmalah or hamdalah. Then an objection is introduced: the two reports (khabarayn) mentioned—one apparently commanding basmalah for every action and another commanding hamdalah—seem to contradict each other. Haqiqi beginning means starting with something that precedes the intended object and was not itself preceded by anything. Idafi beginning means starting with something that precedes the intended object even if it was preceded by something else. Thus, the report about basmalah is interpreted as referring to haqiqi beginning—i.e., when nothing has been said before it—and the report about hamdalah is interpreted as referring to idafi beginning—i.e., when something (like basmalah) has already preceded it. This interpretation follows the practice of the Qur'an (which begins with basmalah) and consensus (ijmāʿ). The order is not reversed because that would contradict the Qur'anic model and consensus.

Thus, the purely linguistic meaning of du‘ā’ effectively applies only to creatures. He then cites Daqā’iq al-Minhāj which states that the only true shared (linguistic and legal) meaning is mercy, and the legal meaning is also mercy, thus avoiding the contradiction.

The Wāw of Resumption in 'Wa Yajibu'
Al-Bajuri explains the wāw's function in the opening phrase.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the wāw in 'wa yajibu' is a wāw of resumption rather than conjunction, and the linguistic distinction between inshā' and ikhbār.

Core Idea
A wāw can indicate resumption, not always conjunction, especially across speech types.

Al-Bajuri begins by clarifying a subtle grammatical point: the wāw at the start of 'wa yajibu' does not connect two clauses of the same type. Instead, it signals a fresh start after a performative statement. This distinction is crucial for understanding the text's flow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two clauses
Read the phrase 'wa yajibu' and note that it follows 'i'lam...' (know...). In Arabic grammar, 'i'lam' is a performative (inshā') while 'yajibu' is a declarative (ikhbār).
This rule is specific to classical Arabic.
2
Understand the rule
The correct view is that a performative and a declarative cannot be conjoined with a wāw al-ʿaṭf. So the wāw here must be isti'nāf, introducing a new independent statement.
3
Apply the concept
Recognize that when reading theological texts, the function of wāw is not always conjunction; context determines its role.
This affects how we parse the argument.

(قوله: وَيَجِبُ... إلخ) الواو للاستئناف لا للعطف؛ لأن ما قبلها - أعني قوله: اعلم... إلخ) - إنشاء، وما بعدها - أعني قوله : (يجب... إلخ) - إخبار، ولا يُعْطَفُ أحدهما على الآخر على الصحيح. وقد عُلِمَ مما مر أن المراد بالوجوب في مثل هذه العبارة بمعنى كَوْنِ الشيء بحيث يُنَابُ على فِعْلِه ويعاقب على تركه، بخلافه في قوله بعد:(فما يجب في حق مولانا ونحوه فإنه بمعنى عدم قبول الانتفاء. وعَبَّر بالمضارع؛ لأنه يَدُلُّ على الاستمرار التجددي، وهو مناسب للمقام هنا؛ لأن وجودَ ذلك يتجدد بتجدُّد المكلفين وقتاً بعد وقت، لكن دلالة المضارع على ذلك ليست بالوضع، بل بالقرينة؛ لأنه موضوع للحَدَث في المستقبل أو في الحال؛ ولو مَرَّةً واحدةً. فتدبر.
Al-Bajuri's gloss on al-Sanūsiyya: (His saying: 'and it is obligatory, etc.') The wāw is for resumption, not for conjunction, because what precedes it - I mean his saying: 'know, etc.' - is a performative statement, and what follows it - I mean his saying: 'it is obligatory, etc.' - is a declarative statement, and one is not conjoined to the other according to the correct view. And it has been known from what passed that what is meant by 'wujūb' in such an expression is that the thing is such that one is rewarded for doing it and punished for leaving it, unlike in his later saying: 'what is obligatory with respect to our Lord, etc.' for it means not accepting non-existence. And He expressed it in the present tense because it indicates continuous renewal, and that is appropriate for the context here because its existence is renewed with the renewal of the morally responsible individuals time after time. However, the present tense's indication of that is not by its original linguistic setting, but by context; because it is originally set for an event in the future or the present, even if only once. So reflect.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bajuri's gloss addresses the phrase 'wa yajibu' (and it is obligatory) that appears in Sanusi's text. He first notes that the wāw at the beginning is for resumption (isti'nāf), not conjunction, because the preceding clause ('know...') is a performative (inshā') and the following ('it is obligatory') is a declarative (ikhbār), and it is incorrect to conjoin the two types. Then he clarifies that the term 'wujūb' (obligation) here means something for which the doer is rewarded and the neglecter punished—this is the legal meaning. Finally, he explains why the present tense (muḍāri') was used: it indicates continuous renewal (istimrār tajaddudī), which fits because the obligation applies to new individuals over time. But he cautions that this continuous sense is not from the word's lexical meaning (it is set for a single future or present event) but from the context (qarīna), specifically 'upon every responsible person' (later phrase). He ends with an invitation to reflect.

The Command to Follow the Prophets: Distribution and Scope
Sanusi clarifies the scope of the command to follow prophets.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the command to follow the prophets in the Qur'an is distributive, meaning each nation is commanded to follow its own prophet, and the issue of whether previous laws are binding on this nation is a point of scholarly difference.

Core Idea
Each nation is commanded to follow its own messenger, not all messengers.

Sanusi begins by clarifying the pronoun in the verse 'because Allah commanded us to follow them'. He explains that the 'us' refers to all nations, not just the Muslim umma. This leads to the important principle that every nation must follow its own prophet, unless one adopts the view that previous laws are binding for us — a view held by the Malikis but considered weak by the Shafi'is. This lesson unpacks that discussion and its implications.

Following the Prophet in All Actions: Scope and Exceptions
Sanusi defines the scope of following the prophets and its exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that following the prophet includes his words, deeds, and tacit approvals, except for matters proven to be specific to him, and that believers must follow him in everything unless a specificity is established.

Core Idea
Follow the prophet in all actions unless proven to be his exclusive specificity.
