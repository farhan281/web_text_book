Al-Anbabi's Refutation of the Suitability Claim
Al-Anbabi rejects the claim that the will is suitable for all permissible things.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Anbabi's refutation of the claim that the divine will is suitable for all permissible things, and his distinction between potential and actualizing attachment.

Core Idea
The will's suitability does not necessitate restriction to a particular; it only specifies the unspecified some.

Al-Anbabi responds to a claim about the divine will's suitability. He argues that the claim is not accepted and its reasoning leads to contradiction. He introduces a crucial distinction: potential attachment (al-suluhi) is for the unspecified possible, while actualizing attachment (al-tanzihi) is for the particular. This short passage refines our understanding of the will's operation.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Begin by reading the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the translation to get the overall meaning.
Notice the compressed style; al-Anbabi is responding to a previous claim.
2
Identify the claim being refuted
The claim is that the will's suitability (salahiyya) for all permissible things implies it can specify any particular. Find where al-Anbabi rejects this.
The phrase 'هذه الدعوى غير مسلّمة' signals rejection.
3
Understand the objection
Al-Anbabi says the reasoning leads to 'combining two contradictories'. Think about how: if the will is suitable for all, it would need to specify every particular simultaneously, which is impossible.
4
Grasp the distinction
Al-Anbabi introduces two types of attachment: potential (suluhi) for the unspecified possible, and actualizing (tanzihi) for the particular. This resolves the contradiction.
The terms 'البعض الدائر' and 'المعين' are key.
5
Reflect on the resolution
Consider why this distinction avoids contradiction: the will's potential covers many possibilities, but actualization selects one particular without implying it must specify all.

Shaykh Tu'aylib's View on the Will's Attachment
Al-Anbabi reports Shaykh Tu'aylib's view and the objection about specification in pre-eternity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Shaykh Tu'aylib's view that the divine will has only a temporal attachment and the objection that an eternal attachment implies specification in pre-eternity.

Core Idea
Eternal attachment to the will implies impossible pre-eternal specification.

Shaykh Tu'aylib held a distinctive view on how the divine will relates to its objects. He argued that the will's attachment is temporally actualizing, not eternal, citing Quranic verses. The main objection to an eternal attachment is that it would constitute specification in pre-eternity, which cannot be because specification requires prior equality of possibilities. This passage presents his view and that objection.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
The passage is a marginal annotation by al-Anbabi.
2
Identify Shaykh Tu'aylib's position
Find the statement of his view: that the will has only a temporal actualizing attachment. Note the Quranic evidence he adduces.
The verse referred to is 16:40.
3
Understand the objection
The objection to the eternal attachment is that it means specification, which in pre-eternity requires prior equality of possibilities, an impossibility.

تقريرات العلامة محمد الأنبابي (قوله: وَعَلَى هَذَا فَيَكُونُ لَهَا تَعَلَّقَانِ... إلخ) اختار الشيخ تعيلب: أنها تتعلق تعلقاً تنجيزياً حادثاً فقط، مستدلاً بالآيات الكثيرة: إنَّمَا قَولُنَا لِشَن إِذا أَرَدْتَهُ () إلى غير ذلك، مُسْتَشْكِلاً القول بالتنجيزي القديم بأن معناه التخصيص، ولا تخصيص في الأزل؛ إذ هو يُشْعِرُ بسَبْقِ استواء. وأجاب عن هذا الإشكال على تسليم إثبات التنجيزي القديم بأن كيفية التعلَّق مجهولة لنا، وككُنْهِ الصفات والذات. قال: وبه يُجَابُ عما وَرَدَ في العلم من لزوم سَبْقِ العلم بالمفردات على العلم بالأحكام معلّلاً ذلك بما عُلِّلَ به في الشاهد اهـ. وقوله يشعر أي لأن معناه قضرَ الممكن على الوجود بدلاً عن العدم مثلاً، فلا بد أن يكون استواؤهما فيه قبل ذلك القصر، وهو لا يصح. وقوله: بسبق استواء، أي: وهو لا يوجد إلا فيما يزالُ. ولك أن تقول: المدارُ على عِلْمٍ الاستواء؛ وإن لم يُوجَدِ استواء بالفعل، فالله يعلم أَزَلاً استواء الممكن في الوجود والعدم فيما لا يزال. وقوله: بما علل به، أي: من أن الحكم على الشيء فرغ عن تصوره، فالتصورُ سابق في الشاهد، أي: الحاضر لنا، وهو الحادث.
Annotations of the scholar Muhammad al-Anbabi: (His words: 'And on this basis it has two attachments... etc.') Shaykh Tu'aylib chose that it (the will) has only a temporal actualizing attachment, adducing numerous verses: 'Our saying to something when we intend it' (Quran 16:40) and others, objecting to the view of an eternal actualizing attachment because its meaning is specification, and there is no specification in pre-eternity since it implies precedence of equality. And he answered this objection, while conceding the proof of the eternal actualizing attachment, by saying that the modality of the attachment is unknown to us, like the essence of the attributes and the Essence. He said: and by this one answers what is raised regarding knowledge concerning the necessity of the precedence of knowledge of the particulars over knowledge of the rulings, reasoning it by what was reasoned in the witnessed realm. And his word 'implies' means that because its meaning is 'He limited the possible to existence instead of non-existence, for example', so there must be equality of the two before that limitation, which is not valid. And his word 'by precedence of equality' means that it does not exist except in what is temporal. But you may say: the criterion is knowledge of equality; even if there is no actual equality, God knows from pre-eternity the equality of the possible in existence and non-existence in what will come. And his word 'by what was reasoned' means that the judgment on a thing is preceded by its conceptualization, so conceptualization is prior in the witnessed realm, i.e., for us, and it is temporal.

Commentary
This is a long marginal annotation by al-Anbabi reporting the view of Shaykh Tu'aylib, who holds that the divine will has only a temporal actualizing attachment, based on Quranic verses. The objection to the eternal actualizing attachment is that it implies specification in pre-eternity, which suggests prior equality of possibilities, which is invalid. The answer to that objection, while still affirming the eternal actualizing attachment, is that the modality (kayfiyya) of the attachment is unknown, just as the essence of the attributes and the divine Essence is unknown. This same principle is used to answer the objection about knowledge: the objection is that knowledge of particulars must precede knowledge of rulings (since ruling presupposes knowledge of the thing), but this is not a problem because the modality of God's knowledge is also unknown. The explanation continues by clarifying that the 'precedence of equality' is not a problem because God's knowledge of the possibilities' equality in pre-eternity suffices, even if actual equality does not occur until later. The final note explains that the reasoning in the witnessed realm is that judgment follows conceptualization, but for God, that is not the case.

Answering the Objections: Unknown Modality
Al-Anbabi reports Shaykh Tu'aylib's answer and its application to the knowledge objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the answer to the specification objection and how the principle of unknown modality also resolves the objection about God's knowledge.

Core Idea
The modality of divine attributes is unknown, so objections based on human reasoning are not decisive.

Ruling on Attributing Action to God's Power
Sanusi rules on statements attributing action to God's power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the ruling on common expressions that attribute action to God's power, and how the underlying belief determines the severity.

Core Idea
Ascribing action to God's power is dangerous if it implies independence from God.

This short but critical passage from Sanusi addresses a common pitfall in everyday speech. Many people, out of simplicity, say things like 'power is active' or 'look at the act of power'. Sanusi provides a precise ruling that depends on what the speaker actually believes. This lesson will unpack that ruling and its implications for faith.

Guided Path

Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic once, then the translation. Notice the key terms: haram, makruh, kufr.
2
Identify the two possible rulings
Sanusi gives two opinions: haram and makruh. What condition distinguishes them?
The condition is whether the speaker believes power acts independently.
3
Understand the condition for kufr
If the speaker believes power effects by itself (without God), that is kufr. Why? Because it denies God's sole agency.
4

This passage guards against attributing independence to created means.

If they do believe that power is an independent agent, that belief constitutes disbelief (kufr) because it denies that God alone is the true agent. This ruling protects the Islamic doctrine that all acts are directly from God, and attributes are only instrumental.

Al-Bajuri's gloss on Umm al-Barāhīn: (His saying: 'And they are concomitant with the seven first') its implication is that the entailment is from both sides, and it is so, even though the implication of their making them caused (maʿlūla) and making the seven first causes (ʿilal) is that the attributive attributes are only the necessary (al-lāzima) because the caused (maʿlūl) is necessary for its cause. For power. (His saying: 'And they are His being powerful (kawnuhu taʿālā qādiran) is an intermediate (wāsiṭa) between the existent and the non-existent, concomitant (mulāzima) with power.' (And his saying: 'and willing') i.e., His being willing, and it is an intermediate between the existent and the non-existent, concomitant with will, and likewise is said for the rest.

Commentary
This passage explains the relationship between the seven essential attributes (al-sabʿ al-ūlā: power, will, knowledge, etc.) and the seven attributive attributes (al-ṣifāt al-maʿnawiyya: being powerful, willing, knowing, etc.). Al-Bajuri states that they are mutually entail (al-tazāzum min al-jānibayn), meaning each implies the other. However, if we consider the attributive attributes as effects (maʿlūlāt) and the essential attributes as causes (ʿilal), then the attribution is one-sided: the attributive attributes are necessary concomitants (lāzima) of the essential attributes, because an effect is necessary for its cause. The gloss 'lil-qudra' ('for power') seems to be a marginal note specifying that this applies to power. Then he defines each attributive attribute: 'being powerful' (kawnuhu qādiran) is an intermediate state (wāsiṭa) between existence and non-existence, and it is concomitant with the attribute of power. Similarly, 'being willing' is concomitant with will, and so on for the rest of the attributes. This underscores that the attributive attributes are not separate from the essential attributes but are their necessary manifestations.

Commentary
