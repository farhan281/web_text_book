The Best Proof Against Infinite Regress

The argument centers on the relationship between a species and its individuals: if the chain of causes regresses infinitely, the species would have to be pre-eternal while its individual members are temporal, yet the species has no existence apart from those individuals. This is a clear contradiction.

Al-Bajuri explains Sanusi's method of splitting qiyam bi nafsihi into two proofs.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Sanusi's proofs are concise but rely on evidence the reader must reconstruct.

Al-Bajuri guides us through Sanusi's method of splitting God's independence into two proofs: one against needing a locus (mahall) and one against needing a specifier (mukhassis). Core Idea

Al-Bajuri demonstrates his logical acumen by analyzing the proof 'the attribute is not qualified' in two different syllogistic figures. Commentary
This passage is a commentary on Sanusi's statement that the phrase 'La ilaha illa Allah' encompasses the meanings of all the beliefs previously discussed. The commentary then delves into a key theological debate: the omitted predicate (khabar) of 'La ilaha illa Allah'. All agree that the predicate is omitted, but scholars differ on whether it is estimated from the root of existence (māddat al-wujūd) or from the root of possibility (māddat al-imkān). The preferred view (al-mukhtār) is the second, namely estimation from possibility. However, an objection is raised: if the predicate is from possibility, the phrase would mean 'no possible god except Allah', which does not affirm His existence—it only says He is possible, not that He exists. Al-Bajuri then presents the answer: the purpose of the sentence is to negate the possibility of any other god, not to affirm the existence of Allah, because His existence is already conceded as established. The well-known view is that it is continuous (muttasil), because the excluded-from universal includes both the excluded and others. A second view says it is discontinuous (munqati'), because the negation is directed at everything other than Allah, so the excluded-from does not include the excluded. A third view says it is neither. Thus three opinions exist on this matter. The passage follows the author's argumentative order: first the explanation of encompassing, then the debate on the predicate, then the objection and answer, and finally the classification of exception types.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on Sanusi's statement that the phrase 'La ilaha illa Allah' encompasses the meanings of all the beliefs previously discussed. Al-Bajuri begins by explaining the context: after covering what is obligatory for a responsible person to know, Sanusi adds the benefit of showing the excellence of the word of Tawhid. He then clarifies that the word 'meanings' is in apposition to 'beliefs' for explanation. The commentary then delves into a key theological debate: the omitted predicate (khabar) of 'La ilaha illa Allah'. All agree that the predicate is omitted, but scholars differ on whether it is estimated from the root of existence (māddat al-wujūd) or from the root of possibility (māddat al-imkān). The preferred view (al-mukhtār) is the second, namely estimation from possibility. However, an objection is raised: if the predicate is from possibility, the phrase would mean 'no possible god except Allah', which does not affirm His existence—it only says He is possible, not that He exists. Al-Bajuri then presents the answer: the purpose of the sentence is to negate the possibility of any other god, not to affirm the existence of Allah, because His existence is already conceded as established. This leads into a discussion about the type of exception (istithnā') in the shahada. The well-known view is that it is continuous (muttasil), because the excluded-from universal includes both the excluded and others. A second view says it is discontinuous (munqati'), because the negation is directed at everything other than Allah, so the excluded-from does not include the excluded. A third view says it is neither. Thus three opinions exist on this matter. The passage follows the author's argumentative order: first the explanation of encompassing, then the debate on the predicate, then the objection and answer, and finally the classification of exception types.

Why Explicit Speech Trumps Indirect Inference in Refuting Opponents
Al-Bajuri presents an objection that the predicate from possibility is superior and answers by noting the dullness of opponents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that the predicate from possibility is superior and Al-Bajuri's answer that explicit speech is better for dull opponents.

Core Idea
Even the best argument is useless if the audience cannot grasp it.

Al-Bajuri has just argued that the predicate in the shahada should be estimated from existence. Now an objector raises a point: wouldn't estimating from possibility yield a stronger demonstrative proof? Al-Bajuri responds by prioritizing clarity for the dull-minded opponents. This lesson examines that exchange and the pedagogical principle behind it.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the objector's argument carefully. Notice how he claims that estimating the predicate from the root of possibility ('imkān') provides a demonstrative refutation of the existence of other gods.
The objector is trying to show that a possibility-based estimation is logically more powerful.
2
Understand the objector's logic
The objector reasons: if the predicate is 'possible', then the shahada says 'no possible god except Allah', which necessarily implies that no other god can exist. This is a burhānī (demonstrative) inference.
This inference is based on the logical category of possibility.
3
Read Al-Bajuri's answer
Al-Bajuri rejects the objector's conclusion. He says the better course is to use explicit speech (ṣarīḥ) rather than indirect expressions (kināya) because the opponents are extremely dull-witted.
Al-Bajuri prioritizes the audience's understanding over logical elegance.
4
Reflect on the pedagogical principle
Consider that even a superior argument can be ineffective if the hearer cannot follow it. This lesson shows that theological communication must adapt to the mental capacity of the audience.
This principle is important in da'wa and teaching.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer (qāla-qultu) format. The objector argues that the preferred view of estimating the predicate from the root of possibility (imkān) is actually superior because it logically implies the denial of the existence of any other god. The reasoning: if the predicate is 'possible', then the shahada says 'no possible god except Allah', and since what is possible may or may not exist, but the statement negates the possibility of any other god, it follows necessarily that no other god can exist. This is a demonstrative (burhānī) inference, so the estimation from possibility is even better than estimating from existence. Al-Bajuri's answer (qultu) is that the better course in this context is to address the opponents explicitly (bi-l-ṣarīḥ), not through indirect expressions (kināya) or the like. The reason is that the opponents are extremely dull-witted (fī ghāyatin min al-balāda) and do not understand indirect or allusive speech. Therefore, using the explicit estimation from existence is clearer and more effective for refutation. The passage thus defends the choice of the predicate from existence on pedagogical grounds, despite the logical power of the possibility-based estimation.

The Structure of the Qiyas Istithna'i
Al-Bajuri describes the logical form of the proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical form of the exceptional syllogism (qiyas istithna'i) and how it establishes the necessity of divine attributes.

Core Idea
The proof for divine attributes is a conditional argument with an omitted premise.

Al-Bajuri unpacks Sanusi's condensed proof by revealing its logical skeleton. Before diving into each attribute, we need to grasp the syllogistic form that underlies them all. This lesson focuses on the structure of the qiyas istithna'i.

Guided Path
1
Identify the syllogism type
Read the opening sentence where Al-Bajuri states that this is a qiyas istithna'i (exceptional syllogism). Notice that it is a conditional argument.
The term qiyas istithna'i refers to a syllogism with a conditional major premise and an exceptive minor premise.
2
Find the missing premise
Al-Bajuri says the author omitted the exceptive premise. Look for it: 'But His need for that is false because it contradicts self-sufficiency.' This is the minor premise that denies the consequent.
3
Reconstruct the full syllogism
The full form is: If these attributes were not necessary, then God would need something. But needing something contradicts self-sufficiency. So the attributes are necessary.
This is modus tollens: If P then Q, not Q, therefore not P.
4
Connect to the attributes
Understand that this pattern will be applied to each attribute individually. The omitted premise remains the same for all.
This lesson prepares you for the detailed application in the next lesson.

Commentary
This passage presents a rational proof (qiyas istithna'i) for the necessity of each divine attribute. This demonstrates the logical structure underpinning the necessity of God's attributes.

The Conditional Syllogism Against Obligation on God

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If something were obligatory on Allah, He would need it—impossible, so nothing is.

This lesson presents a crisp logical demonstration from classical kalam: the impossibility of any obligation on Allah. Using a conditional syllogism, the text shows that if any action were morally required of Allah, He would be in need of that action for completion—a direct contradiction to divine self-sufficiency. The passage also notes a rejected alternative interpretation that is considered far-fetched.

The Conditional Proof That Obligation on God Implies Need

Core Idea
If God were obliged, He would need; but need is impossible for God.

Al-Bajuri unpacks a key theological argument from Sanusi using the tools of logic. He presents it as a conditional syllogism: if something were obligatory on God, He would be in need of it; but need is false for God, so obligation is also false. This demonstrates the rigorous reasoning used in kalam.

This is a modus tollens argument.

Conditional Proof of the World's Contingency

Learning Objective

Core Idea
No part of the world can be independent of God.

Al-Bajuri unpacks a logical argument from the matn that uses a conditional syllogism. The argument shows that if any part of the world were eternal, it would be independent of God – an impossibility. This logical move establishes that every part of the world must be temporally originated and dependent on God.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and a proof. First, al-Bajuri explains the phrase 'bi-asrihi' from Sanusi's text. He says it is not strictly necessary because 'the world' already means everything besides God, but it can be justified if the definite article 'al' indicates the genus, or it is considered emphatic. He then gives the etymology: 'asr' originally meant the rope used to tie a captive, so 'he went bi-asrihi' means he went with all that was his, even the rope. Next, al-Bajuri presents a proof by means of an exceptional syllogism (qiyas istithna'i) for the contingency of the world. The syllogism is: if something from the world were eternal, that thing would be independent of God; but the consequent is false because everything other than God depends on Him; therefore, nothing from the world is eternal. This establishes that the world is originated (muhdath).

The Exceptional Syllogism Proving the Contingency of the World
Al-Bajuri presents an exceptional syllogism proving nothing in the world is eternal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical structure of al-Bajuri's exceptional syllogism and how it proves that nothing in the world is eternal.

Core Idea
If anything were eternal, it would be independent of God—but everything depends on God, so nothing is eternal.

After explaining the phrase 'bi-asrihi', al-Bajuri presents a concise proof using an exceptional syllogism. This argument is central to kalam's proof of God's existence: the world is contingent and therefore needs a Creator. In this lesson, we break down the syllogism and see why its conclusion is inescapable.

Guided Path
1
Read the syllogism passage
Read from 'His saying: For if something from it were eternal...' to the end. Identify the conditional statement and the exception.
This is a concise logical argument.
2
Understand the logical form
This is an exceptional syllogism (qiyas istithna'i). The first premise is a conditional: if something were eternal, it would be independent of God. The second premise denies the consequent (it is false that anything is independent of God). Therefore, the antecedent is denied: nothing is eternal.
This is modus tollens.
3
Reflect on the theological implication
The consequent is false because everything other than God depends on Him. So the conclusion is that no part of the world is eternal; all is originated. This establishes the world's need for a creator.

Grammatical Commentary on 'Al-Ka'inat' and 'Athar'
Al-Bajuri explains the grammar of 'al-ka'inat' and 'athar'.

Core Idea
Grammar clarifies theology: 'al-ka'inat' means non-rational causes, and 'athar' is generic.

Al-Bajuri meticulously explains two key phrases from Sanusi's text. His grammatical analysis prevents misinterpretation: 'al-ka'inat' specifically refers to customary non-rational causes, and 'ma' in 'fi athari ma' indicates generality. These details ground the theological claim about causality.

Commentary
This passage is a grammatical and logical commentary. First, al-Bajuri explains the word 'al-ka'inat': it is the plural of ka'ina or ka'in, and it is formed with alif and ta (feminine plural) because it refers to non-rational customary causes (like fire and knife). Second, he explains the phrase 'fi athari ma': 'ma' is a nominal qualifier indicating generality, meaning 'any effect whatsoever'. Third, he presents another exceptional syllogism: if any created thing had influence on any effect, that effect would be independent of God; but that is false because everything other than God depends on Him. This reaffirms the doctrinal point. Finally, he addresses the phrase 'umuman wa 'ala kulli hal' (generally and in every case). The author did not explain this in his own commentary, but when asked, he clarified: 'generally' refers to all essences (dhawat), and 'in every case' refers to all attributes (sifat). This provides a precise theological qualifier: the negation of influence applies to all created essences and all their attributes.

Contingent Things Need God in Both Existence and Non-Existence
Sanusi argues that contingent things need God in both existence and non-existence, with detailed sub-arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why contingent things depend on God both before and after they come into existence, and the theological arguments that support this.

Core Idea
Contingent things never outgrow their need for God—even in existence.

In this passage, Sanusi establishes that the dependence of created things on God is not limited to their initial creation. He carefully argues that even after a contingent thing exists, it remains in need of God's continuous support, either through sustaining accidents or through the very nature of contingency itself. This lesson unpacks that argument and clarifies the key concepts involved.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its English translation once through to get a sense of the overall argument.

2
Identify the two states and the two views on accidents
Notice how Sanusi splits the discussion into the state of non-existence and the state of existence. Under existence, note the two possibilities depending on whether accidents persist.
The preponderant view is that accidents persist for two moments or more.

Understand the key contrast: imkān vs. ḥudūth
The central point is the source of need. The chosen view is that contingency (imkān) is the source, which never leaves, so need is continuous. The opposing view (ḥudūth) would lead to the absurdity of acquiring what is already acquired.
This is a crucial distinction in kalam theology.

Proof That God Does Not Need Intermediaries
Bajuri presents a syllogism proving God's independence from intermediaries.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical syllogism that proves God’s independence from any created causal intermediary.

Core Idea
If creation had power, God would need them—impossible, so they have none.

Sanusi’s student Bajuri now shows why God cannot rely on any created means. Using a clear syllogism, he demonstrates that ascribing effective power to anything other than God would imply divine need, which is impossible. This argument builds on the earlier proof that God is free from all needs.

This connects to previous teachings on divine self-sufficiency.
4

The conclusion 'فقد بان لك' means it is clear: no created thing has independent causal power. All power belongs to Allah alone.
This is a core tenet of Islamic theology (tawhid af'al).

The Disagreement on the Measure of Madd
Al-Bajuri explains the disagreement on the measure of madd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the differing opinions among scholars on how long the prolongation of 'La ilaha illa Allah' should be.

Core Idea
Scholars disagree whether madd is a specific measured prolongation or natural elongation.

Al-Bajuri records a disagreement among shaykhs about the meaning of 'madd' in the context of dhikr. One group specifies exact durations based on movements (harakāt), while another group holds it to be the natural prolongation. The commentator sides with the transmitted view of the Sufi masters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first opinion
Some shaykhs define madd as a specific prolongation: the alif of 'La ilaha' is prolonged by 14 movements, and the alif of 'Allah' by 6 movements.
2
Identify the second opinion
Others say it is just the natural prolongation (al-madd al-ṭabīʿī).
3
Note the commentator's stance
The commentator says the second view contradicts what is transmitted from the knowers of Allah.

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory commentary on a phrase from the original text ('فعلى العاقل...'). It begins by parsing the grammar: the 'fa' is a response to an implied condition, and 'على' is for emphasis, not obligation, because there is consensus that abundant dhikr is not obligatory. The 'al' in 'العاقل' is for generality, meaning all rational persons. The commentator then states the minimum of abundant dhikr: jurists say 300 times daily, Sufis say 12,000, but the real meaning is to occupy oneself with dhikr in all times and states. He then discusses prolongation (madd): for a disbeliever, it is better to leave it so he can enter Islam quickly; for a believer, prolongation is better unless a shaykh prescribes otherwise. A hadith is cited promising that prolonging 'La ilaha illa Allah' demolishes four thousand major sins, and if none, it affects his family and neighbors. However, a footnote clarifies that this hadith is not in Bukhari but in other collections. The passage then records a disagreement among shaykhs about the meaning of madd: some specify exact measures (e.g., prolonging the alif of 'La ilaha' to 14 movements and the alif of 'Allah' to 6 movements), while others say it is the natural prolongation, which contradicts the transmitted view of the Sufi masters.
