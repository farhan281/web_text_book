Definition of Ahl al-Fatrah and the Correct Opinion
Al-Bajuri defines fatrah and gives the correct ruling on the Arabs before Islam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the meaning of fatrah and why the ancient Arabs are considered ahl al-fatrah according to the correct opinion.

Core Idea
Ahl al-fatrah are those who lived without a messenger; they are judged differently.

This lesson defines the term 'fatrah' and identifies the ancient Arabs as its primary example. Al-Bajuri clarifies the correct opinion that they are indeed ahl al-fatrah, and contrasts it with a weaker view from al-Nawawi.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic and English translation together to grasp the definitions and reasoning.
Pay attention to the logical connectors.
2
Learn the definition of fatrah
Note that fatrah is defined as 'neglect' and 'abandonment' – a period without a prophet.
3
Identify the two opinions
The passage presents the correct opinion (ancient Arabs are ahl al-fatrah) and al-Nawawi's opinion that they are not. Understand why the latter is considered weak.
Al-Nawawi argued that tawhid is not specific to this nation.

الفُتُور وهي الغفلةُ والتَّرْكُ؛ لأنهم تُرِكُوا بلا رسول، فالعرب القدماء الذين أدركوا عيسى من أهل الفترة على الصحيح؛ لأنه لم يُرْسَلْ لهم، وإنما أُرْسِلَ لبني إسرائيل، والعرب لم يُرْسَلْ إليهم إلا سيدنا إسماعيل ونبينا الذي هو من ذرّيَّته وعلى بقية النبيين، فجميع العرب صاروا أهلَ فترةٍ بموت سيدنا إسماعيل إلى بعثة نبينا.
وأما على القول بأن المدارَ على بلوغ دَعْوَةِ أي نبي كان؛ فليسوا أهل فترةٍ، فهم في النار إن بَدَّلوا. وهذا القولُ هو الذي ذهب إليه النَّوَوِيُّ ووُجّه بأن التوحيد ليس أمراً خاصاً بهذه الأمة، لكنه ضعيف.
وحاصل ما يقال: إن الذي لم تَبْلُغه دعوة لا يقول بتعذيبه إلا بعض المَاتُرِيدِيَّةِ والمعتزلة؛ لبناء أمر المعرفة على العقل عند الفريقين، وأما الذي بَلَغَتْهُ الدعوة، فغَيَّرَ أو قَصَّرَ، فكَفَرَ؛ فيقول بتعذيبه مَنْ ذُكِرَ من الفريقين، والنووي ومن معه، فمتى حَصَلَتِ المعرفة والتوحيد لشخص من أهل الفترة؛ نَجَا باتفاق عند من قال بكفاية العقل لقيامه بالواجب، وعِنْدَ من قال بالشرع واكتفى بدعوة رسول ما لقيامه بالواجب أيضاً إن كان سَمِعَها، وإلا؛ فلعدم وجوبها عليه وعِنْدَ مَنْ قال بالشرع بشرط أن يكون مُرْسَلاً إليه لعدم وجوبها عليه، سواءٌ سَمِعَ من رسول لم يُرْسَلْ إليه أو لم يسمع، وتبرُّعُه بها لا يضرُّهُ. ومن هنا يَحْصُلُ جَزْمُكَ بأن أبويه ناجيان؛ لما قيل إنهما كانا على الحنيفية دين إبراهيم كما كان على ذلك طائفة من العرب كزَيْدِ بنِ عَمْرو بن نُفَيْلِ ووَرَقَةَ بنِ نَوْفَلَ، وغيرهما في تلك الأيام، فهما - إن شاء تعالى - في أعلى عليين في دار السلام
Al-fatrah is neglect and abandonment, because they were left without a messenger. So the ancient Arabs who encountered Jesus are from ahl al-fatrah according to the correct opinion, because he was not sent to them; rather, he was sent to the Children of Israel. The Arabs were not sent a messenger except our master Isma'il and our Prophet, who is from his descent, and upon the rest of the prophets. So all Arabs became people of the interregnum from the death of our master Isma'il to the mission of our Prophet. But according to the opinion that the criterion is the reaching of the call of any prophet, then they are not people of the interregnum; they are in the Fire if they alter. This opinion is the one held by al-Nawawi and was argued because tawhid is not specific to this nation, but it is weak. The summary of what is said: that one whom the call has not reached, none says he will be punished except some Maturidis and Mu'tazila, because they base the matter of knowledge on the intellect. As for one whom the call has reached and he changed or fell short, he disbelieves; so those mentioned from the two groups say he is punished, along with al-Nawawi and those with him. So when knowledge and tawhid occur to a person from ahl al-fatrah, he is saved by consensus according to those who say the intellect is sufficient for fulfilling the obligation, and according to those who say by the Law and suffice with the call of a messenger for fulfilling the obligation if he heard it, otherwise because it is not obligatory upon him; and according to those who say by the Law on condition that he be sent to, because it is not obligatory upon him, whether he heard from a messenger not sent to him or did not hear, and his voluntarily doing it does not harm him. From here you become certain that his two parents are saved, because it is said they were upon the Hanifiyyah, the religion of Ibrahim, as a group of Arabs were upon that, such as Zayd ibn 'Amr ibn Nufayl and Waraqah ibn Nawfal and others in those days. So they are, if Allah wills, in the highest 'Illiyin in the Abode of Peace.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification. It first defines 'fatrah' as a period of neglect and absence of a messenger. It then identifies the ancient Arabs before Islam as ahl al-fatrah according to the correct view, since they had no messenger after Isma'il until Muhammad. It presents an alternative opinion by al-Nawawi that the criterion is whether any prophetic call reached them, but deems this weak. The passage then summarizes the positions of different theological groups (Maturidis, Mu'tazila, and Ash'aris) on whether ahl al-fatrah are accountable for knowing God. Those who hold that intellect alone obligates knowledge (some Maturidis, Mu'tazila) would punish those who did not know; others hold that obligation is by divine law only if a call reached. The passage concludes that the Prophet's parents are definitely saved because they followed the Hanifiyyah, the religion of Ibrahim, as did some pre-Islamic monotheists.

Theological Positions on the Accountability of Ahl al-Fatrah
Al-Bajuri summarizes theological views on accountability and affirms the salvation of the Prophet's parents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the different theological positions on whether ahl al-fatrah are accountable for knowing God and the conclusion that the Prophet's parents are saved.

Core Idea
Accountability depends on whether the call reached; intellect versus divine law.

This lesson presents the summary of opinions from Maturidis, Mu'tazila, and Ash'aris regarding the accountability of ahl al-fatrah. It also clarifies that the Prophet's parents are saved because they followed the natural monotheism of Ibrahim.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic and English translation to understand the different positions.
Notice the use of 'man' clauses.
2
Identify the two scenarios
The passage distinguishes between those whom the call has not reached and those whom it has reached. Find what each group says.
3
Understand the three positions on salvation
Learn how salvation is possible for ahl al-fatrah according to those who rely on intellect, those who rely on law with a messenger's call, and those who require the call to be specifically for them.
4
Apply to the Prophet's parents
See why the Prophet's parents are considered saved: they followed the Hanifiyyah.
This is a specific application.

Refutation of the Mu'tazila on Human Creation of Actions
Al-Bajuri refutes the Mu'tazila and states the ruling on their kufr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Bajuri's refutation of the Mu'tazila claim that humans create their own actions and the nuanced ruling regarding their disbelief.

Core Idea
Not all beliefs about human agency constitute kufr; context matters.

The Mu'tazila argued that humans create their voluntary actions by a power given by God. Al-Bajuri addresses this claim, explaining why the majority does not consider it outright disbelief, and contrasts this with the stricter view of the Transoxiana scholars. This passage clarifies the boundaries of takfir in theological disputes.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text carefully, noting the key terms like 'المعتزلة' and 'خلق أفعال العبد'. Then read the English translation to grasp the argument.
The passage begins with a clarification about a preceding statement.
2
Identify the two scholarly positions
Al-Bajuri presents the correct view (non-kufr) and the view of Transoxiana scholars (kufr). Notice the reasoning behind each.
The correct view emphasizes the difference between human and divine creatorship.
3
Understand the analogy with Magians
The Transoxiana scholars considered Mu'tazila worse than Magians. Reflect on why: Magians posit one partner, Mu'tazila many.
This highlights the severity of associating partners with God in creatorship.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the statement 'or that there be with Him in existence an effecter'. The author first clarifies that a preceding similar statement was a depiction of the negation, not of the negated. Then he explains that this statement refutes the Mu'tazila who claim that the servant creates his own voluntary actions by a power that God created in him. The correct view according to many scholars is that this belief does not constitute disbelief (kufr), because the Mu'tazila do not equate the servant's creatorship with God's; they acknowledge the servant's dependence on causes and intermediaries. However, the scholars of Transoxiana (beyond the river) go further and declare them disbelievers, even considering the Magians (Zoroastrians) better off, since the Magians only associate one partner with God while the Mu'tazila associate many partners by affirming multiple creators.

Refuting the Mu'tazila on God's Obligation to Do the Best
Al-Bajuri comments on the matn line and refutes the Mu'tazila doctrine of wajib al-salah wa al-aslah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the matn line 'فَفِعْلُ كُلّ مُمْكِن أَوْ تَرْكُهُ' refutes the Mu'tazila doctrine of wajib al-salah wa al-aslah, and the technical distinctions (imkan khass vs imkan 'amm) that support this refutation.

Core Idea
God is absolutely free; the Mu'tazila's 'obligation' would compromise His freedom.

In this passage, al-Bajuri unpacks a single line from the matn that directly challenges a core Mu'tazilite tenet. The Mu'tazila claimed that God must do what is most beneficial for His creatures. But the matn's phrase 'or its omission' shows that God can choose not to act. Al-Bajuri then clarifies the logical distinction between general and specific possibility and defines the Mu'tazila's understanding of 'obligation'. Let's dive in.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Mu'tazila claim
Read the first part of the passage where al-Bajuri states that the matn line contains a refutation of the Mu'tazila who say that God is obliged to do what is beneficial (salah) and most beneficial (aslah).
This is the core doctrine under attack.
2
See how the matn line refutes it
The phrase 'or its omission' (أو تركه) indicates that God has the choice to act or not act. If God were obliged to do the best, He would have no choice to omit it.
This directly denies the Mu'tazila claim.
3
Understand the objection and answer
An objection is raised: if the act is possible, omission is already implied, so mention 'or its omission' is redundant. The answer distinguishes between general possibility (imkan 'amm) which is true even for necessary acts, and specific possibility (imkan khass) which allows both existence and non-existence.
This shows that 'or its omission' specifies that the possibility is specific.
4
Grasp the Mu'tazila definition of wujub
The passage ends by defining 'obligation' (wujub) according to the Mu'tazila as the impossibility of non-occurrence. This clarifies what they mean when they say God must do the best.
This sets up the logical basis of their claim.

Mu'tazili Objection and Clarification on the Proof
Al-Bajuri presents the Mu'tazili objection and clarifies that the proof targets ma'nawiyya.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Mu'tazili objection fails because the proof addresses the state, not the attributes.

Mu'tazili and Sunni Concepts of Akwan
Al-Bajuri explains the Mu'tazili and Sunni concepts of akwan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazili and Sunni definitions of the seven akwan (states) and their differing ontological status.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazili akwan are descriptions of God's essence; the Sunni akwan are real attributes.

Al-Bajuri explains a key difference between the Mu'tazila and Ahl al-Sunna regarding the seven attributes commonly called akwan. While both use terms like 'being powerful' and 'being willing', they mean fundamentally different things. The Mu'tazila see these as expressions of God's essence itself, while the Sunnis see them as real attributes subsisting in the essence. This passage also touches on the debate about whether these akwan are merely mental considerations or actual states.

Guided Path
1
Identify the seven akwan
Read the list of the seven terms: kawn qādiran, kawn murīdan, etc. Note that they correspond to the seven divine attributes.
2
Understand the Mu'tazili definitions
For each term, the passage provides a definition that emphasizes it as an expression of the essence itself, not an additional attribute.
For example, 'being powerful' means God's ability by His essence, not by an attribute of power.
3
Contrast with the Sunni view
Notice how Ahl al-Sunna interpret these same terms as referring to real attributes subsisting in the essence, based on either negation or affirmation of states.
4
Consider the ontological status
The passage concludes by noting that for the Mu'tazila, these akwan are either mental considerations or states depending on their stance on ahwal.

A gloss by the scholar Ahmad al-Ajhuri: His statement: 'And they only affirm the state (al-maʿnawiyya).' So they affirm being powerful (al-kawn qādiran), being willing (al-kawn murīdan), and so on up to the seven. But these akwān with them are other than with Ahl al-Sunna. According to them, 'being powerful' is an expression of His ability to bring possibles into existence and annihilate them, even if He were not described with it by His essence; 'being willing' is an expression of His specification of the possible with some of what is permissible to it by His essence; 'being knowing' is an expression of His encompassing of things as they are by His essence; 'being living' is an expression of His being described with these previous matters by His essence; 'being hearing' is an expression of His encompassing of existents by His essence; similarly it is said for 'being seeing'; 'being speaking' is an expression of His creation of speech in some bodies like the tree at which Moses heard speech, for they claim that the speech which Moses heard subsisted in the tree at which his speaking occurred. As for Ahl al-Sunna, these akwān with them are expressions of the subsistence of attributes in the essence: 'being powerful' is an expression of the subsistence of power in the most holy essence, and so on, based on the negation of states (aḥwāl), and expressions of attributes established for the essence which are necessary concomitants of the attributes-of-meanings based on the affirmation of states. Then these akwān for the Mu'tazila are mental considerations (umūr iʿtibāriyya) if they say by the negation of states; if they say by the affirmation of states, it is possible that they are states with them or that they are mental considerations.

Commentary
This passage explains the Mu'tazili and Sunni concepts of akwān (states). The gloss begins by quoting the statement 'And they only affirm the state' and then elaborates. The Mu'tazila affirm the seven akwān: being powerful, willing, knowing, living, hearing, seeing, and speaking. However, for them these are not real attributes subsisting in the essence but rather attributive descriptions of the essence itself. The passage defines each: 'being powerful' means God's ability to create and annihilate by His essence (not by an attribute); 'being willing' means His specification of possibles by His essence; 'being knowing' means His encompassing of things by His essence; 'being living' means His being described with these matters by His essence; 'being hearing' and 'being seeing' similarly; 'being speaking' means His creating speech in some body, like the tree at which Moses heard speech. For Ahl al-Sunna, these akwān refer to real attributes (like power, will) subsisting in the essence. This is on the view that denies aḥwāl (states intermediate between attribute and essence), while on the view that affirms aḥwāl, these akwān are attributes established for the essence that are necessary concomitants of the attributes-of-meanings. Finally, the passage notes that for the Mu'tazila, whether these akwān are merely mental considerations or actual states depends on their position regarding aḥwāl: if they deny aḥwāl, they are mental considerations; if they affirm aḥwāl, they could be states or mental.

Refuting the Mu'tazila: Essential Goodness and Badness of Actions
Bajuri refutes the Mu'tazila by showing their view of essential goodness/badness leads to impossibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational argument against the Mu'tazilite claim that actions are inherently good or bad, and how this claim would lead to the impossible transformation of a contingent thing into a necessary or impossible one.

Core Idea
If actions were essentially good or bad, they would become necessary or impossible, contradicting their contingent nature.

The Mu'tazila argued that God is rationally obligated to do the good and better, and that some acts (like the vision of God) are rationally impossible. This passage presents a sharp refutation: if an action's goodness or badness were intrinsic, it would make the act itself essentially necessary or impossible. Since acts are contingent (possible), this would change their very essence—an impossibility. The commentator unpacks this argument, explaining the concept of self-attribute and addressing a potential objection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Mu'tazilite claim
Read the first part of the passage where the commentator explains that the proof targets the Mu'tazila who say actions are good or bad by essence, leading to rational obligation or impossibility for God.
Notice the terms 'wujūb' and 'istiḥāla' as applied to God's actions.
2
Understand the logical implication
The passage argues that if an action were essentially good, it would be essentially necessary; if essentially bad, essentially impossible. This would change its nature from possible to necessary/impossible.
3
Grasp the self-attribute (ṣifa nafsiyya) argument
The commentator explains that possibility is a self-attribute of a possible thing, and self-attributes cannot be removed. If obligation were added, possibility would have to disappear—an absurdity.
This is the core of the refutation.
4
See how a potential objection is resolved
The passage ends by addressing a counterexample: something possible becoming necessary due to God's knowledge is different, as that necessity is accidental, not essential. Reflect on this nuance.
This shows the argument is only against essential necessity.

His saying: 'If something of them were obligatory upon Him, exalted is He, rationally' meaning: as the Mu'tazila say, for they said that the good and the better are obligatory upon Him. And His saying: 'or impossible rationally' meaning: as the Mu'tazila say, for they say that the vision (of God) is impossible upon Him. And His saying: 'then the possible would become necessary or impossible... etc.' meaning: because each of obligation and impossibility, in their view, occurs only due to the action being good or bad by its essence according to reason, and what is by essence does not change; and then if something from among the possible things becomes obligatory or impossible, it entails the change of its reality from possibility to obligation or impossibility. Marginal note of Shaykh Ahmad al-Ajhuri: His saying: 'That is, because each of the obligation... etc.' The purpose by that is to communicate that the obligation and impossibility upon which the change is entailed are essential, not accidental. Taqrīrāt of Shaykh Muhammad al-Anbā'ī: His saying: 'If the action were good or bad by its essence' meaning: so if the action includes an essential good, then it is essentially obligatory, and the assumption is that it is possible; so the possible has changed into an obligatory, meaning that it must be done because it includes the essential good. His saying: 'And what is by essence does not change.' The explanation of that: the possibility of the possible is a self-attribute (ṣifa nafsiyya) for it, and it is known that the self-attribute does not admit removal; so if it were qualified with obligation, it would necessitate the removal of possibility, which is a self-attribute. His saying: 'From possibility' meaning: intrinsic. And His saying: 'To obligation' meaning: intrinsic. And similarly it is said in the case of impossibility; because each of obligation and impossibility in their view is based on intrinsic good and intrinsic bad; so it follows for them that obligation is intrinsic, and likewise impossibility, while the assumption is that the action is permissible (jā'iz) in their view. And by this is refuted what is said: that the change of an intrinsically possible thing into something necessary for another (bi-ghayrihi) is conceivable, as in the possible thing that God's knowledge pertains to its existence. And after this, there is further discussion in this topic. Reflect.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite doctrine that actions are good or bad by their essence, leading to rational obligation or impossibility for God. The commentator first explains that the proof about permissibility of God's acts is directed against the Mu'tazila who claim that God is obligated to do what is good and better, and that certain things like the vision of God are rationally impossible. The commentator argues that if an action were good or bad by essence, then it would be essentially necessary or essentially impossible, which would change its nature from possible to necessary or impossible. This is because what is by essence does not change. The marginal note by al-Ajhuri emphasizes that the obligation and impossibility in question are essential, not accidental. The taqrīrāt by al-Anbā'ī elaborates: if an action has intrinsic goodness, it becomes intrinsically obligatory, but the action is assumed possible, so the possible has changed into necessary. The explanation is that the possibility of a thing is a self-attribute (ṣifa nafsiyya) that cannot be removed. If it becomes obligated, that attribute would have to be removed, which is impossible. Similarly for intrinsic badness leading to impossibility. This refutes the Mu'tazila because they themselves consider actions to be permissible (jā'iz) in some cases, yet their principles would entail intrinsic necessity or impossibility. The commentator also addresses a potential objection: that change of an intrinsically possible thing to necessary for another reason (like God's knowledge of its existence) is conceivable, but that is a different case. The passage ends with an invitation to reflect on this complex topic.
