The Symbolism of the Shahada's 24 Letters
Al-Bajuri explains the spiritual symbolism of the Shahada's letters and addresses an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the spiritual symbolism behind the 24 letters of the Shahada, including their guttural articulation and lack of dots, and how the objection concerning the letter mim is resolved by taghlib.

Core Idea
The Shahada's 24 letters symbolize sincerity, detachment, and hourly expiation.

Al-Bajuri unpacks the deeper meanings hidden in the structure of the Shahada. The fact that it contains exactly 24 letters, all pronounced from the throat and without dots, is not accidental. Each detail carries a spiritual lesson, inviting the believer to reflect on the inner reality of the testimony of faith.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and its translation
Read the passage once to get an overview of the points being made. Notice the structure: the explanation of the 24 letters, their qualities, and the objection and answer.
The translation is literal; pay attention to the key terms.
2
Identify the three symbolic features
The passage highlights three features: (1) all letters are guttural (jawfiyya), (2) none have dots, and (3) there are exactly 24 letters. Understand the spiritual significance assigned to each.
The guttural nature points to sincerity from the heart; lack of dots symbolizes detachment; the number corresponds to the hours of the day.
3
Understand the objection and the principle of taghlib
An objection is raised: the letter mim in 'Muhammad' is labial, not guttural. The answer uses taghlib (predominance): since most letters are guttural, the term 'all' is used loosely.
Taghlib is a common linguistic principle where the majority governs the description.
4
Reflect on the spiritual lessons
Consider how these details encourage the believer to utter the Shahada with sincere hearts, detached from worldly concerns, and to seek forgiveness for every hour of the day.

قوله: مع قلة حروفها أي: لأنها أربعة وعشرون حرفاً، وكانت كلها جوفية للإشارة إلى أنه ينبغي الإتيان بها من خالص الجوف، وهو القلب، ولم يكن فيها حرف معجم، بل كلها مجردة عن النقط إشارة إلى أنه ينبغي لمن نطق بها أن يتجرد عن كل ما عداه تعالى. وكانت أربعة وعشرين حرفاً؛ لأن الليل والنهار أربع وعشرون ساعةً، فكل حرف يكفر ذنوب ساعةٍ. (قوله: كلها جوفية) مراده بالجوفية ما عدا الشفوية كما يعلم من حاشية الهدهدي، وبعد ذلك فيرد عليه الميم من محمد فإنها شفوية، ويجاب عنه بأنه ارتكب التغليب فغلب غير الشفوي لكثرته.
His statement: 'with the paucity of its letters' means because they are twenty-four letters, and all of them are guttural (jawfiyya) to indicate that one should utter them from the pure interior (jawf), which is the heart. And there is no dotted letter in them; rather, all are devoid of dots, indicating that whoever utters them should strip himself of everything other than Him, exalted. And they are twenty-four letters because the night and day are twenty-four hours, so each letter expiates the sins of an hour. His statement: 'all of them are guttural' means by 'guttural' except labial letters, as known from the Hashiya of al-Hadadi. And after that, an objection is raised concerning the mim of Muhammad, for it is labial. And it is answered that he employed predominance (taghlib), giving predominance to the non-labial due to its abundance.

Commentary
This passage explains the spiritual symbolism of the letters of the Shahada. The commentator first clarifies that when the matn says 'with the paucity of its letters', it refers to the fact that the Shahada consists of twenty-four letters. All these letters are classified as 'jawfiyya' — that is, articulated from the interior of the mouth or chest — to signify that the testimony should come from the sincere heart. Additionally, none of the letters have dots (i.e., they are unpointed), which symbolizes that the speaker should be stripped of all attachments except Allah. The commentator then gives a numerical correspondence: the twenty-four letters match the twenty-four hours of the day and night, so each letter expiates the sins of one hour. Next, the commentator addresses a potential objection. The claim that all letters are jawfiyya seems contradicted by the letter mim in 'Muhammad', which is labial (shafawiyya). The answer is that this is a case of taghlib (predominance): the majority of letters are jawfiyya, so the term 'all' is applied by way of predominance, overlooking the exception for the sake of the prevailing pattern.

Definition of the Articles of Faith and the Wisdom of Brevity
Definition of articles of faith and wisdom of Shahada's brevity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the tripartite categorization of creedal obligations (wajib, ja'iz, mustahil) and the wisdom behind the Shahada's concise formulation as a comprehensive creedal statement.

Core Idea
The Shahada's brevity hides the complete creed: wajib, ja'iz, mustahil.

In this passage, Al-Bajuri unpacks what 'articles of faith' means: the obligatory knowledge every Muslim must hold, covering God's necessary attributes, possible actions, and impossible defects. He then reflects on the profound wisdom behind the Shahada's extreme brevity—only 24 letters—while containing all these creedal categories. The author's cautious use of 'perhaps' also teaches us a lesson in theological humility.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the passage begins with a definition and then moves to a reflection.
2
Identify the three categories
The definition of عقائد الإيمان includes three categories: الواجب (obligatory), الجائز (possible), and المستحيل (impossible). These are the foundational rational judgments in kalam.
These categories apply primarily to God and the prophets in Ash'ari theology.
3
Reflect on the wisdom of brevity
Consider why the Shahada is brief yet comprehensive. Note the author's use of 'perhaps' (لعل) and understand why he does not assert certainty—out of politeness to Allah and the Prophet.
This shows adab in theological discourse, acknowledging that full wisdom belongs to Allah.

قوله: من عقائد الإيمان بيان لما يجب على المكلف الشامل للواجب والجائز والمستحيل. قوله: ولعلها... إلخ يعني: لعل الحكمة في جعلها ترجمة على ما في القلب من الإسلام وفي عدم قبول الإيمان من أحد إلا بها اختصارها مع اشتمالها على العقائد التي ذكرها، وإنما لم يجزم، بل أتى بـ (لعل) التي للترجي تأدباً مع الباري بعدم دعوى الغيب، ومع النبي، إذ لا يحيط أحد بأسرار كلماته إلا الله تعالى، فيجوز أن يكون السر في ذلك غير ما ذكر. قوله: لاختصارها أي قلة حروفها لما تقدم من أنها أربعة وعشرون حرفاً. وقوله: مع اشتمالها أي: اشتمال معناها. وقوله: على ما ذكرناه أي: من العقائد السابقة.
His statement: 'from the articles of faith' is an explanation of what is obligatory upon the morally responsible person, encompassing the obligatory, the possible, and the impossible. His statement: 'and perhaps it...' etc. means: perhaps the wisdom in making it a translation of what is in the heart of Islam and in not accepting faith from anyone except by it is its brevity along with its inclusion of the articles of faith that he mentioned. And he did not assert definitely, but used 'perhaps' which indicates hopefulness, out of politeness towards the Creator by not claiming knowledge of the unseen, and towards the Prophet, for no one encompasses the secrets of his words except Allah Almighty. So it is possible that the secret in that is other than what was mentioned. His statement: 'for its brevity' means the fewness of its letters as previously stated, that it has twenty-four letters. And his statement: 'with its inclusion' means the inclusion of its meaning. And his statement: 'of what we mentioned' means from the previously mentioned articles of faith.

Commentary
This passage provides a definition and a theological reflection. First, the commentator explains that the phrase 'from the articles of faith' refers to the obligatory knowledge required of every accountable person (mukallaf), which covers the three categories: the obligatory (wajib), the possible (ja'iz), and the impossible (mustahil) with respect to God and the prophets. This is the standard tripartite division in Ash'ari kalam. Then, the commentator discusses the phrase 'perhaps it' (la'allaha) used in the matn. He explains that the wisdom behind making the Shahada the concise expression of faith is its brevity (only 24 letters) while encompassing all the creedal points previously discussed. The author (Sanusi) deliberately uses the word 'perhaps' rather than a definitive statement, as a sign of respect towards Allah and the Prophet, since no one fully knows the divine wisdom behind the choice of the Shahada. The commentator then glosses the key terms: 'brevity' refers to the 24 letters, 'inclusion' refers to the meaning containing all beliefs, and 'what we mentioned' refers to the creedal points earlier in the text.

The Wisdom of the Basmala as a Summary of Faith
Al-Bajuri explains the wisdom of the basmala as a summary of Islamic beliefs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the twofold wisdom behind the basmala being a comprehensive summary of Islamic beliefs and why the author uses cautious language.

Core Idea
The basmala's brevity and inclusiveness make it the perfect summary of faith, but its true secret is known only to Allah.

Al-Bajuri explains why the basmala serves as a comprehensive summary of the tenets of faith. He highlights its brevity and all-encompassing meaning, while also noting the use of 'perhaps' as an expression of reverence. This passage invites us to reflect on the depth and wisdom of a short phrase that encapsulates the entire faith.

1
Read the passage and translation
