Ruling on Saying Bismillah Over Prohibited Acts
Bajuri reports four opinions on saying 'Bismillah' over prohibited or disliked acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four scholarly opinions on pronouncing the basmalah when about to commit a prohibited or disliked act, and the preferred view that distinguishes between intrinsic and extrinsic rulings.

Core Idea
Saying 'Bismillah' over sin can be kufr—context matters.

The simple act of saying 'Bismillah' before any action reflects seeking blessing from Allah. But what if the action itself is prohibited or disliked? Scholars debated whether such a practice amounts to opposing the Lawgiver. This passage from Bajuri's commentary on Umm al-Barahin presents several opinions on this nuanced issue.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice the different opinions introduced by 'قيل'.
Pay attention to the key term 'مراغمة الشارع' (opposing the Lawgiver).
2
Identify the four opinions
The passage presents four sayings (أقوال). List them and note the ruling for each: disliked on both, forbidden on both, disliked on makruh and forbidden on haram absolutely, and the preponderant view distinguishing intrinsic vs extrinsic.
3
Understand the principle of opposition
The core reasoning is that saying 'Bismillah' over a prohibited act makes that act a locus for blessing, thus opposing the Lawgiver. Ask yourself: why does this opposition matter in kalam?
This connects to the broader theme of respecting Allah's commands.
4
Focus on the preponderant opinion
The preferred opinion distinguishes between acts that are forbidden/disliked in themselves (li-dhatihi) and those due to an external reason (li-‘arad). Why does this distinction matter?
Consider examples like drinking wine vs. eating onion bread.
5
Apply the ruling
Reflect on how this ruling guides everyday practice. What should you do if you are about to do something that is disliked due to an extrinsic reason?

Commentary
This passage discusses the ruling on pronouncing the basmalah (saying 'Bismillah') when one is about to engage in a prohibited or disliked act. The author notes that this topic is not directly derived from the preceding discussion. He then reports several scholarly opinions. The first opinion holds that saying 'Bismillah' is disliked whether the act is disliked or forbidden, even if the ruling is due to an extrinsic circumstance, because it involves opposing the Lawgiver by seeking blessing through what He has prohibited. The second opinion says it is forbidden in both cases, and some even consider it disbelief when said over wine. But these opinions vary in intensity. A third opinion distinguishes: it is disliked upon disliked acts and forbidden upon forbidden acts absolutely. The preferred (rājiḥ) opinion, however, makes a further distinction: it is disliked upon disliked acts in themselves (makrūh li-dhātihi) and forbidden upon forbidden acts in themselves (harām li-dhātihi). This is because genuine opposition to the Lawgiver occurs only when the act's prohibition or dislike is intrinsic, not when it is due to an extrinsic circumstance where the act itself may be permissible. For example, one should not say 'Bismillah' before drinking wine (harām li-dhātihi), but one may say it before eating bread with onion (where the dislike is due to the onion, not intrinsic to the bread). This nuanced ruling reflects the kalām concern for precision in applying principles of veneration (ta'ẓīm) and opposition to the Sacred Law.

The Meaning of Shakur and the Difficulty of True Thankfulness
The author explains the hyperbolic form shakur and the rarity of true thankfulness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why true thankfulness (shukr) is nearly impossible and why the term shakur refers to one who realizes his inability to give thanks.

Core Idea
True thankfulness means recognizing you can never thank enough.

Thankfulness to Allah is not simply expressing gratitude; it is a profound acknowledgment that even the ability to thank is itself a blessing requiring thanks. This passage from al-Baydawi and the author's commentary explains the hyperbolic form shakur and why only a few attain it.

Guided Path
1
Understand shakur as hyperbolic
Notice that shakur is an intensive form of shakir, emphasizing abundance or completeness.
Hyperbole is achieved by expending the whole (sarf al-kull) but not exclusively.
2
Learn al-Baydawi's definition
Read the quote from al-Baydawi: shakur is one who performs shukr with heart, tongue, and limbs most of the time, yet never fulfills God's right.
This shows the gap between effort and due.
3
Grasp the infinite regression
Realize that success in thanking is itself a blessing, requiring further thanks, leading to an endless chain.
Thus, no one can truly fulfill shukr.
4
Conclusion: shakur sees inability
The passage concludes that the true shakur is one who sees his inability to give thanks, as reflected in the verse 'And few of My servants are the thankful.'
This aligns with the common misunderstanding correction.

Commentary

Belief in the messenger means accepting everything he brought about God, prophets, and the Hereafter.

This distinction matters for understanding the nature of prophetic message: it is not merely a personal testimony but a command for others to believe.

Generality of Taraddi and Inclusion of Sinners in Tabi'in
Explains that taraddi is not limited to Companions and that 'tabi'in' includes sinners.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the phrase 'Radiya Allahu 'anhum' is not exclusive to the Companions and how the term 'tabi'in' includes disobedient believers.

Core Idea
Divine pleasure is not limited to the Companions; it extends to all who follow them in faith.

This lesson clarifies two important points about common phrases in Islamic literature. First, the expression 'Radiya Allahu 'anhum' (may Allah be pleased with them) is not reserved only for the Companions; it applies to scholars and righteous servants as well. Second, the phrase 'those who follow them in goodness' (bi ihsan) is interpreted broadly, so that even sinful believers are included. The commentator explains that they are actually more in need of supplication.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully

Notice that the passage makes two distinct claims.
2
Identify the two key claims
The passage first states that taraddi is not exclusive to Companions. Second, it explains that 'tabi'in' includes sinners because 'ihsan' here means faith.
These two points are related: both broaden the scope of mercy.
3
Understand the interpretation of 'ihsan'
The commentator specifies that 'ihsan' here does not mean its full technical meaning (worshiping Allah as if seeing Him), but simply faith. This is why sinners are included.
This shows the importance of context in interpreting religious texts.

Commentary
This passage explains two points. First, the phrase 'Radiya Allahu 'anhum' (may Allah be pleased with them) is not reserved for the Companions only; it applies equally to eminent scholars and righteous servants, because they are also deserving of divine pleasure. Second, the expression 'wa al-tabi'ina lahum bi ihsan' (and those who follow them in goodness) is interpreted broadly: 'goodness' (ihsan) here means simply faith, not the full technical meaning of worshiping Allah as if seeing Him. Therefore, the category 'tabi'in' includes disobedient believers (the 'usat') because they are more in need of prayers than others. The passage thus emphasizes the inclusivity of these blessings.
