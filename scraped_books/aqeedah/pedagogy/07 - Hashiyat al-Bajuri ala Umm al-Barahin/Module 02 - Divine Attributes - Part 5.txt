The Proof from Opposites for Divine Attributes and Its Weakness
Al-Bajuri explains and critiques the argument from opposites for divine attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument from opposites for God's attributes and why it is considered weak in Islamic theology.

Core Idea
The opposites argument fails because it assumes what is imperfect for creation is also imperfect for the Creator.

Al-Bajuri presents a theological argument that seeks to prove God must possess attributes by showing that lacking them would imply their opposites, which are imperfections. But as he explains, this reasoning is flawed because it relies on a comparison between the created world and God that may not hold. Let's examine the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic original and its literal English translation. This allows you to see the author's own wording and logical structure without interpretation.
Notice the technical terms like 'conditional' and 'exceptive syllogism'.
2
Identify the argument structure
The argument is a conditional syllogism with an omitted exceptive part. Identify the conditional premise and the evidence for the omitted part.
The author states the conditional directly and then gives the evidence for the exceptive step.
3
Understand the critique
Al-Bajuri notes that the argument is weak because the notion of deficiency is observational from the created world, and what is deficient for creatures may not be deficient for the Creator.
This is a classic critique against analogical reasoning in theology.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider why this argument is not decisive. Think about the limits of using creation as a measure for the Creator.

Commentary
This passage explains the argument from opposites used by some theologians to prove that God must have attributes because if He lacked them, He would have to have their opposites, which are imperfections, and God is free from imperfections. The commentator clarifies the logical structure: a conditional syllogism where the antecedent is 'if He is not described with attributes' and the consequent is 'He is described with their opposites'. Then the opposite of the consequent is false (because opposites are imperfections), so the opposite of the antecedent is true, i.e., He is described with attributes. However, the commentator notes that this argument has a weakness: what is considered a deficiency in the created world may not be a deficiency in the Divine. Therefore, the argument is not decisive.

The Proof That God's Actions Are Permissible
Al-Bajuri presents the proof that God's actions are permissible via conditional reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof that God's actions regarding possible things are permissible (ja'iz) and how this differs from the absolute possibility inherent in their nature.

Core Idea
God's actions are neither necessary nor impossible; they are permissible.

In this passage, al-Bajuri presents the rational proof that God's creation or non-creation of possible things is not constrained by necessity or impossibility. He uses a conditional argument to show that such constraints would contradict the very nature of possibility itself. This lesson unpacks that proof and clarifies the crucial distinction between a thing's inherent possibility and the permissibility of God's action concerning it.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof carefully
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Pay attention to the conditional structure: 'If... then... but the consequent is false, so the antecedent is false.'
The proof is a hypothetical syllogism (modus tollens).
2
Identify the key terms
Note the terms: wajib (necessary), mustahil (impossible), ja'iz (permissible), mumkin/mumkinat (possible things). Understand their definitions as given in the commentary.
Possible things are those whose existence and non-existence are equally valid in themselves.
3
Understand the distinction between possibility and permissibility
Al-Bajuri explains that the possibility known from the nature of a thing is absolute, not considering God. But permissibility (ja'iz) here means it is valid for God to do or not do it. This is a restricted judgment.
Do not confuse the two: one is about the thing itself, the other about God's relation to it.

(His saying: 'Doing of possible things') By possible things is meant, as previously, the potential things (maqdurat) whose existence and non-existence are valid in themselves regardless of their connection to Allah. These potential things are essences and attributes, such as the essence of Zayd and his whiteness. What is judged as permissible with respect to Him is doing or leaving them, i.e., bringing them into existence or keeping them in non-existence. The permissibility mentioned is not the same as the possibility of action and non-action known from their attribution to possible things, because the possibility known from that attribution is absolute, not considered with respect to Allah. Rather, what is meant is that each of action and non-action is valid in its affirmation and non-affirmation without restricting affirmation and non-affirmation to their connection with Allah. In contrast, the permissibility judged about them means that each of them is valid in its affirmation and non-affirmation from Allah. So what is judged is restricted and what is known from the attribution is absolute, and the restricted is not the absolute. And the 'or' in the author's speech, as previously, can be for one of two absolute things, applied to each of action and omission, and can be in the sense of 'and'.

Commentary
This passage presents the proof that God's actions (creating possible things or leaving them uncreated) are neither necessary nor impossible, but permissible (ja'iz). The proof is a conditional: if something were obligatory or impossible for God, then the possible (mumkin) would become necessary or impossible, which is absurd. Therefore, nothing is obligatory or impossible for God regarding possible things. The passage then defines possible things as those whose existence and non-existence are equally possible in themselves, and distinguishes between the absolute possibility known from the nature of the thing and the restricted permissibility with respect to God.

Proof from Impossibility of Opposites for Divine Attributes
Bajuri explains the proof that God must possess perfect attributes, else He would have their opposites.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument that God must possess perfect attributes, as the alternative would require impossible deficiencies.

Core Idea
If God lacked any attribute, He would have its opposite—a deficiency—which is impossible.

Bajuri unpacks a concise logical argument from Sanusi's text. The proof uses a conditional statement to show that God must be qualified with perfect attributes. Let's follow the reasoning step by step and also note a subtle weakness that scholars have discussed.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the English translation to get the gist of the argument.
Notice the logical terms: 'law' (if), 'lazima' (necessary), 'batala' (false).
2
Identify the two parts of the proof
The proof consists of a conditional statement and an omitted exceptive syllogism. Find where the commentator explains these parts.
The author (Sanusi) stated the conditional; the exceptive is implied and proven by the statement 'they are deficiencies'.
3
Understand the principle of opposites
The argument relies on the principle that anything receptive to a quality must have either that quality or its opposite. Grasp why this applies to God.
God is receptive to perfect attributes; therefore, if He lacks them, He must have their opposites.
4
See how the conclusion is drawn
The opposites are deficiencies, and deficiency is impossible for God. Therefore, the opposite cannot exist, so God must have the original attributes.
This is a classic proof in kalam for the necessity of divine attributes.
5
Consider the known weakness
The passage notes a prior objection: what counts as a deficiency in the witnessed world may not be a deficiency in the absent (God). Reflect on this limitation.
This shows that the proof is not universally accepted without qualification.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a proof regarding God's attributes. He shows that the author of the text (Sanusi) stated the conditional and left the exceptive implicit, but provided its proof by stating that the opposites are deficiencies. But the opposites are deficiencies, and deficiency is impossible for God. Therefore, God must have the attributes. The commentary also notes a prior objection: that what is considered a deficiency in the witnessed world does not necessarily imply it is a deficiency in the absent (God). So the argument is presented, along with a known weakness.

Refuting the Circularity Objection on Self-Subsistence
Al-Bajuri refutes the objection of circularity in defining self-subsistence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Bajuri resolves the objection that the definition of self-subsistence as independence leads to circularity.

Core Idea
Specific independence avoids circularity.

In this short passage, al-Bajuri addresses a logical objection: if self-subsistence (qiyam bi al-nafs) is defined as independence (istighna') from place and particularizer, then saying that independence entails self-subsistence seems to make a thing entail itself. He answers by clarifying that the independence used in the definition is more specific than total independence. This lesson will guide you through the objection and its elegant resolution.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the structure: an objection is presented and then answered.
The passage is concise—every word matters.
2
Identify the objection
The objection claims that if self-subsistence is defined as independence, then making independence entail self-subsistence is circular—like A entails A.
Remember: the definition was given earlier.
3
Understand the answer
Al-Bajuri explains that the independence defining self-subsistence is more specific (akhas) than absolute independence. It is independence from place and particularizer only, not from everything else.
Thus the entailment is not of the same thing.
4
Reflect on the distinction
Consider why the distinction works: general independence includes independence from everything; specific independence is a subset. The objection fails because it confuses the two.
This shows the importance of precise definitions.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection and its answer regarding the relationship between independence (istighna') and self-subsistence (qiyam bi al-nafs). The objection states: if independence from everything entails self-subsistence, and self-subsistence is already defined as independence from place and particularizer (which is a subset of independence), then the entailment is circular (a thing entailing itself). The answer is that the independence that defines self-subsistence is more specific (akhas) than the general independence from everything; the latter includes independence from other things besides place and particularizer, so the entailment is not of the same thing. Thus, the objection fails.

The Conditional Syllogism for Divine Attributes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the conditional syllogism proves the necessity of each divine attribute by showing that the alternative leads to contradiction with God's self-sufficiency.

Core Idea
If an attribute were not necessary, God would need something — but need contradicts His self-sufficiency.

Al-Bajuri unpacks a clever logical argument from Sanusi that proves each attribute is necessary for God. The argument uses a conditional syllogism: if the attribute were not necessary, then God would need an external cause or support. But need contradicts God's absolute self-sufficiency (al-istighna'), so the attribute must be necessary. This lesson walks through the structure of that proof and how it applies to each attribute.

This passage is a definition and proof passage. The commentator, al-Bajuri (may Allah have mercy on him), explains two matters. Al-Bajuri then details how each attribute—life, power, will, knowledge—is essential, showing that the absence of any one leads to the impossibility of any event. Life is the condition for all attributes; power and will are directly tied to causation; knowledge is prerequisite for will. Thus the necessity and universality of God’s attributes are proven.

Divine Pleasure: Meaning, Superiority, and Inclusivity
The author explains the meaning of رضي الله and clarifies who merits it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two possible meanings of divine pleasure (rida) and why it is not exclusive to the Companions but applies to all righteous believers.

Core Idea
Divine pleasure is greater than pardon; it means either bestowing favor or His will.

This lesson examines the meaning of the phrase 'رضي الله عنه' (may Allah be pleased with him) as explained by the commentator. We will see that divine pleasure can be understood in two ways, and it is superior to mere pardon. Moreover, the lesson clarifies that this blessing is not limited to the Companions but extends to all righteous believers.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation. Pay attention to the two interpretations of 'rida' and the comparison with 'afw'.
The Arabic uses technical terms like صفة فعل and صفة ذات.
2
Understand the two meanings of rida
Identify that rida can mean bestowing favor (إنعام) or His will (إرادة), and note the different implications for divine attributes.
First meaning makes it an attribute of act, second an attribute of essence.
3
Compare rida with afw (pardon)
Learn why rida is higher than afw: afw only removes punishment, while rida includes favor. Reflect on Ibn al-Shajari's prayer.
The prayer shows that one can be pardoned without being pleased with.
4
Recognize the inclusivity of tardiya
Understand that requesting divine pleasure is not only for the Companions but also for eminent scholars and righteous servants.
This corrects a common misconception.

قوله: وَرَضِيَ الله ... إلخ) المراد بالرضا في حقه تعالى: الإنعام أو إرادته، فهو صِفَةً فعل على الأول، وصِفَةُ ذاتٍ على الثاني، وهو أغلى من العفو؛ لأنه مَحْوُ الذنب، وعدمُ العقوبة عليه؛ وإن لم يكن إنعام، ولذلك قال ابن الشَّجَرِي: اللهم ارْضَ عنا، فإن لم تَرْضَ عنا؛ فاعْفُ عنا، فإن المَوْلى يعفو عن عبده وهو غير راض عنه. الأخيار ولا يختص الترضِي بالصحابة، بل مِثْلُهُمْ في ذلك العلماء الأعلام، والعباد الأخيار
His words: 'and may Allah be pleased with...' etc.) The meaning of pleasure (rida) with respect to Him, exalted is He, is: bestowing favor (al-in‘am) or His will (iradatuhu). So it is an attribute of act (sifat fi‘l) according to the first, and an attribute of essence (sifat dhat) according to the second. It is higher than pardon (‘afw) because pardon is the erasure of sin and absence of punishment, even if there is no favor. For that reason Ibn al-Shajari said: 'O Allah, be pleased with us, and if You are not pleased with us, then pardon us, for the master may pardon his servant while not being pleased with him.' The righteous—and the request for divine pleasure (al-tardiya) is not specific to the Companions; rather, their likes in that regard are the eminent scholars and the righteous servants.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of the phrase 'رضي الله عنه' (may Allah be pleased with him) as it appears in the original text. The author gives two possible interpretations of 'pleasure' (rida) when attributed to Allah: it either means bestowing favor (إنعام) or His will (إرادة). In the first case, it is a 'sifat fi‘l' (attribute of act), in the second a 'sifat dhat' (attribute of essence). The author then compares it to 'pardon' (عفو), noting that pardon is merely erasing sin and not punishing, even without favor, whereas pleasure is higher because it includes favor. He supports this with a quote from Ibn al-Shajari, who prays for pleasure first, and if not, then at least pardon, because a master may pardon without being pleased. Finally, he clarifies that the prayer for divine pleasure (الترضي) is not exclusive to the Companions; it also applies to eminent scholars and righteous servants. This is a key point in Sunni theology regarding the permissibility of invoking mercy for non-Sahabah.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The phrase 'رضي الله عنه' is only used for the Companions of the Prophet.
Correction: The author explicitly states that requesting divine pleasure is not specific to the Companions; it applies to all righteous believers, including eminent scholars and righteous servants.

Key Takeaway
Divine pleasure (rida) is an attribute that signifies bestowing favor or His will; it is higher than pardon and is sought for all righteous believers, not only the Companions.
