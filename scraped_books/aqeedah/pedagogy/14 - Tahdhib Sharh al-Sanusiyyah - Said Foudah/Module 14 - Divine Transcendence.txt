The Third Attribute: God's Incomparability to Creation
Sanusi defines God's incomparability and provides scriptural evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the meaning of God's transcendence (mukhalafatu l-hawadith) and the evidence supporting it from revelation and consensus.

Core Idea
God is absolutely unlike anything in essence, attributes, and actions.

Sanusi now turns to the third essential attribute: God's incomparability to all created things. This attribute safeguards tawhid by affirming that nothing in creation resembles Allah in any way. The passage defines this attribute, explains its implications, and provides textual evidence from the Quran, the Sunnah, and the consensus of the Muslim community.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition carefully
Start by reading the Arabic definition of mukhalafatu l-hawadith. Notice that it negates likeness in three areas: essence, attributes, and actions.
The definition uses the phrase 'nafy al-mushabaha'.
2
Understand the explanation of incomparability
The passage elaborates that God's essence is not like lights, darknesses, accidents, or bodies. Whatever you imagine, God is different. Consider what this means for concepts like direction, place, and time.
3
Examine the evidence from Quran and Sunnah
Read the Quranic verse (42:11) and the hadith about Surah al-Ikhlas. Reflect on how they explicitly negate likeness to God.
The hadith explains that God does not beget, is not begotten, and has no equal.
4
Note the consensus and deferred rational proof
The passage mentions that the ummah agrees on this attribute. Also, it promises a rational proof later, which will be covered in another lesson.
Consensus (ijma') is an important source of knowledge in Islam.

الثالثة مخالفته تعالى للحوادث
وحقيقة المخالفة للحوادث عبارة عن نفي المشابهة في الذات والصفات والأفعال. فلیست ذاته من جنس الأنوار ولا الظلمات ولا الأعراض ولا الأجرام بل كل ما يخطر ببالك فالله بخلاف ذلك. فليس له جهة ولا مكان ولا يمر عليه الزمان وصفاته قديمة باقية ومخالف في أفعاله لأنه هو الخالق، والعباد ليس لهم تأثير في شيء من الأفعال، وإنما هي قائمة بهم باكتسابهم لها.
والدليل على المخالفة من الكتاب قوله تعالى ( لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِ شَيْءٌ وَهُوَ السَّمِيعُ الْبَصِيرُ )، ومن السنة ما ورد عن أبي بن كعب "أن المشركين قالوا لرسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم انسب لنا ريك فأنزل الله ( قُلْ هُوَ اللهُ أَحَدٌ اللهُ الصَّمَدُ ) فالصمد الذي لم يلد ولم يولد لأنه ليس شيء يولد إلا سيموت ولا شيء يموت إلا سيورث وإن الله عز وجل لا يموت ولا يورث ( وَلَمْ يَكُن لَّهُ كُفُوًا أَحَدٌ ) قال لم يكن له شبيه ولا عدل وليس كمثله شيء. وقد أجمعت الأمة على أن الله تعالى مخالف للحوادث. وأما برهان العقل فسيأتي.
The third: His exalted transcendence of originated things. The reality of transcendence of originated things is the negation of likeness in essence, attributes, and actions. Thus His essence is not of the kind of lights nor darknesses nor accidents nor bodies; rather, whatever occurs to your mind, God is contrary to that. He has no direction, no place, and time does not pass over Him, and His attributes are eternal and permanent, and He is transcendent in His actions because He is the Creator, and the servants have no effect in any of the actions, but rather they are established by them through their acquisition. The evidence for transcendence from the Book is His statement (There is nothing like unto Him, and He is the Hearing, the Seeing) (42:11), and from the Sunnah what is related from Ubayy ibn Ka'b: 'The polytheists said to the Messenger of God, peace be upon him, "Describe your Lord for us." Then God revealed "Say: He is God, the One. God, the Eternal. He begets not, nor is He begotten, because nothing that is begotten will not die, and nothing that dies will not be inherited, and God, mighty and majestic, does not die and is not inherited. And there is none comparable to Him." He said: He has no likeness nor equal, and there is nothing like Him.' And the ummah has unanimously agreed that God, exalted, is transcendent of originated things. As for the rational proof, it will come later.

Commentary
This passage defines the third attribute, which is God's transcendence (mukhalafatu l-hawadith). It is the negation of any resemblance to created things in essence, attributes, and actions. The text emphasizes that God's essence is not like lights, darknesses, accidents, or bodies; whatever you imagine, God is different. He has no direction, place, or temporal extension. His attributes are eternal. He is transcendent in actions: He is the sole Creator, while servants have no real effect but only acquire their actions. Evidence is given from the Quran (42:11) and from a hadith where Ubayy ibn Ka'b reported that when the polytheists asked for a description of God, the chapter al-Ikhlas was revealed, negating generation, death, and likeness. The passage concludes with the consensus of the ummah on God's incomparability and notes the rational proof will follow.

Allah's Dissimilarity to Created Things
Sanusi defines Allah's dissimilarity and gives scriptural proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the meaning and scope of Allah's dissimilarity (mukhālafa) to created things, including its scriptural proofs from the Qur'an and Sunnah.

Core Idea
Allah is unlike anything—in essence, attributes, and actions.

Sanusi now turns to the third divine attribute: that Allah is utterly dissimilar to everything He created. This is a foundational principle of Islamic theology, guarding against anthropomorphism and limiting God to finite categories. The passage defines this attribute, clarifies that it applies to essence, attributes, and actions, and provides proof from revelation, with a promise of rational demonstration later.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage carefully, then the literal translation. Notice how Sanusi begins with a definition before elaborating.
Pay attention to the three areas where resemblance is negated: essence, attributes, actions.
2
Understand the definition
Focus on the sentence: 'وحقيقة المخالفة للحوادث عبارة عن نفي المشابهة في الذات والصفات والأفعال.' This is the core definition.
The word 'حقيقة' signals that a technical definition is being given.
3
Explore the implications
Sanusi explains that Allah's essence is not composed of any created material (light, darkness, etc.) and that He is free from direction, place, time, and change. His attributes are eternal, and His actions are unique—He alone creates; humans only acquire.
This refutes common misconceptions like God being a 'light' or having a body.
4
Review the scriptural proofs
Identify the two proofs: Qur'an 42:11 and the hadith of Ubayy ibn Ka'b explaining Surah al-Ikhlas. Understand why these verses prove dissimilarity.
The consensus of the ummah is also cited as a proof.
5
Self-test with the checkpoint
Answer the multiple-choice question to ensure you grasped the scope of the attribute.
The answer is directly stated in the passage.

Third: His Exaltedness's dissimilarity to created things. The reality of dissimilarity to created things is the negation of resemblance in essence, attributes, and actions (3). Allah's Self-Subsistence: Independence from Locus and Specifier
Sanusi defines Allah's self-subsistence and refutes incarnationism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the meaning of Allah's self-subsistence as the negation of need for a locus and a specifier, and why doctrines of incarnation and union are rejected.

Core Idea
Allah is self-sufficient: He needs no place to dwell and no cause to bring Him into being.

Sanusi now turns to the fourth divine attribute: self-subsistence (القيام بالنفس). This attribute establishes Allah's absolute independence from any created thing. The passage defines it negatively (what it excludes) and then refutes false doctrines that compromise Allah's transcendence. Understanding this attribute is essential for distinguishing Islamic monotheism from incarnationalist beliefs.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic once without translation, focusing on the key terms القيام بالنفس, المحل, المخصص.
Notice the poetic citation from Ida'at al-Dujna.
2
Understand the definition
The passage defines self-subsistence as the negation of need for a locus and a specifier. A locus is a place or subject; a specifier is something that determines existence.
This is a negative attribute: it tells us what Allah does not need.
3
Refutation of incarnation
The passage explicitly rejects the Christian and Batinite claims of indwelling (حلول) and union (اتحاد). These contradict self-subsistence.
4
Scriptural and communal proofs
The passage cites Quran 35:15 and a hadith to prove Allah's self-sufficiency, and notes the consensus of the community.
The rational proof is deferred to a later passage.

Fourth: His Exaltedness's self-subsistence. The reality of self-subsistence is the negation of need for a locus and a specifier. Refuting Ibn Hazm on God's Ability to Take a Son
Sanusi and Ibn al-Arabi refute Ibn Hazm.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the linguistic and rational arguments that refute the claim that God can take a son, as presented by Sanusi and Ibn al-Arabi.

Core Idea
The question of God taking a son is rationally meaningless.

Ibn Hazm controversially claimed that God is able to take a son, misinterpreting a Qur'anic verse. Sanusi and Ibn al-Arabi respond by showing that the verse means 'choosing' not literal fatherhood, and that the very question is logically incoherent. This lesson examines their refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation. Pay attention to how Ibn Hazm's claim is presented and then refuted.
The passage includes both a textual and a rational argument.
2
Identify the two kinds of refutation
Sanusi first critiques Ibn Hazm's interpretation of the verse, then Ibn al-Arabi provides a rational argument. Notice the difference between linguistic and logical reasoning.
3
Reflect on the concept of impossibility in God
Consider why the claim that God can take a son is impossible not because of lack of power, but because the concept is self-contradictory.
This is a key principle in kalam: impossibility is not an object of power.

Incapacity with respect to possibles: It is impossible for Allah to be incapable of any possible thing, and to bring into existence something in the world together with His aversion to its existence, i.e., without His will for it, Exalted is He, or with heedlessness or negligence, or by causation (ta'lil) or by nature (tab'). (1) Aversion here is opposite of will, as we mentioned, not opposite of love, as is clear. Thus it necessarily follows that He does not bring into existence anything except by His will, and it is not said that He does not bring into existence anything except by His love. So ponder, for some of the corporealists interpreted it otherwise and attributed to the Ash'aris that they say that Allah, since He brought kufr into existence and does not bring anything into existence with His aversion to its existence, therefore He brought it into existence with His love. So they said that the Ash'aris say that Allah loves kufr absolutely. This is false, as you see, because aversion here is opposite of will, not opposite of love. So be careful. (2) In the speech of Ahl al-Sunna, the expression 'causation' (ta'lil) occurs, but they do not intend by it the causation that the philosophers speak of; rather, they intend by it the correlation between two things by intellect, law, or custom. So do not be deceived by the outward expressions. Ibn Taymiyya exploited the occurrence of the word 'causation' in the speech of some Ahl al-Sunna in their legal discussions, like when they say 'the cause (illa) for prohibiting wine is intoxication' and so on, and claimed that they contradict themselves and invalidate their principles. His speech is false because Ahl al-Sunna have clarified that when they use the word 'cause (illa)' in fiqh and usul al-fiqh, they mean the sign (al-mu'arraf) as is known in usul al-fiqh, contrary to the cause in the speech of the Mu'tazila, for they mean the effecter (al-mu'aththir). Ibn Taymiyya usually resorts to such a method in his arguments against the Ash'ari masters, for he interprets their statements falsely and then builds his rejection on his interpretation of their speech, and attributes to them what they never said. So ponder and do not be deceived. The same applies to all those who were deceived by his speech, among them al-Mu'allimi, al-Maqbali, al-Shawkani and others, as is known to those familiar. For these followed Ibn Taymiyya, repeating what he said without investigation, while they pretend ijtihad – but where are they from it? (3) The difference between causation (ta'lil) and nature (tab') according to those who affirm them, even though they both share the absence of choice, is that bringing into existence by way of causation does not depend on the existence of a condition or the absence of an obstacle, for it is inconceivable that the hand moves and an obstacle prevents the ring in it from moving, contrary to bringing into existence by way of nature, like fire burning, for it depends on a condition (contact) and the absence of an obstacle (wetness). The agent by causation and the agent by nature according to those who affirm them only produce action without omission, while the agent by choice is the one from whom both action and omission proceed, and He is our Master, Exalted and High, and none other.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof. Commentary
This passage is a refutation of anthropomorphic interpretations of the hadith 'God is not one-eyed.' The author begins by quoting the hadith from al-Tabarani. The hadith thus serves as an argument against them by accommodating their false belief. In sum, the hadith is about negating defect, not affirming bodily parts.

Proof of God's Independence from Locus and Specifier
Sanusi proves God's independence from locus and specifier using rational arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proofs that God does not need a locus (substrate) or a specifier (determining cause), and how these proofs rely on avoiding infinite regress and the necessity of God's pre-eternity.

Core Idea
God needs neither a place to dwell nor a cause to exist; both would imply limitation and origination.

In this passage, Sanusi presents two concise but powerful proofs for God's absolute independence. The first proof argues that God cannot be an attribute inhering in a substrate, because that would lead to infinite regress. The second proof shows that if God needed a specifier to bring Him into existence, He would be originated, contradicting His proven pre-eternity. Together, these proofs establish God's transcendence above any need for a locus or a specifier.

Commentary

The author of the Sharh al-Sanūsiyyah begins by deriving a key attribute of God from His necessary self-sufficiency: that He is transcendent of all purposes. Purposes are the plural of purpose (gharad), which are the motives for observing benefits and repelling harms. God's acts are like bringing into existence, annihilating, honoring, impoverishing, giving life, and giving death, and His rulings are the five religious rulings. If it were possible that He not be free from purposes in His acts and rulings, then His need would be necessary for that which fulfills His purpose, because we observe that everyone who has a purpose in something needs that which fulfills his purpose. Commentary
This passage is a proof from the concept of divine self-subsistence (qiyam bi al-nafs) to establish God's independence and transcendence. The author explains that self-subsistence has two parts: independence from a specifier (mukhassis) and independence from a locus (mahall). He argues that if God lacked these attributes, He would be in need of an originator or a locus, which is impossible given His necessary self-sufficiency (ghina). He then adds a third implication: independence from needing someone to repel imperfections, which means God must be free from all defects such as the absence of hearing, sight, and speech. From this necessary independence, the author derives that God is also free from purposes (aghrad) in His actions and rulings. Purposes are defined as motives for benefit or repelling harm. If God had purposes, He would be in need of something to fulfill that purpose, just as any creature with a purpose needs a means. Since God is absolutely self-sufficient, such need is impossible. Thus, God's acts and rulings are not driven by any benefit or harm to Himself or others; He is transcendent above all need.

Proof for God's Unlikeness to Originated Things
First proof: God's unlikeness to originated things.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof that God must be unlike all originated things.

Core Idea
If God resembled anything created, He would be created — which is impossible.

Sanusi begins the proofs of God's necessary attributes with the argument that God cannot resemble any created thing. This is because resemblance would imply being created, contradicting His proven eternity. The argument is simple but foundational.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then its translation. Notice how Sanusi states the conditional argument (if...then).
The structure is: proof for the necessity of unlikeness, then the conditional, then the impossibility.
2
Identify the conditional reasoning
Sanusi argues: if God resembled any originated thing, He would be originated like it. That is impossible because of His proven eternity.
This is a simple modus tollens: if P→Q, not Q, therefore not P.
3
Connect to prior knowledge
Recall that God's eternity and permanence have been established earlier. This proof uses that as a premise.
The proof is concise; its strength relies on accepting God's eternity.

Proof for God's Self-Subsistence
Second proof: God's self-subsistence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the proof that God is self-subsistent, not needing a substrate or specifier.

Core Idea
God cannot be an attribute because attributes lack the perfections God must have.

The second proof establishes that God is self-subsistent (qiyāmuhu bi-nafsih). Sanusi argues that if God needed a substrate (maḥall), He would be an attribute, but attributes cannot possess the essential attributes (ṣifāt al-maʿānī) and relational attributes (ṣifāt al-maʿnawiyya) that God must possess. Additionally, needing a specifier would make Him originated, contradicting His eternity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice the two parts: the argument about needing a substrate and the argument about needing a specifier.
The passage uses 'if...then' reasoning for both parts.
2
Understand the first conditional
If God needed a substrate, He would be an attribute. But an attribute cannot be described with essential or relational attributes. Since God must be described with these, He cannot be an attribute.
Essential attributes are power, knowledge, etc. Relational attributes are being powerful, knowing, etc.
3
Understand the second conditional
If God needed a specifier (mukhassiṣ), He would be originated. But His eternity is already proven, so He does not need a specifier.
4
Synthesize
Together, these arguments show that God is self-subsistent: He does not inhere in anything nor needs a cause for His existence.

The third proof concerns God's oneness (waḥdāniyya). Sanusi presents the well-known argument: if there were more than one God, they would disagree or compete, leading to the inability of all, and consequently nothing in the world would exist. Since the world does exist, God must be one.

As for the proof of the necessity of His being unlike originated things, exalted, for if He resembled any of them, He would be originated like them, and that is impossible because of what you already know of the necessity of His eternity and permanence. As for the proof of the necessity of His self-subsistence, exalted, for if He needed a substrate, He would be an attribute, and an attribute is not described with essential attributes (ṣifāt al-maʿānī) nor with relational attributes (ṣifāt al-maʿnawiyya), and our Lord, majestic and mighty, must be described with both, so He is not an attribute. And if He needed a specifier, He would be originated, although the proof has already established the necessity of His eternity and permanence. As for the proof of the necessity of oneness for Him, exalted, because if He were not one, it would necessarily follow that nothing of the world would exist, due to His inability then.

Commentary
This passage provides three proofs corresponding to three necessary attributes: being unlike originated things (mukhālafatuhu lil-ḥawādith), self-subsistence (qiyāmuhu bi-nafsih), and oneness (waḥdāniyya). The first proof: if God resembled any originated thing, He would be originated like it, but His eternity and permanence are already proven, so He must be unlike them. The second proof: self-subsistence. If God needed a substrate (maḥall), He would be an attribute (ṣifa). But attributes themselves cannot possess the essential attributes (ṣifāt al-maʿānī like power, knowledge) nor the relational attributes (ṣifāt al-maʿnawiyya like being powerful, knowing). Since God must possess these, He cannot be an attribute. Also, if He needed a specifier (mukhassiṣ), He would be originated, contradicting His proven eternity. Therefore, He is self-subsistent. The third proof: oneness. If God were not one (i.e., if there were multiple gods), it would lead to conflict and inability, preventing the existence of anything in the world. This is a classic argument from the impossibility of multiple omnipotent beings.
