The Virtues of Dhikr and the Condition of Sincerity
Exhortation to frequent dhikr with sincerity and the spiritual benefits.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the benefits of frequent recitation of the word of tawhid and the essential condition of sincerity and servitude for attaining those benefits.

Core Idea
Sincere dhikr with understanding transforms the soul and brings spiritual fruits.

The author encourages the intelligent person to frequently recite the word of tawhid, but not mechanically. He emphasizes that the true benefits—both inner virtues like trust in Allah and outer wonders—are only achieved when the dhikr is done with pure intention and a deep consciousness of its meanings. This lesson unpacks those benefits and the crucial condition of sincerity.

Guided Path
1
Read the exhortation to frequent dhikr
Start by reading the passage that urges the intelligent person to increase recitation of the word of tawhid. Notice the emphasis on frequency being commendable.
The word 'tawhid' means affirming Allah's oneness.
2
Identify the inner virtues mentioned
Look for the specific inward religious virtues that arise from this dhikr: asceticism (zuhd), trust (tawakkul) defined as the heart's confidence in Allah, and modesty out of reverence for Allah.
These are inner states of the heart, not mere outward actions.
3
Understand the condition of sincerity
The author states that all these benefits are only obtained if the dhikr is performed with the intention of servitude (ubudiyya) and sincerity (ikhlas). If one seeks miracles or other worldly goals, the worship is nullified.
Sincerity means doing the action solely for Allah, not for any other motive.
4
Reflect on the consequences of insincerity
Ponder the strong warning: insincere worship becomes 'scattered dust' and the person does not attain what they intended. This highlights the gravity of intention.

Commentary
This passage contains two parts. The first is an exhortation to frequently recite the word of tawhid while being mindful of its meanings and the articles of faith it encapsulates. The author emphasizes that the fruits of dhikr, including inward virtues such as tawakkul and modesty, as well as manifest karamat, are only obtained through sincerity and the intention of servitude. Seeking miracles or worldly aims invalidates the worship. The second part is a footnote by the editor Sa'id Fuda, quoting al-Hadadi's commentary on Umm al-Barahin, where two definitions of tawfiq are given: (1) it is the creation of obedience itself, and (2) the creation of the ability for obedience in the servant. Al-Sharqavi then critiques these definitions, favoring the first as it aligns with Imam al-Haramayn and avoids the problem that the disbeliever also has ability but lacks tawfiq. He argues that 'tawfiq' refers to the coincident accident of obedience, which is absent in the disbeliever. The footnote clarifies the technical kalam concept of divine granting of success.

The Definition of Tawfiq in Kalam
Footnote defining tawfiq and scholarly debate on its correct meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the technical debate about the definition of tawfiq (divine success) and why the definition as 'creation of obedience' is preferred over 'creation of ability'.

Core Idea
Tawfiq is the creation of obedience itself, not just the ability to obey.

In this footnote, the editor discusses two definitions of the theological term 'tawfiq' (divine granting of success). The first defines it as the creation of obedience, the second as the creation of the ability for obedience. A noted scholar argues the first is more correct because it explains why the disbeliever, despite having ability, lacks tawfiq. Let's examine the arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the two definitions of tawfiq
Find the opening of the footnote where al-Hadadi quotes two definitions: 'al-tawfiq is the creation of obedience' and 'the creation of the ability for obedience in the servant.'
Tawfiq means Allah making success possible for the servant.
2
Understand al-Sharqawi's critique
Al-Sharqawi favors the first definition because it aligns with Imam al-Haramayn and the etymological root of agreement (wifaq). He argues that actual obedience directly produces agreement with divine commands, unlike mere ability.
He says the disbeliever has ability but is not 'muwaffaq' (given success).
3
Analyze the response to the objection
An objector might say that ability exists in the disbeliever too. The response clarifies that 'ability' here means the coincident accident that accompanies the act of obedience, which is absent in the disbeliever because he does not actually obey.
This distinguishes between generic ability and the specific ability that occurs with obedience.
