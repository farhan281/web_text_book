Overview of the Shaykh's Major Writings
Catalogs the shaykh's writings with descriptions and praises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the breadth of the shaykh's scholarly output and the central place of the minor creed (Umm al-Barahin) in his theological works.

Core Idea
The shaykh's writings span theology, hadith, and logic, with Umm al-Barahin as his most influential creed.

This passage provides a catalog of the shaykh's major works as recorded by al-Mallali and supplemented by the editor. It shows the range of his contributions, from early commentaries to the famous creed Umm al-Barahin. As you read, notice how the minor creed is praised as unparalleled, and how it connects to the present recension.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the translation. Get a sense of the list of works and the flow of praise.
Notice that the passage begins with a heading 'His books and works.'
2

Find the three creedal works: major (Aqidat Ahl al-Tawhid), middle, and minor (al-Sughra). Note that the minor creed is also called Umm al-Barahin.
The editor's footnote clarifies that the minor creed is Umm al-Barahin, the subject of the present book.
3
Observe the praise for the minor creed
Read the editor's strong praise: 'there is nothing like it as far as I know; it suffices the one who confines himself to it over all other creeds.' Reflect on why this work is so highly regarded.
This praise connects to the importance of the text we are studying.
4
Explore other works
The passage lists many other writings: commentaries on hadith, logic, and devotional formulas. Skim these to appreciate the shaykh's breadth.
Notice the mention of a commentary on Isaghuji and the precision of al-Khunaji's text.

Commentary
This passage is a bibliographic catalog of the works of a shaykh (likely Muhammad al-Sanusi), as narrated by his student al-Mallali and supplemented by the editor (Said Foudah). It begins with the heading 'His books and works.' The first work mentioned is his great commentary on al-Hawfiyyah, composed at age nineteen, so impressive that his teacher advised him to hide it until forty to avoid envy. Then it lists his trilogy of creeds: the major creed 'Aqidat Ahl al-Tawhid', the middle creed, and the minor creed (al-Sughra)—the latter being the famous 'Umm al-Barahin'. The editor praises this minor creed as unparalleled, stating it suffices for all others. He notes commentaries by Shaykh 'Ulaysh and glosses by Yasin, and later by al-Dasuqi. Then it mentions an abridged creed smaller than the minor one, with its commentary, and other works: commentary on the Beautiful Names, on the tasbih after prayers, on the creed of al-Hawdi, on al-Jaziriyyah, an abridgment of Sahih Muslim, a commentary on Sahih Muslim titled 'Mukmil Ikmal al-Ikmal', and a commentary on Isaghuji in logic. The editor adds footnotes identifying the minor creed as Umm al-Barahin, the subject of the present book's recension, and clarifies that 'al-Jaziriyyah' is likely a work on creeds. The catalog shows the breadth of the shaykh's output: theology, hadith, logic, and devotional formulas.

The Heavens Groaning: Literal or Metaphorical?
Commentator explains the word aṭṭat and two interpretations of the hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the meaning of the word 'aṭṭat' in the hadith and the two interpretations of the heavens groaning, including why the literal interpretation is preferred.

Core Idea
The heavens' groaning is best understood literally, as Allah can create perception in them.

In this short textual note, the commentator clarifies a word from a hadith about the heavens groaning under the weight of angels. He presents two possible interpretations: a literal sound or a metaphorical expression. As we read, pay attention to which interpretation he favors and why, as this reflects a broader principle in Islamic theology regarding the attributes of created things.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Note how the commentator first explains the word 'aṭṭat' and then presents two views.
The word 'aṭṭat' is a verb meaning 'it groaned' or 'it made a sound'.
2
Identify the two interpretations
Find the two interpretations: one literal (the heavens actually groaned) and one metaphorical (it is just a figure of speech).
The second interpretation uses the phrase 'ضرب مثل' (a metaphorical expression).
3
Understand the preference
Notice that the commentator says 'الأول أولى' (the first is more appropriate) and explains why.
The reason is that Allah is able to create perception and sound in inanimate objects.
4
Reflect on the principle
Reflect on the theological principle: nothing is impossible for Allah, including giving speech or sound to the heavens.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a previous statement in the text regarding the hadith of the heavens groaning. The commentator first explains the linguistic meaning of the word 'aṭṭat', stating it means to make a sound and moan. He then presents two interpretations: one literal (the heavens actually groaned due to the crowding of angels) and one metaphorical (it is a figure of speech indicating the abundance of angels). He then mentions that some investigators prefer the literal interpretation because it is possible for Allah to create perception and sound in inanimate objects.
