The Prophet's Asceticism and Its Implications
Sanusi declares the Prophet’s asceticism and forbids calling him poor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the Prophet's lack of worldly wealth was voluntary asceticism, not poverty, and why calling him poor is a grave error.

Core Idea
The Prophet’s poverty was chosen; calling him poor is a capital offense.

In this note, Sanusi clarifies a point of immense respect for the Prophet (peace be upon him) and all prophets. He emphasizes that their detachment from worldly goods was a deliberate choice, not a sign of poverty. This distinction is so critical that Sanusi states that calling them poor warrants capital punishment. Let us examine the passage to grasp the reasoning and the evidence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage attentively
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the strong opening assertion and the severe ruling that follows.
Pay attention to the phrase 'فلا يقال إنه فقير' – the prohibition is absolute.
2
Identify the key claim and evidence
Sanusi first states that the Prophet was ascetic, giving the example of mountains offering gold. Then he issues the ruling. Two hadiths are provided as evidence for the low value of the world.
The ruling is justified by the fact that the Prophet’s lack of wealth was due to asceticism, not need.
3
Understand the severity of the ruling
The passage declares that whoever calls the Prophet poor should be killed. This shows the extreme importance of maintaining proper respect.
This ruling applies to all prophets, as mentioned.
4
Reflect on the hadiths
The two hadiths demonstrate that the world is insignificant to Allah. This underscores why prophets would choose to abandon it.
The second hadith includes the addition 'and count yourself among the people of the graves.'

Commentary
This passage is a note (mulahaza) on the asceticism of the Prophet and all prophets. It begins by stating that the Prophet abandoned the world willingly as an ascetic, and so did all prophets. An example is given: the mountains offered to turn into gold for him, but he refused. The note then issues a severe warning: one should not call the Prophet (or any prophet) 'poor' (faqir) or 'indigent' (miskin). Whoever does so is to be killed. This is because their lack of worldly possessions is not due to poverty but to voluntary renunciation. The passage then cites two hadith to support the lowly value of the world: the first hadith states that if the world were worth a mosquito's wing to Allah, He would not give even a drink of water to a disbeliever. The second hadith advises believers to live as strangers or wayfarers in the world, and to count themselves among the dead. The author also provides the hadith sources and their authenticity grades.

Sanusi explains the low status of the world and the prophets' asceticism as evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the prophets' worldly hardships demonstrate the insignificance of this world and why calling the Prophet poor is forbidden.

Core Idea
The world's low worth is proven by the prophets' suffering.

Sanusi offers a profound reminder: the suffering of the prophets in this world is not a sign of their low rank but of the world's low worth. Their voluntary renunciation further elevates their status. This lesson explores that argument and its implications.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Believing in Muhammad means believing in all prophets—honoring them is obligatory, belittling them is apostasy.

The second part of the shahada is not just about Muhammad; it extends to all prophets. This passage from Sanusi's text explains the full implications, from affirming their existence and rank to the severe punishment for disrespect. Let's explore what this declaration truly requires.

Belief in Known and Unknown Prophets
Obligates detailed belief in known prophets and general belief in others

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between detailed (tafsili) and general (ijmali) belief in prophets.

Core Idea
We must know the known prophets and trust the unknown ones.

The obligation to believe in prophets is not limited to those named in revelation. This lesson explains that we must believe in detail in the prophets whose names are known from the Qur'an and Sunnah, and in general in all prophets whom Allah has sent, whether we know their names or not.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation together. Notice the two categories: detailed belief (tafsilan) and general belief (ijmalan).
The passage lists several prophets by name as examples.
2
Understand 'detailed belief'
We must know and believe in each prophet whose name has reached us through authentic revelation.
These include the 25 prophets mentioned in the Quran and others known from hadith.
3
Understand 'general belief'
For prophets not mentioned, we affirm in general that they are true prophets sent by Allah.
This covers all prophets in Allah's knowledge, even if unknown to us.
4
Reflect on the examples
Notice the prophets listed: Muhammad, Ibrahim, Musa, Isa, Ayyub, Ya'qub, Yusuf. These are well-known figures.
The phrase 'and others' indicates there are more known prophets beyond these.

Commentary
This passage classifies the attributes of prophets into necessary (wājib), impossible (mustaḥīl), and permissible (jā’iz). The necessary attributes are truthfulness (ṣidq), trustworthiness (amāna), and conveying (tablīgh) what they are commanded. The impossible are their opposites: lying, treachery (doing forbidden or disliked acts, or concealing divine commands). The permissible are human accidents (aʿrāḍ bashariyya) like sickness that do not lower their exalted rank. The proof for truthfulness: if prophets lied, God's confirmation through miracles would imply false information from God. The proof for trustworthiness: if they committed forbidden/disliked acts, then those acts would become obedience (since we are commanded to follow them), but God does not command such acts. This same proof applies to conveying (the third necessary attribute). The proof for permissible human accidents: we observe these from prophets, and they occur for various wise purposes: to increase rewards, provide legal rulings, comfort from worldly attachment, or to highlight the lowliness of this world. Finally, all these creedal beliefs are summarized in the declaration of faith: 'La ilaha illa Allah, Muhammad Rasul Allah.'

Prophetic Attributes: Necessary, Impossible, Permissible
Sanusi defines the prophetic attributes: wajib (truthfulness, trustworthiness, delivery), mustahil (their opposites), ja'iz (human accidents).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three rational categories of prophetic attributes (necessary, impossible, permissible) and the logical proofs that establish each category.

Core Idea
Prophets must be truthful, trustworthy, and deliver the message; their opposites are impossible; human accidents are permissible.

Sanusi now turns from the attributes of Allah to those of His messengers. He classifies prophetic attributes into three rational categories—necessary, impossible, and permissible—each supported by a clear proof. This passage establishes the theological foundation for prophethood, showing why we can trust the prophets and how their human experiences do not diminish their lofty rank.

Commentary
This passage classifies prophetic attributes into three categories: necessary (wajib), impossible (mustahil), and permissible (ja'iz). It first lists the necessary attributes: truthfulness (sidq), trustworthiness (amana), and delivery (tabligh) of the message. Their opposites—lying, treachery, and concealment—are impossible. Permissible for prophets are human accidents like illness that do not detract from their lofty rank. 

The proof for truthfulness is that God confirms prophets by miracles, which function as divine testimony 'My servant has spoken truthfully.' If they lied, God would be confirming a lie, which is impossible. The proof for trustworthiness is that God commands us to follow them. If they committed forbidden or disliked acts, then those acts would become acts of obedience by virtue of following them, but God does not command such acts. The same reasoning applies to delivery (the third attribute). Finally, the permissibility of human accidents is proven by observed occurrences in prophets' lives, which serve various purposes like reward, legislation, or teaching the lowliness of worldly life.
