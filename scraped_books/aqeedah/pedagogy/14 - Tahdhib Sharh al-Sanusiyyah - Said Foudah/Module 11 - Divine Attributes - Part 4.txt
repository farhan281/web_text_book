Iblis Is Not from the Angels
The author refutes the claim that Iblis was an angel using Quranic evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Iblis's disobedience does not contradict angelic infallibility and how the Quran clarifies his nature as jinn, not angel.

Core Idea
Iblis was jinn, not angel — angelic infallibility remains intact.

Some argue that because Iblis disobeyed Allah, angels are capable of sin, implying Iblis was an angel. The author refutes this by citing a Quranic verse that explicitly identifies Iblis as from the jinn, a separate species. This lesson examines that argument and the evidence that preserves the doctrine of angelic infallibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Notice the author's tone as he declares the opposing view 'very weak, rather false.'
The verse quoted is from Surah al-Kahf (18:50).
2
Identify the claim and the counter-evidence
Find the claim that angels can sin based on Iblis, and then the Quranic verse the author uses to refute it. Understand why the verse is decisive.
The verse explicitly says 'he was of the jinn,' not angels.
3
Reflect on the implications
Consider why it matters that Iblis was jinn. How does this protect the doctrine of angelic infallibility? Also note the author's dismissal of the weak counter-argument that some angels could be called jinn.
The author emphasizes that there is no evidence for that claim.

The Obligation and the First Six Attributes
Sanusi states the obligation and lists the first six necessary attributes with classification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the legal obligation to know God's attributes and the first six necessary attributes, classified as nafsiyya and salbiyya.

Core Idea
Knowledge of God's attributes begins with six that establish His existence and transcendence.

Sanusi begins by making it a legal obligation for every responsible person to learn what is necessary, impossible, and possible regarding God. He then lists the first six of the twenty necessary attributes, explaining the first as essential (nafsiyya) and the next five as privative (salbiyya). This classification shows how we affirm God's existence and remove imperfections from Him.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note the obligation
Read the Arabic and translation, noticing that Sanusi starts with the duty to know the necessary, impossible, and possible for God and messengers.
This sets the importance of the knowledge.
2
Identify the six attributes
List the six attributes: Existence, Pre-eternity, Permanence, Being unlike creation, Self-subsistence, Oneness.
3
Understand the classification
Learn that the first, Existence, is essential (nafsiyya), and the other five are privative (salbiyya) – they negate imperfections.
Privative attributes clarify what God is not.

It is legally obligatory upon every legally responsible person to know what is necessary in respect of our Lord, glory and majesty, and what is impossible and what is possible. Likewise it is obligatory upon him to know the like of that in respect of the messengers, peace and blessings be upon them. What is necessary for our Lord, glory and majesty, are twenty attributes: Existence, Pre-eternity, Permanence, His being unlike originated things, His being self‑subsistent – i.e., He does not need a substratum nor a specifier –, and Oneness – i.e., He has no partner in His essence, nor in His attributes, nor in His actions. The first is essential (nafsiyyah) and it is Existence, and the five after it are privative (salbiyyah). Then for Him, majestic, are necessary seven attributes called attributes of meanings (ṣifāt al‑maʿānī): Power and Will – both relate to all possibles –, Knowledge which relates to all necessaries, possibles and impossibles, Life which does not relate to anything, Hearing and Sight which relate to all existents, and Speech which is not a letter nor a sound and relates to whatever Knowledge relates to of things. Then seven attributes called moral attributes (ṣifāt maʿnawiyyah), which are concomitant with the first seven: that He is Powerful, Willing, Knowing, Living, Hearing, Seeing, and Speaking.

Commentary
This passage first states the obligation on every accountable person (mukallaf) to know what is necessary, impossible, and possible regarding God and the prophets. It then enumerates the twenty attributes necessary for God. The first attribute, Existence, is essential (nafsiyyah). The next five (pre‑eternity, permanence, otherness, self‑subsistence, oneness) are privative, meaning they negate imperfections. Then seven attributes of meanings (ṣifāt al‑maʿānī) are listed: Power, Will, Knowledge, Life, Hearing, Sight, Speech – each with its respective objects (e.g., Power and Will relate to all possibles, Knowledge relates to all necessaries, possibles and impossibles, etc.). Finally, seven moral attributes (ṣifāt maʿnawiyyah) are derived from the attributes of meanings: being Powerful, Willing, etc. These are called 'moral' because they describe the state of the essence as qualified by those attributes.

The Seven Attributes of Meanings
Sanusi enumerates the seven attributes of meanings and specifies their objects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the seven attributes of meanings (ṣifāt al‑maʿānī) and how each relates to its specific objects.

Core Idea
God's attributes of meanings are real qualities that exist eternally and relate to specific categories of things.

After explaining the first six attributes, Sanusi moves to the seven attributes of meanings. These are real, eternal attributes that subsist in God's essence and each has a specific scope of relation. This lesson details them: Power and Will relate to all possibles, Knowledge to all categories, Life to nothing, Hearing and Sight to all existents, and Speech to all that Knowledge knows.

Guided Path
1
Read the list of seven attributes
Identify each attribute: Power, Will, Knowledge, Life, Hearing, Sight, Speech.
2
Note the objects of each
Notice which categories each attribute relates to. For example, Power and Will relate to all possible things.
3
Understand the uniqueness of each
Some attributes have universal relation, some specific. Life has no relation.

The Seven Moral Attributes
Sanusi enumerates the seven moral attributes as concomitant with the attributes of meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the seven moral attributes (ṣifāt maʿnawiyya) and their relationship to the attributes of meanings.

Core Idea
Moral attributes are the necessary consequences of God being qualified by the attributes of meanings.

Sanusi completes the twenty attributes with the seven moral attributes. These are not additional real qualities but rather statements that God is characterized by the attributes of meanings. They are called 'moral' because they describe God's state as being Powerful, Willing, etc., necessarily following from the existence of the attributes of meanings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the seven moral attributes
They are: being Powerful, Willing, Knowing, Living, Hearing, Seeing, Speaking.
2
Understand their nature
They are not separate attributes but descriptions necessarily consequent upon the attributes of meanings.
3
See the classification
They are the fourth category, following nafsiyya, salbiyya, and ma'ani.

Proof of God's Pre‑eternity
Sanusi proves that God must be pre‑eternal by ruling out temporal origination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's proof that God must be pre‑eternal, and why the alternative (temporal origination) leads to absurdities.

Core Idea
If God had a beginning, He would need a creator — leading to an impossible chain.

Sanusi now turns to proving two attributes that follow directly from God's necessary existence. The first is pre‑eternity (qidam): that God has no beginning. He shows that if God were temporal, the very logic of causation would unravel into circularity or infinite regress — both impossible. This lesson explores that proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how Sanusi structures the argument as a conditional chain: if not pre‑eternal, then temporal, then needing a creator, then leading to an absurdity.
The passage is concise; each step follows logically.
2
Identify the two impossible outcomes
Sanusi says that if God were temporal, it would lead to either circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul). Understand what each means: circularity is where the creator is itself, and infinite regress is an endless chain of creators.
Both are impossible in Islamic theology.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
Since the only alternative to pre‑eternity leads to absurdity, God must be pre‑eternal. Ask yourself: why is infinite regress impossible? This connects to the earlier proof of God's existence.

But the existence of a possible thing is always temporal (i.e., it comes into existence after non‑existence). Since God is proven necessary, He cannot be possible, hence He cannot cease to exist. The passage also notes that the necessity of pre‑eternity and permanence was already established in the earlier enumeration of necessary attributes.

'Mumkin' refers to contingent things that can exist or not exist. The proof presupposes that the objects of divine action (created things) are possible in themselves.
The possibility is intrinsic to the thing, not to God's action.
3
Analyze the reductio
Assume a possible thing is obligatory for God: then it would be necessary, contradicting its possibility. Assume it is impossible: then it would be impossible for God to do, also contradicting its possibility. Therefore, divine actions regarding possible things are neither obligatory nor impossible—they are permissible (jā'iz).
This argument safeguards divine freedom.

Commentary
This passage presents proofs for necessary attributes and the permissibility of divine acts. First, the proof for the essential attributes of power (qudra), will (irāda), knowledge (ʿilm), and life (ḥayāh): the existence of originated things (ḥawādith) requires these attributes. For instance, power is needed to create, will to choose, knowledge to direct, and life as a condition for these attributes. If any were absent, nothing could exist. Second, for the attributes of hearing (samʿ), sight (baṣar), and speech (kalām), the proof is from revelation: the Qur’an, Sunnah, and consensus establish them. Additionally, if God lacked these, He would possess their opposites, which are deficiencies, and deficiency is impossible for God. Third, the proof that God's actions (doing or leaving any possible thing) are permissible (jā’iz) rather than obligatory or impossible: if any possible act were mandatory or impossible for God, then the possible would be transformed into necessary or impossible, which is absurd. Hence, God is free to act or not act regarding any contingent matter.

Proof for Hearing, Sight, and Speech
Sanusi provides scriptural and rational proofs for three divine attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the divine attributes of hearing, sight, and speech are proven by both revelation (Qur'an, Sunnah, consensus) and reason (denial entails deficiency).

Core Idea
Denying God's hearing, sight, or speech is rationally impossible because it implies deficiency.

After establishing that God possesses necessary attributes, Sanusi addresses three specific attributes: hearing, sight, and speech. The proof combines scriptural authority with a rational argument: if God lacked these, He would have their opposites, which are deficiencies, and deficiency is impossible for God.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
Read the Arabic text and its translation carefully. Notice the two parts: first the scriptural evidence, then the rational argument.
The passage is dense but contains a complete argument.
2
Identify the two proofs
The first proof is from the Book, Sunnah, and consensus. The second is a rational proof based on the impossibility of deficiency. Notice how Sanusi connects them with 'and also' (wa aydan).
3
Understand the rational argument
The rational proof is a conditional: if God lacked hearing, sight, and speech, He would have their opposites (deafness, blindness, muteness), which are deficiencies. Since deficiency is impossible for God, the attributes must exist.
This uses the principle that attributes and their opposites are mutually exclusive; absence of one entails presence of the other.

Commentary
This passage addresses the proof for the divine attributes of hearing (sam'), sight (basar), and speech (kalam).
