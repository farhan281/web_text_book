Necessary Attributes from Divine Self-Sufficiency
Sanusi deduces six essential attributes and then adds hearing, sight, and speech as necessary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how God's self-sufficiency necessitates the attributes of existence, pre-eternity, everlastingness, dissimilarity to creation, self-subsistence, transcendence, and the inclusion of hearing, sight, and speech.

Core Idea
God's self-sufficiency logically entails the essential attributes of perfection.

Having established that God is self-sufficient, Sanusi now draws out the implications. Self-sufficiency is not a passive concept; it actively necessitates that God must possess certain attributes, without which He would not be truly self-sufficient. This lesson traces that logical deduction.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage listing the necessary attributes
Read the Arabic and translation, noting the six attributes explicitly mentioned.
Attributes: wujud, qidam, baqa', mukhalafah, qiyam bi-nafsih, tanzuh.
2
Understand why each attribute is necessitated
For each attribute, ask: 'What would God lack if He did not have this attribute?' and see how it connects to self-sufficiency.
E.g., if He were not eternal, He would need a cause – contradicting self-sufficiency.
3
Grasp the inclusion of hearing, sight, and speech
Sanusi adds these three. Understand the argument: without them, God would need an originator, a locus, or a remover of deficiencies.
This shows that self-sufficiency requires possession of perfect attributes.

And all the meanings of these beliefs are gathered by the statement: 'There is no god but Allah, Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah.' For the meaning of divinity (al-uluhiyya) is: the self-sufficiency of the deity from everything other than Him, and the dependence of everything other than Him upon Him. So the meaning of 'there is no god but Allah' is: there is no one self-sufficient from everything other than Him, and to Him everything other than Him is dependent, except Allah, exalted is He. As for His self-sufficiency, may He be glorified and exalted, from everything other than Him, it necessitates for Him, exalted is He: existence, pre-eternity, everlastingness, dissimilarity to temporal events, self-subsistence, and transcendence above deficiencies. And included in that is the necessity of hearing, sight, and speech for Him, exalted is He; for if these attributes were not necessary for Him, He would be in need of the originator, or of a locus, or of one who would repel deficiencies from Him. And from it is derived His, exalted is He, transcendence from accidents in His actions and His judgments; otherwise, His need for what realizes His accident would follow, how is it that He, may He be glorified and exalted, is the Self-Sufficient from everything other than Him? And from it is also derived that there is no obligation upon Him, exalted is He, to do or leave any of the possible things; for if any of them were obligatory upon Him, exalted is He, as an act like reward, for example, He would be in need of that thing to complete His purpose, since nothing is obligatory with respect to Him, exalted is He, except what is perfection for Him. How is it that He, may He be glorified and exalted, is the Self-Sufficient from everything other than Him?

Commentary
This passage explains the comprehensive meaning of the declaration of faith, 'La ilaha illa Allah, Muhammad Rasul Allah.' Sanusi defines divinity (uluhiyya) as two aspects: God's self-sufficiency (istighna') from everything other than Him, and the absolute dependence (iftiqar) of everything else upon Him. The Kalima thus means: no one is self-sufficient except Allah, and everything else depends on Him.

The passage then focuses on the implications of God's self-sufficiency. It necessitates for Him: existence (wujud), pre-eternity (qidam), everlastingness (baqa'), dissimilarity to created things (mukhalafat lil-hawadith), self-subsistence (qiyam bi-nafsihi), and transcendence above deficiencies (tanzuh 'an al-naqa'is). Included in this are the attributes of hearing, sight, and speech, because if God lacked them, He would need something else to originate them, a substrate to inhere in, or someone to remove deficiencies. Additionally, God's self-sufficiency implies that He is free from accidents in His actions and judgments. Finally, it entails that God is not obligated to do or refrain from any possible action, because such obligation would imply need and imperfection.

Implications for Divine Actions: Transcendence and No Obligation
Sanusi derives two further implications: God's actions are free from accidents, and no act is obligatory upon Him.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that God's self-sufficiency implies His transcendence from accidents and that He is not obligated to perform any possible action.

Core Idea
God's absolute self-sufficiency means He acts freely and is not compelled by any external purpose.

The logic of self-sufficiency extends beyond attributes to God's actions. If God were subject to accidents (temporal states) in His actions, He would need something to produce them. Similarly, if He were obligated to do or leave any possible thing, He would depend on that thing for completeness. Sanusi closes his argument by affirming God's utter independence in all respects.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on transcendence from accidents
Read the Arabic and translation for the part about God's actions and judgments being free from accidents.
Understand 'a'rad' as accidental, contingent attributes.
2
Understand the reasoning for transcendence
If God's actions had accidents, He would need something to cause those accidents, contradicting self-sufficiency.
3
Read the passage on no obligation
Read the second part about no obligation upon God to do or leave possible things.
The example of reward is key.
4
Reflect on the example of reward
Sanusi uses reward as an example: if reward were obligatory, God would need to reward to achieve a purpose, implying need.
Thus, obligation would compromise self-sufficiency.

God's Life and Universal Attributes
Sanusi deduces life and universal attributes from dependence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the dependence of all things on God requires Him to possess life and universal power, will, and knowledge.

Core Idea
Without life, power, will, and knowledge, nothing could depend on God.

Sanusi has established that everything other than God depends on Him. Now he draws the first set of implications: God must be living and possess attributes that are universal in scope. Without these, the very dependence we observe would be impossible. Let's see how he argues this.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the first part of the source where Sanusi states that dependence necessitates life and universal attributes.
Notice the logical structure: if any attribute were missing, no temporal event could exist.
2
Understand the necessity of life
Why must God be living? Consider that only a living being can act with power, will, and knowledge. Dependence implies He is the source of all temporal events, so He must be alive.
3
Contemplate the universality of the attributes
The attributes (power, will, knowledge) must be universal—they encompass everything, because every temporal event depends on Him. If any event were outside His power, it would not depend on Him.
This is a direct corollary of absolute dependence.

Commentary
This passage completes the explanation of the Kalima by drawing out implications from the second aspect: the absolute dependence (iftiqar) of all things upon God. From this, Sanusi deduces that God must possess life (hayat) and universal power (qudra), will (irada), and knowledge ('ilm). Finally, it negates any real causal efficacy in creatures. If something had an effect by its nature, the effect would be independent of God. The passage concludes that the Kalima encompasses all these doctrines.

The Necessity of God's Oneness
Sanusi derives oneness from dependence using incapability argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the dependence of all things on God implies that He is one, with no partner in divinity.

Core Idea
If there were multiple gods, none would be worthy of dependence.

Having established the necessity of life and universal attributes, Sanusi now draws another implication: God must be one. The logic is simple but profound: if there were two gods, each would be limited by the other, making both incapable and thus nothing would depend on them. Let's explore this argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage on oneness
Read the second part of the source where Sanusi argues that dependence necessitates oneness.
Notice the conditional: 'if there were a second in divinity'.
2
Understand the logic of incapability
Why would two gods become incapable? Because each would be limited by the other's will and power, leading to conflict or inability to act independently. Thus no creature could depend on either.
3
Connect to the Kalima
Realize that this argument reinforces the meaning of 'there is no god but Allah'—the only true God is the one upon whom everything depends.

The World's Temporal Origination
Sanusi deduces the world's temporality from dependence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the dependence of all things on God implies that the entire world is temporally originated (not eternal).

Core Idea
Anything eternal would be self-sufficient, contradicting universal dependence.

Sanusi now draws a further implication: the world cannot be eternal. If any part of the world were pre-eternal, it would be independent of God—contradicting the principle that everything other than God depends on Him. This is a key argument in Islamic theology for the world's temporality.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage on huduth
Read the third part where Sanusi derives the temporal origination of the entire world from dependence.
Notice the conditional: 'if anything from it were pre-eternal'.
2
Understand the concept of qadim
In this context, 'pre-eternal' (qadim) means without beginning. An eternal being is self-sufficient by definition.
3
Apply the rule: no self-sufficiency except God
Since everything other than God depends on Him, nothing else can be eternal. Thus the whole world is temporal (hādith).
This does not mean the world came from nothing; it means it is not eternal.

Commentary

Since the meaning of divinity (al-ulūhiyya) is: the self-sufficiency of the god from everything other than Him, and the need of everything other than Him for Him. So the meaning of 'La ilaha illa Allah' is: there is no one self-sufficient from everything other than Him, and everything other than Him is in need of Him, except Allah the Exalted. As for His self-sufficiency, may He be glorified and exalted, from everything other than Him, it necessitates for Him, the Exalted: existence (al-wujūd), pre-eternity (al-qidam), everlastingness (al-baqā’), dissimilarity to originated things (al-mukhālafa li-l-ḥawādith), self-subsistence (al-qiyām bi-l-nafs), and transcendence above imperfections (al-tanazzuh ‘an al-naqā’iṣ). And included in that is the necessity of hearing (al-sam‘) for Him, the Exalted, and sight (al-baṣar) and speech (al-kalām), for if these attributes were not necessary for Him, He would be in need of an originator (al-muḥdith) or a locus (al-maḥall), or someone to repel imperfections from Him. And from this is derived His transcendence, the Exalted, from accidental attributes (al-a‘rāḍ) in His actions and judgments, otherwise His need for that which would produce an accident for Him would follow, and how can that be when He, may He be glorified and exalted, is the Self-Sufficient from everything other than Him. And from this also is derived that it is not obligatory upon Him, the Exalted, to do any of the possible things (al-mumkināt) or to refrain from them, for if something of them were obligatory upon Him, the Exalted, an act like reward (al-thawāb) for example, He, may He be glorified and exalted, would be in need of that thing to complete His purpose, since nothing is obligatory in His right, the Exalted, except that which is perfection (kamāl) for Him, and how can that be when He, may He be glorified and exalted, is the Self-Sufficient from everything other than Him. And as for the need of everything other than Him for Him, may He be glorified and exalted, it necessitates for Him, the Exalted: life (al-ḥayāh), and the universality of power (al-qudra), will (al-irāda), and knowledge (al-‘ilm), for if any of them were absent, it would be impossible for any of the originated things (al-ḥawādith) to exist, so nothing would be in need of Him, and how can that be when He is the One whom everything other than Him is in need of? And it also necessitates for Him, the Exalted, oneness (al-waḥdāniyya), for if there were with Him a second (thānin) in divinity, nothing would be in need of Him due to the consequent impotence (al-‘ajz) of both at that time, and how can that be when He is the One whom everything other than Him is in need of? And from this is also derived the temporal origination of the entire world (ḥudūth al-‘ālam bi-asrihi), for if any part of it were eternal (qadīm), that thing would be self-sufficient from Him, the Exalted, and how can that be when He is the One of whom everything other than Him must be in need of? And from this is also derived that there is no effect (ta’thīr) for any of the contingent beings (al-kā’ināt) in any effect (athar) whatsoever, for otherwise that effect would be independent of our Master, may He be glorified and exalted, and how can that be when He is the One whom everything other than Him is in need of universally? And in any case, this is if you suppose that something from among the contingent beings is effective by its nature (ṭab‘). But if you suppose it to be effective by a power (quwwa) that Allah placed in it, as many of the ignorant (al-jahala) claim, then that is also impossible, because at that point our Master, may He be glorified and exalted, would become in need, in the creation of some actions, of an intermediary (wāsiṭa), and that is false because you have come to know the necessity of the self-sufficiency of our Master, may He be glorified and exalted, from everything other than Him. Commentary
This passage is an extended definition and proof. The author begins by defining the meaning of divinity (al-ulūhiyya) as two inseparable principles: God's absolute self-sufficiency from all others, and the total dependence of all others on Him. He then unpacks this definition to derive a series of necessary divine attributes. First, from God's self-sufficiency he derives six essential attributes: existence, pre-eternity, everlastingness, dissimilarity to originated things, self-subsistence, and transcendence above imperfections. He insists that these necessarily include the attributes of hearing, sight, and speech, because their absence would imply need. He also deduces that no action can be obligatory upon God—even rewarding the obedient—because obligation would imply a need for that action to achieve a purpose. Second, from the world's need for God he derives the attributes of life, universal power, will, and knowledge, arguing that if any were absent, nothing contingent could exist. He also derives God's oneness, the world's createdness (if any part were eternal it would be independent), and the denial of true causality in creatures—whether by nature or by a power granted by God, as that would make God need a medium. The passage is structured as a series of deductions from the core definition, each ending with the rhetorical question 'how can that be when He is the Self-Sufficient?' showing the contradiction of any alternative. It concludes by asserting that the entire theological system is contained in the phrase 'La ilaha illa Allah'.

Denial of Secondary Causality in Creation
Sanusi denies all forms of secondary causality and concludes the entire argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that according to the author, nothing in creation—neither by its nature nor by a power placed in it—can truly cause any effect; all genuine causality belongs to Allah alone, because any independent causality would compromise His self-sufficiency.

Core Idea
No creature truly causes anything; only Allah is the real agent.

The author now takes the principle of universal dependence to its logical extreme: if any creature (like fire or a human) could truly create an effect, that effect would be independent of God in that aspect, contradicting God's absolute self-sufficiency. He also refutes the idea that God grants creatures a power to cause effects, because that would make God need an intermediary.

Guided Path
1
Understand the first form of causality
The author considers the claim that a creature could cause an effect by its own nature (ṭab‘). He argues that this would make the effect independent of God.
This is the denial of natural causality.
2
Understand the second form
Next, he considers a more subtle claim: that God gives a creature a power (quwwa) to cause effects. He argues that this would make God need that creature as a medium, contradicting self-sufficiency.
Even if the power is granted, the creature would become a necessary intermediary.
3
Appreciate the conclusion
The passage ends by stating that all these implications have been derived from the meaning of 'La ilaha illa Allah'.
This shows how the testimony encompasses even the denial of secondary causality.

حَالٍ، هَذَا إِنْ قَدَّرْتَ أَنَّ شَيْئًا مِنَ الْكَائِنَاتِ يُؤَثِّرُ بِطَبْعِهِ، وَأَمَّا إِنْ قَدَّرْتَهُ مُؤَثِّرًا بِقُوَّةٍ جَعَلَهَا اللَّهُ فِيهِ كَمَا يَزْعُمُهُ كَثِيرٌ مِنَ الْجَهَلَةِ فَذَلِكَ مُحَالٌ أَيْضًا لِأَنَّهُ يَصِيرُ حِينَئِذٍ مَوْلَانَا جَلَّ وَعَزَّ مُفْتَقِرًا فِي إِيجَادِ بَعْضِ الْأَفْعَالِ إِلَى وَاسِطَةٍ، وَذَلِكَ بَاطِلٌ لِمَا عَرَفْتَ مِنْ وُجُوبِ اسْتِغْنَاءِ جَلَّ وَعَزَّ عَنْ كُلِّ مَا سِوَاهُ. فَقَدْ بَانَ لَكَ تَضَمُّنُ قَوْلِ: لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللَّهُ.
Since the meaning of divinity (al-ulūhiyya) is: the self-sufficiency of the god from everything other than Him, and the need of everything other than Him for Him. So the meaning of 'La ilaha illa Allah' is: there is no one self-sufficient from everything other than Him, and everything other than Him is in need of Him, except Allah the Exalted. As for His self-sufficiency, may He be glorified and exalted, from everything other than Him, it necessitates for Him, the Exalted: existence (al-wujūd), pre-eternity (al-qidam), everlastingness (al-baqā’), dissimilarity to originated things (al-mukhālafa li-l-ḥawādith), self-subsistence (al-qiyām bi-l-nafs), and transcendence above imperfections (al-tanazzuh ‘an al-naqā’iṣ). And included in that is the necessity of hearing (al-sam‘) for Him, the Exalted, and sight (al-baṣar) and speech (al-kalām), for if these attributes were not necessary for Him, He would be in need of an originator (al-muḥdith) or a locus (al-maḥall), or someone to repel imperfections from Him. And from this is derived His transcendence, the Exalted, from accidental attributes (al-a‘rāḍ) in His actions and judgments, otherwise His need for that which would produce an accident for Him would follow, and how can that be when He, may He be glorified and exalted, is the Self-Sufficient from everything other than Him. And from this also is derived that it is not obligatory upon Him, the Exalted, to do any of the possible things (al-mumkināt) or to refrain from them, for if something of them were obligatory upon Him, the Exalted, an act like reward (al-thawāb) for example, He, may He be glorified and exalted, would be in need of that thing to complete His purpose, since nothing is obligatory in His right, the Exalted, except that which is perfection (kamāl) for Him, and how can that be when He, may He be glorified and exalted, is the Self-Sufficient from everything other than Him. And as for the need of everything other than Him for Him, may He be glorified and exalted, it necessitates for Him, the Exalted: life (al-ḥayāh), and the universality of power (al-qudra), will (al-irāda), and knowledge (al-‘ilm), for if any of them were absent, it would be impossible for any of the originated things (al-ḥawādith) to exist, so nothing would be in need of Him, and how can that be when He is the One whom everything other than Him is in need of? And it also necessitates for Him, the Exalted, oneness (al-waḥdāniyya), for if there were with Him a second (thānin) in divinity, nothing would be in need of Him due to the consequent impotence (al-‘ajz) of both at that time, and how can that be when He is the One whom everything other than Him is in need of? And from this is also derived the temporal origination of the entire world (ḥudūth al-‘ālam bi-asrihi), for if any part of it were eternal (qadīm), that thing would be self-sufficient from Him, the Exalted, and how can that be when He is the One of whom everything other than Him must be in need of? And from this is also derived that there is no effect (ta’thīr) for any of the contingent beings (al-kā’ināt) in any effect (athar) whatsoever, for otherwise that effect would be independent of our Master, may He be glorified and exalted, and how can that be when He is the One whom everything other than Him is in need of universally? And in any case, this is if you suppose that something from among the contingent beings is effective by its nature (ṭab‘). But if you suppose it to be effective by a power (quwwa) that Allah placed in it, as many of the ignorant (al-jahala) claim, then that is also impossible, because at that point our Master, may He be glorified and exalted, would become in need, in the creation of some actions, of an intermediary (wāsiṭa), and that is false because you have come to know the necessity of the self-sufficiency of our Master, may He be glorified and exalted, from everything other than Him. So it has become clear to you that the statement 'La ilaha illa Allah' contains...

Commentary
This passage is an extended definition and proof. The author begins by defining the meaning of divinity (al-ulūhiyya) as two inseparable principles: God's absolute self-sufficiency from all others, and the total dependence of all others on Him. He then unpacks this definition to derive a series of necessary divine attributes. First, from God's self-sufficiency he derives six essential attributes: existence, pre-eternity, everlastingness, dissimilarity to originated things, self-subsistence, and transcendence above imperfections. He insists that these necessarily include the attributes of hearing, sight, and speech, because their absence would imply need. He also deduces that no action can be obligatory upon God—even rewarding the obedient—because obligation would imply a need for that action to achieve a purpose. Second, from the world's need for God he derives the attributes of life, universal power, will, and knowledge, arguing that if any were absent, nothing contingent could exist. He also derives God's oneness, the world's createdness (if any part were eternal it would be independent), and the denial of true causality in creatures—whether by nature or by a power granted by God, as that would make God need a medium. The passage is structured as a series of deductions from the core definition, each ending with the rhetorical question 'how can that be when He is the Self-Sufficient?' showing the contradiction of any alternative. It concludes by asserting that the entire theological system is contained in the phrase 'La ilaha illa Allah'.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Many people, including some Muslims, believe that creatures have real causal powers (e.g., fire burns by its nature).
Correction: The author argues that any real causality in creation would infringe on God's absolute self-sufficiency and independence.

Checkpoint
According to the text, why is even causality by a God-given power impossible?
A) Because that power would be beyond God's control.
B) Because it would make God need the creature as an intermediary, contradicting self-sufficiency.
C) Because creatures cannot receive power from God.
D) Because only God can have power.

Key Takeaway
No creature has any real causal power; all effects are directly from Allah, as required by His self-sufficiency.
