Definition of Shar‘ and Its Scriptural Evidence
Defines shar‘ linguistically and terminologically, and provides scriptural evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the linguistic and terminological definition of shar‘ (divine law) and the scriptural evidence from the Quran and Sunnah for the obligation to know Allah and His attributes.

Core Idea
Shar‘ is the path from Allah; knowing it is obligatory by Quran and Sunnah.

This lesson establishes the foundational definition of shar‘ (divine law) as brought by the Prophet. Understanding this term is crucial because it sets the scope of what we are obligated to learn and act upon. The passage then provides direct scriptural evidence from the Quran and a key hadith demonstrating that the shahada is the boundary of faith and that actions like zakat are inseparable from it.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of shar‘
Start by reading the linguistic and terminological definitions of shar‘. Notice that the linguistic meaning is 'the path' and the terminological meaning refers to what the Prophet brought from Allah, including both principles (uṣūl) and branches (furū‘).
The term 'shar‘' is central to Islamic theology and law.
2
Examine the Quranic evidence
Read the verse quoted: 'So know that there is no god except Allah' (Muhammad 19). This verse is presented as evidence for the obligation to know Allah and His attributes.
The word 'know' (i‘lam) implies that seeking knowledge is an obligation.
3
Analyze the hadith narrative
Read the long hadith about Abu Bakr and Umar. Focus on why Abu Bakr insisted on fighting those who withheld zakat. Note that the hadith shows the shahada protects life and property but only when accompanied by its rights, such as prayer and zakat.
This hadith is narrated by multiple authorities, showing its authenticity.
4
Reflect on the implication for faith and action
Consider how the hadith resolves the apparent conflict between the simple shahada and the necessity of actions. Abu Bakr’s stance clarifies that the shahada is not merely a verbal utterance but includes commitment to all obligations.
This lesson guards against the misconception that faith is only in the heart or tongue.

The Lam in 'Li Ya'budun' Is Lam al-Aqiba Not Lam al-Ta'lil
Sanusi distinguishes lam al-aqiba from lam al-ta'lil with proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the lam in the verse 'I created jinn and humans only to worship Me' is a lam of consequence, not a lam of causality, and the rational and transmitted proofs for this distinction.

Core Idea
Worship is the commanded outcome, not the cause of creation.

This lesson addresses a crucial Quranic and theological nuance: the lam in 'li ya'budun' is often misunderstood. Sanusi clarifies that it indicates the intended consequence of creation—obligation to worship—not that God's act of creation was motivated by a need for worship. He then provides rational and hadith-based evidence to prevent any implication of divine need.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic text and its translation, noting how Sanusi introduces the two types of lam and his reason for rejecting one.
Focus on the distinction between 'lam al-'āqiba' and 'lam al-ta‘līl'.
2
Understand the rational proof
Notice the rational argument: if worship were the cause, God would need creation, implying imperfection. Understand why this is impossible for God.
This is a key argument in Islamic theology for divine self-sufficiency.
3
Examine the narrative proof
Look at the hadith cited: human piety or disbelief does not affect God. That shows worship is not for God's benefit.
4
Grasp the revised meaning
Sanusi restates the verse meaning: creation is followed by the command to worship, not that worship is a goal that motivated creation.
The phrase 'except to command them to worship' is key.

Commentary
This passage provides an exegetical clarification regarding the Quranic verse 'And I did not create the jinn and humans except to worship Me' (ad-Dhariyat 56). The author argues that the 'lām' in 'liya‘budūn' is 'lām al-‘āqiba' (the lām of consequence) not 'lām al-ta‘līl' (the lām of causal explanation). This is a critical distinction: the verse does not mean that worship is the cause or motive for God's creation, because that would imply God needs their worship, which is impossible. Instead, the 'lām' indicates that worship is the intended outcome that follows creation, not a goal that motivated creation. The author supports this with rational and transmitted evidence. Rationally, if God needed worship to perfect Him, He would be imperfect and unworthy of divinity. From transmission, the Prophet stated that people's piety or disbelief does not affect God. The meaning of the verse becomes that God created them so that they would be commanded to worship, and worship benefits themselves, not God. The commentary then cites al-Qunawi, who explains that the lām is used metaphorically to show the ordering of worship after creation, likening it to a desired end even though it is not a true end. A similar metaphorical use is found in the story of Moses (peace be upon him) being picked up by Pharaoh's family 'to be for them an enemy and a sorrow'—the lām there shows the consequence, not the intention. The passage concludes by noting that in the worship verse, the worship is figuratively desired, while in the other, the consequence is actual but unintended; both are metaphorical uses of the lām.

Core Idea
The lam is a metaphor for intended consequence, not actual cause.

In this continuation, Sanusi cites al-Qunawi's commentary to explain that the lam in both the worship verse and the story of Moses is used metaphorically. The metaphorical usage elevates the understanding: worship is treated as if it were the end goal of creation, even though it is not a true motive. Commentary
This passage provides an exegetical clarification regarding the Quranic verse 'And I did not create the jinn and humans except to worship Me' (ad-Dhariyat 56). The author argues that the 'lām' in 'liya‘budūn' is 'lām al-‘āqiba' (the lām of consequence) not 'lām al-ta‘līl' (the lām of causal explanation). This is a critical distinction: the verse does not mean that worship is the cause or motive for God's creation, because that would imply God needs their worship, which is impossible. Instead, the 'lām' indicates that worship is the intended outcome that follows creation, not a goal that motivated creation. The author supports this with rational and transmitted evidence. Rationally, if God needed worship to perfect Him, He would be imperfect and unworthy of divinity. From transmission, the Prophet stated that people's piety or disbelief does not affect God. The meaning of the verse becomes that God created them so that they would be commanded to worship, and worship benefits themselves, not God. The commentary then cites al-Qunawi, who explains that the lām is used metaphorically to show the ordering of worship after creation, likening it to a desired end even though it is not a true end. A similar metaphorical use is found in the story of Moses (peace be upon him) being picked up by Pharaoh's family 'to be for them an enemy and a sorrow'—the lām there shows the consequence, not the intention. The passage concludes by noting that in the worship verse, the worship is figuratively desired, while in the other, the consequence is actual but unintended; both are metaphorical uses of the lām.

Proof of Creation from Universal Dependence
Sanusi deduces the world's creation from universal dependence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi deduces that the entire world is temporally created from the dependence of all else upon Allah.

Core Idea
If anything were eternal, it would be independent—but everything depends on Allah.

In this passage, Sanusi offers a powerful deductive argument for the temporal creation of the universe. He takes the already established premise that everything other than Allah depends on Him and shows that this dependence cannot coexist with eternity. Let's follow his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path

Commentary
This passage defines faith in angels and explains its obligations. It begins by stating that faith in angels is included in the testimony that Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah, because to affirm his prophethood necessarily entails affirming the messengers (angels) who brought revelation. The definition of faith (iman) in angels is 'tasdiq' (affirmation) of their existence and specific attributes: they are honored, they never disobey Allah, they are faithful executors of commands, they are intermediaries between Allah and creation acting by His permission, truthful in their reports, and their number is immense—only Allah knows it. The author then cites two proofs for their multitude: the Qur'anic verse 'And none knows the soldiers of your Lord except Him' and a hadith where the Prophet states that the sky groans under the weight of prostrating angels—every span of four fingers has an angel prostrating.

The next section addresses a potential objection: the story of Harut and Marut from Isra'iliyyat (Judeo-Christian sources). The author refutes any suggestion that these angels disobeyed, clarifying that the reports are unauthentic (no sahih hadith) and that what historians relate about them being punished is false. The correct understanding is that they taught magic not for practice but to warn against it and to distinguish it from miracles. This refutation leads to a broader objection: some claim angels can sin because they think Iblis was an angel. The author strongly rejects this, citing the Qur'anic text (al-Kahf 50) that Iblis was from the jinn, not angels. The jinn are a separate species; the claim that some angels are called jinn is baseless.

Then a brief note on the hadith: the word 'أطّت' means the sky made a sound of groaning due to the density of angels. Some interpret it metaphorically as an expression of multitude, but the preferred view is literal because Allah can create sound in the sky.

The passage then lists the angels that must be believed in in detail: those known from revelation—Jibril (revelation), Mika'il (provisions), Israfil (soul and Trumpet), the Angel of Death (taking souls), Malik (keeper of Hell), Ridwan (keeper of Paradise), Munkar and Nakir (questioners of the dead), Raqib and 'Atid (recorders of deeds). For others, general belief suffices.

Finally, it states that reviling angels incurs the same punishment as reviling prophets: execution.
