First Principle: Accidents Are Self-Evident
Sanusi asserts the first principle as self-evident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the existence of accidents (attributes) in bodies is considered self-evident in kalam.

Core Idea
Everyone intuitively knows they have attributes beyond their bodies.

The first principle of the huduth argument establishes that accidents—like motion, rest, and other qualities—are additional to bodies. Sanusi claims this is so obvious that no rational person doubts it. This lesson explores why he considers it necessary and how it forms the foundation for the rest of the argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation for the first principle. Notice the author calls it 'daruri' (necessary).
2
Reflect on self-evidence
Think about what makes something self-evident. Can you doubt that you have attributes? The author says no rational person can.
This is a common method in kalam: starting with undeniable premises.
3
Connect to the broader argument
Understand that this principle is the first step in proving the universe's contingency.

And the manner of deducing evidence for these seven principles in brief is that you say: As for the first, which is establishing something additional to bodies that they are qualified by, like motion, rest, and other accidents, it is necessary (daruri) and needs no proof, for there is no rational person who does not perceive that in his essence there are attributes additional to it. As for the second, which is negating the accident's subsistence by itself, and the third, which is negating its transference, their proof is that if the accident subsisted by itself or transferred, a reversal of the reality of the accident would necessarily follow. For motion, for example, its reality is the transference of a substance (jawhar) from one place to another; if it subsisted by itself or transferred itself, a reversal of this reality would necessarily follow—that is, motion would become a substance, and this is invalid. As for the fourth, which is negating concealment and appearance, because the claim of concealment and appearance leads to the combination of two opposites in one substrate. As for the fifth, which is establishing the impossibility of the eternal ceasing to exist, its reason is that if it ceased to exist, its existence would be contingent (ja'iz), and the contingent only has its existence as originated (muhdath); so this eternal would be originated, and this is a contradiction. As for the sixth, which is establishing that bodies are not free from that additional thing, it is necessary (daruri), because it is not conceivable that a body be free from being either in motion or at rest.

Second and Third Principles: Accident Cannot Subsist or Transfer
Sanusi gives a logical proof for the second and third principles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical proof that an accident cannot exist independently or move from one substance to another.

Core Idea
If an accident could stand alone, it would become a substance—a contradiction.

The second and third principles negate two possibilities: that an accident could subsist by itself (without a body) and that it could transfer from one body to another. Sanusi provides a single proof for both: doing so would reverse the definition of accident, turning motion into a substance. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation for the second and third principles. Note that the proof is based on the definition of motion.
2
Understand the definition
Motion is defined as the transference of a substance from one place to another. If motion subsisted by itself, it would be a substance, not an accident.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Apply the reasoning
Consider that if an accident could transfer, it would still require a substance to move from, but the text argues it would become a substance itself.
The key is that an accident's reality is to inhere in a substance.

Fourth Principle: Negating Concealment and Appearance
Sanusi refutes concealment and appearance by pointing to the impossibility of opposites coexisting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument against the theory that accidents can hide and reappear in substances.

Core Idea
Concealment and appearance would require opposites to coexist in one place.

The fourth principle refutes the idea that accidents can be hidden within bodies and later appear. Sanusi argues that this would lead to the impossible situation of two opposites (like motion and rest) existing in the same substance at the same time. This lesson explains the logical contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation for the fourth principle. Notice the brief but powerful argument.
2
Understand the theory
The view of kumun (concealment) says accidents can be latent within a body and then emerge.
This was a view of some natural philosophers.
3
Analyze the contradiction
If a body can contain both a hidden and an apparent accident opposite to each other, then two opposites would be in the same substrate.
This is impossible because one thing cannot have contradictory properties simultaneously.

Fifth Principle: The Eternal Cannot Cease to Exist
Sanusi proves the impossibility of the eternal's non-existence via reductio.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the proof that an eternal being cannot cease to exist.

Core Idea
If the eternal could cease, it would be contingent and originated, a contradiction.

The fifth principle establishes that the eternal (qadim) cannot become non-existent. Sanusi uses a reductio: if it could cease, its existence would be contingent, and all contingent existents are originated (muhdath). That would make the eternal originated, which is a contradiction. This lesson explores the logical steps.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice the conditional reasoning: if it could cease, then it is contingent.
2
Understand contingency
Contingent (ja'iz) means it is possible for it to exist or not exist. Originated (muhdath) means it began to exist after not existing.
The argument links contingency to originiation.
3
Follow the contradiction
If the eternal is contingent, it must be originated, but 'eternal' means without beginning, so contradiction.
The proof is a classic kalam argument.

Sixth Principle: Bodies Always Have Accidents
Sanusi presents the sixth principle as self-evident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why it is self-evident that bodies are never free from accidents like motion or rest.

Core Idea
It is inconceivable for a body to be without motion or rest.

The sixth principle states that bodies cannot be free from accidents (specifically motion and rest). Sanusi classifies this as self-evident: we cannot conceive of a body that is neither moving nor at rest. This lesson explains why this is considered necessary and its role in the argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation. Note the phrase 'لا يعقل' (not conceivable).
2
Reflect on conceivability
Try to imagine a body that is absolutely still and not moving, but also not at rest? Both are states.
In kalam, motion and rest are considered contradictory opposites covering all possibilities.
3
Understand its importance
This principle ensures that bodies always have accidents, which is necessary for the argument that bodies are temporally originated.
If a body could be without accidents, it might be eternal.

This lesson unpacks the basic definition agreed upon by all, and then shows how the Ash'ari and Maturidi schools, despite a seemingly different focus, can be united under one comprehensive formulation.

Guided Path

Read the passage on the basic belief
Start with the first sentence: belief in qadar means believing everything good or evil is by Allah's will, power, and prior knowledge. This is the foundation.
Notice the three elements: will, power, knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is a definition of belief in al-qadr (divine predestination) accompanied by a detailed scholarly discussion of the definitions of qadr and qada’. The commentator begins by stating that belief in qadr consists of believing that everything that occurs, whether good or evil, is solely by the will and power of Allah, in accordance with His prior knowledge. This is the basic definition. Then, in a lengthy footnote, he mentions the disagreement between the Ash'aris and Maturidis on the precise definition of al-qadr. The Ash'aris define it as an attribute of action (sifat fi‘l), meaning it is Allah's act of bringing things into existence according to His prior knowledge and will, and thus it is originated (hadith). The Maturidis define it as an eternal determination (ta‘yin azali) by which every creature is specified with its limits, so it is a relation of knowledge and will, and thus it is eternal (qadim). The commentator notes that some consider this difference purely verbal: if one focuses on the aspect of bringing into existence (ijad), qadr is originated; if one focuses on the eternal determinative knowledge and will, it is eternal. The Maturidis define qada’ as the act of creating with added perfection, thus originated; the Ash'aris define it as Allah's eternal will attached to things, thus eternal. Some say qada’ and qadr are identical. In reality, the two schools are reversed: what the Ash'aris call qada’, the Maturidis call qadr, and vice versa. The second footnote explains al-khafiyyat (subtle matters) as difficult affairs for creatures, but for Allah they are fully known in detail.

The Three Rational Judgments: Foundations of Theology
Sanusi defines the three rational judgments (necessary, impossible, possible) and states the obligation to know them regarding Allah and the messengers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three categories of rational judgment — necessary, impossible, and possible — and why knowing them is a legal obligation in Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Rational judgments are the intellectual framework for describing Allah: necessary, impossible, and possible.

Before diving into the divine attributes, Sanusi establishes the fundamental categories of rational judgment that govern all theological reasoning. These three categories — necessary, impossible, and possible — are the tools by which we determine what can and cannot be said about Allah and His messengers. This short but crucial passage lays the groundwork for everything that follows.

Guided Path
1
Read the definitions
Read the three definitions carefully. Notice how each is defined solely by what the intellect can conceive: necessity (non-existence inconceivable), impossibility (existence inconceivable), possibility (both existence and non-existence conceivable).
These definitions are purely rational, not based on revelation.
2
Understand the legal obligation
The passage states that it is legally obligatory (wajib) upon every mukallaf (responsible person) to know these categories for Allah and the messengers. Ask yourself: Why is this knowledge so important in Islam?
This shows that theological knowledge is a religious duty, not optional.
3
Connect to the broader picture
Realize that these three categories are the basis for all the attributes listed later in the text. The necessary applies to Allah's perfections, the impossible is what must be negated of Him, and the possible applies to contingent creation.
This framework prevents incorrect attributions to Allah.

Praise be to Allah, and blessings and peace be upon the Messenger of Allah. Know that the rational judgment (al-hukm al-'aqli) is limited to three divisions: necessity (al-wujub), impossibility (al-istihala), and possibility (al-jawaz). The necessary is that whose non-existence is inconceivable in the intellect; the impossible is that whose existence is inconceivable in the intellect; the possible is that whose existence and non-existence are both valid in the intellect. And it is obligatory upon every legally responsible person (mukallaf) according to the Law to know what is necessary with respect to our Lord, Mighty and Exalted, what is impossible, and what is possible. Likewise, it is obligatory upon him to know the like of that with respect to the messengers, peace be upon them. With respect to what is necessary for our Lord, Mighty and Exalted, there are twenty attributes: existence (al-wujud), pre-eternity (al-qidam), everlastingness (al-baqa'), being other than the originated things (mukhalafatuhu li-l-hawadith), self-subsistence (qiyamuhu bi-nafsihi): i.e., He does not depend on a substratum nor a specifier, and unicity (al-wahdaniyya): i.e., He has no partner in His essence, nor in His attributes, nor in His actions. So the attributes: the first are essential (nafsiyya), which is existence, and the five after it are negatory (salbiyya). Then for Him, exalted, there are seven attributes called attributes of meanings (sifat al-ma'ani), which are: power (al-qudra) and will (al-irada) — both pertaining to all possible things; knowledge (al-'ilm) pertaining to all necessary, possible, and impossible things; life (al-hayat) which does not pertain to anything; hearing (al-sam') and sight (al-basar) — both pertaining to all existent things; and speech (al-kalam) which is not composed of letters or sound and pertains to whatever knowledge pertains to. Then seven attributes called attributes of meaning-related (sifat ma'nawiyya), which are inseparable from the seven former attributes: His being powerful (kawnuhu qadiran), willing (muridan), knowing ('aliman), living (hayyan), hearing (sami'an), seeing (basiran), and speaking (mutakalliman). And from that which is impossible with respect to Him, exalted, there are twenty attributes which are the opposites of the first twenty: non-existence (al-'adam), origination (al-huduth), liability to non-existence (turu' al-'adam), and resemblance to originated things (al-mumathala li-l-hawadith) such that He would be a body, meaning that His exalted essence occupies a degree of space.

This sets the framework for the rest of the text on divine transcendence.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the twenty attributes necessary for Allah, their classification into four categories (essential, negatory, attributes of meaning, and meaning-related), and the corresponding impossible opposites.

Allah has twenty necessary attributes: one essential, five negatory, seven of meaning, and seven meaning-related — and their opposites are impossible.

After establishing the three rational judgments, Sanusi now applies them to Allah. He lists exactly twenty attributes that are rationally necessary for Allah to possess. These attributes are not random; they are carefully categorized into essential (nafsiyya), negatory (salbiyya), attributes of meaning (sifat al-ma'ani), and meaning-related (sifat ma'nawiyya). Understanding this classification is key to grasping the Islamic conception of God's nature.

Learn the impossible opposites

The Comprehensive Second Testimony
Sanusi explains the implications of the second testimony.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the second testimony (Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah) implicitly includes belief in all prophets, angels, books, and the Last Day, and what this implies about the characteristics of prophets.

Core Idea
The second testimony is a concise summary of all prophetic teachings.

Sanusi shows that testifying to Muhammad's messengership is not limited to one prophet; it entails acceptance of everything he confirmed, which includes all previous prophets, angels, scriptures, and the Hereafter. From this, he derives three essential characteristics of prophets: truthfulness, sinlessness, and the permissibility of human attributes.

بسم الله. الأقسام الثلاثةِ الَّتِي يَجِبُ عَلَى الْمُكَلِّفِ مَعْرِفَتُهَا فِي حَقَّ مَوْلَانَا جَلَّ وَعَزَّ، وَهِيَ مَا يَحِبُ فِي حَقِّهِ تَعَالَى، وَمَا يَسْتَحِيلُ، وَمَا يَجُوزُ. وَأَما قَوْلُنَا مُحَمَّدٌ رَسُولُ اللهِ صلى الله عليه وسلم فَيَدْخُلُ فِيهِ الإِيمَانُ بِسَائِرِ الأَنبِياءِ وَالْمَلائِكَةِ وَالْكُتُبِ السَّمَاوِيَّةِ وَالْيَوْمِ الآخِرِ، لأَنه عَلَيْهِ الصَّلَاةُ وَالسَّلامُ جَاءَ بِتَصْدِيقِ جَمِيعِ ذَلِكَ كُلِّهِ، وَيُؤْخَدُ مِنْهُ وُجُوبُ صِدْقِ الرُّسُلِ عَلَيْهِمُ الصَّلَاةُ وَالسَّلامُ وَاسْتِحَالَةُ الكَذِبِ عَلَيْهِمْ، وَإِلا لَمْ يَكُونُوا رُسُلاً أُمَنَاءَ لِمَوْلَانَا الْعَالِمِ بِالْخَفِيَّاتِ جَلَّ وَعَزَّ وَاسْتِحَالَة فِعْلِ الْمَنْهِيَّاتِ كُلَّهَا لأَنَّهُم أُرْسِلُوا لِيُعَلِّمُوا النَّاسَ بِأَقْوَالِهِمْ، وَأَفْعَالِهِمْ وَسُكُونِهِمْ، فيلزم أن لا يَكُونَ فِي جَمِيعِهَا مُخَالَفَةٌ لأَمْرِ مَوْلانَا جَلَّ وَعَزَّ الَّذِي اخْتَارَهُمْ عَلَى جَمِيع خَلْقِهِ وَأَمَنَهُمْ عَلَى سِرٌ وَحْيِهِ، وَيُؤْخَدُ مِنْهُ جَوَارُ الْأَعْرَاضِ الْبَشَرِيَّةِ عَلَيْهِمْ، إِذْ دَاكَ لا يَقدَح فِي رِسَالَتِهِمْ، وَعُلُو مَنْزِلَتِهِمْ عِندَ الله تَعَالَى بَلْ دَاكَ مِمَّا يَزِيدُ فِيهَا ، فَقَدْ بَانَ لَكَ نَضَمَّن كُلِمَتَي الشَّهَادَةِ مَعَ قِلَةِ حُرُوفهَا لِجَمِيعِ مَا يَجِبُ عَلَى الْمُكَلِّفِ مَعْرِفَتُهُ مِنْ عَقَائِدِ الإِيمَانِ فِي حَمْهِ تَعَالَى وَفِي حَقٌّ رُسُلِهِ عَلَيْهِمُ الصَّلاةُ وَالسَّلامُ وَلَعَلَهَا لاخْتِصَارِهَا مَعَ اشتمالها على ما ذكَرنَاهُ جَعَلَهَا الشَّرْعُ تَرْجَمَة عَلَى مَا فِي الْقَلْبِ مِنَ الْإِسْلامِ، وَلَمْ يَقْبَلُ مِنْ أحد الإيمان إلا بِهَا فَعَلَى العَاقِلِ أَنْ يُكْثَرَ مِن ذِكْرِهَا مُسْتَحْضِيرا لِمَا احْتَوَتْ عَلَيْهَ مِنْ عَقَائِدِ الإِيمَانِ حَتَّى يَمتزج مَعَ مَعْنَاهَا بِلَحْمِهِ وَدَمِهِ فَإِنَّهُ يَرَى لَهَا مِنَ الأَسْرَارِ وَالْعَجَائِبِ إِنْ شَاءَ الله تَعَالَى مَا لاَ يَدْخُلُ تَحْتَ حَصْر، وَبِاللَّهِ التَّوْفِيقُ لا رَبَّ غَيْرُهُ، وَلا تَعْبُودَ سِوَاهُ، نَسْأَلُهُ سُبْحَانَهُ وتَعَالَى أَنْ يَجْعَلَنَا وَأَحِبَتَنَا عِنْدَ الْمَوْتِ نَاطِقِينَ بِكَلِمَةِ الشَّهَادَةِ عَالِمِينَ بِهَا، وَصَلَّى اللَّهُ عَلَى سَيّدِنَا مُحَمَّدٍ ، كُلَّمَا دَكَرَهُ الدَّاكِرُونَ، وَغَفِلَ عَنْ ذِكْرِهِ الْغَافِلُونَ، وَرَضِيَ اللَّهُ تَعَالَى عَنْ أَصْحَابِ رَسُولِ اللهِ أَجْمَعِينَ وَالتَّابِعِينَ لَهُم بِإِحْسَانٍ إِلَى يَوْمِ الدِّينِ، وَسَلَام عَلَى الْمُرْسَلِينَ، وَالْحَمْدُ اللهِ رَبِّ الْعَالَمِينَ.
In the name of Allah. The three divisions that it is obligatory for the morally responsible person to know concerning our Lord, Mighty and Majestic: that which is necessary in His right, Exalted is He, that which is impossible, and that which is possible. As for our statement 'Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah, may Allah bless him and grant him peace,' it includes belief in all the [other] prophets, angels, heavenly books, and the Last Day, because he, peace and blessings be upon him, came confirming all of that entirely. From it is derived the obligation of the truthfulness of the messengers, peace and blessings be upon them, and the impossibility of lying upon them; otherwise they would not be trustworthy messengers for our Lord, the Knower of hidden things, Mighty and Majestic. And [from it is derived] the impossibility of their committing all prohibited acts, because they were sent to teach people by their words, actions, and states of rest, so it follows that there should be no opposition in all of them to the command of our Lord, Mighty and Majestic, who chose them over all His creation and entrusted them with the secret of His revelation. From it is derived the permissibility of human accidents (al-aʿrāḍ al-bashariyya) occurring to them, for that does not detract from their messengership nor from the loftiness of their rank with Allah, Exalted is He; rather that increases them in it. It has become clear to you that the two words of testimony, despite the fewness of their letters, encompass all that is obligatory for the morally responsible person to know of the beliefs (ʿaqāʾid) of faith concerning Him, Exalted is He, and concerning the right of His messengers, peace and blessings be upon them. Perhaps because of their conciseness while including what we have mentioned, the Law made them a symbol (tarjama) for what is in the heart of Islam, and does not accept faith from anyone except with them. So it is upon the intelligent person to frequently mention them, keeping in mind what they contain of the beliefs of faith, until its meaning mixes with his flesh and blood, for he will see of their secrets and wonders, if Allah wills, that which cannot be enumerated. And Allah grants success; there is no lord besides Him, nor is any worshipped other than Him. We ask Him, glorified and exalted, to make us and our loved ones at death utterers of the testimony, knowledgeable of it. May Allah bless our master Muḥammad, whenever the rememberers remember him and the heedless neglect his remembrance. May Allah be pleased with all the companions of the Messenger of Allah and with those who follow them in righteousness until the Day of Judgment. Peace be upon the messengers, and praise be to Allah, Lord of the worlds.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of the content and implications of the two testimonies of Islam: 'There is no god but Allah' and 'Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah.' The author explains that the first testimony encompasses three categories of divine attributes that every morally responsible person must know: what is necessarily true of Allah (wājib), what is impossible for Him (mustaḥīl), and what is possible for Him (jāʾiz). The second testimony includes belief in all prophets, angels, scriptures, and the Last Day because the Prophet Muhammad confirmed them all. From this testimony, the author derives three theological conclusions: (1) the obligation of truthfulness for all messengers and the impossibility of lying for them, otherwise they would not be trustworthy; (2) the impossibility of their committing any prohibited acts, since they were sent to teach people by example; and (3) the permissibility of normal human experiences (such as eating, sleeping, etc.) for prophets, as these do not diminish their station but rather increase it. The author then marvels at how these two short phrases contain all essential beliefs, which is why Islamic law made them the verbal testament of faith. He exhorts the reader to recite them frequently while contemplating their meaning, so that faith becomes deeply ingrained, and concludes with a prayer for a faithful end and blessings upon the Prophet and his companions.
