No Effect for Created Things in Causality
Sanusi denies causal efficacy of created things and explains rulings on beliefs about causality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that no created thing has any effect by itself, and the different beliefs about causality and their rulings in Islam.

Core Idea
Only Allah creates effects; causes are mere occasions, not agents.

In this passage, the author builds on the previous discussion of divine attributes to affirm that Allah is the sole creator of all actions and effects. He explains that ordinary causes like fire or knife have no inherent power, and then clarifies the rulings on various mistaken beliefs about causality.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the main claim
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice that the author begins by asserting that nothing in existence has any effect, whether by nature or by a power deposited by Allah.
The phrase 'لا بطبعها ولا بقوة جعلها الله فيها' is key.
2
Understand the three positions on causality
The footnote lists three beliefs: (1) effect by nature leads to kufr, (2) effect by deposited power leads to innovation with disagreement on kufr, (3) belief in necessary correlation can lead to kufr by denying miracles.
Pay attention to the nuanced ruling for each position.
3
Reflect on the danger of rational correlation
The author warns that believing in an inevitable cause-effect link undermines miracles and resurrection. True belief accepts that Allah can break the usual pattern.
This is connected to the concept of 'خوارق العادات' (extraordinary events).

Involuntary and Voluntary Actions
Sanusi divides actions into involuntary and voluntary, defines them, and explains divine creation and obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the classification of actions into involuntary and voluntary, and how this relates to divine creation and human accountability.

Core Idea
Allah creates all actions, but humans are accountable only for voluntary ones.

After establishing that no created thing has causal influence, the author turns to human actions. He explains that all actions are created by Allah, but they are of two types: involuntary, where the person has no choice or ability, and voluntary, where they do. This clarifies how Allah's creation of actions does not contradict human responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on action classification
Read the Arabic text that divides actions into involuntary and voluntary. Note the example of shivering and the meaning of compulsion.
The definition of involuntary: created by Allah, no choice or power.
2
Understand the implication for obligation
The author states that Allah removes obligation from the compelled. This means that involuntary actions are not subject to divine command.
This is based on the principle that "الله لا يكلف نفسا إلا وسعها" (Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity).
3
Consider the role of will in involuntary actions
The footnote clarifies that in involuntary actions, the person's will has no effect; even if they try to stop, the action occurs.
This is contrasted with voluntary actions where will and power are present.

The Non-Effect of Created Causes and Grading Erroneous Beliefs
Establishes the Ash'ari doctrine on causality and grades different erroneous beliefs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari position on causality, including the denial of any effect from created causes and the rulings on different erroneous beliefs about causation.

Core Idea
Only Allah has true causal efficacy; created causes have no effect by nature or deposited power.

This passage lays out the foundational Ash'ari doctrine on causality. It begins by emphatically denying that any created thing has a genuine causal effect, whether by its own nature or by a power placed in it by God. Then it clarifies a subtle point about action attribution before grading three distinct erroneous beliefs about causation, each with its own ruling. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for avoiding theological error and for properly appreciating the possibility of miracles.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage carefully along with the literal translation. Notice the author's emphatic negation and the three categories of belief he discusses.
The passage includes a footnote marker (1) and a final (ج) – these are part of the text.
2
Understand the core negation
The opening sentence denies any effect from created things in customary or other causes. 'La ta'thir' means no influence whatsoever, not by nature (bi-tab'iha) nor by a power God deposited in them.
The exception about 'connected quantity' is a technical point about actions being created by God.
3
Identify the three erroneous beliefs
The footnote enumerates three mistaken views about causes: (1) believing a cause has effect by itself (kufr), (2) believing it has effect by a power God placed in it (sinful innovator), (3) believing God is the sole agent but that there is a necessary connection (ignorant, may lead to kufr).
The second view includes a comparison to the Qadari belief that humans create their own actions.
4
Identify the saved belief
The only safe position is to deny both effect and necessary connection, accepting the possibility that the cause may exist without the effect, which allows for miracles and resurrection.
This is the orthodox Ash'ari view of occasionalism.
5
Reflect on the theological stakes
Consider why denying necessary connection is so important: it safeguards the possibility of miracles (fire not burning Abraham) and the resurrection of bodies, which are central Islamic doctrines.
The passage warns that the third erroneous belief can drag one to disbelief because it implies a fixed natural order.

Refuting Essential Causation (Ta'lil)
Sanusi refutes the philosophers' theory of essential causation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Ash'ari scholars reject the philosophers' concept of essential causation (ta'lil) and the logical dilemma it entails.

Core Idea
God's action requires will and power, not automatic causation.

This lesson addresses a key theological disagreement: whether God creates through being an essential cause (علة) that necessarily produces effects without will. The philosophers' concept is defined, illustrated, and then refuted by Ahl al-Sunna using a logical dilemma. This distinction is crucial for understanding Islamic theology's rejection of necessitarian causality.

Guided Path
1
Read the philosophers' definition of cause
Begin by reading the passage's definition of a cause (علة) according to the philosophers: it is that from which the action follows necessarily without possibility of omission.
Notice the emphasis on absence of conditions or impediments.
2
Examine the example: finger and ring
Consider the example given: the movement of the finger is said to be the cause of the ring's movement, with a necessary concomitance.
This example illustrates the philosophers' notion of efficient causation in the physical world.
3
Note the Sunni corrective
Observe how Ahl al-Sunna reject this causal link, asserting that both movements are created by God and merely concomitant, not causal.
Some may loosely call it a customary cause, but it is not an effective cause.
4
Understand the refutation's dilemma
Focus on the refutation: if God's essence is eternal, then the world would be eternal (effect cannot be posterior to cause); if originated, then God needs another agent, leading to circularity or infinite regress.
Both outcomes are impossible, thus disproving the theory of essential causation.

The Seventh Proof: The Core Argument
Sanusi presents the proof of impossibility of an infinite chain by a dichotomy about pre-eternity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical structure of the seventh proof against an infinite chain of past events, including the two scenarios concerning pre-eternity.

Core Idea
An infinite chain of past events is impossible because each event has a beginning.

Sanusi presents the seventh proof to refute the possibility of an infinite regress of events. He begins by considering the state of pre-eternity (al-azal). The proof hinges on a simple dichotomy: either an event exists in pre-eternity or not. Both options lead to a contradiction or to the conclusion that the series has a beginning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the premise
Every individual event is contingent and has a beginning. This is the starting point of the proof.
This premise is self-evident and accepted by both parties.
2
Consider the two possibilities
In pre-eternity, the non-existence of all events is established. Two scenarios are possible: either an event accompanies that non-existence or not.
The two scenarios are mutually exclusive and exhaustive.
3
Analyze the first scenario
If an event accompanies the non-existence in pre-eternity, then existence and non-existence would coexist—a logical contradiction.
This is impossible by rational necessity.
4
Analyze the second scenario
If no event accompanies the non-existence, then pre-eternity is empty of all events, meaning the series must have a first event and thus a beginning.
This leads to the conclusion that the series cannot be infinite.

Core Idea
The contingent needs God every moment because of its possibility, not just at creation.

After discussing causality, Sanusi addresses a deeper theological question: Why does a contingent being depend on God at all? Is it because it came into existence (huduth) or because it is possible (imkan)? This discussion clarifies the constant need for God's sustaining power.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the impossibility of deposited power
Read the second remark in Arabic and translation. Understand why a creature having power from God would be rationally impossible.
It would imply God needs a medium for some acts, which contradicts His self-sufficiency.
2
Follow the argument that the source of dependence is imkan
The author states that the source of the contingent's dependence is its possibility (imkan), i.e., its essence being equally related to existence and non-existence. This attribute never leaves it, so it constantly needs God.
This is the position of the majority of Ash'ari theologians.
3
Understand the refutation of huduth as the cause
If dependence were due to origination, then after the contingent exists, it would no longer need God because origination is a past attribute. This leads to the logical absurdity of 'acquisition of what has already been acquired' (tahsil al-hasil).
Origination is an accident that is not essential to the contingent.
4
Examine the saving reinterpretation of huduth
The author offers an interpretation: huduth can be the cause of our knowledge of dependence, not the cause of dependence itself. This makes the huduth position strong and compatible with imkan.
This shows the author's effort to reconcile different views.

Remark: Whoever believes that these causes affect what accompanies them by nature or by a cause, there is no disagreement about his disbelief. Whoever believes that they affect by a power God placed in them, he is a sinful innovator, and there are two opinions on his disbelief, and the more correct is that he is a sinful innovator. As for whoever believes that they do not affect by their nature nor by a power God deposited in them, but claims that the concomitance between them and what accompanies them is rational and cannot be broken, he is ignorant of the reality of the customary judgment and perhaps his ignorance drags him to disbelief, and that is when he denies things like the miracles of the prophets and the resurrection, because they are all contrary to custom. As for whoever believes that they do not affect by their nature nor by a power God placed in them, and claims that the concomitance between them and their effects is customary and that separation is possible, this is the believer with verified faith, who is saved by the grace of God from the perils of this world and the Hereafter. Remark: The effect of anything among creatures by a power God placed in it is rationally impossible, because then our Lord would become in need of a medium for bringing about some acts, and this is false based on what is known: that the source of the contingent's dependence is possibility (al-imkan), i.e., the equal relation of existence and non-existence to it with respect to its essence. For this attribute does not leave it, so it is dependent on Him, Exalted is He, in every moment for preferring its existence over its non-existence. As for the opposite view, that the source of its dependence is origination (al-huduth), i.e., existence after non-existence, then it would not be dependent on Him, Exalted is He, for the continuation of its existence, since this attribute, i.e., existence after non-existence, has already occurred. If it needed Him after its occurrence, it would entail the acquisition of what has already been acquired. And know that the statement that the cause of dependence is origination is very weak, if it is meant that the cause of the thing's dependence in its essence is its origination, because origination is additional to the thing in its essence, and it is impossible for this to be the cause of the thing's dependence in its essence. The correct view here is to say that the cause of the thing's dependence in its essence is its essential possibility. However, it occurred to me to give an interpretation for the statement that the cause of dependence is origination, meaning that the cause of our knowledge that a thing is dependent is our knowledge that it is originated. With this interpretation, their statement becomes very strong, and it does not conflict with those who say that the cause of the thing's dependence in its essence is possibility, as you see, so reflect.

Commentary
This passage provides the detailed rulings on the four beliefs about causality. The first belief (nature/cause) is clear kufr. The second (power placed by God) makes one a sinful innovator, with two opinions on kufr, the more correct being sinful innovation. The third (rational necessity) makes one ignorant and may lead to kufr if it denies miracles and resurrection. The fourth (customary conjunction with possible separation) is true faith. Then the author gives a second remark: the idea that a creature affects by a power God placed in it is rationally impossible, because it would make God need a medium for some acts. This is false because the source of the contingent's dependence is its essential possibility (imkan), which never leaves it, so it always needs God to prefer its existence over non-existence. The alternative view that dependence is due to origination (huduth) is weak if taken as the cause of the thing's essence, because origination is an accident. The correct cause is essential possibility. However, the author offers a way to salvage the huduth view: it can mean the cause of our knowledge of dependence is our knowledge of origination. This interpretation makes the huduth view strong and compatible with the possibility view.

Key Takeaway
A contingent depends on God every moment because of its essential possibility, not merely because it began to exist.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Have you ever lived as though your existence were a settled fact that no longer requires God's attention every moment? ?
