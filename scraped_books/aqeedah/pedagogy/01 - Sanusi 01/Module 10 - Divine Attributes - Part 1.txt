Sanusi's chosen position on divine power and non-existent things.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Divine power reaches both existent and non-existent alike.

In this passage, the author reports Sanusi's decisive choice on a key question about the scope of divine power. Understanding this choice helps clarify the Ash'ari position on whether God's power relates to things before they exist. Let's examine the text to see Sanusi's view and his support for it.

Sanusi chose Ash'ari's broader view on the reach of God's perceptual attributes.

This passage records a key doctrinal choice by Sanusi: he sided with al-Ash'ari over Ibn Kullab and al-Qalanisi on the scope of God's attributes of perception. The disagreement centered on whether these attributes—like hearing and seeing—relate to all existent things or only to their direct objects. This seemingly subtle difference has implications for how we understand God's knowledge of every detail of creation.

Commentary
This passage is a proof followed by an objection and its answer. Sanusi begins by restating the fourth section of his discussion: the attributes of God must necessarily subsist in His essence and must be independent of any specifier (i.e., they do not require an external cause to be attached to the essence). The reason is that being attributes necessitates that they cannot subsist by themselves, because that would invert realities. The reality of an attribute is that it entails a subject (موصوف) that is qualified by it. If an attribute existed on its own, it would not be an attribute. To separate an attribute from its defining reality is impossible. Therefore, the very idea of an attribute subsisting by itself is impossible—that would separate it from its essential nature.
Then Sanusi presents an objection: the objector says that the proof only shows that the concept of an attribute requires a subject, but it does not prove that the attribute must actually subsist in that subject; it might be an attribute of a subject without inhering in it (like a relation). The answer is that the very meaning of being an attribute of a subject is to subsist in it. If it did not subsist in it, there would be no way to specify it as the attribute of that subject rather than another—that would require a preponderance without a preponderator (ترجيح بلا مرجح), which is impossible. So the only way for the attribute to be specifically related to its subject is to subsist in it. Therefore, if the attribute does not subsist in its subject, it is not an attribute for that subject or any other. Thus, an attribute subsisting by itself would be an attribute without any subject, which invalidates its reality. The footnote explains that if an attribute (which in contingent things is an accident) were to subsist by itself, it would turn into an essence, inverting its nature. Sanusi concludes that this is exactly the point he made in the earlier proof.

And we only refrained from mentioning dependency (iftiqar) upon the essence in the attribute of the Lord, blessed and exalted, because dependency and poverty (faqr) linguistically and conventionally imply need for something missing that is sought to be obtained. So it is said: the hungry person is in need of food; when he eats and is satisfied, he is not described as being in need of food. Commentary
This passage is a clarification and critique. Sanusi explains why he avoided the term 'dependency' (الافتقار) or 'poverty' (الفقر) when describing God's attributes. He argues that linguistically and conventionally, 'dependency' implies a need for something missing that one seeks to obtain. For example, a hungry person is dependent on food only before eating; after eating, that dependency ceases. Similarly, a naked person is dependent on clothing until clothed. Applying this to God's attributes: if we say they are dependent on the essence for their existence, that would imply that their existence was not already actual—but their existence is necessary, eternal, and independent of any agent. If we say they are dependent for the existence of their subject (the essence), the essence itself is also necessary, eternal, and its non-existence is inconceivable. So the concept of poverty/dependency simply does not apply to either the essence or its attributes. Therefore, using such language for the eternal attributes is forbidden. Sanusi then criticizes Fakhr al-Din al-Razi (الفخر) for being heedless and ill-mannered (أساء الأدب) by calling the attributes 'poor' or 'needy' with respect to the essence. Al-Razi's reasoning was that attributes cannot subsist by themselves and must subsist in the essence, which is true, but he failed to see the negative implications of the term 'poverty'—that it suggests a lack or deficiency. The footnotes provide cross-references to al-Razi's works and note that the majority of non-Arab theologians (متعكلمي الأعاجم) agree with Sanusi's position, including al-‘Allama al-‘Aḍud and his student al-‘Allama al-Sa‘d.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation and clarification concerning the term 'neediness' (iftiqār) as applied to God's attributes. The author begins by linking this discussion to a previous proof ('the previous proof') which established the error of describing God's attributes as being in need of the essence. He then explains why the author and his school avoid using the term 'iftiqār ilā al-dhāt' for divine attributes. He illustrates this with examples: a hungry person is in need of food, but after eating is no longer described as such; a naked person is in need of clothing, but after being clothed the term no longer applies. By analogy, neediness implies a prior state of lack. Since God's attributes are eternal and necessarily existent, they cannot be described as needing their essence for their existence because both the attributes and the essence are necessary, independent, and without any lack. The author argues: if the supposed neediness is for the existence of the attributes themselves, then their existence is already actual and necessary, requiring no agent. If it is for the existence of the possessor (the essence), then the essence is also necessary and independent. Hence, the concept of poverty (faqr) is inconceivable for the essence and its attributes. Therefore, the term 'poverty' cannot be applied to the eternal attributes. The author then criticizes Fakhr al-Din al-Razi (al-Fakhr) for heedlessness and poor etiquette in applying 'faqr' to the attributes. Al-Razi's reasoning, based on the impossibility of attributes subsisting by themselves and their need to subsist through the essence, is acknowledged, but he failed to see that 'faqr' and 'iftiqār' imply a lack of something needed. Since the attributes and essence have no lack, the term is inappropriate. The passage concludes with a supplication for forgiveness and mercy for all believers, including al-Razi, and a plea for treatment by God's pardon rather than justice.

قوله : ( يتأتى بها إيجادُ كلِّ ممكن وإعدامه ) يعني : يتيسر بها إخراج كل ممكن مِنَ العدم إلى الوجودِ ، وإخراجُهُ مِنَ الوجودِ إلى العدم . وقد مرَّ القولُ في جعل العدمِ الطاري أثراً للقدرةِ الأزلية مباشرةً على مذهب القاضي ، وهو الأصح في النظرِ (۲) ؛ لأنَّ المصحِّحَ لتأثير القدرةِ الأزليَّة إن قلنا : هو الإمكانُ معَ الحدوثِ ، أو الإمكانُ بشرط الحدوث ، أو الحدوث فقط ، أو الإمكان فقط (۳) .. فذلك كلُّه محقق ثابت للعدم الطارئ ، ولا يلزمُ في أثر القدرة أن يكون وجودياً كما صارَ إليهِ إمام الحرمينِ بل إنَّما يلزمُ فِيهِ أنْ يكونَ مُتجدِّداً حادثاً ، كانَ ذلك المتجدِّدُ وجوديّاً أو عدميّاً ، وهذا هو الحق الذي لا شكّ فيه ، والله تعالى أعلم .
His statement: 'by which is possible the bringing into existence of every possible thing and its annihilation' means: by which is facilitated the bringing out of every possible thing from non-existence into existence, and bringing it out from existence into non-existence. And the discussion has passed regarding making the accidental non-existence an effect of the eternal power directly, according to the madhhab of al-Qadi, and it is the soundest in consideration; because the corrector for the effect of eternal power, if we say: it is possibility together with temporality, or possibility conditioned by temporality, or temporality alone, or possibility alone (3) … then all of that is realized and established for the accidental non-existence. And it is not necessary in the effect of power that it be existent, as Imam al-Haramayn came to hold; rather it is necessary in it that it be renewed and temporal, whether that renewed thing is existent or non-existent. And this is the truth about which there is no doubt, and God Almighty knows best.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal argument about the scope of divine power. It begins by explicating the phrase 'by which is possible the bringing into existence of every possible thing and its annihilation' (يتأتى بها إيجاد كل ممكن وإعدامه). Sanusi explains that this means power can bring anything from non-being to being and from being to non-being. He then references a previous discussion (possibly from earlier in the work) regarding accidental non-existence (العدم الطاري) being directly an effect of eternal power, following the view of al-Qadi al-Baqillani, which he considers the most correct. The argument proceeds: the factor that validates the effect of eternal power—whether we say it is possibility together with temporality, or possibility conditioned by temporality, or temporality alone, or possibility alone—all these are realized for accidental non-existence. Therefore, it is not necessary that the effect of power be an existent, as Imam al-Haramayn (al-Juwayni) claimed. Rather, the necessary condition is that the effect be temporal/renewed (متجدداً حادثاً), regardless of whether that renewed thing is an existent or a non-existent. Sanusi declares this the undeniable truth.

Divine Power and Non-Existence as Its Effect
Sanusi defines divine power and argues for non-existence as a direct effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that divine power can directly bring about non-existence, and why this view is considered more correct in theological reasoning.

Core Idea
Divine power's effect is any new thing, existent or non-existent.

Sanusi begins this passage by defining divine power in terms of its effect: bringing every possible thing into and out of existence. He then addresses a key theological question: can non-existence be a direct effect of God's power? The passage defends the view of al-Qadi al-Baqillani against Imam al-Haramayn, grounding it in the conditions that make an effect valid.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Notice that Sanusi defines divine power by its capacity to both bring into existence and take out of existence.
2
Identify the theological disagreement
Sanusi mentions two positions: al-Qadi al-Baqillani (non-existence can be an effect) and Imam al-Haramayn (only existents). Understand the stakes.
Imam al-Haramayn required the effect to be an existing thing.
3
Examine the 'corrector' theories
Sanusi lists four formulations of what makes an effect valid: possibility with temporality, possibility conditioned by temporality, temporality alone, or possibility alone. Notice how he argues that all apply to accidental non-existence.
This is a key move in the argument.
4
Grasp the conclusion
Sanusi concludes that the effect must be temporally new (ḥādith), not necessarily existent. This is the truth without doubt.
