The Three Types of Khabar
Sanusi enumerates three types of khabar based on how they admit truth and falsehood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three divisions of khabar based on how they admit truth and falsehood, focusing on the first two types exemplified in the passage.

Core Idea
Khabar can be true/false absolutely, or only conditionally depending on additional factors.

Having defined khabar, Sanusi now refines the classification by considering different perspectives on how a statement can be truth-apt. Some statements are always open to truth and falsehood, while others are true necessarily due to external factors like the speaker's infallibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the threefold division
Read from 'ودخل في الخبر' to the end of the passage. Identify the three types.
The third type is not fully given but can be inferred.
2
Analyze the first type
The first type admits truth/falsehood absolutely. Example: human statements about unseen matters.
This is the common case.
3
Analyze the second type
The second type admits truth/falsehood in form but becomes necessarily true when considering the speaker's infallibility.
Example: divine reports.
4
Reflect on the third type
Though not fully stated, the pattern suggests a third type that is necessarily false due to external factors.
This completes the logical classification.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification of speech (al-kalam) into two main types: statement (khabar) and construction (insha'), followed by a detailed breakdown of khabar into three divisions. The author begins by stating that speech divides into khabar and insha'. Khabar is defined as that which by its very essence admits the possibility of being true or false, whereas insha' does not admit such a possibility essentially. This is the core logical distinction. The author then reiterates that every speech that conveys a relation intended for itself is confined to these two divisions. Next, a more precise definition of khabar is given: it is speech that accepts truth and falsehood for the sake of its own essence (haqiqa), without considering external factors like the speaker or the material subject matter. The author gives an example: necessary propositions that can only be true in affirmation or false in negation. The first condition—probability of truth and falsehood—excludes insha' statements, which include commands, prohibitions, questions, wishes, offers, urging, and calls. These are not truth-apt inherently. Then, by restricting the probability to the essence, khabar is divided into three types. The first type is that which admits truth and falsehood absolutely, considering both the form of the proposition and additional factors (the subject matter and the meaning). An example is a statement by a non-infallible person about someone's fate in the afterlife; this statement can be true or false from all perspectives. The second type admits truth and falsehood when considering only the form of the relation, but when additional factors (like the speaker's infallibility) are considered, probability is removed and truth is necessitated. An example is reports from Allah and His messengers, which, by virtue of the speaker's veracity, are necessarily true. The passage cuts off before the third type is given, but the pattern suggests a third type that admits falsehood necessarily due to external factors.

Refining the Definition of Insha' with 'Bi-Dhatihi'
Sanusi refines the definition of insha' by adding the qualification 'bi-dhatihi'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the qualification 'bi-dhatihi' is necessary to properly define insha' and exclude certain types of khabar.

Core Idea
A precise definition must capture both inclusiveness and exclusiveness.

In this passage, Sanusi refines the definition of insha' (construct speech) by adding the crucial qualification 'by its essence'. He shows that a simple definition would mistakenly include two types of khabar and exclude insincere commands. Understanding this refinement is key to mastering the logical distinction between insha' and khabar.

Guided Path
1
Examine the examples of insha'
Read the Arabic passage and notice the examples: commands, prohibitions, interrogatives, wishes, and calls. Observe that none of these admit truth or falsity in external reality.
These examples illustrate the core property of insha'.
2
Identify the problem with the simple definition
Sanusi points out that defining insha' simply as 'what does not admit truth or falsehood' would include the necessarily true and necessarily false types of khabar. Understand why these types appear to fit the simple definition.
3
Learn how 'bi-dhatihi' solves inclusiveness
The addition 'by its essence' excludes those two khabar types because they do admit truth/falsity when considered in themselves. See how this rectifies the definition's inclusiveness (al-ṭard).
4
See how 'bi-dhatihi' solves exclusiveness
Sanusi gives the example of an insincere command: it can be called true/false based on implication, but in its own essence it is insha'. Without the qualification, such a command would be excluded, making the definition defective in exclusiveness (al-ʿaks).

Commentary
This passage is a logical refinement of the definition of insha' (construct speech), distinguishing it from khabar (informative speech). The author first provides examples of insha': commands, prohibitions, interrogatives, wishes, and calls, showing that none of these bear truth or falsity because they do not assert something about external reality. Then he identifies a problem: a simple definition of insha' as 'what does not admit truth or falsehood' would inadvertently include two special types of khabar (the necessarily true and the necessarily false) that also appear not to admit truth/falsity in their external judgment. To exclude these, he adds the qualification 'bi-dhatihi' (by its own essence), meaning that insha' does not admit truth/falsity by its very nature, whereas those other types do admit them when considered in themselves. He further illustrates with a command issued insincerely (out of hypocrisy); such a command can be called 'truthful' or 'false' based on the speaker's inner state, which is an informative implication, but in its own essence as a request, it does not bear truth/falsity. Without the 'by essence' restriction, this command would be excluded from insha' even though it is truly insha'. Thus the qualification ensures both inclusiveness (all insha' are covered) and exclusiveness (no non-insha' enters). The author concludes that this addition perfects the definition.

Mu'tazilite Definitions of Truth and Falsehood
Sanusi presents two Mu'tazilite definitions of sidq and kidhb.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite definitions of truth and falsehood as proposed by al-Nazzam and al-Jahiz, and why al-Nazzam's definition is considered corrupt.

Core Idea
Truth is not merely subjective belief; it must correspond to reality.

The previous discussion on the verse about hypocrites led Sanusi to expose a flaw in the Mu'tazilite al-Nazzam's argument. Here, he presents al-Nazzam's definition of truth and falsehood, which severs truth from objective reality, and contrasts it with al-Jahiz's more balanced definition. This sets the stage for the orthodox position that truth requires correspondence to reality.

Guided Path
1

The phrase 'wa bihādhā ta'rifu fasād...' signals that the author is using the previous analysis to critique al-Nazzam.
2
Compare the two definitions
Al-Nazzam defines truth as correspondence to belief only, ignoring reality. Al-Jahiz requires correspondence to both reality and belief. Identify the key difference.
Notice how al-Jahiz includes 'ma'a al-i'tiqād li-dhālika' (along with belief in that).
3
Understand the critique
Sanusi states that al-Nazzam's reliance on the verse is corrupt because his definition ignores objective reality. Reflect on why this is problematic for a coherent understanding of truth.

قوله : ( والكذبُ : عدم مطابقة الخبر لما في نفس الأمر ... ) إلى آخره
مثالُ الكذب الذي يوافق الاعتقاد : قولُ المعتزلي : الحيوانُ المختارُ مُوجِدٌ لأفعالِهِ الاختياريَّةِ بالقدرةِ التي خلق الله تعالى لهُ ؛ فَإِنَّ هذا الخبر كذبٌ ؛ لمخالفتِهِ لما في نفس الأمرِ ؛ لأنَّ العقل والنقلَ مِنَ الكتاب والسنَّةِ وإجماع السلف الصالح قبل ظهورِ البدع. . شاهدة أنَّ جميع الكائناتِ خَلْقٌ لمولانا تبارك وتعالى ، ولا شريكَ معَهُ في أثرِ مِنَ الآثار ، والقدريَّةُ مجوس هذه الأمَّةِ يعتقدون خلاف هذا ، وأنَّ الحيوانات هي المستقلة بإيجاد أفعالها الاختياريَّة ؛ بما خلق الله تعالى لها مِنَ القدرة .
ومثالُ الكذب الذي يخالفُ الاعتقاد : هذا الخبرُ بعينِهِ إِذا صدرَ مِنْ سنّي أخبرَ بهِ بمحضر المعتزلةِ سَتْراً لحاله ؛ للخوف منهم ، فَإِنَّهُ وإِنْ كانَ كذباً لمخالفته لما في نفس الأمر فهو مخالف أيضاً لاعتقادِ السنّي الذي أخبرَ بهِ ، للكنَّه ارتكب هذا الكذبَ المباحَ لدعوى الضرورة إليه
ومِنْ ذلكَ : مَنْ يُكرَهُ على النطق بكلمة الكفر وقلبه مطمئن بالإيمان .
دقة وتحري أهل السنة في حد الصدق والكذب واعلم : أنَّ تفسير أهلِ الحقِّ للصدق والكذب حصلَ بهِ الوثوقُ بأخبار الرسول عليه الصلاةُ والسلامُ في أحكامه ووعده ووعيده (1) وأحوال الآخرة جملةً وتفصيلاً ؛ لأنَّا نعلم بالبرهان القطعي صدقه ؛ أي : مطابقة أخبارِهِ لما في نفس الأمر ، لا لاعتقاده فقط معَ جواز مخالفتِها لما في نفس الأمرِ ، وبالله تعالى التوفيق .
(1) إذ لو تم اعتماد تعريف المعتزلة - ولا سيما النظام - الملتفت للنسبة الذهنية دون اعتبار الخارج .. لكانت الأخبار الشرعية قاصرة على الحكم الذهني فقط ، ويحتمل مخالفتها للواقع عندهم ، وهذا من تدقيقات وتحقيقات أئمة أهل السنة ، فما أخبر به الأنبياء عليهم الصلاة والسلام مطابق ابتداءً لما في الواقع ، وبالضرورة لما في أذهانهم
[The definition of lying]
His statement: (And lying is: the non-correspondence of the report with what is in reality ...) to its end.
An example of lying that agrees with belief: the statement of the Mu'tazili: 'The free-acting animal is the originator of its voluntary actions by the power that Allah Almighty created for it'; for this report is a lie due to its opposition to what is in reality; because the intellect and transmitted evidence from the Book and the Sunnah and the consensus of the righteous predecessors before the emergence of innovations all testify that all created things are the creation of our Lord, Blessed and Exalted, and there is no partner with Him in any effect, and the Qadariyya are the Magians of this Ummah who believe contrary to this, and that animals are independent in originating their voluntary actions by the power that Allah Almighty created for them.
And an example of lying that opposes belief: this same report when it issues from a Sunni who reports it in the presence of the Mu'tazila to conceal his state out of fear of them; for although it is a lie due to its opposition to what is in reality, it also opposes the belief of the Sunni who reported it, but he committed this permissible lie because of necessity for it.
And from that: one who is compelled to utter a word of disbelief while his heart is tranquil with faith.
The precision and verification of Ahl al-Sunnah in the definition of truth and falsehood. And know: that the interpretation of the people of truth concerning truth and falsehood results in trust in the reports of the Messenger, upon him blessings and peace, in his rulings, his promise, his threat, and the conditions of the Hereafter in general and in detail; because we know with decisive proof his truthfulness; that is: the correspondence of his reports with what is in reality, not [based on] his belief alone with the possibility of their opposition to what is in reality. And with Allah Almighty is success.
(1) For if the definition of the Mu'tazila – especially al-Nazzam – that turns to the mental relation without considering the external were adopted, then the religious reports would be confined to mental judgment only, and their opposition to reality would be possible according to them. And this is from the fine points and investigations of the imams of Ahl al-Sunnah. For what the prophets, upon them blessings and peace, reported corresponds initially to what is in reality, and necessarily to what is in their minds.

Commentary
This passage defines lying and truthfulness from the Sunni perspective, provides illustrative examples, and argues why this definition is crucial for certainty in prophetic reports. The author begins by giving the definition of lying as the non-correspondence of a report with reality (not merely with the speaker's belief). Two examples follow. The first example is lying that agrees with the speaker's belief: a Mu'tazili says that an animal creates its own actions by the power Allah gave it. This report is a lie because it contradicts reality, even though the Mu'tazili believes it. The author supports this by noting that reason, revelation, and early consensus affirm that all things are created by Allah alone, and those who disagree (the Qadariyya) are likened to Magians. The second example is lying that also opposes the speaker's belief: a Sunni, fearing the Mu'tazila, repeats the same statement to conceal his true position. This report is a lie both because it contradicts reality and because it contradicts his own belief, but it is permissible due to duress, analogous to one forced to utter disbelief while his heart is firm in faith. The author then shifts to the Sunni definition of truthfulness as correspondence with reality, not merely with the speaker's belief. He argues that this definition gives confidence in the Prophet's reports about rulings, promises, threats, and the hereafter, because we know through decisive proof that his reports correspond to reality directly, not just to his belief. The footnote reinforces this by contrasting the Mu'tazili definition (mental correspondence only), which would leave open the possibility that prophetic reports might not match reality, undermining certainty. Thus, the Sunni definition preserves the reliability of divine communication.

Truthfulness as Objective Correspondence
Defines sidq as correspondence with reality and contrasts with Mu'tazilite view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ahl al-Sunna's definition of truthfulness as correspondence with objective reality and how it differs from the Mu'tazilite view based on subjective belief.

Core Idea
Truthfulness is about matching reality, not just sincerity.

This lesson introduces a foundational kalam concept: what does it truly mean for a prophet to be truthful? The Ahl al-Sunna define truthfulness as correspondence with objective reality (nafs al-amr), not merely conformity to the speaker's belief. This distinction is crucial for trusting prophetic reports about the unseen world, and it directly refutes the Mu'tazilite view, particularly that of al-Nazzam, which grounds truth only in mental relation. Read the passage to see how this argument unfolds.

Commentary
This passage defines truthfulness (sidq) and falsehood (kadhib) according to Ahl al-Sunna and refutes the Mu'tazilite definition. The author begins by stating that the correct interpretation ensures trust in the Prophet's reports (peace be upon him) concerning his rulings, promises, threats, and details of the afterlife. The reason is that we know by decisive proof (burhan qat'i) his truthfulness. The definition of truthfulness is that his reports correspond to what is in objective reality (nafs al-amr), not merely that they conform to his own belief while it would be possible for them to contradict reality. Then the author adds a footnote: if the Mu'tazilite definition were adopted—especially that of al-Nazzam which focuses on mental relation without considering the external—then religious reports would be limited to mental judgments only, and it would be possible for them to contradict reality. This is a precise investigation of the imams of Ahl al-Sunna. The prophets' reports are initially in accordance with reality and necessarily also in accordance with their own minds (i.e., their belief and the reality are necessarily aligned).
