Core Idea
Actions are not inherently good or evil; God's command determines their moral status.

Sanusi connects the doctrine of rational ethical evaluation to the downright disbelief of the Brahmins, who denied prophethood by claiming that certain divine commands are inherently evil. This passage shows the logical flaw in that reasoning and the dangerous consequences of such a view. Let's examine his argument.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the doctrine of rational ethical evaluation (al-tahsin wa al-taqbih al-‘aqliyyan). Sanusi begins by stating that this second matter (the first presumably was about the world's creation by choice) gave rise to the explicit disbelief of the Brahmins, who denied prophethood by claiming that certain revealed commands (like bowing, slaughtering animals) are inherently evil (qabih) and cannot come from a wise deity. Sanusi refutes this by a simple argument: if such acts were evil in God's judgment (i.e., if He commanded them), they would also be evil in His action (i.e., if He caused them directly). But we know that God sometimes causes people to assume these very postures through illness or old age, or even causes them to do far more repulsive things by removing their intellect. Since God clearly does such acts, they cannot be inherently evil; therefore, He may also command them. Thus, the Brahmins are wrong. Sanusi then adds a further argument against the Mu‘tazilite notion that God's actions and rulings must serve purposes: if so, God would be dependent on actions to attain His goals, which contradicts His absolute self-sufficiency. This corrupt principle, he notes, also led to the Mu‘tazilite innovation of imposing certain considerations (probably referring to their doctrine of al-salah wa al-aslah).

Defining Trustworthiness for Prophethood
Sanusi defines al-amanah and applies it to prophetic infallibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's definition of trustworthiness and how it establishes the prophetic attribute of infallibility.

Core Idea
Trustworthiness is protecting all faculties from forbidden or disliked actions.

After establishing truthfulness, Sanusi now defines trustworthiness, another essential prophetic attribute. This passage presents the definition, its opposite, and a detailed explanation of divine limits, culminating in the affirmation that prophets are the most perfect in trustworthiness due to Allah's special protection.

Guided Path
1
Read the definitions
Read the first two sentences defining trustworthiness and betrayal. Notice that trustworthiness involves preserving all external and internal faculties from what is forbidden (haram) or disliked (makruh).
The definition is comprehensive, covering both actions and inner states.
2
Understand the purpose
Sanusi explains that he defines trustworthiness to establish the obligatory trustworthiness of the prophets, just as he defined truthfulness earlier. Miracles serve as divine testimony to their truthfulness and trustworthiness.
This shows the systematic approach of Sanusi in building prophetic attributes.
3
Reflect on divine limits
The passage elaborates on the limits (hudud) set by Allah for responsible servants: obligatory, recommended, and permissible. Transgressing these into the forbidden or disliked is betrayal. Success in preserving the charge is trustworthiness.
Human responsibility is framed within divine boundaries.
4
Apply to prophets
The final part asserts that prophets are the most perfect in trustworthiness because Allah has protected them through His infallibility (ismah), preventing any violation or baseness.
This ties the definition directly to prophethood.

Commentary
This passage defines trustworthiness (al-amanah) and its opposite, betrayal (al-khiyanah), and applies them to the prophets. The author begins by giving a concise definition: trustworthiness is the preservation of all external and internal faculties from engaging in anything forbidden (haram) or disliked (makruh). Betrayal is simply the failure to preserve them from such things. The author then explains the purpose of this definition: just as he earlier defined truthfulness to establish the obligatory truthfulness of the messengers, here he defines trustworthiness to establish their obligatory trustworthiness. The trustworthiness of prophets is proven by miracles, which serve as divine testimony that they are truthful and trustworthy in conveying revelation. The author then elaborates on the concept of divine limits: Allah has set boundaries for His responsible servants—obligatory, recommended, and permissible acts—and forbids transgressing them into the forbidden and disliked. He has charged them with piety and with fleeing from His anger to His obedience. Those whom Allah grants success to uphold this charge are called 'trustworthy' (amin), while those whom He overcomes by His justice and who are deprived of His infallibility and grace are 'betrayers' (kha'in). Finally, the author asserts that the prophets and messengers are the most perfect in trustworthiness because Allah has specially protected them through His infallibility (ismah), preventing them from any violation or baseness.

Commentary
This passage describes the high station of the prophets and the sending of messengers as a divine favor, followed by the two categories of people in response. The author begins by depicting the prophets immersed in the witnessing of Allah's beauty and majesty, enjoying lights of knowledge and closeness. Then Allah sends His special ones (khawass) and messengers, who are adorned with divine protection ('isma), surrounded by miracles (mu'jizat) and signs (ayat), and riding the mounts of guardianship and guidance. The purpose is to guide servants to divine pleasure in both worlds through every aspect of their conduct. The author then presents two outcomes: those who believe the messengers, follow their guidance, and ignore falsehood succeed and are saved. Those who are afflicted with stupidity and blindness, follow Satan and desires, and turn away from the messengers perish eternally. However, a hopeful clause is added: if such a person dies with a firm belief in the messengers and their truthfulness, they may be saved by Allah's grace.

Learning Objective

Belief in prophets brings salvation; disbelief brings eternal perdition.

The Decisive Proof of Prophetic Infallibility and the Consensus of Ahl al-Haqq
Sanusi presents the decisive proof of prophetic infallibility from the absolute command to follow, and cites the consensus of Ahl al-Haqq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the decisive proof for prophetic infallibility derived from God's absolute command to follow them, and the consensus of Ahl al-Haqq on their trustworthiness.

Core Idea
God's command to follow prophets without examination proves they are infallible.

This passage presents the logical and theological proof for prophetic infallibility. It argues that because God commands us to follow the prophets absolutely, without any reflection or investigation, this implies that they must be protected from error. It then cites the consensus of Ahl al-Haqq and culminates in affirming the superiority of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him).

Understand the superiority of Muhammad

The passage then provides a proof of their infallibility: God's absolute command to follow them without reflection or examination is a decisive proof (دليل قطعي) that they are free from any violation or fault in words and deeds, outwardly and inwardly. This is supported by the consensus (إجماع) of Ahl al-Haqq (the people of truth) on the trustworthiness of prophets and messengers and their being exalted above all faults and sins. Finally, it affirms that the best of them—and the best of all creation—is Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him), and prays for salvation through blessings upon him.
