The Prior Non-Existence Is Within God's Power
Sanusi defends that prior possible non-existence is directly within God's power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sanusi considers the prior possible non-existence to be within God's power and why the objection against it is invalid.

Core Idea
God's power truly encompasses even the non-existence that precedes creation.

Sanusi continues his discussion on divine power by addressing a specific case: the state of non-existence that exists before something is created. Some might think that because non-existence is not a 'thing', it cannot be an object of power. Sanusi rejects this, showing that God's power is real and covers preservation and removal of non-existence itself.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context
Recognize that 'prior possible non-existence' refers to the state of a thing before it is created. Sanusi argues this state is directly within God's power.
This non-existence is not absolute nothingness, but a specific state that precedes creation.
2
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the two main parts: the claim that non-existence is within God's power, and the refutation of the objection.
3
Analyze the linguistic argument
Sanusi uses the example of a king having power over people metaphorically. How does this support the claim that God's power over non-existence is real, not metaphorical?
He argues that if metaphorical power is used for lesser things, then real power should apply here.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Sanusi labels the objection as 'bad manners' because it implies a deficiency in God's power. Why is this a serious charge?
In kalam, denying an attribute of perfection is a grave error.

Possible Non-Existence is Within Divine Power
Sanusi defends that possible non-existence is maqdur against objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why possible non-existence is considered within divine power according to Sanusi, and how he responds to objections that non-existence cannot be an object of power.

Core Idea
Divine power attaches to possibles, not just existents, so possible non-existence is maqdur.

Sanusi addresses a subtle but important point: can non-existence be an object of divine power? Some might think only existing things can be subject to God's power. However, Sanusi argues that the relevant criterion is possibility (imkan), not existence. He uses linguistic examples and refutes an implied objection, showing that denying maqduriyya for possible non-existence leads to implying impotence in God.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice the flow from the main claim to the footnote clarifications.
The passage is dense; take it slowly.
2
Identify the three kinds of non-existence
Footnote (1) explains three types: possible (ʿadam mumkin), necessary (wājib), and impossible (mustaḥīl). Possible non-existence is the one at issue.
This classification is key to understanding the scope of the argument.
3
Understand the meaning of maqdur
Sanusi explains that being an object of power means being in the grasp of God's power, such that He can preserve it or replace it with existence. He uses the analogy of a king's metaphorical power to show that applying maqdur to possible non-existence is even more fitting.
The king analogy shows the term is used for less, so for God it is literal.
4
Reflect on the implied objection and response
The passage implicitly responds to the objection: non-existence cannot be maqdur because it is not an existing thing. Sanusi argues that this reasoning is flawed and constitutes bad manners, as it implies impotence.
The phrase 'bad manners' is a strong criticism.
5
Grasp the final clarification from al-Razi and al-Ghazali
The passage ends with a quote from al-Razi clarifying that denying maqduriyya to non-existents means denying attachment of power, not that power was attached and failed. Al-Ghazali notes that when properly understood, there is no disagreement.
This shows the issue is about definition, not substance.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of the claim that possible non-existence (al-‘adam al-mumkin) is within divine power (maqdur). It begins by asserting that the existence of future events is maqdur, as are contingent existence and non-existence. It explains that to be maqdur means to be in the grasp of power, such that the powerful being can preserve or remove it. The author then notes that the term maqdur is used in language and custom for less than this, citing the example of a king's power over his subjects—which is metaphorical. He then poses a rhetorical question: why not apply maqdur to possible non-existence, since God truly has power to preserve or change it? He strongly criticizes those who deny this, saying it implies impotence (ʿajz) and is bad manners. The embedded footnotes clarify classifications of non-existence (possible, necessary, impossible), note that the passage replies to an objection (implicit: non-existence cannot be maqdur because it is not an existing thing), and quote Imam al-Razi that the knowledge of a powerful being's ability to act or refrain is necessary. The passage concludes by clarifying that those who deny maqduriyya for non-existents mean that power does not attach to them at all, not that power attached without effect, and that this issue has no genuine disagreement when properly understood.

The Sole Basis for Divine Power's Attachment
Sanusi states the correct view that imkan alone suffices for eternal power's attachment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the sole basis for the attachment of God's eternal power to possibles is their intrinsic possibility (imkān), regardless of existence, non-existence, or temporal sequence.

Core Idea
Possibility alone suffices for God's power to attach to anything.

This passage presents a decisive theological point: what makes a possible thing an object of God's eternal power is simply that it is possible, not whether it exists or not. The imam (Sanusi) argues that this view is both linguistically sound and safer from implying any deficiency in God. Let's examine his concise reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the English translation. Let the author's own words set the stage.
2
Identify the key term 'muṣaḥḥiḥ'
Notice that the passage begins by stating that the 'corrector' (muṣaḥḥiḥ) of the attachment of eternal power is imkan only. Ask yourself: what does 'corrector' mean in this context?
It means the factor that validates or makes the attachment correct.
3
Understand the scope of 'every possible'
The passage explicitly includes both existent and non-existent possibles, and both prior and subsequent possibles. Make sure you see how this expands the common understanding.
This is key to the argument that possibility alone is sufficient.
4
Reflect on the evaluation
Sanusi concludes that this view is closer to language and custom, and safer from bad manners and implying deficiency. Consider why this matters in a theological context.

Commentary
This passage presents the chosen position of the Imam (likely Sanusi himself or another authority) regarding the condition for the attachment of divine eternal power (al-qudra al-azaliyya) to possibles. It states that the sole factor that validates (muṣaḥḥiḥ) this attachment is possibility (imkān) alone. Consequently, every possible thing—whether it is an existent or non-existent, whether it is a past, present, or future possible—is an object of power (maqdūr) for God. The phrase 'in accordance with what befits it' means that a non-existent possible is maqdūr in a way appropriate to non-existence (i.e., power to bring it into existence or leave it in non-existence), while an existent possible is maqdūr in a way appropriate to existence (power to preserve or annihilate it). The author then evaluates this view as closer to linguistic usage and custom (which includes calling non-existents maqdūr), and safer from the aforementioned bad manners and implying deficiency in God's power. The passage ends with 'Wa-Allāhu aʿlam' (God knows best), a conventional pious disclaimer.

The Universal Relation of God's Attributes
Sanusi argues for the universal relation of hearing, sight, and perception, and against particularization.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God's attributes of hearing, sight, and perception must relate universally to all existents, and why particularization is impossible for Divine attributes.

Core Idea
God's attributes are necessary, so they cannot be limited by particularization.

After defining the attributes, Sanusi tackles a critical theological point: these attributes in the witnessed world (al-shahid) are customarily restricted to certain objects, but that custom does not apply to God. He argues that since God's attributes are necessary (wajiba), they cannot be subject to particularization (takhsis), which would imply need and contingency. This reasoning establishes the possibility of seeing God in the afterlife and hearing His eternal speech.

Guided Path
1
Understand the shared relation
Sanusi says these attributes share a relation (ta'allaq) to all existents, whether eternal or originated. In the witnessed world, they are customarily specific, but that is due to God's particularization.
The 'witnessed world' is the world we perceive; God can break norms.
2
The argument from impossibility of particularization
Sanusi argues that particularization (takhsis) cannot enter God's attributes because it would entail need for a particularizer (mukhassis), which implies originatedness (huduth). Since God's attributes are eternal and necessary, they must relate to everything suitable.
This is a standard kalam argument: anything that requires a particularizer is contingent.
3
Connect to the possibility of seeing and hearing God
Note the conclusion: because of this universal relation, it is permissible (ja'iz) for creatures to see God (ru'ya) and hear His speech (sam' kalamihi) according to Ahl al-Sunna, even though in the witnessed world sight and hearing are customarily tied to bodies and sounds.
This is the practical payoff of the theological argument.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of a potential objection regarding multiple perceptions (hearing, sight, etc.) attaching to the same object as knowledge. The author first states two possible scenarios for what happens when perceptions and knowledge attach to an object: either they attach to something other than what knowledge attaches to, or they attach to the same thing but knowledge misses some aspect—both impossible. He chooses the first possibility and argues that even when multiple perceptions attach to the same object as knowledge, this does not entail 'تحصيل الحاصل' (obtaining what is already obtained) or 'اجتماع المثلين' (conjunction of two likes). This passage defines the attribute of eternal speech (الكلام الأزلي) and provides its proofs. The Hashwiyya Claim and Its Refutation
Sanusi presents the Hashwiyya claim and refutes it with a rational argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Hashwiyya view that God's speech consists of letters and sounds is rationally untenable.

Core Idea
Letters and sounds cannot be eternal because they are preceded and followed by non-existence.

The Hashwiyya, a sect known for anthropomorphism, claimed that God's speech is composed of letters and sounds like human speech, yet eternal. This directly contradicts the rational understanding of letters and sounds as temporal phenomena. Sanusi presents their view and refutes it with a simple but decisive argument about the nature of temporality.

If you say: The statement of the people of truth that eternal speech relates to all objects of eternal knowledge may be undermined by the fact that when Allah Almighty commands some morally responsible individuals to do something that He knows will not happen from them, this entails that His command related to the occurrence of that commanded thing and did not relate to its non-occurrence, while His knowledge related to the non-occurrence of that commanded thing. Thus His knowledge related to what His command—which is His speech—did not relate to. So knowledge is then more comprehensive in relation than speech. I say: The mentioned eternal speech has many relations without end, and its relation is not restricted to the mode of command. Even if His speech—blessed and exalted is He—did not relate to the abandonment of the commanded thing in the example by way of command, it related to it by way of prohibition, by way of threat, and by way of information of non-occurrence. All of these are eternal speech. Therefore it is impossible for eternal knowledge to be unique in having an object among objects of relations that is not an object of eternal speech in some mode of relation. So what the imams of the Ahl al-Sunnah—may Allah be pleased with them—said is correct: that eternal speech relates to all that eternal knowledge relates to. And the objection of whoever objected to them with the previous example, by which some relations of eternal speech were negated, is invalid. And it is known that negating the relation of the more specific does not entail negating the relation of the more general.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer (shubha wa jawab) concerning the relationship between divine knowledge and divine speech. The objector argues that the statement of Ahl al-Haqq (that eternal speech relates to all objects of eternal knowledge) is undermined by the case where Allah commands something He knows will not occur. The command relates to occurrence, but knowledge relates to non-occurrence, so knowledge appears to have an object (the non-occurrence) that speech does not. The answer refutes this by noting that eternal speech has multiple modes: command, prohibition, threat, and information. In the example, although the command does not relate to the non-occurrence, eternal speech does relate to it through prohibition, threat, or information. Therefore, no object of knowledge escapes some relation of speech. The conclusion affirms the original position and points out that negating the specific relation (command) does not negate the more general relation (speech in all its modes).

The Quran's Four Modes of Existence and the Createdness of Recitation
Sanusi explains the four modes of existence and the distinction between created acts and eternal speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quran exists in four modes (external, mental, spoken, written) and why recitation is created while the recited is eternal.

Core Idea
The Quran is eternal speech, not contained in physical things, but indicated by them.

Sanusi explains that when the Salaf said the Quran is preserved in hearts, recited on tongues, and written in mushafs, they meant it literally, but not by indwelling. He clarifies the distinction through the four modes of existence, showing how the created acts indicate the eternal speech.

And when you know the doctrine of the people of truth concerning the speech of Allah Almighty, you know that the Salaf—may Allah be pleased with them—applying the terms 'preserved in breasts, recited on tongues, written in mushafs' to the speech of Allah Almighty is by way of truth (haqiqa), not by way of metaphor (majaz). And they do not mean by that the indwelling (hulul) of the eternal speech of Allah Almighty in these bodies—Allah is exalted above that. Rather they mean that His speech—mighty and majestic is He—is remembered and indicated by the recitation of the tongue, the speech of the heart (i.e., mental recollection), and the writing of the fingers. So it exists in them by understanding and knowledge, not by indwelling. Because a thing has four modes of existence: existence in external entities (a'yan), existence in minds (adhhan), existence on the tongue (lisan), and existence by the fingers (banan)—i.e., by writing with fingers. So the first existence is the essential, real existence, and the rest of the existences are only by way of indication and understanding. And by this you know that recitation (tilawa) is other than the recited (matluw), reading (qira'a) other than the read (maqru'), and writing (kitaba) other than the written (maktub). Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal clarification about the nature of the Quran and its relationship to the physical forms in which it is encountered. Sanusi first states that when one understands the Ahl al-Haqq position on divine speech, one realizes that the statements of the Salaf (the early Muslims) that the Quran is 'preserved in hearts, recited on tongues, written in mushafs' are literal (haqiqa) truth, not metaphorical. They do not mean that the eternal speech of Allah actually dwells in these created objects. Rather, they mean that the eternal speech is remembered and indicated by recitation, mental recall, and writing. Sanusi then introduces the concept of four modes of existence for any thing: external reality (a'yan), mental conception (adhhan), spoken expression (lisan), and written notation (banan). The first is the real, self-subsistent existence; the other three are merely indicative (dalala) and conceptual (fahm). Based on this, he concludes that recitation (tilawa) is distinct from the recited (matluw), reading (qira'a) distinct from the read (maqru'), and writing (kitaba) distinct from the written (maktub). The former are created (hadith) acts of humans; the latter are the eternal (qadim) divine speech itself. This preserves divine transcendence while affirming the genuine presence of the Quran in these forms by way of indication.

Divine Limits and the Two Servants
Defines divine limits and contrasts faithful vs treacherous servants

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the concept of divine limits (hudud) and the contrasting outcomes of the faithful and treacherous servants based on divine grace and justice.

Core Idea
Allah sets clear limits; the trustworthy obey, the treacherous transgress.

Sanusi introduces the foundational idea that Allah has set boundaries for human actions. These boundaries are not arbitrary but define right and wrong. The passage then presents a stark contrast: those who uphold these limits are called trustworthy, while those who violate them are treacherous. This sets the stage for understanding moral responsibility in light of divine will.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text once carefully. Notice the key terms for the five categories of action and the two contrasting descriptions of servants.
Pay attention to the parallel structure: 'fa man... kāna amīnan, wa man... kāna khā'inan'.
2
Understand the five categories
Identify the five categories of actions mentioned: wājib, mandūb, mubāḥ, ḥarām, makrūh. Understand that the first three are within the limits, the last two are transgressions.
These categories form the basis of Islamic legal theory.
3
Contrast the two servants
Focus on the contrast between the 'amīn' (trustworthy) and 'khā'in' (treacherous). Notice that trustworthiness is linked to divine success (tawfīq) and protection, while treachery is linked to divine justice (ʿadl) and being driven.
This highlights the interplay of divine grace and human responsibility.
