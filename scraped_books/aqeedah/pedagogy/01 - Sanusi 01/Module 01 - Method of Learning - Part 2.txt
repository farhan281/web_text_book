The Three Categories of Decisive Religious Knowledge
Sanusi classifies decisive religious knowledge into three categories based on al-Ghazali.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the threefold classification of decisive religious knowledge according to al-Ghazali and the different rulings for error in each category.

Core Idea
Three decisive domains—kalam, usul, fiqh—each with its own penalty for error.

Al-Ghazali, the Proof of Islam, classified decisive religious knowledge into three categories: kalam matters (pure rational theology), usul matters (foundational principles of jurisprudence), and fiqh matters (practical rulings). This passage explains each category and the consequences of being mistaken in them.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it, then read the literal translation to understand the overall classification.
Notice that the passage begins by situating the issue of voluntary actions within decisive knowledge.
2
Identify the three categories
Find the three divisions: kalam, usul, fiqh. Note the examples given for each: for kalam, the world's origination, proving the Creator, etc.; for usul, consensus and solitary report; for fiqh, prayer and adultery.
3
Understand the rulings for error
Observe how the ruling differs: error in kalam can be kufr or bid'a depending on the topic; error in usul is sinful; denial in fiqh is repudiation of revelation.
This nuance is important to avoid misunderstanding.
4
Relate to the issue at hand
The passage states that the issue of the creation of deeds is a kalam matter and error in it is bid'a, not kufr, because it is not in the fundamentals of faith.
This connects the classification to a specific theological debate.

The issue of voluntary actions of living beings falls within the decisive epistemological matters. These decisive matters were divided by the Proof of Islam (al-Ghazali) into three divisions: kalam matters, usul matters, and fiqh matters. The kalam matters are pure rational matters; the truth in them is one, and whoever errs is sinful; such as the issue of the world's origination, proving the Originator and His attributes, and the sending of messengers and their attributes. Our issue here — the creation of deeds by Him, exalted be He — is included in it. And its definition: what it is valid for the observer to grasp its reality by rational inquiry before the arrival of revelation. But the one who errs in it: if he errs in the fundamentals of faith in Allah and His messenger, his error is kufr; otherwise — like our issue here — it is bid'a. The usul matters: such as consensus being a proof, and the solitary report being a proof, and other rules whose evidence is decisive; whoever errs in them is sinful without doubt. The fiqh matters: everything that is known decisively from the religion of Allah, the Exalted, concerning practices; denying it is a repudiation of revelation; such as the obligation of the five prayers and the prohibition of adultery.

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory note classifying decisive religious knowledge. It begins by stating that the issue of voluntary actions of living beings falls under the category of decisive epistemological matters (al-masā'il al-ma‘rifiyya al-qaṭ‘iyya). These decisive matters are then divided, according to al-Ghazali (Ḥujjat al-Islam), into three types: (1) kalam matters (kalāmiyya), (2) usul matters (uṣūliyya), and (3) fiqh matters (fiqhiyya). For the kalam category: these are purely rational issues where the truth is unique and the one who errs is sinful. Examples include the creation of the world, proving the Creator and His attributes, and prophethood. The specific issue at hand—the creation of deeds by God—falls under this category. The definition of a kalam matter is: something whose reality can be grasped by the inquirer through rational inquiry before the coming of revelation. However, the ruling on error in these matters depends: if the error concerns the fundamentals of faith in God and His messenger, it is kufr; but if not—as in the current issue—it is bid'a (heresy). The usul category includes matters like the authority of consensus and solitary reports—these are evidentially decisive, and error in them makes one sinful without doubt. The fiqh category includes all matters known decisively from the religion concerning actions, such as the obligation of prayer and the prohibition of adultery; denying these constitutes a repudiation of revelation. This passage thus situates the theological debate on human actions within the broader framework of knowledge types and their consequences for error.

Justification for Ta'wil of Early Scholars' Statements
Sanusi justifies interpreting early scholars' statements that appear to affirm kufr as dialectical tactics.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why early scholars' apparent statements of kufr should be interpreted as dialectical arguments in debates with innovators.

Core Idea
Early scholars' apparent kufr statements were dialectical weapons, not their actual beliefs.

Sanusi addresses a critical methodological issue: how to handle statements from early scholars that appear to affirm kufr. Rather than taking them literally, we must understand them as dialectical moves in debates with innovators. This lesson explains the necessity of ta'wil in such cases and the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read its literal translation to grasp the overall argument.
Notice the flow from stating the problem to providing the interpretive solution.
2
Identify the core claim
Sanusi claims that it is obligatory to interpret (ta'wil) statements that seem to affirm kufr. Identify what he says should be interpreted and why.
He explicitly mentions 'the kufr of those who attribute invention to other than Allah' and 'consensus on kufr of whoever does not affirm the generality of attributes'.
3
Understand the dialectical context
The interpretation is that the early scholars spoke 'by way of dialectic' (jadaliyyan) in debating innovators. Reflect on why dialectical statements are not creedal declarations.
The purpose was to force innovators into absurd consequences of their own corrupt principles.

Apply the lesson

This prevents misinterpretation of Islamic scholarly heritage.

So we should not take them literally.

The Reductio Refutation of Skepticism
Sanusi refutes a skeptic by turning his own principle against him.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi uses a reductio ad absurdum argument to refute a skeptic who denies the possibility of multiple gods, exposing the logical inconsistency in the skeptic's own principle.

Core Idea
If absence of evidence doesn't prove absence, then everything is possible – even beetles as gods.

Sanusi presents a powerful logical attack on a skeptic who claims that there is no evidence for multiple gods. By using the skeptic's own admission that absence of evidence is not proof of absence, Sanusi forces the skeptic into an absurd conclusion. This lesson walks through the argument step by step.

Guided Path

Notice the Quranic verse at the end – it confirms the outcome.

Sanusi refers to the skeptic's claim that 'absence of evidence does not necessitate absence of the thing evidenced.' Identify this as the key principle the skeptic holds.
3

Sanusi proposes a test: could contemptible animals like beetles be gods? The skeptic says no, due to lack of evidence. But Sanusi points out that by the skeptic's own principle, lack of evidence does not disprove beetle-gods. This creates a contradiction.
The absurd conclusion is that beetles might be gods – something the skeptic must reject.
4
Reflect on the Quranic ending
The verse 'Then the disbeliever was confounded' shows the skeptic is left speechless. Consider how this argument silences the skeptic without needing further evidence.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum – reducing an opponent's position to an absurdity.

Yes; following is not perfection, but it is guidance that the Shari‘a has approved.

The Four Madhabs and the Greater Mass
Affirms the four madhabs as the only ones to follow today.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why following the four Sunni madhabs is obligatory and how they represent the 'greater mass' (al-sawad al-a‘zam) of Muslims.

Core Idea
The four madhabs are the only surviving true schools; following them is following the Muslim majority.

After establishing the goodness of taqlid in general, the passage now specifies which scholars are permissible to follow today: the four madhabs. It cites two authorities—al-Dahlawi and al-Sawi—to affirm that these schools represent the 'greater mass' and that leaving them is misguidance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four madhabs
The passage names the four imams: Abu Hanifa, Malik, al-Shafi‘i, and Ahmad ibn Hanbal. Note that their followers today represent the Muslim masses.
These are the only ones considered permissible to follow.
2
Understand al-Dahlawi's argument
He quotes the hadith 'Follow the greater mass' and explains that since only these four true schools remain, following them is following the greater mass.
This links taqlid with communal solidarity.
3
Analyze al-Sawi's ruling
Al-Sawi states that it is not permissible to follow any other school, even if it agrees with a sahih hadith or a Companion's saying. Leaving the four is being misguided and misguiding.
This is a strong statement on the exclusivity of the four madhabs.

The Scholars of Ahl al-Sunna and the Value of Taqlid
Classifies scholars and concludes on taqlid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the imams of the four madhabs are included in Ahl al-Sunna wa al-Jama‘a and why taqlid, though not perfection, is a divinely approved guidance.

Core Idea
The four madhabs belong to Ahl al-Sunna, and taqlid is a valid, recognized guidance.

The passage concludes by placing the four madhabs within the broader category of Ahl al-Sunna wa al-Jama‘a. Al-Baghdadi's classification includes not only the four but also other early jurists who adhered to orthodox creed. Finally, the author affirms that taqlid is not the highest level of perfection but is a guidance approved by Shari‘a.

Read al-Baghdadi's classification

Identify the creedal conditions
These jurists believed in God's eternal attributes, rejected Qadr and I‘tizal, affirmed seeing God, and avoided tashbih and ta‘til.
This shows the link between fiqh and correct theology.
3
Understand the final statement
The passage ends: 'Yes; taqlid is not perfection, but it is guidance that the Shari‘a has approved.' This tempers the earlier strong affirmations.

Key Takeaway
Sanusi's concluding prayer reflects his humility and his desire that his work benefit all who encounter it, both in this life and the next.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Sanusi prays for neglect of worldly desires. Which desire in your life are you most clinging to, and what would it cost you to abandon it for the sake of sincerity?
?
He asks for a sincere repentance after which there is no disobedience. Is there a sin you keep rationalizing? What would true repentance require you to change today?
?
The prayer includes all who contributed to the book's transmission. How are you contributing to the spread of knowledge? Are you actively learning and teaching, or are you consuming passively?
