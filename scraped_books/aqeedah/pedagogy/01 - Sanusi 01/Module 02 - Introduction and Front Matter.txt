Commentary
This passage is an editorial introduction. The editor begins by lamenting a widespread false claim of specialization: many who claim expertise in the sciences today do not even master their basics ('abjadīyāt'). He describes this as apparent weakness and inflated ego. Then he gives an example: contemporary professors of kalam are ignorant of fundamental concepts like the realities of things, causes of knowledge, definitions of substance and accident, attributes and relations. Even more advanced topics like oneness, unity, twoness, trinity, comprehensives, the four, four existences, four worlds, seven demands, ten categories are like puzzles to them. Yet the editor asserts that goodness remains thanks to God and the scholars. He then introduces the book: a text studied by young students of kalam as an entry point, covering not only belief but also principles of usul and rational inquiry. He argues that caring for such a book is a duty. He criticizes the common approach to small heritage texts: they are merely reprinted without consulting manuscripts, with minimal commentary that is disconnected. This neglect harms manuscripts that remain buried or corrupted by publishers seeking profit. The editor assures the reader that this reprint is not simply a correction of errors; it is part of a destined series (silsila 'aqdiyya) intended to welcome students with ease. He credits Dar al-Taqwa for including this book as one of five stars in this series. The footnote clarifies 'khufuf' as speed of travel.

Commentary
