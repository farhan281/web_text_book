Core Idea
Authorship isn't always who wrote the definitions; it's who compiled them.

Commentary
This passage addresses the attribution of a short creed (al-ʿAqīda al-Ḥafīda) to Imam al-Sanusi. The author begins by listing several names under which this creed appears, noting its brevity, clarity, and accessibility. He then states the core problem: there is no definitive evidence (lā mustanad yaqṭaʿ) confirming its attribution to al-Sanusi. The only textual basis is a quotation from al-Ḥafnāwī that later scholars relied upon. However, the author observes that the multiplicity of early commentaries (tarseʿud shurūḥihā) and their agreement in wording make the attribution plausible. He then cites Imam al-Shuktanī, a commentator, who only says the creed 'was attributed' (kāna min jumlat mā nusiba ilayh) to al-Sanusi, using a tentative expression (al-ʿibāra al-mushakkika). The author interprets this as a sign of fairness (kalimat inṣāf wa-taḥqīq): had al-Shuktanī possessed conclusive proof, he would have stated it definitively. Finally, the author lists other works attributed to al-Sanusi (like a commentary on modal logic and a medical treatise) and notes that some attributions are certainly false, so he avoids discussing them. Thus, the passage carefully weighs the evidence for authorship, neither affirming nor denying it absolutely, but leaning toward probability based on the early commentary tradition.

Al-Qadi ‘Iyad intended here that al-Ghazali is a proponent of al-‘Anbari's view concerning the salvation of the mentioned people, especially the muqallida of Christians and Jews. We seek refuge for Hujjat al-Islam from such a statement — how so, when his books explicitly state the contrary? Rather, the book he attributed to himself does not contain this statement. The utmost in it — i.e., in his book 'Faysal al-Tafriqa' (p. 103) — is his statement: 'Most of the Christians of Rum and the Turks in this time are included in mercy; I mean those who are in the far reaches of Rum and the Turks and to whom the call has not reached. For they are three categories: a category to whom the name of Muhammad (sallallahu ‘alayhi wa sallam) has not reached at all; they are excused. And a category to whom his name, his sending, and his miracles have reached; they are the neighbors of the Islamic lands and those who mix with them; they are the disbelievers who will abide eternally. And a third category between the two levels: to them the name of Muhammad (sallallahu ‘alayhi wa sallam) has reached, but his sending and his description have not reached them; rather, they have heard since childhood that a liar and impostor named Muhammad claimed —'

Commentary
This is a footnote from the editor rebutting the claim that al-Ghazālī agreed with al-‘Anbari's view. It first reports that al-Qāḍī ‘Iyāḍ attributed to al-Ghazālī belief in the salvation of the mentioned groups, especially followers of Christians and Jews. The editor strongly distances al-Ghazālī from this, stating that his own books contradict it, and that the very book cited (al-Tafriqa) does not contain that claim. Then it quotes al-Ghazālī's actual position from 'Fayṣal al-Tafriqa': he divides non-Muslims into three categories regarding who receives mercy. Only those who never heard the name of the Prophet are excused; those who heard and saw miracles are eternally punished; those who heard only a distorted version are in between. This shows al-Ghazālī did not absolve all incapable people.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: al-Ghazali believed that all Christians and Jews who are muqallida are saved, even if they have some knowledge.
Correction: As shown by his own quote, al-Ghazali only excuses those who never heard the name of the Prophet at all; others are either eternal disbelievers or an intermediate category.

Key Takeaway
Al-Ghazali's actual stance distinguishes three categories based on the extent of knowledge of the Prophet; only those completely ignorant are excused.
