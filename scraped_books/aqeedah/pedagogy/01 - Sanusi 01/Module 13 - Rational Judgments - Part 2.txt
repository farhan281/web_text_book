The author describes the second madhab of ta'wil with examples.

Some scholars permit definitively interpreting ambiguous verses by contextual evidence.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the three scholarly positions (madhāhib) regarding the interpretation of ambiguous Quranic verses (al-mutashābihāt) that could bear more than one meaning. The author begins by citing examples: 'Running by Our eyes,' 'with My hands,' and 'upon the Throne established,' which are verses where the apparent literal meaning is impossible for Allah. The scholars differed into three schools.

First school (madhhab al-salaf): Obligation of tafwīḍ (delegation) – after affirming that the apparent impossible meaning is negated (tanzīh), the true meaning is delegated to Allah. This is illustrated by the famous response of Imam Mālik when asked about istiwā': he said 'al-istiwā' is known, the modality is unknown.' Here 'known' refers to the linguistic senses that are valid for Allah metaphorically, but which one is intended is unknown. Therefore, asking to specify what the Law has not specified is an innovation, and the innovator should be expelled to prevent fitna.

Second school: Permissibility of ta'wīl (interpretation) – assigning a specific meaning to the ambiguous expression based on contextual evidence or common Arabic usage, e.g., 'eye' interpreted as knowledge or sight, 'hand' as power or blessing, 'istiwā'' as domination. This is attributed to Imām al-Ḥaramayn (al-Juwaynī) and many others.

Third school: Affirming the attributes without knowing their modality (tafwīḍ al-ma'nā?) – this is described as the position of Abū al-Ḥasan al-Ashʿarī: the ambiguous expressions are understood as affirming real attributes of Allah that befit His majesty, but their true reality is unknown.

Finally, the author (likely the commentator) adds a caution (qultu): the safest approach is to express potential interpretations in terms of probability (iḥtimāl) rather than definitive assertion, because one should not be audacious and decide what has no decisive proof.

The Third Madhab and the Cautious Approach
The author presents the third madhab (Ash'ari) and adds his own recommended cautious method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third madhab of affirming attributes without knowing how, and the author's recommendation to use probabilistic language in interpretation.

Core Idea
The Ash'ari school affirms attributes while caution demands that interpretations be expressed as probabilities.

The third madhab, that of Imam al-Ash'ari, carries ambiguous expressions as affirming real attributes befitting Allah, whose true nature is unknown. The author then adds a personal note: the safest approach is to express any interpretation as a possibility rather than a certainty, to avoid presumption.

Commentary
This passage identifies the seventh critical issue: ignorance of rational judgments (al-aḥkām al-ʿaqliyya) and the Arabic language/eloquence. Sanusi asserts that such ignorance can lead to disbelief. He gives the example of how some people (likely Christians) misunderstood the Qur'anic phrase 'wa rūḥun minhu' (a spirit from Him) in Sūrat al-Nisā' 4:171. They took the preposition 'min' to mean 'part of' (tabʿīḍ), inferring that Jesus is a part of God, implying divine composition. Sanusi points out that this error involves two kinds of ignorance:

1. Ignorance of rational principles (al-qawāʿid al-ʿaqliyya): If they understood rational theology, they would realize that the concept of a composite God implies change, need for specification, and therefore temporality (ḥudūth) and contingency—attributes of created things, not God. Furthermore, if a part of God is taken away from the whole, the whole ceases to exist; but a part is not God, so God would cease to exist entirely. This is rationally absurd.

2. Ignorance of Arabic: The word 'min' has multiple meanings, not only partition (tabʿīḍ). For example, it can indicate the beginning of a point, explanation, or cause. If they were consistent, they would also interpret 'min' in other verses (e.g., 'sakhkhara lakum... minhu' from al-Jāthiya 45:13) as partition, which is absurd. Thus, proper knowledge of Arabic would prevent this error.

The author concludes that a correct understanding of both rational principles and Arabic usage is necessary to avoid falling into kufr.

The sixth introduction: On contrasting possibles. And the contrasting possibles are six: existence and non-existence, sizes, attributes, times, places, and directions. Commentary
This passage is a definition and a proof. It begins the sixth introduction (المقدمة السادسة) of Sanusi's work, dealing with 'contrasting possibles' (الممكنات المتقابلات). The author first enumerates six categories of such possibles: existence and non-existence (الوجود والعدم), sizes (المقادير), attributes (الصفات), times (الأزمنة), places (الأمكنة), and directions (الجهات). These are pairs of opposites that a body (like an atom) can equally accept. The commentary then clarifies that 'possibles' here means contingent beings (الجائزات) – things that may exist or not. 'Contrasting' means opposites that are mutually exclusive, yet the body can potentially receive either. Next, the text makes a key theological point: it is impossible for one of two equally acceptable opposites to be actualized over its counterpart without a cause (معلب) or preponderator (مرجح). Why is this impossible? Because it would entail a contradiction: the thing would be both preponderant over its opposite (since it is chosen) and equal to it (since there is no external factor distinguishing them). This is a classic kalam argument used to prove that the existence of the universe, as a possible, requires an external preponderator – namely, a necessary being (God). The passage concludes with a poetic summary by al-Qassar al-Fasi and a reference to al-Hashimi's commentary.

The Impossibility of Self-Specification and the Necessity of a Specifier
Sanusi argues against self-specification and proves the necessity of a specifier.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a body cannot specify itself with one opposite over another without an external specifier, based on the logical impossibility of a preponderance without a preponderator.

Core Idea
Equal opposites cannot favor themselves; a specifier must decide.

After establishing that a body equally accepts both members of a contingent opposite, Sanusi now argues that the body cannot on its own prefer one over the other. To do so would force a contradiction: the body would be both equal and superior to its opposite. Therefore, a specifier (mukhassis) is necessary to tip the balance.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument text
Read the Arabic passage. It presents the logical problem: if a body is found with one opposite despite equal acceptance, and there is no external cause, then the body would be causing its own preference.
2
Identify the contradiction
Sanusi says self-preference would combine two opposites: the body being both equal to its counterpart and preponderant over it. Understand why this is impossible.
Equal means no preference; preponderant means preference. They cannot coexist.
3
See the conclusion
The heading 'تعيُّنُ افتقار الجرم لمخصص' states the result: every body necessarily needs a specifier. This is the key takeaway.
The specifier is external; it cannot be the body itself.
4
Reflect on the logic
Try to restate the argument: If X is equal to Y in all respects, X cannot prefer itself over Y without a cause. Therefore, a cause must exist.
This is a foundational proof in kalam for the existence of God.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof. It begins by introducing the sixth introduction on the contingent opposites. The author states that the contingent opposites are six: existence and non-existence, quantities, qualities, times, places, and directions. He then clarifies his terminology: 'contingent' means possible things; 'opposites' means mutually exclusive things that a body (jirm) accepts each one with equal acceptance as its opposite. This sets up the problem: a body can equally receive either member of a pair of opposites. The author then argues that despite this equal receptivity, the body is found in actuality to be specified with one of the opposites (e.g., it exists rather than not, has a specific size, etc.). This specification without a preponderator is impossible because it would require combining two contradictory things: the thing being both equal to its opposite and preferring itself over it at the same time. Therefore, a specifier (mukhassis) is necessary. The author then explicitly states the conclusion: after this reflection, it is necessarily certain that every body needs a specifier, which is an agent (fa'il) that specifies it with existence instead of non-existence (despite equal possibility), with a specific quantity (length, shortness, intermediate), with a specific attribute (motion, rest, whiteness, blackness, knowledge, ignorance, etc.), with a specific time, with a specific place, and with a specific direction. Finally, he generalizes: every body in the universe – heavens, earth, Throne, Footstool, humans, jinn, angels, and all their types and individuals – is originated (hadith) and in necessary need of the Lord (al-Mawla) with a necessary and permanent need.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the contingency of all bodies and their need for a specificator (mukhassis) — that is, a Creator who selects one possibility over others. The argument proceeds as follows:

The author begins by stating the conclusion: it is definitively established that every body (jirm) is in need of a specificator. He then defines the specificator as an agent who specifies the body with existence rather than non-existence (both being equally possible for the body). He notes two views: one holds that existence and non-existence are equally possible, another holds that existence is more likely (because it is original in every contingent). But the key point is that the specificator chooses.

Next, the author enumerates five types of specification that the specificator performs:
1. Specification with a particular quantity (length, shortness, medium).
2. Specification with a particular attribute (motion, rest, color, knowledge, etc.).
3. Specification with a particular time (not earlier or later).
4. Specification with a particular place.
5. Specification with a particular direction (south, north, east, west).

After this enumeration, the author draws the conclusion: every body in the entire cosmos — heavens, earth, Throne, Footstool, humans, jinn, angels, all types — is contingent (hadith) and in necessary need of the Creator.

On page 231, the author reinforces the proof by stating that each of the specific properties (existence versus non-existence, quantity, attribute, time, place, direction) itself testifies to the body's need for a specificator. He then personifies the body as speaking to the observer: everything you see about me — my existence, my properties — would have been equally likely not to exist or to be different, had it not been for the specification of a powerful and irresistible will.

Finally, the author gives a concrete example: Zayd (a hypothetical person). Zayd's essence was specified with existence after real non-existence. His attributes (height, width, color, location, time, direction) are all particular and could have been otherwise, but they were chosen. This illustrates the general principle that every contingent being shows its need for a specificator through its specific properties.

The passage avoids circular reasoning by starting from observable properties of bodies and arguing that these properties are not necessary in themselves but are contingent, hence they require an external cause that selects them.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the contingency of all bodies and their need for a specificator (mukhassis) — that is, a Creator who selects one possibility over others. The argument proceeds as follows:

The author begins by stating the conclusion: it is definitively established that every body (jirm) is in need of a specificator. He then defines the specificator as an agent who specifies the body with existence rather than non-existence (both being equally possible for the body). He notes two views: one holds that existence and non-existence are equally possible, another holds that existence is more likely (because it is original in every contingent). But the key point is that the specificator chooses.

Next, the author enumerates five types of specification that the specificator performs:
1. Specification with a particular quantity (length, shortness, medium).
2. Specification with a particular attribute (motion, rest, color, knowledge, etc.).
3. Specification with a particular time (not earlier or later).
4. Specification with a particular place.
5. Specification with a particular direction (south, north, east, west).

After this enumeration, the author draws the conclusion: every body in the entire cosmos — heavens, earth, Throne, Footstool, humans, jinn, angels, all types — is contingent (hadith) and in necessary need of the Creator.

On page 231, the author reinforces the proof by stating that each of the specific properties (existence versus non-existence, quantity, attribute, time, place, direction) itself testifies to the body's need for a specificator. He then personifies the body as speaking to the observer: everything you see about me — my existence, my properties — would have been equally likely not to exist or to be different, had it not been for the specification of a powerful and irresistible will.

Finally, the author gives a concrete example: Zayd (a hypothetical person). Zayd's essence was specified with existence after real non-existence. His attributes (height, width, color, location, time, direction) are all particular and could have been otherwise, but they were chosen. This illustrates the general principle that every contingent being shows its need for a specificator through its specific properties.

The passage avoids circular reasoning by starting from observable properties of bodies and arguing that these properties are not necessary in themselves but are contingent, hence they require an external cause that selects them.

Answering the Infinite Regress Objection to Universal Visibility
Objection and refutation on infinite regress regarding universal visibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection of infinite regress against the universal visibility of existents is raised and refuted through al-Qadi's distinction about the obstacle's scope.

Core Idea
An obstacle prevents itself from being seen only by its own locus, preserving universal visibility.

The Ahl al-Sunna hold that every existent can be seen if no obstacle prevents. But if vision itself is an existent, doesn't it need to be seen? This leads to a potential infinite regress of obstacles. In this passage, Sanusi presents the objection and al-Qadi's masterful reply, showing how a careful distinction about the obstacle's effect rescues the universal principle.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the opening objection: if vision applies to all existents, then vision itself must be visible. If we do not see our own vision, an obstacle must exist. That obstacle is also existent, requiring another obstacle, ad infinitum.
This is the classic infinite regress argument against the universal principle.
2
Al-Qadi's first answer
Al-Qadi argues that the first obstacle prevents seeing both the object it obstructs and itself. Therefore no further obstacle is needed, halting the regress.
The key move is that the obstacle's effect includes itself.
3
The counter-objection
An objector claims that if the obstacle prevents seeing itself, then its invisibility is an intrinsic property (self-characteristic), which would break the universal claim that all existents are visible.
This challenges the uniformity of the universal proposition.
4
Al-Qadi's final refinement
Al-Qadi clarifies: the obstacle's self-prevention is specific to the one in whom it inheres; others can see the obstacle. Thus the universal statement holds for all existents, as the obstacle is visible to others.
This distinction preserves the universal principle.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Truth requires that the speaker believes what they say.
Correction: Ahl al-Sunnah define truth solely by correspondence to external reality, regardless of the speaker's belief.

Checkpoint
What is the definition of truth (ṣidq) according to Ahl al-Sunnah in this passage?
A) Correspondence of verbal existence to mental existence
B) Correspondence of verbal existence to external existence
C) Correspondence of the report to the speaker's belief
D) Absence of contradiction between speech and intention

Key Takeaway
Ahl al-Sunnah define truth by correspondence of speech to external reality, not to the speaker's belief, which allows for truthful statements from liars and necessitates ta'wil of verses where hypocrites are called liars despite speaking truthfully.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you speak about your faith, do you ever say things that are factually true but do not match your inner state? Have you considered that your words might be judged by external reality, not by your sincerity? How can you ensure your speech always aligns with truth?
?
Imagine if Allah held you accountable not for what you meant, but for what you actually said and its correspondence to reality. Would you be a truthful person? What would you change?
?
The hypocrites' statement was true, yet they were liars. How often do you say things that are true in words but your heart is far from them? What would it take for your tongue and heart to be in complete harmony?
