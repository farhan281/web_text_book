Classification of Existents by Locus and Specifier
Sanusi classifies existents by their relationship to locus and specifier.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourfold classification of existents according to Ashʿarī theology based on their need for a locus and a specifier.

Core Idea
Existents are divided by whether they need a locus and a creator — only God needs neither.

This preface establishes a foundational ontological framework for kalam. Sanusi introduces four categories of existents based on two criteria: whether an entity requires a substrate (locus) to exist and whether it requires an external agent (specifier) to bring it into existence. Understanding this division is essential for grasping the distinction between God, His attributes, bodies, and accidents.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the repetition of the key terms 'المحل' and 'المخصص'.
2
Identify the two criteria
The passage uses two criteria: need for a locus (محل) and need for a specifier (مخصص). Identify each category's relation to these.
A locus is a substrate in which something inheres; a specifier is that which brings something into existence.
3
List the four categories
Write down the four categories: God's essence, accidents, bodies, God's attributes. Note what each depends on.
Use the Arabic terms: الذات, الأعراض, الأجرام, الصفات.
4
Reflect on God's attributes
Why are God's attributes placed in a separate category? They exist in a locus (God's essence) but do not need a specifier because they are eternal.
This distinguishes them from accidents, which also need a specifier.
5
Test your understanding
Answer the checkpoint question to ensure you can match each category with its properties.
The question asks about bodies needing a specifier but not a locus.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of existents according to two criteria: whether they need a locus (a substrate in which they inhere) and whether they need a specifier (an external agent that brings them into existence). The author identifies four categories:

1. That which needs neither locus nor specifier: this is only God's essence (ذات الله), who is self-sufficient.

2. That which needs both locus and specifier: accidents (الأعراض), which exist in a subject and require a creator.

3. That which needs a specifier but not a locus: bodies (الأجرام), which are self-subsistent but require a creator.

4. That which exists in a locus but does not need a specifier: God's attributes (صفات الله), which inhere in His essence but are not created.

This division is foundational for Ashʿarī ontology, distinguishing God, His attributes, bodies, and accidents.

The Reductio: A Third God for Neutral Acts
Sanusi refutes dualism by showing it logically requires a third god for neutral acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi uses the existence of neutral acts to refute the Magian dualist claim that good and evil require separate creators.

Core Idea
If every act is either good or evil, neutral acts need a third source – a reductio ad absurdum.

Sanusi continues his refutation of dualism by focusing on actions that are neither good nor evil. The Magians claimed that good and evil must come from different sources. But what about neutral acts like the motion of a stone? Sanusi argues that consistency forces the dualist to either accept a third god or deny obvious reality.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own logical flow come through.
Notice the conditional structure: 'if they deny... then they are obstinate.'
2
Identify the two alternatives
Sanusi presents two possible outcomes for the dualist: either affirm a third god for neutral acts, or deny that neutral acts exist. Identify both in the text.
3
Reflect on the refutation's strength
Ask yourself why this argument is effective. It exposes an internal contradiction in dualism: either it expands to tritheism or it rejects observable reality.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.

This passage is the first refutation. Sanusi argues that if every action is classified as either good or evil, then actions that are neither good nor evil (neutral acts) would require a third god to produce them. This follows from the Magians' own reasoning that distinct types of action require distinct agents. If the Magians deny the existence of neutral acts, they are being obstinate and denying something that is clearly known to exist, like the motion of a neutral object. Thus their dualism either expands to tritheism or denies reality.

Dualism Implies Gods Are Contingent
Sanusi argues that the dualist position implies the gods are contingent and require a third.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi refutes dualism by showing that the specialization of the two gods requires a third cause, making them contingent.

Core Idea
Specialization proves dependency: dualism collapses into tritheism.

Sanusi now delivers a powerful logical blow against the Magian dualists. Rather than directly attacking their claim of two eternal principles, he examines what follows from their own description of those principles. Since each god is defined by a specific motive (good or evil), this very specialization implies that something external must have assigned them these roles. If they were truly self-sufficient, they would not need such determination.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing, then read the literal translation. Let Sanusi's argument structure become clear.
Notice that this is a short, dense sentence carrying a complete refutation.
2
Identify the logical move
Sanusi argues that specialization (takhṣīṣ) forces dependency (iftiqār) and temporal origination (ḥudūth). Trace how he moves from the dualists' own claim to a contradiction.
The key term is 'takhṣīṣ' – it is the hinge of the argument.
3
Reflect on the refutation's force
Ask yourself: why is this refutation decisive? It shows that the Magian gods cannot be eternal and self-sufficient because they need an external specifier for their defining motives.
This connects to the broader theme of tawḥīd versus shirk.

This third refutation targets the metaphysical implications. The Magians claim each god is defined by a specific motive (bā‘ith). Sanusi argues that this specialization (takhṣīṣ) implies that the gods are originated (ḥādith) and dependent on a third cause to assign each its specific motive. If they are eternal and independent, they should not require an external cause for their defining characteristic. Hence, the Magian gods cannot be truly divine.

Refuting Dualism: Four Arguments Against Two Gods
Sanusi refutes the dualist belief in two gods with four logical arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four logical arguments Sanusi uses to refute the dualist (Magian) belief in two gods.

Core Idea
Dualism leads to more absurdities than it solves.

In this passage, Sanusi takes on the dualist belief that there are two gods—one of good and one of evil. He presents four arguments that show the logical inconsistencies of this view, demonstrating that it cannot stand up to rational scrutiny. By the end, he concludes that anyone who understands God's transcendence will see the folly of this position.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage through
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Note the structure: four arguments marked by 'وأيضاً'.
The passage also includes a footnote explaining key terms.
2
First argument: neutral acts require a third god
Identify the first argument: if dualists split acts into good and evil, they must also account for neutral acts, leading to a third god. Denying neutral acts is stubborn.
Sanusi also mentions a fourth god for mixed acts.
3
Second argument: observation contradicts dualism
Notice the argument from the witnessed world: the same person can do both good and evil, so the dualist implication that good-doers cannot do evil is false.
This is an empirical refutation.
4
Third and fourth arguments: temporal origination and mutual hindrance
Understand that dualism implies the two gods are created (need a specifier) and that if they will opposite things simultaneously, they would hinder each other, which is impossible for true gods.
The conclusion ties back to God's transcendence from motives.

And there follows from them, on the basis of this corrupt consideration which they considered: the affirmation of a third god, to do from possible things that which is neither good nor evil. (1) And there follows from them the affirmation of a fourth, to do what combines good and evil, like humans. And if they deny this category of possible things and restrict them to two categories — namely good and evil — then they are blustering and denying what is established with certainty to exist. And also: there follows from them in the witnessed world that the doer from among creatures of good cannot be a doer of evil, and the doer of evil cannot be a doer of good, and observation necessitates the falsity of that. And also: there follows from their statement the temporal origination of the two gods, and their need for a third who specifies each of them with what it is distinguished by, from the cause of good or the cause of evil. And also: there follows between the two supposed gods mutual hindrance when one of them wills to bring about good in a place and the other wills to bring about evil in it at one time. And whoever knows the necessity of the transcendence of the Majestic Lord, blessed and exalted, from motives and being described with the cause for action (2), and His transcendence from the transmission of perfection or deficiency from actions to His sublime Essence, it becomes clear to him the delirium of these Magian disbelievers in what they believed. (2) His saying: "and being described with the cause" is an explanatory apposition of "motives", for motive and cause here are synonymous; and it is the final cause of the action. The Eternal, exalted, is transcendent from final causes in His Essence, attributes, and actions. So for His Essence there is no attribute it is described by, nor for His attributes is there a limit at which their relations stop, nor for His actions are there impulses that compel Him to act or refrain.

Commentary
This passage refutes the dualist (Magian) belief in two independent creators — one of good, one of evil — through four logical arguments. The author begins by noting that on the basis of the dualist's own corrupt reasoning, it would follow that they must affirm a third god to create things that are neither good nor evil (e.g., neutral acts). If they deny the existence of such neutral things and restrict possible things to only good and evil, they are being stubborn and denying what is clearly known to exist. A footnote adds that they would also need a fourth god for things that combine good and evil, like human acts. The second argument: in the witnessed world, it is not the case that a doer of good cannot do evil and vice versa; observation shows that the same person does both, so the dualist implication is false. The third argument: their view leads to the temporal origination (ḥudūth) of both gods, because they are specified to good or evil — thus they need a third specifying principle. The fourth argument: if two gods exist, and one wants to create good in a place while the other wants to create evil in the same place at the same time, mutual hindrance (tamānuʿ) would occur, preventing either from acting, which is impossible for true gods. The author concludes that whoever recognizes God's transcendence from motives (aʿrāḍ) and final causes (bāʿith) — as explained in the footnote — will see the absurdity of the Magian belief. The footnote clarifies that 'motive' and 'cause' are synonymous, and God is free from any purpose or impulse in His actions.

The Logical Contradictions in the Trinity
Sanusi refutes the Christian Trinity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sanusi argues that the Christian doctrine of the Trinity involves logical contradictions, such as combining unity and multiplicity, and treating attributes as separate gods.

Core Idea
The Trinity's claim of three gods in one is a logical impossibility.

Sanusi begins his refutation of Christian theology by addressing the Trinity. He points out that Christians mistakenly inferred from the multiplicity of causes in the world that God must be composed of multiple hypostases. Then he exposes the internal contradictions in their formulation, showing that their claims cannot be rationally sustained.

Proof that the world is created by divine choice
Sanusi proves divine choice by refuting necessitarian views.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's definitive proof that the world was created by Allah's free choice, not by necessity or nature.

Core Idea
The world's existence cannot be necessary; only divine choice can explain it.

Sanusi argues against the necessitarian view that the world emanated from God necessarily. He uses definitive proof to show that if creation were necessary, either infinite regress or an eternal obstacle would make the world impossible. Thus, only divine choice can account for the world's existence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Let the author's own words and structure sink in before analysis.
Notice the strong opening: 'This doctrine is clearly corrupt.'
2
Identify the two rejected positions
Sanusi rejects two types of necessity: 'luzum fi al-azal' (causality) and 'luzum fima yazal' (nature). Understand the difference between these two.
Causality implies a necessary link in pre-eternity; nature implies a necessary, never-ending process.
3
Trace the arguments against each
For causality, he argues that if a condition for the world's existence were absent in pre-eternity, infinite regress results. For nature, if an obstacle existed, it would be eternal and impossible to remove, preventing the world forever.
Both arguments lead to the conclusion that the world could never exist if either were true.
4
Conclude why choice is necessary
Since both necessary paths lead to impossibility, only the way of choice (ikhtiyar) remains. This means Allah created the world voluntarily, not by compulsion.
This is a strong rational proof supporting the Islamic doctrine of divine free will.

Commentary

Bodies Do Not Need a Locus

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Bodies are independent substances; requiring a locus leads to absurdities like circularity or infinite regress.

After establishing bodies' need for a specifier, Sanusi now demonstrates that bodies do not require a locus. He provides three distinct proofs showing why a body cannot subsist in another body like an accident does. These arguments rely on the nature of bodies as substances and the impossibility of certain logical outcomes.

Such a being cannot be opposed by any contingent thing. The author then provides a concrete example: Zayd. His essence was specified to exist after a real non-existence, and his accidental attributes (e.g., length, color, place, time, direction) were specified as they are out of an infinite range of possibilities. Thus, every particular detail of Zayd's existence points to the choosing action of a Creator. This is a classic kalam argument from specification (takhṣīṣ) to establish a voluntary agent (fāʻil mukhtār).

Proofs for Allah's Speech
Sanusi provides scriptural and rational proofs that Allah possesses speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the scriptural and rational proofs that establish speech as a necessary attribute of Allah.

Core Idea
Allah's speech is proven by scripture and reason, and is necessary, not contingent.

Sanusi begins by stating that the Qur'an, Sunnah, and consensus all affirm that Allah speaks through commands, prohibitions, and other forms. Then he offers a rational argument: since Allah knows all things, He can validly speak about them, and any valid attribute of Allah is necessary. This lesson unpacks both proofs.

The scriptural proof cites three sources: Quran, Sunnah, and consensus.
2

3
Understand the rational proof
Follow the logical chain: Allah knows all things → it is valid for Him to speak about them → valid attributes are necessary → therefore speech is necessary.
The key move is that anything validly attributable to Allah must be necessary, not merely possible.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Notice how the rational proof transforms the validity of speech into necessity, answering those who might think speech is a contingent attribute.
This connects to the broader lesson that Allah's attributes are all necessary.

Commentary
This passage provides two proofs for the attribute of speech. First, the scriptural proof: the Qur'an, Sunnah, and consensus all affirm that Allah speaks through commands, prohibitions, promises, threats, etc. Second, the rational proof: since Allah knows all things, it is valid that He speaks about them. Moreover, any attribute that is validly attributable to Allah is necessary for Him, because it is impossible for Him to have merely contingent attributes. Therefore, speech is a necessary attribute of Allah.
