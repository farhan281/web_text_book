Three Schools on Human Acts
Sanusi enumerates the three schools on the origin of human voluntary acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three theological schools on the origin of human voluntary acts and how the Sunni position balances divine omnipotence with human responsibility.

Core Idea
Three schools, one truth: All acts are from God, but humans have a non-effective role.

Sanusi begins the second introduction by classifying the major Islamic theological positions on human voluntary acts. This passage is essential for understanding the Ash'ari doctrine of acquisition (kasb) that follows. It contrasts the extreme views of Jabriyya (all acts from God alone) and Qadariyya (voluntary acts from humans alone) with the Sunni middle path.

Guided Path
1
Read the three schools
Read the Arabic passage once to identify the three schools named. Then read the translation to grasp their positions.
Notice the symmetry: Jabriyya and Qadariyya are opposites, while Ahl al-Sunna combines elements of both.
2
Analyze the Jabriyya position
The Jabriyya hold that all acts exist solely by eternal power, with no temporal power. Ask yourself: what does this imply for human responsibility?
3
Analyze the Qadariyya position
The Qadariyya hold that voluntary acts exist solely by temporal power, either directly or by generation. Compare this with the Jabriyya: they give full power to humans in voluntary acts.
The phrase 'directly or by generation' refers to two modes of causation.
4
Analyze the Sunni position
Ahl al-Sunna hold that all acts exist solely by eternal power, but voluntary acts are accompanied by a temporal power that has no effect. Understand how this differs from both extremes.
This is the foundation for the doctrine of kasb.
5
Reflect on the distinctions
Ask: how does the Sunni position preserve both divine sovereignty and human accountability? The key is 'accompaniment without effect'.
This will be clarified in the next lesson on kasb.

The second introduction: On the schools concerning the voluntary acts of the animal. Commentary
This passage presents the three major theological schools on the origin of human voluntary acts. It is the second 'introduction' (مقدمة) of a larger work. The three schools are: (1) Jabriyya (extreme predestinarians) who hold that all acts, without exception, are produced solely by God's eternal power (القدرة الأزلية) and that human beings have no power whatsoever; (2) Qadariyya (free-will advocates) who hold that voluntary acts are produced solely by the human temporal power (القدرة الحادثة), either directly (مباشرة) or through causation (تولّداً); (3) Ahl al-Sunna (the orthodox Sunnis) who hold that all acts exist solely by God's eternal power, but voluntary acts are accompanied (مقارنة) by a human temporal power that has no effective influence (لا تأثير لها) either directly or through generation. The term 'acquisition' (الكسب) is then defined: it is the relation (تعلق) of the temporal power to the act (المقدور) in its own locus (محلها) without any effective causation. This is the Ash'ari doctrine of kasb, which affirms human responsibility without compromising divine omnipotence.

The Doctrine of Kasb
Sanusi defines acquisition (kasb) as the relation of temporal power to the act without effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari definition of acquisition (kasb) as a non-causal relation of human power to the act.

Core Idea
Kasb is a relation without effect—human responsibility without causal power.

Having outlined the three schools, Sanusi now defines the key term 'kasb' (acquisition) that characterizes the Sunni position. This definition resolves the tension between divine omnipotence and human accountability by describing human involvement as a mere relation without causal influence.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of kasb
Read the Arabic sentence defining kasb. Notice the key phrase: 'without effect' (من غير تأثير).
The term 'maqdur' (المقدور) refers to the act that is empowered.
2
Analyze the components
Kasb is defined as 'the relation of the temporal power to the maqdur in its own locus'. Identify each component: temporal power, relation, locus, and the negation of effect.
The 'locus' (محلها) likely refers to the human being as the site of power.
3
Understand 'without effect'
The phrase 'without effect' is crucial: the temporal power does not cause the act. Ask: why is this needed? It preserves divine creation of all acts while still attributing the act to the human in some sense.
This distinguishes kasb from the Qadariyya view of direct causation.
4
Connect to the Sunni school
Relate this definition back to the Sunni position from the previous lesson. The 'accompaniment without effect' is precisely what kasb means.

The Three Schools on Voluntary Acts and the Definition of Kasb
Sanusi enumerates and defines the three schools on voluntary actions and defines kasb.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three theological positions on the nature of voluntary human actions and the Sunni doctrine of kasb (acquisition).

Core Idea
Human power accompanies but does not cause — the key to the Sunni middle path between Jabr and Qadr.

In this passage, Sanusi lays out the three competing schools on how voluntary actions relate to divine and human power. He begins by presenting two extreme views — the Jabriyya who deny any human efficacy, and the Qadariyya who affirm complete human autonomy. Then he articulates the Sunni position, which affirms God as the sole creator of all actions while preserving a non-causal role for human power. He ends by defining the technical term 'kasb' that captures this delicate balance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three schools
Read the passage and note the three names: Jabriyya, Qadariyya, and Ahl al-Sunna. These are the competing positions on who or what causes voluntary actions.
Remember that 'Jabriyya' comes from 'jabr' (compulsion), and 'Qadariyya' from 'qadar' (power).
2
Understand each school's position
Examine the definitions: Jabriyya says only God's eternal power acts; Qadariyya says only human temporal power acts; Ahl al-Sunna says all actions are from God's eternal power, but voluntary actions are accompanied by human power without influence.
Focus on the words 'only' (faqat) and 'accompaniment' (muqarana).
3
Analyze the Sunni position carefully
Notice that the Sunni position affirms God as the sole creator of all actions, yet human power is present. The key phrase is 'without influence, neither directly nor by generation.' This is the doctrine of kasb.
Sanusi explicitly denies any causal role for human power.
4
Define kasb in your own words
Kasb is defined as the relation of human power to the act without causing it. Try to rephrase this concept to ensure understanding.
Kasb preserves human responsibility without attributing creation to humans.

The Second Introduction: Concerning the Schools on the Voluntary Actions of Living Beings. And the schools concerning actions are three: the school of the Jabriyya, the school of the Qadariyya, and the school of Ahl al-Sunna (the People of the Sunna). Commentary
This passage is a classification and definition passage. Sanusi introduces the Second Introduction concerning the divergent schools on the nature of voluntary actions of living beings (specifically humans). He states that there are three schools: Jabriyya (determinists), Qadariyya (those who affirm human power), and Ahl al-Sunna (the orthodox). He then defines each school in order. The Jabriyya hold that all actions exist solely through God's eternal power, with no accompaniment of a temporal (created) power in the creature. The Qadariyya hold that voluntary actions exist solely through the temporal power of the creature, either directly (mubasharatan) or by generation (tawallud, i.e., one action causing another naturally). The Ahl al-Sunna hold that all actions exist solely through God's eternal power, but voluntary actions are accompanied by a temporal power that has no influence whatsoever, neither directly nor by generation. This accompanies is crucial: the creature's power is present but not causally effective. Sanusi then defines the term 'kasb' (acquisition): it is the relation of the temporal power to the thing that is within its capacity (al-maqdur) in its locus (i.e., the specific act) without any influence. So the creature 'acquires' the act in the sense of being the locus of God's power, not as a true cause.

The Three Schools on Human Actions and the Jabriyya View
Sanusi enumerates three schools and defines the Jabriyya position.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three theological schools regarding voluntary actions and the specific definition of the Jabriyya position.

Core Idea
For the Jabriyya, no created power exists—only God's eternal power acts.

Sanusi begins the second introduction of his work by classifying the major theological positions on how human actions occur. Before diving into detailed arguments, he first maps the terrain: three schools—Jabriyya, Qadariyya, and Ahl al-Sunna. He then immediately defines the first school, the Jabriyya, whose radical view denies any created power in the agent. This sets the stage for understanding the orthodox Sunna position, which we will explore in subsequent lessons.

Read the Arabic passage once without pausing. Then read the literal translation to get a sense of Sanusi's concise style.

Locate the names of the three schools: Jabriyya, Qadariyya, and Ahl al-Sunna. Understand that these are three different answers to one question: where does the power behind an action reside?

3
Analyze the Jabriyya definition
Sanusi defines the Jabriyya position with two key phrases: 'by the eternal power alone' (bi-l-qudra al-azaliyya faqaṭ) and 'without any accompaniment of a temporal power' (min ghayri muqāranatin li-qudra ḥāditha). Understand that 'eternal power' refers to God's power, and 'temporal power' refers to a created power in the agent.
This definition completely denies human agency—a key point of contention.
4
Reflect on the significance
Ask yourself: why would anyone hold such a view? What theological concerns might drive someone to deny human power entirely? This will help you appreciate the debates that follow.
This connects to the larger discussion of qadar (divine decree) and human responsibility.

Commentary
This passage defines the middle position of Ahl al-Haqq (the People of Truth) on the issue of human actions, refuting both the Jabriyya (extreme predestinarians) and the Qadariyya (free-will advocates). He is compelled in that he has no real effect on his actions—he is merely a vessel in which Allah creates events and accidents, with no independent agent. He is a chooser in that Allah's custom (sunnah) runs such that he does not constantly experience the act being done to him; sometimes Allah creates in him aversion or resolve, and supplies the act according to need. Thus, the servant perceives himself as a free agent, capable of action and inaction in the apparent sense, without feeling coercion. This refutes those who, due to ignorance, claim that they have some independent action. The passage concludes by praising Allah, whose subtle subduing hides His compulsion, leading some to misunderstand and arrogantly claim acts for themselves.

Defining Kasb: The Three Qualifications
Sanusi defines kasb, its relation to human power, and three qualifications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari definition of kasb (acquisition) and the three qualifications that distinguish it from creation, actions outside human power, and the Qadariyya belief in human causality.

Core Idea
Kasb is attachment without effect—a middle path between compulsion and self-creation.

In this passage, Sanusi clarifies the central term 'kasb' upon which human accountability rests. He first laments widespread misunderstanding, then presents the correct definition: the attachment of human power to an act within its locus, without any causal effect. He then guards this definition with three crucial qualifications that prevent confusion with divine creation, actions beyond human reach, and the Qadariyya heresy. This lesson unpacks each qualification and its theological purpose.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of kasb
Begin by reading the Arabic and translation of the core definition: kasb is the attachment of the originated power to the object of power in its locus without effect.
Notice the key phrase 'without effect'—this is the heart of the Ash'ari position.
2
Identify the three qualifications
Find the three 'ihtirazat' (guards) introduced by 'fahtrazna'. Each one refines the definition by excluding something that might be confused with kasb.
The qualifications are sequentially marked: al-haditha, fi mahalliha, min ghayri tathir.
3
Understand the first qualification: originated power
The word 'originated' excludes the eternal power of Allah. The attachment of eternal power is called creation (ikhitra'), not kasb. This distinguishes human acts from divine acts.
4
Understand the second qualification: in its locus
This excludes actions that occur outside the locus of human power, like throwing a stone or killing. Such acts are not acquired by the servant, yet they are still subject to reward and punishment because they are created at the time of the servant's kasb.
This shows how actions beyond one's power can still be linked to human responsibility through kasb.
5
Understand the third qualification: without effect
This refutes the Qadariyya who believe human power creates or influences actions. Instead, the attachment is one of concomitance (iqtiran) and indication (dalala), not causation.
This is the most critical guard against theological deviation.
