Sanusi's Death: Patience and God-Consciousness
Narrative of Sanusi's death pangs and spiritual serenity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi's response to death exemplifies unwavering faith and mindfulness of Allah.

Core Idea
True faith remains steadfast even in the agony of death.

This account of Sanusi's final moments offers profound lessons on how a believer should approach death. It shows that even the greatest saints experience death pangs, but their hearts remain connected to their Lord.

Guided Path
1
Read the narrative of Sanusi's death pangs
Read the Arabic passage describing the agonies of death and Sanusi's physical state. Pay attention to his patience.
Notice the vivid description.
2
Analyze his response to the shahada prompt
When his nephew prompts him to recite the shahada, Sanusi responds 'Is there anything else?' Reflect on the meaning of his reply.
He was already mindful of Allah.
3
Consider his conversation with his daughter
His daughter asks if he is leaving her, and he replies that Paradise will gather them soon. What does this teach about his hope?
4
Reflect on the falling prayer beads
When his prayer beads fall, he says 'Worship has walked, O Muhammad.' What does this indicate about his spiritual state?
He considered even the beads as part of worship.

When his death approached by three days, the agonies of death entered him. He returned groaning in anguish, leaning right and left. I looked at him—his cheeks had reddened, his eyes were lowered, and his lips were slack (1). His breathing became severe, and his ascending and descending intensified. I could not have patience for crying because of what I witnessed of the intensity of his suffering and his great patience in that. I thought that he would not survive that night, which was Saturday night. He remained that night and Sunday until after 'Asr. Then his nephew would prompt him to recite the shahada (testimony of faith) time after time. The shaykh turned to him and said in a very weak voice: 'Is there anything else?' Meaning that he (may Allah be pleased with him) was not heedless of it in his heart at that time, even if I did not utter it with the tongue. So the death agony left him. Then they rejoiced at that, and those present knew that he was of sound mind, not heedless of Allah the Exalted. His daughter (may Allah be pleased with her) would say to him at that time: 'You are walking and leaving me?' He said to her: 'Paradise will gather us soon, Allah willing.' There was in his hand (may Allah be pleased with him) a prayer bead. When the throes intensified, the prayer bead fell from his hand. He remained like that for as long as Allah willed. Then he looked for the prayer bead and did not find it in his hand, so he said: 'Worship has walked, O Muhammad'—meaning himself. He (may Allah be pleased with him) would say at his death: 'We ask Him the Exalted to make us and our loved ones at death utterers of the two words of shahada, knowing them.' He died (may Allah have mercy on him and be pleased with him) on Sunday after 'Asr, the 18th of Jumada al-Akhira of the year 895. My mother (may Allah have mercy on her) told me that the daughter of the shaykh (may Allah be pleased with her) smelled a musk fragrance in the house at the moment of her father's death, and also smelled it on his body. And Allah knows best. We ask Him the Exalted to sanctify his soul, to cause him to dwell in the highest expanses of Paradise, to make him among those who enjoy at every moment the vision of His self—exalted, without peer or likeness—to benefit us through him in this world and the next, to gather us with him by His bounty and generosity in the highest luxurious stations, by the rank of our master, our Prophet, our Mawla Muhammad (peace be upon him and his family), to the number of His creation, His pleasure, the weight of His Throne, and the ink of His words. (1) The scholar Abu Ja'far al-Balawi said: 'He was buried on Monday noon next to the grave of his righteous brother, the scholar Abu al-Hasan al-Tanluti (2) (may Allah sanctify his soul) at 'Ayn wa Anzawti (or 'Ayn Anzawa) outside Bab al-Jiyad. We attended his funeral, and it was extremely crowded; the streets were filled with people. The Sultan and those below him attended, and he offered praise befitting him. The people regretted his loss, rightly so, and it was...'

Commentary
This passage is a biographical narrative describing the final days and death of the revered scholar known as Sanusi. The account is rich with detail, illustrating his spiritual state and the reverence of those around him. It begins by noting that three days before his death, the agonies of death (sakarat al-mawt) came upon him. The narrator describes seeing him in distress, with flushed cheeks, lowered eyes, and slack lips, struggling to breathe. Despite the severity, Sanusi exhibited remarkable patience. The narrator thought he would not survive Saturday night, but he continued until Sunday afternoon. His nephew repeatedly prompted him to recite the shahada (testimony of faith). Sanusi responded weakly, 'Is there anything else?'—indicating that he was not forgetful of faith in his heart, even if he was not speaking it. This shows his constant mindfulness of God. When his daughter asked if he would leave her, he replied that Paradise would gather them soon. A poignant moment occurs when his prayer beads fall from his hand during intense throes; he later notices and says, 'Worship has walked,' meaning his own soul. He then prays that he and his loved ones die uttering the shahada knowingly. The date of death is given as Sunday after 'Asr, 18 Jumada al-Akhira 895 AH. A miraculous sign follows: the narrator's mother reports that the daughter of Sanusi smelled musk in the house at the moment of his death and also on his body. The passage concludes with a prayer for him and a burial account by al-Balawi, noting the large funeral attended by the Sultan.

Historical Praise for al-Sanusi's Creedal Works
Historians praise al-Sanusi's five creedal works and their commentaries.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why historians view al-Sanusi's five creedal works as unparalleled contributions to Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Al-Sanusi's five creeds are a golden chain in Islamic theology education.

Historians including al-Shafshāwanī and Ibn al-Qāḍī al-Miknāsī have left us glowing appraisals of Imam al-Sanūsī's creedal writings. Their words highlight both the quality and the lasting influence of works designed for systematic learning of kalām. This passage lets us see how these texts were received by later scholars.

The historian and scholar al-Shafshāwanī said about Imam al-Sanūsī: 'His five creeds and their commentaries are among the best of what has been authored in Islam; they are: al-Muqaddimah, al-Ṣughrā, Ṣughrā al-Ṣughrā, al-Wusṭā, and al-Kubrā. And this is a golden word in evaluating the Sanūsiyyāt.' And the historian Ibn al-Qāḍī al-Miknāsī sufficed in defining Imam al-Sanūsī by saying: 'The author of creeds that none among the later scholars has produced the like of.' And the series of the Sanūsiyyāt with their commentaries are among the things that have clung to the memory of history, so they were not forgotten. Despite the abundance of creedal works and their variety in size and methodology, and their entry into the library of kalām with full confidence and dignity—the Sanūsiyyāt had the greatest effect in the Islamic West and then its East, as they entered with a firm foot the systematic teaching of kalām, due to what distinguished them from other books of the series.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical and methodological introduction describing the unique structure and impact of Imam Sanusi's series of theological works. It begins by stating that belief starts with the most concise abridgment and progresses through levels to the most extensive. This arrangement was not unprecedented; it was anticipated by scholars like al-Ghazali and al-Taftazani, who wrote graded works. The Sanusiyyat had a massive impact, with many commentaries and glosses, especially on the Sughra. Finally, the passage reiterates that the novelty is the layered authorship, ensuring each text offers new benefit despite their varying sizes.

Key Takeaway
Describing God with attributes that contradict His true nature — such as having a body or a child — demonstrates a failure to know Him, regardless of one's claims.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Have you ever caught yourself describing Allah in a way that is not based on the Qur'an and sunnah, such as imagining a form or limit? What will you do to replace that with accurate descriptions from revelation?
?
The dispute in Qayrawan almost led to violence over a theological question. Is there any belief about Allah that you hold so strongly that you would argue or cut ties over it? How can this story remind you to seek knowledge with humility and correct description?
?
If someone asked you to describe the God you worship, would your description match the one in this story ('did not beget nor was begotten, and there is none equal to Him')? What aspects of your daily thoughts about Allah might need realignment with this truth?
