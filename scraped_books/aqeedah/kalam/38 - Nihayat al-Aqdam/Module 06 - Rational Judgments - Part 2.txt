The Objection of One Thing in Two Answered by Symmetry
Objection and initial refutation by symmetry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection against the unity of existence and the author's initial response that the same problem applies to intellectual considerations.

Core Idea
The problem of 'one in two' is symmetrical between existence and intellectual considerations.

The objector claims that if existence is one, then one thing would be in two things. The author responds by pointing out that the same issue arises in the objector's own framework of intellectual considerations (i'tibarat). This lesson examines this exchange and shows how the author grounds existence as a rational meaning encompassing substance and accident.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how the objector's statement is followed immediately by the author's counter: 'So we say: What applies to you... applies to us.'
The author does not deny the seeming problem but shows it is mutual.
2
Understand the symmetry
The author says 'the aspect is like the state and the state is like the aspect.' This means that the objector's 'intellectual considerations' (aspects) are analogous to the author's 'states' (ahwal). The same logical issue appears in both frameworks.
3
See the grounding in rational meaning
The author asserts that existence encompasses substance and accident 'as a rational meaning' (ma'nan ma'qul), not just a word. This sets up the idea that existence is a universal concept, which is necessary for further argument.
This prepares the ground for the next lesson on universals.

Commentary
This passage is a response to an objection against the doctrine of states. The objector argued that if existence is one and similar in all existents, then one thing would be in two things or two things would be in one thing. The author replies that the same problem applies to the objector's own intellectual considerations and rational aspects: the aspect is like the state. Then the author asserts that existence encompasses substance and accident as a rational meaning, not just a word. He then demonstrates the logical consequences of denying the universality of existence: if each existent were distinguished only by its existence, one could not apply a judgment from one existent to another. For example, establishing temporality for one substance would require a separate proof for each substance and each accident. Moreover, no division of intelligibles would be possible because division presupposes sharing in something, and sharing presupposes specification by something. The author gives an example: saying 'this body is composed' does not imply that every body is composed; to reach a universal judgment, one must assert 'every body is composed' and 'every composed thing is temporally originated'. This requires taking body-ness and composition as universal concepts. Therefore, if things were distinguished only by their particular essences, no universal judgment could be made. The author concludes by contrasting their positions: the objector required them to 'invert genera' (i.e., admit one thing in two), while they require the objector to 'abolish genera' (i.e., deny universals). The objector calls to the sensible, while they call to the intelligible. Finally, the objector confounds them with the affirmation of one in two and two in one, but the text breaks off.

The Necessity of Universals for Judgment and Division
Logical argument on universals and division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why denying the unity of existence makes universal judgments and logical divisions impossible, and how the author uses this to refute the objector.

Core Idea
Without shared existence, no universal judgment or division of intelligibles is possible.

The author now deepens his argument. If each existent were distinguished only by its own existence, then no judgment could be transferred from one to another, and no logical division (taqsim) could occur. He uses the example of proving the temporality of bodies to show that universals like 'body-ness' and 'composition' are necessary. This lesson unpacks that logical chain.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument about temporality
Read from 'Man qala...' to '...kullu jismin muhdath'. Notice how the author shows that to prove every body is temporally originated, you need universal statements: 'every body is composed' and 'every composed thing is temporally originated'.
This illustrates the need for shared concepts.
2
Understand the logic of division
The author explains that division (taqsim) requires sharing (ishtirak) in something, and sharing requires specification (ikhtisas) by something. This is a basic logical principle.
Without sharing, you cannot divide cats from dogs, for example.
3
See the conclusion and the final contrast
The author concludes that the objector's position inverts genera (by making one thing in two) while they require abolishing genera (denying universals). The objector calls to the sensible, but the author calls to the intelligible.
The passage breaks off but the main argument is complete.

The Objection and the Ash'ari Response
Presenting the objection and the Ash'ari counter-argument

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Ash'ari responds to the objection that a single existence would entail absurdities, by showing the same issue applies to the opponent's own concepts.

Core Idea
The objection against unity of existence boomerangs onto the opponent.

The objection seems powerful: if existence is one, then either the same thing is in two distinct things, or two things are in one. But the Ash'ari masterfully turns the table: the opponent's own 'rational aspects' are also universals, and the same problem would apply to them. Moreover, existence is not a mere word but a real meaning that applies to both substances and accidents.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the first sentence carefully: it states the problem that would arise if existence were one and the same in all existents.
The objector assumes that if existence is a single universal, it must be literally the same in every thing, leading to absurdities.
2
Analyze the Ash'ari response
Notice how the response uses the phrase 'considerations and rational aspects' to show that the opponent's own conceptual tools are also universal. The Ash'ari argues that no one denies that existence is a real meaning encompassing substance and accident.
The key move is to find a parallel: the objector's own ideas of 'aspects' are just as universal.
3
Reflect on the implications
Consider why this response is effective: it undercuts the objection by showing that the critic would have to reject their own foundational concepts.
This sets up the next part about the necessity of universals for knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is a response to an objection against the Ash'ari position that existence is a universal concept applying to all existents. The objector argues that if existence were one and the same in all things, then either the same thing would be in two distinct things, or two things would be in one thing, both absurd. The author then uses the example of 'body' and 'composition' to show how universals are necessary for syllogistic reasoning to yield knowledge that all bodies are temporally originated. He accuses the opponent of forcing them into absurdities ('inverting genera') while the opponent ends up in worse absurdities ('raising genera'). The passage concludes by stating that the opponent's proof is contradictory, and the rational principle (الدست) stands, leaving the question of who is the judge unresolved.

The Necessity of Universals for Knowledge
Demonstrating the necessity of universals for reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why universals are necessary for making judgments and classifications in reasoning, and how the Ash'ari uses this to defend the concept of a single existence.

Core Idea
Without universals, no judgment can extend beyond a single instance, making knowledge impossible.

The Ash'ari now deepens the argument: if every existent is unique in its essence, then no universal judgment can be made. Classification requires commonality, and commonality requires a universal aspect. Through the example of bodies and composition, he shows that universals are indispensable for syllogistic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument against particularism
Start from 'Whoever says' and follow the reasoning: if each existent is differentiated only by its own existence, then you cannot apply a judgment from one to another. This leads to an infinite regress of proofs.
The Ash'ari is arguing against the position that everything is completely unique.
2
Understand the example of body and composition
Notice the step-by-step reasoning: to prove that all bodies are temporally originated, you need universal statements about body and composition. Without universals, you cannot make such inferences.
This illustrates the role of universals in syllogistic reasoning.

Commentary
This passage presents the argument of the negators (those who deny that non-existence has any inherent reality). They begin by stating their position: they do not affirm that non-existence in itself has any particularity (khusus) or generality (umum). Rather, these are introduced by verbal expression or mental estimation. Knowledge of a non-existent thing requires an estimation of its existence; for example, absolute non-existence is known only by opposing it to absolute existence, and specific non-existence is known either by reference to an actual existent that is absent or by a mental conception that is then negated or affirmed. They conclude that non-existence cannot be known apart from existence or its estimation. They then apply this to the question of the world's atoms: if existence is affirmed of them, it implies they are eternal (thus denying creation), and if it is negated, then according to the affirmers every negated thing is non-existent, and every non-existent is something established (thabit), which would contradict their own denial of reality in non-existence. Thus they charge the affirmers with inconsistency. The argument is meant to force the affirmers into either accepting pre-eternity of the world or accepting that non-existence has reality.

Commentary
This passage contains two related arguments. First, the author presses an objection based on the nature of capability: a truly capable agent must be able to perform opposites (e.g., to create or not create). Speech is a meaning that has opposites (e.g., silence, commands, prohibitions, true/false news). If the opponent defines the speaker as the one who creates speech, consistency demands that the silent one be defined as the one who creates silence, etc. This would force them to attribute all types of speech (commands, news, good, evil, true, false) to God, which is absurd—especially attributing evil and falsehood to God. The author then mentions two other problems (pre-eternity and unity of speech) but defers them to later issues. Second, the author presents his own positive analysis of human speech: linguistic speech (nutq lisani) is composed of letters, which are segmented sounds. Letters are temporal and perish immediately. They combine into words, and words into meaningful speech. The locus of letters/words is the tongue; the locus of meanings is the heart (jinan). A human is called a speaker only when both are present. If only letters exist without meaning, he is mad; if only meaning without words, he is a thinker. This analysis undermines the opponent's simplistic definition of speaker as mere creator of speech, since human speech requires both external material and internal meaning, and it is composed and temporal. The author says he is being lenient to clarify the truth, then they will rightly evaluate each position.

The Ascent and Descent of Meaning and Gnosis
Explains the ascent and descent of meaning and the two paths of gnosis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the concepts of ascent (ṣuʿūd) and descent (nuzūl) of meaning as described by Sanusi, and how gnosis ('irfān) follows these two paths.

Core Idea
Meaning ascends from sensory multiplicity to intellectual unity and descends back; gnosis mirrors this ascent and descent.

After detailing how sound is physically and cognitively processed, Sanusi elevates the discussion to the movement of meaning itself. He describes how meaning ascends from the heard sensible to the known intelligible—from multiplicity to unity—and descends in reverse. Then he connects this to the two paths of gnosis: one starting from differentiation and leading to unity (ascent), and another starting from tawḥīd and leading to multiplicity (descent). This passage integrates epistemology with spiritual journey.

Each path involves a 'gathering' (jamʿ) or 'knowledge' (maʿrifa).
3
Connect to the earlier sound example
Consider how the cognitive processing of sound (from sensible to intelligible) exemplifies the ascent, while teaching or expressing meaning exemplifies descent.
This bridges the physical and metaphysical.
4
Reflect on the spiritual implications

This is a key concept in Sufi and kalam traditions.

Commentary

This challenges the opponent's assumption that the intellect itself can preserve universals without an indivisible substrate. The commentary follows the author's argumentative structure: first stating the objection, then denying its necessity, providing counterexamples, turning the logic around, using the point as a proof, and ending with the dilemma about memory.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Unity of accident demands unity of subject: a point requires an indivisible subject.

Having refuted the necessity of the division claim, the author now turns the tables. He argues that if division of the subject necessarily divides the accident, then by the same token, the unity of an accident should require unity of the subject. This forces the opponent to admit an indivisible subject for indivisible accidents like the point, which they cannot deny.

The Ash'aris said: The answer to the first objection is that the demand for a validating factor is not specific to us; for you have made color and the colored a validating factor. That is because vision, when it pertains to some existents, and these are two types: a type of accidents and a type of substances, it is valid that substance and accident are visible. [We said: What is the validating factor for this validity? Is it that the substance is substance, then it would follow that accident is not visible; or that accident is accident, then it would follow that substance is not visible? So there must be a single proposition combining them, encompassing them, so that validity is caused by it. For validity is a judgment that is a single proposition; so its validating factor cannot be two different causes, for the difference of two causes necessitates difference of two judgments in rational matters. And since vision pertains to different things, we must seek the cause for which its pertaining is valid. And the validating cause must be a proposition that combines them, not like color and the colored, for they are two different propositions differing in genus and species. And it is impossible to link a single judgment to two different causes, for a rational cause must be independent in producing the judgment; if one of the different things is independent, the judgment dispenses with the second. So we sought the cause because vision pertained to different things, and we made the cause a single proposition encompassing them due to the unity of the judgment, which is validity. Thus the demand is valid and the desired is obtained.

Commentary
This passage is the Ash'ari response to the first objection raised against their view on vision. The response defends the Ash'ari method of seeking a single cause (musahhih) for the validity of vision. The Ash'aris argue that the opponent himself uses a validating factor (color and colored) for vision, so the demand for a cause is not unique to them. They then elaborate: vision pertains to both substances and accidents; if the cause were specific to one type (e.g., being a substance), the other type would not be visible. Hence there must be a single proposition (qadiyya wahida) common to both that explains why vision is valid for them. This is because a single judgment (validity) cannot have two different causes, as that would rationally lead to different judgments. The cause must be a unitary rational ground that combines the diverse objects of vision. Thus, the Ash'aris conclude that their search for a single cause (such as existence or some other common attribute) is justified and successful.
