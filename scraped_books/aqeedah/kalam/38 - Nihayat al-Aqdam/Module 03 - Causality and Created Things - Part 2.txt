The Great Difficulty of the Third Premise
Introduces the third premise and the opponent's demand for the aspect of relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the third premise is considered the greatest difficulty in kalam and why the opponent demands an 'aspect of the relation' between the contingent and the Necessary.

Core Idea
The difficulty is not about proving God exists, but about explaining why He created at a specific moment.

This lesson introduces the third premise, which the author calls the 'great difficulty' and 'terrible calamity'. It presents the opponent's demand for the 'aspect of the relation' between the created world and the Eternal Creator, and the standard objection that God's attributes are general, so no particular act can be explained.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Let the author's own words set the stage for the problem.
Notice the author's emotional language: 'great difficulty', 'terrible calamity'.
2
Identify the three parts
Find where the author declares the difficulty, where he quotes his teacher, and where the opponent's challenge is presented. This will help you follow the logical flow.
3
Analyze the objection
The opponent asks: if God's knowledge and will are general, how can they result in a specific act at a particular time? Reflect on why this is a genuine problem for explaining creation.
Compare 'general attachment' of attributes to the specific act that needs explaining.
4
Reflect on the significance
Ask yourself: why might this problem be considered the most difficult in kalam? What does it challenge about our understanding of God's action?
This connects to the learning objective.

As for the discussion of the third premise—and it is the great difficulty and the terrible calamity—many times we would consult our teacher and imam, the supporter of the Sunnah, author of al-Ghunya and Sharh al-Irshad, Abu al-Qasim Sulayman ibn Nasir al-Ansari, concerning it, and he would speak about it, but he would not go beyond that establishing the aspect of the agency of the Creator, exalted and high, is beyond the reach of the intellects of the intelligent. Then what his speech settled upon was that he said: The possibility of the existence of the world is established intellectually, and its temporal origination is established by evidence, and it is known that whatever undergoes temporal origination occurs by itself (i.e., has a cause), so it is necessary to relate its temporal origination to the Necessary of existence by itself. The opponent only demands the aspect of the relation (wajh al-nisba). For if the agent does not create and then creates, the relation of the originated to Him before creation is like its relation to Him during creation and after it, and no thing or cause occurred, so why did it exist and why did He create? In sum, what is the meaning of bringing into existence (ijad) and invention (ibda')? If you say: He knew its existence at this time and willed its existence at it, it is said: Knowledge is general in attachment (ta'alluq), and will is general in attachment. So the relation of its existence to the general will at this time in this form is like the relation of its existence at another time in another form. And similarly for power, for it is in the generality of its attachment like will and knowledge; there is no particularity in the attributes. So how does the act become particular? And this place is the source of the deviation of some theologians. For the Karramites posited temporal events in the essence of the Creator, namely will and speech, and they are what particularize the world to existence rather than non-existence. And the eternal will (al-mashiyya al-qadima) is, according to them, general in attachment, and will (al-irada) is specific in attachment. They distinguished between the causing of events (ahdath) and the brought about event (muhdath), and between creation (khalq) and the created (makhluq). The Mu'tazilites posited temporal wills not in a substrate, which are what particularize the world to existence rather than non-existence, and they did not distinguish between causing events and the brought about event, nor between creation and the created. The better course is that we take, in solving the problem, the path of refuting the opponent's school, then point to what appears to the intellect of the meaning of bringing into existence.

Commentary
This passage is a presentation of the third premise, which the author identifies as the most difficult problem in kalam. He describes it as a great difficulty and a terrible calamity. The author notes that he often consulted his teacher, Nasir al-Sunnah, about it, but the teacher would only say that understanding the aspect of God's agency is beyond the reach of intellects. The teacher's settled view was that the possibility of the world is known rationally, its temporal origination is proven by evidence, and whatever originates has a cause, so we must relate it to the Necessary Being. The opponent, however, demands the 'aspect of the relation' (wajh al-nisba): why did God create the world at this specific moment rather than another, given that no change occurred in God? The author then presents the standard objection: if one says God knew and willed the world at this time, then knowledge and will are general in their attachment (they encompass all times equally), so there is no reason for the specific choice. The same applies to power. This problem led some theologians astray: the Karramites posited temporal accidents in God's essence (such as a temporal will and speech) to particularize the act, while the Mu'tazilites posited temporal wills not in a substrate. The author concludes that the best way to solve the problem is to first refute the opponent's position (i.e., show the falsity of their specific theories) and then indicate what reason perceives about the meaning of 'bringing into existence' (ijad).

Deviant Solutions and the Author's Approach
Describes the deviant positions of the Karramites and Mu'tazilites and the author's method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the deviant solutions proposed by the Karramites and Mu'tazilites regarding the particularization of the act, and the author's preferred method of refutation.

Core Idea
Some theologians invented temporal changes in God to explain the particular act, but the author advocates refutation first.

After presenting the core difficulty, the author discusses how some theologians went astray: the Karramites posited temporal events in God's essence, while the Mu'tazilites posited temporal wills in no substrate. The author then outlines his own approach: first refute the opponent's theory, then indicate what reason can grasp about creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Focus on identifying the two deviant schools mentioned: the Karramites and the Mu'tazilites. Note their key distinctions and how they differ.
Pay attention to the terms 'ahdath', 'muhdath', 'khalq', 'makhluq'.
2
Contrast the two positions
The Karramites posited temporal events in God's essence (will and speech) and distinguished between causing events and the event. The Mu'tazilites posited temporal wills not in a substrate and did not distinguish between these pairs. Compare their approaches.
3
Understand the author's method
The author says the better course is to first refute the opponent's school (i.e., show the falsity of these solutions) and then point to what reason perceives about the meaning of 'bringing into existence' (ijad).
This is a methodological statement: refutation before positive indication.
4
Reflect on why these are deviant
Ask yourself: why did the author consider these positions as deviations? What is problematic about positing temporal events in God or temporal wills not in a substrate?
Connect back to the previous lesson's objection about the generality of attributes.

Human Limitations and the Active Intellect Analogy
Explains human limitation and uses Active Intellect analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why humans struggle to conceive divine creation and how a hypothetical scenario with universal attributes parallels the Active Intellect doctrine.

Core Idea
Our limited attributes blind us to how creation can occur without change.

The author explains that our inability to imagine creation without change stems from our own finite and transient attributes. He then invites us to suppose we had universal, permanent attributes—and shows that creation would then be necessary without any change in us. This hypothetical mirrors the philosophers' Active Intellect, which emanates universally yet appears specific due to receptacles.

Commentary
This passage addresses the difficulty humans have in understanding how God creates without change, and it uses an analogy from the opponent's own doctrine to illustrate the point. The author explains that we find it hard to conceive because we do not experience origination (ījād) and creation (ibdāʿ) ourselves, and our attributes are not universal in attachment: our knowledge knows only one thing at a time, our will wills only one thing, our power can only do one thing at a time, and not in the sense of origination. Moreover, our attributes are accidents that do not endure. Consequently, our reason and sense demand that an event must have a cause that comes into being. However, the author then presents a hypothetical scenario: if we suppose that we had knowledge, will, and power that were universal in attachment—i.e., capable of infinite objects—and if we suppose our power had the capacity for origination, and if we suppose these attributes were permanent, then we would not doubt that when we know, will, and are able to bring about something at a specific time, that thing would necessarily occur without any change in our essence or the occurrence of a new cause. This, the author notes, is exactly how the opponent (the philosophers) conceive of the Active Intellect (al-ʿaql al-faʿʿāl) and its emanation (fayḍ). They say it is universal in emanation, giving forms universally, but then specify through the receptacles (qawābil) and conditions. This specificity does not detract from the generality of the emanation in itself. The author concludes that this is despite the fact that the opponent's essential necessitation (al-ījāb al-dhātī) is supposed to be an essential emanation that is absolutely general without specificity. The passage ends abruptly mid-sentence, continuing onto the next page.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Negations cannot multiply a simple essence.

The philosophers hold that the First Intellect emanates all forms while remaining one, with multiplicity arising from recipients. But the author points out that the First Intellect's defining attribute—abstraction from matter—is a negation. If negations are real, why don't they multiply intellects? The author then states that for the philosophers, 'being an intellect' means being abstracted from matter—this is a negative attribute (a negation).

Objection: The Four Considerations of the First Intellect Lead to Contradiction
The author presents a dilemma against the philosophers' four considerations of the First Intellect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the philosophers' account of the First Intellect's four considerations leads to a contradiction regarding unity and causality.

Core Idea
The First Intellect's four considerations either imply multiplicity from the One or contradict its own nature.

The philosophers claim the First Intellect is one yet has four considerations. This objection reveals a fatal dilemma: if those considerations come from the First Cause, they necessitate multiple effects, violating divine unity; if they are intrinsic, the claim that only possibility is from itself collapses. Let's examine the argument.

Guided Path
1
Understand the philosophers' position
The philosophers propose four considerations for the First Intellect (e.g., its existence, knowledge of the First, etc.) while affirming its unity.
They also claim unity necessitates a soul or a body.
2
Identify the dilemma
The author asks: are these considerations from the First Cause or from itself? Each option leads to a problem.
Only possibility is said to be from itself, leaving three considerations.
3
Analyze the first horn
If the three considerations are from the First, they require three distinct effects, but the Necessary Existence is one in every respect.
4
Analyze the second horn
If they are from itself, then the claim that only possibility is from itself is invalidated. Moreover, possibility is a privative nature suited only to matter, not to form.
Thus form has no cause.
5
Conclusion
The philosophers' scheme is internally contradictory.

Commentary
This passage presents a third objection. The philosophers posit four considerations for the First Intellect (its existence, its knowledge of the First, its knowledge of itself, etc.) and claim that the intellect is one. They also say that unity necessitates either a soul or a body. The author asks: which of its aspects are received from the First and which are from itself? If only possibility is from itself, then the other three aspects must be from the First, but that would necessitate three effects from the One, which is impossible. If those three are from itself, then the claim that only possibility is from itself is false. Furthermore, possibility is a privative nature suited only to matter, not to form. So form has no cause.

Objection: Can a Composite Body Cause a Like Body?
The author points out the absurdity of a composite body causing a similar body

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth objection against the philosophers' causality argument and the inconsistency it exposes.

Core Idea
The philosophers contradict themselves: they deny one type of causation while affirming another.

This passage presents a powerful objection. The author points out that the philosophers' denial that a composite body can cause a like body contradicts their claim that an intellectual substance can cause many distinct intelligible substances. Let's examine the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation. Notice the contrast being drawn between a composite body and an intellectual substance.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the two claims
Identify the first claim: a composite body (matter+form) cannot cause a like body. Then the second claim: something that has existence by another and possibility in itself (the First Intellect) causes real intellectual substances.
The second claim is what the philosophers affirm.
3
Understand the inconsistency
The author finds it 'wonderful' (absurd) that the philosophers deny causation to composite bodies but affirm it for a simple intellectual substance, especially since the intellectual substances exist only in the mind while matter exists externally.
This undermines the philosophers' consistency.

The Arbitrary Assignment of Soul and Intellect
A rhetorical objection against the philosophers' arbitrary attribution of soul and intellect to the first emanation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the philosophical claim that the first emanation produces a soul and intellect is based on arbitrary assignment rather than logical necessity.

Core Idea
The philosophers' causality chain is just arbitrary assignment disguised as reasoning.

In this passage, the author levels a powerful rhetorical objection against the philosophers' emanation scheme. He challenges the basis on which they attribute soul and intellect to the first emanation, claiming it is nothing but arbitrary assignment. Let's read how he deconstructs their argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its English translation without analysis. Get a feel for the author's rhetorical style.
Notice the question form: 'Why did it become...?'
2
Identify the attributes and their assigned products
Find the two attributes (being necessary through another, being immaterial) and which product (soul or intellect) the philosophers assign to each.
The author reverses the assignment to test its necessity.
3
Understand the charge of arbitrariness
The author concludes that the philosophers' assignment is 'cold arbitrariness' built on taqlid (uncritical imitation). Consider why this is a strong critique.

The author concludes that this is nothing but a cold arbitrariness based on taqlid (uncritical acceptance) that itself rests on arbitrariness.

Refuting the Four Considerations
A logical refutation of the philosophers' four considerations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the philosophers' attempt to derive multiple effects from the One Necessary Being by exposing contradictions in their framework of four considerations.

Core Idea
Four considerations cannot explain multiplicity from the One.

In this dense logical passage, the author engages directly with the philosophers' claim that four distinct considerations (such as existence, essence, etc.) explain how the first intellect proceeds from God. He poses a sharp question: which of these are derived from God, and which are intrinsic to the intellect? By exploring both possibilities, he shows that their system breaks down, and that the concept of possibility (imkan) only explains matter, not form, leaving the intellect's form uncaused.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage through once, then read the English translation. Get a feel for the argument before analysing it.
Notice the rhetorical style: 'قلتم', 'فبينوا'—it mimics a dialogue.
2
Identify the author's question
The author asks the philosophers: among your four considerations, which are received from God and which are intrinsic to the intellect? He then states that only possibility of existence is intrinsic. Pay attention to this pivot.
This question is the key to the entire refutation.
3
Follow the two scenarios
The author considers two cases: (1) the remaining three considerations are received from God, which would necessitate three effects from one essence—contradicting divine unity; (2) they are intrinsic to the intellect, which would mean the intellect has more than just possibility, contradicting their earlier claim. See how each is refuted.
Then he adds another argument: possibility only suits matter, not form, so the form of the intellect has no cause.

And we say: You counted four considerations, and you judged the intellect to be one in its essence, and you said that unity necessitates a soul or a body. Commentary
This passage is a logical refutation. The philosophers posit four considerations (perhaps existence, essence, etc.) to explain how from the One (the Necessary) proceeds multiple things (e.g., intellect, soul, spheres). The author asks: Among the four, what is derived from God and what is intrinsic to the first intellect? If only possibility is intrinsic, then the other three must be derived, but then God would produce three effects, yet He is One. If they are intrinsic to the intellect, then it has more than just possibility, contradicting their claim. Moreover, possibility (imkan) is like matter: both are privative and receptive. So possibility can only explain matter, not the form (sura). Thus the form (the intellect as a form) has no cause, breaking the chain.

The Objection on Composite Bodies and the Unnecessary Intermediary
Objection and conclusion against the necessary intermediary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the author argues that the philosophers' intermediary (first intellect) is unnecessary due to a contradiction in their causal framework.

Core Idea
A possible-by-itself being cannot cause something truly immaterial.

The author presents a sharp objection to the philosophers' scheme of emanation. He points out a contradiction: if a composite body cannot produce another body, then how can something merely possible in itself produce multiple immaterial substances? This challenges the need for any intermediate between Allah and creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic passage and translation, noting the two sides of the contradiction: (1) bodies cannot cause bodies, (2) the first intellect is said to cause intellectual substances.
The author finds this 'a thing that causes wonder'.
2

Identify why a composite body (matter+form) cannot cause a like body. Then consider why the philosophers' first intellect cannot cause multiple immaterial substances that are not composed of matter.
Intellectual substances have no material existence, yet the philosophers claim they arise from something possible in itself.
3
Understand the conclusion
Read the conclusion: the intermediary (first intellect) is not needed. The objection shows the emanation scheme is flawed.
The author uses this to support his own Ash'ari position that Allah creates directly.

The conclusion drawn from these objections is that the entire intermediate (the first intellect) is unnecessary because the emanation scheme is flawed.

This passage presents al-Qadi Abu Bakr's argument for a real effect of created power. He begins by noting that a person naturally feels a necessary distinction between involuntary movements (like trembling) and voluntary movements (like deliberate action). Distinguishing Divine Creation from Human Acquisition
Defining khalq and kasb with their respective rulings and conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between divine creation (khalq) and human acquisition (kasb) in terms of their rulings and conditions.

Core Idea
Creation changes nothing in the Creator; acquisition changes the human agent.

In Islamic theology, the relationship between divine action and human action is subtle. This lesson defines two key terms—khalq and kasb—and clarifies how each affects its agent. The passage explains that creation does not alter God, while acquisition does alter the human, and specifies the knowledge conditions for each.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the translation of the passage once to get an overview of the two definitions and their components (ruling and condition).
The passage is structured symmetrically: first creation, then acquisition.
2
Analyze the ruling for each
Notice that the ruling for creation states the Creator is not changed, while for acquisition the acquirer is changed. Ask yourself why this difference matters for understanding divine transcendence and human responsibility.
3
Compare the conditions
Compare the condition for creation (knowledge from every aspect) with that for acquisition (knowledge of some aspects). Reflect on why complete knowledge is necessary for creation but not for human action.
This highlights the difference between God's omniscience and human limited knowledge.

Source Text

And concerning this, the approved answer regarding the distinction between creation (khalq) and acquisition (kasb) is that creation is the existent by the bringing into existence of the creator, and it entails a ruling and a condition. As for the ruling: that the creator is not changed by the bringing into existence, so that it acquires a quality from it nor does it acquire a quality from it. As for the condition: that he be knowing of it from every aspect. And acquisition is that which is within the power of the temporal power, and it entails a ruling and a condition. As for the ruling: that the acquirer is changed by the acquisition, so that it acquires a quality from it and acquires a quality from it. As for the condition: that he be knowing of some aspects of the action, or we say: change is entailed and knowledge of it from every aspect is not required.
Source pages p. 74

Commentary
This passage defines two key terms: creation (khalq) and acquisition (kasb). For creation, the object exists by God's bringing it into existence, and this entails a ruling (hukm) and a condition (shart). The ruling is that God is not changed by the act of creation—He does not acquire any new quality from it nor does He lose any. The condition is that God has complete knowledge of the created thing from every aspect. For acquisition, the act is within the power of the temporal being (human), and it also entails a ruling and a condition. The ruling is that the human agent is changed by the acquisition—he acquires a quality (e.g., being a writer) and he also acquires a quality from the action. The condition is that the human only needs knowledge of some aspects of the act, not complete knowledge. The passage thus distinguishes divine creation from human acquisition by emphasizing that creation does not affect the Creator, while acquisition does affect the human agent.

Ash'ari and Juwayni on Human Power: A Key Difference in Causality
Contrasting al-Ash'ari's denial and Juwayni's affirmation of an effect for human power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari and al-Juwayni differed regarding the effect of human power, and why the author considers al-Ash'ari's position as 'saved' while Juwayni's as 'extreme'.

Core Idea
Al-Ash'ari denied any real effect to human power; Juwayni affirmed an effect but denied independence.

In this short but dense passage, the author positions two major Ash'ari scholars—Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari and Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni—on opposite sides of a crucial theological question: does human power produce any real effect? Al-Ash'ari's answer is a firm 'no,' while Juwayni's 'yes' is seen as going too far. This contrast reveals the boundaries of orthodox Ash'ari theology on causality and divine transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the original Arabic alongside the translation. Notice the key terms: القدرة الحادثة (temporal power) and أثر (effect).
2
Identify the two positions
Locate what al-Ash'ari denies and what Juwayni affirms. Al-Ash'ari limits the effect to 'belief in facilitation'; Juwayni says there is an effect of 'existence' but not independence.
The author uses 'نجا' for al-Ash'ari and 'غلا' for Juwayni.
3
Understand the reasoning behind each
Reflect on why the author favors al-Ash'ari's view: it preserves direct divine causation without a chain of causes. Juwayni's view leads to a chain (تسلسل الأسباب) which compromises divine independence.
This connects to the broader concern for divine transcendence in Ash'ari theology.

Source Text

ونجا أبو الحسن رحمه الله حيث لم يثبت للقدرة الحادثة أثرا أصلا غير اعتقاد العبد بتيسير الفعل عند سلامة الآلات وحدوث الاستطاعة والقدرة والكل من الله تعالى. وغلا إمام الحرمين حيث أثبت للقدرة الحادثة أثرا هو الوجود غير أنه لم يثبت للعبد استقلالا بالوجود ما لم يستند إلى سبب آخر، ثم تتسلسل الأسباب في سلسلة الترقي إلى الباري سبحانه وهو الخالق المبدع المستقل بإبداعه من غير احتياج إلى سبب.
And Abu al-Hasan (al-Ash'ari) was saved, may God have mercy on him, because he did not establish for the temporal power any effect at all other than the servant's belief in the facilitation of the action when the instruments are sound and the occurrence of capacity and power, and all is from God the Exalted. And the Imam of the Two Sanctuaries (Juwayni) went to extremes because he established for the temporal power an effect, which is existence, except that he did not establish for the servant independence in existence unless it is based on another cause, then the causes continue in a chain of ascent to the Exalted Creator, and He is the Creator, the Innovator, independent in His innovation, without need of any cause.
Source pages p. 75

Commentary
This passage compares the positions of al-Ash'ari and Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni on the effect of human power. Al-Ash'ari is praised for denying any real effect to human power; he holds that the only thing attributable to the human is the belief that the action is facilitated when bodily instruments are sound and that capacity and power occur—all from God. Juwayni, by contrast, is criticized for going to extremes (ghala) by affirming that human power has an effect, namely existence (wujud). However, he does not grant the human independence in producing existence; it must depend on another cause, leading to a chain of causes ascending to God, who is the independent Creator. The author implies that Juwayni's position is problematic because it introduces a chain of causes, whereas al-Ash'ari's view preserves direct divine causation.
