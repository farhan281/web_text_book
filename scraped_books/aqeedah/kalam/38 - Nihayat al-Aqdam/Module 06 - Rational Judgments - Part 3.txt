Ash'ari Argument for a Single Cause for Vision
The Ash'ari logical deduction of a single cause for vision.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari logical argument that the cause for vision must be a single proposition common to substances and accidents, because a single judgment (validity) cannot have two different causes.

Core Idea
A single judgment demands a single cause — the core rational principle.

In the second part of their response, the Ash'aris present their main argument. They reason that if the cause of validity were specific to either substance-ness or accident-ness, the other type would not be visible. Since both are visible, the cause must be a single proposition encompassing both. This is grounded in a fundamental rational principle: a single judgment cannot derive from two different causes, as that would lead to a plurality of judgments.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation carefully
This passage contains the core argument. Read it twice, once for flow, once for logic.
Pay attention to the hypothetical: if cause is substance-ness, then accident not visible.
2
Understand the 'single proposition' requirement
The Ash'aris conclude that there must be a qadiyya wahida (single proposition) that combines substances and accidents as the cause.
This is the solution they propose.
3
Recognize the rational principle
The passage explicitly states: 'validity is a judgment that is a single proposition; so its validating factor cannot be two different causes'. This is the logical foundation.
Difference of causes necessitates difference of judgments.
4
Reflect on why this answers the objection
The objection challenged the demand for a cause; the Ash'aris show that rational consistency forces such a demand.
The argument is not arbitrary but rationally necessary.

Commentary
This passage is the Ash'ari response to the first objection raised against their view on vision. The response defends the Ash'ari method of seeking a single cause (musahhih) for the validity of vision. The Ash'aris argue that the opponent himself uses a validating factor (color and colored) for vision, so the demand for a cause is not unique to them. They then elaborate: vision pertains to both substances and accidents; if the cause were specific to one type (e.g., being a substance), the other type would not be visible. Hence there must be a single proposition (qadiyya wahida) common to both that explains why vision is valid for them. This is because a single judgment (validity) cannot have two different causes, as that would rationally lead to different judgments. The cause must be a unitary rational ground that combines the diverse objects of vision. Thus, the Ash'aris conclude that their search for a single cause (such as existence or some other common attribute) is justified and successful.

The author argues for a single cause for visibility of substance and accident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a single proposition must be the cause for the visibility of both substance and accident.

Core Idea
One judgment cannot have two different causes.

This passage presents a rational argument that seeks the cause of visibility for both substance and accident. The author considers and eliminates the possibility that each is visible due to its own essence, leading to the necessity of a single unifying cause. This argument demonstrates a key principle of kalam: a single judgment requires a single cause.

The argument is logically structured to show the necessity of a single cause.

Defending Conceptual Distinctions: The Objection of Modes
The author responds to the objection about modes using two Ash'ari authorities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Ash'ari and al-Baqillani respond to the objection that admitting conceptual distinctions (al-wujuh al-i'tibariyya) commits one to the doctrine of modes (ahwal).

Core Idea
Conceptual distinctions are indispensable for rational inquiry, even for those who deny modes.

The objector argues that the author's position implicitly requires accepting modes (ahwal) or else nullifying commonality and difference. In this passage, the author presents two responses: first, al-Baqillani states he already holds the mode doctrine, so the objection is moot. Second, al-Ash'ari argues that intellectual distinctions—such as considering motion as motion versus as an accident—are undeniable and necessary for thought. This lesson unpacks these defenses and their implications for kalam methodology.

The objection targets the denial of modes while still using distinctions like 'common' and 'different'.

Examine al-Baqillani's response

This is a direct refutation from within the Ash'ari school.

Read al-Ash'ari's statement carefully. He asserts that intellectual propositions and considered aspects are undeniable. Then he provides the example of motion to illustrate how multiple aspects coexist.
The example shows that even a simple entity like motion has multiple aspects (motion, 'having', accident).
4
Understand the consequence
Al-Ash'ari concludes that removing such considerations would block inquiry and confine the intellect to bare particulars. This is why conceptual distinctions are necessary.
The conclusion reinforces the indispensability of i'tibar (consideration).

Without such distinctions, rational inquiry would cease, and the intellect would be confined to particular entities, nullifying all considerations. Thus, even those who formally deny modes still rely on such conceptual distinctions.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the claim that human gratitude for divine blessings obligates God to grant additional reward. The author begins by stating the second argument: gratitude does not cause another blessing; rather, it is the fulfillment of an already established duty. Performing an obligatory act does not entail a further reward. He illustrates this by noting that even if someone devoted his entire life to thanking God for the safety of a single limb, he would still be considered deficient. Thus, comparing meager human gratitude to the overwhelming abundance of divine blessings shows that no increase in blessing is deserved. The same applies to all acts of worship: they are negligible compared to God's perpetual favors.

The author then elaborates that the concept of obligation (wujub) with respect to God is not rationally intelligible, nor is it impossible for the servant to have a claim upon the Lord. At every moment, the servant receives countless blessings—physical form, constitution, faculties, evidence, guidance—such that they cannot be enumerated (cf. Quran 14:34). Therefore, neither the initial bestowal nor a subsequent reward for gratitude is obligatory upon God, since the servant always falls short.

Finally, the author employs a dialectical concession: even if one grants that gratitude is deemed good by custom, then custom also demands that compensation be equivalent in quantity and time. But no equivalence exists between eternal, immense blessings and momentary, minimal gratitude. Consequently, reason, which judges by custom, would require considering proportion, but since proportion is absent, the entire basis for obligation collapses. Thus, no obligation of reward upon God can be established.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the rationalist (especially Mu'tazilite) claim that reason can independently determine that God must impose duties (taklif) on humans and that He must reward and punish. The author argues that since God is not affected by human actions—He is not harmed by disobedience nor benefited by obedience—and His power is unconditioned, reason cannot rationally impose a burdensome obligation on the servant. The author then presents two contradictory considerations: either God imposes duties so that He may reward and punish, or He does not impose duties because He neither gains from obedience nor loses from disobedience. Pure reason is bewildered by this contradiction and cannot decisively choose one. Moreover, the concepts of command and prohibition are metaphorical when applied to God; they do not belong to His essence. Reason only knows God as knowing, powerful, and as the doer of command just as He is the doer of creation. It is impossible for reason to know that God demands something from humans. Therefore, the Mu'tazilite view that reason can know God's obligation to reward and punish is unfounded. The passage concludes by noting that without a prophet and direct speech (like that in the burning tree), there is no way for reason to know God's command and prohibition; they are attributes of His action, not His essence.

ومن العجب أن المعتزلة قالوا بوجوب النظر قبل ورود الشرع، وبنوا ذلك على أن العاقل المفكر لا يخلو عن خاطرين يطريان على قلبه؛ أحدهما يدعوه إلى النظر حتى يعرف الصانع فيشكر فيثاب على شكره، والثاني يمنعه عن ذلك فيختار بعقله أحق الطريقين بالأمن، ويرفض أحقهما بالخوف، فيقال لهم: إذا تحدى النبي بالرسالة، وأخبر أنه رسول الله كان خبره محتملاً للصدق والكذب، فهلا طرأ الخاطران في تصديقه وتكذيبه، ثم يحصل الخبر بالصدق أولى، فإن فيه الأمن إذ لو كان صادقًا فكذبه خسر ولو كان كاذبًا فصدقه لم يخسر، فإنه إن يكن كاذبًا فعليه كذبه وفي طلب المعجزة منه خطر آخر، وهو جعل الخبر أولى بالكذب فكأنه يكذبه في الحال إلى أن تظهر المعجزة فيصدقه في ثاني الحال، وعند المعتزلة القرآن من جنس كلام العرب، فإنهم يقدرون على مثله قدرتهم على كلامهم، فلم يستمر لهم التمسك بإعجاز القرآن.
And it is astonishing that the Mu'tazila said that reflection (nazar) is obligatory before the arrival of revelation, and they based that on the fact that the rational thinker does not escape two thoughts (khatiran) that come upon his heart: one calls him to reflection so that he may know the Maker and thank Him and be rewarded for his thankfulness, and the other prevents him from that, so he chooses with his reason the more deserving of the two ways in terms of safety, and rejects the more deserving of them in terms of fear. So it is said to them: When the prophet challenges with the message and informs that he is the Messenger of God, his report is probable of truth and falsehood. Why then did the two thoughts not occur concerning believing him and disbelieving him? Then the report is more deserving of truth, for in it is safety: if he were truthful and he disbelieved him, he would lose; and if he were a liar and he believed him, he would not lose. For if he is a liar, his lie is against him, and in seeking the miracle from him there is another risk, namely deeming the report more deserving of falsehood, so it is as if he disbelieves him immediately until the miracle appears, then he believes him in the second moment. And according to the Mu'tazila, the Quran is of the same kind as Arab speech, for they are able to produce its like just as they are able to produce their own speech, so they could not consistently hold to the inimitability of the Quran.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazila argument that rational reflection (nazar) is obligatory before revelation. The author first presents the Mu'tazila position: a rational person inevitably experiences two thoughts — one urging him to reflect on the Maker to thank Him, the other discouraging him; he must choose the safer path. The author then turns this reasoning against the Mu'tazila, applying their own logic to the prophet's claim: when a prophet announces his message, his report is equally subject to truth and falsehood. If their principle of choosing safety applies, then one should believe the prophet immediately, because disbelief entails loss if he is true, while belief entails no loss even if he is false. Seeking a miracle before believing actually risks deeming the prophet a liar prematurely, which is unsafe. Finally, the author points out an inconsistency in the Mu'tazila position: since they hold that the Quran is like human speech and that humans can produce its like, they cannot consistently argue for its inimitability (i'jaz) — a key support for prophethood.

The Quran Inimitability Inconsistency of the Mu'tazila
Highlights internal inconsistency in Mu'tazila position on Quran inimitability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Mu'tazila view that the Quran is like human speech undermines their proof of prophethood based on its inimitability.

Core Idea
If the Quran is not unique, it cannot serve as a miracle confirming prophethood.

The author concludes the refutation by pointing out a critical inconsistency: the Mu'tazila hold that the Quran is of the same kind as ordinary Arab speech and that humans can produce its like. But if that is true, they cannot consistently claim the Quran is a miracle (i'jāz), which is a key proof for Muhammad's prophethood.
