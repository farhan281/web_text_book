Expression and Internal Speech: Conventional vs. Rational Indication
Al-Shahrastani cites a verse to prove internal speech via conventional expression.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Shahrastani uses the conventional indication of expressions to prove the existence of internal speech as a distinct attribute.

Core Idea
Expressions point to internal speech by convention, not by reason or identity.

In this passage, Al-Shahrastani employs a well-known line of poetry to anchor a key distinction between two types of indication: conventional (dalālat al-muwaḍaʿa) and rational (dalālat al-ʿaql). By clarifying that expressions, gestures, and writings indicate internal speech through convention, he establishes that internal speech is not reducible to knowledge or will. This distinction is foundational for the Ashʿarī doctrine that the divine speech (kalām Allāh) is an eternal attribute, not a created sound.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage carefully, then its translation. Notice how the author begins with a poetic quote and then unpacks its implication.
The verse is often attributed to Al-Mutanabbī but is used here as a standard proof.
2
Identify the two types of indication
The passage distinguishes between the indication of expressions upon speech (conventional) and the indication of judgments upon knowledge (rational). Find both and note their differences.
Conventional indication varies by language; rational indication is universal.
3
Consider the purpose of this distinction
Ask yourself: Why does Al-Shahrastani need to emphasize that expressions indicate internal speech by convention rather than by identity or causality? This supports the idea that internal speech is a real attribute separate from knowledge.
This distinction is crucial for defending the orthodox Ashʿarī position on the uncreatedness of the Qur'an.

Mu'tazila Definitions: Ṣalāḥ, Aṣlaḥ, Luṭf, Thawāb, ʿIwaḍ, Tafaḍḍul
Mu'tazila define six key ethical and theological terms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise Mu'tazila definitions of ṣalāḥ, aṣlaḥ, luṭf, thawāb, ʿiwaḍ, and tafaḍḍul.

Core Idea
Six key terms define the Mu'tazila moral framework for divine actions.

After proving that God's actions are for benefit, the Mu'tazila define the terms that describe these benefits. They distinguish between benefit (ṣalāḥ), the most beneficial (aṣlaḥ), and subtle grace (luṭf) as categories of divine action in this world, and reward (thawāb), compensation (ʿiwaḍ), and favor (tafaḍḍul) as categories of recompense in the afterlife. These definitions are central to their ethical theology.

Note how each term is contrasted with something else (e.g., ṣalāḥ vs. corruption).
2
Understand ṣalāḥ and aṣlaḥ
ṣalāḥ is benefit directed at worldly and eternal good; aṣlaḥ is the better of two goods.
aṣlaḥ is comparative.
3
Grasp luṭf
Luṭf is facilitation to obedience, but note that there is no grace that would force belief.
This distinguishes Mu'tazila from determinism.
4
Distinguish the recompense terms
Thawāb is reward for good deeds, ʿiwaḍ is compensation for harm, and tafaḍḍul is unmerited favor.
These cover all types of divine recompense.

They said: The proof for the two schools is that the Maker is wise, and the wise does not do an act regarding which a question arises and a proof is required, but rather he removes all impediments, so He does not charge anyone except within his capacity, and capacity is not realized except by completing the intellect and enabling action, and the purpose of action is not accomplished except by establishing recompense: "and that every soul may be repaid for what it has earned" (Quran 45:22). Commentary
This passage provides the Mu'tazila's proof for their two schools and defines key technical terms. It is a proof and definition passage. The proof begins with the premise that the Maker is wise (ḥakīm). A wise agent does not perform an act that invites questioning or requires justification; rather, He removes all impediments (ʿilal). This leads to the conclusion that God does not charge a soul beyond its capacity, and capacity is realized by completing intellect and enabling action. The purpose of action is only fulfilled by establishing recompense (jaza'), citing the Quran. Therefore, the origin of creation and obligation (taklif) is benefit (ṣalāḥ), and recompense is also benefit. The best possible benefit is the most beneficial (aṣlaḥ). The Law includes motives, deterrents, and subtle graces (alṭāf). The definitions follow: ṣalāḥ is the opposite of corruption, directed towards good for both worldly and eternal happiness. Aṣlaḥ is the better of two goods. Luṭf is an act that facilitates obedience, and there is no grace that would make disbelievers believe against their will. Thawāb is reward for good deeds; ʿiwaḍ is compensation for harms like pain; tafaḍḍul is gratuitous favor that deserves praise but not blame if omitted.

General and Specific Meanings of Ni'mah and Rizq
Classifies ni'mah and rizq into general and specific definitions with evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two complementary definitions of blessing (ni'mah) and provision (rizq) in Islamic theology, and why both are valid depending on context.

Core Idea
Ni'mah and rizq have both general and specific meanings—both are correct.

In this passage, the author clarifies that key theological terms like ni'mah (blessing) and rizq (provision) have two levels of meaning: a general sense encompassing all benefits or nourishment, and a specific sense tied to religious outcome and lawfulness. This distinction helps avoid confusion when interpreting Qur'anic verses and theological discussions.

The discussion regarding blessing (ni‘ma) and provision (rizq) is close to what we have mentioned. Whoever considers them in a general sense (umūman) says: blessing is everything by which a human being is blessed (yun‘am) in state and wealth, and provision is everything by which one nourishes himself, whether lawful (ḥalāl) or unlawful (ḥarām). Commentary
This passage is a classification and definition of the key terms ni‘ma (blessing) and rizq (provision) from two valid perspectives: general (umūm) and specific (khuṣūṣ). The author first presents the general view: ni‘ma is any benefit in state or wealth, and rizq is any nourishment, whether lawful or unlawful. He supports this with Qur'anic verses that use these terms in a broad sense (e.g., 17:83 for ni‘ma and 11:6 for rizq). Then he presents the specific view, which restricts ni‘ma to that which has a good outcome in the Hereafter, namely religious blessings, and restricts rizq to what is lawful (ḥalāl) according to the Sharia. This is supported by verses that distinguish true blessing from mere worldly gifts (23:55-56) and the requirement that rizq be from lawful means (2:254). The author concludes that both definitions are correct provided one takes into account whether the context is general or specific. He adds that there is no need for dispute over these conventional definitions (muwāḍa‘āt), implying that both meanings are lexically and legally acceptable depending on usage.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A substance between two neighbors forces a choice: divisibility or absurdity.

The mutakallimun's opponents often challenged the idea of indivisible atoms (al-jawhar al-fard) with clever scenarios. This passage presents one such objection: imagine a single substance sandwiched between two others. The Finite Capacity of the Substratum and the Limits of Imagination
Argument for finite capacity of substratum refuting infinite divisibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the substratum's capacity for accepting conjunction is finite and why imagination cannot posit infinite divisibility.

Core Idea
The substratum's finite capacity prevents infinite division; imagination cannot override rational proof.

In this passage, the author builds on the earlier distinction between the substratum and the form to address a critical question: can the substratum accept an infinite chain of conjunctions? The answer leads to a refutation of infinite divisibility and a defense of the indivisible atom. As you read, note how the author uses rational proof to discipline the imagination.

Guided Path
1
Recall the substratum vs. form distinction
The passage begins by reminding that divisibility is accepted by the substratum, not the form. This sets up the discussion about the substratum's capacity.

The Center Point Analogy: Relations Do Not Multiply Essence
Refuting the claim that multiple relations multiply essence using the center point analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the analogy of the center point of a circle demonstrates that multiple relations do not necessitate a multiplicity in the essence, refuting an objection in kalam atomism.

Core Idea
Many relations, one essence: the center point proves multiplicity of relations does not multiply essence.

The opponents argued that if an atom has relations to multiple other atoms, its essence would have to be multiple. Shahristani refutes this by providing a powerful analogy: a point at the center of a circle has countless relations to the circumference, yet remains one and unchanged. This lesson unpacks that analogy and its application to the indivisible atom (al-juz' al-fard).

Guided Path
1
Understand the opponent's claim
The opponents assert that multiple relations force the essence to be multiple. The author sets out to disprove this.
This is a common objection in kalam atomism against the existence of a single essence with multiple relations.
2
Grasp the analogy of the center point
The point is one but related to every part of the circle. Its relations are many, but the point itself does not become many. This is the core illustration.
Notice that the point is a 'middle thing' (وسطاني) that meets left and right with the same essence.
3
Apply the analogy to the indivisible atom
Just as the point has many relations without multiplying, so too the atom can have relations to parts on its right, left, or all six surrounding parts without its essence multiplying.
The atom (الجزء الفرد) is like the point; it remains one in essence despite multiple relations.
4
Conclude the refutation
The opponent's argument fails because it confuses relation with essence. Multiplicity in relation does not imply multiplicity in essence.

Source Text

And we also say, in response to the division they raised against us: The middle thing meets what is on its right with the very same thing it meets what is on its left in essence and substance, but with something else in relation and addition. It has a relation different from the relation next to it, and wherever we expand the circle, its relations multiply, and that does not necessitate a multiplication in its essence. Likewise, the statement about the indivisible atom: it is related to a part on the right and a part on the left, or to six surrounding parts, and that does not necessitate a multiplication in the essence.

These relations multiply without the point's essence multiplying. Similarly, an indivisible part (al-juz' al-fard) has relations to parts on its right, left, or six surrounding parts, yet it remains one in essence. Therefore, the opponent's argument fails.
