Commentary
This passage presents an objection and the author's answer. The objector argues that God's specific 'size' (or limit) is necessary by His essence, so it does not need a particularizer (mukhasis). The objector claims that created quantities need a determiner because they are possible (ja'iz), and possibility is known by being objects of power (maqdur). Since there is no power above God, the notion of possibility and need does not apply to Him. The objector then draws an analogy with the eight attributes: if we agree that the number of attributes is fixed, is that necessary or possible? If necessary, then the specific limit of God's essence is also necessary; if possible, then it needs a particularizer. The objector forces a dilemma. He states that quantities in themselves, whether in the seen or unseen world, are subject to rational possibility and require a particularizer. Even if we assume a quantity in the physical world, it still requires a cause for its specific measure. The possibility of contingent things is self-evident to reason and does not depend on the power of an agent. The Negators' Proof from Contradiction

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If the non-existent were a thing, negation and affirmation would not be opposites.

Their reasoning is based on a fundamental rational principle: negation and affirmation are contradictory opposites. If the non-existent (which is negated) were also affirmed (as a thing), this principle would be violated.

This is a primary axiom. If someone claims that the non-existent is a thing, then that which is negated (the non-existent) would be affirmed (i.e., subsistent), which violates this axiom. Such a person would be denying this primary rational principle, effectively saying that nothing is intelligible except subsistence (thubut). The conclusion is that every non-existent is negated, and every negated is not subsistent, so the non-existent is not subsistent.
