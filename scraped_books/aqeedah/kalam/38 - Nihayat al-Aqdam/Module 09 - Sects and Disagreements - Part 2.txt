The Mu'tazila-Ash'ari Debate on the Nature of the Quran
Presents Mu'tazila argument from consensus and Ash'ariyya response.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key arguments from both the Mu'tazila and Ash'ariyya regarding whether the Quran is created or eternal, and how each side claims consensus.

Core Idea
The debate hinges on whether sensory attributes of the Quran imply its creation.

This passage presents a classic theological debate. The Mu'tazila argue from the Quran's composition and sensory accessibility that it must be created, while the Ash'ariyya respond that such a view strips the Quran of its divine status. Let's examine their arguments.

Eternal speech cannot be described with such descriptions. Commentary
This passage presents the Mu'tazila argument and the Ash'ariyya response concerning the nature of the Quran. The Mu'tazila begin by claiming consensus that the Quran is speech (kalam), consisting of organized surahs, verses, letters, and words, and that it is read and heard, has a beginning and end, and is a miracle. They support this by noting that the predecessors addressed the Quran as 'O Lord of the Quran,' implying it is something created and addressed. Etymologically, 'Qur'an' means 'collection' (of things separate), and an eternal attribute cannot be described as composed or collected. They cite the consensus that God's speech is among us, accessible to our senses, and the Qur'anic verses that refer to 'hearing' and 'touching' God's speech, asking how an eternal attribute could have such characteristics. The Ash'ariyya respond by accusing the Mu'tazila of being the first to contradict this consensus. They argue that the Mu'tazila posit God's speech as created letters and words that come into existence and then perish. On this view, what we write and recite is our own action and acquisition, not God's eternal speech. Consequently, the speech among us is not God's speech, and God's speech is not among us, nor is it a miracle that we read or hear. Thus, the Mu'tazilite position, by making the Quran created and transient, removes it from being an ongoing divine attribute and miracle.

An-Nazzam's View on Speech as a Subtle Body
Presents and critiques an-Nazzam's theory that speech is a subtle body.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An-Nazzam's speech-as-body theory collapses into confusion and borrowed noise.

This lesson examines a historical view on the nature of human speech proposed by an-Nazzam. He attempted to explain speech as a physical, subtle body that travels from speaker to hearer. The passage presents his mechanistic description and then highlights his confusion about whether the form is singular or multiple, before delivering a sharp critique of his philosophical borrowing.

Refuting Mu'tazila: Internal Speech from Their Own Premises
Al-Shahrastani refutes Mu'tazila by using their own concept of two inner thoughts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Shahrastani refutes the Mu'tazila denial of internal speech by demonstrating that their own concept of inner impulses and the process of reasoning both presuppose internal speech.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila admit inner impulses, which are a form of internal speech, so they cannot deny it.

Al-Shahrastani presents a clever refutation of the Mu'tazila school, which denied that God's speech is internal (kalam nafsi). He points out that their own theory of two inner impulses (khāṭirān) – one calling to good, one to evil – is itself a form of internal speech. Then he shows that even ordinary human reasoning involves internal speech. This passage uses their own premises against them, making the refutation particularly powerful.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Let the author's argument flow naturally.
Notice the two distinct arguments in the passage.
2
Identify the two arguments
The passage contains two arguments: (1) the Mu'tazila's own doctrine of two inner impulses shows they accept internal speech; (2) the process of reasoning itself involves internal speech.
3
Understand the first argument
Al-Shahrastani cites the Mu'tazilite doctrine that a person finds two impulses (khāṭirān) before revelation. These impulses are created and inform about 'speech of the soul' (kalām al-nafs). Thus, the Mu'tazila implicitly affirm internal speech.
This is a tuqlīd (follow-through) argument: using their own premises.
4
Understand the second argument
During reasoning, a person mentally makes and cancels statements, divides universals, and invalidates parts. This mental division is internal speech. One could fill a whole book from inner talk while the tongue is silent.
This shows internal speech is unavoidable in human cognition.
5
Reflect on the overall refutation
Ask yourself why this refutation is effective. It does not rely on scripture but on rational observation and the opponent's own views.
Consider how this connects to the broader debate on God's eternal speech.

The Mu'tazila Denial of Internal Speech
Mu'tazila deny internal speech as real speech, argue it is estimation of verbal expressions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila argument that internal speech is not real speech but dependent on verbal expressions, and how they appeal to common usage.

Core Idea
Internal speech is just an echo of learned language, not real speech itself.

The Mu'tazila present a detailed objection against the view that internal speech is the real speech of Allah. They concede that thoughts occur but argue they are merely estimations based on previously learned expressions. Their argument uses empirical observations about language dependency, culminating in the example of a mute person. The author then adds support from common linguistic custom.

Read the Mu'tazila objection

The Mu'tazila said: We do not deny the thoughts that occur to a person's heart, and we sometimes call them 'inner speech' (aḥādīth al-nafs), either metaphorically or literally, but they are estimations of the expressions (al-ʿibārāt) that are on the tongue. Do you not see that one who does not know a word in Arabic does not think of Arabic speech, and one who does not know Persian does not conceive of Persian speech, and one who knows both languages sometimes speaks to himself in Arabic and sometimes in Persian? So it is known in reality that they are estimations and inner discourses dependent on the expressions that a person learned in his early development, and expressions are their origins from which they issue and to which they recur. Even if we imagine a person completely devoid of all expressions, a mute unable to speak, we do not doubt that his soul would not speak to him in Arabic nor Persian nor any language, while his mind comprehends every intelligible even if it is stripped of everything audible and transmitted. Thus it is known that real speech (al-kalām al-ḥaqīqī) is the structured letters that are on the tongue. Commentary
This passage presents the Mu'tazila objection against the view that internal speech (al-kalām al-nafsī) is real speech, followed by the author's reinforcement of their conclusion by appealing to common linguistic custom. The Mu'tazila begin by conceding that they do not deny the occurrence of thoughts and inner dialogues, which they sometimes call 'inner speech' either metaphorically or literally. However, they insist that these are merely estimations (taqdīrāt) of the verbal expressions that exist on the tongue. To prove this, they offer an empirical argument: a person who does not know Arabic does not think in Arabic, and likewise for Persian; a bilingual person thinks in both languages. This shows that inner speech is dependent on previously learned expressions. The strongest evidence, they say, is a mute person who has never learned any language: such a person's soul does not produce internal speech in any language, yet he can still reason. Therefore, real speech is nothing other than the articulated letters (al-ḥurūf al-manẓūma) spoken on the tongue. After this, the author adds his own support: the common understanding of linguists and rational people is that speech is what is uttered by the tongue, and that one who can utter is called a speaker, while one who cannot is called mute. Thus, the Mu'tazila position that speech is only spoken words is affirmed.

This lesson examines a key objection raised against the Ash'ari view that God must do what is best (al-salah wa al-aslah). The objection argues that if this obligation holds for God, then by analogy it should also hold for humans. Yet the Ash'aris do not hold humans to that standard, exposing a logical flaw.

The argument is a reductio ad absurdum.

They defined faith (iman) as verbal confession (qawl) and inward conviction ('aqd), regardless of whether actions are present. Consequently, they claimed that committing sins does not harm a believer's faith, just as performing acts of obedience does not benefit a disbeliever. This effectively separates faith entirely from works.

Sectarian Views on Grave Sinners: Extremes on Faith and Punishment
Presents Quranic verses on eternal punishment and responses of Khawarij, Murji'ah, Karramiyyah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three major sectarian positions (Khawarij, Murji'ah, Karramiyyah) regarding the grave sinner and how they deviate from the balanced Sunni view.

Core Idea
The grave sinner's fate is a battlefield of extremes: excommunication, license, and hollow confession.

The question of the grave sinner (sahib al-kabira) has long divided Islamic theology. Quranic verses threaten eternal punishment, but does that mean every grave sinner becomes a disbeliever? The Khawarij said yes, the Murji'ah said no action matters, and the Karramiyyah said only the tongue matters. This passage presents these extremes, setting up the Sunni middle path.

Guided Path
1
Read the Quranic proofs
Notice the verses cited about eternal punishment for disobedience and sin. What do they seem to imply about the fate of the grave sinner?
These verses are used by all sides, but interpreted differently.
2
Contrast with the Khawarij argument
Understand how they used the story of Iblis to justify takfir (excommunication) of grave sinners. Iblis was knowledgeable but committed one major sin and was eternally punished.
The Khawarij take this as a precedent for all grave sinners.
3
Examine the Murji'ah response
See how they overcorrected by divorcing actions from faith entirely. For them, faith is only statement and conviction; sin does not harm it.
This view makes faith independent of actions.
4
Reflect on the Karramiyyah extreme
Recognize why their view is considered repulsive: they say faith is mere verbal confession, even if the person is a hypocrite, and that such a person is a true believer before Allah.
This reduces faith to empty words.
