Commentary
This passage is a proof by Ahl al-Haqq (the People of Truth) demonstrating that God is absolutely independent (ghani) and does not act for any motive or cause. The argument proceeds in several steps. First, it states the thesis: God is independent absolutely. Then it argues by contradiction: if God were in need in any way—needing someone to remove his need—then He would be the ultimate object of needs, not the ultimate provider. Next, it lists possible motives for action: a cause, an incentive, a perfection to be gained, or praise and reward. It points out that what is beneficial for one person may be harmful for another, using the example of poison. Therefore, benefit and harm are relative. Since God's actions are not subject to such relativity, He does not act for the sake of benefit. The passage clarifies that while God's actions do contain good and are not aimed at harm, the question is whether a benefit or good anticipated by God impels Him to act. The answer is no: He has no impeller. Finally, a crucial distinction is made: between good necessarily attaching to the outcomes of actions (which is true) and good being the impelling cause for those actions (which is false). This is compared to the distinction between a perfection that necessarily accompanies a thing's existence (like an inherent attribute) and a perfection that causes the thing's existence (like a causal factor). The former is a necessary attribute, the latter is a motive—and God has no motives.

Commentary
This passage is an argument demonstrating the inimitability (i'jaz) of the Qur'an, responding to those who restrict it to long suras. The author (Shahristani) begins by criticizing those who limit the Qur'an's miraculous nature to long suras or specific verses, asserting that they have fallen short. He then directs attention to the very first revealed verse (Surah al-'Alaq, 96:1-2) as a counterexample. He analyzes its eloquence in several aspects: first, if one wanted to express God's creativity both general and specific (the whole world and man as a microcosm), no wording could be more eloquent and concise. Second, he observes the specification of the name 'Lord' (al-Rabb) in the context of tarbiyah (nurturing), the generalization of creation followed by specification of man, and the beginning of man's creation from a clot—likening the stages of revelation (the seven stations of receiving the Qur'an) to the stages of human development. Third, he notes the parallelism with Surah al-Rahman, where the sequence 'taught the Qur'an, created man, taught him eloquence' culminates in the seventh rank of bayan. Fourth, he highlights the pairing of 'Read' and 'your Lord is the Most Generous' and the wisdom in not repeating 'Read in the name of your Lord', but rather emphasizing the generosity of the Lord. Fifth, he marvels at how few letters and words carry such profound meaning without loss of clarity, with noble composition and sound arrangement. He concludes that the entire Qur'an is miraculous for those ignorant of eloquence, but its i'jaz is recognized at different levels: for some, the whole Qur'an; for others, ten suras; for others, one sura; and even one single verse can be sufficient. The degree of recognition depends on the person's knowledge (dirayah) and verification (tahqiq). Thus, the author affirms that i'jaz is not monolithic but graded according to the recipient's capacity.

Commentary
This passage is an argument demonstrating the inimitability (i'jaz) of the Qur'an, responding to those who restrict it to long suras. The author (Shahristani) begins by criticizing those who limit the Qur'an's miraculous nature to long suras or specific verses, asserting that they have fallen short. He then directs attention to the very first revealed verse (Surah al-'Alaq, 96:1-2) as a counterexample. He analyzes its eloquence in several aspects: first, if one wanted to express God's creativity both general and specific (the whole world and man as a microcosm), no wording could be more eloquent and concise. Second, he observes the specification of the name 'Lord' (al-Rabb) in the context of tarbiyah (nurturing), the generalization of creation followed by specification of man, and the beginning of man's creation from a clot—likening the stages of revelation (the seven stations of receiving the Qur'an) to the stages of human development. Third, he notes the parallelism with Surah al-Rahman, where the sequence 'taught the Qur'an, created man, taught him eloquence' culminates in the seventh rank of bayan. Fourth, he highlights the pairing of 'Read' and 'your Lord is the Most Generous' and the wisdom in not repeating 'Read in the name of your Lord', but rather emphasizing the generosity of the Lord. Fifth, he marvels at how few letters and words carry such profound meaning without loss of clarity, with noble composition and sound arrangement. He concludes that the entire Qur'an is miraculous for those ignorant of eloquence, but its i'jaz is recognized at different levels: for some, the whole Qur'an; for others, ten suras; for others, one sura; and even one single verse can be sufficient. The degree of recognition depends on the person's knowledge (dirayah) and verification (tahqiq). Thus, the author affirms that i'jaz is not monolithic but graded according to the recipient's capacity.

The Analogy of Manifestation: Meaning and Expression
Analogy of mental meaning to speech, writing, mirror, and shadow.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how mental meanings are manifested in physical expressions without inherence, using the examples of speech, writing, mirror, and shadow.

Core Idea
Meanings are shown through words and images without residing in them.

The author begins with a simple yet profound analogy to explain how the spiritual relates to the physical. Just as the meaning in your mind appears in spoken words without physically moving into them, so too do higher realities manifest in lower forms. This analogy sets the stage for the discussion of divine transcendence and the nature of prophets.
