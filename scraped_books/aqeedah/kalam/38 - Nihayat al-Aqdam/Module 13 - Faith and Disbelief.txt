External Evils and the Sound Fitra
Classification of external evils and fitra principle

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how external factors can influence a person's fitra and why the original disposition is fundamentally sound.

Core Idea
The fitra is good; evil comes from external influences.

This lesson explores the Islamic concept of fitra, the innate human disposition. The author explains that accidental evils are not from the person's nature but from external factors like upbringing and teaching. Understanding this helps reconcile evil in the world with the goodness of creation.

Guided Path

The passage uses a clear either/or structure.

These examples illustrate the second type (opposing perfection).

Understand the principle of fitra

The hadith is cited as proof for this principle.

Commentary
This passage discusses accidental evils that come from outside the person. These are of two types: that which completes the person (good influences) and that which opposes perfection (bad influences). Examples: ugly habits learned from upbringing, corrupt beliefs from parents and teachers. The original fitra (innate disposition) is good and ready for perfection, but external obstacles corrupt it. The hadith 'Every child is born upon the fitra' supports this, implying that parents and environment are responsible for deviations.

Faith Requires Limited Knowledge of God
The author argues that faith requires limited knowledge of God, with proof from divine omniscience.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why faith does not require exhaustive knowledge of God, only the basic testimony based on clear evidence.

Core Idea
Faith demands recognition of God, not comprehension of His full reality.

The author addresses a common misconception: that one must know God as He knows Himself. He argues that this is impossible, and the Prophet only required a simple confession supported by clear proof. This lesson examines that argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on limited knowledge
Read the Arabic passage carefully, then its translation, focusing on the author's claim that the Prophet did not require exhaustive knowledge of God.
2
Identify the proof from God's detailed knowledge

The proof uses God's knowledge of all details as a measure.
3
Understand the concept of 'excessive burden' (shatat)
Reflect on the term 'shatat' and why demanding more than basic testimony would be an unbearable burden.
4
Reflect on the requirement of 'clear proof' (daleel jali)
The author insists that even minimal knowledge must be based on a clear proof from revelation. Consider why this is important.

ونعلم قطعا أن النبي عليه السلام كما لم يرض منهم بمجرد القول ما لم يقترن به عقد، لم يكلف جميع الخلق معرفة الله تعالى كما هو لأن ذلك غير مقدور للخلق، وأقرب دليل على ذلك أنه تعالى يعلم جميع معلوماته على التفصيل، ويعلم أنه يخلق جميع مخلوقاته على التفصيل، ويعلم جميع مراداته من الخلق وللخلق، وليس يعلم العبد ذلك ولا يقدر أن يعلم، ولم يكلف إلا أن يعرف أنه لا إله إلا الله، وتكون معرفته مستندة إلى دليل جلي، كما ورد به التنزيل، وإلا فتكليف ذلك شطط لا يستطاع، فثبت القول والعقد مصدرًا ومظهرًا، وقد يكتفى بالمصدر في القلب إذا لم يقتدر على الإتيان بالإقرار باللسان، لكن في حكم الله تعالى الإشارة في حق الآخرين تنزل منزلة العبارة في حق الناطق، وقصة الخرساء : «أعتقها فإنها مؤمنة» دليل على صحة ما ذكرناه في أصل الإيمان وقدره
And we know with certainty that the Prophet, peace be upon him, just as he was not satisfied from them with mere utterance unless it was accompanied by conviction, likewise he did not obligate all of creation to know Allah Almighty as He is, because that is not possible for creation. The closest proof for that is that He Almighty knows all His objects of knowledge in detail, and knows that He creates all His creatures in detail, and knows all His wills from creation and for creation. But the servant does not know that nor is he able to know. He was only obligated to know that there is no god but Allah, and his knowledge should be based on a clear proof, as the revelation has brought. Otherwise, to obligate such knowledge is an excessive burden that cannot be borne. So it is established that speech and conviction are source and manifestation. And sometimes the source in the heart suffices if one is unable to produce verbal confession by the tongue, but in the ruling of Allah Almighty, gesture for the mute takes the place of speech for the speaker. Commentary
This passage completes the discussion of the pillars of faith. First, the author clarifies that the Prophet did not require people to know God as He knows Himself—that is impossible. The proof is that God knows all things in detail, including His creation and will, but humans cannot know this. Then he reiterates that both utterance (qawl) and conviction (aqd) are essential: one as the source (musdar) and the other as the manifestation (muzhir). However, if a person cannot speak, the conviction in the heart suffices, and gesture (ishara) substitutes for speech in the divine ruling. The story of the mute woman (al-kharsa')—where the Prophet ordered her to be freed because she indicated her faith—serves as evidence.

Refutation of Extremes on Actions and Faith
Author refutes both extremes regarding whether actions are a pillar of iman.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why actions are not a constitutive pillar of faith (iman) and how the Quran and reason demonstrate the distinction between iman and action.

Core Idea
Actions are obligatory but not part of the reality of faith.

The author addresses a critical debate: are actions a pillar of faith or merely dependent obligations? He presents and refutes two extreme positions—the Murji'ah who say actions don't affect faith at all, and the Wa'idiyyah who say a single sin removes faith. Through Quranic evidence and logical reasoning, he establishes that actions are required but not constitutive of iman.

Guided Path
1
Understand the question
The central question is whether action is a pillar (rukn) of faith or a dependent obligation (wajib tabi'). Recognize the significance of this debate.
2
Identify the two extremes
Read the presentation of the Murjiah and Wa'idiyyah positions. The Murjiah separate actions entirely from iman; the Wa'idiyyah make actions a constitutive pillar with eternal consequences for sins.
3
Examine the refutation of the Murjiah
Consider the author's argument that the Murjiah position nullifies obligations, leads to permissiveness, and contradicts revealed rulings linking actions to reward and punishment.
4
Examine the refutation of the Wa'idiyyah
Analyze the Quranic evidence (separation of iman and amal, addressing sinners as believers) and the logical consequence that only prophets would be believers.
5
Conclude the correct position
The author concludes that action is not a constitutive pillar of iman. Both groups are guilty of deferring something—the Murjiah defer action, the Wa'idiyyah defer iman to full action.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of two extreme positions on the relationship between faith (iman) and actions. The author first frames the issue: whether action is a pillar (rukn) of iman or a dependent obligation. Then he presents the Murji'ah view that actions are entirely deferred from the core of iman, so much so that abandoning all acts of obedience does not harm one's iman. Next, he presents the Wa'idiyyah view that action is a constitutive pillar, so that a major sin causes eternal punishment and strips the name of iman. The author then refutes each. The refutation of the Murji'ah argues that their position would nullify most religious obligations, open the door to permissiveness, and contradict the revealed rulings that link consequences to actions. The refutation of the Wa'idiyyah uses Quranic evidence: many verses separate the word 'iman' from 'action', and Allah addresses sinners as 'believers' when prohibiting acts, proving that iman and action are distinct realities. Additional logical reasoning shows that the Wa'idiyyah position implies that only prophets could be called believers, since others inevitably sin. The passage concludes that action is not a constitutive pillar of iman; both groups are guilty of deferring something—the Murji'ah defer action from iman, the Wa'idiyyah defer iman to future completion of actions.

Proof that Iman is Distinct from Action
Author argues from Quran and logic that iman is not constituted by actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational and scriptural proof that iman (faith) and action are distinct realities, and why both the extreme separation (Murji'ah) and extreme merging (Wa'idiyyah) are incorrect.

Core Idea
Iman and action are distinct: faith is not constituted by deeds, yet deeds are obligatory.

This passage presents a rigorous argument distinguishing iman from action, a central issue in Islamic theology. The author refutes two extremes: the Murji'ah, who claim that actions are entirely separate and do not affect faith, and the Wa'idiyyah (Kharijites/Mu'tazila), who make action a constitutive part of iman. By analyzing Quranic phrasing and logical implications, the author establishes the balanced position of Ahl al-Sunnah: iman and action are distinct yet inseparable in terms of obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two extremes
Read the passage and note the two opposing views: 1) the view that sins do no harm (Murji'ah) and 2) the view that equates iman with action (Wa'idiyyah). The author refutes both.
The first extreme is mentioned briefly; the bulk of the argument is against the second.
2
Analyze the Quranic evidence
The author points to verses that separate 'iman' and 'amal' linguistically, e.g., 'Those who believe and do righteous deeds.' This distinction proves they are not identical.
Also note the address to believers prohibiting sin – this would be contradictory if sin negated belief entirely.
3
Understand the logical consequences
If action were part of iman, only a sinless prophet could be called a believer, and the name 'iman' would depend on future actions – both absurd. The conclusion is that action is neither a constitutive pillar nor a mere extra.
This establishes the middle position: action is a binding obligation, not part of the essence of faith.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal proof that iman (faith) is distinct from 'amal (action). The argument proceeds in several steps. First, the author states the opposing view (implicitly the Murji'ah) that sins do not harm and acts do not benefit, but then focuses on refuting the second view (the Wa'idiyyah, i.e., Kharijites and Mu'tazila) who equate iman with action. The author points to numerous Quranic verses that separate 'iman' and 'righteous deeds' linguistically. If they were identical, the distinction would be meaningless. Second, if iman equals action, then only a sinless prophet could be called a believer, which is absurd. Third, this would make the name 'iman' contingent on future actions, which is impractical. The author labels the two extremes: those who separate iman from action entirely (Murji'ah) and those who merge action into the essence of iman (Wa'idiyyah). The conclusion is that action is not a constitutive part of iman (so its absence does not cause disbelief or eternal punishment), but it is also not a mere optional extra (so its absence still deserves blame and punishment). Thus the correct middle position is established: iman is a distinct reality from action, yet action is a required complement that affects reward and punishment.

The Logical Parity and the Incoherence of Mitigated Eternal Punishment
The author sets up a parity argument and criticizes mitigated eternal punishment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical parity between the claims of obliteration and expiation, and why mitigated eternal punishment is incoherent.

Core Idea
If good deeds are obliterated, they cannot mitigate punishment; mitigation and eternity are incompatible.

The author begins by pointing out that the claim that sins obliterate good deeds has no more logical weight than the opposite claim that good deeds raise sins. He then examines the view that a major sinner can receive eternal punishment with some mitigation due to his faith, and shows that this position is self-contradictory because obliteration negates the very grounds for mitigation, and mitigation is incompatible with eternal duration.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of logical objections against the doctrine of obliteration (ihbat) and eternal punishment for major sins. The author begins by equating the claim that sins obliterate good deeds with the opposite claim that good deeds cancel sins – neither is more valid. Then he criticizes the view that a major sinner suffers eternal punishment with mitigation: if the good deeds were obliterated, how could they mitigate? And mitigation is incompatible with eternal punishment. Eternal punishment for a temporally finite sin appears unjust, using the analogy of a thief who steals 100 dinars and is fined 200 – that is not just. The opponent might argue that the punishment is eternal because the disbelief would have continued forever if left unchecked, but the author counters that hypothetical continuation does not justify infinite punishment for a finite act. The conclusion is that justice requires proportional treatment: a believer who both obeys and disobeys deserves praise and blame in proportion. This upholds the Sunni doctrine that major sins do not necessitate eternal punishment, but the sinner is subject to God's will – he may be punished temporarily or forgiven.

The Justice of Proportional Punishment and the Refutation of Infinite Penalty for Finite Sin
The author uses the thief analogy to argue against eternal punishment and concludes with proportional justice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the thief analogy and how the author argues that eternal punishment for a finite sin is not rationally just, and that proportional justice demands praise and blame according to actual deeds.

Core Idea
Eternal punishment for a finite sin is like fining a thief double; it violates rational justice.

The author extends his argument by challenging the eternal punishment of the disbeliever who only disbelieved for a finite period. He uses the analogy of a thief who steals one hundred dinars and is forced to pay two hundred: this is unjust. Even if the thief intended to continue stealing, the punishment must be based on actual acts, not hypotheticals. The conclusion is that justice requires proportional treatment: a believer who obeys and disobeys deserves praise and blame in proportion.

Defending the Believer Against Verse Threats
Interprets Quranic verses to show they do not condemn believers for major sins

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ash'ari scholars interpret Quranic verses that threaten punishment to affirm that major sins do not expel a believer from faith.

Core Idea
Quranic threats of punishment are directed at disbelievers, not at believers who commit sins.

This passage addresses a critical concern: if a believer commits a major sin, do the severe Quranic warnings apply to him? The author defends the Ash'ari position that a believer's faith remains intact, explaining that verses like 'whoever earns evil' and 'whoever kills a believer intentionally' refer to specific disbelieving attitudes, not mere sin. We will see how he uses linguistic and contextual arguments to preserve the believer's status.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of the believer against the claim that one major sin makes him a transgressor of all divine limits, answering an implied objection. The author begins by stating the objection: how can a believer who obeyed Allah for seventy years become a transgressor of all limits by a single sin? Then he interprets two Quranic verses to show they do not apply to such a believer. First, 'Whoever earns evil' (al-Baqara 2:81) is interpreted as meaning 'shirk' (polytheism), not a lesser sin, because the verse says 'his sin encompasses him,' and encompassing from every side is impossible for a believer since his faith blocks that full encompassing. Second, 'Whoever kills a believer intentionally' (al-Nisa 4:93) is interpreted as referring to one who considers killing lawful, because absolute premeditated murder with no excuse is only conceivable from a disbeliever. The author points to the severe threat of anger and curse at the end of that verse, which by consensus is not applied absolutely to a killer who does not consider blood lawful and who is fearful. He then explains that the mention of the wretched and the blessed in the verse is confined to the final outcome in the afterlife, not to this world. The proof of this interpretation, he says, is the exception at the end of the verse (implying the verse's threat is not unconditional). This reasoning preserves the Ash'ari position that the believer's faith remains intact despite major sins.
