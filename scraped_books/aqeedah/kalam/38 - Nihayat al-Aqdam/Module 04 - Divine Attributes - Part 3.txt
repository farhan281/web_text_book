The Consequence of Restricting Knowledge to Existents
The affirmers argue that restricting knowledge to existents entails finitude of God's knowledge and a dilemma regarding necessary concomitants.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the affirmers argue that limiting knowledge to existents leads to the absurd conclusion that God's knowledge is finite.

Core Idea
If knowledge only attaches to existents, then God's knowledge would be limited to finite things.

The affirmers point out a necessary consequence of the negators' position: if knowledge only attaches to what exists, then the objects of knowledge would be only the finite existents. This would make God's knowledge finite, which is unacceptable. This lesson explores that argument and the further dilemma about necessary concomitants (lawazim).

Guided Path
1
Read the second part of the passage
Read the Arabic passage that begins with 'Wa-qawlukum' and the translation. Focus on the argument structure.
2
Identify the reductio ad absurdum
The affirmers derive a contradiction from the negators' view. If knowledge only attaches to existents, then the objects of knowledge are finite (since existents are finite). That would make God's knowledge finite, which the negators do not accept.
3
Understand the dilemma about necessary concomitants
The affirmers introduce necessary concomitants (lawazim) such as the property of being preceded by non-existence. If these are known, they must be like existents in being objects of knowledge; if they are not known, then knowledge stops at finite objects. Either way, the negators' position is problematic.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The affirmers show that the negators cannot consistently hold that knowledge only attaches to existents without accepting absurd consequences. Therefore, knowledge must also attach to non-existents.

Then those necessary concomitants (lawazim): if they are known, they are like existents equally in the attachment of knowledge to them; and if they are not known, then knowledge and its objects have come to an end.

Commentary
This passage presents the affirmers' (those who affirm the reality of non-existence) refutation of the negators. They make two main points: First, they counter the claim that knowledge requires existence or estimation of existence by citing God's knowledge of the world's non-existence in pre-eternity (azal). In pre-eternity, the world did not exist, and estimation of its existence is impossible because estimation is a mental act that would be impossible for God (since it implies ignorance or deliberation). Yet God had knowledge of the world's future existence, so that knowledge must have attached to something established (thabit) in pre-eternity, namely the non-existence of the world. Second, they point out a consequence of the negators' view: if knowledge only attaches to existents, then the objects of knowledge would be limited to finite existents, making knowledge finite, which is absurd for God. Moreover, the necessary concomitants (like impossibility preceding impossibility etc.) would either be known (and thus treated as existents in that context) or unknown, leading to a halt in knowledge. Thus the negators' position is inconsistent.

The Dilemma of Creation: Essence or Other?
The author presents a dilemma about whether God created essences themselves or something else.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical dilemma that challenges the idea that essences are created by God, and why this dilemma leads to the conclusion that essences are uncreated.

Core Idea
If essences were created, they would either be uncreatable or lead to infinite regress.

The author presents a powerful dilemma to test whether essences themselves are created. The question seems simple, but it exposes a deep problem: if God created the very essence of a thing, then that essence would already be fixed in non-existence and thus not subject to divine power. If instead He created something else, we are stuck in an infinite regress. This short passage forces us to think carefully about what 'creation' means.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Focus on the rhetorical question and the two conditional responses.
Notice the structure: a premise, a question, and two 'if...then' responses.
2
Identify the two horns
The dilemma asks: did God create the essence (ayn/dhat) or something else? These are the two options. Label each and examine their consequences.
3
Understand the first horn
If God created the essence, then the essence is fixed in non-existence and is an intrinsic attribute not subject to God's power. That contradicts the idea of creation.
The terms 'thabita fi al-‘adam' and 'sifat dhatiyya' are key.
4
Understand the second horn
If God created something else, then the same question applies to that other thing, leading to an infinite regress. This is also unacceptable.
Regress is a classic argument against creation of essences.
5
Reflect on the dilemma's force
Ask yourself: why does this dilemma compel the opponent to accept that essences are not created? The only way to avoid both horns is to deny that essences are created at all.
This connects to the broader kalam discussion on the eternity of essences.

The second horn: if He created something other, the same question applies to that other, leading to infinite regress. The dilemma forces the opponent to accept that essences are not created.

God's Pre-eternal Knowledge Requires a Fixed Object
The affirmers' first argument from pre-eternal knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the affirmers argue that God's pre-eternal knowledge of non-existence implies the reality of non-existent objects as fixed entities.

Core Idea
Knowledge of non-existence proves that non-existing things are still something fixed.

The affirmers (al-muthbitun) challenge the previous claim that knowledge only relates to existence or estimation. They point to God's knowledge of the world's non-existence in pre-eternity—a clear case where there is no existence or estimation. How can God have knowledge of something that does not exist? Their answer: there must be a fixed object (ma‘lum thabit) that serves as the object of knowledge, even in non-existence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage for Lesson 1
Read the first part of the objection (up to 'shay’ thabit'). Notice how the affirmers set up their argument.
The passage uses technical terms like 'ma‘lum'.
2
Identify the two reasons estimation is impossible
The text says estimation of the world in pre-eternity is impossible, and estimation from God is impossible. Understand why each is impossible.
Estimation from God would imply deliberation, which is not an attribute of God.
3
Grasp the conclusion: knowledge needs a realized known object
Linking the impossibility to the necessity of knowledge having a relation (muta‘allaq), the affirmers conclude that the object of knowledge must be something fixed (thabit).
This is the key move: the object is real even if not existing.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider why this matters for the larger debate about non-existent entities (ma‘dum).
This supports the view that non-existent things have some kind of reality.

The Reductio: If Knowledge Only Relates to Existence, God's Knowledge Would Be Finite
The affirmers' second argument: a reductio on the finitude of knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the reductio argument that forces the opponent to accept non-existent realities to avoid limiting God's knowledge.

Core Idea
Limiting knowledge to existence makes God's knowledge finite – an absurd conclusion.

The affirmers press their objection further with a second argument. They show that if knowledge only relates to existence, then God's knowledge is confined to existing things. Since existents are finite, God's knowledge would also be finite—a conclusion the opponent would reject. The only way out is to admit that knowledge can relate to non-existents as well.

Guided Path
1
Read the second part of the passage
Read from 'Wa qawlukum' to the end. Notice the logical structure.
This is a reductio ad absurdum (proof by contradiction).
2
Identify the conditional premise
The affirmers state: if knowledge only relates to existence at the time of existence, then God's knowledge of non-existence before existence would be impossible. This forces a dilemma.
3
Understand the consequence: finitude of knowledge
If knowledge is confined to existents, then God's knowledge is limited to finite beings, making it finite. But the opponent denies that God's knowledge is finite.
This is a contradiction.
4
Consider the necessary implications
The passage ends by discussing whether the necessary implications (al-lawazim) are known or not, both leading to problems.
If they are known, they become additional objects; if not, knowledge is finite.
5
Summarize the force of the argument
The opponent must concede that non-existent things (ma‘dumat) are known by God to avoid limiting His knowledge.

The Principle: Existence Negated, Essence Prior
The author explains his principle on existence and intrinsic attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the author's principle that intrinsic attributes (like substantiality) belong to things by their essence, not by God's act, and that existence is not a fixed attribute.

Core Idea
Intrinsic attributes are prior to existence and uncreated.

The author begins by addressing an objection about whether existence is negated or affirmed. He clarifies that existence is not a fixed attribute, but intrinsic attributes like substantiality and accidentalness are real 'things' that belong to essences independently of God's creative act. This distinction is the secret of his doctrine.

The Negators' Objection: Attributes Follow Contingency
The negators argue that contingent attributes imply all essential attributes are contingent and reverse the causal order.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the negators' argument that if some attributes are contingent, then all essential attributes should be contingent, and their reversal of the causal relationship between power and existence.

Core Idea
If some attributes follow contingency, then all essential attributes should do the same.

After asserting the identity of existence and essence, the negators launch a counter-objection against their opponents. They argue that attributes like being in a place and accepting accidents are not directly caused by power but follow contingency. If the opponents affirm these only after contingency, then they should also affirm that all essential attributes follow contingency. The negators further reverse the causal order, claiming that power causes location, and existence simply follows.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. Notice the attributes listed: being in a place (al-taḥayyuz), accepting accidents (qubūl al-ʿaraḍ), and subsistence of accidents in a substratum (qiyām al-ʿaraḍ bil-maḥall).
2
Understand the logical challenge
The negators point out that these attributes are not effects of power but are concomitant with contingency. Their opponents do not affirm them before contingency. Then they ask: why not say all essential attributes follow contingency?
This is a consistency argument.
3
Examine the reversal
In the last part, the negators reverse the follower/followed relationship: they propose that being in a place is caused by power, and existence follows it. This is a key move.

Commentary
This passage presents the reply of the negators (al-nufāt) who deny that existence is an additional attribute to essence. They argue that existence, identity, essence, substantiality, and accidentality are all expressions for a single meaning. When the agent brings something into existence, it is the essence that is brought; power (qudra) attaches to the essence just as it attaches to existence, and it affects substantiality just as it affects obtaining and origination. Any distinction between existence and thingness (al-shay'iyya) is merely verbal, not real. Genera, species, universality, and particularity are merely abstract words or mental considerations. Then the author notes an objection they raise against their opponents: attributes that follow contingency (al-ḥudūth) — such as being located in a place (al-taḥayyuz), accepting accidents (qubūl al-ʿaraḍ), and accidents subsisting in a subject — are not effects of power, yet they are not affirmed before contingency. So why not say that all essential attributes follow contingency as well? The negators also reverse the relationship between follower and followed, insisting that being in a place occurs by power and existence follows it.

Dependence of Essence and Attributes on the Creator
Shahrastani argues that essence and attributes depend on the Creator as much as existence does.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that essential attributes such as essence and identity are not independent of the Creator but require Him just as existence does.

Core Idea
Essential attributes are not independent of the Creator.

Some theologians claimed that only existence is attributed to the Creator, while essential attributes are not. In this passage, Shahrastani refutes this claim by showing that the essence, identity, and attributes of a possible being all depend on the Creator in the same way existence does. The passage presents a logical argument that ties the possibility of existence to the possibility of being an essence or attribute.

Essential Attributes Depend on the Agent as Much as Existence
Refutation of the claim that essential attributes are not attributed to the agent

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Your existence and your essence: both need the Creator equally.

Some theologians argued that only existence is a gift from God; essential attributes like substantiality are not. This passage refutes that by showing that a thing's essence, identity, and attributes are inseparable from its existence. Just as a thing needs a cause for its existence, it needs a cause for what it is.

The Contradiction in the Theory of States
Critique of the Ash'ari theory of states.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Ash'ari theory of states (ahwal) leads to a logical contradiction regarding the knowledge of types.

Core Idea
Types cannot be both known in non-existence and unknowable in existence.

The author begins with astonishment at those who affirm states, claiming that types like substantiality are known in non-existence but become unknowable states in existence. This passage exposes the inherent contradiction in such a view.

The author uses rhetorical questions to highlight the absurdity.
2
Identify the two claims
The affirmers claim types are 'things established in non-existence' (because known) and also 'states in existence not known in themselves'. Identify both claims in the passage.
3
Notice the contradiction
The author points out: a type is known in non-existence but not known in existence. Ask why this is contradictory.
This challenges the coherence of the theory.

Commentary
This passage is a complex refutation of the Ash'ari theory of states (ahwal) and its extension by the Mu'tazila. The author begins by expressing astonishment at the affirmers of states, who claim that types like substantiality, corporeality, color-ness, etc., are things established in non-existence because knowledge attaches to them. They argue that the known must be something for knowledge to lean on. But then, in existence, these same types are states that are not known independently or existing in isolation. The author points out the absurdity: something that is known in non-existence but not known in existence. He then suggests that if they had followed the intellect's method of conceiving things by genera and species, they would realize that intellectual conceptions of quiddities do not require actual external existence, nor do they need to be things outside the mind. The essential constituents in the mind do not depend on an agent's action such that they can be known without considering existence; the causes of existence and causes of quiddity are distinct. Furthermore, sensory perceptions of things' essences as individuals require actual existence and accidental particularizers that depend on an agent. Thus, quiddity and existence have different causes. The author then criticizes the Mu'tazila for misunderstanding the philosophers' distinction between these two divisions (mental and external). The Mu'tazila mistakenly thought that mental conceptions are things established in external entities, leading them to claim that the non-existent is a thing. They also thought that the existence of genera and species in the mind are states in external entities, again concluding that the non-existent is a thing and that the state is established. The author dismisses this with 'evil hearing and evil response.' Finally, he explains his methodological commitment in this book: to clarify the sources and origins of doctrines and the common limit of intellectual endeavors in matters of principles, otherwise he would not have bothered to unveil these secrets.

Thus, the Sifatiyya establish that it is not the judgment but the subject that requires a cause (in the sense of specification), while the judgment is necessitated by its cause.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an argument by the Sifatiyya (those who affirm attributes) concerning the uniformity of conditions (shart) between the witnessed world (shahid) and the unseen (gha'ib). The Sifatiyya argue: 'You (the Mu'tazila) agree that a condition must be applied uniformly in both realms. For example, being alive is a condition for being knowledgeable in the witnessed world, so it must also be a condition in the unseen. Therefore, to prove that God is knowledgeable, you must first prove He is alive, and you cannot distinguish between the possible and the necessary in conditions.' The author then points out a flaw: In the witnessed world, life alone is not a sufficient condition for knowledge; other conditions are needed, such as bodily structure (al-binya) and the absence of opposites (intifa' al-addad). These additional conditions are not uniform—bodily structure does not apply to God, and the absence of opposites is different. Thus, the condition of life is not simply transferable. Furthermore, one condition can serve multiple things, and multiple conditions can serve one thing, so the Sifatiyya's demand for strict uniformity fails. The author thereby shows that the condition argument does not bind the Mu'tazila.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Thinking that the condition for knowledge in the witnessed (life) must apply identically to God without considering additional necessary conditions.
Correction: The passage shows that life alone is not sufficient; additional conditions like bodily structure and absence of opposites are also required in the witnessed, and these are not transferable to God.

Checkpoint

A) Because the Sifatiyya themselves do not apply conditions uniformly.
B) Because life is not a condition for knowledge in the witnessed world at all.
C) Because life alone is insufficient; other conditions like bodily structure and absence of opposites are also required, and these are not transferable.
D) Because the Mu'tazila do not believe in conditions.

Key Takeaway
The Sifatiyya's demand for uniform conditions fails because conditions in the witnessed are composite and not all transferable to God.
