Messengers Establish Divine Speech
Argues that establishing divine speech depends on the possibility of messengers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the possibility of sending messengers establishes divine speech, command, and prohibition.

Core Idea
Messengers prove that God speaks, commands, and prohibits.

This passage presents a key argument in Islamic theology: the very possibility of prophethood necessitates that God possesses speech. The author links the concepts of messenger and divine command, showing that denying God's speech leads to denying prophethood. Let's explore how he builds this connection.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic source and its English translation carefully. Notice how the author presents a conditional argument linking speech, messengers, and commands.
2
Identify the key conditional link
The passage states that establishing speech, command, and prohibition is built on the possibility of sending messengers. Identify how denying speech leads to denying the sending of messengers.
3
Understand the refutation of the alternative view
Some opponents interpret divine commands as mere acts indicating will. The author argues that such an act still implies requirement and judgment, not mere wishful thinking, thus conceding the point.
4
Appreciate the conclusion
The meaning conveyed by the messenger's act is what is called speech, command, and prohibition according to Islamic law. Reflect on how this argument ties prophethood to divine attributes.

We say: The establishment of speech (كلام), command (أمر), and prohibition (نهي) is based on the possibility of sending messengers. For whoever considers it impossible that He be speaking, it follows that he denies that He is a sender of messengers. And whoever considers it possible for God, exalted, to send a messenger, then that messenger conveys the messages of God without doubt, and His messages are His commands and prohibitions. To interpret them as an act He does in a place such that one understands from that act that He wills the obliged to do an action and dislikes an action is to concede the issue. For that will that relates to the action of another until he does it is a will that includes requirement and judgment; otherwise it would be wishful thinking and desire. And that is what is called command and prohibition, and you have called it will and dislike. Thus, the meaning of that act to which they refer is what we call according to the law (شرع) speech, command, and prohibition.

Commentary
This passage argues that divine speech, command, and prohibition are established by the very fact that God can send messengers. If one denies that God speaks, one must also deny that He sends messengers. But if one accepts the possibility of sending messengers, then those messengers convey God's messages, which are commands and prohibitions. Some opponents interpret these as mere acts that convey will or dislike. The author argues that such an interpretation still concedes the point because that will includes requirement and judgment, not mere wish. What is conveyed is precisely what the author calls speech, command, and prohibition.

The Inability of Mutakallimun to Explain the Spirit's Manifestation
The author argues that the mutakallim cannot coherently explain the spirit's manifestation in human form.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the mutakallimun's metaphysical framework cannot coherently explain the miraculous manifestation of spiritual beings in physical form, and how this failure extends to understanding divine communication in human languages.

Core Idea
The mutakallim's explanations for spiritual manifestation are either annihilation or reincarnation—neither is genuine manifestation.

The author challenges the mutakallimun to account for the miracle of the spirit appearing as a human, as in the story of Maryam. He argues that on their principles, the two possible explanations—annihilation or using an existing body—both fail to qualify as true manifestation. This exposes a fundamental weakness in their metaphysical system, which also undermines their ability to explain how the eternal divine command can be expressed in different human languages.

Guided Path
1
Encounter the Quranic verse and the problem
Read the Quranic verse cited (19:17) and note how the author uses it to pose a challenge to the mutakallimun: how can a spirit appear as a physical human?
The verse describes the angel Gabriel appearing to Mary as a well-proportioned man.
2
Identify the two failed explanations
The author presents two possible explanations a mutakallim might offer: either the spirit is annihilated and a new body is created, or the spirit uses an existing human body. Understand why each fails to be genuine manifestation.
The first is not manifestation but replacement; the second is reincarnation (tanasukh), not manifestation.
3
Grasp the broader implication for divine speech
The author extends the critique: if mutakallimun cannot explain spiritual-physical manifestation, how can they conceive of the eternal command manifesting in different human languages (Arabic, Syriac)?
This highlights a deeper inconsistency in their framework regarding divine attributes.

Commentary
This passage is a critique of the mutakallimun's inability to account for the manifestation of spiritual beings in physical form, using the Quranic story of the angel Gabriel's appearing to Mary as a man (19:17). The author presents two possible explanations on the mutakallim's principles, both of which fail. First, the spirit could be annihilated and a new physical body created, but that is not 'manifestation' (tamaththul) but complete replacement. Second, the spirit could take over an existing human body, but that is not manifestation either; it is reincarnation (tanasukh). Since neither is tenable, the mutakallim cannot explain this miracle. The author then draws an analogy: if they cannot explain the spiritual-physical manifestation, how can they conceive of the eternal command (al-amr al-azali) manifesting in different human languages (Arabic, Syriac) over time? The passage ends mid-sentence, but the implication is that the mutakallim's framework is inadequate for both phenomena.

Two Interpretations of Animal Speech in the Quran
The author explains two ways to understand Quranic attributions of speech to animals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two ways Muslim theologians interpret Quranic verses that attribute speech to animals, preserving the miraculous nature while affirming that animals do not naturally speak.

Core Idea
Quranic animal speech is a miracle, not a natural ability.

The Quran mentions ants, birds, and even a sheep speaking. How can this be reconciled with the fact that animals do not possess rational speech? The author presents two acceptable interpretations that treat these events as miracles for prophets rather than as natural occurrences. This lesson examines both views and the example of the speaking sheep's leg.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to understand the two interpretations. Notice the author's structure: first interpretation then second, with an example.
2
Identify the two interpretations
List the two ways the author explains animal speech. Note the key difference: one involves God giving the animal intellect and actual speech, the other involves God causing words to be heard from the animal without its awareness.
3
Reflect on the miracle aspect
Consider why both interpretations are considered miracles. How do they uphold the Quranic text while respecting natural laws? The example of the sheep's leg is especially telling.
The sheep's leg incident is a well-known miracle in Islamic tradition.

Commentary
This passage deals with Quranic verses that attribute speech to animals (e.g., the ant and birds). The author provides two acceptable interpretations to reconcile these verses with the principle that animals do not possess real speech. The first interpretation is that God caused a miracle by giving the animal intellect and making it actually speak with letters and sounds, for the benefit of a prophet. The second is that God caused the words to be heard from the animal without the animal's knowledge or intention, similar to the well-known incident of the roasted sheep's leg that spoke to the Prophet Muhammad and informed him it was poisoned. Both interpretations preserve the miraculous nature while maintaining that animals do not inherently have speech.

The Soul's Power to Actualize Others
The author describes the soul's unique ability to teach and actualize others.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the unique property of the human soul to bring other souls from potentiality to actuality through teaching, and how this distinguishes humans from animals.

Core Idea
Teaching is a unique power of the human soul to actualize others.

This passage from al-Shahrastani's Nihayat al-Aqdam explores a special characteristic of the human soul: its ability not only to develop itself but also to develop other souls. The author provides concrete examples of teaching to illustrate this causal property. He then contrasts this with animal instinct, showing what makes humans unique.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the literal translation. Let the author's description of the soul's property sink in.
2
Identify the special property
Notice that the author states: when the soul itself exits from potentiality to actuality, it can bring other souls from potentiality to actuality. This is the core idea.
3
Examine the three examples
The author gives examples of a teacher of reading, a teacher of wisdom, and a teacher of crafts. In each case, the teacher's soul causes the student's soul to actualize the same skill.
Think about how each example shows the same principle: the teacher's actualized state becomes the student's actuality.
4
Contrast with animals
The author then says this property is not for any animal, because animals only have what is by nature and instinct. Humans can teach, animals cannot.
5
Connect to the Quranic verse
The author cites the verse about Adam learning the names as a proof. Then he notes humans have both the power to receive and to perform knowledge and action.

The Basis of Hearing and the Reality of Divine Speech
The author discusses the basis for hearing and explains how God's speech is heard without means.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic theological explanation of how God's speech is heard without physical means, and the distinction between the modes of revelation.

Core Idea
God's speech is heard directly by the soul, without letters, sounds, or intermediaries.

This passage by the author addresses a key theological question: how can God's speech be heard if it is not composed of letters and sounds? The author begins by noting different opinions on the basis for hearing, then builds an analogy from human experience to explain how divine speech is perceived. The discussion is crucial for understanding the nature of revelation and the distinction between the modes by which prophets receive God's words.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic source once without overthinking. Then read the literal English translation to get the flow of the argument. Notice the author's use of the Shura verse and the analogy from internal speech.
The passage is dense; reading twice helps.
2
Identify the three modes of revelation
The author cites Quran 42:51 to enumerate three ways Allah communicates with humans: (1) direct revelation without means, (2) from behind a veil, (3) through an angel. Find these in the passage and note the examples given.
Each mode corresponds to different prophets' experiences.
3
Understand the analogy from human internal speech
The author argues that just as we have internal speech (كلام نفسي) that we express with the tongue and hear with the ear, we can conceive of a direct communication from soul to soul. This analogy makes sense of how Moses heard Allah's speech without physical sound.
The key is that internal speech is known to the person without external senses.
4
Apply the analogy to Moses and to ordinary believers
The author uses the example of Moses hearing Allah from behind a veil (without means) and then distinguishes that from how we hear the Quran. The passage ends by noting the distinction between recitation and the recited.
This distinction is important for avoiding anthropomorphism.

The First Proof: Miracle as Confirmation by Analogy
Presents the first method: miracle as confirmation by analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a miracle, when conjoined with a prophetic claim and free from opposition, functions as God's definitive confirmation of the prophet's truthfulness, analogous to a king's act confirming a messenger.

Core Idea
A miracle is God's silent 'yes' to a prophet's claim, proven by analogy with worldly signs.

Theologians need a rational method to establish that a miracle truly confirms a prophet. This passage introduces the first method: a logical analogy that compares a prophetic miracle to a king's action confirming a messenger. By analyzing the circumstances, we can see that a miracle accompanied by a challenge and without counter-evidence yields certainty.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the full translation. Notice the logical flow from the general principle to the analogy and back to the conclusion.
The analogy is central; try to visualize the scenario.
2
Understand the analogy (king behind curtain)
Follow the story: a commoner claims to be a messenger of a hidden king and requests the king to move a curtain as a sign. The king moves it immediately. This proves the claimant's truthfulness.
The king's action is a miracle in the analogy because it happens contrary to custom and at the request.
3
Identify the three conditions for certainty
The passage explicitly lists three matters that together constitute a definitive indication: the miracle itself (khāriq li-l-ʿāda), its conjunction with the claim (muqārin li-l-daʿwa), and its freedom from opposition (salāma ʿan al-muʿāraḍa).
These conditions mirror the analogy: the movement (miracle) is done at the request (claim) and without anyone else moving the curtain (no opposition).
4
See how they combine to yield certainty
The combination of these three circumstances functions like verbal confirmation. Just as we know the king intended to confirm the messenger by his action, we know God intended to confirm the prophet by the miracle.
This certainty is akin to knowledge obtained from circumstantial and verbal indications (qarāʾin al-ḥāl wa al-maqāl).

So how could he grant him a delay and leave him to himself, when he is commanded to call people to tawhid and knowledge case by case and person by person? Requesting a delay only arises if the call is empty of a compelling proof and a silencing evidence. For one whose mission is established in itself and who undertakes to call people to the oneness of God, the Exalted, does not leave his call devoid of evidence for tawhid first; then he challenges with the message second, considering the state of all the prophets, peace be upon them, in their call.

As for Lot, he called people with the call of the Friend [Abraham], and what occurred between him and his people—worshippers of idols and stars—is among the clearest that can be imagined. He said: 'The Lord of the heavens and the earth and what is between them,' and in answer to 'Who is your Lord?' he said: 'Our Lord is He who gave everything its creation, then guided' [Quran 20:50]. Then after he had established tawhid, he said: 'Indeed I am a messenger from the Lord of the worlds.'

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite position that prophets must grant a delay (مهلة) for rational reflection before accepting their message. The author begins by stating that the objection of 'silencing the prophets' (إفحام الأنبياء) only applies if one holds that a delay is obligatory—which is the Mu'tazilite view. The author's own position is that the prophet does not grant a delay and is not obligated to do so. Instead, the prophet immediately presents a compelling proof: he points out that the listener necessarily knows he was created and relies on a Creator, which is an undeniable truth. This is exemplified by the Quranic verse 'O mankind, worship your Lord...' (2:21). The author argues: how can the prophet delay when he is commanded to call people to tawhid continuously? The request for delay only arises if the call lacks a convincing proof. One whose prophetic mission is established and who calls to God's oneness must first provide evidence for tawhid, then challenge with the message of prophethood. The author then supports this by examining the practice of all prophets. He lists examples: Adam's truthfulness was established by God's announcement to the angels; he had no polytheists to call. Noah first called to worship God alone, then declared prophethood. Hud first called to tawhid, then said he was a messenger. Salih did the same. Lot argued against idolatry before asserting his prophethood. Moses first taught Pharaoh about the Lord of the heavens and earth and the Giver of all things, then declared his prophethood. The pattern is consistent: tawhid is always established first, followed by the claim of prophethood. Thus, the prophets did not grant a delay but immediately presented evidence for God's oneness.

Commentary
This passage is an answer to the fifth objection. The author argues that the method of recognizing a true prophet is not confined to miracles. He lists several possibilities: 1) Allah could create direct necessary knowledge of the prophet's truthfulness in the listener, so no miracle is needed. 2) He could provide non-supernatural indicators (أمارات) that, through circumstantial evidence (قرينة الحال والمقال), yield certainty for some but not others. 3) He could inform directly, as when Allah told the angels 'I am placing a caliph on earth,' and when that report establishes a prophet's truthfulness, all subsequent hearers must accept it. The author then gives examples from earlier prophets: Jesus announced Ahmad, Moses announced the unlettered prophet, Abraham prayed for a messenger. He mentions that Moses had a house of images of prophets and saints, which served as evidence. He concludes that the Prophet Muhammad's miracles were primarily for the unlettered Arabs, while the People of the Book already had sufficient evidence from previous scriptures and reports.
