The Four Types of Precedence and Posteriority
Enumeration of four types of precedence and posteriority with terminological clarification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four distinct meanings of precedence and posteriority used in kalam and the proper terminological distinction between 'essence' and 'causality'.

Core Idea
Precedence and posteriority are not one-dimensional; they have four distinct meanings.

Theologians use the terms 'precedence' and 'posteriority' in multiple senses. In this passage, the author lists four: temporal, spatial (rank), virtue, and essence (which really means causality). He then clarifies a subtle point about the final cause versus efficient and formal causes, which is crucial for understanding the relationship between God and creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
First, read the original Arabic passage and its literal English translation. Let the author's words settle in your mind before analyzing.
The passage uses technical terms; don't worry if not everything is clear yet.
2
Identify the four types of precedence
The author lists four types: temporal, spatial (also called rank), virtue, and essence (which he later corrects to causality). Find each in the text and note the examples given.
The example for temporal is parent-child; for spatial is imam-ma'mum; for virtue is scholar-ignorant; for essence is cause-effect.
3
Understand the critique of 'essence'
The author points out that using 'essence' for precedence is imprecise because 'essence' is broader than 'causality'. He argues that the correct term should be 'precedence by causality'.
This is a terminological correction important for precision in theological arguments.
4
Distinguish types of causes
The author then distinguishes the final cause (which precedes only in the mind, not in existence) from efficient and formal causes (which are concurrent in existence with their effects). Grasp this distinction.
This has implications for understanding God's relationship to the world: God as efficient cause is concurrent, not separate in time.

The Fifth Type and the Limits of Induction
The author critiques the inductive limitation of precedence divisions and proposes a sixth type.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Classifications of precedence are based on induction, not demonstration.

In this passage, the author challenges the established four- or five-fold classification of precedence. He argues that the only evidence for the limit is induction, not logical necessity. He even suggests a sixth type: precedence in existence without essential causation, time, or place. This opens the door to further rational investigation.

Author introduces two methods and explains the first method's four-step proof.

Core Idea
The mutakallimun prove the world's temporality through a four-step chain involving accidents and substances.

In this passage, the author outlines the two methods of the mutakallimun for addressing the question of whether the world is temporally originated or preeternal. He then elaborates on the first method, which is a foundational proof used by the majority. This proof proceeds in four logical steps, each building on the previous one, culminating in the conclusion that the world must have a beginning.

Al-Ash'ari's Argument from Assembly and Separation
Al-Ash'ari argues that preeternal substances require a cause for assembly or separation, proving their temporality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Ash'ari's logical argument that preeternal substances require an external cause for their states, thereby proving their temporality.

Core Idea
Essences do not change; changing states demand an external cause.

Al-Ash'ari presents a rigorous logical argument to demonstrate that the world cannot be preeternal. By examining substances and their possible states of assembly or separation, he shows that change in state implies an external cause, which must be temporal, thus the substances themselves must be temporal. This passage articulates the core of that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the translation. Pay attention to the logical flow from assumption to conclusion.
2
Understand the enumeration of states
Al-Ash'ari lists all possible states for preeternal substances: assembled, separated, neither, both, or some mixture. Notice he reduces these to the essential fact that substances are subject to assembly and separation.
The reduction is key: substances must be either assembled or separated, or subject to change between these states.
3
Grasp the premise about essences
The crucial premise: a substance's essence does not change, but we observe it changing from assembly to separation. This indicates the change is not from the essence itself.
This premise is the foundation for the need for an external cause.
4
Follow the conclusion
Since change occurs, there must be an external 'assembler and divider' (jami' farq). This cause is temporal, so what is preceded by temporal events must itself be temporal.
Thus the assumption of preeternity is invalidated.

This cause is temporal, so the substances are preceded by events, therefore they must be temporally originated. Proof That an Infinite Body Is Impossible
Geometric proof that infinite body is impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof that an actually infinite body cannot exist, and why the same reasoning applies to numbers and individuals.

Core Idea
An infinite body leads to logical contradiction via superimposition of lines.

The existence of an infinite body is not merely empirically unlikely; it is rationally impossible. This lesson presents a classic kalam proof using two lines of different lengths. By reasoning about what happens when the shorter line is superimposed on the longer, we uncover a contradiction that demonstrates the impossibility of any actual infinite in the physical world.

Guided Path
1
Visualize the two lines
Imagine two lines starting from the same point, one longer than the other by a fixed length (e.g., a cubit). Both are assumed to extend infinitely.
This is a thought experiment, not a physical drawing.
2
Consider the superposition
If the shorter line is superimposed on the longer, either it covers the entire longer line (making them equal) or it falls short. Both options lead to contradictions if the lines are infinite.
The proof examines both cases.
3
Analyze the two cases
Case 1: The shorter equals the longer → impossible because they are unequal by definition. Case 2: The shorter falls short by a finite amount → then both lines are finite, contradicting the assumption of infinity.
The key is that finite difference makes both finite.
4
Extend the proof to numbers and individuals
The same logical structure applies to any supposed infinite collection: it leads to contradictions of size and magnitude, showing that actual infinites cannot exist.
This reinforces the conclusion that the physical world must be finite.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
على خط هو أنقص من الأول بذراع، ونصل بين النقطتين بحيث يطبق الخط الأصغر على الخط الأطول، فإن كان كل واحد من الخطين تمادى إلى غير نهاية، فيكون الخط الأصغر مساويا للخط الأطول، وهو محال، وإن قصر الأقصر عن الأطول بمتناه فقد تناهى الأقصر وتناهى الأطول؛ إذ قصر عنه الأقصر بمتناه وزاد الأطول عليه بمتناه، وما زاد على الشيء بمتناه كان متناهيا. وعلى كل حال فإن كان أحدهما أكبر من الثاني كان فيما لا يتناهى ما هو أصغر وأكبر وأكثر وأقل وذلك محال، فعلم أن جسما لا يتناهى محال وجوده، وإن بعدا لا يتناهى في خلا أو ملا محال، ويمكن أن ينقل هذا البرهان بعينه إلى أعداد وأشخاص لا تتناهى حتى يتبين أن فرض ذلك محال.
On a line that is shorter than the first by a cubit, and we connect the two points such that the smaller line is superimposed on the longer line. If each of the two lines extends indefinitely, then the smaller line would equal the longer line, which is impossible. And if the shorter falls short of the longer by a finite amount, then both the shorter and longer are finite, because the shorter falls short of it by a finite amount and the longer exceeds it by a finite amount, and what exceeds something by a finite amount is finite. In any case, if one is greater than the other, then in what is infinite there would be something smaller and larger, more and less, which is impossible. So it is known that an infinite body is impossible in existence, and that an infinite distance in void or plenum is impossible. And this same proof can be transferred to infinite numbers and individuals until it becomes clear that assuming that is impossible.

Commentary
This passage presents a geometric proof (burhān) against the existence of an actually infinite body. The author imagines two lines, one shorter than the other by a fixed length. If both lines extend infinitely, the shorter line would either equal the longer (if the difference is absorbed in infinity) or remain shorter by a finite amount, making both finite. This contradiction proves that infinite bodies cannot exist. The proof is then extended to numbers and individuals, showing that any actual infinite leads to absurdity. This is a classic kalam argument for the finitude of the world.

The Proof of Universal Finitude
Conditional argument proving finitude of all beings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational argument that all created beings—from souls to spiritual movers—must be finite, and the distinction between actual and estimative infinity.

Core Idea
If an infinite multitude were actual, it would entail logical contradictions.

This passage presents a sophisticated conditional argument to prove that everything in creation—souls, bodies, celestial motions, and spiritual movers—must be finite. Using logical reasoning, the author shows that an actually infinite number of souls leads to impossibility, and then extends the conclusion to all beings. He also clarifies the meaning of 'infinite number,' distinguishing between actual infinity (impossible) and infinity in estimation (possible in thought).

Guided Path
1
Follow the conditional argument about souls
Read the first part of the passage carefully. The author sets up a conditional: if an infinite number of souls have already entered existence, then consider the remaining souls. They must be either finite or infinite. Follow each case and see the contradiction.
Notice the use of 'if-then' reasoning typical of kalam.
2
Understand the proportion argument
The author then introduces a proportion argument: the remaining souls must have a numerical proportion (e.g., half) relative to the existing infinite. But an actual infinite cannot have a proportion. This proves the total must be finite.
This step reinforces why an actual infinite is impossible.
3
See the extension to all beings
The conclusion that souls are finite is then extended: since each soul is attached to a body, bodies are finite; then elemental mixtures, celestial motions, and spiritual movers are all finite. This shows the universal finitude of creation.
The argument cascades from souls to the entire cosmos.

Al-Baqillani's Proof: Power Affects an Attribute, Not Existence
Al-Baqillani argues that power's effect is on an attribute, not existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Baqillani's argument that created power only affects an attribute (a state) of an action, not the existence or non-existence of the action itself.

Core Idea
Power shapes the quality of an act, not its being.

This lesson presents one of the most precise arguments in classical kalām: the distinction between voluntary and involuntary motion leads to a necessary conclusion about the scope of created power. al-Baqillani uses this everyday experience to show that power's effect must be on an attribute, not on existence itself, thereby refining our understanding of causality within Ashʿarī theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its full translation to get a sense of the argument's flow and key terms.
Pay attention to the logical structure: empirical observation → two dichotomies → conclusion.
2
Understand the empirical premise
Al-Baqillani begins with a necessary human perception: we clearly distinguish between voluntary and involuntary movements (e.g., a tremor vs. raising a hand). This distinction is not based on the movements themselves (they are identical as motions) but on something extra: one is willed and powered, the other is not.
This empirical starting point is crucial; it grounds the argument in common human experience.
3
Follow the first dichotomy
The argument then considers two possibilities: either power relates to the voluntary movement like knowledge relates to its object (without any effect) or it relates with an effect. The first is rejected because if power had no effect, the distinction we perceive would vanish — both movements would be similar in that regard.
This rejection forces us to accept that power must have an effect.
4
Follow the second dichotomy and conclusion
Since power has an effect, the effect must be either on the existence/coming-into-being of the movement or on an attribute of it. The first option is invalid because if power could affect existence, it would affect every existent (contradicting the opponent's view). Therefore, the effect must be on an attribute, which al-Baqillani calls a 'state' (ḥāl) additional to existence.
This is the key conclusion: power's scope is limited to affecting attributes, not existence itself.
5
Reflect on the significance
Consider why this argument matters: it clarifies the Ashʿarī position that created power does not create or annihilate things (that is reserved for God) but rather modifies their attributes within an already created substrate.
This argument also responds to opponents who claim that power affects existence directly.

Commentary
This passage contains al-Qadi Abu Bakr al-Baqillani's proof that created power (al-qudra al-ḥāditha) must have an effect on an attribute distinct from existence. The proof begins with an empirical observation: every person necessarily distinguishes between involuntary movement (e.g., a tremor) and voluntary movement. This distinction is not based on the movements themselves, since as movements they are identical, but on an added quality: one is intended and produced by power (maqdūra murāda), the other is not. From this, al-Baqillani considers two possibilities regarding how power relates to the movement: either it relates like knowledge (without affecting the thing), or it relates with an effect. The first option is rejected because it would eliminate the very distinction we experience—if power has no effect, then both movements would be similar in that regard, but we clearly differentiate them. Since the first option fails, the second must be true: power relates with an effect. The effect, however, cannot be on the existence or temporal origination (ḥudūth) of the movement, because if power could affect existence, it would affect every existent (contradicting the opponent's own view). So the effect must be on an attribute (ṣifa) of the movement, which al-Baqillani calls a 'state' (ḥāl) additional to existence. This establishes that created power produces an effect on a quality, not on the very fact of existence.

Commentary
This passage is a rebuttal against an opponent who distinguishes between two similar movements (one from God, one from human) based on the feeling of their existence. The author concedes that inner sense (wijdān) notices a distinction, but questions why the opponent attributes one to human originated power. The author argues that the distinction actually goes back to either a quality in the locus or a state in the movement—these are pre-conscious attributes. The act of bringing into existence (ījād) is imperceptible; what we perceive are specific attributes like being a movement or a color. The author then references the Mu'tazilite doctrine of 'non-existent as a thing' (ma'dūm shay'), but distances himself: the distinction should not be traced to existence (which is from God's eternal power), but to what is rewarded or punished. Because reward/punishment applies to human acts, which are the specific modalities (hay'āt) beyond existence—like standing, sitting, etc. Man has a motive for these modalities, not for existence itself. So these 'faces' (wujūh) and 'considerations' (iʿtibārāt) are the basis of distinction, and they are intelligible and attached to the act through divine will and knowledge, making the divine command, prohibition, etc., meaningful.

The Affirmers' Rational Proof for States

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer unit concerning the doctrine of states (al-ahwal) in kalam. The opponents first ask what is meant by saying that distinction and commonality are a rational judgment. They argue that if it means the same thing is known from one aspect and unknown from another, then rational aspects are merely mental considerations and do not imply real attributes. If it means a thing actually has an attribute that it shares with others and one that distinguishes it, that is exactly the disputed point: a particular entity has no sharing, and a universal common thing has no existence. They then argue that denying the state is better than affirming it, because affirming a state that is neither existent nor non-existent but only subsistent destroys the possibility of definition and inference, since a sound inference divides between existence and non-existence, but the affirmer introduces a middle term. The opponents further explain that definition and reality are the same for the deniers of states: a thing's definition is its reality, which is its specific essence, and each thing has a unique property that is inseparable from it and not shared. They also note that generality and particularity in inference pertain to expressions, not to essences; existence and the most specific attribute are one. The proponents (al-muthbitun) respond by asserting that they do not posit an intermediary between existence and non-existence; rather, the state is subsistent in their view, but not as an existent entity. It is an intelligible attribute of substances and accidents. They provide examples: a substance is known by its substantiality but not by its location or its being receptive of accidents; an accident is known by its accidentally but not by its being a specific color. Distinctions in knowledge of the same thing must refer back to a state. They give the example of motion: one knows necessarily that a moving thing is moving, and later by speculation that it is moving with a motion; if the two meanings were identical, they would not differ in mode of knowledge. Thus, denying states contradicts necessary knowledge.

Proof that the Effect of a Cause is a State (Hal)
Al-Shahrastani proves the necessity of states via a rational division.

وقال المثبتون : إلزام الحال علينا نقضا غير متوجه، فإن العموم والخصوص في الحال كالجنسية والنوعية في الأجناس والأنواع، فإن الجنسية في الأجناس ليس جنساً حتى يستدعي كل جنس جنساً ويودي إلى التسلسل، وكذلك النوعية في الأنواع ليست نوعًا حتى تستدعي كل نوع نوعًا، فكذلك الحالية للأحوال لا حالا فيؤدي إلى التسلسل، وليس يلزم على من يقول: الوجود عام أن يقول للعام عام، وكذلك لو قال : العرضية جنس، فلا يلزمه أن يقول للجنس جنس، وكذلك لو فرق فارق بين حقيقة الجنس والنوع وفصل أحدهما عن الثاني بأخص وصف لم يلزمه أن يثبت اعتبارًا عقليًا في الجنس هو كالجنس، ووجها عقليا هو كالنوع فلا يلزم الحال علينا بوجه لا من حيث العموم والخصوص، ولا من حيث الاعتبار والوجه. وأنتم معاشر النفاة لا يمكنكم أن تتكلموا بكلمة واحدة إلا وإن تدرجوا فيها عموماً وخصوصاً، ألستم تنفون الحال ولم تعينوا حالا بعينها هي صفة مخصوصة مشار إليها ؟ بل أطلقتم القول وعممتم النفي وأقمتم الدليل على جنس شمل أنواعًا أو على نوع عم أشخاصاً حتى استمر دليلكم، ومن نفى الحال لا يمكنه إثبات التماثل في المتماثلات، وإجراء حكم واحد فيها جميعا، فلو كانت الأشياء تتمايز بذواتها المعينة بطل التماثل وبطل الاختلاف، وذلك خروج عن قضايا العقول.
And the affirmers said: The imposition of [the doctrine of] state (al-hal) upon us is an untenable objection. For universality and particularity in the state are like genus-ness and species-ness in genera and species. For genus-ness in genera is not a genus such that every genus would require a genus and lead to infinite regress; likewise species-ness in species is not a species such that every species would require a species. Thus state-ness (al-haliyya) for states (al-ahwal) is not a state such that it would lead to infinite regress. And it is not incumbent upon one who says 'existence is universal' to say that the universal has a universal; likewise if he said 'accidentality is a genus', it is not incumbent upon him to say that the genus has a genus. And likewise if someone distinguished between the reality of genus and species and separated one from the other by the most specific description, it would not be incumbent upon him to posit an intellectual consideration (i'tibar 'aqli) in the genus that is like the genus, and an intellectual aspect (wajh 'aqli) that is like the species. So the state is not incumbent upon us in any way, neither from the perspective of universality and particularity, nor from the perspective of consideration and aspect. And you, O deniers (al-nufat), cannot utter a single word without involving universality and particularity in it. Do you not deny the state without specifying a particular state that is a specific indicated attribute? Rather, you spoke absolutely and universalized the negation and established the proof upon a genus that includes species, or upon a species that encompasses individuals, so that your proof continued. And one who denies the state cannot establish similarity (al-tamathul) among similar things, nor apply a single judgment to all of them. For if things were distinguished by their particular essences (dhawatiha al-mu'ayyana), similarity would be nullified and difference would be nullified, and that is a departure from the dictates of intellects.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of the doctrine of states (ahwal) by the affirmers (al-muthbitun) against the objection that positing states leads to infinite regress. The author begins by stating that the objection is not valid. Then he draws an analogy: the relationship of universality and particularity in states is like the relationship of genus-ness and species-ness in genera and species. Just as genus-ness is not itself a genus, so it does not require another genus ad infinitum; likewise, state-ness is not itself a state, so it does not require another state. The author then extends the analogy: if someone says 'existence is universal,' he is not obliged to say that the universal has a universal; similarly, if someone says 'accidentality is a genus,' he is not obliged to say that genus has a genus. The same applies if someone distinguishes genus from species by an intrinsic description—he need not posit an intellectual consideration or aspect that is like the genus or species itself. So the objection fails on all counts. Next, the author turns to the deniers (al-nufat) and points out a contradiction: they themselves cannot utter a single word without employing universality and particularity. They deny the state without specifying a particular state—they universalize their negation and base their proof on a genus that includes species or a species that encompasses individuals. If states did not exist, one could not establish similarity among similar things or apply a single judgment to all of them. If things were distinguished only by their particular essences, similarity and difference would both be impossible, which contradicts the clear verdict of reason.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Positing states (ahwal) leads to infinite regress because for every state there is another state of that state.
Correction: State-ness (al-haliyya) is not itself a state, so no regress occurs, just as genus-ness is not a genus.

A) Because states are not real entities.

Key Takeaway
The doctrine of states is not vulnerable to infinite regress because the property of being a state is not itself a state, analogous to genus-ness not being a genus.
