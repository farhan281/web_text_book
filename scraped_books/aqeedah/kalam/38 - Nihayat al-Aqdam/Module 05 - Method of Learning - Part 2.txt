Answering the Objections: Ilzam and Tahqiq
Twofold answer to the objections: forcing arguments and verification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two-part answer to the objections against the indivisible atom: the method of forcing arguments (ilzamat) and the method of verification (tahqiq).

Core Idea
The objections are refuted by turning them against the opponent and by providing a rational proof for the atom.

After presenting the objections, the author now gives a twofold answer. The first part, ilzamat wa mu'aradat, shows that the objections are built on assuming the atom's existence, which the opponent does not accept, so they are not valid. The second part, tahqiq, provides a rational proof that a finite body cannot be infinitely divided, establishing the indivisible atom. Let's dive into the answer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two parts of the answer
The text explicitly states that the answer has two aspects: forcing arguments (al-ilzamat wa al-mu'aradat) and verification (al-tahqiq). Read each part separately.
The first part is brief; the second part is longer and contains a proof.
2
Understand the forcing argument
The author argues that the objections are built on the premise of the mutakallim's doctrine, but the opponent does not accept atoms, so the objection is not valid. The opponent's own concepts (hyle and form) are accepted despite being insensible.
This is a dialectical move: turning the argument back on the opponent.
3
Examine the verification
The verification gives a rational proof: a finite body cannot be infinitely divisible, so an indivisible remainder exists. The author then draws an analogy to the philosophers' hyle, which is receptive of form but itself indivisible.
The proof is a reductio ad absurdum if infinite division were possible.
4
Compare the mutakallim and philosopher
The passage ends by contrasting how the mutakallim (atoms combine by conjunction of likes) and the philosopher (atoms combine by conjunction of form) explain the atom's acceptance of multiple connections.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer unit. The author first presents geometric objections against the existence of an indivisible atom (al-jawhar al-fard). The objections involve cases where atoms align on a circle, leading to infinite divisibility. The author then gives a two-part answer. The first part is 'forcing arguments and counterexamples' (al-ilzamat wa al-mu'aradat): the opponent's objection is based on assuming the possibility of an indivisible atom within the opponent's own framework, but the opponent does not accept atoms. Thus the objection is not valid against the proponent's view. The second part is 'verification' (al-tahqiq): the author provides a rational proof for the indivisible atom, arguing that a finite body cannot be infinitely divisible. The proof uses the concepts of hyle (hayula) and form (sura) from the philosophers' own framework to show that the indivisible atom is similar to hyle in not accepting division itself, but only through conjunction with other atoms. The passage ends by distinguishing between the mutakallim's and the philosopher's explanations of how atoms combine.
