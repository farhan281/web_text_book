The World is Created: The Consensus of Ahl al-Haqq
Shahristani states the first principle and lists supporting authorities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the first principle of kalam is the temporal origination of the world, and that this view is affirmed by all religious communities and many ancient philosophers.

Core Idea
All revealed religions agree: the world was created by God from nothing.

Shahristani begins his book by laying down the foundational doctrine that the world is temporally originated. He asserts that this is the unanimous position of the people of truth from all religious communities, and he cites ancient philosophers who also held this view. This lesson establishes the shared ground before contrasting it with opposing views.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim
Read the passage and find the statement that defines the first principle. What is the doctrine of Ahl al-Haqq?
The claim is that the world is temporally originated and created.
2
Note the supporting authorities
Shahristani lists ancient philosophers who agreed. Notice the names and their schools. Why does he include them?
This shows rational support beyond revelation.
3
Understand the consensus
Reflect on the phrase 'from all religious communities'. This establishes a universal consensus that strengthens the argument.

The End of the Stride in the Science of Kalam
First Principle
On the temporal origination of the world and the demonstration of the impossibility of events having no beginning and the impossibility of the existence of bodies infinite in place.
The doctrine of the people of truth from all religious communities is that the world is temporally originated and created; the Creator, exalted, originated it and brought it into existence, and God existed and nothing was with Him. And a group of the masters of wisdom and ancient philosophers agreed with them on that, such as Thales, Anaxagoras, Anaximenes, and their followers from Miletus; and like Pythagoras, Empedocles, Socrates, and Plato from Athens and Greece; and a group of poets and ascetics. They have a detailed doctrine on the manner of origination and a difference of opinion on the first principles, which we have explained in our book titled 'Al-Milal wa al-Nihal'. And the doctrine of Aristotle and his followers, such as Proclus, Alexander of Aphrodisias, and Themistius, and those who supported his doctrine among the later ones, such as Abu Nasr al-Farabi, Abu Ali al-Husayn ibn Abd Allah ibn Sina, and others of the philosophers of Islam, is that the world has a Maker and Originator who is necessary in Himself, or the world is possible of existence in itself, necessary of existence through the Necessary in Himself, not temporally originated with an origination preceded by non-existence; rather, the meaning of its origination is its being necessitated by Him, its emanation from Him, and its need for Him. So it is eternal in existence, never ceasing and never ending. For the Creator, exalted, necessitated by His essence an intellect, which is an immaterial substance, self-subsisting, abstracted from matter. And by means of that He necessitated another intellect, a soul, and a celestial body. And through the two of them the elements and composites came to be. And it is not permissible that there proceed...

Commentary
This passage is the opening of the first principle of the book, where Shahristani lays out the foundational doctrine that the world is temporally originated (hadith) and created (makhluq). He begins by stating the consensus of the people of truth (Ahl al-Haqq) from all religious communities that the world is originated by the Creator, who existed alone before creation. He then lists ancient philosophers who agreed with this view, including Thales, Anaxagoras, Anaximenes from the Milesian school, and Pythagoras, Empedocles, Socrates, Plato from the Greek tradition, as well as poets and ascetics. Shahristani notes that these figures had differing opinions on the details of origination and the first principles, which he has explained in his other work 'Al-Milal wa al-Nihal'. He then contrasts this with the doctrine of Aristotle and his followers—Proclus, Alexander of Aphrodisias, Themistius, and later Islamic philosophers like al-Farabi and Ibn Sina. According to them, the world has a Maker who is necessary in Himself (wajib bi dhatihi). The world itself is possible in essence (mumkin al-wujud bi dhatihi) but necessary through the Necessary Being (wajib al-wujud bi al-wajib bi dhatihi). They deny temporal origination preceded by non-existence; rather, 'origination' for them means the world's being necessitated by God, emanating from Him, and being dependent on Him. Thus the world is eternal, without beginning or end. Shahristani explains their emanationist cosmology: from the Creator proceeds an intellect (a self-subsisting immaterial substance), then from that intellect another intellect, a soul, and a celestial body, and through these the elements and compounds come into existence. The passage ends abruptly with 'and it is not permissible that there proceed...', indicating a continuation. The author is presenting opposing views to set up his own proofs for the temporal origination of the world.

The Emanationist Alternative: Aristotle and His Followers
Shahristani presents the Aristotelian doctrine of eternal emanation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Aristotelian doctrine that the world is eternal but dependent on God, and the emanationist cosmology that explains its origination.

Core Idea
For Aristotle's followers, the world is eternal, not created from nothing, but emanates from God through intellects.

Having presented the consensus of Ahl al-Haqq, Shahristani now introduces the opposing view of Aristotle and his followers. This view denies temporal creation and instead posits an eternal world that is necessary through God. The passage outlines the emanationist chain from God through intellects to the material world. This contrast sets up the arguments that will follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key claim
Read the passage and find the statement that distinguishes this view from Ahl al-Haqq. What do they say about the world's origination?
They deny temporal origination preceded by non-existence.
2
Understand the redefinition of 'origination'
For Aristotle's followers, what does 'origination' (حدوث) mean? Look for the phrase 'بل معنى حدوثه...'
It means necessitation, emanation, and dependency.
3
Trace the emanation chain
Follow the sequence: from God to intellect, then another intellect, soul, celestial body, finally elements and composites.
This is a hierarchical emanation, not creation ex nihilo.

The Condition and Definition of Kasb and Khalq
Defines a condition for act attribution and the nature of kasb vs khalq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the condition for an act being attributed to a created agent and the distinction between kasb (acquisition) and khalq (creation) according to al-Ustadh.

Core Idea
Kasb is when the act occurs by your power but you cannot do it alone; Khalq is when you can do it alone.

The early Ash'ari scholars needed to explain how human actions can be attributed to humans without compromising divine omnipotence. This lesson introduces the foundational distinction between kasb (acquisition) and khalq (creation) as articulated by al-Ustadh, and the condition that makes an act a kasb.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the condition and the two definitions.
The passage is short but dense.
2
Identify the condition
What must the agent have knowledge of? The condition says the agent must be aware of 'some aspects' of the act, not all.
This condition makes kasb possible without full control.
3
Distinguish kasb from khalq
Kasb: act occurs by agent's power but cannot be done alone. Khalq: act occurs by agent's power and can be done alone.
The key difference is the ability to act independently.
4
Reflect on the significance
Ask yourself: Why is this distinction important for the Ash'ari view of divine omnipotence?
It allows human responsibility without attributing creation to humans.

A) Verbal confession alone
B) Action on the limbs
C) A statement in the soul that comprises knowledge
D) Ignorance

Key Takeaway
The Ash'ari school defines iman as inner assent (tasdiq), which is fundamentally a heartfelt conviction expressed through words and actions.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
You read that inner tasdiq is the real essence of iman, and that your words and actions are only evidences of it. Honestly examine your own heart: do your outward practices today—your prayers, your treatment of others, your speech—accurately indicate the depth of your inner conviction? If there is a mismatch, what is one concrete step you can take tomorrow to align them?
?
The passage mentions that some said iman is 'knowledge that Allah and His Messenger are truthful'. Do you truly live as though you are certain that every word of Allah and His Messenger is truth? In what area of your life do you act as if you doubt that—where do your choices reveal a lack of that knowledge?
?
Al-Ash'ari taught that the minimal requirement of faith is the testimony of tawhid and prophethood with no denial of anything revealed. Yet you know more than that now. What revealed truth have you been ignoring or downplaying that you need to fully embrace today, not just with your lips but with your heart and actions?
