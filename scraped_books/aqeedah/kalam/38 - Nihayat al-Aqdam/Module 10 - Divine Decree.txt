Objection and Answer on the Proof of Knowledge for Creation
Objection and answer concerning the proof from necessary knowledge for creatorship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection to the proof from comprehensive knowledge is answered by showing that the proof targets creatorhood, not agency, and that the logical direction is one-way.

Core Idea
The proof denies human creatorhood, not human action; absence of knowledge disproves creatorhood, but its presence does not prove it.

In this passage, an objector challenges the earlier proof that humans cannot be creators of their actions due to lacking comprehensive knowledge. The objector argues that such knowledge is not impossible, and if it existed, the proof would absurdly allow human creation. The answer clarifies the proof's true target—negating the servant as creator—and explains that the absence of knowledge suffices to disprove creatorhood, while the presence of knowledge does not establish it, because the proof is not reversible. This refines our understanding of the Ash'ari position on human action and divine creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
The objector raises two points: (1) comprehensive knowledge is not impossible, so the proof's assumed impossibility is invalid; (2) only knowledge of existence is needed for agency, which humans have.
Notice the objector is trying to reverse the direction of the proof.
2
Read the answer and note its clarification
The answer states the proof's purpose is to deny creatorhood, not all action. It then explains the logical asymmetry: lack of knowledge disproves creatorhood, but having knowledge does not prove it.
The key term here is 'عكس الأدلة' (converse of indications).
3
Understand the asymmetry
The proof is one-way: absence of knowledge → no creatorhood. The objector mistakenly assumed it would also work in reverse (presence of knowledge → creatorhood). The answer rejects that.
4
Reflect on the theological implication
This preserves the Ash'ari doctrine: humans act but are not creators; God is the sole creator of all actions, including human acts.
The common misunderstanding is that Ash'aris deny human action; they only deny creatorship.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer pair. The objector argues that the earlier proof (which claimed that a human cannot be the creator of his actions because he lacks comprehensive knowledge of the action) is invalid. The objector says that comprehensive knowledge of an action is not impossible in itself, even if it does not happen in the specific example given; it is only factually absent, not necessarily impossible. If such knowledge were possible, then according to the proof's logic, a human could create the action in every respect, but the proof's proponents (the Ash'aris) do not accept that, showing the proof is flawed. The objector further claims that the only knowledge required for being an agent is knowledge of the existence of the action, which the human has. The answer clarifies that the proof was never intended to show the impossibility of any act by human power; rather it aims to negate that the human is the creator (khalīq) of his own acts. The reasoning is: if the human were the creator, his act would show perfect wise design (iḥkām) that indicates comprehensive knowledge; but human acts lack such perfect design, so he is not a creator. The answer adds that the direction of the proof is one-way: the absence of knowledge disproves creatorhood, but the presence of knowledge would not prove it; proofs are not reversible. Thus the objection's claim of reversibility is rebutted.

Alternative Proof: Human Knowledge Limits and the Impossibility of Creation
Alternative proof that human origination is impossible using two arguments from knowledge limits.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the alternative proof for the impossibility of human origination of actions, based on two limitations of human knowledge, and its implications for the Ash'ari doctrine of acquisition (kasb).

Core Idea
True creation requires perfect knowledge, which humans cannot possess.

This passage refines the proof that humans cannot be the true originators of their actions by focusing on the limits of human knowledge. The author argues that if humans were creators, they would need instantaneous comprehensive knowledge of the act, which is impossible for two specific reasons. Understanding this argument deepens the Ash'ari position that humans only acquire actions, not create them ex nihilo.

Commentary
This passage presents an alternative proof for the impossibility of human creation of actions, using two arguments from the limitations of human knowledge. The author states that if a human were the originator (mubdiʿ) of an act, he would need to know the act in all its states at the moment of origination, which is impossible. The first reason is that originated (created) knowledge cannot simultaneously know two distinct objects; if it tried, ignorance could overtake the knower, resulting in a contradictory state of being both knowing and ignorant of the same thing. The second reason is that the aspects of an act are partly known necessarily (by immediate intuition) and partly by inference (through reasoning). To know them all at once, the human would need to perform an inference, which is a second cognitive act; this inference itself would require knowledge of its own aspects, leading to an infinite regress (tasalsul). Thus the act would never be actualized. The passage then notes that many rational philosophers recognized this and concluded that true creation (ījād) requires comprehensive knowledge; they held that only the Creator's knowledge of Himself is the source of the first act's existence. They distinguished between active knowledge (which brings about existence) and passive knowledge (which receives existence). The passage ends abruptly by stating that humans need power, will, motives, tools, and instruments precisely because their knowledge cannot be conceived to be complete and active in this way.

The Ash'ari Doctrine of Universal Compulsion (Jabr)
Al-Shahrastani states the Ash'ari position of universal jabr and the nature of intermediaries.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari position that all actions, including human choice and power, are ultimately coerced by God, and that secondary causes are mere preparations, not effective creators.

Core Idea
Everything, even our choices, is compelled by God; intermediaries prepare, only God creates.

Al-Shahrastani opens his refutation by restating the core of Ash'ari determinism: that all forms, actions, and even the capacity for choice are imposed by God. This passage establishes the metaphysical framework in which human agency is understood as acquisition (kasb) rather than origination. The analogy to natural processes clarifies that intermediaries are not true causes.

Guided Path

The author uses a very assertive tone to establish the doctrine.
2

In each case, the apparent cause is only a condition, not the creator.
3
Understand the role of intermediaries
The text states that intermediaries are 'preparers, not creators'. This is a key Ash'ari principle.
Think about what this means for human actions: we prepare, but God creates.
4

This is a metaphysical argument based on the nature of contingency.

Commentary
This passage presents a refutation of the Mu'tazilite rational argument for human autonomy within the context of Ash'arite determinism. The author first reiterates the Ash'arite position that all things, including choice and power, are coerced by God, likening it to natural processes where intermediaries only prepare but do not create. He then presents the Mu'tazilite argument: man feels that his actions follow his motives and intentions, and this self-awareness is a necessary truth. The Mu'tazilites further argue that the distinction between voluntary and involuntary movement must be either in the movement itself (by attribution to one's own power vs. another's) or in the agent's capacity. They also critique the Ash'arite notion of kasb (acquisition) as either admitting effect in existence or being meaningless. Finally, they assert that a power without effect is like denying power, and man feels a distinction between what is within his power and what is not. The refutation begins by denying that actions occur according to motives, stating that this is exactly the point in dispute, not a self-evident truth. The author then provides counterexamples: actions of sleepers, forgetful, and heedless occur without corresponding motives, yet the Mu'tazilites still attribute these actions to the servant. Conversely, many natural effects (e.g., colors from dyeing, heat from contact) occur according to motives but are not attributed to human origination. The object of obligation cannot be existence alone nor a moral aspect beyond human power.

Shahrastani continues his critique of the Mu'tazila by examining their attempts to specify what exactly an obligation requires of a person. He presents two possible definitions — the existence of the act itself, or a particular 'aspect' that makes it praiseworthy — and shows that both undermine their own case. Commentary
This passage is a dialectical refutation of a Mu'tazilite argument. Shahrastani begins by posing an opponent's question: 'Specify what exactly is required by obligation?' The opponent argues that a blanket statement about obligation does not define the scope of human power. The author then examines two possible answers: (1) that the required thing is existence per se, and (2) that it is an 'aspect' (jiha) of the act that makes it praiseworthy or blameworthy. If existence per se is the object, then since existence as such does not vary in moral quality, it cannot be the basis of differentiated commands (some obligatory, some prohibited). If the aspect is the object, then that aspect does not fall under human power—if it did, it would not be a valid object of obligation (because the command would be to something already in one's power, which is pointless). The opponent then tries to split the act: the power is over the existence of the act, but the aspect necessarily follows without being intended. Shahrastani retorts that this fails: if obligation implied an effect of power on existence, the object would be existence alone, not the aspect. But in fact, the command brings about a movement that then becomes good, worship, etc. The intended object (the moral quality) is not produced by the servant's power. Thus the opponent's position reduces to a contradiction: 'Do, O you who do not act!' The author concludes by pointing out that whether the obligated thing falls under the servant's power or not, the Mu'tazilite position leads to an absurdity: if it does not fall under power at all, it is pure jabr; if it falls under power in one respect and not another, it is inconsistent.

Commentary
This passage contains two further arguments against the Mu'tazila (Qadariyya). Learning Objective

Core Idea
God wills the temporal existence of all acts, but not their moral quality as obedience or disobedience.

Shahrastani addresses a powerful objection: if God commands something, does He not will it? How can He command what He knows will not happen? The answer lies in a subtle distinction between the aspects of an act. This passage reveals the key to understanding divine will and human responsibility.

The Distinction Between Divine Will and Command
Shahrastani distinguishes divine will from command and refutes the polytheistic argument using homonymy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why divine will and command are not synonymous, how homonymy can lead to confusion, and how the polytheists exploited this to argue against divine justice.

Core Idea
God's will relates to existence, His command to moral obligation—they are not the same.

Shahrastani now draws a crucial distinction between divine will (irada) and command (amr). Will properly applies to God's creative act of bringing temporal things into existence, while command properly applies to the moral obligations placed on humans. He notes that the terms are sometimes used interchangeably by homonymy, which the polytheists of the Qur'an exploited to argue that if God had willed, they would not have committed shirk. This passage clarifies the kalāmi position on this contentious issue.

Understand homonymy
Shahrastani explains that sometimes these words are used interchangeably (ishtirak fi al-lafz). This can cause misunderstanding if taken literally.
Homonymy means the same word has different meanings in different contexts.

Analyze the polytheistic argument
The polytheists in Q. 6:148 argued that if God had willed, they would not have committed shirk. Shahrastani implies they confused will with command. God's will for their shirk (its existence) does not mean He commanded or approved it.

Accidental Evil and Divine Decree: The Fitra and External Causes
Sanusi explains accidental evil from external causes and its relation to divine decree.

Evil enters God’s decree accidentally; the fitra is originally good.

This passage tackles a difficult question: if God decrees everything, how can evil exist? Sanusi distinguishes between internal and external causes of evil, focusing here on external accidental causes. Through the example of children learning bad habits and beliefs, and the prophetic hadith on fitra, he shows that evil enters divine decree only accidentally, preserving God’s essential goodness and justice.

Commentary
This passage presents a doctrinal argument about tawakkul (reliance on Allah) and the roles of divine and human agency. It begins by contrasting two erroneous positions: that of the Qadariyya (who believe humans are independent agents) and that of the Jabriyya (who believe humans have no real agency). In the Qadari view, the servant is always in khudhlān (divine abandonment) because he relies solely on his own power without seeking Allah's help. In the Jabri view, the servant is also always in khudhlān because he effectively abandons himself by not actively obeying Allah's commands. The author then states the correct position: the formula 'Iyyāka na‘budu wa iyyāka nasta‘īn' perfectly balances servitude and reliance. All spiritual states—tawfīq, khudhlān, sharḥ (expansion), ṭab‘ (sealing), fatḥ (opening), khatm (sealing), hidāya (guidance), and ḍalāl (misguidance)—are attributed to Allah, but with the crucial condition that the worthier of two names (e.g., 'the Merciful' over 'the Avenger') is used when invoking Him, as per Qur'an 7:180. This affirms that attribution does not imply injustice; rather, Allah's actions are the most fitting. The passage concludes with a supplication acknowledging that good comes from Allah's grace and evil from human sin, and that no one can act independently of Allah's power.

Gratitude, Predestination, and the Two Decrees
Defines shukr, states predetermination of ajal and rizq, and introduces two decrees.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how gratitude is defined, the mutual predetermination of provision and lifespan, and how the two decrees in the Preserved Tablet reconcile predestination with the effect of actions like maintaining family ties.

Core Idea
Predestination includes both absolute and conditional decrees, explaining how actions can 'change' destiny.

This passage from al-Ash'ari's Nihayat al-Aqdam tackles a puzzle: if everything is predestined, how can hadiths say that good deeds increase lifespan? The answer lies in distinguishing two types of decrees written on the Preserved Tablet. The passage also opens with a rich definition of gratitude (shukr), grounding practical theology in a heart-tongue-action structure.

Commentary
This passage covers three interconnected topics: the definition of gratitude (shukr), the predetermination of provision (rizq) and lifespan (ajal), and the reconciliation of predestination with the effect of human actions through the concept of two decrees in the Preserved Tablet. First, shukr is defined as a three-part act: perceiving blessings as a favor from Allah with the heart, praising Him verbally, and not using them in what is obligatory (perhaps meaning not misusing them—the text says 'wājib' which is ambiguous; it may be a scribal error or meant as 'in what is not permissible' but we translate literally). Next, the author asserts that provisions are predetermined in relation to lifespans and lifespans are predetermined in relation to provisions—they are mutually fixed. Every event has an end, and these ends are not limited to living beings. Nothing can increase or decrease what Allah has decreed in absolute terms. However, the author then introduces a reported view that there are two types of decrees in the Preserved Tablet: an absolute decree (ḥukm muṭlaq) that is fixed and unchanging, and a conditional decree (ḥukm muqayyad) that is subject to conditions such as performing certain acts (e.g., maintaining family ties). This conditional decree explains hadiths that speak of good deeds increasing lifespan, without contradicting the absolute decree. The author supports this with Qur'anic verses (35:11 and 13:39) that speak of erasing and confirming decrees, and the 'Mother of the Book' (Umm al-Kitāb) which is the source of the absolute decree.
