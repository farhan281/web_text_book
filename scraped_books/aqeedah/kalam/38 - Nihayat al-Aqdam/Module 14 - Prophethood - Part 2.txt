Proof of Prophethood: Necessary Knowledge from Miracles
Distinguishing whether the knowledge from miracles is immediate or theoretical, with both paths leading to certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the occurrence of a miracle at the hands of a claimant to prophethood yields necessary knowledge of his truthfulness, whether through circumstantial evidence or the argument from specification.

Core Idea
Miracles provide immediate necessary knowledge, not mere theoretical reasoning.

The previous question challenged whether the proof of prophethood is certain. Imam Shahrastani now answers by showing that the knowledge derived from miracles is either immediate or theoretical, but in either case it is necessary and certain. He distinguishes two approaches: the first based on circumstantial evidence, the second on the logic of specification. Both lead to the same conclusion: after witnessing a miracle, denial is mere obstinacy.

The conjunction of miracle, claim, and absence of counter-miracle produces certainty.

Commentary

The author then adds that 'the good deeds of the righteous are the evil deeds of those brought near', indicating the high standard for prophets. Commentary
This passage argues for the necessity of a prophet and the requirement that his claim of prophethood be accompanied by an immediate proof of truthfulness (miracle). The author begins by clarifying that the 'descent' of God's speech is not a physical motion, but rather the descent of the angel bearing it; similarly, reports of God's descent to the lower heaven or His coming in the Qur'an are interpreted metaphorically without spatial movement. Next, the author establishes that obligating the opponent depends on first affirming that God gives commands and prohibitions, which in turn rests on the recognition that God is King and Owner, having the right to dispose of His property by commanding, prohibiting, and obligating those who have free choice. Once these premises are accepted, since ordinary people cannot receive divine revelation directly, it follows that God must choose a specific person as a prophet, as the verse states: 'Your Lord creates what He wills and chooses; they have no choice.' Rather, the mere capacity to know (al-tamakkun) is sufficient, and that capacity is actualized when the intellect exists and the proof is present. Therefore, just as the obligation to believe is immediate, the proof must also be immediate—hence the required miracle must accompany the prophetic claim.

If it was all pre-existing, then Moses brought no new law, which contradicts his status as a law-giving prophet. Thus, there must be at least one new ruling, which abrogates the previous one. Then the author lists specific examples: (1) Marriage of sisters in the time of Adam (to enable reproduction) continued until Noah; this ruling was later abrogated. (2) The repentance of the calf-worshippers by killing themselves was a ruling specific to that incident and later lifted. (3) Circumcision on the seventh day was not required for Noah but was commanded for Abraham, and later Moses forbade delaying beyond seven days. (4) The Sabbath was not obligatory before Moses, then became obligatory for him, and could later return to being permissible. (5) Hunting and worldly activities on the Sabbath were permissible before Moses, then prohibited for him. (6) The appointment at the mountain: initially thirty nights, then completed to forty. (7) The severe obligations on the Children of Israel were not required of earlier communities. All these examples show that divine laws change over time, which is the essence of abrogation. The author concludes that abrogation is not only rationally possible but historically attested.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think that once a law is given, it cannot be changed or abrogated.
Correction: The example of the mountain appointment shows that even a divinely set time can be extended, demonstrating that God can change His rulings.

Checkpoint

B) The prohibition of adultery

Key Takeaway
Changes in Sabbath, hunting, and the mountain appointment confirm that abrogation occurred within Moses' prophethood.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
If Allah can change His commands even for a single prophet, are you holding onto any religious practice or interpretation as if it were eternal, while forgetting that Allah's will supersedes all? How will you cultivate readiness to submit to whatever He prescribes, even if it challenges your comfort?
?
The duration of Musa's appointment changed from 30 to 40 nights without protest. When your own plans—set by Allah—are extended or altered without explanation, do you accept it as His absolute wisdom, or do you resent the change? What can you learn from Musa's silence?
?
Consider a command from Allah that you find difficult to follow today. If He were to abrogate it tomorrow, would your obedience have been worthless? Or does each command carry its own reward? How does this lesson reshape your motivation to obey in the present moment?
