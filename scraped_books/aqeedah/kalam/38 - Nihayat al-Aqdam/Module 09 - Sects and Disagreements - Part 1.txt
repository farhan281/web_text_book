Erroneous Solutions of the Karamiyya and Mu'tazila
The author identifies the source of error and describes two heretical views on divine will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Karamiyya and Mu'tazila erroneously attempted to explain the specification of the world through divine will, and the key differences between their positions.

Core Idea
Both sects introduce temporality into God's relation to the world, but in opposing ways.

The author begins by pointing to a pivotal source of theological error. He then contrasts two misguided solutions: the Karamiyya placed temporal events in God's essence, while the Mu'tazila posited temporal wills with no spatial location. Understanding these views clarifies why they are rejected in orthodox Sunni theology.

Guided Path
1

The Karamiyya are described first, then the Mu'tazila.
3
Examine the distinctions made by the Karamiyya
The Karamiyya differentiate between eternal intention and temporal will, and between events and the creator of events. Consider why they made these distinctions.
They try to preserve God's transcendence while explaining particularization.
4
Contrast with the Mu'tazila
The Mu'tazila affirm temporal wills but not in a locus, and they do not make the distinctions the Karamiyya made. Reflect on the implications.
Both views are rejected for introducing temporality in different ways.
5
Reflect on the author's assessment
Why does the author call this point a 'source of error'? Consider how both positions fall short of the orthodox understanding of God's will.
The correct view affirms an eternal will without temporal events.

Commentary
This passage classifies two erroneous theological schools concerning the divine will that specifies the world. The Karamiyya held that events (ḥawādith) — specifically temporal volitions and speech — occur in God's essence as a means of particularization. They distinguished the eternal intention (general) from temporal will (specific), and also made a terminological distinction between 'events' (aḥdāth) and the 'maker of events' (muḥdith), and between 'creation' (khalq) and 'the created' (makhlūq). The Mu'tazila, in contrast, affirmed temporal wills but not located in any subject (lā fī maḥall), and they did not make these distinctions. The author implies that both positions are mistaken because they introduce temporality into God or bypass the problem of specification without a sound basis.

Therefore, if mixing occurred, it would be impossible without a coercer, but they claim it happened, which is a logical contradiction.

Al-Shahrastani presents two objections from the Muʿaṭṭila and the Ashʿari responses.

The Muʿaṭṭila challenge the entire project of kalām by questioning how Ashʿarīs can compare God to human actions. Mu'tazila and Philosophers Object to Distinct Attributes
Mu'tazila argue attributes are not distinct entities; philosophers add independence argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key objections raised by the Mu'tazila and philosophers against the doctrine of eternal attributes distinct from God's essence, including the logical arguments from multiplicity and absolute independence.

Core Idea
If attributes are eternal and distinct, they become other gods or create dependence.

In this lesson, we examine a combined objection from the Mu'tazila and a group of philosophers against the Ash'ari doctrine of eternal, distinct attributes subsisting in God's essence. The Mu'tazila argue that such attributes would either be identical to the essence (contradicting distinctness) or other than it—leading either to temporality (which is rejected) or to a multiplicity of eternal beings, implying other gods. The philosophers then reinforce this by invoking God's absolute independence, showing that distinct attributes would introduce need and dependence on both sides. This passage is central to understanding the rationalist critique of the classical Sunni position on divine attributes.

Commentary
This passage presents the objections of the Mu'tazila and a group of philosophers against the doctrine of distinct eternal attributes of God. Therefore, the absolutely independent must be one in every respect, with no multiplicity. The passage thus combines two arguments against distinct attributes: one from the Mu'tazila focusing on multiplicity and dependence, and another from philosophers focusing on absolute self-sufficiency.

Core Idea
Attributes are neither identical nor other: they are conceptually distinct but inseparable from the essence.

The Sifatiyya respond to the Mu'tazila's insistence that attributes must be either identical to the essence or other. Commentary
This passage presents the beginning of a Mu'tazilite objection against the Ash'ari doctrine of eternal speech. The Mu'tazila present a dilemma: either the Ash'ari holds that God's eternal speech includes command (أمر), prohibition (نهي), information (خبر), and interrogation (استخبار) from eternity, or he does not. If he does, then they claim he falls into absurdities. The first absurdity is that a command requires a commanded being (مأمور) to exist at the time of the command. Since in eternity no created beings exist, there is no addressee. A command is supposed to urge one to do something and deter from something else, but a non-existent being (المعدوم) cannot be addressed or commanded. This is a classic Mu'tazilite argument: an eternal command to a nonexistent being is impossible. The objection is incomplete in the text; only the first of several 'aspects' (وجوه) is given. The author's response is not included in this page window, so the lesson stands as a presentation of the objection only.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some may think the objection is about God's knowledge of future actions rather than the logical requirement of a recipient for a command.
Correction: The Mu'tazila specifically argue that a command is a relational act requiring a recipient, and since no recipient existed in eternity, an eternal command is impossible.

Checkpoint
What is the Mu'tazilite objection against eternal speech being command and prohibition in eternity?
A) There is no such thing as eternal speech.
B) A command requires a commanded being in existence, which did not exist in eternity.
C) God cannot speak until He creates speech.
D) The Ash'ari claim that speech is eternal is self-contradictory.

Key Takeaway
The Mu'tazila argue that an eternal command is impossible because a command requires a commanded being, and there were no created beings in eternity to receive it.

What specific command have you been neglecting because you felt distant from God? Commit to one concrete act of obedience this week to become a true 'addressee' of God's command.
?

How does this intellectual challenge affect your personal reliance on the Qur'an as a guide? Do you find comfort in the belief that God's commands are eternal, or does it create pressure to live up to them?
