The Two Types of Impossible and the Rationality of Abrogation
Distinguishes two types of impossible and defends rational possibility of abrogation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the distinction between two types of impossibility is used to defend the rational possibility of abrogation in Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Abrogation is rationally possible because it leads neither to ignorance nor regret in God.

This passage addresses a common objection against abrogation: that it would imply a change in God's knowledge or purpose. The author begins by clarifying the categories of impossibility and then shows that abrogation does not fall under any form of rational impossibility, thereby defending its rational validity.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two types of impossible
The author first distinguishes between something impossible in itself (like black and white together) and something impossible because it leads to an impossibility. Recognize that abrogation is not impossible in itself.
The first type is intrinsic contradiction; the second involves consequences.
2
See that abrogation is not impossible in itself
Lifting a ruling after its establishment is similar to the first type? Actually the author says it is of that category, but he means it is not the second type. Carefully read: he says 'there is no doubt that removal of obligation... is of that category'—meaning it is not impossible in itself, so it could be possible if it doesn't lead to impossibility.
The author then checks if abrogation leads to any impossibility.
3
Examine the potential pitfalls: badā and regret
The only possible impossibilities abrogation could lead to are badā (appearance of new knowledge) and regret. The author explains two meanings of badā and why neither applies to God. Also regret is impossible because God does not act for purposes.
Badā as appearance would imply ignorance; badā as regret implies changing one's mind.
4
Conclude that abrogation is rationally possible
Since abrogation does not entail any rational impossibility, it is rationally possible. Its occurrence in revelation confirms this.
The author says its occurrence in the Law is proof of its rational possibility.

يقال لهم : المستحيل على نوعين ؛ مستحيل لعينه كاجتماع السواد والبياض في محل واحد في حالة واحدة، ولا شك أن رفع التكليف وحكم التكليف بعد ثبوته ذلك القبيل، ومستحيل لما يفضي إلى المحال كخلاف المعلوم، ولا محال هاهنا يفضي إليه النسخ إلا البدا ،والندم والبدا يطلق على معنيين؛ أحدهما الظهور، يقال : بدا له الشيء إذا ظهر ) وبدا لهم من الله ما لم يكونوا يحتسبون ) الزمر : ٤٧] ، وهذا لا يجوز على الله تعالى، فإن المعلومات كلها ظاهرة لديه مكشوفة عنده، والنسخ لا يؤدي إلى ذلك ؛ لأنه كان عالما بذلك التكليف عند توجهه على العيد ) ، وكان عالما برفعه (۲) عند النسخ فلم يظهر له أمر متجدد لم يعلمه، ولا رفع الحكم لأنه ظهر له شيء آخر. ويطلق البدا على الندم على ما كان والندم هو أن يقول قولا أو يفعل فعلا الغرض ثم يرى أن المصلحة في غير ما صدر عنه قولا وفعلا، وهذا أيضا لا يجوز في وصف الباري تعالى، فإنه لا يفعل فعلا لغرض، وإذا فعل بخلاف ذلك لم يفعله لمصلحة وغرض آخر، بل أقواله وأفعاله لا تعلل كما بينا قبل، فلم يتصور في حقه الندم، ولا أفضى النسخ إلى الندم، فتعين القول بأن النسخ غير مستحيل عقلا، وقوعه شرعًا من أدل الدليل على أنه غير مستحيل عقل.
It is said to them: The impossible is of two types: impossible in itself, like the combination of blackness and whiteness in one location at one time, and there is no doubt that the removal of the obligation and the ruling of the obligation after its establishment is of that category; and impossible because it leads to the impossible, like contradicting the known. And there is no impossibility here that abrogation leads to except badā and regret. And badā is applied to two meanings: one of them is appearance; it is said: 'a thing appeared to him' if it became manifest, and [the verse] 'And there appeared to them from God what they had not reckoned' [az-Zumar 39:47]; and this is not permissible for God the Exalted, for all information is manifest to Him and disclosed to Him, and abrogation does not lead to that because He was knowing of that obligation at the time of its imposition on the servant, and He was knowing of its removal at the time of abrogation, so no new matter that He did not know appeared to Him, nor was the ruling removed because something else appeared to Him. And badā is applied to regret over what was, and regret is that one says a statement or does an action with a purpose, then sees that the benefit lies in other than what issued from him in statement and action. And this also is not permissible in the description of the Creator the Exalted, for He does not do an action for a purpose, and if He does contrary to that, He does not do it for a benefit and another purpose; rather, His statements and actions are not caused as we explained before. So regret is not conceivable in His right, and abrogation does not lead to regret. Thus it is established that abrogation is not rationally impossible; its occurrence in the Law is among the clearest proofs that it is not rationally impossible.

Commentary
This passage is a rational argument defending the possibility of abrogation (naskh). The author addresses those who deny abrogation claiming it entails impossibility. He begins by distinguishing two types of impossible: (1) impossible in itself, like the simultaneous union of black and white in the same spot, which is intrinsically contradictory; (2) impossible because it leads to something impossible, like contradicting known information. He asserts that lifting an obligation after its establishment is of the first type? No, he says 'there is no doubt that the removal of the obligation and the ruling of the obligation after its establishment is of that category' – meaning it is not impossible in itself, but it could be impossible if it leads to something impossible. He then examines what abrogation might lead to: badā and regret. He explains the two meanings of badā: (a) appearance of something new, which would imply ignorance in God, but God knows all things eternally, so abrogation does not entail a new appearance; (b) regret, which involves changing one's mind due to a mistake or a new benefit, but God does not act for purposes or benefits in a way that would necessitate change. Since God's actions are not caused by external purposes (as previously established), regret is not applicable. Therefore abrogation does not lead to any rational impossibility, so it is rationally possible. The fact that it occurred in the Law is itself proof of its rational possibility.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Abrogation implies that God changes His mind or learns new information, making it impossible for God.
Correction: The author shows that abrogation does not entail any new knowledge for God (badā as appearance) nor a change of purpose (badā as regret) because God is eternally knowing and His actions are not caused by external purposes.

Initial Response to Objections: Twofold Answer
Author introduces two-part answer and begins the first.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the author responds to objections by using a two-part strategy: counter-objections and verification, and you will see how the first part relies on exposing the opponents' inconsistency.

Core Idea
The answer to objections has two parts: counter-objections and verification.

The author now turns to answering the objections raised. He announces a twofold answer: first, counter-objections (ilzāmāt wa muʿāraḍāt), and second, verification (taḥqīq). He begins the first part by pointing out that his opponents rely on a premise they themselves reject. This short passage gives us a glimpse into the author's method of refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Note the structure of the answer announced.
The passage ends abruptly; focus on the author's initial move.
2
Identify the two-part structure
The author states that the answer has two aspects. Identify them and notice that only the first begins here.
The second aspect (verification) is not present in this passage.
3
Understand the first part's argument
The author argues that the opponents built their objections on a premise of the author's doctrine (the existence of indivisible atoms), but according to the opponents' own doctrine, such an atom cannot be conceived. This exposes inconsistency.
This is a classic kalam strategy: refutation by showing the opponent's reliance on a rejected premise.
4
Consider the implication
Why is this a powerful response? It shows that the objections lose force unless the opponents concede a premise they formally deny.
This incomplete passage sets up the need for further reading.

الجواب عن هذه الشبهات من وجهين أحدهما: الإلزامات والمعارضات، والثاني: التحقيق. أما الأول فنقول : بنيتم هذه الإلزامات على قضية مذهبنا لا على مقتضى مذهبكم ؛ لأن على مقتضى مذهبكم لا يتصور جوهر فرد، وليس هذا التقدير
The answer to these objections is from two aspects: one of them is counter-impositions and objections, and the second is verification. As for the first, we say: You have built these counter-impositions on a premise of our doctrine, not on the requirement of your doctrine; because on the requirement of your doctrine an indivisible atom cannot be conceived, and this supposition [is not valid].

Commentary
This passage begins the author's rebuttal of the objections. He announces that the answer has two parts: (1) counter-objections (ilzāmāt) and reciprocal arguments (muʿāraḍāt), and (2) verification (taḥqīq). He then starts the first part: the opponents' arguments assume the reality of the indivisible atom (which is the atomists' premise), but according to the opponents' own doctrine, an indivisible atom is inconceivable. Therefore, their objections are based on a concession they do not grant. The passage ends abruptly, leaving the argument incomplete.
