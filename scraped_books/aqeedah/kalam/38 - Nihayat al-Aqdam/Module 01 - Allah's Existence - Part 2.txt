The Proof from Actions for God's Oneness
Shahrastānī proves God's oneness via the contingency of actions and the need for a single determinant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the contingency of observable actions proves that there can be only one God, and why multiple determinants are impossible.

Core Idea
One action requires one determinant—two would destroy independence.

Shahrastānī presents a rational argument for God's oneness based on the actions we observe. Since actions are contingent—they could exist or not—their existence requires a determinant. This argument shows that there cannot be two independent determinants, because that would lead to logical contradictions and undermine the attributes of God. Read the passage carefully to follow the logical steps.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its translation to get the overall flow of the argument.
Pay attention to the key terms: jawāz, murajjiḥ, istiqlāl.
2
Identify the initial premise
The proof starts with the claim that the way to prove the Maker is through actions, since senses cannot perceive Him directly.
Actions are contingent (jāʾiz); they are poised between existence and non-existence.
3
Trace the argument for a single determinant
Follow why contingency leads to a determinant, and why that determinant must be one. The passage spells out the impossibility of two determinants through scenarios.
Think about what happens if two gods both try to determine the same action.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The argument concludes that only one God can be truly independent; any second would introduce deficiency and need, contradicting divinity.
Notice how this reinforces the earlier method of independence (istiġnā).

And we say: It is known that the way to prove the Maker is the proof through actions (al-afʿāl), since the senses do not bear witness to Him. The actions we have observed indicate Him from the aspect of their contingency (jawāz) and possibility in themselves. Contingency is a single judgment that indicates the Maker in that the action is poised between existence and non-existence. When existence becomes preponderant, we are compelled to posit a determinant (murajjiḥ), and this determinant is not within the contingency itself. Its preponderance indicates that there are not two determinants each independently performing the preponderance, for if it were not independent in preponderance, it would not be a god. Existence became preponderant only because He willed specification for existence, and just as He willed, He knew that He is the determinant. So if we suppose another determinant sharing in the preponderance, independence is nullified, and neither His knowledge, will, nor power would be such that He is the determinant. And if He does not share in the preponderance and remains inactive, then His knowledge, will, and power also would not be such that He is the determinant; rather His knowledge would be attached to the preponderance of another, and likewise His will. If His knowledge is attached to another being the determinant, it is impossible that He himself is the determinant, for the contrary of the known is impossible to occur. Similarly His will would become a wish and desire from another, not a purpose, preponderance, and specification from Himself. Thus deficiency affects every one of His attributes; rather He would be in need of another in all that, and need is incompatible with divinity. This method supports the clarification of the method of independence (istiġnā), and it is the best that has been mentioned on this issue.

Commentary
This passage is a proof of the oneness of God based on the contingency of actions (afʿāl). The author states that the way to prove the existence of the Creator is through the actions we observe, since the senses cannot perceive God directly. These actions are contingent (jāʾiz) — they could exist or not exist. When they do exist, we must posit a determinant (murajjiḥ) that tipped the balance towards existence. The contingency itself does not contain this determinant. The preponderance of existence indicates that there cannot be two independent determinants; because if there were two, then either each independently preponderates the action (contradicting independence or leading to competition) or one remains inactive — both scenarios result in deficiency. The author explains: if two gods both will the same action, then neither is independent. If one wills and the other does not, then the one who does not will has his knowledge attached to the other's action, making him unable to act as a determinant — his will becomes a wish for another's action, not his own. Thus his attributes (knowledge, will, power) become deficient and dependent on another, which contradicts divinity. Therefore, there can only be one God. The author notes that this argument supports and reinforces the earlier argument from independence (istiġnā) and is the best one raised on this topic.

Proof of the Creator from Contingent Actions
The author argues from contingent actions to a unique Preponderator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the contingency of observable actions leads to the affirmation of a unique, independent Preponderator (God).

Core Idea
Contingent actions point to a single, independent Cause.

In this passage, the author presents a foundational kalam argument for the existence and oneness of God. He argues that the actions we witness in the world are contingent—they could have been otherwise. This contingency demands a Preponderator who tips the scale toward existence. Moreover, the very fact of preponderance shows that there can only be one such independent Preponderator. This lesson unpacks that reasoning.

Commentary
This passage presents an argument for the existence and oneness of God based on the contingency of observable actions. The author begins by noting that the verse 'There is nothing like unto Him' indicates the impossibility of any likeness in divinity. The main argument: the way to prove the Maker is through actions, since He is not directly perceived. These actions are contingent (possible) in themselves. Contingency is a single judgment that points to the Maker because it oscillates between existence and non-existence. When existence preponderates, we must affirm a Preponderator (murajjih). The preponderance cannot be due to the essence of the action itself. Moreover, the fact of preponderance shows that there cannot be two independent preponderators; if each were independent, then there would be a conflict, and the preponderance would not be uniquely determined. If one is not independent, he is not a god. If they share, independence is lost. If one is inactive, his attributes become parasitic. Ultimately, any partner would entail deficiency and dependence, which contradict divinity. The author concludes that this method supports the 'method of self-sufficiency' and is the best approach to the issue.

Why Sharing Preponderance Contradicts Divinity
The author argues that any partner in preponderance entails deficiency and dependence, contradicting divinity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why any partner in the act of preponderance would make God dependent, contradicting His divinity, and how this supports the method of self-sufficiency.

Core Idea
Any partner would make God dependent, which is impossible for divinity.

In the second part of the passage, the author deepens the argument for oneness by examining what would happen if there were another preponderator. He shows that whether this other being shares in preponderance or remains inactive, the first being's attributes—knowledge, will, and power—would become deficient or dependent on another. This leads to the conclusion that dependence contradicts divinity, reinforcing the doctrine of God's self-sufficiency (istighna').

Guided Path
1
Review the uniqueness argument
This lesson builds on the previous one. Ensure you understand that the Preponderator must be unique from the fact of preponderance.
The author now considers hypothetical partners.
2
Analyze the first scenario: sharing
If another being shares in preponderance, independence is lost. Then the first being's knowledge, will, and power would not make Him the Preponderator.
This means His attributes would not be effective.
3
Analyze the second scenario: inactive partner
If another being is inactive, the first being's knowledge and will would be directed towards that other's preponderance, making His will wishful thinking.
Thus deficiency enters each attribute.
4
Grasp the conclusion
In either case, the first being becomes dependent on another, which contradicts divinity. This supports the method of self-sufficiency.
The author declares this the best approach.

Refutation of the Proof of Mutual Hindrance
Refutes the proof of tamānuʿ by showing difference in will is inconceivable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the proof of mutual hindrance (tamānuʿ) is logically invalid because will must correspond to knowledge, making difference in will inconceivable.

Core Idea
If knowledge is fixed, will must match — so difference in will is impossible.

In this passage, the author rebuts a classic argument for the existence of one God known as the proof of mutual hindrance. The proof assumes that two gods would differ in will, but the author shows that difference in will is inconceivable because will follows knowledge. Since we cannot conceive of knowledge differing from its object, we cannot conceive of will differing either. Thus the proof collapses.

2

Follow the author's reasoning: knowledge cannot differ from its object, so will also cannot differ. Understand that the proof of tamānuʿ requires difference, which is impossible.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the proof of mutual hindrance (dalīl al-tamānuʿ) for the existence of one God. The author points out that if we know one motion, then the will must correspond to that knowledge. Since we cannot conceive of knowledge differing from its object, we also cannot conceive of will differing. The proof of tamānuʿ relies on either actual difference or imagined difference between two deities, but since difference in will is not conceivable (because will follows knowledge), the argument fails. Therefore, the proof is invalid.

Hence, the author concludes, the act indicates one agent, and any additional supposed gods would be equal in number but cannot coexist as truly divine.

Core Idea
Adding necessity to existence reveals a hidden attribute.

Al-Shahrastani targets the philosophers who deny real attributes but still use terms like wujūd for both God and creatures. He points out that when they distinguish between necessary and contingent existence, they are adding a meaning (wujūb) that is not part of wujūd itself. This forces them to admit a real distinction, undermining their own position.

Commentary
This passage is a direct response to the opponent's view. The author first points out that the opponent admits a hierarchy (order) in existence, with some beings more prior than others. If they can admit order, why not also admit opposition (تضاد) such that some beings are entirely good and others entirely evil? The author then appeals to revealed religion: angels are entirely good, devils entirely evil. He also mentions the dualist schools (light and darkness) who would accept this. He then uses a dilemma: either the beings of the world have no source (then they are uncaused, which implies atheism), or their source is pure good (then how does evil come from good?) or pure evil (but pure evil does not exist in your system). Thus the opponent is trapped in a contradiction.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The author is arguing that good and evil are two independent principles, like in Zoroastrianism.
Correction: No, the author is not endorsing dualism. He is using the opponent's own admission of order to force them to accept opposition, and then showing the absurd consequences of that position. He is a theist arguing against a particular ontology.
