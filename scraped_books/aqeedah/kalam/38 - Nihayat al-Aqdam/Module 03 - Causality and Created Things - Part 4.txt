Commentary
This passage is an argument about the essential distinction between cause ('illa) and condition (shart). The author asserts that anyone who posits cause-effect or condition-conditioned relationships must accept that there is a priority in essence (taqaddum bi al-dhat) even if the two are temporally concomitant. For example, the cause deserves priority over its effect in essence, and the effect deserves posteriority. This is demonstrated by the fact that we can say 'the world became knowledgeable because knowledge subsists in it' but not the reverse; the world's being knowledgeable depends on knowledge, not vice versa. Similarly, ability (istita'a) is prior in essence to the act, even though they occur together in time. The same applies to condition and conditioned: the locus must be alive for knowledge to subsist, not the opposite. The author then warns that this distinction is neither merely verbal nor imaginary; it is a rational judgment (qadiyya 'aqliyya). He then presents two alternatives: either abandon causal language entirely and treat names like 'knowing' and 'agent' as purely derivative linguistic terms, which is easier; or accept that knowledge is prior to being knowledgeable, and essence is prior to attributes. The second alternative is left incomplete in the text, but it implies a metaphysical commitment to essential priority.

The Dilemma of Temporal Knowledge and Infinite Regress
Presents a dilemma against temporal knowledge leading to infinite regress

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author uses a dilemma to refute the Karramiyya's claim that God's knowledge is temporal, showing that it leads to an infinite regress unless grounded in eternal knowledge.

Core Idea
Temporal knowledge without eternal knowledge leads to an infinite regress.

This passage presents a powerful logical dilemma against those who assert that God's knowledge is temporal. The author forces his opponents to answer whether these temporal knowledges are known before they exist. Whichever way they answer, they find themselves cornered: either they must admit eternal knowledge or face an impossible infinite chain. This dilemma effectively refutes the Karramiyya sect.

Refutation of the Objection of Potential Infinity
The author answers the objection that a body contains potentially infinite parts.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Potential infinity is no escape; it either becomes actual or remains impossible.

The previous proof established that a finite body cannot contain infinitely many actual parts. Now an objector suggests that perhaps the body contains potentially infinite parts, and that this escapes the proof. In this passage, the author dismantles this objection by examining whether that potentiality can ever be actualized, leading to a decisive refutation.
