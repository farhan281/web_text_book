Abstraction of Intellectual Images and the Role of Awareness
Author explains intellectual abstraction and resolves a philosophical perplexity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the rational soul abstracts intellectual images from particular instances and why awareness of commonalities and differences is not a condition for emanation.

Core Idea
Intellectual images are abstracted not by awareness itself, but by a prior disposition.

This passage delves into the mechanism of abstraction: how the intellect moves from particular sensory images to universal intellectual forms. The author explains the process through observation and awareness, then tackles a subtle philosophical perplexity about whether awareness itself is a condition for receiving the intellectual image. Finally, he resolves a potential contradiction with the principle that mental images conform to external simples.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the full Arabic passage and its English translation. Get a sense of the flow and the main claims before diving into analysis.
The passage is dense; read it twice if needed.
2
Understand the abstraction process
Identify how the author describes the abstraction of intellectual images: through observation of particulars, awareness of commonalities/differences, and emanation from the Generative Principle.
Notice the role of the imaginative faculty and the stripping of accidents.
3
Analyze the perplexity about awareness
The author presents a dilemma: if awareness of commonalities/differences is the same as the intellectual image, it's posterior; if it's awareness of what is common/different, it's the image itself. Either way, it is not a condition for emanation.
This resolves an earlier ambiguity in the text.
4
Examine the objection and response
An imaginary objector says that the author's view contradicts the principle that mental images match external images. The author replies that since the external simple is simple, each mental image when externalized corresponds to that same simple.
This shows how the author defends his position without contradiction.
5
Reflect on the key takeaway
Consider the overall lesson: abstraction is not a conscious act of awareness but a natural emanation based on prior observation and disposition.
This is a subtle point that clarifies a common misunderstanding.

Commentary
This passage explains the process of abstracting intellectual images from external particulars. The author first states that intellectual images differ from imaginary ones and are abstracted by the intellect from external identities based on dispositions and conditions, such as observing many or few particulars and reflecting on their commonalities and differences. The rational soul, using the imaginative faculty, observes imaginary images and becomes aware of shared and distinctive aspects, which causes an image of the commonality and difference to emanate from the Generative Principle, stripped of accidental features. This image then corresponds to all instances. The author then addresses a perplexity: whether the awareness is prior or not, concluding that it is not a condition for the emanation of intellectual images because the distinction between observing commonalities in imaginary images and having them abstracted in the mind is not real. Finally, an objection is raised that this contradicts the principle that mental images conform to external images. The response is that since the external thing is simple, when mental images are externalized, each is identical to the external image of that simple thing, so there is no contradiction.

Introduction to Quiddity: Etymology and Logical Order
Commentary on etymology and order of topics in quiddity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why quiddity (al-māhiyya) is studied after existence and non-existence, and how the term 'māhiyya' is derived from 'mā huwa'.

Core Idea
Quiddity is studied after existence and non-existence because it is their substrate.

In this opening of the Second Observatory, the commentator explains both the etymology of the word 'māhiyya' and the surprising order of topics: although quiddity might seem logically prior, it is placed after existence and non-existence. This introduction sets the stage for the detailed study of quiddity's states by clarifying key terms and the reasoning behind the sequence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how the commentator first establishes the meaning of 'māhiyya' and then justifies why its investigation follows existence and non-existence.
Pay attention to the etymology: 'māhiyya' comes from 'mā huwa' plus suffixes.
2
Examine the etymology of 'māhiyya'
The passage explains that 'māhiyya' is derived from 'mā huwa' by adding a relative yā', dropping one yā' for lightness, and adding a tā' to change from adjective to noun. A similar derivation exists for 'mā'iyya'.
The alternate form 'mā'iyya' is said to be the original, with hamza changed to hā'.
3
Understand the logical order
The author presents the investigations of existence and non-existence before quiddity. The commentator explains that this is because quiddity is studied as the substrate of existence and non-existence, and substratehood is posterior to them.
The key point: quiddity is investigated insofar as it is suitable for being a substrate, not necessarily that it actually is one.
4
Clarify the suitability condition
The commentary emphasizes that the investigation only requires quiddity to be suitable (ṣāliḥ) for being the substrate of existence or non-existence. Actual occurrence is not needed; even potentiality suffices for the study of its accidents.
This is why the author uses 'ṣāliḥa li-maʻrūḍiyya' (suitable for substratehood) rather than 'maʻrūḍa' (actually a substrate).
5
Review key terms
Go over the key Arabic terms: مَاهِيَة (quiddity), مَائِيَة (another form), وُجُود (existence), عَدَم (non-existence), مَعْرُوض (substrate), and عَوَارِض (accidents). Understanding these will help in subsequent discussions.
These terms form the vocabulary of the entire observatory.

Commentary
This passage is an introduction to the second observatory on quiddity (al-māhiyya). It begins by presenting the matn (original text) which states that the investigations of existence (wujūd) and non-existence ('adam) are placed before the investigations of their substrate, quiddity, because quiddity is investigated insofar as it is suitable for being the substrate of one of them, making it posterior. It also mentions that the observatory has twelve purposes, the first being distinguishing quiddity from what is other than it. The commentary then explains the etymology of the term 'māhiyya': it is derived from 'mā huwa' (what is) by adding the relative yā', dropping one yā' for ease, and adding tā' to change from adjective to noun. Similarly 'mā'iyya' is derived from 'mā'. It notes that the intent is to explain the states of quiddity as universal matters whose judgments apply to particular quiddities. Next, the commentary addresses the order: although natural order would suggest investigating quiddity first, it is presented after existence and non-existence because the investigation concerns accidents that attach to quiddity in states of existence or non-existence, so quiddity must first be suitable for such attachment. The commentary clarifies that we only need suitability of substratehood, not actual occurrence. Finally, it states that quiddity is posterior because substratehood is posterior to existence and non-existence.

Why Quiddity is Discussed After Existence and Non-Existence
Explains why existence and non-existence are discussed before quiddity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the methodological reason why the discussion of quiddity is preceded by the discussion of existence and non-existence in Islamic philosophy.

Core Idea
Quiddity is discussed later because it is a substrate for existence and non-existence.

This lesson explains a key methodological choice in the study of general matters: why quiddity is not the starting point. The author argues that quiddity is investigated only insofar as it can be a substrate for existence or non-existence, making it conceptually posterior. Understanding this ordering helps avoid confusion about the natural sequence of topics.

The author uses a 'because' clause to explain the ordering.

Contrast with the natural order
The author notes that the natural order would require quiddity first, but he deviates. Understand why he prioritizes existence and non-existence.
The natural order is based on conceptual priority, while the author's order is based on the investigation's perspective.
4
Understand the subtlety of 'eligibility' vs 'actual occurrence'

The General Rule for I'tibari Matters
The author states the general rule for identifying i'tibari matters and gives its basis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the general rule (ضابط) for identifying i'tibari matters and the logical reasoning behind it.

Core Idea
If a quality can apply to a non-existent essence, it must be i'tibari—not real.

This passage from Sharh al-Mawaqif introduces a crucial methodological tool: a general rule that helps distinguish between real (wujudiyya) and considerational (i'tibari) attributes. The author explains that certain qualities are known to be i'tibari because of their logical relation to existence. By grasping this principle, you can evaluate similar cases across theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice the structure: heading, rule, argument, and concluding exhortation.
The passage contains a mathematical-like proof: if P then Q, but not Q, so not P.
2
Define the key terms
Identify the terms 'i'tibari' (considerational), 'wujudiyya' (existential), 'mahiyya' (quiddity/essence), and 'ta'akhkhur' (posteriority). Understand how they relate.
Posteriority means the quality comes after the existence of its subject in external reality.
3
Understand the logical argument
Trace the reasoning: If the quality were existential (wujudiyya), it could describe an essence even when that essence does not exist externally. But that is impossible. Therefore, it must be i'tibari.
The impossibility is intuitive: a non-existent thing cannot have real attributes.
4
Recognize the rule's application
The author presents the rule as a universal principle (ضابط) covering many cases. Ask yourself: what other qualities are i'tibari by this rule? Examples: possibility, generality, etc.
This rule spares repetition: once understood, you can apply it to all similar instances.

Commentary
This passage evaluates the methods for proving the need of a possible thing for a cause. It begins by noting that the preceding argument (the one about time) even if its premises are conceded, only invalidates the view that origination (hadath) is the cause or part of the cause, but it does not prove the universal claim that all possibles need a cause. These explanations clarify the metaphor: it is a well-trodden path. Thus, the lesson asserts that the most reliable method is an immediate intuitive necessity, not syllogistic arguments.
