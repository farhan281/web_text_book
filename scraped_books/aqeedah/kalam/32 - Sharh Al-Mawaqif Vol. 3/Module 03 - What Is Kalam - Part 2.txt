The Theologians' First Reason: Determination as Non-Existential
Theologians' first reason for determination being non-existential.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first reason why theologians consider determination (taʿayyun) to be non-existential: if it were existential, its joining to the quiddity would rely on a complete refutation due to vagueness.

Core Idea
Existential determination would require an incomplete refutation, so it must be non-existential.

The theologians offer two reasons for determination being non-existential. This lesson covers the first reason: if determination were existential, its attachment to the quiddity would depend on a 'complete refutation' (al-naqḍ al-ijmālī), which is problematic because the meaning of 'existential' was vague. This argument highlights the need for clarity in theological definitions.

Guided Path
1
Read the theologians' claim

Notice the conditional: 'if it were existential, then...'
2
Understand the conditional
The conditional statement: if determination were existential, its joining to the quiddity would depend on a complete refutation (al-naqḍ al-ijmālī).
What is al-naqḍ al-ijmālī? It refers to a general refutation without detailed analysis.
3
Identify the problem of vagueness
The reason for this dependence is the vagueness (ijmāl) and lack of detail in the meaning of 'existential' as used in the earlier rebuttal.
Thus, an existential determination would require a more detailed refutation that is not provided, so it is better to consider it non-existential.

Core Idea
The three modal judgments are mind-dependent concepts, not external realities.

This passage from Mir Sayyid Sharif's commentary establishes a key principle: the three modal conditions (necessity, impossibility, possibility) are not actual properties existing in the world, but rather entitative concepts that the mind uses to analyze realities. The author provides a specific proof for impossibility to illustrate why such judgments cannot have external existence.

So this—i.e., the affirmation of priority for one side—depends on the absence of that cause which is for the other side, necessarily, for with the existence of that cause the other side is preponderant and prior, otherwise it would not be a cause for it. If it is said: 'If you allow the occurrence of priority for one of the two sides from the essence along with...' his statement: 'For with the existence of that cause, etc.' And what was said: 'Intrinsic preponderance of one side does not contradict the preponderance of the other side by a cause, just as intrinsic equality does not contradict preponderance arising from a cause'—is refuted because the conjunction of two preponderances is impossible, even if the source of one is the essence and the source of the other is the cause, because it is impossible for each of the two sides to be preponderant over the other in one time, as in the two pans of a scale. The analogy with equality is invalid because equality does not mean that it necessitates equality of the two sides (otherwise the occurrence of one side would be impossible, necessarily, since what is intrinsic does not cease), but rather it means that it does not necessitate the preponderance of one of them, so it does not contradict accidental preponderance. His statement: 'If it is said, if you allow, etc.' where you said: 'That priority is contrary to the supposition because it is impossible, and the conclusion is that the purpose of negating intrinsic priority is to prove the need for the efficient agent existing, and that is not necessary from what you mentioned, so it is not valid what was said that this objection has no justification according to the rules of disputation because its essence is that the approximation is incomplete because the purpose is to negate intrinsic priority leading to the need for the efficient agent existing, lest the door of proving the Maker be closed.' And what was said: 'Such a thing also applies in the case of equality because the requirement of equality is the need for a preponderator, so why not allow that the preponderator is the absence of the mentioned cause?'—it is not hidden that it is outside the rules of disputation because its application in the case of equality does not harm the incompleteness of the approximation of the proof that the arguer adduced for negating intrinsic priority, especially since there is a difference between the two cases of priority and equality. For in the case of priority, the essence is an agent for existence on condition of the absence of the cause of non-existence, while in the case of equality the essence cannot be an agent, so it would necessitate that non-existence is an influence on existence. And the statement that the essence cannot be an agent for existence is due to what has passed concerning existence being identical with the Necessary. If that is accepted, it entails the completion of the negation of priority because if the essence of the possible cannot be a cause for its existence, its need in its existence for the efficient agent existing is established. Hence it is known from this that the aim of the people is to prove the need for the efficient agent, regardless of the impossibility of a thing being a cause for its own existence.
Source pages p. 170

Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof followed by an objection and its refutation. The commentator begins by stating the topic: the investigation of the possible in itself. He then explains that priority of one side over the other depends on the absence of the cause of the opposite side; otherwise, the priority would not be intrinsic to the possible. An objection is raised: if intrinsic priority is allowed, why cannot the opposite side also have priority? The commentator refutes this by noting that two simultaneous preponderances are impossible, like the pans of a scale. He then addresses another objection about the purpose of negating intrinsic priority: it is to prove the need for an external efficient agent. The difference between priority and equality is highlighted: in the case of priority, the essence could be an agent if the cause of non-existence is absent, whereas in equality the essence cannot be an agent, leading to the absurdity of non-existence being an influence. The commentator concludes that the aim is to prove the need for an efficient agent regardless of whether the essence can be a cause for itself.

The Self-Evident Necessity of Possibility
Al-Jurjani declares the necessity of possibility as self-evident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the claim that possibility is necessary for possible essences is considered a self-evident truth in kalam, and how it grounds rational theology.

Core Idea
Possibility is not an option for the possible—it is a necessary feature of its essence.

In this passage, al-Jurjani (Sharh al-Mawaqif) asserts that the necessity of possibility for possible essences is more evident than any proof. He explains that after proper abstraction, the intellect judges this as a self-evident proposition. This lesson unpacks that claim and shows why it stands as an axiomatic starting point.

Guided Path

2
Understand the claim
The passage states that the possibility (الإمكان) that the essence of a possible being demands is necessary and inseparable. This is the core claim.
3

The author says it is a self-evident proposition that pure intellect judges after abstracting the terms properly. Note the role of تجريد (abstraction) in reaching self-evidence.

The author mentions two proofs but says they are problematic and their premises obscure. This reinforces the self-evidence of the claim.

Commentary
