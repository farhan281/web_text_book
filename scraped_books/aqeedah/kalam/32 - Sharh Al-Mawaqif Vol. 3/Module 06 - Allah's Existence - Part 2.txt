Delay Without Conditions Missing
Al-Iji elaborates on the mechanism of delay and rebuts objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the pre-eternal attachment of the will to a future time allows all conditions of agency to be present in pre-eternity while the effect is temporal.

Core Idea
Pre-eternal will can target a future time, avoiding missing conditions.

The response that God is a free agent raises further questions: if He has all conditions in pre-eternity, why isn't the effect immediate? The commentator explains that the will's attachment to a specific future time is itself a condition that is present in pre-eternity. This lesson covers that explanation and the refutation of related objections about time and regress.

Guided Path
1
Read the delay explanation
The passage explains how delay works through the will's pre-eternal attachment to future existence. Note the key distinction between volitional and necessitative delay.
2
Understand the objection about missing conditions
An objector says that the future time is a missing condition in pre-eternity. The author refutes this by saying time is a concomitant, not a condition.
3
Follow the regress argument and answer
Another objection leads to infinite regress. The answer accepts regress in volitional attachments as acceptable.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer passage responding to the fourth objection: that if God were a free agent, His effect need not be co-eternal with Him. The response is that God is indeed a free agent (mukhtar), not a necessitating cause (mujib bi al-dhat). A free agent can delay action, so the eternity of the effect is not required; only a necessitating cause would have a co-eternal effect. The commentator then clarifies that this choice does not abandon the position of necessitation (ijab) for choice; rather, the objection itself assumes necessitation. He explains that the free agent's delay is possible through the will's attachment in pre-eternity to the future existence of the effect, and this does not constitute a temporal gap in causation—temporal delay only occurs in necessitative causation. He refutes the claim that the future time is a condition that was absent in pre-eternity, arguing it is a necessary concomitant of influence, not a condition. He also addresses a potential regress argument about time, suggesting an answer involving renewal of volitional attachments. Finally, a discussion arises about the appropriate framing of the objection, concluding that all objections should be considered under the assumption of necessitation, and that the free agent's delay is consistent with having all conditions in pre-eternity, since the will's attachment to a future time is itself a condition present in pre-eternity. The passage ends abruptly with 'and there is a discussion in this, for from...'

The Fourth Objection and Its Answer: The Free Agent
Author delivers the answer to the fourth objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the fourth objection fails if the Necessary Being is a free agent, and why claiming freedom is not a retreat from necessitation.

Core Idea
A free agent can delay the act; only a necessary cause yields an eternal effect.

The fourth objection assumes that if God is a necessary cause, His effect must be eternal. But what if God is a free agent? This lesson presents the author's answer: God possesses all conditions in pre-eternity yet freely chooses to bring about the effect later, so eternity does not follow. It also addresses a common misunderstanding that choosing freedom is a change in doctrine.

The objection only works if God is a necessary cause, which is denied.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth objection regarding the eternity of the world and its answer, followed by extensive commentary. The author first states the answer: the Necessary Being is a free agent (mukhtar) possessing all efficient conditions in pre-eternity, but He can delay the act to any time He wills, so the eternity of His effect does not follow—that would only follow if He were a necessary cause (mujib bi al-dhat), which is denied. The commentary then explains that the fourth objection, if valid, would only prove dependence on a necessary cause, not on a free agent. The choice of freedom is not a retreat from necessitation. It further explains that delay of the act does not mean a lag in time from the complete cause; rather, intentional causation allows for a futural attachment of will. Refutations of objections regarding the time of existence and infinite regress of times are provided, along with the possibility of renewed attachments of will. The commentary also addresses a criticism that the answer is not sound because it changes the basis of the argument from necessitation to freedom, but it defends the author's answer by noting the context of the claim. The passage then discusses conditions, will, and necessity, ending with an acknowledgment of further discussion.

How the Free Agent Delays the Effect: Will and Temporal Lag
Explains how delay occurs via will and replies to objections about time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the will of the Necessary Being operates to produce an effect at a later time without implying a temporal lag from the complete cause.

Core Idea
In intentional causation, 'delay' means the effect occurs at the intended future time, not that it lags behind a complete cause.

The opponent might object: if the cause is complete in pre-eternity, the effect must be immediate; otherwise there is a temporal gap. This lesson explains the author's response: the will attaches in pre-eternity to the effect's existence at a future time, so no gap exists. It also addresses objections about time being a condition and the infinite regress of times.

Guided Path
1
Read the explanation of delay
Read how the will's attachment in pre-eternity to a future existence avoids any temporal lag. Note the distinction between lag in intention and lag in time.
The key phrase: 'فإن التخلف في الإيجاد القصدي هو أن لا يقع على نحو قصده لا أن يتخلف عنه زماناً'
2
Understand the 'time as condition' objection
Someone argues that if the time of existence was a condition, the cause would not be complete. The author replies that the time is a necessary consequence, not a condition.
The author calls that objection 'وهم' (delusion).
3
Examine the infinite regress of times
Another objection says we would have to transfer the discussion to the time of existence, leading to infinite regress. The author says time is suppositional, so no regress.
Then he mentions an alternative answer: renewed attachments of will, but commits to impossible infinite regress.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth objection regarding the eternity of the world and its answer, followed by extensive commentary. Commentary
This passage is a definition and discussion of the second of two main topics concerning the attribute of eternity (qidam). It begins by stating that eternity describes God's essence by consensus, and also His attributes according to the Ash'aris. The Mu'tazila verbally denied applying 'eternal' to anything other than God, but they affirmed it in meaning by positing four eternal states (ahwal) for God: existence, life, knowledge, and power (interpreted as the properties of being existent, living, knowing, powerful). Abu Hashim added a fifth state, divinity, as the cause and distinguisher. Al-Razi then critiques that these states are not existent, so they cannot be called eternal in the usual sense unless 'eternal' means merely 'established without beginning'. He also notes the criticism only applies to those Mu'tazila who affirm states. The following commentary (gala) provides extensive clarifications: the consensus of philosophers and religious people, the views of verificationist theologians and Sufis on attributes being relational, the Karramiyya's belief in created attributes, the Mu'tazila's denial of speech subsisting in God, and a detailed defense of the Mu'tazila's position by al-Tusi arguing that 'existence' and 'subsistence' are synonymous when speaking of eternity.

The Consensus on Eternity: Only God and His Attributes
Consensus of mutakallimun on eternity of God and attributes alone.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the consensus of the mutakallimun that only God and His attributes are eternal, and how the philosophers differed on the eternity of the world.

Core Idea
Only the Divine Essence and attributes are eternal; all else is created.

This lesson begins by establishing the theological consensus that only God and His attributes are described as eternal (qadim). The mutakallimun (theologians) unanimously hold that everything other than God is created and originated (hadith). Meanwhile, the philosophers (hukama') allowed for the eternity of the world, a position that will be examined later. This foundational distinction sets the stage for understanding the Harranian doctrine of multiple eternal principles.

The Harranian Five Eternal Principles: Classification
Enumeration of the Harranian five eternal principles with classification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Harranian doctrine of five eternal principles and their classification into two living/acting and three non-living/passive principles.

Core Idea
Harranians posited five eternal things: two active principles and three passive ones.

After presenting the mainstream consensus, Sanusi introduces a sect called the Harranians, a group from the Magians. They affirmed five eternal principles. أثبت صفات متعددة لإله واحد لا يكون كافراً. وسيأتيك في بحث الصفات القائمة بذاته تعالى تتمة لهذا الكلام وأما غير ذات الله تعالى وصفاته، فلا يوصف بالقدم بإجماع المتكلمين؛ لأن ما سوى الله تعالى مخلوق، وكل مخلوق حادث عندهم. وجوزه الحكماء إذ قالوا العالم قديم على التفصيل الذي ستطلع عليه في البحث عن حدوث العالم. وأثبت الحرنانيون من المجوس وهم فرقة منهم منسوبة إلى رجل يقال له حرنان قدماء خمسة: اثنان عالمان حيان، والأولى كما في المحصل اثنان حيان فاعلان وهما الباري والنفس. أما الباري فهو قديم وحي وفاعل لهذا العالم، وأما النفس والمراد بها ما يكون مبدأ للحياة، وهي الأرواح البشرية والسماوية، فهي حية لذواتها وقديمة أيضاً؛ إذ لو كانت حادثة لكانت مادية وفاعلة الأجسام التي تعلقت بها تعلق التدبير والتصرف وثلاثة لا عالمة ولا حية ولا فاعلة بل واحد منها منفعل واثنان لا فاعلان ولا منفعلان هي الهيولى والفضاء والدهر. فالهيولى قديمة وإلا احتاجت إلى هيولى أخرى هي منفعلة بقبول الصور فلا تكون فاعلة، وإلا لكانت مع بساطتها قابلة وفاعلة معاً، وليست بحية وهو ظاهر، والمراد بالفضاء هو الخلاء، ولو لم يكن قديماً لارتفع الامتياز عن الجهات، فلا تتميز جهة اليمين عن اليسار ولا جهة الفوق عن التحت وذلك أمر غير معقول والدهر هو الزمان ولا يتصور تقدم عدمه على وجوده لأنه تقدم زماني فيجتمع وجوده مع عدمه وهذان أعني الخلاء والزمان لا فاعلان ولا منفعلان. قال الإمام الرازي: كان هذا المذهب مستوراً فيما بين المذاهب فمال إليه ابن زكرياء الطبيب الرازي، وأظهره وعمل فيه كتاباً مسمى بالقول في القدماء الخمسة. وستقف على مأخذهم أثناء ما يرد عليك في الكتاب، وقد أشرنا نحن إلى ذلك إشارة خفية.
The Third Observatory - The Fifth Objective: On the Investigations of the Eternal. He affirmed multiple attributes for one God without being a disbeliever. And there will come to you in the discussion of the attributes subsisting in His Essence, exalted, a completion of this speech. As for other than the Essence of God, exalted, and His attributes, it is not described as eternal by the consensus of the mutakallimun, because whatever is other than God, exalted, is created, and every created thing is originated according to them. And the philosophers permitted it, since they said the world is eternal, according to the detail which you will encounter in the investigation of the world's origination. And the Harranians from the Magians—a sect from them attributed to a man called Harran—affirmed five eternals: two knowing, living ones. And preferably, as in al-Muhassal, two living, agents, namely the Creator and the soul. As for the Creator, He is eternal, living, and agent of this world. As for the soul—by which is meant that which is the principle of life, and it is the human and celestial spirits—it is living by its essence and also eternal; for if it were originated, it would be material and active upon the bodies to which it is attached by the attachment of management and disposal. And three that are not knowing, nor living, nor agent, but one of them is passive and two are neither agents nor passive: they are hyle, void, and time. For hyle is eternal, otherwise it would need another hyle that is passive in accepting forms, so it would not be agent; otherwise, with its simplicity, it would be both receptive and agent simultaneously, and it is not living, which is apparent. And by void is meant empty space, and if it were not eternal, distinction would cease among directions, so the right direction would not be distinguished from the left, nor the above from the below, and that is unintelligible. And time is duration, and it is inconceivable that its non-existence should precede its existence, because that would be a temporal priority, so its existence would coincide with its non-existence. And these two—i.e., void and time—are neither agents nor passive. Imam al-Razi said: This doctrine was hidden among the sects, then Ibn Zakariyya al-Razi the physician inclined towards it, made it known, and wrote a book on it called 'The Statement on the Five Eternals.' And you will come upon their arguments during what comes to you in the book, and we have alluded to that with a subtle indication.

Commentary
This passage is part of the investigation of the eternal (al-qadim). It begins by noting that affirming multiple attributes for one God does not constitute disbelief, but that is a side comment. The main discussion then turns to what is described as eternal. The mutakallimun (theologians) are unanimous that only God and His attributes are eternal; everything else is created and originated (hadith). The philosophers (al-hukama') allowed the world to be eternal, with details to be explained later. Then the passage focuses on the Harranians, a sect from the Magians (al-Majus) attributed to a man named Harran. They asserted five eternals (qudama'). Two are knowing, living agents: the Creator (al-Bari) and the soul (al-nafs). The soul is defined as the principle of life, encompassing human and celestial spirits; it is eternal because if it were originated, it would be material. The other three are not knowing, living, or agents: hyle (al-hayula), void (al-fada'), and time (al-dahr). Hyle is eternal, otherwise an infinite regress would result. Void is required for distinguishing directions; time is eternal because its non-existence cannot precede it temporally. These two (void and time) are neither agents nor passive. Imam al-Razi notes that this doctrine was obscure until Ibn Zakariyya al-Razi, a physician, revived it and wrote a book called 'al-Qawl fi al-Qudama' al-Khamsah' (The Statement on the Five Eternals). The author says he will later discuss their arguments, having alluded to them here subtly.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think the Harranians are the same as the Magians in all beliefs.
Correction: They are a sect from the Magians, with a distinct doctrine of five eternals not shared by all Magians.

Key Takeaway
The Harranians believed in five eternals: two living agents (Creator, soul) and three non-living principles (hyle, void, time), each argued by specific reasoning.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
The Harranians believed the soul is eternal and uncreated. Have you ever caught yourself acting as if your soul will live on forever without accountability to its Creator? How does this lesson remind you that your soul is not your own, but a trust from Allah?
?
They also argued that time and void must be eternal for change and direction to be possible. In your daily life, do you take the existence of time and space for granted, forgetting that they were created by Allah's will? What would it mean for your worship to truly believe that every second and every direction is a created sign of His power?
?
The Harranians assigned agency to both the Creator and the soul, claiming two living agents. Search your heart: is there any area of your life where you feel you are the ultimate agent, independent of Allah's will? How can you surrender that illusion and acknowledge Him as the sole Living Agent?
