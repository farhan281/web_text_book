Plato's Argument for Abstract Individuals and Its Critique
Plato's argument for abstract individuals and beginning of critique.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Plato's argument for the existence of abstract individuals and the first two critiques levelled against it.

Core Idea
Plato's argument only proves abstraction from separable accidents, not from all necessary concomitants.

This passage presents Plato's claim that for every species there exists an abstract individual, eternal and free from corruption. The commentator then begins a threefold critique, of which the first two points are given, pointing out that the argument only establishes abstraction from accidental attributes and fails to prove individuality. This lesson unpacks that argument and its limitations.

3

The commentator argues that susceptibility to opposites only proves abstraction from matter, not from all accidents.
4

The first critique: the argument only proves abstraction from separable accidents, not from necessary concomitants. The second: individuality itself is unproven. Understand why these are fatal to Plato's claim.
The third critique is missing, but these two already show the argument is insufficient.

Commentary
This passage presents Plato's claim that there exists for every species an individual abstract from all accidents, eternal, perpetual, and subject to no corruption, and also susceptible to opposites. The commentator first explains the structure: the author of the text (al-Iji) likely implies that Plato said this. He then analyzes each phrase: 'individual' shows it is not the absolute quiddity (which is the species itself); 'abstract' from all accidents except existence (since it is susceptible to opposites); 'no corruption' because corruption is a concomitant of matter and this individual is abstract from all matter. The argument is that man is susceptible to opposites, otherwise opposites would not occur to him, so he must be in himself abstract from something. The commentator notes that if 'susceptible' means to all opposites, it is denied; if to some, it does not prove complete abstraction. Then he begins a threefold critique (only two complete here): First, even if the argument were sound, it only proves abstraction from separable (accidental) attributes, not from necessary concomitants of the quiddity. Second, individuality (fardiyya) is part of the claim and lacks proof. The third point is cut off. The passage thus partially critiques the Platonic argument.

Division of the One into Aggregative and Connective Unity
Al-Isfahani classifies the one into parts based on similarity or difference of nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two types of unity based on parts: the one by aggregation (heterogeneous parts) and the one by connection (homogeneous parts).

Core Idea
Unity can be either aggregative with diverse parts or connective with uniform parts.

Al-Isfahani distinguishes two ways something can be one while having parts: either the parts are of different natures, like a tree's wood and leaves, or they share the same nature, like water's atoms. This classification helps clarify how we speak of 'one' in composite and simple bodies.

This shows that the classification depends on whether parts are united in reality or just conceptually.

Commentary
This passage defines two types of unity (al-wāḥid) based on how parts relate. The first is 'the one by aggregation' (al-wāḥid bi al-ijtimā‘), which refers to a composite of quantitative parts that are different in reality (heterogeneous), like a single tree made of wood, bark, leaves, etc. The second is 'the one by connection' (al-wāḥid bi al-ittiṣāl), which refers to a body like water on the view that atoms exist, where the parts are of agreeing reality (homogeneous). The commentary clarifies that the one by aggregation includes composite individuals like a group of Zayd and Amr if they are considered a single person by convention, provided actual sensible aggregation is a condition. Otherwise, it does not qualify. It also notes that on the view of the homogeneity of individual atoms, the simple body's parts are homogeneous in essence but can differ through accidents, which are constitutive of simple bodies.

Classifying the One: By Aggregation and By Connection
Al-Iji classifies the one into two types: by aggregation and by connection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two types of 'one' in kalam: the one by aggregation (composed of heterogeneous parts) and the one by connection (uniform parts), and how they apply to composite and simple bodies.

Core Idea
The one is not monolithic—it has two types: one by aggregation and one by connection.

In discussing whether essences are made, al-Iji first clarifies what it means for something to be 'one.' He introduces a key classification: the one by aggregation, which has parts differing in reality, and the one by connection, where parts are uniform. This lesson unpacks that distinction with examples.

1

Understand the one by individual

Commentary
This passage defines and classifies the one (al-wāḥid) in the context of discussing whether essences are made. The matn states that the one is divided into two main types: (1) the one by aggregation (al-wāḥid bi al-ijtimāʿ), which is a composite of parts that differ in reality, like an individual tree composed of heterogeneous parts such as wood, bark, etc.; and (2) the one by connection (al-wāḥid bi al-ittiṣāl), which is a simple body like water, whose parts are uniform in reality according to the theory of the indivisible part (al-juzʾ alladhī lā yatajazzaʾ). The sharḥ then expands: The one by aggregation includes aggregates like 'Zayd and Amr' which are considered one by individual (shakhṣ) only if sensory aggregation is a condition; otherwise, such aggregates fall under the division. It also clarifies that the uniformity of parts in a simple body does not entail homogeneity of all simple bodies, because the accidents that differentiate them (e.g., color, taste) are constitutive of their essences according to those who hold the homogeneity of individual atoms (tajāns al-jawāhir al-farda).
