Essential Possibility and Impossibility by Another
Al-Iji explains the distinction between essential possibility and accidental impossibility, using the example of a temporal event.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between essential possibility (imkan dhati) and impossibility due to an external condition (imtina' bi al-ghayr), and why one does not negate the other.

Core Idea
A thing can be possible in itself yet impossible in relation to another.

The passage introduces a subtle but crucial distinction: a thing's possibility relative to its own essence is not invalidated by impossibility that arises from something external. This forms the backbone of al-Iji's reply to objections about the eternity of possible entities.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then its translation. Notice how the author distinguishes between possibility by essence and impossibility by another.
Pay special attention to the phrase 'لا ينافي الإمكان الذاتي'.
2
Identify the two types of impossibility
The passage mentions two kinds: impossibility due to a necessary description (like being instantaneous) and impossibility due to temporal restriction. See how each relates to essential possibility.
Consider the example of the temporal event (hadith) taken alone vs. taken with the condition of temporality.
3
Reflect on the philosophical implications
Think about why this distinction matters for arguments about the eternity of the world. It shows that even if something cannot be eternal due to an external factor, its essence remains possible.
This will be used later in responses to objections.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer sequence. The objection is raised that the earlier proof is refuted by time and motion, because certain entities (like the flowing now and motion as intermediation) are settled and have no parts, so they have eternal possibility and their eternity is actual. Other entities (traversing motion and non-settled time) are impossible, and the commentator did not answer this, perhaps because he denies them. Also instantaneous letters (huruf aniyya) only exist in the instant of their occurrence, so they have the eternity of possibility but not the possibility of eternity. The author responds by distinguishing essential possibility from accidental impossibility. He explains that even if an entity like an occurrence (hadith) is impossible in eternity when taken with the condition of temporality, its essence alone has eternal possibility. The key point is that possibility with respect to the essence does not contradict impossibility due to an external condition or a necessary attribute (like being instantaneous). The commentator's remarks reinforce this: the eternity of events is not impossible in eternity because the impossibility is by another (bi al-ghayr), not by essence. Thus essential possibility (imkan dhati) remains unaffected. The passage concludes that there is no contradiction between the possibility of a thing in itself and its impossibility due to something necessary to its essence.

Responding to the Objection from Non-Continuous Entities
Al-Iji presents and answers an objection based on non-continuous entities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Iji refutes the objection that non-settled entities like time, motion, and instantaneous letters cannot have eternal possibility.

Core Idea
Even if actual eternity is impossible for some entities, their essential possibility remains eternal.

The objection claims that entities which exist only momentarily (like instantaneous letters) cannot have eternal possibility. Al-Iji responds by again appealing to the distinction between essential and accidental impossibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Find the part where the objector mentions time, motion in two senses, and instantaneous letters. Understand why they think these entities undermine the earlier proof.
The objector is using these examples to challenge the claim that all possibles have eternal possibility.
2
Identify al-Iji's response
Al-Iji responds by showing that even if such entities cannot be eternal due to their nature (e.g., they are non-settled), their possibility with respect to their essence is still eternal.
Key phrase: 'فلا تنافي بين إمكان الشيء بالقياس إلى ذاته وامتناعه بالقياس إلى أمر لازم لذاته'.
3
Reflect on the implication
Consider how this response defends the original proof. The objector's examples do not refute the eternity of essential possibility; they only show that some things are impossible by another.
This reinforces the core distinction from the previous lesson.

Commentary

Thus essential possibility (imkan dhati) remains unaffected. The passage concludes that there is no contradiction between the possibility of a thing in itself and its impossibility due to something necessary to its essence.

Notice that eternity is attributed to both the essence and the attributes, but with different levels of agreement.
2
Understand the consensus on essence
All philosophers and religious people agree that God's essence is eternal.
3
Understand the Ash'ari position on attributes
The Ash'aris affirm that God's attributes are also eternal, existing in His essence.
4
Note the Mu'tazila position
The Mu'tazila verbally denied that attributes are eternal, though some interpretations suggest they affirmed it in meaning through their theory of states.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the sixth objection in a series of objections against the eternity of God's attributes. The author first summarizes the reply to the fifth objection (about continuous non-existence), then applies the same logic to the sixth. The key distinction is between 'imaginary attribution' (istinad wahmi) and 'real attribution' (istinad haqiqi). Imaginary attribution is a mental construct without external reality, while real attribution requires both terms to exist externally. Since the number four and its evenness are mental constructs, their attribution is imaginary; similarly, the case of continuous non-existence is imaginary. The commentary then clarifies these terms. The next part introduces the second of the two main topics regarding eternity: that it is attributed to God's essence by consensus of philosophers and religious people, and to His attributes according to the Ash'aris, while the Mu'tazila denied it verbally but affirmed it in meaning through their theory of states (ahwal).

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That the Mu'tazila completely denied eternity of attributes in all senses.
Correction: The passage indicates their denial was verbal, and they might have affirmed it in meaning through their theory of states.

Key Takeaway
Eternity is attributed to God's essence by consensus, but the eternity of His attributes is affirmed by Ash'aris and verbally denied by Mu'tazila.
