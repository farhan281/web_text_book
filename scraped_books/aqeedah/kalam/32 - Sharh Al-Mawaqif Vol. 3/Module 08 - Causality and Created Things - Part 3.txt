Summary of Proof and Refutation of Simultaneous Preponderance
Summarizes proof and refutes simultaneous preponderance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the proof is summarized and why two preponderances cannot coexist, even if one is intrinsic and the other accidental.

Core Idea
Two simultaneous preponderances are as impossible as both pans of a scale rising.

This passage recaps the earlier argument that if both sides of a possibility were preponderant, it would lead to contradiction. It then refutes a key objection: that intrinsic preponderance of one side does not preclude the other side being preponderant due to an external cause. The commentator uses the analogy of a scale to show that two preponderances cannot coexist, and clarifies why the analogy with equality fails.

Guided Path
1
Understand the gist of the proof
The proof is summarized as leading to either conversion or contradiction if the other side is impossible.
2
Recognize the objection
Some claim that intrinsic preponderance does not contradict accidental preponderance, just as intrinsic equality does not contradict accidental preponderance.
3
See the rebuttal
The conjunction of two preponderances is impossible, even if one is intrinsic and the other accidental, like two scale pans cannot both rise.
4
Learn the disanalogy with equality
Equality does not necessitate preponderance of either side, so accidental preponderance is possible. But self-preponderance would necessitate the side, preventing accidental preponderance for the other.

Commentary

Then it addresses an objection about intrinsic preponderance not conflicting with accidental preponderance, using the analogy of equality. The commentator refutes this by arguing that two preponderances cannot coexist, and the analogy with equality is invalid because equality, not self-preponderance, allows accidental preponderance. Purpose of Denying Self-Preponderance and Response to Equality Objection
Clarifies purpose and refutes equality objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the purpose of denying self-preponderance is to prove the need for an external efficient cause, and why the equality objection does not undermine this argument.

Core Idea
Denying self-preponderance opens the door to proving the Maker.

This passage clarifies the goal of the earlier argument: denying self-preponderance leads to establishing the need for an existing efficient cause (المؤثر الموجود). It addresses an objection that similar reasoning applies to the case of equality, and explains the crucial difference between the two cases. In self-preponderance, the essence is an agent conditioned on absence of a cause of non-existence, whereas in equality, the essence cannot be an agent at all.

Guided Path
1

The purpose is to prove the need for an existing efficient cause, so that the door to proving the Maker is not closed.
2
Understand the objection
One might object that the same reasoning applies to the case of equality, because equality also requires a preponderator.
3
Learn the difference between the two cases
In self-preponderance, the essence is an agent conditioned on absence of a cause of non-existence; in equality, the essence cannot be an agent, so non-existence would have to be an agent, which is absurd.
4
See the additional point
Even if the essence cannot be a cause for its own existence, the need for an efficient cause is still established.

Commentary
This passage presents the third investigation concerning the possible: that the possible, because it needs an efficient cause for its existence, requires that the preponderance arising from that cause reach the level of necessity. If it does not, mere preponderance is insufficient, because: if existence becomes preponderant without necessity, we could imagine the possible existing at one time and not at another. The specification of time would then need a preponderator; if none, we have preponderance without cause (absurd); if there is one, the original preponderance was not sufficient. The commentator then addresses an objection: the assumption allows non-existence at that time but not at all times, so the preponderance argument does not force a regress. He proposes a better inference: whenever the complete cause is realized, one side is preponderant; when preponderant, the other side is preponderate; when preponderate, it is impossible. Thus the complete cause necessitates impossibility of the opposite. The passage also clarifies that preponderance arises not from the complete cause itself (if necessity is excluded) but from its other parts. This discussion upholds the principle of sufficient reason and the necessity of the complete cause for determination.

Proof That Persistence Requires a Cause
Proof that persistence needs a cause and refutation of the objection of creating the existent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why persistence requires a cause and how this does not imply creating the existent for the Eternal.

Core Idea
Persistence, like origination, depends on a cause, but for the Eternal it is continuation, not creation.

This lesson tackles a central theological argument: does a thing need a cause to continue existing, or only to begin? The author argues that the need for persistence is self-evident, giving examples from everyday causality. He then addresses a potential objection that this would require 'creating the existent' – an impossibility – and shows that for the Eternal, persistence is simply the continuation of existence dependent on its cause, not a new creation.

The Flaw in the Proof
Sanusi identifies a flaw in the proof for the eternal's need for a cause in persistence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the specific flaw Sanusi identifies in the argument that the eternal (qadim) needs a cause for its persistence (baqa').

Core Idea
The proof fails because it either yields a tautology or addresses no new effect.

Sanusi begins by acknowledging the detailed answers found in longer works, but then directly points out the defect in the proof. He shows that the reasoning collapses into either 'bringing about what is already attained' (tahsil al-hasil) or being unrelated to the persistent aspect. This lesson unpacks that flaw.

Understand why the proof fails

This shows the proof is not even complete, let alone stronger.

Commentary
This passage begins with a heading for the third observation and fifth objective, concerning investigations of the eternal. The author (al-musannif) then states that the detailed answers are found in lengthy works. He then presents his own analysis: the proof that the eternal needs a cause for its persistence has a flaw. The flaw is that if we consider the effect as eternal, then its persistence is already ongoing; positing a cause for its persistence would either be bringing about what is already attained (if the cause is simultaneous) or unrelated to anything new (if the cause is for a renewed aspect). Thus the proof fails. The author then gives a succinct response: either reject the claim that the need for persistence is necessary (since it is contested) or reject the analogy to cause and effect/condition and conditioned, because we do not accept causality or conditionality among created things; all things come directly from the Chosen One. The commentary (the 'qala' sections) explains each part: the first gloss clarifies that the effect's persistence is entire, and the fallacy of 'bringing about what is already attained' only appears when considering a specific time; the second gloss identifies the reference to the editor's clarification; the third explains that the necessity claim is disputed because the argument concerns the eternal's need for persistence, not need in general; the fourth links the prevention of the premise to preventing its proof; the fifth specifies 'interactions among things' means possible existents, and analogies like hand and ring are insufficient because they only show concomitance, not causation.

The Responses: Denying Necessity and Rejecting Causality
Sanusi presents two detailed responses: denying the claim of necessity and rejecting causality among things.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's two main responses to the proof: denying the claim of necessity and rejecting the analogy to cause-effect and condition-conditioned.

Core Idea
Sanusi refutes the proof by challenging its necessity claim and dismissing causality among contingent things.

Having identified the flaw, Sanusi now provides the detailed answers. He first addresses the claim that the need for persistence is known by necessity, arguing that such a claim is not acceptable in a disputed matter. He then rejects the analogy to cause and effect or condition and conditioned, because according to the Ash'ari school, all things are created directly by the Chosen One without inherent causal links.
