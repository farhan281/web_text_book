The Absolute Making of Essences and the Mu'tazila Position
The author presents a second view on essence being made and critiques the Mu'tazila position.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the claim that all essences are made in a general sense and the Mu'tazila's position on fixed non-existent essences.

Core Idea
Even non-existent possibles are not independent of God, except according to the Mu'tazila.

In this passage, the author continues the discussion on whether essences are 'made' (مجعولة). Some theologians held that all essences, whether composite or simple, are made in the sense that they need a cause either for their essence or for their existence. The author then clarifies that no rational person would deny that possible essences depend on an efficient cause for their subsistence in the external world, except for the Mu'tazila who claim that non-existent possibles have fixed essences independent of the cause. This lesson explores this key theological difference.

Guided Path
1
Understand the absolute view
Read the first part of the passage where the author reports some saying that essences are made absolutely, whether composite or simple. Notice that they intend 'being made' in a general sense, meaning that need occurs to the essence.
This view includes both the need for a cause for the essence itself (like a part) and for existence.
2
Examine the clarification
The author then says that a rational person has not claimed that possible essences are independent of the efficient cause for their determination in the external world. This clarifies that denying that essences are made does not imply absolute independence.
3
Identify the Mu'tazila's claim
The only exception is the Mu'tazila, who are reported to believe that non-existent possibles have fixed essences that subsist in themselves without any effect from the cause, and the cause only affects their acquiring existence.
This is a significant deviation from the majority view.
4
Reflect on implications
Consider how the Mu'tazila view affects the understanding of divine causation. If essences are fixed without God, this challenges the doctrine that God is the creator of all things.

Commentary
This passage presents a second position: some thinkers hold that essences are made (مجعولة) absolutely, whether composite or simple. They mean that being made in a general sense — the occurrence of need — applies to all essences, whether the need pertains to the essence itself (like needing a part) or to its existence, and whether the need is for an efficient cause or a constitutive part. The author affirms this as true. Then the passage addresses a possible misunderstanding: no rational person, when negating that essences are made, intends that possible essences are completely independent of the efficient cause for their subsistence in the external world. The only exception is the Mu'tazila, who are reported to hold that non-existent possibles (المعدومات الممكنة) have fixed essences (ذوات متقررة) subsisting in themselves without any effect from the cause, and the cause only affects their acquiring existence. This is a key point of difference.

The Christian Trinity and Its Refutation
Defines the Christian aqanim and explains the reason for disbelief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Christians are considered disbelievers despite affirming multiple eternal attributes, based on the distinction between attributes and separate essences.

Core Idea
The Christian Trinity is disbelief because its three are treated as separate eternal beings, not attributes of one God.

Sanusi clarifies the Christian doctrine of the Trinity and then addresses a critical objection: why are Christians considered disbelievers when Muslims also affirm multiple eternal attributes? The answer lies in the nature of those things—attributes vs. separate entities. Let's read the passage.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of aqanim
First, read the Arabic passage and its translation to understand what the Christians mean by aqanim. Note their three components: knowledge, existence, life.
Al-Jawhari notes the term is likely Roman in origin.
2
Identify the objection
The passage raises an objection: if Muslims affirm many eternal attributes (power, will, etc.), why are Christians singled out?
3
Follow the first part of the answer
The answer explains that Christians affirm the aqanim as separate essences (dhat), not mere attributes. This is proven by the concept of transfer (intiqal) to Jesus, which only an independent essence can do.
Consensus (ijma) holds that multiple eternal essences are disbelief.
4
See the Quranic proof
The passage cites Quran 5:73 to show that Christians are condemned for affirming three gods, not multiple attributes of one God.
The verse ends with 'and there is no god except one God.'

Objections on Literal Transfer of the Hypostasis
Objection and response regarding literal transfer and its conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objections raised against the condition that transfer requires an independent essence, and how the concept of manifestation challenges this condition.

Core Idea
Transfer being literal is not necessary; manifestation suffices, and even then kufr is not automatic.

In this lesson, we examine a key objection in the discussion of the hypostasis (aqnum) transfer. Some argued that for transfer to occur, the transferred entity must be an independent essence. But the response shows that 'transfer' can be interpreted as manifestation, and even if it were literal, the implication of kufr is not the same as the voluntary commitment to kufr.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the structure: an objection is raised, then two responses are given.
The first response reinterprets 'transfer' as manifestation; the second distinguishes between implication and voluntary commitment.
2
Identify the objection
The objection claims that literal transfer requires an independent essence, and that this necessity makes belief in transfer equivalent to voluntarily committing kufr.
3
Understand the responses
The first response: if 'transfer' means manifestation, the condition of essence is not needed. The second response: even if literal transfer implies kufr, that implication is not the same as voluntary commitment, especially when there is doubt (shubha).
Some scholars allow transfer in accidents, so the implication does not automatically necessitate takfir.
4
Reflect on the nuance
Consider why the author makes these distinctions. This shows careful reasoning in kalam: not every necessary implication leads to takfir if there is a valid excuse like doubt.

قوله : ( والمستقل بالانتقال) هذا إنما يتم على قولهم بالانتقال حقيقة وأما إذا أريد به الظهور التام والتجلي فلا يتم وأيضاً التزام الكفر كفر لا لزومه وما قيل من أن لزوم الذاتية للانتقال الحقيقي بين فهو بمنزلة الالتزام فممنوع حيث ذهب البعض إلى جواز الانتقال على الأعراض وأن كونه بمنزلة الالتزام لا يوجب التكفير لتحقق الشبهة . قوله : ( دون إثبات الصفات القديمة إلخ) لأنه لا يستلزم إيجابه تعالى، لأن الموجب والمختار قسمان للفاعل، وذاته تعالى ليست بفاعل لصفاته تعالى وإلا يتقدم عليها بالوجود بل مقتضية لها . قوله : ( كما يدل عليه إلخ يعني أن المراد ثالث ثلاثة في الألوهية أي استحقاق العبادة، بدليل قوله تعالى وما من إله إلا إله واحد ﴾ [المائدة : ٧٣] . قوله : ( أي أوصافاً) فسر الصفات بالأوصاف توجيهاً لقوله ثلاثة مع أن الظاهر ثلاث . قوله : ( والمستقل بالانتقال لا يكون إلا ذاتاً وهذا الانحصار ظاهر معلوم لهم، كما أشار إليه بقوله : لأنهم أثبتوها ذوات فلا يرد ما توهم من أن الكفر التزام الكفر لا لزومه، وقد يقال : بعض النصارى لا يقولون بالانتقال، بل بالتعلق أو الإشراق، فالعمدة في تكفيرهم قاطبة هو إثباتهم آلهة ثلاثة، وإنكارهم لنبوة محمد عليه السلام.
His saying: 'And the independent with respect to transfer' – this is valid only on their view of literal transfer. But if what is intended by it is complete manifestation and disclosure, then it is not valid. Also, the obligation of unbelief is unbelief, not its necessary implication. His saying: 'Without affirming eternal attributes, etc.' – because it does not necessitate that He is the agent of His attributes, since the necessitating and the willing are two divisions of the agent, and His Essence is not an agent of His attributes, otherwise it would precede them in existence, but rather it necessitates them. Commentary
This passage is a series of objections and answers. The first objection is about the condition that literal transfer (intiqal) requires the transferred hypostasis to be an independent essence. The answer is that if transfer is interpreted as manifestation (zuhur) rather than literal movement, the argument fails. But even then, the implication of kufr is present, though some argue that the implication does not make one a disbeliever if there is a doubt (shubha). The next objection is about the relation between God and His attributes: affirming multiple eternal attributes does not imply that God is an agent of His attributes, because He necessitates them. The Quranic verse (5:73) is explained to mean that the Christians assert three gods in terms of divinity and worthiness of worship. Then a further clarification: the phrase 'independent with transfer' cannot be anything but an essence, and this is clear. Finally, the decisive point: even if some Christians do not accept literal transfer but rather connection (ta'liq) or illumination (ishraq), the universal reason for declaring them disbelievers is their affirmation of three gods and their denial of Muhammad's prophethood.

The Decisive Reason for Disbelief: Affirmation of Three Gods
Clarification of attributes, Quranic evidence, and the universal reason for declaring disbelief.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Belief in three gods, not the transfer theory, is the universal basis for kufr.

Commentary
This passage is a series of objections and answers. The first objection is about the condition that literal transfer (intiqal) requires the transferred hypostasis to be an independent essence. The answer is that if transfer is interpreted as manifestation (zuhur) rather than literal movement, the argument fails. But even then, the implication of kufr is present, though some argue that the implication does not make one a disbeliever if there is a doubt (shubha). The next objection is about the relation between God and His attributes: affirming multiple eternal attributes does not imply that God is an agent of His attributes, because He necessitates them. The Quranic verse (5:73) is explained to mean that the Christians assert three gods in terms of divinity and worthiness of worship. Then a further clarification: the phrase 'independent with transfer' cannot be anything but an essence, and this is clear. Finally, the decisive point: even if some Christians do not accept literal transfer but rather connection (ta'liq) or illumination (ishraq), the universal reason for declaring them disbelievers is their affirmation of three gods and their denial of Muhammad's prophethood.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The reason for declaring Christians disbelievers is their specific doctrine of transfer.
Correction: The text states that the decisive reason is their affirmation of three gods and denial of Muhammad's prophethood, not transfer theories.
