Refutation of Another World and Response to Philosophical Objections
The author refutes the existence of another world and answers objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two philosophical arguments against the existence of another world and the theological response that refutes them by rejecting their underlying principles.

Core Idea
Refuting another world requires challenging philosophical premises, not just empirical facts.

In this passage, the author presents and then refutes philosophical arguments against the possibility of another world beyond this one. He shows that these arguments rely on assumptions about void, rupture, and natural places that are not accepted in Islamic theology. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the theological response.

Guided Path

Pay attention to the logical structure of each argument.
2
Analyze the theological response

Notice the emphasis on the free Agent and the possibility of void.
3

This helps resolve a key objection in the first argument.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the existence of another world. The author presents two arguments against the possibility of another world. The first argument: it cannot exist in the celestial spheres because they cannot be ruptured or repaired, and because elemental things and Adam's descent already occupy them; nor can it exist in the elemental world because it cannot contain a Garden as wide as the heavens and earth; and metempsychosis is not a valid concept. The second argument: such a world would have its own directions, but being spherical, it would meet this world only at a point, necessitating a void (خلاء) between them, which is impossible. Moreover, elements would have two natural places, leading to contradictory motions or at least inclinations. Also, the container and contained would determine direction prior to the world, requiring a preponderant for equivalence of directions. The answer: these arguments are based on philosophical principles not accepted by theologians, such as impossibility of void, rupture, and denial of a free Agent Who determines directions arbitrarily. The author explains that the arguments about the container and direction only apply to above and below, and that the two worlds within a circumference do not necessitate void. He also corrects the definition of metempsychosis: it is not return of souls to bodies, but attachment to another body in this world. The footnote defines void (خلاء) as imaginary space according to theologians, and notes the dispute between philosophers (impossible) and theologians (possible).

Nothing is Obligatory on God
Sanusi presents the first and principal proof that reward and punishment are not obligatory on God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first proof of the Ash'ari position that reward and punishment are not obligatory on God.

Core Idea
Nothing is obligatory on God — even reward and punishment are acts of grace.

Sanusi begins his argument for divine transcendence by establishing a foundational principle: nothing is inherently obligatory upon God. This principle, already demonstrated in earlier discussions, directly implies that reward for obedience and punishment for disobedience cannot be required of Him. This sets the stage for the Ash'ari conception of divine grace.

The passage is very concise — every word matters.

This claim is the basis for the entire Ash'ari position on divine justice.
3
Reflect on the implications

Second Proof: Obedience Cannot Repay God's Blessings
Sanusi argues that even abundant obedience cannot repay a fraction of God's blessings, so reward must be from God's grace.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why human obedience cannot earn reward from God, and how Sanusi defends divine grace against the objection that hardships require compensation.

Core Idea
No amount of worship can repay a single blessing; reward is sheer grace.

Sanusi presents his second proof that reward is not obligatory for Allah. He argues that even the greatest human obedience falls utterly short of thanking God for His countless blessings. The passage then anticipates and answers the objection that burdensome worship would be pointless without compensation. This lesson unpacks that argument and shows how Sanusi defends divine grace.

Guided Path

This reinforces God's transcendence and generosity.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof that reward is not obligatory. It argues that even the greatest human obedience is insufficient to truly thank God for His countless blessings. Therefore, it is inconceivable that reward could be earned as a right. The proof then anticipates an objection: obedience cannot be considered thanks because it is burdensome and God could have made it easy. The response concedes the framework of moral reason (husn and qubh) but shows that the fact that thanks is obligatory for a favor does not mean the favor was done for that purpose. Similarly, imposing hardship has a purpose (like testing or showing devotion) but that purpose does not entail that compensation is necessary. Finally, even if compensation were needed, God's grace (tafaddul) fulfills that role.

Third Proof: The Problem of Apostasy and Repentance
Sanusi argues that obligatory reward would wrongly benefit the apostate and punish the repentant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why obligatory reward and punishment would lead to the absurd conclusion that an apostate is rewarded and a repentant disbeliever is punished, and why the muwafah objection fails.

Core Idea
Obligatory reward would force God to reward apostates and punish penitents.

Sanusi continues his series of reductio arguments against the idea that reward and punishment are necessary consequences of acts. In this third proof, he shows that if such a deterministic link held, it would produce outcomes that are universally rejected: a lifelong believer who apostatizes at the end would still be rewarded, while a lifelong disbeliever who repents sincerely would be punished. This short but powerful argument also refutes the common objection that dying in a state of obedience/disobedience is a condition for desert.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage aloud
Read the Arabic text once to get a feel for its rhetorical flow. Notice the conditional structure 'if... then...' and the concluding 'لا يقال' (it cannot be said).
2
Identify the logical structure
Break down the argument into: (1) the premise (obligatory reward/punishment), (2) the absurd consequence, (3) the possible objection, and (4) the rebuttal.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
3
Understand the 'muwafah' objection
The objection claims that dying in a certain state is a condition for desert. Sanusi refutes it by showing that the condition and cause cannot coexist, making desert impossible.
The objection is plausible but fails due to temporal misalignment.
4
Reflect on the theological implication
Ask yourself: why does this matter? It shows that reward and punishment are not automatic consequences but depend on God's will and wisdom, preserving divine freedom and justice.
This supports the Ash'ari position of kasb (acquisition) and the idea that actions are not inherently deserving.

The third: If reward and punishment were obligatory by way of desert (istihqaq), and the effect followed the cause, then it would necessarily follow that one who persists his whole life in acts of obedience and then apostatizes – God forbid – at the end of his life would be rewarded, and that one who persists his whole life in disbelief and then renounces and sincerely believes at the end of his life would be punished, due to the realization of obligation and desert. Commentary
This passage presents the third proof, a reductio ad absurdum. If reward and punishment were necessary consequences (mujib) of acts, then a person who was obedient his whole life but apostatized at the end would still be rewarded, and a lifelong disbeliever who repented sincerely at the end would be punished. This is unacceptable by consensus. The objection that the condition (shart) is dying in a state of obedience or disobedience (muwafah) does not help, because it would make desert impossible: the cause (the act) occurs first, but the condition is not present until death, and when the condition is present, the cause is already past. Thus, desert would never be realized at the same time as the condition.

Commentary
This passage presents two Quranic proofs for the superiority of Ali ibn Abi Talib. First, the author explains that in the verse of mubahala (Quran 3:61), the Prophet Muhammad included Ali as his 'self' (nafs). Although the word is plural (أنفسنا), the Prophet specifically took Ali alone from his clan, indicating that Ali is like the Prophet's own soul, which implies his superiority. Second, the verse 'Say: I do not ask you for any reward except love of the relatives' (Quran 42:23) is interpreted by Sa'id ibn Jubayr that the Prophet identified 'the relatives' as Ali, Fatimah, and their sons. Since Allah commands love for them, they must be the best. The author concludes that one whom Allah makes obligatory to love is superior.

Proof of Ali's Superiority from the Verse of Love of Relatives
Author uses the mawaddah verse to prove the superiority of Ali and his family.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quranic verse commanding love for the Prophet's relatives (42:23) indicates the superiority of Ali, Fatima, and their sons.

Core Idea
The Prophet identified the 'relatives' in the Quran as Ali, Fatima, and their sons — making their love obligatory.

The second proof comes from the verse in Surah ash-Shura (42:23) where the Prophet is told to ask for no reward except love for his relatives. When the Companions asked who these relatives were, the Prophet explicitly named Ali, Fatima, and their two sons, Hasan and Husayn. This lesson examines why this commands their superiority.
