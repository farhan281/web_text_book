Refuting the Fall of Obligations for Saints
Refutes the claim of falling obligations for saints.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the claim that perfect saints are exempt from religious duties is false, and how the states of prophetic perfection and saintly absorption clarify the issue.

Core Idea
Spiritual perfection increases responsibility, not diminishes it.

Some have claimed that perfect saints (awliyā') are no longer bound by religious obligations due to their high spiritual state. The author refutes this by pointing to the generality of divine commands and the example of the prophets, who bear the fullest obligations despite their perfection. He also addresses an exceptional case and explains the nature of saintly absorption that may lead to involuntary neglect, all while emphasizing the superiority of the prophets.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation once. Notice the author's argumentative style: he gives general proofs, then addresses a counterexample, then refines the discussion.
2
Identify the main proofs
Find the two proofs the author offers against the falling of obligations: the generality of address (العموم) and the example of the prophets, especially Muhammad.
3
Analyze the exceptional story
Examine the anecdote about the saint who asked for exemption. Note that Allah did not exempt him but removed his reason, making him non-obligated. This shows exemption is not a reward but a removal of capacity.
4
Understand the state of absorption
Learn the concept of istighrāq (absorption) where a saint becomes oblivious to the world and may neglect obligations unintentionally, being like a sleeper. This is not sinful but is a form of 'divine insanity'.
5
Grasp the conclusion on prophetic superiority
See how the discussion proves that prophets are superior to saints because they maintain perfect obedience even in the height of absorption.

As for the falsity of the claim of the falling of command and prohibition, it is due to the generality of the address, and because the most perfect of people in love and sincerity are the prophets, especially the Beloved of Allah, while obligations in their regard are more complete and perfect, to the point that they are rebuked for the slightest lapse, even for leaving the better. Yes, it is narrated from some saint that he asked Allah for exemption from obligations and requested release from the outward forms of worship, and He responded to him by depriving him of reason, which is the basis of obligation, and despite that, he was of high rank as he was. And you are well aware that the gnostic does not tire of worship, nor slacken in obedience, nor ask to descend from the peak of perfection to the depths of deficiency, nor to come down from the ascents of sovereignty to the stations of animals. Rather, perhaps there occurs to him perfect attraction to the world of holiness and absorption in contemplating the Divine Presence, such that he departs from this world and neglects obligations without being sinful thereby, because he is in the ruling of one not obligated, like one asleep. This is due to his inability to observe both matters and consider both sides. Perhaps he asks for the continuation of that state and not returning to the world of appearance. This absent-mindedness is the insanity that may be preferred over some intellects, and those characterized by it are called the 'majānīn al-'uqalā' (wise fools). By this, the superiority of prophets over saints becomes apparent, for they, despite their more perfect absorption and more comprehensive attraction, do not neglect even the slightest act of obedience.

Commentary
This passage refutes the libertine claim that obligations (takālīf) fall away from perfect saints. The author gives two main proofs: (1) The divine commands are general (to all believers); (2) The most perfect in love and sincerity are the prophets, especially Muhammad, yet their obligations are more complete, and they are even rebuked for minor lapses. The author then addresses an exceptional case: a saint who asked to be exempted from obligations was answered by Allah removing his reason, the basis of obligation, but this is not a normal state. True gnostics ('ārif) never tire of worship. However, there is a state of absorption (istighrāq) where a saint becomes oblivious to the world and neglects obligations unintentionally, like a sleeper; this is not sinful but is a form of 'divine insanity' (junūn al-'uqalā'). This state actually demonstrates the superiority of prophets over saints, because prophets maintain perfect obedience even in their absorption.

The Opponent's First Argument: Hardship Without Benefit is Injustice
Al-Taftazani presents and refutes the first opponent argument regarding injustice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the opponent's first argument that imposing hardship without reward is unjust, and the Ash'ari refutation that the benefit can be gratitude or praise.

Core Idea
Hardship without reward is not unjust; it can serve purposes like gratitude.

The opponent argues that if reward were not obligatory, imposing hardship would be unjust. Al-Taftazani presents this argument and then refutes it by offering alternative purposes for hardship. Let's examine the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument and refutation
Read the Arabic passage and translation carefully to understand the opponent's claim and the response.
Notice that the refutation concedes a purpose but denies obligation.
2
Identify the opponent's assumption
The opponent assumes that hardship must be compensated by reward to avoid injustice. Locate this assumption in the text.
3
Analyze the refutation's alternatives
The refutation offers alternative benefits: gratitude for blessings or joy at praise. Consider how these suffice as purposes.
The refutation also mentions that hardship itself could be the basis for obligation.

احتج المخالف بوجوه : الأول - إلزام المشاق بلا منفعة تقابلها - وهي الثواب ـ ظلم ، وبلا مضرة في تركها - وهي العقاب - مستلزم لوجوب النوافل لثبوت المنفعة في فعلها . ورد بعد تسليم لزوم الغرض بأنه يجوز أن يكون الشكر على النعم، أو السرور بالمدح على أداء الواجب ، وأن يكون إيجاب الفعل بناء على أن له وجه وجوب بصفة المشقة، أو جعل شاقاً لغرض آخر . الثاني - عدم وجوبهما يفضي إلى التواني في الطاعات والاجتراء على المعاصي . ورد بأن مجرد جواز الترك مع شمول الوعد والوعيد وكثرة النصوص في الوقوع غير قادح في المقصود . الثالث - لو لم يجبا، لزم الخلف والكذب في إخبار الصادق . ورد بأن الوقوع لا يستلزم الوجوب والاستحقاق من الله تعالى .
The opponent argued with several points: First - Imposing hardships without a corresponding benefit - which is reward - is injustice, and without harm in abandoning them - which is punishment - necessitates the obligation of supererogatory acts because benefit is established in performing them. It was refuted after conceding the necessity of purpose by saying that it could be gratitude for blessings, or joy at praise for performing an obligation, and that making the act obligatory could be based on it having an aspect of obligation due to hardship, or making it difficult for another purpose. Second - Their non-obligation leads to negligence in acts of obedience and audacity toward sins. It was refuted by saying that mere possibility of abandonment, given the inclusiveness of promise and threat and the abundance of texts about occurrence, does not undermine the objective. Third - If they were not obligatory, that would entail breach and lying in the report of the truthful one. It was refuted by saying that occurrence does not necessitate obligation and entitlement from God Almighty.

Commentary
This passage presents three arguments from the opponent (the Mu'tazila and others) who hold that reward and punishment are obligatory upon God, along with their refutations. The first argument states that imposing hardship without a corresponding benefit (reward) is injustice, and without harm in abandoning it (punishment) would necessitate that supererogatory acts become obligatory since they have benefit. The refutation concedes the need for a purpose but offers alternatives: gratitude for blessings, pride in praise for fulfilling obligations, or the act being made obligatory due to hardship itself, or being made difficult for another purpose. The second argument says that if reward and punishment were not obligatory, people would become lazy in obedience and bold in sin. The refutation counters that mere possibility of abandoning obedience, given the clear promise and threat and abundant scriptural evidence of actual occurrence, does not harm the intended objective (of deterrence). The third argument claims that if reward and punishment were not obligatory, it would entail a breach of promise and lying on God's part, since He has promised reward for obedience and threatened punishment for sin. The refutation clarifies that actual occurrence does not imply obligation or entitlement on God's part; God fulfills His promise out of grace, not necessity. Al-Taftazani follows the Ash'ari position that nothing is obligatory upon God.

The Opponent's Second Argument: Non-Obligation Leads to Negligence
Al-Taftazani presents and refutes the second opponent argument regarding deterrence.

The Opponent's Third Argument: Non-Obligation Entails Lying
Al-Taftazani presents and refutes the third opponent argument regarding breach of promise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the opponent's third argument that non-obligatory reward/punishment would imply God is lying, and the refutation that occurrence does not entail obligation.

Core Idea
God's promise can be true without it being obligatory upon Him.

The opponent argues that if reward and punishment were not obligatory, God would be lying because He has promised them. Al-Taftazani refutes by clarifying that actual occurrence does not imply that God is obligated to fulfill his promise; He does so out of grace.

Guided Path

2
Understand the opponent's reasoning
The opponent assumes that if God promises something and it does not occur, that would be lying. But they also assume that non-obligation means non-occurrence.
3
Analyze the refutation's distinction
The refutation separates occurrence from obligation: God's promise will indeed occur, but that does not make it obligatory upon Him in a necessitating sense.
This preserves God's freedom and grace.

The Justice of Punishment and Promise vs. Threat
Al-Taftazani explains justice of punishment and promise-threat distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Ash'ari school considers punishment just without being obligatory on God, and how the distinction between God's promise and threat preserves His grace.

Core Idea
God may pardon the guilty by grace, but He always fulfills His promise of reward.

This passage from al-Taftazani clarifies a key Ash'ari doctrine: God's punishment is just, but not because He is obligated to punish or because the servant deserves it. Rather, justice here means that punishment is fitting and not unjust. The real controversy is about whether God must always fulfill His threat of punishment, or whether He can pardon. Al-Taftazani explains that breaking a promise would be a deficiency, so God must reward the obedient; but breaking a threat is an act of grace, so He may forgive the disobedient.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the rhythm, then read the literal translation. Notice how al-Taftazani contrasts the cases of reward and punishment.
The passage has a clear logical flow from justice to promise/threat distinction.
2
Identify the key positions
Al-Taftazani states two main points: (a) punishment is just without obligation, (b) breaking a promise is impermissible for God but breaking a threat is permissible. Find where each is stated.
He also mentions that some Mu'tazilites agreed on the second point.
3
Understand the meaning of 'entitlement'
Al-Taftazani clarifies that 'not entitled' means reward/punishment is not a binding right. But he affirms that actions are causes for reward/punishment in the sense of natural consequences, as supported by Quran and consensus.
This resolves a potential misunderstanding about causality.
4
Reflect on the misconception
Consider the common misunderstanding: if punishment is not obligatory, perhaps God could be unjust. The passage argues that punishment is 'just' without being obligatory—justice here means appropriateness, not a debt.

If reward were obligatory, a lifelong believer who apostatizes would still be rewarded — an impossible outcome.

Al-Taftazani continues his refutation of obligatory reward and punishment with a conditional proof. He asks us to consider what would necessarily follow if reward were based on strict cause-and-effect entitlement. The result would be absurd — someone who obeys all his life but apostatizes at the end would still be rewarded, and a lifelong disbeliever who sincerely repents would still be punished. This passage presents the logical consequences that make the premise untenable.

Mu'tazila Proof and Ash'ari Concession-Exclusion Analogy
Mu'tazila cite universal threat verses; Ash'ari answers with concession and analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Ash'ari response begins by conceding the generality of threat verses but then uses the existing exceptions (repentant, minor sins) to exclude major sins by analogy.

Core Idea
Threat verses are not absolute; they already exclude repentant and minor sins, so major sins can also be excluded.

The Mu'tazila argue that Qur'anic verses threatening eternal punishment apply universally to all sinners. The Ash'ari response does not deny these verses but instead examines their scope. This lesson covers the first part of the response: conceding generality but showing that exceptions already exist, so the verses cannot be used to prove eternal punishment for all major sinners.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazila proof
Read the Arabic verses cited by the Mu'tazila and their translation. Notice the universal language used in each verse.
Verses include threats of eternal stay (khulūd) in Hell.
2
Study the Ash'ari response
Read the response that begins 'والجواب...' The author first concedes the generality of these formulations (عموم الصيغ).
Concession is a rhetorical strategy to build common ground.
3
Identify the exceptions
The response notes that the verses already exclude two groups: the repentant (التائب) and those with minor sins (صاحب الصغائر). This means the verses are not absolute.
These exceptions are agreed upon (وفاقاً) by both sides.
4
Understand the analogy
The author then argues: if these exceptions exist, why cannot a major sinner also be excluded? The verse's generality is broken, so it does not prove eternal punishment for every sinner.
This is a logical move: if A does not apply to B and C, it may not apply to D either.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed classification and analysis of the various definitions of iman (faith) in the Shari'ah. The author first concludes the linguistic meaning (the last part of a preceding discussion) and then presents the four possible shari'ah definitions based on what iman is considered to be: an act of the heart only, an act of the tongue only, both heart and tongue without limbs, or all three including limbs. He then elaborates each view with the opinions of different sects and scholars.

For the first view (heart only), the majority hold that iman is tasdiq (affirmation) of the Prophet in matters known necessarily from him, while the Shia, Jahm ibn Safwan, and al-Salihi define it as ma'rifah (knowledge). For the second view (tongue only), it is iqrar (confession) but with different conditions: al-Ruqashi requires ma'rifah, al-Qattan requires tasdiq, and the Karramiyya require no condition. Commentary
This passage presents the fourth definition of iman, which includes action of the heart, tongue, and limbs. The author then explains three positions taken by different sects regarding the status of one who abandons action. First, the Khawarij hold that such a person leaves iman and enters kufr. Second, the Mu'tazila hold the intermediate position (al-manzila bayna al-manzilatayn), neither believer nor disbeliever, but they differ on which actions are required: Abu Ali and Abu Hashim say obligatory acts and avoidance of prohibitions; Abu al-Hudhayl and Abd al-Jabbar include all acts of obedience, even supererogatory. The author comments that expelling from iman and denying Paradise due to abandoning a supererogatory act is irrational. Third, the majority of the Salaf, all hadith imams, and many mutakallimun hold that the one who abandons action does not leave iman; rather, he is definitely a believer who will enter Paradise and not be eternally punished. This is reported from Malik, al-Shafi'i, and al-Awza'i. The author notes an apparent difficulty (ishkal) in this third view: if action is part of the definition, how can the absence of a part not negate the whole? The passage ends with this question, leaving it for further discussion.
