Justification of bodily resurrection through its social utility.

Learning Objective

They recognize that the promise of reward and threat of punishment serve a clear purpose in maintaining social order and benefiting the majority, even if it causes suffering to the punished. This pragmatic view sets the stage for the philosophers' diverse doctrines on Paradise and Hell.

Three Types of Soul Deficiencies
Sanusi classifies soul deficiencies and their punishments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three categories of soul deficiencies—non-existential, existential firm, and existential non-firm—and how they determine the type and duration of punishment in the afterlife.

Core Idea
The soul's deficiency type shapes its afterlife fate: firm theoretical ignorance incurs eternal torment.

Sanusi presents a detailed taxonomy of spiritual defects, linking each to a specific consequence in the next life. Before we examine the afterlife, we must grasp how deficiencies are classified. This passage lays the foundation for understanding divine justice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three main categories
Read the passage and find the three types: non-existential (ʿadamī), existential firm (wujūdī rāsikh), and existential non-firm (ghayr rāsikh). Note the example given for non-existential: deficiency of instinct.
2
Connect each category to the theoretical or practical faculty
Observe that each deficiency can apply to either the theoretical or practical faculty. The author gives the example of compound ignorance as a firm opposite in the theoretical faculty.
3
Understand the punishment outcomes
Notice that deficiency of instinct has no punishment, firm opposite in theoretical leads to eternal punishment, and the remaining three (non-firm theoretical, firm and non-firm practical) have temporary punishment varying in quality and duration. Consider the clause about the empty soul being in God's mercy.
This empty state refers to a soul devoid of perfection, desire for it, and its opposite.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the deficiencies of the soul and their corresponding punishments in the afterlife. The author begins by stating that there are three types of deficiencies: (1) non-existential (ʿadamī), such as a lack of natural instinct; (2) existential firm (wujūdī rāsikh), meaning a firmly rooted opposing quality; (3) existential non-firm (ghayr rāsikh). Finally, the author notes that some do not reject bodily resurrection because it serves a useful purpose for exhortation, even if it is harmful for the punished.

Transmigration and Bodily Resurrection
Sanusi discusses transmigration and bodily resurrection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the theories of transmigration and attachment to other bodies as alternatives to bodily resurrection, and the rationale for affirming bodily resurrection.

Core Idea
Some thinkers propose the soul never ceases perception by attaching to another body, but bodily resurrection remains doctrinally sound.

Having discussed the fate of deficient souls, Sanusi now addresses a philosophical challenge: if the soul is always perceiving, it cannot be inactive after death. This leads to theories of transmigration and use of celestial bodies. The passage concludes by affirming the benefit of bodily resurrection for obedience and warning.

Read the objection

Understand the two possibilities

The second possibility does not require a new soul to flow into the body.
3
Identify the type of body
The body used is not a mixture (mizāj) but a celestial or aerial body.
This avoids the need for a new soul generation.
4
Grasp the rationale for bodily resurrection
The author notes that some do not find bodily resurrection far-fetched because it serves the purpose of reward and punishment for the majority, even if harmful for the punished.
This is a concession that resurrection has practical benefits.

Philosophers' Interpretation of Paradise and Hell
Author expounds the philosophers' view on paradise, hell, reward, and punishment as intellectual states.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the philosophers (al-ḥukamā') reinterpret paradise, hell, reward, and punishment as intellectual pleasures and pains, and why they consider scriptural descriptions as metaphors.

Core Idea
The philosophers see paradise and hell as intellectual states, not physical places.

The philosophers (al-ḥukamā') offered a rational interpretation of the afterlife that differs from the literal scriptural view. This passage expounds their doctrine, distinguishing between those who affirm a world of images and the majority who reduce everything to intellectual states. Let's explore how they reconcile revelation with reason.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to get the overall argument. Notice the distinction between two groups of philosophers.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the two groups
The passage mentions two groups: those who affirm a world of images (ʿālam al-mithāl) and the majority (al-aktharūn) who interpret paradise and hell as intellectual pleasures and pains.
Both groups affirm an afterlife, but differ on its nature.
3
Understand the majority view
The majority view holds that human souls are eternal and experience either delight in perfections (felicity) or pain from loss of perfections and corrupted beliefs (misery). This is their concept of paradise and hell.
They consider souls to be eternal, whether pre-eternal or not.
4
Grasp the reason for unawareness
The passage explains that we are unaware of these intellectual states in this world because we are absorbed in managing the body and the turbidities of the natural world.
Awareness returns after death.
5

The author concludes that scriptural details of reward and punishment are metaphors for states of felicity and misery. Eternal misery is specifically compound firm ignorance, not simple ignorance.
This rationalizes revelation within a philosophical framework.

Soul deficiencies are classified by cause and faculty, determining eternal fate.

This passage from Sharh al-Maqasid presents a detailed taxonomy of defects that prevent the soul from achieving its perfection. By examining whether the defect is due to a lack or an opposing quality, and whether it affects the theoretical or practical faculty, the author identifies six distinct categories, each with its own consequence after death. This classification is crucial for understanding Islamic eschatology and the nature of the soul.

Objection and Answer on the Condition of Muwafaat
Author refutes objection on muwafaat condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the condition of dying in a state of obedience (al-muwafaat) for reward leads to a logical impossibility, as argued by the author.

Core Idea
A condition that can never be met when the cause is present makes entitlement impossible.

The author now addresses a key objection from those who hold that final state (muwafaat) is a condition for reward. If true, entitlement would never occur because the cause and condition cannot coexist. Let's see the argument closely.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice the structure: a claim, an objection, and a reply.
The objection is introduced by 'لا يقال' and the answer by 'لأنا نقول'.
2
Identify the objection
Find what the objector claims: that dying in obedience or disobedience could be a condition for reward/punishment according to the principle of muwafaat.
The objector is likely a Mu'tazilite.
3
Understand the author's reply
The author argues that if muwafaat were a condition, entitlement would never be realized because when the cause (the act) is present, the condition (state at death) is absent; when the condition is present, the cause has already ended.
This is a temporal mismatch argument.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection-and-answer pair. The author first asserts that faith at the end of life necessarily entails obligation and entitlement to reward/punishment, and that the opposite (the necessary consequence being false) is agreed upon. The objector then proposes that perhaps dying in a state of obedience or disobedience is a condition for entitlement, according to the Mu'tazilite principle of al-muwafaat (i.e., that one's final state determines desert). The author replies that if this were the case, entitlement would never occur: because when the cause (the act of obedience/disobedience) is present, the condition (the state at death) is not yet known, and when the condition is fulfilled, the cause has already passed away. Thus, the condition cannot be coherently applied.

Five Proofs for the Non-Eternity of Grave Sinners
Ashʿarī enumeration of five proofs for non-eternity of grave sinners.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the five arguments the Ashʿarīs use to prove that grave sinning believers will not remain eternally in Hell.

Core Idea
The five proofs show that divine justice, scriptural promises, and proportionality require eventual salvation for believers.

The Ashʿarīs offer five proofs to argue that even Muslims who commit grave sins will eventually be admitted to Paradise. These proofs draw on scriptural texts, the principle of justice, and the finitude of sin. In this lesson, we examine each proof and its logical foundation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get a sense of the five proofs. Then compare with the translation to ensure comprehension.
Notice the concise style: each proof is introduced with 'الأول', 'الثاني', etc.
2
Analyze the first proof
Understand that the first proof relies on scriptural texts promising believers entry into Paradise, and that they enter either after Hell or directly, implying eventual entry.
The phrase 'وفاقاً' means unanimously.
3
Analyze the second proof
The second proof cites texts that explicitly state believers will be taken out of Hell. This directly supports non-eternity.
4
Examine the third proof
This is a reductio ad absurdum: if a person who obeyed for a hundred years then drank wine were to be eternally punished, that would be unjust. Since even the Muʿtazila would not call it unjust (they consider it just), the Ashʿarī argue that true justice requires proportional punishment, so eternal punishment is disproportionate.
Common misunderstanding: this proof does not condone sin but highlights proportionality.
5

The fourth proof states that since sin is finite in time and degree, its punishment must also be finite for justice. The fifth argues that the believer's desert of reward is not nullified by grave sin, and reward can only be realized by exiting Hell.
The fifth proof references later discussion ('لما سيأتي').

Commentary
This passage lists the five proofs (wujūh) that the Ashʿarīs use to argue that grave sinning believers will not remain eternally in Hell. The third proof is a reductio ad absurdum: if someone who obeyed for a hundred years but then drank wine were to be eternally punished, that would be unjust; but since even the Muʿtazila would not call such eternal punishment unjust (they consider it just), the implication is that it is not unjust—but Ashʿarīs argue that true justice requires proportional punishment, so eternal punishment for a finite sin is disproportionate. The fourth proof states that since sin is finite in time and degree, its punishment must also be finite to be just. The fifth proof argues that the believer's desert of reward, whether by divine promise or rational consideration, is not nullified by grave sin (this will be explained later); the only way such reward can be realized is by eventual exit from Hell. Thus the believer must eventually be saved.

Responding to Scriptural Proofs for Eternal Punishment
Responds to Quranic verses used to argue eternal punishment for grave sinners.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how classical scholars reconcile Quranic verses that appear to threaten eternal punishment for grave sinners by interpreting them in context and qualifying their application.

Core Idea
Quranic threats of eternal fire are not absolute but qualified by repentance, minor sins, and context.

This lesson presents a detailed response to a key scriptural objection raised by those who argue that grave sinners (fasiq) will suffer eternal punishment. The author cites the problematic verses and then systematically addresses each point, offering interpretations that reconcile the verses with the Sunni position that no believing sinner will remain in Hell forever. By following this argument, you will see how the same verses can be understood differently when read holistically.

Guided Path
1
Read the cited verses
First, read the two Quranic verses (4:14 and 2:81) that threaten eternal punishment. Notice the general wording. Then read the translation to understand the apparent threat.
2
Follow the first move: conceding but excluding
The author concedes the generality of the wording but immediately notes that the repentant and those with minor sins have already been excluded from the first verse. This breaks the decisiveness.
3
Examine the nuanced interpretations
Go through each subsequent point: the condition of seeing punishment, the distinction between deserving and actual occurrence, the interpretation of 'khulud' as a long stay, and the restriction of 'fasiq' to disbelievers. See how each interpretation weakens the proof.
Pay attention to the method of reconciling conflicting evidences (jam' bayn al-adilla).
4
Synthesize the response
Reflect on how the author uses multiple interpretations to show that the verses do not conclusively prove eternal punishment for grave sinners. Consider the overall strategy.

Third Objection: Punishment of the Kafir by Analogy with the Fasiq
Objection and answer regarding the cessation of punishment for the kafir by analogy with the fasiq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the analogy between the fasiq and the kafir regarding the cessation of punishment fails, and the three reasons given by the theologian in response.

Core Idea
Punishment for disbelief does not end because disbelief is infinite and analogy cannot override revelation.

This passage presents a third objection against the view that the punishment of a sinful believer (fasiq) is temporary. The objector argues by analogy that if the fasiq's punishment ends because his sin is finite, then the kafir's punishment should also end. The theologian responds with a three-pronged refutation, denying the causal link, the finitude of disbelief, and the validity of analogy when contradicted by revealed texts.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic and translation of the objection. Note the logical structure: the objector uses analogy (qiyas) based on a common factor (jami') — the finitude of sin.
The objection is concise but contains a full qiyas structure.
2
Identify the three parts of the answer
The answer consists of three denials (man'). List them: (1) denial that finitude is the cause, (2) denial that disbelief is finite in quantity, (3) denial that analogy is valid when there is a revealed text.
Each denial targets a different assumption of the analogy.
3
Understand the special status of beliefs (i'tiqadat)
The passage ends with 'Wa fi al-i'tiqadat' — in matters of belief, analogy may not apply. This is a methodological point about the domain of rational analogy.
This connects to broader discussions in kalam about the sources of theological knowledge.
4
Engage with the checkpoint
Answer the multiple-choice question to test your understanding of why the analogy is rejected.

Commentary
This is another objection-answer pair. The objection uses analogy (qiyas): if the fasiq's punishment ends because his sin is finite, then the kafir's punishment should also end because his sin (disbelief) is also finite. The answer denies three points: (1) that finitude is the cause of punishment ending (it is not automatic), (2) that disbelief is quantitatively finite (it can be considered infinite in gravity), and (3) that analogy is valid when there is a revealed text (nass) that contradicts it. Additionally, in matters of belief (i'tiqadat), analogy may not apply.

Consensus on Eternity and the Philosophical Objection
Consensus statement, then objection-and-answer about bodily finitude.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic consensus on the eternal duration of Paradise and Hell, and how the objection based on finite bodily powers is answered by appealing to God's omnipotence and the possibility of recreation.

Core Idea
Eternity of reward and punishment depends on God's power, not on natural durability.

This passage presents the Muslim consensus on the eternal duration of both Paradise and Hell. It then anticipates a philosophical objection: how can a finite body endure eternal punishment? The answer is not to argue for the body's natural endurance, but to point to God's power to recreate and sustain.

3

The verse is from Surah an-Nisa (4:56) about replacing skins.

Reflect on theological implications
Consider why this answer is important: it grounds eschatology in God's power rather than philosophical assumptions about nature.
This shows the priority of revelation and divine will over rational speculation.

Sincere search does not excuse disbelief; the texts and consensus hold firm.

The author firmly rejects the opinion of Jahiz and Anbari, using Qur'anic verses and consensus to argue that such a person is still accountable. Let's examine the reasoning.

The fate of children of polytheists is debated among scholars based on divine justice and foreknowledge.

This passage from Sharh al-Maqasid presents three different positions regarding children of polytheists who die before maturity. The majority view, a Mu'tazilite view, and a third view based on divine foreknowledge are each supported by scriptural evidence. Fourth Proof: Finite Punishment for Finite Sin

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth proof that punishment for sins other than kufr must be finite, based on the finitude of sin in time and degree, and how the author refutes the objection about eternal punishment.

Core Idea
Sin is finite in time and degree; its punishment must be finite, unlike kufr which is infinite in degree.

This lesson examines the fourth proof in the text, which argues for proportional justice: finite sin deserves finite punishment. The author distinguishes sin from disbelief (kufr) and addresses a potential objection regarding eternal punishment. You'll see how the argument relies on a clear rational principle and a careful analysis of degrees of punishment.

Guided Path

Read the passage carefully

The passage is dense; take your time.

Identify the two aspects of finitude
The author states that sin is finite in time and in degree. Understand how each aspect supports the conclusion that punishment must be finite.
Finitude in degree means there exist worse sins; thus the sin is not the worst possible.
3
Understand the distinction from kufr
Note how kufr is different: it is infinite in degree even if finite in time. That justifies eternal punishment for kufr but not for lesser sins.
This distinction is key to the proof.
4
Examine the objection and response
The author considers the objection that eternal punishment is the most severe and thus only for the worst crime (kufr). The response: punishment levels can vary in intensity even if duration is the same.
This shows that eternal punishment for sins could be less intense than for kufr.

Refutation of the Mu'tazila's First Proof
Al-Taftazani refutes the Mu'tazila's first proof by challenging generality, certainty, and the necessity of occurrence.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
General verses do not yield certainty; eternal desert does not guarantee eternal punishment.

After presenting the Mu'tazila's proof, al-Taftazani systematically refutes it. He concedes that the verses are general in form but argues that they are not universally applicable. He further distinguishes between desert for punishment and actual occurrence, opening the door for divine forgiveness and the possibility that forgiveness may be granted even for major sins.

The refutation shows that the Mu'tazila's proof does not decisively establish eternal punishment for major sinners. The door remains open for divine mercy.

Commentary

Overall, the argument denies that these verses prove eternal punishment for believers, maintaining that general verses allow exceptions and that the evidence for forgiveness remains.
