Understanding General Negation in the Quranic Verse on the Unseen
Explains the general negation in the Quranic verse about knowledge of the unseen.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quranic verse 'He does not inform anyone of His unseen' is interpreted as a general negation that does not preclude prophetic knowledge via revelation, and the linguistic principle behind it.

Core Idea
The verse does not absolutely deny all knowledge of the unseen; it allows exceptions like prophetic revelation.

The Quranic verse 'He does not inform anyone of His unseen' (Surah al-Jinn 26) might seem to absolutely forbid any knowledge of the unseen. However, Islamic scholars interpret it as a general negation that does not preclude certain types of knowledge, such as that granted to prophets through revelation. This lesson explores the linguistic principle that supports this interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then read the literal translation to understand the overall argument.
Pay attention to the technical terms like 'اسم الجنس المضاف'.
2
Understand the linguistic principle
The principle is that the generic noun in construct state (اسم الجنس المضاف) functions like the definite article, indicating generality.
3
Analyze the negation of generality
The verse 'لا يطلع على كل غيبه أحداً' negates informing everyone of all His unseen, but it does not negate all types of informing.
This allows for the possibility of some knowledge via revelation.
4
Recognize the implication
The verse does not conflict with prophets knowing some unseen through revelation, as stipulated in the text.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
The argument is that the speech is for universal negation, but the author notes that this interpretation is not necessary—it is a claim that must be established.
This caution prevents a rigid absolutist reading.

The author is cautioning against taking the verse as an absolute negation without qualification.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Delayed specification clarifies; it does not cancel.

A common objection suggests that excluding some individuals from a general divine statement using later evidence would be abrogation, which is impossible in divine news because it would imply lying. The author responds by clarifying that such later evidence is actually elucidation (bayan), not abrogation, and provides the scholarly consensus of Shafi'i and early Hanafi jurists.

Commentary
This passage addresses two more aspects. First, the duty is not conditional upon the person being pious (war') and free from the sin he forbids. Commentary
This passage provides detailed rulings on the practical application of hisbah. It begins with examples from a Hanafi work (al-Muhit) showing that scholars of different schools may enjoin each other on matters where they differ, such as eating hyena, omitting basmala, drinking wine made from three ingredients, and marriage without a guardian. Then it emphasizes that the duty is not exclusive to the sinless; a sinner must still forbid evil because the obligation to avoid sin and the obligation to forbid are separate. It reiterates that hisbah is fard kifayah, and if enough people perform it, the rest are absolved, but if someone is appointed, it becomes fard 'ayn. It then distinguishes between matters pertaining to God's rights (where one should not spy) and matters of people's rights. For individuals' rights, like debtor procrastination or neighbor encroachment, one only intervenes if the aggrieved party complains. For general public welfare, like water supply, walls, and care for wayfarers, one must command absolutely. The passage lists specific cases: changing the forms of prayer, adding to the adhan, unqualified persons giving fatwas or teaching, judges obstructing or delaying, imams prolonging prayer excessively. It concludes that hisbah covers not only the strictly obligatory and forbidden, and that the method should be gradual—beginning with gentleness and escalating to harshness and, in extreme cases, killing. Specific examples are given: knee (gentle advice), thigh (harsh rebuke but no beating), private parts (discipline, and if persists, kill).

Hisbah as Communal Obligation and Its Scope
Classification of hisbah as communal obligation and scope conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that hisbah is a communal obligation, and that it differs in scope regarding rights of God (no spying) versus rights of people (with conditions).

Core Idea
Hisbah is fard kifayah; for public rights act immediately, for private rights wait for complaint.

This passage explains the legal nature of hisbah as a communal obligation (fard kifayah) that becomes individual if appointed. It then distinguishes between matters pertaining to God's rights, where one should not spy, and matters of people's rights, which require a complaint in specific cases but must be addressed immediately in general public welfare matters.

Guided Path
1
Understand fard kifayah and fard 'ayn
Read the explanation that hisbah is a communal obligation; if enough people perform it, the rest are absolved. But if someone is appointed, it becomes individually incumbent.
This clarifies who is responsible.
2
Distinguish rights of God vs. rights of people
For rights of God, one commands without spying. For rights of people, it depends: specific cases like debt and neighbor trespass require the owner's complaint; general cases like water supply, walls, care for wayfarers require immediate action.
3
Note the examples of general welfare
The passage lists disruption of water supply, collapse of a wall, and neglect of needy wayfarers as cases where one must command absolutely.
These are public interest matters.
