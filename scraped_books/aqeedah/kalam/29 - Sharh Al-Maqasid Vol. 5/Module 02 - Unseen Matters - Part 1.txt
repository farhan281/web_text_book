Refuting the Objection of Discrepancy in the Quran
The author responds to an objection about Quranic variant readings by defining 'ikhtilaf' and analyzing the logic of the verse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise meaning of 'ikhtilaf' in Quran 4:82 and how the logical analysis of 'law' refutes the objection based on variant readings.

Core Idea
The Quran’s denial of discrepancy is about eloquence, not recitation variants.

The objector points to thousands of variant readings in the Quran, arguing they contradict the verse that denies discrepancy. This lesson unpacks the precise meaning of 'ikhtilaf' in the verse and the logical structure of the argument. You will see how the author uses the conditional 'law' to preserve the proof while addressing the objection.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
First, read the Arabic passage and translation to get the full argument. Notice the objector's claim about variant readings exceeding twelve thousand.
The variant readings refer to qira'at.
2
Understand the definition of 'ikhtilaf'
The author explains that the discrepancy denied in the verse is disparity in levels of eloquence (tafawut fi maratib al-balaghah), not differences in recitation. This is the key to refuting the objection.
3
Analyze the logical role of 'law'
The author clarifies two possible interpretations of 'law' in the verse: one negates the consequence due to the negated condition (default reading), the other infers from the negation of the necessary condition to the negation of the conditioned (as in 21:22). Understand both to see how the proof remains valid.
4
Reflect on the dialectical exchange
Notice how the author counters the objection that the argument is invalid by showing it is based on linguistic usage or logical inference. The passage ends with a reference to a fuller treatment in Talkhis al-Miftah.

The Principle of Possibility and the Ilzam Argument
Al-Taftazani states the claim and gives two proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Taftazani's two foundational arguments for the permissibility of re-creating the non-existent: the principle of possibility (al-imkan) and the compulsion (ilzam) that the return is like the beginning.

Core Idea
The default for all things is possibility until a proof shows otherwise.

Al-Taftazani opens the investigation into re‑creation by establishing that the default rational judgment for any act of God is possibility. He then drives home a powerful ilzam: if the first creation was possible, the second must also be possible because it is the same thing. This lesson unpacks those two arguments and the objection they face.

Guided Path
1
Read the statement of the claim
Start by reading al‑Taftazani's opening sentence: 'It is permissible to re‑create the non‑existent, contrary to the philosophers.' Notice who disagrees and why this is a disputed topic.
The philosophers deny re‑creation absolutely; some Mu'tazila deny it for accidents.
2
Examine the first proof: iqna' (inducement)
The author argues that the original principle (al-asl) is possibility (al‑imkan). Anything not proven necessary or impossible remains possible. The burden of proof falls on the one claiming impossibility.
This is a methodological principle shared even by philosophers.
3
Examine the second proof: ilzam (compulsion)
The return (al‑ma'ad) is exactly the same as the beginning (al‑mabda'). If the first existence was possible, the second cannot be impossible. Times do not affect essential possibility.
This argument targets those who accept the first creation but deny the re‑creation.
4
Consider the objection and the response
An objector might say the thing became impossible due to some necessary condition. Al‑Taftazani responds: either that condition renders it impossible or not; if not, the objection fails. Notice the dialectical style.
The response is cut short in the text but shows the method of engagement.

Commentary
This passage presents the first investigation (al-mabhas al-awwal) of the chapter on al-ma'ad, concerning the permissibility of re-creating the non-existent (i'adat al-ma'dum). The author begins by stating the position that it is permissible, contrary to the philosophers who deny it absolutely, and contrary to some Mu'tazila who restrict the denial to accidents or to non‑persistent accidents like sounds. He then gives two arguments: first, an inducement (iqna') that the default judgment for anything is possibility (al-imkan) until a proof establishes necessity or impossibility; second, a compulsion (ilzam) that the return (al-ma'ad) is like the beginning (al-mabda')—indeed the very same entity—so it cannot be possible at one time and impossible at another, since times have no effect on essential possibility. The author notes a possible claim that the first existence confers an increase of preparedness, citing the verse 'and it is easier for Him'. He then raises a consideration: an objection might be that the thing is impossible due to a necessary concomitant, but the response is that either that concomitant renders it impossible or not; if not, the objection fails. Following this, the author gives a methodological note explaining that many theological discussions appear unrelated to creed but are actually useful for defending doctrines. He delays the discussion of i'adat al-ma'dum to this chapter because it is specifically needed for proving the Resurrection as existence after annihilation. He then reports that the majority of theologians (mutakallimun) affirm permissibility, while philosophers (hukama') deny it. The Mu'tazila are divided: the Basran school allows re‑creation of substances based on persistence of essences in non‑existence, but differs on accidents. One group absolutely denies re‑creation of accidents on the ground that the re‑created thing would be re‑created by a meaning, leading to an infinite regress; some Ash'aris hold this. The majority deny re‑creation of non‑persistent accidents (sounds, wills) due to their time‑specificity, and for persistent accidents they distinguish between what is within human power (which cannot be re‑created by anyone) and what is not (which may be re‑created). The author then returns to his own school's proofs: the iqna' argument based on the principle of possibility (quoting the philosophers' maxim), and the ilzam argument that the return is identical to the beginning. He refutes the objection that 'return' (existence a second time) is more specific than absolute existence, so possibility of the general does not entail possibility of the specific. He also discusses a related point: the non‑existent possible is receptive to existence; the first existence may increase preparedness, making the second existence easier. Finally, he interprets the verse 'and it is easier for Him' as referring to the re‑creation of disintegrated parts (not the re‑creation of something from absolute non‑existence), citing another verse about giving life to bones. Re‑creation is easier for God because the first existence increases preparedness, not because re‑creation is a different kind of act.

Commentary
This passage asserts the foundational principle for proving bodily resurrection (hushr al-ajsad). The author declares that the authoritative proof (al-mu'tamad) is revelation (al-sam'), specifically the religion of Islam, the Quran, and the Prophet Muhammad. He contrasts this with the Mu'tazila, who claim to prove both the reality and rational necessity of bodily resurrection by reason (al-'aql). Their argument is that it is obligatory (wajib) upon God to reward the obedient, punish the disobedient, and provide compensations (a'wad) to those entitled, and this is only possible by returning the individuals identically (bi-a'yanihim). Therefore, bodily resurrection is rationally necessary. The author points out that this argument rests on the Mu'tazila's 'corrupt principle' (asluhum al-fasid) that God is under obligation and that withholding recompense would be injustice (zulm) that cannot come from God. The author hints at possible criticism (imkan al-munaqasha) that the obligatory (reward/punishment) cannot be completed without bodily resurrection and that spiritual resurrection alone is insufficient. The passage ends with 'and they repulse' (wa yadfa'un), suggesting a refutation that likely continues on the next page.

Objection and Answer on Personal Identity in Resurrection
Objection that bodily resurrection rewards/punishes a different person, and the answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the soul and original body parts ensure personal identity across bodily changes in resurrection, resolving the objection that different bodies would mean different persons.

Core Idea
Personal identity in resurrection rests on the soul and original parts, not the exact physical form.

The objection that bodily resurrection would reward or punish a different person is a sophisticated challenge. The answer provides a clear criterion: personal identity is preserved through the soul and the original parts of the body. This passage from Sharh al-Maqasid explains why changes in form do not imply a change in the person.

If it is said: According to this, the one rewarded and the one punished with physical pleasures and pains would be other than the one who performed obedience and committed disobedience. We say: The criterion in that is perception. And it belongs to the soul, even if through instruments, and it remains identical. Likewise, the original parts of the body. For this reason, a person from childhood to old age is said to be the same person even though forms and shapes change, indeed many instruments and organs. And it is not said of one who committed a crime in youth and was punished in old age that it is a punishment for other than the criminal.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection-and-answer regarding the identity of the person in bodily resurrection. The objector argues that if the body that is reconstituted is not identical in every part to the body that performed actions, then the person being rewarded or punished would be a different individual from the one who earned the reward or punishment. The answer begins by establishing that the true criterion for identity is perception (idrak), which belongs to the soul (ruh). The soul remains identical (baqi bi 'aynihi) even if the bodily instruments change. Additionally, the original parts (al-ajza' al-asliyya) of the body persist. The author supports this with an example: a person from infancy to old age is considered the same individual despite changes in appearance and even many organs. Likewise, if someone commits a crime as a youth and is punished as an elder, we do not say the punishment is for a different person. Thus, the identity of the agent is preserved through the soul and the enduring original parts, so the objection fails.
