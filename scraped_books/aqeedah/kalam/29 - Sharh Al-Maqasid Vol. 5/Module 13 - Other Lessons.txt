Classification of the Soul's Loss of Perfection and Its Afterlife Consequences
Classification of soul's loss of perfection and its eternal consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the detailed classification of how the soul loses its perfections—whether by privation or positive opposition—and how the afterlife consequences vary based on the type and rootedness of the loss.

Core Idea
Not all loss of perfection leads to punishment; only ingrained, knowing corruption results in perpetual agony.

This passage from al-Maqasid presents a meticulous taxonomy of the soul's loss of perfection. It distinguishes between privative and positive causes, and within positive causes, between ingrained and non-ingrained defects. By mapping these onto the theoretical and practical faculties, the author shows that the afterlife fate of the soul depends on the nature and depth of its imperfection. This classification is essential for understanding Islamic eschatology and the justice of divine punishment.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the literal translation to grasp the overall structure.
Notice the author uses a very systematic classification language.
2
Identify the three main categories
The passage begins by dividing loss of perfection into privative (deficiency in reason) and positive (opposites to perfection). Positive is further split into ingrained and non-ingrained. Find these three categories.
3
Map each category to the two faculties
Each of the three categories applies to either the theoretical faculty or the practical faculty, yielding six types. The author explicitly says this: 'يصير ستة'. Identify the six types in your mind.
The passage does not list all six separately, but you can infer them from the consequences discussed.
4
Analyze the afterlife consequences
Read the description of consequences for each type. Note which type gets no punishment, which gets perpetual punishment, and which get varying punishment that vanishes after death.
Pay attention to the condition that the soul must have known its perfection to be held accountable.

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed classification of the types of loss of perfection (فوات كمالات) that the soul can suffer. It identifies two primary causes: (1) a privative cause, such as a deficiency in the intellect (نقصان غريزة العقل), and (2) a positive cause, i.e., the existence of things that oppose perfections (الأمور المضادة للكمالات), which can be either ingrained (راسخة) or not ingrained (غير راسخة). Each of these three categories (privation, positive ingrained, positive non-ingrained) applies either to the theoretical faculty (القوة النظرية) or the practical faculty (القوة العملية), yielding six total types. The passage then explains the afterlife consequences: the case of deficiency in both faculties results in no punishment at all (no remedy). The case of an ingrained opposite in the theoretical faculty (like compounded ignorance) results in perpetual punishment (عذاب دائم). The remaining three types—non-ingrained theoretical (e.g., common beliefs, blind imitation), ingrained practical, and non-ingrained practical (e.g., habits and bad character traits)—vanish after death due to lack of rootedness or because they are acquired from actions and temperaments that cease. However, they differ in severity and speed of vanishing, so punishment varies in quantity and quality. This applies when the soul was aware of its perfection and lost it through acquisition of opposites, distraction, or laziness.

The True Nature of Repentance: Regret as Essential
Al-Taftazani defines tawbah and refutes the Mu'tazila's lax conception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that true repentance (tawbah) requires genuine regret and sorrow over past sins, not merely verbal declarations or resolutions.

Core Idea
Repentance without regret is like a wound without healing.

Al-Taftazani begins by correcting a widespread misconception: repentance is not merely starting anew or resolving to avoid sin. He insists on an internal, emotional component—regret and sorrow—as essential. To drive his point home, he invokes al-Ghazali and the story of Prophet David, then critiques the Mu'tazila for diluting repentance to suit their theological system.

The Obligation of Repentance: Revelation and Reason
Al-Taftazani states the obligation of repentance with its two bases and immediacy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that repentance is obligatory by revelation according to the Ash'aris and by reason according to the Mu'tazila, and that both agree on its immediacy.

Core Idea
Repentance is mandatory—by God's command and by rational necessity.

Al-Taftazani affirms the obligation of repentance, showing that the Ash'aris base it on the Quranic command, while the Mu'tazila base it on the rational purpose of repelling harm. He also notes the agreement on its immediate performance.

The Quranic verse is cited for the Ash'ari side.

Commentary
This passage presents the foundational disagreement on the obligation of repentance. The author first states that repentance is obligatory: for the Ash'aris, the basis is revelation (sam'an), citing the Quranic command 'Turn to God all of you.' For the Mu'tazila, the basis is reason ('aqlan), because repentance repels the harm of punishment. Both agree it is obligatory immediately (on al-fawr). Then the author addresses the acceptance (qubul) of repentance: on the Ash'ari side, acceptance is established by a probable proof (dalil dhanni), meaning it is not a matter of necessity but of divine promise; on the Mu'tazila side, acceptance is obligatory upon God, because punishing after repentance would be an injustice (zulm). To support their view, the Mu'tazila argue that if a person who has been wronged offers a sincere apology, the wrongdoer's guilt is dropped by necessity. Also, moral responsibility (taklif) continues, which is a means of exposure to reward, and that is only conceivable if punishment is removed; and the only way to remove punishment is repentance. The author dismisses this as weak (wa du'fuhu zahir), implying that the Ash'ari position—that acceptance is rooted in God's promise, not obligation—is correct.

Commentary
This passage enumerates the conditions for the Imam (ruler) in Sunni political thought. It begins by listing the essential conditions: the Imam must be mukallaf (legally responsible, i.e., adult and sane), free (not a slave), male, and just (adil). The author gives a justification for each: a child or insane person cannot manage affairs; a slave is occupied with his master and is despised; women are deficient in intellect and religion (a position from classical fiqh) and are prevented from public roles; a fasiq (sinner) is untrustworthy in religious commands; a zalim (oppressor) corrupts both religion and world. Then it states that the disbeliever is obviously excluded. Next, the majority of scholars add three further conditions: bravery (shuja'a) to execute punishments and resist enemies; being a mujtahid in usul (theology/law principles) and furu' (positive law) to handle religious matters; and possessing sound political judgment (ra'y) to avoid mismanagement. However, some scholars did not require these because they are rarely found together and one can delegate military affairs to the brave, seek legal opinions from mujtahids, and consult advisors for political matters. Thus the core conditions remain those initially listed, while the additional ones are not universally agreed upon.

The Principle of Negation Implying Affirmation
Explains principle of negation implying affirmation and applies to two hadiths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rhetorical principle where negation of another's superiority actually affirms the superiority of the mentioned person, and how it applies to two hadiths.

Core Idea
Negation of superiority implies affirmation when the default is differentiation.

The author introduces a key principle in understanding certain prophetic statements: when the Prophet negates that someone else is better than a particular person, it is to affirm the superiority of that person. This principle resolves apparent contradictions. Let's examine the reasoning and two examples.

Resolve the famous problem
