The author refutes the argument that the Imam's role as preserver of Sharia precludes error.

Learning Objective

The Imam preserves Sharia through means, not by his essence.

This lesson tackles a common sectarian objection: if the Imam is the preserver of Sharia, how can he err? The Objection of Infallible Transmission Refuted
Sanusi refutes the need for an infallible transmitter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the transmission of Sharia does not require an infallible Imam and how the community's collective protection suffices.

Core Idea
The community's collective transmission is sufficient; no infallible Imam is needed.

Sanusi presents the seventh objection: that Sharia must be transmitted by an infallible Imam. He refutes it by showing that conjecture (ẓann) suffices for non-definitive rulings, and the definitive ones are transmitted by tawātur or the entire community, which is collectively protected from error. He then challenges the Shia to explain how their own Sharia reached them from an Imam who is only a name.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the first part of the passage where the objection is stated: Sharia needs a transmitter, and since tawātur is not available for every ruling, only an infallible Imam can suffice.
2
Study the answer
Read the answer: conjecture is sufficient for some matters, so single reports from trustworthy narrators are acceptable. Definitive matters rely on tawātur or the entire community.
Note the distinction between ẓann (conjecture) and qaṭ‘ī (definitive).
3
Reflect on the rhetorical question
Consider Sanusi's closing question: how did the Shia receive their Sharia from an Imam who is merely a name? This exposes a contradiction in their claim.

Objection Five: Competing Contracts
Presents fifth objection regarding competing allegiance contracts and the Sunni response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni response to the objection that two competing contracts of allegiance lead to a period without an imam.

Core Idea
Competing claims can be resolved by preference or new appointment.

The fifth objection raises a procedural issue: if two groups each give allegiance to different individuals, and the order (sabq) is unknown, then the world would be without an imam because no contract can be validated or invalidated, and no third can be appointed. The Sunni response simply states that one is given preponderance or a third is appointed, and there is no corruption. This lesson unpacks that brief response.

Read the fifth objection

2
Understand the dilemma
The objection says: no way to validate both, invalidate both, determine correct one, or appoint a third. So the time between the contracts would have no imam.
3
Read the response
Response: 'بل يرجح أحدهما أو ينصب ثالث، ولا فساد' — rather, one is given preponderance or a third is appointed, and no corruption.
The response is decisive but brief.
4
Reflect on the method of preponderance
The response does not specify how preponderance is determined, but implies that scholars can use methods of tarjīḥ (giving precedence) based on available evidence. Alternatively, a third person can be appointed.
This shows confidence in the community's ability to resolve disputes.

Commentary
This passage is a classification followed by a proof. The objection argues from the Prophet's general practice: he used to appoint deputies (istikhlaf) even for short absences from Medina, and he never failed to give clear explanation (bayan) for the smallest religious obligations, including matters like relieving oneself. Therefore, it is inconceivable (isti'bad) that he would neglect to appoint a successor for his permanent absence (death) and to clarify what is the most fundamental matter (the imamate). The refutation dismisses this as mere 'improbability' (isti'bad), not a logical proof. Moreover, the author points out that delegating the choice to the community's electors (ahl al-hall wa al-'aqd) is no less likely than delegating it to an infallible group (ta'ifat al-'isma) as the Shia claim. In other words, the argument from practice does not rule out the possibility that the Prophet intentionally left the matter to communal choice. Thus the objection is not decisive.

The First Proof: No Explicit Text Because It Would Be Widely Transmitted

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A clear text on a crucial matter would be mutawatir; its absence proves it never existed.

Learning Objective

قال الإمام الرازي: ومن العجائب أن الكاملين من علماء الشيعة لم يبلغوا كل عصر حد الكثرة فضلاً عن التواتر وأن عوامهم وأوساطهم لا يقدرون أن يفهموا كيفية هذه الدعوى على الوجه المحقق، وأن غلاتهم زعموا أن المسلمين ارتدوا بعد النبي ( صلى الله عليه وسلم ) ولم يبق على الإسلام إلا عدد يسير أقل من العشرة ، فكيف يدعون التواتر في ذلك الطريق . الثاني - روايات وأمارات ربما تفيد باجتماعها القطع بعدم النص، وهي كثيرة جداً كقول العباس لعلي : امدد يدك أبايعك . تقول الناس : هذا رسول الله (صلى الله عليه وسلم) بايع ابن عمه فلا يختلف عليك اثنان. وقول عمر لأبي عبيدة (رضي الله تعالى عنه) : امدد يدك أبايعك. وقول أبي بكر : بايعوا عمر أو أبا عبيدة. وقوله : وددت أني سألت النبي ( ) عن هذا الأمر فيمن هو ، وكنا لا ننازعه وكدخول علي رضي الله تعالى عنه في الشورى، فإنه رضي بإمامة أيهم كان. وكقوله (رضي الله تعالى عنه) لطلحة (رضي الله تعالى عنه : إن أردت بايعتك وكاحتجاجه على معاوية ببيعة الناس له، لا بنص من النبي (صلى الله عليه وسلم) وكقوله حين دعي إلى البيعة : اتركوني والتمسوا غيري، وكمعاضدته أبا بكر وعمر والإشارة عليهما بما هو أصلح حين خرج أبو بكر لقتال العرب وعمر لقتال فارس وكعدم تعرضه لذلك النص في شيء من خطبه ورسائله ومفاخراته ومخاصماته، وعند تأخره عن البيعة، وكإنكار زيد بن علي مع علو رتبته هذا النص، وكذا كثير من سادات أهل البيت. وكتسمية الصحابة أبا بكر مدة حياته بخليفة رسول الله (صلى الله عليه وسلم ) .
Al-Imam al-Razi said: Among the wonders is that the perfect ones among the Shi'a scholars have not reached in every age the limit of multitude, let alone tawatur (mass transmission), and their common people and middle ones are not able to understand how this claim is in a verified manner, and their extremists claimed that the Muslims apostatized after the Prophet (peace be upon him) and there remained upon Islam only a number less than ten, so how do they claim tawatur in that path? Second: Narrations and indications that perhaps together yield certainty of the absence of nass (explicit text), and they are very many, like the statement of al-Abbas to Ali: Extend your hand so I may pledge allegiance to you. The people would say: This is the Messenger of God (peace be upon him) pledging allegiance to his cousin, so two would not differ over you. And the statement of Umar to Abu Ubayda (may God be pleased with him): Extend your hand so I may pledge allegiance to you. And the statement of Abu Bakr: Pledge allegiance to Umar or Abu Ubayda. And his statement: I wish that I had asked the Prophet ( ) about this matter as to whom it belongs, and we would not dispute him. And Ali's (may God be pleased with him) entry into the shura (consultation council), for he was pleased with the imamate of whichever of them it was. And his statement (may God be pleased with him) to Talha (may God be pleased with him): If you wish, I will pledge allegiance to you. And his argument against Muawiya by the people's pledge to him, not by a text from the Prophet (peace be upon him). And his statement when summoned to the pledge: Leave me and seek someone else. And his support of Abu Bakr and Umar and his advice to them in what was more beneficial when Abu Bakr went out to fight the Arabs and Umar to fight Persia. And his not mentioning that text in any of his sermons, letters, boasts, or disputes, and at his delay of the pledge. And Zayd ibn Ali's denial, despite his high rank, of this text, and likewise many of the nobles of the Ahl al-Bayt. And the companions' naming of Abu Bakr throughout his life as the Caliph of the Messenger of God (peace be upon him).

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Shi'a claim of an explicit text (nass) designating Ali as the Prophet's successor. It begins by quoting al-Razi, who points out the improbability of tawatur for such a claim, given the small number of knowledgeable Shi'a and the extremist view that most companions apostatized, which would undermine tawatur. Then it presents the second type of evidence: numerous historical narrations and indications that together yield certainty of the absence of nass. These include al-Abbas offering to pledge allegiance to Ali to prevent disagreement, Umar's offer to Abu Ubayda, Abu Bakr's suggestion of Umar or Abu Ubayda, Ali's own statements and actions such as entering the shura council, offering allegiance to Talha, arguing with Muawiya based on general bay'a rather than nass, and even apologizing for delaying his own pledge. Additionally, Zayd ibn Ali and many other Ahl al-Bayt figures denied the existence of such a text, and the companions consistently called Abu Bakr the 'Caliph of the Messenger of God', all of which contradict the notion of an explicit designation.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That the Shi'a claim of an explicit text is supported by historical consensus.
Correction: Al-Razi provides numerous historical evidences from the actions and words of the Prophet's companions, including Ali himself, that collectively indicate there was no explicit text.

Key Takeaway
The collected historical evidences from the early Muslim community, including Ali's own actions, strongly indicate that there was no explicit text designating Ali as the Prophet's successor.
