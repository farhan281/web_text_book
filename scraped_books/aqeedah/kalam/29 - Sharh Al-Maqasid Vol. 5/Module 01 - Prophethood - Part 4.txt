Prophet Sulayman's Trial: Trust vs. Precautions
The author relates the story of Sulayman's son and defends it as leaving the better option.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Prophet Sulayman's action of hiding his son was not sinful but merely leaving the better option, and how taking precautions is compatible with tawakkul.

Core Idea
Taking precautions does not contradict trust in Allah; leaving the better option is not a sin.

This lesson examines the first story in the second objection against Prophet Sulayman. Critics claimed his hiding his son showed lack of trust in Allah. The author clarifies that such caution is not blameworthy and that the Prophet's repentance was for leaving the more excellent practice, not for sin.

Guided Path
1
Read the Quranic allusion
The passage begins by referencing the Quranic verse 'And we certainly tried Sulayman...' Understand that this verse is the basis of the objection.
The verse is in Surah Sad.
2
Understand the story
The tradition says Sulayman fed his son in a cloud to protect him from devils, but the son died on the throne. Recognize that Sulayman's action was a precaution.
3
Analyze the author's response
The author says this is 'leaving the better option' (ترك الأولى), not a sin. Taking means and precautions is commanded, as the Prophet said: 'Tie it and rely.'
This reconciles human effort with reliance on Allah.
4
Reflect on the principle
Prophets are human and may choose a lesser good, but they do not sin. This helps defend prophethood against exaggerated expectations.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sulayman's request for a kingdom no one else would have was not an act of envy but a request for a miracle or religious purpose.

Core Idea
Sulayman's request was a miracle, not envy.

In this passage, the author addresses a potential objection against the prophet Sulayman: that his request for a unique kingdom stemmed from envy. By unpacking the verse and offering multiple interpretations, the author shows that Sulayman's intention was either to seek a miracle fitting his time, demonstrate the compatibility of great kingship with worship, or safeguard religious integrity. Second objection from consistent precedence in mention and its answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the consistent precedence of angels over prophets in Quranic mention does not necessarily indicate their superiority.

Core Idea
Precedence in mention can be due to other reasons, not superiority.

The second objection points to the consistent pattern in the Quran where angels are mentioned before prophets. The author offers two plausible alternative reasons for this order: precedence in existence and the strength of faith required for belief in the unseen.

The answer shows that alternative explanations are sufficient to undermine the objection.
