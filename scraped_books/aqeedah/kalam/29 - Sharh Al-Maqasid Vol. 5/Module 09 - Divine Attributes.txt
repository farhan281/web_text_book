Angels: Nature and Infallibility
Definition of angels and debate on infallibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic concept of angels, their nature, and the arguments surrounding their infallibility and superiority over prophets.

Core Idea
Angels are honored servants, but their infallibility is debated among scholars.

In this section, the author defines angels as created beings of light, obedient to God, and not gendered. He then presents the key theological debate: Are angels infallible? And are they superior to prophets? The passage provides Quranic evidence for both sides and offers resolutions to the objections raised by those who deny infallibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of angels
Read the first part of the passage where the author describes angels as honored servants, constant in obedience, capable of forms and actions, and without gender.
Note the key attributes: they are living bodies, not male/female.
2
Identify the two debated issues
The author states that the community differed on two matters: 'ismah (infallibility) and superiority over prophets. Understand the two positions.
3
Examine the proofs for infallibility
Read the Quranic verses cited by those who affirm infallibility: verses emphasizing lack of arrogance, fear of God, and constant glorification.
These verses show angels' complete obedience.
4
Consider the objections and responses
Read the three objections: Iblis, the angels' question, and Harut and Marut. Then study the author's responses, especially the clarification that Iblis was jinn, not angel.
This resolves the apparent contradiction.

The Meaning of 'Easier' in Re-creation
Author explains the meaning of 'easier' in re-creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the ease of re-creation is from the side of the recipient's preparedness, not from any change in God's power.

Core Idea
Ease of re-creation is about the recipient's preparedness, not God's power.

An objector questions how re-creation can be 'easier' for God when His power is eternal and equal toward all things. The answer clarifies that 'easier' can refer to the agent or the recipient, and here it refers to the recipient's increased preparedness.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the objector's question carefully. Note the key assumption: God's power is eternal and all objects of power are equal to it.
The objector thinks 'easier' implies a change in God's power.
2
Understand the answer's two aspects
The answer distinguishes between ease from the agent's side (conditions of agency) and ease from the recipient's side (preparedness). Identify which one applies here.
This distinction resolves the objection without attributing change to God.
3
Apply the distinction
Explain in your own words why re-creation is considered easier from the recipient's side. Consider what 'preparedness' means for a non-existent thing.
The recipient's preparedness means the thing was already created once and thus has a disposition.

فإن قيل : ما معنى كون الإعادة أهون على الله تعالى وقدرته قديمة لا تتفاوت المقدورات بالنسبة إليها؟ قلنا : كون الفعل أهون تارة يكون من جهة الفاعل بزيادة شرائط الفاعلية، وتارة من جهة القابل بزيادة استعدادات القبول وهذا هو المراد ههنا، وأما من جهة قدرة الفاعل فالكل على السواء. لا يقال : غاية ما ذكرتم أن المعدوم ممكن الوجود في الزمان الثاني كما في الزمان الأول نظراً إلى ذاته وهو لا ينافي امتناع وجوده لأمر لازم له كامتناع الحكم عليه ، والإشارة إليه على أن الكلام ليس في الوجود، بل في الإعادة التي هي الإيجاد ثانياً لذلك الشيء بعينه، وإمكان الوجود لا يستلزم إمكانها لأنا نقول : لو امتنع المعدوم لأمر لازم له لامتنع وجوده اولاً، كما لو امتنع لذاته. ثم إمكان الوجود مستلزم لإمكان الإيجاد سيما بالنظر إلى قدرة واحدة، على أن المراد بالإعادة ههنا كونه معاداً ، وهو معنى الوجود ثانياً.
If it is said: What is the meaning of re-creation being easier for God Almighty while His power is eternal and objects of power do not differ relative to it? We say: The action being easier is sometimes from the side of the agent due to an increase in conditions of agency, and sometimes from the side of the recipient due to an increase in preparedness for reception, and this is what is meant here. As for the side of the agent's power, all things are equal. It should not be said: The utmost of what you have mentioned is that the non-existent is possible of existence in the second time just as in the first time with respect to its essence, and this does not preclude the impossibility of its existence due to a necessary attribute, like the impossibility of judging it and pointing to it, and that the discussion is not about existence but about re-creation which is bringing into existence a second time that very thing, and the possibility of existence does not entail the possibility of re-creation. For we say: If the non-existent were impossible due to a necessary attribute, its existence would be impossible in the first place, just as if it were impossible in itself. Then the possibility of existence entails the possibility of bringing into existence, especially with respect to a single power, and that what is meant by re-creation here is its being re-created, which is the meaning of existence a second time.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. The objector questions the meaning of re-creation being 'easier' for God, since His power is eternal and all objects of power are equal before it. The answer clarifies that 'easier' can refer to the agent's side (when conditions of agency are fulfilled) or the recipient's side (when there is greater preparedness). Here it refers to the recipient's side. Then a second objection is anticipated: that the above only proves possibility of existence in a second time, but re-creation might still be impossible due to a necessary attribute of the thing. The answer retorts: if something were impossible due to a necessary attribute, it would have been impossible from the beginning. Moreover, possibility of existence entails possibility of being brought into existence, especially by one and the same power. Finally, the term 're-creation' here means being re-created, i.e., existence a second time.

The Possibility of Re-creation is Entailed by Possibility of Existence
Author refutes the objection that re-creation might be impossible due to necessary attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the possibility of existence in the first instance entails the possibility of re-creation, and that re-creation is simply existence a second time.

Core Idea
If existence is possible, re-creation is possible; no necessary attribute can prevent it.

The objector further argues that even if existence is possible in a second time, re-creation might still be impossible due to some necessary attribute of the thing. The answer refutes this by showing that such an attribute would have prevented existence from the start, and that possibility of existence entails possibility of being brought into existence.

Guided Path
1
Read the second objection
Read the objection carefully. It claims that the earlier argument only proves possibility of existence, not re-creation, because re-creation might have additional impossibilities (like the impossibility of judging or pointing to the re-created thing).
The objector distinguishes between existence and re-creation as a separate act.
2
Understand the answer's logic
The answer gives two rebuttals: first, if a necessary attribute made re-creation impossible, it would have made first creation impossible too. Second, possibility of existence entails possibility of being brought into existence, especially by a single power.
The answer also clarifies that 're-creation' means being re-created, i.e., existence a second time.
3
Apply the argument
Explain why the objector's distinction between existence and re-creation is invalid according to the answer. Use the concept of necessary attributes.
Think about what it means for something to be 're-created' as opposed to 'created'.

First Argument: Non-Existence Intervening Between the Thing and Itself
Sanusi presents the first argument and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first argument against re-creation and how its refutation clarifies that non-existence merely intervenes between two times of existence.

Core Idea
Refuting the claim that re-creation implies absurd self-interruption of existence.

The first argument of the deniers claims that re-creation would force non-existence to intervene between a thing and itself, which is self-contradictory. The refutation shows that this is a misunderstanding: the non-existence is between two temporal instances of the same thing, not within the thing itself. Let's examine this exchange.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument and refutation
Read the Arabic passage focusing on the first argument beginning with 'أحدها' and its refutation starting with 'ورد'. Notice the claim and the counterpoint.
The refutation uses the concept of 'time' (زمان) to clarify the intervening non-existence.
2
Understand the logical structure
The argument asserts a logical impossibility. The refutation denies the premise by reinterpreting what 'intervening non-existence' means. Compare the two positions.
The refutation also provides an analogy with existence intervening between two non-existences.
3
Consider the OCR note
The text has a problematic word 'العلمين' which may be a corruption; scholars suggest it might be 'العدمين' or 'الوجودين'. Reflect on how this affects the meaning.
Even with uncertainty, the core refutation remains clear.

قال: والمنكرون منهم من ادعى الضرورة . مكابرة ومنهم من تمسك بوجوه : أحدها ـ أنه لو أعيد لزم تخلل العدم بين الشيء ونفسه وهو باطل بالضرورة ، ورد بالمنع بان حاصله تحمل العدم بين زماني وجوده بعينه، وما ذاك إلا كتخلل الوجود بين العلمين [؟] الشيء بعينه . الثاني - أنه لو جاز إعادته بجميع مشخصاته لجاز إعادة وقته الأول، فيكون مبتدأ من حيث أنه معاد وفيه جمع بين المتقابلين، ومنع بكونه معاداً، إذ هو الموجود في الوقت الثاني ، ورفع للامتياز إذا لم يكن معادا إلا من حيث كونه مبتدأ . ورد بأن الوقت ليس من جملة المشخصات. ولو سلم فالموجود في الوقت الأول إنما يلزم كونه مبتدأ لو لم يكن الوقت معاداً ، أولم يكن هو مسبوقاً بحدوث آخر . وهذا ما يقال : إن المبتدأ هو الواقع أولاً، لا الواقع في زمان أول، والمعاد هو الواقع ثانياً، لا الواقع في زمان ثان . الثالث : أنه لو جاز لجاز أن يوجد ابتداء ما يماثله في الماهية وجميع المشخصات، فيلزم عدم امتياز الاثنين ورد بأن عدم الامتياز في نفس الأمر غير لازم وعند العقل غير مستحيل . الرابع : أن المعدوم لا إشارة إليه ، فلا حكم عليه . ورد بعد تسليم عدم ثبوت المعدوم أن التمييز والثبوت عند العقل كاف لصحة الحكم. كما يقال : المعدوم الممكن يجوز أن يوجد .
He said: Among the deniers, some claimed it is a necessity—a stubbornness—and some clung to arguments. First: If it were re-created, non-existence would intervene between the thing and itself, which is false by necessity. It was refuted by denial, for its gist is the bearing of non-existence between the two times of its existence, and that is nothing but like the intervention of existence between the two [؟] of the thing itself. Second: If it were permissible to re-create it with all its specific attributes, then it would be permissible to re-create its first time, so it would be originated insofar as it is re-created, involving a combination of opposites. It is prevented from being re-created, for it is what exists in the second time, and distinction is removed if it is not re-created except insofar as it is originated. It was refuted that time is not among the specific attributes. Even if granted, what exists in the first time would only entail being originated if the time were not re-created, or if it were not preceded by another occurrence. And this is what is said: The originated is what occurs first, not what occurs in a first time, and the re-created is what occurs second, not what occurs in a second time. Third: If it were permissible, it would be permissible for something similar to it in quiddity and all specific attributes to exist originally, leading to lack of distinction between the two. It was refuted that lack of distinction in reality is not necessary and is not impossible for the intellect. Fourth: The non-existent cannot be pointed to, so no judgment can be made about it. It was refuted, after granting the non-establishment of the non-existent, that distinction and establishment in the intellect are sufficient for the validity of judgment, as it is said: The possible non-existent may exist.
Source pages p. 84

Commentary
This passage lists four arguments used by those who deny the possibility of re-creation (i'adat al-ma'dum), along with their refutations. The first argument claims that re-creation would require non-existence intervening between a thing and itself, which is absurd. The refutation denies this, explaining that it is only non-existence between two times of its existence, analogous to existence intervening between two non-existences. The second argument says that if re-created with all specifics, its first time would also be re-created, mixing originated and re-created. The refutation notes that time is not a specific attribute, and even if it were, the first-time existence is originated, not re-created. The third argument leads to two indistinguishable individuals. The refutation states that non-distinction in reality is not necessary and not impossible. The fourth argument denies judgment about the non-existent since it cannot be pointed to. The refutation holds that mental distinction and affirmation suffice for judgment.

The Intuitive Impossibility of Re-creation
Taftazani asserts intuitive impossibility and notes its denial.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the judgment denying the identity of a recreated thing is considered intuitively necessary by some scholars, but is contested by others.

Core Idea
Intuition alone does not settle the possibility of re-creation.

This lesson opens with Taftazani's assertion that the intellect, when free from bias, sees clearly that a thing recreated cannot be the identical original. He then notes Imam al-Haramayn's approval of this view. Yet, he immediately points out that many rational thinkers have affirmed re-creation, and proof supports them. This sets the stage for the arguments and refutations that follow.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic text and its translation carefully. Notice the confident tone of the initial claim and the immediate counterpoint.
The passage begins with 'He said' referring to Taftazani.
2
Identify the intuitive claim
Identify the statement that the second existent is not identical to the first, and note that it is called 'necessary' (ضروري).
This claim is presented as self-evident to a purified intellect.
3
Recognize the denial
Notice that Taftazani then says 'the refutation is by denial' - meaning this intuitive claim is disputed. Many rational people have affirmed re-creation and proof exists for it.
This prepares the reader for the arguments that follow.

First Argument: Interposition of Non-existence
First argument: re-creation implies non-existence between a thing and itself.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first argument against re-creation: that it would require non-existence to intervene between a thing and itself, which is impossible.

Core Idea
Re-creation would entail a logical absurdity: non-existence between a thing and itself.

After noting that the intuitive claim is disputed, Taftazani presents actual arguments from those who deny re-creation. The first argument is a conditional: if a thing were recreated identically, then non-existence would intervene between the thing and itself. This is claimed to be necessarily false. In the next lesson, we will see how this argument is refuted.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument
Read the short argument: 'If the non-existent were recreated identically, non-existence would intervene between a thing and itself, and the consequent is necessarily false.'
This is a clear conditional: if P then Q, and Q is impossible, so P must be impossible.
2
Analyze the logic
Break down the logic: Premise 1: If re-creation (identical) then non-existence intervenes. Premise 2: Non-existence intervening between a thing and itself is impossible. Conclusion: Re-creation is impossible.
The argument relies on the impossibility of something having non-existence between itself and itself.
3
Contrast with intuitive claim
Notice how this argument provides a rational proof, not just intuition. It attempts to prove impossibility through a logical contradiction.
This is the first of several arguments.

Refutation of the First Argument
Refutation: non-existence intervenes between two times, not between a thing and itself absolutely.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the first argument is refuted: non-existence intervenes between two temporal instances of the same thing, not between the thing and itself in an absolute sense, preserving personal identity.

Core Idea
Non-existence between two times of the same thing does not negate its identity.

The previous lesson presented the argument that re-creation would involve non-existence intervening between a thing and itself, which is impossible. Now Taftazani provides the refutation: the 'intervention' is merely temporal—non-existence between two times of the same existence. This does not compromise the thing's personal identity, just as existence can intervene between two non-existences. The author of al-Mawaqif even considered this argument a misrepresentation of the opposing view.

Guided Path
1
Read the refutation
Read the refutation carefully. It denies the impossibility 'with respect to two times.'
The key phrase is 'بحسب وقتين' (with respect to two times).
2
Understand the temporal distinction
The refutation explains that what actually happens is non-existence between two times of a single existence. The thing's existence at two different times does not destroy its personal identity (اتحاد بالشخص).
Analogy: existence can intervene between two non-existences (like creation from nothing).
3
Note the meta-response
Finally, the passage notes that the author of al-Mawaqif considered this argument a mere exposition of a claim against the common view, and distinguishes between the necessity of the premise and the necessity of the conclusion.
The refutation is not just a denial but a clarification of what 'intervention' means.

Redefining Mubtada' and Mu'ad to Answer the Second Argument
Taftazani presents the second argument regarding time as an individuator and gives a comprehensive response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second argument against re-creation concerning time as an individuator and how Taftazani redefines the terms mubtada' and mu'ad to resolve the contradiction.

Core Idea
Time does not individuate; mubtada' and mu'ad are about order, not clock-time.

The second argument attempts to force a contradiction by claiming re-creation of a thing would require re-creation of its time, leading to a thing being both first and second simultaneously. Taftazani responds by denying time is an individuator and by redefining the key terms so that they refer to the order of occurrence rather than to specific temporal moments. This passage shows a masterful use of conceptual refinement to defend the possibility of re-creation.

Read the passage that presents the second argument. Notice how it claims that re-creation of a thing would require re-creation of its time, leading to absurdity. Note the terms mubtada' and mu'ad.
The argument relies on the assumption that time is an individuator.
2
Identify the key components of the argument
The argument has a premise: time is among the individuators. It then deduces a contradiction. Find these parts.
The argument may be seen as three separate objections.
3
Examine Taftazani's response
Read the answer from 'والجواب - أنا لا نسلم...' Observe the two main moves: denying time as individuator, and redefining mubtada' and mu'ad.

Commentary
This passage presents the second argument against the possibility of re-creation, focusing on time as an individuator. The argument claims that if re-creation were possible, then the original time must also be recreated, leading to a contradiction: the same thing would be both a first occurrence (mubtada') and a repetition (mu'ad) from the same aspect at the same time, which is absurd. The response is twofold. First, Taftazani denies that time is an individuator; our common experience (e.g., recognizing a book from yesterday) shows that time does not determine personal identity. Varying considerations and relations do not affect external unity. Second, even if time were an individuator, the definitions of mubtada' and mu'ad can be refined to avoid the contradiction: mubtada' is what occurs first (in order of happening), not what occurs in the first time; mu'ad is what occurs second, not what occurs in the second time. This redefinition resolves the alleged contradiction because the same entity can be first in one respect and second in another without logical conflict.

Refutation of restricting forgiveness to after repentance

Learning Objective
You will understand the Mu'tazila claim that forgiveness after repentance is obligatory and cannot be conditioned on will, and the Ash'ari response.

Core Idea
Even obligatory acts are done by God's will, but conditioning on will is inappropriate for obligations.

The Mu'tazila argue that since forgiveness after repentance is obligatory upon God (in their view), it cannot be conditioned on His will. Then the author discusses the well-known refutation of the Mu'tazila restriction of forgiveness to after repentance: the Mu'tazila hold that forgiveness after repentance is obligatory, so it cannot be conditioned on will. Finally, the author addresses the problem that conditioning on will suggests mere possibility, not actual occurrence, but the point of the verse is to contrast polytheism (never forgiven) with lesser sins (forgiven for some, not all). Commentary

And the manner of differentiation is that the disobedient [believer] rarely lacks fear of punishment, hope of mercy, and other goods that counter what he committed of sin in following desire, unlike the disbeliever. Also, disbelief is a school (madhhab) and the school is believed forever, and its sanctity cannot be removed at all. Thus his punishment is likewise, unlike sin, which is for the time of desire and passion. As for the one who permits forgiveness rationally, and lying in the warning, either saying that lying is permissible because it entails doing good, or that there is no lying with respect to the future, then with the clear informing of Allah Most High that He does not forgive the disbeliever and will eternally punish him in the Fire, the permissibility of breaking promises and the non-occurrence of the content of this report is possible. And since this is false, it is known that the statement permitting lying in the informing of Allah Most High is absolutely false.

Commentary
This passage presents a logical argument from Imam al-Razi (the 'jawab ilzami'). The author begins by noting that according to the Ash'ari school, Allah's speech is eternal, so it cannot be false; impossibility of falsehood follows from eternality. Then he turns to the Mu'tazila position: they say God's lying is impossible because lying is inherently ugly (qabih). Al-Razi asks: if the lie enables forgiveness (the ultimate good), why call it ugly? He illustrates with a parable: someone announces he will kill Zayd tomorrow unjustly. If he actually kills him, that action is evil (qabih), so the announcement would be false; if he does not kill, his announcement is a lie but the action of refraining is good. The good result only occurs with the lie, so the lie becomes good. The Mu'tazila might reply that lying in God's speech is always ugly regardless of benefit. The author acknowledges this objection but counters that it leads to detrimental consequences: it invalidates the consensus on eternal punishment for disbelievers, because if God could break His promise of punishment, the decisive texts become uncertain. He then elaborates a distinction: the disobedient believer (asi) has mitigating factors like fear and hope, while the disbeliever's disbelief is a permanent conviction; thus eternal punishment is appropriate. Finally, the author concludes that any position allowing either the permissibility of lying or the possibility of khulf (breaking a promise) in God's speech, given clear texts threatening eternal punishment, implies that the fulfilment of the threat is uncertain. Since this is false (the threat is certain), the claim that lying is permissible in God's speech is absolutely false.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the claim that God might break His promise (khulf) to punish disbelievers eternally. The author begins by stating that the certainty of eternal punishment for disbelievers is based on consensus and definitive texts. If khulf were possible, that certainty would be lost except for a small group. He then offers a differentiation between a sinner (disobedient Muslim) and a disbeliever: the sinner still has fear and hope, and his sin is momentary, whereas disbelief is a permanent doctrine with no possibility of expiation. Thus punishment for disbelief must be eternal. The author then addresses those who rationally permit divine forgiveness or lying in threats, arguing that even if one claims that future statements are not lies, the explicit divine text says God will not forgive the disbeliever. Therefore, the possibility of khulf leads to uncertainty in God's report, which is false. Hence, any claim that God could lie in His information is definitively false.
