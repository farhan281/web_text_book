The Appointment of the Imam: An Obligation with Differing Views
Al-Sanūsī lists the different sectarian views on the obligation of appointing an Imam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the different opinions among Islamic sects regarding the obligation of appointing an Imam and the basis of that obligation (revelation or reason).

Core Idea
The obligation of imamate is affirmed by revelation and reason, but sects differ on its source and conditions.

This lesson introduces the topic of imamate, beginning with the key question: is appointing an Imam obligatory? The author, al-Sanūsī, lays out the major positions of the Sunnis, Muʿtazila, Shīʿa, and other sects. Understanding these positions is essential before examining the proofs.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Note the author's systematic listing of opinions.
2
Identify the sects and their positions
List each sect or group mentioned and whether they consider imamate obligatory and by what means (samʿan, ʿaqlan, or neither).
The Najdāt say not obligatory; al-Aṣamm says obligatory only when justice prevails; al-Qūṭī says only when injustice prevails.
3
Reflect on the variety
Think about why there is such diversity of opinion. What does this tell you about the importance of imamate in Islamic thought?
The diversity reflects different methodologies (revelation vs. reason) and historical contexts.

قال : وفيه مباحث لبيان وجوب الإمامة وشروطها وطريق ثبوتها، ونبذ من أحكامها وتعيين الإمام الحق بعد النبي (صلى الله عليه وسلم) وإمامة الأئمة الأربعة وترتيبهم في الأفضلية . قال المبحث الأول - نصب الإمام . واجب على الخلق سمعاً عندنا وعند عامة المعتزلة، وعقلاً عند بعضهم، وعلى الله عند الشيعة (۱) ، وليس بواجب أصلاً عند النجدات، وحال ظهور العدل عند الأصم ، والظلم عند القوطي. لنا وجوه : الأول - الإجماع حتى قدموه على دفن النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم الثاني - أنه لا يتم إلا به ما وجب من إقامة الحدود، وسد الثغور، ونحو ذلك مما يتعلق بحفظ النظام. الثالث ـ أن فيه جلب منافع . ودفع مضار لا تحصى ، وذلك واجب إجماعاً . فإن قيل : ويتضمن مضاراً أيضاً، قلنا : لا يعبأ بها لقلتها، فإن قيل : فالأئمة بعد المهديين على الضلالة . قلنا ضرورة فلا معصية ولا ضلالة . الرابع - وجوب طاعته ومعرفته بالكتاب والسنة وهو يقتضي وجوب حصوله وذلك نصبه ) . بعد انقراض زمن النبوة واجب علينا سمعاً عند أهل السنة وعامة المعتزلة، وعقلاً عند الجاحظ(۲) ، والخياط (۳) والكعبي، وأبي الحسين البصري . وقالت الشيعة والسبعية وهم قوم من الملاحدة سموا بذلك لأن متقدميهم قالوا : الأئمة تكون سبعة ، وعند السابع وهو محمد بن إسماعيل توقف بعضهم عليه وجاوزه
He said: In it are investigations to explain the obligation of imamate, its conditions, the way of establishing it, some of its rulings, the designation of the true Imam after the Prophet (peace be upon him), and the imamate of the four Imams and their ranking in excellence. He said: The first investigation - Appointment of the Imam. It is obligatory upon the creation by revealed law (samʿan) according to us and the majority of the Muʿtazila, and by reason (ʿaqlan) according to some of them, and it is obligatory upon God according to the Shīʿa, and not obligatory at all according to the Najdāt, and it is obligatory in the case of the appearance of justice according to al-Aṣamm, and in the case of injustice according to al-Qūṭī. We have proofs: First - Consensus (al-ijmāʿ), as they prioritized it over burying the Prophet (peace be upon him). Second - That by which the obligatory acts of implementing prescribed punishments, guarding frontiers, and similar things related to preserving order are only completed through him. Third - It involves bringing innumerable benefits and repelling harms, and that is obligatory by consensus. If it is said: It also involves harms, we say: They are not heeded because of their rarity. If it is said: The Imams after the guided ones are in error, we say: Necessity, so there is no sin nor error. Fourth - The obligation of obeying him and knowing him from the Book and the Sunnah necessitates his existence, and that is his appointment. After the extinction of the time of prophethood, it is obligatory upon us by revealed law according to the Ahl al-Sunna and the majority of the Muʿtazila, and by reason according to al-Jāḥiẓ, al-Khayyāṭ, al-Kaʿbī, and Abū al-Ḥusayn al-Baṣrī. And the Shīʿa and the Sabʿiyya - who are a group of atheists, so called because their predecessors said: The Imams are seven, and at the seventh, who is Muḥammad ibn Ismāʿīl, some stopped and others went beyond him - said it is obligatory by revelation and reason.

Commentary
This passage introduces the topics to be discussed in the section on imamate and then begins the first investigation on the appointment of the Imam. First, the author lists the topics: the obligation of imamate, its conditions, proofs, rulings, the rightful Imam after the Prophet, and the ranking of the first four caliphs. Then he states the views on the obligation of appointing an Imam: it is obligatory by revelation (samʿan) according to the Sunnis and most Muʿtazila, by reason (ʿaqlan) according to some, on God according to the Shīʿa, not obligatory at all for the Najdāt sect, conditional on justice for al-Aṣamm, and conditional on injustice for al-Qūṭī. The author then presents four proofs for its obligation: 1) Consensus, as the Companions prioritized it over burying the Prophet. 2) The necessities of religion like implementing punishments and guarding borders cannot be fulfilled without an Imam. 3) The Imam brings immense benefits and repels harms, which is an obligation by consensus. To an objection that it also involves harms, he replies that they are negligible. To an objection that later Imams are misguided, he says necessity overrides, so no sin or error applies. 4) The obligation to obey the Imam and know him from the Book and Sunnah requires his existence and thus his appointment. Finally, he clarifies the basis of obligation: after prophethood ends, it is obligatory by revelation for Sunnis and most Muʿtazila, by reason for certain Muʿtazila, and the Shīʿa and the Sabʿiyya go beyond.

Correction: In fact, the predominant view among Ahl al-Sunna is that it is obligatory by revelation (samʿan), not merely by reason, and there are various other positions as well.

Four Proofs for the Obligation of the Imam
Al-Sanūsī enumerates four proofs for the obligation of appointing an Imam and responds to two objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four proofs presented by Al-Sanūsī for the obligation of appointing an Imam and the responses to two objections.

Core Idea
The necessity of an Imam is proven by consensus, religious duties, immense benefits, and scriptural obligations.

After stating the differing opinions, the author now presents the arguments that support the obligation of appointing an Imam. These proofs are based on consensus, the need to implement religious laws, the overwhelming benefits, and the obligation to obey and know the Imam. The author also addresses two objections regarding potential harms and the moral state of later Imams.

Guided Path
1
Read the four proofs
Read the Arabic and translation of the four proofs. Identify each one as you read.
2
Analyze the first two proofs
The first proof is consensus (ijmāʿ) shown by the Companions prioritizing it over burial of the Prophet. The second proof is that religious duties like hudūd and guarding frontiers cannot be fulfilled without an Imam.
3
Analyze the last two proofs and objections
The third proof cites general benefits. Note the objections: that it also brings harms, and that later Imams are misguided. Understand the author's responses.
The responses are: harms are negligible, and later Imams are excused by necessity.
4
Reflect on the strength of the arguments
Consider why the author presents these proofs in this order and how they collectively establish the obligation.
They range from the strongest form of evidence (consensus) to practical necessity to scriptural obligation.

The Basis of Obligation: Revelation vs. Reason and the Shi'a/Sab'iyya View
Al-Sanūsī clarifies the basis of obligation after prophethood and mentions the Shīʿa and Sabʿiyya positions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between the obligation by revelation (samʿan) and by reason (ʿaqlan) regarding imamate, and the unique position of the Shīʿa and Sabʿiyya.

Core Idea
After prophethood, imamate is obligatory by revelation for Sunnis, but some Mu'tazila and Shi'a also add rational obligation.

The author concludes the first investigation by clarifying the basis of the obligation after the extinction of prophethood. He distinguishes between mainstream Sunni and Muʿtazilī views and mentions the Shīʿa and the Sabʿiyya, a group with an esoteric doctrine of seven Imams. This lesson will help you understand the different epistemological foundations for imamate.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation. Note the three categories: Sunni/Mu'tazila (sam'an), some Mu'tazila (ʿaqlan), and Shi'a/Sab'iyya (both).
2
Understand 'after the extinction of prophethood'
Why is this phrase important? It limits the discussion to the post-prophetic period.
The obligation only applies after the Prophet's death.
3
Identify the groups
List the groups mentioned: Ahl al-Sunna, majority of Mu'tazila, specific Mu'tazila scholars (al-Jahiz, al-Khayyat, al-Ka'bi, Abu al-Husayn al-Basri), and Shi'a/Sab'iyya.
4
Reflect on the Sab'iyya
Who are the Sab'iyya? They are atheists according to the author, believing in seven Imams. This is a deviation from mainstream Islam.
Their doctrine is rejected by the author.

Objection and Refutation: Is Imamate Obligatory Grace?
Al-Taftazani presents and refutes the claim that imamate is an obligatory divine grace.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the claim that imamate is a divine grace (lutf) that God must provide is refuted by al-Taftazani on several grounds.

Core Idea
Imamate as divine grace is undermined by corruption, sincerity, and divine alternatives.

The argument that imamate is a grace (lutf) that obligates God appears compelling, but al-Taftazani dismantles it. He attacks the very premises of the syllogism and presents multiple reasons why this claim fails. In this lesson, we will follow his refutation step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and identify its structure
The objector argues that imamate is a lutf (grace) that brings knowledge, obedience, and discourages disobedience, making it obligatory upon God. Note the syllogistic form.
The objection is in parentheses; the refutation begins after 'ورد'.
2
Understand the first refutation: denying the premises
The refutation denies both premises of the syllogism because imamate can cause corruptions like differing opinions and refusal to obey equals.
This is the initial refutation 'يمنع مقدمتي القياس'.
3
Examine the second refutation: greater sincerity without imam
Performing acts of obedience and avoiding disobedience without an imam is more difficult and thus closer to sincerity (ikhlas). This undermines the need for imamate as a grace.
Key phrase: 'مع عدم الإمام أشق، وأقرب الى الإخلاص'.
4
Consider the third refutation: a more gracious alternative
If the goal is grace, creating people infallible would be an even greater grace than appointing an imam. This shows the objection's inadequacy.
The argument uses a better option (ألطف) to highlight the flaw.
5
Reflect on the final refutations: pure benefit and love
The claim that imamate is a pure benefit and a grace unobtainable otherwise is rejected. Additionally, if the grace is in the imam's appearance, people do not love him, so the grace fails.
These points further dismantle the objection.

Commentary
This passage defends the condition that the Imam must be from Quraysh (descendants of Nadr ibn Kinana), a position held by the Sunni majority against the Khawarij and most Mu'tazila. The author presents two types of evidence: Sunna and consensus. The Sunna includes three hadith: 'The leaders are from Quraysh,' 'The governors are from Quraysh as long as they obey God,' and 'Put Quraysh forward.' The consensus is drawn from the Saqifa event: the Ansar proposed shared leadership, but Abu Bakr rejected them by noting they were not Quraysh, and no companion objected, establishing consensus. Then the author handles the opponent's objections. The opponent's transmitted proof is the hadith 'Obey even if a mutilated black slave is appointed' – the response is that this applies to lower governors (hukkam), not the supreme Imam. The rational objection argues that lineage is irrelevant for ability – the response is that noble lineage has a powerful effect on unity, obedience, and stability, and this is empirically evident in how rule rests in specific families. The author then provides a practical allowance: if no suitable Qurayshi is available or the situation is dominated by evil forces, then the imamate can be assumed by any person of power (dhu shawka), and religious rulings dependent on the Imam are valid out of necessity. This shows Sunni realism: the ideal conditions are required when possible, but necessity overrides them.

The author answers by denying the premise—the honor of lineages has a great effect on social cohesion, consensus, and obedience. Quraysh are the most honorable, especially as the final message was confined to them and the Sharia spread from them. However, if no suitable Qurayshi exists or cannot be appointed due to the dominance of falsehood, then anyone with power may take on the imam's duties out of necessity, even if lacking ideal conditions. The passage concludes by summarizing that the imama is normally based on choice and ability, but in dire necessity, leadership becomes one of domination and religious rulings are built upon it, disregarding normal conditions because necessities permit prohibitions.

Fourth Objection: Fallibility Leads to Infinite Regress
Presentation and refutation of the fourth objection on Imam's infallibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the objection of infinite regress fails against a fallible Imam, and how the obligation to appoint an Imam is legal, not rational.

Core Idea
The need for an Imam is legal, not rational; fallibility does not necessitate an infallible chain.

This passage presents a key objection from those who argue that the Imam must be infallible, otherwise we would need an infinite chain of Imams. The author refutes this by distinguishing rational from legal obligation, showing that the Imam's knowledge and ijtihad suffice even without infallibility. Let's examine the argument closely.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and its analogy
Read the objection that since the community needs an Imam due to its fallibility, the Imam himself must be infallible, else we get infinite regress—like the chain of contingents ending in the Necessary Being.

2
Analyze the refutation
The author answers that the obligation is legal (sharʿī), not rational. The harm of absence is repelled by the Imam’s knowledge, ijtihad, apparent justice, and good belief—even without infallibility. Also, error is possible even for the infallible, as infallibility does not remove trial.
The key distinction is between rational necessity and legal command.

Reflect on the footnote on tasalsul
The footnote defines infinite regress (tasalsul) as the arrangement of infinite things, listing four types: among coexisting individuals or not (like temporal events).
This clarifies the concept used in the objection.
