Three Arguments Against the Theory of Sarfa
Three arguments refuting the theory of sarfa as the basis of i'jaz.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand three rational arguments that refute the theory of sarfa (divine prevention) as the basis of Quranic inimitability.

Core Idea
The Quran's inimitability lies in its own eloquence, not in Allah preventing others from matching it.

The theory of sarfa suggests that the Quran is inimitable only because Allah prevented people from imitating it. However, this passage presents three powerful arguments that challenge this view by pointing to the Arabs' actual reaction, the logic of the challenge, and the Quran's own verse. Let's examine these arguments closely.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Let the author's own progression through the three arguments come through.
Notice the numbered structure: 'الأول' , 'الثاني' , 'الثالث'.
2
Understand the first argument: Arab reaction
The first argument points to the actual reaction of the eloquent Arabs, who were amazed by the Quran's composition, not by the impossibility of opposing it. Identify why this refutes sarfa.
The key phrase: 'يرقصون رؤوسهم عند سماع قوله تعالى...'
3
Understand the second argument: logical consistency
The second argument uses a conditional logic: if sarfa were true, the Quran could have been less eloquent. But it is supremely eloquent, showing the challenge is based on its inherent quality.
Think about why a lower eloquence would make the prevention more miraculous.
4
Understand the third argument: the Quranic challenge
The third argument cites verse 17:88, which challenges all humans and jinn to produce a like Quran. The wording implies that the impossibility is due to the Quran's own nature, not divine prevention.
Notice the emphasis on 'اجتماع' and 'استظهار'.
5
Reflect on the overall refutation
After understanding each argument, consider how they collectively dismantle the theory of sarfa. The Quran's inimitability is intrinsic, not extrinsic.
This connects to the core idea of the lesson.

Commentary
This passage presents three sequential proofs against the theory of sarfa (the claim that the Quran's inimitability is due to Allah preventing people from matching it, rather than its inherent eloquence). The first argument: The Arabs were astonished by the Quran's eloquence and beauty, as seen in their physical reaction to verses like the one about the earth swallowing water. Their amazement was due to the Quran's own qualities, not because opposition was impossible despite being easy. The second argument: If inimitability were simply by divine prevention, the Quran would not need to be so eloquent; it could be weak and still be inimitable. But the Quran is at the highest level of eloquence, which shows that the challenge is based on its own merit. The third argument: The challenge verse (Quran 17:88) calls on all humans and jinn to produce its like, even with mutual support. This wording is only meaningful if the challenge is to produce something that is individually impossible but might be thought collectively possible. It negates the idea that prevention is the reason.

Objection and Answer on Variation of Eloquence
Objection and answer about variation in Qur'anic eloquence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the variation in eloquence among Qur'anic verses does not undermine the miracle of inimitability, and how the analogy of a craftsman refutes the objection.

Core Idea
Uneven eloquence strengthens the Quran's challenge.

In this passage, the author anticipates a potential objection: if the Quran's inimitability relies on eloquence, why are some verses more eloquent than others? He answers with a striking analogy, showing that the variation actually makes the challenge more compelling. Let's read his argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the first part of the passage (up to 'مثلاً') to understand the objector's point: if inimitability is by eloquence, the whole Quran should be at the highest level.
Notice the phrase 'خرق العادة' (breach of custom) — a key concept in miracles.
2
Understand the counter-doctrine
The author states that Allah is able to bring even more eloquent speech, and some verses are higher in eloquence than others. This sets up the need for an answer.
The verse example (earth swallowing water) is given as an instance of higher eloquence.
3
Analyze the craftsman analogy
Read the answer part. The author compares the situation to a master craftsman who displays works that are not his absolute best, then challenges experts to match even the easiest. This shows how variation actually clarifies the challenge.
The analogy highlights that the challenge is to match the 'easiest' of what was shown, making it more compelling.
4
Reflect on the overall argument
Consider how the answer resolves the objection: the variation in eloquence does not weaken the miracle; rather, it makes the challenge more accessible and undeniable.

Commentary

It is like a master craftsman who shows some of his works that are not his absolute best, and then challenges others to match even those lesser works. This makes the challenge clearer and more compelling, because if even the easiest of his works cannot be matched, the inimitability is proven.

The Arabs' Inability to Fault the Quran
Sanusi recounts the Arabs' reaction to the Quran as proof of inimitability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Arabs' inability to criticize the Quran, despite their linguistic mastery and enmity, serves as evidence for its divine origin.

Core Idea
The Arabs chose to fight the Quran rather than imitate it.

Sanusi presents the historical argument that the Arabs, masters of eloquence and sworn enemies of Islam, could not find a single flaw in the Quran. They acknowledged its unique sweetness and freshness, and instead of attempting to produce something similar, they resorted to armed conflict. This passage establishes the miraculous nature of the Quran through the testimony of its fiercest opponents.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on Arab reaction
Read the Arabic passage describing how the Arab nobles, despite their skill and hatred, could not criticize the Quran. Notice the contrast between their linguistic expertise and their inability to fault it.
The author emphasizes their complete mastery of speech and extreme enmity.
2
Understand their acknowledgment
Identify the qualities they attributed to the Quran: sweetness, freshness, abundant lower parts, fruitful upper parts. They admitted it is not like oratory or poetry.
This admission is significant because they were the experts.
3
Contrast their actions
Note that they chose fighting over literary opposition. This shows they abandoned all hope of producing a rival text.
The phrase 'آثروا المقارعة على المعارضة' is key.
4
Consider the theological implication
Reflect on why this historical fact is used as a proof of the Quran's inimitability (i'jāz). If the most skilled Arabs could not match it, it must be from Allah.

Refuting Later Criticisms of the Quran
Sanusi presents and refutes three later criticisms of the Quran.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how classical scholars responded to three common objections against the Quran: the presence of non-Arabic words, alleged grammatical errors, and an apparent contradiction regarding the story of Moses.

Core Idea
Later criticisms of the Quran are easily refuted by linguistic and contextual analysis.

After the initial Arab opponents failed to fault the Quran, later enemies of Islam invented superficial criticisms. Sanusi addresses three: non-Arabic words, grammatical anomalies, and an apparent contradiction about the length of the story of Moses. Each is refuted with standard linguistic principles, showing that these criticisms are baseless.

4

5
Assess the overall argument
Reflect on how these refutations demonstrate the thoroughness of Islamic scholarship in defending the Quran.

Commentary
This passage is a statement of the issue and a classification of positions. It begins by announcing the topic: proving resurrection by way of existence after annihilation. Commentary

Divine Decree and the Problem of Evil

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Yet it immediately introduces a critical nuance: evil deeds are not by His approval or command. This distinction is essential to preserve divine justice and human responsibility.

The Third Proof: Benefit and Harm Argument
Taftazani presents a syllogistic proof for Imamate based on benefit and harm.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Taftazani's syllogistic argument that appointing an Imam is obligatory because it brings about countless benefits and repels undeniable harms.

Core Idea
A ruler is necessary for society; therefore, appointing one is a religious duty.

This passage presents Taftazani's third proof for the obligation of Imamate. He constructs a syllogism: anything that secures immense benefits and prevents obvious harms is obligatory; appointing an Imam does exactly that; therefore, it is obligatory. The proof relies on observable reality, including historical evidence and even animal behavior, to demonstrate the universal need for a leader.

Guided Path

Read the Arabic text and its translation once to grasp the overall argument. Let the structure of the syllogism become clear before analyzing details.
Notice the use of the term 'نصب الإمام' (appointment of the Imam) as the central subject.

Commentary
This passage contains the third proof for the obligation of appointing an Imam (ruler). The argument is a syllogism: the major premise is that anything which brings about countless benefits and repels obvious harms is obligatory. The minor premise is that appointing an Imam does exactly that. The author argues that the minor premise is almost self-evident and observed in reality. He supports it with the famous saying that a ruler's power restrains more than the Quran (in terms of outward compliance), and that what is achieved through the tongue (persuasion) cannot be organized by rational proof alone. He explains that any society aiming at worldly and religious welfare requires a powerful ruler to prevent corruption and maintain order. This is evident from historical experiences where the death of a protector leads to chaos and trial, even if that ruler was not fully just. The necessity of a leader applies to the smallest groups, like a caravan, and even to animals like bees, which have a leader whose existence maintains order. Thus, the need for a leader is universal, making the appointment of an Imam obligatory for the Muslim community.
