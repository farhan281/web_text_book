Mu'tazila on Restoration of Reward After Repentance
Three Mu'tazila positions on restoration of reward after repentance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three Mu'tazila positions on whether repentance restores the reward of obedience lost due to sin.

Core Idea
Repentance may not automatically restore lost rewards — the Mu'tazila disagreed.

The Mu'tazila system of divine justice requires that sin and repentance have precise effects on reward and punishment. This passage presents three distinct Mu'tazila views on a subtle question: when a person's sin has nullified the reward of a good deed, does repentance bring that reward back? The answers range from absolute denial to partial restoration, each supported by careful analogy.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the three positions
Read the Arabic passage carefully, then the translation. Note the three distinct answers given by different Mu'tazila scholars.
The passage begins by stating the disagreement, then lists three views.
2
Understand the reasoning of Abu Ali and Abu Hashim
They argue that the obedience ceases immediately at the moment of sin, and the entitlement to reward falls away; what has fallen cannot return.
Their analogy is implicit: once something is gone, it is gone.
3
Understand al-Ka'bi's reasoning
Al-Ka'bi argues that the major sin only prevents the effect of obedience (praise and reward), not the obedience itself. When repentance removes the sin, the effect reappears like the sun after clouds.
He uses the analogy of clouds covering the sun.
4
Understand the middle position of later scholars
Later scholars hold that the previous reward does not return, but the obedience itself becomes effective again for producing future reward, like a tree whose branches were burned but whose roots survive.
This is a compromise: the deed is not lost, but its past reward is not restored.
5
Reflect on the underlying assumptions
Consider how each view reflects a different understanding of the nature of deeds, their effects, and how repentance interacts with them.
This shows the sophistication of Mu'tazila reasoning on divine justice.

ثم اختلفت المعتزلة في أنه إذا سقط استحقاق عقاب المعصية بالتوبة، هل يعود استحقاق ثواب الطاعة الذي أبطله تلك المعصية؟ فقال أبو علي وأبو هاشم : لا ، لأن الطاعة تنعدم في الحال. وإنما يبقى استحقاق الثواب، وقد سقط، والساقط لا يعود . وقال الكعبي : نعم لأن الكبيرة لا تزيل الطاعة وإنما تمنع حكمها، وهو المدح والتعظيم، فلا تزيل ثمرتها . فإذا صارت بالتوبة كأن لم تكن، ظهرت ثمرة الطاعة كنور الشمس إذا زال الغيم . وقال بعضهم، وهو اختيار المتأخرين : لا يعود ثوابه السابق، لكن تعود طاعته السالفة مؤثرة في استحقاق ثمراته، وهو المدح والثواب في المستقبل بمنزله شجرة أحرقت النار أغصانها ،وثمارها ثم انطفأت النار، فإنه يعود أصل الشجرة وعروقها إلى خضرتها وثمرتها .
Then the Mu'tazila disagreed about whether, when the entitlement to punishment for a sin falls away through repentance, does the entitlement to reward for obedience, which that sin had nullified, return? Abu Ali and Abu Hashim said: No, because the obedience ceases immediately. Only the entitlement to reward remains, and it has fallen, and what has fallen does not return. Al-Ka'bi said: Yes, because the major sin does not remove obedience; it only prevents its effect, which is praise and glorification, so it does not remove its fruit. When it [the sin] becomes through repentance as if it had not been, the fruit of obedience appears like the light of the sun when the cloud clears. And some said—and it is the choice of the later scholars—: Its previous reward does not return, but its former obedience returns as effective in entitling its fruits, which are praise and reward in the future, like a tree whose branches and fruits were burnt by fire, then the fire went out, so the root and trunk of the tree return to their greenness and fruit.

Commentary
This passage presents the disagreement among the Mu'tazila concerning the effects of repentance on the reward of good deeds that were nullified by sin. The issue is: when a person commits a sin that makes them deserving of punishment, then repents, does the reward for the good deeds they did before the sin return? Abu Ali and Abu Hashim say no, because the good deed ceased to exist at the moment of sin; the entitlement to reward fell away, and what has fallen does not return. Al-Ka'bi disagrees: the major sin does not annihilate the good deed; it only prevents its effect (praise and honour). So when repentance removes the sin, the good deed's effect reappears, like the sun after clouds. The later scholars choose a middle position: the previous reward does not return, but the good deed itself becomes effective again for producing reward in the future, like a tree whose branches and fruits were burned but whose roots survive; when the fire is extinguished, the tree revives and bears fruit again. This shows the sophistication of Mu'tazila reasoning on divine justice.

No Need to Renew Repentance Upon Remembering Sin
Argues that renewal of remorse is not required each time a sin is remembered.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why renewing remorse each time a past sin is remembered is not required, and how the author refutes opposing arguments.

Core Idea
True repentance stands firm; no emotional renewal needed upon memory.

This lesson addresses a subtle question: when a person remembers a sin they have already repented from, must they feel remorse all over again? Some scholars argued yes, because otherwise it indicates continued attachment. The author presents a clear refutation, showing that the original repentance remains valid and that one may simply turn away from the memory without emotional arousal. Let's examine the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the translation to get the flow of the argument.
Notice the concise style of the argument.
2
Identify the two opposing views
The author states his position (no renewal required) and the Mu'tazilite position (renewal required). Find these in the text.
The opponents are al-Qadi, al-Jubba'i, and Abu Ali.
3
Analyze the opponent's proof and author's denial
The opponent argues that lack of remorse upon remembrance implies desire and invalidates repentance. The author denies this, saying one may simply turn away.
The author provides a counterexample: turning away without desire.
4
Consider the additional objection and response
Al-Qadi further argues that not renewing remorse is a new sin. The author responds that past worship is not nullified by later events.
This reinforces the validity of the initial repentance.

قال : ولا يلزم تجديد الندم كلما ذكر الذنب ولا تعميمها). المعصية، لأنه قد أتى بما كلف به، وخرج عن عهدته خلافاً للقاضي [والجبائي؟] وأبي علي من المعتزلة . وشبهتهما أنه لو لم يندم كلما ذكرها، لكان مشتهياً لها، فرحاً بها، وذلك إبطال للندم (١) ، ورجوع إلى الإصرار. والجواب المنع ، إذ ربما يضرب عنها صفحاً من غير ندم عليها، ولا اشتهاء لها وابتهاج .بها ولو كان الأمر كما ذكر لزم أن لا تكون التوبة السابقة صحيحة . وقال القاضي (٢) : إنه إن لم يجدد ندماً كان ذلك معصية جديدة يجب الندم عليها، والتوبة الأولى مضت على صحتها، إذ العبادة الماضية لا ينقضها شيء بعد ثبوتها .
He said: Renewal of remorse is not required every time the sin is remembered, nor is its generality [required]... (because) he has already done what he was obliged to do and has discharged his responsibility, contrary to al-Qadi [and al-Jubba'i?] and Abu Ali from the Mu'tazila. Their pretext is that if he did not feel remorse every time he remembered it, he would be desiring it and rejoicing in it, and that invalidates the remorse and is a return to persistence. The answer is denial, for perhaps he turns away from it without remorse for it, nor desire for it, nor delight in it. And if it were as they said, then it would follow that the previous repentance was not valid. And al-Qadi said: if he does not renew the remorse, that would be a new sin requiring repentance, and the first repentance remains valid, because a past act of worship is not nullified by anything after its establishment.

Commentary
This passage addresses a subtle issue about repentance: When a person has repented from a sin, does he need to renew his remorse every time he remembers that sin? The author argues no. Some scholars like al-Qadi and al-Jubba'i claimed that if one does not feel remorse upon remembrance, it indicates continued desire for the sin, which would nullify the original repentance. The author's answer is that one might simply turn away from the thought without any desire or delight. If the objection were accepted, it would imply that the original repentance was invalid. Then al-Qadi presents a further objection: if remorse is not renewed, the lack of remorse is itself a new sin requiring repentance. But the author maintains the original repentance stands, as past worship is not invalidated by subsequent acts. The core point is that repentance is a single decisive act; its effects continue without need for constant emotional renewal.

Partial Repentance from Some Sins
Argues that repentance can be from some sins while persisting in others, contrary to Abu Hashim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why partial repentance is valid according to the madhhab, and how Abu Hashim's objection is refuted.

Core Idea
Repentance can be partial; remorse for specific sins suffices.

This passage tackles a key question in repentance theology: Must repentance cover all sins, or can it be from some while persisting in others? The author argues for validity, using the consensus on a convert's Islam as a proof and addressing an objection by Abu Hashim.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
Carefully read the Arabic and translation. Notice the opening statement and the examples used.
2
Identify the main claim and proof
The author states that repentance does not need to be general. Find the consensus proof cited.
The proof involves a disbeliever converting while still committing sins.
3
Understand Abu Hashim's objection
Abu Hashim argues that remorse must be for the ugliness of sin, which is universal. How does the author reply?
The reply distinguishes between the universal quality of ugliness and the specific ugliness of each sin.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The madhhab is that partial repentance is valid. Consider the implications for everyday practice.

قال : ولا تعميمه لتصح للإجماع على صحة إسلام من أصر على بعض معاصيه. ولأن حقيقتها الرجوع والندم والعزم، وقد وجدت . وقال أبو هاشم : يجب أن يكون الندم ،لقبحها، وهو شامل للكل، ورد بأن الشامل للكل هو القبح، لا قبحها . المذهب أنه تصح التوبة عن بعض المعاصي مع الإصرار على بعض، خلافاً لأبي هاشم لنا الإجماع على أن الكافر إذا أسلم وتاب عن كفره، مع استدامة بعض المعاصي، صحت توبته وإسلامه ولم يعاقب إلا عقوبة تلك المعصية. وأيضاً ليست التوبة عن تلك المعاصي إلا الرجوع عنها والندم عليها،
He said: And its [repentance's] generality is not required for its validity, by consensus on the validity of the Islam of one who persists in some of his sins. Because its reality is turning back, remorse, and resolve, and that has been found. And Abu Hashim said: Remorse must be for its ugliness, and it encompasses all, and it is replied: What encompasses all is the ugliness, not the ugliness of it. The madhhab is that repentance from some sins while persisting in some others is valid, contrary to Abu Hashim. For us, there is consensus that when a disbeliever accepts Islam and repents from his disbelief while continuing some sins, his repentance and Islam are valid, and he is punished only for that sin. Also, repentance from those sins is nothing but turning away from them and feeling remorse for them.
Source pages p. 169

Commentary
This passage discusses whether repentance must cover all sins or can be partial. The author begins by stating that generality is not required; repentance is valid even if one continues in other sins. The proof is consensus (ijma') that a disbeliever who accepts Islam and repents from disbelief while still committing some sins has a valid Islam and repentance. Abu Hashim objected that remorse must be for the ugliness of sin, which is a universal quality, so it should cover all sins. The reply is that the universal quality is ugliness itself, not the ugliness of each specific sin. The correct position (madhhab) is that repentance from some sins while persisting in others is valid, because repentance is essentially turning away and remorse for those specific sins. This is based on the observable fact that a convert's Islam is accepted despite ongoing minor sins.
