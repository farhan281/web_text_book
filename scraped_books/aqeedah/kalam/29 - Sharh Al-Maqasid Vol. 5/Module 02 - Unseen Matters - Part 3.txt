Refuting the Mu'tazilite Argument on Annihilation
Al-Taftazani refutes the Mu'tazilite argument on annihilation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Taftazani refutes the Mu'tazilite argument that annihilation is purposeless, and why God's purposes are not limited to human benefit or recompense.

Core Idea
God's actions are never futile, even if we cannot grasp their purpose.

The Mu'tazilites argued that since God is wise, His actions must have a purpose; annihilation, they claimed, has no purpose because it benefits no one. In this passage, al-Taftazani refutes this by broadening the concept of purpose beyond mere benefit and recompense, and by showing that even the announcement of annihilation serves divine wisdom.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazilite argument
Start by reading the Mu'tazilite claim that God's actions must have a purpose, and that annihilation lacks purpose because it provides no benefit and is not a recompense.
Notice how they equate purpose with benefit and recompense.
2
Identify the refutation
Find where al-Taftazani denies that purpose is limited to benefit and recompense, and where he suggests hidden wisdoms known only to God.
3
Examine the additional points
Look at the points about luṭf (kindness) and manifestation of God's majesty and self-sufficiency.
Announcement of annihilation itself serves a purpose.
4
Analyze the separation of parts objection
Read the objection about how dispersed parts might prevent restoration, and note the distinction between atoms and attributes.
5
Understand the analogy
Consider the analogy of a criminal who changes physically but remains the same person for retribution.
This illustrates that the underlying essence persists.

He uses an analogy of a criminal who changes in body but is still the same person. The second aspect—that separation itself has benefits—is also answered.

Refuting the Scriptural Claim for Resurrection without Annihilation
Al-Taftazani refutes the scriptural argument for resurrection without annihilation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Taftazani refutes the claim that Quranic verses prove resurrection without prior annihilation, showing that these verses do not negate annihilation and are counterbalanced by other verses.

Core Idea
Resurrection verses describe how, not whether – they don't deny annihilation.

Some argue that Quranic descriptions of resurrection as reviving the dead and gathering scattered parts imply the body is never annihilated. In this passage, al-Taftazani responds to this scriptural argument, explaining that such verses are about the manner of resurrection, not a denial of annihilation, and that other verses indicate annihilation. Let's examine his reasoning.

3
Understand the counterbalance
He then notes that these verses are counterbalanced by other verses that clearly indicate annihilation and perishing. This shows the evidence is not one-sided.
This demonstrates the principle that conflicting scriptural evidence must be reconciled.

Answering the Objection from Perishing
Sanusi refutes the claim that the verse of perishing contradicts their eternity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the three responses to the objection about perishing preserve the eternity of Paradise and Hell.

Core Idea
The verse of perishing does not disprove the eternity of Paradise and Hell.

The second objection against the pre-creation of Paradise and Hell argues that if they were created, they would be subject to the general perishing mentioned in the Quran. This passage presents the answer, offering three hermeneutical strategies to reconcile the verse with their eternity. We will explore each response and how they maintain consistency with revelation and reason.

Guided Path

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Philosophical objections to theology are not binding unless their premises are necessary.

In this passage, the author confronts a sophisticated objection against the existence of Paradise and Hell now. The objection tries to prove impossibility by examining possible locations. The author's response demonstrates that the objection rests on philosophical assumptions that are not universally accepted, and offers alternative spatial models consistent with divine power.

Read the objection and its structure

2

The objection first rules out being in this world: not in spheres or elements due to size and reincarnation. Then rules out another world: it would create a void and conflicting natural places.

Understand the author's answer
The author dismisses most premises as philosophical, not necessary. He suggests that both worlds could be within a greater sphere, and elements can have different natures in each world. He also clarifies the definition of reincarnation to avoid confusion.
Notice the author does not accept the premise that a second world would necessarily be spherical with a void.
4
Reflect on the broader principle
Consider how this objection and answer illustrate the relationship between reason and revelation in Islamic theology: rational arguments are valid only if their premises are self-evident or derived from revelation.
This lesson shows the importance of knowing what counts as a binding proof in kalam.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection from the deniers of punishment in the grave, who use both transmitted and rational arguments. The key point is that the verse is not addressing the period between death and resurrection, so it does not contradict the possibility of life and punishment in the grave.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the detailed classification of souls based on the loss of perfection and the corresponding afterlife punishments as presented by the philosophers.

Core Idea
Souls are punished according to the type and root of their deficiency.
