The Coexistence of Faith and Sin
Sanusi presents the fifth proof: verses where believers commit sins but retain the label 'believer'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fifth proof: Quranic verses that show faith and sin can coexist, proving actions are not part of faith.

Core Idea
Believers can sin without losing faith; so sin does not negate iman.

The fifth and final proof addresses a potential objection: if actions were part of faith, then committing sins would nullify faith. But the Quran describes believers who commit sins—like fighting each other or being unwilling in battle—while still calling them 'believers'. This proves that faith and sin can coexist, so actions cannot be essential to the definition of iman.

Guided Path
1
Read the fifth proof passage
Read the Arabic and translation, noting the four Quranic examples cited.
The verses include: 'those who believe and do not confuse their faith with wrongdoing', 'those who believe and have not emigrated', 'if two parties among the believers fight', and 'a party of the believers were unwilling'.
2
Analyze each verse
For each verse, note how it describes believers who are committing or experiencing something contrary to perfect obedience: wrongdoing, failure to emigrate, fighting, unwillingness.
These verses affirm that such people are still called believers, so sin does not remove faith.
3
Understand the logical conclusion
If actions were part of the reality of iman, then sin would necessarily remove iman. Since the Quran confirms that sinners remain believers, actions cannot be part of the definition of iman.

Commentary
This passage lists five proofs (wujūh) that actions (aʿmāl) are not part of the reality of faith (ḥaqīqat al-īmān). Each proof is drawn from textual and rational sources. First proof: Faith is linguistically assent (taṣdīq); there is no evidence (naql) that the term's meaning has been transferred to include actions. Second proof: At the moment of death (muʿāyanat al-ʿadhāb), only assent and verbal acknowledgment (iqrār) are beneficial, not actions, as the sources state that faith at that point is called despair (yaʾs) and is not accepted. Third proof: The Qurʾān addresses believers with commands (e.g., fasting) after affirming their faith, showing that faith is prior and distinct from actions. Fourth proof: Many Qurʾānic verses juxtapose 'īmān' and 'ʿamal ṣāliḥ' as two separate categories (e.g., 'those who believe and do righteous deeds'), and a hadith lists faith as the best deed whereas actions like jihad and pilgrimage are separate. Fifth proof: Qurʾānic verses affirm that believers can commit sins (e.g., fighting among themselves, reluctance in battle) without losing their identity as believers, proving that faith and sin coexist.

Five Arguments that Iman is Only Assent (Tasdiq)
The author enumerates five distinct arguments from scripture and reason for iman as tasdiq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand five key scriptural and rational arguments used to support the position that faith (iman) is solely inner assent (tasdiq) and does not include actions.

Core Idea
Faith is inner assent; five proofs from Quran, hadith, and reason separate it from deeds.

In classical Islamic theology, the exact definition of iman was debated. One prominent view—held by the Maturidis and some others—is that iman is only tasdiq (assent of the heart), not including actions. The author here presents five arguments to support this position, drawn from textual and rational sources. These arguments help clarify the distinction between inner faith and external deeds.

Guided Path
1
Understand the first argument: linguistic and lack of transfer
The first argument recalls that iman linguistically means tasdiq, and there is no evidence that the term was transferred to a broader meaning in revelation.
This is a semantic argument.
2
Analyze the second argument: iman at death
The second argument points out that iman is not beneficial when punishment is seen at death; only assent remains, proving iman is inner.
Actions are impossible at that moment.
3
Examine the third argument: commands after faith
The third argument uses verses where Allah addresses believers with commands, showing faith precedes and is separate from the commanded actions.
4
Review the fourth argument: separate mentions of iman and deeds
The fourth argument lists verses that mention iman and righteous deeds as distinct, and the hadith about the best of deeds lists iman first.
This scriptural pattern indicates they are not identical.
5
Consider the fifth argument: coexistence of iman and sin
The fifth argument cites verses where believers commit sins, proving that iman can remain despite disobedience, so it cannot be equivalent to all good actions.
This refutes the idea that iman includes obedience.

The Seventh Argument: Iman Cannot Be All Acts or Each Act Individually
Sanusi presents the seventh argument: a dilemma against iman as acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the reductio argument that exposes the absurd consequences of defining iman as acts of obedience, whether collectively or individually.

Core Idea
If iman were acts, missing one negates it; each act being a separate iman makes moving between acts a change of religion.

The seventh argument sharpens the critique by presenting a dilemma: if iman includes acts, it must either be a name for all acts collectively or for each individually. Both lead to impossible conclusions that contradict clear consensus. This lesson unpacks those consequences and shows why the claim fails.

Guided Path
1
Read the seventh argument
Read the Arabic passage from 'السابع' to 'منتقل من دين إلى دين'. Then read its literal translation. Notice the two conditional scenarios introduced by 'فإما للجميع... وإما لكل عمل على حدة'.
The passage uses a classic reductio structure: assume X, then derive absurdity.
2
Understand the first horn: collective
If iman is for all acts collectively, then negating even one act negates iman entirely. This would mean a person who merely affirms and confesses is not a believer until performing all worship – contradicting consensus.
Consensus holds that a believer who dies before performing all acts is still a believer.
3
Understand the second horn: individual
If iman is for each act individually, then each act is a separate iman. Moving from one act to another would be leaving one religion for another – an absurdity.
This shows the view leads to religious fragmentation.

Commentary

The Eighth Argument and the Rebuttal of Transfer
Sanusi uses the hadith of Jibril and rebuts the Mu'tazila claim of transfer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the hadith of Jibril proves iman is tasdiq, and why the Mu'tazila's claim of transfer does not support their position.

Core Idea
The Prophet defined iman as tasdiq; the Mu'tazila's 'transfer' is just a specification of objects, not a change of meaning.

The eighth argument draws on the famous hadith of Jibril, where the Prophet explicitly defines iman as assent (tasdiq) without mentioning actions. The author then addresses the Mu'tazila's counter-claim that the term has been transferred from its linguistic meaning to a new legal meaning encompassing acts. The response clarifies that the shift is only in the objects of tasdiq, not its essence.

2
Analyze the hadith evidence
The Prophet (PBUH) answered Jibril's question about iman by mentioning only tasdiq – the six articles of faith. Actions were mentioned under Islam, not iman.
This shows the Prophet distinguished iman from actions.
3
Understand the Mu'tazila objection
The Mu'tazila concede the linguistic meaning but claim a legal transfer to include acts. They argue that the legal usage of 'believer' implies more than just tasdiq.
They try to reconcile their view with the hadith by invoking transfer.
4
Grasp the author's response
The author responds that the 'transfer' is only in the objects – tasdiq is now for specific creedal matters, not any claim. This is not a genuine transfer; it just specifies the content. Thus no evidence for including acts.
The response preserves the linguistic continuity while acknowledging legal specification.

The Ash'ari Position on the Grave Sinner
The author defends calling the grave sinner a believer by distinguishing between faith (tasdiq) and acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Ash'aris consider a grave sinner a believer despite deserving blame.

Core Idea
A grave sinner is a believer because his faith (tasdiq) remains, even if his actions are corrupt.

This passage addresses a key theological question: does committing a major sin strip a person of the title 'believer'? The Ash'ari response, rooted in the Salaf's approach, distinguishes between the essence of faith and its perfection. You will see how the author defends the view that a grave sinner is still a believer, deserving both praise for faith and blame for sin.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Notice the objection that a grave sinner deserves only blame, so cannot be called a believer. Then read the answer: he deserves both praise and blame from different aspects.
The answer introduces the concept of tasdiq as the root of faith.
2
Understand the distinction
The passage explains that praise is for tasdiq, which is the head of obedience, while blame is for neglecting works. These are not contradictory.
This is a key Ash'ari principle: faith is affirmation, sin is a separate matter.
3
Connect to the Salaf's doctrine
The author notes that when praise is given absolutely, it refers to perfect faith, as the Salaf held. This clarifies that calling a grave sinner a believer is not the same as claiming they have perfect iman.
This avoids the misconception that Ash'aris equate sinlessness with faith.

Refuting the Mu'tazila Arguments for the Middle Station
The author refutes the Mu'tazila arguments for a middle station between faith and disbelief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two main Mu'tazila arguments for the 'middle station' and how the Ash'ari refutes them.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila middle station collapses because they confuse rulings and abandon the agreed fact that a person is either a believer or disbeliever.

In this concluding section, the author presents the positions of different sects on the grave sinner: Ash'aris say believer, Mu'tazila say neither, Khawarij say disbeliever, Hasan al-Basri says hypocrite. Then he engages the two major Mu'tazila arguments: the first based on taking the agreed term 'fasiq', the second based on mixed rulings. You will see how each argument fails.

The Mu'tazila say: the community agrees he is fasiq, but disagrees on iman/kufr, so we take 'fasiq' as the shared term. The author says this is corrupt because the community also agrees he must be either mu'min or kafir—there's no third option.
This argument tries to avoid the dichotomy.

The Ash'ari agrees he doesn't deserve ultimate praise, but still calls him believer in the basic sense.
4
Summarize the refutation
The Mu'tazila arguments fail because they either ignore the fundamental dichotomy or confuse different meanings of 'believer'.
This preserves the Ash'ari position.

Refuting Khawarij Proofs: Three Interpretive Methods
Author provides three methods to refute Khawarij scriptural proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three methods Sunni scholars use to refute the Khawarij's literalist interpretation of the Qur'an: specification, exaggeration, and directing the absolute to perfection.

Core Idea
The verses are not absolute; they are specified, exaggerated, or directed to the perfect form.

Having presented the Khawarij proofs, the author offers a concise refutation using three hermeneutical tools. These methods—takhsis, taghliz, and sarf al-mutlaq ila al-kamal—allow scholars to harmonize the apparent meaning of the verses with the established creed that grave sinners are not disbelievers unless they deny the essentials of faith.

Guided Path
1
Read the response passage
Read the Arabic sentence summarizing the refutation. It lists three methods: al-takhsis, al-haml 'ala al-taghliz, and sarf al-mutlaq ila al-kamal.
The response is brief; each method requires explanation.
2
Understand specification (takhsis)
This method means that the verses are not general; they apply to specific persons or contexts, such as those who deny the revealed law entirely, not every sinner.
For example, 'whoever does not judge...' refers to those who reject the law, not those who sin out of weakness.
3
Understand exaggeration (taghliz)
This means the term 'kufr' is used hyperbolically to emphasize the gravity of the sin, not to indicate actual exit from faith.
Calling a sin 'kufr' is like calling someone who neglects prayer a 'kafir' in a rhetorical sense, but legally they remain Muslim.
4
Understand directing the absolute to perfection
This means the absolute term is interpreted to refer to the perfect or complete form of that concept. For instance, 'kufr' in its absolute sense is directed to its perfect manifestation (denial of faith), not minor acts.
This is similar to applying 'kufr' only to the extreme, perfect form of disbelief.

Commentary

Learning Objective

Islam and Iman both mean surrender and affirmation, making separation inconceivable.

When you examine your own faith, do you sense that its core is secure even when your actions waver? How does al-Razi's distinction between essence and perfection help you understand the times you feel your faith is weak without despairing that you've lost it entirely? What one specific action will you take this week to move your faith from its bare essence toward its perfect form?
?
If iman's essence is unchanging, do you risk becoming complacent and neglecting growth? Where in your life have you settled for the 'essence' of faith without striving for perfection? Write down one area where you will intentionally seek to increase the perfection of your iman, starting tomorrow.
?
Al-Razi's reconciliation shows that both sets of evidence are true. How does this teaching challenge any tendency you have to view your faith as either all-or-nothing or as hopelessly inconsistent? What is one concrete change in your daily routine that would reflect a deeper commitment to growing the perfection of your iman?
