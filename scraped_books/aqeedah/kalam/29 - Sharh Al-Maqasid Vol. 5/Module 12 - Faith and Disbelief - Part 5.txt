Counter-Argument: Implicit Belief is Sufficient for Faith
Counter-argument to al-Razi: implicit belief suffices for faith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the counter-argument to al-Razi's view: that implicit belief (tasdiq ijmali) in all that the Prophet brought is sufficient for faith, and explicit belief in details is only required when those details are considered.

Core Idea
Faith does not require explicit belief in every detail; implicit acceptance suffices until details arise.

After presenting al-Razi's argument that the Prophet did not demand explicit belief in disputed principles, the author offers a counter-argument that refines the relationship between implicit and explicit faith. This lesson explores the objection based on the denial of the implication (man' al-mulazama) and clarifies that detailed belief becomes necessary only upon consideration.

Guided Path
1
Read the counter-argument
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice that this is a response specifically to al-Razi's earlier argument.
Look for the phrase 'man' al-mulazama' which means 'denial of the implication'.
2
Understand the denial of the implication
The counter-argument says that the Prophet not demanding explicit belief does not mean such belief is unnecessary. Instead, implicit belief in everything the Prophet brought is sufficient for faith.
This refutes al-Razi's assumption that lack of demand implies non-necessity.
3
Apply the distinction to examples
The passage gives examples: many believers do not know terms like 'originated' or 'eternal', but if they reflect on such a detail and disbelieve, they become disbelievers.
This shows that explicit rejection after consideration is what matters, not mere ignorance.

And al-Ustādh Abū Isḥāq al-Isfarāʾinī said: The disbelief of the one who declares us disbelievers, and whoever does not, then not. And the choice of Imām al-Rāzī is that he does not excommunicate anyone from the people of the qibla. He argued that if the correctness of Islam depended on believing the truth in those principles (uṣūl), then the Prophet (peace be upon him) and those after him would have demanded them from those who believed, and would have inquired about their creeds concerning them, and would have alerted them to what is the truth among them. But the consequence is negated definitively. Then he distinguished between them and what is among the principles of Islam by agreement: that some of them are such that it is well-known that they are from the religion, and the Book includes them such that they do not need explanation — like the resurrection of bodies; and some of them are such that their proofs appear in a manner befitting the masses so that the understanding hastens to them — like the originatedness of the world. But the speech is prolonged concerning them only to remove the doubts of the corrupting jurists, unlike the disputed principles (al-uṣūl al-khilāfiyya), for the truth concerning them is hidden, requiring additional reflection and contemplation, and the Book and the Sunnah may include what is imagined to be contradictory to the proof of the people of truth. So if opposing the truth in them were disbelief, there would be no need for explanation at all. Then he answered the proofs of the excommunication of sects by one another with answers, some of them based on the fact that breaking consensus (kharq al-ijmāʿ) is not disbelief, and that consensus is not formed without the agreement of the Mushabbiha, Mujassima, Rawāfiḍ, and their likes. And some of them based on the fact that the one upon whom disbelief is incumbent but who does not say it is not a disbeliever. And some of them based on the fact that the one who makes an interpretation (taʾwīl) — even if it is apparently false — is not a disbeliever. And some later Muʿtazilites agreed with him, cautious about the intercession of excommunication against those whom the earth and sky almost witness to their Islam, and about the necessity of excommunicating many of their great figures. But their speech surges with excommunication of the great ones of the people of Islam — and God is Almighty, Avenger. And it is possible for one to answer, by way of objecting to the Imām's argument by denying the implication (manʿ al-mulāzama), that the affirmation (taṣdīq) of everything that the Prophet (peace be upon him) brought, in summary (ijmālan), is sufficient for the correctness of faith (īmān). Detailed explanation (bayān al-ḥaqq fī al-tafāṣīl) is only needed when considering the details, even if they are among those where there is no dispute about excommunicating the opponent — such as the originatedness of the world. For how many a believer does not know the meaning of the originated (al-ḥādith) and the eternal (al-qadīm) at all, and has never come to mind the matter of the resurrection of bodies at all. But if he considers that, then if he did not believe, he would be a disbeliever.

Commentary
This passage presents the views of al-Isfarāʾinī and al-Rāzī on excommunication, followed by a counter-argument. Al-Isfarāʾinī says he excommunicates those who excommunicate him, but not otherwise. Al-Rāzī holds that no one from the people of the qibla is a disbeliever. Al-Rāzī's argument is that if the validity of Islam depended on correct belief in those disputed principles, the Prophet would have demanded such belief explicitly — yet he did not. He then distinguishes between principles that are unanimously agreed upon (like resurrection) whose proofs are clear, and disputed principles where the truth is hidden. He also answers proofs of excommunication by noting that breaking consensus is not kufr, consensus requires including deviant groups, someone forced into kufr but not saying it is not a disbeliever, and false interpretation does not necessarily entail disbelief. Some later Muʿtazilites agreed with him, but their language still excommunicates great Muslims. The counter-argument objects that implicit affirmation of all that the Prophet brought is sufficient for faith; detailed belief is only required when the details are considered. Many believers have no concept of 'originated' or 'eternal' or resurrection, but if they were to consider and disbelieve, they would be disbelievers.

Afterlife Destinations: Believer, Disbeliever, Hypocrite, and the Sinner's Hope
States the eternal abodes for four categories and the fasiq's hope.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the ultimate judgments for the believer, disbeliever, hypocrite, and the sinful believer according to Sunni theology.

Core Idea
Sunni theology affirms that even sinful believers ultimately reach paradise, through pardon or after punishment.

This passage opens the discussion of eschatological rulings by declaring the final abodes for the believer, disbeliever, and hypocrite. It then addresses the controversial figure of the fasiq (grave sinner) among Muslims, clarifying that his destiny is eventual paradise—contra the Mu'tazila and Khawarij.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on final abodes
Read the Arabic text carefully and note the four categories: believer, disbeliever, hypocrite, and sinful believer. Identify the distinct ruling for each.
The hypocrite is singled out for the lowest depth (darak al-asfal).
2
Examine the fate of the fasiq
Notice that the sinful believer has two possible paths: immediate paradise via pardon or intercession, or after punishment. This is the Sunni position.
The text explicitly mentions disagreement with Mu'tazila and Khawarij.
3
Contrast with other sects
Reflect on why this point is important: it distinguishes Sunni theology from those who claim the grave sinner is eternally in hell.
This is a foundational point in kalam.

حكم المؤمن الخلود في الجنة، وحكم الكافر الخلود في النار، ويختص المنافق بالدرك الأسفل وحكم الفاسق من المؤمنين الخلود في الجنة، إما ابتداء بموجب العفو أو الشفاعة وإما بعد التعذيب بالنار بقدر الذنب، وفيه خلاف المعتزلة والخوارج كما سبق. والفسق هو الخروج عن طاعة الله تعالى بارتكاب الكبيرة، وقد عرفتها وينبغي أن يقيد بعدم التأويل للاتفاق على أن الباغي ليس بفاسق . وفي معنى ارتكاب الكبائر الإصرار على الصغائر ، بمعنى الإكثار منها، سواء كانت من نوع واحد أو أنواع مختلفة وأما استحلال المعصية بمعنى اعتقاد حلها فكفر صغيرة كانت أو كبيرة . وكذا الاستهانة بها، بمعنى عدها هيئة ترتكب من غير مبالاة، وتجري مجرى المباحات. ولا خفاء في أن المراد ما ثبت بقطعي . وحكم المبتدع ، وهو من خالف في العقيدة طريقة السنة والجماعة ينبغي أن يكون حكم الفاسق، لأن الإخلال بالعقائد ليس بأدون من الإخلال بالأعمال، وأما فيما يتعلق بأمر الدنيا فحكم المؤمن ظاهر ، وحكم الكافر بأقسامه من الحربي والذمي والكتابي والمرتد ، فذكروه في كتب الفروع . وحكم المنافق والزنديق إجراء أحكام الإسلام وحكم الفاسق الحد فيما يجب فيه الحد ، والتعزير في غيره، والأمر بالتوبة، ورد الشهادة ، وسلب الولاية على اختلاف في ذلك بين الفقهاء، وحكم المبتدع البغض والعداوة والإعراض عنه . والإهانة والطعن واللعن وكراهية الصلاة خلفه .
The ruling of the believer is eternal abiding in paradise, and the ruling of the disbeliever is eternal abiding in the fire. The hypocrite is distinguished by the lowest depth (dark asfal). The ruling of the sinner among the believers is eternal abiding in paradise, either initially by virtue of pardon (ʿafw) or intercession (shafāʿa), or after punishment in the fire commensurate with the sin. There is disagreement with the Muʿtazila and Khawārij on this, as has preceded. Sin (fisq) is departing from obedience to Allah the Exalted by committing a major sin—you have already known it—and it should be qualified by the absence of interpretation (ta’wīl) due to consensus that the rebel (bāghī) is not a sinner. And in the meaning of committing major sins is persistence (iṣrār) on minor sins, meaning doing them frequently, whether they be of one type or different types. As for deeming disobedience permissible (istiḥlāl al-maʿṣiya) with the meaning of believing it to be lawful, that is disbelief (kufr), whether it is a minor or major sin. Likewise, treating it lightly (istihāna bihā), with the meaning of considering it a matter that is committed without care and is treated like permissible things. There is no doubt that what is meant is what is established by certainty (qaṭʿī). The ruling of the innovator (mubtadiʿ), who is one who opposes in creed the way of the Sunna and the community (ṭarīqat al-sunna wa al-jamāʿa), should be the ruling of the sinner, because failing in matters of creed is not less serious than failing in deeds. As for matters pertaining to the worldly life, the ruling of the believer is apparent. The ruling of the disbeliever in its categories—the harbī, the dhimmī, the kitābī, and the apostate—they have mentioned in the books of furūʿ (branches of fiqh). The ruling of the hypocrite (munāfiq) and the heretic (zindīq) is the application of the rulings of Islam. The ruling of the sinner is the prescribed punishment (ḥadd) where it is due, and discretionary punishment (taʿzīr) otherwise, and commanding him to repent, and rejection of his testimony, and removal of his guardianship (walāya), with disagreement among the jurists on that. The ruling of the innovator is hatred, enmity, turning away from him, insult, imprecation, cursing, and dislike of praying behind him.

Commentary
This passage elaborates on the afterlife and worldly rulings for various categories of people. It begins by stating the ultimate destinies: the believer will abide eternally in paradise; the disbeliever in hell; the hypocrite (munafiq) is in the lowest depth; and the sinner (fasiq) from among the believers will also eventually abide in paradise, either initially through pardon ('afw) or intercession (shafa'a), or after being punished in the fire proportionate to his sin. This is the Sunni position, in contrast to the Mu'tazila and Khawarij who held that the grave sinner is eternally in hell. Next, the passage repeats and expands the definition of fisq: it is committing a major sin, and it is qualified by the absence of ta'wil because there is consensus that the baghi (rebel with an interpretation) is not a fasiq. It then adds that the meaning of 'committing major sins' includes persisting in minor sins, i.e., doing them frequently. Then it reiterates that istihlal (deeming a sin permissible) is kufr, and adds that istihana (treating sin lightly) is also kufr. This applies to what is established by definitive evidence (qat'i). Then it gives the ruling of the mubtadi' (innovator in creed): he has the same ruling as the fasiq, because failing in creed is not less serious than failing in deeds. Then it details worldly rulings: for the believer, the ruling is clear; for the disbeliever in its various categories (harbi, dhimmi, kitabi, murtad), these are covered in books of furu' (practical law). For the munafiq and zindiq (heretic), the rulings of Islam are applied outwardly. For the fasiq, he receives the prescribed hadd punishment if applicable, otherwise discretionary punishment (ta'zir), and he is commanded to repent, his testimony is rejected, and his guardianship is removed (with juristic disagreement). For the mubtadi', the ruling is hatred, enmity, turning away from him, insulting, imprecating, cursing, and disliking praying behind him.

Expanded Definition of Fisq: Major Sins, Ta'wil, and Persistence on Minor Sins
Defines fisq and expands its scope to include persistence on minor sins.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise definition of fisq and its conditions, including the exception for the bāghī and the role of persistence on minor sins.

Core Idea
Fisq is committing a major sin without valid interpretation, and persistence on minor sins counts as major sin.

After stating the afterlife judgments, the text now refines the definition of fisq. It clarifies that fisq is the commission of a major sin, but with an important qualification: if a person has a legitimate interpretation (ta'wil), they are not considered a fasiq, as in the case of the rebel (bāghī). Moreover, the text introduces the concept that persistence on minor sins (iṣrār) equates to committing major sins.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of fisq
Read the Arabic text that defines fisq. Note that it is defined as committing a major sin (kabira) by departing from obedience.
The text says 'بارتكاب الكبيرة'.
2
Understand the exception for bāghī
The definition is qualified with 'absence of ta'wil'. This means that if someone commits a sin but has a valid interpretation (like a rebel with a mistaken interpretation), they are not fasiq. This is based on consensus.
Example: the bāghī (rebel) who acts on a mistaken interpretation is not a fasiq.
3
Learn about persistence on minor sins
The text adds that persistence (iṣrār) on minor sins, by doing them frequently, falls under the meaning of committing major sins. This applies whether the sins are of one type or diverse.
This is a key principle: minor sins become major when persisted upon.

Resolving the Dilemma of Forbidding Evil vs. Obedience to the Imam
Presents a dilemma about forbidding a sinful imam and its rebuttal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical dilemma between the duty to forbid evil and the duty to obey a sinful imam, and how the rebuttal resolves it by limiting obedience to Sharia-compliant matters.

Core Idea
Obedience to authority is conditional on not contradicting the Sharia.

This lesson examines the sixth argument presented in discussions about the imam's authority. The argument sets up a dilemma: if the imam sins, we must either forbid him (contradicting obedience) or remain silent (violating the duty to forbid evil). The rebuttal clarifies that obedience is limited, dissolving the apparent contradiction.

Commentary
The sixth argument presents a dilemma: if the imam commits a sin, we are faced with two possibilities. Either we must forbid him from the sin (enjoin good and forbid evil), but that would contradict the duty to obey him. Or we do not forbid him, which goes against the evidence that commands forbidding evil. Both are problematic. The rebuttal simply states that the obligation to obey the imam is only in matters that do not contradict the Sharia. Therefore, when the imam sins, the duty to forbid him takes precedence, and there is no contradiction because obedience is conditional. Thus, the dilemma is resolved.
