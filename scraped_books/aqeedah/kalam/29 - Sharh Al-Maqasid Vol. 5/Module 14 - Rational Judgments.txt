Proof for the Possibility of Returning the Non-Existent
The author argues for the possibility of returning the non-existent using two arguments and addresses an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational basis for the Islamic creed that Allah can recreate the universe after its annihilation, as argued through the principles of possibility and parity between first creation and resurrection.

Core Idea
Resurrection is possible because possibility is the default and it mirrors the first creation.

The author tackles a core theological dispute: can God bring back what has ceased to exist? He presents two distinct arguments—one based on the default rule of possibility, the other on the symmetry between initial creation and resurrection. This passage also responds to a philosophical objection and considers a subtle claim about preparedness. Through these, the author firmly establishes the possibility of i'ādat al-ma'dūm.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice the structure: first the claim, then two arguments (iqnāʿ and ilzām), then an objection and response.
The author uses very precise technical terms: imkān, wujūb, imtināʿ.
2
Identify the two arguments
Locate the persuasive argument (iqnāʿan) and the compelling argument (ilzāman). The persuasive argument relies on the default of possibility. The compelling argument relies on the identity of resurrection with first creation.
The compelling argument is stronger—it forces the opponent to accept consistency.
3
Analyze the objection and response
Find the objection introduced by 'lā yuqālu' and the response beginning with 'innā naqūlu'. Understand how the author counters the claim that an external cause might make resurrection impossible.
The response is a logical dilemma: either the cause makes it impossible (and we argue) or it doesn't (so possibility remains).
4
Reflect on the underlying principle
The core principle is that the default judgment for any act of God is possibility (al-aṣl huwa al-imkān). This principle underlies many theological proofs. Consider how it applies here.
This principle is foundational in kalām for proving divine actions.

He said: The first investigation: It is permissible to return the non-existent (i'ādat al-ma'dūm), contrary to the philosophers absolutely, and to some Mu'tazilites regarding accidents, and to some of them regarding non-permanent accidents like sounds. For us there is a persuasive argument: that the original principle is possibility until a proof of necessity or impossibility is established. And a compelling argument: that the Resurrection is like the first creation, indeed it is the same, so it is impossible for it to be possible at one time and impossible at another time. Rather, perhaps one claims that the first existence bestows an increase of preparedness for receiving existence, as alluded to by His saying: 'And He is easier for Him' (30:27). Commentary
This passage is the first investigation (mabḥath) of the chapter, arguing for the permissibility (jawāz) of returning the non-existent (i'ādat al-ma'dūm), i.e., God's power to recreate after annihilation. The author opposes the philosophers who deny this absolutely, and some Mu'tazilites who deny it for accidents, especially non-permanent ones like sounds. He gives two arguments. First, a persuasive (iqnāʿī) argument: the default judgment for any action is possibility (imkān) unless there is proof of necessity or impossibility. Since there is no such proof for the resurrection, it remains possible. Second, a compelling (ilzāmī) argument: the resurrection (al-maʿād) is like the first creation (al-mabdaʾ), indeed it is the very same act. If the first creation was possible, then the resurrection cannot be impossible at a later time. The author then mentions a claim that the first existence increases the preparedness (istiʿdād) for receiving existence, citing the verse 'And He is easier for Him' (30:27). However, he finds this claim questionable (fīhi naẓar). He then addresses an objection: perhaps the resurrection became impossible due to some necessary cause. His response is that we then ask whether that cause makes it impossible or not; if it does, then we argue about it; if not, then possibility remains. Thus the argument for possibility is sustained.

Refuting the Claim of Self-Evidence Against Re-creation
Al-Sanusi refutes the claim that impossibility of re-creation is self-evident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Sanusi refutes the argument that the impossibility of re-creation is self-evident, using the method of denial (man') and pointing to contrary rational opinions.

Core Idea
Self-evidence can be challenged by the existence of rational opponents and proof.

In this passage, al-Sanusi confronts a key objection to the possibility of re-creation: the claim that it is self-evidently impossible. He presents the argument and its endorsement by a respected scholar, then refutes it by denying the premise of self-evidence. This lesson shows how theological arguments engage with appeals to innate reason.

Objection and Refutation on Restoration of the Non-Existent
Taftazani presents and answers the second objection concerning the restoration of the non-existent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second objection against the restoration of the non-existent and how Taftazani refutes it by denying that time is an individuating characteristic and distinguishing between temporal order and ontological order.

Core Idea
Time is not an essential property; restoration avoids contradiction by distinguishing first in order from first in time.

In this passage, Taftazani presents a sophisticated objection against the doctrine of restoring the non-existent in its very essence. The objector argues that if a thing is restored with all its individuating characteristics, its original time must also be restored, leading to the thing being both originated and restored simultaneously. This results in a logical contradiction. Taftazani's refutation clarifies the distinction between temporal and ontological priority, showing that the objection rests on a misunderstanding of what makes a thing 'originated' or 'restored.'

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Notice how the objection builds from the assumption that time is an individuating characteristic. Understand the three consequences or corruptions mentioned.
The objection claims that restoring a thing with all its properties necessitates restoring its original time, leading to a contradiction.
2
Identify the three corruptions
The passage mentions that the thing would be both originated and restored, eliminating the distinction between them, and potentially leading to an infinite regress.
These corruptions show why the objection is considered problematic.
3
Study the refutation
Taftazani denies that time is an individuating characteristic, using the example of a book remaining identical over time despite different temporal considerations.
He argues that variations in considerations and relations do not contradict personal unity in external reality.
4
Understand the key distinction
The refutation introduces a crucial distinction: 'originated' means that which occurs first (in order), not that which occurs in the first time; 'restored' means that which occurs second, not that which occurs in the second time.
This distinction avoids the contradiction and also prevents infinite regress.

Commentary
This passage presents the second objection against the doctrine of the restoration of the non-existent (إعادة المعدوم) and its answer. The objector argues that if a thing can be restored with all its individuating characteristics, then its first time must also be restored because time is part of its identity. This leads to contradictions: the thing would be both originated (مبتدأ) and restored (معاد) simultaneously, which are opposites. The objection attempts to show the concept of 'restoration' is self-contradictory. The answer refutes this by denying that time is an individuating characteristic (مشخص). The author gives the example of a book that remains identical over time despite different temporal considerations. He distinguishes between 'first in time' (الواقع في الزمان الأول) and 'first in order' (الواقع أولاً). The originated is what exists first (in order), not necessarily in the first time. This avoids the infinite regress objection: if time were restored, it would not be the same time in a different sense, because the difference is only in priority and posteriority, not in the nature of the time itself.

The Third Objection: Problem of Identical Individuals
Taftazani presents and answers the third objection based on the impossibility of two identical individuals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third objection to the restoration of the non-existent based on the impossibility of two identical individuals and Taftazani's refutation.

Core Idea
Exact similarity does not entail indistinguishability; two identical individuals can still be distinct.

This lesson tackles a sophisticated objection against the doctrine of restoring the non-existent. The objector argues that if a non‑existent can be restored with full identity, then a completely similar individual could be created afresh, leading to a paradox of indistinguishability. Taftazani dismantles the objection by showing that lack of distinction is not necessary and that the argument has no special bearing on restoration.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Start by reading the full passage to get the flow of the objection and its answer. Notice the conditional structure: 'if restoration is allowed, then...'
The objection is a reductio ad absurdum.
2
Identify the objector's reasoning
The objector claims that if restoration is possible, then a similar individual could initially exist because the judgment of similar things is the same. This leads to two indistinguishable individuals, which is absurd.
The key premise: 'because the judgment of similar things is the same' (حكم الأمثال واحد).
3
Understand the rebuttal
Taftazani rebuts by denying that lack of distinction is necessary. If two things were truly not distinct, they would not be two things. Reason does not accept the impossibility of two exactly similar individuals in reality.
The text says: 'ولو لم يتميزا لم يكونا شيئين' – if they were not distinct, they would not be two things.
4
Appreciate the final point
The objector's argument, if sound, would also prevent the initial existence of two identical individuals, which is not generally rejected. Thus, the objection does not specifically undermine restoration.
The upshot: 'لا تعلق لهذا بإعادة المعدوم' – this has no connection to restoration.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of arguments within the second branch of Mu'tazili furoo' (branches) regarding the characteristics of reward and punishment. Commentary
This passage presents the disagreement among theologians about when exactly reward and punishment are deserved. The Basrian school (بصرية) holds that the desert occurs at the moment of obedience or disobedience itself. The Baghdadian school (بغدادية) places it solely in the afterlife. Another opinion places it at the moment of death (اخترام). Yet another says at the time of the act conditioned by continuation (موافاة), meaning the act must not be later invalidated by apostasy or similar before death. The author notes that none of these positions has a strong proof except the observation that praise and blame are immediate for acts, so reward and punishment, being consequences like them, should also be immediate. However, the full bestowal of reward is delayed to the afterlife because a condition of reward is that it be free from the admixture of hardships, whereas the state of commission (تكليف) necessarily involves hardships. Thus, delaying the full reward is necessary to avoid combining opposites in the same state. Those who insist on delay (the latter groups) rely on scriptural texts and the logical point about combining opposites. But this does not negate the establishment of desert in this world. The author reconciles: the first group meant the 'root' of desert (أصل الاستحقاق) while the latter meant the 'obligation of performance' (وجوب الأداء). Finally, some hold that commission (تكليف) cannot coexist with every recompense — some recompenses like glorification, help against enemies, and prescribed penalties (حدود) can coexist with commission, so their delay is not required.

Finite sin requires finite punishment; infinite offense demands infinite penalty.

This sets sin apart from disbelief, which is infinite in degree and therefore warrants eternal punishment. Read the passage where the argument is presented as a syllogism: the imam is either Ali or Abu Bakr or determined by qiyas from consensus; the latter two are impossible, so it must be Ali.
Qalb is a form of argument that inverts or reduces options.
2
Identify the refutation
Note that the same two responses are given: denial of the stipulation and denial of the absence in Abu Bakr.
The refutation does not engage with the syllogistic form but attacks the premises.
3
Understand the consensus note
Read the sentence about consensus: the claim that consensus shows each is suitable is questionable (maḥall naẓar).
This undermines the Shia claim that consensus supports equality.
4
Study the qiyas definition
Read the footnote defining qiyas both linguistically and in Sharia, which clarifies the term used in the argument.
This is a technical definition typical of uṣūl al-fiqh.

أن بعد رسول الله (صلى الله عليه وسلم) إماماً وليس غير علي (كرم الله وجهـه) لانتفاء الشرائط من العصمة والنص والأفضلية .

وأما ما يقال أن الإجماع على أن الإمام أحدهم إجماع على صلوح كل منهم للإمامة فمحل نظر .
(١) القياس في اللغة: عبارة عن التقدير يقال قست الغل بالغل إذا قدرته وسويته، وهو عبارة عن رد الشيء إلى نظيره وفي الشريعة عبارة عن المعنى المستنبط من النص لتعدية الحكم من المنصوص إلى غيره، وهو الجمع بين الأصل والفرع في الحكم.
He said: The first [proof]: That after the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) there is an imam, and it is none other than Ali (may Allah honor his face) because the conditions are absent: infallibility, textual designation, and superiority. The answer: Denial of the stipulation [of these conditions], then denial of their absence in the case of Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him). This is the rational argument. Its exposition: There is no dispute that after the Messenger (peace be upon him) there is an imam, and it is none other than Ali, because the imam must be infallible, textually designated, and the most excellent of his time, and nothing of that exists among the remaining Companions. As for infallibility and textual designation, [their absence] is by agreement. As for superiority, it will come later. And this can be made into three proofs according to the conditions, and perhaps it may be presented in the form of a syllogism (qalb) by saying: The imam is either Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) or Abu Bakr, or [it is determined by] analogy (qiyas) based on consensus that includes the statement of the infallible one, and there is no way to the latter two due to the absence of the condition. The answer: First, denial of the stipulation. Second, denial of the absence of the conditions in Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him). And as for what is said that the consensus that the imam is one of them is a consensus on the suitability of each of them for imamate, it is a matter of consideration. (1) Al-Qiyas in linguistic terminology: it is an expression for estimation; it is said: 'I measured the burden by the burden' when you estimated and equalized it. It is an expression for referring a thing to its equivalent. In the Sharia, it is an expression for the meaning inferred from the text to extend the ruling from the stipulated case to another, and it is the combination of the original and the branch in the ruling.

Commentary
This passage presents the first rational proof for the Shia claim that Ali was the only valid imam after the Prophet, along with its refutation. The proof states three conditions for imamate: infallibility ('isma), textual designation (nass), and superiority (afdaliyya), all of which, it claims, are absent in all Companions except Ali. The refutation proceeds in two steps: first, it denies that these conditions are required (man' al-ishtirāṭ); second, it denies that they are absent in Abu Bakr. The author then elaborates: there is no dispute that an imam is needed, but the conditions are not accepted as necessary. The argument can also be cast as a syllogism (qalb): the imam must be either Ali or Abu Bakr, or determined by qiyas from consensus, but both alternatives are impossible. The response repeats: the conditions are not accepted, and in any case they are present in Abu Bakr. The author then notes that the claim of consensus on the qualification of all is questionable. Finally, a footnote defines qiyas (analogical reasoning) linguistically and in Sharia, explaining its role in extending rulings.

Virtue Is by Knowledge and Piety
Al-Ghazali presents scriptural proofs for virtue being by knowledge and piety.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quran, Sunnah, and consensus establish that true virtue lies in knowledge and piety.

Core Idea
Virtue is not by lineage but by knowledge and piety.

This lesson presents the foundational evidence from Islamic sources that define what constitutes true honor. Al-Ghazali brings together Quranic verses and prophetic hadiths to show that knowledge and piety are the criteria for virtue. As you read, notice the cumulative weight of these proofs.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Pause after each proof text to understand what it asserts.
The passage begins with a general statement of consensus, then lists proofs.
2
Identify each proof and its point
Go through each Quranic verse and prophetic hadith. Note what specific aspect of virtue each one emphasizes (e.g., piety, knowledge, equality).
There are three verses and four hadiths.
3
Reflect on the overall message
Consider how these proofs collectively refute the idea that virtue comes from lineage, wealth, or worldly status. The texts consistently point to piety and knowledge.
This counters common social prejudices.

And in general, the Book, the Sunnah, and consensus have concurred that virtue belongs to knowledge and piety. Allah Almighty said: 'Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous of you' (49:13). And Allah Almighty said: 'Say, 'Are those who know equal to those who do not know?'' And indeed, the scholars are the heirs of the prophets. And he (peace be upon him) said: 'The superiority of the scholar over the worshiper is like my superiority over the lowest of you.' Commentary
This passage presents the general principle (ijmālan) that virtue (al-faḍl) is for knowledge and piety, as established by the Quran, Sunnah, and consensus. Al-Ghazali then provides proof texts: Quranic verses stating that nobility is by piety (49:13), that the knowledgeable are not equal to the ignorant (39:9), and that God raises the believers and the knowledgeable in degrees (58:11). Then he cites several hadiths: the equality of all people in terms of humanity, the superiority of the scholar over the worshiper likened to the moon over stars, that scholars are heirs of prophets, another hadith emphasizing the scholar's rank, and the promise of Paradise for those who seek knowledge. The argument is cumulative: the sources consistently point to knowledge and piety as the true measure of virtue.
