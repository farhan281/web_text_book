Objection from Moses' Prayer and Its Refutation
Objection from Moses' prayer and its refutation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection that the Quran's challenge is contradicted by the story of Moses is refuted through two key arguments.

Core Idea
The story of Moses does not fulfill the Quran's challenge because the wording is not identical.

This passage addresses a clever objection: if the Quran challenges humans and jinn to produce a surah of at least three verses, then doesn't Moses' prayer of eleven verses, which appears in the Quran, count as such a production? The author refutes this by distinguishing between the 'related speech' (al-mahki) and the Quran's own composition, and by noting that the challenge may be limited to long or medium surahs.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
The opponent points to the Quran's story of Moses praying 'Rabbi ishrah li sadri...' and argues this is a surah-length passage that would meet the challenge.
The shortest surah is three verses, and Moses' prayer is about eleven verses.
2
Understand the first refutation
The author says the reported speech (al-mahki) does not have to be in this exact composition (nazm). That is, the Quran quotes Moses but not necessarily in the exact wording Moses used, so it doesn't serve as a counterexample.
The inimitability is in the unique nazm of the Quran, not in the content of quotations.
3
Consider the second refutation
Some scholars hold that the challenge applies only to a long surah (min al-tiwal) or ten medium verses (min al-awsat). A short prayer like Moses' would not fall under the challenge.
This limits the scope of the challenge.

Quranic variants are not contradictions; they are equally eloquent.

Refuting the Objection that Karamat Contradict I'jaz
The author refutes the claim that karamat contradict i'jaz.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the claim that saintly miracles (karamat) contradict prophetic miracles (i'jaz).

Core Idea
The prophet's challenge is only against opponents, not against saints.

In this passage, the author addresses a key objection against the permissibility of saintly miracles. The objection argues that if saints can perform miracles similar to those of prophets, then prophetic miracles lose their evidential force. The author responds by clarifying the precise scope of the prophet's challenge. This distinction is crucial for understanding the relationship between prophetic and saintly miracles.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the first sentence beginning with 'فإن قيل' to understand the objection: permitting karamat contradicts the condition of i'jaz because others can produce the like.
The objection is a standard one in kalam.
2
Identify the author's distinction
In the answer, notice the key term 'mu'araḍa' (opposition). The author says the contradiction is only when someone brings the like on purpose to oppose, not as a saintly miracle.
The prophet's claim is directed at 'al-mutahaddin' (those who challenge).
3
Note the exception
The author acknowledges that for some miracles, like the Quran, there is a decisive text that no one can bring its like at all.
This exception does not negate the general principle.
4
Understand the general principle
Despite the exception, the general rule remains: every miracle that occurs for a prophet may also occur as a karamat for a saint, unless a specific text prohibits it.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection and its answer. The objection states that permitting saintly miracles (karāmāt) contradicts the condition of prophetic miracles (i‘jāz), because one requirement of a prophetic miracle is that no one else can produce its like. If a saint could produce something similar, it would invalidate the miracle and cause the prophet to be accused of lying when he issues a challenge (taḥaddī). The author answers by distinguishing between opposition (mu‘āraḍa) and saintly miracles. The prophet’s claim that no one can produce its like refers specifically to those who challenge and oppose, not to saints who receive honor (karāma) or to other prophets. The prophet says "no one from the opponents (al-mutaḥaddīn) will bring the like." However, the author acknowledges that for some miracles, like the Qur’an, there is a definitive text stating that absolutely no one can produce its like. But this does not negate the general principle that every miracle occurring for a prophet may also occur as a saintly miracle for a saint, except where a specific text prohibits it.
