The Fourth Definition of Iman and the Three Schools on Works
Taftazani defines iman as heart, tongue, limbs, and lists three schools on abandoning works.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth definition of iman as encompassing heart, tongue, and limbs, and the three main schools' positions on whether abandoning works removes one from faith.

Core Idea
Iman is not just inner belief; it includes words and deeds, but schools differ on the consequence of missing some.

This passage introduces a comprehensive definition of faith that includes confession, affirmation, and action. It then outlines three major theological schools—the Khawarij, Mu'tazila, and Salaf—and their stances on whether abandoning works excludes one from faith. Understanding these positions is key to appreciating the spectrum of Islamic thought on iman.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of iman
Start by reading the fourth definition: iman is a name for the action of the heart, tongue, and limbs—confession, affirmation, and works.
Notice that this definition is broader than mere inner belief.
2
Identify the Khawarij position
The Khawarij hold that abandoning works removes one from faith entirely, making him a disbeliever.
3
Understand the Mu'tazila position
The Mu'tazila place the abandoner in a middle station (al-manzila bayn al-manzilatayn), neither believer nor disbeliever. Note their internal disagreement on which works matter.
Abu Ali and Abu Hashim require wajibat; Abu al-Hudhayl and Abd al-Jabbar include mandubat.
4
Contrast with the Salaf position
The Salaf, most hadith scholars, and many mutakallimun hold that the abandoner of works is still a believer and will eventually enter paradise.
This position is attributed to Malik, al-Shafi'i, and al-Awza'i.

(۱) إلى قوله : أولئك هم المؤمنون حقاً . وموضع الخلاف أن مطلق الاسم للأول أم الثاني؟ وذكر الإمام في وجه الضبط أن الإيمان إما أن يكون اسماً لعمل القلب فقط، وهو المعرفة عنـد الإمامية، وجهم، والتصديق عندنا وإما لعمل الجوارح. فإن كان هو القول، فمذهب الكرامية أو سائر الأعمال، فمذهب المعتزلة. وإما مجموع عمل القلب والجوارح ، وهو مذهب السلف وفيه اختلال من جهة ترك عمل القلب في مذهب الاعتزال، وعدم التعرض لمذهب التصديق والإقرار.
As for the fourth [definition], that faith is a name for the action of the heart, tongue, and limbs, according to what is said that it is confession by the tongue, affirmation by the heart, and action by the limbs. So the abandoner of action may be considered outside of faith, entering into disbelief – and to this went the Khawarij – or not entering into it, and that is the position of the station between the two stations, and to this went the Mu'tazila, except they differed regarding actions: according to Abu 'Ali and Abu Hashim, it is performing obligations and avoiding prohibitions; according to Abu al-Hudhayl and 'Abd al-Jabbar, it is performing acts of obedience, whether obligatory or supererogatory, except that exiting from faith and being deprived of entering paradise due to abandoning supererogatory [acts] is not a position befitting a rational person. And sometimes the abandoner of action is not considered outside of faith, rather it is decisively concluded that he enters paradise and does not remain eternally in the fire, and this is the school of most of the Salaf, all the Imams of Hadith, and many of the mutakallimun, and it is reported from Malik, al-Shafi'i, and al-Awza'i. And there is an apparent problem: how does something not become nonexistent due to the nonexistence of its pillar, i.e., works? And how does one enter paradise who does not possess that which is made a name for faith? The answer is that 'faith' is used for that which is the origin and foundation for entering paradise, which is affirmation alone or with confession; and for that which is the complete [faith] that saves without dispute, which is affirmation with confession and action, as indicated by His saying: 'Indeed, the believers are those who, when Allah is mentioned, their hearts tremble...' up to His saying: 'Those are the believers truly.' And the point of disagreement is whether the absolute name is for the first or the second? And the Imam mentioned in a manner of control that faith is either a name for the action of the heart only – which is knowledge according to the Imamiyyah and Jahm, and affirmation according to us – or for the action of the limbs. If it is speech, then the school of the Karamiyyah; or for all actions, then the school of the Mu'tazila. Or it is a combination of the action of the heart and limbs, and that is the school of the Salaf, and in it is a defect from the aspect of neglecting the action of the heart in the Mu'tazila school, and not addressing the school of affirmation and confession.

Commentary
This passage defines the fourth view of iman as encompassing heart, tongue, and limbs (confession, affirmation, and works). It then presents three schools: 1) Khawarij: abandoner of works leaves faith entirely. 2) Mu'tazila: the 'station between the two stations' – the abandoner is neither believer nor disbeliever; they differ on which works matter. 3) Salaf and others: the abandoner still enters paradise eventually, based on tasdiq alone. An objection follows: if works are part of the definition, how can one lacking works be saved? The answer distinguishes between the essential core (tasdiq, with or without iqrar) which is the foundation, and the complete name (tasdiq+iqrar+amal). The dispute is which is the absolute meaning. The Imam then summarizes: iman is either heart-action only (knowledge or tasdiq), limb-action only (speech or all actions), or both. The Salaf school combines both but the Mu'tazila neglects heart-action.

Objection and Answer: Can One Without Works Be Saved?
Taftazani raises and answers an objection to the Salaf position on abandoning works.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection against the Salaf position that the abandoner of works is saved, and the answer that distinguishes between the essential core of iman (tasdiq) and its complete form (tasdiq + iqrar + amal).

Core Idea
Faith's core is affirmation; works complete it, but the saved rely on the core.

After presenting the Salaf view that the abandoner of works still enters paradise, an immediate objection arises: if works are part of the definition of iman, how can one lacking works be saved? The answer resolves this by distinguishing two uses of the word 'iman'—the essential foundation and the complete reality. This distinction is crucial for understanding the apparent contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
The objection states: if works are a pillar of iman, how can iman remain when its pillar is missing? And how can one who does not possess what is called iman enter paradise?
2
Identify the answer's first part
The answer says 'iman' is used for the origin and foundation of entering paradise: affirmation alone or with confession.
This is the minimal definition.
3
Understand the second part of the answer
Iman is also used for the complete, saving faith: affirmation with confession and action, as indicated by the verse.
The verse mentions trembling hearts, etc.
4
Grasp the resolution
The disagreement is about which usage is the absolute name. The Salaf prioritize the foundational meaning for salvation.

The Imam's Classification: Faith as Heart, Limbs, or Both
The Imam classifies definitions of iman into three categories and evaluates schools.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Imam's systematic classification of definitions of iman into three types: heart-action only, limb-action only, and both, along with the schools that hold each view, and the critique of the Mu'tazila position.

Core Idea
Iman definitions are reduced to three: heart alone, limbs alone, or both; each has its proponents and problems.

After resolving the objection, the Imam (likely al-Ghazali or Fakhr al-Din al-Razi) offers a systematic classification of all possible definitions of iman. He reduces them to three categories: iman as action of the heart only, iman as action of the limbs only, and iman as combination of both. This classification helps map the various schools and their shortcomings, particularly pointing out the Mu'tazila's neglect of heart-action.

Guided Path
1
Read the classification framework
The Imam states that the point of disagreement is whether the absolute name 'iman' refers to the first (core) or second (complete) meaning. He then classifies iman definitions into three types.
2
Identify the first type: heart-action only
This is knowledge (ma'rifa) for the Imamiyya and Jahm, or affirmation (tasdiq) for the Ash'aris.
3
Identify the second type: limb-action only
If it is speech, it is the Karamiyya school; if all actions, it is the Mu'tazila school.
4
Understand the third type: combination of heart and limbs
This is the Salaf school, but the Imam notes a defect: it neglects heart-action in the Mu'tazila classification and does not address the affirmation+confession school.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Mu‘tazilite proof from the Jerusalem prayer verse fails because 'iman' there is figurative, not literal.

The debate over the definition of iman often turns on scriptural verses that seem to equate faith with actions. One such verse is Surah al-Baqarah 2:143, where the word 'iman' appears in a context about the change of qibla. The Mu'tazilites seized upon this as evidence that iman can mean prayer. Core Idea
Not everyone in Hell is eternally disgraced; the verse is specific to the Companions.

The Mu'tazila used the fate of a highway robber to argue that sin excludes a person from true faith. They built a syllogism with three Quranic verses. 2
Understand the refutation
The answer denies the general rule (al-kubra) that everyone in Fire is disgraced. Find which verse is limited to the Companions.
3
Reflect on the principle
Consider why this refutation works: a general statement can be restricted by a specific context. This is a common method in kalam.
The answer does not deny the robber enters Fire, only that disgrace applies.

Actions can be signs of disbelief without being disbelief itself.
