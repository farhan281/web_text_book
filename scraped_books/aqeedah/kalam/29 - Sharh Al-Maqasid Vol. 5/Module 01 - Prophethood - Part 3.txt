Muhammad is the Seal of the Prophets: The Objection of Jesus' Descent Answered
The author answers an objection about Jesus to prove Muhammad's sealing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Jesus' future descent does not contradict Muhammad's finality as the Seal of the Prophets.

Core Idea
Jesus will descend as a follower of Muhammad's law, not as a new prophet.

One common objection to Muhammad's finality is that Jesus is still alive and will return. The author anticipates this and provides a clear answer using a prophetic tradition about Moses. This lesson unpacks that argument and shows how it reinforces Muhammad's unique status.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Identify the objection about Jesus being alive and descending. Understand why someone might think this challenges Muhammad's sealing.
The objection assumes that a later prophet would supersede the previous one.
2
Examine the answer
The author concedes Jesus is alive but states he will follow Muhammad's sharia. Note the use of the hadith about Moses to reinforce the point.
The key is that Jesus will not bring a new law.
3
Understand the definition of 'Seal'
The passage defines 'Seal of the Prophets' as no prophet sent after him. Reflect on how the answer fits this definition.
The definition is explicit: لا يبعث نبي بعده.
4
Connect to the broader argument
This objection and answer is part of the proof that Muhammad is the best prophet. The answer preserves the finality while acknowledging Jesus' unique role.

Commentary
This passage is a proof-and-objection structure affirming that Muhammad is the Seal of the Prophets and the best of them. It begins by establishing that following Muhammad’s law is tied to the glad tidings of his mission. The author anticipates an objection: Is not Jesus still alive, raised to heaven, and will descend later? The answer is yes, but Jesus will descend as a follower of Muhammad’s sharia, not as a new prophet. The author supports this with a prophetic statement about Moses — that if Moses were alive, he would have to follow Muhammad. This proves that Muhammad is the Seal, meaning no prophet comes after him. The passage then cites the consensus of Muslims that Muhammad is the best prophet, because his community is the best, as per Quranic verses. The preference of the community implies preference of the messenger. Additional proofs include that he was sent to both jinn and humans, his miracle (the Quran) remains, his law abrogates all others, and his testimony on Judgment Day covers all. Finally, it mentions authentic hadiths where the Prophet humbly acknowledges his status without boasting, and even forbids preferring him over Moses or Jonah, which is itself a sign of his humility and virtue.

The Best of Prophets: Muhammad's Superiority and Its Proofs
The author cites consensus and multiple proofs for Muhammad's superiority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the consensus and multiple proofs that establish Muhammad as the best of all prophets.

Core Idea
The best community implies the best messenger.

Having established Muhammad's finality, the author now turns to his superiority. Citing Quranic verses, hadiths, and logical arguments, the passage shows why Muslims unanimously hold Muhammad as the greatest prophet. This lesson unpacks those evidences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim of consensus
The passage starts with 'وأجمع المسلمون' — the consensus of Muslims. Note that this is a strong source of authority in Islamic theology.
Consensus (ijma) is considered a proof.
2
Analyze the first proof: best community
The author argues that because Muhammad's community is the best (Quran 3:110, 2:143), the messenger is also the best. Understand this logical connection.
The community's virtue reflects on the messenger.
3
Examine the additional proofs
The passage lists several other proofs: sent to jinn and humans, seal of prophets, eternal miracle, abrogating law, testimony on Judgment Day. Recognize each as a unique distinction.
These are specific merits not shared by other prophets.
4
Reflect on the hadiths about humility
The author includes hadiths where the Prophet humbly says not to prefer him over Moses or Jonah. Understand that this humility itself is a sign of his greatness.
True greatness often comes with humility.

Core Idea
Scholars differ on the best prophet after Muhammad, each citing a unique virtue.

This passage lists five opinions among scholars regarding the most excellent prophet after Muhammad. Each opinion highlights a different prophet's distinguishing characteristic, from Adam's fatherhood to Jesus' unique station as Spirit of Allah. The author presents these views as a prelude to discussing the Christian claim.

Sectarian Positions on the Limits of Prophetic Infallibility
Enumeration of sectarian positions on prophetic infallibility regarding forgetfulness, kufr, taqiyya, and deliberate major sins.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of various Islamic sects regarding which sins prophets may commit, especially in relation to kufr, forgetfulness, dissimulation, and deliberate major sins.

Core Idea
Different sects stretched or limited prophetic immunity based on their theological principles about sin and faith.

After establishing the basic categorization of sins, the author turns to the diverse views of Islamic sects and scholars. Each group's stance on prophetic immunity reflects its broader theological commitments—whether it permits sins of forgetfulness, kufr, dissimulation (taqiyya), or deliberate major sins. This lesson unpacks those positions and their justifications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the groups mentioned
The passage lists al-Qadi (Abd al-Jabbar?), al-Azariqa (Kharijites), al-Shi'a, and al-Hashwiyya. Also implicitly the Ash'ari and Mu'tazila positions. Note each one's stance.
Al-Qadi is likely Qadi Abd al-Jabbar, a Mu'tazilite.
2
Understand al-Qadi's view on forgetfulness
Al-Qadi permitted sins of forgetfulness, claiming that forgetfulness does not affect the attestation intended by the miracle.
This is a nuanced exception.
3
Analyze the Azariqa position on kufr
The Azariqa (a Kharijite subsect) permitted kufr from prophets because they considered every sin as kufr.
Their reasoning is based on equating all sins with disbelief.
4
Review the Shi'a view on taqiyya
The Shi'a permitted prophets to manifest kufr under dissimulation (taqiyya) to avoid self-destruction, but this is rebutted by reasoning that taqiyya is more appropriate at the beginning of the prophetic call.
The rebuttal is part of the passage.
5
Compare positions on deliberate major sins after prophethood
On deliberate major sins after prophethood: Ash'aris hold it is forbidden by revelation (sam'an), Mu'tazila by reason ('aqlan), and Hashwiyya permitted it due to lack of proof or apparent conflicting evidence.
The Hashwiyya are a group that accepted traditions literally without critical thought.

Commentary
This passage provides a systematic categorization of all possible sins that could be attributed to prophets, serving as a comprehensive restatement of the issue. The author begins by recalling that miracles demand truthfulness in the prophetic claim. He then divides imagined evil deeds: those that contradict the miracle (e.g., lying in conveying) and those that do not. The latter are further divided into kufr and other sins, which are then classified as major, minor repulsive, and minor non-repulsive. Additional dimensions include deliberateness vs. forgetfulness, and before or after prophethood. The majority view is that immunity (isma) is required only from what contradicts the miracle. The author then reviews the positions of various groups: al-Qadi permits forgetfulness; the Azariqa permit kufr; the Shi'a permit taqiyya but that is rejected; for deliberate major sins, Ash'aris say revelation forbids, Mu'tazila say reason forbids, and Hashwiyya permit due to lack of proof or because of apparent evidence. This passage is a summary of the earlier detailed discussion, and it highlights the consensus on the core of 'isma while noting divergent views on peripheral aspects.

Objection and Answer on Prophetic Sins
Al-Taftazani presents the objection and his summarized answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Taftazani defends the doctrine of prophetic infallibility against apparent Quranic attributions of sin.

Core Idea
Prophetic 'errors' are forgetfulness or leaving the better, not actual sins.

This passage presents a classic theological objection and its concise answer. Opponents of prophetic infallibility cite verses where prophets repent or are said to sin. Al-Taftazani outlines the Sunni method of dealing with such texts: weak reports are rejected, while strong ones are reinterpreted.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
The first sentence states the opponent's argument: transmitted reports attribute sin and repentance to prophets. Note the types of evidence they use.
Opponents rely on both explicit Quranic verses and hadith.
2
Read the answer
The second sentence gives the summary answer. It has two parts: reject weak (ahaad) reports and interpret strong (mutawatir) reports allegorically.
Take note of the categories: forgetfulness (sahu), leaving the better (tark al-awla), and pre-prophetic events.
3
Reflect on the logic
Consider why these categories preserve infallibility. Forgetting a human action is not a sin; leaving the better is not a transgression; pre-prophetic events do not affect prophethood.
This interpretive principle is key to understanding Sunni 'isma.

Sins are classified by their relation to the miracle, kind, repulsiveness, and timing.

The Ash'ari Position on Prophetic Infallibility
Al-Taftazani lists positions on infallibility and states the Ash'ari madhhab.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari doctrine on prophetic infallibility and how it relates to other sects' positions.

Core Idea
Ash'aris affirm infallibility from major sins absolutely and from intentional minor sins.

Commentary
This passage discusses prophetic infallibility ('isma) specifically regarding minor sins (sagha'ir). The author begins by noting that minor sins that repel followers are also denied because they impair the call. He then summarizes positions: many Mu'tazila deny even major sins before prophethood; some Shi'a deny minor sins even if forgetful; the Sunni (Ash'ari) position is that major sins are prohibited absolutely after prophethood, and minor sins only if intentional, not if forgetful, but prophets are warned and they heed. Some scholars like Imam al-Haramayn and Abu Hashim allowed minor sins intentionally. The author then presents eight arguments (luzum) that would follow if prophets sinned, each argued to be false (muntafi) by consensus or explicit scriptural evidence. The first: following them would be prohibited, but it is obligatory. The second: their testimony would be rejected, but such rejection is for worldly matters, not religious ones. The third: we would have to rebuke them, but that would harm them, which is prohibited. The fourth: they would deserve punishment, curse, etc., but this is repugnant and false by consensus. The fifth: they would not attain the covenant of prophethood (interpreted as prophethood or imamate). The sixth: they would not be sincere servants (mukhlis), yet the Qur'an affirms sincerity for some prophets. The seventh: they would be of Satan's party, which is clearly false. The eighth: they would not be hastening in good deeds, yet the Qur'an says some prophets did. After listing these eight, the author provides a more nuanced rebuttal: the obligation to follow concerns only the Sharia and its transmission, not every personal slip; punishment and rejection of testimony apply only to major sins or persistence in minor sins without repentance; rebuke and punishment only on intentionality and lack of repentance; and even if a sin occurs (forgetfully or even intentionally with repentance), it does not make one absolutely a wrongdoer or of Satan's party. The passage ends mid-sentence discussing universality of good deeds, continuing the argument.

Defending Solomon: Allegations of Idol Worship
Taftazani defends Solomon against claims of idol worship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Taftazani defends Prophet Solomon against serious allegations of idol worship by invoking legal possibility and lack of knowledge.

Core Idea
Even if reports about Solomon seem serious, they may be explained by differences in divine law or ignorance.

This lesson addresses the most controversial narratives about Solomon: that a devil tricked him, took his ring, and even worshipped an idol in his house. Taftazani offers a defense by suggesting that making statues might not have been forbidden in Solomon's law, and he was unaware of the idol worship occurring. This lesson shows how scholars protect prophetic infallibility through careful reasoning.

1
Read the passage

2
Understand the defensive strategy
Taftazani uses two arguments: (1) making statues may not have been forbidden in Solomon's shari'a, (2) the idol worship in his house was unknown to him. How do these preserve his innocence?
This shows the principle of giving the best possible interpretation to reports about prophets.
3
Compare with Islamic teachings on idolatry
Reflect that later Islamic law strictly prohibits idol-making and worship, but earlier prophets had different rulings. This highlights the concept of abrogation and diversity of divine laws.
4
Apply the principle of 'good assumption'
Consider how we should approach questionable reports about religious figures: we should seek possible excuses before condemning, as Taftazani does here.
