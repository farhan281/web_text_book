Mu'tazila States and the Problem of Multiple Pre-Eternals
Explains Mu'tazila doctrine of ahwal and the existence vs. subsistence debate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila theory of states (ahwal), why it was criticized for implying multiple pre-eternal entities, and how the debate hinged on the distinction between existence and subsistence.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila's states are subsistent, not existent—but that still risks multiplicity.

The Mu'tazila, in their zeal to protect divine unity, rejected both temporal pre-eternity for anything other than God and the notion of eternal attributes. Instead, some of them developed the concept of 'states' (ahwal)—real but non-existent properties that subsist with God's essence from eternity. This passage explains the specific states they proposed and the ensuing debate about whether this reintroduces multiple co-eternal entities.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage once without trying to parse every detail. Then read the full translation to get the flow of the argument.
Pay attention to the names: al-Razi (Imam), Abu Hashim, and al-Tusi (the wise investigator).
2
Identify the Mu'tazila states
Locate the five states named in the passage: being-knowing, being-powerful, being-living, being-causing-to-exist (or existence), and being-divine (added by Abu Hashim). Note that they are said to be 'subsistent' in pre-eternity with the essence.
The passage mentions four states initially, then Abu Hashim adds a fifth as the cause of the others.
3
Understand al-Razi's criticism
Al-Razi argues that by affirming these states as subsistent in pre-eternity, the Mu'tazila have effectively admitted multiple pre-eternal entities—since subsistent in pre-eternity is what 'pre-eternal' means.
4
Examine al-Tusi's objection and the rejoinder
Al-Tusi objects that the Mu'tazila distinguish between existence and subsistence; states are subsistent but not existent, so they don't fall under the definition of 'pre-eternal' as 'without beginning of existence'. The rejoinder is that 'existence' and 'subsistence' are synonymous in this context, or one can simply change the wording.
This distinction is key to the Mu'tazila defense.

Commentary
This passage discusses the Mu'tazila's position on divine unity (tawhid) and their concept of states (ahwal). The Mu'tazila went to extremes in denying any co-eternal entities besides God, so they rejected both temporal pre-eternity for anything other than God and denied the existence of eternal attributes (preferring states that are neither existent nor non-existent). However, those who affirm states (the Baghdad school and later Basran school) posited four states: being-knowing (al-alamiyya), being-powerful (al-qadiriyya), being-living (al-hayyiyya), and being-causing-to-exist (al-mujidiyya). Abu Hashim al-Jubba'i added a fifth, being-divine (al-ilahiyya), which is the cause of the other four. This results in multiple things subsisting from pre-eternity, effectively admitting multiple pre-eternals despite their denial. The author then quotes al-Razi's argument from al-Muhassal: the Mu'tazila, by claiming these states are subsistent in pre-eternity with the essence, have affirmed pre-eternal entities. Nasir al-Din al-Tusi objected that the Mu'tazila distinguish between existence (wujud) and subsistence (thubut); the states are subsistent but not existent, so they do not fall under the definition of 'pre-eternal' as 'that which has no beginning for its existence'. The author responds on behalf of al-Razi: we do not mean by 'existence' except what they mean by 'subsistence', so the two are synonymous in this context—there is no difference between 'no beginning for its existence' and 'no beginning for its subsistence'. If one insists on differing wording, we can simply change the term. The passage concludes by criticizing an attribution in al-Mawaqif that the four states are existence, life, knowledge, and power, calling it careless because those are attributes, not states.

The terms 'existential' and 'non-existential' are ambiguous and can refer to actual existence or conceptual involvement of nonexistence.

Commentary
This passage is the second investigation, reporting the dispute between philosophers and theologians on whether unity and multiplicity are existential or non-existential. Philosophers' Three Arguments for the Existentiality of Unity
Sanusi lists three philosophical arguments for unity being existential and declares them corrupt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and evaluate the three arguments the philosophers present for unity being existential, and understand why they are considered corrupt.

Core Idea
The philosophers argue unity is existential because it is a part, a contradictory, and necessary for distinction, but all arguments fail.

Mu'tazila Argument on Knowledge and Ignorance and the Ash'arī Refutation
Mu'tazila claim of similarity and Ash'arī refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Mu'tazila argument that knowledge and ignorance are essentially similar is considered logically flawed by Ash'arī scholars.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila example assumes what it tries to prove.

The Mu'tazila claimed that true knowledge and false belief differ only in their relation to reality, not in essence. To support this, they gave an example where a belief changes from knowledge to ignorance while the underlying mental state remains the same. The Ash'arī response pinpoints a logical problem: the example only works if we already accept that essence remains unchanged, which is exactly the point in dispute. This short but dense passage captures a key sectarian debate in Islamic theology.

Commentary
This passage presents a sectarian argument. The Mu'tazila claim that knowledge and ignorance (or correct and incorrect belief) are essentially similar (mumāthil). Al-Ash'ari's View on Inability and Its Initial Refutation
Presents and refutes al-Ash'ari's claim with analogy.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Ash'ari's argument that inability relates only to existents and the author's refutation using the analogy of knowledge and will.

Core Idea
Inability can relate to non-existents, just like knowledge and will.

Al-Ash'ari argued that inability, as an existent attribute, can only relate to existents—so the inability of a seated person is about sitting, not standing. The author calls this 'stubbornness' and counters that even if inability were existential, there is no rational bar to its relating to non-existents, citing knowledge and will as examples that relate to non-existents.
