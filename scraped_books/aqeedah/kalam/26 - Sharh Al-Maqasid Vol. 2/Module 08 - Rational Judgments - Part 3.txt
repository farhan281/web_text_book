The Objection of al-Qadi and Imam al-Haramayn and Its Answer
Presents and refutes the objection of al-Qadi and Imam al-Haramayn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that one knowledge cannot relate to separable objects and why the answer refutes it by distinguishing between general possibility and actual occurrence.

Core Idea
The possibility of separation does not prevent occasional unity of knowledge.

This passage presents an important objection to the doctrine that one knowledge can relate to multiple objects. Al-Qadi and Imam al-Haramayn argue that if objects are separable, then one knowledge cannot cover both, otherwise a thing would separate from itself. The answer clarifies that separation in general does not imply necessary separation at all times, thus preserving the possibility of a single knowledge relating to multiple objects in some cases.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get the rhythm, then read the translation. Notice the structure: first the objection, then the answer.
The passage uses Quranic footnote markers (۲), (۳), (٤) but they are references, not part of the argument.
2
Understand the objection
Al-Qadi and Imam al-Haramayn argue that if two known objects are separable (knowledge of one can exist without knowledge of the other), then it is impossible for one knowledge to relate to both. Why? Because if one knowledge related to both, then separation would mean that knowledge separates from itself, which is impossible.
The objection assumes that if one knowledge covers both, then the two knowledges are identical, so separation would be a thing separating from itself.
3
Understand the answer
The answer says that it is enough for the possibility of separation that the two objects are known by two separate knowledges in general. This does not contradict the fact that sometimes they are known by one single knowledge, in which case there is no separation. Thus, the objection fails because it mistakenly assumes that separation must always occur, but it only occurs when there are two knowledges.
The key is to distinguish between the possibility of separation (which requires two knowledges) and the actual case of a single knowledge (where there is no separation).

Objection on Conceptual Separability and Its Answer
Addresses the objection of two objects separable in conception and answers it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the objection that assumes two objects are separable in conception is circular and does not refute the claim that one knowledge can cover multiple known objects.

Core Idea
Assuming the very point in dispute does not make an objection valid.

In theological and philosophical arguments, it is common to encounter objections that seem plausible at first but actually presuppose the very point under dispute. This lesson presents such an objection regarding the conceptual separability of two known objects and shows how the author identifies it as circular. By recognizing this fallacy, you strengthen your ability to evaluate arguments critically.

The term 'المتنازع' refers to the matter under contention.
3
Identify the logical fallacy
The objection commits the fallacy of begging the question (circular reasoning). It assumes that two objects can be conceptually separate, which is exactly what the original claim denies.
This is a key critical thinking skill.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical argument about the relationship between willing a thing and hating its opposite. It begins with an example: things that are mutually opposed (متخالفين) like blackness and sweetness can coexist with their opposites (e.g., sourness) and with things that are merely different (e.g., smell). The author then states that this would entail that willing a thing and willing its opposite can coexist, because hating the opposite is itself willing the opposite. This is the initial claim. An answer is given: the lack of union (i.e., not being identical) does not necessarily entail one of the three possibilities (the author does not specify what the three are here). The opponent concedes this but then denies that every mutually opposed thing can coexist with the opposite of the other, because two things might be inseparable (متلازمان), and it is impossible for an inseparable (لازم) to coexist with the opposite of its inseparable (ملزوم). He also gives the example of doubt, which is opposite to knowledge and opinion; doubt cannot coexist with both because they are opposites of each other. Then there is an objection: willing and hating require awareness (شعور) of the object. A thing can be willed or hated without awareness of its opposite, so willing does not entail hating its opposite, let alone being identical. The only way to claim identity is to assume awareness of the opposite; otherwise condition-setting is meaningless. The author then notes a difference of opinion among those who hold that will and hatred are distinct (تغاير). Al-Qadi and al-Ghazali hold that willing a thing entails hating its perceived opposite, because otherwise one would will both opposites, which is impossible (since two wills for opposites are contradictory). The answer to this is to deny both premises: (1) it is possible that no will or hatred attaches to the opposite (as in many perceived things), and (2) it is possible that each opposite is willed from a certain aspect, either equally or with a preference due to greater benefit. Further, if the entailment were true, then hating a thing would entail willing its opposite, leading to paradoxes when a thing has two opposites. The only way out is to either differentiate the aspects (جهتين) or restrict the claim to things with only one opposite. But even allowing differentiation does not refute the judge's claim of entailment, because it could be that each opposite is both willed and hated from different aspects. Finally, an objection that making the object of will concomitant with the will (so that willing opposites entails their simultaneous existence) is weak, because the object of will is not necessarily something within the power of the willer and simultaneous; it can be another's action or future, which would be mere wish (تمني) rather than will. This contradicts language, custom, and verification. The passage thus presents a multi-layered argument against the necessary entailment between will and hatred of opposites.

Denying Motion in Accidental Changes: The Ash'ari Proof
Al-Taftazani refutes motion in quantity/quality changes using Ash'ari atomistic reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari argument that gradual changes in quantity and quality are not true motion but a sequence of instantaneous transitions between distinct states.

Core Idea
Accidental change is not motion; it is a series of atomic instantaneous states with intervening pauses.

Al-Taftazani examines a common illusion: when we see a body gradually change size or color, we assume continuous motion. But the Ash'ari school argues that the intermediate states are actually distinct and reached instantaneously. This lesson unpacks their proof, which relies on the finitude of intermediate states and the impossibility of infinite regress.

Analyze the intermediary
Examine the cases of the intermediary between the start and end: if it is one, no motion; if many, it must be finite (bounded). Finiteness leads to composition of motion from indivisible parts.
The argument uses the impossibility of an infinite number of distinct states between two endpoints.

Grasp the concept of atomic change
Understand that the 'motion' would be composed of indivisible atomic transitions, implying the existence of an indivisible atom (jawhar fard) and that slowness is due to pauses between these atomic changes.
This is a key Ash'ari atomistic view.
4
Contrast with locative motion
Note that locative motion (spatial movement) is different: its medium is continuous and divisible infinitely, so it allows true gradual motion. Accidental changes lack this continuous medium.
This contrast reinforces the proof.

Commentary
This passage presents the Ash'ari proof against the reality of motion in accidental changes (quantity and quality). The author, al-Taftazani, begins by stating the common illusion: when people observe a body changing gradually in size or quality, they assume continuous motion. But he argues that the intermediate states are actually distinct entities, each reached instantaneously, not through a continuous process. The key distinction is between locative motion (al-haraka al-ayniyya), which involves a divisible spatial medium, and accidental changes, where the 'medium' is a discrete set of distinct states. He then provides a logical analysis of the intermediary between the starting and ending points. If the intermediary is a single state, there is no motion (only one change). If there are many states, they must be finite because they are bounded, else an infinite regress would occur. Finite distinct states imply that the 'motion' is composed of indivisible atoms of change (each state is an instantaneous transition), which leads to the notion of an indivisible atom (jawhar fard) and the idea that slowness results from pauses between these atomic changes. He refutes the possibility of continuous motion in such changes by showing that any assumption of divisibility leads to contradiction. This proof is foundational for the Ash'ari atomistic view of the world and contrasts with the Aristotelian notion of continuous change seen in locative motion.

Commentary
This passage covers two interrelated issues. Second, an objection is raised: if motion is defined as a continuous extension (imtidad mawhum), it has no external existence; if defined as an intermediate state (kawn fi al-wasat), it is a universal and cannot be particular to different movers. The author answers that the first interpretation (imtidad) is the apparent one; its parts are imaginary but its individual parts exist successively in reality. A single imaginary continuum can be caused by multiple agents, like a line drawn by multiple scribes. He then notes that Imam al-Razi inclines to the second interpretation, but the author provides a verification: motion as the middle state (tawassut) is an existent thing that persists moment by moment, and it becomes one by identity through the unity of subject, time, and what is in it. Even if the distance is divided conceptually, the motion remains one because the distance is continuous with no actual parts. The criterion for universality is the possibility of positing particulars, not actual parts, which is not possible here. The author concludes with his personal resolution.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophical claim that a moment of rest necessarily occurs between any two straight motions and the rational proof based on the impossibility of a part.

Core Idea
Rest must occur between two straight motions because arrival and return instants cannot be simultaneous.

This passage introduces a key debate in physics and kalam: whether motion is continuous or punctuated by moments of rest. Some philosophers and theologians argued that between any two straight motions, there must be a brief instant of rest. The proof rests on the distinction between the instant of arrival and the instant of return, and the impossibility of time being composed of indivisible parts. Let us examine the claim and its reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the claim carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Note that the claim is that between any two straight motions there is a rest (sukūn).
2
Identify the key terms
Focus on the terms 'ān al-wuṣūl' (instant of arrival) and 'ān al-rujū‘' (instant of return). Understand their distinction.
3
Follow the proof
The proof argues that if no time intervenes between these two instants, then two instants would be consecutive, implying indivisible parts (atomism). Since motion requires time, the interval must be rest.
The word 'al-ānayn' means two instants; note the script.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider why this argument leads to the conclusion of rest. How does the impossibility of a part (juz' lā yatajazzā) support the claim?

Commentary
This passage presents the claim that between any two straight motions there must be a moment of rest. The argument is based on the impossibility of a part (i.e., indivisible particle). The claim states that the instant of arriving at a point is different from the instant of departing from it. If no time elapsed between these two instants, then two instants would occur consecutively without any time between them, which would imply the existence of indivisible parts (atoms). Since there is no motion between arrival and return (motion would require time), the body must be at rest during that interval. This is the core assertion that the subsequent response will address.

Time and Instants: The Foundational Response
Presents the initial response distinguishing actual from estimational divisibility of time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between actual and estimational divisibility of time and its implications for the distinctness of instants.

Core Idea
Time's divisibility is only in estimation, not in reality.

This lesson presents the core response to the initial claim. It clarifies that time is not actually divided into parts except in mental estimation, and if we accept estimational divisibility, then the distinctness of instants and the impossibility of their succession are not granted.

Guided Path
1
Read the response passage
Read the Arabic passage once, noticing the phrase 'بالوهم' and the conditional structure 'اللهم إلا أن'.
2
Identify the two scenarios
Notice that the response first describes time as actually continuous ('بالفعل') unless it is considered divisible only in estimation ('بالوهم'). Mark these two cases.
'بالوهم' means 'by mental estimation', not 'by illusion'.
3
Understand the implication
In the estimational case, the response denies that instants of arrival and return are distinct and denies the impossibility of successive instants. Reflect on why this refutes the initial claim.

The answer: After granting the impossibility of a part, it is [already] actually [the case] as long as time is not cut off. Unless what is meant is a time that is not divisible except in estimation; in that case we do not grant that the two instants of arrival and return are distinct, nor the impossibility of the succession of two instants. As for the objection by every straight motion, especially if we rotate a sphere on a surface, then the instant of arrival at each point is other than the instant of departure from it, and there follows the succession of two instants or the interposition of a time of rest. It may be replied that the division of distance here is merely imaginary. Some philosophers and theologians held that between any two straight motions there is a time in which the moving thing is at rest, whether the second is a return to the first direction or a turn to another direction. There is no hiddenness in that the existence of an angle occurs only on the assumption of turning, not return, because the line is one. So the expression of abstraction—that there is no continuity for the essences of angles and no turning—is not as it should be, and it has been interpreted that there is no continuity for the locative motions that produce points that are points of angles of return, nor for those that produce points that are points of angles of turning.

Commentary
This passage presents the response (الجواب) to the initial claim. It begins by accepting the impossibility of an indivisible part (الجزء) but argues that time is not actually divided—it is only divisible in estimation (بالوهم). If we consider time as only conceptually divisible, then we do not accept that the instants of arrival and return are distinct, nor the impossibility of their succession. Then it addresses a counterexample: a sphere rotating on a surface—each point of contact involves an instant of arrival and departure, which would seem to require either successive instants or rest. But this is answered by saying the division of distance is merely imaginary. The passage then discusses the view of some philosophers and theologians that rest occurs between two straight motions, whether the second is a return or a turn to another direction. It critiques the statement that there is no continuity for angles and turning, explaining that the supposed lack of continuity refers to the locative motions that produce points of angles. This lesson covers the nuanced responses and clarifications regarding the nature of time, instants, and motion.
