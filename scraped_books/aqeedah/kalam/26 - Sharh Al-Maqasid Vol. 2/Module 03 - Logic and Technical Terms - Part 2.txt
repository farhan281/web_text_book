Does Multiplicity Necessitate Distinction?
Objection and response on the necessity of distinction, leading to the early Sunni position on multiplicity and التغاير.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that while father-son and cause-effect pairs are distinct in essence, multiplicity does not necessarily entail التغاير according to early Sunni scholars, who require existence and separability.

Core Idea
Multiplicity does not guarantee distinction; existence and separability are required.

This passage begins with a natural objection: aren't father and son, or cause and effect, automatically distinct? The author agrees they are distinct in essence, but the real point of contention is the converse: does multiplicity necessarily imply التغاير? The early Sunni position clarifies that التغاير requires more than just multiplicity — it requires both entities to exist and to be separable from each other.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Read the first two sentences carefully: the objection claims necessary distinction; the response affirms it 'with respect to essence'.
Notice the objector's example pairs are conceptually distinct, but the author immediately shifts to a different question.
2
Identify the point of disagreement
The text says 'the disagreement is in its converse' — that is, whether multiplicity (being multiple) necessitates التغاير. Identify the early Sunni position on this.
The early Sunni scholars said no.
3
Understand the early Sunni definition of التغاير
They defined التغاير as an existential property: two distinct things must both exist and be separable. Hence non-existents and pairs of existent/non-existent are excluded.
This definition is based on the idea that التغاير is 'وجودي' like difference and opposition.
4
Reflect on why non-existents are excluded
Consider why a non-existent cannot be متغاير with another non-existent or with an existent: because التغاير requires existence, and there is no distinction among non-existents.
This ties back to the existential nature of التغاير.

For it is said (1): The distinctness of something like the father and the son (2), and the cause and the effect is necessary (3). We said: Yes, with respect to essence (4). For it (distinctness) can only be conceived between multiple things. But the disagreement is in its converse: whether multiplicity necessitates التغاير. According to the early scholars (متقدمين) of Ahl al-Sunna, it does not. Hence they said: The two distinct things are two existents whose separation is possible. Thus the two non-existents are excluded, as well as an existent and a non-existent. This is based on the fact that التغاير with them is existential, like difference and opposition, so non-existents are not described by it. As for the explanation that there is no distinction among non-existents, it is specific to the two non-existents. And the part with the whole is excluded, and likewise the described with the attribute, because their separation is impossible. And two bodies are included, even if we assume them to be eternal, because they separate by one existing in a spatial location where the other does not exist. Likewise, the separable attribute (الصفة المفارقة) with its described, whether it is eternal or originated, because they separate by the described existing and the attribute ceasing to exist. Thus the permissibility of separation is more general: whether it is according to spatial location or according to existence and non-existence. So there is no need to restrict by saying 'in a spatial location or non-existence' as the Shaykh mentioned.

Commentary
This passage addresses an objection and expands on the criteria for التغاير. The objector argues that pairs like father and son, or cause and effect, are necessarily distinct (متغايران). The author agrees that they are distinct in essence (بحسب الذات), but this does not conflict with the earlier definition because distinction in essence still requires conceptual separability: each can be conceived without the other. The real point of contention is the converse: does multiplicity (تعدد) necessarily imply التغاير? The early Sunni scholars (متقدمين من أهل السنة) say no. They maintain that two things are distinct only if they are both existent and their separation is possible. Thus, non-existents (معدومان) and pairs of one existent and one non-existent are excluded. This is based on the principle that التغاير is an existential property like difference and opposition; non-existents cannot be described by such properties. The author then applies the rule: the part and whole, and the attribute and described, are excluded because separation is impossible (امتناع الانفكاك). Two bodies, even if eternal, are included because they separate by spatial location (حيز). Separable attributes (الصفة المفارقة), like accidents that can cease, are included because the described can exist without the attribute. Therefore, the criterion of 'possibility of separation' is broader than spatial separation; it includes separation by existence and non-existence. The author notes that there is no need to add phrases like 'in a spatial location or non-existence' as the Shaykh (الشيخ) did.

Applying the Criterion: Which Pairs Are Distinct?
Application of the التغاير criterion to specific pairs, concluding that the possibility of separation is broader than spatial separation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the practical application of the criterion of التغاير: which pairs qualify (like bodies and separable attributes) and which are excluded (like part-whole and substance-attribute), and why the possibility of separation can be by spatial location or by existence and non-existence.

Core Idea
Distinction is broader than spatial separation — it includes separation in existence.

After establishing that التغاير requires existential separability, the author now applies this rule to various cases: part-whole, attribute-substance, bodies, and separable attributes. This shows that the criterion of 'possibility of separation' covers both spatial and existential separation, making it simpler and more comprehensive than requiring a specific type of separation.

The Inseparable and Eternal Attributes
Definition of the middle category of attributes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the concept of an attribute that is neither identical to its subject nor distinct, and the two opinions about its nature: essential inseparable attribute vs. eternal attribute.

Core Idea
Attributes that are neither the same as nor other than their subject are either essential inseparable or eternal.

This passage draws an implication about distinctness and identifies a key category of attributes. The author explains that separation from one side suffices for distinctness and then defines the attribute that is neither the described nor other than it. Prepare to encounter two views: one identifying it as the essential inseparable attribute, another as the eternal attribute.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage carefully
Read the first part of the passage, focusing on the terms 'الصفة اللازمة النفسية' and 'الصفة القديمة'. Note the examples: God's knowledge and power vs. blackness and whiteness.
The passage says 'وقيل بل الصفة القديمة' indicating a difference of opinion.
2
Understand the implication about distinctness
The passage begins by saying 'يكفي في التغاير الانفكاك من جانب'. Think about what it means for two things to be distinct if separation is possible from one side only.
3
Identify the middle category
The attribute is neither the described (عين الموصوف) nor other than it (غيره). Identify both opinions presented and consider which one the author might favor.

The Linguistic Argument and Its Refutation
Critique of the linguistic argument about 'other than'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the common linguistic argument 'there is no one in the house other than Zayd' fails as a metaphysical proof and leads to absurd conclusions.

Core Idea
Linguistic usage is not a reliable guide for metaphysical distinctness, as it can lead to absurdities.

The author now critiques the main argument of those who rely on everyday language to deny distinctness between a thing and its attributes. The example 'There is no one in the house other than Zayd' is examined to show its logical flaws. Objections to the Definition of al-Mutaghayirayn and Responses
Objection to the definition of al-mutaghayirayn and responses by al-Amidi and others.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the debate around the definition of al-mutaghayirayn (two distinct things) and the role of intellectual conception (ta'aqqul) in distinguishing entities that are inseparable in reality.

Core Idea
Distinction can be conceptual even if separation is impossible in reality.

This passage dives into a vital debate in Islamic theology: what does it truly mean for two things to be 'distinct'? The definition of al-mutaghayirayn as 'two existents whose dissociation is possible' faces a powerful objection: the world and the Creator are clearly distinct, yet the world cannot exist without the Creator. How can they be 'dissociable'? The answers offered by al-Amidi and others reveal deep insights about the nature of distinction, reality, and intellectual conception. Let's follow the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial objection
Read the first sentence of the passage. It states an objection against the definition of al-mutaghayirayn: the definition is not comprehensive because the world and the Creator are distinct (mutaghayirayn) yet their separation (infikak) is impossible—the world cannot exist without the Creator.
Notice that the objector assumes that 'dissociation possible' must mean actual separation.
2
Examine al-Amidi's first answer
Al-Amidi responds that dissociation from one side is sufficient. It is possible for the world not to exist while the Creator exists. This preserves distinctness even though the reverse is impossible.
The key phrase: 'انفكاك من جانب واحد' (dissociation from one side).
3

The refutation argues that if one-sided dissociation suffices, then the definition is not exclusive (mani'). It would include relationships like part-whole and subject-attribute, where the part can exist without the whole, etc., but they are not considered distinct in the intended sense.
This refutation forces a refinement of the definition.
4
Learn the second answer: intellectual conception
Some scholars answer that what is meant is the permissibility of dissociation in intellectual conception (fi al-ta'aqqul), not in external existence. Even if the world cannot exist without the Creator, we can conceive of the world without conceiving the Creator (though we need proof to know Him).
This aligns with the Mu'tazila view that two things are distinct if one can be known without the other.
5
Resolve the final objection about the world as effect
A final objection says that the world as an effect cannot be conceived without its cause. The answer: distinctness is based on dissociation in essence (dhat) and reality (haqiqa), not in conception. The passage ends with 'wa la 'ibra' (no consideration), implying that conceptual inseparability does not negate real distinctness.
This shows the ultimate criterion is ontological, not merely conceptual.

Commentary

Commentary

Opposition in Propositions: Objection and Answer
Objection about opposition in propositions with a two-aspect answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection that propositions lack a locus is resolved through two distinct aspects: homonymy and a generalized view of opposition of affirmation and negation.

Core Idea
Opposition in propositions works analogously to terms with a shift from truth to existence.

The objector raises a problem: logical relations like contradiction and contrariety seem to apply to propositions, yet propositions lack a single subject or locus like terms do. How can two propositions be opposed without a common subject? The answer provides two complementary perspectives that clarify the nature of propositional opposition.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Understand the problem: the objector claims that opposition occurs in propositions (e.g., contradiction, contrariety), but propositions have no single locus (maḥall) like terms do. Why is this a problem?
2
Analyze the first aspect of the answer

For terms, they consider truth (ṣidq) of being true of something; for propositions, they consider existence (wujūd) of being true in themselves. This aspect distinguishes the two domains by shifting the criterion.

Ibn Sina's Definition of Ijab and Salab as Opposition
Defines Ijab and Salab as a type of opposition with Ibn Sina's wording.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ibn Sina's definition of affirmation (ījāb) and negation (salab) as a type of opposition.

Core Idea
Affirmation and negation are opposites defined by existence or non-existence in any sense.

This passage introduces a key logical concept from Ibn Sina: the opposition between affirmation and negation. It provides a concrete example and then defines each term precisely. Understanding this distinction is foundational for classical logic and kalam.

Commentary
This passage defines the opposition of affirmation (ījāb) and negation (salab) as a type of opposition (taqābul). It first gives an example: "horse, and Zayd is not a horse" – applying these two meanings (being a horse and not being a horse) to one subject at one time is impossible, illustrating contradiction. The term "al-salab" appears as a heading or indicator. Then it quotes Ibn Sīnā: this is a type of opposition called ījāb and salab. Ījāb means existence (wujūd) in any sense, whether existence in itself or existence for something else. Salab means non-existence (lā wujūd) in any sense, whether non-existence in its essence or non-existence in something else. The definition covers both essential and relational existence.
