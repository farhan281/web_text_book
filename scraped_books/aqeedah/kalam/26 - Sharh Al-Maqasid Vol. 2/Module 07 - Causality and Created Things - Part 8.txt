The Criterion for Opposing Motions
Defines the condition for two motions to be opposites and why ascending/descending meet it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why only ascending and descending motions are considered opposites in classical kalam, and what condition distinguishes them from other motion pairs.

Core Idea
Opposition requires extreme difference of species, not just opposite directions.

In this passage, the author establishes a precise criterion for two motions to be considered opposites (mutadaddan). He argues that mere opposite directions are insufficient; the motions must belong to two distinct species within a genus, with the maximum possible difference. Ascending and descending from the center to the circumference and back meet this condition, while other pairs like rectilinear back-and-forth or curved motions do not. This lesson unpacks that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation once to get the overall argument. Notice the author's examples: ascending/descending, rectilinear return motion, and curved motions.
Pay attention to the phrase 'ghāyat al-khilāf' (extreme difference) – it is the key concept.
2
Identify the condition for opposition
The author states that ascending and descending are opposites because they cannot coexist at one time/direction and are two species with extreme difference. What exactly does this mean?
Contrast with rectilinear motion from point A to B and back – that is one species, not two.
3
Understand why curved motions fail the condition
The author argues that between any two points there are infinite arcs, and no curved motion is at the extreme because there is always a more curved one. This shows that extreme difference is not achieved.
The discussion about 'absolute curvature' shows that only specific instances exist, and each has a more different one.

Commentary
This passage defines the condition for two motions to be considered opposites (mutadāddān). The author argues that ascending (al-ṣā‘idah) and descending (al-hābiṭah) are opposites because they are two existent things that cannot coexist at the same time and direction, and they are two species (naw‘ān) of a genus (jins) with extreme difference (ghāyat al-khilāf) between them. This is contrasted with rectilinear motion from one point to another and back, which is considered one species because the path is reversible without qualitative difference. Similarly, a straight and a curved motion, or two curved motions, even if one is above and the other below, are not at extreme difference because between any two points there are infinitely many arcs (qusiyyun), with the larger arc being more curved (ashaddu inḥinā’) and thus more different—but never at an extreme. The author then notes that absolute curvature (muṭlaq al-inḥinā’) cannot be considered because it does not exist in external reality except in a specific instance, and any specific instance has a more different one above it. Thus, only ascending and descending from the center to the circumference and back meet the criterion.

Three Conditions for Opposition of Motions

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three specific scenarios where motion opposition is evaluated, including the role of starting points, endpoints, and the concept of extreme difference.

Core Idea
Opposition of motions requires opposite start and end points, not just opposite directions.

This passage from Sharh al-Maqasid examines the precise conditions under which two motions are considered opposites. The author identifies three investigations, including motions along an arc, rectilinear motions, and the necessity of both starting and ending points being opposite. The discussion also engages with Ibn Sina's view on water's natural motions.

The first: The arc that touches the convexity of the encompassing sphere is at extreme difference, so motion upon it should be opposite to rectilinear motion due to the truth of the definition with all its conditions. Commentary
This passage presents three specific investigations (mawāḍi‘ baḥth) into the conditions for opposition of motions. The first investigation argues that the arc (al-qaws) that touches the convex surface of the encompassing celestial sphere is at the extreme of difference from a straight line, so motion along that arc should be opposite to rectilinear motion, fulfilling all conditions of the definition of opposition. The second investigation shows that even rectilinear ascending and descending are not always at extreme difference. For example, ascending from the earth's surface to the sphere of fire is less different from descending from fire to earth than from other ascents. The third investigation clarifies that the criterion for opposition is the opposition of both the starting point (mabda’) and the endpoint (muntaḥā) together. If either is the same, the motions are not opposite. For instance, ascending from the center to a region of air and descending from the circumference to the same air have the same endpoint, so they are not opposites. Similarly, if the starting point is the same. The passage cites Ibn Sina's view that water's natural motion from above air and from under earth are not opposite because they both end at the same limit (earth's surface). The Imam (al-Imām) explains that the distance between the motions of fire and earth is greater than that between water's two motions, so water's motions are not at extreme distance. Consequently, genuine opposition in local motions only occurs between ascent from center to circumference and descent from circumference to center. The commentators also affirm that a stone's upward and downward motions, whether by force (qasr) or nature (ṭab‘), are opposites.

The Imam's Criterion: Opposition of Descriptions
Presents the Imam's refined criterion for motion opposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Imam's refined criterion that motion opposition depends on the opposition of the descriptions of being a starting point and an endpoint, not the points themselves.

Core Idea
Motion opposes motion when their starting and ending descriptions are opposites, not the points.

In this fourth investigation, al-Imām refines the criterion for opposition of motions. He argues that it is not the physical points that matter, but their roles as 'start' and 'end'. This subtle shift has important implications for understanding how motions relate. Let's read his exact formulation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that Sanusi uses a very compressed style here.
2
Identify the key elements
The passage presents the Imam's view: opposition of motion requires opposition of the starting point and endpoint in terms of their descriptions (being start or end), not the essences themselves.
The essential relation (al-ta‘alluq al-dhātī) is to the descriptions, not the points.
3
Understand the implication

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth investigation, which elaborates the Imam's (al-Imām) refined criterion for opposition of motions. The Imam considers the opposition of starting point and endpoint in terms of their descriptions (waṣf) as 'being a starting point' (al-mabda’iyyah) and 'being an endpoint' (al-muntahā’iyyah), not the points themselves as essences. A second objection follows: if so, then every rectilinear motion from one point to another and its return should be opposites, and even circular motions like from the beginning of Aries to the beginning of Libra and back, with a real path through the zodiac signs. The objector claims this cannot be refuted by saying sequential order and reverse sequential order are not opposites because each motion does the same thing but in alternate halves. The example of the descender from Cancer to Capricorn is given, but the text breaks off at the page end, leaving the answer incomplete.

Commentary
This passage presents two objections against the theory that slow motion (al-baṭʾ) is caused by intermittent rests (takhallul al-sakanāt) interspersed between motions, along with their responses. The author concludes that the arguments for the original theory are strong but the answers are weak.

Why Successive 'Now's Entail a Part but a Single 'Now' Does Not

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A single 'now' is a boundary; successive 'nows' compose an extension.

The opponent raises a key question: if a single 'now' does not require a part, why would two successive 'nows' require one? The author clarifies the difference between a boundary and a composition, using the analogy of points and lines.

Guided Path
1

2

The complete cause of rest—whether natural, voluntary, or forced—operates only when there is no preponderant cause of motion. This differs from motion, which can have multiple causes combining because motion accepts intensity and weakness, as in a stone thrown downward, where the initial thrust and gravity combine as intensification, not composition. The footnote notes a variant reading: 'categories' instead of 'man'.
