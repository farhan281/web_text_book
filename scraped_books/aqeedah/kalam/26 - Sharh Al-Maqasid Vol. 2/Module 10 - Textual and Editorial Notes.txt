Chapter Outline: Sections on Quantity
Lists chapter sections on quantity and its topics

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structural organization of the chapter on quantity, including its three main sections and the topics of the second chapter.

Core Idea
The chapter on quantity is divided into general rulings, time, and space.

This brief passage from al-Maqasid serves as a roadmap for the upcoming discussion on quantity (al-kamm). It lists the three key sections within the chapter and then announces a second chapter dedicated entirely to quantity. Understanding this structure will help you follow the subsequent detailed investigations.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage carefully
Read the Arabic text once to get a sense of its concise style. Notice that it consists of two parts: a list of three sections and a statement about a new chapter.
The passage is very short but packs a lot of structural information.
2
Identify the three sections of the first chapter
The passage begins with 'الأول : في أحكامه الكلية الثاني : في الزمان الثالث : في المكان'. These are the three sections: general rulings, time, and space. Note that they are numbered sequentially.
The pronoun 'ه' in 'أحكامه' likely refers to the subject of the chapter (quantity or perhaps a broader category).
3
Understand the announcement of the second chapter
The passage then says 'الفصل الثاني في الكم وفيه مباحث'. This tells us that the second chapter is on quantity and contains various investigations. This is a separate chapter from the one whose sections were just listed.
This indicates a two-chapter structure: first chapter on something (maybe quality?), second on quantity.
4
Reflect on the purpose of such outlines
Ask yourself why the author includes such a detailed table of contents. It helps the reader navigate the text and anticipate the logical flow of arguments.
In long scholarly works, such outlines are essential for comprehension.

Three Quranic Meanings of the Heart (Qalb)
The footnote enumerates three Quranic meanings of the word 'qalb'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three distinct meanings of the word 'qalb' (heart) in the Quran and why this lexical analysis matters for theological interpretation.

Core Idea
The Quranic 'heart' means intellect, opinion, or the physical organ—context decides.

This footnote from the commentary unpacks the word 'qalb', a key term in Islamic theology. Before diving into theological arguments about the heart as the locus of knowledge, it is essential to clarify what the Quran itself means by 'heart'. The author lists three distinct meanings, drawing on linguistic authority and scriptural evidence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice that the footnote starts by equating qalb with fu'ād and then introduces the three meanings.
The passage includes a hadith distinguishing qulūb and af'ida.
2
Identify the three meanings
The passage lists three meanings of 'qalb' in the Quran. Find each one and note the Quranic verse cited as evidence. Consider how each meaning is derived from the context of the verse.
The three meanings are: intellect, opinion/planning, and the physical heart.
3
Reflect on theological importance
Ask yourself why this lexical clarification is necessary. How might confusion about the meaning of 'qalb' affect understanding of theological statements like 'the locus of knowledge is the heart'?
This connects to later discussions in kalam about where knowledge resides.

(1) القلب : الفؤاد وقد يعبر به عن العقل وقال الفراء القلب العقل . وقيل القلب أخص من الفؤاد ومنه الحديث أتاكم أهل اليمن أرق قلوباً وألين أفئدة وقد ورد القلب في القرآن على ثلاثة معان . الأول : بمعنى العقل: إن في ذلك لذكرى لمن كان له قلب الثاني : بمعنى الرأي. والتدبير وقلوبهم شتى أي آراؤهم مختلفة، الثالث: بمعنى حقيقة القلب الذي في الصدر ولكن تعمى القلوب التي في الصدور). سورة الحج آية رقم ٤٦ .
(1) Al-qalb: the heart (al-fu'ād), and it is used to mean intellect ('aql). Al-Farrā' said: al-qalb is the intellect. It is said that al-qalb is more specific than al-fu'ād, and from that is the hadith: 'The people of Yemen have come to you, the most tender of hearts (qulūb) and the softest of insides (af'ida).' And al-qalb occurs in the Quran in three meanings. First: in the sense of intellect: 'Indeed in that is a reminder for whoever has a heart' (Qaf 37). Second: in the sense of opinion and planning: 'And their hearts are diverse' (al-Tawba 8) meaning their opinions differ. Third: in the sense of the actual heart in the chest: 'But it is the hearts within the chests that become blind' (al-Hajj 46).
Source pages p. 330

Commentary
This footnote provides a lexical analysis of the word 'qalb'. It begins by equating qalb with fu'ād, and notes that it can be used to mean intellect ('aql). Al-Farrā' is cited as defining qalb as intellect. It is also reported that qalb is more specific than fu'ād, supported by a hadith distinguishing between qulūb and af'ida. Then the footnote enumerates three distinct Quranic meanings: (1) intellect, as in Qaf 37; (2) opinion and planning, as in al-Tawba 8; and (3) the physical heart in the chest, as in al-Hajj 46. This analysis helps clarify that when the text says 'the locus of knowledge is the heart,' it does not necessarily mean the physical organ, since the Quran uses 'heart' to mean intellect.

Seven Quranic Meanings of Rūḥ
Lists the seven Quranic meanings of 'rūḥ' with proof verses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the word 'rūḥ' has seven distinct meanings in the Quran, each with a specific context.

Core Idea
The Quran uses 'rūḥ' in seven different ways, from mercy to Jesus.

Before discussing the spirit as a locus of knowledge, the author clarifies that 'rūḥ' is not a single concept. In the Quran, it appears with seven distinct meanings. This lesson enumerates them, showing the richness of the term.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on Quranic meanings
Carefully read the Arabic and translation, noting how each meaning is introduced with 'بمعنى'.
The first meaning is mercy, then angel, Jibril, revelation, Jesus, Adam, and the subtle life-entity.
2
Identify the seven categories
List each of the seven meanings and the verse associated with each. Notice the variety: some are entities (angels, Jesus), others are abstract (mercy, revelation).
3
Understand the underlying connection
Reflect on why these diverse meanings share the same word 'rūḥ'. The common thread is the idea of a life-giving or empowering force from God.
This prepares for the later classification of spirits.

(۲) الروح : اسماً للنفس، وذلك لكون للنفس بعض الروح. فهو كتسمية النوع باسم الجنس . نحو تسمية الإنسان بالحيوان والروح في القرآن على سبعة أوجه الأول بمعنى الرحمة وأيدهم بروح منه أى رحمة، والثاني بمعنى الملك يوم يقوم الروح والملائكة صفا الثالث : بمعنى جبريل نزل به الروح الأمين الرابع : بمعنى الوحي والقرآن أوحينا إليك روحاً من أمرنا الخامس بمعنى عيسى عليه السلام فنفخنا فيه من روحنا السادس : في شأن آدم عليه السلام ونفخت فيه من روحي السابع : بمعنى اللطيفة التي فيها مدد الحياة يسألونك عن الروح. وأقسام الروح من حيث العلم ثلاثة أنواع : حيواني وطبيعي ونفساني. فمركز الروح الحيواني القلب، ومركز الروح الطبيعي الدم ومحل الروح النفساني الدماغ . وأما حقيقة الروح : فهي لطيفة ربانية وعنصر من عناصر العالم العلوي تتصل بمدد رباني إلى العالم السفلي، وعلى حسب درجة الحيوانات وتفاوت الحالات التي لهم تتصل بهم . راجع بصائر ذوي التمييز جـ ٣ ص ١٠٥ : ١٠٦).
(2) Al-rūḥ: it is a name for the soul (al-nafs), because the soul has some of the spirit. So it is like naming the species by the genus, such as naming a human as 'animal'. And al-rūḥ occurs in the Quran in seven aspects: First: in the meaning of mercy: 'and supported them with a spirit from Him' (al-Mujadila 22), i.e., mercy. Second: in the meaning of the angel: 'The day when the spirit and the angels stand in rows' (al-Naba' 38). Third: in the meaning of Jibril: 'The trustworthy spirit has brought it down' (al-Shu'ara 193). Fourth: in the meaning of revelation and the Quran: 'We have revealed to you a spirit from Our command' (al-Shura 52). Fifth: in the meaning of Jesus, peace be upon him: 'We breathed into him of Our spirit' (al-Anbiya 91). Sixth: concerning Adam, peace be upon him: 'and breathed into him of My spirit' (al-Hijr 29). Seventh: in the meaning of the subtle entity that contains the flow of life: 'They ask you about the spirit' (al-Isra 85). And the divisions of the spirit in terms of knowledge are three types: animal (ḥayawānī), natural (ṭabī'ī), and psychic (nafsānī). The center of the animal spirit is the heart, the center of the natural spirit is the blood, and the locus of the psychic spirit is the brain. As for the reality of the spirit: it is a divine subtle entity and an element from the elements of the higher world, connecting by a divine flow to the lower world, and it is connected to animals according to their degree and the variation of their states. (Refer to Basā'ir Dhawī al-Tamyīz, vol. 3, pp. 105-106).

After listing the meanings, it classifies the spirit from a scientific perspective into three types: animal spirit (al-rūḥ al-ḥayawānī) centered in the heart, natural spirit (al-rūḥ al-ṭabī'ī) centered in the blood, and psychic spirit (al-rūḥ al-nafsānī) located in the brain. Finally, it defines the reality of the spirit as a divine subtle entity (laṭīfa rabbāniyya) that connects the higher and lower worlds and relates to animals according to their degree. This footnote enriches the understanding of 'rūḥ' mentioned in the main text as the locus of knowledge.

Classification of Spirits and Their Reality
Classifies the spirit into three types and defines its reality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three divisions of the spirit (animal, natural, psychic) and its ultimate reality as a divine subtle entity.

Core Idea
Spirits are of three types, centered in heart, blood, and brain, but all are divine subtle entities.

After listing the Quranic meanings, the author turns to a scientific classification of the spirit based on knowledge (ʻilm). He identifies three types: animal, natural, and psychic, each with a specific bodily locus. Finally, he defines the spirit's reality as a divine subtle entity connecting the higher and lower worlds.

Read the passage on classification

2

The animal spirit's center is the heart, the natural spirit's center is the blood, and the psychic spirit's locus is the brain.
This shows a medieval understanding of physiology.
3
Understand the ultimate reality
The author then defines the spirit as a divine subtle entity (laṭīfa rabbāniyya) that connects the higher and lower worlds and animates animals according to their states.
This is the metaphysical definition, beyond the physical classification.
