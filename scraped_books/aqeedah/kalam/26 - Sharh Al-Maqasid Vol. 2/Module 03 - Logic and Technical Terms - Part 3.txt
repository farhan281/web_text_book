Types of Taḍādd and Malaka wa ‘Adam and Their Naming
Defines the two types of Taḍādd and Malaka wa ‘Adam and explains the shift in terminology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two types of contrariety (taḍādd) and the two types of possession-privation (malaka wa ‘adam), as well as how the terms 'real' (ḥaqīqī) and 'well-known' (mashhūrī) are applied differently to each category.

Core Idea
Contrariety and possession-privation each have a common and a real sense, but the naming flips between logic and philosophy.

This passage from Sharh al-Maqasid clarifies a subtle but important distinction in the classification of opposites. The terms 'real' (ḥaqīqī) and 'well-known' (mashhūrī) are used differently for contrariety (taḍādd) and for possession-privation (malaka wa ‘adam). Understanding this shift is essential for interpreting philosophical texts correctly, as the same term can refer to different types of opposition in different contexts.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its translation to get an overview of the distinctions being made. Pay attention to the conditions mentioned for each type.
2
Identify the two types of taḍādd
Notice that when extreme difference (ghāyat al-khilāf) is considered, it is called ḥaqīqī (real) taḍādd; without it, it is called mashhūrī (well-known) taḍādd. Understand which is more general.
The absolute (without condition) is more general.
3
Identify the two types of malaka wa ‘adam
For possession-privation, when the subject's preparedness (isti‘dād al-maḥall) is considered, it is called mashhūrī; without that condition, it is called ḥaqīqī. Again, note which is more general.
Here the absolute is called ḥaqīqī, opposite to taḍādd.
4
Examine the examples given
The passage gives examples like white vs. yellow (mashhūrī taḍādd) and sight vs. its absence from a tree (absolute malaka wa ‘adam). It also lists examples where one opposite is a privation of the other (e.g., rest for motion). Consider how these fit the definitions.
5
Understand the terminology reversal
Note that in logic, the definitions are as initially stated, but in philosophy (ḥikma), an additional condition is added for each. Consequently, in taḍādd, the absolute is called mashhūrī and the restricted ḥaqīqī; in malaka wa ‘adam, the absolute is called ḥaqīqī and the restricted mashhūrī. This reversal is a key point.
Memorize the reversed naming to avoid confusion.

Commentary
This passage defines and explains the two types of taḍādd (contrariety) and the two types of malaka wa ‘adam (possession and privation), and clarifies the shift in terminology between the domains of logic and philosophy. It first states that when extreme difference (ghāyat al-khilāf) is considered in taḍādd, it is called real (ḥaqīqī) taḍādd, and without it, it is called well-known (mashhūrī) taḍādd. For malaka wa ‘adam, the condition is the preparedness of the subject (isti‘dād al-maḥall) for existence at that time, and that specified sense is called mashhūrī, while the absolute sense is called ḥaqīqī. The absolute (al-muṭlaq) is more general than the restricted (al-muqayyad). Then it mentions a consequence: by this consideration, the opposition of white with yellow enters into the divisions of opposition (since it is a type of mashhūrī taḍādd). Similarly, the opposition of sight and its absence from a tree (i.e., a tree's lack of sight) is an example of malaka wa ‘adam in the absolute sense. However, it is noted that the philosophers stated that in mashhūrī taḍādd, one opposite may be a privation of the other, giving examples like rest for motion, darkness for light, illness for health, etc. And extreme difference is also a condition in mashhūrī? This seems contradictory to the earlier statement, but the text continues: The interpretation of taḍādd and malaka wa ‘adam that was given earlier is what the ancient philosophers presented in logic. But in philosophy (ḥikma), they added a further condition: for taḍādd, there must be extreme difference (like black vs. white, not white vs. yellow); for malaka wa ‘adam, the privation must be the absence of existence from something that is naturally disposed for it at that time (like lack of beard from a youth who should have one, not from a boy). Thus, the absolute sense in taḍādd is called mashhūrī (common), and the restricted sense is called ḥaqīqī (real). For malaka wa ‘adam, the terminology is reversed: the absolute is called ḥaqīqī, and the restricted mashhūrī.

Objection to the Fourfold Classification and the Later Philosophers' Response
Presents a logical objection, the later philosophers' answer, and the beginning of a critique.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical objection to the fourfold classification of opposites, the later philosophers' defense, and the beginning of al-Sanūsī's critique concerning mashhūrī taḍādd.

Core Idea
Classifications of opposites are not self-evident; they require careful definition and defense.

Logical classifications can seem neat, but exceptions often arise. This lesson explores an objection to the standard fourfold classification of opposition (taḍādd, malaka wa ‘adam, ījāb wa salab, and taḥawwul?). The objector points to pairs like white-yellow that lack extreme difference, and cases like sight-absence in scorpions. The later philosophers defend the classification by appealing to a broader notion of opposition. However, the author—Imām al-Sanūsī—begins to critique their answer, revealing subtleties in how opposites are defined.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation

2
Understand the objection
Identify why pairs like white-yellow and sight-absence in scorpions supposedly fall outside the fourfold classification.
The objector argues they are neither real opposites (taddād) nor possession-privation in the specific sense.

Follow the later philosophers' answer
How do the later philosophers (al-muta’akhkhirūn) respond? They say the classification is based on 'the more important meaning' (al-ma‘nā al-ahamm), i.e., mashhūrī taḍādd and haqīqī malaka wa ‘adam.
4
Examine the author's first criticism
The author says 'fīhi naẓar' and gives the first reason: in mashhūrī taḍādd, one opposite can be a privation of the other, which causes overlap with possession-privation and affirmation-negation. Why is this a problem?
This undermines the neat separation of categories.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection (qādih) against the standard fourfold classification of opposition (taḍādd, malaka wa ‘adam, ījāb wa salab, and taghyūr?). The objector points to pairs like white-yellow, black-red, etc., which lack extreme difference (ghāyat al-khilāf), and also examples like having a beard and manliness (al-iltiḥā’ wa al-murū’a), and the opposition of sight and its absence in scorpions or trees. These fall outside both taḍādd (since no extreme difference) and malaka wa ‘adam in the specific sense (since the subject is not naturally disposed for sight). The later philosophers (al-muta’akhkhirūn) answered that the enumeration is based on the more important meaning (al-ma‘nā al-ahamm), i.e., mashhūrī taḍādd (which does not require extreme difference) and ḥaqīqī malaka wa ‘adam (which requires natural disposition). This would include white-yellow as a kind of mashhūrī taḍādd, and scorpions lacking sight as an example of malaka wa ‘adam in the absolute sense? The author then criticizes this answer with "wa fīhi naẓar" (there is consideration). First, because in mashhūrī taḍādd, the two opposites do not have to be both existents; one can be a privation of the other, as Ibn Sīnā stated, with examples like rest/motion, darkness/light, etc. Therefore, mashhūrī taḍādd overlaps with the opposition of possession-privation and also affirmation-negation. Moreover, in the philosophers' own usage, the term 'taḍādd' sometimes refers to real taḍādd and sometimes to parts of malaka wa ‘adam, specifically cases where the subject can transition from privation to possession (like rest/motion), unlike permanent privations (like blindness/sight). The passage ends mid-sentence, but clearly the criticism undermines the neat fourfold classification.

Reduction of Even-Odd Opposition
Explains that even-odd opposition reduces to affirmation-negation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the opposition of even and odd numbers reduces to the logical opposition of affirmation and negation.

Core Idea
Even and odd opposition is just affirmation and negation in disguise.

This passage from Sharh al-Maqasid investigates the nature of the opposition between even and odd numbers. At first glance, this might seem like a primitive contrast, but upon closer analysis, it reduces to the logical opposition of affirmation and negation. Let's see why.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage about even and odd. Notice how the author defines even and odd.
2
Understand the reduction
The author explains that even is a number divisible into equal parts (affirmation) and odd is not divisible (negation). So the opposition is between having a property and not having it.
3
Recognize the source
Note that this reasoning is attributed to Ibn Sina. This shows the continuity of Islamic logic with Aristotle.

أعم من ذلك إذ لا يمكن الانتقال في النطق، والعجمة، وفي الذكورة والأنوثة، وفي الزوجية والفردية، على أن تقابل الزوجية والفردية عند التحقيق راجع إلى الإيجاب والسلب، فإن الزوج عدد ينقسم بمتساويين، والفرد عدد لا ينقسم بمتساويين، فالأول اسم للموضوع، أعني العدد مع الإيجاب، والثاني اسم له مع السلب، كذا ذكره ابن سينا. وأما ثانياً: فلأنه صرح ابن سينا وغيره بأن غاية الخلاف شرط في التضاد المشهورى أيضاً، وحينئذ يكون تقابل مثل البياض والحمرة خارجاً عن الأقسام.

For even is a number divided into two equal parts, and odd is a number not divided into two equal parts. The first is a name for the subject, i.e., the number with affirmation, and the second is a name for it with negation. This is as mentioned by Ibn Sina. And secondly: because Ibn Sina and others have stated that extreme difference is a condition in the well-known contrariety as well, and in that case the opposition like whiteness and redness is excluded from the divisions.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of two points. First, it discusses the opposition of even and odd (zawjiyya wa fardiyya). It states that upon investigation, this opposition reduces to the opposition of affirmation (ijab) and negation (salb). Even is defined as a number divisible into two equal parts (affirmation of divisibility), and odd as not divisible (negation). So the pair even-odd is not a primitive type of opposition but falls under affirmation and negation. This is attributed to Ibn Sina. Second, it mentions that Ibn Sina and others have made extreme difference (ghayat al-khilaf) a condition in the well-known contrariety (tadhad al-mashhuri). Consequently, oppositions like whiteness and redness, which do not have extreme difference, are excluded from the divisions of true contrariety. Only pairs that have extreme difference (like black and white) are considered true contraries.

Extreme Difference as Condition in Contrariety
States that extreme difference is a condition in contrariety, excluding pairs like white and red.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that extreme difference is a necessary condition for the well-known contrariety, and that oppositions lacking this are excluded.

True contrariety requires extreme difference; otherwise it is not genuine.

Objection and Clarification on Modified Propositions
Sanusi addresses an objection about modified propositions and clarifies the interpretation of negative predicates.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection regarding modified propositions is resolved and why a negative predicate can be equivalent to a simple negative under specific interpretation.

Core Idea
A modified proposition's truth depends on whether the negated term is taken as a positive concept or as the contradictory itself.

The author anticipated an objection: if affirmation and negation are just predication of opposites on a subject, then they too could both be false when the subject is non-existent, just like other opposites. This objection revolves around propositions like 'the phoenix is not black' which seem to require existence. The answer lies in distinguishing two ways of understanding the negative predicate. Let's explore this nuanced clarification.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the Arabic passage from 'فإن قيل' onward. The objection claims that if we consider the predication of opposites on a subject, affirmation and negation could both be false when the subject doesn't exist, because a modified proposition (المعدولة) requires subject existence.
Example: both 'The phoenix is black' and 'The phoenix is not black' might be false if 'not black' is taken as a positive quality.
2
Understand the author's response
The author replies that in affirmation and negation, the intended opposition is between the contradictories themselves, not between two predicates on a subject. Then he distinguishes two interpretations of a negative predicate.
The key distinction: 'لا أسود' can mean either the positive concept of non-blackness or the contradictory of black (i.e., the negation).
3
Grasp the concept of 'mujiba saliba al-mahmul'
If 'not black' means the contradictory (رفع السواد), then the proposition is equivalent to a simple negative ('not black' = 'it is not the case that it is black') and does not require subject existence.
This is called 'موجبة سالبة المحمول' (affirmative with negative predicate) and is in the force of a negative proposition.
4
Apply the clarification
Consider the example: 'The phoenix is not black'. If we mean the contradictory, it is true because there is no blackness, just like saying 'The phoenix is not black' as a simple negative.
This shows the original rule stands: one of 'black' and 'not black' (as contradictory) must be true.

This passage defines the term 'illa (cause) and presents the foundational classification into internal and external causes. The Second Face: Contradiction and Copulative Predication
Al-Taftazani analyzes the second face concerning contradiction and predication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the objection that proceeding of (A) and (B) from a single principle leads to contradiction fails, and the distinction between copulative and derivative predication.

Core Idea
Coexistence is not contradiction; only copulative predication creates true opposites.

Al-Taftazani continues his analysis of the second face by addressing a common logical error. Critics argue that if a single principle produces both (A) and (B), then opposites must be true. But Taftazani shows that this only works if the predication is copulative, not when attributes coexist by derivation. This lesson unpacks his careful distinction.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objection claims that if from a real one (A) proceeds, then if (B) also proceeds, two opposites would be true because (B) is not (A).

2
Identify the refutation: multiplicity of aspects
Taftazani counters that when aspects (jiha) are multiple, the contradiction disappears: the proceeding of (A) and not (A) are attributed to different aspects.
This is a key principle in kalam: attribution to different aspects avoids contradiction.

Distinguish copulative vs derivative predication
The author explains that the impossible conjunction is only when the two predicates are applied to the same subject in a copulative sense (haml al-muwata'a), not when they coexist as attributes (haml al-ishtiqaq).

In the one principle, proceeding of (A) and non-proceeding (which is proceeding of not (A)) coexist, but they do not yield the copulative statement 'proceeded (A) and did not proceed (A)'. So no contradiction.
This refutes the objection.
5
Reflect on the Imam's remark
The Imam's criticism highlights the importance of logical precision even in the highest theological matters.

Form as Configuration in Artefacts
Explains that form in artefacts is a configuration inhering in matter, not a new substance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that in artefacts, form is not a new substance but a configuration (hay'a) inhering in matter, and that the particular individual form necessitates the particular composite, not the type.

Core Idea
Form in artefacts is an accident, not a substance.

This passage from al-Istighnā clarifies a key distinction in kalam philosophy: when an artisan makes a sword, he does not create new substances but imposes a configuration on pre-existing matter. The passage addresses an important objection about the relationship between form and actual functionality, and it refines our understanding of form as a particular accident rather than a general type.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully

Notice that the author uses analogies like whiteness and body to illustrate.
2

Look for the terms 'hay'a' (configuration), 'jawhar' (substance), and 'arad' (accident). Understand how they relate in the passage.

4

Ask yourself: why does this matter? It preserves the idea that form in artefacts is an accident, not a new substance, and that particularity links form to the composite.

الاستغناء قال: وقد يقال لكل هيئة في قابل وجداني بالذات أو بالاعتبار والمادة لمحلها كالبياض والجسم ويشبه أن يكون مثل السيف، والسرير من هذا القبيل، إذ الصانع لم يحدث فيه جواهر بل هيئة وحينئذ لا يرد الاعتراض بأن الهيئة السيفية، ليست مما يجب معها السيف بالفعل كما في الحجر، وأما جواب إمام بأنا لا نعني أن نوع الصورة يوجب المركب بل إن الصورة الشخصية السيفية مثلا توجب ذلك السيف بخلاف مادة الشخصية فيشعر بأن الصورة ههنا بالمعنى السابق على أن السيف مثلا اسم للمركب من المعروض الذي هو الجوهر والعارض الذي هو الهيئة.
Al-Istighnā said: And it is said for every configuration in a recipient, perceptible essentially or by consideration, and matter for its locus, like whiteness and body. And it resembles such as the sword and the bed being of this kind, since the artisan did not produce in them substances but a configuration. Then the objection does not apply that the sword-configuration is not that by which the sword is actually entailed, as in stone. But the answer of an imam is that we do not mean that the type of form necessitates the composite, but rather that the particular sword-form, for example, necessitates that sword, unlike its particular matter. So it suggests that form here is in the previous sense, and that 'sword' for example is a name for the composite of the substrate which is the substance and the accident which is the configuration.

Commentary
This passage discusses the notion of form as a configuration (hay'a) in artefacts. It notes that for every configuration in a recipient, whether perceptible or conceptual, matter is its locus, as whiteness is to body. Artefacts like swords and beds are similar: the artisan does not create new substances but only a configuration. The passage then addresses an objection: the sword-configuration does not necessarily entail an actual sword (e.g., a stone might have a similar shape but not function as a sword). The answer clarifies that it is not the general type of form but the particular individualized form (e.g., this specific sword-form) that necessitates the particular composite (this sword). Thus, form in artefacts is understood in the same sense as before—as an accident (ʿaraḍ) in a substance (jawhar), and 'sword' is the name of the composite of the two.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
If form in artefacts is merely an accident imposed on matter, what does that say about the value you assign to material objects? Do you treat your possessions as mere configurations, or do you attach false substance to them? Consider an item you treasure—if its form is just an accident, to what extent does your attachment reflect a confusion between substance and accident? Challenge yourself to see the artefact as a composite of matter and accident, not as an independent substance. How does this shift your perspective on ownership and attachment?
?
The passage distinguishes between type and particular form. Think of a specific object you use daily. Is your relationship with it based on its general type or its particular individuality? What does that reveal about your perception of reality? Will you change how you interact with that object?
?
If an artisan does not create new substances but only configurations, then human creativity is about arrangement, not creation ex nihilo. How does this humble your own sense of creative power? Do you recognize that all you do is rearrange what Allah has already created? Let this recognition lead you to gratitude and humility.
