Analyzes motion classification and the special case of upward/downward.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how kalam classifies motions as straight/curved and why upward and downward are considered different even though they share the same essence.

Core Idea
Motion differs not by essence but by relational opposition that never reverses.

In this passage, the author explores the nature of motion, specifically whether straight and curved motion are fundamentally different, and whether upward and downward motions are essentially the same. Using the framework of quantity and quality, he explains the classification, and then addresses a key objection about the special case of ascending and descending motion.

Commentary
This passage discusses the classification of motion as straight or curved and the special issue of upward versus downward motion. The author begins by noting that the specific difference or agreement in straightness to the right and left is not obvious. The best that can be said is that motion is applied to a continuous distance, and just as straight and curved are two types of quantity (like straightness and curvature in quality), motion too is described as straight or circular. Time, however, is one and not subject to multiplicity or actual interruption. Then the author turns to upward and downward motion. Imam al-Razi stated that the two endpoints (up and down) do not differ in essence but differ in being a beginning versus an end. They are opposed by contrariety, and this difference is sufficient for the two motions to be different. An objection is raised: this could apply to any motion from a beginning to an end with a return to the beginning. The answer is that the beginning and end of upward and downward are in two real directions that never change; high never becomes low, unlike other directions. Therefore one cannot consider ascending as descending, unlike motion to the right and left.

The Opposition in Motion is Due to Its Termini

Core Idea
Opposition in motion comes from the ends, not the mover or the medium.

This passage systematically eliminates possible causes for why motions can be opposed—the moved object, the mover, time, and the substrate—leaving only one explanation: the termini. Understanding this reasoning is essential for grasping the author's taxonomy of motion and its implications for physics and metaphysics.

Commentary

Therefore, the only remaining cause is the opposition of the termini (ma minhu and ma ilayhi). He distinguishes between essential opposition (as in color change) and accidental opposition (as in upward/downward motion relative to center and periphery). This sets the stage for the subsequent discussion.

Accidental opposition can imply subject opposition when it makes the definition of opposites true.

In this passage, the author addresses an important objection: if an accident's opposition doesn't imply the subject's opposition, how can the opposition of motion's endpoints cause the motions themselves to be opposite? The answer hinges on a crucial qualification—accidental opposition can indeed imply subject opposition when it is specific enough to satisfy the definition of true opposites. Commentary
This passage presents an objection and its answer. The objector points out a known principle: the opposition of an accident does not entail the opposition of the substance. So how can the opposition of an accidental feature (like the termini of motion) cause the motion itself to be opposite? The author responds by qualifying the principle: it applies only when considered absolutely and by itself. But when the accidental opposition reaches the point that the definition of 'two opposites' applies to the subject or to what is connected to it, then it does cause opposition. In this case, the ascending and descending motions truly fulfill the definition of opposites: they are two existent realities that cannot coexist at the same time in the same respect, and they are two species within a genus that exhibit the utmost difference (ghayat al-khilaf). The author then contrasts this with other cases: a rectilinear motion that goes back and forth along the same line without ascent/descent is not opposite because it is one species; and two curvilinear motions, even if one is above the other, do not reach utmost difference because there are infinitely many degrees of curvature. Thus the objection is refuted by specifying the conditions under which accidental opposition can indeed imply opposition in the subject.

قال : وتضاد السكون) يكون لتضاد ما فيه كالسكون في المكان الأعلى والأسفل، أو في الحرارة أو البرودة، ويكون طبيعياً وقسرياً وإرادياً ومستنداً لطبيعي، هو الطبيعة على الإطلاق، ولا يتصور في السكون المركب وسكون الإنسان(۱) الإقسام في المكان الطبيعي، وأثر الإرادة ترك إزالته) . لتضاد ما فيه إذ لا عبرة فيه بتضاد الساكن والمسكن والزمان على ما مر ، ولا تعلق للسكون بما منه وما إليه ،قوله ويكون أي السكون طبيعياً كسكون الحجر على الأرض، وقسرياً كسكونه معلقاً في الهواء ، أو إرادياً كوقوف الطير في الهواء، والطبيعي لا يفتقر إلى مقارنة أمر غير طبيعي كما في الحركة بل يستند إلى الطبيعة مطلقاً، لأن الجسم إذا خلى وطبعه لم يكن له بد من موضع معين لا تطلب مفارقته ولا يتصور في السكون تركب، وإنما تعرض البساطة والتركب للحركة مر في البحث اليابق . كما فإن قيل : سكون الإنسان على الأرض مركب من الطبيعي والإرادي قلنا : لا بل هو واحد وإنما يتوهم التعدد في علته، والتحقيق أنها الطبيعية فقط، وأثر الإرادة ترك إزالته إلى الحركة. فإن كلا من الطبيعة والإرادة والقاسر، إنما يصير تمام علة السكون عند عدم رجحان علة الحركة، وهذا بخلاف الحركة، فإنها لما كانت تقبل الشدة والضعف جاز اجتماع علتين على حركة واحدة كما في الحجر المرمى إلى تحت فظاهر أنها ليست من التركيب شيء وإنما هو اشتداد .
He said: And the opposition of rest is due to the opposition of that in which it is, like rest in the highest and lowest place, or in heat or cold. And it is natural, forced, and voluntary, and is based on the natural, which is nature absolutely. And composition is not conceivable in rest, and the rest of a human being in the natural place; the effect of will is leaving its removal. Due to the opposition of that in which it is, for no consideration is given to the opposition of the resting thing, the place, or the time, as has passed. And rest has no relation to its 'from' and 'to'. His statement: 'And it is' — i.e., rest is natural like the rest of a stone on the ground, forced like its rest when suspended in the air, or voluntary like the standing of a bird in the air. The natural does not require the accompaniment of something unnatural as in motion, but rather is based on nature absolutely, because if a body is left to its nature, it necessarily has a specific place which it does not seek to leave. Composition is not conceivable in rest; rather simplicity and composition occur in motion, as has passed in the previous discussion. So if it is said: The rest of a human being on the ground is composite of natural and voluntary, we say: No, rather it is one, and multiplicity is only imagined in its cause, and the reality is that it is only natural, and the effect of will is leaving its removal to motion. For each of nature, will, and force only becomes the complete cause of rest when the cause of motion is not predominant. This is unlike motion, for since it admits intensity and weakness, it is possible for two causes to combine in one motion, as in a stone thrown downward; it is apparent that there is no composition in it, but rather intensification.

Commentary
This passage discusses the opposition (تضاد) of rest (سكون) and its types. The author first explains that rest is opposed due to the opposition of that in which it is (تضاد ما فيه), for example rest in the highest place is opposed to rest in the lowest place, and rest in heat is opposed to rest in cold. Rest itself is then classified into three types: natural (طبيعي), forced (قسري), and voluntary (إرادي). Natural rest does not need an external cause; it is based on nature absolutely, because if a body is left alone, it remains in its natural place without seeking to leave it. The author emphasizes that composition (تركب) is not conceivable in rest; only motion can be simple or composite. He then addresses an objection: the rest of a human on the ground seems composite of natural and voluntary. The answer is that it is actually one (natural), and the voluntary aspect is merely the will's act of not removing it (i.e., refraining from moving). The complete cause of rest in each case (natural, voluntary, forced) is only effective when the cause of motion is not predominant. This contrasts with motion, which can admit two causes together because it can have intensity and weakness, as in a stone thrown downward where the natural and forced motions combine not as composition but as intensification (اشتداد).

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Human rest on the ground is a composite of natural rest and voluntary rest.
Correction: It is a single natural rest; the will merely refrains from moving, not adding a separate cause.

A) Because the will always causes motion, so rest is always forced.

Key Takeaway
Rest is simple and opposed by its subject, with three types; it never contains composition, unlike motion which can have intensification.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you choose to remain still in a good action, is your rest truly from your own will or from your nature? Are you aware that your will only refrains from moving away from obedience, while the real cause is the natural inclination of your purified soul?
?
The passage says rest is simple, not composite. In your spiritual life, do you seek to combine motives (fear, hope, love) or do you simplify your intention (ikhlas) for Allah? What false compositions have you allowed in your heart's rest?
?
Consider the rest of a stone on the ground: it does not seek to leave its place. Is your heart at rest in its natural place of servitude to Allah, or are you constantly seeking to move away into desires? What is the predominant cause that prevents the motion of sin in your soul?
