The Argument for Matter from Possibility and Its Refutation
Taftazani presents the philosophers' argument for matter from possibility, then refutes it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the philosophers argue for the existence of matter from the concept of possibility and how the author refutes it by distinguishing between essential and dispositional possibility.

Core Idea
Not all possibility is an existent attribute; the argument equivocates on 'possibility'.

The philosophers argue that before a contingent thing exists, it is possible, and this possibility must inhere in a substrate, which is matter. The author counters this by showing that 'possibility' can mean two different things: essential possibility, which is not a real attribute, and dispositional possibility, which is not present in every contingent. This lesson breaks down the argument and its refutation step by step.

2
Identify the refutation's structure
The author refutes by disambiguating 'possibility' into two types. Identify these two types and how each undermines the argument.
Essential vs. dispositional possibility is the key.
3
Understand the implications
Consider why the distinction matters for kalam cosmology. The philosophers need an existent attribute to prove matter, but the author shows the premise is false or the conclusion doesn't follow.

Commentary
This passage presents the philosophers' first argument for the existence of matter and the author's refutation. The argument runs: Before its existence, a contingent (ḥādith) is possible (mumkin). This possibility (imkān) is an existent attribute (wujūdī) that requires a substrate (ḥall). This substrate cannot be the contingent itself, because that would make a thing precede itself. Therefore, the substrate must be matter (mādda). The possibility of the eternal (qadīm) is different because the eternal has no such state before existence. The author then rebuts this argument by disambiguating two types of possibility. If 'possibility' refers to essential (dhātī) possibility—the mere absence of impossibility—then the author denies that it is an existent attribute (it is a conceptual, not real, property). If it refers to dispositional (istiʿdādī) possibility—a real preparedness that admits degrees and variation—then the author denies that every contingent has such a disposition (since some contingents come into existence without a prior disposition). Hence, the argument fails on both interpretations.

Commentary
This passage presents a second, more developed proof for the necessity of matter and addresses a key objection. The proof: Every contingent before existence is possible, and possibility (imkān) is an existent attribute (as argued earlier). Since possibility is relational (iḍāfī) by nature, it is not a substance but an accident, and thus requires a subject (maḥall) that is (a) not the contingent itself (to avoid self-precedence) and (b) not separate from it (since an accident cannot exist in a separate entity). This subject is what the philosophers call matter. The passage then refutes a false notion that identifies possibility with divine power (iqtidār); it argues that power is explained by possibility (i.e., something is within power because it is possible), not the reverse. Therefore, possibility cannot subsist in the agent. The passage then presents an objection: The proof is undermined by eternal possibles (al-mumkin al-qadīm) such as the primordial matter (al-mawādd) and separate intellects (al-mujarradāt), which are possible yet have no matter. The answer is that their possibility is intrinsic: they have no 'before existence' state that would require an external substrate; rather, their possibility is a property that subsists in themselves (qāʾima bihā). Thus, the proof only applies to contingents that come into existence after non-existence.

Three Distinctions Between Essential and Preparatory Possibility
The author enumerates three distinctions between essential and preparatory possibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three key distinctions between essential possibility (al-imkan al-dhati) and preparatory possibility (al-imkan al-isti'dadi), and why this distinction matters for the study of contingency.

Core Idea
Essential possibility is a rational attribute; preparatory possibility is a real quality that admits degrees.

In this passage, the author responds to an objection about the nature of possibility. He clarifies that there are two distinct types: essential possibility, which is a necessary attribute of the quiddity of a possible being, and preparatory possibility, which is a real quality in matter that prepares it for change. He then draws three precise distinctions between them, each highlighting a different aspect of their difference. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for grasping the logic of contingency and change.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the two types
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Note how the author first sets up the two types of possibility: essential and preparatory. This sets the stage for the distinctions that follow.
The author starts with an objection and then clarifies the two meanings of 'possibility'.
2
Examine the first distinction
The first distinction is that essential possibility does not imply preponderance of existence or non-existence—both are equal with respect to it. In contrast, preparatory possibility implies a preponderance because it is a state that brings matter closer to the agent's influence.
Notice the key phrase: 'الذاتي لا يقتضي رجحان الوجود أو العدم'.
3
Explore the second distinction
Preparatory possibility varies in degrees of proximity and distance—for example, the preparedness of an embryo for humanness is closer than that of a clot. Essential possibility, however, does not admit of any variation; the possibility of a human existing is the same regardless of the stage of development.
The author gives a concrete example using stages of embryonic development.
4
Understand the third distinction
Essential possibility is merely a rational consideration with no external reality, whereas preparatory possibility is a real quality in a thing that prepares it for the agent's bestowal of existence. This quality varies in intensity and vanishes when the potential becomes actual—though the author questions whether that vanishing is necessary.
The passage ends with a rhetorical question about the necessity of disappearance.
5
Reflect on the overall significance
Consider why this taxonomy of possibility is important for kalam. It allows theologians to discuss change and potentiality without conflating logical categories with real physical properties.

Essential Possibility Requires a Subject
Philosophers' first way: possibility requires a subject.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' first way of explaining how essential possibility can be existential, namely that it requires a subject in the external world.

Core Idea
Essential possibility is not standalone; it always needs a real substrate.

The philosophers propose that the possibility of a temporal being before its actual existence means that this possibility is attached to an existing subject. This passage unpacks the distinction between two types of existence and shows that even essential possibility, when the thing is dependent, necessitates a substrate. Let's explore how they argue this.

Guided Path
1
Context and purpose
Understand that this passage presents the first of two philosophers' ways to resolve an objection about possibility being existential. The key idea is that possibility is relative to a subject.
2
Distinction between essential and accidental existence
Notice the division of existence into two types: essential (like whiteness in itself) and accidental (like a body being white). This classification is crucial for the argument.
3
Possibility relative to accidental existence
For accidental existence, possibility clearly requires an existing subject. Example: whiteness cannot be possible for a body unless the body exists.
4
Extending to essential existence for dependent things
Even for essential existence, if the thing depends on a substrate when it exists (like accidents, forms, or soul), its possibility also requires that substrate.
5
Conclusion and takeaway
The first way shows that 'essential possibility' before existence is not independent but relational, demanding an actual subject for dependent entities.

This passage presents an alternative, more detailed classification of precedence and posteriority proposed by the mutakallimun. It begins by explaining a classification based on whether the prior and posterior coexist (ijtima' fi al-wujud). If such a thing has no concept other than non-division, it is absolute unity (e.g., a simple individual). If it does have another concept, then we examine whether it accepts division. If it does not accept division, it is either a point (if it has position) or a separate entity (if not). If it accepts division, it can be divisible essentially (like a continuous quantity) or accidentally (like a body). Accidental division can yield similar parts (like water) or heterogeneous parts (like a tree's parts). If the parts are connected, it is one by continuity; if aggregated, it is one by aggregation (e.g., a tree, a house, a dirham). Next, it discusses cases where something is a subject of both unity and multiplicity, but from different aspects. If the aspect of unity constitutes the aspect of multiplicity, we speak of generic, specific, or differential unity. If not, and unity occurs to it accidentally, it is accidental unity; otherwise it is relative unity. Finally, it enumerates the technical terms for unity in various categories: homogeneity (mujānasa) in genus, similarity (mumāthala) in species, equality (musāwāt) in quantity, resemblance (mushābaha) in quality, appropriateness (munāsaba) in relation, similarity (mushākala) in property, correspondence (muṭābaqa) in extremities (like left and right), and parallelism (muwāzāt) in arrangement of parts. This classification helps kalam and philosophical discussions on unity and multiplicity.

He said: It is impossible for two similars to coexist, meaning that if two similars are of the kind of accidents, it is impossible for them to coexist in one substrate, contrary to the Mu'tazila. For us, if two accidents share in essence and essential attributes, no distinction between them can be conceived except by the substrate, because their inherence in it and their existence are dependent on its existence. So if the essence and what follows it, namely identity, are unified, twoness ceases. And it was refuted by the denial of the possibility that each is specified by accidents arising from separate causes, and by this is prevented what was mentioned in al-Muhassal that the relation of the accidents to each of them is equal; so no accident is particular to one of them, but each has accidents, and then no distinction remains at all, and union is necessitated. As for the objection that lack of distinction does not indicate union but rather is lack of knowledge of its twoness, it is nothing, because what was mentioned, if complete, indicates lack of distinction in reality, not only in the intellect. And it may be argued that if the coexistence of two similars were permissible, then it would be permissible for one who has theoretical knowledge of something to reflect in order to attain knowledge of it, since there is no obstacle except the impossibility of the coexistence of two similars. And that if it were permissible, then certainty would not be attained regarding the unity of any accident, because it could be many similars coexisting, and the consequent is false because of certainty in many accidents. And that if their coexistence were permissible, then their separation would be permissible by the removal of one of the two similars, since it is not necessary, and its removal is only by the occurrence of its opposite, which is opposite to the other remaining similar, so the coexistence of opposites would be necessary. The first two arguments were refuted by denying the entailment, because there may be another obstacle, such as the absence of the condition of reflection, which is non-knowledge of the desired, and the possibility of certainty about the non-existence of the possible by necessity or inference. The third was refuted by denial. The Mu'tazila held by occurrence: for a body is subject to blackness, then another, then another until it reaches extreme blackness. And it was answered: We do not accept that; rather, the blacknesses varying in intensity and weakness are types of color differing in reality, sharing in an accident predicated to them by analogy (tashkīk), which is absolute blackness, and the body whose blackness intensifies gradually is subject in each moment to another type.

Commentary
This passage presents a sustained kalām proof for the impossibility of two similar accidents coexisting in one substrate, and responds to Mu'tazila objections. The proof begins from the premise that if two accidents share the same essence and essential attributes, they cannot be distinguished except by their substrate. If the essence and accompanying identity (al-huwiyya) are unified, then twoness (al-ithnayniyya) is eliminated—they become one. The objection that lack of distinction does not necessarily entail union is dismissed because the argument shows real lack of distinction, not merely in the intellect. Three further demonstrations are offered: (1) If it were possible, then theoretical knowledge could be attained without reflection, since the only obstacle to attaining knowledge is the impossibility of two similars; (2) If it were possible, we could never be certain that an accident is one rather than many similars; (3) If it were possible for the two similars to separate, one ceasing to exist, then its opposite would arise, which is also opposite to the other remaining similar, thus requiring the coexistence of opposites. The first two objections are rebutted by denying the entailment, pointing out that other obstacles (like lack of knowledge of the desired) may exist. The third is simply denied. Finally, the Mu'tazila argue from empirical occurrence: a body can acquire successively multiple blacknesses until it reaches extreme blackness, suggesting multiple similars can coexist. The response is that these blacknesses are not truly similar; they are different types of color differing in reality, but are called black through a form of analogical predication (tashkīk). Thus, no two identical accidents coexist.

The Mu'tazila's Empirical Objection and the Answer: Gradation of Blackness
Sanusi presents the Mu'tazila's occurrence argument and the response differentiating types of blackness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila's argument from progressive darkening and the response that different shades of black are distinct types, not truly similar.

Core Idea
The empirical case of increasing blackness does not prove similar accidents coexist; each shade is a different type of black.

The Mu'tazila countered the theoretical proofs by pointing to a common phenomenon: a body can become successively darker, suggesting multiple blacknesses coexist. The answer is subtle: these blacknesses are not truly identical; they differ in intensity and are distinct types of color. Let's examine this important exchange.

See the distinction

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer sequence about a subtle logical point regarding the truth conditions of affirmation and negation when the subject does not exist. The author begins by stating that affirmation and negation, when used in a proposition, are always such that one is true and the other false, regardless of whether the subject exists—this is because contradictories cannot be both true or both false. This distinguishes them from other kinds of opposites (like contraries, correlation, etc.) which can both be false if the subject does not exist or does not possess either opposite. An objection is then raised: But if we consider predication on a subject, then affirmation and negation can also both be false when the subject is non-existent, as in 'The phoenix is black' and 'The phoenix is not-black' (the privative form), because the privative form requires existence of the subject. Alternatively, if we consider the opposition between the two contradictories themselves, this is not applicable to other types of opposition like correlation or possession-and-privation. The author answers that in the case of affirmation and negation, what is meant is the second sense (considering the contradictories themselves), while in other opposites it is the first (predication). He then clarifies that a privative proposition (like 'The phoenix is not-black') is indeed dependent on existence of the subject only when the predicate is a positive concept expressed as a negation. But if the predicate is the very concept of negation (i.e., the contradictory), then it is equivalent to a simple negative and does not require existence. Thus 'The phoenix is not black' is true and does not imply the phoenix's existence because it is in force of 'The phoenix is not black' (simple negation).

Commentary
This passage is an argument about how multiplicity can arise from the First Principle, followed by objections and answers. It begins by stating that if something proceeds from the First Principle (which has no multiplicity of aspects), that thing is one in reality. However, with respect to different considerations, six mental aspects are understood about it: existence, identity, contingency, necessity through another, intellection of its essence, and intellection of its principle. Through these considerations, multiple things can proceed from it, and multiple chains of causation begin. Then it mentions that from the first effect a second effect can proceed, and through the first principle via that second effect a third effect, and so on, leading to unlimited chains. Some investigators have written on this. Then an objection by Imam al-Razi is presented: existence, necessity, and contingency are merely mental considerations, not real causes for external entities; they are only conditions or aspects. Then a further objection: if such mental multiplicity suffices for the one to produce many things, then God's essence, with its many negative attributes and relations, could directly produce the contingent beings without intermediaries—yet the principle holds that only one thing proceeds from Him directly. The answer: negative and relational attributes are only conceivable after the existence of an other; if they caused that other, there would be circularity. Another objection: conception does not require existence, only concept of the other, so no circularity. Final answer: judgment of negations and relations in actual reality requires the existence of the other (since negation needs a negated thing, relation a related thing), so if the other's existence depended on them, circularity would still arise.

The Refutation of Mental Considerations as Independent Causes
Presents al-Razi's objection that mental considerations cannot be real causes and the response that they are only conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's objection that existence, necessity, and contingency are mere mental considerations and the response that they are only conditions, not independent causes.

Core Idea
Mental considerations are conditions, not causes.

After explaining how multiplicity can arise, the author turns to a critical objection by Imam al-Razi: how can mental concepts like existence, necessity, and contingency be real causes of external things? The passage immediately clarifies that these are not independent causes but rather conditions and aspects that modify the existing cause. This lesson focuses on that objection and its initial response.

Guided Path
1
Read al-Razi's objection
Read the sentence in Arabic and translation. Notice the claim that existence, necessity, and contingency are mental considerations that cannot be causes of external entities.
2
Identify the three mental considerations
The objection singles out three: existence (الوجود), necessity (الوجوب), and contingency (الإمكان).
3
Understand the response
The passage states that these are not independent causes (عللاً مستقلة) but rather conditions (شروط) and aspects (حيثيات) that affect the states of the existing cause.
This response is embedded in the objection itself, showing that the objector acknowledges they are conditions.

The Objection of Negations and Relations and the Circularity Argument
Debates whether negations and relations can be efficient causes without circularity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that God's multiple negative attributes could directly produce multiple effects, and the subsequent debate about whether that leads to circularity.

Core Idea
Negations and relations depend on an other, so they cannot be the source of that other.

A further objection is raised: if mental considerations can produce multiplicity, then why can't God's many negations and relations directly cause the contingent beings without intermediaries? The response invokes the principle that negations and relations are only conceivable after the existence of an 'other', which would lead to circularity if they were the cause. A counter-objection and final answer refine this point.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection about negations and relations
Read the objection: if mental multiplicity suffices, then God's essence with its many negations and relations could directly produce many effects without intermediaries.
This objection challenges the need for an intermediary.
2
Read the first answer about circularity
The answer: negations and relations are not conceived except after the existence of the other, so if they caused the other, there would be circularity.
3
Read the counter-objection
The objector retorts: their conception depends only on the conception of the other, not its existence, so no circularity.
This focuses on epistemic vs. ontic order.
4
Read the final answer
The final answer: judgment of negations and relations in reality (not just in mind) requires the actual existence of the other, so circularity still holds.
This distinguishes between mental conception and real-world judgment.

The Fifth Argument Against Infinite Regress: Even and Odd
Al-Taftazani presents the fifth argument against infinite regress using parity and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fifth kalam argument against infinite regress based on parity (even/odd) of the number of terms, the objection that evenness/oddness only apply to finite numbers, and the author's response.

Core Idea
An infinite series must be either even or odd—therefore it is finite.

This lesson presents the fifth argument against tasalsul (infinite regress) drawn from the properties of numbers. Al-Taftazani first states the argument, then raises and responds to a key objection. Commentary
This passage presents alternative views on the number of accident genera. The criterion is whether the accident admits division by itself (quantity), or necessitates relation (relation), or otherwise (quality). Then Taftazani notes the majority view that motion in the category of 'where' (al-ayn) belongs to the 'where' category, but some (including al-Razi) assign it to the category of 'being acted upon' (an yanfaʿil), because motion is a gradual change. The passage cuts off while discussing motion in quantity, quality, and position, but it suggests that such motions clearly are not a separate category.

The Philosophers' Proof: Accidents Are Individuated by Their Substrate
Taftazani presents the philosophers' argument on individuation of accidents and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' argument that an accident's individuation must come from its substrate, and how it is refuted by allowing a free agent to assign special relations.

Core Idea
Accidents cannot be individuals without a substrate; but a free agent can override this necessity.

This passage presents a dense philosophical argument about what gives an accident its unique individuality. The philosophers systematically eliminate other possibilities—quiddity, inherent property, separate cause, or haecceity—and conclude that it must be the substrate. However, a refutation shows that a free agent could provide a special relation, preserving divine power. Let's follow the steps of this intricate reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the full passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to get the overall flow of the argument. Note the logical structure: elimination, conclusion, objection, response, refutation.
The argument is compact; read slowly to track each eliminated possibility.
2
Identify the four eliminated possibilities
The philosophers list four things that cannot be the cause of an accident's individuation: its quiddity, something inherent in it, something separate, or its haecceity. Understand the reason for each elimination.
The fourth (haecceity) is presented as an objection from al-Mawāqif.
3
Grasp the conclusion and its implication
After eliminating four possibilities, the philosophers assert that the individuation must come from the substrate. This implies that an accident cannot survive transfer to a different substrate; it would lose its identity.
This supports the Ashʿarī view that accidents do not persist.
4
Analyze the objection and response
The passage includes an objection: what if individuation comes from something inherent in the substrate? The response is that this leads to an infinite regress or circularity, ultimately requiring the substrate as the cause.
5
Consider the refutation
The refutation denies that a separate cause cannot have a special relation; a free agent (like God) can choose to give a particular accident its individuality without needing a preponderator. This undermines the philosophers' conclusion.
This preserves the possibility of accidents existing without a substrate in miraculous contexts.

The Fourth Argument Against Accident Transition and Its Refutation
Sanusi presents the fourth argument against accident transition and two refutations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth argument against the transition of accidents and how it is refuted by counterexample and by positing an intermediate space.

Core Idea
The argument against accident transition fails because it applies equally to bodies.

This lesson examines a key objection to the possibility of accident transition: if an accident were moving, where would it be? The argument leads to an infinite regress, but we will see how it is refuted by considering the parallel case of moving bodies and by introducing the concept of intermediate space.

Guided Path
1
Understand the fourth argument
Read the argument carefully: if an accident transitions, during the move it must be in the old place, the new place, or some other place. But being in either old or new implies stasis, not transition, and positing a different place leads to infinite regress. This is the core objection.
2
First refutation: counterexample with body motion
Note how the same reasoning would apply to the observable motion of a body. If the argument were sound, body motion would be impossible too, yet it is observed. This shows the argument is invalid.
3
Second refutation: the third space
The second response avoids the dilemma by claiming that during transition, the accident is in a third space that is partly from the old and partly from the new. This allows gradual transition without being fully in either.
4
Recognize the limitation
The passage notes that this solution only fails for accidents in indivisible atoms (al-jawhar al-fard), where no intermediate space exists. So the transition of such accidents remains impossible.
This nuance is important for understanding the scope of the refutation.

The Proof that Accidents Cannot Subsist in Accidents
Sanusi states the rule and gives the first proof for it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two rational arguments Sanusi gives against the possibility of an accident subsisting in another accident.

Core Idea
An accident cannot inhere in another accident because it needs a spatial substrate and avoids infinite regress.

Sanusi begins by stating the position that accidents cannot subsist in accidents. He then provides two concise arguments: one based on the nature of subsistence as dependence on a spatial locus, and another based on the necessity of ending with a substance to avoid infinite regress. Let's examine these arguments closely.

Guided Path
1
Read the original Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text once to get the flow of Sanusi's argument. Note that he first states the rule, then gives two reasons connected by 'because' (لأن).
2
Identify the two reasons
Look for the two 'because' clauses. The first reason concerns the meaning of subsistence as spatial dependence; the second concerns infinite regress leading to a substance.
The second reason begins with 'ولأنه لا بد بالآخرة من جوهر'.
3
Reflect on why infinite regress is a problem
Consider why an infinite chain of accidents is impossible in the kalam framework. The argument is that subsistence requires a foundation, and without a terminating substance, the chain would be absurd.
This connects to the broader kalam principle that all accidents need a substance.
