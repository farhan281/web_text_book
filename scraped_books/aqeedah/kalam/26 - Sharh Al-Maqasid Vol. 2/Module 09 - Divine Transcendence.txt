The First Proof: Essential Difference Precludes Union
First proof: essential difference, with objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first proof against the union of two entities based on essential difference, and the objection that this proof is either question-begging or not clearer than the claim.

Core Idea
Two entities cannot unite because each has an essential particularity that defines it.

The author begins his refutation of the possibility of two entities becoming one by presenting a proof rooted in the nature of identity. He argues that every thing has a unique particularity that cannot be removed without destroying the thing itself. This lesson examines that reasoning and the immediate objection it faces.

Commentary
This passage is a proof-by-reductio refuting the possibility of two distinct entities becoming one numerical entity (al-ittihad). The author presents two main proofs, each followed by an objection and then a further dialectical exchange.

The first proof (al-awwal) argues that any two things — whether they are two essences or two individuals of one essence — have an essential difference (ikhtilāf dhātī) that cannot be removed. Each thing has a particularity (khuṣūṣiyya) that constitutes its identity; if that particularity were removed, the thing would cease to exist. Therefore, two things cannot become one because their essential differences preclude merger.

An objection (iʿtirāḍ) is raised: if this is a direct proof (istidlāl), it merely restates the point in dispute (nafs al-mutanāzaʿ). If it is a reminder (tanbīh), it is not clearer than the claim itself, since one might mistakenly think the difference is essential and impossible to remove when in fact union may occur.

The second proof (al-thānī) examines the outcome of alleged union. If both entities remain after union, they are still two, not one. If only one remains, the other is annihilated (fanāʾ). If neither remains, a third thing arises, again not union. Thus in every case, no union occurs.

An objection is then made: the objector denies that if they remained they would be two, claiming that union would make them one. The author reformulates: after existence (baʿd al-ījād), if both exist, they are necessarily two. Otherwise, if only one exists or none, that is annihilation, not union.

Another objection: the objector denies that two existences entail two entities; they might share one existence. The author repels this (dafʿ) by analyzing: if they exist, either they have two existences (so two) or one existence. That one existence is either one of the original two (annihilation of the other) or a new one (annihilation of both).

Then the opponent answers the repelling (ujība ʿan al-dafʿ): they claim the two exist with one existence that is the two original existences having become one. The author concludes that the only way to block this is to assert the impossibility of union as a necessary (ḍarūrī) judgment. However, he notes that claiming necessity is disputable, and the impossibility of uniting two existences is no clearer than the impossibility of uniting two entities generally. The entire argument is presented as a reminder with detail, not a demonstrative proof.

The Second Proof: Analysis of the Aftermath of Alleged Union
Second proof: outcomes of union, with dialectical exchange

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second proof against union based on the outcomes of assuming union, and the dialectical exchange that follows, including objections and refinements.

Core Idea
Union cannot occur because any outcome after alleged union either leaves two entities or annihilates one or both.

The second proof takes a different approach by examining what would happen if two entities truly united. The author systematically considers all possibilities and shows that none lead to genuine union. This lesson also follows the subsequent objections and refinements of the argument, culminating in an appeal to necessity.

Read the second proof and initial objection

The initial objection claims that if they remain, they could be one.

Follow the objection and reply
The objector then argues that two could exist with one existence. The author replies by analyzing whether that one existence is one of the originals or a new one.
This leads to a regress.
4
Consider the final appeal to necessity
The opponent answers the reply by claiming the two exist with one existence that is the same as the two originals. The author concludes that only a claim of necessity can block this, but notes it's not obvious.

The Objection of Separation and al-Amidi's Answer
Discusses al-Amidi's answer to the objection of separation and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the nuanced debate about the separation (infikak) between God and the world, and why al-Amidi's answer was considered insufficient by later scholars.

Core Idea
Separation between God and the world requires careful definition in intellection.

In this passage, the author delves into a specific objection raised against the relationship between God and the world. The debate centers on whether separation (infikak) is possible in intellection or in physical locus. Al-Amidi's answer attempts to resolve the issue but faces a powerful refutation. By tracing the argument, we gain insight into the precision required in kalam reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage slowly. Then read the translation to get an overall sense of the argument. Notice the technical terms like الانفكاك (infikak) and التعقل (ta'aqqul).
The passage is dense; don't worry about understanding every detail immediately.
2
Identify the objection and al-Amidi's initial answer
The objection is that God cannot separate from the world in a locus or non-locus. Al-Amidi answers that the world can separate from God because it can be localized, even if God cannot.
This is based on a restricted notion of separation in a locus or non-locus.
3
Understand the refutation
The refutation argues that al-Amidi's answer is insufficient because it would imply that a part can be distinct from the whole, and an attribute from the subject, in problematic ways.
This shows the logical consequences of accepting separation from one side only.
4
Explore the refined attempts
The author then considers taking 'intellection' in a broader sense, but that leads to other issues. Finally, the passage suggests the dispute may be limited to necessary attributes.
This reveals the depth of the debate and the need for precise definitions.

Al-Amidi's Claim and Its Refutation
Refutes al-Amidi using part-whole analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Amidi's argument that the world can be separate from God because of location and non-existence, and why this argument is insufficient because it would entail unacceptable consequences like part and whole being distinct.

Core Idea
Mere difference in location does not prove real otherness.

Al-Amidi proposed that the world can be separate from God because God is not subject to space or non-being, while the world is. The refutation shows that this criterion is too broad: if accepted, it would also imply that a part is other than the whole and an attribute is other than its subject. This lesson examines that initial move and its logical force.

Guided Path
1
Identify al-Amidi's claim
Read the first sentence: al-Amidi says the world can separate from God because it can be located in space (tahayyuz) or non-exist, unlike God. Understand the basis of his argument.
Notice that al-Amidi focuses on a difference in modality (possible vs. impossible).
2
Understand the refutation's logic
The refutation argues that if this difference sufficed for otherness, then similarly a part and whole would be other because the whole can cease without the part. Grasp the 'otherwise' reductio ad absurdum.
The analogy also applies to subject and attribute.
3
Reflect on the implication
Ask yourself: why is the part-whole otherness unacceptable? Because the part is not a separate entity from the whole; similarly, the attribute is not separate from the subject. This shows al-Amidi's criterion is too weak.
The refutation does not disprove otherness but shows the criterion is flawed.

Intellectual Separation and Its Problems
Examines and rejects intellectual separation as a criterion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why attempting to limit the discussion to intellectual separation (ta'aqqul) fails, and how it leads to unacceptable otherness between divine essence and attributes.

Core Idea
Even intellectual separation must be meaningful and not lead to absurdities.

Some might try to salvage al-Amidi's position by saying that 'separation' means intellectual separation: we can conceive the world without God. This passage argues that even intellectually, God cannot be conceived in space, so that fails. And if we take intellection in a broader sense including imagination, it would imply that essence and attributes are distinct, which is not intended. This lesson unpacks that intricate argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument against intellectual separation
The text says: you cannot say 'intellectual separation' unless you drop the condition of space or non-being, because God cannot be intellectually conceived in space either.
Al-Ta'aqqul here means rational conception, not imagination.
2
Understand the 'unless' clause
If we take intellection in a broader sense (including imaginary concepts), then we could conceive essence without attribute, but that leads to the unwanted conclusion that essence and attribute are other.
This is a reductio ad absurdum of the broader interpretation.
3
Reflect on the implication
The only consistent way to avoid absurdity is to reject that intellectual separation suffices for real otherness. This strengthens the original refutation.
Note the subtle shift from 'existence' to 'conception' as a possible criterion.

اعتدال المزاج ووجود البنية ليسا شرطاً لإيجاد الحياة
قال : وعندنا لا يشترط اعتدال المزاج ووجود البنية والروح الحيواني للقطع بإمكان أن يخلقها الله تعالى في الجزء، وخالفت الفلاسفة والمعتزلة تعويلا على ما يشاهد من زوالها بانتقاض البنية، وفقد الاعتدال والروح، ومنا من استدل على عدم الاشتراط بأنها لو اشترط فإما أن تقوم بالجزءين حياة واحدة فيلزم قيام العرض بأكثر من محل، وإما أن يقوم بكل جزء حياة وحينئذ فالاشتراط من الجانبين دور ومن جانب واحد ترجيح بلا مرجح ، ورد بأنه قائم بالمجموع على ما سبق . ولو سلم فدور معية، ولو سلم فعدم المرجع ممنوع غايته عدم الاطلاع عليه . ذهب جمهور المتكلمين إلى أن تحقق المعنى المسمى بالحياة ليس مشروطاً باعتدال المزاج والبنية والروح الحيواني للقطع بإمكان أن يخلقها الله تعالى في البسائط، بل هي في الجزء الذي لا يتجزأ، والمراد بالبنية البدن المؤلف من العناصر الأربعة، وبالروح الحيواني جسم لطيف بخاري يتكون لطافة الأخلاط تنبعث من التجويف الأيسر من القلب، ويسرى إلى البدن في عروق نابتة من القلب تسمى بالشرايين.
The equilibrium of temperament and the existence of bodily structure are not a condition for the creation of life.
He said: And in our view, the equilibrium of temperament, the existence of bodily structure, and the animal spirit are not required, due to the certainty that God can create it (life) in the part (atom). The philosophers and the Mu'tazila disagreed, relying on what is observed of its disappearance upon the dissolution of the structure and the loss of equilibrium and spirit. Among us, some argued for the non-requirement by saying that if it (life) were conditioned, then either one life subsists in the two parts, which would entail a quality subsisting in more than one substrate, or a life subsists in each part, in which case the condition from both sides is circular, and from one side is preference without a preponderant. It was refuted that it subsists in the whole, as previously. Even if granted, it is a circularity by simultaneity, and even if granted, the absence of a differentiator is not conceded—at most, it is not known. The majority of mutakallimun held that the realization of the meaning called life is not conditioned by equilibrium of temperament, bodily structure, and animal spirit, due to the certainty that God can create it in simple substances; rather, it is in the indivisible part. By 'bodily structure' is meant the body composed of the four elements, and by 'animal spirit' is meant a subtle vaporous body that arises from the subtlety of humors, emanating from the left cavity of the heart and flowing through the body in veins growing from the heart called arteries.

Commentary
This passage states the Ash'ari position that life is not conditioned on a balanced temperament, bodily structure, or animal spirit, because God can create it directly in an indivisible atom (juz' la yatajaza). It contrasts this with the philosophers and Mu'tazila, who rely on empirical observation that life disappears when the body decays or the temperament is lost. Some Ash'aris present a logical argument against conditionality: if life were conditional on a body composed of parts, then either one life subsists in multiple parts (which is impossible because an accident cannot be in more than one substrate) or each part has its own life (leading to a circular condition or an arbitrary preference). This argument is then refuted by saying that life subsists in the whole composite, not each part. Even if the argument is granted, the opponent might claim it is a circularity by simultaneity or that the absence of a differentiator is not conceded. The passage concludes by reaffirming the majority view: life is not conditioned, and it can exist in simple substances like atoms. It then defines the terms: bodily structure means the body of four elements, and animal spirit means a subtle vapor from the heart that travels through arteries.
