Three Arguments for Surface as Place and Their Refutation
Al-Taftazani presents three arguments for surface and a response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three arguments that place is surface and the response that they rest on an unproven assumption of similarity between dimensions.

Core Idea
The arguments for surface place all assume two dimensions are similar — an unproven claim.

The first station presents three arguments that place must be surface, not dimension. However, the response shows that these arguments are built on a hidden assumption of similarity between dimensions, which itself requires justification.

Guided Path
1
Read the three arguments
Read each argument carefully. Note how each one tries to prove that place is surface.
The arguments are numbered: first, second, third.
2
Analyze the first argument
It argues that surface exists and admits motion, while dimension either leads to regress or is immobile.
3
Analyze the second argument
It claims that if body is located, its dimension would coincide with spatial dimension, causing two dimensions to overlap.

Refutation of the Dimension View of Place
Al-Taftazani refutes the dimension view of place by dividing it into imaginary and real and showing both are invalid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Taftazani's refutation of the view that place is dimension, by analyzing the two variants (imaginary and real) and their logical failures.

Core Idea
Place cannot be dimension, whether imaginary or real, because it requires properties only existence can have.

Al-Taftazani continues to defend the surface view of place by attacking the alternative that place is dimension. He splits the dimension view into two variants: the imaginary dimension of the mutakallimun and the real dimension of Plato. Both, he argues, lead to absurdities. This lesson walks through his reasoning, showing why place must have concrete properties that neither pure non-existence nor infinite regress can sustain.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of dimension
Read the passage and note the two subdivisions: imaginary (suppositional) and real (subsisting). Understand who holds each view.
The mutakallimun hold the imaginary view; Plato holds the real view.
2
Understand the refutation of imaginary dimension
Examine why the author rejects the imaginary dimension: place has properties like equality, sensory indication, and motion—pure non-existence lacks these.
Focus on the phrase 'لا شيء من العدم المحض والنفي الصرف كذلك'.
3
Understand the refutation of real dimension
See the author's argument that a real dimension capable of motion would itself need a place, leading to infinite regress and contradiction.
The argument uses the concept of 'حركة آنية' (instantaneous motion).
4
Grasp the problem of infinite regress
Consider why an infinite series of places is impossible: each place would have to be both inside and outside the series, a contradiction.
This relies on the earlier refutation of تسلسل (infinite regress).

Al-Taftazani's Objections to the Surface Theory of Place
Al-Taftazani lists absurd consequences of the surface theory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key objections raised by Al-Taftazani against the surface theory of place and why they only amount to strong probability rather than certain proof.

Core Idea
If place were a surface, absurd consequences would follow.

Al-Taftazani continues his critique of the prevailing definitions of place. Here he attacks the view that place is the inner surface of the containing body. He presents a series of hypothetical scenarios that would lead to contradictions or impossible outcomes. However, he concedes that these arguments, while compelling, do not constitute a formal proof.

Eight Signs That Place Is Void
Al-Taftazani lists eight arguments for the void theory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the eight key arguments that proponents of the void theory use to argue that place cannot be surface, and why these are considered strong indications but not definitive proofs.

Core Idea
Eight signs show why place must be void, not surface.

Al-Taftazani now presents the case for the void theory (place as bu'd) by compiling eight arguments from its proponents. Each argument shows that if place were surface, something we know by rational intuition would be contradicted. Read these carefully — they are a masterclass in using absurdity to prove a positive claim. Note at the end that the author calls these 'strong probability,' not certainty.

Core Idea
If void existed, motion with resistance would take the same time as without — an impossibility.

This lesson presents the first argument used by theologians to disprove the existence of void. It uses a thought experiment involving motion through substances of varying resistance. By following the proportional reasoning, you will see why assuming void leads to a logical contradiction.

The answer is that simple fire lacks ease of shaping, and softness (al-līn) is defined as accepting pressure into the interior with difficulty of separation. The passage then notes a hesitation about whether softness and hardness are tangible qualities or dispositions. Then, a further objection is cited from some predecessors who defined moisture as adhesion to what touches it, making it a quality requiring adhesion. Ibn Sina refutes this: if adhesion were the criterion, then the moister would adhere more, making honey wetter than water; instead, the true criterion is ease of accepting and leaving shape. The Imam (al-Razi) responds that the criterion is ease of adhesion, and ease of shaping follows from that.

الظاهر من كلام القوم أن أنواع اللون هي السواد والبياض والحمرة والصفرة وغير ذلك، وأنواع الكيفيات الملموسة هي الحرارة والبرودة والرطوبة واليبوسة، ونحو ذلك، إلا أن التحقيق هو أن أنواع اللون هي البياضات المخصوصة التي لا تتفاوت أفرادها كبياض الثلج مثلا، وكذا في السواد وغيره، بل في كل ما يقال بالتشكيك حتى إن النوع من الملموسات ليس مطلق الحرارة بل الحرارة المخصوصة التي تكون في أفرادها على السوية كحرارة النار الصرفة مثلاً، والنوع من المبصرات ليس مطلق الضوء، بل الضوء الخاص الذي لا يتفاوت فيه أفراده كضوء الشمس مثلاً، وإنما يقع الاشتباه من جهة أن الأنواع قد يكون لجملة جملة منها عارض خاص، واسم خاص كالبياضات المشتركة في تفريق البصر وفي اسم البياض والسوادات المشتركة في قبض البصر، وفي اسم السواد وكالحرارات والبرودات، ونحو ذلك فيتوهم أن تلك الجملة نوع واحد بخلاف الأضواء، فإنه لا ينفرد جملة جملة منها بعارض واسم فلا يتوهم ذلك فيه، بل ربما يتوهم كون المجموع نوعاً واحداً فاللون والضوء قد وقعا في مرتبة واحدة من المبصرات، إلا أن اللون جنس الألوان بخلاف الضوء لما فيه من التفاوت، والضوء توهم نوعية لتقارب أنواعه بخلاف اللون، وإنما توهم ذلك في جملة من أنواعه كالبياض مثلاً لتقارب أنواع البياض، وكالسواد لتقارب أنواع السواد وعلى هذا القياس فصار الضوء بمنزلة البياض مثلاً في أنه ليس نوعاً لما تحته ولا جنساً بل عارضاً، ومبنى ذلك على ما تقرر عندهم من أن القول بالتشكيك لا يكون إلا عارضاً لامتناع التفاوت في الماهية وذاتياتها، لأن الأمر الذي به يتحقق التفاوت حيث يوجد في الأشد دون الأضعف إن لم يكن داخلا في الماهية لم يتحقق التفاوت فيها بل كانت في الكل على السواء، وإن كان داخلا فيها لم يتحقق اشتراك الأضعف فيها لانتفاء بعض الأجزاء مثلاً الخصوصية التي توجد في نور الشمس دون القمر، إن كانت من ذاتيات الضوء لم يكن ما في القمر ضوء، وإلا لم يكن تفاوت النورين في نفس الماهية.
The apparent from the speech of the scholars is that the types of color are black, white, red, yellow, and others, and the types of tangible qualities are heat, cold, moisture, dryness, and so forth. However, the verified view is that the types of color are the specific whites whose individuals do not differ, like the whiteness of snow for example, and similarly in blackness and others, rather in everything that is said by gradation (tashkik) such that the type of tangible qualities is not absolute heat but the specific heat whose individuals are equal, like the heat of pure fire for example, and the type of visual things is not absolute light but the specific light whose individuals do not differ, like the light of the sun for example. And confusion only arises from the fact that some groups of types may have a specific accident and a specific name, like the whites sharing in dispersing the sight and in the name 'whiteness', and the blacks sharing in contracting the sight and in the name 'blackness', and likewise heats and colds, and so on, so one imagines that that group is one type, unlike lights, for no group of them has a distinct accident and name, so that is not imagined of them, rather one might imagine that the whole collection is one type. So color and light are in the same rank among visual things, except that color is a genus of colors unlike light because of the difference in it, and light [people] imagined to be a type due to the closeness of its types unlike color, and that was only imagined of a group of its types like whiteness for example due to the closeness of types of whiteness, and like blackness due to the closeness of types of blackness, and analogously, light became like whiteness for example in that it is neither a type for what is under it nor a genus but rather an accident. And the basis of this is what is established with them: that the claim of gradation (tashkik) is only accidental because of the impossibility of difference in the essence (mahiyya) and its essentials (dhatiyyat). For the thing by which difference is realized, when it exists in the more intense (ashadd) without the weaker (ad`af), if it is not included in the essence, difference is not realized in it, rather it is the same in all; and if it is included in it, the weaker does not share in it due to the absence of some parts. For example, the specificity that exists in the light of the sun but not the moon: if it were among the essentials of light, what is in the moon would not be light; otherwise, the difference between the two lights would not be in the essence itself.

Commentary
This passage is a clarification and argument about the true nature of species (anwa') of qualities like colors and tactile properties. It begins by stating the apparent view of scholars: that the types of color are general categories like black, white, red, yellow, etc., and the types of tangible qualities are heat, cold, moisture, dryness. Then it presents the verified view (tahqiq): the true types are the specific individuals that do not differ among themselves, for example, specific whites like the whiteness of snow, and similarly for blackness and others. This applies to everything said by gradation (tashkik). So the type of tangible qualities is not absolute heat but specific heat whose individuals are equal, like the heat of pure fire; the type of visual things is not absolute light but specific light whose individuals are equal, like sunlight. The passage then explains the cause of confusion (ishbah): some groups of species share a common accident and name, e.g., whites share the accident of dispersing vision and the name 'whiteness', blacks share contracting vision and the name 'blackness', heats share the accident of heat, etc. This leads one to mistakenly think that group is one single species. In contrast, lights do not have such a common accident and name per group, so that confusion does not arise; instead, one might imagine all lights are one type. Then it compares color and light: both are in the same rank of visual things, but color is a genus of colors, while light is not a genus due to difference within it; light is mistakenly thought to be a type because its types are close, but that mistake only occurs for groups like whiteness due to closeness. Accordingly, light is like whiteness: neither a type for what is under it nor a genus, but an accident. The basis (mabna) of all this is the established principle: gradation (tashkik) is only accidental because difference cannot occur in the essence (mahiyya) and its essentials (dhatiyyat). The reasoning: if the factor of difference exists in the more intense but not the weaker, and it is not part of the essence, then there is no difference in the essence itself—it is the same in all; if it is part of the essence, then the weaker does not share in that essence, because part of the essence is missing. For example, the specificity that makes sunlight different from moonlight: if that specificity were essential to light, then moonlight would not be light; if not, then the difference between the two lights is not in the essence of light itself. Hence, gradation is only accidental.
