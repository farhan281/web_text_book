Six Conditions for Individual Motion
Sanusi enumerates the six necessary conditions for individual motion and reduces them to three.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the six factors that must be unified for a motion to be considered one individual, and why Sanusi simplifies them to three.

Core Idea
Individual motion requires unity of subject, time, and medium.

Sanusi begins by listing six conditions that must be met for a motion to be one individual: the mover, the subject, the time, the starting point, the ending point, and the path. He then shows that some of these are implied by others, so we can suffice with only the subject, time, and medium. This passage lays the groundwork for understanding how motions are distinguished in kalam.

2
Identify the six conditions
The passage lists six factors: mover, moved subject, time, starting point, ending point, and path. Find each one in the text and note the examples.
The path is given as straight vs. curved.
3
Understand the simplification
Sanusi then argues that because the unity of the medium entails the start and end, we only need three conditions: subject, time, and medium. Ask yourself why this is logical.
4
Reflect on the significance
Think about why this classification matters for kalam. It helps define what makes a motion a single entity, which is crucial for theological arguments about divine action and creation.
This connects to broader discussions of change and temporality.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and objection-and-answer section regarding the individuation of motion. It begins by stating that for a motion to be considered one individual (al-wahda al-shakhsiyya), six conditions must be unified: the mover (who caused it), the moved subject, the time, the starting point, the ending point, and the path (e.g., straight or curved). These are illustrated with examples showing that Zayd's motion differs from 'Amr's due to different subjects, etc. However, the author immediately qualifies this: since the individual unity of the medium (the subject of motion) actually entails the unity of the start and end points (because a given subject moving in a given path must have a specific start and end), one can suffice with the unity of subject, time, and medium. An objection is then raised: why not suffice with subject and time alone, since a given subject moving in a given time must travel a specific distance, i.e., the path is entailed? The answer is that this implication holds only when the genre of motion is the same (e.g., both are translations in location). If the genre is different (e.g., one is a change in location, another in quality), then in the same time a subject can undergo multiple motions of different genres. Even within the same genre, motion over distance can coexist with growth, rarefaction, etc., so subject and time do not uniquely determine the path. Next, the author addresses the mover: a single continuous motion can be produced by multiple successive movers (like a body attracted by different magnets), so the mover does not affect the unity of motion. This does not imply multiple causes acting on one effect, because each mover contributes only a part, and the division is only conceptual, not actual, preserving the motion's continuous unity. Finally, an incomplete objection is presented: if motion is defined as 'traversal' (qat'), i.e., the imagined extension, it has no existence (perhaps meaning it would be unreal). The answer is not present in this text.

Objections on Motion's Individuation
Sanusi answers two objections regarding the sufficiency of subject, time, and medium, and argues that mover unity is not required.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two objections raised against Sanusi's simplification and his responses, including why the unity of the mover is not required.

Core Idea
A single continuous motion can have multiple movers without losing its identity.

After establishing that subject, time, and medium suffice for individuating motion, Sanusi anticipates two objections. First, one might argue that subject and time alone entail the distance, so they should be enough. Second, one might think that a single motion cannot have multiple movers. Sanusi addresses both, showing the subtlety of motion's ontology. This passage deepens our understanding of how motions are identified in kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection about distance
The objector suggests that subject and time alone should suffice because they imply a specific distance. Read the passage to see this objection.
The objection begins with 'لا يقال'.
2
Analyze Sanusi's response
Sanusi argues that this only holds when the genre of motion is the same; otherwise, multiple motions of different genres can occur in the same time and subject.
He gives examples like growth and blackening.
3
Read the objection about multiple movers
Next, the objector might think that a single motion cannot have multiple movers. Sanusi provides counterexamples like a body moving by successive attractions.
He uses water being heated by successive fires.
4
Understand why mover unity is not required
Sanusi explains that multiple movers can act on different parts of a motion, and the division is only imaginary, not actual, preserving unity.
Compare to the celestial sphere's motion.

Rest in Place and Other Categories
Defines rest in different categories and contrasts it with motion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two main definitions of rest (sukun) in Islamic theology: as a positive attribute of preservation and as a privation of motion, and how it applies to the categories of place, quantity, quality, and position.

Core Idea
Rest is not merely absence of motion, but also a positive preservation of relations and type.

In this investigation, the author examines the concept of rest in a detailed manner. He presents two interpretations: one that sees rest as a positive, existent attribute that preserves spatial relations or type, and another that sees it as the absence of motion in a capable subject. He also discusses whether some bodies can be neither moving nor at rest, using the example of an object in flowing water.

The eighth investigation: Rest in place. He said: The eighth investigation: Rest in place is preservation of relations, and in other [categories] is preservation of the type, so it opposes motion. Commentary
This passage is a definition and analysis of the concept of rest (sukun). The author first gives a definition: rest in place (ayna) is the preservation of spatial relations, and in other categories (quantity, quality, position) it is the preservation of the type (naw'). Thus rest opposes motion. He then mentions an alternative definition: rest as the absence of motion from that which is capable of motion, making it a privation rather than a positive attribute. He notes that rest in this positive sense falls under the four categories (ayna, kam, kayf, wad'). He elaborates: in place, rest means maintaining actual relations to surrounding objects by being fixed in one location. In the other three categories, rest means maintaining the actual type without change: for quantity, no growth or diminution; for quality, no intensification or weakening; for position, no change to another position. In this positive sense, rest is an existent thing (amr wujudi) that is opposed to motion. He then discusses the privation interpretation: if rest is defined as the absence of motion from a capable subject, then between motion and rest is the opposition of possession and privation. The qualifier 'from that which is capable' excludes cases where motion is absent due to incapability, such as accidents, separate substances, and bodies at instants of start or end of motion, and also bodies like the celestial spheres that cannot leave their places. Finally, he cites an opinion from 'the Imam' that there are bodies that are neither moving nor at rest, such as an object in flowing water that maintains its relative positions but does not stay in one place. The author adds 'and this [view] has consideration', indicating some doubt or discussion.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That rest is simply the absence of motion in all cases.
Correction: The passage shows that rest can be a positive preservation (existent) in place and other categories, and that the privation definition applies only to capable subjects, excluding bodies that cannot move or are at boundaries.

Checkpoint
According to the eighth investigation, what does rest in place (السكون في الأين) mean in a positive sense?
A) The absence of motion in a place.
B) The preservation of the body's type without change.
C) The preservation of the body's actual spatial relations to things with positions, being stable in one location.
D) The inability of the body to move.

Key Takeaway
Rest in Islamic theology has both a positive meaning (preservation) and a privative meaning (absence of motion), and their application differs across categories like place, quantity, quality, and position.
