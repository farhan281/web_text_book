Three Mu'tazilite Arguments for Power with the Act and Ash'ari Responses
Three Mu'tazilite arguments and Ash'ari responses on power and act.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three Mu'tazilite arguments that power must precede the act and the Ash'ari rebuttals to each.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazilites argue that power must be before the act, but Ash'aris show that power can be simultaneous.

This passage presents three distinct arguments from the Mu'tazilites against the Ash'ari view that power (qudra) is concurrent with the act. The Ash'ari responses highlight key distinctions about the nature of bringing into existence, the conditions of obligation, and the dissimilarity between eternal and created power. Let's read the arguments and their answers carefully.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazilite arguments
Identify the three impossibilities they claim follow from power being simultaneous with the act: (1) bringing into existence what exists, (2) invalidating obligation, (3) making all possibilities eternal.
Notice the logical structure of each argument.
2
Examine the first answer
The Ash'ari response concedes the definition but distinguishes: bringing into existence what already exists is impossible only if the existence comes from another cause; with this very act of bringing into existence, it is not impossible.
This answer saves the Ash'ari position from the charge of tautology.
3
Examine the second answer
The Ash'aris clarify that obligation does not require the act to be actually within power at the moment of command; it suffices that it is generally possible. They give the example of a disbeliever's faith and note that the opposite (refraining) can be within power.
This rebuts the Mu'tazilite claim that taklīf would be invalid.
4
Analyze the third answer
The Ash'ari response denies the necessary similarity between eternal and originated power. The fact that originated power is with the act does not imply that eternal power is also with the act. The response is incomplete but the point is clear.
This guards against the argument that God's power would be temporal.

And the Mu'tazilites argued with several aspects: The first: that if power were not attached to the act except at the time of its existence and coming-to-be, then impossibilities would follow: first, bringing into existence what already exists and obtaining what is already obtained, because this is the meaning of the attachment of power. Second, the invalidity of obligation because obligation to an act is only before its occurrence, necessarily, since there is no meaning to requesting what is already obtained; so if the act before its occurrence is not within power, then all the obligations that have occurred are obligating what is unbearable, which is invalid by agreement, because the one who permits it does not say it occurs, let alone its universality. Third, all possibilities that occur by the power of God the Exalted would be eternal, because that which is simultaneous with the eternal is necessarily eternal. If it is said: the Mu'tazilites do not affirm eternal power. We say: No, rather they only dispute over it being an attribute superadded to the essence, and if it is conceded, then it is obligatory [to accept the consequence]. The answer to the first, after conceding that the meaning of the attachment of originated power to the act is its bringing into existence, is that it is impossible to bring into existence what exists with an existence obtained by other than this bringing into existence; but with this bringing into existence, it is not [impossible]. And to the second, that the one who says that power is with the act does not require that the obligated act be actually within power at the time of obligation, but rather that it be possible of occurrence for the obligated one and within power in a general sense, like the faith of the disbeliever, unlike the creation of bodies and similar things that cannot be within the power of the servant at all. And close to this is what is said: the meaning of the obligated act being conditional upon power is that it or its opposite is that to which power is attached, and here power has been attached to the abandonment of faith. And to the third, by denying the concomitance; it would only hold if eternal and originated power were similar, so that from the second being with the act and not before it, the first would also be so. And it may be answered that the discourse is only about the attachment of power, and the eternal is itself...

Commentary
This passage presents three Mu'tazilite arguments that power must precede the act, followed by Ash'ari responses. The first argument: if power only attaches to the act at its existence, this would entail bringing into existence what already exists. The answer concedes the meaning but distinguishes: it is impossible to bring into existence what exists from another cause, but with this very act of bringing into existence it is not. The second argument: obligation would be invalid because the act is not yet within power. The answer: the Ash'ari view does not require the act to be actually within power at the time of obligation; it suffices that it is generally possible from the servant, like the faith of a disbeliever—it is not actually within his power now but he can choose it. Also, the condition of power can be satisfied by either the act or its opposite being within power; here, refraining from faith is within power. The third argument: if power is with the act, then all things done by God's power would be eternal because what accompanies the eternal is eternal. The answer denies the concomitant similarity between eternal and originated power; the requirement that power come before the act for the eternal does not apply to originated power. The response ends mid-sentence, but the gist is that the discussion is about the attachment of power, not the nature of the eternal.

Eternal Power and Temporal Acts: A Refinement
Author asserts that eternal power's relation to acts is temporal and responds to al-Amidi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the eternity of divine power does not require the act to be eternal, and how al-Amidi's objection is answered.

Core Idea
Eternal power can relate to temporal acts without making them eternal.

The author begins by addressing a potential misunderstanding: if God's power is eternal, does that mean His acts are also eternal? He clarifies that the relation of power to an act can be temporal even if power itself is eternal. This sets the stage for engaging with al-Amidi's objection.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening assertion
Read the first part of the passage where the author states that eternal power does not preclude a temporal relation to acts. Note the key terms: قدرة (power), قديمة (eternal), تعلق (relation), مقارن (concomitant), حادث (originated).
2
Examine al-Amidi's objection
The author paraphrases al-Amidi's argument: eternal power cannot relate to an act in pre-eternity because the act was impossible then; it only relates in the future. Identify the logical structure of this objection.
3
Analyze the author's rebuttal
The author notes that the objection from al-Mawaqif does not apply to al-Amidi's view. Then he refines the point about time: even if the act was impossible in pre-eternity, it became possible later, so the relation can be established. This refutation preserves the distinction between eternal power and temporal acts.

Power, and its being eternal and prior does not contradict its relation being concomitant and originated. So it does not follow from the relation of eternal power with the act that the originated is eternal or that the eternal becomes originated. And if one understands what al-Amidi said: that eternal power, even though it precedes all possible objects, relates only to possible acts, and the act in pre-eternity is not possible, so it does not relate to it in pre-eternity, but only in what never ceases to be – in this sense, the objection of al-Mawaqif is not directed at him: that [this view] entails what the arguer already committed himself to, along with explaining a cause for it in eternal power, so let there be another cause also for originated [power]; and that the act in pre-eternity, though impossible, was possible in what never ceases to be, in a time prior to the time in which it existed, so the relation of power to it is permissible; and if concomitance were required, then the act would have to be in the earlier time without the later. Yes, it is objected: the discussion is about the relation of power in the sense that it is correct for us to say 'so-and-so is capable of such-and-such, able to do it and leave it' – and this does not lag behind power itself, not in the sense that when it is related to the object, it is its emanation from the capable agent; when related to power, it is power's necessitation of the object; when related to the capable agent, it is His creation and bringing into existence. For this is concomitant without dispute, originated in the case of the Eternal as well. His act.

Commentary
This passage refines the concept of divine power (qudra) by addressing an objection from al-Amidi. The author first asserts that the eternal nature of power does not preclude its relation to originated acts. He then paraphrases al-Amidi's argument: eternal power cannot relate to an act in pre-eternity because the act was impossible then; it relates only in the future. The author notes that the objection from al-Mawaqif—that this view leads to self-commitment and could be applied to originated power—does not apply to al-Amidi's. The author then presents a stronger objection of his own: the relevant sense of 'power' is the capacity to do or leave an act, which does not lag behind power itself. Another sense—where the act is the emanation from the agent—is concomitant and originated even for the Eternal. Thus the discussion is about the first meaning, and the author's position stands.

Two Senses of Divine Power
Author distinguishes two meanings of power and clarifies the debate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will distinguish between two meanings of 'power' (qudra) and understand which one is relevant to the debate.

Core Idea
Power as capacity is eternal; power as actualization is temporal.

The author concludes by sharpening the terminology. He argues that the sense of power relevant to the discussion is the capacity to do or leave an act, which does not lag behind power itself. The other sense—power as the actual bringing into existence—is temporal even for God. This refines the argument against al-Amidi.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage identifying two senses
Read the final part of the source where the author explicitly distinguishes two meanings of 'power' (قدرة). Pay attention to the phrases: 'الذي يصح قولنا فلان قادر على كذا متمكن من فعله وتركه' and other descriptions.
2
Understand the first sense: capacity to do or leave
The author states that the relevant sense is when we say someone is capable of doing or leaving an act. This sense does not lag behind power itself and is eternal for God.
3
Understand the second sense: actualization
The author presents another sense where power is related to the act as its emanation from the agent, or as power's necessitation, or as creation. This sense is concomitant and originated even for God.
4
See how this resolves the debate
The author concludes that the discussion is about the first sense, which is eternal, while the second sense is temporal. This clarifies that eternal power does not force eternal acts.

Two Consequences of Power with the Act and the Mu'tazila Objection
Sanusi states two consequences of power with act and records the Mu'tazila's objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two logical consequences of the thesis that power is with the act, and the Mu'tazila's objection based on the distinction between constrained and paralyzed.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila argue that prevention does not negate capability.

The author has established that power is inseparable from the act. Now he draws two implications: first, that someone prevented from acting is not capable; second, that one power cannot relate to two objects. The Mu'tazila immediately object, claiming a necessary distinction between the constrained and the paralyzed.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation to grasp the two consequences and the objection. Focus on the terms 'mamnu' (prevented) and 'zamin' (paralyzed).
2
Identify the two consequences
List them: (1) the prevented person is not capable, like the paralyzed; (2) one power does not relate to two objects.
3
Understand the Mu'tazila objection
They distinguish between constrained (muqayyad) and paralyzed (zamin) - prevention does not imply change in essence or attribute.
This is a key point of contention.

Opposition of Power to Character

Core Idea
Power and character oppose each other because they cause actions in opposite ways.

In this passage, Taftazani explains a subtle point of kalam: how power and character relate as opposites. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the debate on the cause of slowness in motion between philosophers and theologians, including the two objections raised by philosophers and the response that the correlation is merely customary.

Core Idea
Slowness is explained by theologians as rests intercalated, but philosophers object that this would break necessary correlations.

This passage introduces a key debate in Islamic natural philosophy: what causes slowness in motion? The theologians propose that slowness arises from an increase in moments of rest within motion, while the philosophers argue that this would lead to logical absurdities. We will examine the philosophers' first two objections and the theological response, which redefines correlation as customary rather than necessary.

And the cause of its slowness may be the will itself, as in throwing a stone and moving the hand gently, and there is no concealment of the causality of these things in general. But according to the philosophers, from the aspect that they become a cause for the weakness of the inclination (mayl) which is the proximate cause of motion, so the effect weakens. And according to the theologians, from the aspect that then the intercalation of rests (sakanāt) increases, which motion is never free from their existence, and motion varies in speed and slowness according to their fewness and abundance. The philosophers denied this by several arguments: First: If slowness were due to the intercalation of rests, then the correlation (talāzum) of two motions would be impossible with unity of time and difference of distance in length and shortness, because the motion in the shorter distance would be slower necessarily due to unity of time and difference of distance, so the intercalation of rests in it would be greater, and so it would be true that it might not move. Second: When the first moves, it is not true that whenever the second moves the first moves, and vice versa, according to the meaning of correlation – this is a contradiction. But the necessary is false because of the actuality of correlation with difference of distance in many cases, like the movement of the sun with what is imagined of the movement of people's shadows – and we only say 'imagined' because the shadow is an accident with no motion; rather the first light, I mean the light obtained from facing the sun's body, only newly occurs to it, so it is seen as if moving to decrease, or the first light disappears and the shadow occurs little by little, so it is seen as if moving to increase – and like the movement of the two ends of the millstone, I mean the large and small circles, and like the movement of the two arms, the outer and middle, of the compass with three arms when drawing the large and small circles. Commentary
This passage begins by introducing a debate about the cause of slowness (بطء) in motion. The author notes that sometimes the cause is the will itself, as in gentle movements. However, the philosophers and theologians differ: the philosophers hold that slowness is due to a weakening of the inclination (mayl), which is the proximate cause of motion. The theologians, on the other hand, argue that slowness results from the intercalation of moments of rest (sakanāt) that always occur within motion, and speed or slowness depends on their fewness or abundance. The philosophers reject this view with several arguments. The first argument is that if slowness were due to rest intercalation, it would be impossible for two motions to be simultaneous (متحدة الزمان) but with different distances, because the shorter motion would have more rests and thus could be considered non-motion. The second argument concerns the correlation (تلازم) of two motions: if one moves, the other should also move simultaneously; but examples like the sun and shadow show correlation holds despite different distances, implying slowness cannot be due to rests. The author explains that the shadow is not actually moving but appears to due to changing light. Examples include the millstone and compass. The answer given is that this correlation is not necessary (عقلي) but merely customary (عادي), and can be broken, e.g., the sun moving while shadow stays still, and such separation is acceptable.
