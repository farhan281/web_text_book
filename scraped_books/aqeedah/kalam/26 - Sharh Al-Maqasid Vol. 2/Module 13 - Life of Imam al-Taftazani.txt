The Life of Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari: From Mu'tazilism to Ash'arism
Brief biography of Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key events in the life of Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari, including his transformation from a Mu'tazilite to a leading Sunni theologian, and his major works.

Core Idea
Al-Ash'ari was once a Mu'tazilite but recanted to found the Ash'arite school.

This lesson presents a concise biography of Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari, the founder of the Ash'arite theological school. Understanding his life helps contextualize the development of Sunni kalam. The passage covers his name, lineage, early education in Mu'tazilism, his return to Sunnism, his death, and his influential writings.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage carefully, then read the English translation to grasp the biographical details.
2
Identify the key biographical details
Note al-Ash'ari's full name, year and place of birth, his early Mu'tazilite training, his recantation, place and year of death, and his major books.
3
Reflect on the significance of his conversion
Consider how al-Ash'ari's personal intellectual journey from Mu'tazilism to Sunnism shaped the Ash'arite school and influenced Islamic theology.

هو علي بن إسماعيل بن إسحاق أبو الحسن من نسل الصحابي أبي موسى الأشعري مؤسس مذهب الأشاعرة كان من الأئمة المتعلمين المجتهدين. ولد في البصرة عام ٢٦٠ هـ وتلقى مذهب المعتزلة وتقدم فيهم ثم رجع وجاهر بخلافهم. توفي ببغداد عام ٣٢٤ هـ . من كتبه : مقالات الإسلاميين والإبانة ورسالة في الإيمان ومقالات الملحدين، والرد على ابن الراوندي وغير ذلك .
He is 'Ali ibn Isma'il ibn Ishaq Abu al-Hasan, from the lineage of the companion Abu Musa al-Ash'ari, founder of the Ash'arite school. He was among the learned, striving imams. He was born in Basra in the year 260 AH, learned the Mu'tazilite school and excelled in it, then he recanted and openly opposed them. He died in Baghdad in the year 324 AH. Among his books are: al-Maqalat al-Islamiyyin, al-Ibana, Risala fi al-Iman, Maqalat al-Mulhidin, al-Radd 'ala Ibn al-Rawandi, and others.

Commentary
This is a biographical note about Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari, the founder of the Ash'arite theological school. It provides his full name, lineage (descendant of the companion Abu Musa al-Ash'ari), his birth in Basra in 260 AH, his early training in Mu'tazilism where he excelled, his subsequent return to Sunnism and open opposition to Mu'tazilism, his death in Baghdad in 324 AH, and a list of his major works, including the well-known Maqalat al-Islamiyyin and al-Ibana.

Biography of Bishr ibn al-Mu'tamir
Brief biography of the Mu'tazili scholar Bishr ibn al-Mu'tamir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key biographical details of Bishr ibn al-Mu'tamir and his significance as a leading Mu'tazili scholar.

Core Idea
Bishr ibn al-Mu'tamir founded the Bishriyya sect and shaped Mu'tazilism in Baghdad.

Before we dive into theological debates, it helps to know the key figures. Bishr ibn al-Mu'tamir was a leading Mu'tazili jurist and theologian whose followers dominated Baghdad's Mu'tazila. This passage gives us a concise portrait of his life, works, and legacy.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic text once, then the English translation. Notice the concise style of biographical entries in classical sources.
2
Identify the main biographical facts
List the key pieces of information provided: his full name, lineage, sect, location, lifetime, unique doctrines, and date of death.
These facts form the core of his biography.
3
Note the reference to al-Shahrastani
The passage cites al-Shahrastani, who listed six unique doctrines of Bishr. This shows the importance of doxographical sources in sectarian studies.
4
Reflect on the significance of the Bishriyya sect
Consider that the Bishriyya is a Mu'tazili sub-sect named after him. Reflect on what it means for a theologian to have a sect named after him.
This highlights his influence among contemporaries.

Source Text

هو بشر بن المعتمر البغدادي، أبو سهل فقيه معتزلي مناظر من أهل الكوفة قال الشريف يقال : جميع معتزلة بغداد من مستجيبيه تنسب إليه الطائفة البشرية عاش في خلافة الرشيد، وقد عدد الشهرستاني ست مسائل انفرد بها بشر عن أصحابه من علماء المعتزلة له مصنفات في الاعتزال. مات ببغداد عام ۲۱۰ هـ . راجع دائرة المعارف الإسلامية ٣ ٦٦٠ والمللل والنحل للشهر ستاني وأمالي المرتضى (١: ١٣٦) .
He is Bishr ibn al-Mu'tamir al-Baghdadi, Abu Sahl, a Mu'tazili jurist and disputant from the people of Kufa. Al-Sharif said: it is said that all the Mu'tazila of Baghdad are among his responders; the Bishriyya sect is attributed to him. He lived in the caliphate of al-Rashid. Al-Shahrastani counted six issues in which Bishr was unique among his companions from the Mu'tazila scholars. He had works in i'tizal (Mu'tazilism). He died in Baghdad in 210 H. See Dā'irat al-Ma'ārif al-Islāmiyya 3:660, al-Milal wa al-Nihal by al-Shahrastani, and Amālī al-Murtaḍā (1:136).
Source pages p. 351

Commentary
This passage is a biographical note on Bishr ibn al-Mu'tamir. It identifies him as a Mu'tazili jurist and debater from Kufa, states that most Baghdad Mu'tazila were his followers—hence the sect named Bishriyya—and notes his lifetime under Caliph al-Rashid. Al-Shahrastani is cited for six unique doctrines of Bishr. His death is recorded in 210 AH. The passage also lists references for further reading.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Bishr ibn al-Mu'tamir is related to the later Ash'ari theologian Ibn Furak.
Correction: The Bishriyya sect is a Mu'tazili sub-sect, not connected to Ibn Furak (an Ash'ari). Bishr lived earlier (d. 210 AH) and was a Mu'tazili.

Key Takeaway
Bishr ibn al-Mu'tamir was a pivotal Mu'tazili figure whose followers (the Bishriyya) shaped Baghdad's theological scene, and his unique doctrines were cataloged by al-Shahrastani.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?

?
The passage notes that Bishr wrote works on Mu'tazilism and had six unique doctrines. Consider your own beliefs: are there any doctrines you hold that are unique to a particular sect or teacher, and have you critically evaluated them? What would you do if you found they were not from the authentic sources?
