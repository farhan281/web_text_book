The Contrapositive of the Causal Principle and Non-Permanent Accidents
Author explains contrapositive of causal principle and addresses non-permanent accidents.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If the cause is present, the effect is present; thus if the effect is absent, the cause must be absent.

This passage extends the causal principle to its contrapositive and addresses a subtle objection regarding non-permanent accidents. The author explains that even when an accident like movement ceases part by part, the complete cause must have ceased in some aspect. We'll unpack this reasoning step by step.

Commentary

He then provides the proof for the first thesis (impossibility of two independent causes for one effect). The proof is given in two forms. The species as a whole requires multiple causes without contradiction. The passage ends by addressing an objection from Imam (likely al-Razi) about heat being of one species; the response is that 'species' here is more general than the logical species.

Core Idea
A genuine contradiction requires the same proposition to be both true and false.

The philosophers argue that if the One, being simple, produced two distinct effects, that would imply a logical contradiction. ورد بعد تسليم كون القبول أثراً بأنا لا نسلم أن الواحد لا يصدر عنه إلا الواحد، على أنه لو صح ذلك لزم أن لا يكون الواحد قابلاً لشيء وفاعلاً لآخر. فإن دفع باختلاف الجهة، فإن الفاعلية لذاته وقابليته باعتبار تأثره عما يوجد المقبول. قلنا : فليكن حال القابلية والفاعلية للشيء الواحد أيضاً كذلك. فإن قيل : الشيء لا يتأثر عن نفسه. قلنا : أول المسألة، ولم لا يجوز باعتبارين كالمعالج لنفسه. فإن قيل : الكلام على تقدير اتحاد الجهة. قلنا : فيكون لغواً إذ لا اتحاد جهة أصلاً. الثاني : أن نسبة الفاعل إلى المفعول بالوجوب، ونسبة القابل إلى المقبول بالإمكان. لأن الفاعل التام للشيء من حيث هو فاعل يستلزمه، والقابل له لا يستلزمه بل يمكن حصوله فيه، فيكون قبول الشيء للشيء، وفاعليته له متنافيين لتنافي لازميهما أعني الوجوب والإمكان. واعترض بأنه إنما هي إمكان عام، لأن معنى قابلية الشيء للشيء أنه لا يمتنع حصوله فيه، وهو لا ينافي الوجوب. وقيل : بل معناه أنه لا يمتنع حصوله فيه ولا عدم حصوله. وهو معنى الإمكان الخاص، ولو فرضناه الإمكان العام، فليس معناه أحد نوعيه، أعني الوجوب بل معناه مفهومه الأعم بحيث يحتمل الإمكان الخاص فينا في تعين الوجوب الذي لا يحتمله. والجواب بعد تسليم ذلك : أنه يجوز أن يكون الشيء واجباً للشيء من حيث كونه فاعلاً له غير واجب من حيث كونه قابلاً له.
After conceding that receptivity is an effect, [we say:] we do not accept that from one only one proceeds. Moreover, if that were true, it would necessitate that one thing cannot be receptive to something and agent for another. If it is rebutted by [appeal to] difference of aspect, that agency is due to its essence and receptivity is due to being affected by that from which the received thing comes, we say: then let the case of receptivity and agency for one thing also be likewise. If it is said: a thing is not affected by itself. We say: that is the very matter at issue; why is it not permissible under two considerations, like one treating oneself? If it is said: the discussion is on the assumption of unity of aspect. We say: then it is futile, since there is no unity of aspect at all. Second: that the relation of the agent to the act is by necessity, and the relation of the recipient to the received is by possibility. For the complete agent of a thing, insofar as it is agent, necessitates it, while the recipient of it does not necessitate it but rather its occurrence in it is possible. Thus, a thing's being a recipient of something and its being an agent for it are contradictory due to the contradiction of their concomitants, namely necessity and possibility. And it was objected that it is only general possibility, because the meaning of a thing's being receptive of something is that its occurrence in it is not impossible, and that does not contradict necessity. And it was said: rather its meaning is that its occurrence in it is not impossible and its non-occurrence is not impossible. That is the meaning of specific possibility. Even if we assume it is general possibility, its meaning is not one of its two types, namely necessity, but rather its meaning is the broader concept that admits specific possibility, unlike definite necessity that does not admit it. The answer, after conceding that, is that it is permissible for a thing to be necessary for something insofar as it is an agent for it, but not necessary insofar as it is a recipient for it.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical refutation of the philosophers' principle 'from one only one proceeds.' The author first concedes that receptivity is an effect but denies the principle itself, arguing that if it held, one thing could not be both agent and recipient of different things. The philosopher may defend by saying agency is by essence and receptivity by being affected from another; the author retorts that the same can be said for one thing being both agent and recipient under different aspects. When the philosopher objects that a thing cannot be affected by itself, the author says 'that's the issue' and gives an example of self-treatment. When the philosopher demands unity of aspect, the author calls it futile since aspects are different. Then the author provides a second argument: agency implies necessity (wujub) of the effect from the agent, while receptivity implies possibility (imkan) of the received in the recipient—these two relations are incompatible. The objector counters that receptivity only implies general possibility (imkan 'amm), which does not contradict necessity. Another says it is specific possibility (imkan khas). The author answers that even if general possibility, it is a broader concept that does not exclude specific possibility, but necessity does. The final resolution: it is permissible for one thing to be necessary in terms of being an agent for something but not necessary as a recipient—so the contradiction is resolved.
