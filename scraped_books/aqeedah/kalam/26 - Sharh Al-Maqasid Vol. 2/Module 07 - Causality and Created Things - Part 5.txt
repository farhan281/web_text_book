Commentary
This passage presents three new objections against the definition of time as the measure of motion. They are introduced as counter-objections (عورض) to the earlier argument. The implication is that the measure of a changing thing must be changing, and a changing measure cannot correspond to fixed entities because correspondence requires ordered parts. The consequent is false because we are equally certain that fixed entities exist in time. This refutes Abu al-Barakat's claim that permanent things require time for their duration. The objection shows the dilemma: if the measure is changing, it cannot correspond to fixed; if fixed, it cannot correspond to changing. If time measures the first, time would be fixed (impossible); if the second, time would be non-existent (impossible). The third objection argues that time is conceivable even without the sphere's motion, so it cannot be identical to its measure. The denial of time without motion is not self-evident. Two Meanings of Motion and Their Implication for Time
The author distinguishes two senses of motion to show that time cannot be its measure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two distinct senses of motion and why neither can serve as the basis for time as a measure.

Core Idea
Motion has two meanings: a stable state and an illusory extension; time fits neither.

In this lesson, we examine the second argument against identifying time as the measure of motion. The argument hinges on distinguishing two senses of the word 'motion'. Understanding this distinction reveals a logical dilemma: time would either be static or non-existent, both of which are impossible.

Commentary
This passage is the second argument against identifying time as the measure of motion. It begins by noting that 'motion' (حركة) has two meanings. Thus, time cannot be the measure of motion in either sense.

The Third Argument: Time Is Not the Measure of Motion
The author argues that time is conceivable without motion, so it cannot be the measure of the sphere's motion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the conceivability of time without motion refutes the view that time is the measure of the sphere's motion.

Core Idea
If time is conceivable without motion, it cannot be the measure of motion.

This lesson presents a key argument against the Aristotelian view that time is the measure of celestial motion. The author argues that time can be conceived even without any motion or sphere, proving its independence. We will read the passage, break down its logical structure, and see why the opponent's view fails.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and literal translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the English translation to get the overall argument.

Identify the conditional and its refutation
The passage begins with a conditional: if time were the measure of motion, then conceiving the sphere without time would be impossible. Then the author denies the consequent by giving an example of a period without sphere or motion.
This is a modus tollens argument: if P then Q, not Q, therefore not P.

Understand the conclusion about self-evidence

Commentary
This passage is a proof (dalīl) by reduction to absurdity that motion requires the existence of vacuum. The author, al-Taftazānī, presents a detailed case analysis. He begins by assuming that vacuum is absolutely impossible (lā yumkin al-khalāʼ). Then he shows that if a body moves from one place to another, the body that originally occupied the destination must either cease to exist (which is rejected as contrary to reason, e.g., pegs of a wheel) or persist. If it persists, it either stays in place or moves. If it stays, it either remains the same size (leading to interpenetration of two bodies in one space, which is absurd) or becomes denser. If it becomes denser, this is either by accepting a smaller size (hyle, which will be refuted later) or by having internal gaps of vacuum that close up—which would imply the existence of vacuum (contradiction). If the occupying body moves, it either goes back to the original place (leading to circular dependence) or to another place (leading to infinite regress and eventual circularity). The same reasoning applies to the vacated place. The conclusion is that the destination must either be pure vacuum or a body with vacuum gaps. This shows that vacuum must exist. The example of a fish moving through water illustrates the concept of gaps of vacuum (furuj al-khalāʼ) allowing motion. The argument is exhaustive and uses the principle of excluded middle.

Learning Objective

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of the four primary tangible qualities (al-kayfiyyat al-malmusa) and an in-depth analysis of the properties of heat. The author begins by stating that these qualities—heat, cold, wetness, and dryness—are the first perceptible qualities that no elemental body lacks. The author concludes that the non-occurrence of ascent or separation due to an obstruction does not negate that heat's essential property is ascent and separation of dissimilar and similar things.

The Mercury Argument Against Both Views on Heaviness
Uses a mercury and water experiment to refute both views on atom homogeneity and heaviness.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Empirical arguments in kalam often rest on metaphysical assumptions, such as the existence of void.

In classical atomist debates, the question of why objects have different weights was a point of contention. Some said weight depends on the number of atoms; others said it is directly created by God. The argument you are about to read uses a simple experiment with a waterskin, mercury, and water to raise a difficulty for both views. It is a classic example of how kalam thinkers used physical reasoning under specific assumptions.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof against both views (the view that heaviness is due to atom quantity, and the view that it is created by God arbitrarily). The Debate on I'timad: Qadi's View and the Truth about Natural Forces
Presents Qadi's denial, objection, truth about natural i'timad, and debate on unnatural.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Qadi's denial of multiple i'timad, the objection based on observed examples, and the correct position on natural vs. unnatural i'timad.

Core Idea
Natural i'timad are opposed, but natural and unnatural can coexist.

This passage dives into a nuanced debate in kalam about the nature of forces (i'timad) in bodies. Al-Qadi argued for a single force, but others pointed to examples like a rope pulled from both sides to argue for multiplicity. The text then clarifies the truth: natural forces are indeed opposed, but unnatural forces can coexist with natural ones. We'll walk through the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand al-Qadi's position

This is a reductionist view: one attribute with relational names.
2
Explore the objection
Look at the objection: some scholars say i'timad can be multiple because we observe a rope pulled from above and below simultaneously. This challenges al-Qadi's view.
The rope example shows opposite forces coexisting in one body.

The text states the truth: two natural i'timad (heaviness and lightness) are opposed and cannot coexist in the same respect. But a natural and an unnatural i'timad can coexist, as in a stone being lifted.
