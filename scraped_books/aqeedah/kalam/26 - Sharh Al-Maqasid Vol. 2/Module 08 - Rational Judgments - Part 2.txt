The Philosophers' Objection and the Theologians' Response
Sanusi presents the philosophical objection and the theologians' response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' objection to the impossibility of accident-in-accident and the theologians' classification of the examples.

Core Idea
Speed inhering in motion is not an accident inhering in an accident, but a mental consideration or inherence in a substance.

After presenting the proof, Sanusi turns to an objection raised by philosophers, who point to cases like speed of motion, smoothness of a surface, and straightness of a line as examples of accidents describing other accidents. The theologians respond by reclassifying these cases: some are mental considerations, some actually inhere in underlying substances, and some are substances themselves. Let's examine this dialectic.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and examples
Read the Arabic passage containing the objection. Notice the key terms: 'اختصاص ناعت' and the examples: 'سرعة الحركة', 'ملاسة السطح', 'استقامة الخط'.
2
Understand the philosophers' reasoning
The philosophers argue that subsistence can mean attributive specification, which does not require spatiality. Therefore, an accident can be an attribute of another accident.
They use examples like speed of motion, where speed is an accident and motion is also an accident.
3
See how theologians respond
The theologians reclassify the examples: some are mere mental considerations, some inhere in substances (not in accidents), and some are substances. They reject the idea that accidents have independent existence as a gross error.
Focus on the classification: 'بعض هذه اعتبارات وبعضها قائمة بالجواهر وبعضها جواهر'.

First Proof Against Accident Inhering in Accident: Spatial Dependency
First proof against accident inhering in accident based on spatial dependency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first proof used by the majority of mutakallimun to deny that an accident can inhere in another accident, based on the concept of dependency in spatial occupation.

Core Idea
An accident can only inhere in something that occupies space by itself.

The mutakallimun argued that accidents cannot inhere in other accidents because the very meaning of inherence involves dependency in spatial occupation. Only a substance (jawhar) is spatially self-subsistent. This lesson examines the first of two proofs they offered.

3

The final sentence adds that the spatially self-subsistent (substance) is not more deserving; it's a rhetorical reinforcement.
4
Reflect on the implications
Consider why this proof is important: it establishes a fundamental ontological distinction between substances and accidents, and it justifies why accidents must inhere directly in substances.

The proof argues that if an accident could inhere in another accident, then ultimately the chain would need to end in a substance, but it is more appropriate for the entire series to inhere directly in the substance.

Second Proof: Necessity of Ending in a Substance
Second proof: necessity of ending in a substance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second proof against accidents inhering in accidents, which demonstrates that a chain of accidents must ultimately terminate in a self-subsistent substance and that direct inherence in that substance is more appropriate than indirect inherence through other accidents.

Core Idea
The chain of accidents ends where self-subsistence begins.

This lesson presents the second proof from the author, which builds on the impossibility of an accident inhering in itself. If accidents could inhere in other accidents, an infinite chain would arise. This chain cannot continue indefinitely because accidents depend on a self-subsistent sustainer. The argument then shows that it is simpler and more logical for all accidents to inhere directly in the substance rather than through intermediaries.

Philosophers' Objection: Inherence as Attributive Specification
Philosophers' objection redefining inherence and allowing accident-in-accident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' redefinition of inherence as attributive specification and how it permits accidents to inhere in other accidents.

Core Idea
Inherence is not spatial dependency but one thing specifying another as its attribute.

This lesson presents the philosophers' objection to the mutakallimun's proofs that accidents cannot inhere in accidents. The philosophers reinterpret 'inherence' as attributive specification (ikhtisas na'ti) rather than dependency in spatial occupation. This redefinition opens the door for accidents to qualify other accidents, with examples like speed qualifying motion. You will see how this objection challenges the traditional view and its implications for divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the Philosophers' Objection
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Note the key claim: inherence is not about spatial dependency.
Focus on the redefinition of 'قيام' as 'اختصاص ناعت'.
2
Understand the Redefinition
The philosophers argue that inherence means attributive specification, as whiteness specifies the body, not the body specifying place. This definition does not require the substrate to be spatially located.
This allows attributes of God (non-spatial) to exist.
3
Examine the Application to Accidents
The philosophers concede that accidents ultimately terminate in a substance, but they deny all accidents inhere directly in it. They give examples: speed for motion, smoothness for surface, straightness for line.
In each example, the described thing is an accident, not the underlying body.
4
Reflect on the Consequence
This objection challenges the mutakallimun's proofs by showing that accident-in-accident inherence is possible, implying that the chain of inherence need not all be in the substance directly.
The philosophers thus permit accidents to inhere in other accidents.

The Zahiriyya Proof for the Impossibility of Accident Endurance
The commentator presents the Zahiriyya's two-fold argument against the persistence of accidents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two arguments used by the Ẓāhiriyya to deny the endurance of accidents and why the commentator considers them weak.

Core Idea
For the Zahiriyya, the very concept of an accident implies it cannot last.

The Ẓāhiriyya were a literalist school who held strong views on the nature of accidents. In this passage, the commentator presents their two-fold argument against the persistence of accidents, relying on both linguistic definition and a logical dilemma. This lesson will examine each argument and the commentator's assessment.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the structure: a general claim followed by two reasons introduced by 'لأن' and 'ولأنه'.
The introductory phrase 'المبحث الخامس' indicates this is a new topic.
2
Understand the first argument (definitional)
The Ẓāhiriyya claim that the word for accident (العارض) inherently implies non-endurance. Ask yourself: is this a semantic or a metaphysical argument?
3
Understand the second argument (hypothetical dilemma)
If an accident could endure, it would have to endure either by its substrate's endurance (making it like the substrate) or by a separate endurance (which would allow it to survive the substrate's annihilation). Both options are deemed absurd.
Trace the reasoning: either way leads to an unacceptable conclusion.
4
Reflect on the commentator's verdict
The commentator states that both arguments are 'weak and apparent' (ضعفهما ظاهر). Consider why he dismisses them and what this sets up for the stronger proofs to follow.
The weakness likely lies in the definitional argument being too semantic and the dilemma not being exhaustive.

Commentary
This passage presents the position of the Ẓāhiriyya (literalist) school regarding the persistence of accidents. It begins by stating the general claim: many theologians affirm that accidents cannot last for two consecutive moments of time. The Ẓāhiriyya support this with two reasons. First, they assert that the very linguistic and conceptual meaning of an accident (like 'āriḍ) implies instability and transience, so endurance is inherently impossible for it. Second, they argue hypothetically: if an accident were to persist, it would have to do so either (a) through the persistence of its substrate (the body it inheres in), but then the accident would be co-eternal with the body and would acquire all the body's properties, which is not the case; or (b) through a separate, additional persistence that could theoretically remain even after the substrate's annihilation—a scenario they deem absurd. The commentator notes that both arguments are weak and obvious in their flaw, setting the stage for the more rigorous proofs of the Muḥaqqiqūn that follow.

The First Proof: Accident Subsisting in Accident
First proof with immediate rebuttal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first proof of the Muhaqqiqun for the impossibility of accident endurance and its simple rebuttal by denying the premises.

Core Idea
If accidents endured, they would require accidents subsisting in accidents.

The Muhaqqiqun (verifying scholars) begin with a straightforward argument: if an accident were to endure, it would need to persist, and persistence itself is an accident inhering in it. This would mean an accident subsisting in another accident, which is unanimously deemed impossible. The rebuttal comes by flatly denying either that endurance is an accident or that it inheres.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic text once, then its translation. Note the concise structure: conditional statement and immediate rebuttal.
The passage is short but packs a strong claim.
2
Identify the two premises
The proof has two premises: (1) if an accident endures, then endurance is an accident inhering in it; (2) an accident subsisting in an accident is impossible. The rebuttal denies either premise.
Denying any premise suffices to defeat the proof.
3
Reflect on the dialectical move
Notice that the Muhaqqiqun themselves record the rebuttal. This shows intellectual honesty and the dialogical nature of kalam.

And the Muḥaqqiqūn [affirm it] for two reasons: First: If it were enduring, then the subsistence of an accident in an accident would be necessary, and that is impossible; it is replied by denying both premises. Second: If it endured, its cessation would be impossible; for if cessation were possible, then either (a) by itself, then its existence would be impossible, (b) by the removal of a condition, then an infinite regress would follow, (c) by the occurrence of a contrary, then there would be a vicious circle because the locus's being qualified by one contrary is conditioned by the negation of the other, and the removal of the enduring by the arriving contrary is not more appropriate – rather the opposite, because prevention is easier than removal – or (d) by an agent, then it would require an effect, and mere negation does not suffice. And it is replied first by counterexample (naqḍ) with the body; and it may be repelled by saying that it ceases by God creating in it the accident of annihilation, or by His not creating an accident that is the condition of endurance – but an accident is not fit to be a locus for an accident. Second, by inversion (qalb): if it did not endure, then its annihilation would be either by itself or by another. Third, by solution (ḥall): it is possible that its essence necessitates non-existence in some states; or that it is conditioned by accidents that are renewed in alternation until it reaches what has no substitute, then it ceases; or that the occurrence of the contrary and the negation of the other [occur] together, like the entry of each of the links of a ring into the space of the other and the exit of the other from it – and this does not contradict priority in reason by virtue of causality; or that the originated non-existence is an effect of the agent; and even if conceded, let it be in the sense that He does not do it, not in the sense that He does its non-existence.

Commentary
This passage contains the two main proofs of the Muḥaqqiqūn (the verifying scholars) for the impossibility of an accident enduring for two moments, followed by responses to three objections. The first proof argues that if an accident endured, it would necessitate an accident subsisting in another accident (since endurance itself would be an accident inhering in the first accident), which is impossible. The response to this is to deny either that endurance is an accident or that it inheres. The second proof is more elaborate: if an accident endured, it would be impossible for it to cease to exist. The argument considers all possible ways cessation could occur: (a) self-annihilation (impossible because existence would then be impossible), (b) removal of a condition (leading to infinite regress of conditions), (c) arrival of a contrary (leading to a vicious circle because the locus's qualification by one contrary depends on the absence of the other, and removing the enduring by the arriving contrary is no more necessary than the reverse), (d) by an external agent (which would require an effect, but nonexistence is not an effect). The passage then addresses three objections. First objection (naqḍ): the body itself endures – but the reply is that God either creates an accident of annihilation in it or does not create the accident of endurance, and an accident cannot be a locus for another accident. Second objection (qalb): if the accident does not endure, its annihilation would be either by itself or by another – thus mirroring the argument. Third objection (ḥall): various solutions are proposed, such as the accident's own nature causing non-existence in some states, or its endurance being conditioned by a series of renewing accidents until one has no substitute, or that the contrary's arrival and the other's absence occur simultaneously like interlocking rings, which does not conflict with causal priority, or that non-existence is directly produced by the agent, understood as the agent not creating the accident rather than creating non-existence. This detailed dialectic shows the sophistication of kalām argumentation.

The Second Proof: The Impossibility of Cessation
Second proof with four-fold division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the complex second proof of the Muhaqqiqun, which argues that if an accident endured, its cessation would be impossible, and the exhaustive enumeration of possible modes of cessation.

Core Idea
Endurance makes cessation impossible because every possible mode of cessation fails.

The second proof is more elaborate. It assumes an accident endures and then examines how it could cease to exist. The Muhaqqiqun consider four possible ways—by itself, by removal of a condition, by arrival of a contrary, or by an external agent—and argue that each leads to an absurdity. This shows that cessation is impossible, so endurance is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic carefully, noting the conditional structure and the list of modes of cessation.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the four modes
Find each mode: (a) by itself, (b) by removal of a condition, (c) by arrival of a contrary, (d) by an agent. Understand the absurdity argued for each.
The argument for (c) involves a vicious circle and the principle that prevention is easier than removal.
3
Understand why each mode fails
For each mode, the proof shows it leads to an impossibility: self-annihilation prevents existence, infinite regress, vicious circle, or effect from non-existence.
4
Connect to conclusion
Since all possibilities for cessation are eliminated, cessation is impossible. Hence, endurance is impossible.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.

Objections and Responses: Naqd, Qalb, and Hall
Three objections and their responses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three dialectical strategies (naqd, qalb, hall) used to object to the Muhaqqiqun's proofs and how they are countered.

Core Idea
The Muhaqqiqun's proofs are tested by naqd, qalb, and hall, showing the sophistication of kalām.

After presenting the two proofs, the text records three objections raised against the second proof. Each uses a distinct argumentative technique: naqd (counterexample), qalb (inversion), and hall (solution). The Muhaqqiqun then provide responses, further refining the argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the three objections
Read the Arabic and translation, identifying the three parts: naqd, qalb, hall.
Notice the Arabic keywords: النقض, القلب, الحل.
2
Understand naqd (counterexample)
The objection uses the body as a counterexample: it endures. The response explains that God either creates an accident of annihilation or withholds the condition of endurance.
The response also notes that an accident cannot be a locus for another accident.
3
Understand qalb (inversion)
The objection inverts the argument: if an accident does not endure, its annihilation is either by itself or by another—just like the original proof. This challenges the proof's asymmetry.
The text does not provide a separate response to qalb; it is mentioned succinctly.
4
Understand hall (solution)
The third objection offers multiple solutions: essential non-existence, conditional renewal, simultaneous occurrence, and agent-caused non-existence. The response qualifies the last: the agent does not create non-existence but rather does not create the accident.
This shows the depth of possible explanations within the kalām framework.

First Proof of Ahl al-Tahqiq and Its Rebuttal
Taftazani presents and rebuts the first argument of ahl al-tahqiq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first argument of ahl al-tahqiq against the persistence of accidents and how Taftazani rebuts it by denying the implication and the proof of impossibility.

Core Idea
The persistence of an accident is not a new accident; it is just its continued existence.

Ahl al-tahqiq argue that if an accident persisted, its persistence would be a separate accident subsisting in it, which they consider impossible. Taftazani counters this by explaining that persistence is merely the continuation of existence, not a new attribute, and that the impossibility of an accident in an accident is not proven. This debate illustrates the dialectical method in kalām.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the passage once carefully. Notice that it begins with the proof (احتج) and then the rebuttal (ورد).
The Arabic is concise; take your time with the translation.
2
Identify the two parts: proof and rebuttal
The passage has a clear structure: first the proof of ahl al-tahqiq (if persistence then accident-in-accident, which is impossible), then the rebuttal by Taftazani (two denials). Find these parts.
The rebuttal starts with 'ورد' (and it was rebutted).
3
Understand the key rebuttal points
Taftazani denies two things: (1) that persistence is an accident subsisting in the persistent—he says it's just continuation of existence; (2) that the impossibility of accident-in-accident is proven—he says the proof is not established.
Focus on the phrase 'منع الملازمة' (denying the implication) and 'منع انتفاء اللازم' (denying the denial of the consequent).

Life's conditionality on structure leads to impossibility or circularity; responses preserve the doctrine.

This passage presents a refined argument from the mutakallimun against the claim that life depends on bodily structure. Commentary
This passage presents a proof from some mutakallimun against the claim that life is conditional on bodily structure, along with a counter-objection and then the answer. The proof proceeds by analyzing two possibilities if life were conditional on structure: (1) Either a single life (hayāt wāḥida) inheres in two parts of the structure—this would require an accident (al-ʿaraḍ) to inhere in more than one locus (maḥall), which has already been established as impossible. (2) Or a separate life inheres in each part. In this case, two sub-possibilities arise: (a) The inherence in each part is conditional upon inherence in the other—this leads to circularity (dawr). (b) The inherence is not conditional upon the other—then there is no reason (tarjīḥ) to prefer inherence in both parts over one, since the parts are similar and the reality of life is one; so this leads to preference without a preferrer (tarjīḥ bilā murajjiḥ), which is impossible. The answer is: if life only inheres in some parts, then only those parts constitute the living entity, not the composite structure. That would contradict the claim that life is conditional on the entire structure. The passage then gives two accepted answers: First, life inheres in the composite whole (al-majmūʿ) which is the structured body. This is not like an accident in two loci because the composite is a single unit. Second, life does inhere in each part, but the conditionality of each part upon the other is by way of simultaneity (maʿiyyah) without temporal priority, so there is no circularity (since circularity requires priority). This is a sophisticated defense of the view that life does not require material conditions.

And in the aspect of sensory perception there is a repetition in terms of meaning, as if there were two presences: one before the perceiver and the other before the instrument. But it is not so; rather, presence before the soul is presence before the sense. The verification of the position is that when we perceive something, there is no hidden fact that a state occurs for us that was not, and nature almost testifies that it is due to the occurrence of something that was not, not by the removal of something that was. And that is nothing but distinction and manifestation of that thing before the intellect. And that is not its existence in the external world, because we often perceive what has no existence in the external world, from non-existents, even impossibles, and often a thing exists in the external world and the intellect does not perceive it, despite its longing for it. Rather, it is its existence in the intellect, meaning that an effect appropriate to that thing occurs in it such that if it existed externally it would be identical to it. This is the meaning of the occurrence of the form, its presence, its representation, its impression, and the soul's reaching it, and so on. Nothing else is understood from perception of a thing. Commentary
This passage defines the reality of perception and elaborates on its nature. It begins with the statement that perception involves a distinction and manifestation at the intellect, and that this is not the external existence of the perceived thing, because we often perceive non-existent things and fail to perceive existent ones. Rather, it is the intellectual existence, i.e., the form. The commentator then explains that the settled view of the investigating philosophers is that perception is the presence of the thing at the intellect, either by itself (as in intellectual perception of self-evident truths) or by its form (abstracted from matter or originally acquired). This is the meaning of the statement from Avicenna's al-Isharat: 'Perception of a thing is that its reality be represented before the perceiver, witnessed by that by which it perceives.' The passage clarifies that 'witnessing' here means absolute presence, not sight. The phrase 'that by which it perceives' indicates a division: perception can occur either directly in the intellect (without an instrument), where the form is impressed in the perceiver's essence, or through an instrument (the senses), where the form occurs in the sense organ. The example of sight is given: the form occurs in the vitreous humor. For the common sense, the imaginative form occurs in a connected place. The passage then addresses an apparent tautology in Avicenna's definition: for intellectual perception, the wording seems redundant ('presence before the perceiver at the moment of its presence'); for sensory perception, there is conceptual redundancy as if there are two presences. But the author argues that this is not a real problem: intellectual perception's instrument is the essence itself, so the repetition is only verbal; sensory perception involves the sense organ, but presence before the soul is the same as presence before the sense. The verification is that perception is a new state of distinction and manifestation at the intellect, not the external existence, because we perceive non-existents and sometimes fail to perceive existents. Rather, it is the existence of an effect in the intellect that corresponds to the object, i.e., the form. The passage concludes by dismissing the objection that perception is an attribute of the perceiver while the form is an attribute of something else; for the investigators, the terminology is flexible.

Commentary
This passage presents a key epistemological debate: whether sensation (al-ihsas) is identical to knowledge (al-`ilm). The author begins by stating al-Ash'ari's position: sensation of a thing is knowledge of it, differing from other knowledge only in its object (mut`allaq). The objection is raised by noting a necessary (daruri) difference between complete knowledge of something and sensing it. If al-Ash'ari means they are types of knowledge, the dispute becomes verbal, hinging on the definition of 'knowledge'. The author then clarifies terminology: 'knowledge' in its strict sense excludes sensation; it is applied to various cognitive states such as assent (tasdiq) which can be decisive (jazm), conforming (mutabiq), stable (thabit), and these yield certainty (yaqin). Lacking one of these gives rise to zann (conjecture), compound ignorance (jahl murakkab), or belief (i`tiqad). Doubt (shakk) and illusion (wahm) are not considered assent. The author then gives examples: seeing, hearing, smelling are claimed by al-Ash'ari to be knowledge. The majority (jumhur) rejects this by pointing to the obvious difference between knowing something thoroughly and sensing it. An attempted answer is given: it may be denied that what is sensed can be known in another way; even if granted, they could be two different knowledges. But this answer is weak: first, it is obvious that one can have knowledge of something without sensing it (e.g., we judge a thing to be without sensation); second, the majority's point is that the essence of sense perception is different from the essence of knowledge. Once it is admitted they are different types of perception, the argument becomes verbal—whether to call both 'knowledge' or not. The author concludes that applying 'knowledge' to sensation is contrary to common usage and language; 'knowledge' is reserved for other perceptions, and it may be further specified as perception of the whole, the composite, or certain types of assent such as certainty. He ends by detailing the subdivisions: non-decisive is zann, non-conforming is compound ignorance, non-stable is i`tiqad.

This passage presents a key epistemological debate: whether sensation (al-ihsas) is identical to knowledge (al-`ilm). The objection is raised by noting a necessary (daruri) difference between complete knowledge of something and sensing it. If al-Ash'ari means they are types of knowledge, the dispute becomes verbal, hinging on the definition of 'knowledge'. The author then clarifies terminology: 'knowledge' in its strict sense excludes sensation; it is applied to various cognitive states such as assent (tasdiq) which can be decisive (jazm), conforming (mutabiq), stable (thabit), and these yield certainty (yaqin). Imagine a foundational belief you hold—one that feels necessary for your faith. Would you be willing to subject it to the same rigorous analysis that Imam al-Haramayn applied to necessary knowledge? What would you do if you found it rested on circular reasoning?
?
If your intellect's perfection depends on certain necessary truths, and you have never examined whether those truths could become theoretical, then are you building your worldview on unchecked premises? Will you, from now on, investigate which of your beliefs are truly necessary and which are merely assumed?
