Objection from Straight Motion and the Case of Angles
Addresses objection from straight motion and expounds on angles and rest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection from straight motion is answered by considering divisibility as imaginary and how angles relate to motion and rest according to some philosophers.

Core Idea
The division of distance in motion is merely imaginary, and rest may intervene between motions.

This lesson addresses a specific objection: in straight motion, every point of arrival and departure seems to require distinct instants. The response is that the division of distance is purely imaginary. It then discusses the view that between two straight motions there is a moment of rest, and clarifies the nature of angles in turning versus returning.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection paragraph
Read the first part of the passage which presents the objection: the sphere rotating on a surface, with distinct instants of arrival and departure.
Focus on the phrase 'يلزم تتالي الآنين أو تخلل زمان السكون'.
2
Understand the response
Read the response: 'فقد يرد بأن انقسام المسافة هنا محض توهم'. Note that the division is considered imaginary, not real.
3
Examine the philosophers' view
Read the part about some philosophers and theologians holding that rest occurs between two straight motions, whether return or turn.
Note the distinction between 'رجوع' (return) and 'انعطاف' (turn).
4
Analyze the discussion on angles
Read the final part about angles: an angle occurs only in turning, not return. The expression 'لا اتصال لذوات الزوايا' is interpreted in terms of locative motions.

The answer: After granting the impossibility of a part, it is [already] actually [the case] as long as time is not cut off. Unless what is meant is a time that is not divisible except in estimation; in that case we do not grant that the two instants of arrival and return are distinct, nor the impossibility of the succession of two instants. As for the objection by every straight motion, especially if we rotate a sphere on a surface, then the instant of arrival at each point is other than the instant of departure from it, and there follows the succession of two instants or the interposition of a time of rest. It may be replied that the division of distance here is merely imaginary. Some philosophers and theologians held that between any two straight motions there is a time in which the moving thing is at rest, whether the second is a return to the first direction or a turn to another direction. There is no hiddenness in that the existence of an angle occurs only on the assumption of turning, not return, because the line is one. So the expression of abstraction—that there is no continuity for the essences of angles and no turning—is not as it should be, and it has been interpreted that there is no continuity for the locative motions that produce points that are points of angles of return, nor for those that produce points that are points of angles of turning.

Commentary
This passage presents the response (الجواب) to the initial claim. It begins by accepting the impossibility of an indivisible part (الجزء) but argues that time is not actually divided—it is only divisible in estimation (بالوهم). If we consider time as only conceptually divisible, then we do not accept that the instants of arrival and return are distinct, nor the impossibility of their succession. Then it addresses a counterexample: a sphere rotating on a surface—each point of contact involves an instant of arrival and departure, which would seem to require either successive instants or rest. But this is answered by saying the division of distance is merely imaginary. The passage then discusses the view of some philosophers and theologians that rest occurs between two straight motions, whether the second is a return or a turn to another direction. It critiques the statement that there is no continuity for angles and turning, explaining that the supposed lack of continuity refers to the locative motions that produce points of angles. This lesson covers the nuanced responses and clarifications regarding the nature of time, instants, and motion.

The Instantaneity of Arrival and the Common Boundary
Philosophers' proof and response about common boundary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' proof that arrival at a point must be instantaneous and the kalam response that arrival and departure can share a single instant as a common boundary.

Core Idea
Arrival and departure can share a single instant like a shared point between lines.

This passage dives into the heart of the debate about the nature of motion and rest. The philosophers argue that if arrival were temporal, it would lead to absurdities, forcing it to be instantaneous. But the response challenges the necessity that instants must be distinct, offering a nuanced view of time as composed of boundaries. Let's unpack this intricate reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow, then read the literal translation. Notice the structure: first the philosophers' claim, then their argument, then the response.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the philosophers' core claim
Find where they state that arrival is instantaneous (آني). Underline the reasoning: if it were temporal, then in half the time either arrival occurs or not, leading to contradiction.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Understand the response: the common boundary
The response denies that the two instants (arrival and return) must be distinct. It proposes they can coincide in one instant that is a common boundary between two times, like a point shared by two lines.
The analogy with a point on a line is crucial.
4
Clarify why no contradiction
The response explains that this is not a conjunction of opposites (اجتماع النقيضين) because 'arrival' and 'non-arrival' are not predicated of the same identical aspect. The body arrives in one respect and departs in another.
Think of it as two different descriptions of the same instant.

Source Text

والعمدة في احتجاج الفلاسفة أن الوصول إلى النهاية آني إذ لو كان زمانياً ففي نصف ذلك الزمان إما أن يحصل الوصول فلا يكون في ذلك الزمان بل في نصفه، أو لا يحصل فلا يكون المفروض زمان وكذا الرجوع . أعني ابتداءه الذي قد يعبر عنه باللاوصول واللامماسة، والمباينة والمفارقة فلا يرد ما قيل إن كلاً من ذلك حركة وهي زمانية لا آنية ، ثم الآنات متغاير إن ضرورة فإن لم يكن بينهما زمان لزم تتالي الآنات، فيكون الامتداد الزماني الذي هو مقدار الحركة متألفاً من الآنات وهو منطبق على الحركة المنطبقة على المسافة فيلزم وجود الجزء الذي لا يتجزأ، وإن كان بينهما زمان ولا حركة فيه تعين السكون ، ولما كان منع ضرورة تغاير الآنين ظاهر بناء على جواز أن يقع الوصول واللاوصول. أعني نهاية حركة الذهاب وبداية حركة الرجوع في آن واحد هو حد مشترك بين زمانيهما، كالنقطة الواحدة التي تكون بداية خط ونهاية خط آخر، وليس هذا من اجتماع النقيضين . أعني الوصول واللاوصول في شيء لأن معناه أن يصدق على الشيء أنه واصل وليس بواصل ، لا أن يحصل له الوصول، وابتداء الرجوع الذي هو لا وصول كما يحصل للجسم الحركة والسواد الذي هو لا حركة.
The main point in the philosophers' argument is that arrival at the end is instantaneous, for if it were temporal, then in half of that time either the arrival occurs—so it does not occur in that time but in half of it—or it does not occur, so the supposed time is not [time]. Similarly for the return—I mean its beginning, which is sometimes expressed as non-arrival, non-contact, separation, and departure—so what was said that each of these is motion and is temporal, not instantaneous, does not apply. Then the instants are necessarily distinct. If there is no time between them, then a succession of instants follows, so the temporal extension which is the measure of motion becomes composed of instants, and it corresponds to the motion that corresponds to the distance, so the existence of the indivisible part follows. And if there is time between them and no motion in it, then rest is determined. Since denying the necessity of the distinctness of the two instants is apparent based on the permissibility that arrival and non-arrival—I mean the end of the going motion and the beginning of the returning motion—occur in one instant that is a common boundary between their times, like a single point that is the beginning of one line and the end of another line. And this is not at all a conjunction of opposites—I mean arrival and non-arrival—because its meaning is that a thing is truly described as 'arriving' and 'not arriving', not that arrival occurs for it. The beginning of return, which is non-arrival, is like a body acquiring motion and blackness, which are not motion.
Source pages p. 450

Commentary
This passage presents the main argument (العمدة) of the philosophers to prove rest between two straight motions. The argument proceeds in steps: First, arrival at a point must be instantaneous, otherwise there would be an infinite regress. Second, the instants of arrival and return are necessarily distinct. If no time intervenes between them, then successive instants occur, implying that time is composed of instants, which would require an indivisible part (atom of time). If time does intervene and there is no motion during it, then the body is at rest. The response counters by denying the necessity of the distinctness of the two instants. It argues that it is possible for the arrival of the outgoing motion and the beginning of the return motion to occur in a single instant, which is a common boundary between the two times, just as a point can be the end of one line and the beginning of another. This does not involve a contradiction because 'arrival' and 'non-arrival' are not predicated of the same thing in the same aspect: the body is arriving in one sense and beginning to depart in another. This is analogous to a body acquiring both motion and blackness—these two attributes can be simultaneous without contradiction.

Alternative Formulation: Motion, Inclination, and the Necessity of Rest
Presents an alternative argument that motion depends on a cause (ميل) which cannot persist, leading to rest.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the kalam argument for rest is re-framed using the concept of 'motional inclination' (ميل) and why rest necessarily occurs between opposite motions.

Core Idea
Motion requires a cause (ميل); between opposite motions, the body lacks this cause — that is rest.

In this passage, the author presents an alternative version of the proof that motion requires a cause and that rest necessarily follows. Instead of focusing on the general principle that every motion needs a mover, the argument zeroes in on a specific cause called 'ميل' (inclination) that propels the body toward a limit. By examining the instant of arrival and the impossibility of having two opposite inclinations simultaneously, the argument demonstrates that a period of rest must intervene between two opposing motions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core terms
Recognize that 'ميل' is the technical term for the cause of motion. Note its definition: a cause that moves the body from one limit to another.
This term is central to the alternative formulation.
2
Follow the logical sequence
Understand how the argument moves: motion arises from ميل; ميل exists at the instant of arrival; non-arrival requires a second ميل in a second instant; two opposite ميل cannot coexist; thus between the two instants there is a time without ميل, hence without motion.
The impossibility of two consecutive instants without time between them is key.
3
Grasp the conclusion
Realize that the absence of motion and its cause (ميل) is precisely what is meant by rest (سكون).
Rest here is a technical term, not merely absence of motion but absence of the internal cause.

وقرر هذه الحجة بوجه آخر وهو أن الحركة إنما تصدر عن علة موجودة تسمى باعتبار كونها مزيلة للمتحرك من حد ما مقربه له إلى حد آخر ميلاً وهي العلة للوصول إلى الحد، وإن لم يسم باعتبار الاتصال ميلاً فتكون موجودة في آن الوصول، إذ ليس الميل من الأمور التي لا توجد إلا في الزمان كالحركة ثم اللاوصول أعني المباينة عن ذلك الحد لا تحدث إلا بعد حدوث ميل ثان في آن شان ضرورة امتناع اجتماع الميل إلى الشيء مع الميل عنه في آن واحد ولاستحالة تتالي الآنين يكون بينهما زمان يكون الجسم فيه عديم الميل فيكون عديم الحركة، وهو معنى السكون.
And this argument was formulated in another way: that motion only proceeds from an existing cause called (in consideration of its being moving the moving thing from one limit and bringing it near to another limit) an inclination (ميل), and it is the cause for arrival at the limit; even if it is not called an inclination due to continuity, it exists in the instant of arrival, for inclination is not among things that exist only in time like motion. Then non-arrival—I mean separation from that limit—does not occur except after the occurrence of a second inclination in a second instant, due to the impossibility of the combination of inclination toward something and inclination away from it in one instant, and due to the impossibility of the succession of two instants. So between them there will be a time in which the body is without inclination, and thus without motion—and that is the meaning of rest.
Source pages p. 450

Commentary
This passage presents an alternative formulation of the argument for rest. It begins by noting that motion only arises from a cause called 'inclination' (ميل) which propels the moving thing from one limit to another. This inclination is present at the instant of arrival. However, separation from that limit (non-arrival) requires a second inclination in a second instant, because it is impossible to have both an inclination toward something and away from it at the same instant. Since two instants cannot succeed each other without time (as shown earlier), there must be a period of time between the two instants during which the body has no inclination—hence no motion. This absence of motion is what is meant by rest (سكون). Thus, rest necessarily occurs between the two motions.
