Majority Position and the Infinite Regress Argument
Sanusi states the majority view and gives the infinite regress argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the majority position that relation has no external reality and the first argument based on infinite regress.

Core Idea
If relation were real, each inherence would require another relation, leading to an infinite chain.

The theologians argue that relation cannot exist externally because it would cause an infinite regress. In this lesson, we examine this first proof as well as the initial statement of the majority view and a possible rejoinder.

Guided Path
1
Read the initial statement
Read the first part of the passage where the majority position is stated and the three objections are summarized.
Notice that the objections are immediately followed by a rejoinder.
2
Understand the rejoinder
The text provides a possible answer: negating all particular relations does not negate the universal relation. Reflect on this response.
3
Identify the first formal proof
The first proof is introduced by 'الأول'. Read it carefully and see how it leads to infinite regress.
The key is that inherence itself is a relation.

Commentary
This passage presents the majority view (al-jumhūr) of the theologians (al-mutakallimūn) and some philosophers (al-ḥukamāʾ) that relation (al-iḍāfah) has no reality in the external world. It begins by stating that if relation were real, it would entail three problems: infinite regress (al-tasalsul) because inherence in a substrate is itself a relation; circularity (al-dawr) because being qualified by existence is a relation dependent on absolute relation; and infinite attributes (ʿadam tanāhī al-awṣāf) for numbers. A possible rejoinder is noted: the impossibility of every particular relation does not negate the universal, and common sense confirms relations like above/below or fatherhood/sonship. However, the author explains that despite this appearance, the majority holds the opposite, providing three formal arguments. The first argument: if relation existed externally, it would require a substrate; its inherence in that substrate would be another relation between it and the substrate, leading to infinite regress. The second argument: if relation existed, it would be qualified by existence; that qualification is a specific relation whose existence depends on the existence of absolute relation, resulting in circularity (the specific relation presupposes the absolute, which itself presupposes the specific). The third argument (incomplete): for every number, there would be infinite attributes due to its relations to all other numbers. The passage ends mid-sentence, but the intended argument is that each number would have infinitely many relational attributes, which is impossible.

The Circularity Argument Against Relation
Sanusi presents the second proof: circularity from the relation of existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second argument that if relation existed externally, it would entail circularity because its existence depends on absolute relation.

Core Idea
If relation exists, its own qualification by existence is a relation that presupposes the very thing it depends on.

The second proof moves from the nature of existence: a relation, if it exists, must be qualified by existence. But that qualification is itself a relation that depends on the existence of relation in general, creating a circular dependency. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the second proof
Read the proof carefully. It involves the relation being 'qualified by existence' and the concept of absolute relation.
The proof is dense; pay attention to the terms 'اتصاف بالوجود' and 'مطلق الإضافة'.
2
Identify the circularity
The proof says that the relation's existence depends on absolute relation, but absolute relation itself is the same as the relation in question. Trace the dependency.
3
Consider the alternative formulation
The text also mentions an alternative formulation that is unnecessary, but it illustrates the same point.

The Infinite Attributes Argument
Sanusi begins the third proof concerning infinite attributes for numbers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third argument that if relation were real, every number would have infinite attributes, which is impossible.

Core Idea
If relation were real, each number would have infinitely many relations to all other numbers, leading to infinite attributes.

The third proof is based on numbers and their relations. If relation were real, each number would have a relation to every other number, resulting in an infinite number of attributes. This seems impossible. However, the passage is incomplete. In this lesson, we examine what is available and understand the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the third proof fragment
Read the available text. It argues that for every number, there would be endless attributes based on its relations to others.
The text breaks off; we will use context from the earlier summary.
2
Understand the argument
Think about natural numbers: each number relates to infinitely many other numbers (greater, less, etc.), so if relations are real, each number would have infinitely many real attributes.
3
Consider the implication
The actual infinite in attributes is considered impossible in the kalam framework, so this is a reductio ad absurdum.

Three Arguments Against External Relations
Three enumerated arguments against external relations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three arguments used by theologians to deny the external reality of relations (al-idafah): the infinite regress argument, the circularity argument, and the argument from infinite attributes.

Core Idea
Three arguments claim relations cannot exist externally, leading to infinite regress, circularity, or absurdity.

Islamic theologians and some philosophers argued that relations have no external existence. They presented three powerful objections. This lesson presents those three arguments exactly as they appear in the text, so you can see the logical structure behind each one.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the three arguments
Read the Arabic passage once. Notice that after an introductory sentence, the three arguments are explicitly numbered: الأول, الثاني, الثالث. Each argument begins with the conditional 'if it existed...' and concludes with an impossibility.
The arguments are presented as objections to the reality of relations.
2
Understand the first argument: infinite regress
The first argument says: if a relation existed externally, it would need a subject; its inherence in that subject would be another relation, leading to an infinite regress of relations.
This is a classic infinite regress (tasalsul) argument.
3
Understand the second argument: circularity
The second argument says: if a relation is qualified by existence, that qualification is itself a relation; its existence would depend on the existence of 'absolute relation', which is prior to it, causing circularity (dawr).
The argument uses the idea that existing is itself a relation.
4
Understand the third argument: infinite attributes
The third argument says: if relations were real, every number would have an infinite number of attributes due to its relations to other numbers, which is impossible.
For example, 2 is half of 4, a third of 6, etc., leading to infinite properties.

Refutation of the Arguments: The Possibility of Some Relations
Refutation of the three arguments using logical and metaphysical distinctions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the three arguments against external relations by distinguishing between the impossibility of all relations existing and the possibility of some relations existing.

Core Idea
The refutation shows that the arguments only disprove the existence of every relation, not the possibility of some.

The three arguments against external relations seem strong, but the author provides a concise refutation. The key insight is that the impossibility of a universal affirmative (all relations exist) does not entail that no relations exist. This lesson unpacks that logical move and the subsequent clarification about the nature of relation as a generic concept.

Guided Path
1
Read the response passage
Read the Arabic text carefully. Notice that the response directly addresses the three arguments collectively. It begins with 'وقد يجاب عن الوجوه الثلاثة' meaning 'And it may be answered to the three arguments.'
The response is a single paragraph.
2
Understand the logical distinction
The response argues that the impossibilities follow only if we assume that every individual relation exists. The impossibility of all relations existing does not negate the existence of some relations. This is based on the logical principle that the negation of a universal affirmative (كل س) does not imply the universal negative (لا س), but rather the particular negative (ليس كل س).
In Arabic logic: امتناع الإيجاب الكلي يستلزم سلب الكلي? Actually it says سلب الكل (negation of the whole) not سلب الكلي (negation of the universal). But the point is clear.
3

The response also addresses a potential objection: if relation is a single nature, how can some exist and others not? The answer is that relation is a generic nature (طبيعة جنسية), meaning it has different types (أنواع). It is not impossible for some types to exist while others do not.
This preserves the possibility of certain relations existing.

Commentary
This passage defines the category of possession (lahu), also called ownership (al-milk) and possession (al-jidda). It is one of the ten Aristotelian categories. التضاد، فكما أن ينهدم مضاد لأن يبتني، وأن يتسخن لأن يتبرد، وكذلك أن يهدم مضاد لأن يبتني، وأن يسخن لأن يبرد، وعلى هذا قياس البواقي.
وقال ابن سينا: إنما أؤثر لفظ أن ينفعل وأن يفعل على الانفعال والفعل لأنهما قد يقالان للحاصل بعد انقطاع الحركة، وإنما المقولة ما كان توجها إلى غاية من وضع أو كيف أو غير ذلك مستقر من حيث هو [كلك؟]. ولفظ أن ينفعل وأن يفعل مخصوص بذلك.
Contrariety: just as that it is destroyed (yanhadim) is contrary to that it is built (yabtani), and that it becomes hot (yatasakhkhan) is contrary to that it becomes cold (yatabarrud), likewise that it destroys (yahdim) is contrary to that it builds (yabni), and that it heats (yaskhun) is contrary to that it cools (yabrud); and on this is the analogy of the rest.
Ibn Sina said: I prefer the expression 'that it is acted upon' (an yanfa'il) and 'that it acts' (an yaf'al) over 'passion' (al-infial) and 'action' (al-fi'l) because they (the latter) are sometimes said of the result after the cessation of motion, while the category (al-maqula) is that which is a direction towards a goal of position or quality or other such, being established as such [?]. And the expression 'that it is acted upon' and 'that it acts' is specifically for that.

Commentary
This passage presents the tail end of a discussion on contraries within the categories of action and passion, followed by Ibn Sina's terminological refinement. First, the author gives examples of contraries in these categories: destruction is contrary to construction, heating contrary to cooling, both in the passive sense (being destroyed vs. being built) and the active sense (destroying vs. building). This establishes that contrariety applies to both the passive and active forms. Then Ibn Sina is quoted to clarify the correct linguistic expression for these categories. He states that he prefers to use the subjunctive forms 'an yanfa'il' (that it be acted upon) and 'an yaf'al' (that it acts) over the masdar (verbal noun) forms 'al-infial' (the being acted upon) and 'al-fi'l' (the acting). The reason is that the latter (the masdars) may refer to the resulting state after the motion (of change) has ceased, whereas the category (al-maqula) itself is defined as a direction or tendency towards a goal—whether of position, quality, or something else—that is stable as such. Therefore, the forms with 'an' specifically indicate the ongoing process or orientation, making them more appropriate for naming the categories. The passage thereby emphasizes the importance of precise terminology in philosophical discussions of categories.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think Ibn Sina's preference is merely a grammatical preference without deeper significance.
Correction: His choice is based on the philosophical distinction between a process (ongoing direction) and the product (completed result).

Ibn Sina's terminological precision reminds us that category names should reflect the dynamic orientation of actions, not their static results.
