Why the Eternal Cannot Come from a Free Agent
Al-Taftazani proves that the eternal cannot originate from a free agent using the nature of intention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why, in kalām, anything that originates from a free agent must be temporally originated, and why the eternal (qadīm bi al-zamān) cannot be such an effect.

Core Idea
Intention to create requires non-existence of the created, so the effect must be originated.

In this passage, al-Taftazani presents a concise but powerful argument that cuts across philosophical and theological traditions. The argument hinges on the nature of intention (qasd) as always directed toward something that is not yet existent. From this, he deduces a fundamental principle: the act of a free agent can only produce an originated (ḥādith) being, never an eternal one.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First read the Arabic passage once, then read the English translation. Notice the key terms and the flow of the argument.
The passage is short but dense.
2
Understand the key terms
Focus on 'qadīm bi al-zamān' (eternal in time), 'mukhtār' (free chooser), and 'qasd' (intention). These are central to the argument.
These terms appear frequently in kalām discussions of causality.
3
Reconstruct the argument step by step
Identify the thesis (the eternal cannot be from a free chooser), the premise (intention accompanies non-existence), and the conclusion (the effect must be originated).
The argument is a simple syllogism.
4
Reflect on the author's remark about agreement and obstinacy
Note that al-Taftazani claims both philosophers and theologians agree on this point, and anyone who disputes is obstinate. Consider why he makes this claim.
This is a rhetorical strategy to strengthen the argument.

Commentary
This passage presents a concise argument for the impossibility of an eternal being (qadim bi al-zaman) being the act of a free agent (mukhtar). The proof is based on the nature of intention (qasd): intention to bring something into existence can only be directed toward a thing that is not yet existent; hence the effect must be preceded by non-existence, i.e., it must be originated (hadith). The author emphasizes that this point is agreed upon by both philosophers (falasifa) and theologians (mutakallimun), and any opposition is mere obstinacy (mukabara). The passage thus establishes a fundamental principle in kalam: every effect of a voluntary agent is temporally originated.

Al-Amidi's Objection and Its Refutation
Al-Taftazani presents al-Amidi's objection about eternal act and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Amidi's objection that an eternal act from God is possible on analogy with the hand-ring example, and why the author refutes it by denying the causal dependence in the example.

Core Idea
Not every simultaneous cause-effect pair proves an eternal act: the example assumes free choice but fails to show dependence.

In this passage, al-Taftazani reports an objection by al-Amidi from al-Mawaqif. Al-Amidi argues that God's act of creation could be eternal, like the simultaneous movement of a hand and a ring. However, al-Taftazani points out that this analogy rests on a mistaken assumption about causality, and the refutation simply denies that the ring's movement depends on the hand.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation to get the overall argument.
Notice that the passage begins with what is transmitted from al-Amidi.
2
Identify al-Amidi's objection
Al-Amidi claims that priority by intention (qasdan) is like priority by necessitation (ijaban) in that both can be by essence not by time, and their effect can be eternal.
He is arguing for the possibility of an eternal world.
3
Understand the hand-ring analogy
Al-Amidi gives the example of a hand moving a ring: the ring moves simultaneously with the hand, with no temporal priority, only priority by essence.
This illustrates how an eternal act could be simultaneous with its effect.
4
Spot the author's criticism
Al-Taftazani notes that al-Amidi's example assumes the agent is a free chooser (mukhtar), not a necessitator (mujib). Al-Amidi seems unaware of this assumption.
The example only works if the hand chooses to move the ring, but that is not proven.
5
Grasp the refutation
The refutation is simple: we do not accept that the ring's movement depends on the hand. Both may be effects of an external cause, breaking the analogy.
Thus the example cannot support an eternal act from a free chooser.

Commentary
This passage reports an objection from al-Amidi found in al-Mawaqif. He illustrated this with the example of a hand and a ring: the ring moves simultaneously with the hand, with no temporal priority, only priority by essence. The author (al-Taftazani) points out that al-Amidi's example presupposes that the Necessary is a free chooser (mukhtar) not a necessitator (mujib) – a point al-Amidi overlooks. The refutation given is simply to reject the basis of the analogy: we do not accept that the ring's movement depends on the hand's movement; rather both are effects of an external cause. Thus the example does not support the possibility of an eternal act from a free chooser.

The Eternal Cannot Cease to Exist
Sanusi proves that the eternal cannot undergo non-existence by a single logical argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why whatever is eternal—whether by itself or through another—cannot possibly become non‑existent, and how this proof underpins the divine attribute of necessity.

Core Idea
Eternal existence is impossible to undo—whether self‑necessary or dependent.

This short passage packs a powerful logical argument. Before we can discuss God's eternity, we need to understand a basic rule: anything that is eternal (qadīm) cannot become non‑existent. Sanusi gives the proof in one sentence, distinguishing between what is necessary by itself and what is eternally dependent on the Necessary. Let's unpack this argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic sentence once, then its translation. Notice the concise structure: a claim followed by 'because' and a disjunction.
The Arabic uses 'إما ... أو' to present two mutually exclusive cases.
2
Identify the two cases
The proof divides the eternal into two types: self‑necessary and dependent. Understand each case separately.
Self‑necessary means existence is essential; dependent means existence is received from another.
3
Connect to the conclusion
See how both cases lead to the impossibility of non‑existence. Ask yourself why this matters for theology.
If the eternal were to cease, its cause would have to change—impossible for the Necessary.

He said: So the eternal (al-qadim) is impossible to be nonexistent, because it is either necessary (wajib) by essence or dependent on Him in its existence eternally.

Commentary
This passage is a brief proof. It states that the eternal (al-qadim) cannot undergo non-existence (adam). The reason is that the eternal is either (1) necessary by itself (wajib bi al-dhat) – in which case nonexistence would contradict its necessity – or (2) dependent for its existence on the Necessary Being (al-wajib) and is eternal through that dependence. In either case, nonexistence is impossible because the Necessary Being's causality is eternal and the effect persists as long as the cause persists. Thus the eternal, whether self-necessary or dependent, is necessarily existent and cannot cease to be.

The Impossibility of the Eternal Ceasing to Exist
The matn states the argument against the non-existence of the eternal and answers an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the eternal cannot cease to exist and why this impossibility does not imply essential necessity.

Core Idea
The eternal's impossibility of non-existence can be due to a complete cause, not just self-necessity.

This lesson introduces a foundational argument in kalām: the impossibility of the eternal's non-existence. The matn presents a concise proof based on necessitation and infinite regress, and then addresses a key objection that confuses causal necessity with essential necessity. Understanding this distinction is crucial for later discussions on divine attributes and creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing, then read the literal translation. Let the author's own argument structure come through.
Notice the concise style and the logical flow.
2
Identify the logical structure
The passage has four parts: a statement, a proof, an objection, and an answer. Identify each part and understand how they connect.
The proof relies on impossibility of infinite regress and the chain of necessitation.
3
Focus on the key distinction
Pay close attention to the rebuttal: impossibility of non-existence due to a complete cause does not entail essential necessity. This is a subtle but critical point.
This distinction prevents confusion between contingent beings made necessary by God and God's own self-necessity.

قال : فالقديم يمتنع عـدمه لأنه إما واجب أو مستند إليه الإيجاب ابتداء أو انتهاء ، لامتناع التسلسل من غير توقف على شرط حادث ضرورة، فلو عدم عدم الواجب . فإن قيل : فلا يكون إلا واجباً : قلنا : امتناع العدم لتمام علة الوجود لا يستلزم الوجوب الذاتي.
He said: The eternal (al-qadīm) is impossible for its non-existence to occur because it is either necessary (wājib) or dependent upon [the Necessary] through necessitation (al-ījāb) initially or finally, due to the impossibility of infinite regress, without depending on a temporal condition necessarily. For if it were to cease, the [non-existence of] the Necessary would cease. If it is said: Then it would not be except necessary. We say: The impossibility of non-existence due to the complete cause of existence does not entail essential necessity (al-wujūb al-dhātī).
Source pages p. 11

Commentary
This passage is a proof and an objection-and-answer. The author begins with a definitive statement: the eternal cannot cease to exist. He gives the reason: the eternal is either necessary by itself or necessitated by the Necessary Being through a chain of necessitation, without any temporal condition, because otherwise an infinite regress would occur. Therefore, the non-existence of the eternal would entail the non-existence of the Necessary Being, which is impossible. An objector then argues: if the eternal cannot cease, then it must be necessary, not possible. The author replies: the impossibility of non-existence due to the complete cause does not make the thing essentially necessary. The thing could be possible in itself but prevented from non-existence by a necessary cause. This is a crucial distinction in kalām between essential necessity and conditional necessity.

Classification of the Eternal and Proof of Indestructibility
Classification of the eternal into two types and proof of impossibility of non-existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the eternal is divided into necessary-by-itself and possible-dependent, and why both are impossible of non-existence due to their relation to the Necessary Being.

Core Idea
Impossibility of non-existence stems from connection to the Necessary, not from the thing itself.

This lesson focuses on the foundational classification of the eternal. Before tackling objections, the author establishes that whatever is eternal cannot cease to exist, and he divides the eternal into two categories to prove this point. Understanding this division is crucial for the subsequent theological arguments about the nature of God and creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage closely
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the translation. Notice how the author sets up the classification and then gives the proof.
The passage begins with 'المبحث الثاني' and ends with 'وهو محال'.
2
Identify the two categories
Find the two kinds of eternal: wājib bi-dhātihi (necessary by itself) and mumkin (possible) that depends on the Necessary. Note how each is defined.
The possible eternal is further divided into direct and mediated dependency.
3
Understand the proof
The author argues that if an eternal thing could cease, then the Necessary could cease—which is absurd. Trace the logical link from 'requisites and concomitants' to impossibility.
The proof relies on the concept of 'luzūm' (inseparable accompaniment).

Source Text

المبحث الثاني لما امتنع استناد القديم إلى الفاعل بالاختيار، فما ثبت قدمه يمتنع عدمه لأنه إما واجب لذاته، وامتناع عدمه ظاهر، وإما ممكن مستند إلى الواجب بطريق الإيجاب، إما بلا واسطة كمعلولة الأول، أو بواسطة قديمة كالثاني والثالث لما سيأتي من امتناع التسلسل، وأيًا ما كان يمتنع عدمه، لأنه لما كان من مقتضيات ذات الواجب ولوازمه بوسط أو بغير وسط لزم من إمكان عدمه إمكان عدم الواجب وهو محال . فإن قيل: لم لا يجوز أن يتوقف صدوره عن الموجب على شرط حادث . قلنا : لأنه حينئذ يكون حادثاً، والكلام في القديم. فإن قيل : فالقديم إذا امتنع عدمه كان واجباً لا ممكناً . قلنا : امتناع عدم الشيء لا ينافي إمكانه الذاتي، لجواز أن لا يكون ذلك لذاته، بل لتمام علته الموجبة، فعندنا لما كان الواجب فاعلاً بالاختيار لا موجباً بالذات، لم يكن شيء من معلولاته قديماً ممتنع العدم، وإنما ذلك على رأي الفلاسفة .
The second investigation: Since it is impossible for the eternal to be attributed to an agent by choice, whatever is established as eternal is impossible for its non-existence to occur, because it is either necessary by itself—its impossibility of non-existence is evident—or possible dependent upon the Necessary by way of necessitation, either without an intermediary like the first effect, or with an eternal intermediary like the second and the third, due to what will come about the impossibility of infinite regress. In any case, its non-existence is impossible, because since it is among the requisites and concomitants of the essence of the Necessary, with or without an intermediary, then from the possibility of its non-existence follows the possibility of the non-existence of the Necessary, and that is absurd. If it is said: Why may its emanation from the necessitating [agent] not depend on a temporal condition? We say: Because then it would be temporal, and the discussion is about the eternal. If it is said: When the eternal is impossible of non-existence, it is necessary, not possible. We say: The impossibility of the non-existence of a thing does not contradict its essential possibility, because it is possible that it is not by itself but due to its complete necessitating cause. For us, since the Necessary is an agent by choice, not a necessitating agent by essence, none of His effects is eternal and impossible of non-existence; that is only according to the philosophers' view.
Source pages p. 12

Commentary
This passage is a classification and a series of argumentations. The author introduces the 'second investigation' after establishing that the eternal cannot be attributed to a voluntary agent. He then states: whatever is proven to be eternal cannot cease to exist. He divides the eternal into two types: (1) necessary by itself (wājib li-dhātihī) – its impossibility of non-existence is self-evident; (2) possible in essence but dependent on the Necessary through necessitation, either directly (the first effect) or through eternal intermediaries (the second, third, etc.) because infinite regress is impossible. In both cases, non-existence is impossible because the eternal is a requirement or concomitant of the Necessary's essence; if it could cease, the Necessary could cease, which is absurd. The first objection: why not allow a temporal condition for the emanation? Answer: that would make the effect temporal, but the discussion is about the eternal. The second objection: if non-existence is impossible, the thing must be necessary, not possible. Answer: impossibility of non-existence does not negate essential possibility; it may be due to a complete cause. The author concludes: according to us (Ash'aris), since the Necessary is a voluntary agent, none of His effects are eternal; only in the philosophers' view does such an eternal exist.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of objections to the classic kalam argument that only one effect can issue from the One (al-wahid), followed by a unified answer. The passage begins by restating the initial argument: if two things issued from the One, the relation of being a source (al-masadiriyyah) for each would be distinct concepts, either necessitating composition within the agent or a regress of sourcedoms. The Core Argument: Totality of Possible Beings Needs a Necessary Cause
First proof: from the totality of possible beings to a necessary external cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first proof against infinite regress: that an infinite chain of possible causes would make the totality itself possible and thus require an external necessary cause, which breaks the regress.

Core Idea
An infinite chain of possible things cannot be self-sufficient; it needs a necessary cause outside itself.

The first proof against infinite regress (تسلسل) begins by considering the entire chain of possible causes. If the chain had no first cause, then the sum total of all these possible beings would itself be a possible being, since each individual is possible. But every possible being needs a cause. This cause cannot be itself or part of the chain, so it must be external and necessary. In this lesson, we examine the core logic of this argument.

Guided Path
1
Understand the scenario
Imagine an infinite chain of effects, each caused by another, with no pure cause at the beginning. This is the hypothesis to be disproven.
This hypothesis is the starting point of the proof.
2
The totality is possible
Since each member of the chain is possible (could not-exist), the entire collection is also possible and therefore requires a cause.
The cause cannot be part of the collection, as that would lead to self-causation.
3
The cause must be external and necessary
Neither the totality nor any part can cause itself, so the cause must be outside the chain and necessary. This necessary cause brings some part into existence, breaking the infinite regress.
This shows that an infinite chain of possibles cannot stand without a necessary cause.

First Proof: Time as Measure of Motion
First proof for time from motion and the response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first proof for time based on varying speeds of motion and the response that time's existence is self-evident.

Core Idea
Time is the measure of motion, not a proof of existence.

This lesson introduces the first rational proof for the existence of time, which draws on the observable fact that motions differ in speed. The proof argues that such differences require a common measure, which is time. The objection that this measure may be merely mental is addressed by noting that even opponents acknowledge relations of priority and posteriority. The Imam clarifies that the proof aims to define time's essence, not to prove its existence, which is self-evident.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof for time from motion
Read the passage that presents the argument: faster motions cover the same distance in less time, implying a measure. Then notice the objection: these judgments don't require external existence.
The proof is based on observable differences in speed.
2
Identify the Imam's response
The Imam responds that the proof is not meant to establish time's existence from scratch, but to clarify its essence as the measure of motion.
This is a key distinction in kalam: existence vs. definition.
3
Reflect on the self-evident claim
The passage concludes that time's existence is self-evident (badihi). Think about why something that seems obvious still requires rational clarification.
Self-evident truths are foundational but not always fully understood.

Commentary
This passage is a proof and refutation pair. It first presents two arguments for the existence of time (al-zaman). The first proof: motions differ in speed; some cover the same distance in less time, or a longer distance in equal time. This implies a measure (time) apart from the motion. The objection: these judgments do not require time to exist externally; opponents acknowledge simultaneity and sequence. The Imam (al-Razi) responds: the proof clarifies the essence of time as a measure of motion, not its existence, which is self-evident. The second proof: the priority of father over son is necessary. This priority is not the father's existence itself (relational), nor is it the son's non-existence (same for both before and after). It must be something else with inherent priority and posteriority—this is time. Time has parts 'before' and 'after' in such a way that no after precedes a before. The objection: these possibilities of priority are mental considerations, not external realities; even non-existents have such relations (e.g., time from today to month's start is less than to year's start). The philosophers answer: the arguments are reminders; time's existence is self-evident (daruri), acknowledged by all, so denial is like denying first principles.

Second Proof: Priority and Posteriority
Second proof for time using father-son priority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second proof for time based on the necessary priority of father over son and why it leads to the concept of time.

Core Idea
The relation of priority and posteriority forces the existence of time as a container.

This lesson presents the second proof for time, which uses the universally acknowledged fact that a father is prior to his son. This priority is not identical to the father's existence nor to the son's non-existence, but points to an external relation that requires a real measure—time itself.

Guided Path
1
Read the second proof carefully
Read the passage that begins with 'The second aspect: That the father is prior to the son is necessary...'
Notice the claim of necessity: no rational person doubts it.
2
Analyze the elimination of alternatives
The argument shows that the priority is not the father's existence (because it is relational and can coexist with the son), nor is it the son's non-existence (because non-existence before and after are the same).
This elimination points to a distinct entity.
3
Conclude that time is required
The argument concludes that priority must belong to something that has priority and posteriority essentially—this is time, with its 'before' and 'after' parts.
Time is the substratum that grounds all temporal relations.

Objection and Answer: Time as Self-Evident
Objection to both proofs and rebuttal that time is self-evident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that the arguments for time rely on mental considerations and the rebuttal that time's existence is self-evident.

Core Idea
Time's existence is so basic that its denial is like denying first principles.

After presenting two proofs for time, the passage addresses a common objection: that the priority relations used in the proofs are merely mental considerations, not external realities. The response is that time's existence is self-evident to all, even the common people, and thus the arguments are reminders, not demonstrations.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection to both proofs
Read the passage that begins with 'And it was answered for the two aspects...' This objection claims that the possibilities of priority are mental considerations, as evidenced by their applicability to non-existents.
The objection targets the second proof's use of priority relations.

Understand the argument from non-existents
The objection gives an example: the time from today to the beginning of the month is less than to the beginning of the year, even though the event is non-existent. This shows that priority can apply to non-existents, so it may be merely mental.
This challenges the reality of time-based relations.

Grasp the final rebuttal: time is self-evident

Self-evident truths are the bedrock of knowledge.
