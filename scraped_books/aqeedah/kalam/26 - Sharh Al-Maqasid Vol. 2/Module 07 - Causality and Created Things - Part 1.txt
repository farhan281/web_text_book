The Eternal Cannot Originate from a Free Agent
Argument that intention entails non-existence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational basis for why an eternal effect cannot be brought about by a free agent, and why this is a foundational principle in kalam.

Core Idea
Intention requires non-existence — so a free agent’s effect must be originated.

This lesson presents the core argument: an eternal existent cannot be an act of a free agent. The author establishes this by analyzing the concept of intention (qasd) and its necessary link to non-existence. The argument is simple but decisive in kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read the thesis and its justification
Read the opening statement: 'القديم بالزمان يمتنع استناده إلى المختار' and the reason given: 'لأن القصد إلى الإيجاد مقارن للعدم ضرورة'.
Notice the term 'القديم بالزمان' means eternal in time (beginningless).
2
Understand the concept of قصد (intention)
The author explains that intention is directed to obtaining what is not already present. This implies the intended effect must be preceded by non-existence.
3
Recognize agreement among theologians
The author states this view is agreed upon by theologians, and opposition is mere obstinacy. This reinforces the certainty of the argument.

The eternal in time is impossible to be attributed to the free agent, because the intention to bring into existence is concomitant with non-existence necessarily, and the disputant is obstinate. It means that the effect of the free agent cannot be but originated, preceded by non-existence, because the intention is only directed to obtaining what is not attained, and this is agreed upon among the theologians and the dispute in it is obstinacy. Commentary
This passage is a proof and refutation. It begins by stating the thesis: the eternal in time cannot be attributed to a free agent. The reason given is that the intention (qasd) to bring into existence is necessarily associated with non-existence—one only intends to bring about what does not yet exist. The author notes that the opposing view is obstinate. He then unpacks the meaning: the effect of a free agent must be originated (hadith) and preceded by non-existence ('adam), because intention is directed only to obtaining what is not attained. This is agreed upon by theologians. The author then addresses a quote from al-Amidi transmitted in al-Mawaqif, where al-Amidi suggests that the precedence of bringing into existence by intention (qasdan) is like precedence by necessitation (ijaban) in that both can be by essence without time, implying their effect could be eternal. The author explains that al-Amidi's words are only by way of objection, and that al-Amidi's example of the hand and ring (where the ring moves simultaneously with the hand) is based on the assumption that the Necessary is free (mukhtar), not necessitating (mujib). The reply to this objection is that the dependence of the ring's movement on the hand is not conceded; rather both are effects of something external. The author then cites Sharh al-Isharat to clarify the philosophers' actual position: they do not deny that the eternal can be an act of a free agent, nor that the First Principle is powerful and free. Rather, they hold that His power and choice do not imply multiplicity in His essence, and that His agency is unlike that of animals or compelled beings. He is eternal and perfect in agency, and the world is eternal and dependent on Him. The author concludes that this is a precaution to avoid the scandal of denying power and choice to the Maker. Thus the passage defends the Ash'ari position that an eternal effect from a free agent is impossible, and refutes the attempt to reconcile the eternity of the world with divine freedom.

Refuting Objections and Philosophers' Position
Rebuttal of al-Amidi and clarification of philosophers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes al-Amidi's objection that intention can precede essence-wise, and how he clarifies the philosophers' actual view on divine agency and the eternity of the world.

Core Idea
Precedence by intention is not like precedence by necessitation; the hand-and-ring analogy fails.

After establishing the principle, the author tackles an objection from al-Amidi that uses an analogy of simultaneous movement. He then addresses the philosophers' position to prevent misunderstanding: they affirm divine free agency but deny it requires temporal creation. The lesson shows the weakness of the objection and the subtlety of the philosophical claim.

Understand the response

Why an Eternal Effect Cannot Originate from a Necessitating Agent
Proves that an eternal effect cannot arise from a necessitating agent and answers objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key arguments against the philosophical claim that an eternal effect can proceed from a necessitating agent, including the problem of preponderance without a preponderator and the refutation of 'bringing the existent into existence.'

Core Idea
An eternal agent necessitates eternal effects; non-eternal effects would require a preponderator.

This passage tackles a core dispute between Islamic theologians and philosophers: can an eternal, necessary being (Allah) produce an eternal effect by His essence? The philosophers held that the First is a necessitating agent (mujib bi al-dhat), and thus the universe is eternal. The theologians argue that this leads to absurdities. Here, the author presents the problem and responds to a key objection.

Guided Path
1
Introduce the philosophers' claim
Read the opening lines: the philosophers assert that the First is a necessitating agent, not a free agent. Note what this implies for the origin of the universe.
The philosophers' view is more famous than disputable.
2
Understand the first argument (preponderator)
The text argues: if a necessitating agent were possible, its effect must be eternal. If not, then existence after non-existence is a preponderance without cause. Use the commentary to grasp why this is impossible.
Tarjih bila murajjih is a fundamental principle.
3
Examine al-Razi's objection (creating the existent)
The author presents an argument: the agent's influence cannot be during the effect's persistence, or else that would be 'creating the already existent.' Understand why this is problematic.
This is a key objection to the Ash'ari position.
4
See the Ash'ari response
The answer clarifies that 'bringing into existence' during persistence means dependence on the agent, not acquiring a new existence. This resolves the objection.
The response saves the possibility of an agent sustaining the world.

Commentary
This passage is a proof and refutation of the possibility that an eternal effect could originate from a necessitating agent. The author begins by noting that the philosophers' position that the First is a necessitating agent (mujib bi al-dhat) rather than a free agent is more famous. He then states that if a necessitating eternal agent were possible, then an eternal effect would not be impossible; in fact, it would be necessary that its first effect and all subsequent effects (by essence or through eternal intermediaries) be eternal. Otherwise, if the effect came into existence after not existing in pre-eternity, that would be a preponderance without a preponderator (tarjih bila murajjih), which is impossible. He then presents an argument from the Imam (likely al-Razi) to show the impossibility of an eternal effect from a necessitating agent: the agent's influence on a thing cannot be during the thing's continued existence (because that would be creating the already existent), so it must be at the moment of origination or nonexistence, making the effect originated, not eternal. The answer to this argument is that what was previously stated holds: the impossibility of creating the existent (ijad al-mawjud) with an existence obtained by other than this act of creation is not necessary. The true meaning of the agent's influence on a thing during its persistence is that the thing's existence depends on the agent's existence and continues by its continuation, without any new acquisition of what was not already attained, so the charge of 'creating the existent' does not apply. Thus the passage defends the Ash'ari view that an eternal effect from a necessary agent is impossible, and it refutes the philosophical counterargument.

The Philosophers' Argument for Matter and Duration
Presentation and rebuttal of the philosophers' argument for the pre-eternity of matter and time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' claim that every temporal event requires matter and duration, and the Ash'ari rebuttal that distinguishes between essential and receptive contingency.

Core Idea
Every temporal event requires matter and time? Not if contingency is essential, not receptive.

The philosophers argue that for anything to come into existence, it must have a substrate (matter) and a duration (time). However, the Ash'ari response shows that this argument confuses two different types of contingency. This lesson unpacks the argument and its refutation, clarifying key concepts in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the philosophers' claim
Read the Arabic passage once to grasp the philosophers' claim that every temporal event is preceded by matter and duration.
Notice the footnotes (١)-(٤) indicating references in the edition.
2
Understand the argument for matter
The philosophers argue that before existence, the event is possible, and that possibility is existential, thus requiring a substrate. Identify why they think this substrate cannot be the event itself.
Contrast with the contingency of the eternal.
3
Examine the rebuttal
The author replies by distinguishing between essential contingency (al-imkān al-dhātī) and receptive contingency (al-imkān al-isti‘dādī). See how each type affects the need for a substrate.
Essential contingency is not existential; receptive contingency is not conceded for all events.

Commentary
This passage presents two opinions concerning the nature of temporal priority (al-taqaddum al-zamānī). The first opinion, attributed to some, holds that temporal priority can be of two types. The first type occurs when the prior entity exists in a time that is before the time of the posterior entity, as in the case of a father and his son. The second type occurs when the realization of the prior entity occurs before the realization of the posterior entity, but without there being a measurable time interval between them, as in the case of yesterday and today. The point is that some forms of priority do not require a stretch of time. The second opinion, attributed to others, states that genuine temporal priority is only that which exists between the parts of time itself. When we apply the term 'temporal priority' to other things, such as the priority of a father over his son, it is only by virtue of their relation to time: the father's time is prior to the son's time. Therefore, if one defines real priority as that which does not require a mediating factor, then this second category (the priority of father over son) would not be considered genuine temporal priority.

Definition of Cause and Effect as Correlative Concepts
Al-Taftazani defines cause and effect as correlative rational considerations without external existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that causality and effectuality are rational, correlative concepts with no external reality, and why they must involve two distinct things.

Core Idea
Cause and effect exist only in the mind, not in the world.

Al-Taftazani introduces the fifth methodology, focusing on causality and effectuality. He emphasizes that these are relational rational considerations, not independent realities. This lesson unpacks his definition and the logical reasoning behind it, including the necessity of involving two different things and the intermediate cause example.

Guided Path

Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the literal translation to grasp the full meaning.
2
Understand the key concept
Cause and effect are correlative considerations (i'tibaran mutadha'ifan) – they exist only in relation to each other, not as independent properties.
3
Why no external existence?

Think about why an actual causal chain cannot exist in the real world without regress.

Inductive Basis of Causal Division and Status of Conditions
Explains the inductive basis of the fourfold division and refutes including conditions in material cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the fourfold division of causes is based on induction, and why conditions are not parts of the material cause.

Core Idea
The division of causes rests on induction, not proof; conditions are external to the effect.

Sanusi completes his discussion of causality by addressing the basis for the fourfold division and a common confusion about conditions. He explains that the restriction of external causes to efficient and final is founded on induction, not demonstrative proof. He then critiques al-Ghazālī's view that conditions are parts of the material cause, arguing that conditions are external to the effect, while the material cause is internal.

Guided Path
1
Understand the inductive basis
Note that there is no logical proof confining external causes to efficient and final; the division is accepted based on induction (istiqrāʾ). This is the foundation of the fourfold causal classification.
Induction here means empirical survey, not a priori reasoning.
2
See how conditions and instruments are included
Conditions (shurūṭ) and instruments (ālāt) are included under the efficient cause because they revert to 'that by which the thing exists' (mā bihi al-shayʾ). This clarifies their place in the scheme.
3
Refute al-Ghazālī's position on conditions
Al-Ghazālī considered conditions as parts of the material cause, arguing that the recipient is only receptive with them. Sanusi rejects this because conditions are external to the effect, while the material cause is internal. Even al-Ghazālī himself admitted the material cause is internal.
This shows careful attention to consistency in causal theory.

Obligation vs Existence in Causal Analysis
Argues for obligation over existence in considering actuality/potentiality.

Core Idea
Obligation, not existence, preserves the logical precision of matter and form.

This passage delves into a technical debate about where to apply the concepts of actuality and potentiality in causal analysis. The author, following Ibn Sina, argues that considering these in 'obligation' is superior to considering them in 'existence,' as the majority do. He shows that using existence leads to contradictions in the definitions of matter and form, while obligation avoids them. Let's unpack his reasoning.

This is a subtle point that many students misunderstand.

Commentary
This passage presents an argument about whether actuality (fiʿl) and potentiality (quwwa) should be considered in terms of obligation (wujūb) or existence (wujūd). He explains that if we use existence, when form joins matter, the effect's existence becomes actual, making it fit the definition of form and not matter, causing both definitions to fail. The author replies that this does not cause a breach because the definitions as intended by the majority avoid this. He further clarifies that potentiality here is not mere possibility (imkān) that can coexist with actuality, because that would cause even more corruption.

Genus and Matter: Critiquing al-Ghazali's Identity View
Critique of al-Ghazali on genus/matter identity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Ghazali's identification of genus with matter and differentia with form is rejected by verifiers, and how this relates to the definition of species.

Core Idea
Genus and differentia belong to definition, not to the physical parts of a species.

In this passage, the author takes up a subtle point about the relationship between logical terms (genus, differentia) and metaphysical concepts (matter, form). He critiques al-Ghazali for reducing them to mere subjective distinctions, which would have implications for how we understand simple immaterial beings. Let's examine the argument.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context
The author states that genus and differentia are not parts of the species but parts of its definition. This sets the stage for the critique.
Recall the earlier distinction between species and definition.
2
Grasp al-Ghazali's view
Al-Ghazali argued that genus and matter differ only by mental consideration. Understand his reasoning: what is taken 'with the condition of being alone' is matter; 'without that condition' is genus.
3
See the condition for his view
For al-Ghazali's view to be complete, genus must be taken from matter and differentia from form absolutely. This would imply that simple external entities (like immaterial intellects) have no genera or differentiae.
4
Recognize the verifiers' rejection
The verifiers (muhaqqiqun) have explicitly stated the contrary, affirming that genera and differentiae apply even to immaterial beings.
This shows that al-Ghazali's view is not upheld.

Privative Conditions and Efficient Causation
Argument against privative conditions as efficient causes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a privative condition like the absence of an impediment cannot be considered part of the efficient cause, and why this is crucial for proving the Creator.

Core Idea
A non‑existent cannot cause an existent — privative conditions are not efficient causes.

This passage tackles a subtle issue in causal theory: the role of privative conditions (e.g., absence of impediment). Some might think such conditions are part of the efficient cause. The author argues this is impossible because it would make the effect depend on a non‑existent, which is both logically absurd and undermines the proof of God's existence.

Guided Path
1
Identify privative conditions
Privative conditions are non‑existent states like the absence of an impediment. They are necessary for an effect but not efficient causes.
Example: for fire to burn, the absence of moisture is needed. But that absence does not cause the burning.
2
Understand the argument
If a privative condition is part of the efficient cause, then the effect would depend on a non‑existent cause because the whole cause (including the privative part) ceases when the privative part is absent. This is impossible.
The author uses the principle that a non‑existent cannot affect an existent.
3
See the larger implication
Including privative conditions in efficient causation would close the door to proving the Creator, because it would allow effects to arise from non‑existent causes.
This shows the theological importance of the argument.

Critique of Matter and Form Definitions
Sanusi critiques the definitions of matter and form using the example of a composite form's non-final part.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical problem with the Aristotelian definitions of matter and form when applied to composite entities, and why potentiality cannot be interpreted as mere possibility.

Core Idea
The definitions of matter and form fail because they treat potentiality inconsistently.

Sanusi begins by examining the standard definitions of matter and form used in classical philosophy. He shows that these definitions lead to contradictions when applied to the non-final parts of a composite form. This lesson unpacks his critique and clarifies the precise meaning of 'potentiality' in this context.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage slowly. Notice the key terms: مادة, صورة, قوة, فعل. Try to see how the argument flows from the definitions to the contradiction.
The passage uses a conditional structure: 'if potentiality enters definition of form, then...'
2
Identify the definitions
Identify the definitions: form is that with which existence is actual; matter is that with which existence is potential. Note that potentiality (قوة) is the distinguishing feature.
3
Understand the contradiction
The non-final part of a composite form has necessity of the effect potentially, not actually. So it fits the definition of matter, not form. Both definitions are violated: form includes what should be matter, and matter excludes what should be matter.
This is the core of the critique.
4
Reject a misinterpretation
Sanusi rejects interpreting potentiality as mere possibility (الإمكاني) that does not contradict actuality, because that would make the problem worse.
Potentiality here is privation, not possibility.

Non-Existential Conditions and the Efficient Cause
Sanusi argues against including non-existential conditions in the efficient cause and highlights its theological consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why non-existential conditions like the absence of an obstacle cannot be part of the efficient cause, as this would imply an effect depends on non-existence and would undermine the proof of the Creator.

Core Idea
A non-existential condition cannot be part of the efficient cause because non-existence cannot cause existence.

Sanusi extends his critique to a deeper metaphysical principle: conditions for causation. Some had argued that non-existential conditions (like absence of an obstacle) are part of the efficient cause. Sanusi shows this leads to absurdity—the effect would depend on a non-existent cause—and even threatens the proof of God's existence. This lesson explains his argument and why it matters for kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage

The passage builds on the earlier discussion but shifts to a new objection.
2
Understand the background
The restriction of parts to matter and form is based on the idea that genus and differentia are not parts of the species but of its definition. Al-Ghazali's view reduces genus and matter to a conceptual distinction. Sanusi mentions that verifiers reject this.
This sets up the problem of non-existential conditions.
3
Identify the objection
Sanusi says: if a non-existential condition (like absence of obstacle) were part of the efficient cause, then the effect would depend on a non-existent cause, which is impossible because a non-existent cannot influence an existent.
4
Grasp the theological implication
Sanusi adds that accepting this would 'close the door of proving the Creator', because all effects would require a non-existent condition, making it impossible to trace effects back to a necessary existent.
This shows the argument's importance for kalam.

The Agent vs. Conditions in Causality
Sanusi distinguishes the agent's essence from conditions to preserve the proof of the Creator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the true cause of an effect is the agent's essence alone, while conditions are mere prerequisites, and why this preserves the proof of the Creator.

Core Idea
The cause is the agent's essence; conditions are prerequisites, not parts of the cause.

Sanusi now provides a clear answer to the objection that including non-existential conditions in the efficient cause leads to absurdity. He makes a crucial distinction: the effective agent is not the entire efficient cause, but only the essence of the agent. Everything else is a condition for influence, not part of the cause itself. This distinction keeps the door open for proving the Creator, because contingent existence still depends on an existent cause.

Commentary
This passage provides the answer to the objection raised earlier that including non-existential conditions in the efficient cause leads to absurdity.
