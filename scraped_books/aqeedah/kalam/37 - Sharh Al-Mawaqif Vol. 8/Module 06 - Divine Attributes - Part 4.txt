Divine Knowledge Is Not Subject to Time
The author explains that divine knowledge is timeless and thus unchanging.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that God's knowledge is not temporal and that all existents are equally present to Him.

Core Idea
God's knowledge is timeless; past, present, and future are all equal to Him.

The author begins by establishing that since God and His attributes are not temporal, time does not apply to Him. Therefore, His knowledge of all existents from pre-eternity to post-eternity is not divided into past, present, and future. This sets the foundation for understanding how divine knowledge can encompass changing particulars without changing itself.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice the emphasis on time not applying to God.
2
Identify the key claim
The author states that God's relation to all times is equal. Underline this sentence.
3
Understand the consequence
Because God is not temporal, His knowledge does not have 'was', 'is', and 'will be'; rather all things are present before Him.
4
Reflect on constancy
The author says such knowledge is constant and unchanging, like knowledge of universals. Think about why this analogy is used.

Likewise, near and far and intermediate, since He and His real attributes are not temporal, time is not described as past, future, or present relative to Him. Rather, His relation to all times is equal. So existents from eternity past to eternity future are known to Him, each in its own time. There is not in His knowledge 'was', 'is', and 'will be'; rather they are present before Him in their times. Thus He knows the particularities of particulars and their judgments, but not insofar as time enters into them according to their three descriptions, since they have no actuality relative to Him. Such knowledge is constant and continuous, not changing at all, like knowledge of universals. Some virtuous person said: 'This is the meaning of their statement: He knows particulars in a universal manner, not what some have imagined, that His knowledge encompasses the natures of particulars and their judgments without their particularities and the states pertaining to them. How so? What they have held, that knowledge of the cause necessitates knowledge of the effect, contradicts what they imagined, as has been previously indicated.'

Commentary
This passage defines the nature of divine knowledge with respect to time and particulars. The author begins by stating that since God and His attributes are not temporal, time does not have past, future, or present in relation to Him. Therefore, all existents from pre-eternity to post-eternity are known to Him equally, each at its own moment. His knowledge does not involve temporal categories like 'was', 'is', and 'will be'; rather, events are present before Him at their respective times. He knows the particularities of individual things and their judgments, but not in a way that time enters into them with its threefold division, because time has no reality relative to God. Such knowledge is constant and unchanging, similar to knowledge of universals. Then the author quotes a scholar ('some virtuous person') who explains that this is the meaning of the statement 'He knows particulars in a universal manner' – not that He only knows their universal natures without their particularities, but that His knowledge is not subject to temporal change. This is contrasted with a mistaken view that would imply otherwise. The passage thus establishes that divine knowledge of changing particulars is itself unchanging because it is not conditioned by time.

God knows every particular thing, but not in a temporal way.

Some mistakenly think this means God only knows universal natures, not individual details. The author clarifies that it means His knowledge is not subject to temporal change, and it still encompasses all particularities.

God's Concomitance with Time
Commentator critiques and refines the argument about God and time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the commentator argues that God's concomitance with time allows time to be described relative to Him, and his proposed alternative explanation for divine knowledge of temporal events.

Core Idea
God's relation to time is solved by knowledge of time as container, not by denying temporality.

The commentator addresses a common argument that God and His attributes are not temporal, so time cannot be described relative to Him. He critiques this reasoning and offers a better way: God knows each moment as the container for events, not as past or future. This lesson walks through his refutation and solution.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Note the commentator's opening critique of the claim that God and His attributes are not temporal.
2
Understand the problem
The commentator says that while God is not in time, He is concomitant with time. This concomitance is sufficient for time to be described as past, present, or future relative to God. Identify this key point.
The 'three descriptions' likely refer to past, present, future.
3
Compare with space
Notice how the commentator contrasts time with space. Space has all parts present to God, but time's parts are sequential. This makes the simple non-temporality argument problematic.
4
Learn the alternative
The commentator proposes that instead of relying on God's non-temporality, we say that God knows a specific part of time as the container for an event, not in terms of past/future. This knowledge does not change with time.
This is the key takeaway.

قوله : ( كذلك لما لم يكن هو وصفاته الحقيقية زمانية فيه بحث لأن الله سبحانه ليس بواقع في الزمان وكذا صفاته لكن لا شك أنه مقارن له كما مر وهذه المقارنة تكفي في اتصاف الزمان مقيسا إليه تعالى بالأوصاف الثلاثة، وبالجملة المكان مجتمع الأجزاء حاضر عنده تعالى ولا يتصور فيه القرب والبعد بالنسبة إليه سبحانه لأنه ليس بمكاني وهذا ظاهر، وأما الزمان فأجزاؤه ممتنعة الاجتماع في الوجود وكذا الزمانيات قد لا تكون مجتمعة فيه. فالقول بأن نسبته تعالى إلى جميع الأزمنة سواء مع مقارنته لليوم مثلا بناء على أنه ليس بواقع في الزمان لا يخلو عن إشكال، فالأولى في بيان عدم لزوم التغير أصلا أن يترك القياس على المكان ولا يبنى الكلام على أنه تعالى ليس زمانياً، بل يقال : إن الله تعالى يعلم أن الجزء المعين من الزمان لا باعتبار المضي والاستقبال والحضور بل بحسب ذاته تعالى ظرف للحادث الفلاني، والعلم بهذا الوجه لا يتغير بتغير الأزمنة سواء كان العلم زمانياً أولا وهذا ظاهر عند التأمل.
قوله : ( قال بعض الفضلاء إلخ) قال الأستاذ المحقق ما ذهبوا إليه من أن العلم بالجزئيات المتشكلة يحتاج إلى الآلات الجسمانية ينافي ما حمل عليه هذا الفاضل مذهبهم في هذه المسألة فمنافاة مذهبهم على أي محمل حمل لأصل من أصولهم المقررة عندهم لازمة لله لا مجال لتخليصهم عنها .
His statement: 'Likewise, since He and His real attributes are not temporal' is debatable, because Allah is not located in time, and likewise His attributes, but there is no doubt that He is concomitant with it as has passed. This concomitance suffices for time to be described with the three descriptions relative to Him. In summary, space, with its parts gathered, is present before Him and nearness and distance relative to Him are inconceivable because He is not spatial, and this is apparent. As for time, its parts are impossible to gather in existence, and likewise temporal things may not be gathered in it. So the statement that His relation to all times is equal, despite His concomitance with today, for example, based on His not being in time, is not free from difficulty. The better way to explain that change does not occur at all is to abandon the analogy with space and not base the discussion on the fact that He is not temporal. Rather, it should be said: Allah knows that the specific part of time, not in terms of past, future, and present, but in terms of His essence, is the container for such-and-such an event. Knowledge in this manner does not change with the change of times, whether the knowledge is temporal or not. This is apparent upon reflection.
His statement: 'Some virtuous person said, etc.' The verifying teacher said: 'What they have held, that knowledge of changing particulars requires bodily instruments, contradicts what this virtuous person has interpreted their school to hold in this issue. So the contradiction of their school, on any interpretation, to one of their established principles is necessary for them, and there is no way to extricate them from it.'

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the previous main text. The commentator first takes issue with the claim that 'since He and His attributes are not temporal, time is not described as past, future, or present relative to Him.' He argues that while Allah is not in time, He is concomitant with time, and this concomitance suffices for time to have those descriptions relative to Him. He then draws an analogy with space: God is not spatial, so nearness and distance don't apply. But time is different because its parts cannot all be present together. Therefore, saying that God's relation to all times is equal, despite being concomitant with a particular day, is problematic. The commentator suggests a better approach: instead of relying on God's not being temporal, one should say that God knows that a specific part of time is the container for an event, and this knowledge does not change with time regardless of whether the knowledge itself is temporal. Then the commentator addresses a quote from 'some virtuous person', and he reports the 'verifying teacher's' critique: that the claim that knowledge of changing particulars requires bodily instruments contradicts the school's own principles, and there is no way out for them.

Timeless Knowledge: Refuting Abu al-Husayn
Abu al-Husayn's objection and its refutation based on timelessness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Abu al-Husayn's objection that knowledge of future and past must be distinct does not apply to God's timeless knowledge.

Core Idea
God's knowledge is timeless; past and future are irrelevant.

This lesson presents Abu al-Husayn al-Basri's first objection against the claim that God's knowledge of a thing's existence is identical to His knowledge that it will exist. He argues that because the realities of 'it will occur' and 'it occurred' are different, the knowledge must differ. The response shows that this reasoning only works for temporal beings, not for a timeless God.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the flow of the argument come through.
Notice the structure: objection followed by response.
2
Understand Abu al-Husayn's argument

Commentary
This passage is a detailed definition and dispute over the meaning of God's life. The Proof That Life Is an Additional Attribute and Refutation of the Infinite Regress Objection
The commentator presents the proof that life is an additional attribute and refutes the objection based on infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the kalam argument for life as an additional divine attribute and how the objection of infinite regress is refuted by appealing to the uniqueness of God's essence.

Core Idea
Life is a real attribute that makes knowledge and power possible; without it, they'd be arbitrary.

The commentators now turn to the attribute of life (hayat). They need to show that life is not just another name for God's being knowing and powerful, but a distinct attribute that grounds those abilities. The argument involves the principle of preponderance without a preponderator, and an objection that threatens an infinite regress of attributes is answered by the uniqueness of God's essence.

An objector argues that the same logic applies to the life attribute itself, leading to an infinite regress of attributes. The answer is that this regress only occurs if essences are similar; but God's essence is unique, so it can by itself necessitate an attribute without further cause. Core Idea
Will is the specifying principle that power alone cannot explain.

This lesson presents a classic kalam proof for the necessity of a distinct attribute of will in God. The Ash‘arites, joined by many Mu‘tazilites, argue that power and knowledge are insufficient to explain why one of two equally possible opposites actually occurs at a specific time. A specifying principle (mukhaṣṣiṣ) must exist, and that is will.

Commentary
This passage is a classic kalam proof for the existence of a distinct attribute of will in God. The Asharites, along with many Basran Mu'tazilites, assert that will is a third attribute (صفة ثالثة) separate from knowledge and power. The proof proceeds by examining two opposites (ضدان), such as an action and its omission. Both are equally possible in relation to God's power: power can produce either one with equal ease. Therefore, for one opposite to occur rather than the other, and for it to occur at a specific time rather than another, there must be a specifying principle (مخصص) that determines it. It cannot be knowledge, because knowledge is a consequence of the occurrence (تبع الوقوع)—God knows something because it will occur, not vice versa. Therefore, the specifying principle must be something else, namely will. Thus, the argument establishes will as a distinct attribute necessary for the specification of one possibility over an equal possibility.

The second investigation addresses the nature of God's will. The Ash'ari position is that it is eternal. The key proof is a reductio ad absurdum: if will were created, it would depend on another will, leading to an endless sequence. This lesson unpacks that argument.
