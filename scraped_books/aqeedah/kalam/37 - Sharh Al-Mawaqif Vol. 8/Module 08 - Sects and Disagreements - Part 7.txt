Commentary
This passage classifies three early heretical sects and explains their key doctrines. The Mufawwida (Delegationists) held that God delegated the creation of the world to Muhammad, and some said to Ali. This removes absolute creative power from God. They claimed that the divine manifested in Ali and his descendants, speaking through them, which is why they call Imams gods. They even appeal to the Prophet's outward judgment vs. God's hidden knowledge as support. Each doctrine is countered by core Sunni principles of divine transcendence and immutability.

The passage shows the syncretic and hierarchical nature of the sect.

The Bayhasiyya: Faith, Children, and Intoxication
Passage defines the Bayhasiyya sect's doctrines on faith, ignorance, children's status, and agreement with Qadariyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key doctrines of the Bayhasiyya, including their definition of faith, rulings on investigation, children's status, and intoxication.

Core Idea
The Bayhasiyya defined faith as knowledge and confession, and held that ignorance of the law is disbelief.

The Bayhasiyya, a Kharijite sub-sect, held extreme views on faith and legal responsibility. They required exhaustive investigation (faḥṣ) to determine the lawful and unlawful, and considered failure to do so as disbelief. Their doctrines extended to children inheriting their parents' faith status and to intoxication rulings that distinguished between lawful and unlawful drinks. This passage outlines their distinctive positions, including their alignment with the Qadariyya on free will.
