Clarifying the Naming of Qadariyya
Explains a textual clarification about the word 'qudra' in the context of the Qadariyya naming.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the textual clarification that the term 'Qadariyya' derives from the implication of human power (qudra) negating divine power, not directly from the word 'qadar'.

Core Idea
The name 'Qadariyya' is based on the implication of human power, not the word 'qadar'.

This lesson examines a brief but important gloss from the commentary on al-Mawaqif. The commentator, Shaykh Hasan Chalabi, addresses a potential objection to the author's wording—why use 'qadar' when the discussion is about 'qudra'? The answer lies in the logical implication of attributing actions to human power. Understanding this clarifies both the precision of the text and the theological nuance in naming sects.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then follow with the literal translation. Notice the key terms: 'القدرة' (power) and 'القضاء' (decree) are not explicitly present but implied.
The passage starts with 'قوله : ( وإنكارهم القدرة فيها )' referring back to the author's earlier statement.
2
Identify the objection
The objector suggests that the appropriate term should be 'القدرة' (qudra) with a dammah on the qaf, i.e., 'power', not 'qadar'. Why would someone think that? Because the discussion is about human power over actions.
The objection is about word choice, not theology.
3
Understand the commentator's defense
The commentator explains that the author's phrase 'إنكارهم القدرة فيها' (their denial of power in them) is a refutation of that objection. He says that attributing actions to human power (qudra) implies the negation of divine power (qudra) in those actions. So the naming 'Qadariyya' is based on this implication, not directly on the word 'qadar'.
Thus the name is derived indirectly from the concept of human power.
4
Reflect on the purpose
This gloss is not a new theological argument but a technical clarification to defend the author's wording. It shows the importance of precise language in theological discourse.
The passage ends with a supplication, marking the completion of the gloss.
