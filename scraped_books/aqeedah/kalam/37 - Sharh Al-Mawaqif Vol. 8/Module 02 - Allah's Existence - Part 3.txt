Core Idea
Dream vision of God is by similitude, not essence.

This passage addresses a subtle theological question: Can one see God in a dream? The distinction between 'example' (mithal) and 'likeness' (mithl) is key. Al-Ghazali extends the same reasoning to the vision of angel Gabriel appearing in human form. As you read, notice how the commentator builds the argument and the problem he raises about Gabriel's identity in such visions.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
God is seen without impression, direction, or body — a unique Ash'ari doctrine.

Building on preceding groundwork, Sanusi now directly refutes the philosophers' reduction of vision to physical impression. He affirms that vision of Allah is a special creation, not bound by light or direction, and clarifies how this sets Ahl al-Sunna apart from both anthropomorphists and those who deny vision entirely.

The Second Observatory - The Third Section: On the Denial of the Resurrection of Bodies. In narrating the doctrine of the philosophers who deny the resurrection of bodies regarding the spiritual resurrection, which in their view is the separation of the soul from its body and its connection to the intellectual world, which is the world of immaterial things, and its happiness and misery there through its intellectual virtues and vices. (They said: The rational soul does not accept annihilation, i.e., non-existence after its existence, and that is because it is simple, as previously discussed in the topics on the soul, and it is actually existing. So if it accepted annihilation, then the simple entity, which is the soul while it exists, would have act relative to its existence and potency, i.e., a receptive capacity relative to its annihilation and corruption, and that is impossible because the occurrence of two contradictory things can only be in two different loci, which contradicts simplicity.) Its summary is that an actually existing thing cannot itself be qualified by the potentiality of its annihilation and corruption, because the subject must remain with the occurrence of the object, and there is no survival for that existing thing with annihilation and corruption. So between an actually existing thing and the potentiality of its annihilation there is incompatibility; thus they do not combine in a simple entity. So if they do combine as you mentioned, and that does not entail composition, we say: because the attribute of the potentiality of its existence is the bodily matter present at its origination, so there is no need to prove matter for the substance of the soul, unlike what we are discussing. And since the rational soul does not accept annihilation, it remains after separation. His statement: (Because the subject must remain with the occurrence of the object) to be qualified by it, it is objected that we do not affirm the necessity. For the meaning of a thing’s acceptance of annihilation and corruption is not that that thing remains realized and annihilation and corruption occur in it, analogous to a body’s acceptance of accidents inhering in it. Rather, its meaning is that that thing becomes non-existent externally by the occurrence of corruption and annihilation. Yes, conjunction in the mind is permissible, in the sense that the thing may be in the mind and external non-existence is imagined as inhering in it, but conjoining the two opposites does not follow from that. His statement: (unlike what we are discussing) is an investigation of the difference, as refined by some intelligent persons. Indeed, a thing’s being a locus for the potentiality of the existence of what is of a different constitution, or for the potentiality of its non-existence, is unintelligible. Rather, a thing is only a locus for the potentiality of the existence of what is essentially related to it, i.e., it is ready for existence for it, and a locus for the potentiality of its annihilation, i.e., ready for its non-existence from it. So the rational soul, although it is immaterial in its essence, is related to the body by the relation of management and disposal to acquire its perfections through it. Thus the body becomes a locus for the potentiality of its relation to it and its disposal in it. And since its relation to it depends on its existence in itself, this potentiality is attributed primarily and essentially to its relation, and secondarily and accidentally to its existence in itself. So this potentiality suffices for the overflow of existence upon it related to it, and there is no need for a potentiality attributed primarily and essentially to its existence in itself, because that would be impossible for it to inhere in the body, since it is heterogeneous to it in terms of its existence in itself, and a thing is not ready for what is heterogeneous to it. And just as it is permissible for the body to be a locus for the potentiality of its relation to it, so too it is permissible for it to be a locus for the potentiality of the cessation of its relation to it when it departs from the proper temperament to be a locus for its management and disposal. But since the cessation of its management does not depend on its non-existence in itself, this potentiality is not attributed to its non-existence in itself, either essentially or accidentally. Thus the difference appears between the potentiality of its origination and the potentiality of its non-existence, and that the first is permissible to inhere in the body but not the second. And the objection based on [؟] forms is repelled because the non-inherence of forms in matter entails their non-existence in their view. And you are aware that this is based on the simplicity and immateriality of the soul, and it has been realized now, and this summary and detailed answer are mentioned in the longer works.

Commentary
This passage presents the philosophers' argument for the immortality of the rational soul and then responds to a potential objection. The author first states the philosophers' claim that the rational soul cannot perish because it is simple. The soul is simple and actually existing, so if it were subject to annihilation, it would have both actuality (existence) and potentiality (for non-existence) — two opposite states that cannot coexist in a simple entity. The summary clarifies that an actually existing thing cannot itself have the potential for its own annihilation because the subject must remain when the attribute occurs, but with annihilation nothing remains. The author then anticipates an objection: if someone says that the subject must remain for the attribute to occur, the objector denies the necessity. The meaning of 'accepting annihilation' is not that the thing remains and perishes, but that it becomes non-existent. The conjunction of opposites is only in the mind, not externally. The author then provides a detailed answer distinguishing between the potential for existence and the potential for annihilation. The soul's potential for existence is grounded in the body, which serves as a locus for the soul's capacity to receive existence from God. However, the potential for annihilation does not require a locus because the soul's annihilation is not the same as the cessation of its relation to the body. The body can be a locus for the cessation of the soul's relation, but not for the soul's non-existence. The difference is that the potential for origination can inhere in the body, but the potential for annihilation cannot. This refutes the objection.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think that if the soul is simple, it cannot have any potentiality at all, not even in relation to the body.
Correction: The passage shows that the soul's potentiality for existence is not in itself but in the body, which is a locus for its relation, preserving its simplicity.
