God is Free from Sensory Accidents and Psychological Qualities
Al-Iji states the consensus that God is not described with sensory accidents or psychological qualities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that God, being essentially necessary, cannot be described with sensory accidents or psychological qualities because these depend on temperament and composition, which imply composition incompatible with necessary existence.

Core Idea
God's necessary existence precludes all sensory and psychological attributes.

This passage from al-Iji lays a foundational principle of Islamic theology: God is absolutely transcendent above any sensory or psychological trait. The author argues that these qualities are all dependent on physical temperament and composition, which contradict God's essential necessity. Let us examine the reasoning and the examples given.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the list
Read the Arabic and translation, noting the specific sensory accidents and psychological qualities mentioned. They include taste, color, smell, pain, sensory pleasure, rancor, sadness, fear, and the like.
2
Understand the argument's structure
The passage states that all these traits are dependent on temperament (mizāj), which requires composition (tarkīb). Composition is incompatible with God's essential necessity (al-wujūb al-dhātī). Trace this logical chain.
Temperament here refers to the mixture of humors in a physical body.
3
Reflect on the implications
Consider why composition is impossible for God. If God were composed of parts, He would depend on those parts, contradicting His self-sufficiency and necessity. This argument rules out any attribute that implies physicality.

He said: 'And it is permissible in the third category absolutely' — he intended by 'absolutely' the lack of division with respect to the attribute itself and its relation, except that change is permissible in all of its individuals; how? And we have indicated its refutation where we showed that the mentioned proofs are not violated by temporal things. Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof followed by marginal commentary. It begins by asserting unanimous agreement that God is not described by sensory accidents (taste, color, smell, pain) or sensory pleasure, nor by psychological qualities like rancor, sadness, fear — all dependent on temperament and composition, incompatible with God's necessary existence. Intellectual pleasure is mentioned as disputed. Then it discusses the Jubba'iyya's view on the willer, concluding the correct answer is the second. The marginal comments clarify: the philosophers do not affirm all relations; the Ash'ariyya allow change in the third category absolutely; the objection that proofs show absolute immutability is refuted; and the claim that sensory qualities are dependent on temperament is a philosophical view, not universally agreed by mutakallimun who allow them in the atom.

Commentary
This passage presents the seventh objective of the observation: that God is not described with any accident. It begins by stating that all rational people agree that God is not described with any sensible accidents (external or internal) such as taste, color, smell, pain, sensory pleasure, and all psychical qualities like spite, sadness, fear, etc., because these are dependent on temperament, which entails composition and contradicts essential necessity. Then it turns to intellectual pleasure: the theologians negated it, but the philosophers affirmed it. The philosophers' argument is given: pleasure is the perception of the agreeable; perceiving one's own perfection yields pleasure; God's perfection is supreme and His perception is strongest, so He must have the strongest pleasure—thus the 'First Principle' is the greatest rejoicing in His essence. The refutation comes in three parts, though the presentation suggests two main questions. First, the definition is denied: pleasure is not identical with perception but rather the delight that results from it. Second, even if perception causes pleasure, the cause alone does not produce the effect without a receptive subject; God's essence may not be receptive to such pleasure. Third, even if receptivity is granted, our perception is not similar to God's perception, so the analogy fails. A further third question is raised: if God felt pleasure from perceiving the agreeable, He would feel pain from perceiving the disagreeable (e.g., false doctrines like the trinity), but that is false by consensus, so the initial claim is invalidated.

Refuting Intellectual Pleasure in God
Refutation of intellectual pleasure in God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three-part refutation of the philosophers' claim that God experiences intellectual pleasure, covering the nature of pleasure, the need for a receptive subject, and the failure of analogy.

Core Idea
Pleasure is not mere perception, and even if it were, God may not be receptive to it.

The philosophers argued that since God perceives His own perfection, He must experience the strongest pleasure. This lesson presents the theological refutation, which denies that pleasure is identical to perception, questions the receptivity of God's essence, and highlights the disanalogy between human and divine perception.

Guided Path
1
Read the philosophers' argument
Read the passage from 'As for intellectual pleasure...' to the end. Identify the philosophers' claim: pleasure is the perception of the agreeable, so God must have the greatest pleasure.
2
Analyze the first refutation
The first response denies that pleasure is identical with perception. Instead, pleasure is the delight that results from perception. This is supported by direct experience (wijdān).
This refutes the definition.
3
Examine the second refutation
Even if perception is a cause of pleasure, the cause alone does not produce the effect without a receptive subject. God's essence may not be capable of receiving pleasure.
This addresses the need for a subject (qābil).
4
Consider the third refutation
If God felt pleasure from perceiving the agreeable, He would also feel pain from perceiving the disagreeable (e.g., false doctrines like the trinity). This is false by consensus, so the initial claim is invalid.
This is an additional argument based on necessary implications.

Why Combining Proofs Does Not Increase Certainty: The Fourth Objection and Answer
Refutes the idea that combining proofs strengthens knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why classical kalam argues that certain knowledge admits no degrees, and why combining rational and scriptural evidence does not strengthen it.

Core Idea
Certain knowledge is absolute—it cannot be increased by adding proofs.

The fourth objection against denying the vision of Allah argues that Moses sought to strengthen his knowledge by combining rational and scriptural evidence, just as Abraham asked to see how God revives the dead for heart’s tranquility. The answer, however, refutes this by stating that knowledge does not admit variation. This lesson unpacks the objection, the analogy with Abraham, and the refutation that certainty is uniform.

The Praise Argument Rebutted: Negation Does Not Entail Impossibility
Commentator's rebuttal to the Mu'tazilite praise argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the commentator argues that even if the verse is praise, it does not prove the impossibility of seeing God, but rather supports its possibility.

Core Idea
Praise for not being seen only makes sense if being seen is possible.

The Mu'tazilites argued that the verse is praise of God for not being seen, which would be meaningless if vision were possible. The commentator turns this argument around: praise is only meaningful when applied to one who could be seen but chooses to veil Himself. This lesson unpacks the rebuttal to the second aspect of the Mu'tazilite inference.

The commentator argues that praising God for not being seen requires that vision is possible; otherwise, it would be like praising a non-existent thing for not being seen, which is meaningless. Praise is meaningful only for an entity that could be seen but is prevented by its majesty.
This is a key theological argument from common sense.

Apply to the verse
Thus, the verse's praise (if it is praise) actually supports the Sunni claim that God is capable of being seen, but He veils Himself out of majesty.

The Consensus That Allah Does Not Do Evil and the Dialectical Exchange
The consensus on Allah's avoidance of evil and the Ash'ari-Mu'tazila difference with dialectical exchange

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the composite consensus that Allah does not do evil, the difference between Ash'ari and Mu'tazila approaches, and the dialectical response to objections.

Core Idea
Allah does no evil; the debate is over whether that is by definition or by choice.

This lesson examines the sixth objective: Allah's transcendence from evil. Scholars agree on the conclusion but differ on its rational foundation. The Ash'aris argue that evil cannot be ascribed to Allah at all, while the Mu'tazila argue that He refrains from evil. We will also see how objections regarding Allah creating evil actions are answered.

Guided Path
1
Understand the composite consensus
Read the opening statement about the ijmā' murakkab and how it establishes that Allah does no evil and omits no obligation. Note that the conclusion is unanimous.
The consensus is 'composite' because it has a shared conclusion but different underlying rationales.
2
Distinguish the two positions
Identify the Ash'ari and Mu'tazila positions. The Ash'aris say 'no evil from Him' by definition; the Mu'tazila say He avoids evil and does what is obligatory. This difference stems from the foundational principle of al-tahsin wa al-taqbih.
The contrast shows how the same conclusion can be reached via different epistemological paths.
3
Examine the first objection and response
Read the objection that Allah creates evil actions like unbelief and injustice, and the response that creating evil is not itself evil—He is the originator (mūjid) but not the doer (fā'il).
This distinction is crucial for Ash'ari theodicy.
4
Examine the second objection and response
Study the second objection: if Allah does no evil, then He also does no good because there is no command or prohibition upon Him. The response uses revelation's praise of His actions to establish goodness, or the absence of blame as a criterion.
This shows how Ash'aris rely on revealed criteria for moral evaluation of divine acts.

Futility and the Purpose of God's Actions
Defines futility and distinguishes purpose from end in God's actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that while God's actions are full of benefits, these benefits are not motivating purposes but rather ends consequent upon His actions, and why this distinction preserves divine transcendence.

Core Idea
God's actions are not motivated by purposes—they are self-sufficient and wise.

This passage addresses a common objection: if God's actions are full of benefits, does that mean He acts for purposes like humans? The answer lies in distinguishing between a purpose (which motivates) and an end (which is a result). God's actions are not futile, but their benefits are effects, not motives.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Begin by reading the Arabic passage slowly, then the English translation. Notice the careful wording used to distinguish between different types of causes.
Pay special attention to terms like 'فائدة', 'غرض', and 'غاية'.
2
Identify the definition of futility
Find how the author defines 'futility' (العبث) and contrast it with the description of God's actions as 'firm and precise' (محكمة متقنة).
This sets up the problem: how can God's actions be beneficial but not purposeful in the human sense?
3
Understand the key distinction
The author separates benefits into those that are motivating causes (أسباب باعثة) and those that are mere ends or effects (غايات ومنافع). Ask yourself: why can't benefits be motives for God?
The key is God's self-sufficiency (الاستغناء الذاتي).
4
Interpret apparent verses
The passage acknowledges that some Quranic verses seem to attribute causes to God's actions. Note how the author reinterprets these as indicating ends, not purposes.
This shields divine transcendence from anthropomorphism.

Commentary

The Third Proof: Identity Requires Differentiation and Its Refutation
Al-Iji presents the third proof requiring differentiation in non-existence and gives two answers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third proof against bodily resurrection that requires differentiation in non-existence, and the two theological responses (Mu'tazili and Ash'ari) that refute it.

Core Idea
Resurrection does not require real distinction in non-existence; distinction is imaginary.

This lesson presents the third argument against the possibility of returning the same body: that identity would require differentiation in non-existence, which is impossible. Al-Iji then gives two answers, one based on Mu'tazili principles and one on Ash'ari principles, showing how the argument fails. Understanding this debate clarifies the Ash'ari position on the nature of non-existence and possibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument
The third proof states that for the resurrected body to be identical to the original, the original must have been distinguished in non-existence. But non-existence is pure negation with no distinctions. Therefore the identity judgment is impossible.
Notice that the argument relies on the impossibility of differentiation in non-existence.
2
Examine the conditional premise
The soundness of the identity judgment requires that the non-existent be qualified with the possibility of return, which entails differentiation; otherwise the qualification would apply equally to all non-existents.
This conditional shows why the Mu'tazila might accept the antecedent but reject the impossibility.
3
Understand the Mu'tazili answer
On Mu'tazili principles (non-existent is a thing), the impossibility is not conceded because non-existents can have real attributes and distinctions.
This answer is considered apparent from their perspective.
4
Understand the Ash'ari answer
On Ash'ari principles, the conditional is denied; the judgment does not require external differentiation because the possibility of return is a mental attribute. Real distinction occurs only at the time of re-creation.
The distinction in non-existence is imaginary, like that of possibles that never existed.
