Interpreting 'What Muhammad is but a Messenger': Distinguishing Qaṣr Types
Refutes an objection by distinguishing qaṣr types in two verses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the verse about Muhammad (3:144) proves his death despite a similar verse about Jesus, through the concept of qaṣr ifrād.

Core Idea
Context determines qaṣr: one verse negates immortality, the other negates divinity.

In this passage, the author addresses a potential objection raised by the apparent similarity between two Quranic verses: one about Muhammad and one about Jesus. Both use the phrase 'not but a messenger' (illā rasūl), yet one implies mortality and the other does not. How can this be? The answer lies in understanding the rhetorical device of qaṣr (restriction) and its different types. This lesson will guide you through the author's argument, showing how the specific context and type of qaṣr resolve the apparent contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objection is that since the verse about Jesus is similar and Jesus did not die, the verse about Muhammad cannot prove his death.
2
Recognize the author's approach
Instead of dismissing the similarity, the author analyzes the type of qaṣr (restriction) used in each verse.
3
Understand qaṣr ifrād
The verse about Muhammad uses qaṣr ifrād, which restricts the subject to messengership alone, negating any additional attribute like immunity from death.
4
Contrast with Jesus' verse
The Jesus verse uses a different qaṣr, as shown by context, negating divinity, not mortality.

Commentary
This passage is an argument from interpretation (tafsīr) addressing a potential objection. Someone might claim that the verse 'وما محمد إلا رسول' (Muhammad is not but a messenger) cannot prove the Prophet's death because a similar verse appears about Jesus ('ما المسيح بن مريم إلا رسول') yet Jesus did not die. The author responds by analyzing the type of 'qawṣr' (restriction) in each verse. For Muhammad's verse, it is 'qaṣr ifrād' (restriction of individuation), which confines the subject to a single attribute and negates all others. In this case, the verse restricts Muhammad to the attribute of messengership, not immunity from death. The context: at the Battle of Uhud, Satan announced Muhammad's death, and the Companions were so shaken that they began to deny his mortality, as if he combined prophethood with exemption from death. Therefore, the two verses are not parallel in their implications: one negates immortality, the other negates divinity. So the objection is refuted.
