The Sixteenth Proof: Quranic Division of People
Quranic proof and its refutation

Mentioning two categories does not prove a third does not exist.

But this inference is not logically necessary. The Ash'ari response points out that enumerating two groups does not rule out a third, especially when the verse's context may not be exhaustive.

The Seventeenth Proof: Hadiths on Prayer and Hajj
Hadith-based proof and its refutation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Ash'ari school rejects the evidential force of certain hadiths that seem to declare grave sinners as disbelievers, based on the principle that solitary reports cannot override established consensus.

Core Idea
Solitary hadith cannot overturn the consensus of the community.

The opponent brings two prophetic hadiths: one equating deliberate abandonment of prayer with disbelief, and another that harshly condemns one who dies without Hajj. These seem to support the view that grave sinners are disbelievers. The Ash'ari response appeals to the hierarchy of evidence: these are ahad (solitary) reports, and they cannot contradict the ijma (consensus) of the companions and early scholars on the status of grave sinners as believers with shortcomings.

The reply relies on a technical distinction between types of hadith.
2
Understand the concept of ahad vs. mutawatir
Learn that ahad hadith are those not transmitted by a large number at each generation, and they yield probabilistic knowledge. Mutawatir hadith yield certainty.
Consensus (ijma) is a source of certainty in Sunni theology.
3
Apply the principle
Consider why the Ash'aris prioritize consensus over ahad reports in matters of fundamental belief.
This hierarchy protects core doctrines from being overturned by weak or misinterpreted evidence.

The Eighteenth Proof: Wilaya and 'Adawwa as Opposites
Logical proof from opposition and its refutation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the argument from friendship and enmity being opposites does not force an exclusive dichotomy between faith and disbelief, using the example of black and white.

Core Idea
Not all opposites lack an intermediate—black and white have gray.

The eighteenth proof is a logical one: Allah's friendship (wilaya) and enmity ('adawwa) are opposites, so there cannot be an intermediate state. Since faith is friendship and disbelief is enmity, grave sinners cannot be in between. The Ash'ari response counters by observing that not every pair of opposites lacks an intermediate; for instance, black and white are opposites yet there exist intermediate colors like gray. Similarly, there can be an intermediate between faith and disbelief, namely the grave sinner who is neither a complete believer nor a disbeliever.

This is a crucial distinction in kalam logic.

Commentary
This passage states the position of the majority of theologians and jurists that no one from the People of the Qibla (i.e., Muslims) is declared a disbeliever simply for opposing the truth in matters of doctrinal detail. The author begins by citing the founder of the Ash'ari school, Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari, who in his Maqalat al-Islamiyyin noted that despite disagreements and mutual declarations of misguidance, Islam unites them. The author then clarifies that the issues in dispute (e.g., attributes, creation of acts, divine will, speech, beatific vision) were not investigated by the Prophet or the early generations, implying they are not essential to Islam. A crucial distinction is made: matters that are from the necessities of Islam (ضروريات الإسلام) such as the creation of the world and bodily resurrection, if denied, do constitute disbelief. But differences in other issues do not warrant takfir. A caveat is added: some early sages held that certain bodies (like the Throne) are pre-eternic, but this does not entail denial of the Prophet, so it is not kufr. The passage concludes with the phrase 'Allah knows best his intent,' indicating a cautious approach.

Refuting the Objection of the Prophet's Silence on Doctrinal Details
The author rebuts the claim that the Prophet's silence implies tacit approval of all views.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Prophet's silence on detailed doctrinal issues does not imply that all views are acceptable, and how the foundations of Islam are clear while detailed disagreements are not grounds for takfir.

Core Idea
The Prophet's silence does not mean approval of all views—foundational beliefs are clear, details are not essential.

This passage addresses a key objection: if the Prophet did not discuss detailed theological issues, does that mean he tacitly approved of all opinions? The author argues that this assumption is unfounded, because the basic principles of Islam are self-evident and sufficient for salvation, while detailed disagreements involve complex interpretations that are not essential. Through a powerful illustration from Imam al-Razi, we see that even common people can grasp the foundational proofs for God's existence and prophethood. This lesson guides you through the objection, its refutation, and the implications for theology.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer (فإن قيل... قلنا) followed by an argument from Imam al-Razi to support the author's position. The passage then quotes Imam al-Razi, who illustrates that the proofs for the foundational principles of Islam (such as the existence of a Creator and the truthfulness of the messenger) are clear even to common people, using the example of seeing a garden with consistent conditions and a bunch of grapes with only one black grape, which compels the observer to know that the maker is a free agent. Since these foundations are clear, the Prophet did not need to investigate them. The author then adds a final remark: even for clear essentials like the temporal origination of the world, many believers may not have considered them, but if they do and deny, they become disbelievers, but we know that some of the Prophet's audience did know the details even if not all. The comment on 'أرباب الجمل' (people of summary) concludes that such people cannot refine a proof.

ولا نكفر أحداً من أهل القبلة إلا بما فيه نفي للصانع القادر العليم أو شرك أو إنكار للنبوة أو إنكار ما علم مجيئه عليه السلام به ضرورة أو إنكار المجمع عليه كاستحلال المحرمات التي أجمع على حرمتها فإن كان ذلك المجمع عليه مما علم ضرورة من الدين فذلك ظاهر داخل فيما تقدم ذكره وإلا فإن كان إجماعاً ظنياً فلا كفر بمخالفته وإن كان قطعياً ففيه خلاف وأما ما عداه فالقائل به مبتدع غير كافر وللفقهاء في معاملتهم خلاف هو خارج عن فننا هذا
We do not declare anyone of the qibla to be an unbeliever except by what involves negation of the Powerful, Knowing Maker, or polytheism, or denial of prophethood, or denial of what is known necessarily to have been brought by him (peace be upon him), or denial of consensus, such as deeming permissible the prohibited things whose prohibition is agreed upon. If that which is agreed upon is known necessarily from the religion, then that is apparent and included in what has been mentioned previously. Otherwise, if it is a speculative consensus, then there is no unbelief in opposing it; and if it is definitive, then there is disagreement. As for other than that, the one who says it is an innovator but not an unbeliever, and jurists differ in their dealing with him, which is outside our discipline.

Commentary
This passage lays out the careful Sunni doctrine on takfir (declaring a Muslim to be an unbeliever). It begins by restricting takfir to specific grave matters: denial of God's existence, shirk (polytheism), denial of prophethood, denial of something that is known necessarily (darura) to be from the religion (i.e., undeniable essentials), and denial of a matter of consensus (ijma'). An example is deeming permissible what is universally agreed to be forbidden. Then it distinguishes between types of consensus: if the consensus is on a matter known necessarily from the religion, denial constitutes unbelief; if the consensus is speculative (zanni), opposing it does not result in unbelief; if the consensus is definitive (qat'i), there is a difference of opinion among scholars on whether denial constitutes unbelief. For all other errors, the person is considered a misguided innovator (mubtadi') but not a disbeliever. Finally, the passage notes that the rules of dealing with such innovators are left to jurists and are outside the scope of this theological work.

Checkpoint
What is the ruling on opposing a speculative consensus (ijma' zanni)?

C) There is no unbelief in opposing it.
D) It depends on the judge's opinion.

Key Takeaway
Takfir is not a tool for every theological disagreement; it is strictly limited to denial of God, shirk, denial of prophethood, denial of essential religious truths, and denial of definitive consensus.
