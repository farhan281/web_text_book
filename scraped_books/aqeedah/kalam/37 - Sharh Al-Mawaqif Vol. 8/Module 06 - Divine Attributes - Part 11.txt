Defining Karama and Distinguishing It from Mu'jiza
The author defines karama and distinguishes it from mu'jiza by the absence of tahaddi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of a saintly miracle (karama) and how it differs from a prophetic miracle (mu'jiza) by the absence of tahaddi (challenge) and claim of prophethood.

Core Idea
Karamat are extraordinary acts without a claim of prophethood or challenge.

This passage from al-Mawaqif clarifies a critical distinction in Islamic theology: not every extraordinary event is a prophetic miracle. Saints can also perform wonders, but they lack the defining feature of a miracle—the challenge (tahaddi) that accompanies the claim of prophethood. The author defines karama and illustrates it with well-known stories from Islamic tradition, addressing potential objections.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
First, read the Arabic source passage once to get a feel for its rhythm. Then read the English translation to understand the meaning. Notice how the author defines karama and gives examples.
Pay attention to the phrase 'لم يكن معجزة لفقد شرطه' as it introduces the key condition.
2
Identify the definition of karama
Find the sentence where the author defines karama: 'لا معنى للكرامة إلا ظهور الخارق على يد العارف بالله تعالى، وصفاته مقروناً بعمل الطاعات غير مقرون بدعوى النبوة'. Understand its components.
The definition emphasizes knowledge of Allah, obedience, and the absence of a prophetic claim.
3
Examine the three examples
The author gives three examples: the dried date palm for Maryam, Asif bringing the throne of Bilqis, and the Companions of the Cave. Note why each is not a mu'jiza according to the author.
The common reason: lack of public challenge (tahaddi) accompanying a claim of prophethood.
4
Understand the objection and answer
Read the objection: if karamat are not distinct from mu'jizat, then mu'jizat cannot prove prophethood. The answer: they are distinguished by tahaddi in mu'jiza and its absence in karama.
This reinforces the necessity of tahaddi as a condition for mu'jiza.
5
Reflect on the significance
Consider why this distinction matters for proving prophethood. How does the presence or absence of tahaddi affect the evidential value of extraordinary events?
Tahaddi makes mu'jiza a public challenge that confirms the prophet's truthfulness.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof distinguishing the miracles of saints (karamat) from prophetic miracles (mu'jizat). The author begins by stating that a fair-minded person would not consider the dried date palm and similar events as miracles for Zachariah or portents for Jesus. He then gives three examples: the story of Asif bringing the throne of Bilqis, the story of the Companions of the Cave, and others. None of these are miracles because they lack the condition of being accompanied by a claim of prophethood and a challenge (tahaddi). An objection is raised: those who deny miracles have been answered elsewhere; those who accept miracles but deny karamat argue that without a distinction, miracles would not serve as proof of prophethood. The answer is that they are distinguished by the presence of tahaddi in miracles and its absence in karamat. The commentary elaborates: for the first point, a miracle must be public and accompanied by a challenge, occurring in the presence of the people and the messenger, which these events lacked. For example, if they were miracles for Zachariah, their occurrence would have been known, but the Quran shows Zachariah was unaware of the source of Mary's provision. For the second point, if they were portents for Jesus, Mary would have known their source. The definition of karama is the appearance of an extraordinary act at the hand of a knower of Allah, accompanied by obedience but not by a claim of prophethood. These events were mentioned to honor Mary, not to refer to Zachariah or Jesus. Regarding the throne, most say Asif brought it, but some say al-Khidr, Jibril, an angel, or Solomon himself.

Direct Proof from Quran: Resurrection Easier than Creation
Positive proof from Quranic verse that resurrection is easier than creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quranic verse 'and His is the highest attribute' and the concept of disposition support the possibility of resurrection, and why this easiness is relative to created power, not God's.

Core Idea
Resurrection is easier due to prior existence, but for God all acts are equally effortless.

This lesson presents a direct Quranic proof for the possibility of resurrection. The passage argues that restoration to life is easier than initial creation, drawing on the verse 'and His is the highest attribute.' It then clarifies that this easiness applies to created power, not to God's eternal power, which treats all acts as equal.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once for familiarity, then the translation. Note the key terms: إعادة (restoration) and ابتداء (initial creation).
The passage references the Quranic verse within parentheses.
2
Understand the proof structure
Identify the proof: Restoration is claimed to be easier because prior existence gives a disposition for quicker acceptance. The citation of the verse supports this relative ease.
Focus on the concept of 'الملكة' (disposition).
3
Clarify the scope of easiness
The author explicitly restricts the 'easiness' to created power (القدرة الحادثة). For God's eternal power, all acts are equal. This avoids misunderstanding that God has degrees of difficulty.
This guards against a common misconception.

Commentary
This passage gives a direct proof for the possibility of resurrection from the Quranic verse 'And His is the highest attribute' and the understanding that restoration is easier than initial creation. He then clarifies that the easiness mentioned is relative to created power, which can handle easier things more readily. However, with God's eternal power, all acts—whether initial creation or resurrection—are equally effortless. Thus the verse does not imply any limitation on divine power but rather uses an analogy to human experience.

The Objection of Purposeless Resurrection and Its Refutation
Al-Jurjani presents an objection and its answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophical objection that resurrection must have a known purpose to avoid absurdity, and the theological response that God's purposes are not limited to human categories.

Core Idea
Resurrection is not purposeless; God's wisdom transcends our narrow definitions of purpose.

Al-Jurjani presents a rational objection against resurrection: if it has no purpose, it is absurd; if it has a purpose, it must be either for God or for the servant. He systematically eliminates each possibility, only to answer that God's purposes are not confined to our limited categories. This passage trains the mind to distinguish between objections that seem logical but rest on hidden assumptions.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the full Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the logical structure: the objector sets up a dichotomy.
Pay attention to the words 'إما' and 'أو'.
2
Identify the three alternatives
The objector considers three possible purposes: infliction of pain (الإيلام), bestowal of pleasure (الإلذاذ), or a combination. See how each is eliminated.
The elimination of pleasure relies on the claim that pleasure is merely removal of pain.
3
Understand the answer's strategy
The answer does not accept that the purpose is limited to these three. It states that we choose that resurrection is for a purpose, and that there may be other purposes unknown to us.
The answer also references a previous reply about absurdity and rational ugliness.
4
Reflect on the implications
Consider why this objection matters for theology: it challenges the idea that God's actions must align with human conceptions of purpose.
This connects to the broader topic of divine wisdom (حكمة).

Mu'tazilite Argument for Obligatory Reward and Its Refutation
Mu'tazilite argument for obligatory reward and Ash'ari refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite argument that reward is obligatory upon God and the Ash'ari refutation based on denying the necessity of purpose.

Core Idea
God's actions are not bound by external purposes; reward is a grace, not an obligation.

The Mu'tazilites of Basra argued that because God imposes burdensome duties, He must reward the obedient, otherwise His actions would be futile or unjust. This lesson presents their reasoning and the Ash'ari response, which denies that God is obligated by any purpose. Understanding this debate clarifies the Ash'ari stance on divine freedom and the nature of reward.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazilite argument
Read the Arabic passage carefully, noting the structure of the argument. The Mu'tazilites enumerate the possible purposes behind God's commands.
Pay attention to the three possibilities they consider.
2
Identify the three possibilities
The Mu'tazilites argue that God's commands either have no purpose (ugly futility), a purpose returning to God (impossible), or a purpose for the servant (either worldly hardship or future reward). Only reward is fitting.
This leads them to conclude reward is necessary.
3
Understand the Ash'ari response
The Ash'ari answer is simple: 'Denial of the necessity of purpose' (منع وجوب الغرض). God is not obliged to have any purpose for His actions.
This refutation has been repeated multiple times in the text.
4
Reflect on the nature of obligation
The passage clarifies that the Mu'tazilite notion of 'obligation upon God' means what is appropriate for a wise agent, not a constraint on His power. This nuance shows that their argument collapses.
Consider how this differs from the common understanding of obligation.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed proof that Ali is the most knowledgeable of the Companions. The author presents multiple arguments in sequence. First, he cites Ali's unique opportunity to learn from the Prophet from childhood through adulthood, being in his lap as a child and a son-in-law later, with constant access, whereas Abu Bakr only had limited access. Second, he cites the Prophet's saying 'The most judicious among you is Ali,' noting that judgment requires all sciences, so Ali is superior in all sciences; other hadiths like 'the most learned in inheritance is Zaid' only show preference in specific areas. Third, he cites the Quranic verse 'Let an attentive ear retain it,' interpreted by most exegetes as referring to Ali, and the context of praise implies particular merit. Fourth, he gives examples of Ali's superior legal reasoning: his correction of Umar regarding the minimum pregnancy period (using the verses on weaning and gestation) and his preventing Umar from stoning a pregnant adulteress, leading Umar to admit 'Were it not for Ali, Umar would have perished.' Fifth, he relates Ali's claim that he could judge by all four scriptures, which indicates comprehensive knowledge of their contents, and responds to Abu Hashim's objection that the Torah is abrogated by clarifying that Ali's intent is knowledge, not judging by abrogated laws. Sixth, he cites Ali's own statement that he knows every revealed verse and its context. Seventh, he supports this with the fact that Ali's speech contains deep theological insights not found in other Companions. Eighth, he notes that all Islamic sects—kalam, fiqh, Sufism—trace their origins to Ali, and that Ibn Abbas, the chief exegete, was his student. Ninth, he credits Ali with being the originator of Arabic grammar, having instructed Abu al-Aswad al-Du'ali. Tenth, he mentions Ali's superiority in courage, weaponry, chivalry, and ethics. The passage concludes with two editorial notes: one explaining the linguistic meaning of 'sahl' (plain) from the verse, and another offering an alternative explanation for why all sects trace back to Ali—possibly due to his long life, implying that if Abu Bakr had lived longer, he might have had similar influence.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed proof that Ali is the most knowledgeable of the Companions. The author presents multiple arguments in sequence. First, he cites Ali's unique opportunity to learn from the Prophet from childhood through adulthood, being in his lap as a child and a son-in-law later, with constant access, whereas Abu Bakr only had limited access. Second, he cites the Prophet's saying 'The most judicious among you is Ali,' noting that judgment requires all sciences, so Ali is superior in all sciences; other hadiths like 'the most learned in inheritance is Zaid' only show preference in specific areas. Third, he cites the Quranic verse 'Let an attentive ear retain it,' interpreted by most exegetes as referring to Ali, and the context of praise implies particular merit. Fourth, he gives examples of Ali's superior legal reasoning: his correction of Umar regarding the minimum pregnancy period (using the verses on weaning and gestation) and his preventing Umar from stoning a pregnant adulteress, leading Umar to admit 'Were it not for Ali, Umar would have perished.' Fifth, he relates Ali's claim that he could judge by all four scriptures, which indicates comprehensive knowledge of their contents, and responds to Abu Hashim's objection that the Torah is abrogated by clarifying that Ali's intent is knowledge, not judging by abrogated laws. Sixth, he cites Ali's own statement that he knows every revealed verse and its context. Seventh, he supports this with the fact that Ali's speech contains deep theological insights not found in other Companions. Eighth, he notes that all Islamic sects—kalam, fiqh, Sufism—trace their origins to Ali, and that Ibn Abbas, the chief exegete, was his student. Ninth, he credits Ali with being the originator of Arabic grammar, having instructed Abu al-Aswad al-Du'ali. Tenth, he mentions Ali's superiority in courage, weaponry, chivalry, and ethics. The passage concludes with two editorial notes: one explaining the linguistic meaning of 'sahl' (plain) from the verse, and another offering an alternative explanation for why all sects trace back to Ali—possibly due to his long life, implying that if Abu Bakr had lived longer, he might have had similar influence.

Divine Will: Creating vs. Commanding
Al-Nazzam's distinction in divine will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Nazzam defined divine will differently for God's own actions and for human actions.

Core Idea
God's will for His own act is creation; for the servant's act, it is command.

Al-Nazzam made a sharp distinction in the meaning of divine will: when God wills His own action, that means He creates it; when He wills the servant's action, that means He commands it. This lesson examines his definition and its implications for divine agency.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition carefully
Look at the two cases: 'كونه تعالى مريداً لفعله أنه خالقه' and 'كونه مريداً لفعل العبد أنه آمر به'.
2
Compare with the orthodox view
In Sunni theology, God's will encompasses all events, including human actions, as creation. Al-Nazzam separates will into two distinct meanings. Contrast this.
This relates to the Qadari issue: does God will human sin?
3
Reflect on the consequence
If God's will for human action is only command, then He does not create human acts, leaving room for human autonomy.
Al-Nazzam was a Qadari (free-will advocate).

Human Nature: Soul as Subtle Body
Al-Nazzam's definition of man as soul and soul as subtle body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Nazzam's view of the human soul as a subtle body and the body as its instrument.

Core Idea
Man is the soul; the body is merely an instrument.

Al-Nazzam defined the human being as the soul, with the body serving as its instrument. He further described the soul as a subtle body, coursing through the physical body like rosewater in a rose. This lesson presents his materialistic spiritualism.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on human composition
Notice the distinction: 'الإنسان هو الروح والبدن آلة' and the description of the soul as a subtle body.
2
Understand the analogies
The soul's permeation is compared to rosewater in a rose, oil in milk, and sesame. These are physical analogies for a spirit-matter relation.
Al-Nazzam inclined to naturalists (physicists) among philosophers.
3
Contrast with the soul as immaterial
Most theologians held the soul as immaterial. Al-Nazzam's view makes it a kind of fine body. Consider implications for afterlife.
This relates to substance dualism vs. materialism.

Accidents as Bodies and Composition of Substance
Al-Nazzam's ontology of accidents and substance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Nazzam's doctrine that accidents are bodies and that a substance is composed of accidents.

Core Idea
Accidents are bodies, and substance is a collection of accidents.

Al-Nazzam held radical views on ontology: he equated accidents (like colors) with bodies, and claimed that a material substance (jawhar) is actually composed of accidents gathered together. This lesson unpacks these claims and their relation to Hisham ibn al-Hakam's views.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on accidents and substance
The text states: 'الأعراض أجسام' and 'الجوهر مؤلف من الأعراض المجتمعة'.
2
Understand the flip-flop
Note that al-Nazzam sometimes said accidents are bodies and sometimes bodies are accidents—a conflation.
This shows instability in his system.
3
Compare with standard kalam
In standard Ash'ari atomism, atoms (jawhar) are the building blocks, and accidents inhere in them. Here, substance is composed of accidents, reversing the hierarchy.

The Batiniyya on Divine Attributes
Explanation of the Batiniyya stance of negating attributes to avoid tashbih and ta'til.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Batiniyya's distinctive theological position on divine attributes and their attempt to avoid both tashbih and ta'til.

Core Idea
The Batiniyya deny all attributes to avoid extremes, but their solution is logically self-defeating.

This passage introduces the Batiniyya sect's distinctive theological stance on the divine attributes. Unlike mainstream Ash'ari or Mu'tazili positions, the Batiniyya claim that any real description of God leads to anthropomorphism or nihilism. Instead, they propose that God is the bestower of attributes, not their subject. Let us examine their argument and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice the three parts: the negative doctrine, the reasoning, and the positive alternative.
The passage uses a very compressed style; each phrase is packed with meaning.
2
Understand the reasoning behind denial
Identify why the Batiniyya reject both affirmation and negation of attributes. Affirmation implies sharing with existents (tashbih); absolute negation implies sharing with non-existents (ta'til). They want to avoid both.
3
Examine the positive claim
Notice their final statement: God is the 'bestower' of attributes and 'Lord of opposites'. Ask yourself whether this claim itself describes God, which contradicts their initial denial.
This reveals an internal inconsistency in their position.

Commentary
This passage presents the Batiniyya theological position on the divine attributes. They deny that God can be described by any attribute at all, even in a qualified sense: He is neither existent nor non-existent, neither knowing nor ignorant, etc. They argue that affirmation (ithbat) of attributes in a real sense would imply similarity (tashbih) between God and creatures, while absolute negation would treat God like non-existents (ta'til). Thus, they avoid both extremes by saying God is the 'bestower' (wahib) of these attributes and the 'Lord of opposites' (rabb al-mutaddat), meaning He is the source and grantor of attributes but not himself describable by them. This is a form of apophatic theology. The author likely records this to critique it as deviant.
