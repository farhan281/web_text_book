This lesson examines a key objection against prophetic infallibility: the verse where the Prophet is said to have frowned at a blind man. Al-Iji provides a concise answer that distinguishes between sin and a mere departure from the preferable, preserving the doctrine of 'isma.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A prohibition can confirm and continue good practice, not just forbid past actions.

This lesson addresses a subtle objection raised against the Prophet's conduct. The verse 'And do not drive away...' seems to imply that the Prophet was driving away poor believers. Al-Iji's answer clarifies that a prohibition does not always imply that the act was being done; it can serve to confirm and maintain an existing good state. This distinction is crucial for interpreting Qur'anic commands correctly.

Commands to prophets are confirmations, not accusations.

The Conditional Threat and Its Three Answers
Al-Iji presents the eleventh objection and three answers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the eleventh objection concerning the conditional verse 'If you associate partners' and the three answers given by al-Iji.

Core Idea
Conditional threats do not imply actuality; shirk can be hidden or the address indirect.

In this passage, al-Iji presents the eleventh objection that the verse 'If you associate partners' seems to threaten the Prophet with loss of reward. He then provides three carefully reasoned answers that preserve the Prophet's sinlessness. Let's read the objection and its answers.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answers
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice the structure: an objection followed by three answers.
The verse is from Surah al-Zumar (39:65).
2
Understand the first answer
The first answer explains that a conditional sentence does not require the actuality of either part. It is just a hypothetical.
Example: 'If Zayd were a stone, he would be inanimate.'
3
Understand the second answer
The second answer interprets 'shirk' as hidden shirk (al-shirk al-khafi) — paying attention to creation instead of the Creator. This is considered leaving what is better, not actual shirk.
4
Understand the third answer
The third answer says the address is meant for others by way of indirection (ta'rid), supported by Ibn Abbas's saying: 'I mean you but listen, O neighbor.'
5
Reflect on the overall lesson
Consider how these answers defend the Prophet's infallibility while respecting the apparent meaning of the verse.
They show the depth of kalam reasoning.

Commands and Conditional Threats: The Tenth and Eleventh Objections
Al-Jurjani answers the tenth and eleventh objections concerning divine commands and conditional threats.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how divine commands to the Prophet and a conditional threat about shirk are consistent with prophetic infallibility.

Core Idea
Commands and prohibitions given to prophets are means of infallibility, not proofs of sin.

The tenth objection cites direct commands like 'Fear Allah' and 'Convey', which might imply the Prophet was capable of neglecting duty. Al-Jurjani answers that these are for confirmation and continuance, and commands are causes of infallibility. The eleventh objection cites a conditional threat that if the Prophet associated partners, his deeds would be nullified. The answer explains that conditionals do not imply actuality, or that hidden polytheism (turning to others) is meant, or the address is indirect.

Commentary
This passage is a series of five objections and answers defending prophetic infallibility, followed by explanatory notes from the commentator. It opens with a general remark that some objections are weak because they rely on transitive verbal nouns to attribute sin, but 'sin' is intransitive, making such attributions strained. Then the eighth objection cites the verse 'He frowned and turned away' (Surah 'Abasa), answered as an abandonment of the better befitting the Prophet's great character. The ninth objection cites 'Do not drive away those who call upon their Lord' (Surah al-An'am), answered that the prohibition does not imply occurrence but may be for confirmation and continuance. The tenth objection cites 'O Prophet, fear Allah' and 'O Messenger, convey,' answered that commands and prohibitions are among the strongest means of infallibility, not proofs of sin. The eleventh objection cites 'If you associate others with Allah, your work would become worthless' (Surah az-Zumar), answered either as a conditional not implying actuality, or as referring to hidden polytheism (e.g., turning to others), which is abandoning the better, or as an indirect address to others based on Ibn Abbas's principle. The twelfth objection cites 'But if you are in doubt… then ask those who read the Scripture' (Surah Yunus), answered as a conditional assuming doubt hypothetically, not actually attributing doubt, with benefits of strengthening certainty or learning about other prophets. The commentator notes that the author (al-Iji) expanded this discussion to clarify that the issue of prophets forgetting or inadvertently committing major sins is not categorically denied. Then the commentator addresses a subtle point about the ninth answer, noting that while the prohibition could imply abrogation, that is a rational probability not considered in this context. He also explains the indirect address style, and critiques the two benefits of referring to the People of the Book, arguing they only apply to initial referral, not on assumption of doubt, leaving the reader to reflect.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and discussion of the reality of infallibility (cisma) according to different schools, with a refutation of an alternative view. It begins with a note that there is no decisive proof negating prophets deliberately committing minor sins, but the evidence in the disputed case (infallibility from major sins inadvertently and minor sins deliberately) is not strong. Al-Jurjani refutes the view that infallibility is a property making sin impossible

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why infallibility cannot be a property that makes sin impossible, and how this refutation clarifies the nature of moral responsibility.

If sin were impossible, there would be no merit in avoiding it.

Additionally, consensus (ijmāʿ) rejects this view. This lesson explores the refutation and its implications.

Sheikh Arabi's Dream and Prophetic Superiority
Presents a dream-based objection and a rational response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a dream report claiming angels are better does not disprove the individual superiority of prophets, through the distinction between collective and individual reward.

Core Idea
Angels' collective goodness does not negate the Prophet's individual superiority.

Sheikh al-Arabi relates a dream in which the Prophet reportedly said angels are better than prophets, which seems to challenge the doctrine of prophetic superiority. The author provides a response for those who do not accept the authority of dreams, showing that the same hadith can be interpreted as referring to collective reward, not individual merit. Let's examine this careful argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and the English translation. Notice that it contains both an objection (the dream) and a response. The response is conditional: it is for those who do not accept the dream's authority.
The passage ends with 'fata'dabbar' (so reflect), which invites deeper thought.
2
Identify the objection
Sheikh al-Arabi claims he asked the Prophet in a dream about the superiority of angels over prophets, and the Prophet said angels are better. He then asks for a proof to give if someone questions him.
3
Understand the response strategy
The author presents a response for those who do not accept dreams as authoritative. The response uses the very hadith the Prophet pointed to in the dream, but interprets the phrase 'company better than them' as referring to the company's collective reward, not each individual.
The key distinction is between collective goodness (due to number) and individual superiority (in essence).
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The passage concludes that the collective reward of many angels being greater than the reward of a few men (including the Prophet) does not mean each angel is individually better than the Prophet. The dispute is about individual superiority, which remains intact.
This argument preserves the orthodox position that prophets are superior to angels individually.
