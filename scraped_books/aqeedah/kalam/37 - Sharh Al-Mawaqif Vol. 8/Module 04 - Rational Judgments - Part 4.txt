Learning Objective

Eleventh Proof: The Sinner Is Disgraced in Hellfire
Sanusi presents the eleventh proof from Quran 3:192 and 16:27 and refutes it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the eleventh proof against sinful believers and the two Ashʿarī refutations based on the scope of the definite article and the meaning of 'disgrace'.

Core Idea
A definite noun does not automatically imply universality in Ashʿarī logic.

Sanūsī presents a proof that attempts to use two Quranic verses to argue that every sinful believer entering Hellfire must be a disbeliever. The proof relies on the assumption that 'disgrace' in the first verse is universal and the second verse states that disgrace is only for disbelievers. However, the author offers two powerful refutations that dismantle this syllogism by challenging the universality of the definite article and the intended meaning of 'disgrace'.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once and then the literal translation. Focus on grasping the overall argument structure.
2
Identify the syllogism
The proof combines Quran 3:192 and 16:27. Identify the two premises and the conclusion.
Premise 1: Whoever enters the Fire is disgraced. Premise 2: Disgrace is for disbelievers. Conclusion: The sinful believer must be a disbeliever.
3
Understand the first refutation
The first response denies that the definite noun 'al-khizy' implies universality. According to Ashʿarī uṣūl, the definite article does not automatically mean 'all'.
This refutation does not deny the first verse's truth, only its scope.

Understand the second refutation
The second response concedes generality for argument's sake but clarifies that only 'complete disgrace' applies to disbelievers. The partial disgrace of believers is not included.

Proof from Intellectual Obligation to Repel Harm and Its Refutation
Proponents argue that repelling probable harm is intellectually obligatory via analogy; author refutes by denying rational obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that repelling probable harm is intellectually obligatory and the author's refutation that reason does not impose moral obligations.

Core Idea
Reason alone cannot impose obligations; only revelation can.

In this passage, we examine a key argument for the intellectual obligation of repelling harm, used by those who claim the imamate is rationally required. The proponents employ analogies from everyday life, but the author offers a sharp refutation: moral categories like obligation are not known by reason but only through divine law. This lesson unpacks the argument and its rebuttal.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the structure: claim, proof by analogy, and refutation.
2
Identify the proponents' claim and principle
Find the principle that repelling certain harm is obligatory by reason. Understand how they extend this to probable harm.
The key term is 'دفع المضرة' (repelling harm).
3
Analyze the analogies
The proponents give two analogies: poison and falling wall. Ask yourself why these analogies are meant to show rational obligation.
Both involve a certain general principle and a probable particular case.
4
Understand the refutation
The author denies that reason judges obligation at all. Moral judgments come only from revelation (الشرع). This is a fundamental point in the debate.
The phrase 'منع حكم العقل بالوجوب' is the core of the refutation.
5
Reflect on the implications
Consider what this means for the imamate debate: if reason cannot oblige, then the obligation to appoint an imam must come from revelation or not at all.

ثم قال الموجبون : ( لنصب الإمام على الأنام عقلاً ( إن أصل دفع المضرة واجب ( بحكم العقل ( قطعاً، فكذلك المضرة المظنونة ) يجب دفعها عقلا ) وذلك ) لأن الجزئيات المظنونة المندرجة تحت أصل قطعي الحكم يجب اندراجها في ذلك الحكم قطعاً ( مثل أن يعرف الإنسان أن كل مسموم يجب اجتنابه ثم يظن أن هذا الطعام مسموم فإن العقل الصريح يقضي بوجوب اجتنابه وكذا من علم أن الحائط الساقط لا يجوز الوقوف تحته، ثم ظن أن هذا الحائط يسقط فالعقل الصريح يقضي بوجوب أن لا يقف تحته، والجواب : منع حكم العقل ) بالوجوب وأخواته بل هي لا تستفاد إلا من الشرع .
Then the obligers said: The appointment of an imam over the people is intellectually obligatory, because the principle of repelling harm is absolutely obligatory by the judgment of reason, and similarly a probable harm must be repelled intellectually, because the probable particulars subsumed under a certain principle must be subsumed under that judgment certainly—like a person knowing that every poison must be avoided, then suspecting that this food is poison: sound reason judges that he must avoid it. Likewise, one who knows that a falling wall may not be stood under, then suspects that this wall is falling: sound reason judges that he must not stand under it. The answer is the denial that reason judges obligation and its likes; rather, these are only derived from revelation.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof from those who hold that appointing an imam is intellectually obligatory (wajib 'aqlan). The proof is based on the principle of repelling harm (daf' al-madarrah): just as repelling certain harm is intellectually obligatory, so is repelling probable harm, because particulars of probable harm are subsumed under the certain general principle. Two analogies are given: knowing that every poison must be avoided, and then suspecting a particular food is poison, reason obliges avoidance; knowing that a falling wall must not be stood under, and then suspecting a particular wall is falling, reason obliges not standing there. The author's refutation is a simple denial: reason does not judge obligation or similar moral categories; these are only derived from divine law (shar'). Thus the proof fails because it assumes moral obligation is known by reason, which the author rejects.
