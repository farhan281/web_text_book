The Definition of Miracle and Its Three Aspects
Al-Jurjani defines miracle and outlines three aspects of investigation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the technical definition of a miracle in Ash'ari theology and the three areas of investigation into miracles.

Core Idea
A miracle is an act intended by God to verify a prophet's claim.

Al-Jurjani begins by defining the miracle (mu'jiza) in technical terms. He states that a miracle is an act or omission intended to demonstrate the truthfulness of a claimant to prophethood. He then explains that the study of miracles covers three aspects: its conditions, how it occurs, and how it proves the claim. This passage lays the foundation for all further discussion.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition carefully
Read the Arabic definition of a miracle and its translation. Notice that it emphasizes intention (qasd) and purpose (truthfulness of a prophet).
The definition is technical and excludes mere marvels.
2
Identify the three aspects
The passage mentions three matters for investigation: conditions, manner of occurrence, and proof. List them and consider why each is necessary.
These three structure the entire discussion of miracles.
3
Reflect on the definition's scope
Ask yourself: what events are included or excluded by this definition? See how it sets the stage for the conditions.
This definition is the basis for distinguishing miracles from sorcery.

Second Objective: On the reality of the miracle. On the reality of the miracle: it is, according to the terminology of our school, an expression for that by which is intended the manifestation of the truthfulness of one who claims that he is the Messenger of God. And the investigation into it concerns three matters: its conditions, the manner of its occurrence, and the aspect of its indication of the truthfulness of the claimant to the message. The first investigation: on its conditions, and they are seven: The first condition: that it be an act of God or what stands in its place of omissions. And the reason for stipulating that is that verification from Him – i.e., from God Most High – is not obtained by what is not from Him. And our saying 'or what stands in its place' is to include in the definition something like when he says: 'My miracle is that I place my hand on my head, and you are not able to do it' – i.e., to place your hands on your heads – so he does it. His saying: 'On the reality of the miracle' – it is no secret that the reality of i'jāz (making incapable) is the establishment of inability; it is used metaphorically for its manifestation, then it is metaphorically attributed to what is the cause of inability and made a name for it. So the tā' is for transference from adjectival to nominal, as in 'al-ḥaqīqa' (the reality), and it is said to be for emphasis, as in 'al-ʿalāma' (the sign). And Imām al-Ḥaramayn mentioned that there is another metaphor here, based on the more correct view of al-Ashʿarī, which is that inability is the opposite of power and pertains only to the existent, such that the inability of the aged to sit after standing [is an inability regarding an existent]. The aspect of the metaphor according to this is that what is meant by inability is non-existence of power, because if inability were taken as opposition in the existential sense, then involuntary opposition would exist. His saying: 'An expression for what is intended, etc.' – an objection was raised against this definition that it applies to what appears at the hand of a sorcerer claiming prophethood. And it was answered that what is meant is the intention from God Almighty. And the clearer answer is to say: the immediate understanding from the definition is the intention to manifest truthfulness in reality, so it does not apply to sorcery, as is not hidden.

This passage defines the technical term 'miracle' (muʿjiza) and begins to elaborate its conditions. It is a definition-passage. The commentary follows the order: definition, three aspects, first condition, linguistic analysis, and objection-response.

The First Condition and Related Discussions
Al-Jurjani discusses the first condition, linguistic analysis, and objection-response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first condition of a miracle, the linguistic analysis of the word 'miracle', and how the definition avoids including sorcery.

Core Idea
A miracle must be an act of God; otherwise it cannot provide divine verification.

After defining the miracle, Al-Jurjani turns to its conditions. The first condition is that the miracle must be an act of God or something equivalent, like an omission. He then explains the linguistic meaning of 'miracle' and defends the definition against the objection that it might include sorcery. This passage clarifies the essential features that distinguish a miracle from other extraordinary events.

Guided Path
1
Understand the first condition
Read the first condition: the miracle must be an act of God or something that stands in its place, like an omission. Note the reason given: only God can provide verification.
This condition ensures that miracles are divine acts.
2
Examine the example
Consider the example: a prophet says 'my miracle is placing my hand on my head, and you cannot.' Here the act (placing hand) is the miracle, and the inability of others is an omission.
The example illustrates how omissions can be included.
3
Analyze the linguistic derivation
The text discusses the word 'mu'jiza' from 'i'jāz' (making incapable). Understand the metaphorical transfer and the views of Imām al-Ḥaramayn and al-Ashʿarī.
This linguistic analysis grounds the term's meaning.
4
Learn how sorcery is excluded
The objection that the definition could include sorcery is answered: the intention must be from God, and the definition implies manifesting truth in reality, which sorcery does not do.
This clarifies that miracles are distinct from sorcery.

Objection to the Definition: Sorcerer's Claim
Al-Jurjani refutes an objection to the definition of miracle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Jurjani refutes the objection that the definition of miracle could apply to a sorcerer's acts.

Core Idea
The definition of miracle requires manifesting actual truth, not mere appearance.

In this brief but important passage, Al-Jurjani tackles a potential loophole in the definition of miracle. The objection claims that a sorcerer who claims prophethood and performs extraordinary feats could be said to satisfy the definition. Al-Jurjani presents two answers, the second of which is more precise and cuts to the heart of the matter.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the objection and the two answers. The first answer is brief, the second is presented as stronger.
The passage is a marginal gloss, introduced by 'قوله'.
2
Identify the objection
The objection states that the definition of miracle could apply to a sorcerer who claims prophethood. Why might someone think this? Because the sorcerer also intends to show he is truthful.
The objection is based on the wording 'that by which is intended the manifestation of truthfulness'.
3
Understand the first answer
The first answer clarifies that the intention must be from Allah, not from the claimant. This answer is valid but may require additional explanation.
This answer focuses on the source of the intention.
4
Understand the second, preferred answer
The second answer states that the definition's natural understanding is to manifest actual truth (in reality), not just a claim. Sorcery is false, so it cannot be a miracle. This answer is more direct and conclusive.
The phrase 'as is not hidden' indicates the clarity of this reasoning.
5
Reflect on the distinction
Consider why the second answer is considered 'more apparent' (أظهر). It avoids relying on external criteria (like divine intention) and instead uses the internal meaning of the definition itself.
This reflects a common method in Islamic theology: refining definitions to exclude counterexamples.

The Second Condition: Extraordinariness of Miracles
Al-Jurjani enumerates and defends the second condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a miracle must be extraordinary and why it does not need to be beyond the prophet's power.

Core Idea
Miracles must break natural habits to serve as divine attestation.

Al-Jurjani now explains the second essential condition for a miracle: it must be extraordinary. Without this, ordinary events like sunrise cannot distinguish a true prophet from a liar. He also addresses a proposed additional condition and shows why it is invalid.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice how Al-Jurjani structures his argument: condition, examples, extra condition, refutation.
2
Identify the second condition
Look for the phrase 'خارقا للعادة' and understand why it is necessary. Write down the reasoning: without extraordinariness, habitual events provide no proof.
Consider the examples of sun and flowers.
3
Examine the extra condition
Some scholars added that the miracle must not be within the prophet's power. Find this condition and note their rationale.
4
Understand the refutation
Al-Jurjani rejects this extra condition. Explain why: the prophet's ability combined with others' inability is itself miraculous.
This refutation sets up the next lesson's debate.

الشرط الثاني : أن يكون ( المعجز) خارقاً للعادة إذ لا إعجاز دونه فإن المعجز ينزل من الله منزلة التصديق بالقول كما سيأتي وما لا يكون خارقا للعادة بل معتاداً كطلوع الشمس في كل يوم وبدو الأزهار في كل ربيع فإنه لا يدل على الصدق لمساواة غيره إياه في ذلك حتى الكذاب في دعوى النبوة وشرط (قوم) في المعجز ( أن لا يكون مقدوراً للنبي ) إذ لو كان مقدوراً له كصعوده إلى الهواء ومشيه على الماء لم يكن نازلاً منزلة التصديق من الله تعالى (وليس بشيء لأن قدرته مع عدم قدرة غيره عادة معجز) قال الآمدي : هل يتصور كون المعجزة مقدورة للرسول أم اختلفت الأئمة فيه فذهب بعضهم إلى أن المعجز فيما ذكر من المثال ليس هو الحركة بالصعود أو المشي لكونها مقدورة له بخلق الله فيه القدرة عليها إنما المعجز هناك هو نفس القدرة عليها وهذه القدرة ليست مقدورة له وذهب آخرون إلى أن نفس هذه الحركة معجزة من جهة كونها خارقة للعادة ومخلوقة لله تعالى وإن كانت مقدورة للنبي، وهو الأصح ، وإذا عرفت هذا فلا يخفى عليك ما في عبارة الكتاب من الاختلال
The second condition: that the miracle be extraordinary, for there is no miraculousness without it. The miracle is placed by Allah in the position of verbal attestation, as will come. And what is not extraordinary but rather habitual, like the rising of the sun every day and the blooming of flowers every spring, does not indicate truthfulness because others equal him in it, even the liar in his claim to prophethood. And a group stipulated in the miracle that it not be within the power of the prophet, for if it were within his power, like his ascending into the air and walking on water, it would not be in the position of attestation from Allah Most High. But this is not valid, because his ability together with the inability of others is generally miraculous. Al-Amidi said: Is it conceivable that the miracle be within the power of the messenger? The imams differed. Learning Objective

The fourth group presented a series of hypothetical absurdities that would supposedly follow if we allowed any violation of natural custom. Their goal was to show that permitting even one miracle would lead to complete epistemological chaos. This lesson presents that objection in full.

The Reply: Miracles Are Possible and Customary
Reply that miracles are possible and become customary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the reply to the objection that miracles are impossible, including the arguments that miracles are no more wondrous than creation, that custom provides certainty while possibility remains, and that miracles become customary in themselves.

Core Idea
Miracles are possible because creation itself is more wondrous, and custom gives certainty without negating possibility.

The objectors tried to show that miracles lead to absurdity. Now comes the reply, which dismantles their reasoning. The reply first points out that miracles are not more amazing than the original creation of the heavens and earth. Then it argues that we can be certain that some events do not occur based on custom, while still acknowledging their possibility. Finally, it observes that miracles, when they occur regularly for prophets and saints, become a kind of custom in themselves, so the fair-minded person cannot deny them.

The Quran's Prophecies as a Miracle
Response to objection about Quran's prophecies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quran's reports of the unseen constitute a miracle that proves prophethood.

Core Idea
The Quran's prophecies are extraordinary and known by custom to be miraculous.

This passage addresses an objection that the Quran might still be miraculous through its prophecies even if not through eloquence. The author explains that the prophecies are indeed extraordinary and that general awareness of them is sufficient for the proof.

The answer to the first objection is to say: The limit of the miraculous from it—i.e., this report—is not unknown as you have stated; rather it is known, decreed by custom.
