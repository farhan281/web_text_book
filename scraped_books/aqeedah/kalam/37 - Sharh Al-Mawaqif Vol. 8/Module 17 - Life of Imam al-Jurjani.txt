Al-Ash'ari's Debate with al-Jubba'i
Narrates the famous anecdote leading to al-Ash'ari's first opposition to Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the famous anecdote that marks al-Ash'ari's first departure from Mu'tazilism on the issue of divine justice.

Core Idea
A single question about God's justice changed al-Ash'ari's theological allegiance.

This lesson presents a pivotal moment in Islamic intellectual history: the story of al-Ash'ari's debate with his teacher al-Jubba'i. The anecdote captures the logical tension at the heart of the Mu'tazili theory of divine justice and shows how a single objection can unravel a theological system. As you read, notice how al-Ash'ari's question turns his teacher's own principles against them.

Guided Path
1
Read the anecdote
Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Try to imagine the scenario: three brothers, three different afterlives. What is al-Ash'ari's objection?
The story is concise—every sentence matters.
2
Identify the logical structure
al-Ash'ari's objection follows a pattern: if God is just and acts for wise purposes, then the Mu'tazili explanation for each brother fails because it creates a new problem. Follow the chain: why did the first brother sin? Why was the second not saved early? Why was the third not given a chance?
The Mu'tazila held that God must do what is best for His servants.
3
Reflect on the implications
Consider why al-Jubba'i's silence led al-Ash'ari to abandon his school. What does this story reveal about the limits of rational theology when it tries to explain God's actions?
This anecdote symbolizes a broader shift from a purely rationalist to a more tradition-based approach.

Commentary
This passage narrates a well-known anecdote from early Ash'ari history. It describes a debate between the Mu'tazili scholar al-Jubba'i and his student al-Ash'ari about divine predestination and justice. al-Jubba'i's inability to answer al-Ash'ari's logical objection led al-Ash'ari to abandon Mu'tazilism for Sunni orthodoxy, marking his first significant departure from the Mu'tazila. The story illustrates the theological conflict over whether God could be accused of injustice.

Virtue 1: Knowledge (ʿIlm) of Ali (RA)
Ali's comprehensive knowledge and its evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ali's comprehensive knowledge of revelation, theology, and various sciences establishes his superiority over other Companions.

Core Idea
Ali's unparalleled knowledge made him the master of all Islamic sciences.

This passage presents the first and most comprehensive virtue of Ali: his vast knowledge. The author argues that Ali's knowledge encompasses the meanings of every Qur'anic verse, the secrets of theology, and even practical sciences like grammar and chivalry. This section sets the foundation for the claim of his superiority.

3
Reflect on the importance

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration of eight virtues of Ali ibn Abi Talib, presented as evidence for the claim that he is the best (al-afdal) after the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). The passage begins with the virtue of knowledge, citing a hadith where the Prophet states that Ali knows the context of every revealed verse. It also mentions that Ali's sermons contain profound secrets of theology, that all Islamic sects trace their origins to him in kalam and fiqh, that Ibn Abbas was his student, and that he pioneered Arabic grammar and chivalry. The second virtue is asceticism: despite worldly abundance, Ali lived austerely and famously divorced the world three times. The third virtue is generosity, illustrated by the well-known story of the vow: when his children were ill, Ali and Fatima vowed to fast, and each evening they gave their only food to a beggar, an orphan, and a captive, prompting the revelation of Qur'an 76:7-8. The fourth virtue is bravery, evidenced by his feats in battle and the Prophet's praise. The fifth is good character, including jesting. The sixth is extraordinary strength, as he pulled the gate of Khaybar by divine power. The seventh is his noble lineage and closeness to the Prophet through blood and marriage. The eighth is his distinguished family: his wife Fatima, his sons al-Hasan and al-Husayn, and his descendants, whose spiritual influence is exemplified by later saints like Abu Yazid and Ma'ruf al-Karkhi. The passage concludes that the combination of these qualities necessitates that Ali is superior to all other Companions.

The Fourth Observatory - The Fifth Objective: The Best after the Messenger of God, may God bless him and grant him peace. It is permissible to judge by it, and what we mentioned is supported by his saying: 'By God, there is no verse revealed in land or sea or plain or mountain or sky or earth or night or day except that I know about whom it was revealed and concerning what it was revealed.' And it is supported by the fact that his first speech includes supposition and estimation, and it does not necessarily imply the permissibility of judgment, as sound nature testifies. And because Ali mentioned in his sermon from the secrets of monotheism, justice, prophecy, and predestination what has not occurred in the speech of the other Companions, so that indicates that he is most knowledgeable. And because all sects attribute themselves to him in the principles of kalam and the branches of fiqh, and likewise the Sufis in the science of inner purification, for the cloak of the shaykhs ends with him. And Ibn Abbas, the chief of the commentators, was his student, and he was in fiqh and eloquence at the highest degree, and the science of grammar only appeared from him, and he was the first to speak about it, and he ordered Abu al-Aswad al-Duali to record it, as is well-known, and likewise the science of bravery and the practice of weapons, and likewise the science of chivalry and morals, for he was more knowledgeable in them than others. Second: Among those matters is asceticism: It is well-known about him, despite the opening of the doors of the world upon him, he abandoned luxury and was rough in food and clothing, and did not turn to pleasures, until he said to the world: 'I have divorced you three times.' Third: Generosity: It is well-known about him that he preferred the needy and the poor over himself and his family, and that was his habit, until he gave charity with his ring while in prayer, and revelation came down concerning him as has passed, and he also gave charity on the nights of his vowed fasts with what was his breakfast, and there was revealed about him: 'And they give food, despite their love for it, to the needy, the orphan, and the captive.' His saying: 'or plain or mountain' – the plain is opposite of mountain, and its land is plain, and the relative adjective is suhli with dammah irregularly, and the people became plain (went to the plain). And she baked. His saying: 'On the nights of his vowed fast' – It is narrated that al-Hasan and al-Husayn, may God be pleased with them, became ill, so Ali, Fatima, and their servant Fiddah vowed that if they recovered they would fast three days. They recovered, and they had nothing. So Ali, may God be pleased with him, borrowed three sa' of barley from a Jew, and Fatima, may God be pleased with her, ground one sa' and baked five loaves for their number. At the time of breaking the fast, a beggar asked: 'O household of the Messenger of God, a needy Muslim, feed me, may God feed you in Paradise from the tables of Paradise.' So they preferred him with it and spent the night without eating anything, and they fasted the next day. On the second night, she baked five loaves from the second sa', and at the time of breaking the fast an orphan asked, so they preferred him with it and did not eat anything, and they fasted the next day. On the third night, she baked five loaves from the third sa', and at the time of breaking the fast a captive asked, so they preferred him with it, and then the verse was revealed: 'They fulfill vows and fear a day whose evil is widespread, and they give food, despite their love for it, to the needy, the orphan, and the captive' [Al-Insan 7-8]. Fourth: Bravery: His confronting battles and meeting heroes and killing the greats of the Jahiliyyah is mutawatir, until he, peace be upon him, said on the day of the Confederates: 'The blow of Ali is better than the worship of the two weighty beings.' And his exploits in Khaybar and elsewhere are mutawatir. Fifth: His good character: That is well-known from him, until he was attributed with jesting, and he, peace be upon him, said: 'Good character is from faith.' Sixth: His extraordinary strength, until he pulled out the gate of Khaybar with his hand, and he said: 'I did not pull out the gate of Khaybar with physical strength but with divine strength.' Seventh: His lineage and closeness to the Messenger, in lineage and marriage, and that is not hidden. And al-Abbas, although he was the uncle of the Prophet, peace be upon him, he was the brother of Abdullah from the father, and Abu Talib was his brother from father and mother. Eighth: His being distinguished with a spouse like Fatima, the mistress of the women of the worlds, and two sons like al-Hasan and al-Husayn, who are the leaders of the youth of Paradise as stated in the hadith, and then the children of his children, whom all people agree upon their virtue over the worlds, until Abu Yazid, despite his high rank, was a water-carrier in the house of Ja'far al-Sadiq, may God be pleased with him, and Ma'ruf al-Karkhi was a doorkeeper of the house of Ali ibn Musa al-Rida. This is something whose correctness is beyond doubt, for Ma'ruf was a Christian boy and became Muslim at the hand of Ali ibn Musa and served him. As for Abu Yazid, he did not meet Ja'far, but he is later than Ma'ruf, yet he used to receive effusion from the spirituality of Ja'far, so his attribution to him became famous. And when these mentioned qualities are gathered in Ali, it necessitates that he is better than others.

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration of eight virtues of Ali ibn Abi Talib, presented as evidence for the claim that he is the best (al-afdal) after the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). The passage begins with the virtue of knowledge, citing a hadith where the Prophet states that Ali knows the context of every revealed verse. It also mentions that Ali's sermons contain profound secrets of theology, that all Islamic sects trace their origins to him in kalam and fiqh, that Ibn Abbas was his student, and that he pioneered Arabic grammar and chivalry. The second virtue is asceticism: despite worldly abundance, Ali lived austerely and famously divorced the world three times. The third virtue is generosity, illustrated by the well-known story of the vow: when his children were ill, Ali and Fatima vowed to fast, and each evening they gave their only food to a beggar, an orphan, and a captive, prompting the revelation of Qur'an 76:7-8. The fourth virtue is bravery, evidenced by his feats in battle and the Prophet's praise. The fifth is good character, including jesting. The sixth is extraordinary strength, as he pulled the gate of Khaybar by divine power. The seventh is his noble lineage and closeness to the Prophet through blood and marriage. The eighth is his distinguished family: his wife Fatima, his sons al-Hasan and al-Husayn, and his descendants, whose spiritual influence is exemplified by later saints like Abu Yazid and Ma'ruf al-Karkhi. The passage concludes that the combination of these qualities necessitates that Ali is superior to all other Companions.

Virtue 5: Good Character (Ḥusn al-Khuluq) of Ali (RA)
Ali's excellent character and its link to faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ali's excellent character, including his humor, reflects his prophetic model.

Core Idea
Ali's good character was so well-known that even his jesting was seen as a virtue.

The fifth virtue is Ali's excellent character. The passage notes that it was famous, to the point that he was attributed with lightheartedness (dubaʿah). The Prophet's saying 'Good character is from faith' is cited to emphasize that this trait is part of religious excellence. Thus, Ali's character aligns with prophetic teachings.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation, noting that good character is attributed to Ali and associated with faith.
2
Understand the inclusion of jesting
The mention of jesting shows that good character includes approachability and pleasantness, not just solemnity.
3
Connect to faith
The Prophet's statement links good character to faith, making it a religious virtue.
