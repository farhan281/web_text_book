Hadith Evidence for the Punishment of the Grave
Al-Iji enumerates hadith evidence for the punishment of the grave and states its tawatur.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the punishment of the grave is established by numerous authentic hadiths whose common meaning is mutawatir, and you will be familiar with key examples and the role of the angels Munkar and Nakir.

Core Idea
The punishment of the grave is proven by so many hadiths that their shared meaning is mass-transmitted.

Al-Iji begins this section by asserting that the authentic hadiths on the punishment of the grave are too many to count, and their common meaning reaches the level of tawatur (mass-transmission). He then provides several specific examples from the Prophet's sayings and actions, including the famous hadith about the two graves and the hadith about Sa'd ibn Mu'adh. These hadiths also mention the questioning by the two angels, Munkar and Nakir, a matter upon which the Salaf were unanimous.

Commentary
This passage presents the hadith evidence for the punishment of the grave. The author states that authentic hadiths on this topic are so numerous that their common meaning is mutawatir (mass-transmitted), even if each individual hadith is solitary. He then lists examples: the Prophet's statement about two graves being punished for urination and gossip, the command to avoid urine, the squeezing of Sa'd ibn Mu'adh's grave, and the Prophet's frequent seeking of refuge from the punishment of the grave. Some of these hadiths also mention the questioning by the two angels Munkar and Nakir. The author then addresses an objection from Quran 44:56, where the denier argues that if people were revived in the grave they would die twice, but the verse says they will not taste death except the first death. The author answers that the verse describes Paradise: the inhabitants will not die there, and the phrase 'except the first death' is a rhetorical emphasis that they will never die in Paradise; it does not negate a death occurring before entering Paradise. Additionally, some interpret 'the first death' as referring to the genus of death, not a particular instance, so it does not preclude multiple deaths. The author concludes that the objection is merely a counterargument to the Quranic proofs, and the deniers then retreat to saying that only apparent meanings can be employed. The passage ends with a footnote citing the hadith of Munkar and Nakir and an additional point that the description of the first death actually supports the existence of a second death.

Refuting an Objection to the Punishment of the Grave from the Quran
Al-Iji refutes an objection from Quran 44:56 against the punishment of the grave.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection to the punishment of the grave based on Quran 44:56 is refuted by explaining that the verse describes Paradise and does not negate death before entering it.

Core Idea
The verse 'They will not taste death except the first death' refers to Paradise, not to the intermediate state.

After presenting the hadith evidence, Al-Iji addresses an objection raised by those who deny the punishment of the grave. They cite Quran 44:56, which says the inhabitants of Paradise will not taste death except the first death, implying that there is no death in the grave. Al-Iji provides a clear refutation by showing that the verse describes Paradise and regardless of interpretation—whether rhetorical emphasis or genus—it does not negate a death in the grave before Paradise.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and the verse cited
The denier argues from Quran 44:56 that there is no death after the first death, so punishment in the grave cannot happen.
2
Understand Al-Iji's first answer
Al-Iji explains that the verse describes the people of Paradise; the pronoun 'therein' refers to Paradise. They will not die in Paradise, so the verse does not speak about the grave.
3

The phrase 'except the first death' is a rhetorical confirmation that they will never die in Paradise, like saying 'if you could taste the first death, you would die here, but you cannot.'
4
Consider the third answer
Some scholars say 'the first death' refers to the genus of death, not the specific first death, so it does not negate multiple deaths.
5
Understand the final conclusion
The objection is a counterargument to the Quranic evidence, and the deniers then fall back on the claim that only apparent meanings of texts can be used. Additionally, some note that the description of the first death actually implies a second death, making the verse an argument against the deniers.

The Reality of the Scale and Refutation of the Mu'tazila

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The scale is real; deeds are weighed through their records or transformed forms.

The Mu'tazila rejected the literal weighing of deeds on the Day of Judgment, arguing that deeds are transient accidents that cannot be re-created or weighed. This lesson presents their two main rational objections and the Sunni refutation based on authentic hadith, which explains that the books of deeds are weighed, or that good and evil deeds are turned into luminous and dark bodies. By understanding this debate, you will see how the Sunni tradition upholds the literal reality of the scale while addressing rational concerns.

The Hadith on 'Quran is Created' and Its Application to Mu'tazila
Refutes the application of a hadith to the Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the hadith 'Whoever says the Quran is created is a disbeliever' does not apply to the Mu'tazila according to this argument.

Core Idea
The hadith about the created Quran does not automatically condemn the Mu'tazila.

This passage addresses a common accusation against the Mu'tazila: that they are disbelievers for saying the Quran is created. The author provides two key defenses: the hadith is a solitary report (ahad) and thus not definitive, and the term 'created' in the hadith could mean 'fabricated' rather than 'originated'. Additionally, the real point of disagreement is about God's essential attribute, which the Mu'tazila do not affirm.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice the flow of the argument.
The passage is dense; read slowly.
2
Identify the two main defenses
The author gives two reasons why the hadith does not apply. Find them: one about the hadith itself, one about the meaning of 'created'.
Also note the additional point about attributes.
3
Understand the distinction between 'fabricated' and 'originated'
The author explains that 'created' (مخلوق) might mean 'fabricated' (مختلق) in the hadith. This is a different meaning from 'originated' (حادث).
The Mu'tazila affirm the Quran is originated, not fabricated.
4
Connect to the essential attribute vs. uttered words distinction
The author clarifies that true disbelief would be saying God's essential attribute (صفة نفسية) is created, but the Mu'tazila only say the uttered words (ألفاظ) are created, which even Ahl al-Sunnah accept.
This further shows the hadith does not target Mu'tazila.
5
Reflect on the implications for takfir
Consider why it is important to be careful about declaring someone a disbeliever based on ambiguous or weak evidence.
This lesson is about precision in theological judgments.

الثالث : قولهم بخلق القرآن وفي الحديث الصحيح من قال القرآن مخلوق فهو كافر. قلنا آحاد فلا يفيد علماً، أو المراد بالمخلوق هو المختلق أي المفترى يقال : خلق الإفك واختلقه وتخلقه أي افتراه، وهذا كفر بلا خلاف، والنزاع في كونه مخلوقاً بمعنى أنه حادث. وأيضاً المراد أن من قال بخلق صفته النفسية فهو كافر وإلا أهل السنة أيضاً قائلون بخلق الألفاظ كما سبق، والمعتزلة لا يقولون بخلق النفسي لعدم قولهم بذلك فلا يلزم أن يكونوا كافرين.
The third: their statement about the creation of the Quran, and in the authentic hadith: 'Whoever says the Quran is created is a disbeliever.' We said: It is a solitary report (ahad) and thus does not yield certain knowledge, or what is meant by 'created' is 'fabricated' (al-mukhtalaq), i.e., falsehood. It is said: he fabricated a lie (khalaqa al-ifka wa ikhtalaqahu wa takhallaqahu), i.e., he invented it falsely. This is disbelief without dispute, but the dispute is about it being created in the sense of being originated (hadith). Also, what is meant is that whoever says that His essential attribute (as-sifah an-nafsiyyah) is created is a disbeliever; otherwise, the Ahl al-Sunnah also say that the uttered words (al-alfaz) are created, as previously mentioned, and the Mu'tazila do not say that the essential attribute is created, because they do not hold that, so it does not necessarily follow that they are disbelievers.

Commentary
This passage addresses the accusation that the Mu'tazila are disbelievers for saying the Quran is created. The accuser cites a hadith that condemns such a statement. The response offers two lines of defense. First, the hadith is a solitary report (ahad), which does not provide certain knowledge (ilm), so it cannot be used to declare disbelief. Second, the word 'created' in the hadith might mean 'fabricated' (mukhtalaq), which is indeed kufr, but the Mu'tazila use 'created' to mean 'originated' (hadith), not 'fabricated'. Moreover, what would truly be kufr is saying that God's essential attribute (as-sifah an-nafsiyyah) is created, but the Mu'tazila do not affirm such an attribute in the first place; they only say the recited Quran (al-alfaz) is created, which even Ahl al-Sunnah accept. Thus, the hadith does not apply to them.

First Proof: Consensus on Appointing an Imam
First proof that appointing an Imam is obligatory by revelation: consensus of the early Muslims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the consensus of the early Muslim community serves as a proof that appointing an Imam is obligatory by revelation, and how the author defends this proof against the objection that it lacks a transmitted textual basis.

Core Idea
The early Muslims' unanimous agreement on appointing an Imam is a binding religious proof, not just history.

After establishing that the obligation to appoint an Imam is not rational but comes from revelation, the author presents his first proof: the mutawatir consensus of the early Muslims. This consensus is so strong that it even took priority over the Prophet's burial. But is consensus enough without a clear textual basis? The author anticipates this objection and provides two compelling answers.

Guided Path
1
Understand the framework
The author first clarifies what the obligation is not: not upon God, not by reason. This sets the stage for the revealed proof.
2
Read the consensus narrative
Read how Abu Bakr's sermon and the Companions' actions demonstrate their agreement on the necessity of an Imam.
Notice that disagreement on the specific person does not negate the consensus on the principle.
3
Analyze the objection and response
The objection says consensus must have a textual basis; the author gives two alternative answers: either the consensus itself suffices, or the basis was non-transmissible circumstantial evidence.
This shows how kalam deals with potential weaknesses in arguments.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
لنا في إثبات مذهبنا أن نقول : أما عدم وجوبه على الله أصلا وعدم وجوبه علينا عقلاً، أنه لا فقد مر لما تبين من وجوب عليه تعالى ولا حكم للعقل في مثل ذلك، وأما وجوبه علينا سمعاً فلوجهين : الأول : إنه تواتر إجماع المسلمين في الصدر الأول بعد وفاة النبي على امتناع خلو الوقت عن خليفة و إمام حتى قال أبو بكر رضي الله عنه في خطبته المشهورة حين وفاته عليه السلام ) ألا إن محمداً قد مات ولا بد لهذا الدين ممن يقوم به فبادر الكل إلى قبوله ولم يقل أحد لا حاجة إلى ذلك، بل اتفقوا عليه وقالوا : ننظر في هذا الأمر، وبكروا إلى سقيفة بني ساعدة وتركوا له أهم الأشياء وهو دفن رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم واختلافهم في التعيين لا يقدح في ذلك الاتفاق ولم يزل الناس بعدهم على ذلك في كل عصر إلى زماننا هذا من نصب إمام متبع في كل عصر، فإن قيل: لا بد للإجماع المذكور من مستند كما علم في موضعه ولو كان النقل ذلك المستند نقلاً متواتراً لتوفر الدواعي إليه قلنا : استغنى عن نقله بالإجماع فلا توفر للدواعي أو نقول : كان مستنده من قبيل ما لا يمكن نقله من قرائن الأحوال التي لا يمكن معرفتها إلا بالمشاهدة والعيان لمن كان في زمن النبي عليه السلام .
Our proof for establishing our doctrine is to say: As for its not being obligatory upon God at all, and its not being obligatory upon us rationally, that has already been made clear from what has been established of there being no obligation upon Him, and reason has no ruling in such matters. As for its being obligatory upon us by revelation, it is for two reasons: The first: There is a mutawatir consensus of the Muslims in the first generation after the death of the Prophet on the necessity of there not being a time devoid of a caliph and imam, to the extent that Abu Bakr, may God be pleased with him, said in his famous sermon at the time of the Prophet's death: 'Indeed Muhammad has died, and this religion must have someone to uphold it.' So everyone hastened to accept him, and no one said there is no need for that; rather, they agreed upon it and said, 'Let us look into this matter.' They went early to the portico of Banu Sa'ida, leaving aside the most important thing, which was the burial of the Messenger of God, peace be upon him. Their disagreement about the specific person does not detract from that consensus. People after them have continued in every age up to our time on appointing an Imam followed in every age. If it is said: This consensus must have a basis, as is known in its place, and if that basis were a transmitted report (naql) transmitted by tawatur, then there would have been ample motivation to transmit it. We say: It dispensed with transmitting it because of the consensus, so there was no ample motivation. Or we say: Its basis is of the kind that cannot be transmitted, such as the circumstantial evidence (qara'in al-ahwal) that cannot be known except by direct observation and eyewitness for those who were in the time of the Prophet, peace be upon him.

Commentary
This passage presents the first proof that the obligation to appoint an Imam is established by revelation (sam'an). The author first sets the framework: it is not obligatory on God (because He is not subject to obligations) nor on us by reason (because reason alone does not impose such obligations). Then the first of two reasons is given: the consensus (ijma') of the early Muslim community after the Prophet's death. This consensus is mutawatir, meaning widespread and certain. The evidence includes Abu Bakr's sermon, the immediate agreement of the Companions to appoint a leader, their prioritizing this over the Prophet's burial, and their going to the portico of Banu Sa'ida. Their differing on the specific person (Abu Bakr vs. others) does not negate the consensus on the principle of having an imam. This practice continued in every age. An objection is raised: consensus must have a textual basis (mustanad). If that basis were a transmitted text, there would have been strong incentive to transmit it, yet we don't have such a text. The answer is that either the consensus itself obviates the need to transmit the basis, or the basis was non-transmissible circumstantial evidence (qara'in al-ahwal) known only to those present, like the Prophet's implied commands or the community's tacit agreement.

Second Proof and Objection on Obligation by Revelation
Second proof and objection with refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second rational proof for the obligation of the imamate and the objection that it is a revelation-based personal obligation, along with the defense of the ummah's integrity.

Core Idea
The obligation of the imamate is argued by repelling likely harm and defended against the charge of collective neglect.

After presenting the first proof, the author offers a second rational argument: appointing an Imam prevents expected harm. Then an objection is raised that this obligation is only by revelation and that if it were a personal duty, the ummah would have sinned over many ages. The passage responds by affirming the ummah's collective ability and rejecting the claim that no qualified Imam exists.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the full Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice that the second proof is incomplete.
The proof ends with 'وأنه' which likely continues elsewhere.
2
Identify the second proof
The second proof is that appointing an Imam repels likely harm (دفع ضرر مظنون). Understand this brief but rational basis.
3
Analyze the objection
The objection states that if it were an individual obligation, the ummah in most ages would have abandoned it due to lack of a qualified Imam. This would mean the ummah agreed on sin, which is false.
The objection uses a conditional: if wajib 'ayn, then ummah abandoned -> contradiction.
4
Follow the refutation
The refutation has two parts: first, the inability of individuals does not negate collective ability; second, the claim that no qualified Imam exists is denied and considered a bad opinion of the ummah.

الثاني : من الوجهين أن فيه أي في نصب الإمام دفع ضرر مظنون وأنه . قوله : ( وأما وجوبه علينا سمعاً) اعترض عليه بأنه لو وجب عينا لزم إطباق الأمة في أكثر الأعصار على ترك الواجب لانتفاء الإمام المتصف بما يجب من الصفات واللازم منتف لأن ترك الواجب معصية وضلالة والأمة لا تجتمع على الضلالة فإن قلت الضلالة إنما تلزم لو تركوه على قدرة واختيار قلت: عجز كل واحد لا ينافي قدرة الكل ولو أريد بالعجز عدم من يتصف بشرائط الإمامة فهو ممنوع وفيه سوء ظن بالأمة .
The second of the two points is that in it—i.e., in appointing the Imam—there is the repelling of a likely harm, and that it... His statement: (As for its being obligatory upon us by revelation) it was objected against that if it were individually obligatory (wajib 'ayn), then it would necessitate the ummah in most ages concurring upon abandoning the obligatory, due to the absence of an Imam possessing the required attributes. The consequent is false, because abandoning the obligatory is sin and misguidance, and the ummah does not concur upon misguidance. If you say: misguidance only follows if they abandon it while being able and choosing. I say: the inability of each individual does not negate the ability of all. If by 'inability' is meant the absence of one possessing the conditions of Imamate, it is denied, and in that there is an ill opinion of the ummah.
Source pages p. 377

Commentary
This passage begins the second proof: appointing an Imam repels expected harm (daf' darar maznun). The text then cuts off. The rest of the passage is a marginal comment on the statement that the obligation is by revelation (wa amma wujubuhu 'alayna sam'an). An objection is raised: if it were a personal obligation (wajib 'ayn) on the entire ummah, then in most ages the ummah would have collectively abandoned it because there was no Imam with all required attributes. This would mean the ummah agreed on sin, but the ummah is protected from consensus on error. The answer: the inability of each individual to find such an Imam does not negate the ability of all collectively. Also, claiming that no qualified Imam exists is itself a bad opinion of the ummah. This defense upholds the obligation while acknowledging practical difficulties.
