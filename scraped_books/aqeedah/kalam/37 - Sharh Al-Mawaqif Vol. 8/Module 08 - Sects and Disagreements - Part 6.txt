Denying created Paradise and Hell and historical events contradicts clear Quranic and transmitted evidence.

Perverse Ruling on Prayer Nullification
Refutes a ruling that retroactively invalidates the beginning of a prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Hishāmiyya's ruling that corrupting the end of a prayer makes the entire beginning of the prayer sinful, and why this is contrary to scholarly consensus.

Core Idea
Invalidating the whole prayer due to a later corruption contradicts sound fiqh principles.

The final doctrine of the Hishāmiyya listed here concerns a ruling about prayer. They claimed that if someone corrupts the end of a prayer after having started it correctly, then the entire beginning part becomes an act of disobedience. This lesson unpacks this strange ruling and shows how it violates the consensus of scholars that each part of prayer is evaluated independently unless nullified by a condition.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on prayer
Read the Arabic text concerning the person who corrupts the end of a prayer after starting correctly.
Notice their conclusion: the beginning of the prayer becomes sin.
2
Understand the implication
They are retroactively declaring the valid parts as sinful, which is unjust and inconsistent with usual fiqh principles.
Normally, only the invalid action affects the later parts, not the earlier valid parts.
3
Note the consensus
The author states this is against ijmāʿ (consensus).
Scholars agree that valid actions remain valid unless a nullifier occurs before completion.

The Ṣāliḥiyya Sect: Disallowed Views on Attributes and Substance
Lists the deviant beliefs of the followers of al-Ṣāliḥī.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two key doctrines of the Ṣāliḥiyya sect and their theological implications for Ashʿarī thought.

Core Idea
The Ṣāliḥiyya claimed attributes can subsist in the dead, undermining divine uniqueness.

In this passage, al-Ījī introduces the Ṣāliḥiyya, a sect that held two extreme positions. First, they asserted that attributes like knowledge and power can exist in a dead person. Second, they believed a substance could be completely free of accidents. Both views violate core Ashʿarī principles about the nature of attributes and life.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the literal translation. Let the author's words set the stage.
Notice the compact style: two doctrines stated concisely.
2
Identify the two doctrines
Find the two distinct positions: (1) permitting attributes to subsist in the dead, and (2) permitting a substance to be devoid of all accidents. Write them down.
Doctrine (1) is about attributes; doctrine (2) is about substance and accidents.
3
Examine the logical entailment
The author points out that if attributes can be in the dead, then it follows that people could be dead while having attributes, and that God might not be living. Reflect on why this is problematic.
This entailment shows the absurdity of the first doctrine.
4
Connect to Sunni theology
Consider why Ashʿarīs reject these views: life is a necessary attribute for the subject of attributes like knowledge and power; only Allah is necessarily living.
Also, substances always have accidents in the Ashʿarī framework.
5
Test your understanding
Answer the checkpoint question to confirm your grasp of the main point.

Lists the deviant beliefs of Aḥmad b. Ḥābiṭ and his followers.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the deviant beliefs of the Habitiyya sect, including their doctrine of two gods and their misinterpretation of scriptural texts.

The Habitiyya claimed there are two gods: an eternal God and a temporal Messiah.

Lists the deviant beliefs of Faḍl al-Ḥadathī, including reincarnation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the doctrine of the Ḥadathiyya sect, including their belief in reincarnation and the moral responsibility of animals.

Core Idea
The Ḥadathiyya believed animals are reincarnated souls being tested for past sins.

The Ḥadathiyya sect, named after its founder Faḍl al-Ḥadathī, adopted the beliefs of the Ḥābiṭiyya but added two distinctive doctrines: reincarnation (al-tanāsukh) and the moral responsibility of animals. Their complex cosmology explains how souls are reborn in different forms based on their deeds. Let us examine their views as presented in the text.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage carefully along with the English translation. Notice the structure: the author begins by naming the sect and its founder, then states their addition to the Ḥābiṭiyya doctrine, and then elaborates their cosmology.
Pay attention to the three categories of beings based on obedience.
2
Identify the three groups of creation
The passage describes that God created rational animals in another realm, tested them, and then divided them into three groups: the fully obedient (kept in bliss), the fully disobedient (sent to hell), and the partially obedient (sent to this world in various forms).
This is the core of their belief.

Thumamiyya's denial of an agent for generated acts and their argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Thumamiyya's argument that generated acts have no agent and why they rejected attributing them to either humans or God.

Core Idea
Generated acts have no agent—neither the dead nor God can be their source.

The Thumamiyya sect, founded by Thumama ibn Ashras, held a controversial view on generated acts (actions that result from human effort but are not directly willed). They argued that such acts cannot be attributed to any agent, using a striking example of a shooter who dies before an arrow strikes its target. This lesson unpacks their reasoning and its implications for Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on generated acts
Read the Arabic and English translation carefully. Focus on the argument about the arrow and the dead shooter.
Notice how the Thumamiyya present two disjuncts: attributing to dead or to God.
2
Examine the two arguments
First, attributing the act to a dead person is impossible because they are no longer an agent. Second, attributing it to God would imply God causes evil, which they reject.
This is a dilemma designed to show no agent exists.
3
Reflect on the theological stakes
Consider why mainstream theologians found this view problematic—it undermines divine causality and human responsibility for generated acts.
This doctrine was a minority position within the Mu'tazila.

Repentance can be invalid if one persists in another sin or lacks ability.

The Bahshamiyya, followers of Abu Hashim al-Jubba'i, held several controversial positions that set them apart from other Mu'tazilites. Among these are their views on blame and repentance. This passage outlines their unique stances and the logical consequences that follow.

الخطابية : هو أبو الخطاب الأسدي عزا نفسه إلى أبي عبد الله جعفر الصادق فلما علم منه غلوه في حقه تبرأ منه فلما اعتزل عنه ادعى الأمر لنفسه. قالوا : الأئمة أنبياء وأبو الخطاب نبي ففرضوا طاعته، أي زعموا أن الأنبياء فرضوا على الله طاعة أبي الخطاب. بل زادوا على ذلك وقالوا : الأئمة آلهة والحسنان ابنا الله وجعفر الصادق إله، ولكن أبو الخطاب أفضل منه ومن علي. وهؤلاء يستحلون شهادة الزور لموافقيهم على مخالفيهم. والإمام بعد قتله أي قتل أبي الخطاب معمر، أي ذهب إلى ذلك جماعة منهم فعبدوا معمراً كما كانوا يعبدون أبا الخطاب. وقالوا: الجنة نعيم الدنيا والنار آلامها والدنيا لا تفنى. واستباحوا المحرمات وترك الفرائض. وقيل الإمام بعد قتله بزيع، أي ذهب إلى ذلك طائفة أخرى منهم. وقالوا : إن كل مؤمن يوحى إليه مستمسكين بقوله تعالى : {وما كان لنفس أن تموت إلا بإذن الله} [آل عمران : ١٤٥]، أي بوحي من الله إليه. وفيهم أي في أصحاب بزيغ من هو خير من جبريل وميكائيل وهم لا يموتون أبداً بل إذا بلغوا النهاية يرفعون إلى الملكوت. وقيل : هو أي الإمام بعد أبي الخطاب عمير بن بنان العجلي إلا أنهم يموتون أي يقولون بذلك.
The Khattabiyya: He is Abu al-Khattab al-Asadi. He attributed himself to Abu Abdullah Ja'far al-Sadiq, but when Ja'far learned of his extremism regarding him, he disassociated from him. When he left him, he claimed the authority for himself. They said: The imams are prophets, and Abu al-Khattab is a prophet, so they made obedience to him obligatory, i.e., they claimed that the prophets obligated upon God obedience to Abu al-Khattab. Rather, they went beyond that and said: The imams are gods, al-Hasan and al-Husayn are sons of God, and Ja'far al-Sadiq is a god, but Abu al-Khattab is better than him and than Ali. And these people permit bearing false witness for their supporters against their opponents. The Imam after his death, i.e., the killing of Abu al-Khattab, is Ma'mar, i.e., a group of them went to that, so they worshipped Ma'mar as they used to worship Abu al-Khattab. And they said: Paradise is the pleasure of this world and Hell is its pains, and this world is never-ending. And they made forbidden things permissible and abandoned religious obligations. And it is said: The Imam after his killing is Bazigh, i.e., another sect from them went to that. And they said: Every believer receives revelation, clinging to His saying: {No soul can die except by Allah's permission} (Quran 3:145), i.e., by revelation from God to him. And among them, i.e., among the followers of Bazigh, there are those who are better than Gabriel and Michael, and they never die, rather when they reach the end, they are raised to the celestial kingdom. And it is said: He, i.e., the Imam after Abu al-Khattab, is 'Umayr ibn Banan al-Ijli, except that they die, i.e., they say that.

Commentary
This passage describes the Khattabiyya sect founded by Abu al-Khattab. It recounts his claim after being rejected by Ja'far al-Sadiq. Their beliefs escalate: imams as prophets, then as gods; Abu al-Khattab is superior. They permit false witness and believe in a succession of worshipped figures after Abu al-Khattab: Ma'mar, Bazigh, and 'Umayr. They have a radically this-worldly view of Paradise and Hell, and abrogate the law. They claim that every believer receives revelation (wahy), using a Quranic verse for support, and that some of Bazigh's followers are superior to angels and immortal. The passage presents these claims sequentially.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That the claim of 'revelation for every believer' is similar to Sufi inspiration (ilham).
Correction: The Khattabiyya used the term 'wahy' (revelation to prophets) and misapplied it to all believers, claiming prophethood for them, which is distinct from ilham.

Key Takeaway
The Khattabiyya rejected core Islamic teachings by permitting the prohibited, denying the afterlife, and claiming every believer receives revelation and that some are superior to angels.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you read that the Khattabiyya made forbidden things permissible and abandoned religious obligations, examine your own heart: In what areas of your life are you tempted to make allowances that dilute or discard what Allah has commanded? Are there any acts of worship you have neglected or justified away? Commit today to returning to one obligation you have been lax in.
?
The Khattabiyya claimed that every believer receives revelation (wahy), misinterpreting the Qur'an. Have you ever interpreted a verse of the Qur'an in a way that justifies your desires, rather than submitting fully to its clear meaning? How can you ensure that you approach the Book with humility, allowing it to guide you instead of imposing your own understanding?
?
Consider the extreme claim that some followers are better than the angels Gabriel and Michael. This arrogance led them to deny death and claim immortality. Do you ever feel a sense of spiritual superiority over others, silently judging their faith or practice? What would it take for you to truly internalize that you are nothing but a servant, that your deeds are purely by Allah's mercy, and that death is an appointment you cannot escape or surpass?
