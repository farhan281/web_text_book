Conditions and Proofs for Commanding Right and Forbidding Wrong
Presents the conditions for obligation, the proofs, and the different opinions on its basis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the conditions that must be met for commanding right and forbidding wrong to be obligatory, and the evidence from consensus, Quran, and the practice of the Companions.

Core Idea
Commanding right is obligatory only when it won't cause fitna and is likely to succeed, and without spying.

This lesson continues the discussion on commanding right and forbidding wrong, focusing on the conditions that make it obligatory and the proofs supporting its obligation. The text presents the different scholarly opinions on its basis (revelation vs reason) and then provides evidence from consensus and the actions of the Companions.

Conclusion of the Fourth Observatory: On commanding right and forbidding wrong. Some people made it obligatory and others prohibited it. The truth is that it follows the commanded and forbidden act: thus commanding the obligatory is obligatory, commanding the recommended is recommended, forbidding the forbidden is obligatory, and forbidding the disliked is recommended. Moreover, it is a communal obligation (fard kifaya), not a personal obligation (fard 'ayn). If some people undertake it, the obligation falls from the others, because its purpose is achieved by that. If every group thinks that the other has not undertaken it, then all are sinful for abandoning it. In our view, it is a branch (far') of the religion, while according to the Mu'tazila it is a root (asl). Al-Amidi said: Some of the Rawafid held that commanding right and forbidding wrong is not obligatory and not even permissible except with the appointment of an Imam and his authorization, as in the execution of prescribed punishments. Those besides them held that it is absolutely obligatory. Then they differed: Ahl al-Sunna held that it is obligatory by revelation (shar'an), while al-Jubba'i and his son held that it is obligatory by reason ('aqlan). Then they differed further: al-Jubba'i said it is absolutely obligatory in matters whose goodness and badness are perceived by reason. Abu Hashim said: If commanding right and forbidding wrong involves repelling harm from the commander and the forbidden, and that harm cannot be repelled except by it, then it is obligatory; otherwise it is not. What indicates its obligation in our view is consensus (ijma'), for there are two groups: one says it is absolutely obligatory, the other says it is obligatory with the authorization of the Imam – thus all have agreed on its obligation in general. And [evidence from] the Book, as His saying: 'Let there be from you a nation inviting to good, commanding right and forbidding wrong' (Al-Imran 104). And the Sunna, as his saying, peace be upon him: 'You will surely command right and forbid wrong, or Allah will cause the worst of you to rule over the best of you, and the best of you will supplicate and their supplication will not be answered.' As for the non-dependence of its permissibility on the authorization of the Imam, it is indicated by the fact that every individual among the Companions used to engage in commanding right and forbidding wrong without authorization and permission from the Imam, and that was widespread among them with no objection, so it was a consensus on its permissibility. For its obligation, after he knows that what he commands is right and what he forbids is wrong, and that it is not among the ijtihadi matters in which the belief of the commander and the commanded differ, there are two conditions: One: that he conjectures that it will not cause a fitna to break out; otherwise it is not obligatory. Similarly, it is not obligatory if he conjectures that it will not lead to the intended goal; in that case it is recommended to show the symbol of Islam. So its obligation is only when he considers the goal achievable without stirring up fitna. The second: not spying and investigating the conditions of people, according to the Book.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and ruling on commanding right and forbidding wrong. It begins by stating the differing opinions: some made it obligatory, others prohibited it. The correct view is that it follows the commanded or forbidden act: commanding what is obligatory is obligatory, commanding what is recommended is recommended, forbidding what is haram is obligatory, and forbidding what is makruh is recommended. It is a communal obligation (fard kifaya), not a personal one; if some fulfill it, the rest are absolved. It is considered a branch (far') in Sunni theology but a root (asl) among the Mu'tazila. Al-Amidi reports that some Rawafid require the appointment of an Imam to authorize it, but the majority disagree. Sunni scholars say it is obligatory by revelation (shar'), while al-Jubba'i and his son say it is obligatory by reason ('aql), with differences among them on conditions. The proof of obligation is ijma' (consensus) that it is obligatory in general terms, plus Quran and Sunna. The proof that it does not require authorization from the Imam is that the Companions practiced it without such authorization, and that was widespread with no objection, thus constituting consensus. The conditions for its obligation are: 1) that one conjectures it will not lead to fitna (otherwise it is not obligatory, and if it may not achieve the goal, it is recommended to show the symbol of Islam), and 2) that one does not spy on people.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That commanding right and forbidding wrong is always obligatory even if it leads to fitna or is ineffective.
Correction: The text states it is not obligatory if it will cause fitna or if success is unlikely; in such cases it is recommended to show the symbol of Islam.

Key Takeaway
Commanding right and forbidding wrong is obligatory only when it is likely to succeed without causing fitna, and without spying, as proven by consensus, Quran, and the example of the Companions.
