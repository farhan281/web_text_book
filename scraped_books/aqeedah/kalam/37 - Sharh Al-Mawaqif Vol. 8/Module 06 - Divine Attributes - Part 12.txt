Divine Attributes: Visibility, Will, and Transcendence
Lists divine attributes: vision, will, self-sufficiency, justice, transcendence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari doctrine of God's beatific vision, absolute will, self-sufficiency, and transcendence beyond partition, limit, and injustice.

Core Idea
God is seen without modality, wills all, needs nothing, and is beyond all imperfection.

This passage from Sanusi's creed presents a series of essential Ash'ari beliefs about Allah. It begins with the beatific vision in the afterlife—a vision without physical or emanationist interpretation—then affirms His absolute will and self-sufficiency, and concludes with negations of any imperfection or limitation. Let's examine each point in turn.

Guided Path
1
Read the attribute statements
Read the first part of the Arabic passage that focuses on divine attributes. Note how each statement builds on the previous one.
The passage uses concise, creedal language.
2
Understand the beatific vision
Focus on the phrase 'bala intiba' wa la shu'a''. This negates any physical or ray-based interpretation of seeing God.
Ash'aris affirm vision but without modality (bila kayf).
3
Grasp God's will and self-sufficiency
The statements 'ma sha Allahu kana' and 'ghani' affirm that God's will is absolute and He is free from need. Note that nothing is obligatory upon Him.
This counters any notion that God is compelled by external factors.
4
Reflect on transcendence
The negations of injustice, partition, limit, and the affirmation that change applies only to creatures highlight God's utter transcendence.
Increase and decrease are attributed to creatures, not God.

مرئي للمؤمنين في الآخرة بلا انطباع ولا شعاع ما شاء الله كان وما لم يشأ لم يكن غني لا يحتاج في شيء إلى شيء ولا يجب عليه شيء إن أثاب فبفضله وإن عاقب فبعدله لا غرض لفعله ولا حاكم سواه لا يوصف فيما يفعل أو يحكم بجور ولا ظلم وهو غير متبعض ولا له حد ولا نهاية وله الزيادة والنقصان في مخلوقاته والمعاد الجسماني حق وكذا المجازاة والمحاسبة والصراط والميزان وخلق الجنة والنار وخلود أهل الجنة فيها وخلود الكفار في النار ويجوز العفو عن المذنبين
He is visible to the believers in the afterlife without impression or ray. Whatever He wills is and whatever He does not will is not. He is Self-sufficient, in need of nothing for anything. Nothing is obligatory upon Him. If He rewards, it is by His bounty; if He punishes, it is by His justice. His action has no purpose, and there is no judge other than Him. He is not described in what He does or judges as having injustice or wrongdoing. He is not partitioned, and He has no limit or end. He has increase and decrease in His creatures. The bodily resurrection is true, as is recompense, reckoning, the bridge, the balance, the creation of Paradise and Hell, the eternal abiding of the people of Paradise therein and the eternal abiding of the disbelievers in the Fire. And it is permissible for Him to forgive sinners.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of Ash'ari-Sunni doctrinal points concerning God's attributes and eschatology. It begins by affirming the beatific vision (ru'ya) for the believers in the hereafter, specifying that this vision occurs 'without impression or ray' (bala intiba' wa la shu'a'), thereby rejecting any physicalist or emanationist interpretation. Then it affirms God's absolute will (mashi'a) and self-sufficiency (ghina), denying any obligation upon God: reward is by His bounty (fadl) and punishment by His justice ('adl). It states that God's actions have no 'purpose' (gharad) in the sense of being subject to external ends, and there is no judge above Him. God is not described as unjust or wrongdoing. Then it negates partition (mutabayyid) and limit (hadd) or end (nihaya) for God. The phrase 'He has increase and decrease in His creatures' means that change applies only to creatures, not to God. The passage then affirms bodily resurrection (ma'ad jismani), recompense (mujaza), reckoning (muhasaba), the bridge (sirat), the balance (mizan), the creation of Paradise and Hell, and eternal abiding (khulud) for both believers and disbelievers. Finally, it affirms that God may forgive sinners ('afw), emphasizing His mercy.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: God's actions have no purpose at all.
Correction: Ash'aris deny that God's actions serve external purposes (gharad) that would imply need, but affirm that they have intrinsic wisdom and are for the benefit of creatures.

Key Takeaway
God's essence is transcendent: He is seen without modality, wills absolutely, needs nothing, and is beyond all imperfection.

Eschatological Realities: Resurrection, Reckoning, and Eternal Abode
Lists eschatological realities: resurrection, judgment, eternal states, forgiveness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari affirmations of bodily resurrection, the stages of the afterlife, and the permissibility of God's forgiveness.

Core Idea
The afterlife is real and physical, with judgment, reward, and punishment, yet God may forgive.

The second half of the passage turns to what happens after death. It affirms bodily resurrection, the scales, the bridge, and the eternal states of Paradise and Hell. It also states that God may forgive sinners, highlighting both justice and mercy. Let's examine each component.

Guided Path
1
Read the eschatological statements
Read the second part of the Arabic passage that lists eschatological realities. Note the sequence from resurrection to eternal abode.
These are standard Sunni beliefs.
2
Identify each stage
Identify the terms: bodily resurrection (ma'ad jismani), recompense (mujaza), reckoning (muhasaba), bridge (sirat), balance (mizan), creation of Paradise and Hell, eternal abiding (khulud), forgiveness ('afw).
Each term represents a distinct event or state.
3
Understand the eternal abiding
Note that believers abide eternally in Paradise and disbelievers in Hell. This is a non-negotiable doctrine.
Ash'aris affirm eternal punishment for unbelievers.
4
Reflect on forgiveness
The passage says 'yajuzu al-'afw' meaning it is permissible for God to forgive sinners. This does not mean He will, but that He may.
This balances mercy with justice.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: God must forgive sinners if they are Muslims.
Correction: The passage says 'yajuzu' (it is permissible), not 'yajibu' (obligatory). Forgiveness is at God's discretion.

Key Takeaway
The afterlife includes a physical resurrection, a judgment process with signs and scales, eternal reward or punishment, and the possibility of God's forgiveness.
