First Objection on Sharing Attributes and Its Answer
Objection and response on sharing attributes and persistence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first objection against the persistence of necessary dependencies and how it is answered by distinguishing between shared essential attributes and the specific attribute of being necessary or acquired.

Core Idea
The objection fails because sharing essential attributes does not guarantee sharing persistence.

This passage presents the first objection to the view that necessary dependencies (like heaviness) persist. The objection argues that if a necessary dependency shares all attributes with an acquired dependency, then it must also share persistence. But the response shows that the premise is false: the most specific attribute of a dependency is not shared between necessary and acquired types. Let's examine the details of this debate.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the passage describing the objection: the necessary dependency would share all attributes with the acquired, so it must share persistence. But persistence is false for acquired, so necessary must not persist. Note how the objection uses a conditional.
The objection is based on a premise that Abu Hashim allegedly holds.
2
Identify the response
The response denies that the premise 'sharing in all attributes' is conceded. The most specific attribute for Abu Hashim is either being necessary or being acquired, and these are not shared. Therefore the conclusion about persistence does not follow.
The response also clarifies that sharing in essential attributes does not imply sharing in persistence.
3
Understand the commentary's distinctions
The commentary explains that 'sharing in all attributes' refers to essential and non-essential attributes. But persistence is existence in a second time, which is temporal. Sharing in essential attributes does not necessitate sharing in persistence.
Also, the falsehood of the consequent is both compulsory and demonstrative, while the implication is merely compulsory.
4
Reflect on the logical structure
Notice the distinction between an implication that is only compulsory (based on opponent's assumption) and a conclusion that is demonstratively false. This weakens the objection.

Analogy without a common cause is not a valid argument.

The second objection tries to force the conclusion that necessary dependencies cannot persist by comparing them to sounds and movements. The response points out that this comparison is a mere analogy with no shared cause. This lesson examines the objection and its refutation.

Commentary
This passage presents the second objection against the persistence of necessary dependencies. The objection argues that among accidents that cannot persist (like sounds and movements), there is no difference between those that are voluntary (moved by choice) and those that are involuntary (natural). Therefore, the same should apply to dependencies: there should be no difference in persistence between the acquired (voluntary) and the necessary (involuntary); both should be non-persistent. The response denies the analogy, stating it is a mere analogy without a common cause (jami'). The specificity of sounds and movements might be the reason they cannot persist at all, while dependencies might have a different specificity that allows the necessary to persist. The commentary then clarifies that the first objection (lesson 1) is compulsory (ilzami), not demonstrative (burhani), meaning it is a reductio based on the opponent's own assumptions. It notes that al-Amidi's objection—that such compulsory arguments can be turned around—does not apply here. The commentary also addresses a possible counter: that the proofs of non-persistence of accidents in general already include the case of dependencies, so the argument from analogy is actually an inference from general principles. However, this is left for the reader to ponder.

Compulsory Proof (Ilzami) and Its Defense
Commentary on the nature of the first objection: it is ilzami, not burhani, and defends against al-Amidi's critique.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what makes a proof compulsory (ilzami) and why the first objection is of that type, and how it avoids al-Amidi's criticism.

Core Idea
A compulsory proof only requires the opponent to be inconsistent with his own principles.

The commentary on the second objection reveals that the first objection (previous lesson) was not a demonstrative proof but a compulsory one (ilzami). This lesson explains the nature of ilzami proof and why it is not vulnerable to al-Amidi's critique about arbitrary selection between two statements.

Guided Path
1
Understand ilzami vs burhani
Learn the difference: a burhani proof establishes a truth independently, while an ilzami proof shows the opponent's own position leads to a contradiction.
2
See how the first objection is ilzami

3
Comprehend al-Amidi's critique
Al-Amidi argued that in a compulsory proof, if the opponent has two statements, accusing error in one implies correctness in the other, which is arbitrary.
4
Understand why it doesn't apply
The commentator explains that since the implication is not established in the opponent's doctrine, the critique does not apply; the proof only needs to show inconsistency.

The objection argues that among accidents that cannot persist (like sounds and movements), there is no difference between those that are voluntary (moved by choice) and those that are involuntary (natural). Therefore, the same should apply to dependencies: there should be no difference in persistence between the acquired (voluntary) and the necessary (involuntary); both should be non-persistent. The response denies the analogy, stating it is a mere analogy without a common cause (jami'). The specificity of sounds and movements might be the reason they cannot persist at all, while dependencies might have a different specificity that allows the necessary to persist. The commentary then clarifies that the first objection (lesson 1) is compulsory (ilzami), not demonstrative (burhani), meaning it is a reductio based on the opponent's own assumptions. It notes that al-Amidi's objection—that such compulsory arguments can be turned around—does not apply here. The commentary also addresses a possible counter: that the proofs of non-persistence of accidents in general already include the case of dependencies, so the argument from analogy is actually an inference from general principles. However, this is left for the reader to ponder.

Commentary
This passage is from the discussion on the fifth objective of the third observation, concerning smoothness (al-malāsa) according to the theologians. It begins by stating the disagreement over whether edges (aṭrāf: point, line, surface) are visible by themselves (bi-al-dhāt) or by mediation (bi-al-wāsiṭa). Then an objection is raised: the only thing visible by itself is light, because its vision does not depend on seeing something else; color is seen by means of light, so it is secondary and accidental. The answer clarifies the meaning: a thing is visible by essence if there is a single vision directed primarily at it, and then that same vision is directed at something else, making that other thing visible secondarily and accidentally. The analogy of motion on a ship and its rider is given: the motion is one but attaches to both the ship and the rider, though primarily to the ship. When we see an illuminated color, there are two distinct visions: one for the light and one for the color, but the latter depends on the former. This is why both light and color are fully unveiled to the senses, unlike shape and other attributes, which are not perceived by a separate vision but by the same vision that perceives the color. Then the commentary addresses a potential misunderstanding: the phrase 'a single vision attaches to something else' does not mean one vision attaches to two things simultaneously, which is impossible. Rather, vision attaches to one thing, and the other thing becomes qualified by the effect of that vision through the first. This resolves the objection that a single vision cannot attach to two things. Finally, a proof from Avicenna's Shifa is given: sensory perception occurs by the sense receiving the form of the perceived object; if the form occurs independently, it is perceived by essence; if it follows another form, it is perceived by accident.

The overall purpose is to highlight the properties of light and color rather than truly define them.

Action of Heat on Receptive Bodies Producing Tastes
Al-Jurjani explains how heat causes bitter, pungent, and salty tastes based on density.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how heat, through its quality of separation, produces different tastes (bitter, pungent, salty) depending on the density of the receptive body.

Core Idea
Taste arises from heat's separation effect, moderated by the body's density.

This passage explains a key natural philosophical concept: how a single agent (heat) can produce different qualities in different subjects. Al-Jurjani breaks down the mechanism by which heat's natural tendency to separate causes tastes ranging from bitter to salty, depending on how much the body resists. This illustrates the general principle that effects are shaped by the recipient's nature—a foundational idea in Islamic natural theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage carefully
Start by reading the Arabic text once without stopping. Notice how the author moves from the general principle to three specific cases. Then read the English translation to fix the meaning.
The Arabic uses technical terms like الكثيف (dense), اللطيف (fine), المعتدل (moderate).
2
Identify the three cases and their tastes
The passage enumerates three types of receptive bodies: dense, fine, and moderate. For each, note the taste produced (bitter, pungent, salty) and the reason given (degree of resistance and separation).
3
Understand the causal mechanism
Heat causes separation (تفريق). The degree of separation depends on the body's resistance. A dense body resists strongly, so heat gathers and separates greatly, producing extreme non-compatibility (bitterness). A fine body offers little resistance, so heat penetrates and separates weakly, producing pungency. A moderate body is intermediate, producing saltiness.
This is a classic 'golden mean' pattern in natural philosophy.
4
Examine the verification for saltiness
Read the final part where the author gives a practical verification: mixing bitter ash with water and cooking it yields saltiness. This shows saltiness as a balance between burnt (earthly) and moist (watery) components.
This connects theory to observable experience.
5
Reflect on the broader lesson
Consider how this example illustrates the principle that the agent's action is modified by the recipient's disposition, a key idea in kalam discussions of causality and divine action.

Death as a Positive Quality: Objection and Response

Core Idea

This lesson addresses a theological objection about the nature of death. Some argued that since Allah says He created death, death must be a positive quality. The response clarifies that 'creation' in this context means estimation or predestination, not bringing something into existence from nothing.
