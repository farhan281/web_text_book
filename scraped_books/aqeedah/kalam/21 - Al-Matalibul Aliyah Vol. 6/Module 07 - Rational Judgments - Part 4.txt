Refutation of the Third Objection: The Supposition of the Infinite
Al-Razi refutes the objection that the proof only applies to existing lines.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi refutes the objection that the proof only applies to existing lines, by showing that the impossibility arises from the supposition of the infinite, not from the supposition itself.

Core Idea
A supposition of a possible thing cannot cause an impossibility; the impossibility reveals the falsehood of the supposition.

Abu al-Barakat objects that the impossibilities al-Razi derived only follow if the two lines actually exist. Since they are hypothetical, the proof seems invalid. Al-Razi answers by clarifying the logical status of suppositions. This lesson unpacks his refutation and the key distinction between possible and impossible suppositions.

If a supposition of a possible thing leads to an impossibility, the problem is not the act of supposing but the content being supposed. Here, the impossibility is due to the infinite line.
This is the core of the refutation.

Thus the proof remains valid.

The closest [approach] in this chapter: It should be said: There is no doubt that bodies are equal in being space-occupying (mutahayyiza). This concept is a common factor. Since bodies are equal in this concept, if they were different in another respect, then that by which they share would be other than that by which they differ, so it must be that the consideration of being a body (hajmiyya) and being space-occupying (mutahayyiziyya) is something different from those other considerations. When that is so, we say: Either each of these two considerations is inhering (hal) in the other and a locus (mahall) for it, or neither is inhering in the other nor a locus for it, or one of them is inhering and the other is a locus.

The first is invalid for two reasons: One: The inhering is dependent on the locus; so if each were inhering in the other and a locus for it, a vicious circle (dawr) would result. Second: We show that hajmiyya and tahayyuz cannot inhere in a locus.

The second is invalid: because on this assumption, bodies would be identical essences in themselves. As for the considerations by which difference occurs, they are things other than the bodies and separate in essence. That does not damage our thesis.

As for the third division: that one of the considerations inheres in the bodies and the bodies are loci for it. This does not damage our statement, because on this assumption bodies are identical in the entire quiddity and differ by inhering accidents (a'rad hala). That does not damage our thesis.

As for the fourth division: that hajmiyya and tahayyuz inhere in that consideration and are an attribute of it. That is impossible, because hajmiyya and tahayyuz are concepts specific to space (hayyiz), place (makan), and direction (jiha). So if those considerations are specific to space and direction, then those considerations would also be bodies. That would entail the inherence of a body in a body. If they are not specific to space and direction, then inherence of bodilyness (jismiyya) in them is impossible, because the inherence of that which must be specific to space and direction in something that cannot be specific to space and direction is impossible in the immediate intuition of reason.

This is the utmost that can be said in this chapter. And God knows best.

Commentary
This passage presents the author's preferred approach (al-aqrab fi hadha al-bab). He starts from the premise that bodies are unequivocally equal in being space-occupying (mutahayyiza). This is a common factor (qadr mushtarak). If bodies also have differentiating aspects, then the aspect of being a body (hajmiyya) and being space-occupying must be different from those differentiating aspects. Then he considers four possible relations between the two considerations (hajmiyya/tahayyuz and the differentiating aspects). He then eliminates three possibilities: (1) each inheres in the other (circular), (2) neither inheres (then bodies are identical in essence and difference is external, which is fine), (3) one inheres in the body (then bodies are identical in quiddity and differ by accidents, also fine). The fourth possibility (hajmiyya inheres in the differentiating aspect) is impossible because hajmiyya requires spatial location, leading to absurdities. Thus the only coherent positions are the second and third, which actually support the thesis that bodies are identical in essence (quiddity) and differ by accidents or separate attributes. This argument aims to establish the equality of bodies in their quiddity, a key principle in kalam.

The Third Argument: From the One Only One Proceeds
Aristotelians argue from the One's necessary emanation that only one world can proceed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third Aristotelian proof for the unity of the world based on the emanationist principle that from the one only one proceeds.

Core Idea
If only one world emanates from the One, then multiple worlds are impossible.

The third argument for the unity of the world relies on a metaphysical principle familiar to Aristotelians: from a single, self-necessitating cause only one effect can directly proceed. This passage presents that reasoning, shows how the emanation of intellects and spheres follows, and then uses a reductio ad absurdum to prove that multiple worlds would violate that principle.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two premises
The argument starts with two key premises: (1) God is one and self-necessitating (موجب بالذات), and (2) from the one only one can proceed (الواحد لا يوجد إلا الواحد). Grasp these before moving on.
The second premise is a cornerstone of Neoplatonic emanationism.
2
Follow the emanation chain
According to the philosophers' doctrine, the first emanation from God is a single intellect. From that intellect emanate another intellect and a sphere, and so on, building the hierarchy of intellects and spheres. Understand this sequence.
This emanationist cosmology is adapted from Aristotle and Plotinus.
3
Consider the hypothetical of two worlds
If there were two completely separate worlds, each would need its own series of intellects and spheres. That would mean the One would have to directly cause more than one effect (the first intellects of each world), violating the principle.
This is a reductio ad absurdum: the assumption leads to a contradiction.
4
Reflect on the argument's scope
Note that this argument depends on accepting the emanationist framework. It is not a scriptural proof but a philosophical one, based on specific metaphysical commitments.
Many Muslim theologians rejected emanationism and thus would not accept this proof.

Razi presents the fifth proof for body's composition from matter and form.

Core Idea

Razi builds his fifth proof on the previously established existence of void as dimensional extension. He then shows that if body were merely dimensions, then void would also be a body, leading to the absurdity of interpenetration. This forces the conclusion that body is dimension subsisting in matter, hence composite.

Commentary
This passage begins the second chapter, which focuses on objecting to the proofs of those who affirm the atom (al-muthbitun). It addresses the first proof with two objections (su'al). The first objection challenges the proof's reliance on denying the indivisible atom (al-jawhar al-fard), noting that this issue has been thoroughly discussed elsewhere and cannot be presupposed. The second objection is more substantive: even if we concede that the body is one continuous thing, the only change when division occurs is the loss of unity and the gain of multiplicity. The philosophers agree that unity and number are accidents (a'rad) that inhere in bodies, not essential properties. Therefore, the proof only shows that unity and number are accidents, not that the body's essence (mahiyya) or constituents (muqawwimat) are composite. The objector reinforces this by noting that after division, each part remains continuous as before; only the attribute of unity is lost. The author remarks that this objection is sound, and when he posed it to opponents, he found no satisfactory answer. However, he then states that there are three ways to repel this objection, though they are not elaborated in this passage.

Geometric Proof Against Atomism
Geometric proof that diagonal and side are incommensurable, refuting atomism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how geometric incommensurability between the diagonal and side of a square refutes the atomistic claim that all objects are composed of identical indivisible parts.

Core Idea
Geometry proves the diagonal and side are incommensurable — atomism cannot explain this.

The second argument against atomism uses a well-known geometric fact: the diagonal of a square and its side have no common measure. If atoms were identical units, any two lengths would be multiples of the same atom and thus commensurable. Since the diagonal and side are not commensurable, atomism fails. This short but powerful proof shows how mathematics can be used in theological debates.

Third Argument: Euclid's Parallel Lines Refute Atomism
Third argument: Euclid's principle of equal areas under parallels used to refute atomism, with objection and answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Euclid's geometric theorem about equal areas under parallels is used to argue against the existence of indivisible atoms, and how a geometric objection is resolved.

Core Idea
Geometry shows that area is conserved, disproving atomism's discrete parts.

This lesson presents a sophisticated argument that uses Euclidean geometry to refute the atomist theory of the indivisible atom. The author invokes a theorem about parallelograms to show that atomism leads to a contradiction. An objection is raised and answered using the same geometric principles, revealing the depth of the theological engagement with mathematics.

If this is established, we say: This invalidates the claim of the indivisible atom. For if we suppose that one of the surfaces is ten by ten, so its total is one hundred, and the other surface is one hundred, then the total of the parts obtained in that surface must be equal to one hundred parts, and that is impossible. Commentary
This passage presents a third argument against atomism using Euclid's geometry. The author invokes Euclid's theorem: parallelograms on the same base and between the same parallels have equal area. If surfaces were made of indivisible atoms, two such surfaces with the same base and between the same parallels would have the same number of atoms (area). But if one surface is a 10x10 square (area 100) and the other is a very long but thin rectangle (also area 100), they would both have 100 atoms. However, according to atomism, the number of atoms in a line is discrete, so the long rectangle would have more atoms along its length, leading to more total atoms—a contradiction. An objection is raised: Euclid himself faces an absurdity if one surface is 1x1 and the other is extremely long, because how can they be equal? The answer is that the inclined surface compensates by decreasing in width as it increases in length, preserving area equality. Thus the objection fails, and the argument stands.
