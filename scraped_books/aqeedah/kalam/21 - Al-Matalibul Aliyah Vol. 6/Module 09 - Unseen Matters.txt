The Quranic Proof That the Heavens Have No Fissures
The author argues from Quranic text that the heavens have no fissures.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quranic verse from Surah al-Mulk is used to argue that the celestial spheres are free from fissures and how this refutes a contrary claim.

Core Idea
The Quran directly declares the heavens have no cracks, so any claim of fissures is false.

In this passage, the author presents a simple but powerful Quranic argument for the integrity of the heavens. He first notes that describing the celestial spheres as having intensity and solidity would contradict the Quran. Then he directly cites the verse from Surah al-Mulk, inviting the reader to look for any fissures. The argument is that if the heavens were torn, they would be full of holes, but the Quran denies this. This lesson unpacks the structure of that argument.

Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Pay close attention to the key terms: 'فطور' (fissures) and 'متخرقة' (torn).
2

Notice that the author first makes a negative statement (describing the heavens with intensity/solidity is contrary to the Quran) and then provides a positive proof from the Quranic verse.
3
Analyze the logical structure
Reflect on why the author says 'if the celestial spheres were torn, they would all be holes.' This is a reductio ad absurdum: the claim leads to a conclusion that contradicts the Quranic text.
4
Consider the misconception
Understand that 'fissures' here are physical cracks, not metaphorical flaws. The argument depends on a literal reading.

Refuting Atomism: The Circle Argument
Proof that circle from indivisible parts is impossible, refuting atomism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof that refutes the doctrine of the indivisible atom (jawhar fard) by showing the impossibility of forming a circle from indivisible parts.

Core Idea
If atoms were indivisible, circles would be impossible — but they exist, so atoms must be divisible.

This lesson presents a sophisticated rational proof from Imam al-Razi against atomism. The argument focuses on the impossibility of constructing a circle from indivisible parts, using geometric reasoning that leads to contradictions. By understanding this proof, you will see how kalam philosophers used necessary rational truths to refute metaphysical claims.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the full translation. Let the author's rhythm come through.
The argument is dense; focus on the logical flow.
2
Understand the first part: the absurdity
The author supposes a line of indivisible parts is made into a circle. Then the inner and outer sides become contiguous, leading to an impossible equality in volume with the Throne. Recognize this as a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Examine the alternative scenario
Since the first supposition leads to absurdity, the alternative is that inner sides are contiguous but outer sides are separate. This sets up the next arguments.
This is a key pivot in the proof.
4
Follow the two arguments for divisibility
From the alternative, two arguments show that the indivisible parts must be divisible. The first uses the difference between contiguous and separate. The second considers gaps and their size relative to an atom.
These are numbered in the text as 'the first' and 'the second'.
5
Grasp the final conclusion
Since a line of indivisible parts cannot form a circle, and a body is just lines joined, circles would be impossible on atomism. But circles and spheres exist, so atomism is false.
This is the overall conclusion of the proof.

Commentary
This passage is a proof refuting the doctrine of the indivisible atom (jawhar fard) by demonstrating the impossibility of forming a circle from indivisible parts. The argument proceeds as follows: The author first supposes that a line composed of indivisible parts is made into a circle. In such a circle, the inner sides of the parts become contiguous (touched) while the outer sides also become contiguous. This leads to an absurdity: the volume of the circle could equal the Throne and Chair with the same number of parts, which is impossible. Since the impossibility arises from this supposition, the supposition is false. The alternative is that the inner sides are contiguous but the outer sides are separate. Then, from this established premise, two arguments show the indivisible atom is divisible: (1) Since inner sides are contiguous and outer sides separate, the parts themselves must be divisible because contiguity and separation are different. (2) The gaps between parts either accommodate an indivisible atom or not. If they do, that atom is smaller than others and thus divisible; if they do not, the gaps are smaller than the atom, so the atom is divisible. Therefore, a line of indivisible parts cannot form a circle. Since the body on atomism is just lines joined together, the impossibility of one line forming a circle makes the circle impossible. But circles and spheres are possible (in reality), so atomism is false.

Second Proof: Circles Require Infinite Division of the Atom
The author proves that forming a circle from an atom necessitates infinite division of that atom.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Circles force the atom to divide infinitely.

The author presents a geometric argument: if atoms were indivisible, no circle or sphere could exist. Because forming a curve requires an oblique connection between parts, and varying circle sizes demand infinite degrees of inclination, atomism collapses.

Guided Path

Understand the geometry

The atom must accommodate varying inclinations.
3
Grasp the infinite division conclusion

The second objection: It is from the doctrine of the philosophers that the rational souls separated from bodies have no end. Yet the proof of increase and decrease applies to them. For the sum of souls that existed a hundred years before this is less in number than the sum of souls existing at this time by the amount of the number of souls that came into existence in these hundred years. Commentary
This passage presents two more objections to the first proof. The second objection uses the example of rational souls. Philosophers hold that souls are infinite, but the proof of increase and decrease (applied to finite series) seems to apply: the number of souls existing a hundred years ago is less than now. If the proof held, then the lesser number would imply finitude, contradicting the philosophers' claim. Thus the proof must be flawed. The third objection similarly points to past events: from the time of the Flood to the infinite past (al-azal) are a smaller set compared to all past events, and the argument would wrongly imply that this smaller set is finite, while it is actually infinite. The third objection is cut off but the idea is clear.
