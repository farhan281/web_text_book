Thesis and Classification of Proofs for the Indivisible Atom
Author states thesis on motion and time and classifies three proof types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the author's thesis that motion and time consist of indivisible parts and how this leads to the atomic composition of bodies, as well as the threefold classification of proofs in this chapter.

Core Idea
Motion, time, and space are parallel; if one is atomic, all are atomic.

This passage serves as the introduction to the chapter on proofs for the indivisible atom. The author first lays out his central thesis: motion is composed of discrete 'obtainments' in contiguous spaces, and time is composed of discrete instants. He then explains the interdependence of motion, time, and distance, contrasting his approach with that of the philosophers. Finally, he divides the chapter into three types of proofs, providing a roadmap for the rest of the discussion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the thesis
Read the first part of the passage where the author states what he will prove: motion consists of successive obtainments in contiguous loci, and time of successive instants. Note the claim that each of these is indivisible.
2
Note the parallelism and contrast
Observe how the author ties motion, time, and distance together. He says they are parallel matters. Also notice the contrast with the philosophers who uphold infinite divisibility. This sets up the opposing view.
3
Understand the classification
The author divides the chapter into three types of proofs: for motion, for time, and for the body. Recognize that this structure organizes the entire discussion.
The third type depends on the first two.

Commentary
This passage is both a thesis statement and a classification. The author begins by stating his central claim: motion consists of discrete, indivisible positions (ḥuṣūlāt) in contiguous spaces, and time consists of discrete, indivisible instants (ānāt). If this is true, then the body must be composed of indivisible parts (the atom). He then notes the parallelism of time, motion, and distance: if indivisibility is proven for one, it applies to the others. He contrasts the philosophers' view—that body, motion, and time are infinitely divisible—with the approach of the mutakallimūn (us), who argue the opposite. Finally, he classifies the chapter into three types: proofs for motion's composition of indivisible obtainments, proofs for time's composition of indivisible instants, and the deduction that the body must be atomic. This classification structures the entire chapter.

The Second and Third Faces: Point-to-Point Contact
Razi presents two physical analogies demonstrating juxtaposition of points.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi uses the motion of an epicycle and a dragged cube to demonstrate that continuous lines and circles are composed of juxtaposed points.

Core Idea
Continuous lines arise from point-to-point contact, proving atomic juxtaposition.

Razi presents two intuitive physical demonstrations to support the atomist claim that points are juxtaposed. By considering a moving epicycle center and a dragged cube, he shows that circles and lines are traced through successive point contacts. These arguments are meant to be simpler and independent of abstract geometry.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage for both faces
Read the Arabic and translation for the second and third faces together. Notice how each begins with 'الوجه الثاني' and 'الوجه الثالث'.
The two arguments are parallel.
2
Analyze the second face (epicycle)
Understand the motion of the sphere carrying the epicycle's center. The center traces a circle by contacting successive points.
The conclusion is that the circle is generated from juxtaposed points.
3
Analyze the third face (cube)
Consider a cube with a point dragged along a distance; the point traces a line by contacting points part by part.
Both arguments rely on point-to-point contact.
4
Synthesize
Notice that both faces lead to the same conclusion: points are juxtaposed. This supports the atomist doctrine.

The second face in establishing this proof: We have shown that when the sphere carrying the center of the epicycle moves, a circle is traced from the center of the epicycle, and that circle is generated only by the contact of that point which is the center of the epicycle. And what a point contacts is a point. So that circle is generated only from juxtaposed points. The third face in establishing this proof: We have shown that the cubic body has a point in actuality. When that cube is dragged along the distance, that point is dragged along that distance, and from its dragging a line occurs. And that line is only traced from the contact of that point part by part. And a point contacts only a point, so juxtaposition of points becomes necessary. And these faces as a whole are evident and clear, and are independent of establishing the sphere and circle. And it must be known that whenever a proof's premises are fewer and their strength is more apparent to the intellect, that proof is stronger and more worthy of acceptance. And with Allah is success.

Commentary
This passage presents two additional faces of the proof and then a general methodological principle. The second face uses the motion of an epicycle: as the center moves, it traces a circle through point-to-point contact, showing that the circle is composed of points. The third face uses a cube dragged along a distance: the cube's point traces a line by contacting successive points. Both arguments lead to the conclusion that points are juxtaposed (mutashāfiʿa), meaning they are indivisible atoms. The author then emphasizes that these faces are evident and do not depend on prior acceptance of circles and spheres. Finally, he states a general rule: the fewer and more evident the premises, the stronger and more acceptable the proof. This reflects the methodological preference for simple, intuitive demonstrations.

Commentary
This passage is a critique of the method of geometers, particularly Euclid, regarding how they prove fundamental geometric shapes. The author begins by describing how geometers prove the lens shape, cone, and cylinder using a method called 'moving geometry': they assume one element (like a diameter or side) remains stationary while others move. He points out that if this method were used for a minor branch of geometry, they would deem it weak, yet they rely on it for the most fundamental principles. Then the author turns to Euclid's fifth book, where Euclid gives complex proofs for propositions about ratios that are self-evidently true, such as equal magnitudes having equal ratios to a fixed magnitude. The author insists these are necessary, intuitive truths. Similarly, Euclid proves the converse and transitivity of ratios with subtle arguments, but these are also obvious. The author concludes that Euclid's approach is inconsistent: he provides elaborate proofs for self-evident truths, but when it comes to proving the basic shapes (circle, sphere, cone, cylinder), he relies on the weak moving geometry. This indicates a great deficiency: they use proof where it is unnecessary and neglect it where it is essential.

Commentary
This passage is an introduction to a discussion on the nature of bodies. Fakhr al-Din al-Razi begins by stating the general agreement among rational people that bodies have a substrate (هيولى).
