Why the Indivisible Atom Has No Shape
Refutes the claim that the indivisible atom must be spherical or polygonal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the indivisible atom cannot be described as having a shape, according to kalam reasoning.

Core Idea
An indivisible atom has no shape because shape requires parts.

In kalam, the concept of the indivisible atom (al-jawhar al-fard) is a fundamental building block of matter. Opponents often argue that such an atom must have some shape, such as being spherical or polygonal. Our author refutes this claim by showing that shape is only conceivable for composite bodies, not for simple, partless entities.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Opponent's Claim
The opponent asserts that the indivisible atom must have a shape, either spherical or polygonal. Recognize that this is an attempted limitation on the atom's nature.
This claim is based on the assumption that every physical thing has a shape.
2
Read the Author's Refutation
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice how the author begins by stating the truth: the atom is neither sphere nor polygon.
3
Analyze the Logical Reasoning
Identify the key reason: shape is only conceivable in something composed of sides and parts. Since the atom is indivisible and partless, it cannot have shape.
This is a clear argument from the nature of compositeness.
4
Apply the Conclusion
Understand the broader implication: the indivisible atom cannot be described by any shape attribute. This refutes the opponent's dichotomy.
This illustrates a core principle of kalam physics: simplicity precedes shape.

Third Proof: Triangle Side Lengths and Infinite Divisibility
Sanusi constructs a proof from geometry to infinite divisibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the impossibility of certain triangles and the progression of triangle types leads to the conclusion that magnitudes are infinitely divisible.

Core Idea
Triangles from increasing numbers show angles shrink without limit, proving infinite divisibility.

This passage presents the third proof for infinite divisibility, based on geometric construction from arithmetic sequences. Sanusi demonstrates that as the side lengths increase, the triangle types progress from obtuse to right to acute, and the acute angles become progressively narrower. Since numbers can increase without bound, the angles can become arbitrarily small, implying that points on a line can be approached without end — hence magnitudes are infinitely divisible.

Guided Path
1
Understand the starting impossibility
Read the opening statement about triangle (1,2,3) and why it is impossible. Note the condition that the sum of any two sides must exceed the third.
This failure sets the stage for the progression that follows.
2
Observe the progression of triangle types
Identify the three cases: (2,3,4) gives obtuse, (3,4,5) gives right, (4,5,6) gives acute. Note the pattern of side lengths increasing by one each time.
The sequence is consecutive integers starting from 2.
3
Note the narrowing of acute angles
Compare the acute triangles from (4,5,6) and (5,6,7). The author states that the angle opposite the largest side is smaller in the second triangle, showing a trend of decreasing angle size.
This narrowing continues as numbers increase.
4
Connect to the conclusion of infinite divisibility
Since numbers can increase without limit, the angles can become infinitely small. The author argues that this implies magnitudes are divisible into infinitely many parts.
This is the key link between geometry and the atomic theory.

Fourth Proof: Equilateral Triangle and Perpendicular Line Suggest Divisibility
Fourth proof using an equilateral triangle divided by a perpendicular to argue for divisibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the geometry of an equilateral triangle demonstrates the necessity of divisibility in lines composed of indivisible parts.

Core Idea
The perpendicular's length must be between whole parts, proving divisibility.

This proof uses a geometric construction to challenge the idea that lines are composed of indivisible parts. By assuming an equilateral triangle with sides of three parts and dropping a perpendicular, the author derives a contradiction if the perpendicular has an integral length. This forces the conclusion that the perpendicular line must be divisible, thereby undermining the notion of indivisible parts.

Guided Path
1
Read the geometric setup
Start by understanding the initial assumption: an equilateral triangle with each side composed of three indivisible parts, and a perpendicular dropped from one vertex to the opposite side.
Visualize the triangle as having sides of length 3 units.
2
Identify the resulting triangles and angles
The perpendicular divides the equilateral triangle into two right triangles. Notice the types of angles: right, acute (from the original triangle), and half-acute (the remaining angle at the base).
The acute angle is 60°, the half-acute is 30°.
3
Analyze the hypotenuse lengths
The text assigns lengths to the hypotenuses: the hypotenuse of the right angle (the original side) is 3 parts; the hypotenuse of the half-acute angle is 2 parts. Then consider the remaining hypotenuse (the perpendicular).
These lengths are derived from the geometry.
4
Deduce the necessary length of the perpendicular
The author argues that the perpendicular cannot be 3 (would equal the side) nor 2 (would equal the other hypotenuse), so it must be between 2 and 3. This implies that the perpendicular is divisible, contradicting the assumption of indivisible parts.
This is the key step: the impossibility of integer lengths forces divisibility.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Consider why this forces the rejection of indivisible parts: if the perpendicular had an indivisible length, it would have to be an integer multiple of the assumed part size, but both integer options lead to contradictions.
The proof does not depend on the specific numbers; any integer parts would lead to similar contradictions.

Fifth Proof: Pythagorean Theorem Refutes Indivisible Atom
Sanusi presents the first aspect of the fifth proof using the Pythagorean theorem.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Pythagorean theorem is used to demonstrate the impossibility of indivisible parts (atoms).

Core Idea
The hypotenuse of a right triangle proves atoms cannot exist.

The fifth proof employs the famous Pythagorean theorem (known as 'شكل العروس') to argue against atomism. By constructing a right triangle whose legs consist of two indivisible parts each, the hypotenuse becomes the irrational √8, which cannot be measured by any number of indivisible parts. This shows the logical inconsistency of the doctrine.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the passage begins with 'الحجة الخامسة' indicating a new proof.
2
Identify the Pythagorean theorem
The author invokes 'شكل العروس' (the Bride's Shape), which is the Pythagorean theorem. Understand that it relates the sides of a right triangle.
3
Understand the triangle construction
Imagine a right triangle where each leg is composed of two indivisible parts (atoms). The author says: 'we compose a line from two parts, and place upon one of these two parts another part' — so the legs are each two atoms long.
4
Calculate the hypotenuse
Using the theorem, the hypotenuse squared equals sum of squares of legs: 2^2 + 2^2 = 8. So the hypotenuse is √8, which is an irrational number.
The author says it is 'أصم' (deaf), meaning irrational.
5
Reflect on the contradiction
If atoms are indivisible, any length must be a whole number of atoms. But √8 cannot be expressed as a whole number. Therefore atomism is false.
The passage cuts off but the argument is complete for this aspect.

Same base, same parallels, same area — impossible for atoms.

Commentary
This is the fourth proof against atomism, based on Euclid's theorem (Book I) that parallelograms on the same base and between the same parallels have equal area. The author applies this to the atomic theory: suppose one surface (a square) is 10×10 = 100 square units; another surface, a parallelogram with the same base and between the same parallels, also has area 100. If surfaces are composed of atoms, then the number of atoms in each must be equal—100 each. But this leads to contradiction because the parallelogram could be extremely elongated yet still have area 100, implying an enormous number of atoms compressed into a thin strip, which is impossible if atoms are indivisible. An objection is raised: this reasoning seems to fail in the extreme case where one surface is one cubit square and the other is a line-like strip from east to west; how can they have equal area? The author answers by invoking Euclid's own demonstration: the inclined surface gains length but loses width proportionally, so the area is preserved only if the width is less than the base. The impossibility would occur only if the width equaled the base, which is not the case. Thus the objection is resolved, and the proof stands: atomism forces an impossible equality of atomic count in very different shapes, so atomism is false.

Polygon sides increase infinitely, so interior angles never reach two right angles, proving infinite divisibility.

The author begins by establishing the first of three geometrical proofs against the atom. This continuous expansion proves that the sum of two right angles is infinitely divisible.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer unit. The author presents a second objection (السؤال الثاني) to the proof of bodily composition offered by those who affirm composition (المثبتين). The objection argues that the proof only shows that unity and multiplicity are accidents (أعراض) subsisting in the body, not that the body's essence is composed. The objector distinguishes between the body's being one continuous thing and its being a compound of parts. Upon division, only the accidents of unity and multiplicity change; the body's continuity remains in each part. Thus the proof fails to establish composition in the essence. The author then notes that when he presented this objection, the proponents had no sufficient answer, but he found three possible counterarguments (وجوه ثلاثة). He expounds the first one: upon division, the original body is annihilated and two new bodies come into existence. This implies that corporeality can cease and be generated, which requires a substrate (مادة). He then breaks down this response into two premises. The first premise is that the original simple body is annihilated upon division; he proves this by noting that if the two resulting bodies had existed before division, the original would have been composite, contradicting the assumption of its simplicity. The second premise is that anything subject to generation and corruption requires matter; he briefly refers to his own book for its proof. The passage concludes with the end of the exposition of this first aspect and a lead-in to another possible objection.

The First Argument: Division Shows Body Requires Matter
Razi presents the first argument that body requires matter based on division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi argues that a body must have a material substrate because division shows it can cease and arise.

Core Idea
Division proves body is not self-subsistent—it depends on matter.

Razi presents a rigorous argument that a simple body, when divided, loses its identity and two new bodies emerge. This change shows that corporeality can pass away and be generated, which implies it needs a material substrate to receive these changes. Let's follow his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation, to grasp the overall argument. Notice the two-premise structure.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the two premises
The argument rests on two premises: (1) Division annihilates the original body and creates two new ones. (2) Anything that can be annihilated and originated must have a material substrate. Pinpoint these in the text.
3
Examine the proof of the first premise
Razi proves the first premise with a dilemma: the two bodies after division either existed before or not. If they existed, the original would be composite (contradicting its simplicity). So they are new, meaning the original body ceased.
This is a reductio ad absurdum argument.
4
Understand the proof of the second premise
The second premise is proved by noting that every originated thing is preceded by possibility (imkan al-huduth), which requires a substrate—the hyle (matter). This is a standard kalam argument.
Razi refers to another work for elaboration.
5
Reflect on the significance
Ask yourself: Why does this argument matter? It shows that body is not self-sufficient; it depends on a material substrate, which is crucial for arguments about the creation of the world and the nature of substance.
This connects to broader theological debates.

The Second Argument for Composition of Body and Its Refutation
Argument and first of several refutations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second argument for the composition of body from jismiyya and hayula and its refutation through the principle that a single thing can have opposing attributes under different conditions.

Core Idea
A single thing can have opposing properties under different conditions, refuting the composition argument.

This passage presents a second argument that attempts to prove the body is composed of two distinct principles: jismiyya (corporeality) which entails continuity, and hayula (hyle) which receives separation. After laying out the argument, the author immediately refutes it by showing that the inference is based on a false premise: that one thing cannot be both necessary for something and receptive to its opposite. Instead, opposing attributes can arise from different conditions.

Guided Path
1
Read the full argument
The argument claims that the simple body is one yet receptive to infinite potential divisions. Those divisions cannot be actualized. The sum implies that corporeality entails continuity, and since body also receives separation (opposite), these cannot belong to one thing, so body must be composed of jismiyya and hayula.
Notice the logical structure: premises leading to a conclusion of composition.
2
Identify the key inference
The critical step is: 'A single thing cannot be both necessary for something and receptive to its opposite.' This is the premise that the argument relies on. Mark it.
This premise is what the refutation targets.
3
Understand the refutation
The author rejects the premise by giving an analogy: nature in a natural place necessitates rest, but the same nature in a foreign place allows motion. Similarly, body by itself necessitates continuity, but with a divider it accepts separation. Thus one thing can have opposites conditionally.
The refutation does not deny the observable fact, only the inference drawn from it.

Commentary
This passage presents a second argument for the composition of body from two principles: corporeality (jismiyya) and hyle (hayula), and then refutes it. The argument, attributed to some people, begins by noting that a simple body is one thing yet is receptive to infinite potential divisions. Those infinite divisions cannot be actualized. The sum of these divisions implies that corporeality entails continuity—however small the body becomes, the remainder is continuous. Since continuity is a necessary concomitant of corporeality, and since body is also receptive to separation (its opposite), a single thing cannot be a necessary condition for one thing and receptive to its opposite. Therefore, body must be composed of two parts: one (jismiyya) entailing continuity, and the other (hayula) receptive to separation. The author then declares this argument weak and gives the first of several criticisms. The criticism is that it is not impossible for a single thing to have opposing attributes under different conditions. He gives the analogy of nature: a body in its natural place tends to rest, but when in a foreign place it tends to move—one substance receives both rest and motion depending on condition. Similarly, body by itself necessitates continuity, but in the presence of a divider it accepts separation. Thus, the argument's inference of composition is invalid because it ignores conditional factors.

Since categorical proofs (adilla qāṭiʿa) have established the atom, one must ignore probable judgments. The author notes that he has provided many examples from philosophical investigations. Then he addresses the geometrical objections based on the concepts of the circle and superposition. He admits that these objections, though remote from imagination, are remotely possible. Nevertheless, to safeguard the categorical proofs for the atom, one must accept those objections. This shows the author's commitment to rational certainty over probable speculation.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think that any objection, however remote, suffices to refute a proof.
Correction: The author insists that only categorical proofs are decisive; remote probabilities do not overturn them.

Categorical proofs for the indivisible atom are certain; merely probable geometrical objections do not undermine them.
