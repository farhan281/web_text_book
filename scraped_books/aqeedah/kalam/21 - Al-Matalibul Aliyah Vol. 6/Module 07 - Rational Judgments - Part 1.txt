Answer to the Fourth Objection: Motion and Indivisible Parts
Razi answers the objection that motion is transition not instantaneous presence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's refutation of the objection that motion is transition, not presence, and how it reinforces the concept of the indivisible atom.

Core Idea
Motion as transition does not refute the instantaneous present; it forces the indivisible atom.

This lesson examines a critical objection against the existence of indivisible particles (al-jawhar al-fard). The objector argues that motion is a transition between places, not a state of presence in a place, thus the earlier argument about present motion fails. Razi systematically dismantles this objection by exploring the logical possibilities during a state of transition and showing that all are necessarily false.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objector claims motion is transition, not presence. Grasp why this challenges the previous argument for indivisible atoms.
This objection targets the idea that instantaneous motion can be divided.
2
Follow Razi's three-case analysis
Razi examines three possibilities for the moving thing during transition: present in a specific place, present in a non-specific place, or not in any place. Notice how each leads to absurdity.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
3
See the conclusion
All possibilities are necessarily false, so the objection fails. Understand how this supports the existence of indivisible atoms.
Razi appeals to necessary knowledge (al-ilm al-daruri).

In that present thing, two parts occur such that one is anterior to the other in time or not possible. If this type of division occurs, then the composite is not existent. Our discussion concerns what is existent. This is a contradiction. And if this type of division is impossible, then the intended is existent, because the extent of distance moved over by that extent of motion—if it is divisible, then the motion to its half is anterior in time to the motion from its half to its end; then at that point two parts are posited in that extent of motion such that one is anterior to the other in time. But we have assumed that it is not so. This is a contradiction. And if that extent of distance is not susceptible to division, then that is the indivisible atom. So it is established that our purpose in this argument is fulfilled by showing that the present extent of motion does not admit division such that one of the two parts is anterior to the other. And regarding the fourth question: which is his statement: (Motion is transition from place to place, and it is not defined as being present in a place. So we say: In the state of his being in transition from place to place, either he is present in a specific place—and that invalidates your statement that his being in motion contradicts his being present in a specific place—or he is present in a non-specific place, or it is said that he is not present in any of the places. And knowledge of the corruption of each of these two categories is necessary knowledge. So the objection is removed. And God knows best.)

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer unit. It begins by continuing the response to the fourth objection about motion. The argument proceeds by considering the present moment of motion: if it has two parts with one temporally prior, the whole is not instantaneous (contradiction). If not divisible, the distance is composed of indivisible atoms. Then it directly answers the objection: the objector claims motion is transition, not presence. The response considers three possibilities during transition: the moving thing is present in a specific place (contradicts motion), present in a non-specific place, or not in any place. All are necessarily false, so the objection fails.

Razi's Second Proof: The Line is Composed of Indivisible Points
Razi proves that a line is composed of indivisible points through the movement of a point.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi's second proof uses the motion of a point to demonstrate that a line must be composed of indivisible atomic parts (jawhar fard).

Core Idea
A moving point must touch every part of a line, proving the line is made of points.

Razi presents a geometric argument to establish the existence of atoms. By analyzing how a cube's corner point traces a line, he shows that the only way to avoid a logical absurdity (the 'leap') is for the line to be composed of indivisible points. This proof is a cornerstone of Islamic atomism.

Guided Path
1
Understand the cube model
Visualize a cube with six finite surfaces, each surface bounded by four finite lines, each line ending in two points. When the cube is dragged, its corner point traces a line.
The point is the indivisible atom (jawhar fard) in motion.
2
Follow the logical deduction
Notice that the dragged point must meet all of the traced line – if it skipped any part, that would constitute a 'leap' (ṭafrah), which is impossible. Therefore, the point meets every part of the line.
Razi assumes the falsehood of the leap without proof here.
3
Apply the principle: point meets only point
Since a point can only contact another point, the entire line must be composed of points – nothing else can be contacted. Thus, the line is an aggregate of indivisible points.
This step links contact to composition.
4
Consider the finite/infinite dilemma
The points are either finite or infinite. If infinite, they cannot be traversed in finite time; if finite, the desired conclusion (atomism) is established. Either way, the argument holds.
This strengthens the proof by eliminating an alternative.

الحجة الثانية في بيان أن الحركة عبارة عن حصولات متعاقبة ، في أحياز متلاصقة : أن نقول : الجسم المكعب يحيط به سطوح ستة متناهية ، وكل واحد من تلك السطوح يحيط به خطوط أربعة متناهية ، وكل واحد من تلك الخطوط له طرفان ، هما نقطتان . فإذا انجر ذلك المكعب على سطح ، فقد انجرت تلك النقطة . فنقول : إن تلك النقطة لما انجرت على ذلك السطح إلى أخره : خط ، لا محالة . إذا ثبت هذا ، فنقول : إن تلك النقطة ، لقيت جميع ذلك الخط . لأنها لو لقيت بعض أجزاء ذلك الخط ، ثم لقيت جزءاً آخر ، يباين الجزء الأول من غير أن يمر بما بينهما . فحينئذ يلزم القول بالطفرة . وإنه باطل فثبت : أن تلك النقطة لقيت جميع ذلك الخط . ومعلوم أن النقطة لا تلاقي إلا النقطة . فلما ثبت أن تلك النقطة لقيت كل الخط ، وثبت : أن النقطة لا تلاقي إلا النقطة ، لزم القطع بأن ذلك الخط متألف من النقط المتشافعة المتلاصقة . وذلك يوجب تألف الخط من النقط التي لا تتجزا وذلك يفيد المطلوب. ثم نقول : تلك النقط إن كانت غير متناهية ، قطعها في زمان متناهي. وإن كانت متناهية العدد فذلك هو المطلوب فيمتنع.
The second proof in explanation that motion is successive obtainings in contiguous places: we say: The cube is surrounded by six finite surfaces, and each of those surfaces is surrounded by four finite lines, and each of those lines has two ends, which are two points. So when that cube is dragged on a surface, that point is dragged. We say: That point, when dragged on that surface to its end, inevitably traces a line. Once this is established, we say: That point met that entire line. Because if it had met only some parts of that line, then met another part that is distinct from the first without passing through what is between them, then at that point it would be necessary to affirm the leap (ṭafrah), which is false. So it is established that that point met all of that line. And it is known that a point only meets a point. Thus, when it is established that that point met all the line, and it is established that a point only meets a point, it is necessarily concluded that that line is composed of mutually contiguous points. That necessitates the line being composed of points that are not further divisible, and that yields the desired conclusion. Then we say: Those points, if they are infinite, then it is impossible for them to be traversed in a finite time; and if they are finite in number, that is the desired conclusion, so it is impossible.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the existence of atomic parts (jawhar fard). The argument uses a geometric model: a cube dragged on a surface causes one of its corner points to trace a line. Since the point must contact every part of that line to avoid a leap (ṭafrah), and a point only contacts a point, the line must be composed of points. These points are either finite or infinite. If infinite, traversing them in finite time is impossible; if finite, the desired conclusion is reached. The proof thus establishes that the line is made of indivisible atoms.

Refinement of the Second Proof: Instantaneous Contact
Razi explains how to present the proof without need for motion and time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi refines the second proof for the indivisible atom by focusing on the instantaneous nature of contact and separation, without requiring motion or time.

Core Idea
Contact between points is instantaneous, not gradual.

Razi presents a refined version of the proof for the indivisible atom. He argues that the contact and separation of points happen instantly, which avoids the need to invoke motion or time. This strengthens the case for atomism by focusing purely on the geometry of points.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context
Recall that the second proof for the indivisible atom (al-jawhar al-fard) originally involved motion and time. Razi now shows a more elegant formulation that dispenses with those concepts.
This refinement makes the proof more fundamental.
2
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that Razi begins by stating the aim: a proof without needing motion and time.
3
Identify the key claims
Razi claims that contact between a moving point and a point on the distance occurs instantaneously (دفعة). Similarly, the removal of that contact is instantaneous. Identify these two parallel statements.
The reasoning is that gradual contact would divide the point, which is impossible.
4
Follow the argument about intermediates
After the first contact ends, a new contact begins with another point. Razi argues that no intermediate can exist between these two points, because any intermediate would either be a point (acceptable) or divisible (impossible).
This supports the conclusion that space is composed of indivisible points.
5
Grasp the overall conclusion
Understand that this refined proof establishes the atom (جوهر فرد) without appealing to motion or time, using only the instantaneous contact and separation of points.
This is a methodological improvement.

This strengthens the claim that the proof for the atom is independent of considerations of motion and time.

Proof That Change in Quality Occurs in Indivisible Instants
The author proves change requires discrete instants via a logical dilemma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a logical dilemma about blackening a body demonstrates that all change, including change in quality and motion in place, must occur in discrete indivisible instants.

Core Idea
Change cannot be gradual; it must happen in indivisible instants.

This passage presents a key kalam proof that change in quality—like a body turning black—cannot occur gradually. The author uses a logical dilemma to show that a single attribute can only come into existence or cease at a single, indivisible moment. This conclusion leads to the doctrine of the succession of instants, which then applies to motion in place as well. Let's work through the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic once, then the translation. Notice the logical structure: a series of dilemmas.
The argument uses the example of blackness (sawād).
2
Understand the initial dilemma
The author asks: when the body begins to blacken, does any blackness occur? If no blackness occurs, it remains unchanged. If some occurs, does any remain? Work through the possibilities.
This is a classic 'if-then' logical analysis.
3
Follow the impossibility of gradual existence
If a part of blackness occurs and another part remains, then the thing that occurred is either identical to what remains (contradiction) or different (then each exists wholly). This shows that a single thing cannot exist or cease gradually.
The key is the impossibility of one thing being both existent and non-existent.
4
Connect to instants and motion
From the impossibility of gradual change, the author concludes that each attribute exists only for a single instant, necessitating a succession of instants. This then applies to motion in place.
See how the argument builds from quality to motion.

ولتقرر هذا الكلام في الكيف، ليظهر وجه الكلام فيه: فنقول: الجسم في أول ما يأخذ في الاسوداد. فهل حصل فيه شيء من السواد، أو لم يحصل؟ فإن لم يحصل، فهو بعد باقي على العدم الأصلي. وإن حصل فيه شيء، فهل بقي من ذلك السواد شيء، أو لم يبق؟ فإن تم ولم يبق منه شيء، فقد كان ذلك الحدوث والحصول واقعاً دفعة. وإن بقي منه شيء، فالذي وجد، إن كان عين الذي بقى، فالشيء الواحد يصدق عليه: أنه وجد، وأنه بعد لم يوجد. وذلك محال. وإن كان غيره، فحينئذ الذي وجد، فقد وجد بتمامه، والذي بعد لم يوجد، فهو معدوم بتمامه. وذلك يدل على أن الشيء الواحد، لا يوجد ولا يعدم، إلا دفعة واحدة. إذا ثبت هذا، فحينئذ يلزم أن يقال: إن ذلك التغير والتبدل والحركة: عبارة عن صفات متعاقبة متوالية، كل واحد منها لا يوجد إلا أناً واحداً. وذلك يوجب القول بتتالي الآنات. ومتى ثبت هذا، وجب أن تكون الحركة في الأين: عبارة عن حصولات متعاقبة، غير منقسمة. وذلك هو المطلوب.
And to confirm this statement regarding quality, so that the aspect of the statement in it becomes clear: We say: The body at the first moment it begins to blacken. Did any part of blackness occur in it, or did it not occur? If it did not occur, then it remains in its original non-existence. And if some part occurred in it, then did any of that blackness remain, or did it not remain? If it completed and nothing remained of it, then that occurrence and attainment happened at once. And if something remained of it, then that which came into existence, if it is identical to that which remained, then the one thing is described as having come into existence and as not yet having come into existence. That is impossible. And if it is other than it, then that which came into existence has come into existence in its entirety, and that which has not yet come into existence is entirely non-existent. That indicates that a single thing does not come into existence nor cease to exist except at a single moment. If this is established, then it follows that it must be said that that change, alteration, and motion are expressions of successive, consecutive attributes, each of which does not exist except for a single instant. That necessitates the assertion of the succession of instants. And when this is established, it becomes necessary that motion in place (al-ayn) is an expression of successive, indivisible occurrences. That is what is sought.

Commentary
This passage is a proof. The author begins by establishing the argument in the context of quality (al-kayf), specifically the example of a body becoming black. He poses a dilemma: when a body first begins to blacken, either some blackness occurs or it does not. If no blackness occurs, the body remains in its original state of non-existence of blackness. If some blackness occurs, then it either remains or does not. If it does not remain, the change occurred instantaneously. If some remains, then the part that came into existence is either identical to the remainder (impossible, as one thing cannot both have come and not come) or different, in which case the part that came exists wholly and the part that has not yet come is wholly non-existent. This shows that a single thing can only come into or cease to exist at one moment. From this, the author concludes that change, alteration, and motion consist of successive attributes, each existing only for an instant. This necessitates the succession of instants (tatālī al-ānāt). Finally, he extends this to motion in place (al-ayn), which must be a succession of indivisible occurrences. The proof follows the author's own order: a dilemma, its analysis, and the conclusion.

The second proof: the point is the extremity of a line. Thus, the point must be indivisible. This entire proof establishes the existence of the indivisible atom by showing that a point, which exists and has position, is necessarily indivisible, and therefore the atom (which is like a point) exists.

The First Proof: Finite Body and Infinite Parts

Core Idea
Infinite divisibility would imply infinite actual parts, which is impossible.

Razi begins his demonstration with a clear conditional proof. He assumes that a finite body could be divided infinitely, then shows this would force the body to have infinite actual parts — something all philosophers agree is absurd. By rejecting the consequent, the original assumption is refuted. This lesson walks through each logical step, helping you see why infinite divisibility is rationally impossible.

Core Idea
Al-Nazzam avoids infinite regress by proposing motion can 'leap' over intermediate points.

In this exchange, Abu al-Hudhayl uses the half-distance argument to challenge atomism. Al-Nazzam replies that the mover does not need to traverse every part of the distance—it can leap. He then turns the tables, arguing that the atomist's own view of the indivisible atom leads to absurdities, so they cannot reject his leap theory as too implausible. This passage captures a key moment in the debate over atomism and infinite divisibility.

The First Proof Against Infinite Composition
Razi presents the first proof against infinite composition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's first proof that a finite body cannot have infinite parts, and why the argument leads to atomism.

Core Idea
Traversing infinite parts would require infinite time; finite motion proves finite parts.

Razi presents a crisp logical proof that a finite body cannot be composed of actually infinite parts. The argument uses a simple observation about motion across a distance. By following the chain of necessary conditions, Razi shows that infinite composition leads to a contradiction with our experience. This proof is foundational for the Islamic atomist position.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to grasp the argument's flow.
Notice that the proof has a clear conditional structure: if infinite parts, then infinite time; but finite time, so finite parts.
2
Analyze the logical chain
Identify the key premises: to reach the end, one must reach the half; to reach the half, one must reach its half, ad infinitum. This creates an infinite regress of necessary conditions.
This is a classic 'dichotomy' paradox, similar to Zeno's.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
Observe that Razi concludes the distance must have finite parts since we observe finite traversal times. Connect this to the broader kalām argument for atomism (al-jawhar al-fard).

الحجة الأولى : إنه من المحال أن ينتهي المتحرك من أول المسافة إلى آخرها ، إلا إذا وصل إلى نصفها. ومن المحال أن يصل إلى نصفها ، إلا إذا وصل إلى نصف نصفها . فلو كانت المسافة مركبة من أجزاء غير متناهية ، لوجب أن يمتنع الوصول من أولها إلى آخرها، إلا في زمان غير متناه . وحيث لم يكن الأمر كذلك، علمنا : أن المسافة مركبة من أجزاء متناهية .
The first proof: It is impossible for a moving body to reach from the beginning of a distance to its end unless it reaches its half. And it is impossible for it to reach its half unless it reaches the half of its half. So if the distance were composed of infinite parts, it would be necessary that reaching from its beginning to its end would be impossible except in infinite time. And since the matter is not so, we know that the distance is composed of finite parts.
Source pages p. 70

Commentary
This passage is a proof (ḥujja) that a finite body cannot be composed of parts that are actually infinite. The argument is based on the impossibility of traversing an infinite distance in finite time. The author, Fakhr al-Dīn al-Rāzī, presents the reasoning: to traverse a distance, one must first reach its midpoint, and before that, the midpoint of that midpoint, ad infinitum. If the number of parts were infinite, then traversing them would require passing through infinitely many points, which would take infinite time. Since we observe that bodies do traverse finite distances in finite time, it follows that the distance must be composed of a finite number of parts. This proof is a classic argument in kalām against infinite regress in spatial extension, often used to defend atomism (al-jawhar al-fard). The conclusion is that the distance is composed of finite parts, and by implication, the body itself is ultimately composed of finite indivisible parts.

Correction: The proof only requires that the distance is potentially divisible infinitely, not that it is actually divided. The argument shows that if there were actually infinite parts, traversal would be impossible, but since traversal occurs, there cannot be actual infinite parts.

Second Objection: Infinite Parts in Time and Motion
An objector asks why not allow infinite parts in both body and time simultaneously.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second objection raised against the first proof, which challenges the assumption that finite bodies cannot have infinite parts by extending the same reasoning to time and motion.

Core Idea
If time has infinite instants, motion through infinite spatial parts becomes possible.

In this passage, an objector presents a clever parallel to the first proof. The first proof argued that a finite body cannot be composed of infinite spatial parts because it cannot be traversed in finite time. The objector turns the argument around: what if both space and time are infinitely divisible? This objection challenges the claim of necessary knowledge (ḍarūra) and forces the defender to explain why the same logic does not apply to time and motion.

Commentary
This passage presents a second objection (suʾāl thānī) against the first proof. The objector raises a parallel: the first proof assumed that an infinite number of spatial parts cannot be traversed in finite time. But what if both time and space are composed of an infinite number of parts? Specifically, if the body has infinitely many spatial parts, and time also has infinitely many instants (aanaat), then the mover could traverse the infinite spatial parts in the infinite instants. The objector challenges the claim that it is self-evident (ḍarūrī) that between two ends there cannot be an infinite number of actual parts. They argue: if you insist that this is self-evident, then apply it only to body (space), not to time or motion. If you accept it for body because of some special reason, then you must also apply the same reasoning to time and motion—but you cannot, because time and motion are also finite yet seemingly have infinite potential divisibility. Thus, the objection seeks to undermine the proof by showing that it either leads to an infinite regress in time as well, or the claim of self-evidence is not uniformly applicable. The Intuitive Impossibility of Leaping
Razi gives the first answer: leaping is intuitively known to be false.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's first argument against leaping (ṭafra) and the role of intuitive knowledge (badīha) in rejecting it.

Core Idea
Leaping is intuitively impossible: a body cannot skip intervening space.

Razi begins his response to al-Nazzam's concept of leaping by appealing to the most basic form of knowledge: intuition. He asserts that the very idea of a body moving from one place to a distant one without passing through the intermediate space is self-evidently false. This lesson explores that argument and what it means to claim something is known by badīha.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice how concise and direct Razi's statement is. He presents his first answer (al-wajh al-awwal) in a single sentence.
2
Understand 'intuitive impossibility'
Razi claims that the impossibility of leaping is known by badīha (intuition/self-evidence). Reflect on what that means: it is a truth that anyone can recognize immediately, without needing proof. Can you think of other self-evident truths?
Intuitive knowledge is considered certain and foundational in Islamic theology.
3
Analyze the key elements of leaping
Identify the three components in Razi's description: the moving body (mutaharrik), transferring to a distant place, without passing through the intermediate space, while remaining the same body throughout. Ask yourself why each element is necessary for the argument.
The phrase 'while it remains the same in all states' rules out the idea of disappearance and reappearance.
4
Connect to the broader debate
Razi's first response is an appeal to common sense. Consider why al-Nazzam might have proposed leaping and why this intuitive argument is a strong counter. This sets up further responses (to be covered in later lessons).

الأول : إن كون المتحرك منتقلاً من مكان إلى مكان بعيد عنه ، من غير أن يمر بما بينهما ، مع كونه باقياً في الأحوال كلها : معلوم الفساد بالبديهة

Commentary
This passage is the first answer (al-wajh al-awwal) from Razi against al-Nazzam's objection of leaping. It asserts that the idea of a body moving from one point to a non-adjacent point without traversing the intervening space is self-evidently false. This is an appeal to necessary knowledge (ḍarūra) or intuition (badīha): every person intuitively knows that to go from point A to point C, you must pass through point B if it lies between them. The phrase 'while it remains the same in all states' (مع كونه باقياً في الأحوال كلها) emphasizes that this is a single continuous motion, not a disappearance and reappearance. Thus, the concept of ṭafra is rejected as contrary to immediate rational evidence.
