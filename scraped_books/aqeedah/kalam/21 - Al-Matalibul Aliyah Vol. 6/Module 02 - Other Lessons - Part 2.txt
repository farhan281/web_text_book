Refutation of the Application Principle
Argues against the possibility of applying one line or surface to another.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument against the principle of applying one magnitude to another (tatbiq) and why it leads to absurd consequences.

Core Idea
Applying one line to another leads to impossibility—either division or interpenetration with no distinction.

This passage presents a sophisticated proof against the principle of application (tatbiq), which was used to argue for atomism. The author acknowledges its difficulty but constructs a dilemma showing that any attempt to apply one magnitude to another results in logical absurdities. Understanding this argument is crucial for grasping the foundations of Islamic atomism and the debate on indivisible parts.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Notice the dense logical structure and technical terms.
Pay special attention to the conditional statements and the final conclusion.
2
Identify the initial dilemma
The author presents two possibilities when applying a line to another: partial meeting or whole meeting. Understand why both lead to problems.
Partial meeting requires division of the line, which is impossible for an indivisible atom.
3
Examine the case of whole meeting
Whole meeting means one line fully penetrates the other, leading to interpenetration. The author then asks whether distinction remains or not.
This is the core of the argument against application.
4
Understand why distinction is impossible
Distinction cannot occur by essence (since both lines share the same essence) nor by accidents (since any accident would be shared due to interpenetration).
5
Grasp the final conclusion
Without distinction, either the two lines become one or both cease to exist—both absurd. Therefore, the principle of application is false.
This refutation upholds the indivisibility of atoms.

The second principle remains: application. We say: This principle is difficult to challenge, and if its soundness is established, the soundness of the arguments derived from it is established. What can be said about it, while acknowledging that it is extremely difficult, is: we do not concede the possibility of applying a line to a line, or a surface to a surface. Inductive Proof: Triangle, Square, and Pentagon
Induction for triangle, square, and pentagon.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the sum of exterior angles for a triangle, square, and pentagon is shown to be four right angles through induction.

Core Idea
The sum of exterior angles is constant: four right angles, proven by examples.

The author begins by stating the proposition and then offers induction as the first proof. By examining specific polygons—triangle, square, pentagon—he demonstrates that the exterior angles sum to four right angles in each case, suggesting it holds for all polygons.

General Deductive Proof for All Polygons
General deductive proof from the demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the general deductive proof that the sum of exterior angles of any polygon is four right angles.

Core Idea
The general proof uses algebra: exterior angles = 2n - 2(n-2) = 4.

After induction, the author presents a rigorous deductive proof. He shows that for any polygon with n sides, the total of interior and exterior angles is 2n right angles, and interior angles are 2(n-2) right angles, so exterior sum is always 4.

Guided Path

Read the general proof
Read the Arabic passage for the demonstration (البرهان).

Understand the per-side relation

Relate interior angles to triangles

Know that these arguments are strong and there is no way to repel them, except that we say: Euclid built the theories that he set forth in his book on two principles: one, the proof of the circle; and the other, the application of one magnitude to another. That is because the analysis of most of the figures of Book I ends at the fourth figure, and the proof of this figure is not based on the circle but on application. If you understand this, we say: As for the [proof of the] circle, we have shown that their proofs for it are extremely weak, and our proofs against it are extremely strong, so reliance on that principle has fallen. There remains the second principle, which is application. We say: This principle is difficult to impugn. And if its correctness is established, the validity of the proofs that branch from it is established. What can be said about it, while acknowledging that it is extremely difficult, is: We do not accept the possibility of applying a line to a line or a surface to a surface. The proof for this is: if we apply one line to another line, then either it meets it partially or entirely. The first requires the division of the line in length, which is impossible. The second requires that one of the two lines be distinct [in such a way that the indication points to the other], and that is impossible. Because if this penetration occurs, then either there remains distinction or not. The first is false, because distinction cannot occur by the essence itself, since the two lines share in the entire essence; nor by the necessary concomitants of the essence, because the necessary concomitants of the essence are common among the individuals of the essence, and what is common cannot be a cause of distinction; nor by accidental properties, because every accident supposed to be an accident of one of them must also be an accident of the other, because since they have interpenetrated and are not distinguished from each other in any way, every accident that occurs will have the same relation to one as to the other, so that accident is common, and what is common cannot be a cause of distinction. Thus it is established that the distinction of one line from the other is impossible in reality. And if distinction does not remain, then either the two become one, which is impossible, or they are together, which is even more impossible. Thus it is established that the doctrine of application leads to these false consequences, so the doctrine must be false.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of Euclid's method. The author acknowledges the strength of the previous geometrical arguments against the atom but notes that Euclid's work rests on two foundations: the proof of the circle and the principle of application (تطبيق). The author quickly dismisses the circle proof as weak and relies on his own refutation. The main focus is on the application principle. The author argues that applying one line to another is impossible because it leads to contradictions: either partial meeting requires division of the line (which is impossible if the line is composed of indivisible atoms), or complete meeting leads to loss of distinction between the two lines, which would require either unification (impossible) or coexistence without distinction (also impossible). Through a detailed analysis of essence, concomitants, and accidents, the author concludes that no distinction can be maintained, hence application is invalid. Therefore, the Euclidean arguments based on application are also invalid.

Commentary
This passage is a personal supplication (duʿā') where the author invokes God by various attributes and sacred entities. He asks by the gleams of divine benevolence, by lights deposited in stars, by favors given to certain saints, by pure souls, by angels and spirits ruling in various realms, by the mysteries of creation, and by the fact that God answers prayers. The request culminates in asking for deliverance from blameworthy morals (akhlāq dhamīma) and blameworthy beliefs (ʿaqā'id dhamīma), and for ease in worldly and religious affairs. This prayer demonstrates the author's piety and his concern for correct belief and character.
