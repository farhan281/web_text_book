Three Additional Views on Body Composition
The author lists the remaining three views: infinite parts, infinite divisibility, and finite divisibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand three alternative views on the composition of bodies: infinite parts, infinite divisibility, and finite divisibility.

Core Idea
Three more schools offer distinct answers to how bodies are put together.

The author has already presented the mutakallimun's view that bodies are composed of finite indivisible parts (atoms). Now he turns to three other positions: the view of al-Nazzam, that of most philosophers, and a unique opinion from al-Shahrastani. Each offers a different answer to the question: what is the ultimate structure of a physical body?

Razi's Water Division Dilemma Against Simple Bodies
Razi refutes the philosophers' simple body via water division dilemma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi refutes the philosophers' claim that simple bodies are one in themselves by showing that their definition of 'separating' leads to an inescapable dilemma.

Core Idea
The simple body dilemma: division either reveals pre-existing parts or annihilates the whole.

This passage presents Razi's sharp critique of the philosophers' concept of the simple body. He challenges their definition of 'separating' and uses the example of water to expose a logical dilemma that forces us to reject their position. Through this, we see the power of reductio ad absurdum in kalam arguments.

Guided Path
1
Understand the philosophers' claim
Read the passage where the philosophers assert that the simple body is one in itself and in sensation, and their definition of 'separating' as producing multiplicity rather than moving parts apart.
This sets up the target of the refutation.
2
Follow the water division scenario
Imagine a single unit of water that the philosophers consider simple and non-composite. Then consider dividing it into two parts.
The division is the key action that tests the philosophers' definition.
3
Examine the first horn of the dilemma
If the two parts existed before division, then the water was actually composite, contradicting the claim of simplicity.
This horn shows the philosophers' view false if they admit pre-existence.
4
Examine the second horn
If the two parts did not exist before division, then the division annihilates the original water and creates new water, leading to absurd consequences like dipping a finger in the sea annihilating the entire sea.
This horn shows an even more far-fetched result than other unpopular views.
5
See the overall conclusion
The author concludes that all three possible opinions (atomism, infinite parts, philosophers' simplicity) lead to great impossibilities, so none can be accepted.
This establishes the impasse.

Commentary

The author concludes that all three views (atomic, infinite parts, philosophers' simplicity) lead to great absurdities, proving that none can be sustained.

Refuting the Democritean Objection to Infinite Division
Author answers objection and defends his proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author defends his proof against the objection that Democritean atomism escapes the problem of infinite actual division.

Core Idea
Atomism does not escape the problem of infinite regress.

In this passage, al-Sanusi addresses a critical objection: that his proof against infinite regress only refutes Aristotle and Avicenna, not Democritus, who posits indivisible atoms. The author clarifies that his argument is based on the collective possibility of all potential divisions, not on any particular theory. He then shows that even atomism collapses when imaginary divisibility entails actual divisibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic text and translation of the objection. Note that the objector claims Democritus' theory avoids the problem because atoms are only imaginably divisible, not actually divisible.
The objector is trying to limit the scope of the author's proof.
2
Understand the author's general response
The author says his proof does not depend on the philosophers' specific claims. Instead, it rests on the principle that each potential division is possible and non-conflicting, so the entire set is possible as a collective.
This is the key logical move.
3
See the objection applies to all philosophers
The author argues that whether a philosopher affirms or denies actual infinite division, the collective possibility of all potential divisions implies an actual infinite. So the objection is universal.
4
Consider the point about divine power and infinite numbers
The author addresses a potential counter: if God can create infinite numbers (like rational souls), then the objection is not impossible. This shows the argument is consistent.
This is a subsidiary point to strengthen the response.
5
Note the promise of a future proof
The author ends by stating he will prove that imaginary divisibility implies actual divisibility, which will directly refute Democritus.
This sets up the next logical step in the overall discussion.

Commentary

Refuting Atomism: The Impossibility of a Circle from Atoms
First argument against atomism: a line of atoms cannot form a circle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the existence of circles and spheres refutes the doctrine of indivisible atoms by showing that a line of such atoms cannot form a circle without contradiction.

Core Idea
If atoms were indivisible, circles could not exist — yet they do.

This passage presents a geometric argument against the atomist doctrine of the indivisible particle (al-jawhar al-fard). The author builds on the prior establishment of the reality of circles and spheres to demonstrate that atomism leads to a logical impossibility. As you read, follow the reasoning step by step: assuming a line of atoms, why can it not be bent into a circle?

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing, then read the translation. Get a feel for the overall argument.
The argument is dense; don't worry if you don't grasp every detail immediately.
2
Identify the key assumption
The argument assumes a straight line composed of indivisible parts (atoms). This is the starting point for the proof.
Indivisible parts are the building blocks of atomism.
3
Follow the rotation argument
Consider rotating that line to form a circle. The author shows that interiors must meet, then exteriors also meet, leading to equality of interior and exterior areas.
This is the core of the first argument: a contradiction in area equality.
4
Understand the layering argument
Adding an outer ring of atoms leads to a chain of equalities that ultimately forces the exterior of the outer circle to equal its interior, which is impossible.
The argument is cut off, but the conclusion is that the initial assumption (atoms) is false.

Commentary
This passage begins the second station of the overall argument: refuting the doctrine of the indivisible atom (al-jawhar al-fard) based on the prior establishment of the circle and sphere. The author states that since circles and spheres are true, atomism is false. He presents several arguments, beginning with the first. Suppose a straight line is composed of indivisible parts (atomic parts that cannot be divided). Such a line cannot be bent into a circle because if it were, the interiors of the atoms forming the circle would meet inside. Then the exteriors of those atoms would also meet (since they are all touching). Consequently, the area inside the circle would equal the area outside the circle (if the atoms are the same size). Now place another layer of atoms around it to form an enclosing circle. The interiors of the enclosing circle coincide with the exteriors of the inner circle. Since the exteriors of the inner atoms meet, the interiors of the enclosing atoms must also meet, and then their exteriors also meet. This leads to a regress where the exterior of the enclosing circle equals its interior, which is a contradiction or impossibility. The argument is cut off, but the clear conclusion is that a line of indivisible parts cannot form a circle, yet circles exist, so atomism is false.

Second Objection: Infinite Souls Become Finite
Applies the same argument to infinite souls, forcing them to be finite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the argument of increase and decrease is applied to the specific case of rational souls to refute the claim that they are infinite.

Core Idea
An infinite number of souls contradicts the observable fact that souls increase over time.

This lesson presents the second objection from a kalam text against the philosopher's claim that rational souls are infinite. The objection uses a simple but powerful argument based on the observable increase in the number of souls over time. By comparing the number of souls from a hundred years ago to today, the argument forces the conclusion that the total number of souls must be finite, thus contradicting the claim of infinity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the philosophers' claim
Read the passage and note that philosophers assert rational souls are infinite in number. This is the target of the objection.
The claim is stated at the beginning: 'النفوس الناطقة ... لا نهاية لها'.
2
Apply the argument of increase and decrease
See how the argument is applied: the number of souls from 100 years ago is less than the current number by a finite amount. This difference is the 'increase' (الزيادة) and 'decrease' (النقصان).
The passage explicitly calculates the difference: 'بمقدار العدد الذي حدث من النفوس'.
3
Analyze the logical possibilities
Two cases are considered: if the smaller total equals the larger total, that's impossible. If it is less, then the smaller total must be finite because it differs from the larger by a finite amount.
Focus on the conditional reasoning: 'إن كان مثل عدد الجملة الزائدة ... وإن كان أقل'.
4
Draw the conclusion
Since the increase is finite, the smaller total is finite, and thus the larger total (current souls) is also finite. This contradicts the philosophers' claim of infinity.
The objection concludes that the total is finite 'مع أنها عند الحكماء غير متناهية'.

The second objection: Indeed, according to the philosophers, the rational souls separate from bodies are infinite. Yet the argument of increase and decrease applies to them. Commentary
This passage presents the second objection against the proof from application. It targets the philosophers' claim that rational souls are infinite in number. The objection applies the argument of increase and decrease: the number of souls existing a hundred years ago is less than the number now by a finite amount. If the smaller total were equal to the larger, that would be absurd. If it is smaller, then it must be finite, since it differs from the larger by a finite amount. Therefore the total number of souls would be finite, contradicting the philosophers' position. The objection highlights a contradiction in applying the proof to actual infinite series.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think that even if there is a difference in number, an infinite set could still be infinite and the difference is just an addition of infinite elements.
Correction: The argument assumes that the difference (increase) is finite and that the smaller total differs from the larger by that finite amount.
