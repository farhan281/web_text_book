The Four Doctrines on the Composition of Bodies
Enumerates the four views on body composition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four major philosophical views on the composition of bodies and their key differences.

Core Idea
Four views, one body: are its parts finite, infinite, or none at all?

This passage presents a concise survey of the four possible positions on how a body is composed. After explaining the first view (finite indivisible parts) in the previous section, the text now lists the remaining three, attributed to different schools and thinkers. Understanding these views is essential for grasping later arguments about causality and divine transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the four views
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. As you read, note that the text lists four distinct views, each introduced with 'As for the...'. Identify the core claim of each view.
The first view is briefly restated; it was explained in the previous lesson.
2
Note the proponents of each view
Pay attention to which thinker or school is associated with each view. For example, the second view is attributed to al-Nazzam and some ancient philosophers.
The fourth view is uniquely attributed to al-Shahrastani.
3
Compare the third and fourth views
Both views describe a 'simple body' that is one in itself and in sensation. The difference lies in the nature of its divisibility: the third view says divisions are infinite (potential), while the fourth says they are finite.
This distinction is crucial for theological debates about the nature of creation.
4
Reflect on why these distinctions matter
Ask yourself: why would a theologian or philosopher care about whether a body is composed of finite or infinite parts? How might these views relate to arguments for the existence of God or the nature of causality?
Later lessons will build on these concepts.

The Philosophers' Three Causes of Actual Division in a Body
Razi reports and analyzes the three causes of body division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three causes of actual division in a body according to the philosophers, and how all three ultimately reduce to the difference of accidents.

Core Idea
Division becomes actual only when some accident distinguishes parts.

This passage from Imam al-Razi reports the philosophers' agreement that a simple body is potentially divisible, but actual division requires a specific cause. Razi enumerates three such causes—cutting, difference of accidents, and estimation—then traces them back to a single principle: the difference of accidents. Understanding this classification is key to grasping the philosophical theory of composition and division.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage carefully, then the translation. Notice the structure: an opening agreement, a list of three causes, and a closing investigation.
Pay attention to the terms for potentiality (قوة) and actuality (فعل).
2
Identify the three causes
Find and list the three causes of actual division: cutting, difference of accidents (real or relational), and estimation with indication.
Consider how each cause introduces a distinction between parts.
3
Follow the unifying argument
Trace Razi's investigation: he shows that in each cause, the underlying reason is that the two parts have different accidents (spatial location, attribute, or mental indication).
The conclusion is that multiplicity always stems from difference of accidents.
4
Reflect on the philosophical implication
Think about why this matters: if a body had no accidental difference, it would remain one undivided entity.
This connects to later discussions on the nature of matter and form.

In cutting, it's location difference; in accidents, it's obvious; in estimation, it's the relational accident of being indicated. Thus the conclusion is that, on the philosophers' view, multiplicity is caused by difference in accidents.

All multiplicity in bodies stems from difference of attributes—even cutting and estimation.

Divisions by Estimation Are Infinite
States that divisions by estimation are infinite with a brief proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why philosophers agree that mental divisions of a body are infinite and how the proof relies on the inability to reach a final limit of smallness without distinguishing sides.

Core Idea
No matter how small a body is, you can always imagine smaller.

After establishing the three causes of division, the author turns to the first: divisions by estimation and supposition (mental division). Philosophers universally agree that such divisions are infinite. This short passage provides a concise proof: any smallness limit can be surpassed by distinguishing sides. Let's examine the argument.

The proof: a body never reaches a limit of smallness except that one side becomes distinguished from the other. This means at any point, we can mentally separate sides, allowing further division without bound.

This infinite divisibility is potential (in the mind), not actual. The body is not composed of infinitely many parts in reality; rather, the process of mental division can continue indefinitely.

This shows that mental divisions do not entail an actual infinite in the physical world. It prepares for discussing causality and the nature of bodies.

Aristotle's Dilemma for Infinite Division
Aristotle and his followers prove that separative division is infinite via a dilemma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Aristotle's dilemma argument for infinite separative division and how it applies to both composite and simple parts.

Core Idea
Every part, composite or simple, can be separated further without end.

In this passage, the author presents the proof of Aristotle and his followers (al-Fārābī and Ibn Sīnā) for the claim that separative division continues without limit. The argument is a classic dilemma: whichever way we analyze a part, we end up with the possibility of further division. Let's unpack the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases of the dilemma
Notice that the proof begins by dividing every part into two kinds: composite or simple. Each case leads to a different reason for divisibility.
This classification is exhaustive—every part must be one or the other.
2
Understand the composite case
If the part is composite (made of smaller parts), it can be dissolved (انحلال) and dispersed (تفرق). This obviously allows further division.
This case is straightforward: composite parts are already divisible into their constituents.

Understand the simple case
If the part is simple, the parts imagined within it (in estimation) are similar. It is then possible for any two such imagined parts to separate completely, breaking the real connection (اتصال حقيقي). This also leads to infinite division.
The key is that even simple parts are not absolutely indivisible—they can separate if they are distinct in estimation.

The First Proof: The Triangle Contradiction
First geometrical proof using triangle contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first geometrical proof that the point of contact between a sphere and a surface must be indivisible, based on a contradiction involving triangle geometry.

Core Idea
Geometry shows the contact point cannot be divisible without contradiction.

The first of four proofs from Euclid uses a triangle formed by radii to show that if the contact point were divisible, the radii would be unequal, contradicting the definition of a sphere. This proof establishes the indivisibility of the point of contact.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof passage
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. Identify the assumption, construction, and conclusion.
The proof is a reductio ad absurdum.
2
Visualize the geometry
Imagine a sphere touching a plane. If the contact is over an area, draw two lines from the center to the ends of that area and a line along the surface.
These three lines should all be radii, hence equal.
3
Understand the perpendicular drop
Drop a perpendicular from the center to the base (the contact line). This creates two right triangles. Compare the chord lengths.
The chord opposite a right angle is longer than that opposite an acute angle.
4
Recognize the contradiction
The perpendicular becomes shorter than the other two lines, but all three should be equal as radii. This contradiction forces the assumption to be false.
5
Conclude indivisibility
Therefore, the contact point must be indivisible. This is the desired conclusion.
The proof does not yet involve the atom; it only establishes the point's indivisibility.

This passage presents a set of four geometrical proofs from Euclid and a concluding argument to establish that the point of contact between a sphere and a surface is indivisible, leading to the affirmation of the indivisible substance (jawhar fard), which is the atom in Islamic kalam.

Two Euclid-based geometrical proofs for indivisibility.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof for the conclusion that a body cannot be infinitely divisible in a way that infinite parts actually exist. It uses the example of water: a single body of water, when divided, becomes two waters. Two possibilities are considered: either the two waters pre-existed or they did not. If they pre-existed, division merely separates contiguous parts, confirming the existence of parts. If they did not pre-exist, then division annihilates the original body and creates new ones, leading to absurd consequences (e.g., a mosquito's pinprick annihilating the entire sea). An objection is raised that matter (hyle) persists even if the corporeality is annihilated. The author answers with two arguments. First, hyle is not the entire essence of the body, so its persistence does not preserve the body's identity. Second, a detailed analysis of the hyle's state: whether it remains one or becomes multiple, each option leads to a contradiction or to the conclusion that infinite parts are already actual. The proof ends by affirming that if a body were infinitely divisible, infinite parts would actually exist in it.

Sanusi presents a geometric argument using a rotating millstone to challenge the idea that slow motion is caused by rests. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Shadow length proves motion is continuous, not interrupted by rests.

This lesson presents a clever astronomical proof from Al-Matalib al-Aliyah. By considering the shadow of a stick, the author eliminates all possible explanations except one, which demonstrates that speed variation cannot be due to inserted moments of rest.
