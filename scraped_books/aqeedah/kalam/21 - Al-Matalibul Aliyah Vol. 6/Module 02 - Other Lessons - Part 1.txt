Hyle Defined with Examples: The First Rank – Hyle of Craft
Defines hyle via examples and the first rank.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the concept of hyle as the underlying matter that takes different forms, using everyday examples, and you will learn the first rank of hyle: hyle of craft.

Core Idea
Hyle is the common matter shared by objects of different forms.

This lesson introduces the philosophical concept of hyle (matter) by beginning with tangible examples. You will see how a knife, sword, axe, and saw all share the same matter—iron—yet differ in form. This sets the stage for a formal classification of hyle into four ranks. Here we focus on the first rank, hyle of craft, which is the specific material used by artisans.

Guided Path
1
Read the examples carefully
Notice how the author uses common items to illustrate the difference between matter and form. The knife, sword, etc. share iron as matter but differ in shape.
2
Identify the four ranks listed
The author announces four ranks: hyle of craft, nature, whole, and prime. Note their names for later lessons.
3
Focus on the first rank
Hyle of craft is defined as the body from which an artisan works, e.g., wood for carpenters. The shapes he creates are forms.
This establishes the matter-form distinction in craftsmanship.
4
Reflect on the analogy
Why is it important to separate matter from shape? This principle underlies all subsequent discussions of hyle.

The introduction concerning the meaning of hyle. We say: We find bodies differing in forms, similar in matter, like the knife, sword, axe, and saw. For all of them are made of iron, yet despite their sharing in this meaning, each one of them differs from the other in form and shape. So we said: These things—their hyle is iron, and their forms are different. Likewise, the door, bed, chair, and ship share in being made of wood, and differ in shapes and forms. When you know this, we say: Hyle has four ranks: hyle of craft, hyle of nature, hyle of the whole, and prime hyle. As for the first rank: it is hyle of craft. It is every body from which something is made, and in which the artisan has a craft—like wood for carpenters, iron for blacksmiths, soil and water for builders, yarn for weavers, and flour for bakers. And by this analogy, everything must have a body from which it is made and in which its craftsmanship resides. That body is the hyle for that thing. As for the shapes and engravings that the artisan makes in that body, they are the forms. As for the second rank: it is hyle of nature. It is fire, air, water, and earth. That is because everything under the sphere of the moon among generated things—namely minerals, plants, and animals—comes to be from these four, and into them it changes upon corruption. As for the third rank: it is hyle of the whole. It is absolute body from which the entirety of the celestial world is obtained—namely the spheres, planets, the four elements, and the three generated things. As for the fourth rank: it is prime hyle. According to some, it is the indivisible parts; according to others, it is a self-subsistent entity in which corporeality inheres, and from that subsistent entity and that receptivity the essence of body is generated. When you know this, we say: Our purpose in this book is to explain the states of body insofar as it is body, and to explain the prime hyle from which body is generated. And God knows best. And the discussion in it is arranged into chapters.

Commentary
This passage is an introductory classification of the concept of hyle (matter). The author begins by illustrating the distinction between matter and form through everyday examples: various iron tools share the same matter (iron) but differ in form; similarly, wooden furniture shares wood but differs in shape. This establishes that hyle is the underlying substrate. Then the author announces that hyle is divided into four ranks. The first rank is hyle of craft, which is the specific material used by an artisan, such as wood for a carpenter or iron for a blacksmith; the shapes he imparts are forms. The second rank is hyle of nature, which comprises the four classical elements (fire, air, water, earth); everything under the moon (minerals, plants, animals) is composed of these and returns to them upon decay. The third rank is hyle of the whole, identified as 'absolute body'—the universal matter underlying all celestial and earthly things: spheres, planets, the four elements, and the three types of generated beings. The fourth rank is prime hyle, about which there are two views: some say it is indivisible atoms, others say it is a self-subsistent entity that receives corporeality, and from their combination the essence of body arises. The author concludes by stating the purpose of the book: to explain the states of body and prime hyle. This classification provides a foundation for subsequent discussions.

Hyle of Nature and Hyle of the Whole
Explains second and third ranks of hyle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second rank (hyle of nature) as the four classical elements and the third rank (hyle of the whole) as absolute body underlying all celestial and earthly beings.

Core Idea
Beyond crafted objects, matter exists at natural and cosmic levels.

After discussing hyle in the context of human craftsmanship, the author now elevates the concept to the natural world. The second rank, hyle of nature, identifies the four elements—fire, air, water, earth—as the matter from which all sublunary beings (minerals, plants, animals) are composed. The third rank, hyle of the whole, is a universal matter called 'absolute body' that underlies the entire cosmos, including spheres, planets, elements, and generated beings.

Guided Path
1
Read about the second rank
Hyle of nature is defined as the four elements. The author explains why: everything under the moon is composed of these and returns to them upon corruption.
2
Understand the scope
Sublunary generated things (minerals, plants, animals) are included—this covers all living and non-living things below the moon.
3
Read about the third rank
Hyle of the whole is described as 'absolute body' from which the celestial world is obtained, including spheres, planets, elements, and the three generated things.
4
Compare the two ranks
The second rank is specific to the sublunary realm, while the third rank encompasses the entire universe including the heavens.
This shows a hierarchical widening of the concept of matter.

Prime Hyle and the Purpose of the Book
Defines prime hyle with two views and states the book's purpose.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth rank of hyle (prime hyle) and the two philosophical views regarding it, and you will grasp the overall purpose of the book.

Core Idea
Prime hyle is the ultimate philosophical substrate, either as indivisible atoms or a receptive essence.

The final rank of hyle is the most abstract. The author presents two conflicting views: some say prime hyle consists of indivisible parts (atoms), while others describe it as a self-subsistent entity that receives corporeality, resulting in body. This rank is the foundational matter for all bodies. The author then states the book's purpose: to explain the states of body and the prime hyle from which body is generated.

Guided Path
1
Read the description of prime hyle
Note the two opinions: indivisible parts (atomist view) vs. a self-subsistent entity that receives corporeality (Aristotelian view).
2
Understand the generation of body
From the subsistent entity and its receptivity, the essence of body (dhat al-jism) arises.
3
Read the author's purpose
The book aims to explain the states of body as such and the prime hyle from which body is generated.
4
Reflect on the hierarchy
Consider how the four ranks build from specific craft matter to the ultimate prime hyle.
This shows a progressive abstraction of the concept of matter.

Objection and Reply: Contact and Accident Multiplicity
Refutation of an objection and geometric reinforcement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes an objection that even if contact exists externally, it only causes multiplicity in accidents, not in the essence, and how a geometric example reinforces this.

Core Idea
Multiplicity in relations does not multiply the essence.

The author anticipates a possible objection: suppose contact does exist externally and causes one face of a part to differ from another. Couldn't these faces be accidents? The objector might say accidents cannot have contact, so the faces must be parts. The author rebuts by showing that, according to the opponent's own doctrine, bodies contact via surfaces, lines, and points—which are all accidents. Thus contact is judged of accidents, not substances. He then uses a geometric example from Euclid to further prove that relative alignment does not entail division.

Guided Path
1
Read the third question (objection)
Understand the concession: suppose contact exists and makes one face differ from another. The objector then suggests these faces could be accidents, not parts of the essence.
2
Analyze the author's initial reply
The author rejects the claim that accidents cannot have contact. He points out that according to the opponent, bodies contact by surfaces, surfaces by lines, lines by points—and all these are accidents. So contact is judged of accidents.
3
Connect to the implication
Thus the opponent's proof only shows multiplicity of accidents, not of the essence itself. Multiplicity in sides (jānib) means multiplicity of accidents, not division in the substance.
4
Examine the geometric example
The author cites Euclid: a point at the center is aligned with every point in the circle and even all points in the world. This alignment does not make the point divisible because alignment is a relative matter; multiplicity of relations does not affect the essence.
5
Apply the analogy to contact
Just as alignment of a single point with many points does not divide the point, differences in contact do not divide the part. The argument is closed with 'And God knows best.'

The Compass Argument Against the Indivisible Part
Author presents the third argument (compass) against the indivisible part.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third argument against the indivisible part (the compass argument) and how it demonstrates the logical contradictions inherent in the atomistic conception of space.

Core Idea
A three-legged compass proves the indivisible part is impossible.

This lesson presents the third of a series of arguments refuting the existence of the indivisible part (al-juzʾ alladhī lā yatajazzaʾ). Using a three-legged compass placed on two concentric circles, the author forces a trilemma: each possible outcome leads to a contradiction. The passage also clarifies that all three arguments are essentially the same reasoning expressed through different examples.

Guided Path
1
Visualize the compass setup
Imagine a compass with three legs placed on two concentric circles. The first leg is fixed at the center. The second leg rests on a small circle of 50 parts, and the third leg rests on a larger circle of 100 parts.
The numbers 50 and 100 are arbitrary; any numbers showing a 1:2 ratio would work.
2
Consider the three possibilities
When the third leg moves one part on the large circle, what happens to the second leg on the small circle? The author presents three logical possibilities: it moves exactly one part, it does not move at all, or it moves less than one part.
3
Analyze each absurdity
Each possibility leads to an absurd conclusion. The first implies the circles are equal in circumference. The second would break the compass. The third requires the part to be divisible, contradicting its indivisibility.
4
Appreciate the unity of the proofs
The author notes that this argument is essentially the same as the previous two proofs (not shown here), differing only in the illustrative example.

Commentary
This passage refutes the atomist doctrine of the indivisible atom (al-jawhar al-fard) by using geometric reasoning based on circles. It begins by asserting that since circles and spheres exist, atomism must be false. The argument proceeds by considering a straight line composed of indivisible parts. If such a line is curved into a circle, then the inner sides of the parts become contiguous. But then one must consider whether the outer sides also become contiguous. If they do, then the area inside the circle equals the area outside, leading to absurd consequences when concentric circles are added. For example, one could build up layers that equal the size of the Throne while using the same number of parts as the first small circle, which is impossible. Therefore, the assumption that outer sides also become contiguous is false. So the alternative is taken: only the inner sides become contiguous, while outer sides remain separate. From this, two refutations follow. First, since parts have contiguous sides and separate sides, they must be divisible (because contiguous and separate are different, implying parts). Second, the gaps between the parts are either large enough to fit one atom or not. If they are, then that atom would be smaller than the original atoms, making the original divisible; if they are not, then the gaps are smaller than the indivisible atom, which is impossible because gaps are nothingness. Thus the atomist view is refuted.

The Contradiction in Attributing Spherical Shape to Simple Bodies
Refutation via contradiction in a simple body's nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the claim that simple bodies (like celestial spheres) are spherical leads to a logical contradiction between nature's demand for spherical shape and its demand for connection.

Core Idea
A single nature cannot demand two opposing effects.

The author presents a logical refutation of the idea that simple bodies are inherently spherical. He shows that even if we grant the principle of a simple body's unity, pointing to a specific part reveals a contradiction: the same nature that supposedly necessitates a sphere also necessitates connection, preventing the sphere. This lesson walks through that argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Pay attention to the logical flow: concession, then distinction of a part, then the two necessities from nature, then the contradiction.
The author uses a dialectical style: 'we grant... then we say'.
2
Identify the initial assumption
The argument begins by acknowledging the opponent's principle: that a simple body is one unified thing and parts are only mentally distinguished. Note that the author questions this principle.
The author says he has already refuted this principle, but here he grants it for argument's sake.
3
Follow the argument: one part, two necessities
When we point to a specific part of a simple body (like the celestial sphere), that part's nature is said to necessitate spherical shape. But the same nature also necessitates connection to other parts, which prevents the spherical shape.
The part cannot be an independent sphere because it must connect to other parts.
4
Recognize the contradiction
A single nature requiring two opposite things (spherical shape and prevention of spherical shape) is impossible. Thus the claim that simple bodies are spherical is false.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.

The Right Triangle Proof Against Indivisible Parts
Razi presents a geometric proof by contradiction using a right triangle.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A right triangle with indivisible parts forces the hypotenuse to be fractional, contradicting indivisibility.

Fakhr al-Din al-Razi presents a geometric proof against the existence of indivisible parts (al-juz' alladhi la yatajazzā). Using a right triangle composed of such parts, he demonstrates that any attempt to assign a rational integer length to the hypotenuse leads to an impossibility, forcing the conclusion that the parts must be divisible. This proof is a clear example of reductio ad absurdum in kalam cosmology.

Guided Path
1
Understand the setup of the first form
Begin by visualizing a line composed of four indivisible parts, with one additional part placed perpendicularly at one end, forming a right triangle. Note that all sides are made of whole indivisible units.
The diagram is represented as '00000' in the text.
2
Examine the three possibilities for the hypotenuse
The hypotenuse must be an integer number of parts. Consider the three cases: it equals 4, it equals 5, or it is between 4 and 5. Each leads to an impossibility: equal to one side, equal to the sum, or requiring division of the parts.
This is the core of the reductio.
3
Analyze the second form
Repeat the reasoning with a smaller triangle: two parts for one side, one part for the perpendicular. The same contradiction arises: the hypotenuse cannot be 2 (equals side) nor 3 (equals sum), so it must be between, requiring division.
This reinforces the argument for any number of parts.
4
Grasp the conclusion
Both forms show that assuming indivisible parts leads to a geometric contradiction. Therefore, indivisible parts cannot exist in reality, supporting the continuous nature of magnitudes.
This conclusion is a key point in kalam cosmology.

Seventh Proof: The Ladder on a Wall
Razi argues that pulling a ladder down a wall forces division of indivisible parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's geometric argument that pulling a ladder down a wall forces the division of indivisible parts.

Core Idea
If indivisible parts existed, pulling a ladder would make it longer than itself.

This lesson presents the seventh proof from Razi against the concept of indivisible parts (atoms) in space. The proof uses a simple geometry of a ladder against a wall. As you'll see, Razi shows that if space were made of indivisible units, then moving the ladder would create a contradiction. This proof highlights the necessity of continuous divisibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the literal translation. Notice the precise geometric setup and the logical conclusion.
The passage is a single continuous argument.
2
Visualize the initial setup
Imagine a ladder of fixed length placed against a wall. The top is 5 units above ground, the bottom is 5 units from the wall. So the ladder length is √(5²+5²)=√50.
The ladder is rigid, so its length cannot change.
3

If the top is pulled down by exactly one unit, it becomes at height 4. If the bottom also moved exactly one unit, it would become 6 units from the wall. Then the ladder length would be √(4²+6²)=√52, which is longer than √50.
This is impossible for a rigid ladder.
4
Identify the contradiction
Therefore, the bottom cannot move exactly one unit. It must move less than one unit. But if space is composed of indivisible parts, there is no 'less than one unit' — so indivisible parts cannot exist.
The argument shows that division is necessary.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Razi concludes that divisibility is required, hence indivisible parts are impossible. This proof is a reductio ad absurdum.
Understand how geometry is used in kalam.
