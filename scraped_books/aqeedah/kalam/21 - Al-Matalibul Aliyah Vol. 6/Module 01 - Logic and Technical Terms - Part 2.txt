First Sophistical Objection: The 'Now' Analogy
Fakhr al-Razi presents the first sophistical objection regarding the necessity of a present part of motion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first sophistical objection Fakhr al-Razi raises against the proof for motion, and how it uses the analogy of the 'now' to deny the need for a present part of motion.

Core Idea
The 'now' itself is never present—so why must a part of motion be present now?

In this passage, Fakhr al-Razi introduces a sophisticated objection that targets a key premise in the standard proof for motion. He compares the required 'present part of motion' to the elusive nature of the 'now', arguing that just as the 'now' is never actually present without infinite regress, the same may hold for a part of motion. This objection is framed as a challenge to self-evident truths, characteristic of sophistical interrogation.

Second Objection: The Limit of Motion
Second objection: what occurs in the now is the limit of motion, not motion itself.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second objection against the kalām argument that motion consists of consecutive indivisible parts, and why it does not force that conclusion.

Core Idea
The limit of motion is indivisible, but divisible parts may intervene.

This lesson presents the second objection to the kalām proof that motion requires consecutive indivisible parts. The objection concedes that something of motion occurs in the now, but argues that it is only the limit of motion—an indivisible point—not motion itself. The objector then challenges the claim that this forces a succession of indivisibles, suggesting divisible parts could separate them.

Guided Path

Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice the concession: 'We grant that something must occur in the now.'
2
Identify the key claim
What does the objector say occurs in the now? He says it is the limit of motion, not motion itself. The limit is indivisible.
The term 'limit of motion' (طرف الحركة) is crucial.
3
Understand the counterargument and reply
The objector anticipates a response: 'When that limit perishes, another indivisible must occur.' But he replies: why cannot a divisible thing occur between them?
This blocks the conclusion that indivisibles follow each other directly.
4
See the consequence for the proof
The objection shows that the proof does not force a succession of indivisibles. The existence of a divisible interval is possible.
5
Reflect on the lesson's insight
Consider how this objection weakens the original argument for atomism in motion. It does not deny indivisible parts, but their direct succession.

The Present Defined: Resolving the First Objection
First objection denies necessity of a present part of motion; answer defines 'the present'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author defines 'the present' to resolve the objection about motion and avoid infinite regress.

Core Idea
The present is what exists and is neither past nor future — no container needed.

This passage presents the first objection to the argument about motion and time. The objector claims that a part of motion need not occur in the present moment, using the example of the 'now' itself. The author answers by clarifying what 'present' truly means, thereby dissolving the objection without falling into infinite regress. Understanding this answer sharpens our grasp of temporal concepts in kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and opponent's argument

This objection challenges a key premise about motion's continuity.

Examine the objector's counter-example about 'now'
The objector points out that the 'now' itself becomes past and future, yet it is not 'present in the now' without regress. Understand why this is posed as a problem.
The objector argues that defining 'present' as containing part of motion leads to infinite regress.
3
Study the author's definition and its effect
The author responds by defining 'the present' as that which exists and is neither past nor future. The 'now' fits this definition directly, so no infinite regress follows. See how this answer dissolves the objection.
The key is that 'present' does not mean being contained in something else.
