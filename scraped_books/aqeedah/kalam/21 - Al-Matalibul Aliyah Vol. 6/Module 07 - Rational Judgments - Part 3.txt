The Shadow Argument for Divisibility
A proof using shadows to demonstrate the divisibility of atoms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the phenomenon of shadows is used to logically demonstrate the divisibility of the indivisible atom.

Core Idea
A body's shadow reveals its own divisibility.

This proof uses a simple observation about shadows to challenge the idea of an indivisible atom. The author shows that if a body's shadow can be halved, then the body itself must be divisible. This is a clever application of logical reasoning to refute the atomist position.

Guided Path
1
Read the Proof
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow of the argument. Then read the literal translation.
Notice the conditional structure: if this, then that.
2
Understand the Premise
The proof starts with an observational premise: a body's shadow can be twice its length at certain times, meaning half of the shadow equals the body's own shadow.
3
Follow the Logical Steps
Trace the reasoning: if a body's parts are odd (like individual atoms), its shadow must be even. An even shadow has a half. That half is the body's own shadow, so the body must have a half—hence it is divisible.
This is a reductio ad absurdum against the indivisible atom.
4
Reflect on the Conclusion
The conclusion is that the indivisible atom must be divisible. Ask yourself how this proof undermines the atomist claim.
The proof does not rely on experimental measurement but on logical necessity.

Fourth Way: Proof from Sphere and Polygon
Fourth argument using sphere and polygon to prove divisibility of parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi argues for the divisibility of all parts by considering them as spheres or polygons, thereby refuting the indivisible atom.

Core Idea
Every finite body is either a sphere or a polygon—both are divisible.

This passage presents a geometric argument against the concept of an indivisible atom (jawhar fard). Al-Razi classifies any part of a body as either a sphere or a polygon, then shows that in both cases, division necessarily follows. The argument is a clear example of a disjunctive syllogism in kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation

Notice the structured logical flow: classification, then two cases, then conclusion.

The passage states that each part is either a sphere (enclosed by one boundary) or a polygon (enclosed by multiple boundaries). This is a key disjunction.

If a part is a sphere, packing spheres together creates gaps smaller than the spheres. Since something smaller exists, the sphere must be divisible.

Since both cases lead to divisibility, all parts under consideration are divisible. Hence the indivisible atom is impossible.
This is a disjunctive syllogism: P or Q, both imply R, therefore R.

The new hypotenuse would then be √(4²+6²)=√52. The same ladder cannot have two different lengths (√50 and √52). Therefore, the assumption that pulling the top by one unit results in the bottom moving by exactly one unit is false. Instead, the bottom must move by less than one unit, implying that the unit (the indivisible part) is divisible. Thus, the indivisible part must be divisible, which is a contradiction to its definition. The proof concludes that division (al-qisma) is necessitated. This is a standard kalam argument for atomism's internal inconsistency.

The author explains that comparison only requires rank matching.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some may think the argument assumes the existence of an infinite line rather than proving its impossibility.
Correction: It is a reductio ad absurdum: it assumes an infinite line for the sake of argument and derives a contradiction, thus proving that assumption false.

Checkpoint
What does the proof conclude about the supposition of an infinite line?
A) It is possible but not actual.
B) It necessitates a first point of tangency.
C) It leads to a contradiction and is therefore impossible.
D) It is only impossible if the line is straight.

Key Takeaway
An infinite line leads to a logical contradiction regarding the first point of tangency, proving that such a line cannot exist.
