The Fourth Objection: The Bodily Form and Accidents
Fourth objection: bodily form would need to be composite to receive accidents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the bodily form itself would have to be composite by the opponents' logic.

Core Idea
The bodily form, which is supposed to actualize hyele, would itself need to be composite to receive accidents.

The fourth objection targets the bodily form. The opponents claim that the body, not hyele alone, is receptive of motion, rest, color, and shape. But the bodily form is what actualizes hyele into a body. If the bodily form itself makes the body receptive, then the bodily form must be able to receive accidents. And whatever receives must be composed of hyele and form. Therefore, the bodily form would itself be composite, leading to an infinite regress.

And the objection is based on the principle that the one does not issue except from the one, and the weakness of that has already been clarified. Then we say: This objection also applies to you in several issues: First: The hyele (matter) either exists or does not exist. If it has no existence in itself, it cannot be a part of the essence of an existing body, because the non-existent cannot be a part of the existent. If it has existence in itself, then it is true that it exists in actuality, and it is true that it is receptive of forms and accidents. Then the argument you mentioned recurs, necessitating that hyele is in need of another hyele, ad infinitum, which is false. Second: The rational soul is composed of hyele and form, which is false. Third: The essence of God Most High is effective in bringing possibles into existence, and He is knowledgeable of universals. Knowledge, according to you, is the attainment of forms corresponding to the known things in the essence of the knower. This entails that the essence of God is both effective and receptive of those forms simultaneously, necessitating that He be composed of hyele and form, which is false. Fourth: The receptive of motion and rest, color and shape, is the body, not hyele alone. We say: The bodily form necessitates the actualization of hyele and necessitates the reception of these accidents. Thus the form in itself must be composed of hyele and form, which is false. So it is established through these points that this argument is false.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the opponents who posit matter (hyele) as a component of bodies, based on the principle that 'the one does not issue from the one.' The author begins by recalling that this principle has already been shown to be weak. Then he turns the objection back on the opponents by showing that their own principle leads to absurdities in four specific cases. First, hyele itself must either exist or not; if not, it cannot be a part of anything, and if it exists, it would require another hyele, leading to infinite regress. Second, the rational soul would be composed of hyele and form, which is false. Third, God's essence, being both an agent and a knower, would have to be both effective and receptive, implying composition. Fourth, the body receives motions and qualities, but if the bodily form actualizes hyele and then itself receives accidents, then the form itself would be composite. All four objections demonstrate internal inconsistencies in the opponent's doctrine, leading to the conclusion that their argument is false.

The Third Argument: 'Long' and the Indivisible Atom
Refutation of the argument that 'long' implies an attribute, using the indivisible atom theory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the argument that the term 'long' implies an attribute necessitating composition, using the indivisible atom theory.

Core Idea
The term 'long' does not prove composition; it only denotes a sum of atoms.

In this passage, the author addresses the third argument that attempts to prove composition in bodies by appealing to the term 'long'. The argument claims that 'long' suggests length is an attribute, implying composition. The author responds by clarifying the interpretation of 'long' among those who affirm the indivisible atom, showing that the argument does not force composition in the essence.

وأما الحجة الثالثة : إن لفظ الطويل وإن كان يوهم أن الطول صفة قائمة بالمحل، إلا أن الذين يثبتون الجوهر الفرد ، يقولون : ولا معنى للطويل إلا مجموع جوهرين، تركبا في سمت واحد ، فاللفظ وإن أشعر بكون الطول صفة ، إلا أن بعد هذا التفسير ، يزول هذا الاشتباه . ثم نقول : إن سلمنا تفي الجوهر الفرد . إلا أن هذا الكلام يدل على أن المقدار عرض زائد على ذات الجسم. وهذا مسلم . إلا أن هذا لا يقتضي وقوع التركب في ذات الجسم وفي ماهيته
As for the third argument: The term 'long' (tawīl), even though it suggests that length (tūl) is an attribute subsisting in a subject, those who affirm the indivisible atom (al-jawhar al-fard) say: There is no meaning of 'long' except the sum of two atoms combined in one direction. So the term, even though it hints at length being an attribute, after this interpretation this ambiguity is removed. Then we say: If we grant the indivisible atom, yet this statement indicates that magnitude (al-miqdār) is an accident superadded to the essence of the body. This is granted. But this does not necessitate the occurrence of composition in the essence of the body and in its quiddity.

Commentary
This passage deals with the third argument used to prove composition. The argument is that the term 'long' (طويل) might imply that length is an attribute inhering in a subject, which would require composition. The author responds by noting that those who affirm the indivisible atom (الجوهر الفرد) interpret 'long' simply as the sum of two atoms arranged in one line. Once this interpretation is accepted, the suggestion of an attribute disappears. Then the author makes a concession: even if we accept the indivisible atom, the argument would show that magnitude is an accident added to the body's essence. However, this does not force composition within the essence or quiddity of the body itself. In other words, magnitude being an accident does not make the body's essence composite; the body's essence remains simple.
