Cosmic Witnesses to Divine Transcendence
A declaration of God's transcendence using cosmic witnesses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how cosmic phenomena serve as witnesses to God's absolute transcendence and why God is beyond all human concepts.

Core Idea
Creation's change and limitation point to a Creator who is unchanging and limitless.

In this passage, the author invokes the grandeur of the universe—the Throne, stars, and celestial bodies—as silent witnesses that declare God's absolute uniqueness. By observing the imperfection and dependence of created things, we come to know that the Creator is beyond all comparison. This poetic declaration forms a cornerstone of Islamic theology on divine transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the English translation. Let the poetic rhythm and imagery sink in before diving into analysis.
This passage uses a highly elevated style; pay attention to the cosmic imagery.
2
Identify the witnesses
List the cosmic phenomena mentioned: the Throne, the Footstool, the ascents of heavens, the fixed stars. Notice how each is described as bearing witness to God's transcendence.
The author uses 'shahida laka' — 'bear witness to You'.
3
Understand their testimony
The moon's waning shows deficiency, the sun's changes show need, and natural forces are subdued. These examples demonstrate that all created things are changing and dependent, thus pointing to an unchanging, independent Creator.
The argument is from contingency: what changes cannot be divine.
4
Grasp the theological conclusions
From these observations, the author concludes that God is pre-eternal (azal) without end, everlasting (dawam) without beginning, and 'a thing unlike all creation'. Reflect on the final affirmation: 'He is the Living who does not die.'
The phrase 'shay'un bi-khilafi kulli khalq' is crucial; it affirms God's existence while denying any similarity.

And I say: The radiance of the Throne, the light of the Footstool, the ascents of the heavens, and the lights of the fixed stars in the planets upon their pulpits penetrating the highest high, and their ascents purified from the dust of the world of generation and corruption — all bear witness to You that the First, the Eternal Truth, is not comparable to anything from the connections of intellects, the likenesses of thoughts, or the associations of ideas. For the moon, by its waning, declares deficiency, and the sun, by the change of its states, is in need of the management of the Merciful; and the natures are vanquished under His overwhelming power, effaced in the reproach of the high ascents. So changing things bear witness to His unchangingness, and successive things to the eternity of His everlastingness. His pre-eternity is free from termination, and His everlastingness is transcendent from coming and passing away. And everything of which it is true that it has passed and will come, He is its Creator and higher than it. Through His generosity, generosity and creation came to be, and by His signs, annihilation and corruption. And everything other than Him is lost in His power, persisting at the rising of the light of His majesty. And in the minds of creation, He is nothing but 'a thing' unlike all creation. He possesses holiness, power, might, and dominion. And He is the Living who does not die.

Commentary
This passage is a declaration of God's transcendence (tanzih). The author begins by calling upon cosmic witnesses — the Throne, the Footstool, the ascents of heavens, and the fixed stars — to testify that God, the First Eternal Truth (al-awwal al-ḥaqq al-azalī), is not similar to anything that can be connected by intellects, likened by thoughts, or associated by ideas. He then gives examples from creation: the moon wanes, showing its deficiency; the sun changes, showing its need for God's management; the natural forces are subdued under God's power. From these changing and successive things, we infer that God does not change and is eternal. God's pre-eternity (azal) is free from termination, and His everlastingness (dawām) is free from coming and passing. Everything that has a past and future is created by Him and subservient. Generosity and existence come from His generosity, while annihilation and corruption are signs of His control. Everything else is lost in His power. Human intellects can only say that God is 'a thing' unlike all creation. He alone possesses holiness, power, might, and dominion. The passage concludes with 'He is the Living who does not die', affirming God's eternal life.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think that referring to God as 'a thing' implies anthropomorphism or similarity to creation.
Correction: The author clarifies that God is 'a thing unlike all creation', emphasizing absolute difference. The term 'thing' (shay') here simply affirms His existence while negating any likeness.

Key Takeaway
God is absolutely transcendent; everything in creation testifies to His uniqueness and incomparability.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?

?
The passage says everything other than God is 'lost in His power'. In what area of your life do you still act as if you are in control—your health, your finances, your relationships? What would it look like to truly surrender that area to Him?
?
God is 'the Living who does not die'. If you lived with the constant awareness that you are accountable to an Eternal Being, what would you do differently tomorrow? Write down one concrete action you will take this week to live in light of this truth.

Supplication through Divine Attributes
Author opens with a supplication invoking divine attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author's supplication incorporates the divine attributes of Malakut and Jabarut as a means of invoking God's mercy and power.

Core Idea
Supplication is an act of acknowledging God's absolute sovereignty and mercy.

This passage opens with a profound supplication that weaves together an invocation of God's hidden knowledge, His majestic dominion (Malakut), and His overwhelming power (Jabarut). The author then asks for deliverance from moral and spiritual darkness and for ease in both worlds. Let's explore how this prayer serves as both a personal plea and a theological statement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the invocations
Read the supplication and note the different 'rights' by which the author asks. The author invokes God's hidden knowledge, His Malakut, His Jabarut, and the answered prayers of servants.
These invocations implicitly affirm that these divine realms are beyond human comprehension.
2
Recognize theological implications
Notice that each invocation affirms a divine attribute or realm that is unique to God. The author uses these as a means of drawing near to God.
3
Examine the petition for deliverance
See how the author asks for deliverance from blameworthy morals and beliefs, connecting directly to the goal of the book: purifying theology and ethics.

Cosmic Testimony to Divine Transcendence
Author argues from cosmic change to divine immutability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the changing celestial bodies, such as the moon and sun, serve as evidence for God's immutability and eternal existence.

Core Idea
Creation's change points to the Unchanging Creator.

After the supplication, the author presents a profound argument using the cosmos. The radiance of the Throne, the Kursi, the stars, and the change of the moon and sun all bear witness that the Eternal First is beyond any mental similarity. This lesson unpacks this argument and its implications for divine transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Observe the cosmic witnesses
The author lists the Throne, Kursi, ascensions of heavens, and stars as bearing witness to God. Read the passage and identify each witness.
These are physical or metaphysical entities that reflect God's majesty.
2
Analyze the examples of the moon and sun
The moon's waning and the sun's changing states indicate deficiency and need. They thus point to a perfect, unchanging Creator. Note the contrast between change and immutability.
3
Understand the conclusion
The author generalizes: changing things testify to His unchangeability, successive things to His perpetual eternity. This is the core of the argument from contingency.
4
Reflect on the final affirmation
The passage ends with a strong declaration that everything other than Him is lost in His jabarut, and He is the Living who never dies. This sums up the lesson's key point.
