The Walking Person Argument: Psychological Proof Against Rests
A psychological proof using conscious action to refute rests in slow motion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the walking person argument uses conscious awareness to show that slowness cannot be due to alternating motion and rest.

Core Idea
If slowness required rests, the walker would know them; he doesn't, so it doesn't.

This lesson examines a fascinating psychological argument that challenges the idea that slow motion is composed of motion interspersed with rests. By reflecting on our own experience of walking slowly, we can test the logical implications of that theory. Let's follow the author's reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Set up the scenario
Understand that a rational person walking slowly is the example. Note that if slowness were motion+rest, then the walker would be voluntarily performing both motion and rest.
2
Identify conditions for voluntary action
The author states that voluntary action requires intention, choice, and knowledge of the specific action. Therefore, the walker would need to know each rest and motion.
3
Draw the conclusion
Since the walker is not aware of any rests (he perceives continuous motion), slowness cannot be due to rests. This refutes the theory.

Core Idea
The compass argument shows that indivisible parts must be divisible.

This lesson presents one of the most ingenious proofs against atomism, using a simple geometric device: a three-legged compass. By considering what happens when the outer leg moves by one indivisible part, the argument forces a contradiction, showing that the very concept of an indivisible part leads to absurdity. Let's examine the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the compass setup
Visualize a compass with three legs: one fixed at the center, one touching a small circle (50 parts), and one touching a larger concentric circle (100 parts).

Since all possibilities are impossible, the initial assumption that parts are indivisible must be false. Therefore, parts are divisible.

Commentary
This passage is a proof via reductio ad absurdum. The author first establishes a metaphysical principle: a single true unity cannot originate or cease gradually; gradual change implies a succession of distinct things. He then applies this to the shadow. Since the shadow is perceived to cease gradually, it must consist of discrete parts that cease one by one. If the shadow were infinitely divisible, then its gradual non-existence would entail an infinite series of non-existences, each occurring in a distinct instant. This would result in an actual infinite number of instants, which is impossible because an actual infinite cannot be bounded. Therefore, the shadow can only have finite divisions. The author concludes that this reasoning also resolves a previous objection about a compass with three branches, effectively linking this argument to a broader discussion of physical divisibility.

The Fourth Proof: Spherical Shells and the Indivisible Atom
The author presents the fourth proof from spherical shells for the necessity of the indivisible atom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the argument from spherical shells leads to the necessity of the indivisible atom.

Core Idea
Infinite division of a sphere's thickness leads to absurdity, so atoms must exist.

In this passage, the author presents a fourth proof for the existence of indivisible atoms (jawhar al-fard). By analyzing the properties of a hollow sphere—such as a celestial sphere—he demonstrates that its thickness cannot be infinitely divisible, because that would require an actual infinity of parts. This argument serves as a key piece of evidence in the defense of atomism.

If infinite division were possible, the sphere would contain actually infinite parts, which is impossible. Therefore division is finite.
This proves the need for indivisible atoms.

The fourth proof: Every hollow sphere, such as the celestial spheres, has its outer surface greater than its inner. Also, its outer surface is convex and its inner surface is concave. And if this is so, then each of these two surfaces has become distinguished by attributes whose existence in the other is impossible. So it is necessary that the thickness of that celestial sphere separates into two halves, and the situation with each of its halves is as before. So if its thickness were susceptible to infinite divisions, then it would necessarily be composed of actually infinite parts. And that is false. Thus the assertion of affirming the sphere and the circle is false, according to what has preceded in its elaboration. Commentary
This passage contains two distinct parts: a proof from spherical shells for the indivisible atom, and a refutation of the claim that the atom must have a shape. The first part is a proof (al-ḥujja al-rābiʿa). The author begins by considering any hollow sphere, like the celestial spheres. He notes that the outer and inner surfaces differ—the outer is larger and convex, the inner is smaller and concave. Since they have opposite attributes (convex vs. concave, larger vs. smaller), the thickness between them must be separable into two halves. This reasoning leads to infinite regress: if the thickness could be divided infinitely, it would actually be composed of infinite parts, which is absurd. Therefore the thickness must be finitely divisible, meaning matter is composed of indivisible atoms (al-jawhar al-fard). Consequently, the reality of a continuous sphere or circle is false. The second part addresses an objection: opponents claim the atom must be either a sphere or a polygon. The author denies this, arguing that shape is only conceivable for something composed of sides and parts. The indivisible atom has no parts, so it cannot have a shape. Thus the atom is neither sphere nor polygon.

Third Proof: Numerical Sides and Triangle Types
Demonstrates through numerical examples that triangles become acute, forcing infinite divisibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the sequence of triangle types from obtuse to increasingly acute demonstrates the necessity of infinite divisibility, thereby refuting atomism.

Core Idea
Triangles can become arbitrarily acute, proving matter is infinitely divisible.

This lesson presents the third argument against atomism, which uses a simple numerical progression of side lengths to show that triangles can vary continuously from obtuse to right to acute angles. By observing that the angle becomes ever sharper with each successive set of integer sides, the argument demonstrates that the parts of a triangle must be divisible without limit. This challenges the concept of indivisible atoms (jawhar) that would not allow such continuous variation.
