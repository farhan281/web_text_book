This passage presents three alternative arguments for the divisibility of any extended entity (mutahayyiz). If the right side were identical to the left side, then both contradictory alignments would apply to the same thing, which is impossible; therefore the sides are distinct, implying division. All three arguments are presented as concise alternatives to the longer proof previously given.

The Philosophers' First Argument and Its Three Conclusions
Philosophers' first argument deriving three conclusions about time, space, and motion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the philosophers derive three conclusions about time, distance, and motion from a simple scenario of moving parts, and why these conclusions are important for their cosmology.

Core Idea
A single motion scenario yields three radical claims about time, space, and rest.

The philosophers use a thought experiment involving two parts moving in different numbers of spaces to argue for fundamental properties of the physical world. This passage presents their first argument, which leads to three striking conclusions about time, distance, and motion. Understand the scenario

This shows that speed difference does not require intervening rests.

Commentary
This passage presents the first proof for the denial of the part (presumably the indivisible atom), using the analogy of a rotating millstone. The argument is a classic reductio ad absurdum to establish the infinite divisibility of magnitude and, by extension, time. The proof begins by stating the scenario: if a millstone rotates, there are two possibilities regarding the movement of its concentric circles (the large hoop and the small hoop). The first possibility: whenever the large hoop moves by some amount (a 'part'), the small hoop moves by exactly the same amount. This is deemed impossible because it would imply that the circumference of the small circle equals that of the large circle, contradicting geometry. The second possibility: the large hoop moves a part while the small hoop does not move at all. This is also false, because it would cause the millstone to disintegrate—its parts would separate. The author then provides four reasons (wujuh) to demonstrate why the second possibility leads to absurdity. The first reason: empirical observation shows that a hard, smooth stone does not crumble into unconnected pieces when it rotates, unlike flour. The second reason: if we consider the celestial spheres (al-falak), which constantly rotate, their fragmentation is impossible because they are described in the Quran as 'strong' (shidad) and without 'rifts' (futur). If rotation caused disintegration, the heavens would not be firm. The third reason: a human turning on his heel does not suffer the disintegration of his limbs; sensation confirms bodily continuity. The Mu'tazilite view that separation of bodily parts causes death is cited as further evidence. The fourth reason: if the millstone were to disintegrate, each part would need to 'know' how much to move or rest to preserve its relative positions—an impossible knowledge for inanimate stone. Thus both initial possibilities are rejected. At this point, the author notes that the theologians (mutakallimun) might object that God could orchestrate the movements of each part to prevent disintegration, which is not impossible for the wise God. However, this does not refute the core argument. The author then concludes: since the first two options are impossible, the only remaining possibility is that when the large circle moves a part, the small circle moves less than a part. This implies that magnitude is divisible without end (infinite divisibility). Finally, the author states that this proof also necessitates the infinite divisibility of time and distance simultaneously, extending the conclusion beyond mere spatial magnitude.
