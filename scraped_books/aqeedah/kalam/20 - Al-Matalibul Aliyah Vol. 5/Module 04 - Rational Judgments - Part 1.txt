The First Reductio: Collapsing Events into One Day
Presents the first reductio: the day of flood example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how assuming the eternity of time leads to the absurd conclusion that distinct historical events collapse into the same moment.

Core Idea
Eternal time forces all events into a single eternal present.

The first argument against the reality of time as an eternal substance uses a concrete example: if a specific day today were identical to the day of the flood, then any event occurring today would have occurred then. This shows the absurdity of treating time as an independent entity. Let's examine the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation to get the flow of the argument.
2
Identify the conditional premise
Notice the starting assumption: if this day is the day of the flood. What follows from that?
The author uses a consequence that is contradictory.
3
Understand the conclusion
The conclusion is that this leads to a contradiction (خلف), so the assumption is false.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.

لوجب أن يكون هذا اليوم بعينه هو يوم الطوفان، فيلزم على هذا التقدير أن يكون الحادث في هذا اليوم حادثاً في يوم الطوفان، بل قبله بل أزلاً. هذا خلف. محال. وأيضاً: فبتقدير كونه دائم الوجود بعينه، فلا بد وأن يصدق عليه أنه كان موجوداً قبل، وسيبقى موجوداً بعد. فلو كان هذا المفهوم يقتضي إثبات مدة وزمان لذلك الشيء، فحينئذ يلزم افتقار المدة والزمان إلى مدة أخرى، وزمان آخر. ويلزم التسلسل وهو محال. والثاني أيضاً باطل، لأنه يقتضي أن تكون الأجزاء المفترضة فيه متعاقبة متوالية، وحينئذ يصح في كل واحد من تلك الأجزاء أن يقال: إنه حدث الآن، لا قبل ولا بعد. فلو كان الحكم على الشيء بأنه حدث الآن، أو كان حادثاً قبل ذلك، أو سيحدث بعد ذلك، يقتضي شيئاً آخر يكون ظرفاً له، ويكون لأجل وقوع هذا الشيء في ذلك الظرف، يصدق عليه بأنه حصل الآن، أو في الماضي، أو سيحصل في المستقبل، فحينئذ يلزم أن يكون للزمان زمان آخر إلى غير النهاية، وذلك محال.
It would be necessary that this very day be the day of the flood, so upon this supposition it would follow that the event occurring on this day occurred on the flood day, rather before it, rather eternally. This is a contradiction. Impossible. And also: upon the supposition that it is perpetually existent identically, it must be true of it that it existed before and will continue to exist after. If this concept entails establishing a duration and time for that thing, then it would necessitate that the duration and time depend on another duration and another time, and infinite regress would follow, which is impossible. The second [division] is also false, because it requires that the parts posited in it be successive and consecutive, and then it is valid for each one of those parts to be said: it occurs now, not before nor after. If the judgment upon a thing that it occurs now, or was occurring before, or will occur after, requires another thing that is its container, and because of the occurrence of this thing in that container it is described as having occurred now, or in the past, or will occur in the future, then it would necessitate that time has another time ad infinitum, and that is impossible.

Commentary
This passage presents two reductio arguments against the reality of time as an eternal entity. The first argument begins from a conditional: if a particular day were the very day of the flood, then any event occurring today would have occurred on the flood day, or even before that, or eternally — which is a contradiction (خلف). Then it introduces a second argument (وأيضاً): if time is perpetual existence and that concept implies a duration (مدة) and time (زمان) for that thing, then that duration and time would themselves require another duration and time, leading to an infinite regress (تسلسل). The author then considers a second division (والثاني): if time is composed of successive parts, each part can be said to occur 'now'. But if the judgment 'now' requires a container time, then that container also requires another time, again leading to infinite regress. Both arguments aim to show that the very concept of time as an independent entity leads to absurdity.

The Second Reductio: Infinite Regress of Time
Presents two sub-arguments against eternal time leading to infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the concept of time as an independent container leads to an infinite regress of times.

Core Idea
If time requires a container, that container would also need a container ad infinitum.

The second argument tackles the idea of time as a duration or container. If we say something exists perpetually, and that existence implies a duration and time, then that time itself would require another duration, and so on, leading to an infinite regress. Similarly, if time is composed of successive parts, each part's 'now' would require another time. This lesson unpacks these two sub-arguments.

3
Understand the second sub-argument
If time has parts, each part's 'now' requires a container time, again leading to regress.
Both point to infinite regress (تسلسل) which is impossible.

The Indivisible Present: Time as Successive Moments
Introduces the dichotomy and deduces the indivisibility of the present moment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the initial dichotomy of the second argument and the reasoning that leads to time being composed of indivisible moments.

Core Idea
If time's parts are successive, each present moment must be indivisible.

This lesson begins the second proof against real time. The author starts with the necessary fact that time's parts are successive, then poses a crucial either-or. By examining the first option, he shows that any present moment must be indivisible, leading to the conclusion that time is a series of dimensionless nows.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Notice the author's style of proof and the key terms: آن, تعاقب, انقسام.
The passage is dense; read slowly to follow the logic.
2
Identify the dichotomy
The author presents a dichotomy: either some part of time has presence or none does. Focus on the first horn.
The second horn (none present) is not discussed here but implies time is non-existent.
3
Understand the indivisibility argument
If a part has presence, it must be indivisible. Otherwise it would have halves, the first half would precede the second, and the present would not be present—a contradiction.
This shows that 'presence' requires indivisibility.
4
Grasp the conclusion about time
Since each present part is indivisible and time extends by their succession, time is composed of successive indivisible moments (آنات).
This conclusion is the basis for the next lesson's argument.

الحجة الثانية: إنا نعلم بالضرورة أن الأجزاء المفترضة في الزمان لا تحصل معاً، بل حصولها لا يعقل إلا على سبيل التعاقب والتقضي. فنقول: إما أن يكون لشيء من تلك الأجزاء حضور وحصول، أو لا يكون، فإن كان الأول كان واحد من تلك الأجزاء حال حضوره غير منقسم وإلا لحصل له نصفان، ويكون النصف الأول سابقاً على النصف الثاني وحينئذ لا يكون الحاضر حاضراً. هذا خلف. وإذا ثبت أن كل واحد من تلك الأشياء غير قابل للقسمة - ولا شك أن الزمان إنما يمتد بسبب تعاقب تلك الأشياء الحاضرة - لزم كون الزمان مركباً من الآنات المتتالية التي يكون كل واحد منها غير قابل للقسمة ولو كان الأمر كذلك لزم كون الجسم مركباً من الأجزاء التي لا تتجزأ، لأن القدر الذي يتحرك عليه المتحرك من المسافة في الآن الواحد، الذي لا يقبل القسمة - إن كان منقسماً - كانت الحركة إلى نصفه واقعة في نصف ذلك الآن، والحركة الواقعة في النصف الثاني من ذلك الآن، فحينئذ ينتصف ذلك الآن وكنا قد فرضنا أنه لا ينتصف. هذا خلف. فثبت أن القول بكون الزمان مركباً من أشياء حاضرة، كل واحد منها لا ينقسم، يفضي إلى إثبات الجوهر الفرد، وهو باطل. فكان القول بوجود الزمان باطلا.
The second proof: We know necessarily that the parts posited in time do not occur together, rather their occurrence is not conceivable except in the manner of succession and passing away. So we say: Either some of those parts have presence and occurrence, or not. If the first, then one of those parts at the moment of its presence is indivisible—otherwise it would have two halves, and the first half would be prior to the second half, and then the present would not be present. This is a contradiction. And if it is established that each one of those things is indivisible—and there is no doubt that time extends only because of the succession of those present things—then time must be composed of successive moments (ānāt) each of which is indivisible. If that were the case, then body must be composed of indivisible parts, because the distance that the moving body traverses in a single indivisible moment—if it were divisible, then the motion to its half would occur in half of that moment, and the motion occurring in the second half of that moment would then divide that moment, whereas we had assumed it does not divide. This is a contradiction. So it is established that the statement that time is composed of present things, each indivisible, leads to the affirmation of the indivisible atom (al-jawhar al-fard), which is false. Thus, the statement of the existence of time is false.
Source pages p. 11

Commentary
This passage is a second proof (الحجة الثانية) against the existence of time as an independent reality. It begins by stating a necessary truth: the parts of time do not exist simultaneously but only successively. The argument then poses a dichotomy: either some of these parts have actual presence (حضور وحصول) or none do. If some do, then such a part must be indivisible—otherwise it would have halves and the present moment would not be truly present. If it is indivisible, then time is composed of indivisible moments (آنات). The author then shows that this leads to a further conclusion: if time is made of indivisible moments, then motion and space must also be composed of indivisible parts (the atom). This is demonstrated by considering a moving object: if the distance traversed in an indivisible moment were divisible, the moment itself would be divided—contradicting the assumption. Since the indivisible atom (الجوهر الفرد) is considered false in this tradition, the original assumption that time exists is also false. The second horn of the dichotomy (that no part has presence) outright denies time, which is also unacceptable. Thus the proof refutes the reality of time.

From Moments to Atoms: The Refutation of Time
Extends the argument to motion, derives atomism, and concludes the falsity of time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the composition of time from moments leads to the false doctrine of atomism, thereby refuting time's reality.

Core Idea
If time is moments, then motion forces matter into atoms, which is false.

The second part of the proof shows the fatal consequence of accepting that time is made of indivisible moments. By analyzing motion through space, the author demonstrates that it leads to atomism, which is rejected. Thus the original assumption that time exists collapses.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Focus on the chain of reasoning from time as moments to atomism.
This builds on the conclusion of the previous lesson.
2
Follow the motion argument
Consider a moving body traversing a distance in an indivisible moment. If the distance were divisible, the moment would be divided, contradicting its indivisibility.
This shows that the distance must be indivisible as well.
3
Understand the link to atomism
If every distance traversed in an indivisible moment is indivisible, then all bodies are composed of such indivisible parts (الجوهر الفرد).
This is the atomism of the kalam tradition.
4
Grasp the final refutation
Atomism is considered false (باطل) in this context, so the assumption that time exists must also be false.
The argument is a reductio ad absurdum.

The Third Argument: Time and Priority
Sanusi presents a dichotomy on time's nature and argues that priority is independent of time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the traditional argument refutes the necessity of time for the concept of priority, and why both possibilities lead to the same conclusion.

Core Idea
Priority does not depend on time; it is a rational concept.

This passage presents a third proof against the reality of time. The author examines two possibilities—time is originated or eternal—and shows that in either case, priority (قبلية) can exist without time. This challenges the assumption that time is a necessary framework for sequence.

Guided Path
1

Read the Arabic passage carefully with its translation. Notice that the argument begins by dividing the nature of time into two possibilities: originated (حادث) or eternal (قديم).
The passage is incomplete, but the core argument is present.

Priority and simultaneity only make sense if time exists.

This lesson presents the third proof for the intuitive knowledge of time. The proof draws on our everyday understanding of 'before' and 'together' to show that time is a fundamental concept embedded in our rational nature. It then addresses a subtle objection and refutes it, solidifying the argument.

The fourth proof argues that the concepts of motion and rest necessarily imply time.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Motion and rest cannot be conceived without time.

This proof begins with something every rational person knows: a body is either moving or at rest. By analyzing these everyday experiences, the author shows that they already contain the idea of time. Let us follow the argument to see why time must be known intuitively.

The author argues that the meanings of eternal and originated require time, making time intuitively known.

The Sixth Proof: Time's Divisions Prove Its Existence
Sanusi presents the sixth proof that time is known through its necessary divisions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's sixth proof that time is a real external entity based on the necessary knowledge of its divisions.

Core Idea
Time is known to be real because its divisions are necessarily known and cannot belong to physical objects.

Sanusi now presents a sixth proof for the existence of time. The argument is simple: we necessarily know that time can be divided into years, months, days, and hours. These divisions are known with certainty, and the thing that receives these divisions cannot be a physical object like a stone or a color. Therefore, time must be a real entity existing externally.

Our necessary knowledge of motion relations proves time is real.

Second proof that priority cannot be an existing attribute.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If priority is a real attribute, it needs another priority—an infinite chain.

The author presents the second proof against priority being an existing attribute. It argues that priority itself, if real, would require another priority relative to something else, leading to an infinite regress. An objection that priority's relation might be identical to its essence is refuted through logical analysis.
