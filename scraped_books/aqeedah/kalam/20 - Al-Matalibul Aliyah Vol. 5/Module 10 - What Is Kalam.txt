Definition of Tawqit (Timing) and Its Basis in Human Practice
Razi defines tawqit and explains its basis in human practice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's definition of tawqit (timing), its logical structure of pairing an unknown with a known, and how it is validated by sound intuition and original nature.

Core Idea
Timing makes the unknown known by pairing it with the known.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi introduces the concept of tawqit (timing) as a fundamental tool for defining unknown events by linking them to known ones. He grounds this concept in everyday human practice and argues that its validity is affirmed by sound intuition and natural disposition. Understanding this definition is essential for grasping how kalam thinkers approached temporal relationships in theological arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Begin by reading the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the literal English translation to grasp the flow of Razi's argument.
Notice the everyday example of 'I will come when the sun rises.'
2
Analyze the logical structure
Identify the core logical move: an unknown event is paired with a known event, making the unknown known through this pairing. Razi calls this 'qarn' (pairing).
The term 'mawhum' (imagined) refers to the future event not yet actual.
3
Understand the definition from ahl al-tahqiq
Focus on the precise definition: 'pairing an imagined recurring thing with a known recurring thing to remove ambiguity.' Note the key components: recurring, imagined, known, and removal of ambiguity.
'Recurring' (mutajaddid) indicates both events are repeatable.
4
Reflect on validation by intuition and fitra
Razi asserts that sound intuition (badiha sahiha) and original nature (fitra asliyya) affirm the validity of this timing. Consider why he grounds timing in innate human reasoning rather than convention.
This counters the idea that timing is arbitrary.
5
Apply to future and past dates
See how the same logic extends to all future and past dates: they define unknown occurrences by known times. This shows the universality of the concept.
Both future and past dates follow the same principle.

Commentary
This passage defines the concept of tawqit (timing) and explains its basis in common human experience and sound intuition. The author begins by giving an everyday example: a man says 'I will come to you when the sun rises' or 'when spring comes.' He then analyzes the meaning: the man's coming is unknown, while the sun's rising is known, so the unknown event is paired with a known one, making the unknown known through this pairing. Based on this, the people of verification define tawqit as 'pairing an imagined recurring thing with a known recurring thing to remove ambiguity.' The term 'imagined' here refers to the future event that is not yet actual but conceived. 'Recurring' indicates that both the unknown and known events are repeatable or periodic. After the definition, the author explains that sound intuition and original nature affirm the validity of this timing, and it forms the basis for all future and past dates. Future dates define the existence of unknown future events by known future times; past dates define the occurrence of unknown past events by known past times.

Duration and Time: Definitions and the Role of Motion
Defines duration and time, and clarifies motion's role as a measure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between duration and time, and why motion only measures, not creates, them.

Core Idea
Motion measures time; it does not create it.

The author begins by clarifying two key terms—duration and time—which are often confused. He emphasizes that time exists independently, and motion's role is merely to measure it, much like a clock measures a day without creating it. This lesson unpacks these definitions and the crucial analogy.

Guided Path
1
Understand the first term: duration
Read the definition of duration (المدة) and note how the mind becomes aware of it through the succession of present moments.
Duration is described as existent and continuous in itself.
2
Understand the second term: time
Read the definition of time (الزمان) and note its independence from motion.
Time exists whether motion occurs or not.
3
Grasp the analogy of the clock
Analyze the analogy comparing motion's role to a clock: it only estimates, not creates.
The clock does not create the day; similarly, motion does not create time.

Commentary
This passage is a lexical and metaphysical definition of two key terms: duration (المدة) and time (الزمان). The author begins by noting that explaining the difference between these concepts is precise and obscure. For the first term, duration, he restates that it is in its essence existent, abiding, permanent, and continuous. However, our conscious awareness of it arises only through the succession of present moments. This succession gives the impression that some moments are extensions of others, making time seem extended. For the second term, time, the author emphasizes that time exists independently of motion. Motion does not cause time or duration; rather, it only measures or determines them. He draws an analogy with a clock: the clock does not create the day; it only estimates its parts. Similarly, the motion of the celestial sphere does not create time; it only measures it. The passage thus establishes a realist view of time and duration as existing entities, with motion serving as a measure, not a cause.

The Two Philosophical Schools on the Nature of Place
Classifies two main opinions on the nature of place.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two main philosophical opinions on what place (makān) is: whether it is a space that bodies penetrate or the inner surface of the containing body.

Core Idea
The nature of place is a philosophical dispute with only two coherent views.

We all experience motion and change of location, but what is place itself? Philosophers have debated whether place is a kind of empty space that bodies fill, or simply the boundary of the container. In this passage, the author clarifies these two schools and explains the intuitive definition that underlies them.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and observe motion
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how the author begins with the empirical fact of motion and the many names people use for location.
The author lists terms like hayyiz, jiha, makān – these are not all synonyms in technical usage.
2
Understand the working definition of place
The author gives a definition of place that is known by necessity: it is that which contains a thing, from which it departs by motion, cannot hold another simultaneously, and is occupied successively.
This definition is meant to be pre-philosophical and intuitive.
3
Distinguish the two views
The author presents a dichotomy: either place is something the body penetrates (dimension/space) or it is the inner surface of the containing body. Identify these two options clearly.
The first view is attributed to Plato and most rationalists; the second is simply stated as the containing surface.
4
Note the alternative names for the first view
Proponents of the first view sometimes call place 'matter' (hayūlā) or 'form' (ṣūra), because its essence remains while forms change.
This is a brief note on terminology; the passage cuts off here.

Chapter One: On Detailing the Schools of People Regarding It. Know that we observe that the body moves and shifts, and we know by intuition that it moves from one direction to another, from one side to another. People call that from which and to which it moves sometimes al-hayyiz (place), sometimes al-jiha (direction), sometimes al-muḥadhāh (parallelness), sometimes al-janb (side), sometimes al-jānib (aspect), sometimes al-makān (place), and sometimes other names which we have no need to enumerate fully. In summary, the makān (place) of a thing is that in which the thing is, and it separates from it by motion, and nothing else can contain it along with it, and moving things succeed each other in it by way of substitution. Then we say: this thing is either something into which the body's essence penetrates and permeates, or it is not like that, but rather it is the inner surface of the containing body that touches the outer surface of the contained body. Commentary
This passage defines the concept of place (makān) and classifies the two main philosophical opinions about its nature. The author begins by observing that bodies move by intuition from one direction to another. People use various names for the location from which and to which motion occurs, including hayyiz, jiha, muḥadhāh, janb, jānib, and makān. The author then gives a working definition: the place of a thing is that which contains it, from which it departs by motion, cannot accommodate anything else alongside it, and is successively occupied by moving things. The first view identifies place with dimension (bu'd) and space (fada'), attributed to Plato and most rationalists; the second view identifies place with the containing surface. The author asserts that these are the only two refined and intelligible views on place and space.
