The Fourth Argument: Priority and Posteriority Cannot Be Real Attributes
Sanusi argues that priority and posteriority cannot be real attributes due to infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why priority and posteriority cannot be real attributes, based on the dilemma that mutuality either leads to infinite regress or is not an additional attribute.

Core Idea
Priority and posteriority are relative notions, not real attributes—they would cause infinite regress.

In this fourth argument, Sanusi tackles the question of whether relational concepts like 'priority' and 'posteriority' can be real, objective attributes existing in external entities. Using a clever logical move, he shows that if they were real, they would either lead to an infinite regress or become indistinguishable from the essences themselves. Let's follow his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow, then read the English translation. Notice the logical structure: a conditional dilemma followed by an objection and refutation.
The passage is compact; take your time.
2
Understand the two horns of the dilemma
The argument first assumes two relatives exist together. Then it considers whether the mutuality between them is an additional attribute or not. Trace each branch and its consequence.
If additional → infinite regress. If not additional → why not for all relative attributes?
3
Examine the objection and its refutation
The objection says mutuality is just the essences themselves. Sanusi refutes this by noting that in the proposition 'the two relatives exist together,' the predicate is distinct from the subject.
This refutation relies on the principle that predication requires distinction.

The objector presents a reductio argument against the necessity of time for priority.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The objector turns the argument against itself: if priority needs time, then time needs time.

This lesson presents a critical objection to the idea that priority and posteriority necessarily imply time. The objector argues that if we accept that all priority requires time, then the priority of parts of time themselves would require another time, leading to an infinite regress. This challenge forces us to reconsider the relationship between time and priority.

Core Idea

Sanusi presents a proof for the existence of time that starts with the most basic human experience: we divide time into years, months, days, and hours. Time exists because we meaningfully divide duration and compare lengths.

Al-Razi begins with an everyday observation: we count years, months, days, and hours without hesitation. This division is so natural that anyone who doubts it is considered irrational. He then builds a logical argument: the thing being divided must be real because nothingness cannot be partitioned or measured. Commentary
This passage is a rational proof for the existence of time. It begins by noting the common human practice of dividing duration into years, months, days, and hours. This division is self-evidently valid; anyone who denies it is considered irrational. The author then presents a disjunctive syllogism: the entity that undergoes these divisions is either pure nothingness or something existent. The first option is impossible because nothingness cannot be divided into parts, nor can its parts be compared as longer or shorter—yet we clearly judge that a year is longer than a month, etc. Moreover, reason distinguishes past and future in this entity: the past is what was present and has gone, the future is what will become present. If the entity had no presence at all, reason could not apply past/future to it. Since reason does apply these categories, the entity must be real, not a mere nothing. Thus time exists.

Time as a Non-Stable Existent
Al-Razi classifies existents and proves time is non-stable and distinct.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's classification of existents into stable and non-stable, and the proof that time is a non-stable existent distinct from bodies and their accidents.

Core Idea
Time's parts never coexist – this separates it from all stable beings.

Al-Razi begins by introducing a fundamental division among all existents: those whose parts are present together (stable) and those whose parts are not (non-stable). He then applies this to time, showing that its sequential nature makes it non-stable, and uses this to prove that time is neither a body nor any accident of a body. This lesson presents that argument.

Proof that Time is not Motion and Motion Depends on Time

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Motion requires time, but time does not require motion.

Al-Razi continues his investigation into the nature of time by addressing the common identification of time with motion. After dismissing other possibilities, he presents a fresh argument: the very definition of motion involves succession in time, making motion dependent on time. Moreover, we can conceive of time without motion, proving time's independence. This passage solidifies the distinction between time and motion and establishes time as a fundamental reality.

So it is established that its non-existence cannot be conceived except with the assumption of its existence, and whatever is like that, its non-existence is impossible in the intellect by itself. The second: When we conceive that it was non-existent, we have conceived that its non-existence preceded its existence, and that precedence has no meaning except duration and time. So it is established that the intellect cannot conceive of its non-existence. The third: When we say that it was non-existent or will become non-existent, the words 'was' and 'will be' must refer to a past duration and a future duration. The statement of one who says that this is due to narrowness of expression is corrupt speech; rather, this is because the intellect cannot assume its non-existence, and understanding cannot express the estimation of its non-existence. Third Argument: Language Reveals the Impossibility
Third self-evident argument for the eternity of time, based on language.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third self-evident argument, which shows that even our language about time's non-existence presupposes its reality.

Core Idea
Words like 'was' and 'will be' necessarily reference past and future durations.

The third argument examines language. When we say 'time was non-existent' or 'will become non-existent', the words 'was' and 'will be' refer to past and future time. Some might object that this is just a limitation of language, but the passage rebuts that: it is not a linguistic quirk but a reflection of the intellect's inability to truly assume time's non-existence. The conclusion is that time's removal is rejected by clear reason and understanding.
