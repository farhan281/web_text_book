Refutation of Abu al-Barakat's Definition of Time
The author refutes Abu al-Barakat's definition of time as measure of existence using a reductio ad absurdum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why defining time as the measure of existence leads to logical absurdity.

Core Idea
If time is the measure of existence, each enduring thing would have its own time, making many times impossible.

Abu al-Barakat proposed that time is the measure of existence. The author here shows that this definition is ambiguous and, once clarified, leads to a contradiction. Let's follow the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the author starts by calling the statement ambiguous and condensed.
2
Identify the core claim
Abu al-Barakat says time is the measure of existence. The author interprets this as 'measure of the extension of existence'.
3
Follow the reductio ad absurdum
The author argues that extension of existence is continuance. Continuance either is not additional to the thing or is an attribute. If it is not additional, time would be the thing itself; if it is an attribute, each thing has its own continuance, leading to many times.
Pay attention to the conditional reasoning.
4
Understand the conclusion
The author concludes that many times would have to exist simultaneously, which is impossible, so the definition is false.
This is a common method in kalam: showing a view leads to impossibility.

As for the statement of Abu al-Barakat that time is an expression of the measure of existence—this is ambiguous and condensed speech. The best of his statements is that it is said: what is meant is that time is the measure of the extension of existence. If that is what is meant, then it is false. That is because the extension of existence is an expression of the thing's continuance, permanence, and persistence. This continuance and permanence either is not an additional matter to the essence of the enduring thing, or if it is additional, still the continuance and permanence of each thing is a quality subsisting in it. Also, the continuance and permanence of each thing is other than the continuance and permanence of another. So if time were an expression of this meaning, it would necessitate that the number of times gathered in this single hour would be according to the number of enduring things in this hour. But we have indicated that the statement of the gathering of many times at once is impossible.

Commentary
This passage refutes the view of Abu al-Barakat that time is the measure of existence. The author first notes that the statement is ambiguous. The best interpretation is that time is the measure of the extension of existence. But this is false because the extension of existence is just the continuance of a thing, which either is identical to the thing or an additional attribute. If it is identical, then time would be the thing itself, which is not the case. If it is an attribute, then each thing has its own continuance, so time would be multiple, one for each enduring thing, leading to an impossibility of many times coexisting at once. The author concludes by noting they have already proven that many times cannot gather at once.

Defending Time as a Self-Subsistent Substance
A defense of the ancient philosophers' view that time is a self-subsistent substance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author defends the ancient philosophers' view that time is a self-subsistent substance using an analogy with the unchanging essence of God.

Core Idea
Time can be an eternal substance whose relations change, just as God's essence remains unchanged despite changing relations.

The ancient philosophers held that time is a self-subsistent substance, independent in its essence. The later philosophers objected that time is flowing and ever-renewed, so it cannot be a permanent substance. In this passage, the author presents a defense by arguing that time's essence can be eternal while its relations to events change, using the example of God's relations to creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation

The passage ends mid-sentence, but the argument is complete.
2
Understand the later objection
The later philosophers argue that time cannot be a substance because it is flowing and ever-renewed. Identify their reasoning.
3
Analyze the author's counter-argument
The author claims that time's essence is permanent; only its relations to events change. Find the analogy with the Necessary Existence (God) and see how it supports this claim.
4
Consider the conclusion

Extremist View: Time as the God of the World
Describes an extreme group among those who say time is necessary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the extreme view that identifies time with God, its argument, and why it is considered excessive.

Core Idea
Some went so far as to claim time itself is the god of the world.

As we explore the various opinions on time's nature, we encounter an extreme position among those who affirm time as necessary in itself. This group argued that because everything depends on time for its duration while time depends on nothing, time must be the ultimate director—indeed, the god of the world. Let's examine their claim and its basis.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage

The passage is incomplete but gives the core claim and start of proof.
2
Understand the argument
The proof is that everything depends on time for its perpetual existence, while time's own perpetual existence depends on nothing else. This leads to the conclusion that time is the director of all.
3
Recognize the exaggeration
Note that the author labels this view as extreme and excessive. Why would identifying time with God be problematic?
This highlights the boundaries of orthodox kalam.
4
Connect to the broader debate
This view is part of a larger discussion on time's necessity. It shows how far some rational arguments can lead away from orthodoxy.
