Core Idea
Not every 'now' comes after time; some 'now' precedes and produces time.

This passage tackles a subtle point in the philosophy of time: two different kinds of 'now'. The philosophers debated whether time is a continuous quantity, and what the 'now'—the indivisible instant—represents. The author distinguishes between the 'now' that results from dividing time and the 'now' that flows to generate time, linking each to different ancient theories.

2

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Time is one independent reality; its different names reflect relations to different kinds of existents.

This passage is an enumeration of the first five properties (خصائص) of the temporal categories: past, present, and future. The author begins with a chapter heading stating he will discuss the branches of the past, present, and future, and that there are ten matters. Refuting Place as Substance or Accident
Refutation that place is a substance or accident

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why, according to kalam, place cannot be an existent entity—whether as an independent body or a dependent accident.

Core Idea
Place is not an extramental reality; both substance and accident views lead to absurdities.

Before settling what place truly is, the mutakallim first eliminates wrong possibilities. He reasons: if place exists and can be pointed to, it must be either independent (a substance) or dependent (an accident). In this passage, he systematically refutes both, showing that each leads to impossible consequences such as infinite regress or mutual inherence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the logical division
The mutakallim begins by dividing every existent pointed to into two categories: independent (istiqlal) or dependent (taba'iyyah). Grasp this key distinction.
This division is exhaustive; there is no third option.
2
First possibility: place as a body
Read the argument that if place is a body, it would need another place, leading to an infinite chain of bodies. Then consider the sub-argument about two bodies: if one occurs in another, either they just touch or interpenetrate—both impossible.
The touch option reduces place to a surface (Aristotle's view), not void space.
3
Second possibility: place as an accident
If place is a dependent existent (accident), it would inhere in a body. But the placed object also inheres in place, causing each to be both a locus and an occupant—an absurd mutual inherence.
An accident cannot be a locus for a substance.
4
Synthesize the refutation
With both possibilities refuted, conclude that place is not an existent pointed to at all—it is a mental consideration, not an external reality.
This denial is only extramental; place as a concept remains useful.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Contact defined by shared boundaries allows interpenetration through logical necessity.

This lesson presents a powerful objection against the Peripatetic definition of contact. The author shows that the definition, if analyzed rigorously, leads to a series of logical dilemmas. Guided Path

Identify the definition of contact

Assume a surface touches a surface. Their boundaries (lines) are together. If the lines do not remain during contact, a single shared line forms, which is union (ittisal), not contact. This contradicts the assumption of contact.
The definition requires that the extremities remain, so this horn is eliminated.

If the boundaries remain, either one penetrates the other completely or not. If no penetration, they are separate, and indication to each differs, contradicting the definition. If one completely penetrates the other, then consider the total magnitude.

If one line completely penetrates the other, the total magnitude either increases or stays the same. If it increases, penetration is not complete; if it stays the same, then two extents interpenetrate without increase. This is conceivable.
This logical possibility opens the door to interpenetration.
5

The author argues that if such interpenetration is conceivable for lines, then it is also conceivable for the dimension of a body and the dimension of place, thus supporting the possibility of interpenetration in physics.

The Dilemma of Remaining Boundaries: Penetration or Separation
Logical sub-divisions of remaining boundaries during contact.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that if boundaries remain during contact, they must either completely interpenetrate or remain separate, both leading to contradictions.

Core Idea
Remaining boundaries force an impossible choice between separation and penetration.

Having established that boundaries must remain during contact, the author now examines a crucial distinction: do the two lines vanish into one another or not? Each option leads to a logical contradiction. This lesson unpacks these two sub-cases and shows why the Aristotelian definition faces a serious dilemma.

Guided Path
1
Recall that boundaries must remain
From the previous lesson, contact requires that the two boundaries (lines) remain during contact. Now we ask: what happens to these two lines?
2
Explore the first sub-case: one line does not vanish in the other
If the lines do not interpenetrate, they remain separate. Then the sensible indication of each is distinct, contradicting the definition that they are perceived together.
This is a contradiction (khalaf).
3
Explore the second sub-case: one line vanishes completely in the other
If one line completely penetrates the other, then we must check whether the total quantity increases. If it does, the entirety of one line is not fully penetrating the other, again a contradiction.
The author will address the sub-case where quantity does not increase in the next lesson.
