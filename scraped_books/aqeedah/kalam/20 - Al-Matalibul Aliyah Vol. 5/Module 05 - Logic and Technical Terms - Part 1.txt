Why Time Cannot Be Made of Indivisible Instants
Proof by reductio that time cannot be composed of indivisible instants.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the reductio argument that time composed of indivisible instants leads to atomism, and why that conclusion is considered false.

Core Idea
If time were indivisible instants, motion would force matter into indivisible atoms—a false view.

This lesson examines a key kalam argument against the idea that time consists of indivisible moments. The argument shows that such a view leads to the doctrine of the indivisible atom (jawhar fard), which was rejected by many theologians. By tracing the logic, you will see how the structure of motion and distance forces a contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the English translation. Let the logical flow emerge naturally.
The passage is a compact reductio; notice the conditional 'if...then' structure.
2
Identify the assumption and conditional
Find the initial assumption: that time is composed of indivisible instants. Then see how motion in such an instant implies indivisible distance.
The phrase 'لزم كون الجسم مركباً من الأجزاء التي لا تتجزأ' is the key consequence.
3
Trace the contradiction
The argument shows that if the distance were divisible, the instant would be divided, contradicting the assumption. This leads to the conclusion that the premise (time composed of indivisible instants) must be false.
The term 'خلف' (contradiction) signals the reductio.

The successive instants, each of which is [indivisible], and if it were so, then it would necessitate that the body is composed of indivisible parts, because the distance that the moving object traverses in a single instant, which is indivisible—if it were divisible, then the motion to half of it would occur in half of that instant, and the motion occurring in the second half of that instant, then that instant would be divided, whereas we had assumed it is not divided. This is a contradiction. Thus it is established that the statement that time is composed of present things, each of which is indivisible, leads to affirming the indivisible atom (al-jawhar al-fard), which is false. So the statement of the existence of time is false.

Commentary
This passage presents a reductio ad absurdum argument against the view that time is composed of indivisible instants. It assumes that if time were made of such instants, then motion would require that the distance traversed in an instant be indivisible, leading to the conclusion that bodies are composed of indivisible atoms, which is held to be false. Therefore, time cannot be composed of such instants, and thus the existence of time in that sense is negated.

Proof that time cannot have a present part, leading to complete denial of time.

Core Idea
Without a present 'now', time cannot exist at all.

This passage presents a profound argument: if time has no 'present' moment, then past and future lose meaning. The author argues that because past and future are defined relative to the present, their existence depends on it. Consequently, denying the present negates time entirely.

The Dilemma of Time: Created or Eternal?
Proof that time cannot be created or eternal without contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the existence of time as an independent entity is rationally impossible according to this kalam argument.

Core Idea
Time cannot be created or eternal without contradiction — so time is not an independent reality.

This passage presents a powerful dilemma against the reality of time. If time were created, its own priority would require a time before time. If it were eternal, its changing nature contradicts the unchanging nature of eternity. This argument forces us to reconsider the very concept of time.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Observe the two-horned structure of the argument.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Analyze the first horn: time as created
Examine the reasoning: if time is created, its non-existence preceded its existence. This priority cannot be temporal because time did not exist. Hence, priority does not require time.
This refutes the idea that time is necessary for priority.
3
Analyze the second horn: time as eternal
If time is eternal, it involves succession and change, which require being preceded by another. But eternity (al-azali) by definition cannot be preceded. Thus, the combination is impossible.
The argument also adds a secondary point about an infinite regress of containers.
4
Understand the conclusion: negation of time
The passage concludes that the illusion of time as a container is false, and this necessitates the definitive negation of time as an independent entity.
This is the ultimate goal of the proof.

The third proof: This time is either created (hadith) or eternal (qadim). If it is created, then its non-existence preceded its existence, and this precedence is not temporal, because this discussion occurs concerning the non-existence which is prior to the existence of all time, and when this non-existence obtains, time has no existence; so the meaning of priority and precedence occurred without time obtaining. Thus we know that the occurrence of priority and precedence does not depend on the existence of time. If we say time is eternal, we say: This is false. And if we assume it, the goal is achieved. As for its falsity, it is because the existence of time is only established by the sequence of priorities and posteriorities, and this sequence and succession is change (taghayyur), and its essence necessitates being preceded by another, whereas the essence of the eternal (al-azali) is incompatible with being preceded by another, and combining them is impossible. So combining the reality of time and the reality of eternity is impossible. As for the explanation that if we assume it, the goal is achieved: its exposition is that the eternal is that which persists eternally, and just as the created suggests the establishment of two successive times, in the first of which is the thing's non-existence and in the second its existence, so too the eternal suggests the existence of a continuous time from eternity to eternity, in which that eternal thing occurs. And if we judge time to be eternal, that judgment would suggest that time occurs in another time. If this illusion is false, then let such an illusion also be false regarding creation, for there is no rational difference between the two cases. Then the upshot is that the fact that something is continuous and that something is changing—even if it suggests the existence of a time that is a container (zarf) and receptacle (wi'a) for that continuity sometimes and for that change at other times—nevertheless that illusion is false, and that imagination is invalid. And that necessitates the definitive negation of time. That is what was sought.

Commentary
This passage presents the third argument against the existence of time as a dilemma. First, if time is created, the priority of its non-existence over its existence cannot be temporal because time did not exist; thus priority does not require time. Second, if time is eternal, it involves change and succession, which imply being preceded, yet eternity (al-azali) by definition cannot be preceded—so the two are incompatible. Moreover, even if we suppose time is eternal, the notion of a container for time leads to an infinite regress or a false illusion, and the same reasoning applies to created time. Therefore, the concept of time as an independent entity is false.

Priority Does Not Require a Container
Proof that priority does not require a temporal container.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the priority of events does not necessitate a temporal container and how the argument uses an infinite regress to prove this.

Core Idea
Priority is intrinsic to events, not dependent on a container.

This passage presents the fourth argument against the necessity of time as a container. The argument begins with an obvious observation: yesterday's event is prior to today's. If this priority required a container, then that container would itself need another container, leading to an infinite regress. Since that is absurd, priority must be an intrinsic relation between events.

First Proof: Priority as Attribute of Non-Existence
First proof that priority is an attribute of non-existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why priority cannot be an existent attribute based on the argument that it is an attribute of non-existence.

Core Idea
Priority is an attribute of non-existence, so it cannot be an existent attribute.

The first proof begins with a simple observation from plain reason: every originated thing is preceded by its non-existence. This suggests that priority itself is a quality of non-existence rather than a real attribute. Let's see how the author establishes this.

Then we say: That which indicates that priority and posteriority cannot be among existing things are aspects: The first: Indeed, plain reason judges that every originated thing is preceded by its non-existence, and that its non-existence is prior to its existence. This necessitates that priority, precedence, and being earlier are attributes of non-existence, and whatever is an attribute of non-existence is impossible to be an existent attribute, because of the impossibility of an existent attribute subsisting in pure non-existence and pure negation. So it is established that priority and posteriority are not among existent attributes. The second: If priority were an existent attribute, then that existent attribute, in relation to other than itself, would either be prior, simultaneous, or posterior, and an infinite regress would follow. It cannot be said: Why is it not permissible that priority, in relation to other than itself, even if it is prior, yet its being so is identical to its essence, not an additional attribute to the essence? Because we say: When we judge that priority is prior to something else, here the essence of priority is the subject, then we judge that it is prior to something else which is the predicate, and the predicate of a proposition is necessarily other than its subject. So it is established that if priority were an existent attribute, its being prior in relation to something else would be impossible for it to be identical to its essence, but it must be something different from its essence. The third: Indeed, priority and posteriority are two relations. Each one of them is predicated relative to the other, and it has been established that the two relatives exist together in external objects and in minds. So if they were existent, they would necessarily exist together, and if they existed together, their subjects would occur together, necessarily because a relation does not exist alone, but only exists when its described thing and its subject exist. Then it would be necessary to say: Posteriority necessitates that they exist together, so it would be necessary to say: A thing, insofar as it is prior to another, must exist with it, which is impossible. The fourth: We say: Since it is established that the two relatives exist together, so this togetherness, if it is an additional attribute to them, then that additional thing would also be with them, so this second togetherness would be additional, and an infinite regress would follow. And if it is not an additional attribute, then why is the like of this not permissible in all other togethernesses and priorities? It cannot be said: That the two relatives togetherness is identical to their essences, because we say: When we say 'the two relatives exist together', it is a proposition whose subject is our saying 'the two relatives', and its predicate is our saying 'exist together', and the predicate in every proposition is other than the subject, due to the impossibility of predicating one thing by one consideration upon itself. And upon this supposition, this excuse is invalidated. So it is established by this aspect that priority and posteriority cannot be among the existent attributes in external objects.

Commentary
This passage presents four independent proofs (wujuh) demonstrating that priority (qabliyya) and posteriority (ba'diyya) are not existent attributes (sifat mawjuda) in external reality. The author's overarching claim is that these temporal relations are not real properties inhering in things. First proof: Plain reason tells us that every originated thing is preceded by its non-existence; thus priority is an attribute of non-existence, and an existent attribute cannot subsist in pure non-existence. Therefore priority and posteriority are not existent attributes. Second proof: If priority were an existent attribute, it would itself relate to other things as prior, simultaneous, or posterior, leading to infinite regress. The author refutes a potential objection that priority's being prior might be identical to its essence, by analyzing the propositional structure: the subject (essence of priority) and predicate (being prior to something) are distinct, so the relation is additional, not identical. Third proof: Priority and posteriority are relational concepts (idafatan); relatives exist together. If they were existent, they would have to exist simultaneously, but then a prior thing would exist with its posterior, which is impossible. Fourth proof: Relatives exist together. If the togetherness is an additional attribute, it leads to regress; if not, why not allow similar togetherness in all cases? The author rejects the claim that togetherness is identical to essences by again appealing to the subject-predicate distinction in propositions. Thus all four proofs conclude that priority and posteriority are not real attributes in external objects (a'yan).

Second Proof: Infinite Regress Argument
Second proof: regress from assuming priority is existent.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If priority were existent, it would need another priority, causing regress.

The second proof takes a different approach: assuming priority is an existent attribute, it must relate to other things. This relation would itself require explanation, leading to an infinite chain. The author also refutes a potential objection by analyzing propositional structure.

Third Proof: Relational Nature and Simultaneity
Third proof: relational nature forces simultaneity, contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why priority and posteriority cannot be existent because they are relations that would require simultaneous existence, which is absurd.

Core Idea
Priority and posteriority are relational; relatives exist together, so a prior would need to exist with its posterior.

The third proof draws on the nature of relations (iḍāfa). Priority and posteriority are correlatives: each is defined relative to the other. Since correlatives exist simultaneously, if they were real attributes, they would have to exist together, but a prior thing cannot coexist with its posterior in the same moment.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read carefully. The argument hinges on the concept of 'iḍāfa' (relation) and the principle that relatives exist together.
2
Understand the relational nature
Priority and posteriority are a pair of relatives. Each is only meaningful relative to the other.
3
Apply the simultaneity principle
Relatives exist at the same time. If priority and posteriority were real, they would have to coexist, but that would mean a prior thing exists at the same time as a posterior thing, which is contradictory.
Note the phrase 'واجتمعا معا'.

The Objection That Time Is a Distinct Cause and Its Refutation
Refutation of the objection that time is distinct from motion's measure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two-fold refutation of the claim that time is a distinct cause of motion's measure, and why this refutation is crucial for defending the definition of time as the measure of motion.

Core Idea
The objector first claims time is the measure of motion, then calls it a cause—contradiction.

This passage presents a typical kalam objection followed by a crisp two-part refutation. The Eighth Proof: Measure of Motion Cannot Exist
Al-Razi refutes the existence of a measure of motion's extension by negating all temporal modes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Razi's trilemma argument that the measure of motion's extension cannot exist because it cannot be located in any temporal mode.

Core Idea
If a measure of motion existed, it would have to be present, past, or future—but each is impossible.

Al-Razi continues his critique of Aristotelian physics by targeting the notion that time is simply the measure of motion. He argues that even if such a measure existed, it could not be located in any temporal mode. This proof further undermines the idea that motion itself generates time. Let's examine the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the literal translation. Notice the trilemma: the measure must be present, past, or future.
Pay attention to the structure: a conditional statement followed by refutation of each option.
2
Analyze the refutation of the present
Understand why the present moment cannot contain a divisible measure. The present is indivisible, but the measure is divisible—a contradiction.
The key concept is that the present moment ('al-ḥāl al-ḥāḍir') does not admit division (inqisām).
3
Analyze the refutation of past and future
Identify the two reasons given: past and future are non-existent (ma'dūm), and non-existence cannot bear attributes of existence like division and extension.
Note that the first reason is ontological (non-existence), the second is about predicates (attributes).
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Combine the refutations: the measure cannot exist in any temporal mode. Therefore, the very notion of a 'measure of motion's extension' is impossible.
This argument strengthens the kalām view that time is not merely a measure of motion.

Time is Not Motion: Al-Razi's Argument
Al-Razi argues that time is distinct from motion using a conditional thought experiment.

How do we know time is not just the movement of the heavens? Al-Razi presents a clever argument: if time were identical to celestial motion, then saying 'the sphere was non-existent a million years ago' would lead to a logical contradiction. Since that statement is meaningful and not absurd, time must be something else. This lesson unpacks that reasoning.
