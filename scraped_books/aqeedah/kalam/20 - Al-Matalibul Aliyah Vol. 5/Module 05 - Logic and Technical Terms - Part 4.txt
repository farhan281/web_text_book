First Argument Against Void and Its Refutation
The author presents the first argument and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first argument for the existence of void and its refutation, which demonstrates that what appears as empty space is actually occupied by a body.

Core Idea
The supposed void is not empty—comparability proves it is filled.

In this chapter, the author recounts the arguments of those who deny void. The first argument attempts to prove void exists because we can compare larger and smaller voids. However, the refutation shows that such comparability actually means the void is a body, not an empty dimension. This passage walks through the logic step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation carefully. Notice the structure: first the argument, then the dilemma, then the conclusion.
The passage begins with 'الحجة الأولى' marking it as the first argument.
2
Understand the argument from comparability
The argument says that voids of different sizes (basin, portico, city, heaven-earth) can be compared as larger or smaller. Therefore, they are existent dimensions.
This is a common argument: if something can be compared, it must be real.
3
Examine the dilemma and its resolution
The magnitude of the void is either self-subsistent or it is the magnitude of the occupant. The first leads to impossible interpenetration, so the second must hold. Hence the void is actually a body.
Interpenetration means two dimensions occupying the same space, which is absurd.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The refutation concludes that void is filled (ملاء), and Aristotle's comment reinforces that 'void' is an empty term.
This refutes the need for empty space altogether.

Commentary
This passage is the beginning of the sixth chapter, which narrates the arguments of those who deny the void (al-khalāʼ) and their refutations. The first argument is presented: The void assumed between two ends of a basin is smaller than that between the walls of a portico, and so on up to the void between heaven and earth. Since these voids can be compared as larger or smaller, they are existent dimensions (buʻd) and magnitudes (miqdār). The magnitude in question must either be self-subsistent (qāʼim bi-nafsih) and independent, distinct from the magnitude of the occupant (al-mutamakkin) so that the occupant's magnitude penetrates into it, or it must be nothing other than the magnitude of the occupant. The first option leads to the absurdity of interpenetration of dimensions (tadākhul al-abʻād), so the second option is accepted. Therefore, what is thought to be void is actually a body (e.g., air), so void is filled (malāʼ). The author concludes by citing Aristotle, who said 'void' is a term empty of meaning.

The First Proof Against the Void: Hierarchy of Magnitudes
First proof against the void using comparative magnitudes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the first argument against the void uses comparative magnitudes to demonstrate that what is thought to be void is actually a body.

Core Idea
The supposed void between objects is actually a body, making 'void' an empty term.

This passage presents the first of several proofs against the existence of void (khala'). The argument ingeniously uses a hierarchy of spaces—from a basin to the heavens—to demonstrate that any alleged void must have magnitude, and that magnitude must belong to a body. As you read, notice how the argument forces the conclusion that void is ultimately a plenum (mala').

Guided Path
1
Examine the hierarchy of voids
The argument posits voids of increasing size: between basin ends, chamber walls, city walls, and between heaven and earth. Notice that this implies comparability.
This step sets up the inference that void has measurable magnitude.
2
Infer existence and magnitude
Since voids can be compared as smaller or larger, they must be existent and have magnitude (miqdar) and extension (bu'd).
3
Analyze the dilemma about magnitude
The magnitude of the void is either independent of the located body or identical to the body's magnitude. Consider both possibilities.
4
Reject independent magnitude
If the void's magnitude were independent, the body's magnitude would penetrate it, causing interpenetration of dimensions (tadakhul al-ab'ad), which is impossible.
5
Conclude that void is body
The only remaining possibility is that the void's magnitude is the body's magnitude itself. Therefore, what was thought to be void is actually a body, making the void a plenum.
The argument ends with Aristotle's quip that 'void' is an empty term.

Second Premise: Parts of the Void May Differ in Essence
The author presents the Second Premise: critiquing necessary similarity of void parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that sharing properties like dimensionality does not guarantee a common essence, so the world's location in one part of the void is not necessitated by similarity.

Core Idea
Shared properties don't imply shared essence.

The Second Premise challenges the assumption that all parts of the void are identical in essence. The author argues that even if parts share the capacity for dimensions, they may differ in their true nature. This undermines any claim that the world's placement in a particular part of the void is necessary due to uniformity.

Commentary
This passage presents three sequential premises (muqaddimat) that critique the claim that the world's location in the void is necessary. The Second Premise argues against the assumption that all parts of the void are similar in essence. It notes that even if parts share the capacity for dimensions, they might differ in essence, so the world's placement in one part rather than another could be due to essential variance. Thus, necessity is not proven. The Third Premise grants similarity in essence but raises the possibility of a free agent (al-fa'il al-mukhtar) choosing a location by sheer will. This turns the question into the broader debate between a capable agent and a necessitating cause, which has been addressed earlier. The Fourth Premise grants that the idea of a free agent is rejected by opponents, but then points to observable particularization in natural bodies (animals, plants, minerals) that have specific forms, places, and sizes. Since these particularizations are not logically necessary, they must be explained either by no determining factor, or by a free agent, or by an infinite regress of events where each prepares the matter for the next. The same three options are then applied to the entire corporeal world's location in the void. Thus, the premises collectively undermine any necessitarian explanation of the world's spatial location.

And that has already been discussed thoroughly.

The Fourth Premise: Suppose the statement of the agent by choice is false according to you, except that we observe that every one of animal, plant, and mineral bodies is particularized with a specific form, a specific location, and a specific quantity, while it is not intellectually impossible for the opposite of what occurred to occur. So the particularization of each of these bodies with its specific attributes is either without a determining factor, or the determining factor is the agent by choice. Or if that determining factor is a necessitating cause by essence, except that every event is preceded by another event, and the preceding event prepared the matter to receive the subsequent event. And according to this arrangement, before every event there is another event without a first. In summary, if we understand these particularizations in these bodies as one of these divisions, then why is the like not permissible in the particularization of the entire corporeal world to a specific place in the infinite void? That is, it is said: It occurred in that specific location either without a determining factor, or the determining factor is the agent by choice, or because this world, before it occurred in this location, was occurring in another location of the void, and its occurrence in that location of the void prepared it to move from it to this second location of the void, and so on without a first: that everything they say about specific accidents occurring in sublunary bodies, we say the same regarding the particularization of the entire world to the specific part of the void.

Commentary
This passage presents three sequential premises (muqaddimat) that critique the claim that the world's location in the void is necessary. The Second Premise argues against the assumption that all parts of the void are similar in essence. It notes that even if parts share the capacity for dimensions, they might differ in essence, so the world's placement in one part rather than another could be due to essential variance. Thus, necessity is not proven. The Third Premise grants similarity in essence but raises the possibility of a free agent (al-fa'il al-mukhtar) choosing a location by sheer will. This turns the question into the broader debate between a capable agent and a necessitating cause, which has been addressed earlier. The Fourth Premise grants that the idea of a free agent is rejected by opponents, but then points to observable particularization in natural bodies (animals, plants, minerals) that have specific forms, places, and sizes. Since these particularizations are not logically necessary, they must be explained either by no determining factor, or by a free agent, or by an infinite regress of events where each prepares the matter for the next. The same three options are then applied to the entire corporeal world's location in the void. Thus, the premises collectively undermine any necessitarian explanation of the world's spatial location.

Fourth Premise: Observable Particularization Applies to the World
The author presents the Fourth Premise: analogy from bodies to the cosmos.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the particularization observed in physical bodies (form, place, size) can be explained by the same options—no cause, free agent, or infinite regress—and this reasoning applies to the entire world's location in the void.

Core Idea
Worldly particularization mirrors cosmic placement.

The Fourth Premise builds on observable facts: every natural body (animal, plant, mineral) has a specific form, place, and size, even though alternatives are logically possible. Three explanatory options are offered—no determining factor, free agent, or infinite regress. The same options are then applied to the entire corporeal world's position in the infinite void.

Answer to Remaining Four Proofs: Plenum, Not Void Impossibility
Author states remaining four proofs only indicate occurrence of plenum, not impossibility of void.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the remaining four proofs for void's impossibility actually only establish the occurrence of plenum, not its necessity or void's impossibility.

Core Idea
Proving plenum exists does not prove void impossible.

The author now addresses the final four proofs that opponents use to argue for the impossibility of void. His answer is concise but crucial: even if these proofs are valid, they only show that plenum (ملاء) occurs in the universe, not that void (خلاء) cannot exist. This distinction prevents an unwarranted leap from observation to necessity.
