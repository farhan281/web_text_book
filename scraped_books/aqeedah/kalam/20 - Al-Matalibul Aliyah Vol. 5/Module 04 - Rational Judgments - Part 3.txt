Refuting the Claim that Void is Pure Non-Existence
The author refutes the claim that void is pure non-existence with three proofs, two complete and one incomplete.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two rational proofs that void must be an existent with magnitude and extension, not pure non-existence.

Core Idea
If void has measure and can be pointed to, it cannot be nothing.

The author tackles the claim that empty space is pure nothing. He argues that if void existed, it would have magnitude and extension. Two clear proofs are given: one based on ratios, another on sensory indication. The third proof is incomplete due to a page break, but the first two sufficiently establish the point.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice the author's structure: he states the false claim, asserts his counterclaim, then provides proofs.
The third proof is cut off; focus on the first two.
2
Understand Proof One: Ratio
The first proof uses common sense: a void of one cubit is half of a void of two cubits. Pure non-existence cannot have halves or thirds, so void is not pure non-existence.
This proof relies on the necessity that quantified things are real.
3
Understand Proof Two: Sensory Indication
The second proof says we can point to space and say 'from here to there'. Whatever can be pointed to by the senses must exist, so space is not nothing.
This proof grounds the reality of space in sensory experience.
4
Note the incomplete third proof
The third proof begins by saying that space is judged such that a body obtains. The argument is cut off, but it likely concludes that space must exist as a receptacle.
Do not over-interpret the missing part.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation. The author first states the false claim that void and space are pure non-existence (ʿadam maḥḍ) and absolute negation (nafy ṣirf). He then asserts the contrary: if void existed, it would be an existent with magnitude and extension, not nothing. This is the position of the theologians (mutakallimūn). He then provides three proofs. The first proof is based on ratio: a void of one cubit is half of a void of two cubits, a third of three cubits, etc. Anything that can be halved, thirded, or quartered is measured and quantified, whereas pure non-existence cannot be described as having halves or being more or less. Therefore void cannot be pure non-existence. The second proof appeals to sensory indication: one can point to space and say 'from here to there', indicating length and magnitude. Whatever can be pointed to by the senses cannot be pure non-existence, so space must be existent and extended. The third proof begins but is cut off in the source; it starts by stating that space is judged such that a body obtains (meaning it is the receptacle in which bodies exist). The argument as a whole follows the kalam method of establishing the positive reality of what some claim to be nothing.

The Fourth Argument: The Shared Boundary of Void
Presents the fourth proof for the existence of space based on the common boundary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that the existence of a shared boundary in the void implies that the void itself is a real existent with magnitude and extension.

Core Idea
A shared boundary only exists if the thing it boundaries is real; therefore the void is real.

This lesson examines the fourth argument for the existence of space (void). The author uses a clever analogy: just as a shared boundary between two surfaces proves the surfaces are real, so too does a shared boundary in the void prove the void itself is real. We'll walk through the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the argument uses an analogy with a physical surface.
2
Identify the key terms

These terms are crucial for understanding the argument.
3
Follow the analogy
The author compares the void to a surface. In both cases, the phrase 'from here to there' implies a shared boundary. That boundary must be real because it is a property of a real thing.
The strength of the analogy lies in the symmetry between the two cases.
4
Draw the conclusion
Since the shared boundary of the void functions exactly like that of a surface, the void must be a real existent with magnitude and extension.
This conclusion directly opposes those who deny the reality of space.

Commentary
This passage presents a definitional argument for the existence of space (al-khala') as a real entity. It begins by considering the phrase 'the void from here to there.' The key observation is that the words 'here' and 'there' refer to a common boundary (fasl mushtarak) between the specified void and the void outside it. An analogy is drawn with a physical surface: when we say 'this surface from here to there,' the terms 'here' and 'there' refer to a shared boundary that actually exists between that surface and an adjacent surface. Since the shared boundary in the case of a surface is a real existent (because it is part of a real surface), the same reasoning applies to the void: the shared boundary must be real, and therefore the void itself must be an existent with magnitude and extension. The argument assumes that the void is not a pure nothingness, because nothingness cannot have boundaries.

The Fifth Argument: From the Mutakallimun's Own Judgments

Core Idea
The mutakallimun's own judgments about places prove places are real.

This argument turns the mutakallimun's own practice against them. When they discuss motion, they describe places as distinct, adjacent, and having variable distances. The argument forces the conclusion that such real relations cannot belong to pure nothingness. Let's see how the passage builds this case.

The Sixth Proof: Directional Qualities Prove Places Exist
Sanusi argues that directional qualities entail the existence of places.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the existence of directional qualities (above, below, etc.) logically entails the existence of places (al-ahiyaz).

Core Idea
Directional qualities cannot be mere negations; they prove places must exist.

Sanusi presents the sixth proof for the existence of places. He argues that the different qualities of direction (above, below, right, left) cannot be explained away as mere absences or negations. If they were, they would each be negations of another negation, which is a positive existence. Alternatively, if they are real attributes, they require a real existent subject. This passage shows that places must be real.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the literal translation. Let the author's logic unfold.
Notice the two prongs of the argument: the 'negation of negation' and the 'attribute inherence'.
2
Understand the double negation argument
Sanusi first considers what happens if we treat 'above' as a negation of 'below'. He shows that leads to each being a negation of a negation, which is actually an existent. Trace this logical step.
3
Understand the attribute inherence argument
Sanusi then considers the alternative: if directional qualities are real attributes, they need a real subject. Since they cannot inhere in nothingness, the places must be existent.
Consider why an existent attribute cannot inhere in pure non-existence.

This passage presents a sixth proof focusing on the directional properties assigned to places (e.g., above, below, right, left). The argument first rules out the possibility that these properties are mere negations. Alternatively, if the directional qualities are taken as real positive attributes (sifat mawjuda), then they must inhere in a real subject, because an existent attribute cannot subsist in pure non-existence. Therefore, the places (al-ahiyaz) that bear these qualities must themselves be existent. The author concludes that these arguments and their like are clear proofs that these places must exist.

Places Are Mental Constructs: The Mutakallimun's Proof
Presents the mutakallimun's refutation: places are mental, not real, and a proof against their reality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the mutakallimun's argument that places are mental constructs and why they cannot be real bodies.

Core Idea
Places exist only in the mind, not in external reality.

The mutakallimun respond to the claim that places are real by arguing that they are mere mental constructs. They present a proof by division, showing that if places were real, they would lead to absurdities such as infinite regress. This passage establishes the conceptual foundation for understanding space in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage carefully, then the literal translation. Notice the dense logical structure.
The passage uses technical terms like 'musharan ilayhi' (pointed to).
2
Identify the main claim
The mutakallimun assert that places (ahiyaz) are mental posits (yafriduha al-dhihn) and have no external existence.
This is the thesis they will defend.
3
Trace the proof by division
The proof begins: if places were externally existent, they would be either 'pointed to' (musharan ilayhi) or not. Both are impossible.
Follow the two branches carefully.
4
Understand the infinite regress argument
If a place is pointed to independently, it is a body. But every body needs another place, leading to an infinite regress, which is impossible.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
5
Consider the dependent case and the occurrence problem
The dependent case (accident) is also problematic. Then the passage discusses what it means for a body to 'occur' in a place—either contact or penetration—both of which are flawed.
The argument cuts off, but the implication is clear.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Contact between bodies forces a choice: either interpenetration or no contact at all.

But the refutation reveals that this argument conflates two different kinds of possibility. Core Idea

Sanusi presents the third argument against the view that place is a dimension. Commentary
This passage presents the fifth proof against the view that place (al-makan) is dimension (al-bu'd). The argument begins by dividing space (al-fada') into two possibilities: finite or infinite. Both are deemed impossible. The impossibility of an infinite dimension is deferred to other proofs. The impossibility of a finite dimension is argued as follows: every finite thing has limits, hence a shape. So a finite dimension must have a shape. The determinant (al-muqtaḍā) of that shape must be one of four: (1) the nature of the dimension itself, (2) an accident inhering in it, (3) a substrate for it, or (4) something that neither inheres nor is a substrate. Al-Razi eliminates (1) because a part of the dimension would then have the same shape as the whole, which is false. He eliminates (2) because if the accident is a necessary concomitant of the quantitative nature, the part problem recurs; if not, it cannot cause a necessary attribute. He eliminates (4) through a regress argument: if the distinct thing is equally related to all dimensions, no particular shape results without a preponderator; if not, another distinct thing is needed, leading to infinite regress. The only remaining option is (3): the shape is determined by a substrate. This implies that the dimension inheres in a substrate, contradicting the idea of an independent empty dimension. Thus, the concept of space as an independent dimension is false. The text breaks off before explicitly concluding, but the intended conclusion is clear.

The Epistemic Proof for the Reality of Extended Dimensions
Third proof using epistemic priority to show extended dimensions are distinct from bodies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the logical priority of necessary knowledge over contingent knowledge demonstrates that extended dimensions (places/directions) are distinct from the essences of bodies.

Core Idea
What we know necessarily (space) cannot be identical to what we know contingently (bodies).

This proof takes a different approach from the previous ones. Instead of examining physical properties, it looks at the structure of our knowledge. The author argues that we know certain things—like that there is a 'here' or 'there'—with absolute certainty, while the presence of a body in that location is not known with the same certainty. This difference in epistemic status reveals a real distinction between space and bodies.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation once. Notice that the proof is structured as a syllogism with two premises and a conclusion.
The argument is purely conceptual; it does not rely on empirical observations.

The first premise: the existence of a location (pointed to as 'here' or 'there') is known by necessity. The second premise: the presence of a body in that location is not known by necessity; it depends on evidence against void.
The key is the contrast between necessary and contingent knowledge.

Understand the conclusion
Since what is known necessarily must be distinct from what is not known necessarily, the extended dimensions (places/directions) are distinct from the dimensions and extensions of bodies.
This conclusion does not require proving that void is impossible; it only requires that body presence is contingent.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof (al-hujja al-thaniya) for the existence of void (al-khala') as distinct from bodies. The proof begins by establishing a premise known by necessity (bi-al-darura): between the two ends of a basin (al-tas) there is a specific extension (imtidad) and distance (bu'd). The argument then poses a disjunction: this distance is either the distance of the body that occupies the basin, or it is a different distance. The first alternative is invalid for three reasons.

Second reason: Even if we assume that plenum (al-mala') is necessary, its existence is not known by intuition (bi-al-badiha) but only through subtle proofs. Prior to those proofs, bodies are unknown. Third reason: The distance between the basin's ends remains one individual distance, unchanging and permanent, regardless of what bodies occupy the basin (water, air, etc.). The bodies themselves are successive and changing. A thing that is unchanging is different from things that change. Therefore the distance is different from the bodies occupying those spaces.

The conclusion: The distance (or void) is different from the bodies that fill the spaces.

This lesson unpacks one of Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's geometric arguments against the classical definition of place as the containing surface. Three Impossible Consequences of Place as Surface

Commentary
This passage presents the third argument (al-hujja al-thalitha) against the view that place is the containing surface (al-sath al-hawi). The author refutes this view by showing three impossible consequences (ilzamat). First, he states the general principle: if place were the containing surface, then absurdities would follow: place could increase while the occupant remains unchanged; place could remain while the occupant decreases; place could increase while the occupant decreases. Since these are impossible, the premise is false. Then he elaborates each objection.

1. First objection (al-ilzam al-awwal): Two forms. (a) If a single body is cut into many pieces, the total volume remains the same, but the total surrounding surface increases. So the occupant's quantity is unchanged, yet its place (if place is surface) would increase. (b) Geometrically, a sphere has a smaller surface area than a cube of the same volume. So if a wax ball is reshaped into a cube, the occupant remains the same, but the surrounding surface increases. So again place would increase while the occupant is constant.

2. Second objection (al-ilzam al-thani): If a waterskin is squeezed, some water exits, but the inner surface remains surrounding the remaining water. So the occupant decreases while place (surface) remains the same.

3. Third objection (al-ilzam al-thalith): If a cube is indented, its volume decreases, but the surrounding surface increases. So the occupant decreases while place increases.

All these consequences are absurd, showing that place cannot be the surface. The author concludes by contrasting: if we instead say place is the dimension (al-bu'd) in which the body extends, none of these absurdities follow.

Proof of Vacuum from Surface Separation
Proof that vacuum necessarily occurs when one surface rises from another.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how kalam reasoning proves the existence of vacuum through an exhaustive refutation of alternative explanations.

Core Idea
When surfaces separate, vacuum must occur because no body can fill the gap.

This passage presents a sophisticated proof from the kalam tradition that vacuum necessarily arises when two surfaces separate. By systematically ruling out every possible way a body could enter the gap, the argument forces us to accept that the space must be empty. This method of exhaustive refutation is a hallmark of kalam reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the claim
The third premise states: when one surface rises from another, vacuum occurs between them. Read the opening sentence carefully.
This is the conclusion to be proved.
2
Consider the first possibility
If a body is between them, could it have been there before separation? The author rejects this by noting that surfaces can coincide, so no body was present.
This refutation relies on the possibility of two surfaces coinciding without gap.
3
Consider the second possibility
The body could have moved into the gap after separation. Two sub-possibilities: from pores or from sides. Follow the refutation of each.
Focus on the argument that porous surfaces still have continuous parts that rise at once, preventing entry.

Conclude the proof
Both possibilities are false, so the middle must have been empty when the surfaces separated. That is the desired vacuum.
This is a reductio ad absurdum: assuming a body leads to contradiction, so vacuum is necessary.

الفصل السابع في تغاريع القول بالظاء البتة وهي فروع: الفرع الأول: اتفق جمهور القدماء على أن الخلاء لا يقبل العدم واحتجوا عليه بوجهين: الأول: إن كل ما كان ممكناً، فإنا لا يلزم من فرض وقوعه محال، فلنفرض أن الخلاء قد عدم، فنقول عند وقوع هذا الفرض: هل يتميز جانب اليمين عن جانب اليسار، وجانب الفوق عن جانب التحت، أو لا يتميز؟ والثاني باطل بالبديهة، لأن القول بحصول حالة، وفرض أخرى لا يبقى معها هذا الامتياز: أمر لا يقبله العقل، ولا يتصوره الخيال [وقول من يقول: إنه من حكم الوهم، ومن عمل الخيال: فقد أبطلناه فيها تقدم] ولما بطل هذا القسم ثبت أن الحق هو القسم الأول، وهو أن عند فرض عدم هذا الخلاء، لا يكون امتياز اليمين عن اليسار، والفوق عن التحت: حاصلاً. وإذا كان كذلك، فهذه الأبعاد الخالية أمور يلزم من فرض عدمها، فرض وجودها. وكل ما كان كذلك، كان فرض عدمه محالاً. فيثبت: أن فرض عدم هذا الفضاء [وهذا الخلاء أمر لا يقبله العقل البتة، فكان ذلك موجوداً واجب الوجود لذاته. الوجه الثاني في تقرير هذا القول: وهو أن العقل لا يستبعد زوال الجبال والبحار عن أحيازها، ولا يستنكر أيضاً عدمها وفناءها في أنفسها. فإما أن يقال: إن هذه الأحياز الخالية والفضاء الخالي يفنى ويبقى. وهذا أمر لا يتصوره العقل، ولا يقبله الخاطر، فيثبت: أن الأمر كما ذكرناه. وأما أهل التحقيق من العلماء فإنهم قالوا: هذه الأبعاد ممكنة الوجود لذواتها، وكل ما كان كذلك فإنه من حيث إنه هو يكون قابلاً للعدم. واحداً. أما بيان أنها ممكنة الوجود لذواتها، فلوجهين: الأول: إنه ثبت بالبرهان أن واجب الوجود لذاته، لا يكون إلا واحداً. والثاني: أن هذا الخلاء منقسم، وذلك لأنه يمكن أن يقال: الخلاء من ههنا إلى هناك: ذراع، ومن ههنا إلى موضع آخر أبعد من الأول: ألف ذراع. فثبت: أن الخلاء قابل للقسمة والتجزئة. وكل منقسم: ممكن. على ما قررنا في الفصول السالفة. فيثبت: أن هذه الأبعاد ممكنة الوجود. ولا شك أن كل ممكن، فإنه قابل للعدم.
Chapter Seven: On the branches of the statement regarding the void absolutely. And they are branches: The first branch: The majority of the ancients agreed that the void does not admit non-existence, and they argued for it with two proofs: The first: Everything that is possible, it is not necessary that from supposing its occurrence an impossibility follows. So let us suppose that the void has become non-existent. We say at the occurrence of this supposition: Is the side of the right distinguished from the side of the left, and the side of the above from the side of the below, or is it not distinguished? And the second is false by intuition, because the statement of the occurrence of a state and the supposition of another state with which this distinction does not remain is a matter that the intellect does not accept and the imagination does not conceive. [And the statement of one who says: It is from the judgment of illusion and from the work of imagination: we have already refuted it in what preceded.] And when this division became false, it is established that the truth is the first division, which is that upon the supposition of the non-existence of this void, the distinction of the right from the left and the above from the below does not occur. And if that is the case, then these empty dimensions are matters from the supposition of whose non-existence the supposition of their existence is necessary. And everything that is such, the supposition of its non-existence is impossible. So it is established that the supposition of the non-existence of this space [and this void is a matter that the intellect does not accept at all, so it is an existent necessary of existence in itself. The second proof in establishing this statement: It is that the intellect does not consider improbable the removal of mountains and seas from their places, nor does it deny their non-existence and annihilation in themselves. But is it said that these empty spaces and the empty void perish and remain? And this is a matter that the intellect does not conceive and the mind does not accept. So it is established that the matter is as we mentioned. As for the people of verification among the scholars, they said: These dimensions are possible of existence in themselves, and everything that is such, then from the aspect that it is, it is receptive to non-existence. One. As for the demonstration that they are possible of existence in themselves, it is through two proofs: The first: It is established by proof that the Necessary of Existence in itself is only one. The second: That this void is divisible, because it can be said: The void from here to there is a cubit, and from here to another place farther than the first is a thousand cubits. So it is established that the void is divisible and partitionable. And everything divisible is possible, according to what we established in previous chapters. So it is established that these dimensions are possible of existence. And there is no doubt that every possible is receptive to non-existence.

Commentary
This passage presents two proofs for the majority opinion that the void cannot cease to exist, followed by a counterargument from 'the people of verification' who claim it is possible. The author first states the first proof: If the void were annihilated, we could ask whether spatial distinctions (right/left, above/below) would remain. It is self-evident that they would not, because the void provides the framework for such distinctions. Thus, the void cannot be annihilated without absurdity. The second proof: The mind can conceive of mountains and seas being removed from their locations, but it cannot conceive of the void itself being removed or annihilated; therefore, the void is necessary. Then the author reports that some scholars ('people of verification') disagree, asserting that the void is possible because it is divisible (a property of contingent things) and because only God is necessary. They provide two arguments: (1) only God is necessary, so everything else is possible; (2) the void is divisible into parts, and everything divisible is possible. So according to them, the void is possible and could cease to exist. The author does not explicitly refute this view here but presents it as a contrasting position.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Divisibility proves necessity (e.g., space is infinite and necessary).
Correction: The people of verification argue the opposite: divisibility is a mark of contingency, not necessity.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?

?
The void is divisible; you can measure it. But what about your own life? Is it not also divided into moments that pass away? How does knowing that you are not necessary affect how you use your time right now?
?
You accept the Necessary of Existence as one. But have you lived today as if everything else is merely possible? What would change if you woke up tomorrow and truly believed that every blessing could vanish? Are you ready to surrender?
