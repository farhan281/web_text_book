The Sixth Argument: Eternity Does Not Require Time
Rāzī presents a proof by contradiction that God's eternity does not depend on time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the proof by contradiction that God's eternity does not depend on time, and how the argument refutes the necessity of time for temporal relations.

Core Idea
God's eternity does not need time; if it did, He would be contingent.

Fakhr al-Din al-Razi presents a powerful proof by contradiction against the idea that God's eternity requires time. He shows that the very concept of perpetuity can be understood without time, and that accepting time as necessary leads to a contradiction with God's necessity. This argument also applies to temporal relations like before and after, negating the need for a temporal container.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to grasp the overall argument.
Notice the structure: premise, two branches, conclusion.
2
Identify the two possibilities
Razi presents two possibilities: either the supposition that perpetuity requires time is true, or it is false. Identify these two branches in the text.
Look for 'فإن كانت' and 'فإن كانت' indicating the two cases.
3
Understand the contradiction in the first possibility
If the supposition is true, God's perpetuity would depend on time, making God contingent (mumkin bi-dhātih), which contradicts His necessity (wājib bi-dhātih). This is the 'khulf' (contradiction).
This is the core of the proof by contradiction.
4
See the conclusion from the second possibility
If the supposition is false, perpetuity does not require time. By analogy, temporality also does not require time. Therefore, notions of before, after, and simultaneity do not need a temporal container.
The conclusion negates the necessity of time for temporal relations.

Commentary

The author begins by stating that both temporality (ḥudūth) and perpetuity (dawām) are commonly conceived only with the supposition (tawahhum) of a duration (mudda) and time (zamān). Consequently, the notions of before, after, and simultaneity do not need a temporal container (ẓarf/wi'ā'). The conclusion is that negating time is valid, and temporal priority does not depend on the existence of time.

The Seventh Argument: Time Is Contingent and Its Necessity Is Impossible
Rāzī proves that time is contingent and cannot be necessary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the seventh argument from the contingency of time and why temporal relations do not require the existence of time.

Core Idea
Time cannot be necessary because its own contingency prevents it from grounding before and after.

Fakhr al-Dīn al-Rāzī presents a sophisticated argument that time itself must be contingent, and that any attempt to make it necessary leads to a contradiction. This argument not only demonstrates the contingency of time but also shows that the notions of priority and posteriority do not depend on the existence of time. By working through this argument, you will see how kalam reasoning uses the structure of composition and dependence to refute philosophical assumptions.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice the two main parts: first the proof of contingency, then the reductio ad absurdum.
The passage is dense; take your time with each sentence.
2
Identify the components of time
Rāzī breaks time into successive parts. Each part is temporally originated (ḥādith) and therefore possible. Understand why this makes the whole possible.
The argument uses composition to show contingency.
3
Understand the reductio
If posteriority depends on time, then supposing time's non-existence would require time to exist, leading to a contradiction. See how this forces time to be both possible and necessary.
This is a classic kalam argument showing absurdity.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The conclusion is that temporal priority and posteriority do not depend on the existence of time. Ask yourself what this means for arguments that rely on time as a necessary backdrop.
This refutes those who require time for creation.

The seventh argument: This duration is possible in itself. Proof: because it is composed of successive parts, and each one of those successive, consecutive parts is temporally originated, and every temporally originated thing is possible. Therefore, each of the parts of time is possible in itself, and the whole is dependent upon each of its parts. And what is dependent in its existence upon what is possible in itself is more fitting to be possible in itself. Thus it is established that duration and time – if it exists – would be possible in itself. And everything that is possible in itself, its hypothetical non-existence after its existence does not entail an impossibility. If this is established, we say: If this posteriority is not conceivable except by means of time, then it would be necessary that time exists at the very moment we suppose it non-existent. Because its non-existence after its existence, since it is not established except by time, so if we suppose that it becomes non-existent after its existence, then it would follow from supposing this non-existence occurring after that existence that time exists while we suppose it non-existent. Thus it is established that if time existed, then from the mere supposition of its non-existence, the supposition of its existence would follow, and this is absurd. It is established that the supposition of the non-existence of time entails a contradiction in itself, and everything that is so is necessary in itself. So time, assuming it exists, would be, according to the first consideration, possible, and according to the second consideration, necessary in itself. And since that is absurd, it is established that the occurrence of these prior and posterior relations does not depend on the existence of duration and time. That is what was sought.

Commentary
This passage presents the seventh argument, which is a direct proof of the contingency of time and a reductio ad absurdum against its necessity for posteriority (ba'diyya). The author first establishes that duration/time is contingent (mumkin) because it is composed of successive temporal parts (ajzā' muta'āqiba), each of which is temporally originated (ḥādith) and therefore possible. Consequently, time itself is possible in itself. If posteriority were inconceivable without time, then time would have to exist whenever we suppose it non-existent, leading to a self-contradiction. Thus, supposing the non-existence of time would entail its existence, which is absurd (muḥāl). Therefore, time cannot be necessary; rather, it would be both possible and necessary under different considerations, an impossibility. The conclusion is that temporal priority and posteriority do not depend on the existence of time.

The Sixth Proof: Priorness and Posteriority Are Additional Attributes
The author presents the sixth proof that priorness, posteriority, and withness are additional to God's essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the sixth rational proof that priorness, posteriority, and withness are additional attributes to God's essence and do not imply change in Him.

Core Idea
God's temporal relations are additional attributes, not His essence — change would otherwise follow.

In this lesson, we examine the sixth and final proof presented by the author that the concepts of priorness, posteriority, and withness are not identical to God's essence but are additional attributes. The proof uses a simple observation: God exists before, during, and after any temporal event. If these relational concepts were the essence itself, God's essence would change as the relations change — an impossibility. Thus, they must be additional.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing, then read the translation to grasp the overall argument.
The passage is short but dense; note the three temporal stages.
2
Identify the three temporal stages
The proof mentions God's existence before the event, at the time of the event, and after the event. These correspond to priorness, withness, and posteriority.
3
Understand the hypothetical
The author says if these concepts were identical to the essence, change would occur. Ask yourself: why would change follow?
Because the essence would have to be different when it is 'prior' vs 'posterior'.
4
Recognize the impossibility of change in God
Change in God's essence is impossible because He is necessary and perfect. Thus the concepts cannot be identical to the essence.
5
Conclude the proof
The proof concludes that these concepts are 'matters additional to the essence and reality.' This is the sixth proof that establishes this doctrine.

But change in God's essence is impossible. Thus it is concluded through these six proofs (the sixth being the one presented) that these concepts are additional to the essence and reality of God.

The refutation provides three proofs: (1) We describe God as existing before the world, with the world, and after the world, using temporal language, but no change occurs in God. Therefore time does not entail change. (2) Events were non-existent in eternity, yet we apply temporal judgments to those non-existents, but change in pure non-existence is impossible. (3) Priority, withness, and posteriority are not real entities but mental relations. These shows time is not dependent on motion.
