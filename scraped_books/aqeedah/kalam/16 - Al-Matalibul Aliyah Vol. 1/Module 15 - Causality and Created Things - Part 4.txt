Objections 3-5: Sequential Causation in Arrows, Thought, and Falling Bodies
Razi presents objections 3-5 using examples of sequential causation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand three objections that use empirical examples of sequential causation to argue that cause must precede effect.

Core Idea
In arrows, thoughts, and falling bodies, each step is caused by the previous — showing cause precedes effect.

The opponents continue with concrete examples from everyday experience and mental processes. The third objection uses the example of an arrow shot in the air, where successive air currents cause its continued motion. The fourth objection points to reasoning: thought must precede knowledge. The fifth objection analyzes a falling body, where each segment of motion is caused by the previous one.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage for objections 3-5
Read the Arabic text for the third, fourth, and fifth objections. Notice how each uses a specific phenomenon to illustrate temporal precedence.
These objections are empirical in nature.
2
Analyze the arrow example (objection 3)
In archery, the arrow's flight depends on successive air currents. Each current causes the next, showing a chain of causes and effects in time.
If cause and effect were simultaneous, the arrow would fall immediately.
3
Understand the thought example (objection 4)
Thought about a topic produces knowledge of the result. The thinking process must occur before the knowledge is obtained, not at the same time.
This is a psychological argument.
4
Study the falling body example (objection 5)
A heavy object falling: each part of its motion is a cause for the next part. This can be seen as an effective cause (Mu'tazila view) or preparatory cause (philosophers view).
Both schools agree on the sequential nature.
5
Synthesize the common theme
All three objections demonstrate that in natural and mental processes, causes precede their effects sequentially.
This challenges the idea of simultaneous causation.

Those who opposed the existence of this simultaneity argued by several matters: First: If this simultaneity were considered, it would either be an expression of essential simultaneity or temporal simultaneity. Commentary
This passage enumerates seven objections raised by those who deny that the cause must be simultaneous with its effect. (5) A falling body's motion occurs in successive parts, each caused by the previous. (6) The complete cause must exist entirely before its effect. (7) Empirical examples like moving a hand and then a key show temporal order. These objections collectively argue that causality requires temporal precedence, not simultaneity.

Core Idea
A cause must exist in its entirety before its effect, and common experience confirms this order.

The opponents conclude with two more arguments. The sixth objection is based on the principle that a cause must be fully present before its effect can occur. The seventh objection points to simple empirical observation: when we move our hand, the key moves only after the hand, not at the same instant.

Six Proofs for the Temporal Priority of Cause over Effect
Six arguments demonstrating that cause must precede effect in time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand six independent proofs from everyday observation and rational necessity that establish the temporal priority of cause over effect.

Core Idea
Cause must precede effect in time; otherwise, causality is absurd.

This passage presents six concise arguments drawn from physics, psychology, and common experience, each demonstrating that a cause cannot be simultaneous with its effect. These proofs form a critical foundation for understanding causal relationships in Islamic theology and philosophy.

Guided Path

The Arabic text numbers proofs from second to seventh. Read the translation to grasp each argument before diving into analysis.
Notice that the first proof is not given here; it was covered earlier.
2
Analyze the 'impossible creation' argument
The second proof: if cause and effect were simultaneous, the cause would be 'creating an already existent' thing, which is impossible. So cause must precede effect.
This is a direct logical argument from impossibility.
3
Examine the empirical arguments
The third, fourth, and fifth proofs use examples of an arrow, thought, and a falling object to show temporal sequence in causality.
These examples cover different domains: physics, psychology, and motion.
4
Understand the necessity argument
The sixth proof states that the cause must exist completely before the effect can issue. The seventh proof uses common experience ('I moved my hand, so the key moved') to affirm temporal order.
These final proofs ground the conclusion in necessary and observed reality.
5
Reflect on the unified conclusion
All six proofs converge on the same point: cause is temporally prior to effect. This refutes any notion of simultaneity.
Consider how this principle underpins causal reasoning in kalam.

Commentary
This passage presents six independent proof-arguments for the necessary temporal priority of the cause over the effect. Each proof draws on a different kind of observable or rationally necessary causal relation.

1. The second proof: If the cause were to act on the effect while the effect is already present (i.e., simultaneous), that would amount to 'creating the already existent' (ijad al-mawjud), which is impossible. Therefore the cause must be prior so that its action can be understood as bringing the effect from non-existence to existence.

2. The third proof: When an arrow is shot, the successive resistances (al-mudafi'at) from the air each become a cause for the next repulsion in temporal succession. If the cause and effect were simultaneous, the arrow would drop immediately upon leaving the hand, since there would be no temporal sequence of causes.

3. The fourth proof: Thought (al-fikr) produces knowledge of the desired conclusion, but the thought must be prior to that knowledge, because thinking about something at the very moment of knowing it is impossible. Thus cause (thought) precedes effect (knowledge).

4. The fifth proof: A heavy object descending has parts of its motion; each part is a cause for the next part, whether as an efficient cause (according to some Mu'tazila) or as a preparatory cause (according to the philosophers). In either case, temporal priority is required.

5. The sixth proof: We know necessarily that unless the cause exists in its complete state, the effect cannot issue. The complete existence of the cause is prior to the effect, so the complete cause must be prior.

6. The seventh proof: Empirical observation ('I moved my hand, so the key moved') shows a clear order: the cause (hand movement) precedes the effect (key movement). Reason attests to this order, so the cause must be prior.

All six proofs converge on the same conclusion: the cause must be temporally prior to the effect.

The answer to the first objection: Their saying: 'This withness (al-ma'iyya) is either considered in essence or in time.' We say: This is based on your claim that time (al-mudda) is a concomitant of motion and change, which is false in our view. Rather, we say: The decisive proof of its falsity is that we know necessarily that God, exalted, exists at this time, along with the occurrence of every originated thing (hadith), and that invalidates your statement.

The answer to the second objection: Their saying: 'If the cause existed with the effect, creating the existent (ijad al-mawjud) would be necessary.' We say: If by this speech you mean creating the existent a second time, that is false. If you mean that it is an origination (ijad) of that existent in the sense that were it not for that origination, that existent would not exist, then this is true in our view. So why do you say it is impossible?

The answer to the third objection: The archer (al-rami) places in the arrow a remaining power (quwwa baqiya) that is the necessitator of those motions, except that its bestowal upon each part of the motion is conditional upon the passing away of the preceding part. And this is the answer concerning the descent of the heavy object. As for his saying: 'Thought (al-fikr) necessitates knowledge of the desired conclusion, while thought must occur before the attainment of knowledge of the result,' we say: Thought is the totality of knowledge of the two premises (muqaddimatayn), and they are both present with the knowledge of the result. As for his saying: 'The cause (al-mu'aththir) as long as it does not exist completely, the effect does not issue from it,' we say: If by 'the cause exists completely' is meant temporal priority (taqaddum zamani), then this is precisely what is sought. If what is meant is priority in essence (taqaddum bi al-dhat), this is granted but does not harm our objective. As for clinging to what is said in common usage: 'I moved my hand, so the sleeve moved,' or 'then the sleeve moved,' we say: This is clinging to words in intellectual difficulties, and it is weak. Then, assuming its validity, we interpret it as priority in essence.

Commentary
This passage refutes three objections (shubhat) that opponents might raise against the doctrine that causes must be temporally prior to their effects. The author responds to each in turn.

1. First objection: The opponent argues that the simultaneity ('withness') of cause and effect can be conceived either in essence or in time. If in essence, then temporal priority is not required. The author replies that this objection presupposes that time is a property of motion and change, which is false. The decisive proof is that God exists at the same time as each originated event (hadith) — this shows that time is not restricted to motion, thus undermining the objection's foundation.

2. Second objection: If cause and effect coexist, then creating an already-existing thing would be necessary, which is impossible. The author distinguishes two meanings: if 'creating the existent' means producing it a second time, that is absurd; but if it means that the cause brings the effect from non-existence to existence (even if simultaneous), that is actually true. The opponent's charge of impossibility is baseless.

3. Third objection: This response reframes several arguments. For the archer's arrow: the arrow possesses a remaining power that causes successive motions, but this power is imparted successively conditioned on the previous part's expiration — thus priority is temporal. The same applies to the heavy object's descent. For thought: thought is not a separate entity but the knowledge of the two premises, which coexist with the knowledge of the result — so no temporal priority is needed here. For the complete cause: if 'complete existence' means temporal priority, it confirms the author's position; if it means essential priority, it is admitted but irrelevant. Finally, conventional expressions like 'I moved my hand, so the key moved' are weak proofs in rational matters; even if accepted, they indicate essential priority, not necessarily temporal.

The passage ends with a closing formula affirming the doctrine of causality with divine help.

Refuting the 'Cause of a Cause' Argument

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A seemingly logical premise can be either false or useless in argument.

This passage dissects a second argument against circular causation (dawr). The argument hinges on a commonly accepted premise: 'the cause of a cause of a thing is a cause for that thing.' The author shows that this premise, when applied to a circular causal chain, leads to either a contradiction or a meaningless tautology. By carefully distinguishing two possible interpretations, he exposes the fallacy.

1
Read the argument presented

This passage sharpens skills in analyzing logical premises.

The author answers an objection about the simultaneity of correlatives.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Correlatives exist together but not by depending on each other.

This lesson addresses a common objection against the relationship between cause and effect: if cause and effect are correlatives, they must be simultaneous. But does that lead to absurd circularity? The author clarifies the nature of correlation to avoid this pitfall.

Guided Path

Then he says it is impossible for each one to be in need of the other. This refers to a mutual dependency in existence, which would lead to circularity. So correlatives are simultaneous but not causally dependent on each other.

4

Think about cause and effect as correlatives: they coexist, but the cause is not dependent on the effect for its existence. The effect depends on the cause, not vice versa.
This resolves the worry that cause and effect would have to be mutually dependent.

Commentary
This passage is a proof by counterexample, presenting two additional arguments for infinite regress and then defending them against a counter-objection. The author first states the sixth argument: the ranks of numbers are infinite, so regress occurs in them. Then the seventh: God knows a thing; if He knows a thing, He can know that He knows it, and this possibility must be actual in God, leading to an infinite regress of knowledge-of-knowledge ranks, all existing simultaneously. This is presented as a strong refutation of the claim that infinite regress is impossible. The author then anticipates an objection: this regress does not apply to philosophers who identify God as intellect, intelligent, and intelligible as one, nor to theologians who say knowledge of knowledge is identical to knowledge of the thing. The author refutes both: the philosophers' view is nonsense because knowledge is a relation; God's knowledge of blackness is a specific relation different from His essence, and knowledge of that knowledge is another relation. Thus they are distinct. He provides additional arguments: (1) If they were identical, one would know infinite ranks in detail (false). (2) We can believe someone knows something while doubting they know that they know it. (3) We intuitively distinguish between 'knows X' and 'knows that he knows X.' Therefore, the regress stands.

The answer: we do not accept that the matter is as you stated, and the evidence for it is two aspects. First: when we say that causes and effects have no end, we have made infinity a predicate, and it is known that the subject of this predicate is not each individual cause, but only the totality. So it is established that it can be described as a sum and aggregate, whether we suppose it finite or infinite. Second: when we say 'that which has no end exists,' we have judged those things with no end to be existent, and we mean by 'total,' 'aggregate,' and 'sum' nothing but those existent things as a whole.

Commentary
This passage responds to the objection that the totality of an infinite causal series cannot be meaningfully described as a whole or set unless it is finite. The author provides two proofs. First, when we say 'causes and effects have no end' (infinite), we are making 'infinity' a predicate. The subject of that predicate is not each individual cause, but the totality itself. Therefore, we can speak of the totality as a sum regardless of finitude. Second, when we say 'the infinite exists,' we are judging that those infinite things are existent, and by 'totality' we simply mean all of those existent things together. Thus, the totality is a legitimate concept even for an infinite series.

Answer to Third Question: The Cause of the Totality is Not the Parts or Their Dependence
Author refutes that the totality's cause is each part's dependence on another.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why an infinite chain of dependencies cannot serve as the cause of the totality, because it introduces a problematic duality between the parts and their relations.

Core Idea
The cause of a chain cannot be the chain itself.

In this passage, the author addresses an objection that an infinite regress of dependencies could explain the existence of the whole. He shows that this explanation actually contains two distinct concepts—the parts themselves and their relations—and neither can be the cause without circularity or presupposition.

This conclusion follows from the dichotomy.

As for the third question, which is his statement: 'Why is it not permissible to say that that totality exists because each one of its individuals depends on another, ad infinitum...?' The answer: we say that this too is false, because when we say 'the totality exists due to the dependence of each individual on another,' two concepts arise: one is the sum of the essences of those individuals, and the second is the fact that each one is dependent on the other. There is no doubt that these two concepts are distinct. Once you understand this, we say: that which you have made the cause of that totality is either those individuals or those dependence relations that are accidents of those individuals.

Commentary
This passage refutes the objection that the infinite causal chain itself explains the existence of the totality. The objector suggests that the totality exists because each part depends on another without end. The author points out that in this explanation, we have two distinct concepts: (1) the sum of the essences (dhawat) of the individuals, and (2) the relations of dependence (istinadat) that are accidents (arida) of those individuals. The cause of the totality cannot be both at once. If it is the individuals themselves, that is circular because they are part of the totality. If it is the dependence relations, those are accidents that presuppose the existence of the individuals. Thus, the objection fails to provide a genuine cause for the totality beyond the individuals themselves, which is insufficient.
