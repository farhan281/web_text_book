Commentary
This passage is a survey of the commentary tradition showing how Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's kalam views were transmitted and cited by later scholars from both Ash'ari and Shi'i circles.

It begins by mentioning two key works: Tawali' al-Anwar by al-Baydawi and al-Maqasid by Sa'd al-Din al-Taftazani. The commentary of al-Maqasid, authored by Sa'd al-Din himself, refers to al-Razi with the honorific 'Imam', citing him on the intuitiveness of the conception of knowledge and other points.

Next, a quote from Shaykh Salih Musa Sharaf is provided, explaining that al-Taftazani in his Maqasid frequently borrows from earlier sources such as al-Mawaqif (by al-Iji), al-Matalib al-Aliya (by al-Razi himself), works of Ibn Sina, and others. This shows al-Taftazani's reliance on al-Razi's magna opus.

The passage then turns to the Imami (Shi'i) tradition. It notes that Tajrid al-I'tiqad by al-Tusi is authoritative, and its commentary Kashf al-Murad by al-Hilli is a major work. This commentary was further glossed by al-Tehrani in Tawdih al-Murad. The author observes that these Shi'i works also contain al-Razi's ideas, paralleling their presence in al-Taftazani's Maqasid.

To illustrate, the passage quotes al-Hilli stating that 'Fakhr al-Din mentioned in refuting the definition of existence two aspects.' Al-Tehrani then expands on this, explaining that existence and non-existence are intuitive in conception (تصور) and thus indefinable—a point al-Razi acknowledged for conception but not for judgment (تصديق). This demonstrates how later commentators debated and transmitted al-Razi's arguments.

Finally, the passage addresses al-Razi's religious identity. A gloss by al-Tehrani quotes al-Qifti, who says al-Razi was Ash'ari in usul (foundations) and Shafi'i in furu' (branches). The gloss clarifies that al-Razi was not known for nusb (hostility to 'Ali), meaning he was not a prominent Shi'i. This remark situates al-Razi within the Sunni tradition, despite his ideas being studied by Shi'i scholars.

Overall, the passage illustrates the wide reception of al-Razi's kalam across sectarian divides, preserved through a chain of commentaries and glosses.

Understanding the Chronogram in Manuscript Authentication
Copyist's chronogram verses dating the manuscript to 864 AH.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how medieval Islamic manuscripts were authenticated using chronograms (hisab al-jummal) and what the copyist's verses reveal about the manuscript of al-Matalib al-'Aliya.

Core Idea
The copyist's poetic chronogram dates the manuscript to 864 AH using the abjad system.

This lesson explores a fascinating aspect of manuscript studies: the use of chronograms. The copyist of al-Matalib al-'Aliya composed verses that not only praise the book but also encode its copying date. By learning to interpret such chronograms, we gain insight into the provenance and history of the text.

Guided Path
1

Identify the chronogram phrase
Find the phrase 'al-Muhammad al-Razi kitab Matalib' in the text. This phrase is used as a chronogram. Understand that each Arabic letter has a numerical value, and the sum indicates the year.
The copyist explicitly states that the sum is 864.
3
Calculate the numerical value
The sum is given as 864. You can try verifying using abjad values: alif=1, lam=30, etc. Note that the copyist explicitly says the sum is 864.

Understanding Abjad Dating in Islamic Manuscripts
Records copyist's poetic verses and abjad dating.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how copyists used abjad calculation to date manuscripts, as illustrated by the poetic dating of Al-Matalib al-Aliya.

Core Idea
A manuscript's date can be hidden in a poem using the abjad numerical system.

Islamic manuscript copyists often embedded the date of copying within poetic verses using the abjad system. This lesson examines a specific example from the manuscript of Al-Matalib al-Aliya, where the copyist reveals the year 864 AH through the numerical value of a phrase. Understanding this practice helps us appreciate the preservation of textual history.
