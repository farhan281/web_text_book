Aristotle's Four Arguments Against Atomism
Aristotle refutes Democritus using four arguments: self-motion, void, indivisible parts, and symmetry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Aristotle's four-part refutation of Democritus's atomism and why the fourth argument is particularly decisive.

Core Idea
Even if atoms exist, their similarity would make motion impossible.

This passage presents Aristotle's critique of Democritus's atomic theory. After summarizing three fundamental objections already proven in natural science, Aristotle adds a powerful fourth argument that shows atomism fails on its own assumptions. Let's explore each argument and see how they combine to dismantle the atomist worldview.

Commentary
This passage presents Aristotle's refutation of Democritus's atomist cosmology. It begins by stating that Aristotle refuted this view in multiple ways. The first three arguments are referenced as already established in natural science: (1) a body cannot move by itself, (2) there is no void outside the world, and (3) bodies are not composed of indivisible parts. Thus, Democritus's scheme fails even on its own terms. This is a complete objection-and-answer unit: the atomist challenge is met with a multi-point refutation.

The First Refutation: Celestial Bodies vs. Generated Beings
Al-Razi's first argument: the absurd consequence of Democritus' view on the nobility of celestial bodies.

Core Idea
If Democritus were right, the heavens would be less perfect than earthly things—an absurdity.

Al-Razi begins his first refutation by comparing the origins of celestial spheres and earthly beings according to Democritus. He argues that if atoms caused the heavens accidentally, they would be less noble than natural beings—contradicting what we observe.

Commentary
This passage presents three additional refutations by the scholars (theologians) of Democritus' atomistic theory. The passage begins by stating that the scholars invalidated that theory from other aspects (wujuh), then enumerates them.

The first refutation: If Democritus were correct, the origin of celestial spheres would be due to rare, accidental causes, while the origin of three kinds of generated beings (minerals, plants, animals) would be due to natural, essential causes. This would imply that the celestial bodies are less noble and less perfect than earthly generated beings—which is contrary to sensory evidence. Since sense perception shows the falsity of this implication, the original theory is also false.

The second refutation concerns the generation of fire from the motion of spheres. The argument notes that the concave surface of the lunar sphere and the convex surface of the sphere of fire are smooth. Smooth surfaces sliding on each other do not transfer motion from one to the other. Two possibilities are examined: (1) The sphere's motion makes it very hot, and that heat transfers to the adjacent body, causing fire. (2) The sphere's rotation causes the fire sphere to rotate, and that rapid rotation generates heat, producing fire. Both are rejected: the first because celestial spheres are known to be neither hot nor cold; the second because the motion of the celestial sphere does not necessitate motion of the interior fire. Hence, the theory cannot explain the origin of fire.

The third refutation reduces the theory to an infinite regress: If motion of the sphere turns adjacent body into fire, then that fire would turn its adjacent body (air) into fire, and so on successively until all elements become fire. This absurd consequence disproves the theory.

The passage concludes by stating that this summarizes the discussion on this topic.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange containing two objections (the third and fourth) against a previous proof, along with a response to the fourth objection. The first objection (third) challenges the claim that 'origination' (ḥudūth) is the cause of the need for an agent, asserting that origination cannot be a cause, part of a cause, or condition for need. The second objection (fourth) argues that the origination of our actions is a restricted origination (muqayyad) tied to our actions, so it does not follow that the unrestricted origination of the world would require an agent. The author then responds to the fourth objection by considering a possible counterargument: one might claim that the cause of need is simply 'origination as such' (al-ḥudūth min ḥaythu huwa ḥudūth), not the particular restricted instance. The author points out that if this is known by intuition, then the lengthy proof is unnecessary; if it requires proof, then the objection's syllogism depends on establishing that origination as such is a cause, which is exactly what needs to be proved, leading to circular reasoning (dawr). Thus the fourth objection is refuted, and the third remains as a challenge. The passage concludes with 'This completes the discussion in this place'.

Reconciling Independence with Inherence

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Independence does not prevent inherence; cause can be inherent in its effect.

The objection claims that if existence is independent of essence, it cannot be an attribute. The author begins his answer by exploring two possible ways existence could be independent yet still inhere in essence. This lesson presents the third argument for the unity of the Creator.
