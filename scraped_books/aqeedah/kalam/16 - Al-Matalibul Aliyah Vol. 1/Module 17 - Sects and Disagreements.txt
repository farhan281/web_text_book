Islamic Tawhid: Unity and Transcendence
Author defines Islamic tawhid with tanzih and cites Surat al-Ikhlas as proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Islamic tawhid necessarily includes both affirming God's perfect attributes and negating all deficiencies (tanzih), and that this is the basis for the impossibility of agreement with Christianity.

Core Idea
Islamic tawhid is not just monotheism—it is tawhid with tanzih.

The author begins by declaring that agreement on tawhid between Islam and Christianity is impossible. He then lays out the Islamic conception: God is one, possessing all perfect attributes and free from every imperfection. This core teaching, grounded in Surat al-Ikhlas, establishes that true tawhid requires tanzih (transcendence).

Guided Path
1
Read the declaration
The author states the conclusion upfront: agreement is impossible. Notice the categorical tone.
2
Identify the two components of Islamic tawhid
The text explains that Muslims must ascribe perfect attributes and negate deficiencies. Find these two movements in the passage.
Key terms: كمال (perfection) and نقص (deficiency).
3
Analyze the proof from Surat al-Ikhlas
The author cites Surat al-Ikhlas as evidence. Note that the surah points to two matters: oneness (وحدانية) and transcendence (تنزيه).
This connects directly to the lesson's core idea.

Commentary
This passage begins with a definitive declaration: agreement on tawhid between Islam and Christianity is impossible. The author then explains Islamic tawhid: belief in one God to whom all perfect attributes are ascribed and from whom all deficiencies are negated. Tawhid necessarily includes tanzih (transcendence). The proof is Surat al-Ikhlas, which affirms both oneness and transcendence. In contrast, Christian tawhid is professed verbally but not believed, because Christians believe in the Trinity. Thus Islamic oneness (with tanzih) cannot converge with Christian creed, which includes Trinity, anthropomorphism, and lack of tanzih. The passage then describes Orthodox Christian belief in detail: God incarnated in Mary, Jesus is God in human form (analogy with Gabriel appearing as Dihya), the narrative of Jesus' mission, crucifixion, descent to hell, resurrection, and ascension. The author notes the two hypostases: Father (before incarnation) and Son (after incarnation). The text cuts off but clearly intends to describe the third hypostasis as well. The author presents this description as part of the argument for impossibility of agreement.

Why Agreement on Tawhid Is Impossible: The Christian Conception
Author contrasts Christian tawhid with Islamic tawhid and lists three creedal obstacles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why, according to the author, Christian tawhid is not genuine tawhid because it lacks tanzih and includes trinity and anthropomorphism.

Core Idea
Christian tawhid is verbal only, not believed, due to trinity.

After establishing Islamic tawhid with tanzih, the author turns to Christianity. He acknowledges that Christians profess belief in one God, but argues that this profession is not genuine because their actual beliefs—Trinity, anthropomorphism, and lack of tanzih—contradict true tawhid. Thus, agreement on tawhid is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the description of Christian tawhid
The author says Christians say they believe in tawhid but don't truly believe it. Notice the distinction between speech and belief.
2
Identify the three reasons for impossibility
The text lists three elements of Christian creed that make agreement impossible: trinity, anthropomorphism, and lack of tanzih. Find them.
These are explicitly enumerated.
3
Contrast with Islamic tawhid
The author then reminds that Islam has tawhid in both speech and belief, and includes tanzih. This contrast drives home the impossibility.

Commentary
This passage is both an exposition and a refutation of Orthodox Christian trinitarian doctrine. The author first describes the Orthodox belief: Mary conceived Jesus by the Holy Spirit, God Himself dwelt in her and became a child, and people saw God in the form of Jesus, using an analogy of Gabriel appearing as Dihyah. He cites two Quranic verses condemning their belief: the prohibition of saying 'three' and the declaration of disbelief against those who say God is the Messiah. Thus the passage is a clear polemical argument showing the irreconcilable difference.

Commentary
This passage is a multi-part argument from the Gospels and Pauline letters to demonstrate that original Christian scripture teaches monotheism and divine transcendence, and to refute the doctrines of Trinity and incarnation as later innovations. The author proceeds in the following order:

**First (أ):** The author cites Mark 12:28-32, where a scribe asks Jesus about the greatest commandment. Jesus responds by quoting the Shema: 'The Lord our God is one Lord.' The scribe affirms this, agreeing that God is one. The author concludes that Jesus answered with the unity of God as in the Torah, and the scribe confirmed this monotheism.

**Second (ب):** The author cites John 17:3-4, where Jesus prays to God, calling the Father 'the only true God' and himself as the one sent. Also John 16:5 where Jesus says he goes to the one who sent him, and John 9:1-4 where Jesus says he must do the works of the one who sent him. The author interprets these verses to show that Jesus is a teacher and messenger, not God incarnate or a second god. Jesus performs the works of God who sent him, implying his subordination.

**Third (ج):** The author cites John 1:18 ('No one has ever seen God') and 1 Timothy 1:17 and 6:15-16, which describe God as invisible and unapproachable. Since people saw Jesus, he cannot be God. Paul's letters reinforce that God alone is immortal and invisible.

**Then 'ثالثاً':** The author draws a conclusion from the above: that the Torah, Gospel, and Quran all declare monotheism and transcendence. He then poses the question: whence comes the Christian belief in incarnation and trinity? He answers by turning to church history: Emperor Constantine convened the Council of Nicaea in 325 AD to merge Roman and Christian beliefs, producing a creed that said God is one but Christ is a second god. Later councils (Constantinople 381, Chalcedon 451) added the Holy Spirit as a third god and led to splits between Catholics (three gods) and Orthodox (one God incarnate). The author appeals to Christians to return to their own scriptures, which teach monotheism, and to recognize Jesus as a prophet like others, thus agreeing with Jews and Muslims. He warns that insistence on incarnation and trinity puts them in opposition to monotheistic faiths.

The footnote '(1) Note that they are four types' likely refers to the upcoming classification of proofs, but is placed here.

Jesus as Messenger and God's Invisibility (John and Paul)
Jesus as sent messenger and God as invisible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the Gospels and Paul's letters depict Jesus as a sent messenger and emphasize that God is invisible and unapproachable.

Core Idea
The Bible repeatedly calls Jesus a messenger and declares God invisible — contradicting incarnation.

In this lesson, we examine passages from the Gospel of John where Jesus explicitly says he was sent by God and refers to God as the only true God. Furthermore, Paul's letters and John's Gospel affirm that no one has seen God, implying that Jesus, who was seen, cannot be God.

Guided Path
1
Read the John passages
Read the passages from John 17, 16, and 9. Notice that Jesus calls God the one who sent him and the only true God.
2
Identify the key claim
Jesus says 'this is eternal life: that they know you the only true God, and Jesus Christ whom you have sent.' This distinguishes God from Jesus.
3
Read the invisibility passages
Read John 1:18 and 1 Timothy 1:17 and 6:15-16. They state that God is invisible and cannot be seen.
4
Connect the dots
Since Jesus was seen, he cannot be God. The author uses this to argue that Jesus is not divine but a messenger.
