Two Scenarios: Celestial Motion Needs a Mover
Al-Razi presents two scenarios for celestial motion and deduces the need for a mover.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Razi uses two possible scenarios for celestial bodies (created and moved, or pre-existing and set in motion) to argue that they necessarily require a mover and director.

Core Idea
Whatever the origin of celestial motion, it demands a mover.

Al-Razi begins his proof for God's existence by considering two possibilities: either the celestial bodies were created and then moved, or they existed motionless and were later set into motion. In both cases, reason points to a director and mover. This lesson focuses on that foundational argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage carefully. Notice the two conditional scenarios and the repeated conclusion.
2
Identify the two scenarios
Find where Al-Razi presents the first scenario (bodies non-existent then existed and moved) and the second (bodies existed but stationary then moved).
The word 'أما' introduces each scenario.
3
Analyze the rational conclusion
For each scenario, notice how sound reason ('العقل') testifies to the need for a mover and director. Reflect on why a body cannot move itself.
4
Reflect on the universality
The argument is designed to work for both a created and an eternal universe (with a beginning of motion). Consider how this makes the proof flexible.

Bodies in their entirety were non-existent then they existed and moved, or it is said: bodies, even if they were existent, were stationary and motionless, and when that time came, He initiated rapid movements in them, some to the east and some to the west, and some to the north and some to the south. And upon both suppositions, sound reason testifies to their need for a god (ilāh) who moves and directs them. And as for if we say: they were purely non-existent then they existed and moved, then reason judges their need for the originator (al-mūjid). And as for if we say: they were existent but were stationary and then began at that time to move, then reason judges their need for the mover (al-muḥarrik) and the director (al-mudabbir). So it is established that the motions of the spheres (ḥarakāt al-aflāk) indicate in all respects their need for a director who manages them and a mover who moves them. And know that the philosophers (al-falāsifa) said: the motions of the spheres have no beginning, so necessarily they need a mover who moves them and a director who manages them. And the theologians (al-mutakallimūn) said: the motions of the spheres have a beginning, so necessarily they need a mover and a manager. And it can also be said: these motions — either it is said that they have no beginning, or it is said that they have a beginning. For if we say that they have no beginning, then necessarily they need the deity (al-ilāh) who is not a body (jism) nor corporeal (jismānī) by the way mentioned by the philosophers. And if we say that those motions have a beginning and a start, then necessarily they need the effective mover (al-muʼaththir al-muḥarrik) by the way mentioned by the theologians. So it appears that upon all suppositions, one must acknowledge the existence of a god (ilāh) for this world who manages it and disposes in it as He wills and desires: (Indeed, His is the creation and the command; blessed is Allah, Lord of the worlds) (Quran 7:54). And with Allah is success.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof for the existence of God based on the motion of celestial bodies. The author first considers two scenarios: either the bodies were non-existent and then came into existence and moved, or they were existent but stationary and then began to move. In both cases, reason testifies that they need a mover and director. He then addresses the disagreement between philosophers and theologians: philosophers claim celestial motions have no beginning, requiring a non-corporeal mover; theologians claim they have a beginning, requiring a creator. The author concludes that regardless of which view one takes, all roads lead to the necessity of acknowledging a God who manages the world. The argument is structured to be universally persuasive, using the observable fact of motion as a starting point.
