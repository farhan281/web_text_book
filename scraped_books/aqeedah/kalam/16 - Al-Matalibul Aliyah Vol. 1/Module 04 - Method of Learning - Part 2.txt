The Value of the Premise of Similarity of Bodies
The author explains the value of the premise of bodies' similarity for proving God, prophethood, and resurrection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the premise of similarity of bodies serves as a foundation for proving God's existence, prophethood, and the resurrection in Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Similarity of bodies is the key premise that unlocks proofs for God, prophethood, and the afterlife.

This passage presents the essential premise of similarity of bodies, which the theologians consider a noble and highly beneficial foundation in Islamic theology. By establishing this premise, they build arguments for three central doctrines: the existence of a free-agent God, the possibility of miracles and prophethood, and the reality of the resurrection. The author emphasizes that this premise makes possible the breaking of natural habits and the events described in the Qur'an.

The Two Views on the Need for a Cause in Origination
Introduces consensus that origination suffices, then classifies two views on need for a cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two positions among theologians regarding whether the knowledge that an originated thing needs a cause is necessary (intuitive) or inferential, and why mere origination is considered sufficient to prove the existence of an agent.

Core Idea
Mere origination suffices; the debate is whether the need for a cause is intuitive or requires proof.

In this passage, the author sets the stage for the 'method of origination' that does not require adding possibility. He notes that most theologians agree that simply observing an originated event is enough to infer an agent. But they split into two camps: those who see this knowledge as intuitive and those who see it as inferential. Al-Ka‘bi’s view is highlighted with an intuitive argument, including the example of a sound.

Guided Path
1
Read the consensus statement
Begin by reading the author's opening claim: the majority of theologians agree that mere origination suffices for inference to the agent. This is the foundation of the chapter.
Note the phrase 'جمهور المتكلمين' (majority of theologians).
2
Identify the two groups
The passage divides those who hold this consensus into two factions. Identify each group and its epistemological claim: one says knowledge is necessary (ḍarūrī), the other says it is inferential (istidlālī).
The first group includes al-Ka‘bi and a great many Muslims; the second includes Abu ‘Ali and Abu Hashim.
3
Examine al-Ka‘bi's argument
Read al-Ka‘bi's reasoning: rational people immediately seek a cause when they perceive an originated event, without hesitation. This, he argues, shows the knowledge is intuitive and ingrained.
Focus on the phrase 'بديهة العقل' (intuition of the intellect).
4
Understand the intuition's requirement
Notice the intuition of the intellect judges that an originated event must have an originated cause—not a pre-existing one from months or years before. This strengthens the immediacy of the need.
The example of the sound illustrates this point clearly.
5
Reflect on the debate's significance
Consider why this debate matters: if the need for a cause is intuitive, then the proof from origination is immediate; if inferential, it requires additional premises. Yet all agree on the sufficiency of origination.
This connects to broader kalām discussions on causality and the existence of God.

Commentary
This passage is the beginning of the nineteenth chapter, titled 'Exposition That the Method of Origination Does Not Require Joining Possibility'. The author first states a consensus: the majority of theologians (jumhūr al-mutakallimīn) agree that mere origination (mujarrad al-ḥudūth) is sufficient to infer the existence of an agent. Then he divides those who hold this view into two groups. The first group holds that the knowledge that an originated thing needs an originator is necessary (ḍarūrī) and intuitive; this is the position of al-Ka‘bī (a Mu‘tazilī) and many from Ahl al-Islam. The second group holds that this knowledge is inferential (istidlālī), requiring a proof; this is the position of Abu ‘Ali and Abu Hashim (also Mu‘tazila). The author then elaborates on al-Ka‘bī's argument: rational people instinctively seek a cause for any new event without reflection, so the need for a cause is intuitive. The intellect's intuition (badīhat al-ʿaql) rules that an originated event must have an originated cause (sabab ḥādith), not a pre-existing cause from months or years earlier. He gives the example of a sound: when you hear a sound, you immediately seek its cause. The passage thus presents a key debate within kalam about the epistemological status of the need for a cause, and argues for the sufficiency of origination alone.

The Two Positions on Knowledge of Dependence
Classification of two groups and elaboration of one group's argument

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two main positions on how we know that originated things depend on an agent, and the intuitive argument of al-Ka'bi.

Core Idea
Some theologians see the dependence as intuitive, others as inferential; both agree huduth suffices.

The majority of theologians agree that the mere fact that something is originated (huduth) is enough to prove an agent. But they disagree on how we know that dependence—whether it is immediately obvious (intuitive) or requires a rational proof. This passage introduces these two camps and presents the intuitive argument with a vivid example.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening classification
The author states the agreement among most theologians that huduth suffices, then divides the claimants into two groups. Note the consensus and the division.
2
Study the first group (al-Ka'bi)
Al-Ka'bi holds that knowledge of dependence is necessary (ḍarūrī) and intuitive (badīhī). His reasoning: rational people spontaneously seek a cause for any new event without reflection.
This group includes a large party of Muslims.
3
Study the second group (Abu Ali, Abu Hashim)
This group holds that knowledge of dependence is inferential (istidlālī) and requires a proof. They are Mu'tazili theologians.
4
Analyze al-Ka'bi's example
Al-Ka'bi uses the example of hearing a sound: one instinctively seeks a cause. If the explanation is a pre-existing condition (sky above, earth below), the intellect rejects it because that condition existed before the sound. This shows that the cause must be contemporary (ḥādith) and that this judgment is intuitive.
This example illustrates why the knowledge is considered intuitive.

Commentary

The Four Pillars of the Mu'tazilite Qiyas
Mu'tazilites present the four pillars of their qiyas for contingency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite claim that the argument from contingency requires a detailed analogical proof and the four pillars (asl, far', illa, hukm) that structure it.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazilites insist that the inference from contingency to a creator must be a formal qiyas with four components.

The Mu'tazilites, led by figures like Abu Ali and Abu Hashim, argued that the simple statement 'the world is contingent, therefore it needs a creator' is insufficient. They demanded a detailed analogy (qiyas mufassal) with clearly defined components. This lesson introduces that structure, which sets the stage for the rest of their proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow, then read the English translation. Notice how the Mu'tazilites present their claim as a formal analogy.
This passage is a report of the Mu'tazilite doctrine, not an original argument.
2
Identify the four pillars (arkan)
The passage explicitly states that every qiyas has four pillars: asl, far', illa, hukm. Locate these in the text and note what each one represents in this specific argument.
The asl is the human servant originating his own actions, the far' is the world, the hukm is the need for an agent, and the illa is contingency.
3
Understand the role of the qiyas
Why do they insist on a qiyas? Reflect on why they think a direct inference is not enough. The commentary will help explain the reasoning behind this methodological requirement.
4
Connect to the next lesson
The passage ends by stating the need to establish the asl (original case). This lesson sets up the next lesson, which will cover how they prove the asl.
The asl is the most critical part to establish for the analogy to work.

Chapter Twenty
Statement of the one who says: The inference from contingency to the agent is not complete except with a detailed proof.
We have reported that this statement is the doctrine of Abu Ali and Abu Hashim, who is al-Qadi Abd al-Jabbar ibn Ahmad, and their companions from the Mu'tazila. They said: The arrangement of this proof is that it is said: The world is contingent, so it necessarily depends on an agent, by analogy to our actions which need us in their origination. They said: Every analogy necessarily has four pillars: the original case (al-asl), the derivative case (al-far'), the cause (al-illa), and the ruling (al-hukm). So the original case is that the servant is the originator of his own actions, the derivative case is the world, the ruling is the need for an agent, and the cause is contingency.
So we need to establish these four stations: As for the original case, it is our statement: Our actions need us in their origination. This station requires the establishment of two matters: one: establishing the accidents (al-a'rad), i.e., establishing that our movements and rests are existing things. The second: clarifying that our actions occur by us, meaning we are their originator, not another. As for the first station, they mentioned in it the proofs that demonstrate the existence of accidents. As for the second station, they argued for it by saying that these actions necessarily occur when our motives are pure and necessarily cease when repulsive factors exist. And if the matter is thus, then it is necessary to be certain that they occur by us and not by another.
The proof of the necessity of their occurrence when strong motives are obtained: That the fasting person in summer [the thirsty] when his desire for drinking water intensifies, and the legislator commands him to drink, and the doctor commands him to drink, and this person knows that there is no harm in this drinking, neither in his religion nor in his worldly affairs, then he obtains through this drinking the greatest pleasures. For when these beliefs are present in his heart, it is impossible that he does not drink water. So it is established that the action, when pure motives are available free from repellants, is necessary of occurrence. And the proof that the action is impossible of occurrence when pure repellants are obtained: That one who knows what is in entering the fire of great harm, and knows that there is no benefit for him in entering it at all, neither in the present nor in the future, and he does not seek through that entry to repel a harm greater than it, then when these beliefs are present in his heart, entering the fire is impossible for him.
So it is established by this clarification that when motives are pure, actions necessarily occur, and when repellants are pure, their occurrence is impossible. And once this is established, we say: It is necessary to be certain that these actions occur by us and not by another. For if their occurrence were not by us, then their occurrence would either be without an effect, or by an effect other than us. On both hypotheses, it would not be impossible that they occur even if we dislike them, nor that they do not occur even if we want them. Just as the action of Zayd, since it does not occur by Amr, inevitably perhaps occurs even if Amr dislikes it, and perhaps does not occur even if Amr wants it. And that would contradict what we explained: that those actions are necessary in occurrence when motives are available and impossible in occurrence when repellants are available. So it is established by this clarification we mentioned that our actions occur by us. And this is the establishment of the original case of the aforementioned analogy.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed presentation of the Mu'tazilite position on the argument from contingency (al-istidlal bi al-huduth). The author reports that Abu Ali, Abu Hashim (Qadi Abd al-Jabbar), and their followers argue that the simple inference 'the world is contingent, therefore it needs an agent' is insufficient without a detailed analogical proof (qiyas mufassal). They structure their proof as a qiyas with four pillars: the original case (asl), the derivative case (far'), the cause (illa), and the ruling (hukm). For them, the asl is that the human servant (al-abd) is the originator of his own actions, the far' is the world, the hukm is the need for an agent, and the illa is contingency (al-huduth). To establish the asl, they must prove two things: first, that human actions (movements and rests) are existing entities (accidents, a'rad), and second, that these actions are produced by us and not by another. The author notes that the Mu'tazilites have separate proofs for the existence of accidents. Then he presents their argument for the second point: human actions necessarily occur when our motives (dawa'i) are pure and free from repellants (sawari'), and necessarily cease when pure repellants are present. To prove this, they give two examples: a fasting person who is commanded to drink by both the legislator and a doctor, knowing no harm, will inevitably drink because the pleasure is great and motives are pure. Conversely, a person who knows the immense harm of entering fire and sees no benefit will inevitably refrain. Thus, actions are contingent upon motives and repellants. From this, they conclude that actions are from us, because if actions came from another, they could occur against our will or fail to occur when we want, contradicting the observed necessity. This establishes the asl (original case) of their analogy.

Establishing the Asl: Proving Human Actions are from Us
Mu'tazilites prove the asl by showing human actions are from us via motive/repellant argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Mu'tazilites prove that human actions are produced by the agent using the argument from motives and repellants.

Core Idea
Actions necessarily follow pure motives and are prevented by strong repellants, proving they are from us.

Having presented the four pillars of their qiyas, the Mu'tazilites now focus on establishing the asl (original case): that human actions are originated by the human agent. They do this by proving two things: first, that actions are existing entities (accidents), and second, that they occur by us. The second point is proven through a detailed argument about motives (dawa'i) and repellants (sawari'). This lesson walks through that argument, including the famous examples of the fasting person and the person facing fire.

Guided Path
1
Read the full passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation in full. This is a long but self-contained argument. Try to grasp the overall structure: two stations, with a detailed proof for the second.
The first station (establishing accidents) is only mentioned; the focus is on the second station.
2
Identify the two stations (maqamat)
The passage says the asl requires two matters: proving accidents exist, and proving actions are from us. Locate these in the text.
The first station is not elaborated; the author says they have proofs elsewhere.
3
Analyze the proof from motives and repellants
The second station uses the principle that actions necessarily occur when motives are pure and free from repellants, and necessarily do not occur when repellants are pure. The examples of the fasting person and the fire illustrate this.
Note the specific conditions: pure motives, no repellants, knowledge of harm/benefit.
4
Understand the reductio ad absurdum
After establishing the necessity of actions based on motives/repellants, the Mu'tazilites argue that if actions were not from us, they could occur against our will, contradicting the observed necessity. This forces the conclusion that actions are from us.
This is a classic kalām argument from observed regularity.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
The passage ends by stating that the asl of the analogy is established. Connect this back to the overall qiyas: if human actions are from us, then the world, being contingent like them, must also need an agent.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand why ambiguous scriptural descriptions of God's limbs must be interpreted metaphorically to align with clear verses affirming God's transcendence.

Core Idea
Clear verses forbid likening God to creation; ambiguous verses must yield.

In this lesson, we examine how scriptures describe God using human-like features (limbs) and abstract attributes (power, will). We see examples from the Torah, Gospel, and Quran, and then learn a key principle: when verses conflict, the clear ones (muhkam) that deny similarity override the ambiguous ones (mutashabih). This leads to metaphorical interpretation of anthropomorphic language.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of attributes
Read the passage and notice the distinction between 'attributes of limbs' (e.g., hand, foot, throne) and 'attributes of meanings' (e.g., power, will, justice). These appear in all three scriptures.
Attributes of limbs, if taken literally, imply God has a body.
2
Recognize the apparent contradiction
The author presents verses that describe God with limbs and verses that say 'nothing is like Him.' Understand why this creates a theological problem: affirming literal limbs would violate God's transcendence.
The author argues that affirming corporeality leads to anthropomorphism, which is prohibited.
3
Learn the solution: prioritize muhkam over mutashabih
The author explains that verses denying similitude are muhkam (clear, single meaning), while limb verses are mutashabih (ambiguous, two meanings). The clear verses take precedence, so limbs must be interpreted metaphorically as attributes like power.
This principle is known as ta'wil in Islamic theology.

Commentary
This passage presents a comparative analysis of scriptural anthropomorphic language and establishes a hermeneutical principle for interpreting such verses. The author first gives examples from the Gospel and Torah showing both 'attributes of limbs' (e.g., God's throne, footstool) and 'attributes of meanings' (e.g., power, will). Jesus' words about glorifying God are cited to show God's perfection and transcendence. Quranic examples follow: 'the hand of God' (limb attribute) and the story of destroyed people (implying power and justice). The author then argues that across scriptures there are verses affirming that God is unlike anything, implying He is not a body. The core argument is that affirming corporeality would entail size and form, which leads to anthropomorphism, prohibited by verses like 'There is nothing like unto Him.' The dilemma is posed: how can we negate limbs when scriptures appear to affirm them? The answer is that the negation verses (like 'not like anything') are muhkam (clear, definitive) with one interpretation, while the limb verses are mutashabih (ambiguous) with two possible interpretations: literal (physical) or metaphorical (power, etc.). Since the clear verses forbid similitude, the metaphorical interpretation must be adopted. Thus 'hand' is interpreted as power, 'istiwa'' as dominance. The author follows the classic Ash'ari method of tafwid or ta'wil, prioritizing the clear verses over the ambiguous.

The Argument from Infinite Regress for Self-Difference
Sanusi presents the first proof that essences can differ by themselves, avoiding infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical structure of the first proof that essences can differ by themselves, and why the infinite regress is rejected.

Core Idea
If difference requires an infinite chain, then self-difference is the only option.

Sanusi begins his first proof by setting up a conditional scenario. He supposes that essences are equal in essentiality and only differ through attributes. Then he examines whether those attributes can themselves differ, leading to either an infinite regress or self-difference. This argument establishes a key premise for the broader discussion.

Guided Path
1
Understand the supposition
Start by grasping the hypothetical scenario: essences are assumed equal in essentiality, and their difference is attributed to their associated attributes.

Since the only remaining possibility is that the attributes differ by themselves, it follows that self-difference is possible. This supports the main claim.
The conclusion is that it is not impossible for things to be different by themselves.

Two Methods for Divine Knowledge and the First Method
Introduces two methods for divine knowledge and elaborates the first method (الأولى والأخلق).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two approaches to divine knowledge in Islamic theology and the reasoning behind the method of 'the first and more worthy' (al-awla wa al-akhlaq) which employs a parsimony argument regarding intermediaries.

Core Idea
The first method argues that since only a Necessary Being is proven, any intermediaries are superfluous.

This passage from al-Matalib al-Aliyah sets out two approaches to proving God's existence. The first, known as the method of 'the first and more worthy', bases itself on the principle of parsimony: once the existence of a Necessary Being is established, any intermediaries in the causal chain are not required. The second approach demands certain proofs. We will explore the first method in detail.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Read the opening line where two positions are introduced. Notice that the first is called 'way of al-awla wa al-akhlaq' and the second insists on certain proofs.
The term 'al-awla wa al-akhlaq' literally means 'the first and more worthy'.
2
Understand the causal argument
The passage presents a causal argument: the world is originated, so it needs a maker. If that maker were also originated, it would need another, leading to an infinite regress or a cycle, both invalid.
So the chain must end with a being that is eternal and necessary.
3
Apply the principle of parsimony
The argument then says: since the proofs only establish the Necessary Being, and do not prove any intermediaries, those intermediaries should be dropped. This is the core of the method.
This is similar to Ockham's razor.
4
Reflect on the significance
Consider why this method is called 'the first and more worthy' — it is seen as more straightforward and simpler.
It avoids unnecessary metaphysical entities.

And know that there are two positions here: One of them: the way of those who say that there is no way to acquire divine knowledge except by adhering to the method of the first and more worthy. And the second: the way of those who say that certain proofs are necessary. As for the first way: its exposition is that it is said: Since it is established that the world is temporally originated, it must need a maker. Commentary
This passage first identifies two positions (maqaman) regarding the attainment of divine knowledge (al-ma'arif al-ilahiyya). The first is the method of 'the first and more worthy' (tariqat al-awla wa al-akhlaq), which holds that one must adhere to this principle. The second is the method that insists on certain proofs (al-dala'il al-yaqiniyya). The author then explains the first method in detail: since the world is temporally originated (muhdath), it requires a maker (fa'il). If that maker were also originated, it would require another maker, leading to an infinite regress (tasalsul) or a cycle (dawr), both of which are invalid. Therefore, the chain must end with an eternal maker that is necessary of existence in itself (wajib al-wujud li-dhatihi). The proponents then argue: since the proofs necessitate acknowledging a necessary being, but they do not necessitate the existence of any intermediaries (al-wasa'it) between the world and that necessary being, those intermediaries should be dropped, and we should be content with the necessary being alone. This is the essence of the method of 'the first and more worthy'.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That al-awla wa al-akhlaq is a distinct argument for the existence of God, similar to the cosmological argument with multiple steps.
Correction: It is actually a principle of parsimony (like Ockham's razor) applied after establishing a Necessary Being through other proofs, to eliminate unnecessary intermediaries.
