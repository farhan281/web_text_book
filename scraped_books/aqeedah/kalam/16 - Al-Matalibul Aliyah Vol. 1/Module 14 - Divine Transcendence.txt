Three Degrees of Perception: Sight and Intellect
Analogy comparing three levels of sight and insight

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the threefold classification of objects of perception in both physical sight and intellectual insight, and how the intellect's inability to perceive God's essence is due to His overwhelming radiance, not any deficiency.

Core Idea
Like the eye overwhelmed by sunlight, the intellect is blinded by God's manifest glory.

This passage from Al-Matalib al-Aliyah uses a powerful analogy to explain why the human intellect cannot fully comprehend the essence of God. By examining how our eyes perceive objects of varying intensity, the author reveals a parallel structure in intellectual perception. As you read, note how the same three categories—weak, moderate, and overpowering—apply to both physical sight and intellectual insight, leading to a profound conclusion about divine transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the complete Arabic passage carefully, then the English translation. Notice the author's use of the sun analogy to explain the limits of perception.
The passage is dense; take your time to absorb the details.
2
Identify the three categories of physical sight
The author describes three types of objects of vision (mubsirat): overpowering (like the sun), moderate, and weak. Note how each affects the power of sight.
The weak objects are mentioned only briefly; focus on the sun example.
3
Map the analogy to intellectual insight
The author explicitly says the same three levels apply to objects of knowledge (ma'lumat). Identify the three categories: weak (changing bodily states), overpowering (holy substances, especially God's essence), and moderate.
The overpowering category includes a hadith and a saying of a righteous person.
4
Understand the key conclusion
The passage concludes that just as the eye cannot see the sun's disk directly, the intellect cannot know God's essence directly. This is due to the intensity of God's manifestation, not any defect in God.
This guards against the misconception that God is unknowable because He is absent or deficient.

Commentary
This passage presents an extended analogy between physical sight and intellectual insight, arguing that there are three categories of perceptible objects in each domain. The author first describes three levels of physical objects of vision (mubsirat): overpowering objects like the sun's disk, which the eye cannot directly perceive due to their intensity, causing the eye to imagine darkness where there is light; weak objects (though these are not described in detail here, the third state is moderate objects); and moderate objects, such as the qualities of ordinary bodies, which the eye can perceive fully. The conclusion is that the power of sight is incapable of perceiving both overpowering and weak objects, but can perceive intermediate ones. The author then explicitly states that the same three levels apply to the power of insight (basira) and intellect ('aql), with objects of knowledge (ma'lumat) divided into three categories: (1) weak and lowly objects, such as the changing states of generated bodies, which human intellects cannot fully grasp due to their subtlety and transience; (2) overpowering, lofty, holy objects, which are the immaterial essences and ultimately the Essence of God and His attributes—the intellect is incapable of perceiving God's essence due to the extreme radiance of His majesty, supported by the prophetic hadith about seventy veils of light and a saying of a righteous person; and (3) moderate objects, which the intellect can perceive and encompass. The author concludes that intellects are driven (the text cuts off, but the argument is that they are driven to recognize their limits and focus on God's manifestations rather than His essence). The passage guards against the misunderstanding that the human intellect can fully comprehend God's essence, emphasizing that the inability to perceive is due to the intensity of God's manifestation, not any deficiency in God.

Commentary
This passage categorizes intellectual objects into three types and argues for the limitation of human reason regarding the divine. The author first describes the second category: overwhelming, lofty, holy intellectual objects—namely, the holy substances and essences abstracted from matter. The most noble is God's Essence, attributes, and descriptors. Due to the extreme brilliance of His majesty, intellects cannot perceive Him; even imagination and understanding fall short. The author cites the Prophet's hadith about seventy veils of light and a saying of the righteous: God is veiled by His very manifestation and hidden by His perfect light. The intellect is thus incapable of grasping Him. An analogy: the eye cannot see the sun's disk directly—it sees only blackness or darkness there, and perceives light only from the edges. So the intellect, when striving to comprehend God's essence, becomes bewildered and sees nothing, yet it can perceive the outpouring of His mercy and generosity reaching creation. Next, the author describes the third category: moderate intellectual objects—neither extremely powerful nor extremely lowly—which the rational faculty can perceive and encompass. The conclusion drawn from both categories is that intellects and thoughts are subdued; the reality of truth cannot be reached by mental fancies, and divine grandeur cannot be comprehended by syllogisms. The only outcome for intellects is general affirmation of God's absolute perfection and negation of all deficiencies, while detailed knowledge is beyond human rational power.

The Proof That God's Essence is Inconceivable
Sanusi proves God's essence is not among perceived essences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key kalam argument that God's essence cannot be conceived because it would otherwise be composite.

Core Idea
If God shared any aspect with creation, His essence would be composite, which is impossible.

In this passage, Sanusi applies the earlier premise about the four concept types to the divine essence. He argues that God's reality is absolutely different from all perceived essences. If it shared any aspect, it would be composite, and composition is impossible for God. Therefore, His essence is inconceivable, and no judgment can be made about it.

Commentary
This passage presents a complete kalam proof for the everlastingness of God, specifically that what is proven to be eternal cannot cease to exist. It is a structured argument with an introduction, an enumeration of possible causes of non-existence, and a refutation of each. The author begins by stating the principle: since God's existence has been proven eternal and beginningless, that which is eternal cannot possibly cease. Then the proof (hujja) of the Ash'aris is given: if the eternal were to cease, its non-existence would have to be caused by one of three things: annihilation by an agent, overtaking by an opposite, or loss of a condition. All three are shown to be impossible. The passage then explains the enumeration: for any thing that ceases, its non-existence must be possible (jā'iz), not necessary, because if it were necessary it would have always been non-existent, contradicting its prior existence. Since it is possible, it needs a preponderant (murajjih), which is either non-existential (i.e., loss of a condition) or existential. The existential cause is either by choice (annihilation by an agent) or by necessitation (overtaking by an opposite). This establishes the threefold division. Next, each division is refuted. The first division (annihilation by an agent) is impossible for two reasons: (1) non-existence is pure negation, so it cannot be the effect of an agent because an agent must produce a positive effect; (2) the agent that annihilates must be either eternal or originated. If eternal, then neither eternal has superiority over the other to annihilate it, so it is impossible. If originated, it is weaker than the eternal and cannot annihilate it. The second division (overtaking by an opposite) is refuted by three arguments: (1) if an originated opposite overtakes, its overtaking is conditional on the prior non-existence of the original eternal, but if we make that non-existence caused by the overtaking, circularity (dawr) follows; (2) since opposition is mutual, the non-existence of the original due to the overtaking is not more likely than the repulsion of the overtaking by the original, and repelling is easier than removing; (3) if the opposite is eternal, then both opposites would have coexisted in pre-eternity, which is impossible; if originated, it is weaker and cannot overpower the eternal. The third division (loss of a condition) is impossible because if the condition is eternal, then the same difficulty recurs about its non-existence; if it is originated, it cannot be a condition for the eternal because the eternal is prior and cannot depend on something originated. The passage concludes that all three possibilities are impossible, therefore the eternal cannot cease.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: It is possible for God to annihilate another eternal being, as God is all-powerful.

Refuting Overtaking by an Opposite and Loss of Condition
Sanusi refutes the second and third possible causes: overtaking by an opposite and loss of a condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the arguments against the second and third possible causes of non-existence: overtaking by an opposite and loss of a condition.

Core Idea
An opposite cannot overtake the eternal without circularity, and the eternal cannot depend on a condition that is originated.

After refuting annihilation by an agent, Sanusi turns to the second and third divisions: overtaking by an opposite (طريان ضد) and loss of a condition (انتفاء شرط). For the opposite, he gives three arguments: first, it leads to circularity (دور) because the opposite's overtaking requires the prior non-existence of the original, but that non-existence would be caused by the overtaking; second, mutual opposition means the original could just as easily repel the opposite; third, the opposite cannot be eternal (coexistence in pre-eternity impossible) nor originated (too weak). For loss of condition, the condition cannot be eternal (same problem recurs) nor originated (the eternal cannot depend on something originated). This lesson clarifies these refutations and shows why all three causes are impossible, so the eternal cannot cease.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the opposite
The passage gives three reasons why overtaking by an opposite is impossible. Read them one by one.
The first reason involves circularity; the second is about symmetry; the third is about the nature of the opposite.
2
Read the passage on loss of condition
Then the passage refutes the third division: loss of condition. It argues the condition cannot be eternal or originated.
3
Analyze the first reason for opposite
The overtaking of an originated opposite requires the prior non-existence of the original. If that non-existence is caused by the overtaking, a circularity (dawr) results. Trace this logic.
Think: which is cause and which is effect?
4
Analyze the second and third reasons
The second reason: since opposition is mutual, the original could repel the opposite. The third reason: the opposite cannot be eternal (coexistence in pre-eternity) nor originated (too weak).
5
Understand the refutation of loss of condition
If the condition is eternal, it has the same issue; if it is originated, the eternal cannot depend on it because the eternal is prior.
This is a strong argument based on priority of existence.

This passage is the first proof (الحجة الأولى) that the eternal (al-qadim) cannot become non-existent. If originated, the originated is weaker in existence and cannot overpower the eternal. Third, the author considers destruction by an opposite. He gives three reasons why this is impossible: (a) circularity – if the originated opposite occurs only when the eternal opposite is absent, then making the absence caused by the opposite is circular; (b) symmetry – opposite-ness is mutual, so it is not more likely that the newcomer eliminates the existing one; rather, repelling is easier than removing; (c) if the opposite is eternal, both would have existed from pre-eternity, which is absurd; if originated, the weaker cannot eliminate the stronger. Fourth, he rejects the possibility that the eternal ceased to exist due to absence of a condition. The condition itself would either be eternal (leading to the same problem) or originated (impossible because the eternal precedes the originated and cannot depend on it). Therefore, all three possibilities are impossible, so the eternal cannot become non-existent.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think the eternal's existence depends on conditions just like contingent things.
Correction: The eternal is prior to all originated things and cannot be conditioned by them.

Key Takeaway
The eternal cannot cease to exist by absence of a condition because any such condition would either be eternal (same problem) or originated (impossible dependence), and thus all possible causes are eliminated, proving the eternal's permanence.
