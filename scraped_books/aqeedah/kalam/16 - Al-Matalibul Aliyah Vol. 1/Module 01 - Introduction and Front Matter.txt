Editor's Introduction
Praise be to Allah, Lord of the worlds. And blessings and peace be upon the seal of the prophets, Muhammad son of Abdullah, and upon his pure family, his righteous Companions, and those who follow them in goodness until the Day of Judgment.
To proceed:
This is the book Al-Matalib al-Aliyah from the divine science (al-ʿilm al-ilāhī) by the noble Imam: Muhammad ibn Umar ibn al-Husayn, famously known as Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, who died in the year 606 AH. It is a book in the science of kalam (ʿilm al-kalām), which no one has printed before us. Its manuscripts contain nine parts:
The first part: on the proofs indicating the existence of God (ithbāt al-ilāh) for this sensible world, and the explanation that He is necessary of existence (wājib al-wujūd) in His essence.
The second part: on the proofs indicating unity (al-tawḥīd) and transcendence (al-tanzīh).
The third part: on mentioning the positive attributes (al-ṣifāt al-ījābiyya), which are: that He, glorified, is powerful, knowing, living, hearing, seeing, speaking, eternal, wise.
The fourth part: on the investigations of origination (al-ḥudūth) and pre-eternity (al-qidam), and the secrets of time (al-dahr) and eternity (al-azal).
The fifth part: on time (al-zamān) and space (al-makān).

Commentary
This passage is the editor's introduction to the book Al-Matalib al-Aliyah. It begins with the basmala and hamdala, then states the book's title and author: Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, a renowned Ash'ari theologian who died in 606 AH. The editor notes that this is a work of kalam (ʿilm al-kalām) and that it has never been printed before. He then lists the nine parts (ajzāʾ) of the book based on the manuscripts. The third part lists the positive attributes: power, knowledge, life, hearing, sight, speech, eternity, and wisdom. The fourth part discusses ḥudūth (origination) and qidam (pre-eternity), along with the secrets of dahr (time/age) and azal (eternity without beginning). The fifth part treats zamān (time) and makān (space). The introduction sets the stage for the book's comprehensive treatment of theology.

Refuting a Claim with Internal Evidence
Refutes a claim using two internal evidences from the author's own text.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi's own table of contents and cross-references disprove the claim that volume 8 is the last volume of Al-Matalib al-Aliya.

Core Idea
Internal textual evidence can overturn external claims about a book's structure.

This lesson examines a case study in scholarly refutation. A researcher claimed that volume 8 on prophecies is al-Razi's final volume. But al-Razi himself left clear clues in his introduction and text that he intended to cover predestination after prophecies. By tracing these clues, we see how careful reading solves the problem.

Guided Path
1
Read the refutation passage
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice the structure: it states the false claim and then presents two refutation points.
The passage is incomplete but the argument is clear.
2
Identify the two pieces of evidence
The first evidence is from the book's introduction: al-Razi says he will discuss predestination (al-qada wa al-qadar) as part of the fourth section. The second is from a chapter on prophecies: he begins an introduction stating that the doctrine of jabr is true.
3
Understand how each evidence refutes the claim
If the book ended at prophecies, there would be no room for predestination. But al-Razi explicitly includes it in his outline. Moreover, the chapter on prophecies itself starts discussing jabr, showing that predestination is already being treated within the prophecies section, so the work continues.
The second evidence is even stronger: it shows overlap.
4
Reflect on the method
Think about why using internal references is more reliable than a researcher's assumption. Al-Razi's own words override external speculation.
This is a valuable skill for textual criticism.
