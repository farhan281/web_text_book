Proof that the Necessary Being is Pure Existence
Razi proves that the Necessary Being has no quiddity apart from existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that the Necessary Being has no quiddity apart from His existence, and why any attempt to posit a quiddity leads to logical impossibility.

Core Idea
The Necessary Being is pure existence — no quiddity, no composition.

This passage presents a rigorous proof for divine simplicity. Razi systematically eliminates every possible way that the Necessary Being (واجب الوجود) could have a quiddity (ماهية) distinct from His existence. Each option is shown to lead to a logical contradiction, establishing that He is nothing but pure existence. This is a foundational argument in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem
The question is whether the Necessary Being's existence is an accident of its quiddity (i.e., whether it has a quiddity that then receives existence). Read the first line to see how Razi sets up the framework.
Razi starts by considering the Necessary Being as possible-in-itself, which demands a cause.
2
First case: quiddity necessitates existence with condition
If the quiddity's necessitation of existence is conditioned on its being existent, then either the condition is the same as the effect (circular) or different (duplication). Both are absurd. Also leads to infinite regress or circle.
Pay attention to the distinction between condition and effect.
3
Second case: quiddity necessitates existence without condition
If the condition is not required, then a non-existent thing would be a cause of existence. This is self-evidently impossible because nothing can cause without existing.
This is a direct appeal to intuitive reason (بديهة العقل).
4
Third case: cause is something other than the quiddity
If the cause of existence is external to the quiddity, then the Necessary Being would be dependent on another, contradicting its necessity.
This shows the only remaining option is ruled out.
5
Conclusion and significance
All possible ways to have a quiddity are impossible. Therefore the Necessary Being has no reality except pure existence. Reflect on why this is important for divine simplicity.
The conclusion is that He is 'وجود مجرد عن جميع الماهيات'.

It is possible in itself. And every possible-in-itself must have a cause, and that cause is either that quiddity or something else. It is not permissible that the cause of that existence be that quiddity, because the fact that that quiddity necessitates that existence is either conditioned by its being existent or not conditioned by this condition. It is not permissible that the necessitation of that quiddity for that existence be conditioned by its being existent, because the existence which is the condition of necessitation, if it is the same as the existence which is the effect, then one thing would be a condition for itself, which is impossible; and if it is other than it, then the same thing would exist twice, which is impossible. And because the discussion about the second existence is like the discussion about the first, there follows either an infinite regress or a circle, both of which are impossible. All this if we say that the necessitation of that quiddity for that existence is conditioned by that quiddity being existent. But if we say that this condition is not considered, then this is a statement that what is not existent is a necessitator of existence, and that is impossible by the intuitive intellect, because a thing, as long as it is not existent in itself, is prevented from being a cause for the existence of another. And all this if we say that the necessitating cause of that existence is that quiddity. But if we say that the necessitating cause of that existence is something other than that quiddity, then the existence of the Necessary Being in itself would be derived from a separate cause, so the Necessary Being in itself would be necessary by another, and that is impossible. So it is established that the statement that the existence of the Necessary Being in itself is an accident of its quiddity leads to these impossibilities, so it must be that this statement is impossible. So it is established that He has no reality except existence stripped of all quiddities. This is the exposition of this argument in the strongest way.

Commentary
This passage is a proof that the Necessary Being (واجب الوجود) has no quiddity (ماهية) distinct from His existence. The author, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, begins by noting that anything possible in itself requires a cause for its existence. He then examines whether the cause of the Necessary Being's existence could be its own quiddity. He considers two cases: either the quiddity's necessitation of existence is conditioned by its being existent, or it is not. If conditioned, then the existence that is the condition is either the same as the effect (leading to circularity) or different (leading to duplication of existence). Both are absurd. Also, it would lead to infinite regress or circle. If not conditioned, then a non-existent thing would be a cause of existence, which is self-evidently false. Then he considers whether the cause could be something other than the quiddity. If so, the Necessary Being would be caused by another, contradicting its necessity. Thus all possibilities lead to absurdity, so it is impossible that existence is an accident of the quiddity. The conclusion: the Necessary Being is nothing but pure existence, without any additional quiddity. This is a standard kalam argument for divine simplicity.

The Form-Matter Analogy for the Independence of Existence
Razi's first answer: the form-matter analogy and two sub-possibilities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi uses the analogy of form and matter to show that existence can be independent of quiddity while still inhering in it.

Core Idea
Existence can be both independent and inherent, like form in matter.

Razi's first answer to the objection that existence must be dependent on quiddity draws on a familiar Aristotelian example: form is the cause of matter's existence yet inheres in matter. This lesson unpacks that analogy and the two sub-possibilities Razi proposes to resolve the apparent contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and Razi's first response
Read the Arabic passage from 'والجواب عن هذه الشبهة من وجوه : الأول' up to 'وهذان الاحتمالان قائمان'. Notice how Razi begins by questioning the premise.
Razi uses a question: 'Why is it not permissible to say existence is independent of quiddity?'
2
Understand the form-matter analogy
Razi points out that in natural philosophy, form is the cause of matter's existence, yet form inheres in matter. This shows that one thing can be a cause of another's existence and still be an inherent attribute.
This is key: independence in existence does not preclude inherence.
3
Analyze the two sub-possibilities
Razi offers two ways to understand how existence can be independent yet inherent: either existence itself causes its inherence, or the quiddity causes the inherence. Both avoid contradiction.
Both possibilities are logically coherent, so the objection fails.
4
Reflect on the lesson's core idea
Ask yourself: what does this analogy reveal about the relationship between existence and quiddity in the necessary being? The necessary being is pure existence, not a quiddity with an accident.
This prepares for the next lesson on the second answer.

Existence Cannot Be Described as Possible
Razi's second answer: even if existence were dependent, it cannot be possible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's second argument that even if existence were dependent on quiddity, it cannot be described as 'possible' because possibility requires a quiddity that can alternate between existence and non-existence, which cannot apply to existence itself.

Core Idea
Existence cannot be 'possible' because it would lead to an infinite regress of existences.

Razi's second answer grants the objection's hypothetical—that existence would be dependent on quiddity and thus 'possible'—but then argues that existence cannot be described as possible at all. The notion of possibility applies to quiddities that can be with or without existence, but applying it to existence itself leads to absurdity. This lesson examines this argument, which is unfortunately incomplete in the source text.

Guided Path
1
Read the second answer
Read from 'ههنا الوجه الثاني في الجواب' to the end of the provided passage. Notice that Razi grants the conditional but then introduces a proof that existence cannot be possible.
The text cuts off mid-argument; we work with what is present.
2
Understand the definition of 'possible'
Razi defines the possible existent as that whose quiddity can occur with existence sometimes and with non-existence other times. This definition is key.
Possibility implies contingency on a quiddity.
3
See why this definition cannot apply to existence
If we try to apply possibility to existence itself, we would have to say the quiddity of existence can be with existence (giving two existences) or with non-existence (giving a contradiction). Both are impossible.
This shows that existence is not a possible being; it is necessary or impossible.
4
Reflect on the incomplete argument
The passage ends mid-sentence. The full argument would likely further elaborate the absurdity. Consider how this supports the doctrine that the Necessary Being is pure existence without quiddity.
Despite incompleteness, the main point is clear: existence cannot be possible.

The Two Meanings of 'Essence as Such'
Sanusi distinguishes two interpretations of a key phrase and proves one true, the other false.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two possible interpretations of the statement 'the essence as such is neither existent nor non-existent' and why only one is correct.

Core Idea
Conceptual distinction is real; actual absence is impossible.

Before tackling the nature of the First Principle, Sanusi establishes a crucial premise. He takes a common phrase – 'essence as such is neither existent nor non-existent' – and shows it can mean two very different things. Which meaning we choose will determine our entire understanding of the First Principle.

The fourth question: On the verification of the statement explaining whether the First Principle is that specific essence.
Know that this inquiry must be preceded by a premise. It is: We say: When we say: 'such-and-such essence as such is neither existent nor non-existent,' this statement admits two interpretations:
One: That what is meant is that such-and-such essence as it is itself is an expression distinct from its being existent or non-existent.
Two: That what is meant is that such-and-such essence is devoid of existence and of non-existence. The first concept is true, and the second concept is false. For if we consider the triangle insofar as it is a triangle, this consideration insofar as it is itself is distinct from its being existent or from its being non-existent. The proof: that the concept of the word 'existent' and the concept of the word 'non-existent' are distinct from the concept of the word 'triangle'. The evidence: that it is valid to conceive of the existent and conceive of the non-existent while being heedless of conceiving the meaning of triangle, and likewise the triangle is also conceived insofar as it is a triangle with heedlessness of its being existent or its being non-existent. Thus it is apparent by this that its being a triangle is other than its being existent or non-existent. As for the second interpretation, which is that it be said: 'Its being a triangle is disconnected from the existent and from the non-existent,' this is impossible because a thing either has existence in concrete entities or does not have existence in concrete entities, and its being devoid of both is impossible in the intellect. The conclusion: that its being a triangle is distinct from its being existent or non-existent, but it is impossible for it to be disconnected from both together.
And when you have known this, we say: If we suppose that a certain reality among realities and a certain essence among essences was a cause for the existence of itself, then the cause of existence is that essence as such, not its being existent nor its being non-existent. Then it is true to say: 'The principle of that existence is neither existent nor non-existent,' and it is also true to say: 'The principle is existent, and it is the worthiest of existents of being existent.'
As for the first: it is true because we have shown that the principle of that existence is that essence, and we have shown that that essence as such is neither existent nor non-existent. As for the second: it is true because when that essence was a cause for that existent by its very identity and essence, then the intellect prevents that essence from being devoid of existence, so it is necessarily existent, and it is the worthiest of existents of the attribute of existence.
When you have known this, we say: It is reported from some people that they said: 'The First Principle is neither existent nor non-existent, but it is only Itself, and it is a haecceity that cannot be expressed by existence and non-existence.' As for the majority, they agreed that the First Principle is existent, rather it is the worthiest of existents of the attribute of existence. Long disputes occurred between the two parties on this matter. And if you contemplate the speech we have discussed and explained, you will know that the dispute is purely verbal.

Commentary
This passage is a resolution of the question whether the First Principle is that specific essence (i.e., whether the First Principle is the cause of its own existence). The author begins by stating that this inquiry requires a premise. The premise concerns the statement 'the essence as such is neither existent nor non-existent.' The author explains that this statement has two possible interpretations. The first interpretation is that the essence as such is merely conceptually distinct from existence and non-existence. The second interpretation is that the essence is actually devoid of both existence and non-existence. The author declares the first concept true and the second false. He provides the proof using the example of a triangle: the concept of triangle is distinct from the concepts of existent and non-existent, but that does not mean a triangle in external reality can be neither existent nor non-existent. It can be non-existent in concrete reality, but it cannot be both non-existent and existent simultaneously. The conclusion is that the essence is conceptually distinct from existence and non-existence, but it cannot be disconnected from both together; it must either be existent or non-existent.

Applying this to the First Principle: if we suppose that an essence is the cause of its own existence, then the cause of existence is that essence as such. From this, two true statements follow: (1) the principle of that existence is neither existent nor non-existent (because the essence as cause is distinct from existence), and (2) the principle is existent and the worthiest of existents (because the essence, being the cause of existence, cannot be devoid of existence, so it must be existent). The author explains each statement: the first is true because the cause is the essence as such, which is not characterized as existent or non-existent (it is beyond these categories in itself); the second is true because if the essence causes existence by its very essence, then the intellect necessitates that existence cannot be absent from it.

The author then reports two positions: some said the First Principle is neither existent nor non-existent (it is a pure haecceity beyond expression), while the majority held that it is existent and the worthiest of existence. After examining the argument, the author concludes that the dispute is purely verbal: both sides are correct from different angles, and the apparent contradiction is resolved by the distinction made in the premise.

Commentary
This passage is a resolution of the question whether the First Principle is that specific essence (i.e., whether the First Principle is the cause of its own existence). The author begins by stating that this inquiry requires a premise. The premise concerns the statement 'the essence as such is neither existent nor non-existent.' The author explains that this statement has two possible interpretations. The first interpretation is that the essence as such is merely conceptually distinct from existence and non-existence. The second interpretation is that the essence is actually devoid of both existence and non-existence. The author declares the first concept true and the second false. He provides the proof using the example of a triangle: the concept of triangle is distinct from the concepts of existent and non-existent, but that does not mean a triangle in external reality can be neither existent nor non-existent. It can be non-existent in concrete reality, but it cannot be both non-existent and existent simultaneously. The conclusion is that the essence is conceptually distinct from existence and non-existence, but it cannot be disconnected from both together; it must either be existent or non-existent.

Applying this to the First Principle: if we suppose that an essence is the cause of its own existence, then the cause of existence is that essence as such. The author explains each statement: the first is true because the cause is the essence as such, which is not characterized as existent or non-existent (it is beyond these categories in itself); the second is true because if the essence causes existence by its very essence, then the intellect necessitates that existence cannot be absent from it.

The author then reports two positions: some said the First Principle is neither existent nor non-existent (it is a pure haecceity beyond expression), while the majority held that it is existent and the worthiest of existence. After examining the argument, the author concludes that the dispute is purely verbal: both sides are correct from different angles, and the apparent contradiction is resolved by the distinction made in the premise.

Proof that God's Essence Differs by Itself
Sanusi proves that God's essence is distinct from all possible essences by itself, not by an attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God's essence must be inherently different from all created essences without needing a distinguishing attribute.

Core Idea
God's essence is distinct by itself, not by an attribute; otherwise infinite regress follows.

In this passage, Sanusi applies a key logical principle to the question of God's essence. He argues that the difference between God and creation is not due to an added attribute, but is intrinsic. The proof uses a careful division of possibilities to show that any alternative leads to impossibility.

Proving God's Eternity and Clarifying the Converse
The author proves the eternity of the Necessary Being and clarifies the converse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Necessary of Existence by itself must be eternal, and why not every eternal being is necessarily God.

Core Idea
Eternity is a necessary property of God, but not exclusive to Him.

In this passage, the author establishes a foundational distinction: while everything that is necessary of existence by itself is eternal, the reverse does not hold. This prevents a common confusion—thinking that any eternal entity must be divine. Let's examine the proof and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, carefully read the Arabic passage and its literal English translation. Let the author's own argument unfold before you analyze it.
Notice the logical connectors 'أما بيان الأول' and 'وأما بيان الثاني'.
2
Identify the two propositions
The passage makes two claims: (1) The Necessary Being must be eternal; (2) Not every eternal being is necessarily the Necessary Being. Find these in the text.
3
Analyze the proof for the first proposition
Follow the argument: if the Necessary Being could be non-existent at any time, it would be receptive to non-existence, contradicting its nature. Therefore it must exist eternally.
The key term is 'غير قابلة للعدم' (not receptive to non-existence).
4
Analyze the proof for the second proposition
The author gives an example: an effect of an eternal cause can itself be eternal through dependence, without being necessary by itself. This shows that eternity does not imply necessity.
Consider the example of a thing that is 'معلول' (effect) of a necessary cause.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof regarding God's eternity. It opens the sixth issue (المسألة السادسة) on explaining that God is eternal and pre-eternal. The author lays down two propositions: (1) Whatever is necessary of existence by itself must be eternal and pre-eternal. (2) The converse is not true. He then proves the first: A thing necessary of existence by itself has a reality not receptive to non-existence; if it were non-existent in the past or would cease to exist in the future, it would be receptive to non-existence, contradicting its nature. Therefore it must exist eternally without beginning or end. He proves the second by citing that it is intellectually possible for something to be an effect of a cause that is necessary of existence by itself, and the effect can persist as long as its cause persists. Such an effect would be eternal (since its cause is eternal) but it is not necessary of existence by itself. Thus eternal existence does not entail necessary existence; it is a property that belongs primarily to God but can also belong to created effects that depend on an eternal cause. The passage concludes by restating the two points.

The Mutakallimūn's Proof for God's Eternity
Presentation of the mutakallimūn's argument for God's eternity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical structure of the mutakallimūn's argument that God must be eternal, based on the temporality of the world and the impossibility of infinite regress or circularity.

Core Idea
If the Creator were temporal, we would face an impossible infinite chain: He must be eternal.

After establishing God's necessary existence, the mutakallimūn turn to proving His eternity. They derive this from the world's temporality using a regress argument. This passage presents that classic argument in its concise form, showing how the nature of causality demands an eternal first cause.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the context
The author first states that God's necessary existence implies eternity, but he wants to present different schools' arguments. This passage gives the mutakallimūn's method.
The author is compiling arguments from various groups.
2
Understand the first premise
The mutakallimūn begin by asserting that the physical world is temporal (ḥādith). They then use the principle that every temporal thing requires a temporizer (muḥdith).
This principle is foundational in kalām.
3
Apply the principle to the world's maker
They consider the maker of the world: if the maker were itself temporal, it would need another temporizer, leading to either circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul), both deemed impossible.
This is the key step that forces the conclusion.
4
Draw the conclusion
Since a temporal maker leads to absurdity, the maker must be eternal (qadīm). From eternity, they infer that its non-existence is impossible, so it is also perpetual (bāqī sarmadī).
Eternity implies necessary permanence.

Commentary
This passage is a presentation of the mutakallimūn's argument for God's eternity. The author first reminds that we have already proven God's necessary existence, so He is eternal. But then he wants to present the methods of different groups. Here he gives the mutakallimūn's method: They first prove that the physical world is temporal (ḥādith) through their proofs. Then they argue that every temporal thing has a temporizer (muḥdith). So the world has a maker. They then consider the maker: if that maker were itself temporal, it would also need a temporizer, leading to either circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul), both of which are false. Therefore, the maker cannot be temporal; it must be eternal. Once its eternity is established, they argue that what is eternal is impossible to cease, so it must also be perpetual (bāqī sarmadī). This is a classic kalam argument for the eternity of God based on the temporality of the world and the impossibility of infinite regress.

The Kalam Proof for God's Eternity
Razi presents the full kalam proof for God's eternity and everlastingness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the kalam argument uses the impossibility of circularity and infinite regress to prove that the creator of the world must be eternal.

Core Idea
The creator must be eternal, else an infinite regress of creators would occur.

The mutakallimun argue that since the world is originated, it needs an originator. That originator cannot itself be originated, as that would lead to either circularity or an infinite chain, both impossible. Therefore, the originator must be eternal—a foundational conclusion for all subsequent theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof's structure
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the logical flow: world originated → needs originator → originator cannot be originated (else regress) → therefore eternal.
The proof uses 'huduth' (temporal origination) as its starting point.
2
Identify the key concepts
Identify the terms 'دور' (circularity) and 'تسلسل' (infinite regress) and understand why they are considered invalid.
Circularity means A depends on B and B depends on A; regress is an endless chain.
3
Reflect on the deduction
Notice how the argument moves from the impossibility of an originated originator to the necessity of an eternal originator, and then from eternity to everlastingness.
Eternity (qidam) implies both beginninglessness and everlastingness.

Commentary
This passage presents the classic kalam argument for the eternity of God and then raises two objections that challenge its conclusiveness. The author first restates the argument: since we have proven that God is necessary of existence (wajib al-wujud li-dhatihi), it follows that He is eternal and everlasting. He then notes that he wants to record the various opinions. The mutakallimun's proof is summarized: they first prove the temporal origination (huduth) of the corporeal world, then argue that every originated thing needs an originator (muhdith). If that originator itself were originated, it would also need an originator, leading to either circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul), both impossible. Therefore, the originator must be eternal (qadim). Then, from its eternity, they deduce its permanence (baqa'). This is the kalam position. An objector then responds: this proof does not necessarily show that the creator must be eternal. The objector gives two aspects. First, assuming God is a voluntary agent (qadir mukhtar), He could create another being and delegate the creation of the world to it, so that the immediate creator is originated while the ultimate creator is eternal. In this scenario, no circularity or regress follows. Second, assuming God is a necessitator by essence (mujib bi-dhat), not a free agent, He could emanate a non-corporeal being (like an intellect or soul) that possesses knowledge and power, and that being creates the world. This being is eternal but not necessary in itself; it is an effect of the First. The objector then elaborates on the second case: if the First is a necessitator, then daily events cannot be directly attributed to Him; they must be mediated by an endless chain of preceding events. Similarly, one can posit an eternal substance that undergoes successive perceptions until it becomes the creator. Commentary
This passage presents the classic kalam argument for the eternity of God and then raises two objections that challenge its conclusiveness. The author first restates the argument: since we have proven that God is necessary of existence (wajib al-wujud li-dhatihi), it follows that He is eternal and everlasting. He then notes that he wants to record the various opinions. The mutakallimun's proof is summarized: they first prove the temporal origination (huduth) of the corporeal world, then argue that every originated thing needs an originator (muhdith). If that originator itself were originated, it would also need an originator, leading to either circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul), both impossible. Therefore, the originator must be eternal (qadim). Then, from its eternity, they deduce its permanence (baqa'). This is the kalam position. An objector then responds: this proof does not necessarily show that the creator must be eternal. The objector gives two aspects. First, assuming God is a voluntary agent (qadir mukhtar), He could create another being and delegate the creation of the world to it, so that the immediate creator is originated while the ultimate creator is eternal. In this scenario, no circularity or regress follows. Second, assuming God is a necessitator by essence (mujib bi-dhat), not a free agent, He could emanate a non-corporeal being (like an intellect or soul) that possesses knowledge and power, and that being creates the world. This being is eternal but not necessary in itself; it is an effect of the First. The objector then elaborates on the second case: if the First is a necessitator, then daily events cannot be directly attributed to Him; they must be mediated by an endless chain of preceding events. Similarly, one can posit an eternal substance that undergoes successive perceptions until it becomes the creator. Thus, even on the necessitarian view, the possibility remains that the immediate creator is not the First. The passage ends by noting that these investigations are presented.

This passage presents the classic kalam argument for the eternity of God and then raises two objections that challenge its conclusiveness. The author first restates the argument: since we have proven that God is necessary of existence (wajib al-wujud li-dhatihi), it follows that He is eternal and everlasting. He then notes that he wants to record the various opinions. The mutakallimun's proof is summarized: they first prove the temporal origination (huduth) of the corporeal world, then argue that every originated thing needs an originator (muhdith). If that originator itself were originated, it would also need an originator, leading to either circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul), both impossible. Therefore, the originator must be eternal (qadim). Then, from its eternity, they deduce its permanence (baqa'). This is the kalam position. An objector then responds: this proof does not necessarily show that the creator must be eternal. The objector gives two aspects. First, assuming God is a voluntary agent (qadir mukhtar), He could create another being and delegate the creation of the world to it, so that the immediate creator is originated while the ultimate creator is eternal. In this scenario, no circularity or regress follows. Second, assuming God is a necessitator by essence (mujib bi-dhat), not a free agent, He could emanate a non-corporeal being (like an intellect or soul) that possesses knowledge and power, and that being creates the world. This being is eternal but not necessary in itself; it is an effect of the First. The objector then elaborates on the second case: if the First is a necessitator, then daily events cannot be directly attributed to Him; they must be mediated by an endless chain of preceding events. Similarly, one can posit an eternal substance that undergoes successive perceptions until it becomes the creator. Thus, even on the necessitarian view, the possibility remains that the immediate creator is not the First. The passage ends by noting that these investigations are presented.

Essential necessity cannot be subject to change; if it could, it would not be essential.

The Inapplicability of 'Was' and 'Will Be' to God
Argues that applying past and future tenses to God implies change, which is impossible for the Necessary Being.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why attributing temporal tenses such as 'was' and 'will be' to God is problematic because they imply change, whereas God is necessarily unchanging.

Core Idea
Was and will be imply change; God is necessarily unchanging.

This passage presents a linguistic and metaphysical argument against using temporal language for God. The author analyzes the implications of the words 'was' (kāna) and 'will be' (yakūnu), showing that they inherently indicate change through successive states. Since God's essence is necessary and unchanging, such language cannot be truthfully applied to Him. This argument highlights the limitations of human language in describing the divine.

Commentary
This passage is the third investigation, a linguistic and metaphysical argument about the inapplicability of temporal language to God. The author notes that the words 'was' (kāna) and 'will be' (yakūnu) imply change: 'was' indicates something that existed but is now past, while 'will be' indicates something not yet realized. Anything that can be described by both 'was' and 'will be' undergoes change and renewal. Since God's essence is necessary in duration (wājib al-dawām) and impossible to change (mumtani‘ al-taghayyur), it cannot be that He 'was' in azal and 'will be' in abad, because that would imply temporal transition. Moreover, the expression 'was now' also implies change. Since these three expressions (was, will be, was now) are false of God, then according to the argument, whatever they are false of is impossible to exist. But God exists, so this seems contradictory. The author may be using this as a reductio to show the inadequacy of language. He then notes that these matters are treated fully in his book on place and time. The passage concludes the first book of the divine science with a colophon giving the completion date.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think saying 'God was eternal' is harmless and accurately describes God.
Correction: This argument shows that 'was' implies a past state that has ended, which would entail change in God. True eternity for God is not captured by temporal language.

Key Takeaway
