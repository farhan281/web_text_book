Learning Objective

Core Idea
Intensifying necessity cannot intensify absence; therefore necessity is real and positive.

This passage presents the third proof in al-Isfahani's argument for the positivity of wujub. It uses a concise conditional argument: if wujub were non-existence, then its intensification would lead to an absurdity. Let's examine the text and unpack its logical structure.

Since the absurdity follows from assuming wujub is non-existence, wujub must be positive existence.
The proof does not define wujub further but establishes its positive nature.

The argument uses a reductio ad infinitum: if wujub is existential, then it must itself be described by necessity, and that description must be either necessary or possible. Existence as a Single Concept: The Self-Defeating Denial Argument
Tenth proof: the denial of shared existence is self-refuting.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Denying that existence is shared self-destructs: if it were true, it would imply sharing in another sense.

The author presents a clever logical argument: if someone claims that existence is not shared among all existents, that claim itself uses a concept of existence that must be one. If existence had multiple meanings, the denial could only apply to one meaning, not all. Thus the denial undermines itself. Let's examine this reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is a complete proof—the second of Razi's arguments—that non-existence (al-ʿadam) cannot apply to the eternal being (al-qadīm). In all scenarios, the eternal either is itself necessary of existence or is an effect of a necessary cause; in both cases, its non-existence is impossible. The conclusion is: everything proven to be eternal cannot undergo non-existence.

The Second Proof: Necessitating Cause and Conclusion
Razi completes the proof by establishing a necessitating cause and concluding the impossibility of non-existence for the eternal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that the determiner of an eternal being must be a necessitating cause that is itself necessary, and why this leads to the impossibility of non-existence for the eternal.

Core Idea
An eternal effect requires an eternal cause, and its non-existence becomes impossible.

Having ruled out a free agent, Razi shows that the determiner must be a necessitating cause. He then argues that this cause cannot be possible, leading to an infinite regress, so it must be necessary by itself. From this, he concludes that every eternal being is either necessary by itself or caused by a necessary being, and in both cases non-existence is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the logical progression from the previous lesson.
2
Understand the necessitating cause argument
Razi establishes that the determiner must be a necessitating cause. Then he examines whether that cause itself is possible or necessary. If possible, the same division applies, leading to an infinite regress or a necessary cause. The regress is avoided by stopping at a necessary cause.
This is a classic regress argument.
3
Follow the condition argument
Razi considers whether the effect (the eternal) depends on a condition. If the condition is possible, regress recurs. If necessary or absent, the effect is permanent. This reinforces that the effect cannot undergo non-existence.
This part may seem subtle; note that it strengthens the conclusion.
4
Grasp the final conclusion
Razi concludes that every eternal is either necessary by itself or caused by a necessary being. In both cases, non-existence is impossible. Therefore, if something is proven eternal, it cannot cease to exist.
This is the core of the proof.

Commentary
This passage contains three rational arguments demonstrating that the past (or time before the present) must be finite, not infinite. The author presents these proofs as part of a larger discussion on azal (eternity without beginning) and abad (eternity without end).

The first argument (though incomplete here) begins by noting that if the past were infinite, then the arrival of today would depend on the completion of an infinite duration, which is impossible. Consequently, the occurrence of today would be impossible; but since it has occurred, the past must be finite.

The second argument uses the relationship between azal and abad as contradictory opposites. If the past were infinite, there would be no distinction between azal and abad because any supposed end of azal would necessarily have something prior to it, thus falling within abad, making them indistinguishable. This contradiction proves that azal must have an end, i.e., the past is finite.

The third argument examines the implications of applying temporal language (was, is, will be) to God. If we say God was in azal and will be in abad, then these terms imply change and passing away. But God's essence is immutable and eternal, so such language cannot apply. Therefore, the concepts of azal and abad as infinite durations are incoherent when applied to the eternal God, indirectly proving that the past as we experience it must be finite.

Each argument concludes with 'this is a contradiction' (هذا خلف), indicating that the assumption of an infinite past leads to an absurdity, thus establishing its finitude.

Second Proof: The Contradiction Between Azal and Abad
al-Razi's second proof using the contradictory pair azal and abad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the contradictory relationship between azal (beginningless eternity) and abad (endless eternity) forces the conclusion that the past is finite.

Core Idea
If the past were infinite, azal and abad would be indistinguishable — a contradiction of their contradictory nature.

The second proof examines the relationship between two concepts: azal (eternity without beginning) and abad (eternity without end). If the past were infinite, these would not be distinct, but they are mutually exclusive. Al-Razi shows that an infinite past would collapse their distinction, thereby proving its finitude.

Guided Path
1
Understand the terms
Azal means 'no beginning', abad means 'no end'. They are conceptually opposite. Grasp their meanings before reading.
2
Follow the reductio
Assume an infinite past. Try to locate the boundary between azal and abad. The argument shows any proposed boundary fails.
Think of time as a line: if azal is infinite, there is no first point of abad.
3
Recognize the contradiction
The failure to distinguish azal from abad contradicts their assumed contradictory nature, so the assumption of infinite past is false.

Third Proof: Temporal Language and God's Immutability
al-Razi's third proof from inapplicability of temporal predicates to God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why temporal expressions like 'was' and 'will be' cannot apply to God, and how this implies the finitude of the past.

Core Idea
If God were subject to 'was' and 'will be', He would be changeable, but He is immutable; therefore, temporal extension is finite.

The third proof examines the implications of using temporal language for God. Words like 'was' and 'will be' imply change and passing away, which are incompatible with God's eternal and immutable nature. Al-Razi argues that because these terms cannot apply to God, the very concepts of azal and abad as infinite durations are problematic, showing that the past must be finite.

Guided Path
1
Examine the common phrase
Consider the statement 'God was in azal and will be in abad'. Note the use of 'was' and 'will be'.
2
Analyze the implications of 'was' and 'will be'
'Was' implies past existence that has ceased; 'will be' implies future existence not yet attained. Both imply change.
3
Apply to God's nature
God is necessarily perpetual and immutable. Therefore, such temporal terms cannot apply to Him. This contradicts the assumption of an infinite temporal framework.
The conclusion is that the past (and future) as we experience them must be finite.
