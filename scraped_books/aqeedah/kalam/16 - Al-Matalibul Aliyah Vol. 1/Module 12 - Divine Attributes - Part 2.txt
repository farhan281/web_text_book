The Third Proof: Similarity and Difference Are Intrinsic
Sanusi gives the third proof that similarity and difference are intrinsic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's third proof that similarity and difference between things arise from their essences themselves, not from extra attributes.

Core Idea
Similarity and difference are not added properties; they are in the very nature of things.

Sanusi continues his defense of essential attributes with a sharp logical analysis. He argues that when we say two things are similar in some respects and different in others, that is figurative language. In reality, every object is composed of parts, and similarity occurs between corresponding parts by their very essence, not by an extra property. This leads to the conclusion that similarity and difference are intrinsic.

2
Identify the composition argument

3
Follow the conclusion

The third proof for the validity of this claim: that we say: If we say: 'This thing resembles that thing in some respects and differs from it in all other respects,' this is figurative speech. Commentary
This passage presents the third proof, which analyzes statements about similarity and difference. The author argues that saying two things are similar in some respects and different in others is figurative; in reality, similarity and difference cannot come from the same aspect, as that would be contradictory. Instead, each thing is composed of parts: one part from the first is similar to a part from the second by their very realities, and another part from the first is different from the corresponding part from the second by their very realities. Hence, similarity and difference are not caused by extra attributes but by the things themselves. The conclusion is that these relations are intrinsic.

Commentary
This passage presents an opponent's objection to the author's earlier conclusion that God's essence differs from all other essences by itself, not by an added attribute. The opponent gives three arguments. The first argument: All essences share the common concept of being 'dhāt' (independent entity). Since they are equal in that concept, any distinction must come from something else, namely an attribute. The second argument: God's essence is either just 'an essence' (a mere universal) or a specific essence. If just an essence, then every essence would be God, which is false; if specific, then the specification is an additional concept, i.e., an attribute. He concedes that essences share the same concept of being 'dhāt', but questions whether that concept must be positive. He suggests it might be a negative concept: 'something not subordinate to another'. If so, then equality in a negative concept does not preclude differentiation by essence itself, without needing attributes. The answer is incomplete, but it opens a key move: undermining the opponent's assumption that the shared concept is a real positive commonality.

Core Idea
Equality in concept demands a separate attribute for distinction.

The opponent begins by asserting that all essences share the same concept of being essences. Since they are equal in that concept, any difference must come from an external attribute. This lesson presents that argument in detail, including its two key stations.

Refuting the Proof: Negative Concepts and Logical Inconsistency
Response to the opponent's first proof

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The shared concept of essence may be negative, and the opponent's logic leads to absurdity.

Having presented the opponent's argument, the author now offers a two-part refutation. First, the common concept might be negative, implying nothing about real distinction. Second, the same logic would force attributes to be equal, preventing any difference at all—contradicting observable reality.

Understand the negative concept refutation

3
Examine the tu quoque refutation
The author shows that the opponent's own proof would also conclude that attributes are equal in concept, leading to the absurd result that no difference exists anywhere, which contradicts reality.
This demonstrates the proof is invalid.
