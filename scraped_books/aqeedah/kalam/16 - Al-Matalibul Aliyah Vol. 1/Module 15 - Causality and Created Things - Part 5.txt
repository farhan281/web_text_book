Answer to the Third Objection on Infinite Regress
The author refutes the claim that the sum of regress is caused by its parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the sum of an infinite regress cannot be explained by each part causing the next, and how the author refutes that objection.

Core Idea
Neither the individuals nor their relations can be the cause of the sum without circularity or absurdity.

The author now tackles a third objection to the infinite regress argument. The objector claims that the entire chain could be explained if each element is caused by the next. The author dismantles this by distinguishing two possible causes—the individuals themselves or their relations of reliance—and showing both lead to impossibility. Let's follow the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation once to get the overall flow of the argument.
2
Identify the objector's claim
The objector asks: why not say the sum exists because each individual relies on another, ad infinitum? This is the core of the objection.
3
Distinguish the two concepts
The author points out that the objector's statement implies two distinct concepts: the sum of the individuals' essences, and the reliance-relations that are accidental to them.
These two concepts are not identical.
4
Examine why the individuals cannot be the cause
If the cause is the individuals themselves, then the sum is caused by its own parts, which is circular—the thing causing itself.
That is logically impossible.
5
Examine why the relations cannot be the cause
If the cause is the reliance-relations, then accidents (the relations) are prior to their substances (the individuals), which is also impossible.
The substance is prior in rank to the accident.

The Fifth Proof: Comparing Infinite Sets
Presents the fifth proof: comparing two infinite sets leads to contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how comparing two allegedly infinite sets of causes leads to a contradiction, demonstrating that an infinite regress is impossible.

Core Idea
Comparing two infinite chains shows they cannot both be infinite.

Islamic theologians formulated multiple proofs against infinite regress of causes. This fifth proof uses a clever comparison: if we take two different contingent beings, each with its own infinite chain of causes, then the combined set would be larger than either alone — but an infinite cannot be larger than another infinite. This contradiction shows the assumption of an infinite regress is false.

Guided Path
1
Understand the setup
Read the passage and note that the proof begins by assuming a contingent being with its infinite chain of causes.
The proof uses two such beings.
2
Follow the comparison
The argument then compares the number of causes in one chain versus the combined causes of two chains.
The combined chain should be larger.
3
Identify the contradiction
If both chains are infinite, they cannot differ in magnitude, but the combined chain is definitely larger. This is a contradiction (khalaf).
The conclusion is that the original chain must be finite.
4
Reflect on the logical structure
This proof is a reductio ad absurdum: assume infinite regress, derive a contradiction, therefore the assumption is false.

So the number of the one totality is finite, but we have assumed it to be infinite. This is a contradiction.

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth proof for the impossibility of an infinite regress of causes. The argument works by comparing two sets of an infinite chain. If you take one contingent existent along with its infinite chain of causes, that constitutes one infinite set. Then take another contingent existent with its own infinite chain; that is a second infinite set. Since both sets are infinite, each is equal in magnitude, but because the second set includes the first plus additional members, it is larger. But if both are infinite, they cannot differ in size. Therefore the assumption that they are both infinite is false. The conclusion: the chain must be finite. The proof ends with 'this is a contradiction' (khalaf).

The Middle Term Argument Against Infinite Regress
Sanusi presents the sixth proof against infinite regress using the logical properties of 'middle' and 'endpoint'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the classification of causes and effects into last effect, first cause, and intermediates leads to the logical necessity of an endpoint in any causal chain, proving the existence of a Necessary Being.

Core Idea
An infinite causal chain has no endpoint, so every link is a middle—but a middle must depend on an endpoint, which is the Necessary Being.

This lesson presents the sixth proof from Al-Matalib al-Aliyah, which uses the logical properties of 'middle' and 'endpoint' to refute infinite regress. The argument begins by defining three types of entities in a causal chain, then shows that an infinite chain would be self-contradictory, ultimately affirming the existence of a Necessary Existent.

Guided Path
1
Understand the three categories
The proof first distinguishes three roles: the last effect (only an effect, not a cause), the first cause (only a cause, not an effect), and intermediates (both cause and effect). Notice the defining property of each.
These categories are crucial for the logical structure of the argument.
2
Consider the supposition of an infinite chain
Imagine the causal chain extends infinitely without beginning or end. Under this supposition, every link becomes an intermediate—none has the property of being a true endpoint.
3
Analyze the dependence of the middle
An intermediate depends on something above it. If the whole chain is just a collection of intermediates, it must either depend on something outside the chain (an endpoint) or be self-sufficient. The proof examines both possibilities.
If self-sufficient, each link would be an endpoint, contradicting its status as a middle.
4
Conclude the necessity of an endpoint
The only consistent outcome is that the chain depends on an endpoint—a cause that is not itself an effect. This endpoint is the Necessary Existent. Thus, denying an endpoint leads to a contradiction, proving that an endpoint must exist.
This is the key conclusion: the negation of the endpoint forces its affirmation.

The sixth proof: The last effect has a cause, and its cause has a cause. The last effect has the property that it is an effect and not a cause. The first cause, if it existed, would have the property that it is a cause and not an effect. As for the intermediate things, they share one attribute, namely that each one of them is a cause for what is below it and an effect for what is above it. If this is established, we say: If we suppose the going of causes and effects to infinity, then everything would be in the status of the middle, and none of them would acquire the property of the endpoint at all. Then we say: If this middle depends on something that does not have the property of the middle, then that is what is sought. And if this cause is independent of dependence on a third thing, then it is an endpoint, so it is necessary of existence in itself. Thus it is established that the negation of the endpoint necessitates its affirmation, so this negation must be false, and the affirmation of an endpoint for contingent things must be necessary, and that is what is sought.

This passage presents the sixth proof against infinite regress, based on the properties of 'middle' (wasat) and 'endpoint' (taraf). Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the philosophers' answer to a problem about the eternal cause and the occurrence of contingent events. It presents three arguments (wujuh) showing the weakness of the philosophers' response. The first argument (al-wajh al-awwal) begins by dividing the scenario into two cases: either the eternal cause was fully sufficient in eternity or not. If it was fully sufficient, then the later occurrence of the contingent event would be a preponderance (tarjih) of a possible without any preponderator (murajjih), which is impossible. If it was not fully sufficient, then the collection of conditions that makes it sufficient becomes a new contingent event. This new event must have a cause, leading to one of four unacceptable consequences: no cause at all, a prior cause (infinite regress), circularity (dor), or another contingent event (infinite regress). All these undermine the proof. The second argument considers the eternal cause's state at two times: when a prior contingent event existed and after it passed. If the cause was already effectual for the later event, then its becoming effectual after the prior event's passing is a new contingent event, requiring its own effectual—leading to circularity or regress. If it was not yet effectual, the same problem arises. The third argument focuses on the prior event's passing: if it perishes by itself, it would be impossible; if by another, that other is the later event, which then depends on the prior event's passing, creating circularity. All three arguments conclude that the philosophers' answer is weak.

Razi presents an objection that a body might be necessary due to its parts and gives the philosophers' refutation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This passage from Razi's al-Matalib al-Aliyah presents a crucial objection: even if a body is contingent in itself, could it become necessary because its parts are necessary? Guided Path
1
Understand the objection

Matter is receptive to form; if it were also active in producing form, the same thing would be both receptive and active—a contradiction. So matter cannot cause form. Similarly, form cannot cause matter because form inheres in matter and depends on it; what depends on something cannot be its cause. Since neither causes the other, and they are concomitant in existence, they must both be effects of a single, separate cause. If they had separate causes, each would be independent of the other, contradicting their necessary concomitance. Therefore matter and form are co-effects of one cause, and anything that is an effect of another is possible in itself. Hence body is possible in itself, both in its complete essence and in each part of its essence. The passage ends by stating that this completes the proof.

The Core Objection and Its Refutation
Main objection and two-part refutation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objector's alternative proposal that matter and form are self-concomitant like correlatives, and the philosopher's two-part refutation based on the principle of the One and the impossibility of form being a partner in causality.

Core Idea
Self-concomitance of matter and form is refuted by the principle of the One and the impossibility of form being a partner in causality.

After the preliminary objections are dismissed, the objector presents his main challenge: maybe matter and form are concomitant by themselves, like correlatives, without needing a separate cause. The philosopher responds with two powerful arguments: first, the principle that the One only produces one thing at a time, and second, that the objector's own proof against form causing matter also rules out form being part of the cause. If positive, then we ask why this essence was given this particular positive specification and not the opposite, thus leading back to the same problem of arbitrary assignment. The author concludes that all counterexamples (nuqūḍ) to this argument are refuted.
