The Analogy of Mountains and Minerals for the Hierarchy of Souls
Razi uses an extended analogy to explain the hierarchy of human souls.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Fakhr al-Din al-Razi uses the analogy of mountains and minerals to illustrate the varying degrees of human souls' receptivity to divine knowledge and love.

Core Idea
Souls differ in essence; most are spiritually barren like mineral-less mountains.

Razi begins by affirming that human souls differ in their very essence. To make this abstract truth tangible, he offers a detailed analogy comparing souls to mountains and minerals. This lesson walks through that analogy step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation once to get an overall sense of the analogy Razi is drawing.
Pay attention to the structure: Razi first makes a claim, then gives the analogy.
2
Identify the two main categories of mountains
Razi first divides mountains into those with and without minerals. Note how he uses induction to claim that most mountains are barren.
This corresponds to two basic types of souls.
3
Trace the gradations within mineral-bearing mountains
Notice the further subdivision into base and noble minerals, and then within noble minerals, the variation in effort required to extract them.
Razi emphasizes rarity of noble minerals and the range of labor needed.
4
Apply the analogy to human souls
Razi maps each mountain type to a soul type. Observe the correspondence between spiritual discipline (riyada) and mining labor.
The goal is to see how the analogy clarifies the hierarchy of souls.

When you know these ranks, it becomes apparent to you that such a mountain containing such a cave is very rare, and reaching it nor winning it occurs except in very distant cycles. And when you know this, we say: Let the human spirits be running the course of the mountains and hills, and let the lights of the knowledge of God and His love be running the course of pure refined gold. And just as most of the mountains and hills of the world are devoid of minerals, likewise the spirits of most people are devoid of inclination toward the world of spiritualities. Then, indeed, this category, even if it were to exert in bodily discipline, its benefit from it would be little, just as the mountain devoid of minerals, if a person exhausts himself in working it, he finds nothing in it at all. As for the second category—which is the spirits in which these minerals have occurred—just as the mountains containing the minerals of gold and silver differ, some of them requiring much work to obtain benefit of little profit, so too this category of spirits: among them there is that which requires severe, abundant discipline to obtain the small amount of unveilings (al-mukāshafāt), and among them there is that which is not so, but rather little work leads it to winning great bounties.

Commentary
This passage is an extended analogy by Fakhr al-Din al-Razi to explain the varying natures of human souls. The author first asserts as established that rational human souls differ in essence and substance: some are noble and divine, others dark and lowly. He then announces that he will provide an example for thorough clarification. The analogy is between mountains and minerals: just as mountains vary in containing minerals—some have none, some have base minerals, some have noble minerals, and among those with noble minerals there is further variation in ease of extraction—so too do human souls vary in their receptivity to divine knowledge and love. He emphasizes that most mountains are devoid of minerals, just as most souls are devoid of spiritual inclination. For those that do have minerals, the amount of labor required to extract them corresponds to the degree of spiritual discipline needed to attain unveilings. The author draws a clear parallel: mountains with abundant base minerals are common, while those with precious minerals are rare and often require great effort; similarly, souls with high spiritual potential are rare and may require severe discipline, while some rare souls attain great bounty with little effort. The conclusion reinforces the hierarchy: the majority of souls gain little from spiritual practice, while a few are highly receptive. The passage is a continuous explanatory argument, not a definition or proof in the strict sense, but rather a metaphysical illustration.

The Strong Proof of Body Similarity in Complete Essence
The author gives a strong proof that bodies are similar in complete essence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical structure of the strong proof that all bodies are similar in their complete essence.

Core Idea
If bodies are self-subsistent equal essences, they must share all attributes.

In this passage, the author presents a decisive argument for the similarity of bodies in their complete essence. He first dismisses a common objection, then constructs a conditional proof that leads to the conclusion that bodies are essentially identical. Let's examine this reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage carefully
Read the Arabic text once without analyzing. Then read the translation to grasp the overall argument. Notice the author's use of conditional statements ('if false', 'if true').
The passage begins with 'أن يكون حقا' which sets up a conditional.
2
Identify the two parts of the argument
First, the author argues that the opposing view (that bodyness and attributes are separate) is false because we know by necessity that fire and earth differ. Second, he presents a positive proof: if bodies were equal essences, they must share all attributes.
The transition is marked by 'وأما أن بتقدير أن يكون حقا'.
3
Understand the key term 'تماثل في تمام الماهية'
This phrase means 'similarity in complete essence.' The author aims to prove that bodies are identical in their core essence, despite apparent differences in accidents.
‘تماثل’ implies equality in essence, not numerical identity.
4
Reflect on the strength of the proof
Ask yourself: Why does the author call this a 'strong and clear proof'? Consider how the conditional logic establishes the conclusion without relying on empirical observation.
The proof is rational (a priori) and generalizes from any example.

First Objection Against Body Similarity and Its Refutation
The opponents' first objection that determinacy would be additional to essence, leading to regress, and its refutation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first objection against the similarity of bodies and its refutation, including the argument that determinacy would lead to regress and why that does not apply if determinacy is the specific essence.

Core Idea
The first objection fails because determinacy is not additional—it is the specific reality of each body.

The first objection against body similarity is a sophisticated argument using a logical conditional and the threat of infinite regress. It challenges the idea that bodies share complete essence by alleging that their distinct determinacy would then be extra, leading to absurd consequences. The refutation cuts the objection at its root by redefining determinacy as the very specific reality of each body, thus avoiding regress. Let's examine this foundational theological debate.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the structure: first the objection, then the refutation.
2
Understand the objection's conditional
Identify the conditional premise: if bodies were similar in complete essence, then determinacy would be additional. Understand why that is claimed.
The conditional is based on the observation that bodies share bodyness but differ in determinacy.
3
Follow the regress argument
Trace the argument that if determinacy is additional, then by the same logic, each determinacy would need another determinacy, leading to infinite regress, which is impossible.
This demonstrates the absurdity (impossibility) of the consequent, so by modus tollens the antecedent is denied.
4
See how the refutation resolves it
The refutation denies that determinacy is additional; instead, it is the specific reality of each body. Hence, no extra determinacy is needed, and no regress occurs.
This preserves the possibility of body similarity if one holds that determinacy is part of the essence.

Second Objection Against Body Similarity
The opponents' second objection that determinacy cannot be due to essence, cause, or agent, leading to impossibility of similarity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second objection against body similarity and why it forces the conclusion that bodies cannot be equal in complete essence.

Core Idea
The determinacy of each body cannot be explained by essence or agent, only by the recipient, making bodies dependent attributes.

The opponents of body similarity present a logical dilemma: if bodies share the same essence but have different determinations, how can those determinations be explained? This passage systematically eliminates each possible cause, leading to a conclusion that undermines the very idea of body equality.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Let the argument's flow become clear.
Notice the logical structure: possibilities are listed and then eliminated one by one.
2
Identify the four possibilities for specification
The passage lists four possibilities for how each body gets its specific determinacy: no cause, essence, distinct agent, or recipient. Find each in the text.
3
Follow the reasoning for rejecting the first three
First, no cause is impossible because the contingent needs a preponderator. Second, essence cannot be the cause because bodyness is shared and determinacy is not. Third, the distinct agent cannot cause specificity because all bodies are equal in essence.
Understand the logic behind each rejection.
4
Understand the fourth possibility and its consequence
The only remaining cause is the recipient (qābil). But if bodies are equal in essence, then the recipient must have prior states, making bodies attributes in recipients, not self-subsistent. This is impossible.
This is the key conclusion: bodies would be accidents, not substances.
5
Grasp the final conclusion
Since bodies cannot be attributes in recipients, the assumption that they are equal in complete essence is false. Thus bodies are not similar in that sense.
This refutes the claim of body similarity from this angle.
