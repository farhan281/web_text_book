Commentary
This passage contains a proof establishing that contingent essences require a priority (al-awlawiyya) for existence over non-existence, and that such a priority implies independence from an external cause. The argument begins by dividing essences into three categories: (1) those that do not necessitate either existence or non-existence, (2) those that necessitate one of them without specification, and (3) those that necessitate one of them with specification. The contradiction shows that priority necessitates self-sufficiency. The passage ends by stating that this discourse establishes the intelligibility of the notion of priority and its consequence of independence from a cause.

Commentary
This passage presents and refutes an objection against the claim that contingent beings always need an effecting cause (al-mu'aththir). The objector argues: celestial and terrestrial bodies, even if proven contingent, might be eternal and persistent; if so, they cannot be judged to need a cause, because the persistent thing in its persistence is (according to the text) in need of a cause—but the objector is actually trying to show that if they are persistent, they do not need a cause, given a variant reading. The author then explains that if one establishes temporal origination (huduth), the argument reduces to the standard kalam proof for the existence of God. To answer the objection, one must prove that the persistent thing (al-baqi) in its state of persistence does need a cause. Four proofs are given: (1) If it needed a cause, it would mean bringing into existence what already exists, which is impossible. (2) Analogies: a building does not need a builder after completion; henna colour remains after the henna is gone; a stone's motion continues after the thrower stops. (3) Non-existence, if it persisted, would also need a cause, which is absurd. (4) Common sense: things remain in their state until a remover appears; we trust that things persist when we leave them, and we act on that assumption. This shows that the default is persistence, not need for a continuous cause.

The first two objections attack the very concept of simultaneity, arguing that it must be either essential (by essence) or temporal (by time). The first shows essential simultaneity is impossible for cause and effect, while the second argues temporal simultaneity leads to absurdities like creating what already exists.

Those who opposed the existence of this simultaneity argued by several matters: First: If this simultaneity were considered, it would either be an expression of essential simultaneity or temporal simultaneity. The first is invalid by the consensus of rational people that the cause is not essentially simultaneous with the effect. The second is invalid because temporal simultaneity only applies to what is temporal, and everything temporal is moving and changing, and what is not so, temporal simultaneity is impossible with respect to it. Second: If the cause were to affect the effect while the effect is present, that would be bringing the existent into existence, which is impossible. So it must be said that the cause precedes the effect so that it can be understood as effective in transferring that effect from non-existence to existence. Third: When we shoot an arrow, each one of the preceding draughts [air currents] continues to be a cause for a subsequent repulsion, occurring after it in order and succession; otherwise, the arrow would have to fall when it leaves the hand. Fourth: Thought necessitates knowledge of the desired conclusion, while thought must precede knowledge of the result, because thought about a thing at the time of obtaining knowledge of it is impossible. Fifth: A heavy descending body, each part of its motion is a cause for the occurrence of the part that follows it, either as an effective cause as with some Mu'tazila, or as a preparatory cause as with the philosophers. Sixth: We know by necessity that as long as the effective agent does not exist in its entirety, it is impossible for the effect to emanate from it, and the complete existence of the effective agent precedes the existence of the effect; thus the complete effective agent must precede the effect. Seventh: It is said, 'I moved my hand, then the key moved,' or 'then the key moved,' and the discernment of reason witnesses the occurrence of this order, and that indicates that the cause must precede the effect.

Commentary
This passage enumerates seven objections raised by those who deny that the cause must be simultaneous with its effect. Each objection provides a counterexample or a logical argument: (1) Simultaneity can only be by essence or by time, but essential simultaneity is impossible for cause and effect, and temporal simultaneity only applies to changing things, not to eternal causes. (2) If the cause acts when the effect is already present, it would be creating what already exists, which is absurd; therefore, the cause must precede. (3) In shooting an arrow, successive air currents cause the arrow's motion in sequence, showing cause precedes effect. (4) Thought must precede knowledge, not be simultaneous with it. (5) A falling body's motion occurs in successive parts, each caused by the previous. (6) The complete cause must exist entirely before its effect. (7) Empirical examples like moving a hand and then a key show temporal order. These objections collectively argue that causality requires temporal precedence, not simultaneity.
