Refutation of the Second Objection: Three Relations Refuted
Refutation of the second objection by showing three possibilities are impossible

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the claim that existence must relate to quiddity in one of three ways is refuted, and why none of these relations can hold for the Necessary Being.

Core Idea
None of the three possible relations between existence and quiddity can apply to the Necessary Being.

The second objection against the Necessary Being argues that its existence must relate to its quiddity in one of three ways: as the whole, a part, or an external attribute. This lesson walks through the refutation of each possibility, showing that all lead to contradictions or infinite regress. By examining these refutations, you will see why the objection fails.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three divisions
Read the passage and note the three possible relations: existence as the whole quiddity, a part of quiddity, or an external concept.
This sets the structure of the refutation.
2
Refutation of the first division
Study the three reasons why existence cannot be the entire quiddity: conceptual difference, beneficial speech, and logical modality.
Focus on the third reason from logicians.
3
Refutation of the second division
Understand why existence as a part leads to composition, and composition implies contingency, contradicting necessity.
Remember: every composite is contingent.
4
Refutation of the third division
See why existence as an external attribute leads to dependence and infinite regress.
This is the most subtle argument.
5
Conclude the refutation
All three divisions are impossible, so the objection fails.

الشبهة الثانية : قالوا : لو فرضنا موجودا واجب الموجود لذاته ، لكان ذلك الوجود إما أن يكون تمام ماهيته أو جزء ماهيته أو مفهوما خارجا عن ماهيته ، والأول محال لوجوه : الأول : إن المفهوم من الوجوب الذاتي معلوم ، والحقيقة المخصوصة التي لتلك الذوات غير معلومة ، والمعلوم مغاير لغير المعلوم . الثاني : إنا إذا قلنا : واجب الوجود ، لم يفد شيئاً ، وإذا قلنا : إن الذات الفلانية واجبة الوجود ، كان الكلام مفيدا . الثالث : إن أهل المنطق قالوا : وجوب الوجود جهات ، ومعناه : أنه لا بد في القضية من موضوع ومن محمول ومن رابطة . ومعناه : اتصاف ذلك الموضوع بذلك المحمول ، ثم إن الوجوب والامتناع كيفيات عارضة لهذه الرابطة ، وإذا كان الأمر كذلك ، امتنع أن يقال : بأن الوجوب تمام تلك الماهية . وأما القسم الثاني : وهو أن يقال : الوجوب الذاتي جزء من أجزاء تلك الماهية ، فهذا أيضاً باطل، لأن على هذا التقدير يكون واجب الوجود مركبا ، وكل مركب ممكن ، ينتج أن واجب الوجود لذاته ممكن الوجود لذاته ، وهو محال . وأما القسم الثالث : وهو أن يقال : وجوب الوجود صفة خارجة عن الذات فنقول : هذا أيضاً باطل لأن كل ما كان صفة خارجة عن الذات لاحقة لها فهو مفتقر إلى تلك الماهية، وكل ما كان مفتقراً إلى غيره فهو ممكن لذاته ، فالوجوب بالذات ممكن بالذات، وكل ما كان ممكنا بذاته ، فإنه لا يجب إلا بوجوب علته ، فقبل هذا الوجوب وجوب آخر ، وذلك يوجب الذهاب إلى ما لا نهاية له. فثبت أن هذه الأقسام الثلاثة باطلة
The second objection: They said: If we suppose an existent necessary of existence in itself, then that existence is either the entirety of its quiddity, or a part of its quiddity, or a concept external to its quiddity. The first is impossible for reasons: First: The concept of self-necessity is known, while the specific reality of those essences is unknown, and the known is other than the unknown. Second: When we say 'necessary of existence', it conveys no benefit, but when we say 'that particular essence is necessary of existence', the speech is beneficial. Third: The logicians said: Necessity of existence has modes, and its meaning is that in a proposition there must be a subject, a predicate, and a copula; its meaning is the attribution of that subject with that predicate; then necessity and impossibility are qualities accidental to this copula. If that is the case, it is impossible to say that necessity is the entirety of that quiddity. As for the second division: that it is said that self-necessity is a part of the parts of that quiddity, this is also false, because on this assumption the necessary of existence would be composite, and every composite is contingent, resulting in the necessary of existence in itself being contingent in existence in itself, which is impossible. As for the third division: that it is said that necessity of existence is an attribute external to the essence, we say: this is also false, because everything that is an attribute external to the essence, attaching to it, is in need of that quiddity, and everything that is in need of another is contingent in itself; thus necessity-in-itself is contingent-in-itself. And everything that is contingent in itself does not exist except by the necessity of its cause; so before this necessity there is another necessity, and that necessitates going on to infinity. So it is established that these three divisions are false.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the second objection (الشبهة الثانية) against the existence of a Necessary Being. The objector claims that if a Necessary Being existed, its existence would have to relate to its quiddity in one of three ways: either existence is the whole of the quiddity, a part of it, or something entirely external. The author systematically disproves all three. 

First possibility (existence as the whole): The concept of self-necessity (الوجوب الذاتي) is known to us, but the specific reality (الحقيقة المخصوصة) of that essence is unknown; the known and the unknown are different, so they cannot be identical. Also, saying 'necessary of existence' alone is uninformative—it only becomes meaningful when attached to a specific subject (e.g., 'that essence is necessary'). Moreover, logicians treat necessity as a modality (جهة) of the copula (رابطة) in a proposition, not as the entire quiddity. Hence necessity cannot be identical to the quiddity.

Second possibility (existence as a part): If necessity were a part of the quiddity, the Necessary Being would be composite (مركب). Every composite is contingent (ممكن) because its parts are prior to it and it depends on them. Thus the Necessary Being would be contingent, contradicting its necessity.

Third possibility (existence as an external attribute): If necessity is an attribute added to the essence, it would depend on that essence for its existence. Anything that depends on something else is contingent in itself. This contingency would require a cause, and the only cause could be the essence itself, leading to a regress—there would have to be another necessity before it, ad infinitum. Since an infinite regress is impossible, this division is also false.

Therefore, all three possible relationships are impossible, so the objection fails.

Refutation of the Third Objection: Existence and Quiddity
Sanusi refutes the third objection by eliminating both identity and difference of existence and quiddity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the third objection against the Necessary Being is refuted by showing that both identity and difference of existence and quiddity lead to contradictions.

Core Idea
Both identity and difference of existence and quiddity are impossible for the Necessary Being.

This passage presents a critical objection and its refutation. The objector argues that the Necessary Being's existence must either be identical to its quiddity or distinct from it. The refutation shows both options are false, thereby reinforcing the doctrine of the Necessary Being. Understanding this argument is key to grasping the distinction between essence and existence in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation once. Notice the structure: an objection followed by two alternative possibilities, each refuted in turn.
The passage is dense. Do not worry about every detail on first read.
2
Identify the two alternatives
The objector presents two possibilities for the relation between existence and quiddity of the Necessary Being: identity or difference. The refutation addresses each.
3
Analyze the refutation of identity
The first alternative (identity) is refuted because it would make the term 'existent' not univocal, which is previously established as false. Understand why univocity is required.
This relies on an earlier argument about the univocity of existence.
4
Analyze the refutation of difference
The second alternative (difference) leads to an infinite regress or self-contradiction: existence would depend on the quiddity, which would then be prior to itself.
Key concept: cause must be prior to effect in existence.
5
Reflect on the overall argument
Consider how this refutation eliminates both options, leaving no coherent way to distinguish existence from quiddity in the Necessary Being. This supports the classical Ash'ari position.

الشبهة الثالثة : لو وجد موجود واجب الوجود لذاته ، لكان إما أن يكون وجوده نفس ماهيته، أو مغايرا لها . والأول باطل ، لأنه لو كان وجوده نفس ماهيته ثم إن ماهيته مخالفة لماهية سائر الماهيات ، ولجميع صفات تلك الماهيات ، فيلزم أن يكون لفظ الموجود واقعا عليه وعلى غيره ، لا بحسب مفهوم واحد ، وقد ثبت أن ذلك باطل . والثاني أيضاً باطل ، لأن بتقدير أن يكون الوجود صفة قائمة به ، كانت مفتقرة إلى ذلك الموصوف ، والمفتقر إلى الغير ممكن لذاته، والممكن لذاته لا بد له من علة ، ولا علة له إلا تلك الماهية ، فتكون علة ذلك الموجود ، هو تلك الماهية ، لكن كل علة فهي متقدمة على معلولها بوجودها ، فيلزم كون تلك الماهية متقدمة بوجودها على وجودها ، فيلزم أن يكون ذلك الوجود متقدما على نفسه ، وهو محال
The third objection: If there existed an existent necessary of existence in itself, then either its existence is the same as its quiddity, or different from it. The first is false, because if its existence were the same as its quiddity, then its quiddity is different from the quiddity of all other quiddities and from all attributes of those quiddities, so the term 'existent' would apply to it and to others not in a single concept, and it has been established that this is false. The second is also false, because on the assumption that existence is an attribute subsisting in it, it would be in need of that described, and what is in need of another is contingent in itself; and what is contingent in itself must have a cause, and it has no cause except that quiddity, so that quiddity would be the cause of that existent. But every cause is prior to its effect in its existence, so that quiddity would be prior in its existence to its existence, so that existence would be prior to itself, which is impossible.

Commentary
This passage refutes the third objection (الشبهة الثالثة) against the Necessary Being. The objector offers two possibilities: the existence of the Necessary Being is either identical to its quiddity (ماهية) or distinct from it. The author shows both to be false.

First possibility (identity): If existence and quiddity are the same, then the Necessary Being's quiddity is unique—different from all other quiddities and attributes. Consequently, the word 'existent' (الموجود) would not apply univocally to both the Necessary Being and contingent beings; it would be used equivocally. But it has been previously established that existence is predicated in a single sense (مفهوم واحد), so this cannot be.

Second possibility (distinctness): If existence is an attribute subsisting in (قائم به) the essence, then it depends on that essence. Whatever depends on another is contingent in itself (ممكن لذاته). Anything contingent requires a cause (علة). The only possible cause here would be the quiddity itself. But a cause must be prior in existence to its effect. This would mean the quiddity is prior in existence to its own existence—a contradiction, as existence would be prior to itself. Hence this is impossible.

Thus both alternatives are refuted, and the objection fails.

Refutation of the Third Objection: Existence vs. Essence
Refutes the third objection by dismantling both horns of the dilemma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the third objection against the Necessary Existent is refuted by showing that neither identity nor distinctness of existence and essence is possible.

Core Idea
The dilemma of existence being identical or distinct from essence is impossible for the Necessary Existent.

The third objection attempts to undermine the concept of a Necessary Existent by posing a dilemma: either its existence is its essence or it is not. Both options seem problematic. In this passage, the author refutes both horns, establishing that the Necessary Existent's existence is neither identical to nor distinct from its essence in the ordinary sense. Let's examine the argument.

The Fourth Objection: Necessary Existent Would Be Composite
Presents objection that Necessary Existent entails composition due to description distinct from essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth objection to the Necessary Existent: that the distinction between essence and attribute implies composition, making the Necessary Existent contingent.

Core Idea
If the Necessary Existent has a description distinct from its essence, it becomes composite, hence possible, not necessary.

Before the refutation is presented, the objection itself must be clearly understood. This lesson examines the fourth objection against the Necessary Existent, which argues that the very concept of 'necessary of existence' implies composition. The objection claims that since the attribute 'necessity' is distinct from the essence, the Necessary Existent would be a composite of essence and attribute, and every composite is possible — contradicting its necessity. Grasping this objection is crucial to appreciate the subsequent answer.

This passage presents the sixth proof for the possibility of bodies (imkān al-ajsām) in themselves, used by philosophers. The argument begins by noting that the quiddities (māhiyyāt) of bodies are receptive to attributes and accidents. This reception is necessitated by the quiddity itself. Commentary
This passage begins Chapter Sixteen, which explains how to use the contingency of attributes (imkan al-sifat) to prove the existence of God. The argument proceeds as follows:

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the existence of a non-corporeal cause for the diversity of attributes in bodies. The author begins by stating the observation: bodies share the same essence (corporeality) yet differ in qualities, places, and magnitudes. Therefore, each body's specific attributes must have a cause (otherwise a possible would occur without a preponderant, which is impossible). The cause is then divided into three possibilities: (1) something inherent in the body (like an accident), (2) something that is a locus for the body (like substance?), (3) something neither inherent nor a locus. The third possibility is further divided into three: (a) a body, (b) a corporeal (like a subtle body?), (c) neither body nor corporeal. All except (c) are invalid, leaving that the cause is a non-bodily, non-corporeal existent. The author then refutes four specific alternative candidates: (i) inherent powers (leads to infinite regress), (ii) the material substrate (impossible because a body cannot be inherent in a receptacle), (iii) another separate body (that body would itself require a cause, leading to regress), (iv) attributes in other bodies (also regress). After refuting these, the author concludes that the true cause is a separate existent that is neither body nor corporeal, which is the intended proof for a non-material agent (God).

The Cause Must Be Non-Corporeal
Classification and elimination of possible causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how through a process of elimination, the only viable cause for bodies' attributes is a non-corporeal, non-bodily existent.

Core Idea
The cause of diversity is neither inside the body nor another body; it must be immaterial.

Having established that bodies' attributes require a cause, the author now narrows down what that cause could be. He classifies possible causes into categories and systematically refutes all except one: a separate existent that is neither a body nor a corporeal entity. This is the classical kalam proof for a transcendent Creator.

The 'neither' category is divided into three: a body, a corporeal entity, or neither. Only the last survives.

The author refutes four specific proposals one by one: inherent powers (leads to infinite regress), material substrate (body cannot inhere in a receptacle), another separate body (that body would need a cause), and attributes in other bodies (same problem).
Each refutation uses a reductio ad absurdum.

After eliminating all alternatives, the only remaining possibility is a separate existent that is neither body nor corporeal – the intended proof for God.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the existence of a non-corporeal cause for the diversity of attributes in bodies. The author begins by stating the observation: bodies share the same essence (corporeality) yet differ in qualities, places, and magnitudes. Therefore, each body's specific attributes must have a cause (otherwise a possible would occur without a preponderant, which is impossible). The cause is then divided into three possibilities: (1) something inherent in the body (like an accident), (2) something that is a locus for the body (like substance?), (3) something neither inherent nor a locus. The third possibility is further divided into three: (a) a body, (b) a corporeal (like a subtle body?), (c) neither body nor corporeal. All except (c) are invalid, leaving that the cause is a non-bodily, non-corporeal existent. The author then refutes four specific alternative candidates: (i) inherent powers (leads to infinite regress), (ii) the material substrate (impossible because a body cannot be inherent in a receptacle), (iii) another separate body (that body would itself require a cause, leading to regress), (iv) attributes in other bodies (also regress). After refuting these, the author concludes that the true cause is a separate existent that is neither body nor corporeal, which is the intended proof for a non-material agent (God).

The Contingency of Planetary Position
