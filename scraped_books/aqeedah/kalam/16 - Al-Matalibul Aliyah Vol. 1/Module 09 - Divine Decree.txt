Learning Objective

Core Idea
Choice created by God does not negate human responsibility; it is the basis for reward and punishment.

In this passage, Sanusi addresses a critical objection: if everything depends on God's causal power, then human choice is an illusion. He responds by affirming that God creates the choice within us, and that choice becomes the cause of the action. This raises the specter of coercion, which Sanusi then refutes by explaining that reward and punishment follow necessarily from our actions, as will be detailed later. The objector grants that actions coincide with motives but denies that this implies causation, suggesting pure coincidence. The defender appeals to the necessary consistency of causal patterns, but the objector counters that consistency itself could be coincidental. This dialectic reveals deep issues about induction and causation in theology.

السؤال الثاني : سلمنا وقوع تصرفاتنا عند دواعينا ، وامتناع وقوعها عند صوارفنا . فلم قلتم : إن ذلك يدل على كونها واقعة بنا ؟ قوله : « لو لم تكن واقعة بنا ، الجاز أن تقع حال ما نكره وقوعها ، وأن لا تقع حال ما نريد وقوعها ، قلنا : لم لا يجوز أن يقال : إنها تقع عند حصول إرادتنا ، لا لمؤثر ولا لمرجح ، بل لمحض الاتفاق. وتقدير هذا السؤال : أن يقال : إن حدوث الحادث لمحض الاتفاق من غير سبب ومرجح ، إما أن يكون معلوم البطلان بالبديهة ، أو ليس كذلك، فإن كان الحق هو الأول ، فحينئذ من علمنا أن العالم محدث ، علمنا بالضرورة افتقاره إلى الفاعل، كما هو قول الكعبي) وحينئذ يصير هذا القياس الذي ذكرتموه ضائعا . وإن كان الحق هو الثاني ، وهو أن امتناع حدوث الحادث لا بسبب لا يعلم ، إلا بالدليل . فحينئذ يبقى للسائل أن يقول : لم لا يجوز أن يقال : إن أفعالنا وإن وقعت عند حصول إرادتنا إلا أنها وقعت على سبيل الاتفاق من غير مؤثر أصلا ؟ فإن أثبتنا هذه المقدمة بقياس آخر ، لزم التسلسل وهو محال . فإن قالوا : الدليل على فساد هذا الاحتمال : أنه لو كان وقوع أفعالنا عند حصول ارادتنا ، وقوعـا لمحض الاتفاق من غير تأثير مؤثر ولا حصول أصلا ، وجب أن لا يطرد على نسق واحد. لأن الأحوال الاتفاقية لا مرجع تطرد ، بل تختلف أحوالها . فكان يلزم أنه قد يتفق حدوثها عند كراهاتنا ، وان لا يتفق حدوثها عند إرادتنا . فنقول في الجواب عن هذا السؤال: لم لا يجوز أن يقال : أنها وإن حدثت على سبيل الاتفاق ، إلا أنه اتفق في هذه الصورة الواحدة من الاتفاقيات أنها بقيت دائمة على نسق واحد ؟ وذلك لأن بتقدير أن لا يكون الحدوث على سبيل الاتفاق ممتنعا في أول العقل لم يكن كون بعض الاتفاقيات دائما على نسق واحد ممتنعاً أيضا في أول العقل . وحينئذ يسقط هذا الدليل . وهذا السؤال واقع على هذا الدليل .
The second question: We grant the occurrence of our actions when we have motives, and their non-occurrence when we have hindrances. Why do you say that this indicates that they occur by us? He said: 'If they were not occurring by us, it would be possible for them to occur when we dislike their occurrence, and not to occur when we will their occurrence.' We say: Why is it not permissible to say that they occur when our will arises, not due to an effect or a preponderant, but by pure coincidence? And the formulation of this question: that the occurrence of an event by pure coincidence without cause and preponderant is either known to be false by intuition or not. If the truth is the first, then when we know that the world is originated, we know necessarily its need for the agent, as is the saying of al-Ka'bi, and then this analogical argument you mentioned becomes useless. And if the truth is the second, that the impossibility of an event occurring without cause is known only by evidence, then the questioner can say: Why is it not permissible to say that our actions, even if they occur when our will arises, occur by way of coincidence without any effect at all? If we establish this premise with another analogical argument, it would lead to infinite regress, which is absurd. So if they say: The evidence against this possibility is that if our actions' occurrence when our will arises were by pure coincidence without any effect or cause at all, it would be necessary that they not be consistent in a single pattern. Because coincidental states are not consistent; rather, their states differ. So it would follow that they could occur when we dislike them and not occur when we will them. We say in answer to this question: Why is it not permissible to say that they, even if they occur by coincidence, it is just that in this particular instance of coincidences it happened that they remained consistent on a single pattern? Because given that occurrence by coincidence is not impossible in the first intellect, it is also not impossible for some coincidences to be consistently on a single pattern. Then this evidence collapses. And this question is directed at this evidence.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer (السؤال الثاني) concerning the inference from the regularity of human actions to their being produced by the agent. The objector concedes that actions occur when motives are present and are hindered when counter-motives are present (صوارف), but denies that this proves the action is caused by the agent. The objector suggests that the alignment could be pure coincidence (محض الاتفاق). The defender of the original argument counters that if it were coincidence, it would not be consistent—actions would sometimes occur when disliked and fail to occur when willed. The objector then replies that consistency itself could be a coincidence; it is not impossible in the first intellect that a particular coincidence produces a consistent pattern. The defender's argument thus collapses. This passage shows the dialectic of kalam: the objector uses a skeptical possibility (coincidence) and the defender appeals to the impossibility of consistent coincidence. The answer reframes the issue by noting that the impossibility of coincidence is not self-evident; if it is not self-evident, the defender's argument fails, and if it is self-evident, then the original long proof is unnecessary.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Regularity of actions is sufficient to prove they are caused by the agent.
Correction: The objector shows that even consistent coincidence is logically possible, so regularity alone does not prove causation.

Key Takeaway
The regularity of actions is not sufficient to prove agency because consistent coincidence remains a logical possibility.
