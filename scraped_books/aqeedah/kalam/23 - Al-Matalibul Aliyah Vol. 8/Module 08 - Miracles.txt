The Mu'tazila's Inconsistent Stance on Miracles
Describes the Mu'tazila's inconsistent stance on miracles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Mu'tazila's position on miracles is described as contradictory and lacking a consistent principle.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila had no fixed rule for miracles.

In surveying various schools on miracles, the author now turns to the Mu'tazila. Their position is not a single view but a contradiction: they sometimes affirm extraordinary events and sometimes deny them, without a clear criterion. This lesson explores that inconsistency.

Commentary
This passage provides a brief but sharp criticism of the Mu'tazila. The author states that their position on miracles is contradictory (muḍṭarib): sometimes they allow them, sometimes they forbid them. They lack a consistent principle (qānūn ma‘lūm) or a fixed method (ṭarīq maḍbūṭ) to unify their views. This concludes the author's survey of the various schools—philosophers, Ash'ari, and Mu'tazila—on the topic of miracles.

Commentary
This passage is an extended argument responding to an objection that miracles might be due to natural properties of drugs, thus undermining their evidential value for prophethood. The author (likely Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, given the book title) structures his response by first stating the objection, then listing four specific arguments made by the objector, and then answering the first three.

The passage begins with a recognition that philosophers have described amazing effects from natural properties like the magnetic stone. The author says that even if we cannot verify their truth, we also cannot deny them with certainty; we must allow the possibility. If such a possibility exists, then a claimant to prophethood might have found a drug that produced the 'miracle,' and this would invalidate the miracle's proof.

The objector then raises four points. First: allowing a drug to produce wonders like turning a staff into a serpent would absurdly require allowing a drug to create the heavens and earth, which is irrational. Second: most such properties when tested are false. Third: if the drug existed, others would know and be able to oppose the claimant. Fourth: if only the claimant knew, that itself is a miracle, affirming his truthfulness.

The author answers each in turn. To the first, he argues that no one claims miracles involve creating heavens and earth; the ability to perform one type of miracle does not imply ability for all. So the drug's effect can be limited. He adds that there are two extremes: drugs that do the impossible (like moving spheres) are non-existent, but drugs that increase natural powers exist. Prophetic miracles might be of either type, so doubt remains.

To the second, he responds that we cannot be certain those properties are false, citing a book by Ahmad ibn Wahshiyyah that claims strange effects, and we have no proof of falsehood, so we must suspend judgment.

To the third, he suggests the claimant might have had exclusive knowledge, which is rationally possible, or that others knew but were prevented from using or revealing it.

The fourth objection about exclusive knowledge making it a miracle is not explicitly answered here; perhaps it is considered implicitly addressed by the answer to the third.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth doubt (shubha) regarding the proof of prophethood through miracles. The opponent grants that God has purposes but asks: what proof is there that the specific purpose of this miracle is only to authenticate the claimant? Purposes are many, and God's wisdom is vast. Commentary
This passage is a sustained theological argument from Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's Al-Maṭālib al-ʿĀliya. It continues a discussion on miracles and the possibilities that could undermine the evidential force of a miracle for confirming a prophet. 2. The seventh possibility: The questioner does not need to enumerate all possible explanations; he can simply challenge the proponent to prove that no other explanation exists except confirmation. If the proponent cannot negate all other possibilities, his proof collapses. Thus the burden of proof is on the proponent.
3. The objectors then raise a concern: Even if the assumption is not definitive, if God creates a miracle that strongly appears to confirm a false claimant, that would be casting doubt into minds, which is evil (qabīḥ). 
4. Al-Razi responds with five arguments (wujūh) to show that casting probable doubt is not necessarily evil. 
   - First argument: God revealed many ambiguous verses (mutashābihāt) in the Qur'an that give the illusion of falsehoods, but because they are probable (muḥtamala), they are not evil. Similarly, the miracle's illusion is not evil because it remains probable.

   - Third argument: Many doubts exist in the world, such as correlations between events and celestial bodies or mixtures, which give the illusion of causality. Physicians use such correlations to infer the natures of medicines, yet these correlations are not evidence of actual causality but are accepted. God creates such phenomena despite their potential for doubt.

   - Fifth argument: Righteous people sometimes suffer while sinners prosper, which gives the illusion that piety is not rewarded. Again, this is not evil because there is a higher wisdom.
5. Conclusion: Even on the assumption that God's wisdom is necessary and that intellectual judgments of good and evil apply to God's actions, God is not obligated to avoid everything that gives an illusion of falsehood. The passage ends with 'And with God is success'.

The Seventh Possibility: Shifting the Burden of Proof
Introduction of the seventh possibility and the burden of proof shift.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the seventh possibility, which argues that the questioner does not need to enumerate all alternatives; the proponent must negate them all.

Core Idea
The burden is on the proponent to rule out all other explanations.

Al-Razi now introduces a seventh, more strategic possibility. He argues that the skeptic does not have to list every conceivable alternative explanation for a miracle. Instead, the one who uses the miracle as proof must prove that no other explanation exists. This shifts the burden of proof and sets up the coming rebuttal.

Guided Path

2
Understand the shift

This is a methodological point.
3
Reflect on the consequence
If the proponent cannot negate all other possibilities, his proof fails. This makes the miracle argument much harder to sustain.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained theological argument from Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's Al-Maṭālib al-ʿĀliya. It continues a discussion on miracles and the possibilities that could undermine the evidential force of a miracle for confirming a prophet. The passage presents two additional possibilities (the sixth and seventh) that could explain why a miracle occurs without necessarily confirming the claimant's truth, and then responds to an objection that God would not create a miracle that gives a strong illusion of confirmation if He did not intend to confirm the prophet, as that would be casting doubt (shubha) and would be evil (qabīḥ). The argument proceeds as follows:

1. The sixth possibility: God might have created the miracle at that specific time as a kindness (luṭf) for some morally responsible beings, to encourage them to perform intellectual duties and avoid intellectual evil acts. This means the miracle's purpose is not solely to confirm the claimant.
2. The seventh possibility: The questioner does not need to enumerate all possible explanations; he can simply challenge the proponent to prove that no other explanation exists except confirmation. If the proponent cannot negate all other possibilities, his proof collapses. Thus the burden of proof is on the proponent.
3. The objectors then raise a concern: Even if the assumption is not definitive, if God creates a miracle that strongly appears to confirm a false claimant, that would be casting doubt into minds, which is evil (qabīḥ). 
4. Al-Razi responds with five arguments (wujūh) to show that casting probable doubt is not necessarily evil. 
   - First argument: God revealed many ambiguous verses (mutashābihāt) in the Qur'an that give the illusion of falsehoods, but because they are probable (muḥtamala), they are not evil. Similarly, the miracle's illusion is not evil because it remains probable.
   - Second argument: God allowed Iblīs and his troops to remain active in whispering falsehoods, and He caused prophets and righteous people to die, which gives the illusion that God is striving for misguidance. Yet these are not evil because they are part of a larger divine plan.
   - Third argument: Many doubts exist in the world, such as correlations between events and celestial bodies or mixtures, which give the illusion of causality. Physicians use such correlations to infer the natures of medicines, yet these correlations are not evidence of actual causality but are accepted. God creates such phenomena despite their potential for doubt.
   - Fourth argument: The world is full of afflictions, and when people pray fervently, they may not be answered, which gives the illusion that prayers are ineffective or that God is not listening. This does not make God's action evil.
   - Fifth argument: Righteous people sometimes suffer while sinners prosper, which gives the illusion that piety is not rewarded. Again, this is not evil because there is a higher wisdom.
5. Conclusion: Even on the assumption that God's wisdom is necessary and that intellectual judgments of good and evil apply to God's actions, God is not obligated to avoid everything that gives an illusion of falsehood. The passage ends with 'And with God is success'.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained theological argument from Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's Al-Maṭālib al-ʿĀliya. It continues a discussion on miracles and the possibilities that could undermine the evidential force of a miracle for confirming a prophet. The passage presents two additional possibilities (the sixth and seventh) that could explain why a miracle occurs without necessarily confirming the claimant's truth, and then responds to an objection that God would not create a miracle that gives a strong illusion of confirmation if He did not intend to confirm the prophet, as that would be casting doubt (shubha) and would be evil (qabīḥ). The argument proceeds as follows:

1. The sixth possibility: God might have created the miracle at that specific time as a kindness (luṭf) for some morally responsible beings, to encourage them to perform intellectual duties and avoid intellectual evil acts. This means the miracle's purpose is not solely to confirm the claimant.
2. The seventh possibility: The questioner does not need to enumerate all possible explanations; he can simply challenge the proponent to prove that no other explanation exists except confirmation. If the proponent cannot negate all other possibilities, his proof collapses. Thus the burden of proof is on the proponent.
3. The objectors then raise a concern: Even if the assumption is not definitive, if God creates a miracle that strongly appears to confirm a false claimant, that would be casting doubt into minds, which is evil (qabīḥ). 
4. Al-Razi responds with five arguments (wujūh) to show that casting probable doubt is not necessarily evil. 
   - First argument: God revealed many ambiguous verses (mutashābihāt) in the Qur'an that give the illusion of falsehoods, but because they are probable (muḥtamala), they are not evil. Similarly, the miracle's illusion is not evil because it remains probable.
   - Second argument: God allowed Iblīs and his troops to remain active in whispering falsehoods, and He caused prophets and righteous people to die, which gives the illusion that God is striving for misguidance. Yet these are not evil because they are part of a larger divine plan.
   - Third argument: Many doubts exist in the world, such as correlations between events and celestial bodies or mixtures, which give the illusion of causality. Physicians use such correlations to infer the natures of medicines, yet these correlations are not evidence of actual causality but are accepted. God creates such phenomena despite their potential for doubt.
   - Fourth argument: The world is full of afflictions, and when people pray fervently, they may not be answered, which gives the illusion that prayers are ineffective or that God is not listening. This does not make God's action evil.
   - Fifth argument: Righteous people sometimes suffer while sinners prosper, which gives the illusion that piety is not rewarded. Again, this is not evil because there is a higher wisdom.
5. Conclusion: Even on the assumption that God's wisdom is necessary and that intellectual judgments of good and evil apply to God's actions, God is not obligated to avoid everything that gives an illusion of falsehood. The passage ends with 'And with God is success'.

The King Analogy: How Miracles Confirm Prophethood
Presents a king analogy to argue that miracles confirm a prophet's truthfulness.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Just as a king's unusual act confirms a claimant's truth, a miracle confirms a prophet.

This passage uses a vivid analogy from everyday experience to establish a key principle in proving prophethood. By observing how a king's actions confirm a messenger in a public assembly, we can understand how God's miracles confirm a prophet. The argument follows the method of reasoning from the witnessed to the unseen.

This lesson presents the first objection to the evidential value of miracles. The objector argues that miracles function like a verbal declaration of prophethood, which is inherently open to interpretation and therefore provides only probable indication. You will see how this challenge is posed and why it is a significant test for the certainty of prophetic proof.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A miracle's unusualness does not automatically prove a prophet's truthfulness.

This passage presents a critical objection to the standard argument for prophethood through miracles. The objector argues that a breach of custom (khāriq li-l-ʿāda) is not inherently a sign of truthfulness, using the example of the first event of creation. It then addresses a common defense—that the miracle must coincide with the claim—and challenges whether such concomitance proves causality with certainty.

The Necessity of Relevant Miracles
States that a miracle must be appropriate to the claim it supports.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a miracle must be specifically relevant to the claim it supports, and why an irrelevant miracle carries no evidential value.

Core Idea
A miracle must be suitable to the claim, or it is worthless.

This short but powerful passage from al-Maṭālib al-ʿĀliyah sets a crucial condition for prophetic evidence: a miracle cannot be arbitrary. Before we accept any miraculous event as proof of prophethood, we must check whether it actually addresses the specific claim being made. The author illustrates this with a clear example that exposes a common logical mistake.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage carefully, then its literal translation. Let the argument sink in before analyzing its parts.
Pay attention to the sharp contrast between 'this meaning' and 'the staff into a snake.'
2
Identify the logical structure
Notice how the passage first states a general requirement (clarify the claim by manifesting the intended meaning), then gives a negative example, then evaluates it, and finally draws a conclusion.
3
Understand why the example fails
The claimant says 'I turn the staff into a snake' as proof. The author says this proof does not suit the requested claim. Ask yourself: what is the claim? If the claim is simply 'I am a prophet,' then changing a staff into a snake does not by itself prove that the message is from God. It only shows power, not truthfulness or divine commission.
The key word is 'munāsib' (suitable) – the miracle must fit the claim.
4
Apply the principle
Think of other examples in Islamic theology where miracles are tied to the specific claim of the prophet, like the Qur'an itself as the miracle of Muhammad ﷺ directly addressing his claim of prophethood and message.
