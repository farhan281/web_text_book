The Core Proof: Two Impossible States for Power
Sanusi presents the third proof with two branches against power over action and its opposite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Ash'ari concept of power over both an action and its opposite is impossible in two rational scenarios: balanced motivations and preponderant motivations.

Core Idea
Power over an action fails in both balanced and preponderant motivations — only determinism remains.

The third proof targets the idea that a person has power to do an action or its opposite. By analyzing the two possible states of motivation, the argument shows that such power is inconceivable. Let's examine this logical dilemma step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then its translation. Notice how the proof is structured as a dilemma: if power exists, it must be in one of two states.
2
Identify the two states
Recognize the two possible states: balanced motivations (istiwā' al-dawā'ī) or preponderance (rujḥān) of one side over the other.
These are the only logical possibilities for the state of motivation when an agent acts.
3
Understand the impossibility in balanced state
In balanced motivations, achieving preponderance would contradict the balance. Since the agent cannot cause preponderance while balance remains, power over the action cannot exist in this state.
The key point: preponderance (rujḥān) contradicts balance (istiwā'), so power to produce preponderance is impossible.
4
Understand the impossibility in preponderant state
In a state of preponderance, the preponderant side becomes necessary (wājib) and the outweighed side impossible (mumtaniʿ). Neither the necessary nor the impossible can be subject to power.
Thus, power over the action (or its opposite) cannot exist in this state either.
5
Conclude the proof
Since both possible states are impossible for power, the very notion of such power is impossible. This leads to the conclusion that determinism (jabr) is necessary.
The proof is a powerful example of kalam reasoning using logical impossibility.

The third proof: If power over the action existed, it would exist either in a state of balanced motivations (istiwā' al-dawā'ī) toward the act and its opposite, or in a state of preponderance (rujḥān) of one side over the other. Both divisions are false. So the assertion of the existence of this power is impossible. We say: It is impossible for capability (makna) to exist in a state of balanced motivation, because preponderance contradicts balance; thus the occurrence of preponderance while balance exists is impossible, and the impossible cannot be subject to power. And we say: It is impossible for this capability to exist in a state of preponderance of one side, because the preponderant (al-rājiḥ) is necessary (wājib) and the outweighed (al-marjūḥ) is impossible (mumtaniʿ), as its proof will come repeatedly, and the necessary and the impossible are not subject to power at all. If they say: In the state of balance, he can bring about preponderance in the second moment from it. We say: This is false in two ways. First: Either it is meant that when the second moment arrives, he can bring about preponderance in that moment, or it is meant that in the first moment he can bring about preponderance in the second moment. The first is false, because when the second moment arrives, either balanced motivations or preponderance of one side occurs, and then the first division returns. The second is also false, because when the first moment occurs, the occurrence of the second moment is impossible, but the bringing about of the action in the second moment is conditional upon the presence of the second moment, which is impossible to be present in the first moment. That which is dependent on the impossible is impossible, so it follows that bringing about the action in the second moment at the presence of the first moment is impossible, and the impossible is not subject to power. Second: On this assumption, the bringing about (īqāʿ) of the action occurs in the first moment, and the actual occurrence (wuqūʿ) of the action occurs in the second moment. This necessitates that the bringing about of the action is different from its occurrence. We say: That different thing called 'bringing about' (īqāʿ), if it is not actual by the power of this agent, then the action occurring due to this bringing about must not be actual by this powerful being (qādir). But if it is actual by the power of this agent, then his power must precede it. Then the discussion about it is as in the first case. This necessitates that every bringing about is preceded by another bringing about, ad infinitum in time. And that necessitates that the power of the powerful being precedes the occurrence of the effect by an infinite number of times. All of that is impossible.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof (al-ḥujja al-thālitha) against the Ashʿarī notion of power (qudra) over both an action and its opposite (al-fiʿl wa al-tark). The argument proceeds as follows: If a person had power over a specific action, this power would exist either (1) when motivations (dawāʿī) for the action and its opposite are perfectly balanced (istiwāʾ), or (2) when one side is preponderant (rujḥān). Both cases are shown to be impossible. In the balanced case, preponderance would contradict balance, so power over preponderance cannot coexist with balance. In the preponderant case, the preponderant side becomes necessary (wājib) and the outweighed side impossible (mumtaniʿ), and necessary and impossible things are not subject to power. Thus power over the action cannot exist. The text then addresses an objection: perhaps in the balanced state the agent can postpone preponderance to a second moment. The refutation has two parts. First, if the agent can bring about preponderance only when the second moment arrives, the same dilemma repeats (balance or preponderance at that moment). If he can bring it about from the first moment, then the second moment is not yet present, so bringing about the action in it is impossible (since the condition—the second moment's presence—is absent). Second, if one distinguishes between 'bringing about' (īqāʿ) in the first moment and 'occurrence' (wuqūʿ) in the second, then the bringing about itself must be caused by power; this leads to an infinite regress of bringings-about, which is impossible. The conclusion is that power over an action is impossible, implying determinism (jabr).

Refutation of the Objection: Two Ways to Deny Postponed Preponderance
Sanusi refutes the objection of postponed preponderance with two arguments: a repeated dilemma and infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection that preponderance can be postponed to a later moment is refuted using two logical arguments: a repeated dilemma and an infinite regress.

Core Idea
Postponing preponderance leads to either a repeated dilemma or an infinite regress, both impossible.

An objector might argue that even in balanced motivations, the agent can postpone the act of preponderance to a later moment. The author dismantles this with two incisive arguments, showing that such postponement is either incoherent or leads to an infinite chain of 'bringings-about'. Let's follow the refutation step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Read the passage from 'فإن قالوا' to the end. Notice that the response is divided into two main parts: the first refutation, and the second refutation.
2
Understand the first refutation: the dilemma repeats
The first refutation has two sub-parts: either the preponderance is brought about in the second moment, leading to the same dilemma, or it is brought about from the first moment for the second moment, which is impossible because the second moment is not present.
The key point: the condition for the action (presence of the second moment) is absent in the first moment.
3
Understand the second refutation: infinite regress
The second refutation distinguishes between 'bringing about' (īqāʿ) in the first moment and 'occurrence' (wuqūʿ) in the second. The bringing about itself must be caused by power, leading to an infinite series of bringings-about before the effect occurs.
This regress is impossible because it would require an infinite number of prior power acts.
4
Conclude the refutation
Both refutations show that the attempted escape through postponement fails. Thus, the original proof stands: power over the action is impossible.
This reinforces the deterministic conclusion.

The Impossibility of Action in a Future Time
Refutation of performing an action in a future moment while present in the present.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why it is rationally impossible for a capable agent to perform an action in a future moment while being present only in the present moment, and how the distinction between 'performance' and 'occurrence' leads to an infinite regress.

Core Idea
You cannot act in a future time because it leads to an infinite chain of performances.

This lesson unpacks a subtle argument from classical Islamic theology about the nature of action and time. The author, likely drawing on Ash'ari or Maturidi reasoning, targets the idea that an agent can 'perform' an action at a future moment while only existing in the present. By analyzing the relationship between the performance and the occurrence of an act, he demonstrates a logical contradiction. Prepare to think carefully about causality, power, and temporal succession.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then read the literal translation. Notice the key terms: إيقاع (performance) and وقوع (occurrence). Pay attention to how the argument builds.
The passage is dense, so take your time.
2
Identify the two main parts of the argument
The argument first states a direct impossibility: performing in the second moment while in the first is impossible because the second moment is absent. Then it considers an alternative scenario where performance occurs in the first moment and occurrence in the second.
3
Trace the infinite regress
Follow the reasoning: If performance is distinct from occurrence and caused by the agent's power, then that performance itself must be caused by a prior performance, leading to an infinite chain. Understand why this is deemed impossible.
This is the core of the proof.
4
Reflect on the theological implication
Consider how this argument supports the view that a capable agent's power is directly connected to the act in the present moment, and that temporal gaps cannot exist between power and its effect.
This connects to broader discussions of divine and human agency.

Commentary
This passage presents an argument against the possibility of a human agent having genuine power to initiate an action at a future moment while being present in the present moment. The author first states that the second moment cannot be present in the first moment, so performing an action in the second moment while in the first is impossible. Then he considers the alternative: if we suppose that the performance occurs in the first moment and the action occurs in the second, then performance and occurrence are distinct. If this performance is not caused by the agent's power, then the action is not from him; if it is caused by his power, then his power must precede the performance, leading to an infinite regress of performances. Such infinite regress is impossible. Therefore, the initial assumption that the agent can perform an action in a future moment is invalid.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Two independent causes cannot produce the same effect; divine power is uniquely capable.

This lesson presents one of the most sophisticated arguments against human creation, known as the eighth proof. The argument begins by establishing that any act falling under human power also falls under divine power, because the two objects of power are similar. From there, it proceeds to show that if both God and the servant were independent causes of the same act, logical absurdities follow. The only consistent conclusion is that the act is solely from God, negating the servant's role as a creator.

Guided Path

The Inductive Argument for Astrology's Validity
Al-Razi presents the philosophers' inductive argument based on observed correlations and historical consensus on astrology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two-pronged inductive argument used by philosophers to support astrology: empirical correlation and historical consensus.

Core Idea
Inductive reasoning from nature and history jointly supports astrology's claims.

Al-Razi presents the philosophers' inductive argument (حجة إقناعية), which does not prove astrology definitively but offers strong circumstantial evidence. The argument first points to observable connections between celestial motions and earthly seasons, then appeals to the universal historical practice of astrology. This lesson unpacks both components and their logical force.
