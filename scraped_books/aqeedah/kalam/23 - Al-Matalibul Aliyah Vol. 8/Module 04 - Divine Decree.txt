The Determinism Argument from Preference
Arguing that any preference entails jabr via exhaustive analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that any preference for action over inaction leads to determinism (jabr) as presented in this passage.

Core Idea
Any explanation of human choice leads back to determinism.

This passage presents a classic determinist argument: whenever an agent prefers one action over another, that preference must either have a cause or not. If it has a cause, that cause either comes from the agent or from outside; both paths lead to jabr. If it has no cause, the choice is arbitrary coincidence, which also amounts to jabr. The author systematically eliminates all possibilities to conclude that determinism is unavoidable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial scenarios
The passage considers three possibilities for how preference for action arises: it could have a cause (marjih) from the agent, a cause from outside, or no cause at all. Read the beginning and note how each is introduced.
The first two are mentioned briefly; the third is the main focus.
2
Understand the two objections to uncaused preference
The author gives two reasons why preference without a cause is invalid: it undermines the proof from contingency for the Creator, and it reduces choice to pure coincidence, which is jabr. Find these two reasons and explain them in your own words.
The second reason is elaborated more than the first.
3
Trace the conclusion across all divisions
The author concludes that whether preference depends on a cause or not, jabr is necessary. He then adds that if the agent cannot abandon the action, jabr is even more obvious. See how this covers all possibilities.
This is the final paragraph starting from 'فيثبت'.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof (or part of a proof) for determinism (jabr) by analyzing the concept of tarjīḥ (preference) between action and inaction. The author argues that any preference for action over inaction ultimately leads to jabr. He considers two initial possibilities: if the cause of preference (marjiḥ) comes from the agent (the slave), then the first division (presumably a previous classification) returns; if it comes from outside, then jabr follows. Then he examines a third scenario: preference occurring without any marjiḥ at all. This he declares invalid for two reasons. First, if preference can occur without a marjiḥ, then one side of a possible thing can become preferable without a cause, which would undermine the argument from contingency for the existence of a Creator, leading to the denial of obligation and prophethood—an unacceptable consequence. Second, such uncaused preference would be pure coincidence, making the action no more likely to occur than its opposite. Under this view, whether the action happens or not is independent of the agent's will, which is pure jabr. Thus, whether preference depends on a marjiḥ or not, jabr is necessary. He then adds a second division: if the agent is capable of action but not capable of abandon (i.e., unable to refrain), then jabr is even more obvious. In all cases, jabr is inevitable.

The Inability to Will Inaction: Second Jabr Argument
Sanusi presents the second proof for jabr from inability to will inaction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational argument that the one capable of action cannot have power over inaction, and why this leads to the doctrine of jabr (determinism).

Core Idea
Inaction is pure non-being; power cannot affect non-being, so action is necessitated.

The second proof for jabr argues that if an agent has power to do an action, they cannot also have power to refrain from it. Why? Because refraining (tark) is simply remaining in the original state of non-existence, and non-existence cannot be the effect of power. Therefore, power necessarily produces the action, implying jabr. This lesson unpacks that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic source and its translation. Notice the logical flow: claim → definition of inaction → two reasons why non-being cannot be an object of power → conclusion.
2
Understand the definition of 'tark'
The author defines inaction as 'remaining upon the original non-being'. This is crucial: it is not a positive act, but the absence of action.
Contrast with the common view that refraining is a voluntary act.
3
Examine the two arguments against power over non-being
First: Power is an effective attribute producing existence, so pure non-being cannot be its effect. Second: Causing something to persist is impossible because it already exists (taḥṣīl al-ḥāṣil). Both show inaction cannot be intended.
4
Connect to jabr
Since the agent has no power to not act, power necessarily results in action. Thus the agent is compelled to act, which is the essence of jabr.
This argument targets free will defenders who claim ability to do otherwise.

The second proof: The one capable of action has no power at all over inaction. And if the matter is so, then power necessitates the action, so jabr is necessary. And we only say that the one capable of action has no power over inaction because inaction is the term for remaining upon the original non-being. And non-being is not suitable to be an object of power for the capable, because power is an effective attribute, and non-being is pure negation and sheer non-being, so it is impossible to make it an effect of power. And because a thing during its remaining, it is impossible for it to be brought about by another; otherwise, it would necessitate the attainment of what is already attained, which is impossible. So two faces have occurred regarding the remaining non-being, each of which prevents it from being an object of power, and that is impossible. And if that is the case, it is established that inaction is impossible to be an object of power. So it is established that the one capable of action has no power over inaction. And if that is so, power necessitates action, and that necessitates the statement of jabr.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof for jabr. It begins with the assertion that the one capable of action has absolutely no power over inaction (tark). If that is true, then power necessarily produces the action, leading to jabr. The proof is that inaction is defined as remaining upon the original non-being (al-ʿadam al-aṣlī). Non-being cannot be an object of power because power is an effective attribute that produces something existent, whereas non-being is pure negation. Thus, non-being cannot be the effect of power. Additionally, a thing during its state of remaining (i.e., its persistence) cannot be brought about by another agent, because that would be 'attaining the attained' (taḥṣīl al-ḥāṣil), which is impossible. Therefore, inaction cannot be an object of power. Consequently, the agent who is capable of action cannot be capable of inaction, so his power necessarily results in the action, and jabr is established.

The Impossibility of Power in States of Motive
Proving that power cannot occur in either state of motives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why power over action cannot exist in either a state of equal motives or preponderant motives, according to the third argument for jabr.

Core Idea
Power cannot coexist with equality or necessity; it requires preponderance within possibility.

This lesson presents the core of the third argument for jabr. The author argues that if a person had power over an action, that power must exist either when motives are perfectly balanced or when one motive outweighs the other. He then demonstrates that both scenarios are impossible, thereby establishing that human power over action is illusory.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once through. Notice how the author first sets up a conditional division, then refutes each case.
2
Identify the two states of motives
The passage says power would have to occur either in a state of equality (istiwā') of motives or preponderance (rujhān) of one side. Underline these two terms.
3
Understand why equality fails
In equality, preponderance is absent, but action requires preponderance. Since preponderance contradicts equality, you cannot have both. Therefore, power cannot exist in equality.
Key concept: preponderance (tarjīḥ) is needed for action.
4
Understand why preponderance fails
When one side is preponderant, that side becomes necessary (wājib) and the opposite impossible (mumtaniʿ). Power cannot affect necessary or impossible things.
This is a foundational kalam principle.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Since both cases are invalid, the very idea of power over action is impossible. This supports the doctrine of jabr.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof for jabr. It begins by positing that if power over action were to occur, it must occur either when motives (dawāʿī) for action and inaction are equal, or when one side is preponderant. Both cases are argued to be invalid. If motives are equal, then there is no preponderance, but preponderance is needed for action; hence power cannot be effective in that state because preponderance contradicts equality. If one side is preponderant, then that side is necessary (wājib) and the opposite is impossible (mumtaniʿ), and power cannot apply to the necessary or the impossible. Therefore, power over action cannot exist at all. The author then addresses an objection: one might say that in the state of equality, the agent can create tarjīḥ in a subsequent moment. The author refutes this by considering two possibilities: either the agent creates tarjīḥ in that second moment, or he creates it from the first moment for the second moment. The first fails because at the second moment, the situation is again either equality or preponderance, leading back to the original division. The second fails because the first moment cannot produce the second moment, and the action in the second moment is conditional on its presence, which is impossible. Thus, the objection is invalid, and jabr is established.

Refuting the Objection of Tarjīḥ in a Second Moment
Refuting the objection of tarjīḥ in a second moment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the objection that one can create preponderance in a subsequent moment fails, according to the third argument for jabr.

Core Idea
Logically, you cannot create preponderance from a state of equality, either in the same or a later moment.

In this lesson, we examine the author's rebuttal to an objection. The opponent claims that when motives are equal, the agent can still act in a later moment by creating preponderance then. The author dismantles this claim by analyzing two possible interpretations, both of which lead to contradictions.

Guided Path

3
Understand the second interpretation
The second interpretation: in the first moment, the agent can create preponderance for the second moment. The author refutes this by arguing that the first moment cannot bring about the second moment; it is impossible.
The passage ends mid-sentence, but the argument is clear.
4
Reflect on the overall argument
Both ways of trying to salvage human power fail, strengthening the case for jabr.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof for jabr. It begins by positing that if power over action were to occur, it must occur either when motives (dawāʿī) for action and inaction are equal, or when one side is preponderant. Both cases are argued to be invalid. If motives are equal, then there is no preponderance, but preponderance is needed for action; hence power cannot be effective in that state because preponderance contradicts equality. If one side is preponderant, then that side is necessary (wājib) and the opposite is impossible (mumtaniʿ), and power cannot apply to the necessary or the impossible. Therefore, power over action cannot exist at all. The author then addresses an objection: one might say that in the state of equality, the agent can create tarjīḥ in a subsequent moment. The author refutes this by considering two possibilities: either the agent creates tarjīḥ in that second moment, or he creates it from the first moment for the second moment. The first fails because at the second moment, the situation is again either equality or preponderance, leading back to the original division. The second fails because the first moment cannot produce the second moment, and the action in the second moment is conditional on its presence, which is impossible. Thus, the objection is invalid, and jabr is established.

Commentary
This passage presents the thirteenth argument in a series supporting determinism (Jabr). It is a deductive proof based on God's omniscience of particulars. The argument proceeds in two parts. First: God knows all particular events. Whatever He knows will happen must happen (wajib al-wuqu‘), and whatever He knows will not happen is impossible (mumtani‘). This is because knowledge of occurrence directly contradicts non-occurrence, and combining contradictories is impossible. Since knowledge of occurrence is actual, non-occurrence is impossible. The impossible cannot fall under human power; thus, human acts are necessitated by God's knowledge. Second: The same argument is restated as a dilemma: either God knows particulars or not. If He does, determinism follows, negating moral obligation and prophethood (as will be detailed later). If He does not, then the invalidity of obligation and prophethood is even more certain, because God would be ignorant. Thus both horns lead to determinism or the collapse of obligation. The author follows a structured kalam deduction: from a premise (divine omniscience) to necessity of outcome, to conclusion of determinism. He also provides an alternative formulation for clarity.

A necessary attribute cannot be non-specific—it must be one concrete thing.

This lesson examines a key argument from Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's fifteen proofs for determinism (jabr). Razi uses a logical dilemma: when an attribute is necessarily present, it must either be specifically one thing or non-specific. He then shows that the non-specific option is self-contradictory, forcing the conclusion that determinism is true. This argument relies on the principle that what is not determined in itself cannot exist, and thus cannot characterize anything else.

First Objection: Determinism and Deceptive Miracles

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the doctrine of determinism (jabr) is used to challenge the probative value of miracles, and the logical structure of the objection.

Core Idea
If God creates all actions, He could also create deceptive miracles.

This lesson presents the first of several objections to the standard argument from miracles for prophethood. The objection attacks the reliability of miracles by starting from a determinist premise: if God is the sole creator of all human actions, then He also creates lies and ignorance. The objection then argues that it is even more plausible for God to produce speech that misleads people, thereby undermining the trustworthiness of miracles as signs of truth.

The Objection of Contradiction in the Quran on Jabr and Qadar
An objection claiming Quranic contradictions on predestination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure of the objection that the Quran contains irreconcilable contradictions on predestination, and why this objection fails.

Core Idea
The claim of contradiction in the Quran on jabr and qadar is itself based on a misunderstanding.

This passage presents a classic objection from critics who argue that the Quran's verses on divine compulsion (jabr) and human predestination (qadar) are contradictory. The objection claims that the sheer number of such verses and their apparent inconsistency prove that the prophet was confused. Understanding this objection is essential for preparing the theological response.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the translation. Let the objection's own reasoning come through.
Notice the hyperbolic comparison to sand and pebbles.
2
Identify the three claims
The passage makes three claims: (a) the Quran is filled with jabr and qadar verses, (b) these verses are contradictory, (c) this shows the author was confused and indecisive.
These form a logical argument.
3
Understand the rhetorical force
Consider why the critic uses hyperbole (more than sand and pebbles) and the term 'taʿassuf' (forced interpretation) to undermine the Quran's coherence.
This is a polemical strategy.
4
Reflect on the underlying assumption
Ask yourself what the critic assumes about revelation and consistency. Does the objection allow for nuanced theological reconciliation?
This connects to the broader Sunni response.

Commentary
This passage presents the second objection (al-wajh al-thani) against the Quran's divine origin. It claims that the Quran is filled with verses about compulsion (jabr) and predestination (qadar) — so many that they exceed the number of sand and pebbles. These verses are said to be contradictory (mutanaqida), and reconciling them requires forced interpretation (taʿassuf). From this, the author infers that the prophet (the 'author' of the Quran) was confused about the issue of predestination, unable to take a decisive stance. This is a classic objection from those who deny prophethood, arguing that divine revelation would not contain such unresolved contradictions.
