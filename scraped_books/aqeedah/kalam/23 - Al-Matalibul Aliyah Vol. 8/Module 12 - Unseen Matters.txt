The Crucifixion Confusion
Response to Christian-Jewish transmission of crucifixion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the mutakallimun's response to the claim that Jesus was killed and crucified, namely that Allah cast his likeness upon another person, causing confusion.

Core Idea
The crucifixion was a case of mistaken identity by divine design.

One of the objections to mass transmission (tawatur) is that historical reports about Jesus’ crucifixion are widely accepted. The mutakallimun respond by explaining that Allah caused a visual confusion: Jesus’ likeness was cast onto another person. This lesson examines that response and its basis in scripture and reason.

Guided Path
1
Read the Passage
Read the Arabic and its translation carefully. Notice that the answer begins with 'وأجابوا' indicating a collective response of the mutakallimun.
2
Identify the Claim and Response
The claim: the Jews and Christians transmitted that Jesus was killed and crucified. The response: Allah cast the likeness of Jesus onto another person, creating confusion.
This answer relies on divine intervention rather than rejecting the transmission outright.
3
Reflect on the Rationale
Why would this answer be given? Consider that the mutakallimun want to preserve the integrity of tawatur while also maintaining the impossibility of killing a prophet. This answer harmonizes both.
The term 'شبهة' (ambiguity/confusion) is key: the event was real but the identity was confused.

Miracles Require True Prophethood
Response to Zoroastrian and Manichaean miracle claims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the mutakallimun's response to claims of Zoroaster's miracles: that prophethood depends on correct knowledge of God, and since Zoroaster and Mani affirmed two creators, they are disbelievers, and miracles cannot be performed by disbelievers.

Core Idea
A miracle cannot come from someone who denies the oneness of God.

The Zoroastrians and Manichaeans claimed that Zoroaster and Mani performed miracles. If accepted, this would challenge the link between prophethood and miracles. The mutakallimun respond by pointing out that Zoroaster and Mani affirmed two gods (dualism), which is the worst form of disbelief. Since a disbeliever cannot perform genuine miracles, the claims are rejected. This lesson examines that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the Passage
Read the Arabic and translation. Note the structure: first the claim, then the response, then the justification.
2
Understand the Dependency
The response states that prophethood (nubuwwa) depends on knowledge of God (ma'rifat al-ilah). This means true prophethood requires correct belief about God's nature.
The term 'متفرعة' (dependent/branched) indicates that prophethood is a branch of knowing God.
3
Identify the Consequence
Since Zoroaster and Mani affirmed two gods, they are disbelievers. The passage states that a disbeliever is prevented from performing miracles (يمتنع ظهور المعجزات). This is a key kalam principle.
The word 'يمتنع' implies rational impossibility, not just prohibition.
4
Reflect on the Rationale
Why would miracles not appear through a disbeliever? Because miracles confirm prophethood, and prophethood requires true knowledge of God. A dualist does not have that, so any extraordinary act attributed to them would not be a true miracle.
This answer safeguards the theological integrity of miracles as confirmatory signs.

Are Great Events Always Famous?
Objector refutes necessity of fame for great events with three proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the claim that great events must be widely known is not necessarily true, and how the objector uses examples from Islamic miracles and history to refute it.

Core Idea
Great events are not automatically famous; transmission depends on circumstances.

The objector challenges a key premise in the argument for the prophethood of Muhammad (peace be upon him). He asserts that even if great events occur, they do not necessarily become widely known. This lesson examines his three proofs, including well-known miracles and historical examples.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic source passage and its English translation carefully. Notice the structure: first the objection, then the counter-argument, then three pieces of evidence.
The passage is an objection-and-answer unit.
2
Identify the claim being refuted
The objector refutes the claim that 'great events must be famous.' Identify this claim in the passage and understand why it is being criticized.
The claim is used to argue that some events would have been widely known if they occurred.
3
Examine the three examples
The objector provides three examples: (1) miracles of Prophet Muhammad, (2) differences in prayer rituals, (3) events of past nations. For each, note how they show that great events are not necessarily transmitted widely.
Consider the specific miracles mentioned: water flowing from fingers, splitting the moon, etc.
4
Understand the conclusion
The objector concludes that whether a great event becomes famous or not is not certain; both outcomes are possible. This weakens the argument that great events must be famous.
The conclusion is that 'it is not permissible to be certain of either judgment definitively.'
5
Reflect on implications for tawatur
This discussion relates to the concept of tawatur (mass transmission). The objection shows that absence of widespread transmission does not prove an event did not occur.
Think about how this argument is used in debates about miracles.

The Objector's Reductio: How Casting Likeness Leads to Sophistry
Objector uses reductio to refute casting of likeness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objector's logical argument that allowing the casting of likeness undermines tawatur and leads to universal doubt.

Core Idea
If likeness can be cast, all identity becomes doubtful.

In this passage, the objector fires back at the earlier answer about Jesus. He argues that if Allah can cast a likeness on someone, then the same could happen to any prophet—even Muhammad—and in fact to anyone. This would destroy the reliability of tawatur, since we could never be sure who anyone really was. The result is sophistry, where nothing can be known for certain.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the logical structure: the opponent's claim, the objection, the reasoning, and the conclusion.
The objector uses an reductio ad absurdum—showing the opponent's view leads to absurdity.
2
Identify the key terms
Focus on 'إلقاء الشبه' (casting of likeness), 'التواتر' (tawatur), and 'السفسطة' (sophistry). These are central to the argument.
Mark how each term functions in the objection.
3
Trace the logical chain
Map out the argument step by step: (1) The opponent claims Allah cast Jesus's likeness. (2) Objector says this is a defect in tawatur. (3) Because if allowed, we could doubt Muhammad's identity too. (4) This extends to all people. (5) Result: sophistry.
The chain is a reductio—assuming the opponent's view leads to a self-defeating conclusion.
4
Reflect on the implications
Consider why this is a powerful critique. If tawatur is destroyed, then all transmitted knowledge (including the opponent's own claims) becomes unreliable.
This is a common form of argument in kalam: showing that an opponent's position leads to an unacceptable consequence.

The Objection from Zoroaster's Miracles
The objector argues that Zoroaster's tawatur was proven false, showing tawatur's fallibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objector uses the case of Zoroaster's widely transmitted miracles to argue that tawatur does not yield certainty.

Core Idea
A 1200-year tawatur can be false—so tawatur alone is not enough for certainty.

The final objection in this section turns the mutakallimun's own argument against them. They had said Zoroaster's disbelief prevents miracles. The objector retorts that this very concession proves that a mutawatir report—transmitted by an entire nation for centuries—can be false. This challenges the reliability of tawatur as a source of certain knowledge.

1
Read the objection carefully

2

See how the objector uses the mutakallimun's own premise (Zoroaster was a disbeliever, so miracles are impossible) to derive a conclusion: the tawatur reports about Zoroaster's miracles were false, so tawatur can be false.
This is a classic argument from inconsistency.
3
Reflect on the implication
Ask yourself: If a tawatur that lasted 1200 years and was accepted by an entire nation could be proven false by theological reasoning, can we rely on tawatur alone for certainty?
This connects to the larger debate about the value of tawatur in establishing religious truths.
