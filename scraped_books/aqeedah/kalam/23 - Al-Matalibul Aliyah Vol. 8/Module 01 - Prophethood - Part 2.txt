The Eighth Possibility: Infinite Regress of Preparedness
Al-Razi presents the eighth possibility: infinite regress of preparedness in bodies nullifies miracle's evidentiary value.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's argument that if a miracle depends on a special preparedness in the body, an infinite regress follows, making the miracle a necessary consequence of past states and thus not a proof of truthfulness.

Core Idea
If bodies need infinite preparedness, miracles become necessary, not evidentiary.

Al-Razi's eighth possibility delves into the nature of material bodies. All sublunar bodies share a common matter (hyle) that is receptive to forms. How does a particular body acquire a specific form? If it requires a special preparedness, that preparedness itself needs another, leading to an infinite regress. This lesson traces the argument and its conclusion that miracles lose their indicative power.

Guided Path
1
Read the eighth possibility passage
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. This is a longer argument with multiple steps: from shared hyle to specification, to priority or no priority, to infinite regress.
Note the key terms: هيولى (hyle), استعداد (preparedness), مرجح (preponderator).
2
Understand the initial division
Al-Razi starts: a body's particular form is either because it was more worthy (priority) or not. The latter leads to preponderance without preponderator, which is impossible. So priority must be true.
This is a classical kalam argument about sufficient reason.
3
Trace the infinite regress
If priority is true, the body's preparedness itself requires a prior preparedness, ad infinitum. This regress is analyzed in two ways: simultaneous (impossible) or successive (makes miracle a necessary consequent of past states). Either way, the miracle loses its evidentiary value.
4
Consider the conclusion
The final summary: if body and time are equal in receptivity, a miracle occurrence would be a preponderance without preponderator, absurd. Or if it depends on preparedness, it becomes part of an infinite chain, no longer a special sign.

Commentary
This passage presents two possibilities (al-ih'timal al-sabi' and al-ih'timal al-thamin) that challenge the standard theological argument that miracles prove prophethood. The first possibility invokes the philosophers' doctrine of separate intellects and souls as possible agents of miracles, arguing that if these exist, they could be the true causes rather than divine intervention through a prophet. Al-Razi distinguishes this from the question of angels, which theologians accept as different entities. The second possibility is more detailed: it begins from the fact that all sublunar bodies share a common hyle (matter), which is receptive to all forms and accidents. The specification of each body with its particular form and quality is not due to any priority of that body, otherwise infinite regress would follow. Al-Razi argues that if the specification is not due to priority, then the body is equal to others, and thus the miracle would be a preponderance without preponderator, which is absurd. Alternatively, if the occurrence depends on a special preparedness in the body, then that preparedness requires another preparedness ad infinitum. If all these preparednesses happen at once, infinite causes and effects occur at once, impossible. If they occur in succession, the miracle becomes a necessary consequence of an infinite series of past states, hence no longer a miracle that indicates truthfulness. The conclusion is that if miracles could be explained by natural or celestial predispositions, they lose their evidentiary value.

Al-Razi's Systematic Classification of Miracle Agents
Al-Razi provides a systematic classification of miracle agents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi systematically classifies all possible agents of miracles into a dichotomous tree, covering natural, celestial, angelic, spiritual, and demonic explanations.

Core Idea
Al-Razi maps every possible non-divine source of miracles into a clear taxonomy.

Al-Razi, in his characteristic analytical style, does not merely list alternatives but organizes them into a logical tree. This classification is crucial for understanding the philosophical challenge to prophethood: if miracles can be explained through natural or celestial causes, their evidential value is undermined. This passage lays out the full range of alternatives that a defender of prophethood must address.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the translation. Let al-Razi's taxonomic structure emerge naturally.
Notice the binary divisions: prophet vs. other, corporeal vs. incorporeal, etc.
2
Identify the first division
The top-level division is between the prophet himself and some other agent. Al-Razi then subdivides each branch. Start by noting this primary split.
3
Trace the corporeal branch
Follow the 'other' branch into corporeal substances (elemental, celestial, mixed, and angelic bodies). List each subcategory and its example.
Angels are described as luminous celestial bodies capable of heavy tasks.
4
Trace the incorporeal branch
Follow the 'other' branch into incorporeal substances (lower spirits/devils and upper spirits/intellects and souls). Note how al-Razi covers all possibilities.
This branch shows that philosophical explanations include both demonic and angelic spiritual agencies.

Commentary
This passage is a systematic classification (taqsīm) of all possible agents of miracles. Al-Razi organizes the possibilities into a dichotomous tree. The first division is whether the agent is the prophet himself or something else. If the prophet, his power may be due to his specific bodily temperament (mizāj) or his specific soul (nafs). If the agent is not the prophet, it is either a corporeal substance (jawhar jismānī) or an incorporeal substance (jawhar mujarrad). Corporeal agents are further divided into elemental bodies (e.g., a specific medicine), celestial bodies (e.g., planetary conjunctions and aspects), a mixture of both, or another kind of body (angels, described as luminous celestial bodies capable of heavy tasks). Incorporeal agents are divided into lower spirits (devils) and upper spirits (intellects and souls). This classification shows the breadth of alternatives that philosophers and others have proposed to explain miracles without divine intervention, thereby challenging the standard proof of prophethood. Al-Razi ends with 'And God knows best,' indicating humility before the full scope of possibilities.

The Miracle as Contingent and Its Indication of Truthfulness
The author defines the miracle and gives two arguments that it ceases to be proof if necessary from past states.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why some philosophers argue that miracles cannot be definitive proofs of truthfulness, and how the author presents two arguments to that effect.

Core Idea
Miracles lose their evidential power if they are necessary consequences of past states.

This passage challenges the common assumption that miracles are always clear proofs of divine intervention. The author argues that if a miracle is a necessary outcome of a body's past states, then it cannot serve as a sign of truthfulness. Read on to see two distinct arguments that support this claim.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of miracle
Start by reading the passage's definition of a miracle as an extraordinary act that is a necessary consequence of past states.
2
Grasp the first argument: preference without preponderator
Consider the hypothetical: if the body and time are equivalent to others, then the miracle's occurrence would be a preference without a preponderator, which invalidates its purpose.
This argument shows that without a specific need, the miracle could be arbitrary.
3
Analyze the second argument: the dichotomy
Examine the either-or scenario: either the miracle depends on a specific preparedness (making it necessary) or it does not (making it arbitrary). Either way, it fails as a proof.
This reinforces the conclusion that miracles are not reliable signs.

Commentary

The passage thus undermines the certainty of miracles as proofs by showing that under certain assumptions they could be arbitrary or necessary.

Agents of Miracles: A Systematic Enumeration
The author classifies possible doers of miracles into prophets and others, with subcategories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational classification of possible agents of miracles according to Islamic theological reasoning, distinguishing between prophetic and non-prophetic sources and their subcategories.

Core Idea
Miracles do not accidentally prove prophethood — their agent must be identified first.

This passage from al-Matalib al-Aliyah provides a systematic method for enumerating all possible agents of miracles. Before evaluating a claim of prophethood, one must consider whether the extraordinary act is performed by the prophet himself or by another entity, and then further categorize the nature of that entity. This classification is essential for analyzing the validity of prophethood claims by eliminating alternative explanations.

Third and Fourth Possibilities: Coincidence with Another Miracle or Divine Test
The author presents the third and fourth skeptical possibilities concerning miracles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third and fourth possibilities that undermine a miracle's evidentiary value: coincidence with another miracle/karama, and the possibility that God creates the miracle as a test to strengthen doubt.

Core Idea
A miracle might be coincidental or a divine test, not a confirmation.

The author continues with two more powerful skeptical challenges. The third possibility suggests that the alleged miracle might actually be a miracle for another prophet or a saint that coincidentally aligns with the false claimant's speech. The fourth possibility introduces a theological twist: God might intentionally create the miracle after a false claim to test believers, making it a strong doubt that they must resist.

Commentary

Learning Objective

A king avoids harming his kingdom; God is unaffected by benefit or harm.

3
Conclude the analogy's failure
The difference in nature (self-interest vs. self-sufficiency) makes the analogy invalid.

Commentary
This passage presents three differences (furūq) between the case of a worldly king and the case of God, in order to refute an objection that if a king's actions could be ambiguous, then God's miraculous actions could also be ambiguous. The first difference: the king's hidden wisdom and deep understanding means that his actions are not straightforward indicators of truth or falsehood, because he may have hidden reasons. The author then concludes that mentioning these differences is actually unnecessary because the method of qiyās al-ghā'ib 'alā al-shāhid (analogical reasoning from the visible to the invisible) has already been shown in logic to be weak and not yielding certainty. Thus the whole discussion is a cautionary note; the real refutation is based on the invalidity of such analogy.

Refuting the Third Objection: Miracles and the Condition of No Opposition
Author refutes the third objection by analyzing the condition of no opposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the condition of no opposition cannot serve as a valid condition for a miracle's indication of truth, and how the author refutes both possible formulations of this condition.

Core Idea
The condition of no opposition is either insufficient or unknowable — making it an invalid basis for miracles.

The third objection against miracles claims that if a miracle indicates truth, it must be because no one can oppose it. But this condition itself is problematic: does 'no opposition' mean at the moment, forever, or something in between? The author examines each possibility and shows that none can work. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ignorance cannot be self-caused; it must originate from an external source, namely God.

The deniers present a clever argument to show that God is the creator of ignorance. They start with the undeniable existence of ignorance in people's hearts and then ask who causes it. By eliminating the possibility that the servant causes his own ignorance, they force the conclusion that God is the cause. This argument also serves to support the idea that God can create miracles, since creating ignorance is an even greater departure from truth.

This passage presents a second philosophical argument for why angels are better suited to convey divine messages than humans. It builds on the idea that perfection requires freedom from the defects inherent in human nature, such as lust and anger. The argument uses the impossibility of infinite regress to show that the perfecter must be free from deficiency, and only angels meet this condition.
