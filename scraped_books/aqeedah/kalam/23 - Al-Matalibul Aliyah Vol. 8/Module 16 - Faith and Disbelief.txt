The Two Types of Listeners to Prophetic Reminder
Commentator explains why not all benefit and describes two listener types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why prophetic reminders can harm some people and distinguish the two categories of listeners described by the commentator.

Core Idea
Prophetic reminders heal or harm depending on the soul's state.

This lesson examines the commentator's analysis of why not everyone benefits from prophetic reminders. The rational soul varies, leading to different responses: some gain, some remain unaffected, and some are even harmed. The passage then describes the two main groups in detail, linking their condition to their inner disposition.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage about diverse rational souls
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how the commentator begins by stating that not all listeners benefit, and then explains the diversity of rational souls.
Pay attention to the statement that some are harmed because hearing stirs envy, rage, and anger.
2
Identify the two groups and their characteristics
The commentator describes one group that benefits, characterized by fear and awe, and another that does not benefit, described as the most wretched. Note the specific descriptions for each.
3
Understand why the reminder can cause harm
The passage explains that for some, hearing the reminder triggers negative emotions like envy and anger, leading to harm. Reflect on how this shows that the same message can have opposite effects based on the listener's inner state.
This is a key insight: the reminder itself is not harmful, but the soul's reaction can be.

That not everyone who hears that reminder benefits from it, for the rational souls are diverse: some benefit from that reminder, some do not benefit from it, and some are harmed by hearing that reminder, because hearing it stirs in their heart the motives of envy, rage, anger, and persistence in ignorance. Then when the Exalted pointed out that the listener to that reminder may benefit from it or may not benefit from it, He followed it with a statement of the specific quality of each of these two categories. He made clear that the characteristic of one who benefits from this reminder is that fear predominates in his heart and awe overwhelms his soul. Because of that fear he seeks provision for the final abode, so inevitably he benefits from the guidance of this truthful one. As for the one who does not benefit from this reminder, and keeps away from it, and avoids being near it, it is the soul described as being the most wretched of souls; it remains in the distress of this world, and after death falls into the fires of regret and loneliness. And when he made this clear, he explained its description, saying: 'Then he will neither die therein nor live.' He only said 'then he will neither die therein' because it is established that the soul does not die with the death of the body, and he only said 'nor live' because although it remains alive, it remains in punishment. Death is better than this life, so he said: 'Then he will neither die therein nor live.' And when he made clear the one who does not benefit from that reminder, he made clear the perfect state of the one who benefits from it, saying: 'Indeed he who purifies himself has succeeded.' That is because the purpose of the teaching of the prophets, their reminding, and their guidance is two things: one is the removal of blameworthy bodily traits from the soul, and the second is the acquisition of praiseworthy spiritual attributes in the soul. Since removal of what is not fitting is a prerequisite for acquiring what is fitting, he inevitably began with His statement: 'Indeed he who purifies himself has succeeded,' and the intended meaning is purification of the soul and cleansing it from blameworthy attributes. And when he mentioned that, he followed it with the acquisition of what is fitting, and that is either in the theoretical power or the practical power. The chief of theoretical knowledge is remembrance of Allah and knowledge of Him, and the chief of virtuous deeds is service of Allah. Therefore he said: 'And mentions the name of his Lord,' which is an indication of the human's felicity in perfecting his theoretical power through the guidance of the prophets. And His statement: 'and prays' is an indication of his felicity in perfecting his practical power through their guidance and direction.

Commentary
This passage is an exegetical analysis explaining why not all listeners benefit from prophetic reminders. The commentator begins by stating that rational souls vary: some benefit, some do not, and some are harmed because the reminder stirs negative emotions like envy and anger. He then contrasts the two groups: the one who benefits has fear and awe predominating, leading him to seek provision for the afterlife; the one who does not benefit is described as the most wretched soul, suffering in this world and facing torment after death. The commentator explains the verse 'then he will neither die therein nor live' by noting that the soul does not die with the body, but this existence is worse than death. He then discusses the successful one who purifies himself, noting two purposes of prophetic teaching: removal of blameworthy traits and acquisition of praiseworthy ones. Purification comes first as a prerequisite. The commentator links 'mentioning the name of his Lord' to perfecting the theoretical power (knowledge of God) and 'prayer' to perfecting the practical power (service of God).

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some may think the reminder always benefits; the passage shows it can harm if the soul is predisposed to vice.
Correction: The reminder itself is beneficial, but its effect depends on the listener's inner state. For those with vicious souls, hearing can trigger negative emotions and harm.

Key Takeaway
Prophetic reminders can harm those with vicious souls, while those with fear and awe benefit and seek the afterlife.

?

?
The passage says that the harmful listener 'keeps away from the reminder and avoids being near it.' Are there any reminders you have been avoiding because they make you uncomfortable? What does that avoidance say about your spiritual health? Commit to facing one such reminder this week.
?
Death is better than the life of the wretched soul in punishment. Do you truly live in a state of fear and awe seeking provision for the afterlife, or are you living as if this world is all there is? What concrete step will you take to change that today?

The Purposes of Prophetic Teaching: Purification and the Two Powers
Commentator explains purposes of prophetic teaching: purification and two powers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two main purposes of prophetic teaching: removing blameworthy traits and acquiring praiseworthy ones, and how they relate to the theoretical and practical powers of the soul.

Core Idea
Prophetic guidance purifies the soul and perfects both knowledge and action.

In the second part of his commentary, the author explains the deeper objectives behind prophetic reminders. The ultimate goal is twofold: purification from vices and adornment with virtues. This purification is a prerequisite, after which the soul can perfect its theoretical power through knowledge of Allah and its practical power through worship.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the purposes of prophetic teaching
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Focus on the commentator's explanation of why purification is mentioned first and how he links it to the two powers.
2
Understand the priority of purification
The commentator states that removal of blameworthy traits is a prerequisite for acquiring praiseworthy ones. This is why the Quranic verse 'Indeed he who purifies himself has succeeded' comes first.
Think of purification as clearing the ground before planting good qualities.
3
Distinguish the theoretical and practical powers
The commentator divides the acquisition of virtues into two categories: theoretical (knowledge of Allah) and practical (service of Allah). Note how he links 'mentioning the name of his Lord' to the theoretical power and 'prayer' to the practical power.
These two powers correspond to belief and action.

The Seven Ranks of Faith: A Conclusion to Prophethood
Concludes prophethood discussion with seven ranks of faith and traveler analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the seven ranks of faith in the Quran encompass belief in all prophets and the Hereafter, and how this completes the discussion on prophethood.

Core Idea
The seven ranks of faith cover past, present, and future—belief in all prophets and the Last Day.

This passage wraps up the discussion on prophethood by revisiting the seven ranks of faith mentioned in the Quran. The author shows how these ranks encompass faith in the present messenger, previous prophets, and the Hereafter, and then draws a beautiful analogy of the believer as a traveler guided by Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to grasp the overall meaning. Pay attention to the key phrases like 'to you' and 'what was sent down before you' and 'of the Hereafter they are certain'.
The passage is concluding a larger discussion.
2
Identify the three explicit ranks
The author explicitly mentions the first rank (faith in the present messenger), the collective rank of previous prophets, and the seventh rank (certainty in the Hereafter). Notice how these cover past, present, and future.
The other four ranks are assumed from the Quranic verse but not listed here.
3
Reflect on the traveler analogy
The author compares the believer to a traveler: guidance is knowing the path, success is reaching the destination (Paradise). Think about how this connects to the seven ranks.
This analogy reinforces the importance of faith in all seven ranks.

Commentary
This passage concludes a discussion on the seven ranks of faith as derived from a Quranic passage (likely Surah Al-Baqarah 2:4-5). The author explains that the phrase 'to you' refers to the obligation of faith in the present messenger (Muhammad), and 'what was sent down before you' refers to faith in earlier prophets. The seventh rank, 'of the Hereafter they are certain', refers to faith in resurrection and the Day of Judgment. These seven ranks cover the past (previous revelations), present (the current messenger), and future (the Hereafter). The author then notes that fulfilling these ranks completes the objectives and interests. The verse 'Those are upon guidance from their Lord, and those are the successful' is then cited to show that the one who possesses these ranks is guided and successful. The author uses the analogy of a traveler: as long as one is in the world, he is on a journey; the best state is to have guidance to the path; upon death, the traveler reaches his destination; success is to achieve the good (al-khayrat). The conclusion is that this path of calling (da'wa) is the best path. The author refrains from further elaboration on the secrets of the Sharia, stating that the discussion would be too lengthy, and ends with 'Allah knows best.'
