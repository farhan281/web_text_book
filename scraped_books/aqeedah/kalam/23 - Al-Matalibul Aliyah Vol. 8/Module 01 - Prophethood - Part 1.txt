The Philosophers' Denial of Prophethood
First group: philosophers denying free will and particular knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the first group of deniers of prophethood—the philosophers—reject prophethood by denying God's free agency and knowledge of particulars.

Core Idea
Denial of prophethood can stem from denying God's free will and particular knowledge.

The author begins his classification by identifying the first group of deniers, who are the philosophers. They hold that God is necessary by essence, not a free agent, and that He does not know particulars. From this, they conclude that prophethood is impossible. Let's examine their reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage about the first group
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the translation to get the meaning.
Notice the phrase 'طريقة الفلاسفة' – the method of the philosophers.
2
Identify the two denied attributes
The group denies that God is a free agent (فاعل مختار) and that He knows particulars (عالم بالجزئيات).
These two denials are key to their rejection of prophethood.
3
Understand the consequence
Because of these denials, the door to proving prophethood is closed. Ask yourself why prophethood would require free agency and particular knowledge.
Prophethood involves God choosing to send messengers and knowing specific human conditions.
4
Recognize the source
The author identifies this view as the method of the philosophers. This helps situate the argument in a historical debate.

Denial of Prophethood Through Denial of Obligation
Second group: those who affirm free agency but deny obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the second group of deniers concedes God's free agency but denies obligation, thus concluding that prophethood is invalid.

Core Idea
Denying divine obligation makes prophethood unnecessary.

The second group takes a different approach: they affirm that God is a free agent, but they deny that He issues commands, prohibitions, and obligations. Since prophets came to establish obligations, denying obligation undermines the entire purpose of prophethood, making it invalid. Let's examine their argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage for the second group
Read the Arabic passage and translation. Note that they concede free agency but deny taklīf.
Taklīf means religious obligation.
2
Trace the logical argument
They say: Since there is no obligation, then prophethood is invalid because prophets only came to convey obligations.
The argument uses a root-branch analogy.
3
Understand the root-branch analogy
Obligation is the root (أصل), prophethood is the branch (فرع). If the root is invalid, the branch is even more invalid.
This is a common logical structure in Islamic theology.
4
Reflect on the implication
This shows that accepting God as a free agent is not enough; one must also accept that God imposes obligations.

Core Idea
Denying God's free choice and knowledge of particulars shuts the door to prophethood.

This passage introduces the first sect that denies prophethood: the philosophers. They claim God's actions are necessary, not free, and that He does not know particular events. The author shows how these two denials logically prevent any proof of prophethood.

The Second Denying Sect: Denying Divine Commands and Obligations
Classification of the second group: they admit God as free agent but deny commands, prohibitions, and obligations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument of those who accept God's free agency but deny divine obligations, and why they conclude that prophethood is invalid.

Core Idea
Rejecting divine obligation makes prophethood impossible.

In the previous lesson, we met a group that denied God's free agency. Now we encounter a second group that concedes God's free agency but denies that He issues commands, prohibitions, or imposes obligations. They argue that since prophethood is founded on delivering obligations, denying obligation necessarily entails denying prophethood. Let's examine their reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation. Get a feel for the argument's flow.
Notice the logical structure: concession → denial → implication → conclusion.

Identify what they accept and deny
The passage clearly states they accept God as a free agent (فاعل مختار) but deny commands (الأمر), prohibitions (النهي), and obligations (التكليف). Mark these two parts.
This distinguishes them from the first sect who denied free agency.
3
Trace the logical argument
They argue: prophethood is only for establishing obligations; obligation is invalid; therefore prophethood is invalid. The author uses a 'root and branch' analogy: obligation is the root, prophethood is the branch.
Understand why they see prophethood as dependent on obligation.
4
Reflect on the theological implication
Ask yourself: why does denying obligation lead to denying prophethood? What does this imply about the necessity of divine commands for prophethood?
This argument is a reductio ad absurdum against those who accept God's will but reject His law.

The Third Denying Sect: Rationalists Who Deem Prophets Unnecessary
Classification of the third group: they accept commands but claim reason suffices, making prophets redundant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rationalist position that claims intellect alone is sufficient for knowing divine obligations, rendering prophethood redundant.

Core Idea
Reason alone cannot replace prophecy because it cannot fully know divine commands.

This lesson examines the third group of deniers of prophethood—those who accept God's commands but argue that human intellect alone can discern them, thus rendering prophets unnecessary. We'll explore this claim and its implications for the necessity of divine revelation.

Guided Path

Read the passage carefully

The group concedes that God commands and prohibits. Their claim is that intellect alone suffices. This is the core of their rationalist position.

Reflect on the consequence
The author concludes that if intellect suffices, then sending prophets has no benefit. Ask yourself: Is this conclusion valid?
This anticipates Islamic critiques of pure rationalism.
4
Consider the implicit refutation

Think about the limitations of intellect mentioned elsewhere in Islamic theology.

Fourth Group's Attack on Miracles
Fourth group denies miracle-based proof of prophethood and lists five sub-arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth group's argument that miracles fail to prove prophethood, along with their five sub-arguments attacking the probative value of miracles.

Core Idea
Miracles are rejected not because they are impossible, but because they fail to guarantee truthfulness.

This passage presents the fourth major group of deniers of prophethood. Unlike previous groups, they accept that knowledge of prophethood comes only through miracles—but then argue that miracles have no evidentiary value. The author systematically dismantles the miracle proof by listing five sub-arguments, each attacking a different assumption. This lesson examines their core claim and the five sub-arguments, showing how they challenge the standard proof from miracles.

Guided Path
1
Read the core claim
Read the first part of the Arabic passage where the fourth group states their position: miracles have no indication of truthfulness, so prophethood is invalid.
Notice that they accept miracles as the only means but deny their value.
2
Identify the five sub-arguments
The passage lists five sub-sects (وجوه) that attack miracles. As you read, note each one's main point: denial of possibility, unknown doer, no proof of truthfulness, jabr argument, and historical transmission issues.
The fifth sub-sect is incomplete in the source.
3
Understand the first three sub-arguments
The first sub-sect denies miracles are possible. The second admits possibility but says we cannot attribute them to God. The third admits divine causation but denies they prove truthfulness.
These build on each other: from impossibility to lack of evidence.
4
Analyze the fourth sub-argument (jabr)
The fourth sub-sect uses the doctrine of divine compulsion to argue that God can affirm a liar, so a miracle doesn't guarantee truthfulness.
This is a theological argument based on predestination.
5
Review the incomplete fifth sub-argument
The fifth sub-sect questions the historical transmission of miracles, saying we only have reports, not direct observation.
Though incomplete, it raises the issue of historical certainty.

Drug/Sorcery Objection to Miracles and Its Fourfold Refutation
Author presents drug/sorcery objection and refutes it with four arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that miracles might be due to drugs or sorcery, and how Islamic theology refutes it with four rational and empirical arguments.

Core Idea
Miracles cannot be reduced to drug effects; rational and empirical refutations prove their divine origin.

One objection to miracles claims that a prophet might have used a special drug to produce wondrous effects, citing strange properties reported by philosophers. The author refutes this by showing that such a drug would lead to absurd consequences, that reports are empirically false, that no replication occurred, and that exclusivity itself is miraculous.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the beginning of the passage where the objection is presented: different medicines have different effects, and philosophers report strange properties like the magnet. The objector claims we must suspend judgment and allow the possibility that a prophet used a drug.
Notice that the objector admits uncertainty about the truth of the reports, but insists that mere possibility invalidates the miracle's evidential value.
2

The first refutation argues that if we allow a drug for turning a staff into a serpent or splitting the sea, we would logically have to allow a drug for creating the heavens and earth, which is absurd and leaves reason.
This is a logical argument that shows the objection leads to an irrational conclusion.
3
Second refutation: empirical falsification
The second refutation points out that most of the claimed properties of such drugs, when tested, turn out false. This shows the reports are lies.
This appeals to empirical evidence against the reliability of the philosophical reports.
4

The third refutation says that if the drug existed, others would know it and could replicate the miracle, but no competition occurred. The fourth refutation states that if the drug's knowledge was exclusive to the prophet, that exclusivity itself is extraordinary and thus a miracle.

Commentary
This passage presents three objections to the idea that miracles could be performed by jinn, and then refutes each. The first objection argues that since prophets curse devils and jinn, it is irrational for devils to assist them. The author dismisses this as weak: the jinn might tolerate the cursing to preserve their deceptions, or the group cursed may be different from those performing the miracles. The second objection claims that if jinn did miracles, Allah would have to support a sinner to avoid confusion; since He does not, the miracle must be from Allah. The author counters that the possibility of jinn involvement means that being certain of divine origin without justification is the fault of the believer, not of Allah — analogous to how ambiguous verses can be sent despite possible misinterpretation. The third objection asserts that jinn lack the power to perform great miracles, otherwise they would harm prophets and scholars. The author refutes this by noting that prophets themselves affirm the great power of jinn, so denying it would accuse prophets of falsehood, which is a major flaw in prophethood. Thus all three objections are shown to be weak.

Core Idea

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth objection and its partial answer. The objectors argue that angels, as established by widespread belief and the Qur'an, possess immense power and are the agents of revelation. Therefore, miracles could be their actions, not exclusively God's. This creates a circular dependence: knowledge of infallibility depends on the validity of transmitted proofs, and the validity of transmitted proofs depends on the truthfulness of the prophets. The passage ends abruptly, but the argument highlights the need for a foundational criterion to break the circle.

The Circularity Argument Regarding Angelic Infallibility
Author argues that angelic infallibility is needed for miracles to prove prophethood, but it can only be known through revelation, creating circularity.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Proving prophethood via miracles requires prior knowledge of angelic infallibility, leading to a vicious circle.

The author states the circle is invalid, setting up the need for an alternative foundation.

Commentary
This passage presents two possibilities (al-ih'timal al-sabi' and al-ih'timal al-thamin) that challenge the standard theological argument that miracles prove prophethood. The first possibility invokes the philosophers' doctrine of separate intellects and souls as possible agents of miracles, arguing that if these exist, they could be the true causes rather than divine intervention through a prophet. Al-Razi distinguishes this from the question of angels, which theologians accept as different entities. The second possibility is more detailed: it begins from the fact that all sublunar bodies share a common hyle (matter), which is receptive to all forms and accidents. The specification of each body with its particular form and quality is not due to any priority of that body, otherwise infinite regress would follow. Al-Razi argues that if the specification is not due to priority, then the body is equal to others, and thus the miracle would be a preponderance without preponderator, which is absurd. Alternatively, if the occurrence depends on a special preparedness in the body, then that preparedness requires another preparedness ad infinitum. If all these preparednesses happen at once, infinite causes and effects occur at once, impossible. If they occur in succession, the miracle becomes a necessary consequence of an infinite series of past states, hence no longer a miracle that indicates truthfulness. The conclusion is that if miracles could be explained by natural or celestial predispositions, they lose their evidentiary value.
