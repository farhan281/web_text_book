Razi distinguishes two paths to establishing prophethood: miracles and rational perfection.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Prophethood can be proven either by miracles or by rational recognition of a prophet's truth-turning impact.

Razi opens his chapter on prophethood with a fundamental distinction. Some rely on miracles to confirm a prophet's truthfulness, while others first determine correct beliefs and actions through reason, then recognize a prophet by the strong effect of his call in turning people to truth. And if that is so, this science is only completed by knowledge of the effective celestial causes and knowledge of the receptive terrestrial causes. So whoever knows these two types and is able to combine them reaches his goal, because when the effective cause connects with the receptive, the effect must appear. And if you understand this, we say: the discussion on it is arranged in two sections: one: on the celestial effective causes, and the second: on the terrestrial passive [causes].
Source pages p. 168

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'talisman' and lays out the epistemological foundation for the science of talismans. The author begins by stating that a talisman is a combination of active celestial causes and passive earthly causes, aimed at producing an extraordinary event or preventing a normal one. He then explains that mastery of this science requires knowing both types of causes: the celestial active causes and the terrestrial receptive causes. When the active cause connects with the receptive, the effect necessarily follows. The passage concludes by dividing the discussion into two main sections: first, explaining the celestial influences, and second, explaining the terrestrial receptive things. This division follows from the definition because the talisman involves both an active celestial component and a passive earthly component.
