The Antidote of Four in Surah al-Asr
Author explains the four elements of Surah al-Asr as a spiritual antidote proving prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author interprets Surah al-Asr to reveal a spiritual antidote comprising four elements that constitute the proof of prophethood.

Core Idea
The four elements of Surah al-Asr form a spiritual antidote that proves prophethood.

In this lesson, we explore the author's unique approach to proving prophethood by analyzing Surah al-Asr. He argues that human beings are in loss due to the overwhelming worldly powers in their bodies, but those who take the 'antidote of the four'—faith, righteous deeds, enjoining truth, and enjoining patience—are saved. This antidote corresponds to the perfection of theoretical and practical faculties, both for oneself and others.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on Surah al-Asr
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice how the author starts by explaining why mankind is in loss.
2
Identify the nineteen powers
The author lists nineteen powers that drag a person to worldly pleasures. Find them in the text and note their grouping.
He mentions external senses, internal senses, appetite, anger, and seven vegetative powers.
3
Understand the intellect's role
The intellect is described as a weak lamp that arises after these powers dominate. Reflect on why this supports the idea of loss.
4
Examine the four components of the antidote
The author extracts from the verse 'except those who believe and do righteous deeds...' four components corresponding to perfecting theoretical and practical faculties for oneself and others. Note how 'patience' is specified.
Patience counteracts appetite and anger which are greatest trials.
5
Connect to prophethood proof
Consider how this analysis serves as a proof of prophethood. The Prophet’s message provides this antidote, thus rescuing humanity from loss.

فظهر الفرق وقد ظهر بهذه الآيات : أن الطريق الذي ذكرناه في إثبات النبوة هو الطريق الأكمل الأفضل [ والله أعلم (٤) ] .
And among the sūrahs suitable for this meaning is Sūrat al-‘Aṣr. He began with His statement: 'Indeed, mankind is in loss' because we have shown that there occur in his body nineteen types of powers, all of which drag him to the world and its pleasures and delights. They are the five external senses, the five internal senses, appetite and anger, and the seven vegetative ones. Their total is nineteen, and they are the zabāniyah (angels of punishment) standing at the door of Hell: the body. As for the intellect, it is a weak lamp. It only arises after those nineteen have taken over the kingdom of the body. If that is the case, then it is apparent that love of the world overpowers souls and spirits. So when the body dies, the soul remains in loss and deprivation. Therefore He said: 'Indeed, mankind is in loss.' Then He excepted from this loss a person who takes the antidote of the four, which is a spiritual antidote composed of four spiritual ingredients. The first of them: perfection of the theoretical faculty, which is His statement: 'except those who believe'; the second: perfection of the practical faculty, which is His statement: 'and do righteous deeds'; the third: striving to perfect the theoretical faculty for others, which is His statement: 'and enjoin truth upon one another'; the fourth: striving to perfect the practical faculty for others, which is His statement: 'and enjoin patience upon one another.' He specified patience because the greatest trial is in the call of appetite to corruption and anger to harm and bloodshed, as He informed about the angels when they said: 'Will You place therein one who causes corruption therein and sheds blood?' So if a person is able to exercise patience by not responding to appetite and anger, he has achieved all good in the practical faculty. And among the verses that indicate the correctness of what we have mentioned: that He, exalted, related about the disbelievers that they demanded from him overwhelming miracles in His statement: 'And they say: We will not believe you until you break open for us from the earth a spring.' Then He, exalted, said to him: 'Say: Exalted is my Lord! Was I ever but a human messenger?' Meaning: the fact that a person is a human described with messengerhood means that he is perfect in his theoretical and practical faculties, and capable of treating those deficient in these two faculties. It is not a condition for obtaining this attribute that he be able to do the things you requested from me. And among the verses indicating the correctness of what we have mentioned also: that when He, exalted, said in Sūrat al-Shu‘arā': 'And indeed it is the revelation of the Lord of the worlds,' an objection was raised against it, namely: Why is it not possible that this is from the revelation of the devils? So He replied to it: 'And the devils have not brought it down.' Then He clarified the answer, saying: 'Shall I inform you upon whom the devils descend? They descend upon every sinful liar.' The meaning: If the call was for seeking the world and seeking pleasures and appetites, then that caller would be a sinful liar, and those who assist him in it are the devils. But I call to God, to turning away from the world, and to focusing on the Hereafter, and this is not with the help of the devils, but with the help of God, exalted. So he used the fact that his call is to God and to the truth as evidence that he is a truthful prophet, not a wicked sorcerer. And when another objection was raised against him, namely: For every poet there is a devil that assists him in his poetry, so why is it not possible that your situation be similar? He replied with His statement: 'And the poets – the erring follow them. Do you not see that they wander in every valley?' The meaning: The poet only calls to greed for the world and to encouragement of bodily pleasures, while I call to God, exalted, and to the abode of the Hereafter. Thus it is impossible that the helper and assistant in this way be the devils. So the difference became clear, and it has become evident through these verses that the way we have mentioned in establishing prophethood is the most complete and best way. And God knows best.

Commentary
This passage is an argument for the proof of prophethood using Quranic verses. The author begins by explaining that Sūrat al-‘Aṣr is particularly suited for this purpose. He notes that God's statement 'Indeed, mankind is in loss' refers to the nineteen types of powers in the human body (external senses, internal senses, appetite, anger, and seven vegetative faculties) that drag a person toward worldly pleasures, leaving the soul in loss after death. The intellect is described as a weak lamp overwhelmed by these powers. However, God excepts those who take the 'antidote of the four'—a spiritual cure composed of four elements corresponding to the perfections of theoretical and practical faculties, both for oneself and for others. These are faith (perfection of theoretical faculty), righteous deeds (perfection of practical faculty), enjoining truth (perfecting theoretical faculty for others), and enjoining patience (perfecting practical faculty for others). Patience is singled out because it counteracts the greatest trials of appetite and anger. The author then supports his method by citing other verses: from Sūrat al-Isrā', where disbelievers demanded miracles, and God responded that the messenger is merely a human with perfect theoretical and practical faculties, not necessarily a performer of miracles; and from Sūrat al-Shu‘arā', where objections about revelation from devils are refuted by pointing out that the call of the Prophet is to God and the Hereafter, whereas devils assist those who call to worldly pleasures. Thus the author concludes that his method—establishing prophethood through the perfection of faculties and the nature of the call—is the most complete and best way.

Supporting Proofs from Surah al-Isra' and al-Shu'ara'
Author supports his method with verses from Surah al-Isra' and al-Shu'ara'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author uses other Quranic verses to support his method of proving prophethood through the nature of the Prophet's call and his perfect faculties.

Core Idea
The Prophet's call to God and the Hereafter, not miracles, is the decisive proof of prophethood.

In this lesson, we continue with the author's argument by examining two additional Quranic passages. From Surah al-Isra', he argues that the Prophet is a human with perfect theoretical and practical faculties, not necessarily a miracle worker. From Surah al-Shu'ara', he refutes the objection that revelation could come from devils by contrasting the Prophet's call to God and the Hereafter with the poets' call to worldly pleasures.

Guided Path

Read the passages from Surah al-Isra' and al-Shu'ara'
Read the Arabic and translation of the second part of the source, which includes these verses.
2
Understand the argument from Surah al-Isra'
The disbelievers demanded miracles, but God's response shows that being a prophet does not require performing such miracles; it requires being perfect in theoretical and practical faculties and teaching others.
3
Analyze the refutation in Surah al-Shu'ara'
The objection that revelation could be from devils is refuted by pointing out that devils assist those who call to worldly pleasures, whereas the Prophet calls to God and the Hereafter.
The response uses the content of the call as evidence.
4
Connect the two arguments
Both arguments support the author's method: the proof of prophethood lies in the perfection of the prophet's faculties and the nature of his call, not in miracles.
5
Appreciate the conclusion
The author concludes that his way is the most complete and best way to establish prophethood.

Commentary
This passage presents the philosophers' method for explaining a specific type of miracle: miracles of foreknowledge (informing of unseen things). The author first distinguishes between two human powers—theoretical and practical—and correlates each with a type of miracle. The theoretical power receives intelligibles from the separate world, and its miracle is prophetic knowledge of the unseen. The practical power enables action in the physical world, and its miracle is extraordinary acts. Then, focusing on the first type (foreknowledge), the passage explains the philosophers' account. They argue that the common sense (al-ḥiss al-mushtarak) can receive images from two sources: (1) external senses bringing images from outside, and (2) the imaginative faculty (al-quwwa al-mutakhayyila) composing images internally. These internal images, when imprinted in the common sense, are perceived just as if they came from outside, analogous to a mirror reflecting any image placed before it. The philosophers then use this to explain visions experienced by the pious, soothsayers, sleepers, and the melancholic: these images are not external but produced by the imagination. They prove this by arguing that if such images were external, they would be visible to everyone with sound senses under normal conditions—but they are not. Therefore, they must originate internally. Finally, they note that normally such images do not constantly appear because of two obstacles: (1) the common sense is occupied with external images, leaving no room for imaginative images; (2) the rational faculty controls the imagination and restrains it from composing images. Read the next part where they support their claim by noting that weak souls, when gathered in large numbers (e.g., prayer for rain), can produce effects. Understand this as an analogy: if weak souls can have some effect, a strong soul can perform wonders.
The analogy is from collective intention to individual psychic power.

Understand the Critique
Read the author's response: while the psychic explanation is possible, so are others (angels, jinn, celestial connections, etc.). Therefore, the philosophers' exclusive attribution is a preference without a preferrer (ترجيح من غير مرجح).
The author does not deny the possibility, only the exclusivity.

Chapter Seven: Narrating the statement of the philosophers concerning the reason why prophets and saints are able to bring miracles and karamat. The summary of their speech in it: We have explained in the book 'The Soul' that the estimative faculty that a human has may be capable of affecting bodies. And we mentioned many aspects in establishing this chapter. And upon this they said: It is not impossible that there exists a human whose soul is perfect in this power, so without doubt he is able to dispose of the matter of this world however he wishes and desires. And what strengthens that is that weak souls when they gather, they may acquire some type of effective power. Such as a great multitude when they gather to direct thought toward a specific thing, and like what is witnessed in the prayer for rain and other things. And if this is sensible, it is not impossible that the strong soul is capable of producing these wonders and marvels. And know that the gist of this speech is that that soul is described by a property, because of which it is able to produce these miracles and karamat. And just as this is possible, so other aspects are possible. Such as attributing these states to angels or jinn, or celestial connections, or the actions of stars which are rational living beings, or intellects and souls. And if all are possible, then their certainty in attributing these miracles only to the psychic power is a preference without a preferrer. And this is the end of the speech in this chapter. And next follows the speech on sorcery, so that the difference between the miracle, sorcery, the prophet, and the sorcerer may be attained. And God knows best.

Commentary
This is a new chapter where the author recounts the philosophers' explanation for miracles and karamat of prophets and saints. He summarizes their view: based on their book on the soul, they argue that the human estimative faculty (قوة وهمية) can affect bodies. They posit that a person with a perfect soul in this power can manipulate the matter of the world. To support this, they note that even weak souls, when gathered in large numbers (e.g., in prayer for rain), can produce an effect. Therefore, a strong soul can produce extraordinary wonders. The author then critiques this position: while this psychic explanation is possible, other explanations are equally possible—such as attributing miracles to angels, jinn, celestial connections, stars (as rational beings), or intellects and souls. Since all are possible, the philosophers' exclusive attribution of miracles to psychic power is an arbitrary preference without grounds. The author closes by noting that the next topic will be sorcery, to distinguish miracle from sorcery and prophet from sorcerer.

Misconception & Correction

Correction: The author only calls it arbitrary, not false; he acknowledges it as one possible explanation among many.

?

The philosophers' error was not in their explanation being false, but in claiming it exclusively. Where in your daily spiritual life do you treat one permissible practice or belief as the only correct way? Are you willing to accept that other paths may be equally valid in God's sight? What would change if you did?
