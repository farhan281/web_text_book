Human Perfection: Theoretical and Practical Powers
Razi defines human perfection as two powers: theoretical and practical.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's definition of human perfection as the integration of both theoretical and practical powers, and why both are necessary for true happiness.

Core Idea
Human perfection requires both perfect knowledge and righteous action.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi lays out the foundational principle that human perfection is not merely theoretical but also practical. He defines two distinct powers that must be fully developed: the theoretical power to perceive realities without error, and the practical power to perform righteous deeds that turn the soul toward the afterlife. This preliminary is presented as universally accepted by prophets and philosophers.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage in Arabic and English
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the translation to get a clear sense of Razi's definition.
Notice the strong claim of consensus at the end.
2
Identify the two powers
The passage presents two components of perfection: theoretical power (قوة نظرية) and practical power (قوة عملية). Find how each is described.
3
Understand righteous deeds
Razi defines righteous deeds as those that cause aversion to bodily pleasures and desire for the afterlife. This sets the direction for human perfection.
This definition is key to understanding the practical power.
4
Reflect on the consensus claim
Razi states that prophets and divine philosophers agree on this preliminary. Ask yourself: why is this point considered universally accepted?
This reinforces the importance of the definition.

For the sake of acting by it, and what is intended from it: that his perfect state is restricted to two matters: one of them: that his theoretical power becomes complete such that images and realities of things are manifested in it, a complete, perfect manifestation, free from error and slip. And the second: that his practical power becomes complete such that its possessor acquires a faculty by which he is able to perform righteous deeds. And what is meant by righteous deeds: the states that cause aversion to bodily pleasures and cause desire for the world of the hereafter and for spiritualities. So it has become clear by this: that there is no happiness for man except by reaching these two states. And this preliminary is a preliminary upon which the prophets unanimously agreed on its correctness, and the divine philosophers also agreed on its correctness, and there is no complete rational person in the world except he supports it.

Commentary
This passage defines the first preliminary (muqaddamah) about human perfection. It states that perfect human state is confined to two things: the theoretical power becoming perfect so that the forms and realities of things are manifested in it completely, free from error; and the practical power becoming perfect through a faculty enabling righteous deeds, which are defined as those that turn away from bodily pleasures and increase desire for the afterlife and spiritual matters. From these two, the author concludes that human happiness (sa'adah) is only attained by achieving both states. He then emphasizes that this preliminary is unanimously accepted by prophets and divine philosophers, and every complete rational person supports it.

The Infinitude of Degrees of Perfection and Deficiency
Razi's third preliminary on the infinitude of degrees.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the claim that the degrees of perfection and deficiency in both theoretical and practical powers are virtually infinite and known by necessity.

Core Idea
Perfection and deficiency have infinite degrees, and this is self-evident.

Before Razi can argue for a single highest degree of perfection, he needs to establish that degrees themselves are unlimited. This short but crucial passage asserts that perfections and deficiencies in both intellect and action have no finite bound — a fact he says is known by intuition. Let's read it carefully.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then its translation. Notice the phrasing 'as if infinite' — it signals a conceptual rather than literal claim.
The phrase 'as if' softens the claim; it means 'effectively infinite' or 'beyond finite enumeration.'
2
Identify the two domains
The passage mentions two powers: theoretical (intellectual) and practical (moral/action). Degrees of perfection and deficiency apply to both.
3
Understand 'known by necessity'
Razi says this is 'ma‘lum bi al-ḍarūrah' — known by necessary, intuitive knowledge. Ask yourself: Is it self-evident that there can always be a greater or lesser degree of something?
This is a foundational premise; it doesn't require proof.

The author asserts that this is known by necessity (intuitively self-evident). This prepares for the argument that a single perfect person must exist at the highest degree.

How Dreams Arise: The Soul's Connection and the Imaginative Faculty
Razi describes the mechanism of dream formation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's explanation of how dreams are produced through the soul's connection to higher principles and the subsequent activity of the imaginative faculty.

Core Idea
Dreams are the soul's natural reception of forms, translated into images by the imaginative faculty.

In this passage, Razi begins by describing the psychological process that underlies dreaming. He explains how the soul, when temporarily freed from bodily management, connects to higher principles and receives abstract forms relevant to its own concerns. These forms are then translated into sensory images by the imaginative faculty, which appear in the common sense as dreams. This lesson focuses on that foundational process.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the sequence: soul respite → connection to principles → forms impression → imaginative faculty depiction → common sense.
Pay attention to the terms: النفس الناطقة, القوة المتخيلة, الحس المشترك.
2
Identify the key faculties
The passage mentions three key faculties: the rational soul, the imaginative faculty, and the common sense. Understand their roles in the dream process.
The imaginative faculty is described as 'imitating' the abstract forms.
3
Trace the causal chain
Write down the step-by-step process from the soul's detachment to the observable dream. Ensure you see why each step is necessary.
The soul naturally connects to principles when free from bodily tasks.
4
Reflect on the suitability of forms
Razi says the forms most suitable to the soul relate to the person's own affairs or interests. Consider why this makes sense for dreams.
This explains why dreams often reflect our personal concerns.

Source Text

إذا عرفت هذا فنقول : النفس إذا حصل لها أدنى فراغ من تدبير البدن ، اتصلت بطباعها بتلك المبادىء ، فتنطبع فيها بعض تلك الصور الحاضرة عند تلك المبادىء ، وهي الصورة التي هي أليق بتلك النفس . ومعلوم أن أليق الأحوال بها ، ما يتعلق بأحوال ذلك الإنسان وبأصحابه وأهل بلده وإقليمه . وأما إن كان ذلك الإنسان منجذب الهمة إلى تحصيل علوم المعقولات ، لاحت له منها أشياء . ومن كانت همته مصالح الناس رآها ، ثم إذا انطبعت تلك الصور في جوهر النفس الناطقة أخذت المتخيلة التي من طباعها محاكاة الأمور ، في حكاية تلك الصور المنطبعة في النفس ، بصور جزئية تناسبها ، ثم إن تلك الصور تنطبع في الحس المشترك فتصير مشاهدة. فهذا هو سبب الرؤيا في المنام . ثم إن تلك الصور التي ركبتها القوة المتخيلة ، لأجل تلك المعاني قد تكون شديدة المناسبة لتلك المعاني ، فتكون هذه الرؤيا غنية عن التعبير وقد لا تكون كذلك ، إلا أنها أيضاً مناسبة لتلك المعاني من بعض الوجوه ، وههنا تحتاج هذه المنامات إلى التعبير . وفائدة التعبير : التحليل بالعكس ، يعني : أن المعبر عن هذه الصور الحاضرة في الخيال إلى تلك المعاني وأما القسم الثالث أن لا تكون هذه الصور مناسبة لتلك المعاني البتة . وذلك يكون لأحد وجهين : أحدهما : أن يكون حدوث هذا الخيال الغريب ، إنما كان لوجه من الوجوه الثلاثة المذكورة في أسباب أضغاث الأحلام . والثاني : أن يكون ذلك لأجل أن القوة المتخيلة ركبت لأجل ذلك المعنى صورة ثم ركبت لأجل تلك الصورة صورة ثانية ، وللثانية ثالثة وأمعنت في هذه الانتقالات، فانتهت بالآخرة إلى صورة لا تناسب المعنى الذي أدركته النفس أولاً البتة . وحينئذ يصير هذا القسم أيضاً من باب أضغاث الأحلام، ولهذا السبب قيل : إنه لا اعتماد على رؤية الكاذب والشاعر ، لأن القوة المتخيلة منهما قد تعودت الانتقالات الكاذبة الباطلة والله أعلم.
If you have understood this, then we say: When the soul obtains the slightest respite from the management of the body, it naturally connects to those principles, and some of those forms present with those principles become impressed in it, and these are the forms most suitable to that soul. It is known that the most suitable states for it are those pertaining to the states of that person, his companions, his countrymen, and his region. But if that person is inclined in aspiration towards acquiring the sciences of rational matters, things from them become manifest to him. And one whose aspiration is the interests of people sees them. Then when those forms are impressed in the substance of the rational soul, the imaginative faculty, which by nature imitates things, takes to depicting those forms impressed in the soul by means of particular forms that correspond to them. Then those forms become impressed in the common sense and become observable. This is the cause of dreams in sleep. Then those forms constructed by the imaginative faculty for the sake of those meanings may be strongly appropriate to those meanings, so this dream is independent of interpretation; sometimes they are not like that, yet they are also appropriate to those meanings in some respects, and here these dreams need interpretation. The benefit of interpretation is analysis in reverse, meaning that the interpreter goes from these forms present in the imagination to those meanings. As for the third division, that these forms are not at all appropriate to those meanings. This occurs for one of two reasons: First, that the occurrence of this strange imagination is due to one of the three mentioned causes of confused dreams. Second, that it is because the imaginative faculty constructed, for that meaning, a form, then for that form it constructed a second form, and for the second a third, and it went deep into these transitions, finally ending at a form that does not at all correspond to the meaning that the soul first apprehended. Then this division also becomes part of confused dreams. For this reason it is said: No reliance is placed on the dream of a liar or a poet, because the imaginative faculty of both has become accustomed to false and invalid transitions. And God knows best.

Commentary
This passage classifies dreams (ar-ru'ya) and explains their causes. It begins by describing how, when the soul is free from bodily management, it connects to higher principles and receives forms suitable to its condition. The forms received relate to the person's own affairs or interests. Then the imaginative faculty (al-quwwa al-mutakhayyila) translates these abstract forms into particular sensory images that appear in the common sense (al-hiss al-mushtarak), making them visible as dreams. The passage then identifies three types of dreams based on the relationship between the constructed image and its original meaning: (1) strongly appropriate images that need no interpretation; (2) partially appropriate images requiring interpretation, which works by reversing the process from image to meaning; (3) images entirely inappropriate. The inappropriate type arises from two causes: either the image comes from one of the three causes of confused dreams (adghath al-ahlam), or it results from a chain of successive image constructions that lose connection to the original meaning. This latter case also counts as confused dreams, explaining why unreliable people's dreams are not trusted.

Three Types of Dreams: From Clear to Confused
Razi classifies dreams into three categories and explains the causes of confused dreams.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's classification of dreams into three types based on the appropriateness of the dream image to its original meaning, and the role of interpretation.

Core Idea
Dreams are classified by how well the dream image matches its underlying meaning: some need no interpretation, some require it, and some are meaningless.

Having explained the basic process of dreams, Razi now classifies them based on the relationship between the constructed image and the original meaning. He identifies three types: those whose images are strongly appropriate and need no interpretation, those that are partially appropriate and require interpretation, and those that are completely inappropriate and are considered confused dreams. This lesson explores this classification and the causes of confused dreams.

Guided Path
1
Read the classification passage
Read the Arabic and translation. Identify the three types. Notice how each is defined by the degree of appropriateness.
The passage uses 'قد تكون شديدة المناسبة' and 'قد لا تكون كذلك' and 'لا تكون مناسبة البتة'.
2
Understand the first two types
The first type needs no interpretation (غنية عن التعبير); the second needs interpretation (تحتاج إلى التعبير). Understand why interpretation is possible: analysis in reverse from image to meaning.
Interpretation works by reversing the process of construction.
3
Explore the third type and its causes
The third type is completely inappropriate. It arises from two causes: one of the three causes of confused dreams (adghath al-ahlam), or a chain of image constructions that lose connection. Notice the mention of poets and liars.
The imaginative faculty's habitual false transitions lead to unreliable dreams.
4
Reflect on the practical implications
Consider how this classification helps in evaluating dreams. Why are some dreams trustworthy and others not? How does interpretation work?
This classification has implications for dream interpretation practices.
