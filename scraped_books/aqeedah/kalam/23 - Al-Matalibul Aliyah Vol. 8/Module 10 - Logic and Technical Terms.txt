Refuting Non-Opposition as a Condition for Miracles
Refutation of non-opposition condition via two arguments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two key arguments (the second and third aspects) that refute the claim that a miracle's validity depends on absence of opposition.

Core Idea
Non-existence cannot distinguish; a proof must stand out.

This passage continues the critique of the condition that a miracle must face no opposition to be valid. The author presents two additional, sharp arguments. The first breaks down the notion of 'non-opposition' into three impossible scenarios. The second attacks the very idea that absence (non-existence) could serve as a distinguishing mark, which is essential for any proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then read the literal translation. Notice how the author structures two separate arguments (second and third aspects).
The passage is concise; each aspect is a single sentence.
2
Analyze the second aspect
Identify the three possible interpretations of 'non-opposition' that the author lists. Ask yourself why each is invalid.
The author says these are invalid 'with speech like what we mentioned in the preceding aspect.' This refers to a previous argument not in this passage.
3
Analyze the third aspect
Focus on the claim that non-existence (ʿadam) is pure negation and cannot provide distinguishment (imtiyāz). Understand why a proof must be distinguished from others.
This is a logical argument: a proof must have a positive distinguishing feature.
4
Connect to the overall debate
Consider why this critique matters: if non-opposition cannot be a condition, then miracles are valid proofs of prophethood even if others claim to perform similar acts.
This refutes a key objection against miracles.

The Second Division: Refuting Unconditioned Miracles
Refutes the claim that miracles indicate unconditioned by non-opposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the argument that miracles indicate truth unconditionally (without requiring non-opposition) is invalid and how its refutation completes the case against the opponent's position.

Core Idea
Miracles cannot be proof if they are unconditioned by non-opposition.

In the previous lesson, we examined the first division of the opponent's argument—that non-opposition is a condition for a miracle's indication. Now we turn to the second division: some claim that no condition is needed at all. The author declares this view clearly false. This lesson unpacks why this second claim is untenable and how its falsity, combined with the first, demonstrates that the opponent's entire objection collapses.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the structure: statement of the second division, then the verdict, then the broader conclusion linking to the first division.
Focus on the phrase 'غير مشروطة بعدم المعارضة' (not conditioned by absence of opposition).
2
Identify the second division
Locate where the author presents the opponent's claim: that the miracle's indication is unconditioned by non-opposition. Understand what this means.
3
Examine the author's verdict
The author says 'ظاهر الفساد والبطلان' (clearly corrupt and invalid). That is a strong refutation. Ask yourself why it is automatically invalid.
Think about the logical consequence: if unconditional, any opposing miracle could claim truth, leading to contradiction.
4
Connect to the broader argument
The author says 'فثبت بما ذكرنا: فساد القسمين' (so it is established by what we have mentioned: the corruption of the two divisions). This ties the refutation of the second division to the earlier refutation of the first.
The conclusion 'المعجز لا يمكن أن يكون دالاً على الصدق' is a reductio ad absurdum showing the opponent's position is untenable.
5
Reflect on the lesson
Consider why this refutation is crucial. If both divisions are false, the opponent's objection to miracles is completely refuted.

The author presents the first example of contradictory tawatur.

Learning Objective

He presents the first example: the Jewish claim that Moses' law is eternal and the Sabbath binding forever. This claim is transmitted tawatur among Jews, yet Muslims consider it false. The author forces a dilemma: if true, Islam is invalid; if false, tawatur is unreliable. This sets the stage for a broader discussion about contradictory tawatur reports.
