Sense Perception and the Origin of Self-Evident Truths
Argues that sense perception underlies self-evident truths and divides sense judgments into universals and particulars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Islamic theologians argue that denying sense perception leads to denying self-evident truths, and how they analyze the judgment of sense.

Core Idea
Self-evident truths depend on sensation, so doubting senses threatens them.

This lesson examines the foundational claim that self-evident truths (badihiyyat) arise from sensation of particulars. The author explains that children acquire self-evident truths through a readiness (isti'dad) that comes from sensory experience. Therefore, any attack on the reliability of sense perception ultimately undermines the self-evident truths that are the basis of all knowledge. We will explore the initial argument and the logical division of sense judgments into universals and particulars.

Guided Path
1
Read the main claim
Read the Arabic passage from the beginning through the statement that attacking senses leads to attacking self-evident truths. Notice how the author states the connection and the reasoning about readiness.
Identify the keywords: البديهيات and الاستعداد.
2
Examine the disjunction
Focus on the phrase 'إذا اعتبر حكم الحس فإما في الكليات أو في الجزئيات'. The author divides the judgment of sense into two categories and argues both are invalid.
The disjunction is a key logical move.
3
Understand the invalidity of universal judgments
Read the commentary about why sense cannot yield universal judgments. Sense only perceives particulars, so universal judgments based solely on sense are invalid.
Sense perceives only particulars, so universal judgments cannot be derived from it.

He said: 'Because sense does not perceive, etc.' And because its judgment often errs in particulars, as we will explain, so if it were assumed to perceive all particulars, even imaginary individuals, its universal judgment would still not be certain.

Commentary
This passage argues that denying sense perceptions leads to denying self-evident truths, because self-evident truths arise from sensation of particulars. It then examines the judgment of sense: either in universal propositions or particular propositions. Both are invalid: universal judgments cannot be based on sense since sense only perceives particulars, and particular judgments are also problematic. The commentary then discusses a counterargument: that attacking senses as not yielding certainty does not necessarily lead to denying self-evident truths, because sensation and conjectural judgment might be sufficient for the mind to acquire self-evident truths through habituation. The passage further refines the discussion by considering other types of propositions and concludes that sense judgments are inherently uncertain.

Will you thank God today for the gift of senses that ground your knowledge?

The Counterargument and Refinement of the Sense Perception Debate
Presents and responds to a counterargument, then refines the analysis of sense judgments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the counterargument that attacking senses as non-certain may not affect self-evident truths because habituation via conjecture suffices, and the subsequent refinement of the argument.

Core Idea
Doubt in sense certainty doesn't destroy badihiyyat; habituation via conjecture may be enough.

After establishing that sense perception underlies self-evident truths, the text addresses a potential objection: attacking senses only denies sensory certainty, but perhaps sensation and conjectural judgment provide sufficient habituation for the mind to grasp self-evident truths. The author then refines the discussion by considering other types of propositions and concludes that sense judgments are inherently uncertain. This lesson unpacks the counterargument and the final refinement.

Guided Path
1
Read the counterargument
Read from 'قوله : فالقدح في الحسيات... يمكن أن يناقش فيه' to the end of that paragraph. Notice the objection that attacking senses as non-certain may not lead to attacking badihiyyat because conjecture might suffice for readiness.
The key term here is الظن (conjecture).
2
Examine the refinement about proposition types
Read the next part about indefinite and particular propositions. The author considers other possibilities and argues they share the same corruption.
Indefinite and particular propositions are additional cases.
3
Understand the final conclusion
Read the last sentence about sense judgment being uncertain even if it perceived all particulars. The passage concludes that sense judgments are inherently not certain.
Sense judgments err even in particulars, so universal judgments from sense are also uncertain.

The Third Group: Skeptics of Self-Evident Truths
Presents the third group's claim and proof that self-evident truths are weaker than sense perceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument of the third group of skeptics who claim that self-evident truths are weaker than sense perceptions because they are derived from them.

Core Idea
Self-evident truths are weaker because they depend on sense perceptions.

The third group in the hierarchy of skeptics does not deny sense perceptions but targets self-evident truths. They argue that such truths are less reliable because they originate from sensory experience. In this passage, you will encounter their reasoning and see how they claim all conceptual knowledge ultimately derives from the senses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the group's claim
Read the passage and notice that the third group only finds fault in self-evident truths (badīhiyyāt), not in sense perceptions (ḥissiyyāt). They state that self-evident truths are weaker (aḍʿaf).
They are not total skeptics; they accept sensory knowledge.
2
Understand the proof structure
The proof is that self-evident truths are a branch (farʿ) of sense perceptions. Look for the causal link: self-evident truths depend on sensations.
The argument uses the metaphor of branch and root.
3
Trace the cognitive process
The passage describes how a person begins with no perceptions, then uses senses to perceive particulars, notices similarities/differences, and abstracts universal concepts. These universals form the basis of self-evident judgments.
This shows the empirical origin of even the most basic concepts.
4
Reflect on the illustration
Consider the example of the blind man (al-akmah) who lacks knowledge of colors because he lacks the relevant sense. This illustrates the dependence of knowledge on sensory input.
The example reinforces the claim that losing a sense loses related knowledge.

الفرقة الثالثة القادحون في البديهيات فقط لا في الحسيات فإنهم معترفون بها قالوا: هي أضعف من الحسيات لأنها فرعها وذلك لأن الإنسان في مبدأ الفطرة خال عن الإدراكات كلها فإذا استعمل الحواس في الجزئيات تنبه لمشاركات بينها ومباينات وانتزع منها صوراً كلية يحكم على بعضها ببعض إيجاباً أو سلباً إما ببديهة عقله كما في البديهيات أو بمعاونة شيء آخر كما في سائر الضروريات والنظريات فلولا إحساسه بالمحسوسات لم يكن له شيء من التصورات والتصديقات ولذلك قيل: من فقد حساً فقد علماً متعلقاً بذلك الحس ابتداء أو بواسطة كالأكمه فإنه لا يعرف حقائق الألوان
The third group are those who find fault (al-qādiḥūn) only in self-evident truths (al-badīhiyyāt), not in sense perceptions (al-ḥissiyyāt), for they acknowledge them. They said: They (i.e., self-evident truths) are weaker (aḍʿaf) than sense perceptions, because they are a branch (farʿ) of them. That is because at the beginning of innate disposition (al-fiṭrah), man is empty of all perceptions (al-idrākāt). Then when he uses the senses in particulars, he becomes aware of similarities and differences among them, and he extracts from them universal images (ṣuwar kulliyyah), by which he judges some in relation to others positively or negatively, either by the intuition of his intellect as in self-evident truths, or with the aid of something else as in other necessary and theoretical matters. So were it not for his sense perception of sensed things, he would have nothing of conceptualizations (al-taṣawwurāt) and judgments (al-taṣdīqāt). Therefore it is said: Whoever loses a sense loses knowledge related to that sense, either initially or indirectly, like the blind man (al-akmah) who does not know the realities of colors.

Commentary
This passage presents the position of the third group of skeptics (al-qādiḥūn) and their proof. It begins by defining them: they only reject self-evident truths (badīhiyyāt) as certain, not sense perceptions (ḥissiyyāt). They claim that self-evident truths are weaker (aḍʿaf) than sense perceptions. The proof is that self-evident truths are derived from sense perceptions (farʿuhā). The author explains the process: a newborn is initially devoid of all perceptions. When he uses the senses to perceive particulars, he becomes aware of commonalities and differences and from these he abstracts universal concepts (ṣuwar kulliyyah). These universals become the basis for judgments, either intuitive (self-evident) or with assistance (necessary or theoretical truths). Without sense perception, he would have no concepts or judgments. Therefore, the saying: 'He who loses a sense loses a science (contingent on that sense),' e.g., the blind man knows nothing of colors. The argument proceeds sequentially: claim, proof (branching relationship), explanation of human cognitive development, and illustration.
