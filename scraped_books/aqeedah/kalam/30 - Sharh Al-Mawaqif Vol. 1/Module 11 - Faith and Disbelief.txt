Including Adam in the Honor
The commentator explains the interpretation of 'bani Adam' as 'human species' to include Adam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the commentator interprets 'bani Adam' as 'human species' to include the Prophet Adam in the honor.

Core Idea
The honor of humanity includes Adam himself.

In this passage, the commentator begins by explaining the author's choice of words. He clarifies that 'type of man' is used instead of 'children of Adam' so that the first human, Adam, peace be upon him, is not excluded. This careful wording reflects a theological precision about who is honored.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage about 'naw' al-insan'
Read the first part of the passage where the commentator explains why the author used 'human species'. Note the reference to the Quranic verse.
2
Understand the need to include Adam
Consider why 'bani Adam' might exclude Adam himself, and how 'human species' corrects that.
3
Reflect on the concept of takrim
Notice the commentator's point that takrim (honoring) does not require an implied construct like 'over others' because it is absolute.
4
Connect to the verse
Reflect on the Quranic verse (17:70) that says 'We have honored the children of Adam' and how this interpretation aligns with it.

His saying: 'the human species (naw' al-insan).' He interpreted 'bani Adam' as 'the human species' to include Adam, peace be upon him. And there is no need to assume an implied construct because 'honoring' (takrim) means exaltation and that does not need a construct, as occurred in the revelation: 'And We have honored the children of Adam' (Q 17:70). And there is no need to carry it on the meaning of preference (tafdil) as in His statement: 'This is whom you have honored over me' (Q 17:62), so that it is complete without assuming an implied construct. And despite that, it is necessary to specify 'others' (al-ghayr) as excluding angels and jinn, because they, being obligated, are partners with humans in the mentioned honor. Therefore they said: The causes of knowledge for creation, i.e., angels, jinn, and humans, are three. His saying: 'the human species over others.' He interpreted 'bani Adam' as 'the human species' to include Adam in the ruling of honor, and he intended by 'others' the mute animals, not the jinn, nor even the angels.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and clarification of the phrase 'type of man' (naw' al-insan) in the context of God's honoring of Adam's children. The commentator explains that the author interprets 'bani Adam' as 'human species' to include the Prophet Adam. He argues that 'takrim' (honoring) does not require an implied construct (like 'over others') because it is an absolute exaltation. However, when 'others' is specified, it must be restricted to non-human animals, excluding angels and jinn, because they are also honored as moral agents. He cites the famous classification of three types of creatures capable of knowledge: angels, jinn, and humans. The second part reinforces that 'others' means mute animals only.

Correction: The commentator shows that 'human species' is used specifically to include Adam because 'bani Adam' could be interpreted as offspring.

The Meaning of 'Others' in Human Honor
The commentator specifies that 'others' refers only to mute animals, excluding angels and jinn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that when God honors humans over 'others', those 'others' are only mute animals, not angels or jinn.

Core Idea
Angels and jinn share in human honor as obligated beings.

The commentator now turns to the second part of the phrase 'over others'. He specifies that the 'others' cannot include angels or jinn because they too are honored as moral agents. Instead, 'others' refers only to animals without speech (al-hayawanat al-ajam). This clarifies the scope of human superiority.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage about 'others'
Read from 'and despite that' to the end of the passage. Note the exclusion of angels and jinn.
2
Understand why angels and jinn are excluded
Consider the reasoning: they are also obligated (mukallaf), so they are partners in honor.
3
Identify the intended meaning of 'others'
The commentator explicitly says 'others' means mute animals, not jinn or angels.
4
Reflect on the classification of creatures
The passage mentions that the causes of knowledge are three: angels, jinn, and humans, showing they share honor.

The Ash'ari Position on Those Unreached by Prophecy
Ash'ari ruling on the unreached

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Ash'aris consider people who never received prophecy as excused from both faith and actions, and how this differs from the Mu'tazila.

Core Idea
The unreached are fully excused in Ash'ari theology, even when reason can discern good and evil.

This brief passage captures a key difference between the Ash'ari and Mu'tazili schools. The question of what happens to people who never receive a prophetic message has deep implications for divine justice and human accountability. Let's examine the text to grasp the Ash'ari position and its contrast with the Mu'tazila.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice that it is a short statement reporting a position.
2
Identify the two schools mentioned
The passage mentions the Ash'aris and the Mu'tazila. Understand that the Ash'ari view is presented as the primary statement, with the Mu'tazila as the opposing view.
The word 'خلافاً' indicates disagreement.
3
Understand the scope of the excuse
The excuse covers both 'faith' (الإيمان) and 'actions' (الأعمال). This means the unreached are not held accountable for belief or moral deeds.
Note that the actions are specifically those that reason can independently judge as good or bad.
4
Consider the role of intellect
The passage adds 'which the intellect has independence in perceiving their goodness and badness.' This clarifies that even when reason can discern moral value, the Ash'aris still grant an excuse, unlike the Mu'tazila.
This is a key point of divergence.
5
Reflect on the implication
Ask yourself: What does this say about Ash'ari views on divine justice? How does this affect the status of people in remote times or places?
The Ash'ari position emphasizes God's grace and the necessity of prophetic warning.

Commentary
This passage is a brief statement about the theological position regarding people who never received a prophetic call. It reports that the Ash'aris consider such a person excused from both faith (īmān) and actions (a'māl), even in matters where the intellect independently can discern good and evil. This is in contrast to the Mu'tazila, who hold that human reason alone can determine the obligation to know God and follow moral laws, so those unreached by prophecy may still be held accountable based on rational judgment. The Ash'ari view, as stated here, grants a full excuse.

Objection on Ijmā' for Obligation of Ma'rifah and Reply
Objection on ijmā' for obligation of ma'rifah and author's reply using implicit knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author defends the obligation of ma'rifah against the objection that ijmā' is against it, by showing that common people have implicit knowledge from creation.

Core Idea
Implicit knowledge from creation suffices for ma'rifah, even if one cannot articulate proofs.

This lesson presents a powerful objection against the claim that ma'rifah (knowledge of God) is obligatory for everyone. The objector points to the practice of the Prophet and the community, who accepted the faith of common people without requiring detailed proofs. How does the author respond? He argues that even common people have an implicit, summary knowledge of God from the signs of creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice how the objection is presented first, then the reply.
Pay attention to the Bedouin's statement — it is central to the reply.
2
Identify the objection's evidence
The objector claims consensus is against the obligation of ma'rifah. What evidence does he give? He says the Prophet and the community approved the faith of common people without requiring proofs.
This is a strong empirical argument from practice.
3
Analyze the author's reply
The author does not deny the practice but argues that common people had implicit knowledge. Find the Bedouin's analogy and explain how it shows this.
The analogy uses natural signs to prove the existence of the Maker.

The sixth: Denying the occurrence of consensus upon the obligation of knowledge, rather consensus is actually contrary to it, due to the Prophet, the Companions, and the people of all ages up to our time approving the common people upon their faith, and they are the majority in every age, without inquiring from them about the proofs indicating the Maker and His attributes, but rather with the knowledge that they do not know them at all, for the utmost of their effort is verbal confession and pure imitation which has no certitude, and if knowledge were obligatory such approval and judgment of their faith would not be permissible. We said: They used to know the proofs summarily, as the Bedouin said: The dung indicates the camel, and the footprints indicate the journey; is it not that a sky with constellations, and an earth with ravines, and a sea with waves do not indicate the All-Subtle, the All-Aware? The utmost is that they fell short in expression and clarification.

Commentary
This passage is the sixth objection and its answer. The objector claims that there is no consensus (ijmā') on the obligation of ma'rifah (knowledge of God); in fact, consensus is against it. The evidence is that the Prophet and Companions and all succeeding generations approved the faith of the common people, who are the majority, without asking them for proofs of God and His attributes, and knowing that they lack detailed knowledge—their faith is mere tongue confession and blind imitation (taqlīd) without certainty. If detailed knowledge were obligatory, such approval would not be allowed. The author answers that the common people did have knowledge of the proofs in a summary (ijmālī) manner. He cites the Bedouin's analogy: just as camel dung indicates a camel and footprints indicate a journey, so do the sky, earth, and sea indicate the Creator. Their shortcoming was only in articulation and detailed explanation, not in the essence of knowledge.
