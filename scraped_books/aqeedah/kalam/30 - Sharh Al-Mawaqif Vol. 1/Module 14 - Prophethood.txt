Rank of Prophethood vs. Command to Reflect
Commentator explains the rank of prophethood relative to the command to reflect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the sending of prophets is considered later in rank than the command to reflect, even though prophets are chronologically prior.

Core Idea
The intellectual rank of divine knowledge precedes prophethood.

This lesson unpacks a subtle point about the order of divine actions. The commentator explains that the word 'then' (ثم) in the phrase 'Then He sent prophets' indicates a delay in rank, not time. He clarifies how the command to reflect on God's signs is intellectually prior to prophethood, even though prophets deliver that command. Let's explore the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation to understand the literal meaning. Note how the commentator interprets the word 'then'.
The passage is dense with technical terms. Focus on the flow.
2
Understand the role of 'ثم'
The commentator says 'ثم' indicates delay in rank, not time. Why? Because the sending of prophets includes many rulings, but the command to reflect is a prior obligation. Find the sentence that explains this.
The word 'ثم' in Arabic can indicate temporal order or logical priority.
3
Distinguish chronological vs. intellectual order
Even though prophets came before the command to reflect in time (since the command comes through them), the command to reflect is intellectually prior because it is the first obligation. Identify the commentator's reasoning.
This is a key concept in Islamic theology: some things are prior in rank even if later in time.
4
Address the confusion about the exception
The commentator corrects a mistaken view: some think the sending includes the command to reflect, but it actually includes the command to know. Notice how he distinguishes 'command to know' from 'command to reflect'.
The exception 'besides the command to reflect' is valid because reflecting is a distinct act.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn's phrase 'ثم إنه بعث إليهم الأنبياء والرسل' (Then He sent to them the prophets and messengers). The commentator explains that the word 'ثم' (then) indicates a delay in rank, not necessarily in time. He clarifies that the sending of prophets, though chronologically prior to the command to reflect (since the command to reflect comes through the prophets), is later in the intellectual order: the command to reflect is the first obligation and pertains to divine matters, while prophethood comes after. He also addresses a potential confusion: the matn says the sending is 'besides the command to reflect' (سوى الأمر بالتفكر), but some might think it includes that command; the commentator corrects that the command here is just 'to know Him' (المعرفة), not 'to reflect' (التفكر), so the exception stands.

Definition of a Messenger and the Objection
Al-Jurjani defines rasul and addresses objections about scriptures.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of a messenger (rasul) and the famous objection regarding the number of messengers and scriptures.

Core Idea
A messenger is a prophet with a scripture or law, but defining it raises puzzles.

Al-Jurjani begins by examining the standard definition of a messenger: a prophet who brings a scripture. However, variants exist, and a famous objection emerges: how can every messenger have a scripture when the number of messengers exceeds the number of revealed books? This passage explores possible solutions and their limitations.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the translation to grasp the flow of argument.
The passage contains multiple definitions and counterarguments.
2
Identify the two variant definitions
Note the two manuscript readings: ‘a nabi with a scripture’ and ‘a nabi with a scripture and a law’. Understand the implication of each.
The second variant requires both scripture and law.
3
Trace the objection
The objection is that there are more messengers than scriptures. Think about why this challenges the definition.
Many messengers, like Isma'il, are said to have no scripture.
4
Consider the attempted solutions
Al-Jurjani offers an interpretation that the definition means ‘either a scripture or a law’. Evaluate whether this solves the objection.
He then raises a counterexample: Isma'il is a messenger with neither.
5
Examine the counterexamples
The case of Dawud shows that having a scripture does not necessarily mean having a new law, as his scripture was supplications.
This refutes the claim that scripture implies law.

قوله : ( والرسول نبي معه كتاب هكذا وقع في بعض النسخ ، وهو موافق لما وقع في شرح العقائد النسفية من أنه يشترط في الرسول الكتاب وفي بعض النسخ. معه كتاب وشرع، وهو موافق لما وقع في شرح المقاصد من أن الرسول قد يخص بأن له شريعة وكتاب، وهذه العبارة ظاهرة في أنه يشترط فيه كلاهما، وحينئذ يرد الاعتراض المشهور، كما يرد على النسخة الأولى من زيادة عدد الرسل على عدد الكتب، ويجوز أن يكون معناه من يكون معه كتاب ومن يكون معه شرع فلا يشترط اجتماعهما، ويكون مآله إلى من يكون معه كتاب أو شرع، فلا يرد الاعتراض المذكور، لكن يرد النقض بإسماعيل عليه السلام، فإنه رسول وليس صاحب كتاب ولا شريعة، وقد يقال : إن مآل التعريفين واحد لأن من له كتاب فله شرع وليس بشيء لأن الكتاب لا يجب أن يكون ناسخاً، لأن داود عليه السلام كان صاحب كتاب كله أدعية، على ما قالوا . قوله : ( والنبي غير الرسول من لا كتاب معه، إنما لم يقل والنبي أعم كما هو المشهور، لأن النبي الرسول معلوم، والمحتاج إلى البيان هو النبي غير الرسول وأراد بمن لا كتاب معه بقرينة السوق، فلا يرد لزوم كون آحاد الناس نبياً، نعم يلزم أن يكون من يحكم من الأنبياء بدون كتاب ولا متابعة من قبله خارجاً عن النبي والرسول معاً، اللهم إلا أن يبين أن لا وجود لمثله ودونه خرط القتاد .
His statement: 'The messenger (rasul) is a prophet (nabi) with a scripture (kitab).' This occurs in some manuscripts, and it agrees with what is found in Sharh al-'Aqa'id al-Nasafiyyah that a scripture is required for a messenger. In some manuscripts: 'with a scripture and a law (shar'),' agreeing with Sharh al-Maqasid that messenger may be specified as having a law and a scripture. This apparent wording requires both. Then the famous objection arises, as it also arises for the first version, of the number of messengers exceeding the number of scriptures. It is possible that its meaning is: one who has a scripture and one who has a law, so both are not required together. Its conclusion is one who has a scripture or a law. So the mentioned objection does not arise. But a counterexample arises with Isma'il (peace be upon him), for he is a messenger but does not have a scripture or a law. It may be said that both definitions amount to the same because whoever has a scripture has a law. But this is not sound, because a scripture does not have to be abrogating; for Dawud (peace be upon him) had a scripture entirely of supplications, according to their claim. His statement: 'The prophet other than the messenger is one who does not have a scripture.' He did not say 'prophet is more general' as is well-known, because the prophet-messenger is known, and what needs clarification is the prophet who is not a messenger. He intended by 'one who does not have a scripture' by context. So the objection that ordinary people could be prophets does not apply. Indeed, it necessarily follows that any prophet who rules without a scripture or following a previous one falls outside both prophet and messenger together, unless it is explained that such a person does not exist, and to do that is extremely difficult.

Commentary
This passage defines and distinguishes between a rasul (messenger) and a nabi (prophet). The author begins by citing a definition from the matn: 'The messenger is a prophet with a scripture.' He notes that in some manuscripts the definition adds 'with a scripture and a law,' requiring both. This raises a famous objection: the number of messengers is greater than the number of revealed scriptures, so how can every messenger have a scripture? The author offers a possible interpretation: the definition may refer to a messenger having either a scripture or a law, not both. But this faces a counterexample: Prophet Isma'il is considered a messenger without a scripture or a new law. The author then mentions a weak response that having a scripture implies having a law, but rejects it by noting that Prophet Dawud's scripture was only supplications, not a law. The author then turns to the definition of a prophet who is not a messenger: one without a scripture. He explains why the text didn't simply say 'prophet is more general' — because the prophet-messenger is already known, and what needs clarification is the non-messenger prophet. He also addresses an objection: if a prophet is defined as someone without a scripture, could any ordinary person be a prophet? No, because the context implies a prophet with divine revelation. However, he notes a remaining problem: a prophet who neither has a scripture nor follows a previous prophet's law would be outside both definitions, but such a case might not exist.

The Mu'tazilite Argument of Ifham al-Anbiya' and the Ash'arite Response

Core Idea
The Mu'tazilite argument against revelational obligation is self-defeating.

This passage presents a pivotal debate in Islamic theology: the Mu'tazilites argue that if rational consideration (nazar) is only obligatory by revelation, a skeptic can refute the prophet by pointing out a vicious circle. So the argument is mutual and does not favor either side. The commentator then clarifies details: the indication of a miracle is theoretical, requiring two premises (claim of prophethood and the miracle itself); and the variation in manuscripts regarding the wording. He also clarifies that the intended meaning is not a real circularity, because the consideration of the miracle does not depend on the absolute obligation of consideration; rather, the obligation of consideration is known through other premises (e.g., that knowledge is obligatory, that it requires consideration, etc.), so there is no circularity (la dawr).
