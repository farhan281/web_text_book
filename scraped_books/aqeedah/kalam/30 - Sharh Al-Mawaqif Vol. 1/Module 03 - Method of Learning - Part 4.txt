The objection that kalam is an unlawful innovation often rests on the fact that the Prophet and his Companions did not practice it. In this passage, the author dismantles that objection by showing that circumstances change: what was unnecessary in their time became essential in later times. He reinforces his point by comparing kalam to fiqh, which also underwent later systematization without being deemed blameworthy.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of the science of kalam against the charge that it is a blameworthy innovation (bid'ah). The author acknowledges that the Prophet and Companions did not engage in kalam as it came to be practiced later—they did not write it down, use technical terminology, systematize doctrines, elaborate proofs, or engage in extended dialectical argumentation. However, the author argues that this was due to their unique circumstances: they had pure souls, strong intellects, sharp minds, direct access to revelation, and the ability to consult the Prophet whenever doubts arose. Moreover, they faced few opponents and few doubts. In later times, by contrast, many doubts accumulated from past eras, making it necessary to formulate and write down kalam to preserve correct beliefs. The author draws an analogy with fiqh: the Companions did not systematically codify fiqh into quarters, chapters, sections, or technical terms like al-naqd, al-qalb, al-jam', al-farq, tanqih al-manat, and takhrij. Yet no one considers later fiqh scholarship blameworthy. Similarly, the later development of kalam is not blameworthy. The author concludes that some innovations are good (bid'ah hasanah).

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer argument. The commentator first states an objection: if the obligation of reflection (nazar) for knowing God were an innate syllogism (fitri al-qiyas) — meaning a proposition whose demonstration is always already present in the mind — then the prophet would only need to place premises that are immediately grasped. But then the judgment of obligation would be either necessary or close to necessary, requiring only a slight reminder. The commentator explains each phrase of the objection, noting that the denial (man') applies even if obligation is considered legal. He then highlights a problem: the text seems contradictory because calling it an innate syllogism yet needing premises for its acquisition is inconsistent. He resolves this with two interpretations: the first treats the premises as giving only conceptualization, not the judgment itself; the second treats the judgment as 'like' an innate syllogism after premises are presented. The first solution (al-man') then argues that the objection's premise is false: such an innate syllogism with subtle premises is impossible, and even if it existed, it would not compel action before obligation is established, thus undermining the prophet's mission. The second solution (al-hall) directly addresses the circularity: the objector claims reflection cannot be obligatory until its obligation is known, but this confuses the dependency. Obligation in itself does not depend on knowledge of it; rather, knowledge of obligation depends on the obligation itself. The marginal commentary elaborates, refuting a counter-argument that ignorance would excuse inaction by pointing out that many obligations are known only after reflection, and the lack of blame for ignorance depends on the context of revelation, which is not yet established.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer argument. The commentator first states an objection: if the obligation of reflection (nazar) for knowing God were an innate syllogism (fitri al-qiyas) — meaning a proposition whose demonstration is always already present in the mind — then the prophet would only need to place premises that are immediately grasped. But then the judgment of obligation would be either necessary or close to necessary, requiring only a slight reminder. The commentator explains each phrase of the objection, noting that the denial (man') applies even if obligation is considered legal. He then highlights a problem: the text seems contradictory because calling it an innate syllogism yet needing premises for its acquisition is inconsistent. He resolves this with two interpretations: the first treats the premises as giving only conceptualization, not the judgment itself; the second treats the judgment as 'like' an innate syllogism after premises are presented. The first solution (al-man') then argues that the objection's premise is false: such an innate syllogism with subtle premises is impossible, and even if it existed, it would not compel action before obligation is established, thus undermining the prophet's mission. The second solution (al-hall) directly addresses the circularity: the objector claims reflection cannot be obligatory until its obligation is known, but this confuses the dependency. Obligation in itself does not depend on knowledge of it; rather, knowledge of obligation depends on the obligation itself. The marginal commentary elaborates, refuting a counter-argument that ignorance would excuse inaction by pointing out that many obligations are known only after reflection, and the lack of blame for ignorance depends on the context of revelation, which is not yet established.

Obligation Does Not Depend on Knowledge
Sharh al-Mawaqif presents the second solution to the objection about reflection's obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the obligation of reflection does not depend on the mukallaf's prior knowledge, and how this resolves objections about circularity and the disbeliever's case.

Core Idea
Obligation is grounded in reality, not in the mukallaf's awareness of it.

In this passage, the commentator addresses a key objection: how can reflection be obligatory before the law is known? The answer lies in distinguishing between obligation in itself and knowledge of it. By showing that obligation depends on the existence of the law in the divine decree, not on human awareness, the argument avoids circularity and explains why even the heedless can be accountable.

Commentary
This passage presents the second solution to a common objection in kalām about the obligation of reflection (naẓar). Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof-against-objection. It begins by stating the ninth aim of the fifth observation: the condition for reasoning to yield knowledge is attentiveness (tafattun) to how the minor term is subsumed under the major and how the premises are connected. I say: It is apparent that the heedlessness is due to his inability to combine the two premises.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the condition of arrangement in reasoning. It begins by stating that the acquired sciences terminate at necessary ones, and therefore the premises must have a specific form. The author argues that after arranging the two premises in the first figure, no further matter is needed. However, the mere observation of the arrangement and form is not a condition; what matters is the subsumption of the minor under the middle. An example from al-Bulgha is given to illustrate that heedlessness of one premise can lead to a false conclusion. The commentator then explains the phrase 'denied' and clarifies that the denial has been refuted. He further elaborates on 'observing the arrangement', explaining that it means observing the subsumption of the particular minor under the middle, which is indeed a condition. The objection about the dull person who arranges premises correctly but still misses the conclusion is answered: his heedlessness is due to his inability to combine the two premises mentally.
