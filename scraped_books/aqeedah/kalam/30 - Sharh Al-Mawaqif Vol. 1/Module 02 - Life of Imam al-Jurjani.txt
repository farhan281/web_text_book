The Author and His Context
Editor introduces the book and gives a biography of al-Iji.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the life and scholarly achievements of 'Adud al-Din al-Iji, the author of al-Mawaqif.

Core Idea
Knowing the author's life deepens your engagement with his work.

Before studying al-Mawaqif, it is valuable to know its author. This lesson presents the editor's preface and a biography of 'Adud al-Din al-Iji, covering his birth, education, teachers, expertise, works, and the tragic end of his life.

Guided Path
1
Read the editor's preface
Read the opening lines that include the basmala, hamdala, and the editor's statement about correcting the book. Understand that this is not part of the original work.
The editor's preface sets the context for the modern publication.
2
Read the biography of al-Iji
Read the full name, birth year (708 AH), place (Ij near Shiraz), and his early education. Note his teachers, especially Zayn al-Din, a student of al-Baydawi.
Al-Iji studied under leading scholars of his time.
3
Identify his fields of expertise
The biography lists grammar, fiqh, ma'ani, bayan, and rational sciences. This shows he was a polymath with strong grounding in both transmitted and rational disciplines.
4
Reflect on his imprisonment and death
He died in 756 AH in a fortress after a conflict with the ruler of Kirman. This historical context reminds us that scholars sometimes faced persecution.
His death in prison adds a layer of tragedy to his legacy.
5
Review his famous works
Al-Asnawi mentions his commentary on Mukhtasar Ibn al-Hajib, al-Mawaqif, al-Jawahir, and others. al-Mawaqif is his most famous theological work.
These works solidified his reputation as a leading scholar.

Imam al-Ījī was a master of rational sciences whose works illuminate kalam.

This lesson introduces the author of our text, Imam Adud al-Din al-Iji. Knowing his background helps us appreciate the depth of al-Mawaqif. The passage gives his name, studies, expertise, and the high praise of later scholars.

Biography of the author:
He is the Imam Abd al-Rahman ibn Ahmad ibn Abd al-Ghaffar, the judge Adud al-Din al-Iji, a scholar of grammar, jurisprudence, rhetoric, elucidation, and the rational sciences.
He was born in the year 708 AH in Ij, a district of Shiraz, and began acquiring knowledge from the scholars of his time. He closely followed Zayn al-Din, the student of Imam al-Baydawi. He was an imam in rational matters, adept in the principles (usul), and participated in various disciplines. He died imprisoned in a fortress near Ij in the year 756 AH after undergoing a trial with the ruler of Kirman, who imprisoned him in the fortress, where he died.
Al-Isnawi said about him: 'He was an imam in multiple sciences, a thorough investigator, possessing famous works including the commentary on Mukhtasar Ibn al-Hajib, al-Mawaqif, al-Jawahir, and others in the science of kalam, and al-Fawa'id al-Ghiyathiyya on rhetoric and elucidation. He was a man of wealth, generosity, and honor to visitors, and he served as chief judge in the kingdom of Abu Sa'id, and his conduct was praised.'
Al-Subki said: 'He was an imam in rational sciences, knowledgeable in the two principles (usul), rhetoric, elucidation, grammar, and participated in jurisprudence. He authored al-Mawaqif and other works in kalam.'
Al-Taftazani said in praise of him: 'We have nothing left but to acquire his works, uncover the hidden gems of his secrets, and to draw from the oceans of his fruits and seek illumination from his lights.'

Then it mentions his birth year (708 AH) in Ij near Shiraz, and his studies under the scholars of his time, especially Zayn al-Din, a student of al-Baydawi. He was known as an imam in rational sciences, firm in the principles (usul), and participated in other arts. The notice then records that he died imprisoned in a fortress near Ij in 756 AH after a conflict with the ruler of Kirman. Commentary
This passage is a definition and enumeration of the Prophet Muhammad's superior attributes and his lineage, with explanatory glosses. He is the one who strengthened religion when it was about to collapse, revived the noble qualities of Sharia, and is hailed as the beauty of the world and religion, Abu Isḥāq. The passage ends with a prayer that the heavens follow his desire and destinies seek his pleasure.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The praise is historically accurate and not exaggerated; the Sultan actually possessed all these qualities exactly as described.
Correction: Panegyric poetry and prose in classical Islamic literature often employ hyperbole and conventional motifs to honor a patron. While the Sultan may have been virtuous, the description is idealized and follows standard literary conventions.

Key Takeaway
The ideal patron in classical Islamic literature is praised for a combination of moral, intellectual, and martial virtues, all aimed at supporting religion and justice.
