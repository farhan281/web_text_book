The Opening: From 'Tahayyartu' to Lexical Foundations
Author explains the rhetorical placement of 'tahayyartu' and gives lexical definitions of key terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rhetorical placement of 'tahayyartu' in the opening doxology and the lexical definitions of essential theological terms like dhat and tawalluh.

Core Idea
Key theological terms gain precision through their linguistic roots and usage.

The author begins by explaining why the word 'tahayyartu' (I was bewildered) appears without the expected conjunction, suggesting a deliberate rhetorical shift. He then provides detailed lexical analysis of the words dhat (essence), tawalluh (bewilderment), dhihn (intellect), and bayda' (desert), grounding theological concepts in language.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage from 'إحسان منه تعالى' to 'والبيداء الفلاة' once without stopping. Then read the translation. Notice the flow from explaining 'tahayyartu' to defining individual words.
The passage moves from rhetorical explanation to lexical definitions.
2
Analyze the explanation of 'tahayyartu'
The author says 'tahayyartu' is like the result of the preceding prayer, and the missing 'fa' indicates a turn to a stronger proof. Reflect on why the author would want to emphasize this rhetorical nuance.
The 'fa' omission is a literary device to shift focus.
3
Identify the lexical definitions
The passage defines four key words: dhat, tawalluh, dhihn, bayda'. Write down each definition as given by the author. Note that dhat has a special feminine form and is used to refer to God's essence.
These definitions are from the Arabic language, not theological innovation.
4
Connect to the broader purpose
Ask yourself: why does the author spend time on these definitions? They establish the linguistic foundation for the theological arguments that follow. This is a typical method in Islamic scholarship: clarify terms before using them.
Precision in language prevents confusion in theological debate.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the opening phrases of the book al-Mawaqif, followed by the author's statement of purpose. The author begins by explaining why the word 'tahayyartu' (I was bewildered) is joined to the preceding, likening it to a result (natija) of what came before. He notes that the conjunction 'fa' was omitted to suggest a stronger proof. Then he provides lexical definitions: the root of 'dhat' (essence), its feminine form, and its usage; 'tawalluh' (bewilderment), 'dhihn' (mind), 'bayda' (desert). He then interprets the phrase 'ya man dalla 'ala dhatihi bi dhatihi' (O He who indicated His essence by His essence) as referring to God's self-evident signs in the universe, quoting Quran 41:53. Similarly, 'shahida bi wahdaniyyatihi nizam masnu'atihi' (the order of His creations testified to His oneness) is supported by Quran 21:22. He explains the blessing on the Prophet as preserving his law and elevating his station. He then gives morphological explanations for 'zulum' and 'diya'' and the phrase 'al-maqul wa al-manqul' as terms of praise. Finally, he praises the book al-Mawaqif as universally recognized for its excellence and then declares his own intention to write a comprehensive commentary (hawashi) to facilitate understanding, clarify difficulties, and reveal its secrets. He mentions his long-standing desire and his effort to accomplish this task out of compassion for students and seekers of truth.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Commentary
This passage is a classification and explanation of the divine attributes derived from the verse commanding reflection. Finally, the phrase 'true Maker' is clarified: it means a Maker who has no other, i.e., the Eternal Necessary. The attributes are not merely theoretical but are derived from rational proofs based on contingency (imkān).

Al-Razi's Proof: The Two Conditions
Al-Razi's two-part conditional argument against composition of existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's core argument that existence cannot be composite because either its parts are existences (leading to self-precedence) or they are not (making existence composite of non-existences).

Core Idea
Existence cannot be composite without leading to absurdity.

Imam al-Razi presents a powerful argument against the composition of existence. He offers a conditional: if the parts of existence are themselves existences, then each part would equal the whole in quiddity, causing a logical contradiction. If they are not existences, then existence would be made of non-existences. Let's examine this argument closely.

2
Identify the two conditions
The argument has two branches. First, if the parts are existences, then each part equals the whole in quiddity, leading to absurdity. Second, if the parts are not existences, then existence is composed of non-existences.
The commentator clarifies what 'existence' means in each branch.
3
Understand the absurd conclusion
The first branch leads to the conclusion that a thing precedes itself, which is impossible. The second branch leads to the impossibility of composition from non-existences.
This shows the power of the argument.

The First Objection: Possibility and Obligation of Knowing God
Al-Ījī presents the first objection regarding possibility of knowledge and the need for reflection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first objection against the obligation of reflecting on God's existence and how it hinges on the distinction between necessary and theoretical knowledge.

Core Idea
Obligation depends on possibility, but is knowledge of God possible as necessary or theoretical?

Al-Ījī begins by presenting the first objection to the obligation of reflection. The objector argues that before we can say reflection is obligatory, we must establish that knowledge of God is possible. But this possibility cannot be of a necessary kind—because no one finds such knowledge innately—so it must be theoretical, acquired only through reflection. This sets up a key tension in Islamic theology: is knowledge of God a direct necessity or a hard-won conclusion?

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully

Understand the principle behind the objection
Identify the principle: 'وجوب المعرفة يتوقف على إمكانها' (obligation of knowledge depends on its possibility). Why must this be true? Because the impossible cannot be obligated.
This principle is a cornerstone of Islamic legal and theological reasoning.
3
Distinguish between necessary and theoretical knowledge
The objector argues that if knowledge of God were necessary, people would find it innately without reflection. But we don't. So it must be theoretical. This distinction is crucial for understanding the objection.
Necessary knowledge (ضروري) is self-evident and unavoidable; theoretical knowledge (نظري) requires inference.

If knowledge is theoretical, its possibility is not self-evident. Does that undermine the obligation? The objector says yes, but the answer will come in later lessons.
The force of the objection is that it challenges the very foundation of the obligation.

The Nature of the Obligation of Knowing Allah
Defines absolute obligation and discusses relativity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the obligation to know Allah is considered absolutely obligatory in relation to reflection, even if it is conditioned by other factors.

Core Idea
Obligation is relative: what is absolute in one relation may be conditional in another.

The passage begins by discussing the obligation of ma'rifah and whether it is absolute or conditional. It defines the absolute obligatory (al-wajib al-mutlaq) and explains how the same obligation can be viewed as absolute or conditional depending on the perspective. This foundational concept is crucial for understanding the later argument about the obligation of reflection.

Commentary
This is a complex argumentative passage that addresses a key question in kalam: Does the obligation to know Allah necessitate the obligation to engage in rational reflection (nazar)? The passage begins by defining the obligation of knowledge (ma'rifah) and noting disagreement about whether it is absolute or conditional. It then defines the 'absolute obligatory' (al-wajib al-mutlaq) as that which does not depend on a premise for its obligation. The passage presents an objection (the ninth) which denies that the means to an absolute obligatory are legally obligatory. The response argues that knowledge is not intrinsically within human power; rather it is within power only through its cause, which is nazar. Therefore, commanding knowledge is in reality commanding its cause. The example of commanding to kill (which is actually a command to strike the sword) illustrates this point. The passage then distinguishes between a cause (sabab) that necessitates the effect and a condition (shart) that does not. Only with a cause does the command to the effect become a command to the cause. An alternative answer is also considered. The commentator's summary and remarks clarify the logical structure and address a previous objection about consensus. The passage concludes by noting that the commentator's verification implies a rejection of a certain refutation against the Samaniyya.

The Means to an Absolute Obligatory: Are They Obligatory?

Learning Objective

If an act is not directly within your power, the command is actually a command for its cause.

The ninth objection challenges the idea that the means to an absolute obligatory are themselves obligatory. Commentary
This is a complex argumentative passage that addresses a key question in kalam: Does the obligation to know Allah necessitate the obligation to engage in rational reflection (nazar)? The passage begins by defining the obligation of knowledge (ma'rifah) and noting disagreement about whether it is absolute or conditional. It then defines the 'absolute obligatory' (al-wajib al-mutlaq) as that which does not depend on a premise for its obligation. The passage presents an objection (the ninth) which denies that the means to an absolute obligatory are legally obligatory. The response argues that knowledge is not intrinsically within human power; rather it is within power only through its cause, which is nazar. Therefore, commanding knowledge is in reality commanding its cause. The example of commanding to kill (which is actually a command to strike the sword) illustrates this point. The passage then distinguishes between a cause (sabab) that necessitates the effect and a condition (shart) that does not. Only with a cause does the command to the effect become a command to the cause. An alternative answer is also considered. The commentator's summary and remarks clarify the logical structure and address a previous objection about consensus. The passage concludes by noting that the commentator's verification implies a rejection of a certain refutation against the Samaniyya.

The Difference Between a Cause and a Condition in Obligation
Elaborates the cause/condition distinction with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a cause (sabab) that necessitates the obligatory and a condition (shart) that does not, using the example of commanding to kill.

Core Idea
When the obligatory is not directly within power, the command is directed at its necessitating cause, not its condition.

The passage continues by elaborating on the principle that commanding an effect that is not directly within power is actually a command for its cause. The example of commanding to kill illustrates this: the command to kill is actually a command to strike with a sword. The passage then distinguishes this from mere conditions (shart) which do not necessitate the effect, such as purification for prayer.

Commentary
This passage presents and defends the Ash'arite argument that obligation (wujub) to know God is not rational ('aqli) but only through revelation (shar'). The argument proceeds as follows: If obligation were rational and established before the sending of messengers, then rational people before prophethood would have been obligated. Since they necessarily neglected some obligations (given their ignorance), they would deserve punishment. But the Qur'an (al-Isra 15) states that Allah does not punish until a messenger is sent, so such punishment is impossible. Therefore, obligation cannot be rational. The author then considers and refutes two objections: (1) That 'messenger' in the verse might mean 'reason' (because both guide)—this is rejected as a forced interpretation contrary to linguistic convention without evidence. (2) That the verse only negates punishment for neglecting legal obligations, not rational ones—again, this is rejected as an unwarranted restriction. The author further explains that the proof is initially presented as 'persuasive' (ilzami) against the Mu'tazilites, but it can be strengthened into a 'veridical' (tahqiqi) proof by noting that if rational obligation were true, one would not be safe from punishment, contradicting the verse which grants safety. The discussion also addresses a special case: the Shi'a, who accept rational obligation but allow divine pardon, thus negating the need for revelation exactly. Against them, the refined proof focusing on 'deservingness' (istihqaq) and lack of safety is effective. Finally, the author notes that the argument requires an additional premise: that rational people did indeed abandon obligations before prophethood, otherwise the chain fails. This entire section clarifies a foundational proof in kalam regarding the necessity of revelation for moral obligation.

Commentary
This passage presents and defends the Ash'arite argument that obligation (wujub) to know God is not rational ('aqli) but only through revelation (shar'). The argument proceeds as follows: If obligation were rational and established before the sending of messengers, then rational people before prophethood would have been obligated. Since they necessarily neglected some obligations (given their ignorance), they would deserve punishment. But the Qur'an (al-Isra 15) states that Allah does not punish until a messenger is sent, so such punishment is impossible. Therefore, obligation cannot be rational. The author then considers and refutes two objections: (1) That 'messenger' in the verse might mean 'reason' (because both guide)—this is rejected as a forced interpretation contrary to linguistic convention without evidence. (2) That the verse only negates punishment for neglecting legal obligations, not rational ones—again, this is rejected as an unwarranted restriction. The author further explains that the proof is initially presented as 'persuasive' (ilzami) against the Mu'tazilites, but it can be strengthened into a 'veridical' (tahqiqi) proof by noting that if rational obligation were true, one would not be safe from punishment, contradicting the verse which grants safety. The discussion also addresses a special case: the Shi'a, who accept rational obligation but allow divine pardon, thus negating the need for revelation exactly. Against them, the refined proof focusing on 'deservingness' (istihqaq) and lack of safety is effective. Finally, the author notes that the argument requires an additional premise: that rational people did indeed abandon obligations before prophethood, otherwise the chain fails. This entire section clarifies a foundational proof in kalam regarding the necessity of revelation for moral obligation.
