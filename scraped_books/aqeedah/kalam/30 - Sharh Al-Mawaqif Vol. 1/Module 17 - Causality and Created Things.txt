The Second Fallacy on Composition
Al-Sharh refutes a second fallacy concerning the distinction between the sum of parts and the whole.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the second fallacy concerning composition is actually an error based on confusing the sum of parts with the whole (quiddity).

Core Idea
The fallacy is not a refutation but a confusion between 'sum of parts' and 'whole'.

In this passage, the author critically examines a second fallacy presented in response to a doubt about composition. He points out three preliminary objections before revealing the core mistake: the commentator has conflated the notion of 'sum of all parts' with 'the whole as quiddity'. This lesson unpacks that error and shows why the alleged refutation fails.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation once to get an overview of the discussion.
The passage is dense; focus on the overall structure first.
2
Identify the three objections
The author begins by raising three objections (first, second, third) against the commentator's presentation. Notice the language: 'أما أولاً', 'أما ثانياً', 'أما ثالثاً'.
These objections are about the commentator's method, not the substance of the fallacy.
3
Understand the core fallacy
After the objections, the author explains that the real error is in the distinction between 'all the parts' and 'the whole' (which is the quiddity). This is the key insight.
4
See the application to existence
The author then shows how this confusion leads to faulty reasoning about existence: if parts are non-existent, existence would be pure non-existence; if parts are existent, the extra is existence. Both rely on mixing up parts and whole.
This demonstrates the fallacy's implications.
5
Reflect on the lesson
Consider why distinguishing the sum of parts from the whole is crucial in kalam arguments about composition.
Reflection helps internalize the concept.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis (بحث) of a second fallacy (المغلطة الثانية) presented by the commentator (al-Sharh) in response to a doubt. Similarly, the claim 'that extra is existence' assumes the parts are the sum of parts but are not existence, making the extra existence. The author concludes with 'so reflect' and invokes divine success.

Philosophers on origination only from matter

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Muslims affirm creation from nothing; philosophers require a material cause.

This passage presents a key point of contention between Muslim theologians and philosophers regarding the nature of origination (huduth). The philosophers argue that everything that comes into being must arise from pre-existing matter, while Muslims maintain that God can create things directly without any material substrate, such as indivisible atoms and incorporeal entities. The Ninth Proposition: Preponderance and the Free Agent
Philosophers' ninth proposition and Muslim exception on preponderance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophical principle that a possible requires a preponderator, and why Muslims make an exception for the powerful agent.

Core Idea
For possibles, preponderance needs a cause; but the Creator needs none.

This short but crucial proposition pits philosophers against Muslim theologians on whether a free agent can choose without a motive. The philosophers argue that every possible must have a preponderator to tip the scales from equivalence to existence. Muslims agree for all possible things except the powerful agent himself, who can preponderate one equal side over another without any external motive. This lesson unpacks their disagreement and its implications for divine freedom.

Refuting the Fourth Objection: Reasoning Leads to Knowledge by Custom
Presentation and refutation of the fourth shubha against reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Ashʿarī response to the fourth objection preserves the efficacy of theoretical reasoning by redefining entailment as a customary sequence.

Core Idea
Reasoning entails knowledge customarily, not necessarily—saving both causality and conditionality.

The fourth objection against theoretical reasoning presents a dilemma: either reasoning entails knowledge or it does not. Both horns seem problematic. The Ashʿarī response redefines what 'entailment' means—it is not a necessary causal connection but a customary sequence (ʿādah). This distinction resolves the apparent contradiction and upholds the role of reasoning in acquiring knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dilemma
The objection claims that if reasoning entails knowledge, it cannot be conditioned by its absence, but it is so conditioned. If it does not entail knowledge, it is useless. Notice the two alternatives.
This is a classic logical dilemma.
2
Learn the Ashʿarī response
The response distinguishes between necessary entailment (istilzām) and customary consequence (istiʿqāb). Reasoning leads to knowledge by habit or divine custom, not as an efficient cause.
This preserves the condition of ignorance beforehand.
3
See how the response avoids contradiction
Because knowledge follows reasoning in time, it does not coexist with the reasoning process. The condition of ignorance applies only to the period before completion.
The commentator also dismisses a subtle attempt to restrict the condition to the beginning of reasoning.

The fourth objection: Either (reasoning) entails knowledge of the object of reasoning or not. The first is incompatible with the condition that lack of knowledge of the object of reasoning is a condition for it (reasoning), because absence of the necessary condition is incompatible with the existence of the conditioned, so it would not be a condition for it. But lack of knowledge of the object of reasoning is a condition for reasoning, to avoid obtaining the obtained (taḥṣīl al-ḥāṣil) as will come. And the second, that reasoning does not entail knowledge of the object of reasoning, is the desired conclusion. We say: It entails it in the sense that it leads to it customarily (ʿādatan), as is our doctrine, or by preparation (iʿdādan) or generation (tawlīdan) according to the doctrine of the philosophers and the Muʿtazila. So when reasoning is completed, knowledge occurs, just as when a physical movement is completed, one reaches the place intended for attainment by it. Not in the sense that it is an efficient cause (ʿilla mūjiba) for it, like the movement of the hand causing the movement of the key, such that they would necessarily coexist in time simultaneously. And this entailment (istilzām) which is in the sense of being consequent (istiʿqāb) does not conflict with the condition that lack of knowledge of the object of reasoning is a condition for it. His statement: (Reasoning either entails, etc.) Its exposition: If reasoning were useful for knowledge, then either it entails knowledge of the object of reasoning or not. The first is false, so the second is determined, which is the desired conclusion. His statement: (And the first is incompatible, etc.) Means that reasoning, because it consists of two movements or of an arrangement that is a necessary condition for them, is a temporal matter that occurs in the entire time whose beginning is the object of reasoning being perceived in some way, and its end is obtaining the object. So if it entailed knowledge, it would coexist with it throughout that entire time, while it is conditioned by the absence of knowledge throughout that entire time. Thus the coexistence of knowledge of the object and its absence in that time would follow, which is impossible. Based on what we have mentioned, it appears that what was said – that the entailer is the complete reasoning and the absence of knowledge of the object of reasoning is a condition during reasoning and at its beginning, not at its completion – is nothing, whose source is lack of reflection. It was said: This objection also applies to the claim that sensation yields knowledge, and the response is that they do not claim that sensation yields knowledge in the sense that sensation does not ever fail to be followed by it, for sensation is often mistaken. Rather, knowledge may sometimes follow upon it, so no refutation. His statement: (It entails it in the sense that it is consequent upon it, etc.) Its summary: If you intend by entailment (istilzām) the consequent (istiʿqāb), i.e., its occurrence after reasoning without delay, then we choose the first alternative and we do not accept the mentioned incompatibility because of the difference in the times of knowledge and its absence. And if you intend the impossibility of separation in existence, then we choose the second alternative and we do not accept that the desired conclusion is obtained, i.e., that reasoning does not yield knowledge, because it is consequent upon it without delay.

This passage presents and refutes the fourth objection (shubha) against the efficacy of theoretical reasoning (naẓar). The objection is a dilemma: either reasoning entails knowledge of its object or not. If it entails knowledge, then it cannot be conditioned by the absence of that knowledge, because the absence of the necessitated is incompatible with the existence of the necessitator. But reasoning is indeed conditioned by the absence of knowledge (to avoid obtaining the obtained). If it does not entail knowledge, then reasoning is useless, which is the opponent's conclusion. The response is that reasoning entails knowledge in the sense of being followed by it customarily (ʿādatan), not as a necessary efficient cause. This is the Ashʿarī position (customary sequence), while the philosophers and Muʿtazila speak of preparation (iʿdād) or generation (tawlīd). When reasoning is complete, knowledge arises, just as physical movement leads to reaching a destination. This kind of entailment does not require simultaneous existence, so the condition of prior ignorance is not violated. The commentator also addresses a nuance: the thorough objection considers that complete reasoning is the entailer and that the condition of ignorance holds only during reasoning, not at its completion. He dismisses this as lack of reflection. He further notes that the objection would also apply to sensation yielding knowledge, but the response is that sensation does not always yield knowledge (it can err), and knowledge may follow it without entailment. Finally, he summarizes: if the opponent means entailment as necessary concomitance, the Ashʿarī chooses the first horn (entailment) but denies incompatibility due to different times; if they mean impossibility of separation, he chooses the second horn but denies that reasoning does not yield knowledge, because it is followed by knowledge without delay.

Commentary
This passage presents the Ash'ari doctrine on the relationship between reflection (nazar) and knowledge. It begins by establishing that God is the sole agent: nothing is necessary from Him or upon Him by essence, but He acts by choice. There is no causal connection between events; rather, God creates events in succession by His habit (adah). For example, fire does not cause burning by its nature; God creates burning after contact as a habit. Therefore, miracles are possible as habitual breaks. The passage then applies this to knowledge after reflection: knowledge is a contingent, originated entity that requires an effecter, and only God is the effecter. So knowledge is God's act, created after reflection, but not necessitated by it. The commentary clarifies key points: 'no thing is necessary from Him' means by essence, so it does not contradict obligation through choice; 'no thing is necessary upon Him' by essence, so it does not contradict obligation through promise. It explains that the absence of causal connection indicates God's direct power over each event. The discussion of 'constant or most often' addresses how a miracle is defined: if an act is not preceded by its like, it is extraordinary; but repetition does not negate extraordinariness if the first instance was extraordinary. It then deals with an objection about repeated miracles of prophets—reply: the extraordinariness is in the particular instance, while the general habit of God is to create miracles at their hands. It also notes the difficulty of claiming non-repetition for saintly wonders. Finally, it qualifies the claim that correct reflection always yields knowledge: it yields knowledge constantly or most often, accounting for obstacles like heedlessness or death. The commentator concludes that the qualified predicate ('constant or most often') is established for every correct reflection, preventing an absolute claim that might be refuted.

The Ash'ari Refutation via Analogy to Recollection
Al-Iji presents the Ash'ari use of analogy to refute the Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari argument that since recollected reflection does not generate knowledge, initial reflection must also not generate knowledge.

Core Idea
If recollection of reflection doesn't generate knowledge, neither can initial reflection.

The Ash'aris counter the Mu'tazila by pointing to an agreed-upon case: when one recollects a previous reflection, that recollection does not generate new knowledge. Since there is no relevant difference between initial and recollected reflection, initial reflection must also not generate knowledge. This analogical argument forces the Mu'tazila to explain the difference.

Guided Path

2
Follow the analogy
The Ash'aris analogize initial reflection to recollection, arguing that if recollection doesn't generate, then initial reflection should be similar.
This is a logical analogy based on the entailment of knowledge.
3
Understand the reasoning
There is no difference between initial and recollected reflection in terms of the relation that would entail knowledge (the matter and form are the same). Only the time differs, which is irrelevant.

The conclusion is that the companions' analogy is actually a compound analogy: it avoids proving the ruling in the original case by securing the opponent's agreement. The opponent is seen as either denying the commonality in the branch or denying the ruling in the original. The opponent's position is that non-generation in recollection is due to lack of power; if that holds, the cause is absent in initial investigation; if not, then non-generation is denied.

Commentary
This passage is an extended refutation and clarification. The commentary then explains that the author of the original text restricted validity to deleting the condition of direct attribution, even though he had initially listed three incompatible premises. The reason is that the condition of direct attribution already entails the other two (God's free will and no obligation), so deleting it automatically suffices. This interpretation refutes an objection that the doctrine would still be valid without deleting the condition if direct attribution is understood in the second sense (God creates through intermediaries). The overall argument shows how the position on knowledge generation can be reconciled with free will and divine power by properly understanding 'ibtidā''.

Commentary
This passage defines and proves the principle that when an obligatory act (like knowing God) is not directly within human power, the divine command to it is actually a command to its cause (like speculation) which is within power. The author uses the analogy of killing: the taking of life is not possible directly, so the command to kill is actually a command to strike with a sword, the cause that necessitates death. He then presents a key distinction: if the premise (cause) is necessitating (mustalzim) of the act such that the act cannot occur without it, then the obligation of the act is truly an obligation of the premise. This is because power attaches to the cause, not the effect directly. However, if the premise is merely a condition (shart) that does not necessitate the act (like purification for prayer), then the act itself is within power, and the command to the act does not become a command to the condition.

Commentary
This passage presents a third doubt of the deniers of resurrection, likely from philosophers. They argue that resurrection, as described in religious texts, requires the annihilation (إعدام) of this world and the creation of a new one, which they consider impossible based on their principles (e.g., the eternity of the world or the impossibility of a vacuum). The author's answer begins by noting that if the opponent already acknowledges God as the Creator of the heavens and earth, then He must be capable of annihilating them. The argument relies on the contingency of the world: whatever can come into existence can also cease to exist, unless it is necessary. Since the world is created, it is possible for it to be destroyed, and the same power that created can recreate. The passage stresses that admitting divine creation logically entails admitting divine power over annihilation and re-creation.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think that God's power is limited to initial creation and does not extend to destroying and recreating the world.
Correction: The passage argues that if God is the Creator, He must also be capable of annihilation and re-creation, as the same power that brings about existence can remove it.

Key Takeaway
The theologian refutes the philosophical doubt by showing that belief in God as Creator logically entails His power to annihilate and recreate.
