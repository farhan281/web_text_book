Rational Connection in False Premises: Validity vs. Truth
Argues that false premises can have a rational connection and distinguishes truth from validity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that logical entailment does not depend on the truth of premises; false premises can still yield a valid conclusion, and error lies in material falsity, not the logical form.

Core Idea
A valid argument can have false premises; truth is separate from validity.

This passage from al-Mawaqif clarifies a common confusion: many think that a good argument must have true premises. Al-Ījī argues that the rational connection that produces entailment works regardless of the truth value. The difference between true and false premises is only in whether the premise actually holds in reality — that doesn't affect the logical link itself. Let's read the text to see this crucial distinction.

2

The passage begins by stating that it is possible for false propositions to have a rational connection that makes them entail each other. Look for that claim.
3

The text says there is no difference between true and false premises in the first figure regarding the entailment of the conclusion. Understand why: the logical form alone produces the entailment.
4
Understand the source of error
Error is not in the logical connection but in the truth of the premises. The passage explains that the connection itself is sound, but the premises may be false.
5
Contrast with the second usage of 'proof'
The passage ends with a contrasting terminology: when a single term like 'world' is taken as a proof of the Maker, the rational connection is intrinsic and certain knowledge follows from that proof.
This is a different usage, but it reinforces the idea that rational connection can exist even if the premise is false in a different sense.

Commentary
This passage makes a crucial distinction between formal logical entailment and material truth. It first asserts that false propositions can have a rational connection (rābiṭa ʿaqliyya) that makes some entail others. For example, in a valid syllogistic form like the first figure, the entailment of the conclusion does not depend on the truth of the premises; it works regardless. The difference between true and false premises is only in the actual realization (taḥaqquq) of the premise (the malzūm) in reality. This has no bearing on the logical entailment itself. Thus, when we see an error in incorrect consideration, it is not due to a flaw in the rational connection, but rather due to the falsity of the premises. Knowledge or ignorance depends on whether one believes the premises to be true. The passage then contrasts this with the terminology used by those who treat a single concept (e.g., 'world') as a proof (dalīl) of its cause (e.g., the Maker). In that usage, the rational connection is intrinsic and certain knowledge follows from the proof itself. The overall point is that the formal structure of argumentation is neutral with respect to truth; the epistemic outcome (knowledge or ignorance) hinges on belief in the premises.

Does Correct Reasoning Always Yield Knowledge?
Al-Jurjani examines an objection to the claim that correct reasoning yields knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why correct reasoning alone does not guarantee knowledge without belief in the truth of the premises.

Core Idea
Correct reasoning is not enough; you must also believe the premises.

This passage addresses a critical objection to a foundational claim in Islamic logic and theology: that correct reasoning necessarily leads to knowledge. The objection points out that even if the reasoning is formally valid, a person may not actually accept the premises, and thus no knowledge results. Al-Jurjani responds by distinguishing between logical necessity and psychological belief, affirming that the rational connection between premises and conclusion is independent of their truth.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Start by reading the objection that correct reasoning might not yield knowledge because one may not believe the premises. Note how the objector frames the issue.
The objection is at the beginning of the passage.
2
Follow the author's response
Al-Jurjani first refutes the idea that without belief there is no correct reasoning, then introduces the crucial concept of a rational connection that can exist even among false propositions.
Look for the phrase 'التحقيق أنه لا استحالة'.
3
Understand the role of logical form
Notice the example of the first figure: true and false premises can have the same logical necessitation. The difference is in the actual truth of the premises, not the logical structure.
This shows that validity does not depend on truth.
4
Grasp the necessity of belief
Conclude by recognizing that knowledge (al-‘ilm) depends on believing the premises to be true. Without that belief, even correct reasoning does not produce knowledge.
This is the key takeaway against the misconception.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer (i‘tirāḍ wa-jawāb) addressing whether correct reasoning (al-naẓar al-ṣaḥīḥ) necessarily yields knowledge (al-‘ilm). The objection argues that even if one performs correct reasoning, one may not believe the premises to be true, and then no knowledge results. The answer distinguishes between the logical structure (the rational connection) and the truth of the premises. The author (al-Jurjānī) argues that the logical necessitation of the conclusion from premises does not depend on the truth of the premises—false premises can still be connected by a rational relation that necessitates a conclusion in the abstract. The difference between a valid and an invalid argument lies not in the logical form but in the actual truth of the premises. Knowledge results only when the premises are believed to be true and are in fact true. The discussion then extends to the nature of proof (dalīl) as a single term versus compound premises, but the central point remains: correct reasoning alone does not entail knowledge without belief in the truth of the premises.

Invalid Reasoning: Matter vs. Form
Classification of invalid reasoning and its relation to ignorance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two types of invalidity in reasoning (matter vs. form) and why invalid form produces no belief at all.

Core Idea
Invalid form yields no belief; invalid matter may yield truth.

This passage from al-Jurjani's commentary examines invalid reasoning and its connection to ignorance. It distinguishes between invalidity in matter (false premises) and invalidity in form (logical structure), showing that only the former can sometimes lead to belief, while the latter produces none. The ninth purpose introduces the condition of attentiveness as necessary for reasoning to yield knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation. Note the structure: it begins with the ninth purpose, then discusses types of invalidity.
The passage uses parentheses from the original commentary.
2
Identify the three types of invalidity
Find where the passage distinguishes invalidity in matter only, form only, and both together. This is a key classification in logic.
3
Understand the effect of matter-only invalidity
Notice that the passage argues that invalidity in matter does not always lead to ignorance; it can sometimes produce true conclusions, as shown by the example 'Zayd is a donkey'.
This counters a common assumption that bad premises always lead to false conclusions.
4
Understand the effect of form-only or both invalidity
Learn that when the logical form is corrupt (whether premises are true or false), no belief at all is produced—neither true nor false.
This is why valid form is necessary for reasoning to produce knowledge.
5

Relate the discussion back to the ninth purpose: reasoning only yields knowledge if the reasoner is attentive to the rational connection.
This condition is from Ibn Sina and is a prerequisite for knowledge.

The author first presents a possible objection: it might be thought that the face of indication could be distinct from both the proof and the proven, but then it would have to be either inside the existence of what is other than God (i.e., part of the world) or inside God's existence, which is impossible. The answer given is that the face of indication is distinct but not as an external existent; it is a mental consideration (أمر اعتباري) like possibility (الإمكان) and origination (الحدوث)—these are not extra beings but relations or mental constructs. Objection to the Mental Consideration Answer
Objection to the mental consideration answer based on the definition of distinctness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that the mutakallimun's concept of distinctness requires external existence, and why the mental consideration answer is challenged.

Core Idea
For mutakallimun, distinct things must exist externally—so a mental consideration cannot be truly distinct.

After explaining that the face of indication is a mental consideration, the commentator presents an objection from the mutakallimun: they define 'distinct' (mutaghayiran) as two things that exist in the external world. If the face of indication is not externally existing, then it cannot be considered distinct in the proper sense. This objection raises a deep issue about the ontology of relations in kalam.

Guided Path

Understand the objector's reasoning
