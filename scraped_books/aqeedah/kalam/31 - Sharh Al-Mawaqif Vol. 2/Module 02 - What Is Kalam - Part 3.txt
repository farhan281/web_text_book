Reality Is Existence and Quiddity Requires Existence
Defines reality as identical to existence and clarifies the condition for quiddity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that in Islamic kalam, the reality (taraqqur) of a thing is identical to its existence, and that a quiddity, when determined, must have either external or mental existence.

Core Idea
Reality and existence are the same; a realized quiddity cannot be without existence.

This lesson explores a foundational principle of kalam ontology: the identity of existence and reality, and the necessary condition that a determined quiddity must possess either external or mental existence. The passage clarifies a common misunderstanding about quiddity's relation to existence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice the main thesis and the subsequent clarification.
The passage uses a conditional structure.
2
Identify the two existences
The passage mentions 'one of the two existences'. Recognize that these are external existence (al-wujūd al-khārijī) and mental existence (al-wujūd al-dhihnī).
These are the only two modes of existence in kalam.
3
Grasp the conditional nature
The statement applies only to quiddities that are 'determined' (mutaraqqira) and 'realized' (mutaḥaqqiqa). A quiddity that is neither external nor conceived is free of both existences.
This avoids the misunderstanding that every quiddity necessarily has existence.
4
Understand the synonymity claim
The commentary equates determination (taraqqur) with existence (wujūd). This is the core theoretical claim: reality is nothing other than existence.
This is a key principle of the Ash'ari school.

Commentary
This passage defines the relationship between existence and reality (traqarrur). It begins with a core statement: when something is determined and realized, it necessarily exists in one of two ways—either externally or mentally. The commentary clarifies that this does not mean every quiddity (māhiyya) always has existence; a quiddity that is nonexistent in the external world and unconceived has no existence at all. The point is that whenever quiddity is determined or realized, determination is precisely the same as existence in the view of these scholars. Thus, the commentary emphasizes that the intended meaning is conditional: given determination, existence is inseparable.

The Five Weak Proofs for the Non-Existent
The author lists five weak proofs for the non-existent being a thing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the five weak arguments that were proposed to support the claim that the non-existent is a thing, and why the author dismisses them as weak.

Core Idea
The weak proofs confuse the state of non-existence with existence.

Before presenting his own argument, the author lists five arguments that others have used to claim that the non-existent is a thing. These arguments are all considered weak because they fail to properly distinguish between the state of non-existence and the state of existence. In this lesson, we will examine each of these arguments and understand their shortcomings.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once to get an overview of the five aspects. Then read the translation to understand the arguments.
Notice that the author groups them as 'weak paths'.
2
Identify each weak proof
The passage enumerates several points. Identify the first about the impossibility of succession, the second and third about particularizers and multiplicity, and the fourth about essence requiring unity.
The text says 'five aspects' but only four are explicitly listed; the fifth may be implied.
3
Understand why they are weak
Reflect on why each argument fails. The author considers them weak because they are based on assumptions about attributes that are not valid in the state of non-existence.
Compare these with the valid proof in the next lesson.

Commentary
This passage presents the sixth objective of the first observation: whether the non-existent (al-maʿdūm) is a thing. The author first enumerates five weak proofs (masālik ḍaʿīfa) that might be adduced for the existence of the non-existent. These include: (1) the impossibility of the succession of attributive attributes upon it while being established in non-existence, (2) the argument that particularizers only apply when things come into existence and not in the state of non-existence, (3) the notion that multiplicity does not exist in non-existence, (4) the idea that the essence under condition of non-existence requires unity, which ceases upon existence. The author dismisses these as weak. Then he presents the first of two relied-upon proofs (al-muʿtamad): the statement that the non-existent exists in the state of non-existence would negate the ability to be an object of divine power (al-maqdūriyya), because essences (al-dhāt) would be eternal and thus not subject to power. The commentary elaborates on this proof. It explains that this proof is argumentative (ilzāmī), composed of premises that are considered established: essences are eternal, eternity precludes being an object of power, and existence is a state (ḥāl). The proof forces the opponent to accept a contradiction: either the effect of power is in the state (which the opponent denies) or the effect is not in the state (which leads to impossibility). The commentary also discusses al-Abharī's responses and objections, including the issue of whether the essence (al-māhiyya) in non-existence is single or multiple. The commentator concludes by noting that the later discussion of the essence being 'made' (majʿūla) will clarify that power attaches to making the essence qualified by existence, not to the essence itself.

The First Valid Proof: The Argument from Power
The author presents the first valid proof that the non-existent cannot be a thing, and explains its logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first valid proof that the non-existent cannot be a thing, based on the fact that if it were established in non-existence, it would be eternal and thus not subject to divine power.

Core Idea
If the non-existent were a thing, it would be eternal and beyond God's power.

After dismissing the weak proofs, the author presents the first of two relied-upon proofs. This proof is argumentative (ilzāmī) and forces the opponent to accept a contradiction. The key idea is that if the non-existent were a thing (thābita) in the state of non-existence, it would be eternal (azaliyya), and what is eternal cannot be an object of divine power. In this lesson, we will unpack this argument and see how it is defended against objections.

The proof is concise but contains key premises.

This is based on the Ash'ari understanding of power.
3
Follow the ilzāmī structure
The author explains that the proof is argumentative, forcing the opponent to choose between two impossible options. Walk through the two options and see why both lead to contradictions.
The commentary elaborates on this.

Consider the objections and responses

This part is more advanced; focus on the core argument.

Commentary
This passage defines the four attributes of the indivisible atom (al-jawhar al-fard) and classifies the types of attributes. Classification of Attributes into Three Types
Al-Jurjani classifies attributes of accidents into three types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three types of attributes (descriptions) according to Al-Jurjani: accidentalness (ʿaraḍiyya), existence, and obtaining in a locus.

Core Idea
Attributes are classified into three types: what persists in existence and non-existence, what is from the agent, and what follows existence.

After discussing the atom's attributes, Al-Jurjani turns to the classification of accidents. He delineates three types of descriptions: accidentalness (which belongs to genera), existence (from the agent), and obtaining in a locus (which follows existence). This classification clarifies the causal relationships underlying accidental qualities.

Guided Path
1
Read the second part of the passage
Read from 'وللأعراض الأنواع الثلاثة الأول' to the end, along with the translation.
Focus on the three types listed and their definitions.
2
Identify the three types
The three types are: (1) al-waṣf al-ḥāṣil fī ḥālatay al-wujūd wa al-ʿadam, which is al-ʿaraḍiyya; (2) mā bi al-fāʿil, which is al-wujūd; (3) al-ṣifa al-tābiʿa li al-wujūd, which is al-ḥuṣūl fī al-maḥall.
Note that the first type is an attribute of genera, not of individuals.
3
Understand the relationship between accidentalness and inherence
The author clarifies that accidentalness alone is not the cause of inherence; existence must be present. Also, the cause of obtaining in the locus is not an additional entity beyond the accident itself.
This distinguishes the role of existence as a prerequisite.

Is the Non-Existent a Thing?
Al-Mawaqif presents the issue and positions on attributes of the non-existent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the classical theological debate about whether non-existents can have attributes, and the different positions of al-Shahham and al-Basri.

Core Idea
The non-existent's attributes depend on existence — a key debate in kalam.

This passage from al-Mawaqif introduces a central issue in Islamic theology: can non-existent things (like the world before creation) possess attributes? The text presents two major positions — those who affirm substantiality for non-existents but differ on spatiality, and a unique view that non-existence itself can be a property. The passage also reminds us that even after knowing God's attributes, His existence still requires proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the original Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the translation. Let the author's own words set the stage.
2
Identify the two scholars
Find the names al-Shahham and al-Basri. Note each one's key claim about the non-existent.
Al-Shahham and al-Basri both affirm substantiality and being in space, but with a difference.
3
Understand al-Basri's unique point
Notice that al-Basri is distinguished by affirming non-existence as a property. Others deny that the non-existent has any property by virtue of being non-existent.
4
Reflect on the final agreement
All parties agree that after knowing God's attributes, one still needs proof to demonstrate His existence. This connects to the broader theme of rational theology.
This shows that attributes alone do not imply existence.

First Observation - Sixth Purpose: Is the Non-Existent a Thing? Al-Shahham affirms both (the substance and being in space) because they are identical, and it is not possible for the substance not to be a substance with the affirmation of being in space, because it is an effect of spatiality, so it does not separate from it. And Abu Abdullah al-Basri affirms both because of their identity and the impossibility of the negation of substantiality without being in space, for it is an effect of spatiality conditional upon existence, so it is not affirmed in the state of non-existence. And he (al-Basri) is distinguished among them by affirming non-existence as a property, and all others agreed that the non-existent does not have, by virtue of being non-existent, a property. And all (i.e., all those who affirm that non-existents are fixed and qualified by properties) agreed that after knowing that the world has a Maker powerful, knowing, living, one needs to prove Him (i.e., demonstrate His existence) by proof.

Commentary
This passage is the original text (matn) of al-Mawaqif. It opens by stating the problem: whether the non-existent (المعدوم) is a thing. It then presents the view of al-Shahham, who affirms both substantiality and being in space for the non-existent because they are identical, and the denial of substantiality while affirming being in space is impossible. Abu Abdullah al-Basri also affirms both but distinguishes: he affirms substantiality but not being in space, because being in space is conditional upon existence. Al-Basri is unique in affirming 'non-existence' itself as a property (صفة). All other theologians agree that the non-existent does not have non-existence as a property. This sets up the subsequent discussion on whether attributes imply existence.
