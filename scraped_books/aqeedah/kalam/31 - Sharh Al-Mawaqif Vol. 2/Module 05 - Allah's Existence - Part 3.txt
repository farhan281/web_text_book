The Third Proof for Addition of Existence and Its Initial Response
Al-Mawaqif presents the third proof for addition of existence and the response that necessity is the essence itself.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third proof that existence is additional to the essence of the Necessary Being and the initial response that necessity is identical to the essence.

Core Idea
The third proof treats necessity as a relation, but the response affirms necessity is the essence itself.

In this passage, al-Mawaqif presents a third argument for the addition of existence to the divine essence. The argument claims that essential necessity is a relation requiring two sides: essence and existence, so existence must be additional. The response, however, denies that necessity is a relation; instead, it identifies necessity with the essence itself. As you read, notice how the argument hinges on the nature of necessity.

Core Idea

This passage from the commentary critiques an initial answer and establishes the correct definition of the Necessary Being (wajib) according to the philosophers. It addresses a potential contradiction in Ibn Sina's works and shows that his statements are consistent. The lesson will guide you through the commentary's argument step by step.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the main text's proof and response. It first critiques the initial response (denying necessity as addition) by showing it entails a contradiction with the author's own position that existence is identical to essence (a position of al-Ash'ari). Then it discusses the definition of 'wajib' (the Necessary Being). The philosophers (hukama') do not define it as 'that whose essence demands its existence' but as 'self-sufficient from others' (mustaghni 'an al-ghayr). A quote from Ibn Sina's Ilahiyyat al-Shifa' is given to support the division into necessary and contingent based on rational analysis, not external existence. However, a letter from Ibn Sina defines wajib as 'that whose existence is not from another', which implies self-necessitation. The commentary reconciles this by noting that the letter's definition implies that the essence demands existence, while the Shifa' division is rational and does not contradict that. The passage ends by noting that the correct answer (al-sawab) will mention that wujub has three meanings: self-sufficiency, etc.

Two Interpretations of Essential Necessity
Two interpretations of essential necessity and their analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two ways essential necessity (al-wujub al-dhati) is interpreted and why it is considered a secondary intelligible (ma'qul thani).

Core Idea
Essential necessity is either a negative attribute or an intellectual abstraction from the essence.

The concept of essential necessity lies at the heart of theological arguments for God's existence. This commentary clarifies two distinct interpretations: one as self-sufficiency (istiqhna'), a negative concept, and another as the essence's demand (iqtida') for existence, which leads to a discussion of how the intellect abstracts universal concepts. Reading closely will reveal how necessity is not a real attribute but a mental consideration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two interpretations
The commentary starts by presenting two interpretations of wujub dhati: 1) as self-sufficiency from others (istiqhna'), and 2) as the essence's demand for existence. Read carefully to see how each is defined.
The first interpretation is negative, the second positive.
2
Understand the role of muwata'ah
The second interpretation uses the concept of muwata'ah (concomitance). This means the particular existence (the essence) 'demands' absolute existence not as a real relation but as an abstraction: the intellect notices that the particular existence shares effects with contingents and thus abstracts the universal 'existence' from it. Grasp this distinction.
Muwata'ah is an intellectual operation, not a causal relation.
3
Recognize necessity as a secondary intelligible
Because the demand is a mental abstraction, necessity (wujub) belongs to the category of secondary intelligibles (al-ma'qulat al-thaniyya). This means it does not exist externally but is a concept derived by the mind after considering primary realities.
This is crucial for later arguments about the nature of God's attributes.

And if it is interpreted as the essence's demand for existence, we say: his particular existence — which is his essence — demands by its essence its accident which is absolute existence. If you say: so too the rest [of judgments], for possibility and impossibility and the inference to it being among the mental considerations, and the judgment that it is a modality of the relation of existence to essence and the rest of its judgments indicate that it is an addition, and its being in another sense the essence itself does not repel the inference in this sense. His saying: (If necessity is interpreted etc.) since its being an addition between the two terms applies to both interpretations, because self-sufficiency is the absence of need, and need is an addition, he answered both interpretations, although if the objection is restricted to the first interpretation, it cuts off the material of inference. His saying: (to the realization of two things) rather to the conceptualization of two things: the essence and existence, or rather three things. His saying: (demands by its essence etc.) what is meant by it is not the demand of a described for a description, because then the objection concerning other existences would not occur, but rather the demand of an individual for the truth of the universal about it by way of concomitance (muwata'ah). That is, when the intellect observes that particular existence and notices its participation with contingent existence in the ordering of effects upon them, it abstracts from it absolute existence and judges that it demands it. Thus necessity belongs to the secondary intelligibles. Then if that existence is independent in demanding the truth of the absolute about it, it is self-subsisting, so it exists by itself. So its independent demand — because it is existence — demands that it is by its essence existent, i.e., it demands its attribution with existence as an abstract attribution not a real one; otherwise it would not be existent by itself. So its independent demand for existence by concomitance necessitates its demand by its essence for existence by derivation. Thus the doubt that the commentator al-Qushji mentioned is repelled: that the necessary is that whose essence demands that it is existent, not existence, just as the impossible is that whose essence demands that it is non-existent, not non-existence; if it were like that, then the impossibles whose essences demand that they are non-existent would be included in the contingent, because the basis of the commentator's speech is that its demand for existence independently by concomitance necessitates its demand for existence by derivation, not that necessity is an expression of that from the other and its demand for its existence. Core Idea
The necessary being demands existence by concomitance, which implies derivation, and only one real existence belongs to it.

Al-Qushji raised a subtle objection: the necessary being's essence demands that it is 'existent' (mawjud), not 'existence' (wujud) itself. This could imply that impossibles are contingent. The commentary responds by distinguishing between two types of demand: concomitance (muwata'ah) and derivation (ishtiqaq). Guided Path
1
Understand al-Qushji's objection
Al-Qushji argues that the necessary is that whose essence demands that it is existent (mawjud), not existence (wujud). Similarly, the impossible demands non-existence (ma'dum), not non-existence ('adam). This distinction is meant to avoid an infinite regress.
If the necessary demanded existence, one could ask for another existence, etc.
2
Follow the commentary's response
The commentary says that the independent demand for existence by concomitance (muwata'ah) entails a demand by derivation (ishtiqaq). That is, if the particular existence independently demands absolute existence by concomitance, then it also demands existence by derivation — meaning the essence is 'existent' (mawjud) by that existence. So the objection is repelled.
The key is that muwata'ah and ishtiqaq are two sides of the same coin.
3
Resolve the two-existences issue
Another objection arises: if the necessary has a particular existence that demands absolute existence, it might be existent by two existences. The commentary refutes this by stating that the particular existence is the essence itself, and absolute existence is a mere concept. Thus, there is only one real existence.
The absolute existence is not a distinct entity but an abstraction.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed kalam discussion on whether existence (al-wujud) is identical to essence (al-mahiyya) or part of it, specifically regarding the Necessary Being (al-wajib). The author, al-Sharif al-Jurjani, is responding to multiple objections and interpretations.

He begins by distinguishing two interpretations of essential necessity (al-wujub al-dhati): (1) as independence from others in existence (a privative concept), and (2) as the essence's requirement for existence (a positive relation). Under the second interpretation, he argues that the particular existence (al-wujud al-khas) which is the essence itself requires absolute existence (al-wujud al-mutlaq) as an accident. This is done through 'correspondence' (mutaba'ah): when the intellect observes a particular existence and notes its effects, it abstracts the universal concept of existence. Thus necessity is a secondary intelligible. He then addresses a criticism from al-Sharh al-Qushji: that the Necessary requires being existent (kawnuhu mawjudan) not existence (wujud). Al-Jurjani's response is that the independent requirement via correspondence entails a derivative requirement for existence, so the criticism fails.

Next, he treats an objection: the requirement of the particular for the absolute means the particular is an individual of the absolute, but the Necessary requires being existent, not existence. The answer distinguishes: God's particular existence requires being existent by absolute existence, not being an individual. This is refuted by the charge of two existences. Al-Jurjani defends by noting that if the Necessary is a particular existence, it is not two existences; one is the essence, the other is its existence, so it has one existence. He discusses abstractive vs. real attribution: the description of the essence with absolute existence is abstractive (intiza'i), not real, so no multiplicity arises. The principle of abstraction is the particular existence itself, so the absolute is an accident of the universal to its individual.

He then refutes the claim that the accidentally is purely mental: if abstractive, the intellect merely extracts the universal from the external particular without requiring the intellect's existence. He then cites Ibn Sina's al-Isharat to show that the philosophers do affirm the Necessary in the sense of essence requiring existence. An objector then says this entails an essence and distinct existence, negating identity. Al-Jurjani responds by presenting a threefold classification of ranks of existence: (a) lowest: exists by another, separation conceivable; (b) middle: exists by itself with a different existence, separation inconceivable but conceivable; (c) highest: exists by itself with identical existence, separation and its conception impossible. He argues that the philosophers' goal is not this classification but to avoid priority of essence over existence. Even if we accept the classification, the remaining issue is whether the accidentally of the absolute is external or mental.

Responding to al-Qushji: From Correspondence to Derivation
Al-Jurjani refutes al-Qushji's objection by connecting correspondence to derivation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Jurjani responds to al-Qushji's objection that the Necessary requires being existent, not existence, by showing that independent correspondence entails requirement by derivation.

Core Idea
Independent correspondence implies derivation, so the Necessary requires existence itself.

Al-Qushji raised an important objection: if the essence requires existence, it does so in the sense of being existent, not existence itself. Al-Jurjani's response is subtle: he argues that the independent requirement of the particular existence for the universal via correspondence (مواطأة) entails a requirement by derivation (اشتقاق) for existence itself. This resolves the apparent distinction between 'being existent' and 'existence'.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed kalam discussion on whether existence (al-wujud) is identical to essence (al-mahiyya) or part of it, specifically regarding the Necessary Being (al-wajib). The author, al-Sharif al-Jurjani, is responding to multiple objections and interpretations.

He begins by distinguishing two interpretations of essential necessity (al-wujub al-dhati): (1) as independence from others in existence (a privative concept), and (2) as the essence's requirement for existence (a positive relation). Under the second interpretation, he argues that the particular existence (al-wujud al-khas) which is the essence itself requires absolute existence (al-wujud al-mutlaq) as an accident. This is done through 'correspondence' (mutaba'ah): when the intellect observes a particular existence and notes its effects, it abstracts the universal concept of existence. Thus necessity is a secondary intelligible. He then addresses a criticism from al-Sharh al-Qushji: that the Necessary requires being existent (kawnuhu mawjudan) not existence (wujud). Al-Jurjani's response is that the independent requirement via correspondence entails a derivative requirement for existence, so the criticism fails.

Next, he treats an objection: the requirement of the particular for the absolute means the particular is an individual of the absolute, but the Necessary requires being existent, not existence. The answer distinguishes: God's particular existence requires being existent by absolute existence, not being an individual. This is refuted by the charge of two existences. Al-Jurjani defends by noting that if the Necessary is a particular existence, it is not two existences; one is the essence, the other is its existence, so it has one existence. He discusses abstractive vs. real attribution: the description of the essence with absolute existence is abstractive (intiza'i), not real, so no multiplicity arises. The principle of abstraction is the particular existence itself, so the absolute is an accident of the universal to its individual.

He then refutes the claim that the accidentally is purely mental: if abstractive, the intellect merely extracts the universal from the external particular without requiring the intellect's existence. He then cites Ibn Sina's al-Isharat to show that the philosophers do affirm the Necessary in the sense of essence requiring existence. An objector then says this entails an essence and distinct existence, negating identity. Al-Jurjani responds by presenting a threefold classification of ranks of existence: (a) lowest: exists by another, separation conceivable; (b) middle: exists by itself with a different existence, separation inconceivable but conceivable; (c) highest: exists by itself with identical existence, separation and its conception impossible. He argues that the philosophers' goal is not this classification but to avoid priority of essence over existence. Even if we accept the classification, the remaining issue is whether the accidentally of the absolute is external or mental.

The Objection of Two Existences and the Abstractive Defense
Al-Jurjani addresses the objection of two existences using abstractive attribution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that the Necessary would have two existences, and al-Jurjani's defense that the attribute of absolute existence is abstractive, not real.

Core Idea
Absolute existence is an abstractive attribute, not a real one, so no composition arises.

A critical objection arises: if the particular existence requires absolute existence, then the Necessary would have two existences—its own particular existence and the absolute existence it 'requires'. Al-Jurjani defends by distinguishing between real and abstractive attribution. The attribute of absolute existence is merely abstracted by the intellect from the particular existence itself, so no real addition occurs. This preserves the unity of the Necessary while allowing the language of 'requiring existence'.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed kalam discussion on whether existence (al-wujud) is identical to essence (al-mahiyya) or part of it, specifically regarding the Necessary Being (al-wajib). The author, al-Sharif al-Jurjani, is responding to multiple objections and interpretations.

He begins by distinguishing two interpretations of essential necessity (al-wujub al-dhati): (1) as independence from others in existence (a privative concept), and (2) as the essence's requirement for existence (a positive relation). Under the second interpretation, he argues that the particular existence (al-wujud al-khas) which is the essence itself requires absolute existence (al-wujud al-mutlaq) as an accident. This is done through 'correspondence' (mutaba'ah): when the intellect observes a particular existence and notes its effects, it abstracts the universal concept of existence. Thus necessity is a secondary intelligible. He then addresses a criticism from al-Sharh al-Qushji: that the Necessary requires being existent (kawnuhu mawjudan) not existence (wujud). Al-Jurjani's response is that the independent requirement via correspondence entails a derivative requirement for existence, so the criticism fails.

Next, he treats an objection: the requirement of the particular for the absolute means the particular is an individual of the absolute, but the Necessary requires being existent, not existence. The answer distinguishes: God's particular existence requires being existent by absolute existence, not being an individual. This is refuted by the charge of two existences. Al-Jurjani defends by noting that if the Necessary is a particular existence, it is not two existences; one is the essence, the other is its existence, so it has one existence. He discusses abstractive vs. real attribution: the description of the essence with absolute existence is abstractive (intiza'i), not real, so no multiplicity arises. The principle of abstraction is the particular existence itself, so the absolute is an accident of the universal to its individual.

He then refutes the claim that the accidentally is purely mental: if abstractive, the intellect merely extracts the universal from the external particular without requiring the intellect's existence. He then cites Ibn Sina's al-Isharat to show that the philosophers do affirm the Necessary in the sense of essence requiring existence. An objector then says this entails an essence and distinct existence, negating identity. Al-Jurjani responds by presenting a threefold classification of ranks of existence: (a) lowest: exists by another, separation conceivable; (b) middle: exists by itself with a different existence, separation inconceivable but conceivable; (c) highest: exists by itself with identical existence, separation and its conception impossible. He argues that the philosophers' goal is not this classification but to avoid priority of essence over existence. Even if we accept the classification, the remaining issue is whether the accidentally of the absolute is external or mental.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed kalam discussion on whether existence (al-wujud) is identical to essence (al-mahiyya) or part of it, specifically regarding the Necessary Being (al-wajib). The author, al-Sharif al-Jurjani, is responding to multiple objections and interpretations.

He begins by distinguishing two interpretations of essential necessity (al-wujub al-dhati): (1) as independence from others in existence (a privative concept), and (2) as the essence's requirement for existence (a positive relation). Under the second interpretation, he argues that the particular existence (al-wujud al-khas) which is the essence itself requires absolute existence (al-wujud al-mutlaq) as an accident. This is done through 'correspondence' (mutaba'ah): when the intellect observes a particular existence and notes its effects, it abstracts the universal concept of existence. Thus necessity is a secondary intelligible. He then addresses a criticism from al-Sharh al-Qushji: that the Necessary requires being existent (kawnuhu mawjudan) not existence (wujud). Al-Jurjani's response is that the independent requirement via correspondence entails a derivative requirement for existence, so the criticism fails.

Next, he treats an objection: the requirement of the particular for the absolute means the particular is an individual of the absolute, but the Necessary requires being existent, not existence. The answer distinguishes: God's particular existence requires being existent by absolute existence, not being an individual. This is refuted by the charge of two existences. Al-Jurjani defends by noting that if the Necessary is a particular existence, it is not two existences; one is the essence, the other is its existence, so it has one existence. He discusses abstractive vs. real attribution: the description of the essence with absolute existence is abstractive (intiza'i), not real, so no multiplicity arises. The principle of abstraction is the particular existence itself, so the absolute is an accident of the universal to its individual.

He then refutes the claim that the accidentally is purely mental: if abstractive, the intellect merely extracts the universal from the external particular without requiring the intellect's existence. He then cites Ibn Sina's al-Isharat to show that the philosophers do affirm the Necessary in the sense of essence requiring existence. An objector then says this entails an essence and distinct existence, negating identity. Al-Jurjani responds by presenting a threefold classification of ranks of existence: (a) lowest: exists by another, separation conceivable; (b) middle: exists by itself with a different existence, separation inconceivable but conceivable; (c) highest: exists by itself with identical existence, separation and its conception impossible. He argues that the philosophers' goal is not this classification but to avoid priority of essence over existence. Even if we accept the classification, the remaining issue is whether the accidentally of the absolute is external or mental.

Imam al-Razi's Objection: The Conceivable as Platonic Form
al-Razi's objection and the subsequent critique and defense

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam al-Razi's objection that conceived entities might be self-subsistent Platonic Forms, not necessarily mental existents.

Core Idea
Conceiving something does not prove its mental existence; it may be a separate Form.

Imam al-Razi challenges the proof of mental existence by pointing out that when we conceive an absent object, it might not be in the mind at all—it could be a 'self-subsistent' Form, as Plato argued. This objection requires us to re-examine the relationship between conception and reality, leading to a deeper discussion about the nature of judgments and existence. In this lesson, we will unpack his argument and the subsequent critical exchange.

Guided Path
1
Read al-Razi's objection
Begin by reading the opening lines where al-Razi states that the conceived thing might be self-subsistent (qā'im bi-nafsihī) and thus not necessarily mental.
Pay attention to the phrase 'إما قائم بنفسه' – this is the core of the objection.
2
Examine the 'master investigator's' criticism
The text then presents a critique of an earlier answer: the affirmative judgment (ḥukm thubūtī) does not guarantee that the subject exists at the time of judgment; if the subject is mental, it does not truly oppose absolute existence.
The criticism shows that the concept 'absolute non-existent' cannot have existence if it truly opposes absolute existent.
3
Understand the defense
The defense argues that the opposition is based on the concept (mafhūm) itself, which includes negation of existence; the fact that the concept has mental existence does not contradict that conceptual opposition.
This is a subtle distinction between the reality of the object and the content of the concept.
4
Reflect on the final clarification
The concluding remark clarifies that when existence is attributed to the conceived entity in the state of judgment, it is as a conception, not as an external existent.
This reinforces that mental existence is not the same as external reality.

This passage is a refutation of the opponents' arguments against mental existence. The commentator first addresses the claim that mental existence is impossible because the mental image would inherit external properties. He gives a concise answer: the recipient (the mind) is a condition for receiving the effect, and we do not admit that the mind receives external existents in their external mode. The Third Proof: Burhan al-Tatbiq and the Condition of Thubut
The author presents the third proof and the objection from the ranks of numbers, with the response that thubut is required for the proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the burhan al-tatbiq is applied to the claim that non-existent essences are infinite, and why the condition of thubut (fixed existence) is critical for the proof's validity.

Core Idea
The burhan al-tatbiq only works on things with actual thubut, not on mere conceptual infinities.

In this passage, the author presents the third argument against the Mu'tazila position that non-existent things are fixed entities. The argument uses a logical proof known as burhan al-tatbiq (proof of superimposition) to show that if non-existent essences were infinite, they would lead to a contradiction. However, the key response is that the proof requires the objects to have thubut (fixed existence) in actuality, which the ranks of numbers—a counterexample—do not possess.

2

The proof applies the burhan al-tatbiq: if non-existent essences are infinite, removing a finite part leaves a remainder less by a finite amount, so the original set must be finite—a contradiction. Identify each premise and conclusion in the text.
3
Understand the condition of thubut
The response to the numbers objection is that the ranks of numbers lack thubut (fixed existence) in actuality, so the proof does not apply to them. Ask yourself why thubut is necessary for the proof to work.
The author also mentions that the non-finiteness of numbers is only in the sense of 'absence of termination', not actual infinity.

The third aspect (the essences). He said: (They are non-finite). The answer is by stipulating [the condition of] thubut in the application of the burhan al-tatbiq. There is no thubut for the ranks of numbers according to us, just as non-existent things have thubut according to you -- an obstinate claim because pure negation does not possess non-finiteness. Yes, it can be answered that the non-finiteness of the ranks of numbers is in the sense of absence of termination, so the burhan al-tatbiq does not apply to them. The statement: (And if one is satisfied [with mere description of fewness and manyness] etc.): namely, that one not mention that the greater than another by a finite amount is finite. It is said: The essences according to you are non-finite; if they are taken without what has gone out of them into existence, they are less by a finite amount, so the whole which is the greater is finite because fewness and manyness are attributes of the finite. His statement: (Even though each of them is non-finite): As for the objects of knowledge, it is apparent; as for the objects of power, it is by virtue of the eternal relations by which there is ability to act and refrain. His statement: (So they are also finite): It is not hidden that the argument is obligational (ilzami). They affirm thubut together with non-finiteness, and they do not affirm thubut together with finitude. So the claim that this aspect, if conceded, indicates that the fixed individuals are finite, not that they are non-fixed, is not heeded. His statement: (So the whole which is the greater): It can be said: the meaning is that the whole, i.e., the greater and the lesser, are finite. His statement: (And it is refuted by the ranks of numbers): For if it is answered by stipulating thubut in the summary, and there is no thubut for the ranks of numbers according to us, just as possible non-existents have thubut according to you, it is repelled by [saying] that the condition is existence. Whoever says that it is thubut must provide evidence. It may be said: The difference between existence and thubut does not affect the application of the proof, because it indicates that things existing in re (al-a'yan) cannot have their chain go to infinity, whether the in-re existence is called thubut or existence. In my view, because possible non-existents have no in-re existence according to them, even if they have thubut, as in the previous division, so it is better to drop the talk of in-re existence altogether. His statement: (And if one is satisfied etc.): i.e., he did not stipulate that the excess be of finite amount. His statement: (Even though each of them is non-finite): As for His objects of knowledge, their non-finiteness is apparent. As for His objects of power, the Almighty: if what is meant is the objects of power by the eternal conceptual relation that does not entail the existence of the object of power, but rather enables the powerful one to create or leave it, then they are also actually non-finite. But if what is meant is the objects of power by the relation that entails the existence of the object of power, which is the temporal relation according to the more apparent view, then the meaning of their non-finiteness is that His power does not reach a limit that it does not exceed nor relate to another object of power after it. So non-finiteness in the objects of knowledge has one meaning, and in the objects of power another meaning, as is not hidden.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof (al-wajh al-thalith) that non-existent things are not things. The proof applies the burhan al-tatbiq (the proof of superimposition) to the essences (al-dhat). The premise is that if non-existent essences are fixed (thabita), then they are either finite or infinite. The opponent claims they are infinite. The proof argues that if you take the infinite set and remove a finite number, the remainder is less by a finite amount, so the greater set is finite—a contradiction. This is the burhan al-tatbiq. The objection is raised that the ranks of numbers (maratib al-a'dad) are infinite, yet the same reasoning would make them finite. The answer is that the burhan al-tatbiq requires that the entities have thubut (fixedness) in actuality; but the ranks of numbers do not have thubut according to the Ash'ari position (and the Mu'tazila claim they do, but this is stubborn). Alternatively, the non-finiteness of numbers is in the sense of absence of termination (adam al-inqita'), so the proof does not apply. Further objections and answers follow, including the distinction between objects of knowledge and objects of power regarding non-finiteness. The passage also discusses that the proof is ilzami (obligational), merely showing that if the opponent's premises are accepted, it leads to absurdity. The commentary explains that the condition for the proof is existence in re, not merely thubut. The overall argument is that non-existent essences cannot be infinite, so they cannot be fixed.

The Ilzami Nature and the Non-Finiteness of Divine Attributes
The author explains the ilzami nature of the proof and distinguishes the non-finiteness of objects of knowledge and power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the third proof is an ilzami (obligational) argument that forces a contradiction from the opponent's premises, and how the non-finiteness of objects of knowledge and power is understood differently.

Core Idea
The proof is a dialectical tool that shows the opponent's position leads to absurdity, regardless of the thubut distinction.

The author now clarifies the nature of the third proof: it is an ilzami (obligational) argument, meaning it is used to force the opponent into a contradiction based on their own premises. He then discusses the non-finiteness of divine objects of knowledge and power, explaining that they are infinite in different senses. This passage refines the understanding of the proof and its application.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the passage and translation to get a sense of the discussion. Notice the shift from the earlier response to a deeper analysis.
This part includes the statement that the proof is ilzami and the detailed analysis of objects of knowledge and power.
2
Recognize the ilzami nature
The author states that the proof is 'ilzami'—it does not require the opponent to accept the Ash'ari premise about thubut. Instead, it shows that if the opponent's own view (that non-existents have thubut) is taken with non-finiteness, a contradiction follows.
This is important for understanding the strategy of the argument.
3
Analyze the non-finiteness of divine objects
The passage explains that the objects of divine knowledge are infinite in a straightforward sense, but objects of power are infinite in a different sense—either actually (by the eternal relation) or potentially (in terms of the capacity to create without end).
This distinction prevents a misapplication of the burhan al-tatbiq to God's attributes.

The Disjunctive Proof: Non-Existent Essences Must Be Created
Sanusi presents a disjunctive argument on fixed essences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the disjunctive argument for why non-existent essences cannot be fixed in themselves and must be created.

Core Idea
Non-existent essences are either necessary or contingent; both lead to creation.

This lesson presents the central argument of the third proof: if non-existent essences have any fixedness, they must be either necessarily fixed (impossible) or contingently fixed, in which case they are created. The argument establishes that essences have no independent thubut.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the translation. Notice the disjunctive structure (either...or...).
2
Identify the two options
The passage presents two possibilities for fixed essences in non-existence: necessarily fixed or contingently fixed. Trace the consequence of each.
The first option leads to multiplicity of the Necessary (impossible). The second leads to them being created.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
The conclusion is that essences are created and preceded by non-existence. Note the clarification that 'wajib' means necessary in existence, not in fixedness.
This clarifies a potential misunderstanding.

Commentary

The commentator then analyzes the logical possibilities: if 'al-ma'dum' is applied to 'al-manfi', it could be equal to it, absolutely more specific, more specific in some respect, or more general. The negation of existence is more general than the negation of establishment because the contradictory of the more specific is more general. Hence, the original claim of generality stands, and the objective is achieved. The passage ends by noting that this is clear.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof for the establishment (thubut) of the non-existent (al-ma'dum) based on its being distinct (tamayyuz). The commentator begins by stating that the opponent denies the claim, and then presents two aspects (wajhan). The first aspect is the focus: the non-existent is distinct, and everything distinct is established, therefore the non-existent is established. The commentator justifies the first premise (that the non-existent is distinct) by noting that the non-existent is conceivable (mutasawwar), and conceiving a thing requires its distinction from others; otherwise, no particular thing would be conceived. An objection arises: perhaps only some non-existents are conceivable, not all. The commentator replies that it suffices that some are conceived and some are not, and each is distinct from the other. Further support comes from the fact that some non-existents are intended (murad) and some are capable (maqdur), which would be unintelligible without distinction among non-existents. The second premise (that every distinct thing is established) is justified by the argument that every distinct thing has a self-identity (huwiyyah) that the intellect points to, and this self-identity requires determination (ta'ayyun) and establishment in itself. The commentator then introduces pure negation (al-nafy al-sarraf) as a contrast: pure negation has no self-identity, so if a distinct thing were pure negation, it would lead to a contradiction (khalaf). Therefore, the non-existent is not pure negation but is established. The commentator also addresses a possible reinterpretation: the desired result might be the establishment of the concept of the non-existent, which is distinct from the negated. Subsequent glosses clarify points: that conceiving requires distinction from at least the opposite aspect; that the conception must be in detail to necessitate distinction; and that the term 'also' indicates agreement between two proofs. The commentator also notes that the argument relies specifically on the attribute of distinction, not on the thing being a positive attribute, as both capability and intentionality are positive attributes. Finally, the passage considers an objection: the objector wants to negate the generality of the non-existent, but the answer given does not prove this. The commentator dismisses this as acceptable in disputation, as the objection itself implies a failure to prove the original claim due to lack of sound proof.

مفيداً للوجود كيف يتصور الشك في وجوده؟. والموجد لا بد أن يكون موجوداً بالبداهة، قلت : كأنهم أرادوا بالعالم جملة المعدومات الثابتة، وبالصانع له من يفيدها الوجود أعم من أن يكون موجوداً بالفعل أو لا، والبداهة إنما تدل على وجود الصانع حالة الصنع لا حالة عدم المصنوع . قوله : (قال الإمام الرازي أنه جهالة (بينة أجيب بأنهم إنما جوزوا اتصاف المعدوم بالصفات المعدومة إذ كما يجوز أن يتقرر الموصوف في العدم يجوز أن تتقرر الصفات فيه أيضاً، فلا يلزم ما ذكر من السفسطة الظاهرة
His saying: '(that after knowledge etc.)' means that knowledge of His being described with making this world, with power, knowledge and life is not sufficient for believing in His existence unless His existence is demonstrated by proof, such as saying: He is the Maker. Imam al-Razi said: It is plain ignorance and manifest sophism because it implies that the places of these motions and rests could be non-existent things, thus requiring a separate indication for knowledge of their existence. And the author said: Perhaps they meant that after we know that the Maker of the world is an essence described with these attributes, we need to demonstrate that the world has a Maker, i.e., an essence described with them, just as we know that the Necessary is impossible without that; we need to prove it with a proof, and this is a correct statement, no ignorance or sophism in it, because its meaning is that only the one with these attributes is fit to be the Maker of the world, and with this alone His existence in the external is not entailed. What do you say about one who says: The partner of the Creator must be described with these attributes, otherwise he would not be His partner, and it is impossible in the external? So it appears that the estimation of being described with external attributes does not necessitate the actual existence of the described entity in the external, and this apology is far-fetched... (continues) ...because something whose existence is not entailed, so it is said: The world is a name for all existents other than God, so after knowing that the world has a Maker, i.e., one who benefits existence, how can doubt about His existence be imagined? And the one who brings into existence must necessarily exist by self-evidence. This is pure fantasy, and the answer given by that speaker is more amazing than the question, as is clear to anyone who considers it. His saying: '(because it implies etc.)' because being described with those attributes is like being described with existent attributes, because they have observable effects from the existence, perfection, and temporality of the world, so if that were possible in the state of non-existence, then being described with motion and rest would be possible in the state of absence of the described entity. Thus the objection of the commentator of al-Tajrid is refuted that there is no sophistry in describing a fixed non-existent with non-existent fixed attributes; rather the sophistry is describing it with existent attributes, because there is no difference between the affirmation of external existence and mental existence in terms of lack of desired effects, and there is no doubt in imagining a non-existent described with non-existent attributes. 'one who benefits existence' - how can doubt about His existence be imagined? And the one who brings into existence must necessarily exist by self-evidence. I say: It seems they meant by 'world' the totality of fixed non-existents, and by 'Maker' the one who benefits them existence, whether actually existent or not, and self-evidence only indicates the existence of the Maker at the time of making, not at the time of the absence of the made. His saying: '(Imam al-Razi said it is ignorance etc.)' It was answered that they only allowed describing the non-existent with non-existent attributes, for just as the described entity can be fixed in non-existence, the attributes can also be fixed in it, so the mentioned sophistry does not follow.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary that explains and defends the statement from al-Mawaqif: 'after knowing the world has a Maker with attributes, one still needs a proof for His existence.' The commentator first explains that knowing God is described with attributes like power, knowledge, and life is not enough; one must prove that such a being exists. Then Imam al-Razi's severe criticism is reported: he calls this view 'plain ignorance and manifest sophism' because it would imply that the very motions and rests of the world could be non-existent, requiring a separate proof for their existence. The author (al-Jurjani) responds by clarifying that the intended meaning is simply that knowing the concept of a qualified entity does not entail its external existence. He gives an analogy: someone might say that a 'partner of the Creator' must have the same attributes, but that partner is impossible in reality. Thus, the estimation of qualities does not guarantee existence. The commentary then addresses further objections: some argue that the world consists of existents, so the Maker must be existent by self-evidence. The author rejects this, suggesting that the proponents might be using 'world' to refer to fixed non-existents and 'Maker' to the one who gives them existence, which does not require the Maker's actual existence at all times. Another objection from the commentator of al-Tajrid is answered by distinguishing between describing a non-existent with non-existent attributes (which is allowed) and with existent attributes (which would be sophistry). Finally, another response repeats that they only allow non-existent attributes for the non-existent, so no sophistry follows.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The existence of the Maker is self-evident at all times, even before creation.
Correction: Self-evidence (badaha) only indicates the Maker's existence at the time of creation, not eternally.

Checkpoint
According to the author, when does self-evidence (badaha) indicate the existence of the Maker?
A) At all times, regardless of creation
B) Only at the time of making (state of making the world)
C) Only after the world exists
D) Never, because existence is always proven by proof

Key Takeaway
Self-evidence does not prove eternal existence; it only indicates the Maker's existence during creation, reinforcing the need for a separate proof.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you claim to believe in God, is that belief based on a self-evident 'feeling' or on a reasoned proof that you could articulate to someone? If your belief were tested, would it hold up like the Maker's existence at creation—or would it crumble if you had to defend it beyond the obvious? What proof do you rely on for the eternal existence of God?
?
This passage shows that even the existence of the world does not automatically prove God's eternal existence without further reasoning. How many of your assumptions about God are merely inherited or assumed? Are you willing to examine them as carefully as this scholar examines the objections? What would it cost you to let go of a comfortable but unexamined belief?
?
The distinction between mental and external existence is crucial here. In your daily life, do you treat God's attributes—like His power or knowledge—as mere mental concepts that don't change how you live? Or do you act as if they are real, external realities that demand a response? If God's attributes were truly external to you, what would you do differently starting today?
