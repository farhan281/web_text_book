Contextual Clues and the Conclusive Discussion
Objection, response, and famous discussion on transmitted proofs and rational certainty.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Commentary
This passage presents a further objection and a famous concluding discussion. The objector argues that if contextual clues negate all nine possibilities (including metaphor), then the literal meaning must be intended, leading to certainty of no rational contradiction, otherwise the speaker would be lying. The author responds that the contextual clues only negate possibilities relative to the words themselves, not the possibility of a rational contextual clue like impossibility, which can override intention. The commentator (al-Jurjani) adds that the word 'perhaps' is used to indicate sufficiency or emphasis. Then a second objection is raised: if some rational matters are proven possible by certain rational proof, then transmitted proofs about them should yield certainty. The answer is that in legal matters all transmitted proofs with clues yield certainty, but not in rational matters because reason can find contrary evidence. However, a famous discussion (bahth mashhur) follows: the basis for certainty in legal matters is the speaker's truthfulness, which also applies in rational matters. Moreover, matters that reason does not deem impossible may still be possible, so we should interpret all revealed matters as possible to avoid calling the speaker false. Therefore, the correct view is that transmitted proofs do yield certainty in rational matters too. The commentator's earlier distinction does not hold. The passage concludes that the only escape is to say the commentator meant that the combination of proofs and contextual clues in legal matters yields certainty of no contradiction because it gives intention from the speaker. This is a self-contained argumentative unit covering an objection, its answer, and a resolution.

Do Transmitted Proofs Yield Certainty?

Commentary
This passage defines the scope of the Second Position (al-Mawqif al-Thani) on universal matters (al-umur al-'ammah) and introduces its preamble. The commentator begins by quoting the matn of al-Mawaqif: 'The Second Position: On the Universal Matters.' He explains that universal matters are considered in two ways: either absolutely (ala sabīl al-iṭlāq) like general contingency (al-imkān al-ʿāmm), or by way of correlativity (ala sabīl al-taqābul), such that a concept and its opposite together encompass all things. A further condition is that each of the two correlatives must have a scientific purpose (gharaḍ ʿilmī), meaning they are relevant to establishing religious creeds, directly or indirectly. An example given is existence (al-wujūd) and non-existence (al-ʿadam). The commentator then justifies why this position is placed separately: the three previous divisions covered matters specific to their categories, so only universal matters remain, requiring a distinct chapter. Within this position, there is a preamble (muqaddima) that must precede the investigations of these universal matters because it contains the division of the known (al-maʿlūmāt) into their subjects (maʿrūḍāt) and five goals (marāṣid). The commentator then addresses the phrase 'like general contingency,' explaining that inquiry about it consists of predicating its necessary concomitants upon it as it is realized in its individuals—specific contingency, necessity, and impossibility—so research on these is actually research on general contingency, thus refuting the idea that there is no research on general contingency in universal matters. Next, he explains the restriction about correlativity: it excludes concepts like human and not-human (where no scientific purpose attaches), or where purpose attaches only to one (e.g., necessity and not-necessity), ensuring the definition is not too broad.
