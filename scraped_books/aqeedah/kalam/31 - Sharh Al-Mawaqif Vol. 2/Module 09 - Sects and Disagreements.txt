The Second Premise: Equality of Things Sharing a Quality
The commentator presents the second premise with three theological examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second premise of the opponents (Mu'tazila and theologians) that things sharing a quality must be equal absolutely, and how it is used to deny divine attributes and abstract entities.

Core Idea
If two things share a quality, they must be equal in all respects.

The commentator introduces a crucial logical premise used by theological opponents. This premise states that if two entities share any quality, whether positive or negative, they must be equal in every way. The passage then illustrates this principle with three concrete examples from debates about God's attributes and abstract entities. Understanding this premise helps you grasp the logic behind major theological positions.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
2
Understand the premise
Grasp the statement that things sharing a quality must be equal absolutely. This is the core idea.
The premise is close to the first but applied more broadly.
3
Examine the first example (attributes)
See how the Mu'tazila use this premise to deny God's eternal attributes. If attributes existed, they would equal the essence in eternity, leading to absurdity.
4
Examine the second example (knowledge)
The Mu'tazila also use it to deny that God knows by a knowledge. If He did, that knowledge would equal human knowledge, implying either temporal origination of divine knowledge or eternity of human knowledge.
5
Examine the third example (abstract entities)
Theologians use it to deny abstract entities like intellects. If they existed, they would be like God in being non-material, entailing either contingency of God or necessity of contingent things.

Mu'tazilite Division of the Known into Categories
Al-Mawaqif presents the Mu'tazilite division of the known into categories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite division of the known into categories: the negated (impossible) and the established (encompassing existent, non-existent possible, and possible), and their further division into existent and non-existent.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazilites divided the known into the negated impossible and the established, which includes possibles even if non-existent.

The Mu'tazilite school held a distinctive ontology where non-existent possibles have some reality as objects of knowledge. In this passage, al-Mawaqif presents their classification of the known (al-ma'lum) into two divisions: the first based on verification in itself, the second based on existence in external entities. This lesson unpacks these two divisions and clarifies the key distinction between the negated impossible and the established non-existent possible.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the English translation to grasp the overall structure.
Notice that the passage first states the Mu'tazilite position on the non-existent being 'established'.
2
Identify the first division and its two categories
Find where the text divides the known into 'negated' (al-manfī) and 'established' (al-thābit). Understand that the negated has no verification in itself and is equivalent to the impossible, while the established has some verification and includes the existent, the non-existent, and the possible.
The 'non-existent' here refers to possible non-existents, not the impossible.
3
Understand the second division
The text then presents another division: the known is either (a) that which has no existence in external entities (the non-existent, whether possible or impossible) or (b) that which has existence. Note how this division differs from the first.
The second division focuses on actual external existence, whereas the first focuses on verification in itself.
4
Compare the two divisions
Reflect on the relationship between the two divisions. The first division's 'negated' corresponds only to the impossible, while the 'non-existent' in the second division includes both possible and impossible non-existents.
The possible non-existent has some verification (first division) but no external existence (second division).
5
Address the common misconception
Avoid confusing the 'non-existent' in the second division with the 'negated' in the first. The negated impossible has no verification at all, while the non-existent possible has some verification.
This distinction is crucial for understanding Mu'tazilite ontology.

Commentary
This passage outlines the Mu'tazilite ontology of the 'known' (al-ma'lum). The context is a discussion of intermediate states (wasiṭa) between existence and non-existence. The third possibility states that the non-existent is 'established' (thābit) in some sense, and there is no third category like the 'state' (ḥāl). The known is then divided in two ways. First division: (1) that which has absolutely no verification in itself—this is the 'negated' (al-manfī), equivalent to the impossible (al-mumtani‘); (2) that which has some verification in itself in some respect—this is the 'established' (al-thābit), which includes the existent, the non-existent (i.e., possible non-existents), and the possible. Second division: (a) that which has no existence in external entities (al-a‘yān)—this is the non-existent, whether possible or impossible; (b) that which has existence. The two divisions are not identical; the first focuses on having any kind of verification, while the second focuses on actual external existence. This reflects the Mu'tazilite view that non-existent possibles have some reality in the mind or as objects of knowledge.

Refuting Platonic Forms by Specific Nature
First refutation of Platonic forms using specific nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that the specific nature’s uniform requirement proves Platonic forms cannot be self-subsistent.

Core Idea
A single nature cannot have individuals that need a substrate and others that don't.

The first refutation of Platonic forms relies on the concept of specific nature. The author explains that the requirement of a specific nature is uniform, so all individuals of that nature must share the same ontological status. If some need a substrate, then the nature itself necessitates the substrate, making self-subsistent forms impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to grasp the logical flow of the argument.
Note the use of 'مقتضى الطبيعة النوعية' as the key premise.
2
Identify the premise and conclusion
Identify that the premise is 'the requirement of the specific nature does not differ' and the conclusion is that all individuals must be similar in needing hyle, thus Platonic forms are impossible.
The commentary clarifies the implication for self-subsistent forms.
3
Apply the logic to examples
Consider how this applies to human, equine, water, and fire forms as mentioned. Ask yourself: If any human requires a body, can there be a human without a body?
This solidifies understanding.

Refuting Democritus's Atomism via Continuity and Hyle
Refutation of Democritus's atomism using the analogy of continuous and separate parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that similar bodies, whether continuous or separate, imply the existence of hyle as a receiver of division.

Core Idea
If bodies are alike in reality, what holds for their parts in a continuum holds for them separately, requiring a common substrate.

Democritus believed atoms were indivisible. The refutation presented here argues that since all simple bodies share the same reality, the properties of continuous parts apply to separate parts, leading to the necessity of hyle (prime matter) as the underlying substance.

Guided Path
1
Read the refutation and the response
Read the Arabic passage and translation focusing on the argument that similar bodies force the existence of hyle.
The passage includes a refutation and a counter-response.
2
Trace the argument from continuous to separate
Understand that the argument takes two continuous parts in one body and asserts that what is possible for them (like separation) is possible for two separate parts, implying a subject that receives separation (hyle).
The denial that bodies are a specific nature is a potential objection.
3
Evaluate the response
The response denies that the bodies share a specific nature, and the rejoinder says that increase and abstraction are not necessary consequences of division. Reflect on whether this rejoinder saves the atomist view.
Consider if the atomist can avoid hyle by denying the premise of similarity.

Then they differed: Abu Ishaq ibn 'Ayyash said: The essences in non-existence are stripped of all attributes and do not acquire attributes except in the state of existence. And others besides Ibn 'Ayyash said: They are in the state of non-existence qualified by attributes of genera (sifat al-ajnas), such as blackness being blackness, whiteness being whiteness, substance being substance, and accident being accident. And these attributes (i.e., attributes absolutely) are either returning to the whole (i.e., the composite structure of several matters) or to the detail (i.e., to each one of multiple without considering composition between them). His saying: (not in their being essences) indicated by that the specificity of effect in their being existent is relative, so it does not contradict the realization of effect with regard to composition and qualification by accidents. His saying: (Abu Ishaq said etc.) is a precaution against falling into sophistry. His saying: (qualified by attributes of genera) i.e., the essential attributes (al-sifat al-nafsiyya) are what are not acquired due to an added meaning to the essence; they said because they are equal in essentiality, so if they did not differ by attributes they would be one; and the answer: they differ by essences (mahiyyat), even if they are equal in the concept of essence. His saying: (absolutely) whether they are attributes of genera or not, and whether they are existent or not; for the attribute according to them is more general than accident, so it includes the existent on the assumption of it being non-existent. His saying: (for they are in need etc.) because life is conditioned by the body because it is a balance of temperament or dependent on it, and the rest are conditioned by it. His saying: (qualified by attributes of genera they said because they are equal in essentiality, so if they did not differ by attributes they would be one; and because they are different, for if they were similar in non-existence they would be similar in existence, because what is by essence does not cease; and difference is only by attributes by virtue of sharing in essentiality; and the answer: the concept of essence is accidental to realities for the completion of their reality, as they imagined; and equality in the accidental does not prevent difference in reality, like realities that share in existence; and then nothing remains of what was mentioned, and by this also refuted is Ibn 'Ayyash's clinging to the definition that since they are equal in essentiality, the specification of some of them by a particular attribute is not for their essence—this is apparent—nor for another attribute, otherwise infinite regress; nor for a necessitating agent because its ratio to all is equal; rather, it is for a free agent and His act is originated, so it would necessitate that the non-existent is a locus for successive accidents, which is invalid by consensus. Commentary
This passage presents the Mu'tazilite position that non-existent possibles are things (dhawat, a'yan, haqa'iq) before existence, and then discusses their disagreement about attributes in nonexistence. Ibn 'Ayyash held that essences in nonexistence are devoid of all attributes; others held they possess essential attributes (sifat al-ajnas) like blackness, whiteness, etc. The author then divides attributes into those pertaining to the whole (like life and its concomitants) and those pertaining to details. The commentary elaborates on the arguments and refutations, including a detailed discussion of why attributes in nonexistence are possible and how Ibn 'Ayyash's position is refuted by the argument that essences can be distinguished by their specific essences rather than accidental attributes. It also distinguishes between attributes of genera and other attributes, and explains the conditions for attributes subsisting in the non-existent.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: All Mu'tazilites agree that non-existents have attributes in non-existence.
Correction: Ibn 'Ayyash denied that non-existent essences possess any attributes, while others affirmed only essential attributes of genera (sifat al-ajnas).

Key Takeaway
The Mu'tazilite view that non-existent things have a reality leads to an internal debate about whether they possess any attributes before existence, and a classification of attributes into those of the whole and those of detail.
