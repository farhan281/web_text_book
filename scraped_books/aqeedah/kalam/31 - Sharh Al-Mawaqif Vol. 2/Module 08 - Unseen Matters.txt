Al-Mutawatirat: Recurrent Reports as Certain Knowledge
Defines mutawatirat and explains conditions for certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what recurrent reports (mutawatirat) are, the condition that makes them certain (impossibility of collusion), and the hidden syllogism that justifies their certainty.

Core Idea
Certainty from mass transmission is grounded not in numbers but in the impossibility of collusion.

In this lesson, we examine the sixth category of certain knowledge: recurrent reports (mutawatirat). The author defines them as judgments based solely on the report of a group that cannot collude on a lie, and he explains the conditions that yield certainty, including a hidden syllogism.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the English translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
The passage includes both the matn and its commentary within the same flow.
2
Understand the definition
Identify the core definition of mutawatirat: judgments from a group whose collusion on lying is impossible. Notice the examples (Mecca, Galen) to see what kind of knowledge this produces.
The condition 'impossible collusion' is key to certainty.
3
Refute the fixed-number idea
The author argues that specifying a particular number for tawatur is wrong. Instead, the criterion is the attainment of certainty, which varies by context.
The number is not predetermined; certainty indicates the number is sufficient.
4
Unpack the hidden syllogism
The passage states that mutawatirat require a hidden syllogism. Understand its structure: if the report were false, the group would have to have colluded, but collusion is impossible, so the report must be true.
This syllogism is 'hidden' because it is not explicitly stated in everyday judgment.

( السادسة المتواترات ) وهي ( ما يحكم بها بمجرد خبر جماعة يمتنع تواطؤهم على الكذب ) كحكمنا بمكة وجالينوس، ومن اعتبر في التواتر عدداً معيناً، فقد أحال فإن بوجود ذلك مما يختلف بحسب الوقائع والضابط مبلغ ما يقع معه اليقين، فإذا حصل اليقين فقد تم العدد ولا بد في المتواترات من تكرار وقياس خفي وأن تكون مستندة قوله : ( كحكمنا ) أي الذين لم يشاهدوها قوله : من تكرار أي تكرار السماع قوله: (وقياس خفي وهو لو لم يكن حقاً لما أخبر به جماعة يمتنع تواطؤهم الكذب لكن التالي باطل.
(The sixth: recurrent reports) They are (what is judged by mere report of a group whose collusion on lying is impossible) like our judgment about Mecca and Galen. And whoever considers a specific number for tawatur has erred, for its existence varies according to circumstances. The criterion is the amount with which certainty occurs. When certainty is obtained, the number is complete. And in recurrent reports there must be repetition and a hidden syllogism, and that they be based [on sense]. His saying: ('like our judgment') i.e., those who have not witnessed them. His saying: 'from repetition' i.e., repetition of hearing. His saying: ('and a hidden syllogism') which is: if it were not true, a group whose collusion on lying is impossible would not have reported it, but the consequent is false.

Commentary
This passage defines recurrent reports (al-mutawatirat) as judgments based solely on the report of a group that cannot collude on a lie, giving examples like Mecca and Galen. It then refutes the idea that a fixed number of reporters is required; rather, the criterion is the attainment of certainty, whatever the number. It also states that mutawatirat require repetition (of hearing) and a hidden syllogism: if the report were false, such a group would not have reported it, but they did, so it must be true. The commentary clarifies that 'our judgment' refers to those who have not witnessed the reported things, 'repetition' means repetition of hearing, and the hidden syllogism is as described.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That tawatur requires a specific number of reporters (e.g., 10 or 20).
Correction: The passage states that certainty is the sole criterion; no fixed number is prescribed. The number varies according to circumstances.

Checkpoint

Key Takeaway
Recurrent reports yield certain knowledge when the group reporting cannot have colluded, and this certainty is justified by a hidden syllogism that rules out falsehood.

?

?
The passage says the number of reporters varies with circumstances. Are you willing to apply that flexibility to your own path to certainty? Or do you demand a fixed number, like 'two witnesses' or 'ten companions'? Could that be a sign of your own arrogance? Commit today to grounding one belief in the rigorous conditions described here.

Do Non-Existents Differ? Setting Up the Issue
The commentator sets up the issue of whether non-existents are distinguished by contrasting with existents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the commentator frames the question of whether non-existents are distinguished and why he begins by discussing existents.

Core Idea
Things are known by their opposites — to understand non-existents, we first clarify existents.

This lesson introduces the Fifth Objective, which asks whether non-existents can be distinguished from one another. The commentator begins not with non-existents but with external existents, because clarifying the distinction of existents helps us understand what distinction of non-existents could mean. He also notes that the author phrases the question with an interrogative to indicate that the answer is not simple.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read both the Arabic and translation to get the overall flow. Notice the structure: the objective is stated, then existents are discussed, then an example is given, and then the commentator explains the author's wording.
The passage is introductory and ends mid-sentence, but the main points are clear.
2
Identify the key terms
Look for the terms 'non-existents' (معدومات), 'distinction' (تمايز), 'external existents' (موجودات خارجية), and 'obtaining' (حصول). These are central to the discussion.
These terms will recur throughout the topic.
3
Understand the method of opposites
The commentator states: 'since things are clarified by their opposites, he undertook to explain the distinction of existents.' This is a key methodological point.
This principle is common in kalam to clarify abstract concepts.
4
Analyze the example of disbelief
Consider the example of a disbeliever's disbelief and its two modes of obtaining: by itself in the mind and by its image. This illustrates the distinction between existence in the mind and external reality.
The example avoids objections about necessary properties by focusing on the two modes.
5
Reflect on the significance
The author's use of an interrogative ('هل') shows that the issue is not settled; the truth will be detailed later. The commentator's explanation sets the stage for the detailed discussion.
This lesson is just the introduction; further lessons will delve into the details.

And since things are clarified by their opposites, he undertook to explain the distinction of existents and existences, to show that the disputed distinction of non-existents is in this sense. His statement: ('And the distinction of existences') i.e., on the assumption that they are additional to quiddities in themselves, i.e., with cutting off...

Commentary
This passage introduces the fifth objective on whether non-existents are distinguished. The commentator first establishes that external existents are clearly distinguished in the external world without confusion, and that their distinction according to themselves is also beyond doubt. The distinction of existents is the difference between their two modes of obtaining: obtaining by themselves (in external reality) and obtaining by their images (in the mind). He gives an example: consider the disbelief of a disbeliever and its obtaining for the disbeliever; this does not open the door to objections about the necessary properties of quiddity or mental necessary properties like impossibility. The commentator notes that the author phrased the question with an interrogative to indicate lack of certainty at the outset, and then will give a detailed answer later. The commentator explains that the author begins by clarifying the distinction of existents because things are clarified by their opposites; by showing how existents are distinguished, we can understand what would be meant by the distinction of non-existents. The passage ends by explaining that the phrase 'the distinction of existences' assumes that existences are additional to quiddities in themselves.
