Refutation of Dawaran as Proof of Causality
Refutes dawaran by showing it leads to impossible causality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why dawaran (co-extension) cannot be a valid proof of causality, as it leads to absurd consequences such as the effect being the cause of its own cause.

Core Idea
Co-extension alone cannot prove causality; it would imply the effect causes its own cause.

This passage tackles a common logical fallacy in reasoning about causality. The method of dawaran—arguing that if two things always occur together, one must cause the other—seems plausible but leads to absurdities. Al-Ījī demonstrates that if taken literally, it would force us to say the effect causes its cause, which is impossible. Let's examine his argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the passage uses a reductio ad absurdum structure.
2
Identify the main claim being refuted
The claim is that dawaran (co-extension) indicates causality. Find where this claim is stated and note its formulation.
3
Follow the reductio ad absurdum
The argument assumes dawaran is valid, then shows it leads to impossible consequences. Trace the logic: if A and B co-extend, dawaran would say A causes B. But when applied to cause and effect, it would imply effect causes cause.
This is the core of the refutation.
4
Note the examples given
The passage gives three examples: effect with cause, conditioned with condition, and effect with last part. Each shows a case where co-extension exists but causality cannot be in the expected direction.
Understanding each example solidifies the argument.
5
Understand the conclusion
The conclusion is that the inference of causality from dawaran is refuted (manqūḍ). Reflect on why this refutation is effective.

For if [such an act] had issued from God Almighty, it would be necessary to judge it as ugly due to the existence of its cause. And if what is mentioned—that co-extension indicates that the co-extensive (al-mudār) is the cause of the co-extending (al-dā'ir)—were correct, then it would indicate that the effect is the cause of its cause, for the cause co-extends with it in existence and non-existence, but it is absolutely impossible for it to be a cause of it. Likewise, the conditioned (al-mashrūṭ) co-extends in this way with the equal condition, and the effect also co-extends with the last part. So the inference of causality from co-extension is refuted by this scenario.

Commentary
This passage presents a refutation of the method of dawaran. It argues that if dawaran were a valid proof of causality, then it would lead to absurd consequences. For example, if an act of injustice could be attributed to God, it would be judged ugly because its cause exists. But the argument of dawaran would also imply that the effect is the cause of its cause, since cause and effect are mutually co-extensive—but that is impossible. Similarly, the conditioned co-extends with its condition, and the effect with its last part. Thus, co-extension alone cannot prove causality because it would imply contradictory causality relationships. The refutation uses the principle that co-extension exists between equals, but only one direction of causality holds, so dawaran is insufficient.

Suitability for Causality in Dawaran
Explains the requirement of suitability for causality in dawaran.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why dawaran requires the co-extensive to be suitable as a cause (a motive containing wisdom) and why mere correlation without suitability does not yield certainty.

Core Idea
Dawaran alone is insufficient; the co-extensive must be a genuine motive.

This lesson delves into a key condition in the argument from co-extension (dawaran): the co-extensive must be suitable as a cause—it must be a motive (bā'ith) that includes a wisdom intended by the Lawgiver, not merely an indicator (amāra). The passage explains this requirement, addresses the possibility of an accompanying factor, and refutes attempts to prove causality by dawaran alone using the example of correlatives.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Start by carefully reading the Arabic passage and its English translation. Pay attention to the key terms and the flow of the argument.
The passage contains embedded quotations and commentary, which is part of the original text.
2
Identify the condition of suitability
Notice that the passage begins by stating that the co-extensive must be suitable for causality, meaning it must be a motive, not a mere indicator. Understand the distinction between a motive (containing wisdom) and an indicator.
This condition is essential for dawaran to be a valid method of proving causality.
3
Follow the objection and response
The passage then considers the possibility that the true cause is an accompanying factor. It presents two attempts to negate this possibility and shows their weaknesses, especially through the example of correlatives.
The example of fatherhood and sonship illustrates that mutual entailment does not imply causation.

Commentary
This passage discusses the condition that for dawaran to indicate causality, the co-extensive must be suitable (ṣāliḥ) to be a cause. That is, it must be a motive (bā'ith) rather than a mere indicator (amāra), meaning it should contain a wisdom intended by the Lawgiver. The text then explains that many co-extensives (like effects or conditions) are not motives but only indicators. Therefore, the inference of causality from dawaran alone is insufficient. It further considers the possibility that the true cause is some accompanying factor, not the observed co-extensive. Attempts to negate this possibility are examined: the first (lack of evidence for the accompanying) is dismissed; the second (mutual entailment in knowledge) is refuted by the example of correlatives like fatherhood and sonship, where knowledge of one entails knowledge of the other without one causing the other. Ultimately, the passage argues that dawaran alone cannot yield certainty; it requires that the co-extensive be suitable as a cause, which itself is not certain. Thus, the intended certainty is not achieved.

Those aspects, for example, are suitable for the causality of ugliness in the intellect, which is not certain at all, though it may be conjectured. Commentary
This passage is a critique of the method of co-variation (دوران) for proving causality. The author begins by stating that the aspects used as examples are suitable for being causes of ugliness in the intellect, but this suitability is not certain; it is only conjectural, whereas the method requires certainty. He then raises an objection: it is possible that the effective factor in the judgment that co-varies with an attribute is actually a concomitant (مقارن) of that attribute, not the attribute itself. To negate this possibility, two arguments are proposed. The first argument is that there is no evidence for the concomitant, so it should be negated—but this argument has already been shown to be corrupt. The second argument uses the idea of mutual concomitance in knowledge: if knowing the attribute alone yields knowledge of the judgment, and knowing something else without the attribute does not yield the judgment, then the attribute is the cause. This is refuted by the example of correlatives (متضايفين) like fatherhood and sonship, which are mutually concomitant in knowledge but are not causally related. The author then points out that many customary causes (like fire causing burning) are known together but are not real causes in Ash'ari theology—only Allah creates effects. The passage also critiques the premise that knowledge of the cause necessitates knowledge of the effect, and clarifies that the method of co-variation does not prove causality without further conditions.

Refuting the Criterion of Knowledge Alone for Causality
The author refutes the claim that knowledge of the co-variant alone implies causality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the mere fact that knowledge of one thing implies knowledge of another is insufficient to establish a causal relationship, especially in light of Ash'ari theology.

Core Idea
Co-variation alone does not prove causality; many customary correlations are not real causes.

In this passage, the author tackles a fundamental methodological claim: that if knowledge of A alone implies knowledge of B, then A must be the cause of B. He argues this is false because many everyday correlations—like fire burning cotton or food causing satiety—meet that criterion yet are not real causes in Ash'ari thought, where Allah directly creates effects. The lesson unpacks his refutation and the two premises underlying the original argument.

The author notes that Ash'aris agree that customary causes are not real; Mu'tazilites may disagree. This shows the theological stakes: causation is attributed to Allah alone in Ash'arism.

Commentary
This passage continues the refutation of the method of co-variation (دوران) and specifically the claim that knowledge of the co-variant alone implies knowledge of the revolving judgment, which would establish it as a cause. The author argues that such a criterion is invalid because many customary causal correlations (like fire burning cotton, or food causing satiety) also exhibit this property, yet in Ash'ari theology these are not real causes—Allah creates the effects directly. Thus, the mere fact that knowledge of one thing alone implies knowledge of another does not prove causality. The passage then clarifies the two premises of the original argument: (1) that if something is not a cause, knowledge of it alone does not entail knowledge of the effect, and (2) that knowledge of a cause necessitates knowledge of the effect. Both premises are rejected. The commentator also discusses an error made by some thinkers who misunderstood the second premise, and he shows that the argument can be reconstituted without that premise by interpreting 'alone' as a state of the knowledge, implying that the co-variant is the exclusive known condition. The discussion concludes that the objection is rebutted.

The Two Premises of the Argument from Circulation
Lays out the two premises of the circulation argument.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The argument from circulation rests on two controversial premises about causality.

The argument from circulation attempts to prove causality by claiming that knowledge of the cause necessitates knowledge of the effect. Circulation (Dawran) and Its Refutation
Presents the third method (circulation) and its refutation from an Ash'ari perspective.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third method of establishing causality—circulation (dawran)—and how Ash'ari theologians refute it by distinguishing between identity and distinctness of cause and effect.

Core Idea
Circulation cannot prove causality when cause and effect are identical.

The philosophers used a method called circulation to argue that if two things always accompany each other in existence and non-existence, one must be the cause of the other. In this passage, the author presents an objection against this method and then gives an Ash'ari response. The key move is to show that circulation only works if cause and effect are genuinely distinct, but for Ash'aris, motion and motivity are identical in essence, so no causality is needed.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the objection: if circulation does not prove causality, then one could attribute motivity to something other than motion, even though they coexist, leading to skepticism.
The objection aims to show that circulation is necessary to avoid doubt.
2
Identify the first part of the answer
Note the Ash'ari view that motion and motivity are identical; hence, there is no circulation or causality issue.
This is based on the denial of real attributes distinct from the essence.
3
Identify the second part of the answer
Even if we concede distinctness (as per Muthbiti al-Ahwal), motion is defined as that which necessitates motivity, so attributing motivity to something else is contradictory.
Thus, causality is known directly, not via circulation.
4
Grasp the conclusion
Causality here is known independently of circulation; attacking the argument does not undermine the causal relation.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer regarding the third method of establishing causality, namely circulation (dawran). The objection states that if circulation does not prove causality, then one could attribute motivity (the property of being in motion) to something other than motion itself, even though motivity and motion are correlated in existence and non-existence. So to attribute motivity to something else would be self-contradictory. Therefore, causality is known independently of the circulation argument, and attacking the argument does not undermine the known causal relation.

The Fourth Method: The Concomitant and Its Refutation
Jurjani refutes the concomitant method of causality by showing its failure if the concomitant is not coextensive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the refutation of the concomitant method for establishing causality, including the logical issues of coextension and circular reasoning.

Core Idea
A concomitant must always accompany the circle to prove causality, but it may be more specific or more general, breaking the proof.

In this passage, Jurjani presents the fourth method of establishing causality — the concomitant — and then refutes it. The method claims that if something always accompanies the circle and equals it in extension, then it must be the cause. The refutation shows that the relationship may be asymmetrical, leading to a logical fallacy. This lesson unpacks the objection and the counterargument, highlighting the importance of precise logical relationships in causal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Read the opening part of the passage where the concomitant method is described. Notice the condition: the concomitant must necessarily accompany the circle and equal it in extent.
The method is presented as a claim by opponents.
2
Understand the coextension condition
The objection argues that if the concomitant is coextensive with the circle (madar), then its presence yields the judgment, proving causality.
Coextension means they always occur together without exception.
3
Examine the refutation
The refutation provides two scenarios: if the concomitant is more specific, the circle exists without it; if more general, the circle is not a circle in absence. Both break coextension.
The refutation uses the idea that the circle may be necessary for the concomitant but more general.
4
Recognize the circular reasoning
Claiming that the concomitant is still a circle in the disputed case is a petition of the question (circular reasoning), as it assumes what needs to be proved.
This is a logical fallacy.

Thus, it is established that a cause (the part) is described as prior to its effect while being non-existent, proving that priority need not be by existence. This suffices to refute the opponent's demand that the efficient cause must be existentially prior, because the same reasoning applies: the efficient cause may be prior in a non-existential sense.
