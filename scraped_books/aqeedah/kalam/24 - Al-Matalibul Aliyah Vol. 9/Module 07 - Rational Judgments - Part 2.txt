Refuting Objections on 'Creator of All Things'
Author refutes linguistic objections against the general term 'كل' and its specification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author defends the general term 'كل' (all) against objections, showing that even if specification occurs, the term remains evidence for the unspecified portions.

Core Idea
A specified general term still proves for the rest.

In this passage, the author addresses several objections raised against the claim that Allah is the Creator of all things. Opponents argue that 'كل' does not imply universality, its indication is only conjectural, and that specification renders it useless. The author refutes each, providing logical proofs that a general term retains its evidential force for what remains.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first objection and response
Read the objection that 'كل' does not indicate comprehensiveness. Notice the author's counterexample: if a master says 'All my slaves are free,' all are freed. This shows the term does imply universality.
The counterexample is from everyday language.
2
Understand the second objection and response
The opponent says the indication is only conjectural. The author accepts this, stating that verbal evidence yields only probability, which is sufficient in juridical arguments.
This concedes a point but argues it does not undermine the proof.
3
Examine the third objection and the two proofs
The opponent claims specification makes the general term useless. The author first denies specification, then concedes and gives two demonstrations that even if specified, it remains proof for the unspecified parts.
Focus on the two proofs: independence of indication and effective cause.
4
Note the fourth objection and brief denial
The opponent mentions connected specifying indicators. The author simply denies their existence, reserving fuller refutation for later.
This is a short rebuttal; the main arguments are the two proofs.

Commentary
This passage is a series of three objections and responses regarding the generality of the term 'كل' (all) in the phrase 'خالق كل شيء' (Creator of all things). The first objection: the word 'كل' does not imply universality. The author responds by giving a counterexample: if a master says 'All my slaves are free,' the freeing applies to all of them, proving that 'كل' does indicate universality. The second objection: the indication is only conjectural. The author accepts this, stating that all verbal evidence yields only probability, but that is sufficient in juridical arguments. The third objection: this general term has been specified (by exclusion of God Himself and other exceptions), so it cannot be used as evidence. The author first denies that specification has occurred, then concedes for argument's sake and provides two logical proofs that a specified general term remains evidence for the unspecified portions. Proof one: the indication of a general term for each individual object is independent; the departure of one does not necessitate departure of others. Proof two: the effective cause for the ruling applies to unspecified items and specification does not negate it, because negation in some forms does not contradict affirmation in others. Then a fourth objection is raised: connected specifying indicators exist (that specify the general immediately). The author simply denies this, reserving fuller refutation for later.

Refutation of Interpreting 'Ja'ala' as Judicial Declaration
Answers the first objection by defining 'ja'ala' as making a thing possess a quality and refutes the interpretation as mere declaration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Mutakallimun refute the interpretation of the verb 'ja'ala' as mere judgment or naming, and how the true meaning entails creation of qualities.

Core Idea
The verb 'ja'ala' means to make something possess a quality, not merely to declare it.

In this passage, the author responds to the first objection against the Ash'ari doctrine of God's creation of human acts. The objection had misinterpreted the Qur'anic verb 'ja'ala' (to make) as mere judicial declaration. The author defines its linguistic reality and provides three decisive arguments to show that even if one accepted the opponent's interpretation, it would lead to absurd consequences or actually prove the Ash'ari position.

3
Identify the three arguments

Commentary
This passage is an answer to the first objection, which likely claimed that the verse proves God creates human faith directly. The author gives two aspects of the answer, but the text focuses on the first: the meaning of the verb 'ja'ala' (جعل) in the verse. The author defines 'ja'ala' when used with two objects as causing something to be described by a quality (تصيير), which is the creation of that quality in the thing. The alternative interpretations (the 'وجوه' mentioned) that the opponent gave are dismissed as metaphorical; the literal meaning (الحقيقة) is the primary one. Then the author refutes the interpretation of 'ja'ala' as mere judgment or naming (الحكم والتسمية) with three arguments:

1. If 'ja'ala' meant declaring something to be, then one could say 'he made God existent, living, knowing, powerful' just by declaring it, which is absurd.
2. Linguists agree that 'ja'ala' is literal in the sense of making (تصيير), so it cannot be equally literal in another sense without equivocation (اشتراك).
3. Even if we accept the interpretation of judgment and naming, it still implies compulsion (الجبر) because God's information (خبر) about something necessitates its occurrence; otherwise God's speech would be false, which is impossible. Thus the thing must happen necessarily.

The author concludes that even this weak interpretation, if accepted, would actually support his own doctrine (that God creates all acts) rather than refute it.

Refutation of Interpreting 'Ja'l' as Judgment and Naming
The author refutes the claim that 'ja'l' means judgment and naming with three proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why interpreting the term 'ja'l' (making) as merely judgment or naming is linguistically and rationally untenable, and how it inadvertently supports the doctrine of compulsion (jabr).

Core Idea
Reading 'ja'l' as judgment conceals the logical necessity of divine compulsion.

This passage is a focused refutation of an opposing interpretation of the Quranic term 'ja'l'. The author argues that the opponents' reading is not only linguistically forced but also leads to absurd theological consequences. By examining three distinct proofs, he shows that 'ja'l' must imply real transformation and existence, and that even if we accept their reading, it backfires by entailing the very compulsion they deny.

1
Understand the linguistic foundation
The author first establishes that 'tasbīr' (making into) means bringing an attribute into existence, and that alternative readings are metaphorical. This sets the stage for the proofs.
Notice that the consensus of linguists is cited for the metaphorical status of alternative readings.
2
Examine the first two proofs
The first proof shows an absurd consequence: if 'ja'l' meant judgment, then one could say 'he made God existent,' which is false. The second proof argues against homonymy: since 'ja'l' is literal for transformation, it cannot be literal for judgment.
These proofs target the linguistic and logical flaws directly.
3
Analyze the third proof
Even on the opponents' own interpretation, compulsion (jabr) follows logically. Because God's information about something makes its opposite impossible, so the reported state must necessarily occur. Thus their weak interpretation actually strengthens the author's position.
This is a turning the tables argument.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Infinite regress is impossible; the preponderant for human will must be God.

The author presents a concise rational proof that complements the Quranic evidence. The argument focuses on the principle that any preference (tarjih) requires a preponderant (murajjih) outside the will itself. If the servant were the preponderant, an infinite regress would occur. This lesson will trace the logical steps of this proof.

Guided Path

Identify the two possible wills

Second Answer: 'Aghfalna' Means Creation of Forgetfulness
Al-Razi argues that 'aghfalna' must mean creation of forgetfulness, with a proof that the servant cannot create his own forgetfulness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Razi argues that 'aghfalna' must mean creation of forgetfulness, based on a rational proof that the servant cannot create his own forgetfulness.

Core Idea
The servant cannot create his own forgetfulness; only Allah can.

Al-Razi now provides a second answer to the objection, conceding that the word 'aghfalna' could apply to multiple meanings. However, he insists that the context forces it to mean 'creation of forgetfulness.' He supports this with a powerful rational proof showing that the human being is incapable of originating their own forgetfulness, thus it must be from Allah. This lesson walks through that proof step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation, noting how al-Razi structures his argument. He first states his position, then provides a proof by dividing the attempt to create forgetfulness into two cases.
Pay attention to the logical flow: premise, division, refutation of each part, conclusion.

Al-Razi says if the servant tried to create forgetfulness, it would be either absolute forgetfulness or forgetfulness of a specific thing. Grasp why each is impossible.

Al-Razi calls this a 'decisive rational point' (nuktah qāṭi‘ah ‘aqliyyah).

This argument defends divine omnipotence: even forgetfulness, which seems like a lack, is a positive creation from Allah.

And from that is what was narrated that he—peace be upon him—performed hajj individually (ifrad), or in conjunction (qiran), or in tamattu', even though the hajj was one hajj, and we know that he did not do all. Commentary
This passage presents the first aspect (wajh) of the second proof that isolated reports (akhbar al-ahad) cannot yield certainty in matters of belief. The argument begins by acknowledging the high status of the Companions as narrators, but then demonstrates that even their narrations fail to provide certainty. The author uses the example of the most public and frequently repeated acts of worship: the adhan (call to prayer) and iqama (second call). The Prophet performed these daily for many years, and the caliphs continued them for decades in front of large congregations. Despite this, narrators could not agree on the details: whether the adhan includes tarjih (raising the voice), and whether the iqama is recited singly or in pairs. The author refutes the possibility that both forms occurred interchangeably, arguing that if that were the case, narrators would have transmitted the variation. Their failure to do so, despite the abundance of witnesses, undermines confidence in their transmission of more subtle matters heard only once. The passage then adds examples: the type of hajj performed by the Prophet (ifrad, qiran, or tamattu'), and whether he raised his hands at ruku' and intisab. Ibn Mas'ud and Ibn 'Umar disagreed on the latter. The author concludes with a rhetorical question: if they could not agree on such clear and commonly observed acts, how can they be trusted with fiqhi issues they heard only once, never wrote down, and transmitted decades later from memory? This argument is used to challenge the reliability of ahad reports in establishing certain knowledge.

Commentary
This passage presents the first of three proofs that necessary knowledge (al-ʿilm al-ḍarūrī) affirms that human beings are the creators (mūjidūn) of their own actions. The author begins by recalling that Abu al-Husayn claims this necessary knowledge, and then outlines the proof. The argument proceeds in several steps: First, every rational person knows from introspection that his actions depend on his motives (dawāʿī); without motives, no action would occur. Examples illustrate this: when hungry and food is available, one eats; when one believes food is poisoned, one refrains. Second, one also knows from observing other rational people in sound condition that their actions necessarily follow their motives—e.g., a rational child who knows water benefits him when thirsty will inevitably drink, and if he knows fire harms him, he will not enter it. Third, the definition of 'creator' (mūjid) is that from whom the act proceeds in accordance with his motives. Therefore, rational people know by the primary dictates of their intellects (badāʾih ʿuqūlihim) that humans are such creators. The conclusion is that this knowledge is necessary (ḍarūrī). The proof relies on immediate self-awareness and universal observation, not on theoretical reasoning, to establish the certainty of human agency.

Commentary
This passage presents the author's response to the third objection (shubha) commonly raised against the Ash'ari position on divine burdening (taklif). The author identifies two main responses. First, he asserts that through ten proofs (al-dala'il al-'ashara), it has been established that God does indeed burden His servants beyond their capacity (kallafa bi ma la yutaq). This directly refutes the opponent's claim that God would not do such a thing. Second, the objection rests on the premise of rational good and evil (al-husn wa al-qubh al-'aqliyyayn), which the author states is invalid, promising a later refutation. The author then notes that many of the opponent's examples reduce to this same principle of rational good and evil. He lists a few: the impermissibility of sending messengers to inanimate objects, the impermissibility of the Wise creating reviling of Himself, and the absurd scenario of binding someone and commanding him to stay in the air. All these are deemed rationally wrong (qabiha fi al-'uqul). Thus, if one denies the foundation of rational good and evil, all these objections lose their force. The passage is both a refutation and a methodological reduction, showing the opponent's reliance on a principle the author rejects.

Commentary
This passage is a rational proof for the Ash'ari position that God creates human actions. The author presents the first argument: The disbeliever who commits disbelief either is capable of faith or not. If not, then the point is conceded (God prevents him). If he is capable, then his power is equally related to both opposites (faith and disbelief). His choice of disbelief over faith therefore requires a preponderator (murajjih) to explain why one possibility occurs rather than the other. This preponderator cannot come from the servant because that would lead to an infinite regress (the same question reapplies to the servant's act of preponderating). Therefore, the preponderator must be from God, meaning God creates the motive that determines the action. The author declares it a conclusive proof. The text includes an editor's marginal note criticizing the proof, but the author's original argument ends before that note.

The Third Proof: Knowledge Requires Prior Knowledge
Al-Matalib al-'Aliyah vol.9 p.383 - Third rational proof on impossibility of acquiring knowledge without prior knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third rational proof that the acquisition of knowledge by the servant requires prior knowledge, leading to an infinite regress, thereby proving that all knowledge is directly created by God.

Core Idea
True knowledge of a thing requires prior knowledge, making self-acquisition impossible.

This lesson examines a powerful rational proof that challenges the notion that humans can acquire knowledge purely through their own effort. The author argues that before we can intentionally seek knowledge, we must already know that our belief corresponds to reality, which would require prior knowledge of the object itself. This leads to an infinite regress, demonstrating that initial knowledge must be granted directly by God.

Guided Path
1

2

3
Recognize the Impossibility
Understand why this infinite regress is impossible (it would mean no knowledge can ever be acquired).
4
Connect to Divine Creation
See how this proof supports the Ash'ari position that all knowledge is created by God, not acquired by human effort.

Commentary
This is a third rational proof focusing on the acquisition of knowledge. The author argues that for the servant to intentionally acquire knowledge of something, he must first know that his belief corresponds to reality. Therefore, knowledge must be created directly by God. The author notes that these arguments were already detailed earlier in the book, so he does not repeat them at length.
