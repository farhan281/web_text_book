Third Interpretation: iḍlāl as Leaving and Not Preventing
The author presents the third interpretation that iḍlāl means leaving the servant alone with his misguidance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third interpretation of iḍlāl as leaving the servant alone (takhliya) and how linguistic analogies support this understanding.

Core Idea
iḍlāl means Allah leaves the servant to his own misguidance, not coercively causing it.

In this lesson, we explore the third interpretation of the term iḍlāl (misguidance) in the context of divine attributes. The author presents a nuanced view that avoids attributing direct causation of evil to Allah. By examining linguistic examples, this interpretation reframes iḍlāl as a permissive act rather than a causative one.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the literal translation to get the literal meaning.
Notice the key terms: إضلال, تخلية, منع, جبر.
2
Identify the key analogies
The passage uses two analogies: a father who does not discipline his son, and a man who leaves his sword in damp ground. Identify how each analogy illustrates 'leaving alone' leading to harm.
3
Reflect on responsibility
Consider how this interpretation shifts responsibility for misguidance from Allah to the servant. Ask yourself: Does this interpretation solve the problem of evil?
This connects to the broader discussion of divine justice and qadar.

Commentary
This passage presents the third interpretation. Here, 'iḍlāl' is understood as 'takhliyah' (leaving alone) and refraining from forceful prevention. The idea is that Allah does not actively create misguidance but rather leaves the servant to his own choice, not preventing him from going astray. The author provides linguistic analogies: if a father does not discipline his son, he is said to have 'corrupted him' even though he did not actively corrupt him; similarly, if someone leaves a sword in damp ground, he is said to have ruined it. This interpretation shifts the responsibility to the servant and sees Allah's role as permissive.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: This interpretation implies that Allah is indifferent or weak, unable to prevent misguidance.
Correction: Rather, it is an interpretation of justice: Allah does not coerce people into misguidance but leaves them to their own choices as a test, and 'iḍlāl' is understood metaphorically as leaving them alone.

Key Takeaway
The third interpretation understands iḍlāl as Allah's permissive leaving of the servant to his own choice, supported by linguistic usage that describes omission as causing harm.

Fourth Interpretation: Ḍalāl as Punishment
The author argues that 'ḍalāl' means punishment in the afterlife, citing two Quranic verses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth interpretation that ḍalāl in certain Quranic verses refers to punishment rather than misguidance, and how this interpretation resolves theological concerns about attributing misguidance to Allah.

Core Idea
Ḍalāl in the Quran can mean punishment, not just spiritual misguidance.

The author presents a key interpretative move: when the Quran speaks of Allah leading disbelievers astray (iḍlāl), it may refer to His punishing them in the afterlife, not to creating misguidance in their hearts. This interpretation avoids attributing the origin of disbelief to Allah and aligns with verses describing criminals as being 'in ḍalāl' on the Day of Judgment. Let's examine the evidence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the literal translation. Notice the author's style of citing Quranic verses as evidence.
The Arabic may contain OCR errors; focus on the argument's structure.
2
Identify the evidence
The author cites two Quranic verses. Find them and see how each one links 'ḍalāl' to punishment. The first describes criminals being in 'ḍalāl' on the Day of Resurrection. The second describes disbelievers being led astray by Allah in a context of punishment.
Pay attention to the second verse's conclusion: 'Thus Allah leads the disbelievers astray.'
3
Understand the theological payoff
Reflect on why this interpretation matters. If 'ḍalāl' in these verses means punishment, then Allah's 'leading astray' is His punishing, not His creating misguidance. This resolves the problem of evil and preserves Allah's justice.

والتأويل الرابع : الضلال هو العذاب والإضلال هو التعذيب . والدليل عليه : قوله تعالى : ( إن المجرمين في ضلال وسعر . يوم يسحبون في النار على وجوههم ، ذو قوامي سقر (۱) ) فوصفهم الله تعالى بأنهم يوم القيامة في ضلال . وذلك الضلال ليس إلا العذاب . وقال تعالى : ( إذا الأغلال في أعناقهم ، والسلاسل يسحبون في الحميم ثم في النار يسجرون ثم قيل لهم : أينما كنتم تشركون من دون الله ؟ قالوا ضلوا عنا، بل لم نكن ندعو من قبل شيئا . كذلك يضل الله الكافرين (۲) ) وهذه الآية تدل على أن الضلال المذكور في هذه الآية هو العذاب .
The fourth interpretation: ḍalāl is the punishment and iḍlāl is the tormenting. The evidence for it is the saying of Allah, exalted: 'Indeed the criminals are in ḍalāl and blaze. On the day they are dragged in the fire on their faces, taste the touch of Saqar.' So Allah described them as being in ḍalāl on the Day of Resurrection, and that ḍalāl is nothing but punishment. And this verse indicates that the ḍalāl mentioned in this verse is the punishment.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth interpretation. It takes the words 'ḍalāl' and 'iḍlāl' to refer to punishment and torment, especially in the afterlife. The author cites Quranic verses where 'ḍalāl' is associated with the punishment of the criminals in hell. The argument is that in those contexts, 'ḍalāl' cannot mean misguidance, but rather refers to the state of being in torment. So when Allah says 'kadhālika yuḍillu Allāhu al-kāfirīn' (Thus Allah leads the disbelievers astray), it means He punishes them. This interpretation avoids the problem of attributing the creation of actual misguidance to Allah.

Correction: This interpretation does not deny Allah's guidance; it simply reinterprets the punitive aspect of 'iḍlāl' in specific verses. Allah still guides whom He wills, but 'iḍlāl' in the context of punishment means He punishes, not that He creates misguidance.

Fifth Interpretation: Iḍlāl as Destruction
Author presents fifth interpretation of iḍlāl as destruction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fifth interpretation of iḍlāl as destruction and its linguistic basis.

Core Idea
When Allah says He 'leads astray,' He may mean He destroys deeds.

This lesson explores a specific interpretation of divine 'leading astray' (iḍlāl) found in the Qur'an. Rather than misguidance, some scholars interpret it as destruction or nullification of deeds. The author presents this view with a verse and a linguistic analogy.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once and then the English translation. Let the author's own argument structure come through.
Notice the clear structure: interpretative claim, Qur'anic evidence, linguistic analogy.
2
Identify the core claim
The author states that iḍlāl can be interpreted as halāk (destruction). Locate this claim and the supporting verse from Surah Muhammad.
The verse is: 'Those who disbelieve and hinder from the path of Allah, He will cause their deeds to be lost.'
3
Study the linguistic analogy
The author gives examples from Arabic: 'water lost in milk' and 'people made their leader lost (buried)'. Understand how these illustrate the meaning of disappearance or annihilation.
This shows that the root ḍ-l-l can imply concealment and destruction, not just misguidance.

Source Text

والتأويل الخامس: أن يحمل الإضلال على الهلاك قال تعالى : الذين كفروا وصدوا عن سبيل الله أضل أعمالهم (۳) ) قيل : أبطلها وأهلكها . ومجازه : من قولهم : ضل الماء في اللبن ، إذا صار مستهلكاً فيه . ويقال : أضللته أنا : إذا أهلكته وصيرته كالمعدوم. ومنه يقال : أضل القوم رئيسهم . إذا واروه في قبره فأخفيوه حتى صار لا يرى
The fifth interpretation: That iḍlāl is interpreted as destruction. Allah said: 'Those who disbelieve and hinder from the path of Allah, He will cause their deeds to be lost (aḍalla).' It was said: He nullified them and destroyed them. Its figurative meaning is from their saying: 'Water became lost (ḍalla) in milk,' when it becomes consumed in it. And it is said: 'I made it lost (aḍlaltuhu)' if I destroyed it and made it as if non-existent. From this it is said: 'The people made their leader lost (aḍalla)' when they buried him in his grave and hid him until he became unseen.
Source pages p. 387

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth interpretation. Here, 'iḍlāl' is understood as destruction or annulment. The author cites the verse from Surah Muhammad where Allah says He will 'aḍalla' the deeds of the disbelievers, which is explained as nullifying and destroying them. The linguistic analogy is given: when water is poured into milk, it becomes lost (ḍalla) meaning it is consumed and no longer distinct. Thus, 'aḍalla' can mean to cause something to be lost or destroyed. This interpretation is applied to verses where Allah is said to 'lead astray' — it means He destroys their deeds or brings about their ruin.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: This interpretation makes Allah directly destroy deeds implying injustice.
