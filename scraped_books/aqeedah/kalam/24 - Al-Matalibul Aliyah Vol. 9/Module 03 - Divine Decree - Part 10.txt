First Interpretation of 'Yudill': Occasion of Misguidance
The author presents the first ta'wil of 'yudill' as occasion-based misguidance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first ta'wil of 'yudill' as an occasion-based figurative expression, and how it applies to Allah's attribute of leading astray.

Core Idea
Allah's 'misguidance' is the occasion for people's self-chosen error.

This lesson explores the author's first interpretive proposal for the verses mentioning Allah's leading astray. He argues that when someone goes astray in the presence of something, that thing is figuratively called a 'misleader'. The same logic applies to Allah: He is the occasion for misguidance when people choose error in circumstances He provides. We will examine how this interpretation uses Quranic verses about idols to support its plausibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the translation to grasp the author's main point.
Notice the pattern: 'when a person goes astray in the presence of X, X is said to have led him astray'.
2
Understand the core concept: occasion vs. cause
The author distinguishes between something being the direct cause of error and being merely the occasion (the context) in which a person freely chooses error. The idols did not create disbelief; they were present when people chose disbelief.
This is a key distinction in kalam discussions about divine attributes.
3
Examine the Quranic examples
The author cites three verses: Ibrahim's statement about idols, the mention of Yaghuth, Ya'uq, and Nasr, and a partial verse about revelation. For each, he explains that 'they led astray' means 'people went astray at the sight of them'.
The third verse is incomplete in the source but continues the same idea.
4
Reflect on application to Allah
Ask yourself: How does this ta'wil help reconcile the verse 'Allah leads astray whom He wills' with human responsibility? The answer is that Allah's 'leading astray' is the occasion, not the creation, of error.
This avoids attributing evil directly to Allah.

Commentary
This passage is an interpretive proposal (ta'wil). After proving that 'idlaal' cannot mean literal creation of error, the author offers possible alternative meanings. Here he presents the first one: when a person freely chooses to go astray in the presence of some object (like an idol), that object is figuratively called the 'misleader' (mudill), even though it had no causal effect. The author cites Quranic examples: Ibrahim's speech about idols ('they have led many astray') and the mention of pre-Islamic idols (Yaghuth, Ya'uq, Nasr) in Surah Nuh, where the idols are said to have led many astray. In both cases, the meaning is that people went astray when they saw or worshipped those idols, not that the idols themselves created error. The third example is cut off but continues a similar point: revelation may 'increase' many in error, meaning it becomes the occasion for their existing misguidance.

Thus, the phrase 'yudillu man yashaa' (Allah leads astray whom He wills) can be understood in this metaphorical sense: Allah is the ultimate occasion for misguidance when He wills, by allowing circumstances in which people choose error, but not by creating that error directly.

Conclusion: The Method of Dialectic
Author concludes that the intended meaning is not creating disbelief and dialectic does not require specifying it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author concludes his arguments on the verse 'He misguides whom He wills' and why specifying the correct interpretation is unnecessary in dialectic.

Core Idea
In dialectic, refuting a false interpretation is sufficient without needing to specify the true one.

In this final passage, the author draws together his earlier arguments to establish that the intended meaning of divine misguidance cannot be the creation of disbelief. Once this negative conclusion is reached, he makes a key methodological point: in the context of dialectic (jadal), we are not required to identify the correct positive meaning. This clarifies the scope of theological debate.

Guided Path

Recap the preceding arguments

Consider the previous lesson's proofs.

Identify the negative conclusion
The author states that the intended meaning cannot be the creation of disbelief and ignorance. Understand this as a refutation of a specific interpretation.
3
Grasp the methodological point
The author says we know the meaning is something else but don't need to specify it in dialectic. This is a crucial point about the limits of debate.
This shows the sufficiency of negative argumentation.
4
Apply the lesson
Consider why it might be tempting to demand a positive alternative. The author's approach shows that refuting a false interpretation can be sufficient in argumentation.
