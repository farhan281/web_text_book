The Condition for Voluntary Action

Voluntary action depends on subjective belief of benefit, not objective goodness.

This passage from Razi's second preliminary explains the psychological basis of voluntary action. Before we can analyze moral responsibility, we must understand what drives an agent to act. Razi gives a clear condition: the agent must believe the action is better for itself than leaving it. This belief is relative to the agent's own perception, not absolute truth.

Pleasure and Pain as Ultimate Ends
Razi classifies desires into intrinsic and instrumental with a regress argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that pleasure and pain are the only intrinsically desired ends, and why a regress must be avoided.

Core Idea
Pleasure and pain are the ultimate anchors of all animal motivation.

Razi begins this preliminary by laying a foundational claim about the structure of desire. He argues that all actions ultimately aim at either obtaining pleasure or avoiding pain; everything else is a means. This is not yet an ethical claim but a psychological one about the necessary end-points of motivation. Understanding this helps us see why infinite chains of desire are impossible.

Guided Path

Read the passage and translation

Look for the intrinsic desires (pleasure and pain) and everything else as instrumental. Understand the distinction between 'desired for itself' and 'desired for something else'.
3
Understand the regress argument
Razi argues that without ultimate ends, we would have infinite regress or circularity. Ask yourself why this is a problem and how the classification solves it.
This connects to the learning objective.

The author then argues that if every action were done for another end, an infinite regress or circularity would occur, so some actions must be intrinsically desired. This establishes a foundation for motivation.

Capacity is the readiness of sound constitution to act when motive is present and obstacle absent.

He provides a precise definition of what it means for an animal to be 'capable' (qādir) of an action, grounding capacity not in mere possession of limbs but in a conditional relationship between constitution, motive, and obstacle. Second Argument: The Implication of Human Power on Divine Power
The author refutes that a servant's power can cause an act God wills, showing it implies divine impotence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second argument against the view that a human's power can cause an act that is within God's power, and the distinction between God not being able to create an already existing thing versus being prevented by another.

Core Idea
If a human caused what God wills, the human would have incapacitated God — an impossibility.

This lesson presents the second argument from the author's critique of the claim that a servant's power can produce an act that God also wills. The argument shows that such a scenario would imply that the human prevents God from acting, which is absurd. The author then answers an objection clarifying that God's inability to create an already existing thing is not the same as being prevented.

Find the logical steps: if an act within God's power occurs by a human's power, then at that moment God cannot bring it into existence (since it already exists), so the human must have prevented God. That is impossible.

Commentary
This passage is the third proof (البرهان الثالث) that a human being is not the creator of his own actions. The author uses a reductio ad absurdum argument based on a scenario where both God and a human will contradictory states for one object—God wills stillness, the human wills motion. Three cases are possible: both intentions occur, neither occurs, or one occurs. Each is shown impossible. If both occur, the body would be simultaneous moving and still, a contradiction. If neither occurs, then God's will for stillness and the human's will for motion are frustrated, but since both wills necessitate their effects if no hindrance exists, the lack of effect implies an external hindrance; however, each will would be hindered by the other's effect, so if both are hindered, they must actually occur to serve as hindrances, returning to the first case. Thus the second case collapses into the first. The third case, where one occurs without the other, is also impossible because both agents are independent and their powers are equal, as the intended act is indivisible and admits no degrees. If one power were stronger, its intention would preponderate without a preponderating factor, which is impossible. Hence all three cases are false, proving that the initial assumption—that a human can be a creator of his actions—is false. The author notes this is the famous proof for divine unity.

Commentary
