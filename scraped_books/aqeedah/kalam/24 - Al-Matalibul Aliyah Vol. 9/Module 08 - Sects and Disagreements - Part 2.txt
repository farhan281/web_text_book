The Dilemma of Satan's Misguidance
Sanusi presents the dilemma and the first refutation of self-causation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the dilemma posed by the origin of Satan's misguidance and why the Mu'tazilite position leads to either infinite regress or a futile concept of seeking refuge.

Core Idea
Satan's misguidance cannot come from another Satan or himself without logical absurdity.

Sanusi begins a sophisticated argument against the Mu'tazilite view that human misguidance stems solely from Satan. He forces a dilemma on the origin of Satan's own misguidance: if it comes from another Satan, we get an infinite regress; if from himself, then seeking refuge from Satan becomes pointless. This short passage sets up the first part of his refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic text once, then the translation. Notice how Sanusi presents the two possibilities: external source (another Satan) and self-causation.
2
Identify the dilemma
Find the two branches of the dilemma: 'if from another Satan' and 'if from himself'. Notice the immediate consequence of each.
The first branch leads to infinite regress (impossible); the second is said to be invalid for two reasons.
3
Analyze the first aspect
Focus on the first reason why self-causation is invalid: if Satan can misguide himself, then humans can too, making seeking refuge from Satan futile. Ask yourself why this is a problem for the Mu'tazilite.
4
Connect to the broader debate
Consider how this argument fits into the larger discussion about divine responsibility for evil. The Mu'tazilites wanted to absolve God; Sanusi shows their logic backfires.

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth argument, which is more complex. The argument begins with the Mu'tazilite claim that the misguidance of humans (disbelievers and sinners) comes from Satan's whispering. The author then considers the source of Satan's own misguidance. If Satan's misguidance comes from another Satan, we get an infinite regress (tasalsul), which is impossible (muhal). If it comes from himself (i.e., he misguides himself), then the author gives two refutations: First, if Satan's misguidance is self-caused, then why can't human misguidance also be self-caused? This would make isti'adha pointless. Second, the author analyzes how a mukallaf (accountable person) would choose ignorance: a person never chooses ignorance for its own sake, but only if he mistakenly thinks it is knowledge. This requires a preceding ignorance, which leads to a first ignorance. That first ignorance cannot be self-caused; it must come from Allah. Therefore, Satan's misguidance ultimately comes from Allah (idlaal). Thus, seeking refuge from Satan while attributing his misguidance to Allah is absurd. The author then cites Quran 28:63 to support this: the devils say they led people astray just as they themselves were led astray, implying an ultimate divine origin. This argument forcefully refutes the Mu'tazilite denial of divine involvement in Satan's evil.

The Psychology of Choosing Ignorance
Sanusi presents the second refutation and the Quranic proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a person cannot choose ignorance without a preceding ignorance, and how this leads to the conclusion that all misguidance ultimately comes from Allah.

Core Idea
Ignorance is never chosen for its own sake, so the first ignorance must be from Allah.

Sanusi now delves deeper into the second refutation of self-caused misguidance. He analyzes the psychology of the accountable person (mukallaf) and shows that no one chooses ignorance unless they mistake it for knowledge. This forces a chain of ignorances terminating in a first ignorance, which cannot be self-caused but must come from Allah. The passage concludes with a Quranic verse confirming this.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation. Focus on the logical steps: choice of ignorance -> preceding ignorance -> first ignorance -> divine origin.
2
Understand the psychology
The key premise: a mukallaf never chooses ignorance or disbelief unless he mistakenly thinks it is knowledge. Ask yourself why this is plausible.
3
Trace the chain
Each instance of ignorance (except the first) must be preceded by another ignorance. This chain stops at a first ignorance. How does that first ignorance occur? It cannot be from a previous mistake; it must be from Allah.
4
Connect to the Quranic evidence
The verse (28:63) shows that the devils themselves admit they were led astray, implying a common source. Reflect on how this supports the argument.

The Mu'tazilite Argument that Predestination Prevents God from Deserving Worship
Mu'tazila argue that God deserves worship only if He is beneficent, which is incompatible with creating disbelief and then punishing it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Mu'tazila argued that divine predestination (jabr) is incompatible with God's worthiness of worship, and the two lines of evidence they offered.

Core Idea
If God compels disbelief and then punishes it, He cannot be worthy of worship.

The Mu'tazila, known for emphasizing divine justice, argued that the very concept of 'god' entails being worthy of worship. But if God predestines people to disbelieve and then eternally punishes them, He would not be beneficent and thus not deserving of worship. In this passage, they present two aspects of this argument, citing the Quranic verse 'What an excellent Protector' to illustrate their point.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Mu'tazilite definition of 'god'.
The passage opens by stating that 'the god' is the one who deserves worship. This sets the premise: worship is due only to a being that meets certain conditions.
This is a key foundational claim in their theology.
2
Examine the first aspect: beneficence.
The first argument links worthiness of worship to utmost beneficence. If God creates disbelief to lead people to hell, He is not beneficent. Moreover, a believer cannot be sure of final perseverance, so faith becomes a burden.
The author cites al-Jubba'i's commentary on the Quranic verse to reinforce this.
3
Examine the second aspect: human ability.
The second argument, though cut off, asserts that worship is only due if the servant has the ability (free will) to choose obedience.
This is the core of the Mu'tazilite stance on free will.

Commentary
This passage introduces a new chapter on the Basmala and presents the Mu'tazilite critique of predestination (الجبر). The Mu'tazila define 'god' (الإله) as 'the one who deserves worship' and argue that this is incompatible with the doctrine of jabr (compulsion). They provide two points. The first point: God deserves worship because of His utmost beneficence (الإنعام والإحسان). If He were to create disbelief in people in order to lead them to the Fire, He would not be beneficent. Moreover, a believer cannot be sure he will not end in apostasy, making his faith a burden; such a God would be the greatest fabricator against the greatest benefactor, and thus not worthy of worship. The author cites al-Jubba'i's commentary on the verse 'What an excellent Protector and what an excellent Helper' (Quran 22:78) to reinforce this: if God creates disbelief and then punishes it eternally, He would not be excellent but evil. The second point is only partially given: God deserves worship only if the servant has the ability (قادراً) to choose, implying that without free will, worship is meaningless. The passage is polemical, presenting the Mu'tazilite case against Ash'ari predestinarianism.

Mu'tazila Objection to Attributing Evil to God Based on al-Rahman al-Rahim
Mu'tazila argue that if evil comes from God, He cannot be fully merciful.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila argument that if God creates disbelief and sin, His punishment outweighs His mercy, contradicting the names al-Rahman al-Rahim, and how they support their view with Quranic verses.

Core Idea
If God creates evil, His punishment exceeds His mercy, so He cannot be fully merciful.

This lesson presents the Mu'tazila's objection to the Ash'ari view that all actions are created by God. They argue that if God were the creator of disbelief and sin, then the people of the Fire would outnumber the people of the Garden, making God's punishment greater than His mercy. Since God is called al-Rahman (the All-Merciful) and al-Rahim (the Especially Merciful), such a state would be incompatible with these names. The passage shows how the Mu'tazila use rational reasoning and Quranic verses to support their denial that God creates evil.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the literal translation. Let the structure of the argument sink in.
The Arabic uses conditional phrasing and Quranic evidence.
2
Identify the logical argument
Find the 'if...then...' structure. The Mu'tazila say: If evil from God → punishment > mercy → not al-Rahman. Write this chain in your own words.
3
Examine the scriptural support
Look at the three Quranic verses cited. For each, consider how it supports the idea that evil comes from human choice, not God's creation.
The verses emphasize human responsibility and God's mercy.
4
Understand the alternative explanation
The Mu'tazila propose that God created all for mercy, and evil arises from human selves. Contrast this with the opposing view that God creates all actions.
5
Reflect on the theological stakes
Consider why this debate matters: it affects how we understand God's justice, mercy, and human free will.
This is a classic point of contention between Ash'aris and Mu'tazila.

Commentary
This passage presents the Mu'tazila's objection. They argue that if disbelief and sin were created by God, then the people of the Fire would outnumber the people of the Garden, making God's punishment greater than His mercy. Since 'al-Rahman' denotes perfect mercy and 'al-Rahim' requires that no opposite (i.e., punishment without cause) occur, God would not merit these names. Therefore, they conclude that God cannot be the creator of evil actions. Instead, they claim, God created all people for benefit and mercy; evil comes from the servants themselves. The imperative 'Guide us' proves the servant is a genuine agent who requests guidance.

They found support in the Qur'anic verse 'Ihdina al-sirat al-mustaqim' (Guide us to the straight path), which they interpreted as evidence that humans are capable of requesting and responding to divine guidance. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Divine wrath precedes misguidance – word order reveals causation.

This passage presents a key Mu'tazilite interpretation of Quranic verses about God leading people astray. They argue that 'idlaal' means leading away from Paradise, not actively creating disbelief.
