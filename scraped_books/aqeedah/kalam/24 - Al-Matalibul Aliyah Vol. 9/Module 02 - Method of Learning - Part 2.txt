Differences in I'rab: Grammatical Case Endings
Definition of seventh aspect with example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how variations in grammatical case endings (i'rab) can alter the meaning of a hadith and why this is a critical aspect of hadith criticism.

Core Idea
A single case ending can flip a hadith's meaning entirely.

Hadith scholars were meticulous not only about the chain of narrators but also about the exact wording. Sometimes, even the grammatical case of a word—whether it is nominative, accusative, or genitive—can change the entire meaning. In this lesson, we explore the seventh aspect of criticism: differences in i'rab, using a famous example to see how a subtle shift can lead to opposite interpretations.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage closely
Start by reading the Arabic text and its translation. Notice how the author defines the seventh aspect and then provides a concrete example.
Pay attention to the two possible case endings for the word 'Adam'.
2
Identify the key terms
The terms 'إعراب', 'نصب', and 'رفع' are central. Make sure you understand what each grammatical case means and how it affects the sentence structure.
I'rab refers to the system of case endings in Arabic.
3
Analyze the example
Examine the example 'فحج آدم موسى'. Try to construct both possible interpretations: one with Adam in the accusative and one with Adam in the nominative. What would each mean?
The example is short but powerful.
4
Reflect on the significance
Consider why this aspect is included in hadith criticism. How could a simple case ending error lead to a misrepresentation of the Prophet's words?

Commentary
This passage defines the seventh aspect of criticism of hadith narrators: differences in i'rab, i.e., grammatical case endings. The author explains that case endings (like nominative, accusative, genitive) can be difficult to hear or distinguish, leading to changes in meaning. He gives the example of the phrase 'Fa-hajja Adamu Musa' (he said: Adam argued with Moses). If one misplaces the case ending—making 'Adam' accusative ('Adams argues with Moses')—the meaning becomes completely different from the correct nominative reading ('Adam argued with Moses'). Hence, even a small grammatical error can distort the hadith.

Narrating by Ta'wil: The Eighth Aspect of Hadith Criticism
A narrative illustrating narrating by ta'wil and its implications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how narrators may report hadith by interpretation (ta'wil) and why this is a potential source of variation in hadith transmission.

Core Idea
Narrators may legitimately report the meaning, not just the exact words.

This lesson explores a crucial aspect of hadith criticism: that a narrator might convey a hadith according to its interpreted meaning rather than its literal wording. Through the example of Abu Hurairah, we see how such interpretation can cause apparent contradictions, and how clarification resolves confusion. The passage concludes that this possibility exists for every report.

Guided Path
1
Read the story of Abu Hurairah
Read the story carefully. Notice that Abu Hurairah initially says 'I am fasting' but then begins to eat, causing confusion among the companions.
2
Identify the apparent contradiction
Why would someone claim to be fasting and then eat? The companions were confused because they took his statement literally.
3
Understand Abu Hurairah's explanation
He clarifies that he fasted three days earlier, and based on the Prophet's hadith and the Qur'anic principle, he considers himself fasting for the whole month in a figurative sense.
This is an example of ta'wil: interpreting the literal words to convey a broader meaning.
4
Grasp the author's conclusion
The author states that if Abu Hurairah had not clarified, confusion (talbis) would have occurred. He then generalizes that for any report, it is possible for the narrator to add or subtract by way of interpretation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

The Twelfth Argument: The Prophet's Warnings Imply Lying Occurred
Al-Razi presents the twelfth argument: the Prophet's warnings prove lying was occurring among Muslims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the twelfth argument that the Prophet's strong warnings against lying indicate that some Muslims were fabricating hadith.

Core Idea
The Prophet's severe threats against lying would be unnecessary if no one lied.

Al-Razi points to the numerous hadiths in which the Prophet sternly warns against lying about him. He argues that such warnings are only meaningful if the Prophet knew that lying was occurring among his followers. Moreover, these warnings are directed at Muslims, not hypocrites, because hypocrites would not be deterred by Islamic threats.

Guided Path
1
Read the Prophet's warnings cited.
Al-Razi quotes several hadiths: the threat of Hell for lying, encouragement to convey accurately, and counting false attribution as a major slander.
2
Analyze the logical inference.
If no one were lying, the warnings would be unnecessary. The Prophet's knowledge of future lies implies he knew Muslims would lie.
This is an argument from the existence of the prohibition to the existence of the prohibited act.
3
Refute the counterargument that hypocrites are meant.
Hypocrites already reject prophethood, so they wouldn't care about Islamic threats. Therefore, the warnings must target Muslims.

If the Prophet promoted the Quran over hadith, then ahad reports are less reliable.

Al-Razi brings a textual argument: the hadith scholars themselves transmitted reports that the Prophet urged people to hold fast to the Quran and prohibited the writing of hadith. The Fourteenth Argument: Anthropomorphic Traditions Are Clearly False and Hadith Scholars Double Standard
Al-Razi presents the fourteenth argument: anthropomorphic traditions are false and hadith scholars' criteria are inconsistent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourteenth argument that anthropomorphic traditions are obviously false, yet hadith scholars accept narrators who hold such beliefs, revealing a double standard.

Core Idea
Hadith scholars reject Rafidi narrators but accept anthropomorphists, who are more ignorant.

Al-Razi's final and most polemical argument points to the existence of hadiths that describe God in anthropomorphic terms—like a human body. He argues that these traditions are so absurd that they cannot be true, and any narrator who believes in anthropomorphism is ignorant of God and thus unreliable. Yet hadith scholars reject narrators accused of Rafidism (Shi'ism) but do not reject anthropomorphists, which is inconsistent.

Guided Path
1
Understand the content of anthropomorphic traditions.
Al-Razi says these reports describe God as a huge human with limbs, and they have multiplied beyond interpretation.
2
Identify the logical consequence.
Since anthropomorphism is ignorance of God, and an ignorant person cannot be a true messenger, most of these narrations are false.
3
Analyze the inconsistency of hadith scholars.
They reject narrators for partisan reasons (Rafidism) but not for theological ignorance (anthropomorphism), which is a greater offense.
Al-Razi is criticizing the criteria for accepting narrators.
4
Connect to the conclusion.
The abundance of absurd traditions further proves that ahad reports are weak and yield only speculation.
