The 19th Proof: Protection and Preponderant Motives
Author presents a hadith proof, then addresses two objections about divine graces and motives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari argument from the hadith on protection that a servant's sinlessness is by divine tawfiq, and the principle that an unopposed preponderant motive necessitates action.

Core Idea
A truly unopposed motive must lead to action; otherwise it's not truly preponderant.

This lesson presents the nineteenth proof from the text: a prophetic hadith that attributes protection from sin to Allah, not the servant. The author then tackles two objections from those who interpret this protection as mere divine graces, and the key principle that a preponderant motive (murajjih) necessitates its effect. Understanding this exchange clarifies the Ash'ari view on divine causality and human action.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith proof
Read the hadith and the author's inference that the protection (wiqaya) is from Allah, not the servant. This establishes the foundation that sinlessness is by divine tawfiq.
Note the phrase 'من وقى الذي بين لحييه ورجليه' refers to guarding tongue and private parts.
2
Understand the first objection and answer
The opponents claim the hadith refers to divine graces (al-altaf). The author replies that Allah has done every possible grace, and that a grace must be a preponderator, which necessitates its effect.
3
Understand the second objection and answer
Opponents argue that not every preponderant motive leads to action, citing personal experience. The author clarifies that such inclinations are not truly preponderant because they are opposed by stronger motives.
The key principle: a preponderant motive free from opposition necessarily leads to action.
4
Reflect on the principle of preponderant motive
Consider why this principle matters for the Ash'ari theory of causality. It shows that even in human actions, a fully determined cause (the preponderant motive) inevitably produces its effect.
This connects to broader discussions of qadar and human responsibility.

Commentary
This passage presents the nineteenth proof and includes two objections and answers. The proof is a hadith about being protected between the jaws (tongue) and legs (private parts) leading to Paradise. The author argues that the protection is attributed to Allah, not the servant, showing that the servant's sinlessness is by divine tawfiq. The opponents (likely Mu'tazila) interpret 'protection' as referring to divine graces (al-altaf) that make obedience easier but do not compel. The author's first response: Allah has already done every grace He could, implying that if more grace were possible, He would have done it. Also, a grace (latif) must be a preponderator (murajjih), and every preponderator necessitates its effect. This is a key Ash'ari principle: a preponderant motive necessitates the action. The second objection: Not every preponderance of motive results in action, because we often feel inclined but do not act. The answer: That inclination is not truly preponderant because it is opposed by a stronger inclination; a genuine preponderant motive free from opposition necessarily leads to action. This reinforces the deterministic causality underlying Ash'ari kalam.

Author presents a hadith narrative on divine decree and accountability, then begins analysis of 'qada'.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Decree does not negate striving; both are from Allah's wisdom.

The twentieth proof presents a hadith narrative that directly addresses the apparent tension between divine decree and human accountability. Through a conversation between 'Imran ibn Husayn and a companion, and later a man from Murcia with the Prophet, the passage clarifies that actions are decreed yet people are still responsible for their choices. This lesson unpacks the hadith and its theological implications.

This implies divine purpose and utilization of actions.

Note the Quranic support
The author cites verse 91:7-8 about inspiration of wickedness and righteousness as confirmation.

The 21st Proof: Hadith on Creation and Diversity
Author presents a hadith to prove that differences in actions and beliefs are divinely ordained.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a hadith about Adam's creation from diverse earth is used to argue that differences in human actions and beliefs are divinely ordained.

Core Idea
Our physical diversity points to a deeper, divinely ordained diversity in character and belief.

In this proof, the author cites a hadith from Abu Musa al-Ash'ari to support the doctrine that human actions are not independent but determined by God. The hadith describes how Adam was created from a handful of earth taken from all over, resulting in diversity of temperaments. The author then argues that this diversity in temperament necessarily leads to diversity in morals, actions, and beliefs. Let's examine the text and its theological implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith and its context
Read the Arabic passage once, including the hadith and the author's commentary. Then read the translation. Notice how the author presents the hadith as evidence.
The hadith is narrated by Abu Musa al-Ash'ari.
2
Understand the link between physical diversity and temperament
Focus on the phrase 'فجاء بنو آدم على قدر الأرض' (so the children of Adam came according to the measure of the earth). The author interprets this as diversity of temperaments (اختلاف الأمزجة).
3
See how the author deduces moral and behavioral differences
The author states 'فوجب اختلاف الأخلاق والأفعال والعقائد' (so diversity of morals, actions, and beliefs is necessitated). This is the key conclusion of the proof.
The word 'وجب' indicates necessity, not mere possibility.
4
Reflect on the specific examples
The author gives examples of yellow bile leading to anger and savagery, and phlegm leading to calmness and dullness. Consider how these humors are linked to behavior.
This is based on ancient humoral theory.

Commentary
This passage presents the twenty-first proof in a series of arguments for the doctrine that the servant has no independent power over action (العبد لا استقلال له بالفعل والترك). The author cites a hadith from Abu Musa al-Ash'ari in which the Prophet explains that God created Adam from a handful of soil taken from all parts of the earth, resulting in the diversity of human temperaments—red, black, white, yellow, and varying dispositions like easy, difficult, bad, good. The author interprets this as an indication of the diversity of temperaments (اختلاف الأمزجة), implying that differences in moral character, actions, and beliefs are necessitated (فوجب) by these divinely determined temperaments. He then gives a specific example: if yellow bile (الصفراء) is predominant, then anger, savagery, and harshness are predominant; if phlegm (البلغم) is predominant, then calmness, tranquility, and dullness are predominant. Thus, the hadith is used to support the idea that human actions are ultimately determined by God's creation and not by independent human choice.

The 22nd Proof: Hadith of the Dispute over Qadar
Author narrates a hadith about a dispute between Abu Bakr and Umar on qadar, resolved by the Prophet citing a precedent from angels.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how this hadith serves as a proof for the Ashʿari position that both good and evil are decreed by Allah.

Core Idea
The Prophet settled the qadar dispute by citing angelic precedent: both good and evil are from Allah.

This lesson presents the twenty-second proof from Al-Matalib al-ʿAliyah, a hadith that directly addresses the debate over divine decree and human action. The narration records a historical dispute between Abu Bakr and Umar, and how the Prophet resolved it by referring to an earlier judgment among the angels. This story serves as a powerful support for the Ashʿari doctrine of qadar.

Guided Path
1
Read the Hadith
Read the Arabic passage once through, then follow the translation. Notice the narrative flow: the dispute, the Prophet's inquiries, and the angelic parallel.
The hadith is narrated from Amr ibn Shuʿayb, with a chain of transmission.
2
Identify the Two Positions
Abu Bakr said: 'Good deeds are from Allah, evil deeds are from ourselves.' Umar said: 'Good and evil are both from Allah.' Contrast these views.
Abu Bakr's position resembles the Muʿtazili view, while Umar's is the Ashʿari view.
3
Observe the Prophet's Reaction
The Prophet turned away from Abu Bakr but became cheerful at Umar's statement. Why? His cheer indicates approval of Umar's view.
This shows the Prophet's endorsement of the view that both good and evil are from Allah.
4
Understand the Angelic Precedent
The Prophet reveals that Gabriel and Michael had a similar disagreement, and Israfil judged that 'good and evil are from Allah.' The Prophet confirms this as his judgment.
This elevates the ruling to divine authority, as angels are infallible.
5
Reflect on the Final Statement
The Prophet says: 'If Allah had willed that no one would disobey Him, He would not have created Iblis.' This reinforces that evil and disobedience are part of Allah's decree.
This ends the hadith on a note of divine sovereignty.

Commentary
This passage is an introductory exposition of the perplexing nature of the theological issue of predestination (jabr) and free will (qadar). The author begins by stating that the state of this issue is 'strange' because people have always differed over it due to conflicting factors. He then lists the various contradictory aspects.

The first contradiction is between the foundational premises of the Jabriyya (determinists) and the Qadariyya (free-will advocates). The Jabriyya rely on the self-evident principle that every possible thing requires a preponderator (murajjih) to bring it into existence. Since a human agent capable of two opposites cannot cause one to occur without an external preponderator, that preponderator must be God, leading to determinism. The Qadariyya rely on the equally self-evident principle that praise and blame are only meaningful if the agent has genuine choice and ability. If human actions were determined, praise and blame would be unjust. Thus two self-evident premises apparently conflict.

The second contradiction is in rational proofs: the Jabriyya argue that the detailed particularities of actions are unknown to us, so they cannot be created by us; the Qadariyya argue that actions follow our intentions and motives, so they originate from us. Thus the proof from knowledge (daliil al-'ilm) and the proof from intention (daliil al-qasd) conflict.

The third is from considerations of perfection and deficiency: the Jabriyya hold that creative power is a perfection, so it befits God alone, not the deficient servant; the Qadariyya hold that if the servant's actions are created by God, then God would create frivolity and evil, which is a deficiency unbecoming of God.

The fourth is scriptural evidence: the Quran contains verses that suggest determinism and others that suggest free will.

The fifth is the testimony of nations: every religious community has both Jabri and Qadari factions.

The sixth is cultural artifacts: games like backgammon are said to be designed on the deterministic view, while chess on the free-will view.

All these contradictory lines of evidence show that contradiction pervades this issue. The passage ends abruptly with 'except...' indicating a possible exception or resolution to come.

The author begins by stating that the state of this issue is 'strange' because people have always differed over it due to conflicting factors. He then lists the various contradictory aspects.

The first contradiction is between the foundational premises of the Jabriyya (determinists) and the Qadariyya (free-will advocates). The Jabriyya rely on the self-evident principle that every possible thing requires a preponderator (murajjih) to bring it into existence. Since a human agent capable of two opposites cannot cause one to occur without an external preponderator, that preponderator must be God, leading to determinism. The Qadariyya rely on the equally self-evident principle that praise and blame are only meaningful if the agent has genuine choice and ability. If human actions were determined, praise and blame would be unjust. Thus two self-evident premises apparently conflict.

The second contradiction is in rational proofs: the Jabriyya argue that the detailed particularities of actions are unknown to us, so they cannot be created by us; the Qadariyya argue that actions follow our intentions and motives, so they originate from us. Thus the proof from knowledge (daliil al-'ilm) and the proof from intention (daliil al-qasd) conflict.

The third is from considerations of perfection and deficiency: the Jabriyya hold that creative power is a perfection, so it befits God alone, not the deficient servant; the Qadariyya hold that if the servant's actions are created by God, then God would create frivolity and evil, which is a deficiency unbecoming of God.

The fourth is scriptural evidence: the Quran contains verses that suggest determinism and others that suggest free will.

The fifth is the testimony of nations: every religious community has both Jabri and Qadari factions.

The sixth is cultural artifacts: games like backgammon are said to be designed on the deterministic view, while chess on the free-will view.

All these contradictory lines of evidence show that contradiction pervades this issue. The passage ends abruptly with 'except...' indicating a possible exception or resolution to come.
