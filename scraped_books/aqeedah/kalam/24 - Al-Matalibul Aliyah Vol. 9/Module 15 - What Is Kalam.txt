Repentance is Only from God: The Second Argument
Defines repentance as three sequential elements and proves it is necessarily from God.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three components of repentance (knowledge, state, action) and why their necessary sequence proves that repentance can only come from God.

Core Idea
Repentance is a necessary sequence of knowledge, pain, and action — all created by Allah.

This passage presents the second rational argument for the Ash'ari position that repentance is entirely from God. Imam al-Razi defines repentance as three sequential elements — knowledge, a state, and action — and demonstrates that each step necessarily follows from the previous one, leaving no room for human choice. This argument not only defines what repentance truly is but also grounds it in divine causality.

Follow the causal chain: knowledge of harm generates pain, and pain generates actions (abandonment, making amends, resolve). Notice that each step is necessary, leaving no choice for the servant.
The sequence is described as 'necessary arrangement' (ترتب ضروري).
3
Grasp the conclusion and objection
The author concludes that repentance is only from God because even the acquisition of knowledge is not in human power. Consider the objection and its rebuttal.
The argument is strengthened by rational proofs, making the doctrine superior.

Commentary

But the author counters that even acquiring knowledge is not in the servant's power, as proven earlier. Therefore, repentance is entirely from God. The conclusion affirms that their doctrine is supported by rational proofs.

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration of Quranic terms for human actions followed by a theological objection and its Ash‘ari answer. The author first lists six terms (al-san‘, al-kasb, al-ja‘l, al-khalq, al-ihdāth, al-ibtidā‘) each with Quranic verses that ascribe actions to humans. The list is introduced as ongoing from previous terms (the third through eighth). After the list, the author states that these are all the terms used in language for ascribing an act to an agent, and that they all appear in the Quran for human acts. Therefore, denying this ascription would be denying the Quran and constitutes clear disbelief. This is the objection: some opponents (likely the Mu‘tazila or similar) argue that to deny human agency is kufr. The author then gives the Ash‘ari answer (al-jawāb). The answer clarifies that this objection is outside the chosen madhhab. The chosen madhhab is that the combination of power (qudra) and motive (dā‘ī) necessitates the act—i.e., when a person has the ability and a decisive will, and obstacles are removed, the act necessarily occurs. The servant is truly a doer (fā‘il) and a maker (sāni‘). However, the power and motive themselves are created by Allah, so the entire chain is by Allah’s decree (qaḍā’), destiny (qadar), creation (takhlīq), and bringing into existence (takwīn). By this explanation, the Quranic terms are used in their proper, literal sense without denial (inkār) or innovation (ibtidā‘). The author concludes that this chosen position is best.

اللفظ الثالث : الصنع . قال في المائدة : ( لولا ينهاهم الربانيون والأحبار عن قولهم الإثم ، وأكلهم السحت . لبئس ما كانوا يصنعون ) وقال في العنكبوت : ( إن الصلاة تنهى عن الفحشاء والمنكر ، ولذكر الله ، والله يعلم ما تصنعون ) اللفظ الرابع : الكسب قال تعالى : أ- ووفيت كل نفس ما كسبت ب ظهر الفساد في البر والبحر بما كسبت أيدي الناس ج کل امریء بما كسب رهين د - كل نفس بما كسبت رهينة هــ اليوم تجزى كل نفس بما كسبت اللفظ الخامس : الجعل ، قال تعالى : أ - ( يجعلون أصابعهم في أذانهم ) ب - ( وجعلوا الملائكة الذين هم عباد الرحمن إناثاً ) ج - وجعلوا الله شركاء من الجن اللفظ السادس : الخلق . قال الله تعالى : أ - ( فتبارك الله أحسن الخالقين ) وهذا يقتضي كون غيره خالقاً . كقوله : زيد أفضل الفضلاء فإنه يقتضي كون غيره فاضلا ب - قال مخاطباً لعيسى : ( وإذ تخلق من الطين كهيئة الطير ) ج - وقال عيسى عليه السلام ، حاكياً عن نفسه : و أخلق لكم من الطين د - وتخلقون إفكا هــ ( إن هذا إلا خلق الأولين اللفظ السابع : الإحداث قال تعالى حكاية عن بعض البشر : حتى أحدث لك منه ذكرا اللفظ الثامن : الإبداع قال تعالى : ( ورهبانية ابتدعوها ) . إذا عرفت هذا ، فنقول : الألفاظ المستعملة في إسناد الفعل إلى فاعله في اللغة ليس إلا ما ذكرناه . وكلها ورد في كتاب الله تعالى في إسناد أفعال العباد إليهم ، فمن أنكر في هذا الإسناد كان ساعياً في تكذيب كتاب الله تعالى ، مجاهراً بالإنكار والإبطال . وأنه محض الكفر . والجواب : إن هذا أيضا خارج عن المذهب الذي اخترناه ، وهو أن مجموع القدرة مع الداعي مستلزم الفعل لأنه فعل من الفاعل ، إلا الذي يكون بحال متى اتصف بالقدرة والإرادة الجازمة، وارتفعت الموانع فإنه يجب الفعل . والعبد كذلك . فيكون فاعلا وصانعا . إلا أن خالق تلك القدرة وتلك الداعية لما كان هو الله سبحانه كان الكل بقضاء الله وقدره وتخليقه وتكوينه . وبهذا الطريق ظهر أن جميع ألفاظ القرآن مستعملة في موضعها الأصلي من غير حاجة إلى الإنكار ولا إلى الابتكار. وبهذا الطريق ظهر أن الذي اخترناه هو خير
The third term: 'al-san‘ (making). He said in al-Ma’idah: 'Why do the rabbis and scholars not forbid them from uttering sinful speech and consuming unlawful gain? Evil is what they are making.' And He said in al-‘Ankabut: 'Indeed, prayer prohibits immorality and wrongdoing, and the remembrance of Allah is greater. And Allah knows what you do.' The fourth term: 'al-kasb (acquisition). Allah Most High said: A: 'And every soul will be fully repaid [for] what it acquired.' B: 'Corruption has appeared on land and sea because of what the hands of people have acquired.' C: 'Every man, for what he acquired, is retained.' D: 'Every soul, for what it acquired, is retained.' E: 'Today every soul will be recompensed for what it acquired.' The fifth term: 'al-ja‘l (making/assigning). Allah Most High said: A: 'They put their fingers in their ears.' B: 'And they made the angels, who are servants of the Most Merciful, females.' C: 'And they made the jinn partners with Allah.' The sixth term: 'al-khalq (creation). Allah Most High said: A: 'So blessed is Allah, the best of creators.' This implies that others are creators. Like His saying: 'Zayd is the most virtuous of the virtuous,' which implies that others are virtuous. B: Addressing ‘Isa: 'And when you create from clay something like the form of a bird.' C: And ‘Isa, peace be upon him, narrating from himself: 'I create for you from clay.' D: 'And you create falsehood.' E: 'This is nothing but the creation of the ancients.' The seventh term: 'al-ihdāth (origination). Allah Most High said, narrating from some humans: 'Until I originate something from it [i.e., from the cave] as a mention.' The eighth term: 'al-ibtidā‘ (innovation). Allah Most High said: 'And monasticism which they innovated.' When you know this, we say: The terms used in [Arabic] language for ascribing an act to its doer are only what we have mentioned. All of them have occurred in the Book of Allah Most High in the context of ascribing the actions of servants to them. So whoever denies this ascription is striving to deny the Book of Allah Most High, openly displaying denial and nullification. And that is pure disbelief. The answer: This too is outside the school which we have chosen, which is that the combination of power and motive necessitates the act, because it is an act of the agent, except when one is in a state of being described with power and decisive will, and obstacles are removed, then the act is necessary. And the servant is likewise. So he is a doer and a maker. However, since the Creator of that power and that motive is Allah, may He be glorified, everything is by Allah’s decree, destiny, creation, and bringing into existence. By this way it becomes clear that all the words of the Quran are used in their proper place without need for denial or innovation. And by this way it becomes clear that what we have chosen is best.

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration of Quranic terms for human actions followed by a theological objection and its Ash‘ari answer. The author first lists six terms (al-san‘, al-kasb, al-ja‘l, al-khalq, al-ihdāth, al-ibtidā‘) each with Quranic verses that ascribe actions to humans. The list is introduced as ongoing from previous terms (the third through eighth). After the list, the author states that these are all the terms used in language for ascribing an act to an agent, and that they all appear in the Quran for human acts. Therefore, denying this ascription would be denying the Quran and constitutes clear disbelief. This is the objection: some opponents (likely the Mu‘tazila or similar) argue that to deny human agency is kufr. The author then gives the Ash‘ari answer (al-jawāb). The answer clarifies that this objection is outside the chosen madhhab. The chosen madhhab is that the combination of power (qudra) and motive (dā‘ī) necessitates the act—i.e., when a person has the ability and a decisive will, and obstacles are removed, the act necessarily occurs. The servant is truly a doer (fā‘il) and a maker (sāni‘). However, the power and motive themselves are created by Allah, so the entire chain is by Allah’s decree (qaḍā’), destiny (qadar), creation (takhlīq), and bringing into existence (takwīn). By this explanation, the Quranic terms are used in their proper, literal sense without denial (inkār) or innovation (ibtidā‘). The author concludes that this chosen position is best.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Ash‘aris deny human agency altogether.
Correction: Ash‘aris affirm that humans truly act (they are doers and makers), but the power and motive for the act are created by Allah, so the act is a genuine acquisition (kasb) by the human.

Key Takeaway
The Ash‘ari position holds that humans are real agents of their acts, but the ability and motive are created by Allah, so the act is a true acquisition (kasb) by the human and a creation (khalq) by Allah.
