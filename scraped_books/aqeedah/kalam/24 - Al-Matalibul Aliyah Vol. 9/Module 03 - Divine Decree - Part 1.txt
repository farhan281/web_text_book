The First Opinion: God Alone Creates Actions, but What of Human Power?
The author presents the first opinion only God's power affects the action, and the Ash'ari-Baqillani sub-disagreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the shared Ash'ari stance that human power has no effect on the existence of an action, and the internal disagreement between al-Ash'ari and al-Baqillani on whether human power affects the action's moral attribute (kasb).

Core Idea
Human power affects not the action's existence but perhaps its moral character—that is the Ash'ari-Baqillani debate.

This passage sets out the first major position in the debate over human agency: God alone creates the action. The Ash'aris unanimously deny that human power contributes to the action's being, but they disagree on whether it affects the action's attribute of obedience or disobedience. We will unpack this nuanced disagreement and see how al-Baqillani introduces the concept of kasb.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the first opinion
Read the Arabic passage once to see the structure: first the shared opinion, then the sub-disagreement. Note the key figures mentioned.
Pay attention to the terms 'المؤثر' and 'قدرة العبد'.
2
Distinguish the shared premise
Both al-Ash'ari and al-Baqillani agree that human power has no effect on the existence (وجود) of the action. This is the common ground.
The difference lies only in attributes.
3
Understand al-Ash'ari's position on attributes
Al-Ash'ari argues that since human power does not affect existence, it also does not affect any attribute of the action. This is a strict denial.
4
Analyze al-Baqillani's position and his argument
Al-Baqillani says human power does affect one attribute: the moral quality (kasb). He uses the example of movement being shared between obedience and disobedience to argue that the distinguishing attribute comes from human power.
Follow his reasoning: 'ما به المشاركة غير ما به الممايزة'.
5
Reflect on the significance of kasb
Kasb is the term for the human contribution—an attribute, not the essence. This allows human responsibility without compromising God's sole creative power.
This prepares for later discussions on free will.

Commentary
This passage presents the first opinion among rational people regarding the effect of power in causing an action. It states that the one who brings about the action (المؤثر) is only God's power, and the servant's power has no effect in its existence. This is the view of Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari and most of his followers, including al-Qadi Abu Bakr al-Baqillani and Ibn Furak. However, there is a further disagreement between Ash'ari and al-Qadi. Ash'ari holds that since the servant's power does not affect the existence of the action at all, it also does not affect any of its attributes. Al-Qadi, on the other hand, argues that although the servant's power does not affect the existence of the action, it does affect one attribute of it, namely the attribute called acquisition (الكسب). He explains that a movement that is obedience and a movement that is disobedience share in being movements but differ in being obedience or disobedience. That which is shared is distinct from that which differentiates. Therefore, the essence of the movement and its existence come from God’s power, while its being obedience or disobedience is an attribute produced by the servant’s power. The passage concludes by stating that this is a summary of al-Qadi's view in the best manner.

The Second Opinion: Both Powers Are Effective (First Possibility)
Author presents the second opinion and its first probability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second opinion on human action, specifically the view that both God's power and human power are effective, and the first possibility that each is independently effective and their combination is permissible.

Core Idea
Two independent powers can combine on one effect — a permissible possibility in this view.

After presenting the first opinion that only God's power is effective, the author now introduces the second opinion: that both God's power and the servant's power bring about the action. Under this opinion, there are two possibilities. In this lesson, we examine the first possibility: that each power is independently effective, yet they can combine on one effect. This is a key point in the Islamic theology of human agency.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that it first states the second opinion, then mentions two probabilities, and gives only the first probability.
This passage is short but dense; take your time.
2
Identify the two parts of the passage
Divide the passage into: 1) the statement of the second opinion, and 2) the announcement of two probabilities with the first one explained.
3
Understand the first probability
The first probability claims that God's power is independent in effect and the servant's power is also independent, yet their combination on a single effect is permissible. Ask yourself: what does 'independent in effect' mean?
Independent here means each power can produce the effect on its own.
4
Reflect on the theological significance
This view affirms both divine omnipotence and human responsibility. It avoids the problem of two independent causes producing the same effect by deeming it permissible (ja'iz).
This is a sophisticated position in kalam.

The Three Opinions All Entail Compulsion (Jabr)
Author declares that three prior opinions share in asserting jabr due to servant's lack of independence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why three different opinions about human action — despite their apparent differences — all lead to determinism (jabr) because they remove independent creative agency from the servant.

Core Idea
Any view denying the servant's independence in creating the act is ultimately a form of jabr.

The author has previously outlined three possible positions on how human actions relate to divine power. In this passage, he drops a bombshell: although they seem distinct, all three share a fatal flaw — none grants the servant true independence in bringing about the act. Let's see why each one collapses into jabr.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the literal English translation. Notice the author's confident tone as he states his conclusion first.
Pay attention to the phrasing 'مشتركة في القول بالجبر' (share in asserting jabr).
2
Identify the three conditions
The passage lists three scenarios under which the act occurs from the servant. Find each one and note how they differ.
They are: (1) dependence on God's assistance, (2) dependence on power and motive together, (3) necessity when both are present.
3
Analyze why each removes independence
Ask yourself: in each scenario, does the servant have full control over the act? The answer is no — in all three, the act is contingent on external factors.
The key phrase is 'لا يكون العبد مستقلاً بالإيجاد' (the servant is not independent in bringing about).
4
Connect to the conclusion of jabr
Understand that because the servant lacks independence, the act is effectively compelled. The author concludes 'فكان الجبر لازماً' (then compulsion is necessary).
Jabr here means the opposite of free will; the servant is not the true originator.
5
Reflect on the broader implication
Consider why this matters: many theological positions that seem moderate still entail determinism. This is a key insight for the debate on qadar.
The common misunderstanding is that only overtly deterministic views are jabr — but the author shows otherwise.

The Mu'tazili Position Entails Compulsion in Both Cases
Author analyzes the Mu'tazili position and shows it entails jabr whether he says the act is necessary or not.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Mu'tazili doctrine of human action, whether it considers the act necessary or not after power and motive, inevitably leads to compulsion (jabr) and negates human independence.

Core Idea
Whether the act is necessary or not after power and motive, the Mu'tazili ends up affirming compulsion.

This passage is a incisive logical analysis by the author of the Mu'tazili position on human action. He takes the Mu'tazili's acceptance of power and motive as causes and shows that regardless of whether the act is considered necessary or contingent upon those causes, the result is the same: the servant has no real independence. The author then addresses a possible objection and seals the argument.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two horns of the dilemma
The author sets up a dilemma: the Mu'tazili must either say that the act is necessary after power and motive, or not necessary. Both options are analyzed.
This is a classic dilemma argument.
2
Examine the first horn: if the act is necessary
If the act is necessary, then the chain of motives must end with a God-given motive, leading to jabr. The servant has no independence.
Infinite regress is impossible, so the first motive must come from God.
3
Examine the second horn: if the act is not necessary
If the act is not necessary, then its occurrence is pure coincidence, not within the servant's power. This also results in jabr.
The same conditions can produce different outcomes, so the servant cannot control the act.
4
Consider the objection and final conclusion
An objector might claim to simply affirm occurrence without necessity. The author shows that even then, the occurrence is either with or without necessity, both leading to jabr.
Thus jabr is unavoidable on all assumptions.

Know that there are subtle investigations in determining the locus of dispute, which must be pointed out. They are in several aspects. The first investigation: that the Mu'tazili either says that the occurrence of the act after the combination of power (al-qudra) and motive (al-dāʿī) is necessary (wājib), or he says it is not necessary. If he says it is necessary, then Mu'tazilism is entirely invalidated, because those motives cannot each depend on another motive, due to the impossibility of infinite regress (al-tasalsul) and circularity (al-dawr). Rather they must end at a motive that occurs by the act of God Most High. And if it is so, we say: when power and motive are present, since the act is necessary, and when one of them is absent, since the act is impossible, then compulsion (al-jabr) follows, and the servant has no independence, neither in the act nor in the omission in anything. Because the combination of power and motive occurred completely, with the absence of the act at one time and its occurrence at another, and nothing new occurred at the time of the act such that it could be said that the act occurred at that time because of it. On this assumption, the occurrence of the act at that time is pure coincidence, and it is not within his capacity nor his choice. And this is also pure compulsion (maḥḍ al-jabr), because he has no way at all to bring about the act. Commentary
This passage is a detailed objection-and-answer analysis (baḥth) of the Mu'tazili position on human action. The author begins by stating that there are subtle investigations needed to determine the precise locus of dispute. He then presents the first investigation: the Mu'tazili must either say that the act's occurrence after the combination of power (qudra) and motive (dāʿī) is necessary (wājib) or not necessary. 

First case: If he says it is necessary, then Mu'tazilism is invalidated because the chain of motives cannot go back infinitely or circularly; it must end at a motive created directly by God. When power and motive are present the act is necessary, and when absent the act is impossible—thus jabr follows, and the servant has no independence in either doing or refraining.

Second case: If he says it is not necessary, then the act may sometimes occur and sometimes not despite the same conditions. This means the occurrence is pure coincidence (ittifāq) without any cause from the servant. Since nothing new occurs at the moment of action, the act is not within the servant's capacity or choice—again pure jabr.

Thus in both scenarios, jabr is unavoidable. The author then addresses a potential objector who says he simply affirms occurrence without calling it necessary or not. The reply is that the occurrence is still either with necessity or without it, and in either case jabr follows. The conclusion is that compulsion is necessary on all assumptions.

Core Idea
Human acts are ultimately determined by God, not by independent human power.

Al-Razi begins his detailed refutation of human independence in actions with a rigorous logical argument. He systematically divides the possibilities for the agent's power and the determinant of action, showing that all alternatives lead either to predetermination (jabr) or to the denial of God as Maker. Proof of Determinism from the Necessary Sequence of Causes
Al-Razi presents a causal chain argument for determinism (jabr).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's argument for determinism (jabr) based on the necessary causal sequence that leaves no room for independent human choice.

Core Idea
Every human action is necessitated by an unbroken chain of causes beyond one's control.

Al-Razi presents a rigorous deterministic argument that traces every voluntary action back to an inevitable chain of internal and external causes. He invites the reader to reflect on their own experience to see that choices are compelled by prior beliefs, perceptions, and coincidences that are not within one's power. This passage lays out the full proof, culminating in a vivid example of an angry man whose action becomes unavoidable given the convergence of multiple factors.

Trace the internal chain: will, belief, perception
Al-Razi then describes how a specific movement is preceded by will, which is preceded by belief, which is preceded by perception. This chain shows that the action is not independent but caused by prior states.

3
Recognize external coincidences as beyond control
The final link in the chain depends on external coincidences like seeing or hearing something. These are clearly beyond one's capacity and power.
This is the key point: the causes originate outside the agent's control.

Consider the six factors and their role
Al-Razi refers to six previously mentioned factors that affect beliefs and character. When these combine with external coincidences, determinism becomes evident.
These six factors are: innate disposition, temperament, physical makeup, upbringing, social circumstances, etc.

Second Proof: Logical Contradiction of Existence and Non-Existence
Second proof: logical contradiction between knowledge and existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second proof that belief cannot co-exist with God's knowledge of non-belief because it would require belief to be both existent and non-existent simultaneously.

Core Idea
Knowledge must correspond to reality; contradiction is impossible.

The second proof deepens the argument by examining the nature of knowledge. For God's knowledge to be true, it must correspond to the known. Since God knows non-belief, belief cannot actually exist, otherwise the knowledge would not correspond, leading to a logical contradiction where belief is both existent (as an actual event) and non-existent (as required by God's knowledge).

Guided Path
1
Read the second proof
Read the Arabic text. Notice it builds on the concept that knowledge is only knowledge if it matches reality.
2
Understand the correspondence condition
Grasp the idea that knowledge 'عدم الإيمان' (non-belief) only corresponds if non-belief actually occurs. If belief happens, the knowledge would not correspond.
3
Recognize the contradiction
If belief exists, then belief would have to be both existent (as an actual event) and non-existent (because God's knowledge requires non-belief). That is a logical contradiction (محال).
4
Apply the principle
Since contradiction is impossible, the scenario (belief occurring) is impossible. This applies to both belief and non-belief symmetrically.

The third proof relies on divine news (khabar) from the Quran, where God explicitly states that certain groups will not believe; if they were to believe, God's truthful report would become false, which is impossible; hence their belief is impossible. Each proof concludes that the act (e.g., belief) cannot occur from that person, reinforcing the doctrine of qadar (divine decree). The author does not elaborate rebuttals here; the proofs are presented as straightforward logical deductions from agreed premises.

First Mu'tazilite Response: The Logical Error in the Conditional
The first Mu'tazilite way: analyzing the conditional on divine knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first Mu'tazilite argument that the conditional 'If something other than God's knowledge occurs, then God's knowledge becomes ignorance' is logically flawed because it links an impossibility to a possibility.

Core Idea
God's knowledge cannot change; the conditional itself is invalid, not foreknowledge.

The Mu'tazila were not content to simply deny that God's knowledge could change. Instead, they analyzed the logical structure of the conditional 'If something other than what God knows occurs...' and found it to be an invalid linkage of an impossibility (knowledge becoming ignorance) to a possibility (the event occurring). This lesson unpacks their subtle reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazilite argument carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that the Mu'tazila are responding to an objection by analyzing the conditional statement itself, not by affirming or denying divine knowledge.
2
Identify the key terms 'المحال' and 'الممكن'
These terms are crucial. Understand that 'المحال' means impossible and 'الممكن' means possible. The Mu'tazila argue that the conditional links an impossible consequence (knowledge becoming ignorance) to a possible antecedent (the event occurring).
3
Understand the conclusion: الإمساك عن القولين
The Mu'tazila conclude that one must refrain from both saying that knowledge becomes ignorance and that it does not. Because the entire conditional is invalid; the premise is impossible, so no judgment about its consequent is warranted.
This is a subtle logical point aimed at preserving God's immutability.

That is because He, exalted, knows in eternity that the existence of something will preponderate over its non-existence at a certain time in the temporal future.

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth proof (al-burhan al-khamis) that God must be the creator of human actions or their causes in order for His foreknowledge to be true. The proof begins with a conditional: if God were not the creator of actions nor of their necessitating causes, He could not know them beforehand. Since opponents agree God does know actions beforehand, He must be their creator or the creator of their causes. The author then establishes the second premise: the essences (mahiyyat) of actions are equally receptive to existence and non-existence with no inherent preference. If only the servant's power (qudra) were the determinant, and that power is not necessitating (it can do or not do), and there is no other determinant from God (directly or indirectly—otherwise compulsion would result), then actions would remain in perfect equilibrium. Any belief in their future existence would be a non-corresponding belief (i'tiqad ghayr mutabiq), i.e., ignorance (jahl), which is impossible for God. Therefore, God's eternal power must be related to causing these actions or their causes in the future, so His knowledge is true. An objection is then raised: God knows eternally that the action will be preponderant at a certain time because at that time the determinant (the servant's power) exists. The author answers that this is a misunderstanding; God knows eternally that the existence will preponderate at that future time. The answer cuts off, but the point is that the determinant must be traceable back to God in eternity to avoid ignorance.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some may think that God can know future actions based solely on the future existence of the servant's power, without any eternal determination.
Correction: The proof shows that without an eternal determinant (God's power creating or causing the action), God's foreknowledge would be a non-corresponding belief, i.e., ignorance. Therefore, the determinant must be eternal.
