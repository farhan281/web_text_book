The Seventh Proof: Evil is Not from Allah
Seventh proof using a hadith to show that evil is not from Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the hadith of seeking refuge from evil demonstrates that evil is not from Allah.

Core Idea
Evil is sought refuge from, not created by Allah.

In this lesson, we examine the seventh proof presented by the author, which draws on a prophetic hadith. The Prophet taught Fatimah a supplication that includes seeking refuge from the evil of every creature. The author argues that this very act shows that evil is not from Allah, but rather from the creatures themselves.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith carefully
Read the Arabic hadith once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice the supplication's structure and the key phrase 'I seek refuge in You from the evil of every creature'.
The hadith contains a long supplication; focus on the part about seeking refuge.
2
Identify the point of evidence
Look for the phrase 'وجه الاستدلال' which introduces the author's argument. Understand how the supplication is used as evidence.
3
Understand the logical implication
Reflect on why seeking refuge from evil implies that evil is not from Allah. The act of seeking refuge from something suggests that the thing is harmful and not from the One being sought for refuge.
The author explicitly states that whoever says otherwise is false.

The seventh proof: What Abu Salih narrated from Abu Hurayrah, may Allah be pleased with him, said: Fatimah came to the Messenger of Allah and asked him for a servant. He said to her: 'Say: O Allah, Lord of the seven heavens and Lord of the Great Throne, our Lord and Lord of everything, You are the Manifest so nothing is above You, and You are the Hidden so nothing is beneath You, Revealer of the Torah, the Gospel, and the Criterion, Splitter of the grain and the date-stone. I seek refuge in You from the evil of every creature whom You seize by the forelock.' The point of evidence: His saying 'I seek refuge in You from the evil of every creature' indicates that evil is not from Allah, and whoever says otherwise is false.

Commentary
This passage presents the seventh proof (al-hujjah al-sabi'ah) that evil is not from Allah. It cites a hadith where the Prophet taught Fatimah a supplication that includes the phrase 'I seek refuge in You from the evil of every creature.' The argument is that seeking refuge from the evil of creatures implies that evil is not something that Allah creates or does; rather, it is from the creatures themselves. Any claim to the contrary is deemed false.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some might think that the phrase 'evil of every creature' attributes evil to Allah indirectly, because all creatures are created by Allah.
Correction: The proof argues that the act of seeking refuge from evil shows that evil is not from Allah. If evil were from Allah, seeking refuge from it would be contradictory, as one would be seeking refuge from what comes from the same source. The supplication's very structure reveals that evil is from the creatures, not from the Creator.

Key Takeaway
The seventh proof uses the Prophet's own supplication of seeking refuge from evil to demonstrate that evil is not from Allah.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Have you ever blamed Allah for a tragedy or hardship in your life, even if only in your heart? How does this hadith challenge that attitude? Starting today, will you consciously remind yourself that evil is not from Allah and seek refuge in Him instead?
?
When you face an evil act from someone, do you immediately attribute it to Allah's decree or do you recognize it as a choice of the creature? How can you train yourself to separate the act from Allah's will, as this proof demands?
?
If you truly believe that evil is not from Allah, how will that change the way you respond to injustices inflicted upon you? Will you be able to forgive the doer while still seeking refuge from their evil, as the Prophet taught?

Scriptural Proof: God Leads Astray Whom He Wills
Author introduces scriptural evidence citing the Quranic verse on divine guidance and misguidance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the Quranic verse 'He leads astray whom He wills and guides whom He wills' serves as a key scriptural proof for divine control over guidance and misguidance.

Core Idea
God's will determines both guidance and misguidance, as stated in the Quran.

The author now moves from rational arguments to scriptural proofs. He focuses on one type of impediment mentioned in the Quran: that God leads some people astray. The first proof he presents is the well-known verse from multiple surahs: 'He leads astray whom He wills and guides whom He wills.' This verse establishes that misguidance is subject to divine will.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the transition from rational to scriptural evidence.
2
Identify the first proof
Find the verse 'yudillu man yashā' wa yahdī man yashā'. Note that the author mentions it appears in five places in the Quran.
The author provides the first occurrence from Surah Ibrahim and starts the second from Surah Ar-Ra'd.
3
Understand the author's method
The author selects one type of impediment from the Quran and begins listing verses. This shows his systematic approach to scriptural evidence.
4
Reflect on the meaning
Consider what this verse implies about God's control over guidance and misguidance. Is it arbitrary or based on wisdom?
The common misunderstanding is that it implies arbitrariness.

Attributing Guidance and Misguidance to Allah: The Quranic Pattern
The author uses Quranic verses to establish a pattern of attribution by temporal association, then applies it to guidance and misguidance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Quran attributes increase in faith and disbelief to revelation, and how this pattern justifies attributing guidance and misguidance to Allah.

Core Idea
Quranic patterns reveal that attribution follows temporal association, not direct causation.

In this lesson, we examine how the Quran uses a pattern of attributing increase in faith or disbelief to a revealed surah. The author shows that this attribution is based on the coincidence of the increase with the revelation, not on direct causation. He then applies this same logic to the attribution of guidance and misguidance to Allah, establishing that attribution does not imply that Allah directly creates the act without the servant's role.

Guided Path
1
Read the Quranic examples
Read the verses presented by the author about increase in faith and disbelief. Notice how each verse attributes the increase to the surah or the calling.
Pay attention to the phrase 'فزادتهم' (increased them).
2
Understand the author's reasoning
The author argues that the increase is attributed to the surah because it occurs at the time of its revelation, not because the surah causes it directly. Recognize the logical move: temporal association allows attribution.
This is a key principle for understanding divine attribution.
3
Apply to guidance and misguidance
Now see how the author applies this pattern to guidance and misguidance being attributed to Allah. The same logic holds: if these occur from the servant at a specific act of Allah, they can be attributed to Allah, even though the servant brings them into existence.
This resolves the theological tension.

And He also said: 'And you took them in mockery until they made you forget My remembrance' It is known: that they did not forget them in reality, but rather they were mentioning Allah to them and calling them to Him. Commentary
This passage is a theological argument explaining the attribution of guidance and misguidance to Allah. The author first presents several Quranic verses where increase in faith or disbelief is attributed to a revealed surah, even though the actual increase is a change in the people themselves. The author argues that this is because the increase occurs at the time of that surah's revelation. Then he applies this pattern to the issue of guidance and misguidance (al-hudā wa al-iḍlāl) being attributed to Allah. He says that if these occur from the servant at a specific act of Allah, then it is proper to attribute them to Allah, even though in reality they are brought into existence by the servant. This sets the stage for the interpretations that follow.
