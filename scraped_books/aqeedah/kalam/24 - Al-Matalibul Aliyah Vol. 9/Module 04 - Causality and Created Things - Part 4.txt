Proofs from Commands to Seek Help and Help Others
Objection-and-answer argument affirming human capacity via scriptural commands.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the commands to seek help from God and to help one another serve as rational proofs for human capacity, and how the author's position (power + motive + removal of obstacles) resolves the objection that humans lack any real power.

Core Idea
Commands to seek help imply that humans have real but incomplete capacity.

This passage introduces the fourth type of proof: scriptural commands that order people to seek God's help and to assist each other. The author argues that such commands only make sense if humans possess some genuine ability to act. He then answers the objection that if humans have no real power, these commands would be absurd. The answer clarifies the nature of human capacity and divine assistance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of commands
Read the passage and note that it presents commands to seek help from God and commands to help one another. Find the example verses given.
Verses like 'إياك نستعين' and 'وتعاونوا على البر والتقوى' are cited.
2
Understand the logical implication
The author explains that seeking help is only conceivable if the person has some capacity to act but needs assistance to complete it. If the servant had no capacity at all, the command would be futile.
3
Examine the objection and answer
The author poses the objection: if humans cannot act at all, how can they help others? Then gives his answer: the cause (malzūm) of an action is the combination of power (qudra) and motive (dāʿī), conditioned on the removal of obstacles. God's help removes these obstacles.
This upholds both divine omnipotence and human responsibility.
4
Reflect on the significance
Consider how this argument defends human agency without compromising God's power. The passage shows that commands are meaningful because humans have a real but dependent capacity.

Commentary

The argument proceeds: seeking help is only meaningful if the person has some capacity to act but needs assistance to complete it. Thus, God's help is the removal of obstacles that prevent this combination from entailing the effect. Learning Objective

Core Idea
An effect requires the combined capacities and motives of many agents, not just one.

This short but dense passage tackles a subtle point in causality: can one agent's power and motive alone bring about an effect? The author argues no, and that a multiplicity of agents and motives is needed. Let's examine the argument.

Proof from Quran 3:78 That Human Actions Are Not from Allah
The author cites Quran 3:78 and uses a conditional argument to prove that human actions are not created by Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quranic verse 3:78 is used as a proof that human actions are not created by Allah.

Core Idea
If Allah created their lies, the Jews would be truthful—but Allah denies that.

The author presents a powerful argument from the Quran to show that human actions, particularly distortion and lying, cannot be attributed to Allah's creation. By examining verse 3:78 and its implications, we see that if Allah had created those actions, the Jews would be right in claiming it is from Allah, but Allah explicitly says it is not from Him. This lesson walks you through this logical reasoning and its significance.

The Dilemma of Evil Acts and Its Resolution
Objection based on Quran 4:112 and answer distinguishing two aspects of actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a kalam objection based on a Quranic verse is resolved by distinguishing two aspects of actions.

Core Idea
Evil actions are acts of servants by their power and motives, and acts of God by His creation of that power.

Some argue that if God creates evil, He is unjust. The objection uses Quran 4:112 to claim that either God or the servant is the doer, leading to blasphemy or condemnation of the Jabrites. The answer saves divine transcendence by showing that actions have two valid aspects—one relating to the servant's acquisition, another to God's creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and its dilemma
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Identify the two horns of the dilemma: either God is the doer of evil or the servant is.
The verse is used to argue that if God is the doer, He would be unjustly accusing the innocent.
2
Analyze each horn's consequence
Notice that if God is the doer, the servant is innocent but described as sinful—this leads to blasphemy. If the servant is the doer, then Jabrites who ascribe all actions to God fall under the verse's condemnation.
The Jabrites are those who deny any human agency.
3
Study the answer's distinction
The answer distinguishes two aspects: actions are acts of the servants in that they proceed from the servants' power and motives, and acts of God in that they are necessitated by God's creation of the power and motive together.
This resolves the dilemma because both ascriptions are valid from different perspectives.
