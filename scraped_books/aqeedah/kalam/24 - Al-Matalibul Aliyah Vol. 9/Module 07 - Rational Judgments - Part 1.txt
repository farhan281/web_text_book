The First Three Views Lead to Compulsion
Argues that the first three views all entail jabr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the first three views on human agency all entail compulsion (jabr).

Core Idea
The first three views on human agency all ultimately lead to jabr.

In this passage, the author critiques three earlier views on how human actions occur, showing that each view denies the slave true independence. The central claim is that all three reduce to compulsion (jabr). This argument exposes a key inconsistency in the theological positions discussed.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the logical structure: the author states the conclusion first, then provides the reasoning by enumerating the three views.
The passage begins with 'والثالث' indicating this is a third point in a larger discussion.
2
Identify the three views
The passage lists three ways the slave's act might be dependent: (1) on God's assistance, (2) on power and motive together, (3) as a necessary consequence when power and motive occur. Identify each one in the text.
Each view is introduced with 'كان' or 'إذا كان'.
3
Understand why they entail jabr
In each case, the slave is not fully independent (mustaqillan). The act is conditioned on external factors, so it cannot be truly voluntary. The author concludes that compulsion (jabr) necessarily follows.
This shows that even views that seem moderate (like Ash'ari kasb) reduce to jabr.

Commentary
This passage is a third point related to the Mu'tazila's internal discussions, but it functions as a critique of the three preceding views (presumably those of the philosophers, Ash'aris, and another). The author argues that all three views share the characteristic of leading to jabr (compulsion). The reasoning is that under any of those views, the slave is not fully independent in originating his actions. The three views are: (1) the act depends on God's assistance (i'ana), (2) the act depends on the simultaneous presence of power and motive, and (3) when power and motive are present, the act becomes necessary, and when either is absent, the act is prevented. In each case, the slave's role is not fully voluntary, and thus the result is jabr. The author concludes that compulsion necessarily follows from these positions.

The First Profound Research: The Mu'tazili Dilemma on Necessity of Action
Introduces the first profound research on the Mu'tazili stance on necessity of action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the dilemma that al-Razi presents for the Mu'tazili position: if the act is necessary given power and motive, it leads to jabr; if not necessary, another difficulty arises.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazili is caught between jabr and an alternative problematic consequence.

This passage introduces a series of profound investigations into the exact point of dispute between the Ash'aris and Mu'tazila. The first investigation presents a dilemma that challenges the coherence of the Mu'tazili claim of human independence. By examining this dilemma, we see how theological arguments often proceed by reducing the opponent's position to absurdity.

Commentary
This passage serves as a conclusion to the previous enumeration of doctrines and then introduces a series of 'profound investigations' (abhath ghamidah) aimed at precisely locating the point of dispute. The Dilemma of Possibility: Compulsion or Denial of the Creator
Razi argues that the Mu'tazila view on possibility is logically inconsistent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical dilemma that Razi poses against the Mu'tazila position on possibility and causality.

Core Idea
Possibility either demands a cause or it doesn't — there is no coherent middle ground.

Razi presents a powerful logical argument against the Mu'tazilite attempt to steer a middle path between compulsion and absolute free will. He shows that their view leads to contradiction, forcing us to choose between two stark alternatives. This lesson unpacks the dilemma and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Understand key terms
Familiarize yourself with the terms: possibility (imkan), agent cause (mu'aththir), and preponderator (murajjih). These are central to the argument.
Possibility here means contingency: a thing whose existence and non-existence are equally possible.
2
Follow the two horns of the dilemma
Razi lays out two conditionals: if possibility requires a cause, then every possible needs a preponderator leading to jabr; if it does not require cause, then one cannot infer a Creator from possibility.
Notice that both horns lead to extreme positions.
3
See why the Mu'tazila view is contradictory
The Mu'tazila claim that possibility necessitates a cause in some cases but not others. Razi calls this inconsistent and false.
The contradiction lies in applying the same rule differently without justification.
4
Examine the objection and response
An opponent might object that jabr leads to a necessitated Creator, but Razi argues that denying a Creator altogether is far worse.
Razi promises a future distinction between the witnessed and unseen to clarify.

Commentary
This passage presents a dialectical argument against the Mu'tazilite position on free will. The author begins by stating a conditional: if possibility (al-imkan) necessarily requires an agent cause (mu'aththir), then every contingent thing needs a preponderant (murajjih) to bring it into existence. This leads to compulsion (jabr) because the preponderance of action over inaction must be caused by a necessitating cause. Alternatively, if possibility does not require a preponderant, then one cannot infer the existence of a Creator from possibility, leading to outright denial of the Maker. The author then presents the two horns of the dilemma: either all possible things need a cause (jabr) or all are independent (denial of the Creator). The Mu'tazilite middle ground — that possibility requires a cause in some cases but not others — is dismissed as contradictory. Finally, the author addresses an objection: the compulsion view leads to the Creator being necessitated (not a free agent), but the alternative (denial of a Creator) is far worse. He promises to later explain the certain distinction between the witnessed (shahid) and the unseen (gha'ib).

The Core Thesis: Free Will Problem as Proof of the Creator
Razi presents the argument that the jabr/qadr issue is identical to proving the Creator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Razi argues that the free will issue is identical to proving the existence of the Creator, and how the premise of possibility needing a cause leads to either jabr or atheism.

Core Idea
The free will debate is inseparable from proving God's existence.

Razi opens this investigation by citing many verifiers who hold that the problem of jabr (compulsion) and qadr (free will) is not a standalone issue. Instead, it directly parallels the proof of the Creator. The core argument hinges on the principle that a contingent thing (mumkin) requires an external cause (mu'aththir) and a preponderant (murajjih). Let's trace how this leads to a stark choice: either jabr or denial of the Creator.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening claim
Read the first sentence where Razi cites the verifiers' thesis. Note that the issue of jabr and qadr is said to be identical to proving the Maker.
This is a strong claim that frames the entire argument.
2
Understand the main premise
Identify the premise that possibility (imkan) necessitates a cause (mu'aththir) and a preponderant (murajjih). This is the key to the argument.
This premise is borrowed from kalam cosmology.
3
Follow the conditional reasoning
Trace the 'if-then' structure: if the premise is valid, then all possible things need a cause. Then examine ability (qudra): if not fit for leaving, jabr follows; if fit for leaving, a preponderant is needed, also leading to jabr.
Razi shows that either way, jabr is necessary.
4
Grasp the conclusion
The conclusion is a disjunction: either jabr or denial of the Creator. Understand that rejecting the premise makes proof of God impossible.
This is a powerful dilemma.

Commentary
This passage is a third investigation (al-bahth al-thalith) that identifies the problem of jabr and qadr with the problem of proving the existence of the Creator. The author begins by citing many verifiers (muhaqqiqun) who hold that the free will issue is not separate but identical to the proof of the Maker. The reason is that the main argument for God is that possibility (imkan) necessitates a cause (mu'aththir) and a preponderant (murajjih). If this proposition is accepted, then all contingent things need both. Then the author examines ability (qudra) to act: if it is not fit for leaving (i.e., incapable of refraining), then compulsion (jabr) follows. If it is fit for leaving, then the preponderance of acting over leaving must come from a preponderant, which also leads to compulsion. Thus, if the premise 'the possible needs a preponderant' is valid, jabr follows; if it is invalid, then the proof of God from possibility collapses. Therefore, either jabr or denial of God. The Mu'tazila middle ground—that possibility necessitates in some cases but not others—is rejected as corrupt. The author then gives an analogy: a chain of creatures identical in constitution, form, strength, and weakness. If they are identical, one cannot judge some as strong and others as weak. Similarly, all contingent things share the nature of possibility; if possibility necessitates a cause, it must do so for all; if not, for none. Judging some as independent despite shared possibility is arbitrary and false. The passage concludes with 'And with God is success', and marks the end of this introduction.

The First Case: Sufficiency of Conditions Leads to Necessity
First part of the eighth proof: reduction to absurdity and first case

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author proves that if the totality of conditions for an action is sufficient, then the action must be necessary, implying jabr.

Core Idea
If the conditions are sufficient, the action cannot be merely possible—it must be necessary.

The eighth proof in Al-Matalib al-Aliyah aims to establish that human actions are necessitated (jabr) by God's creation of the conditions. The author begins by considering what happens when the totality of conditions sufficient for an action is present. The argument is concise but foundational for understanding the determinist position.

Guided Path
1
Read the initial argument

وبالله التوفيق
The eighth proof: If something occurs by the power of the servant, it would be impossible to say: He only performed this action because he performed another action. Otherwise, infinite regress would follow, which is impossible. Rather, the actions of the servant must end with a first action that originated from him without mediation of another action from him. So we say: The origination of that action from him must necessarily be due to his essence being qualified by attributes that came into being through the creation of Allah, the Exalted. So we say: His essence together with the totality of those matters considered in his being an originator of that. Either it is sufficient for the occurrence of that effect from him, or it is not. If it is sufficient, then the action with it is necessary. For if it were not necessary, it would be possible for it to be absent. And if it were possible for it to be absent, then the occurrence of the effect from that totality would be possible, not necessary. Then it would need another additional factor. And if that were the case, that which existed before would not have been sufficient for the occurrence of the effect from him. And we had assumed it to be so. This is a contradiction. Thus it is established that the occurrence of the effect upon that totality is necessary. And that is jabr (compulsion). And also: If the occurrence of the effect upon the occurrence of that totality is not necessary, but rather it is possible for the action to occur sometimes and not to occur at other times, and there is no distinction between the occurrence of the action and its non-occurrence by another matter that the servant adds to the totality that existed. Then the occurrence of the action would be pure coincidence. And this too is pure jabr. As for the second division: That it is said: The essence of the servant together with the totality of those matters is not sufficient for it to be the source of that action. Then it is necessary to attach something else to it. And that other matter, if it is an act of his, it is impossible, for then an action would occur before the occurrence of the first action, and that is impossible. And if it is not an act of his, but rather an act of Allah the Exalted, then upon its occurrence everything necessary for him to be the source of his actions has been completed. Then either the occurrence of his actions from him is necessary, or it is not. And then the aforementioned division returns exactly as it was. Thus it is established that the occurrence of the action upon the occurrence of everything necessary for effectiveness (al-mu'aththiriyya) is necessary. And it is established that when all those matters are absent, or some of them are absent, the occurrence of the action from the servant is impossible. And on this assumption, the assertion of jabr becomes necessary. And with Allah is the success.

Commentary
This passage is a proof (al-burhan al-thamin) demonstrating that human actions are necessarily determined (jabr). The author begins by considering a scenario where an action occurs by the servant's power. He argues that if an action occurs, it cannot be traced back to another action ad infinitum, so there must be a first act. That first act requires the servant's essence to be qualified with attributes created by Allah. Then the author presents a division: either the totality of conditions (the essence plus these attributes) is sufficient for the action or not. If sufficient, the action is necessary—if it were not necessary, it could fail to occur, meaning the conditions were not actually sufficient, contradicting the assumption. Therefore, necessity (jabr) is proven. Then the author considers the second possibility: the totality is not sufficient. In that case, an additional factor is needed. If that factor is the servant's own act, it leads to regress. If it is Allah's act, then the servant's action becomes necessary upon the completion of all conditions, again leading to jabr. The proof systematically eliminates alternatives and concludes that the servant's actions are necessitated by God's creation, which is the position of jabr.

So we say: His essence together with the totality of those matters considered in his being an originator of that. Either it is sufficient for the occurrence of that effect from him, or it is not. If it is sufficient, then the action with it is necessary. For if it were not necessary, it would be possible for it to be absent. And if it were possible for it to be absent, then the occurrence of the effect from that totality would be possible, not necessary. Then it would need another additional factor. And if that were the case, that which existed before would not have been sufficient for the occurrence of the effect from him. And we had assumed it to be so. This is a contradiction. Thus it is established that the occurrence of the effect upon that totality is necessary. And that is jabr (compulsion). And also: If the occurrence of the effect upon the occurrence of that totality is not necessary, but rather it is possible for the action to occur sometimes and not to occur at other times, and there is no distinction between the occurrence of the action and its non-occurrence by another matter that the servant adds to the totality that existed. Then the occurrence of the action would be pure coincidence. And this too is pure jabr. As for the second division: That it is said: The essence of the servant together with the totality of those matters is not sufficient for it to be the source of that action. Then it is necessary to attach something else to it. And that other matter, if it is an act of his, it is impossible, for then an action would occur before the occurrence of the first action, and that is impossible. And if it is not an act of his, but rather an act of Allah the Exalted, then upon its occurrence everything necessary for him to be the source of his actions has been completed. Then either the occurrence of his actions from him is necessary, or it is not. And then the aforementioned division returns exactly as it was. Thus it is established that the occurrence of the action upon the occurrence of everything necessary for effectiveness (al-mu'aththiriyya) is necessary. And it is established that when all those matters are absent, or some of them are absent, the occurrence of the action from the servant is impossible. And on this assumption, the assertion of jabr becomes necessary. And with Allah is the success.

Commentary
This passage is a proof (al-burhan al-thamin) demonstrating that human actions are necessarily determined (jabr). The author begins by considering a scenario where an action occurs by the servant's power. He argues that if an action occurs, it cannot be traced back to another action ad infinitum, so there must be a first act. That first act requires the servant's essence to be qualified with attributes created by Allah. Then the author presents a division: either the totality of conditions (the essence plus these attributes) is sufficient for the action or not. If sufficient, the action is necessary—if it were not necessary, it could fail to occur, meaning the conditions were not actually sufficient, contradicting the assumption. Therefore, necessity (jabr) is proven. Then the author considers the second possibility: the totality is not sufficient. In that case, an additional factor is needed. If that factor is the servant's own act, it leads to regress. If it is Allah's act, then the servant's action becomes necessary upon the completion of all conditions, again leading to jabr. The proof systematically eliminates alternatives and concludes that the servant's actions are necessitated by God's creation, which is the position of jabr.

This passage is a refutation of a classical kalām proof intended to demonstrate that only God can create bodies and life. The author first presents the opponents' argument in detail, then critiques it step by step. The rational proof proceeds as follows: a human agent can choose to perform an act that is more, less, or different from what he actually did. Since he is capable of all, the actual occurrence of one particular possibility must be due to the agent's choice specifying that specific type and amount. For choice to be directed to a specific detail, the agent must have knowledge of that detail. Thus, any creator must have knowledge of what it creates. This establishes the necessary connection between creation and knowledge.

The author answers that the two aspects (known and unknown) are different attributes of the same thing. If they were not different, it would be contradictory for the same thing to be both known and unknown. But once they are distinct, the known aspect cannot be sought (as it is already known), and the unknown aspect cannot be sought (as the mind is heedless of it). Thus, the original exhaustive division (either known or not known) applies to both aspects, making acquisition impossible.

Learning Objective

Thus, on this assumption, it is impossible for that judgment to occur by the servant's acquisition and choice. Rather, it is a blind belief (iʿtiqād taqlīdī) that man brings about without justification. And that is also impossible. Whenever man tries to doubt himself concerning it, that is possible, and prior to that it is not knowledge nor certainty. So it is established by what we have mentioned: that the sciences are either concepts (taṣawwurāt) or judgments (taṣdīqāt). And it is established that each one of them is beyond the servant's power and his capacity. So it is established that cognitions and sciences are beyond human power, and their occurrence is only by the creation of God, exalted is He.
Source pages p. 107

Commentary
This passage presents the second argument (al-wajh al-thānī) that no judgment (taṣdīq) is acquired by human effort. The argument is a dilemma. Acquired judgments, by definition, occur only when mental images are present in the mind. Given those images, the judgment is either necessarily produced (ḍarūrī or lāzim) or not. If it is necessarily produced, then it is not within the servant's power, because both the images and the necessitation are beyond his control. If it is not necessarily produced, then it is not knowledge (ʿilm) or certainty (yaqīn), but merely a blind belief (iʿtiqād taqlīdī) that can be doubted. Since true knowledge must be certain, such a belief cannot be considered acquired knowledge. Thus, in both cases, the judgment cannot be acquired by human power. The argument concludes that all sciences, whether conceptual or judgmental, are beyond human capacity and are created solely by God. This reinforces the Ashʿarī doctrine that human acts, including cognitive acts, are created by God.
