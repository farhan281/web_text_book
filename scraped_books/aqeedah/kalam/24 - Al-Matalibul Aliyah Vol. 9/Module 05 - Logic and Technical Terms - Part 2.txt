The Ninth Argument: Repentance and Regret
The author states the ninth argument: repentance obligatory, regret not within capacity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the ninth kalam argument that repentance is obligatory yet its essential component, regret, is beyond human capacity, and how this serves as a reductio.

Core Idea
Regret is not within your power, so how can repentance be obligatory?

This lesson examines a very concise argument from the author. It states that repentance (tawba) is an obligation in the sharīʿa, but the core of repentance — genuine regret (nadam) — is not within human capacity. The author claims this has already been proven. This brief statement is a powerful reductio: if repentance requires regret, and regret is impossible, then demanding repentance would be an impossible obligation. This argument attacks the opponent's position that all obligations must be within capacity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic and English carefully. Notice how short it is: two claims connected by 'and'. The first claim is a premise; the second is a supporting assertion.
The phrase 'we have demonstrated' refers to an earlier argument in the text.
2
Identify the two parts of the argument
The first part states 'repentance is obligatory'. The second part states 'obtaining regret is not within capacity'. Understand that regret is considered the essence of repentance.
In Islamic theology, repentance (tawba) is often defined as regret for past sins and a resolve not to return to them.
3
Understand the implied conclusion
If repentance is obligatory but its core component (regret) is beyond capacity, then the obligation is impossible (taklīf mā lā yuṭāq). This disproves the opponent's claim that all divine obligations are within human ability.
The argument is a reductio ad absurdum, showing that the opponent's own criteria would render repentance impossible.

The Reciprocity Argument: Turning the Tables
The author concludes by asserting reciprocity of implication and response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author concludes his ten arguments by demonstrating that the opponents' objection is self-defeating.

Core Idea
The ten arguments show that what the opponents demand of us is actually required of them.

In this final passage, the author wraps up his ten arguments with a powerful logical move. He asserts that the very obligation the opponents claim follows from the Ashʿarī position is equally binding on them. Moreover, whatever answer the opponents give to these ten points can serve as the Ashʿarī answer to their original objection. This is a classic dialectical technique known as 'turning the tables' (لازم).

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Notice the compactness of the conclusion. The author uses two statements: one establishing symmetry of implication, the other symmetry of reply.
The key word is لازم (binding/necessary implication).
2
Understand the logical structure
The argument is: The opponents say that the Ashʿarī view entails impossible consequences. The author claims that the same consequences follow from the opponents' own view. So if the opponents object to Ashʿarī for that reason, they must also reject their own view.
This is a self-defeating objection.
3
Examine the strategic value
The second sentence points out that whatever answer the opponents give to these ten issues, the Ashʿarī can use that same answer to deflect the original objection. This is a strong defensive move that neutralizes the attack.
It shows the opponents' criticism is not new.
4
Reflect on the role of this conclusion in the larger text
This short passage serves as the climax of the ten-argument section. It doesn't introduce new content but summarizes the dialectical outcome.
It reinforces the coherence of the Ashʿarī position.
