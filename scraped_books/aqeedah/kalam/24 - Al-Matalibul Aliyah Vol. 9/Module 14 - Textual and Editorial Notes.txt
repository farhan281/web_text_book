Understanding Editorial Notes in Manuscripts
Editorial note on manuscript variants

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how editorial notes identify variant manuscript passages and their importance for textual criticism.

Core Idea
Editorial notes reveal which passages are unique to specific manuscripts.

This lesson introduces a brief editorial note from a classical theological work. The note signals that a particular manuscript contains additional material not found in other copies. By learning to read such notes, you gain insight into the textual history of Islamic scholarship.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic note carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Notice the key terms: مخطوطة, زيادات, and the reference to manuscript sigla (م, ط).
The phrase 'خلقت هؤلاء للجنة ولا أبالي' is a well-known hadith about predestination.
2
Examine the translation and identify the key information
Compare the Arabic with the literal translation. Identify which manuscript has additions, which manuscripts lack them, and where the addition begins and ends.
The addition runs from this point until the mention of the eighteenth proof.
3
Understand the purpose of such notes
Reflect on why a modern editor would include this note. It helps scholars know when they are reading material from a different manuscript tradition.
This is crucial for verifying the authenticity of arguments attributed to the author.

It informs the reader that a particular manuscript, designated 'As'ad' (likely a manuscript name), contains additional material that does not appear in other manuscripts (sigla M and T). The note specifies the start and end of this addition: from this point in the text up to a phrase that begins 'And the way of inference by this report which follows the eighteenth proof' and contains the well-known hadith 'I created these for Paradise and I do not care' (a reference to a hadith about predestination). The addition is located in the second chapter of the third section of the work. This helps scholars track variant readings and recognize when they are reading material from a different manuscript tradition.

Understanding the Colophon: Manuscript Completion Notes
Colophon recording completion date and copyist.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the purpose and typical contents of a colophon in Islamic manuscript culture, using the example from Al-Matalib Al-Aliyah.

Core Idea
Colophons are scribal fingerprints that date and authenticate manuscripts.

This lesson introduces the colophon, a scribal note found at the end of many Islamic manuscripts. The colophon of Al-Matalib Al-Aliyah by Fakhr al-Din al-Razi provides the exact completion date and the name of the copyist, which are crucial for understanding the manuscript's history and authenticity. By studying this short passage, you will learn how to read and interpret such notes.

Guided Path
1
Read the colophon passage
Read the Arabic colophon once, then its English translation. Notice the key details: date, name, and phrases of completion.
The passage includes both the original note and a later printing date.
2
Identify the components of a colophon
A colophon typically states when and by whom the manuscript was copied. Here, find the phrase for completion, the month and year, and the copyist's name.
Look for 'وقع الفراغ من نسخه' as the standard opening.
3
Understand the significance of dates
The date 606 AH corresponds to 1209 CE, which is the year of al-Razi's death. This shows the manuscript was copied during his lifetime or soon after, adding historical value.
4
Recognize the scribe's humility
The copyist describes himself as 'the poor, broken, needy of God's bounty'—a common trope in colophons showing piety.
5
Distinguish original from added notes
The colophon includes a modern printing date (1404 H/1984 CE) in brackets, which is not part of the original scribal note.
Manuscripts often have later annotations.

Commentary
This passage is a colophon, a scribal note at the end of a manuscript. It states that the copy (of Al-Matalib Al-Aliyah) was completed in the last ten days of Muharram 606 AH (which corresponds to August 1209 CE). The book is attributed to Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi (d. 606 H). The note also includes the modern printing date (1404 H / 1984 CE) and a variant reading from another manuscript (S) which adds the name of the copyist: Abd al-Jabbar ibn Muhsin. This information is valuable for textual history and dating the transmission of al-Razi's work.
