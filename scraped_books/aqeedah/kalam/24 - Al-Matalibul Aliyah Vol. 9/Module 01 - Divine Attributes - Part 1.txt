Al-Razi's Doxology: Divine Attributes and Transcendence
Al-Razi opens his work with an extended doxology praising God's attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi uses doxology to systematically affirm God's essential attributes and transcendence, laying the foundation for kalam arguments.

Core Idea
Praise in kalam is systematic theology, not mere poetry.

Al-Razi opens his major theological work with an extended doxology that is far more than a rhetorical flourish. He uses the language of praise to establish the core attributes of God—such as life, power, will, wisdom, and creatorship—while simultaneously negating all imperfections from them. This passage grounds the entire ensuing discussion in a framework of divine transcendence and absolute sovereignty.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage with reverence
Read the Arabic doxology aloud if possible, letting its rhythm and vocabulary sink in. Then read the translation to grasp the overall meaning.
Note the repeated use of verbs like 'تقدست', 'تنزهت', 'تعالت' indicating negation of imperfections.
2
Identify the attributes affirmed
Al-Razi explicitly names five attributes: life, wisdom, will, power, and creatorship. Find each one in the text and see what he denies about them.
He pairs each attribute with a specific negation (e.g., life above sickness, wisdom above doubt).
3
Understand the tanzih framework
Recognize that the doxology consistently distances God from any imperfection or limitation. This is a form of negative theology (tanzih) that complements positive attributes.
The phrase 'تعالت قدرته عن الاحتياج إلى الآلات' shows God's power needs no instruments.
4
Connect to the closing shahada
The doxology concludes with the testimony of faith and praise of the Prophet's miracle, the Quran. See how the entire passage leads to affirming tawhid and prophethood.

Commentary
This passage is the opening doxology of al-Razi's work, in which he praises God and affirms His attributes. It begins with the basmala and a supplication, then al-Razi identifies himself. The praise proceeds by listing divine attributes: God's transcendence (ta'ālī) above the understanding of creatures, His self-sufficiency (ṣamadiyya) making Him unlike any contingent being, and His roles as Creator of all things, from individual entities and compounds to essences and attributes, animals and plants, and as apportioner of all movements and stillness. Al-Razi then emphasizes God's life (ḥayāh) being free from imperfection, His wisdom (ḥikmah) from doubt, His will (irādah) from base inclinations, His power (qudrah) from need of instruments, and His creatorship (khalāqiyyah) from any benefit to Himself. These negations (tanzīh) establish God's absolute perfection. Next, al-Razi describes God's providence: providing for wild beasts, preserving sea creatures, knowing the finest details, aiding the perplexed, and answering the distressed. The phrase 'by the necessity of His existence the chain of needs is cut off' highlights that all other existence is contingent upon God. The doxology concludes with praise for blessings, the shahādah affirming tawḥīd and the prophethood of Muhammad, and the mention of his miracle (the Quran) and prayer upon him and his family. The entire passage functions as a theological preamble, grounding the ensuing discussion in the recognition of God's absolute sovereignty and perfection.

God is a Free Agent, Not Necessitated by Essence
Responding to the objection that divine action requires necessity by essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author defends God's free will by distinguishing human originated will from God's eternal will, and by turning a further objection back onto the opponent's own view of God's knowledge.

Core Idea
God's eternal will is self-sufficient; human will needs an external preponderator.

This passage responds to a key objection: if God's action depends on something, doesn't that make Him a necessitated cause? The author explains the difference between human and divine will, and then turns a further objection back on the opponent. Understanding this argument is crucial for grasping the Ash'ari position on divine freedom and the role of will.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and initial answer
Read the first part of the passage where the objection is stated and the initial answer begins. Notice the claim that God might be necessitated by essence, and the response that action depends on an incentive and a preponderating will.
2
Identify the key distinction
Focus on the contrast between human will (originated, needs a preponderator) and God's will (eternal, independent). This is the core of the first answer.
Human will is muhdath, God's will is qadim azali.
3
Understand the second objection
The opponent reformulates: could God's will have attached to a different time for creation? If yes, a preponderator is needed; if no, God is necessitated. Read this part carefully.
4
See the counter-objection on knowledge
The author answers by turning the same dilemma onto God's knowledge. If the opponent's theology accepts that God's eternal knowledge does not imply necessity, the same applies to will.
This is a dialectical move showing consistency.

Commentary
This passage is an answer to the third objection, which claims that the author's earlier argument implies God is necessitated (mujib bi al-dhat) rather than a free agent (fa'il bil-ikhtiyar). The author first summarizes his earlier proof: action depends on an incentive and a preponderating will. Since all human incentives and wills are created (muhdath), they require another preponderator. But God's will is eternal and self-sufficient, needing no further will. This establishes a distinction. The opponent then reformulates: God's will is attached to creating the world at a specific time; could it have been attached to another time? If yes, then God would need a preponderator to choose that time; if no, then God is necessitated. The author responds by turning the objection back on the opponent: the same dilemma applies to God's knowledge (if His knowledge is eternal and specific, does He have alternatives?). Thus, if the opponent's own theology accepts that God's eternal knowledge does not imply necessity, the same applies to His will. This is a classic dialectical move, showing consistency in the author's position.

First Proof: Faith Would Entail God's Ignorance
A brief proof by reductio ad absurdum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first proof that faith cannot occur from those God knows will not believe, because it would require God's knowledge to become ignorance.

Core Idea
Faith from the foreknown would turn God's knowledge into ignorance, which is impossible.

This lesson presents a crisp logical argument from the text: if someone God knows will not believe were to believe, then God's knowledge would no longer match reality, becoming ignorance. Since that is impossible for God, the occurrence of such faith is also impossible. We will walk through the proof step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage once slowly. Let the words sink in before analyzing them. Notice the key terms: علم الله, جهل, محال.
The passage is only three sentences but packed with meaning.
2
Understand the logical structure
Identify the conditional statement, the claim of impossibility, the principle applied, and the conclusion. This is a reductio ad absurdum argument.
3
Reflect on the implications
Consider why this argument is important for the doctrine of God's unchangeable knowledge. It guards against thinking that God's knowledge is affected by human choices.

This passage is a brief proof by reductio ad absurdum. This consequence is impossible (محال) for God. Then it applies the principle that whatever leads to the impossible is itself impossible. Therefore, the occurrence of faith from that person is impossible. The argument relies on the definition of knowledge as immutable correspondence to reality.

Second Proof: Contradiction of Existence and Non-Existence
The author presents the second proof that faith cannot coexist with God's knowledge of its non-occurrence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God's knowledge of non-occurrence makes the occurrence of faith impossible, based on the correspondence definition of knowledge.

Core Idea
True knowledge demands that the known state actually holds.

This lesson examines a second rational proof against the compatibility of faith with God's foreknowledge. It hinges on the nature of knowledge as correspondence (mutaabaqa). Understanding this proof clarifies a key point in the qadar debate.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the literal translation. Familiarize yourself with the logical flow.
Notice the key term 'mutabaqa' (correspondence).
2
Identify the definition of knowledge
The proof begins by asserting that knowledge is only knowledge if it corresponds to the known. This is the foundational premise.
Knowledge is truth-tracking.
3
Follow the logical deduction
Trace the conditional: If God knows faith will not occur, then non-occurrence must be true. If faith occurs despite that, a contradiction arises (simultaneous existence and non-existence).
The conclusion: such coexistence is impossible.

The second: The existence of faith cannot occur alongside knowledge of the non-occurrence of faith, because knowledge is only knowledge if it corresponds to the known. And knowledge of the non-occurrence of faith only corresponds if the non-occurrence of faith actually obtains. So if faith existed together with knowledge of its non-occurrence, it would necessitate that faith be both existent and non-existent simultaneously, which is impossible. Since He, exalted, knew from him the non-occurrence of faith, the existence of faith from him is impossible. And if He knew from him the occurrence of faith, then the non-occurrence of faith from him would be impossible.

Commentary
This passage is a proof from the nature of knowledge. It begins by defining knowledge as correspondence (مطابقة) between knowledge and the known. God's knowledge of the non-occurrence of faith requires that the non-occurrence actually be the case. If faith were to occur despite this knowledge, then faith would both exist (by actual occurrence) and not exist (because that is what God knows), a contradiction in terms. Hence, faith cannot arise from someone God knows will not believe. The same logic applies in reverse: if God knows faith will occur, then non-occurrence is impossible.

God's foreknowledge creates a logical barrier to faith for those He knows will never believe.

This lesson presents the fourth proof in a series refuting the possibility of what God has foretold will not happen. The author argues that if God commands faith to those He knows will never believe, then faith would require them to affirm that very foreknowledge, leading to a contradiction. Understanding this argument deepens one's grasp of the relationship between divine knowledge and human obligation.

The Eighth Proof: God's Power Includes Human Acts
Eighth proof: reductio ad absurdum for divine power over human acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the eighth proof that God's power encompasses human acts, and why denying this leads to the absurd conclusion that God's power is limited.

Core Idea
To deny God's power over human acts is to limit the infinite.

This lesson presents the eighth proof from the author's series demonstrating that human acts fall under God's power. The argument uses a reductio ad absurdum: if human acts were outside God's power, then God's power would be smaller than the combined power of God and humans, implying finiteness. Commentary
This passage is the first of three aspects demonstrating that the denial of human agency (the servant not being the creator of his own actions) contradicts the claim that the Qur'an is a definitive proof (hujja). The author argues as follows: First, if God can create misguidance and foul acts without it being deemed bad (qabih) by Him, then why cannot He lie? If lying is possible for God, then the Qur'an cannot be relied upon as proof. The opponent might object that God's speech is an eternal attribute, and an eternal attribute cannot be false. The author refutes this in several ways: (a) They do not concede that God's speech is eternal—this is disputed. (b) Even if it were eternal, it would be false because eternal speech informing of past events would require the events to be simultaneous with speech, but past events are prior, so the speech would be false. The opponents then argue that since God is all-knowing, He can only tell the truth, and if He lied, that falsehood would be eternal and could not be replaced by truth. The author dismisses this as weak: just as a knower can tell truth, he can also lie—eternal truth does not preclude the possibility of eternal falsehood. If they deny that possibility, symmetry demands denial on both sides. Even conceding that eternal speech must be true, the author points out that the opponents rely on the audible, composite words of the Qur'an, not the eternal attribute. These words could be false even if the eternal is true. Thus, the argument does not secure certainty.

Commentary

This argument only works if we assume that God is the creator of these heart actions, because only the creator of something fully knows it. Thus the verse becomes a syllogism with the missing minor premise that God is the creator. The author then addresses a grammatical objection: the phrase 'من خلق' could be interpreted as either the subject or the object of the verb 'يعلم'. The objector claims that if 'من خلق' is the object, the verse would mean 'Does God not know who created the hearts?' implying another creator. The answer uses grammatical principle: the verb's connection to the subject is necessary (ضروري) while the connection to the object is not, so the interpretation making 'من خلق' the subject (the necessary element) is more appropriate. Additionally, the objection-supported interpretation only validates the second way of inference, while the first interpretation validates both ways, making it superior.
