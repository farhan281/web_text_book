Allah's Transcendence and Attributes in al-Razi's Introduction
Al-Razi opens his work with praise of Allah, affirming His transcendence and attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi's opening praise systematically affirms Allah's transcendence, attributes, and providence, setting the theological foundation for the entire work.

Core Idea
Allah's absolute transcendence means He is independent of all creation and limitations.

Al-Razi begins his monumental work with a doxology that is far from mere rhetoric. Each phrase carries theological weight, affirming core Islamic beliefs about Allah's transcendence, power, and absolute independence. This lesson unpacks that opening passage to see how it sets the stage for the entire book's kalam arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once with the translation to get the rhythm and flow. Notice the repetition of verbs and the pattern of praise.
Pay attention to words like 'كبرياء' and 'صمدية'.
2
Identify the three main themes
The passage divides into: (1) Allah's transcendence above creation, (2) enumeration of His creative acts, (3) transcendence of His specific attributes. Identify each section.
3
Analyze the attribute transcendence
Focus on the five attributes (life, wisdom, will, power, creativity) and what each is declared free from. Understand the theological implications.
These affirm tanzih (transcendence) without negating attributes.
4
Reflect on the examples of providence
The passage ends with examples of Allah's care for wild beasts, fish, and atoms. Consider how these demonstrate His all-encompassing knowledge and provision.
5
Connect to kalam methodology
Realize that this introduction is not just devotional but also method: it establishes clear distinctions between Creator and creation before arguments.

The Necessary Premise of Human Action
The author sets forth the necessary knowledge about the agent and the subsequent disagreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the necessary premise about the capable agent's action and how it sets the stage for theological disagreements on human agency.

Core Idea
When a capable agent is motivated and unhindered, the action necessarily occurs.

This opening passage from the author lays the conceptual groundwork for the entire discussion on human action and divine capability. It asserts a simple, universally acknowledged truth about agency: if an agent has the power to act, is motivated to do so, and faces no impediment, then the action will happen. This starting point is crucial because it frames the subsequent debates—rational thinkers agree on this premise but differ on its implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic text once, then the translation. Notice the structure: an introduction, a statement of necessary knowledge, and a note about disagreement.
The passage begins with 'المقدمة' (Introduction) and sets the scene.
2
Identify the necessary premise
Locate the sentence that begins with 'نعلم بالضرورة'. Understand the three components: capable agent (قادر), motive (داعي), and hindrance (مانع). The conclusion is that the action occurs.
This is presented as knowledge by necessity (بالضرورة).
3
Reflect on the meaning of necessity here
Ask yourself: why is this knowledge 'necessary'? It means it is self-evident and cannot be rationally denied. Consider how this premise is used as a foundation for further discussion.
The author does not prove this; it is taken as a given.
4
Note the transition to disagreement
The passage ends with 'ثم اختلف العقلاء بعد ذلك على أقوال'. This signals that the author will now present various opinions that arise from this common starting point.
Prepare to encounter multiple viewpoints in subsequent lessons.

Commentary
This passage is a demonstration of the prevalence of deletion (ḥadhf) and insertion (iḍmār) in the Quran. The author begins by discussing the omission of the word 'la' (not) in certain verses. His first example is the verse 'I do not swear by the Day of Resurrection' (Q. 75:1). The author explains that the word 'la' is actually superfluous or omitted in meaning; the intended sense is 'I swear by the Day of Resurrection', with the 'la' being inserted for emphasis but not negating the oath. He then cites the verse 'And forbidden upon a town which We destroyed: that they will not return' (Q. 21:95). The author argues that the word 'la' is omitted, because if it were present, it would imply that the people must return, which contradicts the consensus that they do not return. Therefore, the true meaning is 'that they will return', with the negation removed in interpretation. Next, he turns to insertion (iḍmār), where a word is implied but not written. For example, in the verse 'Indeed We have placed over their hearts coverings, lest they understand it' (Q. 18:57), the word 'an' (that) is actually 'li-allā' (lest), so the estimated reading is 'lest they understand it'. Similarly, in 'Allah makes clear to you that you should not go astray' (Q. 4:176), the implied reading is 'lest you go astray'. The author concludes that the Quran is filled with such deletions and insertions, often reversing negation into affirmation and vice versa. Consequently, assuming that a verse contains no deletion or insertion is only a conjecture (ẓann), not certain knowledge (ʿilm). This is a key methodological point: one cannot be absolutely sure that a literal reading is correct without considering possible ellipses.

Khabar al-wahid is speculative because narrators are not infallible, so certainty is impossible.

Core Idea

The author strengthens his case by arguing that if even the testimony of four people does not produce certainty, then a single report is even further from certainty. He explains the role of verification (جرح وتعديل) in judicial processes.

And the fourth: The statement of four does not give knowledge, so the statement of one is even less likely to give it. For it is known that if a judge has four witnesses testifying that so-and-so committed adultery with so-and-so, and the judge does not know their truthfulness, he does not reject their testimony. Rather, he refers to the verifier. If verification is obtained, he accepts their testimony; if discrediting is obtained, he rejects it. If knowledge were obtained by their testimony, then verification and discrediting would be unnecessary. Since we know the necessity of referring to the verifier, while certainty of what they testified is absent, we know that the report of four does not give knowledge. Commentary
This passage presents four distinct arguments (وجوه) to prove that khabar al-wahid yields only probability (ظن), not certainty (علم). The first argument (الوجه الثاني) is introspective: by human nature, we cannot be certain of a single person's statement without external corroboration like miracles or circumstantial evidence; we know it could be a lie. This knowledge is necessary (ضروري). The second argument (الوجه الثالث) appeals to consensus and practice: Islamic law requires two male witnesses or one male and two females for testimony. The third argument (الوجه الرابع) extends the logic: if even four witnesses do not provide certainty (since their testimony must be verified by checking their reliability), then a single report is even weaker. The author explains that if four witnesses gave certainty, there would be no need for verification (جرح وتعديل). Since verification is required, four reports do not yield certainty, so a single one certainly does not. The conclusion (فثبت) synthesizes the four arguments: khabar al-wahid gives only probability and cannot be used in definitive matters (مسائل قطعية).

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth argument (الوجه الخامس) against the reliability of solitary reports (akhbar al-ahad). The author claims it is a matter of necessary knowledge (علم ضروري) that the Companions did not write down hadith when they heard them from the Prophet; they relied solely on memory. Furthermore, they did not verify their narration by reciting it back to the Prophet for correction. They would hear it once, and then after decades—thirty, forty, or fifty years—they would narrate it. This leads to certain knowledge that significant discrepancy occurred in both wording and meaning. The author then elaborates on wording: even a trained jurist who habitually receives lessons from a teacher cannot reproduce the teacher's exact words after a single hearing; there will be changes. Since the Companions were not trained in this craft, they would certainly fail to preserve exact wording after fifty years. Thus, it is impossible to repeat the exact words. Regarding meaning: when a large crowd hears a speech and each person attempts to repeat it, great variation emerges even shortly after. With longer time, more intermediaries, and the biases of transmitters, meaning is also distorted. Therefore, these transmitted reports are neither the exact words of the Prophet nor the exact meanings intended.

The Sixth Argument: The Risk of Missing Context in Hadith Narration
The author presents the sixth argument against solitary reports: risk of missing context, with three examples from Aisha.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why missing context in hadith narration can affect the reliability of a report, and see how Aisha corrected misunderstandings based on context.

Core Idea
Context is crucial; even great Companions can miss it.

Commentary
This passage presents the sixth argument (الوجه السادس) against the reliability of solitary reports. The argument is that a narrator may have only heard part of a speech and missed the preceding context, thus transmitting an incomplete or misleading report. The author then provides three examples to illustrate. The third example is cut off at the beginning of her explanation. These examples demonstrate that even prominent Companions could miss context, so solitary reports must be treated with caution.
