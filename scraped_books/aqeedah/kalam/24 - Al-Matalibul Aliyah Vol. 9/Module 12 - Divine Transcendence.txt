The Eighth Condition: Safety from Rational Objection
Explains the eighth condition and its logical justification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the eighth condition for textual evidence in kalam: it must be safe from rational objection, and why reason takes precedence over apparent meanings of scripture when they conflict.

Core Idea
When reason and scripture conflict, reason is the foundation, so scripture must be interpreted allegorically.

Have you ever wondered how Muslim theologians reconcile verses that seem to describe God in human terms? The eighth condition provides the key: textual evidence must be free from rational contradiction. In this passage, the author explains why reason is the ultimate arbiter and how apparent anthropomorphic verses are to be understood.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation to grasp the overall argument before analyzing details.
2
Understand the condition
Note that the eighth condition requires transmitted evidence to be safe from rational objection (al-mu'arid al-'aqli).
3
Examine the example
See how the author uses verses of anthropomorphism (tashbih) to illustrate: they are many, but due to conflict with rational proofs, they are interpreted metaphorically.
This shows the condition in practice.
4
Trace the logical argument
Follow the reasoning: when conflict occurs, we cannot accept both, reject both, or prioritize transmission over reason. The only valid option is to accept reason and interpret scripture allegorically.
The argument uses a four-part division.
5
Apply the conclusion
Understand that transmitted evidence depends on absence of rational objection, but that absence is speculative, not certain.
This limits the certainty of scriptural interpretation.

الثامن : ويتوقف على سلامتها عن المعارض العقلي . وإن آيات التشبيه كثيرة . لكنها لما كانت معارضة بالدلائل العقلية [ القطعية ] لا جرم أوجبنا صرفها عن ظواهرها. فكذا ههنا. وأيضاً فعند حصول التعارض بين ظواهر النقل وقواطع العقل ، لا يمكن تصديقهما معاً. وإلا لزم تصديق النقيضين [ ولا تكذيبهما ، وإلا لزم رفع النقيضين ] ولا ترجيح النقل على القواطع العقلية ، لأن النقل لا يمكن التصديق به إلا بالدلائل العقلية . فترجيح النقل على العقل الطعن في العقل. ولما كان العقل أصلاً للنقل ، كان الطعن في العقل موجباً للطعن في العقل والنقل معاً . وأنه محال . فلم يبق إلا القسم الرابع وهو القطع بمقتضيات الدلائل العقلية القطعية ، وحمل الظواهر النقلية على التأويل فثبت بهذا : أن الدلائل النقلية يتوقف الحكم بمقتضياتها على عدم المعارض العقلي . إلا أن ذلك مظنون لا معلوم .
The eighth: It depends on its safety from rational objection (al-mu'arid al-'aqli). And indeed the verses of anthropomorphism (tashbih) are many, but since they are opposed by rational proofs [definitive], we necessarily require turning them from their apparent meanings. So likewise here. Also, when conflict occurs between the apparent meanings of transmitted evidence and rational certainties (qawati' al-'aql), it is not possible to affirm both together, otherwise affirmation of two contradictories would be necessary [nor to negate both, otherwise negation of both contradictories would be necessary], and it is not permissible to give precedence to transmitted evidence over rational certainties, because transmitted evidence can only be affirmed through rational proofs. So giving precedence to transmitted evidence over reason is an attack on reason. Since reason is the foundation of transmitted evidence, attacking reason necessitates attacking both reason and transmitted evidence together. And that is impossible. So nothing remains except the fourth division: affirming the implications of definitive rational proofs (al-'aqliyya al-qat'iyya) and interpreting transmitted apparent meanings allegorically (ta'wil). Thus it is established that transmitted evidences depend for judgment on their implications on the absence of rational objection. But that is speculative, not known.

Commentary
This passage explains the eighth condition: textual evidence must be safe from rational contradiction (al-mu'arid al-'aqli). The author illustrates with verses of anthropomorphism (tashbih) which are many, but because they are opposed by definitive rational proofs, scholars interpret them metaphorically. The same principle applies here. The author then argues: when the apparent meaning of transmitted texts conflicts with rational certainties, we cannot accept both (that would be affirming contradictories) and cannot reject both (that would be negating both). We cannot give priority to transmitted evidence over rational certainties because transmitted evidence itself depends on rational proofs for its authenticity. Hence, prioritizing transmission over reason would undermine reason, and since reason is the foundation (asl) of transmission, undermining reason undermines both. Therefore, the only valid approach is to accept the definitive rational proofs and interpret the transmitted texts allegorically. Thus, any textual evidence is subject to the condition that there is no rational objection, but the absence of rational objection is only speculative, not certain.

Why the Quran Has Muhkam and Mutashabih (With Torah Parallel)
The author argues for necessity of both muhkam and mutashabih and resolves a Torah contradiction to defend tawhid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why both muhkam and mutashabih verses are necessary to distinguish true scholars and how apparent contradictions in revelation are resolved.

Core Idea
Apparent contradictions in scripture test understanding and affirm God's unity.

This passage explains that the Quran's mixture of clear and ambiguous verses serves a purpose: to distinguish the sincere seeker from the heedless. The author then uses an example from the Torah where Isaiah and Genesis seem to conflict about evil, showing that God creates the potential for good and evil, but moral evil arises from human misuse.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on necessity
Read the opening lines explaining why all of the Quran cannot be clear.
Notice the author's logic: distinction of scholar from ignorant.
2
Trace the Torah example
Follow how the author contrasts Isaiah (Creator of evil) with Genesis (everything very good).
This is the key test case.
3
Understand the resolution
The author resolves by saying God creates origins; evil comes from human misuse.
This prevents the concept of two gods (dual deity).
4
Connect to divine unity
See how this argument defends tawhid by attributing all creation to one God.

The author supports each type with verses from the Torah and Quran to show that evils are not arbitrary but linked to causality and human responsibility.

The Verse 'Creator of Everything' and Its Specification
The author argues the verse is general but specified, defending against objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the verse 'Allah is the creator of everything' can be general yet subject to specification, and the two arguments the author uses to defend this.

Core Idea
General verses can be specified; here, God cannot create Himself.

This passage tackles a common theological objection: if a verse says 'Allah is the creator of everything', how can anything be excepted? The author concedes the word 'kull' indicates generality, but argues that the verse is indeed general yet has been specified, because God cannot create Himself. He then defends this by addressing two possible objections: first, that the term 'thing' does not apply to God; second, that even if it did, specifying the noblest being is impermissible.

Guided Path

But if the doer of these ugly deeds and immoralities is the servant, then God is innocent of their doing. So the Jabriyya (determinists) who cast the God of the world with the doing of these ugly deeds while He, exalted is He, is innocent of them, they must fall under His statement, exalted is He: 'He has burdened himself with calumny and manifest sin.' The answer: These matters are acts of the servants insofar as they occur from their power and motives, and acts of God, exalted is He, insofar as they are necessitated by the act of God, exalted is He, which is the combination of power and motive. And then this question falls away from them.
Source pages p. 335

Commentary
This passage presents an objection and answer typical of kalam theodicy. The objection is raised by the Jabriyya (those who assert absolute divine determination) or perhaps by opponents of the Ash'ari position. The author first quotes the Quranic verse (4:112) which condemns casting sin upon an innocent person. The objector builds a dilemma: either God creates evil acts, or the servant does. If God creates them, then He attributes evil to the servant (by calling him thief, etc.) while knowing the servant is innocent—thus God would be guilty of calumny and manifest sin, which is impossible (and saying so is disbelief). If the servant creates them, then God is innocent. Therefore, the Jabriyya who claim that God creates evil acts are falling under the verse's condemnation. The answer resolves the dilemma by distinguishing two perspectives: these acts are acts of the servants insofar as they arise from the servant's power and motive (i.e., the servant's acquisition), and they are acts of God insofar as they are necessitated by God's act of creating the combination of power and motive (i.e., God creates the complete cause). Thus, the objection that God attributes evil to an innocent agent fails because the acts are genuinely the servant's own doing in one sense, and God is not unjust. The author concludes that this answer removes the question entirely.

Fourth Interpretation: 'Idlaal' as Punishment (Ta'dhib)
Definition of the fourth interpretation that 'idlaal' means punishment (ta'dhib)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth interpretation that 'idlaal' means punishment (ta'dhib) in the Hereafter, and how the author uses Quranic verses to support this.

Core Idea
'Idlaal' can mean punishing in the Fire, not just causing error.

In this passage, the author presents the fourth interpretation of 'idlaal' as 'ta'dhib' (punishment). This is a key refinement because it distinguishes between causing someone to stray (misguidance) and inflicting punishment as a consequence. The author provides two Quranic proofs that show 'dhalal' and 'idlaal' used in the context of Hellfire.

Commentary
This passage introduces the fourth interpretation, which equates 'dhalal' (straying) with 'adhaab' (punishment), and 'idlaal' (causing to stray) with 'ta'dheeb' (punishing). The author provides two Quranic proofs. The first is from Surah al-Qamar (54:47-48), where 'dhalal' appears alongside 'sa'ir' (blaze) and the description of being dragged into the Fire; thus, the 'dhalal' here must mean the actual punishment of Hell. The second is from Surah Ghafir (40:71-74), where the disbelievers say 'they have strayed from us' (dallu 'anna) in the context of being punished; and the verse concludes 'thus does Allah cause the disbelievers to stray' (yudillu Allahu al-kafireen). The argument is that the 'idlaal' in these contexts refers to the infliction of punishment, not the causation of error.
