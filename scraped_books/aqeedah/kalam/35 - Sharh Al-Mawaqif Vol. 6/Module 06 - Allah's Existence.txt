Knowledge Is Distinct by Necessity
First argument from necessary distinctness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first argument that knowledge cannot be a privation because it is necessarily distinct from other states.

Core Idea
Knowledge is inherently distinct; a privation cannot be distinct.

The author begins with a direct observation: knowledge is immediately recognized as distinct from other mental states. This self-evident feature shows that knowledge cannot be a mere absence or privation, because privations lack such inherent distinctness.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening sentence
Focus on the claim that knowledge is not privative because it is distinct by necessity. Underline the phrase 'ممتازة عن غيرها بالضرورة'.
The author uses 'بالضرورة' to indicate a self-evident truth.
2
Understand what 'distinct by necessity' means
Reflect on how you know that the feeling of knowing is different from doubt or ignorance. This immediate awareness is the basis of the argument.
3
Compare with a privation
Think of any privation (e.g., darkness, silence). Does it have a distinct identity of its own? The author says no—a privation is defined only by what is absent.
This contrast reinforces why knowledge cannot be a privation.

Commentary
This passage presents the proof that knowledge is not a privation ('adami). The author gives two arguments. First, knowledge is necessarily distinct from other things, whereas a privation (like absence) is not distinct in itself. Second, if knowledge were a privation, it would be the privation of its opposite, which is ignorance. Ignorance is of two types: simple ignorance (jahl basit), which is a privation of knowledge, so knowledge would be the privation of a privation, making it positive (thubuti) while being assumed privative—a contradiction. Compound ignorance (jahl murakkab) is also invalid because a subject (like an inanimate object) can be devoid of both knowledge and compound ignorance simultaneously. An objector suggests knowledge might be abstraction from matter (tajarrad 'an al-madda), but the author refutes this: abstraction can be conceived without knowledge, and knowledge is relational (it can be of one thing and not another), unlike abstraction. Therefore, knowledge is not privative; it is existent (wujudiyya), either real (haqiqiyya) or relational (idafiyya).

The Second Argument and Refutation of Abstraction
Second argument and refutation of abstraction objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second argument that knowledge cannot be a privation because it would lead to a contradiction, and how the author refutes the view that knowledge is abstraction from matter.

Core Idea
If knowledge were a privation, it would be the privation of ignorance—leading to absurdity.

The author now presents a more intricate argument. If knowledge were a privation, it would have to be the privation of its opposite (ignorance). But ignorance itself is a privation, so knowledge would be the privation of a privation—making it positive. The author also tackles an objection: maybe knowledge is just abstraction from matter. He shows why that fails.

Guided Path
1
Read the second argument
Start from 'وأيضاً لو كانت عدماً' and follow the logic: knowledge would be the 'عدم' of its opposite. Identify the two types of ignorance mentioned.
Simple ignorance (جهل بسيط) is absence of knowledge; compound ignorance (جهل مركب) is false belief.
2
Analyze the contradiction
Notice that simple ignorance is a privation, so knowledge would be a privation of a privation, which yields a positive (ثبوتي). The author says this is contradictory with the assumption that knowledge is privative.
3
Understand the refutation of abstraction
Read from 'لا يقال' to the end. The objector suggests knowledge is abstraction from matter. The author gives two counter-arguments: abstraction can be conceived without knowledge, and knowledge is relational (this but not that) while abstraction is not.
This shows that abstraction is a different concept from knowledge.

Presential Knowledge and the I'tibar Solution
Explains the preferred huduri answer and resolves the question of how a thing can be present to itself.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Islamic theologians explain the possibility of a thing being present to itself in presential knowledge through conceptual distinction (i'tibar).

Core Idea
A thing can be present to itself by a conceptual distinction within its own essence.

This lesson explores the verifiable answer to how self-knowledge and knowledge of attributes are possible: through presential knowledge (huduri). The author addresses a key objection—how can something be present to itself if presence requires two entities? The solution lies in a conceptual distinction (i'tibar) within the same essence, such as the soul's dual aspects of being knower and being known.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Note that this is presented as the third and preferred answer (the verifiable answer).
The passage begins with 'And another' referring to another answer.
2
Identify the objection
The objector asks: how can a thing be present to itself? Presence implies a relation between two distinct things. Without two entities, relation seems impossible.
This is a logical objection that challenges the very notion of self-knowledge.
3
Understand the answer: i'tibar
The response is that a conceptual distinction (i'tibar) within the same essence is sufficient for a relation. The soul, for example, can be considered in two ways: as potentially knowing and as potentially being known. These two aspects are distinct in consideration, not in reality, allowing presence.
This subtle distinction preserves the unity of the essence while allowing a relation.
4
Reflect on the implication
This solution not only resolves the problem for the soul's self-knowledge but also for God's knowledge of Himself. It shows that presential knowledge does not require real multiplicity.
The passage ends mid-sentence, but the argument is complete in its core.

Transformation of Theoretical into Necessary and the Mu'tazila's Exception
Consensus on permissibility of theoretical becoming necessary and Mu'tazila's exception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the consensus on the permissibility of theoretical knowledge transforming into necessary knowledge, and the specific denial of its occurrence by the Mu'tazila regarding knowledge of Allah and His attributes.

Core Idea
Theoretical knowledge can become necessary, but the Mu'tazila make an exception for God's knowledge.

This passage addresses an important issue in Islamic theology: can theoretical knowledge (acquired through reasoning) become necessary (innate) knowledge? The mutakallimun agree it is permissible, but the Mu'tazila deny its actual occurrence specifically for knowledge of Allah and His attributes, due to the condition of obligation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the issue
The passage discusses whether theoretical knowledge can transform into necessary knowledge. This is a key topic in epistemology and theology.
Theoretical knowledge is acquired through reasoning, necessary knowledge is innate or directly given.
2
Note the consensus on permissibility
All mutakallimun agree that such transformation is permissible. This is a point of agreement between Ash'aris and Mu'tazila.
3
Identify the Ash'ari position
According to the Ash'aris, the actual transformation occurs by Allah creating necessary knowledge related to the theoretical.
This emphasizes divine power in knowledge acquisition.
4
Understand the Mu'tazila's exception
The Mu'tazila deny that this transformation actually occurs for the knowledge of Allah and His attributes, even though they permit it in principle.
They restrict the exception to this specific domain.
5
Grasp their rationale
They argue that the servant is obligated to acquire knowledge of Allah. If it became necessary (innate), it would not be within the servant's capacity to acquire it through effort, making the obligation impossible and thus unjust.
This relies on their principle that obligation must be within human capacity.

Third Argument: Reductio Against Simultaneous Power
Jurjani presents the third argument as a reductio against simultaneous power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the third argument uses a reductio ad absurdum to prove that divine power must precede its object of power.

Core Idea
If power were simultaneous with its act, God's power would be created or His act eternal — both impossible.

This passage presents the third argument against the Mu'tazilite claim that power is simultaneous with the act. By examining the impossible consequences, the argument demonstrates that power must precede its object. It also adds a supporting reasoning about the analogy between originated and eternal power, showing that precedence is not impossible for either.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow, then read the translation. Notice the logical structure: the argument starts with a conditional and draws two impossible horns.

The argument says: if power and act are together, then either God's power is originated (since the act is originated) or His act is eternal (since His power is eternal). Locate these in the text.

The argument adds that power is established by consensus to be pre-eternal and attached in pre-eternity. It then uses an analogy: if precedence were impossible for originated power, it would be impossible for eternal power too. This strengthens the conclusion that power must precede its object.

Reflect on the conclusion

Commentary
This passage introduces a central kalam debate about the nature of composition (ta'lif) when atoms interact. It begins by stating the topic: the agreement of theologians who affirm the indivisible atom (jawhar fard). The initial claim is that an atom is associated with six compositions, six contiguities, and one adjacency—the six contiguities specifying the atom's location without requiring a seventh state. The Mu'tazila then assert that adjacency between a wet atom and a dry atom generates a single composition that subsists in both. However, they disagree on what happens when one atom is surrounded by six others. One group holds that one composition subsists in all seven atoms, reasoning that if composition can subsist in two, it can subsist in more—so whether the surrounding atoms are two or six, the composition is one. The other group holds that there are six compositions, one for each surrounding atom, to avoid each part having its own composition individually. They refute the unity view by arguing that the essence (mahiyya) of composition is contradictory to the condition of composition, i.e., adjacency, so if that condition is lost the composition vanishes—but a single composition would have to vanish from one direction only, which is impossible. The subsequent glosses (sharh) clarify the phrasing: the pronoun in 'six contiguities' refers to contiguities, not compositions; the 'seventh state' specifies that the atom in question is already surrounded, not isolated; 'when the atom composes' means the wet atom; the referent is not the mixing of wet and dry; and a long note explains that according to the Shaykh (al-Ash'ari) contiguity produces the state of rest, and different accidents cannot substitute for each other's effects. The passage ends with a concrete example: if five surrounding atoms remain and the sixth ceases, the composition of the five persists while that of the sixth ceases, showing that compositions are multiple and cannot be one.

Abu Ishaq: Contiguity is Identical to Adjacency
Abu Ishaq identifies contiguity with adjacency and derives its opposite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that al-Ustadh Abu Ishaq equates contiguity and adjacency as one accident, and how this leads to separation being opposite to both contiguity and composition.

Core Idea
Contiguity and adjacency are the same reality—not two separate accidents.

Al-Ustadh Abu Ishaq (al-Isfara'ini) takes a distinctive position on the relationship between contiguity and adjacency in atoms. While others might treat them as distinct, he argues they are identical. This lesson explores his argument and its implications for the ontology of accidents.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice how Abu Ishaq states his position clearly.
2
Identify the key claim
The central claim is that contiguity (mumassa) is exactly adjacency (mujawara). Understand what this means for the atomistic model.
3
Understand the multiplicity
Abu Ishaq says contiguity/adjacency are multiple according to the number of adjacent atoms. Each pair of adjacent atoms has its own contiguity.
This implies contiguity is not a single accident but many.
4
Trace the consequence
Since adjacency is opposite to separation, and contiguity is identical to adjacency, separation is also opposite to contiguity and composition.
This simplifies the ontology.

Commentary
This passage presents the position of al-Ustadh Abu Ishaq (al-Isfara'ini) on the relationship between contiguity (mumassa) and adjacency (mujawara). He asserts that contiguity between atoms is exactly the same reality as adjacency between them—they are not two different accidents. Both are multiple, meaning there is a distinct contiguity/adjacency for each pair of adjacent atoms. Since adjacency is agreed to be opposite to separation (mubayana), and contiguity is identical to adjacency, separation is therefore opposed both to contiguity and to composition (ta'lif) as well. This view simplifies the ontology by reducing contiguity and adjacency to one accident, and it draws the logical consequence that separation is their real opposite.

Commentary
This passage presents the doctrine of al-Qadi Abu Bakr al-Baqillani on the nature of the 'state' (kawn) of an atom. He argues that when an atom occupies a location and then contiguities and adjacencies from other atoms come and go, the state of that atom remains one and unchanged in its essence; it does not become multiple. The different names—stillness (sukun), union (ijtima'), composition (ta'lif), adjacency (mujawara), contiguity (mumassa), and separation (mubayana)—are merely names applied according to different considerations (i'tibarat) of the same underlying state. The state before joining is called stillness; during joining, even if identical to the first, it is called union, composition, etc.; after joining ceases, it is called separation. The various states are not different entities but are the very states that specify the atom with different locations. The commentator remarks that this view is closer to the truth based on Ash'ari principles, because it does not require a specific structure for an accident to subsist, and it avoids the impossibility that the atom itself could be the cause. Al-Qadi's view thus reconciles the unity of the state with the diversity of names based on different relations.

Natural Motion Is Not Identical to Corporeality
Proof by contradiction that natural motion's cause is not corporeality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why corporeality alone cannot be the cause of natural motion, based on a proof by contradiction.

Core Idea
If corporeality caused motion, bodies would never rest.

This lesson presents a crisp logical argument from the Islamic theological tradition. The author wants to show that natural motion is not caused simply by a body being a body. If it were, motion would be perpetual, contradicting our observation that bodies rest. Let's walk through the proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice the structure: a statement, a conditional, a consequence, and a reason.
The passage is a classic proof by contradiction.
2

The author assumes the opposite of what he wants to prove: that corporeality is the cause of natural motion. Trace the chain of reasoning from this assumption.
3
Understand the consequence
If corporeality were the cause, then motion would continue as long as the body exists, making rest impossible. Ask yourself: what does this imply about the cause?
This consequence contradicts observed reality.
4
Grasp the underlying principle
The argument relies on the principle that a thing's essence, by itself, necessitates its effect perpetually. This is why the author says 'مقتضى ذات الشيء وحدها يبقى.' Consider what this means.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Since we know bodies do rest, the assumption that corporeality is the cause must be false. Therefore, natural motion must have a different cause.
This shows the power of logical reasoning in theology.
