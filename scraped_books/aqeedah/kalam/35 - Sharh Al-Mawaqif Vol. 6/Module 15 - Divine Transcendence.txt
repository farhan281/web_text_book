Matter's Receptivity to Magnitudes: Basis of Rarefaction and Condensation
Explains matter's receptivity to magnitudes and the role of forms in preventing change.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how matter's lack of inherent magnitude makes rarefaction and condensation possible, and why this does not require all bodies to be changeable due to obstacles like specific forms.

Core Idea
Matter's lack of magnitude enables change; forms can lock it in place.

In this passage, the author argues that matter's nature as receptive to different magnitudes is the justifier for rarefaction and condensation. He then addresses the objection that if matter has no magnitude, all bodies should be changeable, clarifying that obstacles such as specific forms or part-hood can prevent change. This lesson unpacks his reasoning and the key distinction between a justifier and a necessitating cause.

The author resolves the apparent contradiction by explaining that matter's nature is only a justifier (musahhih), not a necessitating cause. Obstacles like specific forms or part-hood can prevent change. The final reply addresses the drop-of-sea objection.
This final part clarifies that celestial matter's restriction is due to its specific form, reconciling the two statements.

Matter in some things, as in the elements, is receptive to different magnitudes, those magnitudes succeeding upon it according to what prepares it from external causes beyond its essence. So when a magnitude greater than what it had supervenes upon it, rarefaction is established, and when what is smaller supervenes, condensation is established. And it does not follow from matter having no magnitude in itself that everything is like that—i.e., that all bodies are such that different magnitudes succeed upon them by way of alternation—because it is possible that some bodies are restricted to a specific magnitude not exceeded by another, due to separate causes requiring its restriction to that magnitude, or that some bodies are restricted to a specific magnitude because their matter accepts only that specific magnitude, as is their view concerning the celestial spheres, for each of them has a matter different in reality from the matter of another, and each matter accepts only a particular magnitude according to some of them. And since the statement that the matter of the celestial spheres accepts only a specific magnitude contradicts the statement that matter has no magnitude in itself, and since if it were so, its relation to all magnitudes would be equal, he turned away from that by saying: In summary, this which we have mentioned concerning the state of matter is a justifier for rarefaction and condensation, and it does not follow from its actualization—i.e., from the actualization of the justifier—the actualization of the effect, such that the existence of rarefaction and condensation in all bodies would be necessitated. Rather, it is possible that with the justifier there is an obstacle that prevents the actualization of the effect, like the specific forms in the celestial bodies, for each of them necessitates its adherence to its matter and its restriction to a specific magnitude, and like the part-hood in elemental bodies, for the part as long as it is a part, it is impossible for its magnitude to equal the magnitude of its whole; but if it separates, it can be qualified by the magnitude of the whole. And separation is not permissible in the parts of the celestial sphere according to them, unlike the elements. So there is directed against them the permitting that a drop of the sea, when separated from it, be receptive to the magnitude of the celestial sphere—a specific magnitude such that its relation to the individuals of that degree is equal, thus necessitating the permissibility of its being exchanged upon the matter of the celestial sphere, the falsehood of which according to them all arises from lack of acquaintance with their statement that the matter of the celestial sphere accepts only a specific magnitude, based on the fact that it accepts only a specific form, and that specific form requires a specific magnitude, so there is no contradiction between the two statements.

Commentary
This passage is a proof and explanation of how matter's nature justifies the possibility of rarefaction (takhalkhul) and condensation (takathuf). The author begins by stating that matter in some things (like the elements) is receptive to different magnitudes that succeed upon it due to external causes. When a larger magnitude comes, rarefaction occurs; when a smaller comes, condensation occurs. He then addresses a potential objection: if matter has no magnitude in itself, then all bodies should be able to undergo such changes. He replies that this does not follow, because some bodies may be restricted to a specific magnitude due to separate causes or due to their matter only accepting one magnitude—like the celestial spheres according to some. He notes that the claim that celestial matter only accepts one magnitude seems to contradict the claim that matter has no inherent magnitude. To resolve this, he states that the foregoing is merely a justifier (musahhih) for rarefaction and condensation, not a necessitation. An obstacle may prevent the effect, such as the specific forms in celestial bodies that bind their matter to a specific magnitude, or the part-hood of elemental parts that cannot equal the whole's magnitude while attached. He then presents an objection from the opponent: if a drop of the sea separates, it should be capable of receiving the magnitude of the celestial sphere, leading to absurdity. The reply is that this objection arises from not understanding that celestial matter is restricted to a specific magnitude because it accepts only a specific form, which entails that magnitude. Thus, there is no contradiction between matter's capacity and the restriction imposed by form.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think that because matter has no inherent magnitude, all bodies must be able to change volume.
Correction: The author explains that specific forms or part-hood can act as obstacles that permanently restrict a body to one magnitude, even though matter itself is receptive.

Key Takeaway
Matter's receptivity to different magnitudes provides the basis for rarefaction and condensation, but specific forms or part-hood can act as obstacles giving some bodies fixed magnitudes.
