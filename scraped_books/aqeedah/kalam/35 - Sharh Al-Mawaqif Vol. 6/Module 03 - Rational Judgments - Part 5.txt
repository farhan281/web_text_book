The Common Meaning of All Types of Priority
Second note on the common meaning of all types of priority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that all five types of priority share a single common meaning: the prior has an extra attribute that the posterior lacks, and you will recognize how this attribute differs in each type.

Core Idea
All priority types share one meaning: an extra attribute.

The second note unifies the five types of priority (essential, causal, temporal, honorific, positional) under a single conceptual framework. Instead of treating them as unrelated, the author shows that in every case priority is a form of having something extra that the posterior does not have. This passage defines that common meaning and illustrates it for each type.

Commentary
This passage presents two important notes (tanbīhān) regarding priority. The first note discusses how priority is understood in different contexts: among parts of the future, priority is based on closeness to the present 'now'. Between past and future, the majority view is that the past is prior to the future with respect to their essences (the past has already occurred, so its essence is prior). Some, however, reverse this based on accidents, since every time is first future and then becomes past, so futurehood is an accident that occurs before pastness. The second note states that all types of priority share a common meaning: the prior possesses an extra attribute (amr zāʾid) that the posterior lacks. This attribute varies with each type: in essential priority it is being a constitutive part (muqawwim), in causal priority it is being the efficient cause (mūjid), in temporal priority it is having more time elapsed, in honorific priority it is an increase in perfection, and in positional priority it is being reached first from the starting point. The marginal note clarifies that this extra attribute is essential for distinguishing priority and posteriority, as discussed in Avicenna's Shifa. The passage thus unifies the five types under a single conceptual framework.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Concomitance is not just temporal; it includes honorific, positional, essential, and causal forms.

Building on the concepts of priority and posteriority, Islamic philosophers extend the idea of 'togetherness' (ma'iyya) into five distinct categories. This passage lays out those categories with clear examples, showing how each type of priority has a corresponding type of concomitance. You'll also encounter a nuanced debate about the definition of temporal concomitance between theologians and philosophers.

Commentary
This passage derives the five types of concomitance (al-maʿiyya) from the five types of priority by analogy. After listing the types of priority and posteriority, the text states that concomitance is understood by comparison. It then gives examples: temporal concomitance (two things in the same time), honorific concomitance (two people equal in virtue), positional concomitance (two species opposite under one genus, or two people equally close to the prayer niche), essential concomitance (two constituent parts of a single essence at the same rank), and causal concomitance (two causes for two individual effects). The marginal notes provide additional clarification: first, the theologians define temporal concomitance as two things occurring in one time, while the philosophers define it as the negation of the impossibility of coexistence; this definition applies to temporal things but not to parts of time, whereas temporal priority and posteriority apply to both time and temporal things. The note criticizes the New Commentary on al-Tajrid for defining concomitance merely as negation of priority and posteriority. The second marginal note suggests a refinement to the definition of temporal priority: instead of simply 'a longer time has passed for it,' one should say 'a longer time has passed from the beginning of its existence' to cover cases where the prior and its time no longer exist when the posterior exists. This lesson shows how the concepts of priority and posteriority systematically generate parallel concepts of concomitance.

Critique of the Prior Division of Substance
Al-Jurjani critiques two demonstrations for the prior division of substance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Jurjani considers the prior classification of substance into body and non-body to be unproven and problematic.

Core Idea
A division without demonstration is just an assertion, not knowledge.

Al-Jurjani begins by examining an existing classification of substance. He points out that the arguments for it are not demonstrative and that hidden assumptions—like the impossibility of a substance being a locus for another—are unjustified. This lesson unpacks his critique, showing the fragility of the earlier division.

Guided Path
1
Read the critique passage
Read the Arabic passage carefully with its translation. Notice that al-Jurjani lists two attempts to prove the division and then undermines both.
The passage starts with 'يصح أيضاً' and ends with 'جسماً'.
2
Identify the two demonstrations
Find where al-Jurjani states the two demonstrations (the first about composition, the second about non-composition). Understand what each tries to prove.
The first is about non-body not being composed of state and locus; the second is about no substance being a locus.
3
Analyze the refutation
See why each demonstration fails: the first contradicts what is apparent, and the second is not certain because it allows for imperceptible substances that could be loci.
Al-Jurjani uses possibility (جوز) to show the second is not necessary.
4
Consider the implication
If the second demonstration is false, then the very definition of body as composite of state and locus is threatened. Ask yourself: why does this matter?
This opens the door for substances that are not bodies and not simple atoms.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of a previous division of substance into body and non-body, followed by a reformulated division that attempts to avoid certain objections. The author (likely al-Jurjani) begins by stating that it is also possible for a substance to be composed of two substances, one being a state (ḥāl) and the other a locus (maḥall), and that no substance is a locus for another. After demonstrating that non-body substances are not composed of two parts where one is a state in the other, the author points out that neither of these two demonstrations is proven by a demonstrative proof (burhān). The first contradicts the apparent, and the second is not certain because it is possible for a substance to be a locus for another substance without either being sensorily perceptible—so the locus would not be matter (hayūlā), the state not a form (ṣūra), and the composite not a body. Then the author proposes a reformulated division to avoid these problems: substance is either a body (having three dimensions) or not. If not a body, it is either a part of the body or not. If a part, then if the body is actual through it, it is a form; otherwise, it is matter. If not a part, then if it is disposed of by the body (mutasarrif fīh), it is a soul (nafs); otherwise, it is an intellect (ʿaql). The author notes that this exhaustive division does not rely on substance inhering in another or on the composition of body from a state and a locus. However, it is still based on the denial of the indivisible atom (jawhar fard), because if body is composed of atoms, then the components are all atoms—none being a form or matter—and the aggregate configuration is external to the essence of the body. An objection remains: something that is neither a body nor a part of it nor disposed of by it need not be an intellect; it could be a part of the soul or the intellect, implying the division is not exhaustive.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of a previous division of substance into body and non-body, followed by a reformulated division that attempts to avoid certain objections. The author (likely al-Jurjani) begins by stating that it is also possible for a substance to be composed of two substances, one being a state (ḥāl) and the other a locus (maḥall), and that no substance is a locus for another. After demonstrating that non-body substances are not composed of two parts where one is a state in the other, the author points out that neither of these two demonstrations is proven by a demonstrative proof (burhān). The first contradicts the apparent, and the second is not certain because it is possible for a substance to be a locus for another substance without either being sensorily perceptible—so the locus would not be matter (hayūlā), the state not a form (ṣūra), and the composite not a body. The author notes that this exhaustive division does not rely on substance inhering in another or on the composition of body from a state and a locus. Commentary
This passage presents the Ash'ari position on substance and includes two notifications (tanbīhān) regarding the definition of body. The mutakallimun (theologians) assert that the only substance is the extended (al-mutaḥayyiz), which is by essence receptive to sensory indication. They deny immaterial substances and deem them impossible. Accordingly, the extended either accepts division (in one or more dimensions) — which is the body according to the Ash'aris — or does not accept division at all, which is the indivisible atom (jawhar fard). Thus substance is limited to these two kinds, and the minimum composition of a body is two atoms. The first notification (al-tanbīh al-awwal) addresses a disagreement among Ash'aris: The majority (jumhūr) define body as the aggregate (majmūʿ) of the two constituent parts, not each individual part. Al-Qadi (al-Baqillani) and his followers, however, define body as each individual part. Their reasoning: body is that in which composition (ta'līf) subsists, by agreement. Composition is an accident (ʿaraḍ), and on Ash'ari principles an accident cannot subsist in two things (i.e., cannot be in two loci simultaneously), because a single individual accident cannot be in many. Therefore, each of the two atoms must have its own composition, making them two bodies, not one. The author then clarifies that this is not a verbal dispute (nizāʿ lafẓī) about whether the term 'body' is applied to what is intrinsically composed (i.e., among its internal parts) or to what is composed with another, as al-Amidi mistakenly thought. The dispute is real because it concerns the very essence of body.

Substance is a genus for its species but not for their differentiae, thus avoiding infinite regress.
