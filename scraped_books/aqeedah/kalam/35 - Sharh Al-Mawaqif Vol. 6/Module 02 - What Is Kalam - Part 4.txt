Second Doubt: Does the Definition of Body Apply to Imaginary Dimensions?
Author presents and refutes the doubt that the definition applies to imaginary dimensions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the definition of body does not apply to imaginary dimensions and how the author refutes this doubt by distinguishing between the corporeal form and the composite body.

Core Idea
The definition of body is for real extended substance, not for imaginary geometrical abstractions.

In this lesson, we examine the second doubt raised against the definition of body: that it would apply to imaginary dimensions studied in geometry. The author clarifies that the definition is for the corporeal form, not the composite, and explains why this does not harm the definition. We will follow the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the doubt and its statement
Read the opening sentence where the author presents the doubt: that the definition applies to imagination (wahm) and thus to imaginary dimensions called 'educational body'.
The term 'educational body' (jism taʿlīmī) refers to abstract geometrical solids.
2
Understand the gloss on geometry
Notice the gloss explaining that geometry discusses such imaginary bodies. This sets up why the doubt might seem plausible.
3
Identify the first part of the refutation
The author argues that the definition is for the corporeal form (ṣūra jismiyya), not for the composite body. Therefore, applying it to an existential part (the form) is not a problem.
This addresses the worry about part-whole confusion.
4
Examine the objection and answer about the whole
Read the objection: if the definition applies to the part, it also applies to the whole? The author answers by noting the tanwīn indicates one substance, while the composite body is two substances (form and hyle).
5
Analyze the rejection of external acceptance for the composite
The author refutes the claim that external acceptance belongs only to the composite, using the analogy of quantity (miqdār).
This strengthens the point that the definition targets the form alone.

Commentary
This passage presents the second doubt on the definition of body: that it applies to imaginary (wahm) dimensions, which are called 'educational body' (jism taʿlīmī) in geometry. The author then provides a gloss explaining that geometry deals with such imaginary bodies. The refutation is given in the form of a discussion on the commentator's (shāriḥ) intent. The response states that the definition is for the corporeal form (ṣūra jismiyya) alone, not for the composite of form and hyle. Therefore, applying the definition to the existential part (i.e., the form) is not a problem. If one objects that the definition would then also apply to the whole composite body (since the form is part of it), the author answers that the indefinite article (tanwīn) indicates a single substance, whereas the composite body is two substances (form and hyle). Finally, the claim that external acceptance (qabūl khārijī) is only for the composite is refuted: the form's external existence always accompanies hyle, but that does not mean acceptance belongs to the composite; analogy is made with quantity (miqdār) which depends on a cause for existence but can be accepted alone.

Definition of Body and the Roles of Hylè and Form
Definition of body and the roles of hylè and form.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that in Islamic metaphysics, the term 'body' (jism) primarily refers to the extended substance (corporeal form), while hylè (matter) and form are its metaphysical components.

Core Idea
Body is primarily the extended substance (corporeal form), not the composite of matter and form.

In this passage, the commentator clarifies the definition of 'body' in the context of substance theory. He explains that hylè (matter) cannot have dimensions in itself, but receives the corporeal form, which in turn receives dimensions. The definition 'extended substance' applies directly to the corporeal form, not the composite. This distinction is crucial for understanding how philosophers conceive of physical bodies.

The analogy of the ship passenger is used to illustrate indirect acceptance.

The definition 'extended substance' targets the corporeal form itself, not the composite. The phrase 'therein' (fīh) indicates dimensions are supposed in the substance itself.

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'body' (jism) in the context of substance (jawhar) and its components, namely hylè (hayula) and corporeal form (sura jismiyya). The commentator begins by stating that hylè in itself is not such that dimensions can be supposed in it; rather, it receives the corporeal form, and the corporeal form receives supposed dimensions. The definition of body as 'extended substance' implies the possibility of supposing dimensions in the substance itself, without mediation. An objection is raised: if the definition applies directly to the corporeal form alone, not the composite of hylè and form, then it is incomplete. The answer is that in the first analysis, 'body' means this extended substance, which is the corporeal form; the fact that it subsists in another substance (hylè) is a subtle point not relevant to the common understanding. The commentator then explains each phrase of the main text: 'His statement: (Hylè is not, etc.)' means hylè cannot have dimensions supposed in itself because it is not continuous; 'His statement: (Rather it receives the form, etc.)' means its acceptance of the supposition is indirect, like a passenger's movement relative to a ship. 'His statement: (And what is understood, etc.)' clarifies that the definition's phrase 'therein' (fīh) refers to dimensions being supposed in the substance itself, not in something accompanying it. 'His statement: (Its definition)' refers to defining the extended substance, which is apparently the composite, so the definition applies to the whole, not the part. The commentator then refutes an objection: the aim is to define the composite, and external acceptance (qabul khariji) is intended, but the corporeal form cannot have external acceptance without hylè. This is dismissed because existence of a mediator in subsistence does not preclude its absence in attribution; the extended thing is capable of having dimensions supposed in itself even when conjoined with hylè. Another objection: the definition is for the corporeal form alone, indicated by the indefinite tanwin (for unity). The commentator rejects this because the definition would then not apply to the composite, which is described by unity of genus, species, or individual; composition does not preclude unity, and interpreting the indefinite as simplicity is far-fetched.

The Objection from Imagined Dimensions and Its Answer
The objector says the definition applies to wahm; the commentator answers it means external existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the definition of body as 'extended substance capable of having dimensions supposed in it' does not apply to the imagination (wahm) and how external existence is the key qualifier.

Core Idea
The definition of body concerns external existence, not mental supposition.

The second objection against the definition of body claims that it applies to imagined dimensions, which are called a 'mathematical body'. The commentator clarifies that the definition refers to acceptance in external existence, not mental imagination. This distinction is crucial for understanding the nature of body in kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the opening statement: 'This definition applies to the imagination, and therefore the imagined dimensions are called a mathematical body.' Understand the challenge.
Notice that geometry studies mathematical bodies as solid shapes.
2
Identify the commentator's answer about external existence
The commentator says the truth is that what is meant is 'external acceptance' (al-qabul al-khariji). This means the definition is about existence in the external world, not in the mind.
External acceptance is only for the composite of matter and form.
3
Understand why wahm is not a substance
The doubt only applies if wahm is a substance, but it is actually an accident (a bodily power). The definition of body applies to substances, not accidents.
Even if wahm could have dimensions supposed, it is not a substance.
4
Summarize the key distinction
The definition is about a substance's capacity to receive dimensions in external reality. This does not apply to mental entities like imagined dimensions.
External existence is the standard; mental supposition is irrelevant.

Commentary
This passage presents the second objection (al-shakk al-thani) against the definition of body as 'extended substance capable of having dimensions supposed in it': the definition applies to the imagination (wahm), because imagined dimensions are called a 'mathematical body' (jism ta'limi). However, the main answer to the objection is that the substance's 'acceptance of the supposition of dimensions' means acceptance in external existence, not mental existence. The doubt only arises if we consider wahm to be a substance; but wahm is actually an accident (a bodily power). Moreover, the possibility of supposing dimensions in wahm is not due to wahm itself, but due to the imagined dimensions. Thus the definition does not apply to wahm qua substance.
