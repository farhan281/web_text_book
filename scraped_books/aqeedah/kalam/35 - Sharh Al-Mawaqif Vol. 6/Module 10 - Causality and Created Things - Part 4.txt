Philosophers' Proof of Rest: The Seed and Mountain
Philosophers prove rest via seed-mountain thought experiment and answer the absurdity objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the philosophers use the thought experiment of a mustard seed and a mountain to prove the existence of rest between opposite motions, and how they respond to the objection that this leads to the absurd conclusion of the mountain stopping.

Core Idea
Rest exists because the seed must pause, but the mountain doesn't stop — it never touches.

The philosophers present a vivid thought experiment: a mustard seed ascending and a mountain descending. At first glance, it seems that if the seed must rest between its ascent and descent, then the mountain must also stop to avoid interpenetration. This would be absurd. However, the philosophers have a clever answer: the seed never actually collides with the mountain; it is repelled by the mountain's wind. In this lesson, we will examine the proof and the response, clarifying the nature of rest.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Begin by reading the Arabic passage from the source. Notice the structure: the philosophers' statement, the thought experiment, the absurd consequence, and the answer.
The text is dense, so take your time.
2
Understand the thought experiment
Visualize the scenario: a tiny mustard seed ascends, a huge mountain descends, and they meet in midair. What must happen at the moment of contact?
3
Identify the problem
The philosophers point out that if the seed stops between its motions, the mountain must also stop to avoid passing through the seed. This seems absurd — a mountain cannot be stopped by a seed. This is the mountain objection.
This is the apparent flaw in the proof.
4
Examine the response
The answer is that the seed never actually touches the mountain. Instead, it is pushed back by the mountain's wind before making contact. Thus, the seed stops and returns, but the mountain continues moving. The absurdity is avoided.
This shows that the thought experiment's problematic scenario is impossible.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
The philosophers' proof stands: rest between opposite motions is possible, and the mountain objection does not refute it.
Consider the role of wind (rīḥ) in the argument.

Commentary
This passage presents the philosophers' proof for the existence of rest between two opposite rectilinear motions and a refutation of an objection. The philosophers argue: if rest exists between two motions, consider a thought experiment: a mustard seed ascending and a mountain descending, meeting in midair such that their surfaces touch. At the moment of contact, the seed must stop (rest) between its ascent and descent, because it cannot be moving in opposite directions at the same instant. This rest of the seed would force the mountain to also stop at that instant, because two bodies cannot interpenetrate (imtinaʿ al-tadākhul). But the conclusion that the mountain stops due to a tiny seed is absurd—every rational person knows this is impossible. Therefore, the assumption that rest exists between the motions leads to an absurdity, so rest does not exist. The answer to this objection is that in the imagined scenario, the seed does not actually touch the mountain; instead, it is repelled by the mountain's wind (rīḥ). The wind from the mountain pushes the seed back before it makes contact. So the seed stops and then returns due to the wind, not due to direct collision. Thus the scenario where they meet is impossible, and the argument does not prove the absurdity of rest.

Mu'tazilite Denial of Rest and Al-Jubba'i's Response
Mu'tazila deny rest; Al-Jubba'i replies with motion as generator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite argument against rest between motions and Al-Jubba'i's alternative causal principle involving preceding motion and equilibrium.

Core Idea
Rest between motions is possible when preceding motion balances inclinations.

The Mu'tazila and their opponents debated whether rest can occur between two contradictory motions. This passage presents the Mu'tazilite claim that no rest is possible because only inclination generates motion, and neither inherent nor acquired inclination produces rest. Then it offers Al-Jubba'i's reply, who introduces preceding motion as a generator and explains rest as a result of equilibrium between the two inclinations.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazilite argument
The Mu'tazila state that there is no rest between two motions. Understand their reasoning: they claim only inclination generates motion and rest, and neither type of inclination necessitates rest.
They refer to two types of inclination: inherent (lāzim) and acquired (mujtalab).
2
Analyze the two inclinations
Note that inherent inclination requires downward motion, acquired inclination requires upward motion. Since neither produces rest, and no other generator exists, rest cannot occur between the opposite motions.
This argument hinges on the exclusivity of inclination as a cause.
3
Examine Al-Jubba'i's counter
Al-Jubba'i rejects the premise that inclination is the sole generator. Instead, the preceding motion itself can generate both motion and rest. Rest occurs when the two inclinations are in equilibrium.
This introduces a new causal factor: the preceding motion, not just inclination.

Commentary
This passage presents the Mu'tazilite position denying the existence of rest between two opposite motions, followed by a reply on behalf of Al-Jubba'i. The Mu'tazila argue that neither the inherent inclination (al-iʿtimād al-lāzim) nor the acquired inclination (al-iʿtimād al-mujtalab) can produce rest: the inherent inclination demands downward motion only, the acquired inclination demands upward motion only, and there is no other cause (muwallid) for motion or rest besides inclination. Therefore, no rest can occur between the ascending and descending motions. In response, Al-Jubba'i (or his follower) argues, based on his own principle, that they do not accept that inclination is the sole generator. Instead, the generator of motion and rest is the preceding motion itself. Specifically, an ascending motion can generate a descending motion when the inherent inclination dominates, and it can generate rest when the two inclinations reach equilibrium. This has already been discussed in the section on inclination. Thus, rest is possible as a result of the preceding motion, not directly from inclination.

The Philosophers' Claim and the Mu'tazila Response
Refutation of philosophers' cause of slowness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' argument about the mountain-mustard seed and how the Mu'tazila and al-Jubba'i refute it.

Core Idea
Collision without interpenetration is not the cause of slowness; motion is generated by i'timad.

In this passage, the commentator presents the philosophers' claim that a mountain stops due to collision with a mustard seed, then immediately refutes it as absurd. He then discusses alternative explanations from the Mu'tazila and al-Jubba'i.

Guided Path
1
Read the philosophers' claim
Read the Arabic passage carefully. Identify the philosophers' argument: stillness between ascending and descending motions causes slowness, and collision with a mustard seed prevents interpenetration, stopping the mountain.
Notice that the claim is presented as a 'cause of slowness'.
2
Understand the wind answer
The commentator presents an answer: the mustard seed does not actually touch the mountain but is repelled by its 'wind' (air pressure). This makes the collision an impossible assumption.
This answer is not the final position; it is a possible response.
3
Examine the Mu'tazila and Jubba'i positions
The Mu'tazila deny stillness between motions, arguing that i'timad only generates motion. Al-Jubba'i disagrees, saying preceding motion generates either motion or stillness depending on i'timad balance.
This shows the diversity of kalam opinions on causality.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of a specific natural philosophical argument. It begins by stating the philosophers' claim: that the mountain stops between its ascending and descending motions due to collision with a mustard seed, because interpenetration is impossible. The commentator immediately rejects this as absurd. Then he presents an answer: the mustard seed does not actually touch the mountain; it is repelled by the mountain's 'wind' (air pressure), so no collision occurs. The Mu'tazila are then cited, denying that stillness is generated by i'timad; rather, i'timad produces motion. Al-Jubba'i offers an alternative: the preceding motion itself generates the next motion or stillness depending on the balance of i'timads. The commentator then addresses several objections: first, that interpenetration could allow the mustard seed to pass through the mountain without collision; second, that the idea of the seed being repelled by wind is obstinate because direct collision is observable; third, that even if the seed were repelled, the mountain could still stop. The final objection argues that empirical observation shows collision occurs before return, but the commentator concludes that even if that is conceded, the stopping of the mountain is merely improbable, not absolutely impossible.

Objections and Replies on the Mountain-Mustard Seed Argument
Response to objections on the slowness argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objections raised against the wind answer and the final resolution that the mountain's stopping is merely improbable.

Core Idea
Even if collision occurs, the mountain stopping is improbable, not impossible.

The commentator now addresses several objections to the wind answer, including the possibility of interpenetration and empirical observations. He concludes that even if the objections are granted, the mountain's stopping is only improbable, not absolutely impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection about interpenetration
Read the first part of the Arabic passage in this lesson. An objector suggests that the mustard seed could pass through the mountain without interpenetration due to density and rarefaction, so no stopping would occur.
This objection targets the premise that collision is necessary for stopping.
2
Examine the response to empirical observation
The commentator addresses the argument that direct collision is observable (e.g., an arrow hitting a falling mountain). He responds that this is mere conjecture and that the mountain stopping is not required.
Observe the distinction between factual and hypothetical scenarios.
3
Understand the final conclusion
Even if collision is conceded, the mountain stopping is described as 'improbable' (mustab'ad) rather than 'impossible' (mustahil). This is a significant theological nuance.
The commentator avoids absolute impossibility, leaving room for divine power.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of a specific natural philosophical argument. It begins by stating the philosophers' claim: that the mountain stops between its ascending and descending motions due to collision with a mustard seed, because interpenetration is impossible. The commentator immediately rejects this as absurd. Then he presents an answer: the mustard seed does not actually touch the mountain; it is repelled by the mountain's 'wind' (air pressure), so no collision occurs. The Mu'tazila are then cited, denying that stillness is generated by i'timad; rather, i'timad produces motion. Al-Jubba'i offers an alternative: the preceding motion itself generates the next motion or stillness depending on the balance of i'timads. The commentator then addresses several objections: first, that interpenetration could allow the mustard seed to pass through the mountain without collision; second, that the idea of the seed being repelled by wind is obstinate because direct collision is observable; third, that even if the seed were repelled, the mountain could still stop. The final objection argues that empirical observation shows collision occurs before return, but the commentator concludes that even if that is conceded, the stopping of the mountain is merely improbable, not absolutely impossible.

Commentary
This passage defines and proves the first type of priority (t.qaddum) as identified by the philosophers: priority by causality. The commentator begins by stating that there are five types of priority. He then gives two examples: the priority of the luminous source over the light emanating from it, and the priority of the finger's movement over the ring's movement. The key argument is that the finger's movement is prior to the ring's movement, and the intellect recognizes this as a causal relation rather than temporal or essential. Commentary
This passage presents the philosophers' attempt to provide a proof for the classification of precedence into five types. It is a definitional and classificatory passage. The philosophers argue that precedence is either real (ḥaqīqī) or nominal (iʿtibārī). Real precedence requires that the posterior depend on the prior in some way, without the prior depending on the posterior (to avoid circularity). This real dependence is either (1) in essence (dhāt): the posterior's essence is constituted by the prior's essence, e.g., the dependence of two on one—essential precedence (taqaddum bi-dh-dhāt); or (2) in existence (wujūd): the posterior's existence depends on the prior's existence, but not its essence. Existential dependence is further divided into two subtypes: (a) temporal precedence (taqaddum zamānī): the posterior's existence is conditioned by the prior's non-existence after its existence—as in the parts of time, where the prior part must cease for the posterior to exist; (b) causal precedence (taqaddum bi-l-ʿilliyya): the posterior's existence depends on the prior's existence without the condition of the prior's non-existence—this includes the complete cause and incomplete causes (except parts of the effect). Nominal precedence is then divided into precedence by honor (sharaf) and by rank (rutba). The passage thus provides a systematic classification based on logical dependency.
