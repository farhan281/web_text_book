The Two Connections of Eternal Power and the Full Resolution
The author explains the two connections of eternal power and concludes that the problem is fully resolved.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the two connections (conceptual and temporal) of eternal power explain why its effects are not eternal.

Core Idea
Eternal power has a conceptual connection that does not necessitate effects, and a temporal connection that does.

Building on the essential distinction, this lesson examines how eternal power relates to the act. The author introduces two types of connection: a conceptual one in pre-eternity that does not cause the act, and a temporal one at the moment of creation that necessitates the act. This explains why the act does not exist eternally despite the power being eternal.

It is like a disposition or capacity, not an efficient cause.
3
Understand the temporal connection
The temporal connection (ta'alluq ḥādith) occurs at the moment of the act and necessitates its existence.
4
See the resolution
Combine the two connections to see why the eternalness of power does not make the act eternal.

Commanding the Impossible: The Ash'ari Defense
Refutes the claim that commanding the impossible is absurd and distinguishes two cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari response to the objection that commanding the impossible is absurd, and the key distinction that a command is valid if the opposite act is within one's power.

Core Idea
Commands are valid if the opposite act is within one's power.

This passage addresses a central objection from the Mu'tazilites against the Ash'ari view that commanding the impossible is permissible. The Mu'tazilites argue that if something is impossible, it cannot be commanded. The Ash'aris respond by pointing out that the command to believe is valid because the opposite—abandoning disbelief—is within the person's power, even if the act of believing itself is not. This nuanced distinction resolves the contradiction and clarifies the nature of divine commands.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Mu'tazili objection
Read the beginning of the passage where the Mu'tazili argument is presented: that the strongest excuse is the inability to perform the commanded act, and if one cannot do it, blame must be removed.
This objection would make the command to believe absurd for a disbeliever who cannot currently believe.
2
See the Ash'ari counter
The Ash'ari response uses a reductio: if the objection held, then commanding the disbeliever to believe would be invalid, but the community agrees it is valid. Therefore, the objection must be false.
The consensus of the community (ijma') is invoked as evidence.
3
Grasp the distinction
Focus on the distinction drawn: the abandonment of faith (i.e., not remaining in disbelief) is in the disbeliever's power, even if the attainment of faith is not. The command is enforced because the opposite act is possible.
The command is to abandon disbelief, which is possible, not to acquire faith directly.
4
Apply the general condition
The passage states the condition for a valid command: either the commanded act itself or its opposite must be within one's power. This is satisfied for faith (the opposite is possible) but not for creating substances and accidents (neither act nor opposite is possible).
This distinguishes between two types of impossible commands.

How Ash‘aris Classify Actions and the Scope of Power
Jurjani classifies actions into two types and states the Ash'ari rule on power's scope.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash‘ari classification of actions into those requiring an instrument and those that do not, and the principle that originated power only relates to acts within its own locus.

Core Idea
Ash‘ari power is limited to the agent's immediate act, not its external effects.

Jurjani lays out a foundational distinction in Ash‘ari theology: not all acts that appear to be caused by us are directly under our power. Some actions, like moving your hand, are within the locus of power, while others, like moving a stone by moving your hand, are external. The Ash‘ari rule is clear: originated power only attaches to what is within its own locus, not to external effects. This passage clarifies that distinction and explains why the stone's movement is not 'generated' by the hand's movement in the Ash‘ari sense.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to get the author's own flow.
Notice the technical terms: al-āla (instrument), al-maḥall (locus), al-mubāshara (directness).
2
Identify the two categories of actions
Look for the division: actions that do not need an instrument (like hand movement) and those that need an instrument (like stone movement via hand). Understand the concept of 'locus of power'.
The first category is 'subsisting in the locus', the second is 'external to the locus'.
3
Grasp the Ash‘ari rule
Find the sentence: 'وعند الأشاعرة أن القدرة الحادثة لا تتعلق بما في غير محلها'. This is the core doctrinal point – originated power does not relate to what is outside its locus.
This means you cannot directly cause something external; you only cause the action in your own limb.
4
Understand the gloss on generation (tawlīd)
Read the explanation of the stone example. Note that both movements occur directly (bi-l-mubāshara), but the stone's movement is not 'generated' from the hand's movement because it requires intention and grasping. The term 'muwallid' is defined as that which necessitates another act, intended or not.
This counters the idea of necessary causal generation between two events.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of the seventh objective (maqsad) concerning the nature of power (qudra) and its relation to the act and to opposites. The author begins by defining the power in question as 'the gathered strength' (al-quwwa al-mustajamma) that does not relate to both opposites simultaneously, because that would require their coexistence. Instead, it relates to each power (maqdur) individually, whether or not they are opposites, due to the differing conditions required for each act. The specific condition of each act determines its existence among those shared in that bare strength (al-quwwa al-mujarrada). For example, the intention (qasd) is a condition for that act's existence but not for others. The author then states that power is with the act because the existence of the act does not lag behind the complete agent (al-mu'aththir al-tamm). He then offers a reconciliation between the Ash'ari and Mu'tazili positions: al-Ash'ari meant by power the gathered strength complete with conditions of effect, hence ruling that it is with the act and does not relate to opposites; the Mu'tazila meant mere muscular strength, hence they allowed existence before the act and relation to opposites. This reconciliation is then critiqued in an appended discussion (wa fihi bahth). The critique argues that according to al-Ash'ari, the originated power (al-qudra al-haditha) is not effective, so he cannot have meant the gathered strength for conditions of effect. Also, interpreting it that way would make the term 'power' applied homonymously, but the response says the concept of gathered strength is common. The following glosses (qawlahu) further analyze the evidence, pointing out that al-Ash'ari's own argument implies the gathered strength is before the act, and the response clarifies that 'gathered strength for conditions of effect' means the strength that coincides with the act; the effect is intended initially. A final gloss addresses the objection that the originated power is not effective; the answer explains that the Imam (al-Razi) in his works explicitly considers the servant's power effective in one sense, and the investigation concludes that if by power we mean the effective power when it has all conditions, the act cannot be delayed, except metaphorically by custom. Thus, power with all aspects of the act's customary occurrence does not relate to opposites; without those aspects, it does.

The fourth type - the eighth objective: Incapacity is opposite to power.
[The eighth objective: Incapacity is opposite to power.]
(Incapacity is an accident) existing (opposite to power) by agreement of the Ash'aris and the majority of the Mu'tazila, contrary to Abu Hashim in his last opinions where he held that it (i.e., incapacity) is the absence of power and denied it being an existent meaning, even though he acknowledges the existence of accidents. And (contrary) to al-Asam, for he denied incapacity being an existent accident (in that he) denied accidents absolutely. (For us) in proving it to be existent (is the necessary distinction between the paralyzed and the prevented from the act; for every rational person finds in himself the distinction between being paralyzed and being prevented from standing while being sound. And it is only that in the paralyzed there is an existent attribute, which is incapacity, and this attribute is not in the prevented. (And for Abu Hashim) to make it (i.e., the necessary distinction) refer to the absence of power in the paralyzed and its presence in the prevented; for the prevented is capable in his opinion as has passed. Imam al-Razi said: There is no proof for incapacity being an existent attribute, and what is said that making incapacity an expression for the absence of power is not more likely than the reverse is weak, for we say both are possible, and if no proof is established for one of them...
His saying: (And it is only that in the paralyzed, etc.) For it is not because of the existence of power in one of them without the other; this does not contradict what has preceded that the difference between them is by the custom of creating the act and power in one of them without the other, for that is something in which some have slipped.
His saying: (And what is said, etc.) i.e., regarding the proof for the existence of incapacity. His saying: (weak) for we say, etc. If what is said is formulated thus: that one of them being existent and the other non-existent is a preferential weighting; then either both are non-existent, which is false because there is no opposition; so both are existent, hence incapacity is existent; the weakness is removed and it becomes strong, because safety is the absence of harm... non-existents.
His saying: (the necessary distinction) — it should not be said that that distinction possibly refers to the custom of creating the power with the act in the prevented not in the paralyzed, as has passed in the sixth objective as an answer to their argument that the prevented is capable by claiming necessity in the difference between the paralyzed and the restricted. For we say: The mentioned distinction exists regardless of custom and assuming creation all at once. If you say: Then the answer mentioned in that objective is not valid because the difference is realized by the existence of the existent attribute, namely incapacity in the paralyzed not the restricted. I say: Restricting the difference there to the custom is relative as we alerted you to it. Yes, if they argued by the distinction regardless of custom, then the answer would be that that distinction is with respect to the existence of incapacity in the paralyzed not the restricted, and we have also indicated that.
His saying: (And what is said, i.e., in the proof that incapacity is an existent attribute.)

Commentary
This passage addresses the eighth objective concerning incapacity ('ajz) as an attribute opposite to power (qudra). It begins by defining incapacity as an existent accident (arad) opposite to power, which is agreed upon by Ash'aris and most Mu'tazila, but disputed by Abu Hashim (who considered it the mere absence of power) and al-Asam (who denied accidents altogether). The proof (lana) for its existence is the necessary distinction (al-tafriqa al-daruriyya) that every rational person finds between a paralyzed person (zamin) and a person prevented from acting (mamnu') despite being physically sound. The difference is that the paralyzed has an existent attribute—incapacity—which the prevented lacks. Abu Hashim could respond that this distinction is due to the absence of power in the paralyzed and its presence in the prevented, as the prevented is capable in his view. Imam al-Razi is cited as stating there is no proof for incapacity being existent, and the argument that calling incapacity absence is no more likely than the reverse is weak because both are possible. However, the commentary (qawlahu) defends the proof: the distinction remains even apart from custom; if one argues that the difference is due to custom (as in the sixth objective), the response is that the distinction exists independently of custom. The text then shows how to strengthen the proof: if we consider that one attribute is existent and the other non-existent, then both cannot be non-existent because there would be no opposition; thus both are existent, so incapacity is existent (analogy with safety as absence of harm). The commentary also addresses an objection that the distinction might be due to custom, but the answer reiterates that the distinction holds even without considering custom, and the difference is precisely the existence of incapacity in the paralyzed.

Strengthening the Proof: Responding to Objections on Incapacity
Objections to the existent nature of incapacity and the strengthening of the proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objections to the existent nature of incapacity and how the responses, including the argument from opposition, strengthen the proof.

Core Idea
Objections to incapacity's existence are refuted by showing that both opposite attributes must be existent.

After presenting the primary proof, the passage considers objections from Abu Hashim, who reduces the distinction to the absence or presence of power, and from al-Razi, who questions the preference for existence over absence. The commentary defends the proof by strengthening it: if incapacity were non-existent, there would be no real opposition between power and incapacity; thus both must be existent attributes.

Guided Path
1
Review the objections
Read Abu Hashim's objection that the distinction is due to the absence of power in the paralyzed and its presence in the prevented, and al-Razi's doubt about proving existence over absence.
Also note al-Asam's wholesale denial of accidents.
2
Understand the responses
The commentary argues that the distinction holds even apart from custom (it is not due to customary creation of power). It also shows that if both attributes were non-existent, there would be no real opposition; hence both must be existent.
The analogy: safety is the absence of harm, but harm itself is an existent attribute.
3
Appreciate the strengthened proof
Consider how the opposition argument can be used to turn the tables: if the opponent says both are possible, the proof can be reformulated to show that it is more plausible that both are existent because non-existents do not truly oppose each other.

The Proof from Power Does Not Prove Universal Knowledge
Al-Jurjani comments on the base text's claim about power and knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the proof from power to knowledge does not establish that God knows every particular object of His power.

Core Idea
The proof only shows God knows and wills, not that He knows all particulars.

Al-Jurjani begins by addressing a common theological argument: since God is powerful, and power requires intention and knowledge, God must know all His actions. However, the commentator points out a crucial limitation. He argues that the proof only establishes that every powerful being is knowing and willing, not that every object of power is known and willed. This distinction is vital to avoid overextending the proof to claim universal knowledge of particulars.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how the commentator distinguishes between the general requirement of knowledge for a powerful agent and the specific claim that all objects of power are known.
2
Identify the two claims
Find the two key statements: 'every powerful being is knowing and willing' vs. 'everything connected to power is known and willed'. The commentator accepts the first but rejects the second.
3
Reflect on the warning
The commentator ends with 'so reflect, for feet have slipped therein'. Consider why this is a common error and how this clarification helps.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the base text. It begins by clarifying that power and will are not always dependent on knowledge, only in intentional actions. The commentator then addresses a proof used to establish God's knowledge: because He is powerful, and every powerful being is knowing (since power requires intention and will, which require knowledge). He argues that this proof only shows that every powerful being must know and will, not that every object of power is known and willed. So it does not prove universal knowledge. Next, he explains that actions not within the sleeper's power are natural, not acquired, since acquisition requires will and consciousness. He then discusses al-Amidi's point about the distinction between involuntary and voluntary movements, noting that some say consciousness resides in one part and sleep in another, but this is problematic because power is supposed to reside in the same locus as sleep. He clarifies al-Asfahani's view, reconciling it with a previous statement about perception: the lack of difference refers to perception, not to power. Finally, he analyzes the phrase about doubting equation, explaining that the doubt is about the distinction, not the equation itself.

Acquisition (Kasb) and the Sleeper's Actions
Al-Jurjani comments on the base text regarding actions not within a person's power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the concept of kasb and why actions during sleep are not within a person's power.

Core Idea
Acquisition requires will and consciousness, so involuntary actions are not acquired.

The commentator now turns to the nature of acquisition (kasb). Some actions, like those of a sleeping person, occur without will or consciousness. Are they within our power? Al-Jurjani explains that true acquisition requires intentionality and awareness. He also discusses a view by al-Amidi that consciousness resides in a part of the body, but rejects it because power must reside in the same locus as sleep, and that locus must have knowledge of the power's object.

Analyzing Doubt about Equating Perceptions
Al-Jurjani analyses the base text's phrase about doubt and equation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Jurjani's analysis of doubt regarding the equation of perceptions of the sleeper and the awake.

Core Idea
Casting doubt on equation implies doubt about the distinction, not the equation itself.

In this final part of the commentary, al-Jurjani addresses a subtle point about the phrase 'casting doubt on equating them' in the case of the awake. He clarifies that the intended meaning is that the doubt is about the distinction between the two states, not about the equation itself. He explains that the equation implies doubt about the distinction, and that taking it at face value would mean allowing the possibility of equality, which is far from reason. This careful linguistic analysis shows the commentator's precision.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on doubt
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice the commentator's interpretation of the phrase 'casting doubt on equating them'.
2
Distinguish between equation and distinction
The commentator says the doubt is not about the equation itself but about the distinction. Understand the difference: equation means treating two things as equal; distinction means recognizing a difference.
3
Understand the alternative interpretation
The commentator also considers the possibility of taking the phrase literally, which would mean the doubt is about the equation, but that would be far from reason because the equation is established. He prefers the first interpretation.
