The Mu'tazila's First Proof: Similarity Through External Relation
First proof of Mu'tazila: similarity via external relation of conformity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first Mu'tazilite argument that compound ignorance is similar to knowledge based on the nature of the relation of conformity.

Core Idea
The only difference between knowledge and compound ignorance is a relation, which is external to the essence.

The Mu'tazila argued that compound ignorance is not opposite to knowledge but similar. Their first proof focuses on the relation of each to its object. Knowledge conforms to reality, compound ignorance does not. Since this conformity is merely a relation external to the essence, the two share the same quiddity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the use of the word 'مماثلة'.
2
Identify the key claim
Find where the Mu'tazila state that compound ignorance is similar to knowledge, not opposite. Note why they say the impossibility of coexistence is due to similarity.
3
Understand the role of the relation
The proof says the only distinction is the relation of conformity. Why is this relation considered external to the essence?
Consider the premise that a relation is posterior to its relata.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
If the difference is only external, then knowledge and compound ignorance share the entire quiddity. What does this imply about their similarity?
This is a strong claim that other schools reject.

Commentary
This passage presents the Mu'tazila claim that compound ignorance is not an opposite of knowledge but is similar to it. They give two proofs. First, the only difference between knowledge and compound ignorance is that knowledge conforms to reality while compound ignorance does not; this difference is a mere relation, which is external to the essence, so the two share the same quiddity. Second, they give an example: a person believes Zayd is in the house all day; Zayd leaves at noon; the belief is one continuous thing, first true (knowledge) then false (compound ignorance). Since it changes without change of essence, it must be the same essence differing only by accident. Thus they are similar, not opposites. The commentary adds objections: the relation argument only pushes conformity outside the essence, but does not prove unity of essence; also, change from knowledge to ignorance does imply essence change if they are truly different. The glosses also discuss the meaning of 'impossible change.'

The Mu'tazila's Second Proof: The Continuous Belief Example
Second proof of Mu'tazila: example of continuous belief changing from knowledge to ignorance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second Mu'tazilite argument for the similarity between knowledge and compound ignorance using the example of a continuous belief that changes from true to false.

Core Idea
A belief can change from true to false without changing its essence, showing similarity.

The Mu'tazila's second proof is a vivid example: a person believes from morning to evening that Zayd is in the house. At noon, Zayd leaves, but the belief continues. Initially it is knowledge (true), later it becomes compound ignorance (false). Since the belief is one continuous entity, its essence must remain the same, only accidents change.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and translation carefully. Focus on the example and the reasoning that follows.
Note the phrase 'انقلب جهلاً مركباً'.
2
Understand the example
The example involves a person who believes Zayd is in the house all day. Zayd leaves at noon. The belief is one continuous thing.
3
Analyze the argument
The belief first conforms (knowledge), then does not (compound ignorance). Since it is one continuous entity, the essence must be the same; only the accident of conformity changes.
Contrast with true opposites, where change would require change of essence.
4
Draw the conclusion
Because the change is merely accidental, knowledge and compound ignorance are similar in essence.

Ash'ari Refutation of Similarity Between Knowledge and Compound Ignorance
Ash'ari response to Mu'tazila on similarity of knowledge and compound ignorance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Ash'ari school refutes the Mu'tazila's argument that compound ignorance is similar to knowledge, using two strategies: opposition by specific attributes and clarification of the impossibility of change of realities.

Core Idea
Ash'aris distinguish knowledge from compound ignorance by their essential attributes, not mere similarity.

The Mu'tazila had argued that compound ignorance is similar to knowledge, implying that both could be attributed to Allah. The Ash'ari school responds on two fronts: first by showing that their most specific attributes differ, and second by refining the concept of impossible change of realities. This passage presents both arguments concisely, though it breaks off mid-sentence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation once to get the overall flow. Pay attention to where the Ash'ari response begins and the two distinct arguments.
The text ends abruptly with "ويحتمل"—do not expect a conclusion here.
2
Identify the two response strategies
The Ashab use two methods: (1) opposition (mu'arada) based on most specific attributes, and (2) resolving the Mu'tazila's proof by clarifying what change of realities is impossible.
3
Analyze the attribute argument
Al-Amidi explains that similarity requires sharing the most specific attributes. Knowledge has the attribute of being attained by sound reasoning, which compound ignorance lacks, so they cannot be similar.
The example of the muqallid's belief is used to illustrate a case where belief matches reality but is still not true knowledge.
4
Understand the change-of-realities response
The Ash'aris argue that the impossible change is from one category (necessary, possible, impossible) to another, not that two different things cannot share some attributes. This undermines the Mu'tazila's proof.
This part is incomplete in the source.
5
Reflect on the overall refutation
Consider how these two arguments collectively show that knowledge and compound ignorance are essentially different, not just contingently.

And the Ashab said in their response by way of opposition: 'Conformity and non-conformity are the most specific of their attributes' – i.e., the attributes of knowledge and compound ignorance – 'so difference in them, i.e., in the most specific attributes, necessitates difference in essence' as has passed: similar things share in the most specific attributes of the soul. Learning Objective

Knowledge permanence sparks a triple disagreement: Ash'aris vs Mu'tazila, then al-Jubba'i vs Abu Hashim.

This passage from al-Mawaqif presents a classic kalam dispute: does knowledge remain after it occurs? The Ash'aris deny permanence for all accidents. The Mu'tazila agree that necessary knowledge remains, but split over acquired knowledge. Al-Jubba'i argues that acquired knowledge cannot remain, while his son Abu Hashim insists it does. Let’s examine their reasoning.

The Hamdani View on Power and Its Refutation
Dialectical refutation of al-Hamdani's definition of power.

Core Idea

In this passage, the Mu'tazili scholar al-Hamdani proposes that we can identify power (qudra) by observing whether an act is feasible (ta'ti) for a particular agent. Al-Hamdani defines power as the feasibility of the act. Understand what 'feasibility' means in this context.
3

Al-Jubba'i's Exceptions to the Continuity of Rest
Al-Jurjani explains al-Jubba'i's two exceptions to the continuity of rest and the resulting nickname for Abu Hashim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Jubba'i's two exceptions to the continuity of rest and why Abu Hashim was forced to accept sin without power, leading to his nickname 'al-Dhihnī'.

Core Idea
Rest is continuous except in two cases: falling heavy bodies and commanded rest for the living.

The Mu'tazila agreed on most points about the nature of rest, but al-Jubba'i introduced two crucial exceptions. Understanding these exceptions reveals a deeper principle about potentiality and divine command. This passage from al-Jurjani's Sharh al-Mawaqif lays out the argument and its surprising consequence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage together with its English translation. Note that the passage describes two exceptions to the general rule that rest continues (yabqā).

2
Understand the first exception
Identify the first exception: when a heavy body falls and God holds it without support. Al-Jubba'i argues that rest must be renewed because the newly occurring (al-ṭāri' al-ḥādith) is stronger than the remaining (al-bāqī).
This exception applies to inanimate bodies.
3
Understand the second exception
Identify the second exception: the rest that is within the power (maqdūr) of a living being. It must be renewed because power (qudra) only brings about new events (ījād), not continuance.
If rest were continuous, a commanded act of rest could not be a potential act, leading to absurdity.
4

Al-Jubba'i's argument forced Abu Hashim to concede that in the second exception, the agent is sinful and punishable despite lacking power over motion. This earned him the nickname 'al-Dhihnī' (the mentalist).
The nickname reflects a retraction from the principle that sin depends on a potential act.

Commentary
This passage presents the second Mu'tazilite disagreement concerning whether rest (sukūn) continues (yabqā) or is renewed (yatajaddad). It states that Abu Hashim and most Mu'tazila affirm the continuation of rest without qualification, but al-Jubba'i and his followers make an exception for two cases. The first exception: when a heavy body falls due to its accumulated dependencies (i‘timādāt) and God holds it up without anything supporting it, the rest must be renewed because al-Jubba'i holds that a newly occurring accident is stronger than a remaining one; if rest were continuous, the body would fall. This led to Abu Hashim being called 'al-Dhihnī' (the mentalist) either because he retreated from the principle that reward and punishment depend on a potential act, or because he posited sin as a mental perception. The commentary explains each gloss: the sin and punishment are for not moving despite inability; the explanation of why there is no power over the opposite; and the critique that this commits Abu Hashim to affirming sensation of all particular qualities like good and bad, which is implausible.

Abu Hashim's Refutation: Motion as Unperceived Accident of Being
Abu Hashim's argument and counterexamples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Abu Hashim's argument that motion and rest are not perceived by the senses because they are identical to the accident of being (kawn), which is only known through its particularity, and the examples he uses to support this.

Core Idea
If motion is just an accident of being in a place, we cannot sense it directly, only infer it.

In response to al-Jubba'i, Abu Hashim offers a sophisticated argument: motion is really the accident of being (kawn) in one place after having been in another, and the same applies to rest. Since kawn is not perceived by the senses—perception requires grasping the particularity of the perceived, which is not possible with constantly changing places—motion and rest are not directly sensed. He provides powerful examples, such as the ship passenger who does not perceive the ship's motion, and the sleeper who does not notice a change in his location upon waking.

Guided Path
1
Understand the core argument
Abu Hashim argues that motion is identical to the accident of being (kawn) in a place after having been elsewhere, and rest is the same in the next moment. Kawn is not perceived by the senses because perception requires grasping particularity.
This is a key move: reducing motion to a non-sensible accident.
2
Analyze the ship passenger example
A ship passenger on a smooth ride does not perceive the ship's motion or the shore's stillness because he does not detect the changing particularities of the accidents of being.
This illustrates the lack of direct perception.
3
Consider the sleeper and falling person examples
A person moved in sleep does not perceive the change in his accident of being upon waking; similarly, a falling person whose places change rapidly does not notice the difference. This supports the claim that kawn is not sensed.
These examples show that change of location can go unnoticed.
4
Reflect on the color contrast
Abu Hashim contrasts with color: if someone's color changes in sleep, they perceive it immediately. This highlights that kawn is not like color; it is not directly sensed.
The contrast reinforces the non-sensible nature of kawn.

The Mutakallimun Who Denied Substantial Change in Elements
Some mutakallimun deny form change, assert one element.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Some early mutakallimun believed that all elements are one substance, only changing in qualities.

This passage presents a distinctive doctrine held by some mutakallimun: they denied that substances undergo genuine generation or corruption, arguing instead that changes are merely in qualities. They further proposed that all elements originate from a single primal element, with others formed through condensation or rarefaction. Understanding this view helps illuminate the diversity of kalam positions on physical change.

Commentary
This passage is a survey of Mu'tazilite positions on the minimal composition of a body from indivisible atoms (jawahir fard). All Mu'tazilites agree that the body is composed of atoms, but they disagree on how many atoms are minimally required. The passage lists three views: (1) al-Nazzam's view of infinite parts (which will be refuted later). The author then states the 'truth' (al-haqq): a body can be constituted from only four parts, arranged as two parts stacked with a third and then a fourth above, achieving all three dimensions. Refuting Faulty Definitions of Body
Criticizes three alternative definitions of body from specific sects and shows their linguistic and logical flaws.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why three alternative definitions of body (self-subsistent, existent, thing) are rejected on logical and linguistic grounds.

Core Idea
Body (jism) must be defined by composition, not by generic attributes.

In this passage, the author points out that some theologians defined body too broadly. He refutes each definition by showing it includes non-bodies like God, atoms, and accidents. Then he adds a linguistic point: the Arabic word 'jism' implies bulk and composition, which these definitions miss.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three definitions
Read the passage and note the three definitions proposed: self-subsistent by al-Salihiyya, existent by some Karamiyya, and thing by Hisham. Also note the proponents.
These are all abstract, non-committal terms.
2
Understand the logical counterexamples
For each definition, see why it is too broad: self-subsistent includes God and atom; existent includes God, atom, and accident; thing includes everything.
The refutation uses counterexamples that are covered by the definition but are not bodies.
3
Learn the linguistic argument
The author cites the comparative 'ajsam' (more jism) meaning greater bulk and composition. This shows that the word 'jism' in Arabic inherently implies composition and extension, which the proposed definitions lack.
This is a strong argument from language, a key resource in kalam.
4
Reflect on the importance of precise definitions
Consider why theologians argued over definitions: a vague definition leads to errors in reasoning about God and creation. This passage shows how to test a definition by checking its scope against clear examples.

Then he pointed to the falseness of definitions reported from some theologians and said: 'And such is the saying of al-Salihiyya from the Mu'tazilites in defining body: it is the self-subsistent; and the saying of some Karamiyya: it is the existent; and the saying of Hisham: it is the thing. Commentary
This passage refutes three faulty definitions of body proposed by certain theologians. The first is from al-Salihiyya (a Mu'tazilite subsect) who define body as 'al-qa'im bi-nafsihi' (self-subsistent). The author counters that this applies to God and to the individual atom, which are not bodies. The second is from some Karamiyya who define body as 'al-mawjud' (the existent); but both God and the atom exist, and accidents also exist, so it is too broad. The third is from Hisham (likely Hisham ibn al-Hakam) who defines body as 'al-shay' (the thing); this applies to everything including bodies, atoms, accidents, etc. The author then adds a linguistic argument: the Arabic word 'jism' connotes composition and bulk, as seen in the comparative 'ajsam' meaning greater in size and composition. These definitions do not capture that linguistic sense, so they are invalid on both logical and linguistic grounds.
