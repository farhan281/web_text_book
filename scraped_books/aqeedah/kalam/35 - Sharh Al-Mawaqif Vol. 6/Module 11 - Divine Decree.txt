Human Will vs. God's Will – The Objection and Answer
Objection-and-answer about human will opposing God's will, with two interpretations of the verse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection that human will cannot oppose God's will is answered through two different interpretations of the Quranic verse.

Core Idea
The verse can be read as God willing your willing, not necessarily the same thing.

This passage addresses a common objection in discussions of divine predestination: if God wills all things, how can a human will something contrary? The objector cites a Quranic verse to prove impossibility. The answer shows that the verse's meaning is not fixed—one interpretation removes the contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Identify the claim that the servant willing the opposite of God's will is impossible based on the verse. Notice the verse quoted.
The objection relies on a specific reading of the verse.
2
Understand the first interpretation
The answer presents two possible meanings of the verse. First, if it means you will only what God wills, then opposition is impossible. See why that reading leads to the objection.
3
Learn the second interpretation
If the verse means you only will when God wills your willing itself, then God can will one thing while the servant wills another. Opposition is possible without contradiction.
This is the key solution.
4
Note the rejection of a deterministic view
The author warns against interpreting the servant's will as a necessary cause that always produces its object. That would make opposition impossible but contradicts language and revelation.
Implicitly, human will is not an efficient cause but an attribute.
5
Reflect on the logical structure
The passage ends by noting that the invalidity of a second necessary consequence (both wills not occurring) can be understood from the third branch (both beings incapable). This may be obscure, but it shows the depth of Ash'ari reasoning.
You may skip this in first reading.

Commentary
This passage addresses an objection: the assumption that the servant's will can oppose God's will is impossible because the Quran says 'You do not will except that God wills.' The answer provides two interpretations of the verse. Firstly, it could mean that whatever you will, God wills that same thing. In that case, opposing wills is indeed impossible. Secondly, it could mean that your act of willing itself is willed by God, i.e., God wills your willing. In this case, it is possible for God to will one thing (e.g., Zayd's movement) while the servant wills the opposite (e.g., stillness). The servant fails to get what he wills, but there is no logical contradiction. The only problematic interpretation is if one takes the servant's will as a necessary cause that brings about its object (a deterministic view), but that is rejected as contrary to language and revelation. The passage also notes that the invalidity of the second possible consequence (both not occurring) can be understood from the third branch (both being incapable).

The Common-Sense Proof of Human Power
Refutes Jahm by distinguishing voluntary from involuntary action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ash'ari scholars refute Jahm's denial of human power using the intuitive difference between voluntary and involuntary action.

Core Idea
Voluntary action proves human power, even if that power is not independently effective.

Jahm ibn Safwan denied that humans possess any power, claiming that attributing power to humans would conflict with God's power. The Ash'ari response is that this is extremism. By examining the obvious difference between a person climbing a mountain by choice and falling accidentally, we see that the first has an attribute called power. This lesson explains that common-sense distinction and how it refutes Jahm's position.

Guided Path
1
Understand Jahm's denial
Jahm ibn Safwan argued that if humans had power, it would conflict with God's power over the same act. He therefore negated human power entirely.
This is the background to the refutation.
2
Read the passage on voluntary vs. involuntary
The passage gives the example of a person ascending by choice and one falling involuntarily. Note the difference: the first has an attribute called power, the second does not.
3
Identify the solution to the conflict
The conflict is resolved by noting that human power does not actually create the act; it is merely a locus of acquisition (kasb). God's power is the sole creator.
This is the Ash'ari doctrine of kasb.
4
Reflect on why Jahm's view is called extremism
The passage calls Jahm's denial 'extremism' because it contradicts intuition. Understand why the middle path (kasb) is the truth.

Commentary
This passage refutes Jahm's denial of human power. It begins by noting that Jahm used the same proof (that two powers over one act lead to conflict) to negate human power entirely. Then several glosses clarify aspects of the earlier argument. The main refutation follows: Jahm's position is extremism, because common sense distinguishes voluntary action (ascent) from involuntary (fall). The voluntary agent has an attribute called power, even if its effect is not independent. The problem of God's power conflicting with human power is solved by the Ash'ari doctrine that human power does not actually create the act; it is merely an acquired attribute (kasb). If Jahm objects that power by definition is effective and since human power is ineffective it is not power, the response is that it is a verbal dispute—the attribute is real and called power, and effect is a concomitant that can be absent, as in originated power according to the Ash'aris.

The Definition of Power: A Verbal Dispute?
Addresses Jahm's objection that power is effective attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Jahm's objection that power must be effective reduces to a verbal dispute, and how Ash'ari scholars define power as a real attribute even when effect is absent.

Core Idea
Power is a real attribute; its effect is a concomitant that may be absent.

After the common-sense proof, Jahm might object: if power by definition is effective, and human power is not effective, then it is not power. The Ash'ari response is that this is merely a dispute over names. The attribute is real and known by intuition; calling it power is justified. Furthermore, effect is not essential to power but a concomitant that can be separated, as in the case of originated power. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Understand Jahm's objection
Jahm defines power as the effective attribute. Since human power lacks effect, he denies it is power. Note his definition.
2
Read the Ash'ari response
The passage replies that the attribute is real—known by intuition—and we call it power. If Jahm acknowledges the attribute but disputes the name, it is a verbal dispute.
3
Consider the deeper claim
If Jahm insists that power's essence is effectiveness, the Ash'ari response is that effect is a concomitant (tabi') that may be absent, as in originated power.
This is a key Ash'ari distinction.
4
Reflect on the implications
This shows that the real disagreement is about the definition of power, not about the existence of the attribute. Ash'aris maintain a middle position.

For we affirm for the servant the essence of the attribute known by intuition and call it power. So if Jahm acknowledges that attribute and says it is not power because of its lack of effect, his dispute with us is over the application of the word power to that attribute, and it is a verbal investigation. And if he says: The reality and essence of power is that it is an effective attribute, we deny that, because effect is among the concomitants of power, and it may be separated from it as in originated power according to us.

Commentary
This passage refutes Jahm's denial of human power. It begins by noting that Jahm used the same proof (that two powers over one act lead to conflict) to negate human power entirely. Then several glosses clarify aspects of the earlier argument. The main refutation follows: Jahm's position is extremism, because common sense distinguishes voluntary action (ascent) from involuntary (fall). The voluntary agent has an attribute called power, even if its effect is not independent. The problem of God's power conflicting with human power is solved by the Ash'ari doctrine that human power does not actually create the act; it is merely an acquired attribute (kasb). If Jahm objects that power by definition is effective and since human power is ineffective it is not power, the response is that it is a verbal dispute—the attribute is real and called power, and effect is a concomitant that can be absent, as in originated power according to the Ash'aris.

The Fourth Argument: Power with the Act Makes Obligation Impossible
The author presents the fourth argument that if power is only with the act, obligation becomes impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the view that power is only with the act leads to the impossibility of obligation, as argued in the fourth wajh.

Core Idea
If power is only with the act, no one can be obligated to act.

This passage presents a powerful reductio ad absurdum against the idea that power is simultaneous with the act. The author shows that if power is not prior, then obligation becomes meaningless—the unbeliever cannot be commanded to believe, and sin becomes impossible. Furthermore, the strongest excuse (lack of power) would exonerate everyone, contradicting the consensus of the Muslim community.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premise
Read the opening phrase: 'If power is with the act, not before it.' This is the hypothesis being refuted.
The phrase 'القدرة مع الفعل' is the key.
2
Trace the first consequence
Notice that the author applies the premise to the unbeliever: he cannot be obligated to believe because faith is not within his power before the act.
3
Trace the second consequence
The author then argues that sin (disobedience) becomes inconceivable for anyone, because without prior power there is no obligation.
This is a universal implication.
4
Understand the excuse argument
The strongest excuse for avoiding punishment is that the act is not within one's power. If power is only with the act, then the person is never able beforehand, so he is always excused.
The author calls this the 'strongest excuse' (أقوى أعذار المكلف).
5
Recognize the consensus refutation
The conclusion that the person should be excused is false by the consensus of the ummah (ijmā‘), which holds that the unbeliever is blameworthy.
This shows the original premise is false.

The Distinction in Obligation: Power Over the Opposite
The author defends the obligation of the unbeliever by distinguishing between the power to leave faith and the inability to create substances.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari distinction that allows obligation of the unbeliever to faith based on the power to leave unbelief, while rejecting obligation to create substances.

Core Idea
Obligation is possible when the opposite of the act is within one's power.

In this passage, the author responds to an objection that if the act of faith is not within the unbeliever's power, then consistency would require allowing obligation to create substances, which is absurd. He provides a crucial distinction: while the act of faith itself is not in his power, the opposite—abandoning unbelief—is. This distinction preserves the rationality of religious obligation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the objector's claim: if obligation to an impossible act (faith) is allowed, then obligation to create substances should also be allowed. See why this is presented as a reductio ad absurdum.
The objector assumes that power over the act itself is necessary for obligation.
2
Identify the author's distinction
Notice the key difference: abandonment of faith (tark al-īmān) is within the unbeliever's power, whereas the non-existence of substances is not. This is the linchpin of the response.
3
Grasp the general condition for obligation
The author states the Ash'ari condition: the thing obligated must itself be connected to power, or its opposite must be connected to power. Understand that 'connected to power' means the agent can bring it about.
This condition avoids Taklīf al-Muḥāl (obligation of the impossible).
4
Apply the condition to the case
See how the condition applies to faith: the opposite of faith (unbelief) is within the unbeliever's power, so obligation is valid. Compare with creating substances, where neither the act nor its opposite is in his power.
