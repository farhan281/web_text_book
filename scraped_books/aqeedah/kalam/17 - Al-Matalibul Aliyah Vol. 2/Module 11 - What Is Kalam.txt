First Interpretation of Jawhar: The Indivisible Extended Atom
First of four interpretations of jawhar, with ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Allah cannot be described as a jawhar according to the first interpretation (the indivisible extended atom).

Core Idea
Allah is not a material body, so He cannot be a jawhar in this sense.

The term 'jawhar' has multiple meanings. The first interpretation defines it as an extended entity that does not admit division, associated with the concept of the indivisible atom. Since Allah cannot be extended or spatial, the author argues that this meaning cannot apply to Him.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Focus on the definition and the logical conclusion.
2
Identify the two premises
The argument has two premises: (1) jawhar is defined as indivisible extended; (2) Allah is not extended. Find them in the text.
The second premise is stated as 'we have demonstrated that He cannot be extended'.
3
Understand the conclusion
From the two premises, the necessary conclusion is that Allah is not a jawhar. Note how the author uses 'وجب القطع' to indicate certainty.
4
Reflect on the significance
Ask yourself: why is it important to clarify that Allah is not a material substance? This prevents anthropomorphism.
This connects to tanzih (transcendence).

Know: the term 'jawhar' may be mentioned and intended by it one of four matters. The first interpretation: that which is extended (mutahayyiz) and does not admit division, and this is according to the one who affirms the indivisible atom (jawhar fard). And since we have demonstrated that He, exalted, is impossible to be extended, it is necessary to decide that He is not a jawhar. The second interpretation: it is said: jawhar is the essence (dhat) that admits the succession of opposite attributes. This is only conceivable if the essence is receptive to successive renewed attributes. And since we have demonstrated that the succession of attributes upon the essence of Allah, exalted, is impossible, it is impossible that He be a jawhar in this respect. The third interpretation of jawhar: it is the quiddity (mahiyya) that, when it exists in external entities, is not in a location. This concept applies only to a thing whose quiddity is other than its existence. Since the doctrine of Abu 'Ali is that the existence of Allah, exalted, is identical to His quiddity and not an attribute distinct from His quiddity, it is impossible that He, exalted, be a jawhar in this respect. But according to us, since jawhar is that the existence of Allah, exalted, is an attribute subsisting in His specific essence, He is a jawhar in this respect. The fourth interpretation of jawhar: it is the existent that is independent of a substrate in which it inheres. And the most deserving of things to be a jawhar according to this interpretation is Allah, exalted, because He is independent of the substrate in which He would inhere, of the space which He would occupy, of the agent that would bring Him into existence, and of the form that would complete Him. So He is the most deserving of things to be a jawhar in this sense.

Commentary
This passage provides a classification of the term 'jawhar' (substance) into four distinct interpretations, each followed by an examination of whether it applies to Allah. The first interpretation considers jawhar as an extended entity that does not admit division, associated with the atomist view. Since Allah cannot be extended, this does not apply. The second interpretation defines jawhar as an essence that receives successive opposite attributes, but Allah's attributes do not change, so it is inapplicable. The third interpretation defines it as a quiddity that exists not in a location, applicable only to things whose essence and existence are distinct. According to the Mu'tazili Abu 'Ali (al-Jubba'i), Allah's essence is identical to His existence, so this fails; but according to the author's own school (Ash'ari), Allah's existence is an attribute distinct from His essence, so He is a jawhar in this sense. The fourth interpretation defines jawhar as the existent independent of any substrate, and Allah is the most deserving of this because He is independent of substrate, space, agent, and form. Thus, only the third (under Ash'ari view) and fourth interpretations allow the term for Allah.

Correction: Allah is not a body and does not occupy space, so He cannot be described as an extended indivisible entity. This interpretation is explicitly negated.

Key Takeaway
The first interpretation defines jawhar as an indivisible extended body, which cannot apply to Allah because He is not extended.

Second Interpretation: Essence Receptive to Opposite Attributes
Second interpretation of jawhar, with ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Allah cannot be a jawhar according to the second interpretation, which involves receptivity to changing attributes.

Core Idea
Allah's attributes are eternal and unchanging, so He cannot admit successive opposite attributes.

The second interpretation defines jawhar as an essence (dhat) that can receive a succession of opposite attributes, like a substance that can be white then black. Since Allah's attributes are eternal and do not change, this definition cannot apply to Him.

Guided Path
1
Read the second interpretation
Read the Arabic and translation for the second interpretation. Note the definition: jawhar is an essence capable of receiving sequential opposite attributes.
2
Identify the condition
The definition only makes sense if the essence can actually receive changing attributes. Find where the author states this condition.
The phrase 'إنما يعقل ثبوته إذا كانت الذات قابلة للصفات المتجددة المتعاقبة' indicates the condition.
3
Apply the proof
The author says it is impossible for Allah's essence to have successive attributes. Understand why: Allah's attributes are eternal and do not change.
4
Draw the conclusion
Because the condition is not met, Allah is not a jawhar in this sense.

Third Interpretation: Quiddity Not in a Location
Third interpretation with contrasting rulings from two schools.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the third interpretation of jawhar applies differently depending on whether existence is identical to essence or not.

Core Idea
Jawhar as quiddity not in a location applies to Allah only if His existence is distinct from His essence.

The third interpretation defines jawhar as a quiddity (mahiyya) that exists without being in a location. This applies to things whose essence is distinct from their existence. The passage contrasts the Mu'tazili view of Abu 'Ali al-Jubba'i with the Ash'ari view to determine whether Allah fits this definition.

Guided Path
1
Read the third interpretation
Read the Arabic and translation. Note the definition: jawhar is a quiddity that when existing is not in a place.
2
Identify the condition
This concept applies only to things whose quiddity is other than their existence. Find this condition in the text.
The phrase 'إنما يصدق على الشيء الذي تكون ماهيته غير وجوده' indicates the condition.
3
Understand the two school positions
The author presents two views: Abu 'Ali says Allah's existence is identical to His quiddity, so not jawhar. The Ash'ari view says existence is an attribute distinct from quiddity, so Allah is a jawhar. Compare them.
4
Reflect on the outcome
Note that the ruling depends on one's theological position regarding the relationship between essence and existence.

Fourth Interpretation: The Self-Sufficient Existent
Fourth interpretation and its application to Allah as the most deserving.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Allah is the most deserving of being called 'jawhar' according to the fourth interpretation.

Core Idea
Allah is the most independent being, so He is most deserving of the title 'jawhar' in this sense.

The fourth and final interpretation defines jawhar as an existent that is independent of any substrate in which it inheres. The author argues that Allah is the most deserving of this description because He depends on nothing—neither a substrate, nor space, nor an agent, nor a form.

Guided Path
1
Read the fourth interpretation
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice the definition: jawhar is an existent independent of a substrate.
2
Identify the four aspects of independence
The passage lists four things Allah is independent from: substrate, space, agent, and form. List them in your notes.
These are: المحل (substrate), الحيز (space), الفاعل (agent), الصورة (form).
3
Understand why Allah is most deserving
The author uses the term 'أحق' (most deserving) because Allah's independence is absolute, unlike any creature.
4
Reflect on the overall lesson
Consider how this positive description of Allah as jawhar contrasts with the negative rulings of the first two interpretations.

اعلم : أن الجوهر قد يذكر ويراد به أحد أمور أربعة : التفسير الأول : المتحيز الذي لا يقبل القسمة ، وهذا على قول من يثبت الجوهر الفرد ، ولما دللنا على أنه تعالى يمتنع أن يكون متحيزا ، وجب القطع بأنه ليس بجوهر . والتفسير الثاني : أن يقال : الجوهر هو الذات القابلة لتوارد الصفات المتضادة عليه ، وهذا إنما يعقل ثبوته إذا كانت الذات قابلة للصفات المتجددة المتعاقبة ، ولما دللنا على أن تعاقب الصفات على ذات الله تعالى محال ، امتنع أن يكون جوهرا ، بهذا الوجه . والتفسير الثالث للجوهر : إنه الماهية التي إذا وجدت في الأعيان كانت لا في موضع . وهذا المفهوم إنما يصدق على الشيء الذي تكون ماهيته غير وجوده ولما كان مذهب الشيخ أبي علي : أن وجود الله ـ تعالى ـ نفس ماهيته وليس صفة مغايرة لماهيته ، امتنع كونه تعالى جوهرا بهذا الوجه ، وأما عندنا فلما كان الجوهر ، هو أن وجود الله تعالى صفة قائمة بذاته المخصوصة كان جوهرا بهذا الوجه . والتفسير الرابع للجوهر : إنه الموجود والغني عن محل يحل فيه ، وأحق الأشياء بأن يكون جوهرا بهذا التفسير هو الله تعالى لأنه غني عن المحل الذي يحل فيه وعن الحيز الذي يحصل فيه ، وعن الفاعل الذي يوجده ، وعن الصورة التي تتممه ، فكان أحق الأشياء بالجوهرية بهذا هو الله تعالى
Know: the term 'jawhar' may be mentioned and intended by it one of four matters. The first interpretation: that which is extended (mutahayyiz) and does not admit division, and this is according to the one who affirms the indivisible atom (jawhar fard). And since we have demonstrated that He, exalted, is impossible to be extended, it is necessary to decide that He is not a jawhar. The second interpretation: it is said: jawhar is the essence (dhat) that admits the succession of opposite attributes. This is only conceivable if the essence is receptive to successive renewed attributes. And since we have demonstrated that the succession of attributes upon the essence of Allah, exalted, is impossible, it is impossible that He be a jawhar in this respect. The third interpretation of jawhar: it is the quiddity (mahiyya) that, when it exists in external entities, is not in a location. This concept applies only to a thing whose quiddity is other than its existence. Since the doctrine of Abu 'Ali is that the existence of Allah, exalted, is identical to His quiddity and not an attribute distinct from His quiddity, it is impossible that He, exalted, be a jawhar in this respect. But according to us, since jawhar is that the existence of Allah, exalted, is an attribute subsisting in His specific essence, He is a jawhar in this respect. The fourth interpretation of jawhar: it is the existent that is independent of a substrate in which it inheres. And the most deserving of things to be a jawhar according to this interpretation is Allah, exalted, because He is independent of the substrate in which He would inhere, of the space which He would occupy, of the agent that would bring Him into existence, and of the form that would complete Him. So He is the most deserving of things to be a jawhar in this sense.

Commentary
This passage provides a classification of the term 'jawhar' (substance) into four distinct interpretations, each followed by an examination of whether it applies to Allah. The first interpretation considers jawhar as an extended entity that does not admit division, associated with the atomist view. Since Allah cannot be extended, this does not apply. The second interpretation defines jawhar as an essence that receives successive opposite attributes, but Allah's attributes do not change, so it is inapplicable. The third interpretation defines it as a quiddity that exists not in a location, applicable only to things whose essence and existence are distinct. According to the Mu'tazili Abu 'Ali (al-Jubba'i), Allah's essence is identical to His existence, so this fails; but according to the author's own school (Ash'ari), Allah's existence is an attribute distinct from His essence, so He is a jawhar in this sense. The fourth interpretation defines jawhar as the existent independent of any substrate, and Allah is the most deserving of this because He is independent of substrate, space, agent, and form. Thus, only the third (under Ash'ari view) and fourth interpretations allow the term for Allah.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some might think that since 'jawhar' implies independence, Allah is only called jawhar in a metaphorical sense.
Correction: The author uses the term 'أحق' (most deserving), indicating that the term applies to Allah in a real and perfect sense, not metaphorically.
