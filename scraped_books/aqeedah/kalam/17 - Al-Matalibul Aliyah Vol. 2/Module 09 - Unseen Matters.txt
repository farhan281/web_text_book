Seventh Shubha: Scriptural Evidence for God Being Above
Opponents cite all prophetic books and the Qur'an for God being above.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the seventh shubha presented by opponents, which uses scriptural evidence to argue that God is spatially above.

Core Idea
Opponents claim all scriptures show God is above, especially the Qur'an's explicit mention of Him on the Throne.

This lesson presents the seventh doubt raised against the Ash'ari understanding of God's transcendence. Opponents appeal to the consistent message of all prophetic books, and specifically to the Qur'anic verse stating that God is on the Throne. We will analyze this claim and prepare to respond to it in subsequent lessons.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the translation. Note the precise wording of the doubt.
The phrase 'explicitly' (صريح اللفظ) is key.
2
Identify the two layers of evidence
Notice that the doubt first mentions all prophetic books, then specifically the Qur'an. These are two arguments combined.
3
Reflect on the claim of explicitness
Consider why opponents consider the Qur'anic wording explicit. What does 'istiwa' imply to them?
The Ash'ari response will reinterpret this without spatial location.
4
Prepare for the Ash'ari response
Think about how a theologian might respond while maintaining scriptural integrity. This prepares for the coming refutation.

الشبهة السابعة : إن جميع كتب جميع الأنبياء والرسل عليهم السلام مملوءة من كونه في جهة فوق . أما القرآن فقد جاء فيه كونه على العرش بصريح اللفظ .
The seventh doubt: All the books of all the prophets and messengers (peace be upon them) are filled with Him being in the direction of above. As for the Qur'an, it has come with Him being on the Throne explicitly.

Commentary
This passage presents the seventh shubha, which appeals to scriptural authority. Opponents claim that all revealed books are filled with descriptions of God being in the direction of above. Specifically, the Qur'an explicitly states that God is on the Throne (al-'arsh). This literal wording is presented as evidence that God is spatially located above.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Scriptural literalism is the only valid interpretation of divine attributes.
Correction: Ash'aris interpret such verses metaphorically or without spatial location, as God is transcendent.

Checkpoint
What specific evidence from the Qur'an do opponents cite in the seventh shubha?
A) 'He is the First and the Last.'
B) 'He is on the Throne' explicitly.
C) 'There is nothing like unto Him.'
D) 'He is with you wherever you are.'

Key Takeaway
The seventh shubha argues from scripture, especially the Qur'an's explicit mention of God on the Throne, to support the view that God is spatially above.

Does this lesson's presentation of this doubt as a 'shubha' make you stop and question whether your own understanding might be shaped by literal assumptions?

Third Premise: The Locus of Ru'ya
Author examines the locus of ru'ya, favoring the soul over the eye.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third premise concerning the locus of ru'ya and why the author favors the soul over the physical eye.

Core Idea
If ru'ya occurs, its locus is the soul, not the eye.

In the third premise, the author addresses where the vision of Allah would take place if it were possible. He presents two possibilities: the physical eye or the substance of the rational soul. This lesson explores his reasoning for favoring the soul.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic text and its translation. Notice the two options presented and the author's conclusion.
2
Understand the terms
Identify key terms: al-mahall (locus), al-'ayn (eye), al-hadaqa (pupil), jawhar al-nafs al-natiqa (substance of the rational soul). Consider why these are distinct.
The eye is a physical organ; the soul is a non-physical substance.
3
Reflect on the reasoning
The author says the eye is 'extremely unlikely' while the soul is 'closer to reason'. Think about why that might be — is it because the perception of God is too noble for a physical organ?
This prepares for the proofs to come.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
المقدمة الثالثة : إن رؤية الله تعالى بالتفسير المذكور بتقدير أن تحصل فمحلها هو هذه العين والحدقة أم جوهر النفس ؟ والأول كالمستبعد جدا وأما أن محل ذلك الإدراك الشريف هو جوهر النفس الناطقة فهذا أقرب إلى العقل بوجوه . فهذه مقدمات خصناها قبل الشروع في ذكر الدلائل .
The third premise: if vision of God, according to the aforementioned interpretation, were to occur, is its locus the eye and pupil, or the substance of the soul? The first is extremely unlikely. As for the locus of that noble perception being the substance of the rational soul, this is more reasonable to the mind for several aspects. So these are the premises we have specified before beginning to mention the proofs.
Source pages p. 82

Commentary
This passage is the third premise (al-muqaddima al-thalitha). It raises the question of the locus (mahall) of ru'ya: is it the physical eye (al-'ayn wa al-hadaqa) or the substance of the soul (jawhar al-nafs)? The author states that the first option (the eye) is very unlikely (mustab'ad jiddan), while the second option—the rational soul (al-nafs al-natiqa)—is closer to reason (aqrab ila al-'aql) due to several considerations (bi wujuh). The author then concludes that these three premises (the first about the nature of ru'ya, the second about its intuitive possibility, and this third about its locus) have been laid down before proceeding to the actual arguments for and against ru'ya.

Key Terms
المحل
العين
الحدقة
جوهر النفس الناطقة
الإدراك الشريف

Key Terms from Source Passage

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some might think the third premise denies the possibility of ru'ya altogether because it says the eye is unlikely.
Correction: The passage does not deny the possibility; it discusses the locus assuming ru'ya were to occur. The author favors the soul as the locus, not the eye, but does not yet argue whether ru'ya actually occurs.

Checkpoint

A) The eye and pupil
B) The substance of the rational soul
C) The heart
D) The brain

Key Takeaway
The third premise establishes that if ru'ya occurs, its locus is more reasonably the rational soul than the physical eye.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?

?

?
The author calls the soul's perception 'noble perception.' Are you living as if your soul's capacity to know God is the highest pursuit? What worldly 'vision' has distracted you from seeking this inner vision?

The First Argument Against Ru'ya: Conditional Logic and Eight Conditions
Opponents present a conditional argument with eight conditions for vision to deny ru'ya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first argument against the possibility of seeing God, including its conditional structure and the eight conditions for vision that opponents use to argue that we should see God now.

Core Idea
If we cannot see God now despite all conditions for vision being met, then ru'ya is impossible.

Opponents of ru'ya present a logical argument based on human visual perception. They claim that if seeing God were possible, we would see Him now because all necessary conditions for vision are present. Since we do not see Him, the possibility of ru'ya is false. This lesson examines the conditional argument and the eight conditions that the opponents list as necessary for any visual experience.

Guided Path
1
Understand the conditional argument
Read the passage and identify the main conditional: if ru'ya were valid, we would see God now. Then note that the conclusion is 'the antecedent is false' because the consequent is false.
2
Examine the explanation of the entailment
The author introduces a premise to show why the conditional holds: when eight conditions are present, vision must occur. Identify those conditions.
3
List and analyze the eight conditions
Go through each condition one by one. Notice that they cover the physical requirements for vision: sound sense, object properties, distance, size, subtlety, absence of veil, and orientation.
These conditions are standard in optics.
4
Consider the opponent's implied premise
The opponent assumes that God meets all these conditions. Ask yourself: is that assumption valid? This will be addressed later by the author.
5
Summarize the argument's force
The argument seems strong on physical grounds, but it depends on treating God as a physical object. Reflect on what this argument reveals about the opponent's worldview.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
ولنرجع إلى المقصود فنقول : أما القائلون بأنه تمتنع رؤية الله فقد احتجوا الحجة الأولى : قالوا : لو صحت رؤيته ، لوجب أن نراه الآن ، والثاني باطل . فالمقدم باطل . بيان الملازمة إنما يظهر عند تقديم مقدمة نقول : إن عند حصول ثمانية أنواع من الشرائط فإنه يجب حصول الرؤية أولها : كون الحاسة سليمة . والثانية : كون الشيء بحيث تصح رؤيته وثالثها : أن لا يحصل القرب القريب. ورابعها : أن لا يحصل البعد البعيد . وخامسها : أن لا يكون المرئي في غاية الصغر. وسادسها : أن لا يكون في غاية اللطافة مثل الهواء. وسابعها : أن لا يتوسط بين الرائي وبين المرئي شيء من الحجب . وثامنها : أن يكون المرئي مقابلا للرائي أو في حكم المقابل .
Let us return to the purpose and say: As for those who say that vision of God is impossible, they adduced the first argument: They said: If His vision were valid, we would necessarily see Him now. The second is false, so the antecedent is false. The explanation of the entailment only appears when we present a premise. We say: When eight types of conditions are present, vision must occur. First: the sense being sound. Second: the thing being such that its vision is valid. Third: that extreme proximity not occur. Fourth: that extreme distance not occur. Fifth: that the visible not be extremely small. Sixth: that it not be extremely subtle like air. Seventh: that nothing from the veils intervene between the seer and the seen. Eighth: that the visible be facing the seer or in the ruling of facing.
Source pages p. 82

Commentary
This passage presents the first argument (al-hujja al-ula) of those who deny the possibility of seeing God. The argument is a conditional: if ru'ya were valid, we would see God now. Since we do not see Him now, ru'ya is impossible. The entailment (al-mulazama) is explained by eight conditions that must be present for any vision: sound sense organ, the object being visible (not too small, subtle, etc.), no extreme proximity or distance, no intervening veil, and the object being directly opposite or in the ruling of facing. The opponent's point is that since God exists and presumably meets all conditions (e.g., He is not too small, not too far, no veil, etc.), we should see Him. Since we do not, ru'ya is impossible. The author will likely answer this argument later, but here only the objection is presented.

Key Terms
الحجة الأولى
الملازمة
الشرائط الثمانية
الحاسة سليمة
المقابلة

Key Terms from Source Passage

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The eight conditions are merely a list of physical requirements; they apply to God as a physical object.
Correction: The author will later argue that God is transcendent and not subject to physical conditions; the conditions only apply to physical objects in space.

Checkpoint
What is the conclusion of the first argument against ru'ya?
A) Ru'ya is possible only after death
B) Ru'ya is impossible because the necessary conditions for vision are met yet we do not see God
C) Ru'ya is possible only for prophets
D) Ru'ya requires a different set of conditions

Key Takeaway
The first argument against ru'ya uses a conditional and eight conditions for vision to conclude that since we do not see God now, ru'ya is impossible.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
After reading this argument, do you feel any doubt about the possibility of seeing God? If you cannot immediately see a flaw, what does that reveal about your reliance on logical reasoning over divine revelation?
?
The eight conditions are physical limitations of sight. Are you willing to limit God by such physical categories in your own heart? What does your answer say about how you truly think of His transcendence?
