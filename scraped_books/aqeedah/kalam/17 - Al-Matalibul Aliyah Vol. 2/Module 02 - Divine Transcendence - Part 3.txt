The Fifth Doubt: Adjacent and Distinct Existence
Razi presents the fifth doubt: an argument for God's spatial adjacency or distinctness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the anthropomorphist's argument that every two existents must be adjacent or distinct, and why existence is proposed as the cause of this judgment.

Core Idea
This doubt uses existence to force God into spatial categories.

This passage presents a doubt from an anthropomorphist debating a monotheist. The argument is that if every two existents are either adjacent or distinct by direction, and God exists, then God must also be adjacent or distinct. The anthropomorphist attempts to isolate existence as the sole cause of this rule, ignoring other factors.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening scenario
Notice that Razi summarizes a debate between an anthropomorphist (Mushabbih) and a monotheist (Muwahhid). This sets up the doubt as a challenge to be refuted, not a truth.
2
Identify the core claim
The claim is: for any two existents, one must be adjacent (mujanib) to the other or distinct (mubayin) from it by direction. Understand the definition of adjacency as one penetrating the other.
3
Follow the causal reasoning
The argument tests whether this rule holds due to substance, accident, or a common factor (existence or temporality). It eliminates all except existence.
Notice the logical structure: testing alternatives and discarding them.
4
See the conclusion and its implication
The conclusion is that existence is the cause, so since God exists, He must be adjacent or distinct from the world in direction. This is the doubt's force.
Remember, this is presented as a doubt to be refuted later.
5
Reflect on the purpose
Ask yourself: why would Razi include this doubt? It likely represents an objection that the monotheist must answer to defend God's transcendence beyond space.

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth doubt, which is a debate between an anthropomorphist (Mushabbih) and a monotheist (Muwahhid). The author summarizes and expands it. The opponent claims that since in the witnessed world existence causes spatial judgments, and God exists, the same spatial dichotomy (adjacent or separate) must apply to Him. The Non-Necessity of the Premise and Circularity
Refutation by showing non-necessity and circularity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the premise that an existent cannot be neither inside nor outside the world is not self-evident and how the objectors' proofs commit circularity.

Core Idea
The objectors' proofs are circular because they presuppose what they try to prove.

The author begins by addressing the first two objections. He shows that the premise in question is not necessary knowledge but inferential, and that the objections' own arguments depend on refuting that premise, creating a circular reasoning that undermines their case.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of knowledge
The author states that knowledge of the premise must be either necessary (badihi) or inferential (istidlali). Notice the dichotomy.
This is a common move in kalam arguments.
2
See why it cannot be necessary
The author says its necessity was refuted by many reasons mentioned earlier. Accept that as given for now.
3
Trace the circularity argument
The objectors' proofs depend on the falsity of the premise. If they use those proofs to refute it, the argument is circular.
Circularity (dawr) invalidates an argument.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the first two objections (shubha) raised against the claim that affirming an existent that is neither inside nor outside the world is impossible. He then engages with Ibn Sina's position. Ibn Sina admitted certainty about the premise but attributed it to imagination (wahm) rather than intellect. The author then presents a dialectical question: do you (the objector) admit that the certainty from this premise equals the certainty from self-evident truths? If you say it is weaker, then it needs proof and we need not discuss the source. If you say it is equal, then we ask: Is the judgment that one certainty comes from intellect and the other from imagination itself necessary or inferred? If necessary, then the falsity of the premise would be known necessarily, contradicting the claim that it is equal in strength. If inferred, then self-evident truths would depend on inference, leading to circularity again, and the whole system of knowledge collapses. Hence, the author concludes that Ibn Sina's answer contains a deep problem.

The Second Branch: Equal Certainty Leads to Contradiction or Circularity
Dilemma showing equal certainty leads to contradiction or global skepticism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the second branch of the dialectic, if certainty equals self-evident truths, leads to either a contradiction or a destructive circularity that undermines all self-evident knowledge.

Core Idea
Equal certainty with self-evident truths forces either necessary knowledge of its falsity or circularity that destroys all knowledge.

The author continues the dialectic by considering the second branch: that the certainty about the premise equals that of self-evident propositions. He then presents a dilemma: either we can distinguish the sources (intellect vs. imagination) by necessary knowledge, leading to a contradiction, or by inference, leading to circularity that invalidates all self-evident truths. Either way, Ibn Sina's position fails.

1
Assume equal certainty
Consider the case where the certainty about the premise is as strong as self-evident truths.
2
Distinguish the sources
We ask: is the knowledge that one certainty is from intellect and the other from imagination itself necessary or inferred?
Two possibilities.
3

If necessary, then we know by necessity that the premise is false, but we assumed it is certain; contradiction.
This is the 'khalaf' (contradiction) argument.
4
If inferred: circularity
If inferred, then self-evident truths depend on inference, which itself depends on self-evident truths — circular. This would also make all self-evident knowledge false.
This is a devastating result.

The first: The basis of this objection is that for every two existents in the empirical world, one must be adjacent to the other or separate from it by direction. This premise is denied. Refuting the Spatial Dichotomy: Three Arguments
Refutes the premise that all existents must be adjacent or separate in direction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the claim that all existents must be either adjacent or separate in direction, using counterexamples from philosophy, Mu'tazila theology, and the ontology of relations.

Core Idea
Not all existents fit the dichotomy of adjacency or separation in direction.

This passage addresses a key premise used by those who argue that God must occupy a direction. The author denies this premise and provides three independent arguments: first, from the philosophers' incorporeal entities; second, from the Mu'tazila's non-spatial wills and aversions; third, from the nature of relational entities like paternity, which cannot be spatially characterized. By showing that such existents exist, the premise is shown to be false.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premise being refuted
Read the opening lines to understand the premise: any two existents in the observable world must be either adjacent or separate in direction. Note that the author immediately denies this.
This premise is the foundation of the specious argument (shubha) that God must be in a direction.
2
Examine the first argument from philosophers
The author cites the majority of philosophers (al-ḥukamā') who affirm intellects, celestial souls, rational souls, and hyle. These are not spatial and cannot be described as adjacent or separate.
These entities are immaterial and not located in space, providing a clear counterexample.
3
Examine the second argument from the Mu'tazila
The Mu'tazila affirm wills, aversions, and annihilation existing not in a substrate. These too are non-spatial and escape the dichotomy.
Even within Islamic theological schools, there are entities that are not spatial.
4
Examine the third argument from relations
The author argues that relations (al-iḍāfāt) are real existents and shows they cannot be adjacent (else paternity would be divisible) nor separate (else paternity would be a substance).
This argument is more technical but decisive: relations are inherent yet not spatial.
5
Understand the conclusion
The demonstrations establish existents that are neither adjacent nor separate, thus refuting the premise.
This refutation paves the way for affirming God's transcendence beyond direction.

Commentary
This passage presents the first proof of the chapter's thesis that in this life we do not know Allah's specific essence (al-dhāt al-makhṣūṣa). The author, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, structures the argument by first stating the conclusion that this knowledge is not attained, then introducing the first proof. The proof relies on the observation that everything known to us about Allah falls into four categories: existence (wujūd), modality of existence (kayfiyyat al-wujūd, which includes necessity and eternity), negations (sulūb, e.g., not being a body or location), and relations (iḍāfāt, e.g., being able to act or being knowing). Al-Razi then argues that these four types of knowledge are not identical to knowledge of the specific essence, nor do they necessitate it. To elaborate, he provides two premises. The first premise explains that human beings indeed know only these four categories from Allah, giving examples for each. The second premise demonstrates that none of these categories constitutes knowledge of the essence itself: existence is an attribute distinct from the essence; modality is a attribute of that existence; negations are not the essence but mere denials; relations are distinct from both relata. The passage ends by establishing that these four knowledges are not the same as knowledge of the specific essence. Note that the proof is incomplete in this window—the author intended also to argue that these four do not even lead to knowledge of the essence, but that part is cut off. Nevertheless, the core idea that our knowledge of Allah is limited to these categories and is not a direct apprehension of His essence is clearly stated.

Commentary

Knowledge as Domination: The Seventh Argument
An argument that if humans fully knew God, they would dominate Him, which is impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that knowledge implies domination, and why full knowledge of God would imply human domination over God, which is impossible.

Core Idea
Knowledge is conquest; to fully know God would be to conquer Him.

The seventh argument in al-Matalib al-Aliyah draws a striking analogy: knowledge is a form of domination. If we could fully know God, we would, in a sense, dominate Him. But that is impossible, for only God has true power. This lesson unpacks that argument and its implications for divine transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation

Notice that the argument uses a rhetorical question to make its point.
2
Examine the analogy
The argument compares ignorance to being subdued and knowledge to dominating. Identify how this analogy is built and why it is effective.
The analogy relies on common human experience of learning.
3
Apply the conclusion
Understand the logical step: if knowledge is domination, then knowing God fully would imply dominating God. Since that is impossible, intellects cannot reach His essence.
This conclusion reinforces divine transcendence.

The seventh proof: They said: Knowledge is a type of domination over the known. Do you not see that one who is not knowledgeable of something, his soul in relation to that known is like an incapacitated, subdued person; then when he knows it and encompasses it, he becomes like one dominating it and controlling it? If this is established, then we say: If intellects reached the depth of His reality, then creatures would have domination over the Truth in some respects. But as for when intellects are incapable of knowing Him, creatures remain perpetually in that subjugation and incapacity of knowledge, and domination and subjugation belong to the Truth, and that is what is obligatory.

Commentary
This is a proof that human intellects cannot fully know God. It uses the analogy that knowledge is a form of domination (استيلاء) over the known object. When one does not know something, his soul is like a subdued captive; when he knows it, he becomes like a conqueror. Therefore, if humans could fully know God's reality (كنه حقيقته), they would dominate God in some respect, which is inappropriate (since only God should have domination). Thus, intellects are necessarily incapable of such knowledge, so that domination and power remain solely with God. This argument ties the impossibility of fully knowing God to the theological principle of divine supremacy.

The Intellect's Limitation: A Case for Divine Transcendence
An argument that since the mind cannot contemplate two objects at once, it cannot grasp the greatest object.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the mind's inability to handle multiple objects at once proves its incapacity to grasp the essence of the greatest reality.

Core Idea
The mind cannot grasp two objects at once, so it cannot grasp the infinite.

Sanusi presents a simple yet profound argument from human cognitive limitation. By observing that we cannot simultaneously hold two known objects in our mind, he argues that we are fundamentally incapable of comprehending the essence of the Supreme Being. This argument reminds us that our intellectual capacity is finite and cannot encompass the Infinite.

Guided Path

2
Identify the premise and evidence
The argument first states a general claim about the intellect's limitation. Then it provides experiential evidence: when focused on one object, it cannot turn to another.
The evidence is drawn from common human experience.
3
Understand the conclusion
The conclusion is a rhetorical question: if the intellect cannot handle even two finite objects, how could it possibly reach the depth of the greatest reality? Reflect on why this is a powerful theological point.
4
Reflect on divine transcendence
Consider how this argument supports the idea that God's essence is beyond human comprehension. This is a key theme in Islamic theology.

The mind's inability to hold two thoughts at once proves it cannot grasp the greatest thought.

Al-Razi now presents a concise yet powerful argument for divine transcendence. The proof rests on a simple observation about how our minds work: we cannot think of two things at the same time. If that is true for ordinary objects, how could we ever hope to comprehend the highest reality of all?

First Proof: The Proposition 'The Essence is Unknown' is Self-Contradictory
Al-Razi presents the first argument that the proposition 'the essence is unknown' is self-contradictory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Razi demonstrates that the claim 'God's essence is unknown' is logically self-contradictory because every judgment requires prior conceptualization of its subject and predicate.

Core Idea
To deny knowledge of the essence, you must first know it.

Al-Razi opens his first proof with a fundamental logical principle: every judgment (tasdiq) depends on prior conceptualization (tasawwur) of its subject and predicate. He then applies this to the common claim that God's essence is unknown, showing that the very act of making such a claim presupposes knowledge of the subject, leading to a contradiction. This lesson walks through the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the logical premise
Learn the foundational rule that tasdiq is preceded by tasawwur. This is the key to the argument.
2
Apply the premise to the claim
Identify the subject and predicate of the proposition 'His essence is unknown,' and see how the rule forces knowledge of both.
Notice that the subject 'His essence' must be conceived by the speaker.
3
Explore the counter-argument and its refutation
Consider the objection that the essence is known through attributes. Al-Razi examines two interpretations of the subject and shows both lead to contradiction.
This covers all possibilities, making the proof exhaustive.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
الحجة الأولى : إن كل تصديق ، فإنه يجب أن يكون مسبوقا بتصور الموضوع والمحمول. فإذا قال القائل : إن حقيقته غير معقولة للخلق ، كان موضوع هذه القضية : هو قولنا حقيقته . ومحمولها : هو قولنا : غير معقولة للخلق ، والحاكم باستناد هذا المحمول إلى هذا الموضوع ، يجب كونه عالما بهذا الموضوع، وبهذا المحمول ، حتى يمكنه هذا الاستناد . فيثبت : أن صدق قولنا : إن حقيقته غير معلومة للخلق ، يقتضي كون حقيقته معقولة للخلق من حيث إن موضوع القضية يجب أن يكون معلوما ، وما أدى نفيه إلى ثبوته كان نفيه باطلا . فوجب أن يكون قولنا : حقيقته غير معقوله للخلق : قولا باطلا . فإن قالوا : إن تلك الحقيقة معقولة بحسب بعض صفاتها وأحوالها . فنقول : إذا قلنا إن حقيقته غير معقولة فموضوع هذه القضية ، إما أن يكون تلك الحقيقة من حيث هي هي ، أو تلك الحقيقة بحسب بعض صفاتها وأحوالها . فإن كان الأول لزم كون تلك الحقيقة من حيث إنها هي معقولة للخلق . لما بينا أن موضوع القضية من الاعتبار الذي به صار موضوعاً للقضية ، يجب أن يكون متصورا. فإذا كان موضوع هذه القضية هو تلك الحقيقة من حيث إنها هي ، وجب كون تلك الحقيقة من حيث هي هي ، معلومة ، وحينئذ يلزم التناقض . وأما إن قيل : إن موضوع هذه القضية هو تلك الحقيقة بحسب بعض صفاتها المعلومة ، أو بحسب بعض أحوالها المعلومة ، فحينئذ قد حكم على الأمر المعلوم من حيث إنه معلوم بأنه غير معلوم ، وذلك أيضا يوجب التناقض . فقد ظهر بما ذكرنا : أن قولنا : إن تلك الحقيقة ، غير معلومة : يوجب التناقض على جميع التقديرات .
The first proof: Every judgment (tasdiq) must be preceded by a conceptualization (tasawwur) of its subject and predicate. So if someone says, 'His essence is incomprehensible to creatures,' the subject of this proposition is our saying 'His essence,' and its predicate is our saying 'incomprehensible to creatures.' And the judge who attributes this predicate to this subject must be knowledgeable of this subject and this predicate so that he can make this attribution. Thus it is established that the truth of our saying 'His essence is unknown to creatures' necessitates that His essence be comprehensible to creatures, since the subject of the proposition must be known. And what leads from its negation to its affirmation renders the negation false. Therefore our saying 'His essence is incomprehensible to creatures' must be a false statement. If they say: That essence is comprehensible through some of its attributes and states, we say: When we say 'His essence is incomprehensible,' the subject of this proposition is either that essence itself (min haythu hiya hiya) or that essence through some of its attributes and states. If it is the first, then it would necessitate that that essence itself is comprehensible to creatures, because we have shown that the subject of the proposition, by the aspect by which it is subject, must be conceptualized. So if the subject of this proposition is that essence itself, then that essence itself must be known, and this entails a contradiction. If it is said that the subject of this proposition is that essence through some of its known attributes or known states, then one would have judged a known thing, insofar as it is known, to be unknown, and this also entails a contradiction. So it has become clear from what we have mentioned that our saying 'that essence is unknown' entails contradiction in all suppositions.
Source pages p. 94

Commentary
This passage is a proof by contradiction that it is impossible to coherently state that God's essence is unknown. The argument proceeds in two stages. First, it analyzes the proposition 'His essence is unknown' itself: every judgment requires prior conceptualization of both subject and predicate. The person making the claim must have a concept of what 'His essence' is in order to say it is unknown. Therefore, the very act of asserting that the essence is unknown implies that the essence is known as subject. This leads to contradiction: the assertion denies knowledge while presupposing it. Second, the argument considers a possible rejoinder: perhaps the essence is known only through some of its attributes, not in itself. The argument then splits the possibilities: if the subject is the essence in itself, then it must be known in itself (contradiction). If the subject is the essence under some known attribute, then the attribute is known, and one is judging a known thing to be unknown, which is also contradictory. Thus, under every interpretation, the statement 'the essence is unknown' is self-contradictory. This is a subtle dialectical argument that challenges the very coherence of claiming divine incomprehensibility in absolute terms.
