Knowledge of Allah's Essence is Unattainable
The author concludes the discussion on the impossibility of knowing Allah's essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why human intellect cannot attain true knowledge of Allah's essence and how the author resolves objections while leaving some questions unanswered.

Core Idea
Human reason cannot comprehend Allah's essence; only divine bestowal grants true knowledge.

This passage concludes a deep discussion on the limits of human knowledge regarding Allah's essence. The author summarizes key arguments, addresses objections, and then raises the question of whether angels can attain such knowledge, leaving it undecided. Finally, he reminds us that when reason reaches its limits, we must turn to Allah for true understanding.

The Necessity of Negating All Qualities from Allah
The author argues against attributing any physical qualities to Allah and refutes an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the intellect negates all physical qualities from Allah, even those that might appear perfect, and how the author refutes an objection that different quiddities could share such qualities.

Core Idea
Allah must be absolutely free from all physical qualities to avoid rational inconsistency.

Having established that Allah is not a body or substance, the author now addresses the question of attributes like colors, tastes, and smells. Although some might argue that certain qualities could be perfections, the intellect sees them as properties of bodies. This lesson unpacks the author's argument, his response to an objection, and the necessity of negating all such qualities from Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the full passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Focus on the main claim: Allah is free from qualities.
Notice the objection starts with 'إلا أن لقائل أن يقول'.
2
Identify the premise and its implications
The author begins by recalling that Allah is not a body or substance. This already negates quantity and some qualities (shape, form).
This is the foundation for the rest.
3
Understand the objection
An objector says: even if Allah is not a body, He could still have qualities like color because different things can share attributes.
The objection challenges the link between body and quality.
4
Follow the refutation
The author responds by noting that divine attributes (creation, etc.) do not depend on these qualities. Also, we cannot claim some qualities are perfections and others defects to pick and choose.
This is the key argument: either affirm all (leading to contradictions) or negate all.
5
Grasp the conclusion
The only consistent position is to negate all physical qualities from Allah. This is what the author intends.
Thus Allah's transcendence is complete.

Commentary
This passage, opening Chapter Twelve, argues for Allah's transcendence from qualities (kayfiyyat). The author begins by stating that since Allah is not a body (jism) or substance (jawhar), He is free from quantity and from some qualities like shape and form. For other qualities like colors, tastes, and smells, the intellect (aql) rejects attributing them to Allah because they are attributes of bodies. An objection is anticipated: different quiddities (mahiyyat) can share attributes, so a non-body could have these qualities. The author responds by appealing to a group who say that divine attributes like creation (khalq) and bringing into existence (takwin) do not depend on these qualities. Furthermore, one cannot claim that some of these qualities are perfections and others defects, because that would allow affirming only the perfections for Allah. But if one affirms some, there is no basis to stop at some rather than others; affirming all would combine opposites (like light and darkness), so the only consistent position is to negate all such qualities from Allah. Thus the intended conclusion is established.

Argument for Allah's transcendence from all corporeal qualities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational argument that Allah is transcendent from all corporeal qualities, including colors, tastes, and smells, and why affirming them would lead to contradiction.

Core Idea
Allah's transcendence from physical qualities is a logical necessity, not mere tradition.

In this passage, the author builds on the earlier proof that Allah is not a body or substance. Since corporeal qualities like shape, color, and taste are attributes of bodies, they cannot apply to Allah. But what about an objection that different essences can share attributes? The author responds with a powerful argument that affirming any such quality would force affirming all or none, and since some qualities are opposites, they cannot all be affirmed.

Quantity and shape are directly linked to corporeality.

The same logic applies to colors, tastes, and smells—they are attributes of bodies, so they cannot be affirmed for Allah. But an objection arises: different essences can share attributes.
The objection challenges the direct inference.

The Impossibility of Divine Indwelling: Two Rational Proofs

Divine indwelling would make God dependent, contradicting His necessary existence.

He first outlines the majority's approach: if God indwells, it must be either necessarily or contingently—both impossible. Commentary
This passage provides a refined approach to refuting inherence by first defining it clearly. The author states that the inquiry into inherence must begin with understanding what inherence means. Two intelligible meanings are given: (1) spatial inherence, like color in a body, which requires the subject to occupy physical space—impossible for God who transcends space. (2) inherence of an attribute in a subject, like knowledge in a knower, which requires the attribute to be dependent on the subject—impossible for God who is necessarily self-sufficient. Any other meaning is unintelligible. Then the author adds a reductio ad absurdum: if inherence were possible for God, one would have to remain in doubt whether He inheres in every vile creature (flea, ant, mosquito), since lack of evidence of such inherence does not disprove it. Such doubt is absurd, so inherence is false.

The Argument from Necessary Doubt Against Hulul
The author argues that allowing hulul leads to necessary doubt about God's indwelling in base bodies, which is absurd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why allowing the possibility of hulul leads to necessary doubt about God's indwelling in lowly creatures, which is absurd.

Core Idea
If hulul were possible, you couldn't be sure God doesn't indwell in a bedbug—an unacceptable doubt.

The author now shifts to a dialectical argument. He points out that those who say hulul is possible must also admit the possibility that God indwells in every lowly creature. Since there is no way to disprove that possibility, they would be doomed to perpetual doubt—and that is clearly false.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument from necessary doubt
Read the Arabic passage once to grasp the flow: the premise, the implication, and the conclusion.
Look for the conditional 'إذا' which sets up the logical implication.
2
Understand the logical implication
If one allows hulul for God, then it is possible for Him to indwell in any body, including lowly ones. The author forces this conclusion.
3
Analyze the counter-argument
A possible objection: 'We don't see any awe in lowly creatures.' The author rejects this: absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.
This is a key epistemological point.
4
Conclude the refutation
Since the doctrine leads to absurd doubt (and certainty is required), the doctrine is false.

Union with God in essence is impossible; only attributes can change.

Commentary
This passage presents an argument demonstrating that God's essence cannot be subject to accidental originated attributes (ḥawādith). The author begins by analyzing the nature of potentiality (qabiliyya) in relation to an essence. He posits that any potentiality for an attribute is either an essential concomitant of that essence or not. If it is a concomitant, the essence is never free from it, which is the desired conclusion (that the essence is always qualified by that attribute). If it is not a concomitant but a separable attribute, then the essence must have a prior potentiality to receive that potentiality, leading to an infinite regress (tasalsul), which is impossible. Therefore, potentialities must terminate in a potentiality that is an essential concomitant. Next, the author argues that this potentiality itself is an originated attribute. He shows that the potentiality for an originated attribute can only occur when the attribute itself is possible, and possibility of an originated thing requires a beginning because eternal occurrence is impossible. Consequently, the potentiality also has a beginning. From this, he concludes that anything capable of originated events cannot be free from them. Then he invokes the agreed principle among the theologians: anything that is not free from originated things is itself originated. Since God is transcendent above originated events, it follows that He cannot be potential for them.

Razi's Second Proof: The Dilemma of Eternal vs. Originated Pleasure
Razi presents a second argument showing that pleasure in Allah leads to absurdity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Razi's second proof shows that attributing sensory pleasure to Allah leads to the absurd conclusion that the world is eternal.

Core Idea
If Allah experienced pleasure, the world would be eternal—impossible.

Razi presents a powerful dilemma: any pleasure Allah experiences must involve an object. That object must either be eternal or created. He then shows both options lead to the impossibility of the world being co-eternal with Allah. This argument further establishes divine transcendence beyond physical attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the two branches of the dilemma
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that Razi sets up a clear 'either-or' structure: if Allah experiences pleasure, the pleasurable object is either pre-eternal or originated.
Pay attention to the logical flow: 'إما أن يكون حاصلا في الأزل وإما أن لا يكون'.
2
Understand the first horn: eternal pleasure implies eternal object
The first branch argues that if the pleasure is eternal, then the object causing it must also be eternal. This implies the world (the object) is pre-eternal, which contradicts Islamic doctrine.
Razi notes that the pre-eternity of the world has already been refuted elsewhere.
3
Understand the second horn: originated pleasure leads to infinite regress
The second branch argues that if the pleasure is originated, then Allah, knowing eternally that He can attain it and having no obstacle, must originate the object. But at any supposed time of origination, the motive and lack of obstacles existed earlier, forcing origination even earlier—leading to an infinite regress and ultimately the object's pre-eternity.
This regress shows that the 'originated' horn collapses into the first horn.
4
Recognize that both horns are impossible, so the premise is false
Since both branches lead to the absurd conclusion of the world's pre-eternity (which is unacceptable), the original assumption that Allah experiences sensory pleasure must be impossible.
The proof is a classic reductio ad absurdum.

Why 'Jawhar' Does Not Imply Genus for Allah
An objection and its answer regarding the term 'jawhar' and divine attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the term 'jawhar' (substance), when applied to Allah as 'independence from a substrate,' does not imply genus and composition, and how negative concepts differ from positive attributes in Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Negative concepts cannot be parts of a quiddity, so 'independence' is no genus.

In Islamic theology, a common objection to describing Allah as 'jawhar' (substance) is that it would make Him composed of genus and differentia, implying contingency. The author refutes this by clarifying that the term 'jawhar' here means independence from a substrate, which is a negative concept. A negative concept cannot be a part of an essence, so it cannot serve as a genus. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and shows how theological precision preserves divine transcendence.
