The Sixth Proof: Four Aspects of Divine Action Beyond Sense
Al-Razi enumerates four aspects proving divine actions beyond sense.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four rational aspects that demonstrate that knowledge of God's actions transcends sensory imagination.

Core Idea
God's actions are known by reason, not by sense or imagination.

Al-Razi presents a powerful proof that our understanding of God's creative acts does not rely on sensory experience. He begins by noting that even the anthropomorphists agree that knowledge of God's attributes is beyond sense perception. Then he elaborates four distinct aspects that show how our theological convictions contradict what sense and imagination would lead us to believe. Each aspect highlights a discontinuity between ordinary experience and divine action.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the structure: a general claim, then four numbered 'wujuh' (aspects), then a conclusion.
2
Identify each aspect
Go through each of the four aspects. For each, note what sense tells us (e.g., change requires matter, agent change, purpose) and what we affirm about God that contradicts that.
3
Understand the conclusion
After the four aspects, Al-Razi concludes that wahm wa khayal (illusion and imagination) are excluded from knowledge of God's acts. Reflect on how the four aspects collectively support this.

The sixth proof: we say that knowledge of the acts [ (1) ] of God, exalted, and His attributes is closer to reason than knowledge of the essence of God, exalted. Then the anthropomorphists (mushabbiha) agreed with us that knowledge of God's acts and knowledge (2) of His attributes are contrary to the judgment of sense and imagination.

As for the elaboration of this meaning concerning God's acts, it is from aspects:
First: that which we have seen and witnessed is [only (?) ] change of qualities, such as the transformation of melted water into plants and the transformation of plants into animals (4). As for the origination of substances and bodies, this is something we have not witnessed nor known at all.
Second: we do not conceive the origination of something fashioned except from a particular matter and except in a particular time. For the coming into existence of a ring and bracelet from nothing from which that ring and bracelet are fashioned is inconceivable and not sensed. Also: the coming into existence of a ring and bracelet without a time and moment is irrational. Then the people of religions and creeds assented to that and acknowledged it, even though it is not imagined nor sensed.
Third: we do not conceive an agent acting (5) after not having been an agent except due to a change in his state and transformation of his attribute. Then the people of religions acknowledged that He is the Creator of the world without any change in His attributes nor transformation in His states.
Fourth: we do not conceive an agent performing an act except for attracting benefit or repelling harm. Then we acknowledged that He, exalted, created this world without any of these conditions. So it is established that illusion and imagination are excluded from knowledge of God's acts.

Commentary
This passage presents the sixth proof, which starts by stating that knowledge of God's actions and attributes is more accessible to reason than knowledge of His essence. The anthropomorphists (mushabbiha) agree that such knowledge does not rely on sense perception. The author then elaborates on this point regarding God's actions through four aspects (wujuh).

First: In our observable experience, we only witness changes in qualities (e.g., water becoming plants, plants becoming animals), not the origination of substances themselves. The origination of bodies is beyond our direct observation.

Second: We cannot conceive of something being created without pre-existing matter and a specific time. Yet religious people affirm creation ex nihilo and God's timeless act, which are contrary to imagination.

Third: We cannot conceive of an agent who previously was not an agent becoming an agent without a change in his state. Yet Muslims affirm that God created the world without any change in His attributes.

Fourth: We cannot conceive of an agent acting without a purpose of benefit or harm. Yet God's creation is free from such motivations.

Each aspect shows that universally accepted religious beliefs about God's actions go beyond what sense and imagination can grasp. The conclusion is that imagination (wahm wa khayal) is excluded from knowledge of God's acts.

The Sixth Proof: Imagination Fails for Divine Actions and Attributes
Al-Razi presents the sixth argument, with four aspects for actions and three for attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Razi systematically demonstrates that imagination and fantasy are incapable of grasping God's actions and attributes, using seven distinct aspects.

Core Idea
Imagination cannot reach divine actions or attributes; only intellect can.

Al-Razi continues his series of rational proofs for God's transcendence. In this sixth argument, he targets the anthropomorphists (المشبهة) who rely on imagination to understand God. He argues that since even human actions and attributes often exceed imagination, divine ones certainly do. The argument is structured in two clear sections: four aspects concerning actions, three concerning attributes. Each aspect contrasts what we normally perceive or conceive with what we affirm about God.

Guided Path
1
Read the full passage carefully
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Notice the clear division into actions (أفعال) and attributes (صفات). Pay attention to the numbered 'aspects' (وجوه) in each section.
The passage is long but logically structured.
2
Identify the two main sections
The passage has two halves: one on divine actions (4 aspects) and one on divine attributes (3 aspects). Mark where each begins. The conclusion ties them together.
Look for 'أما تقرير هذا المعنى في أفعال الله تعالى' and 'وأما تقرير هذا العجز في الصفات'.
3
Study each aspect under 'actions'
For each of the four aspects, ask: what does imagination normally assume? How does God's action contradict that? Al-Razi's pattern is: 'we do not conceive of X except under condition Y, yet God does X without Y'.
The four aspects cover: creation of essences, creation from nothing/timeless, agent change, and motivation.
4
Study each aspect under 'attributes'
Similarly for the three aspects: infinite knowledge, action without tools/fatigue, and all-encompassing hearing/sight. Each shows imagination's limitation.
The first aspect about infinite knowledge is particularly subtle.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
Al-Razi wraps up by stating that if imagination fails for actions and attributes (the nearer domain), it is even more incapable for the essence, which is more sublime and obscure. This is an a fortiori argument.
This strengthens the overall case for tanzih (transcendence).

Fourth: we do not conceive of an agent performing an act except for gaining benefit or repelling harm. Then we acknowledged that He the Exalted created this world without any of these conditions. So it is established: that imagination and fantasy are isolated regarding knowledge of God’s actions. And as for the elaboration of this incapacity regarding attributes, it is from aspects: First: we do not conceive of an essence that knows infinite particulars all at once. For if we examine ourselves, we find that whenever we are occupied with recalling a particular known thing, it becomes impossible for us in that state to recall another known thing. Then, despite that, we believe that He the Exalted knows all particulars in detail without any confusion or obscurity occurring in that knowledge. So His being knowledgeable of all particulars is contrary to what imagination and fantasy require. Second: every doer of an action must have a tool or instrument, and that strenuous actions cause fatigue, exhaustion, and weariness. Then we believe that He the Exalted manages from the Throne to beneath the earth, even though He is free from hardship and weariness. Third: we believe that He the Exalted hears the voices of the creatures from the Throne to beneath the earth, and sees the small and the large above the layers of the high heavens and beneath the lowest earth. And it is known that imagination and fantasy cannot conceive these states. So it is established by what we have mentioned: that imagination and fantasy are incapable of knowing God’s actions and attributes. Nevertheless, we affirm that God’s actions and attributes are contrary to imagination and fantasy. And it is obvious by necessity: that knowledge of the inner reality of the essence is higher, more sublime, and more obscure than knowledge of actions and attributes. If we have excluded imagination and fantasy in the nearer and clearer domain, then excluding them in the more obscure and difficult domain is even more appropriate.

Commentary
This passage presents the sixth argument, structured in two main sections. First, the author states that knowledge of God's actions and attributes is more accessible to reason than knowledge of His essence. The anthropomorphists (المشبهة) already concede that these are contrary to sense and imagination. The author then elaborates on divine actions through four 'aspects' (وجوه). First, our empirical experience only shows changes of attributes (e.g., water to plant, plant to animal), not the actual creation of essences—we have never witnessed absolute origination. Second, we cannot conceive of something coming into existence from nothing or without time; yet religions affirm creation ex nihilo, which is beyond imagination. Third, we normally require a change in the agent to explain a new act, but God created the world without any change in Himself. Fourth, we naturally think of acts as motivated by benefit or harm, but God created without such motives. Each point shows that God's actions contradict imagination, so imagination is isolated (معزول) regarding them. The second section covers attributes through three aspects. First, our minds cannot hold infinite knowledge simultaneously, yet God knows all particulars at once. Second, our experience ties action to tools and effort, but God acts without any instrument or fatigue. Third, our perception is limited in range, but God hears and sees everything everywhere. These show that attributes also exceed imagination. The conclusion: since knowledge of the essence is even higher and more obscure, if imagination fails regarding actions and attributes (the nearer domain), it is even more appropriate to exclude it regarding the essence.

First Definition of Jawhar: The Indivisible Atom
First of four definitions of substance and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God cannot be a substance in the sense of an indivisible atom that occupies space.

Core Idea
If one atom were necessary, all atoms would be necessary—impossible.

The first definition of jawhar is the extended indivisible atom. This definition only matters if one accepts atomism. Here, the author shows that if the atom were necessary and eternal, then all atoms would share that property, which is absurd. Let's examine the argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the first definition
Read the Arabic and translation for the first meaning of jawhar: the extended indivisible atom.
Notice the conditional: it only applies to those who affirm the indivisible atom.
2
Understand the argument from similarity
If one atom is necessary, then all atoms are similar and would also be necessary. This impossible consequence refutes the possibility.
The argument relies on the principle that all atoms are of the same kind.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
The author states that this is impossible (muhal), so God cannot be a substance in this sense.

Chapter Four: On establishing proofs that it is impossible for Him to be this. Know that 'substance' (jawhar) may refer to one of four things: First: that which is extended (mutahayyiz) and does not accept division. This is based on the affirmation of the indivisible atom. Whoever does not affirm it is not engaged in this inquiry. And whoever affirms it says that all atoms are similar, so if one of them were eternal, necessary of existence by itself, then all would be likewise. That is impossible. The second interpretation of substance: that which is susceptible to accidents (a‘rād). So if we prove that the Necessary Existent by itself must be necessary in all His attributes, that is a proof that change (taghayyur) is impossible for Him. The third interpretation of substance: that to which another thing is added, resulting from its composition with another a third thing. That is also impossible, because its composition with another depends on its being extended in itself, and that is impossible. The fourth interpretation: The Chief Master Abu Ali said: 'Substance is the quiddity (māhiyya) which, when it exists in re, is not in a locus.' We have elaborated on this definition in the chapter on substance and accident. However, this definition applies only to a quiddity whose existence is other than its quiddity. The Chief Master said: 'This is impossible with respect to God, because we have proven that the existence of God is identical to His quiddity.' And know that this issue has been discussed thoroughly before. And it is a discussion.
Source pages p. 34

Commentary
This passage is Chapter Four of the work, which aims to establish proofs that God cannot be a substance (jawhar). The author first clarifies that jawhar can have four different meanings, and then refutes each in turn. First meaning: the indivisible atom that occupies space and cannot be divided. The author notes that this definition only applies if one accepts the theory of atoms. If one does, then all atoms are similar, so if one were eternal and necessary, all would be; this is impossible. Thus God cannot be an atom. Second meaning: that which can receive accidents. Since the Necessary Existent must be necessary in all His attributes, He cannot undergo change, so He cannot be a substance in this sense. Third meaning: that which can combine with another to form a third thing. Composition requires extension, which is impossible for God. Fourth meaning: a quiddity whose existence in re is not in a locus, as defined by Avicenna. But this definition only applies to quiddities where existence is distinct from essence; in God, existence is identical to essence, so this definition does not apply. The author states that this issue has been discussed thoroughly elsewhere. The passage ends with 'and it is a discussion' (wa huwa bahth), indicating a conclusion.

Ninth Objection: Existence as Cause and the Hindrance of Particular Essence
Objection and response on existence as cause of division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection that existence as cause would force impossible divisions in substances and accidents is answered by appealing to the particularity of essence as a preventing factor.

Core Idea
Existence may cause possibility, but particular essence can block the effect.

This passage presents the ninth objection in a theological debate about the relationship between existence and the possibility of division. The objector argues that if existence is the cause for being divisible into adjacent or distinct, then all existing things would have to be divisible in that way, leading to absurdities. The response, however, introduces the concept of a 'preventive factor' (mani')—the particularity of essence—showing that existence does not necessitate universal application of its effects.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the objector's argument: if existence is the cause for division into adjacent (mujanib) and distinct (mubayin), then since existence is equally present in all existents, substances and accidents would have to be divisible in that way.
The key term is 'muqtada' (prerequisite/cause).
2
Identify the reductio
Notice that the objector claims division of substance and accident into those two categories is known to be impossible. This is the absurd consequence meant to refute the premise.
Think about why a substance cannot be both adjacent and distinct to itself in direction.
3
See the defender's reply
The defender says that the possibility holds in view of existence, but actual division is prevented by the particularity of essence (hususiyyat al-mahiyya). This is the core response.
The term 'mani'' (preventive factor) is crucial.
4
Examine the counter-reply
The objector turns this admission against the defender: if particular essence can block the effect, then existence is not a sufficient cause, so the original position is weakened.
This shows the dialectical back-and-forth.
5
Reflect on the nuance
Consider how this argument relates to divine attributes: God's particular essence prevents Him from being subject to division, even though He exists.
This is the broader theological application.

Commentary
This passage contains the ninth objection and its response. The objector constructs a reductio: if existence is the cause for the possibility of being divided into adjacent (mujanib) and distinct (mubayin) in direction, then since existence is equally present in substances and accidents, they would all have to be divisible in that way. Commentary

So knowledge of that relation is not itself knowledge of that specific essence. So it is established by what we have mentioned that these four knowledges are not knowledge of that specific essence. And as for the explanation that knowledge of these four objects of knowledge does not necessitate knowledge of the specific essence, it is obvious. Because when we know these four premises, we find within ourselves nothing but that it is something (shay' ma). We do not know what it is, except that it is described by these four types of attributes. And that indicates that knowledge of these objects of knowledge does not necessitate knowledge of the specific essence.
As for the third premise: It is an exposition that nothing is known of God Almighty except these four types of information. Whoever reflects on himself and considers the state of his mind and understanding knows necessarily that he has nothing of divine cognitions except these four types. And since it is established that he has nothing except these four cognitions, and it is established that they are not themselves knowledge of God's essence, nor do they necessitate knowledge of God's essence, then it becomes clear that knowledge of the specific essence of God Almighty is not attained by us at all.
Source pages p. 88

Commentary
This passage is the first of two arguments demonstrating that human beings cannot attain knowledge of God's essence. The author begins by listing the third and fourth types of knowledge about God (the first two were presumably discussed earlier): the third is knowledge of negations (sulub)—attributes that deny imperfections—and the fourth is knowledge of relations (idafat), such as God's ability to act or abstain and His knowledge. He states that these four types are attainable by human minds.

Then he presents the second premise (the first premise, that these four types exist, is implied). The second premise is that knowledge of these four items is not knowledge of God's specific essence. He argues for each: knowledge of existence is not the essence because existence is an attribute; knowledge of necessity and perpetuity are knowledge of modalities, not the essence; knowledge of negations are merely negative concepts, not the essence; knowledge of relations are different from the relata. Therefore none of these four is identical to knowledge of the essence. Moreover, having all four does not necessitate knowing the essence; we only find in ourselves that it is 'something' described by these attributes, not what it is.

The third premise asserts that we have no other knowledge of God besides these four. By introspection, one sees that human minds contain only these four types of divine cognitions. Combining the second and third premises, the author concludes that knowledge of God's essence is utterly unattainable. The argument is carefully structured: it identifies the types of knowledge we have, shows each is not the essence, and then shows we have no alternative access.

Second Proof: Attributes Require Knowledge of the Essence
Al-Razi's second proof that essence must be known for attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second proof that affirming divine attributes necessitates prior knowledge of the essence, and the role of the impossibility of infinite regress.

Core Idea
The subject of any divine attribute must be known, otherwise infinite regress follows.

Al-Razi presents a second argument that to attribute any quality to God, we must already have some knowledge of His essence. The proof relies on the logical structure of judgments and the impossibility of an infinite chain of attributable attributes. Let's follow his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the starting premise
Al-Razi begins with a logical principle: every judgment (tasdiq) is preceded by a conceptualization (tasawwur) of its subject and predicate. This means that when we make a claim like 'God is knowing,' we must have some concept of 'God' and 'knowing'.
2
Identify the two possibilities for the subject
When attributing attributes (sifat) to the essence (dhat), the subject of those attributes can be either the essence itself (min haythu hiya hiya) or the essence through other attributes. Read the Arabic to see how Al-Razi sets up this dichotomy.
The phrase 'من حيث هي هي' means 'insofar as it is itself', i.e., without any added condition.
3
Trace the first alternative
If the subject is the essence itself, then the subject of the predicates (attributes) is that specific essence, and it must be known (ma'luma). This directly shows that the essence is known.
4
Trace the second alternative
If the subject is the essence through other attributes, then we must explain how those attributes are attributed. This leads either to infinite regress (tasalsul) or to stopping at an attribute that is directly attributed to the essence itself. Both paths ultimately require the essence to be known.
Infinite regress is impossible because it would require an endless chain of attributes without a foundation.
5
Conclude the argument
Since both possibilities force us to accept that the essence itself must be known, the proof is complete. The essence is known prior to any attribute.
This proof does not tell us what the essence is, only that it must be known in some way.

If it is the essence in itself, then the essence itself is known (because the subject must be known). If it is the essence under some attribute, then we must examine how that attribute is attributed. Either we go back to another attribute ad infinitum (impossible), or we eventually reach an attribute that is directly attributed to the essence itself, which again requires the essence to be known. Thus, in either case, the essence must be known. This proof uses the impossibility of infinite regress (tasalsul) to force the conclusion.
