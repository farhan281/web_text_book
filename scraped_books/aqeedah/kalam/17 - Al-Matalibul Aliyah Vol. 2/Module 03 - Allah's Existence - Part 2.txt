Second Refutation: Determination Entails Necessity
Second refutation: determination causes necessity leads to contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the claim that determination necessitates necessity leads to a contradiction: necessity in itself would be contingent.

Core Idea
If determination causes necessity, then the necessary would be contingent.

The second possibility is that the specific determination of a necessary existent is what makes it necessary. This lesson unpacks why that argument fails: if necessity is caused by determination, then necessity becomes dependent on something else, which violates its essential nature.

Guided Path
1
Read the second refutation
Read the Arabic and translation, noting the conditional: if determination causes necessity in itself, then necessity would be necessary by another.
The key phrase: 'الوجوب بالذات إذا كان معللا بذلك التعين'.
2
Trace the chain of dependency
Follow the argument: necessity in itself would be necessary by another; but what is necessary by another is possible in itself; so necessity in itself would be possible in itself—a contradiction.
3
Understand why this is impossible
Necessity in itself cannot be contingent; therefore, the premise that determination causes necessity must be false.
This refutes the second possibility.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of three possible relationships between the necessity of existence (wujub al-dhat) and the specific determination (ta'ayyun) that distinguishes one necessary existent from another. Third Refutation: Neither Entails the Other
Third refutation: independence requires external cause, leading to contingency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the claim that necessity and determination are independent of each other leads to an infinite regress or external cause, making the necessary existent dependent.

Core Idea
If neither entails the other, an external cause is needed, contradicting self-necessity.

The third and final possibility is that necessity in itself and specific determination are completely independent. If so, their conjunction in a single being would require an external cause, which would make the necessary existent contingent. This lesson examines that reasoning.

Guided Path
1

Read the Arabic and translation. Note that the argument assumes that if two things are independent, their combination needs a cause outside them.
Key phrase: 'يكون اجتماعها لا بد وأن يكون بسبب منفصل'.
2
Understand the implication
If conjunction requires an external cause, then the necessary existent (which combines both necessity and determination) would be necessary by that external cause.
3
Recognize the contradiction
A necessary existent cannot be dependent on an external cause. Thus this possibility is also impossible.
This completes the refutation of all three possibilities, leaving only the oneness of the Necessary Existent.

The Fourth Refutation: Composition of the Determined Essence is Impossible
Trilemma refuting composition of the determined essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical trilemma that proves the Necessary Existent cannot have a composite determined essence, and that it must be absolutely one.

Core Idea
The Necessary Existent's oneness is proven by exhausting all relations to its determination.

In this lesson, we examine the fourth argument against the claim that the determined essence (al-dhat al-muta'ayyina) is composite. The author presents an exhaustive trilemma regarding the relationship between the Necessary Existent and its determination. By analyzing each possibility, we see that only absolute oneness is rational, and any alternative leads to contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the three possibilities
Read the Arabic passage and the translation carefully. Notice that the author lists three exhaustive relations between the Necessary Existent and its determination.
The three possibilities are: identity, causation, or neither.
2
Understand the implications of the first two possibilities
The first two possibilities (identity and causation) both lead to the conclusion that the Necessary Existent is one. Consider why each forces oneness.
If identical, determination is the Essence itself. If caused, determination is a necessary concomitant, still implying singularity.
3
Analyze the third possibility and its absurd consequence
The third possibility (neither identical nor cause) would require an external cause for determination, making the Necessary Existent contingent. This is impossible, so the third possibility is rejected.
The argument proves that the only coherent position is that the Necessary Existent is one and its determination is not a separate added attribute.

Commentary
This passage contains the third objection from the opponent against the proof for the oneness of the Necessary Existent (tawhid) and the author's refutation through three arguments. The opponent begins by conceding that both necessity of existence (wujub al-wujud) and determination (ta'ayyun) are fixed conceptual realities. They then argue that the multiplicity which the author claims follows from the assumption of multiple Necessary Existents also follows from the assumption of one, so the proof for oneness is invalid. The author responds by stating that this difficulty (mahdhur) is indeed necessary even with the affirmation of oneness, and they explain this through several reasons. The first reason: The First Principle (al-mabda' al-awwal) is not bare necessity of existence, which is a universal essence. A universal essence cannot exist in external entities; rather, what exists externally is a determined essence described as necessary. This determination (ta'ayyun) is additional to the essence of necessity, and it must be an attribute (sifa). Attributes depend on their subject, and anything dependent on another is possible (mumkin) in itself. Thus, the determination is possible and is an effect of necessity, making the determined entity composite from a necessary and a possible part, leading to the conclusion that everything in external existence is possible—thus the problem the opponent raised against multiplicity equally applies to the one. The second reason: The opponent's argument assumes that if there were more than one Necessary, each would be composed of shared necessity and distinguishing determination. The author shows that regardless of which is cause, the necessity is an accidental attribute external to the essence, making it possible and caused. This applies even to the one Necessary, so the difficulty remains whether one or many. The third reason: There are many essential necessities in reality—e.g., the entailment of oddness by three and evenness by four are necessary in themselves. These multiple essential necessities show that there are things necessary in themselves beyond just one. The author then answers an anticipated objection: these are not self-subsisting essences but relational states. The response is that the opponent's own division in proving oneness is based on essential necessity (wujub bi al-dhat), and these considerations do possess essential necessity, thereby refuting the claim of oneness.

First Reason: Composition Through Determination
The first reason in refuting the third objection: composition of the determined essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the first refutation shows that any existent essence, including the One, is composed of necessity and determination, leading to contingency.

Core Idea
Determination added to necessity makes every existent composite and contingent.

The author presents the strongest proof for God's oneness: the argument from mutual hindrance.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Two gods willing opposites leads to logical impossibility.

Among the strongest proofs for tawḥīd presented by the theologians is the argument from mutual hindrance. It shows that if two gods with absolute power will opposite things for the same creation, all possible outcomes lead to contradictions. This lesson unpacks that argument step by step.

The Proof of Mutual Prevention: Initial Reasoning and First Two Refutations
Sanusi presents the proof structure, enumerates three possibilities, and refutes first two.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure of the proof of mutual prevention and why both wills being fulfilled together or both being prevented are impossible.

Core Idea
Two gods with conflicting wills lead to logical contradictions, proving each cannot be divine.

This lesson introduces the famous kalam proof of mutual prevention (dalil al-tamanu') for the oneness of God. The theologians present a scenario of two gods with opposite wills and examine all possible outcomes, showing each leads to an impossibility. By working through these possibilities, you will see how reason alone can establish God's unity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the three possibilities
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that the theologians enumerate three possible outcomes when two gods will opposite actions on a single body.
The three possibilities are: both wills fulfilled, neither fulfilled, or only one fulfilled.
2
Understand the first refutation: both wills together
The first possibility is impossible because it would make the body simultaneously moving and still, a direct contradiction. Reflect on why this is inherently absurd.
3
Follow the second refutation: the circularity argument
The second possibility (neither will is obtained) is refuted by showing that the prevention of one will causes the other will to occur, leading to a self-defeating cycle. This is the key logical move that shows the impossibility of neither will being obtained.

Understand the conclusion
Since the first two possibilities are impossible, and the third will be refuted later, the existence of two gods is false. Note that this proof relies on pure reason, not revelation.
The third possibility is addressed in the next lesson.

Commentary
This passage presents the famous kalam proof of mutual prevention (dalil al-tamanu') for the oneness of God (tawhid). The author states that the theologians have mentioned various proofs, the strongest of which is this proof. They posit two gods capable of all possible things, then suppose they will opposite actions for a body: one wills motion, the other stillness. For (3), he gives two reasons: first, since both gods are equally omnipotent, there is no basis for one will to prevail over the other without a preponderating factor; second, the god whose will fails would be powerless, and a powerless being cannot be divine. Thus, all three possibilities are impossible, so the existence of two gods is false.

Commentary
This passage presents the famous kalam proof of mutual prevention (dalil al-tamanu') for the oneness of God (tawhid). The author states that the theologians have mentioned various proofs, the strongest of which is this proof. They posit two gods capable of all possible things, then suppose they will opposite actions for a body: one wills motion, the other stillness. This leads to three logical possibilities: (1) both wills are fulfilled together, (2) neither is fulfilled, (3) only one is fulfilled. Each possibility is shown to be impossible, proving that two gods cannot exist. The author then explains each impossibility in detail. For (1), it would make the body simultaneously moving and still, a contradiction. For (2), he gives a subtle argument: the prevention of one will is caused by the other will occurring. If both are prevented, that would require both wills to occur to cause prevention, leading to a self-contradictory circularity. He also adds a note that if accidents are not permanent, then the body would be left without motion or stillness, which is also impossible; but if accidents are permanent, the body remains as before, so no impossibility arises from that angle. For (3), he gives two reasons: first, since both gods are equally omnipotent, there is no basis for one will to prevail over the other without a preponderating factor; second, the god whose will fails would be powerless, and a powerless being cannot be divine. Thus, all three possibilities are impossible, so the existence of two gods is false.

Refuting Two Gods: The Argument from Identical Knowledge
A deductive proof showing two gods would have identical knowledge, leading to contingency.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If two gods existed, their knowledge would be interchangeable, making them contingent and thus impossible.

This lesson presents a powerful deductive argument against the existence of two gods by examining the nature of divine knowledge. If there were two gods, each would know the same things in the same way, making their knowledge identical. By the principle that what holds for one holds for its similar, this leads to the conclusion that each god's knowledge could subsist in the other's essence, implying that the specification of knowledge to one essence requires a cause. This cause would make the knowledge originated, contradicting the divine nature. The passage thus demonstrates the impossibility of multiple gods.

Second Dualist Argument: Concept of Necessary Existence
Dualists argue that the concept of necessary existence can apply to many beings.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The concept of necessary existence does not logically forbid sharing; multiplicity must be argued away.

This passage presents the second dualist argument against the uniqueness of God. The dualists claim that the very concept of 'necessary existence in itself' (wajib al-wujud li-dhatihi) does not logically exclude being shared by multiple beings. They then consider whether something else could prevent sharing, and argue that it cannot. The argument is a reductio ad absurdum aimed at showing that multiple necessary existents are conceptually possible. Understanding this argument is crucial for grasping the full range of challenges to tawhid in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the argument is structured as a proof by contradiction.
2
Identify the dualist claim
The dualists first assert that the concept of necessary existence is not inherently exclusive. They argue that if it were, that would be known intuitively, but it is not.
This is the foundation of the whole argument.
3

They then consider two possibilities: either something accompanies the concept to prevent sharing, or nothing does. They analyse the first possibility by examining whether the accompanying thing is a quiddity or not.
Both subcases are argued to be impossible or ineffective.

If nothing accompanies the concept, then sharing is not impossible. They then argue that if sharing were possible, the beings would be merely possible (imkan khas), which contradicts the assumption that they are necessary. This is a classic reductio to show the necessity of multiplicity.

Commentary
This passage presents a complex argument against the possibility of multiple necessary existents. The author begins by noting that the mere concept of necessary existence does not inherently preclude multiplicity (shirkah). He then considers two possibilities: either this concept is accompanied by another concept that prevents sharing, or it is not. The first option is refuted by examining the nature of the accompanying concept. If it is a quiddity (māhiyyah), it is universal and thus cannot prevent sharing; if it is not a quiddity, it has no reality and cannot serve as a distinguisher. The second option leads to a dilemma: if no concept prevents sharing, then plurality is not impossible. That plurality would then be either impossible (but it's not) or possible with specific possibility. If possible, then none of the things could be necessary existents, contradicting the assumption that there are multiple necessary existents. Hence, the assumption is false, and the only conclusion is that there cannot be multiple necessary existents.

But if humanity is shared among many persons, then the whole of this quiddity is not present in a single person. And it is known by necessity that the first is more perfect. [And God knows best.]

Commentary
This passage contains the author's responses to three objections (shubah) against the doctrine of the unity of the necessary existent. The first objection concerns the problem of evil in a dualistic system with a good god and an evil god. The answer argues that the good god cannot be truly good if he fails to prevent evil, leading to a contradiction. The second objection questions why the necessary existent must be determined as this particular one; the answer simply states that its essence necessitates that determination. The third objection uses an analogy with the quiddity of humanity: if humanity were instantiated in only one person, that person would contain the whole essence, making him more perfect; this supports the idea that the one necessary existent is complete and perfect.
