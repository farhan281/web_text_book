The Two Main Views on Divine Corporeality
Classification of two main views on divine location.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two opposing views on whether God occupies space: the majority view of transcendence and the minority anthropomorphist view.

Core Idea
The universe splits into those who transcend God from space and those who restrict Him to it.

Al-Razi begins by stating a fundamental division in religious thought: the overwhelming majority affirm God's transcendence from body and place, while a minority claims He is spatial. This passage establishes the core disagreement that all subsequent debates hinge upon.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the clear contrast between 'the overwhelming majority' and 'the rest'.
The terms 'tanzih' (transcendence) and 'tajsim' (corporealism) are key.
2
Identify the two groups
Name the two opinions: (1) the majority who affirm transcendence from corporeality and location, (2) the minority who assert spatiality. Note the label 'al-mujassimah' for the latter.
This classification sets the stage for all that follows.
3
Reflect on the significance
Ask yourself why this distinction matters for theology. How does each view affect one's understanding of God?
Transcendence preserves God's uniqueness; corporealism risks limiting Him.

Chapter Three: On establishing proofs that the Exalted One cannot be... The people of the world in this regard have two opinions: the overwhelming majority of them agreed on the transcendence of God, the Exalted and Majestic, from corporeality and from being located in space. And the rest said: He is spatial, located in space. These are the anthropomorphists. Then those who asserted that He is a body disagreed on several issues. First: They are on two opinions regarding form. Some said He is in the form of a human. Others did not say so. As for the first, it is transmitted from the anthropomorphists among the Muslims that the Exalted One is in the form of a young man. And it is transmitted from the anthropomorphists among the Jews that He is in the form of an old man. (1) In the Torah that God is one God. And in the Torah that there is nothing like Him. In the sixth chapter of Deuteronomy... And Maimonides said in The Guide for the Perplexed: That which is mentioned in the Torah that suggests that God resembles a human in his limbs and attributes is to be interpreted metaphorically to mean that God draws His essence close to the minds of creation, speaking about Himself as if He were one of them. But He is not like a human and nothing is like Him. This is in order for creation to understand the essence of God. And as for those who say He is not in the form of a human, they say: He is in the form of a great light. Abu Ma'shar the astrologer mentioned that the reason people turned to the worship of idols is that in ancient times people followed the doctrine of anthropomorphism. They believed that the God of the world is a great light, and that the angels are lights except that they are smaller in body than the first light. When they believed this, they took an idol—the greatest of idols—in the form of God, and other smaller idols in various forms—which were the forms of angels—and engaged in worshipping them, believing that they were worshipping God and the angels. Thus it is established that the religion of idol worshippers is like a branch of the doctrine of the anthropomorphists.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and historical analysis. The author begins by stating the two main views among people regarding whether God is located in space: the overwhelming majority affirm His transcendence from corporeality and location, while the rest (the mujassimah, anthropomorphists) assert that He is spatial and located. Then he notes that those who affirm that God is a body disagreed on several points. The first point of disagreement concerns God's shape (surah). He reports two opinions: some say He is in the form of a human, and others say He is not. Among the former, the Muslim anthropomorphists say He is a young man, while Jewish anthropomorphists say He is an old man. At this point, the author includes a lengthy footnote citing Torah passages and Maimonides' interpretation, indicating that apparent anthropomorphic descriptions in the Torah are metaphorical. He then returns to the second opinion among anthropomorphists: those who deny human form yet say God is in the form of a great light. Here the author cites Abu Ma'shar al-Munajjim's theory that ancient idol worship originated from the anthropomorphist belief in a great light deity and lesser light angels, leading people to make idols in their likeness. The passage concludes that idolatry is a branch of anthropomorphism.

The First Disagreement: God's Shape Among Anthropomorphists
Enumeration of the first disagreement on shape among anthropomorphists.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first point of disagreement among anthropomorphists: whether God has a human form or is a great light, including the Jewish anthropomorphist view and Maimonides' metaphorical interpretation.

Core Idea
Anthropomorphists disagree on God's shape: some see Him as a man, others as a light.

After establishing the anthropomorphist position, al-Razi examines their internal disagreements. The first issue is God's shape: some say He resembles a human (with Muslim and Jewish variants), others reject this and say He is a great light. He also includes a footnote citing Torah verses and Maimonides' metaphorical interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the two opinions
Read from 'ثُمَّ القائِلون' to 'على صورة نور عظيم'. Note the two main opinions: human form vs. non-human form (light).
The human form is further divided into Muslim (young man) and Jewish (old man).
2
Understand the footnote
The footnote provides Torah citations and Maimonides' view that anthropomorphic descriptions are metaphorical. Why is this important?
Maimonides argues that such language is meant to make God understandable to humans.
3
Examine the light theory
The second opinion says God is a great light. Compare this to the human-form view. How is it still anthropomorphic?
Both views attribute a form to God, even if light is less tangible.
4
Reflect on the diversity
Notice that even among anthropomorphists, there is no single view. This shows the complexity of theological disagreement.

From Anthropomorphism to Idol Worship: Abu Ma'shar's Theory
Historical explanation linking anthropomorphism to idol worship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how, according to Abu Ma'shar, the belief in God as a great light led to the practice of idol worship, making idolatry a branch of anthropomorphism.

Core Idea
Anthropomorphism is the root of idolatry: belief in a light deity led to making idols in its image.

After presenting the light theory among anthropomorphists, al-Razi cites the astrologer Abu Ma'shar who traces the origin of idol worship to this very belief. He argues that in ancient times, people believed God is a great light and angels are smaller lights, so they made idols representing them. Idolatry, then, is a natural outgrowth of thinking God has a form.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the cause-and-effect chain
Read from 'وذكر أبو معشر' to the end. Abu Ma'shar presents a historical explanation: belief in God as light led to idol-making.
Notice the logical progression: belief -> representation -> worship.
2
Understand the mechanism
The ancient anthropomorphists made a great idol for God and smaller idols for angels. They worshipped them thinking they were worshipping God and angels.
This shows how theological error leads to practical error.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
The author concludes that idolatry is a branch (far') of anthropomorphism. What does this imply about the importance of correct theology?
Al-Razi uses this to warn against even subtle forms of anthropomorphism.

He then addresses two objections: one about why the Necessary of Existence must be one, and another about the nature of shared quiddities. This lesson unpacks these arguments and their significance for tawheed.

Guided Path
1
Understand the core argument against dualism
Read the dilemma: if the good god intervenes, the evil god is powerless; if He does not intervene despite ability, He becomes complicit in evil. Both are impossible, so dualism is invalid.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
2
Examine the answer to the second objection
The author asserts that the essence of the Necessary of Existence necessitates its own particular determination, so it must be one.
This establishes the necessary oneness of God.
3
Examine the answer to the third objection
The author analyzes the concept of 'every human except one': if human nature is restricted to one person, that person is perfect; but if shared, no single person contains the whole essence—the first is more perfect.
This shows that perfection aligns with oneness.

If He does it, then the evil one is repelled and his inability appears, so he is not fit for divinity. And if He does not repel him from evil despite being able to do so, then the good god would be pleased with the act of evil. And the one pleased with evil is evil, so it would follow that the good god is evil. That is impossible. Thus it is established that the claim of dualism is invalid in all estimations. And the answer to the second objection: Why is it not permissible to say: The essence of the Necessary of Existence in itself necessitated that particular determination itself, so necessarily the Necessary of Existence must be that specific one, and for this reason the Necessary of Existence in itself is one? And the answer to the third objection: If every human except one were (such that the entire reality of humanity is restricted to that person), but if humanity is shared among many persons, then the full reality of this quiddity is not present in one person, and it is known by necessity that the first is more perfect.

Commentary
This passage concludes the refutation of dualism and provides responses to two objections. First, the author summarizes the argument: if the good god intervenes to stop evil, the evil god is shown impotent and unworthy of divinity; if he does not intervene despite ability, he becomes complicit in evil, which is impossible. Thus dualism is invalid. Then he answers the second objection by asserting that the essence of the Necessary of Existence necessitates its particular determination, so it must be one. The third objection is answered by analyzing the concept of 'every human except one': if human nature is restricted to one person, that person contains the entire essence, but if it is shared, no single person contains the whole essence, and it is obvious that the first case is more perfect.
