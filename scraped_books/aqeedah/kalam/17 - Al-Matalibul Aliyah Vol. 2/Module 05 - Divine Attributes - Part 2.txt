The Flaw in the Argument of No Priority
Refutes objection about lack of priority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between subjective and objective priority and why the claim of 'no priority' does not justify describing God with deficiency attributes.

Core Idea
Our ignorance does not make all options equally possible.

The second objection concedes that the attributes in question are not perfections, but argues that God could still be described with some of them. The refutation hinges on the meaning of 'no priority'. We'll see how Sanusi distinguishes between subjective ignorance and objective reality.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
The objector says: even if none are perfections, why can't God be described with some? He claims there is no priority.
2
Understand the premise
The objector's premise is that there is no reason to prefer one option over another.
3
Analyze the two meanings
The author distinguishes between subjective lack of knowledge and objective impossibility. Note the logical distinction.
4
Conclude
The objection fails because the premise is ambiguous. Real priority may exist even if we are ignorant.

Commentary
This passage refutes two objections against the necessity of God being free from all deficiency attributes. The first objection asks why God cannot be described with the attribute of light (النور) while still being transcendent, since light is often seen as noble. The author responds by stating the foundational principle: God must be described with all attributes of perfection and praised qualities, and must be transcendent over all attributes of deficiency; light, if considered a deficiency, cannot be attributed to God. The second objection concedes that none of the attributes under discussion are perfections, but asks why God cannot be described with some of them and not others. The author refutes this by analyzing the claim of 'no priority' (عدم الأولوية). He distinguishes between two meanings: (1) lack of priority in our knowledge, which only leads to agnosticism, and (2) impossibility of priority in reality, which is denied. It is possible that in reality some attributes are more appropriate for denial, even if we do not know the reason. Thus the objections fail.

Arguing that God Cannot Be a Locus for Accidents
Opening the fifteenth chapter: demonstrating that God cannot be a locus for other things, with first research on Ash'ari position.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author argues that the Ash'ari position necessarily implies that God is a locus for accidents, contrary to their own claims.

Core Idea
The Ash'aris unwittingly make God a locus for change.

The author begins the fifteenth chapter by addressing a challenging question: can God be a locus for accidents? While only the Karamiyya openly admit this, the author contends that many other schools, especially the Ash'aris, are logically committed to it. This lesson unpacks the two arguments he uses to show that Ash'ari theology leads to God being a locus.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and grasp the claim
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the author's assertion that the Ash'aris, despite denying it, are logically forced to accept that God is a locus for accidents.
The author gives two arguments to support this.
2
Examine the first argument
Focus on the argument about God's power to create a specific body. Before creation, God has power; after creation, that power relation ceases because re-creation is impossible. This implies a change in God's attributes.
Think about why this might entail God being a locus for accidents.
3
Examine the second argument
Consider the argument about God's command. In pre-eternity, God does not command Zayd because Zayd doesn't exist. Once Zayd exists, God commands. The command (a demand) arises anew, implying an attribute that occurs in time.
This is another example of change in God.

Commentary
This passage opens the fifteenth chapter, which aims to prove that God cannot be a locus (mahall) for anything else. It begins by noting that there are several researches under this chapter. The first research asks whether it is conceivable that God is a locus for accidents (hawadith). The author remarks that only the Karamiyya sect explicitly held this view, but he asserts that in fact most school leaders implicitly hold it. He then focuses on the Ash'aris, who deny this, but argues that it is necessarily entailed by their own principles from two aspects. The argument is that these imply that God is a locus for temporal events (mahall li'l-hawadith), which the Ash'aris deny, but their own positions lead to it.

The First Objection: God's Power Cannot Cease
First proof that Ash'ari position entails God being a locus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first wajh against the Ash'aris: that God's power to create a specific body must cease after creation, implying a change in His attribute.

Core Idea
Creating a body removes God's power to create it — a change in His attribute.

In this lesson, we examine the first wajh (aspect) that the author uses to show that the Ash'ari position necessarily implies that God is a locus of accidents. The argument focuses on God's power: if God from eternity has the power to create a specific body, then when He creates that body, that power must cease because creating an already-existent thing is impossible. This cessation implies a change in God's attribute of power, which the Ash'aris reject. Let's unpack the logic.

Since the power relation ceased, God's attribute of power changed. If the Ash'aris deny change in God, they are forced into a contradiction.

The Second Proof: Originated Attribute Dilemma
The author argues that any originated attribute in God must be either a perfection or not, leading to impossibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why any originated attribute in God leads to an impossibility, whether it is perfect or not.

Core Idea
An originated attribute in God is impossible in both cases.

This passage presents the second proof against originated attributes in God. The author uses a simple dichotomy: any supposed originated attribute must be either a perfection or not. Both options are shown to be impossible, either because it would imply prior deficiency or because it contradicts the consensus that all divine attributes are perfect.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation to grasp the argument's flow.
Notice the dichotomy and the two branches.
2
Analyze the first case
If the attribute is perfection, consider: before it originated, God lacked it. Lack of perfection is deficiency, which is impossible for God.
3
Analyze the second case
If the attribute is not perfection, then affirming it for God contradicts the consensus that all divine attributes are perfect. Hence impossible.
4
See the logical conclusion
Both branches lead to impossibility, so the supposition of an originated attribute in God is false.

The second proof regarding the issue: The attribute that originated in the essence of God, exalted is He, is either from the attributes of perfection, or it is not from the attributes of perfection. If it is from the attributes of perfection, then that essence before the occurrence of that attribute in it is devoid of the attribute of perfection, and being devoid of the attribute of perfection is deficiency, so it follows that that essence is deficient. And deficiency is impossible for God. And if that attribute is not from the attributes of perfection, then its affirmation with respect to God, exalted is He, is impossible because of the agreement that all of God's attributes, exalted is He, must be attributes of perfection and praise.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof against the possibility of an originated attribute in God. If it is a perfection, then God would have lacked that perfection before it occurred, implying deficiency in His essence, which is impossible. If it is not a perfection, then affirming it for God would contradict the consensus that all of God's attributes must be perfect and praiseworthy. In either case, the supposition leads to an impossibility, so God cannot have originated attributes.

Third Proof: The Essence Would Depend on an External Cause for the Attribute
A logical proof against originated attributes in God's essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third proof that originated attributes cannot subsist in God's essence because doing so would entail dependence on an external cause, contradicting His necessary existence.

Core Idea
If the divine essence is insufficient to determine an attribute, an external cause is required, making God contingent.

In this lesson, we examine the third proof presented by the author to demonstrate that God's essence cannot be the locus of originated attributes. The proof argues by analyzing the relationship between the essence and any conceivable attribute: either the essence is sufficient to cause the attribute or its absence, or it is not. The third scenario leads to an unacceptable conclusion that would make the Necessary Being dependent on something else, which is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Understand the premise
The intellect considers any attribute, and the divine essence is either sufficient to necessitate its existence, sufficient to necessitate its non-existence, or sufficient for neither. This sets up three exhaustive cases.
Notice that the proof covers every possibility.
2
Examine the first two cases
If the essence is sufficient for existence, the attribute is perpetual like the essence. If sufficient for non-existence, the absence is perpetual. Both cases yield eternal attributes (or their negation).
In these two cases, no external cause is needed.
3
Focus on the third case
If the essence is not sufficient for either, then the state (existence or non-existence) of the attribute must be determined by an external cause because the essence cannot be in both states simultaneously.
The proof then shows that this leads to dependence.
4
Follow the logical chain to impossibility
The essence is dependent on one of the two states, each state is dependent on an external cause, so the essence is dependent on an external cause. That which depends on another is contingent, making the Necessary Being contingent—an impossibility.
This contradiction proves the original assumption false.
5
Conclude about God's attributes
Therefore, God's essence cannot be the locus of originated attributes. Just as His essence is eternal, His attributes are eternal, without beginning or end.
This affirms the doctrine of eternal attributes.

الحجة الثالثة : كل صفة يشير العقل إليها ، فإما أن تكون ذات الله كافية في استلزام حصولها ، أو كانت كافية في استلزام عدمها ، أو لا تكون كافية في واحد منهما ، فإن كان الأول لزم دوام وجود تلك الصفة بسبب دوام تلك الذات ، وإن كان الثاني لزم دوام عدم تلك الصفة بسبب دوام تلك الذات وإن كان الثالث فحينئذ لا تكون تلك الذات كافية لا في وجود تلك الصفة ولا في عدمها . معلوم : أن تلك الذات لا تنفك عن وجود تلك الصفة وعن عدمها . ونقول : الذات موقوفة على حصول أحد هذين القسمين ، إما وجود تلك الصفة وإما عدمها ، لكن وجودها وعدمها لما لم يكف فيه تلك الذات ، فلا بد فيه من سبب منفصل فتكون تلك الذات موقوفة على أحد هذين القسمين ، وكل واحد منهما موقوف على سبب منفصل، والموقوف على الموقوف على الشيء ، يجب كونه موقوفا على الشيء ، فيلزم كون ذات الله تعالى موقوفة على سبب منفصل ، والموقوف على الغير ممكن لذاته ، فواجب الوجود لذاته ممكن الوجود لذاته ، وذلك محال . فيثبت : أن ذات الله تعالى لا تقبل الصفات الحادثة ، وأنه كما أن ذاته دائم لم يزل ولا يزال ، فكذلك صفاته دائمة لم تزل ولا تزال .
The third proof: Every attribute to which the intellect points is such that either the essence of God is sufficient to necessitate its existence, or it is sufficient to necessitate its non-existence, or it is not sufficient for either. If the first, then the attribute must be perpetual due to the perpetuity of that essence. If the second, then the non-existence of the attribute must be perpetual due to the perpetuity of that essence. If the third, then that essence is not sufficient for either the existence of that attribute or its non-existence. It is known that that essence cannot be free from either the existence of that attribute or its non-existence. And we say: the essence is dependent on the occurrence of one of these two divisions, either the existence of that attribute or its non-existence. But since its existence and non-existence are not sufficiently determined by that essence, there must be an external cause. Thus that essence becomes dependent on one of these two divisions, and each of them is dependent on an external cause. And that which is dependent on that which is dependent on something must itself be dependent on that something. So it follows that the essence of God, exalted is He, is dependent on an external cause. And that which is dependent on another is possible in itself. So that which is necessary of existence in itself would be possible of existence in itself, and that is impossible. Therefore it is established that the essence of God, exalted is He, does not accept originated attributes, and that just as His essence is eternal, without beginning and without end, so too His attributes are eternal, without beginning and without end.

Commentary
This third proof examines every conceivable attribute that the mind might posit for God. The author divides the possibilities: either the divine essence is sufficient to necessitate the attribute, or to necessitate its absence, or it is not sufficient for either. If it is sufficient for existence, the attribute must be perpetual because the essence is perpetual. If it is sufficient for non-existence, the absence is perpetual. If it is not sufficient, then the essence is not the cause of the attribute's presence or absence. Since the essence must be in one of the two states (either having the attribute or not), but cannot determine which, it becomes dependent on an external cause. This leads to a chain of dependencies, ultimately meaning that God's essence would be dependent on something external, implying that He is contingent (mumkin) in Himself, which contradicts His being necessary of existence (wājib al-wujūd). Therefore, God cannot be subject to originated attributes; His attributes, like His essence, are eternal.

موقوف (mawqūf) - dependent
واجب الوجود (wājib al-wujūd) - necessary of existence
ممكن (mumkin) - possible/contingent

Correction: The proof only addresses attributes subsisting in God's essence. It does not deny that God can cause attributes in creatures, nor does it claim God's attributes are separate from His essence; rather, it affirms that if any attribute were to inhere in His essence, it would have to be eternal, not originated.

Second proof: a temporal attribute would be either a perfection or not, both impossible for God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second proof against temporal attributes in God, which uses a dichotomy of perfection and deficiency to show impossibility.

Core Idea
Temporal attributes require either deficiency or imperfection, both impossible for God.

This lesson presents the second proof from the author, a clear disjunctive argument. The key move is to consider whether a temporal attribute would be a perfection or not. Both possibilities lead to an impossibility, so such attributes cannot be ascribed to God. Pay close attention to how the argument covers all cases.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the structure: it starts with a dichotomy, then examines each branch.
The argument is concise but powerful.
2
Understand the first branch
If the attribute is a perfection, then before it occurred God lacked it — that lack is deficiency, which is impossible for God.
Deficiency (nuqṣān) is the opposite of perfection.
3
Understand the second branch
If the attribute is not a perfection, then it cannot be a divine attribute because all divine attributes must be perfections.
This is based on consensus.

The Argument from God's Knowledge of Changing Particulars
First argument for temporal attributes from knowledge of particulars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first argument for temporal attributes based on God's knowledge of particulars and its implication.

Core Idea
God's knowledge of changing things requires change in His essence.

This lesson presents the first of three proofs cited by those who affirm temporal attributes in God. It argues that since God knows particulars that change, His knowledge must also change, implying a temporal event in the Divine essence. Understanding this argument helps clarify a major point of contention in classical kalām.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how the argument is structured: a premise (God knows particulars) leads to a conclusion (knowledge changes, hence temporal).
The passage is part of a larger discussion; focus on this specific inference.
2
Identify the key premise
The argument assumes that knowledge must change when the known object changes. Consider why this premise might be accepted or challenged.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
The conclusion is that temporal knowledges occur in God's essence. Ask yourself: does this necessarily follow? What alternatives might exist?
This argument is often debated; the lesson aims to present it clearly.

وأما القائلون بحدوث الصفات فقد بنوا كلامهم على مسائل : أحدها : أنه تعالى عالم بالجزئيات ، والعلم يجب تغيره عند تغير المعلوم ، وهذا يقتضي حدوث العلوم في ذات الله. وثانيها : أنه تعالى مريد وثبت أن القصد إلى إحداث الشيء ، لا يحصل إلا حال الإحداث ، وهذا يقتضي حدوث الإرادة لكن الإرادة والمريدية صفة الله فيلزم حدوث هذه الصفة في ذات الله . وثالثها : إنه تعالى سميع بصير ، وسماع الكلام قبل حدوثه محال ، وإبصار الصورة قبل حدوثها محال فيثبت : أن ذلك السماع إنما حدث عند حدوث ذلك الصوت ، وأن ذلك الإبصار إنما حدث عند حدوث ذلك المرئي. وأما الدلائل الدالة على أنه لا يحدث في ذات الله تعالى شيء ، فهي بأسرها مشكلة الصفات الإضافية . فإنا قد بينا : أنه لابد من الاعتراف بحدوثها ، مع أن الدلائل التي ذكرتموها قائمة فيه . Commentary
This passage presents the case of those who affirm temporal attributes (al-qa'ilun bi-huduth al-sifat). For we have shown that we must acknowledge their temporal occurrence, while the proofs you (the opponents of temporal attributes) have presented still stand concerning it. This is a concluding note that the issue revolves around relational attributes, and the previous proofs might not apply to them.

Therefore, the essence of 'one' is different from the essence of 'necessary'. Then al-Razi presents a third argument: the concept of 'one' is not a negative concept. If it were negative, it would be the negation of number. But number is a collection of units. If unity were non-existence, then number would be a collection of non-existences, making number a negative concept. If unity is the negation of number and number is negative, then unity would be the negation of non-existence, which is a positive concept. Therefore, unity is a positive attribute. Thus, it is established that unity (al-wahda) is a positive attribute additional to the attribute of being necessary of existence. This concludes the discussion on divine transcendence (tanzih).

The Fourth Proof: Distinction Through Predication

Since the predicates have different extensions, the concepts they express must be different. This reinforces that oneness is an additional attribute.

Unity is a Positive Attribute Additional to Necessity
Al-Razi's proof that unity is a positive attribute and conclusion of the discourse on transcendence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's proof that the attribute of unity is a positive (existential) attribute, not a negative one, and that it is additional to necessary existence.

Core Idea
Unity is not just 'not many'; it is a real positive quality.

In this concluding argument, al-Razi addresses a potential misunderstanding: that unity might be a negative attribute (simply the negation of number). He demonstrates that if unity were negative, it would lead to absurd consequences, and instead shows it must be a positive existential attribute. This establishes that unity is an additional positive attribute over and above the essence of the Necessary Existent.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation from 'ثم نقول' to the end. This is a self-contained proof.
2
Understand the assumption and its consequence
Al-Razi assumes unity is negative, i.e., the negation of number. He then examines the nature of number.
Number is an aggregate of units, so if unity is non-existence, number becomes an aggregate of non-existences, which is absurd.
3
Follow the reductio ad absurdum
If unity is non-existence, then number is an aggregate of non-existences, making number a negative concept. Then if unity is the negation of number, it becomes the negation of a negative, which is positive. Alternatively, if unity is directly existential, that also yields the desired result.
Either way, unity must be existential.
4
Conclusion
Therefore, unity is an existential (positive) attribute additional to being necessary of existence. This ends the discussion on transcendence.
