The Self-Refutation of Denying Knowledge of God's Essence
A rebuttal arguing that stating God's essence is unknown implies it is known as the subject of the statement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument that the claim 'God's essence is unknowable' self-destructs because it presupposes knowledge of that essence as the subject of the claim.

Core Idea
Denying knowledge of God's essence requires knowing it as a subject.

In this lesson, we examine a classical kalam argument that exposes a fundamental logical flaw in the claim that God's essence is completely unknowable. The argument uses the basic structure of propositions (subject-predicate) to show that every judgment requires prior conceptualization of its terms. By applying this to the denial of knowledge, the arguer forces the opponent to admit that the denial itself implies some knowledge. Let's follow the reasoning.

1

Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Notice the technical terms and the logical flow.
Pay attention to the terms تصديق and تصور.
2
Identify the main premise
The argument starts with a general premise about every تصديق (affirmative judgment). Understand this premise.
The premise is that every judgment requires conceptualization of subject and predicate.
3
Apply the premise to the claim
See how the premise is applied to the specific claim 'His reality is unintelligible'. Identify the subject and predicate.
The subject is 'His reality', the predicate is 'unintelligible'.
4
Draw the conclusion
From the application, conclude that the one judging must know the subject. Therefore, the claim implies knowledge, leading to contradiction.
The argument says: negation implies affirmation, so the negation is false.
5
Consider the objection and response
The passage then considers an objection: knowledge of the reality by some attributes. The response distinguishes two interpretations of the subject.
The response is incomplete, but it starts to show that if the subject is the essence as such, it must be known as such.

Commentary
This is the first counter-argument from those who affirm that creatures can know God's particular essence. It points out a logical problem in the claim that His essence is unknowable. It argues that every affirmative judgment (تصديق) requires prior conceptualization (تصور) of the subject and predicate. If one says 'His reality is unintelligible,' the subject 'His reality' must be known in some way for the judgment to be meaningful. Therefore, the very statement that His essence is unknowable implies that it is known. If this leads to a contradiction (negation implies affirmation), then the statement is false. The argument then considers an objection: perhaps the reality is known by some attributes. It responds by distinguishing two possibilities: either the subject is the essence as such, or it is the essence with some attributes. If it is the essence as such, then it must be known as such. The passage ends incomplete, but the argument is clearly attempting to show that the claim of unknowability is self-refuting.

The Objection: Necessity as a Negative Concept
Objector argues necessity is negative and provides two proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objector's challenge that necessity (wujub) may be a negative concept, and how this undermines the composition argument.

Core Idea
If necessity is negative, sharing it does not cause composition.

The author has argued that positing two necessary beings leads to composition in each essence. But this argument assumes necessity is a positive attribute. The objector now raises a critical challenge: what if necessity is merely a negative concept? Then sharing in necessity implies no real composition. This lesson presents the objector's two proofs for the negative nature of wujub.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and its setup
Read the Arabic passage once. Notice how the objector frames the challenge: he denies that composition follows if wujub is negative. Then he states two things that need to be proven.
Pay attention to the distinction between 'sharing' (ishtirak) and 'distinction' (imtiyaz).
2
Understand the first proof from predication on non-existence
The first proof argues that wujub is predicated of non-existence (e.g., 'its non-existence is necessary'). But a predicate of non-existence cannot itself be existent; therefore wujub is not a positive concept.
This relies on the principle that a positive attribute cannot inhere in pure non-existence.
3
Analyze the second proof by elimination
The second proof considers three possibilities if wujub were positive: it would be the whole essence, part of it, or external. All are shown false, so it must be negative.
Consider why each option fails: whole? No, essence is more than necessity. Part? Then composition. External? Then accident, but necessity is essential.
4
Reflect on the implication for the main argument
If the objector succeeds in proving wujub negative, then the earlier proof of composition fails because the shared element is not a real positive attribute. This sets up a deeper debate about the nature of divine attributes.

Commentary
This passage presents the objector's first challenge to the previous proofs. The objector claims that the author's reasoning assumes that wujub (necessity) is a positive concept (mafhum thubuti). If wujub is instead a negative concept (mafhum 'adami), then sharing in wujub does not entail composition because the shared element is not a real positive attribute. The objector needs to establish two things: that wujub is negative, and that on that basis composition is avoided. He gives two proofs for the first. Proof one: everything whose existence is impossible has necessary non-existence; wujub is predicated of non-existence, but a predicate of non-existence cannot itself be existent, so wujub is not a positive concept. Proof two: if wujub were positive, it would either be the whole essence, a part, or something external; all are false: it cannot be the whole because essence is not just necessity; it cannot be a part because then composition; it cannot be external because then it would be an accident. Therefore wujub is negative.

Refuting the Objection: Wujub as a Negative Concept
Sanusi presents the first objection and refutes it with two proofs that wujub is a negative concept.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first objection to the argument against two necessary existents and how Sanusi proves that wujub is a negative concept to avoid composition.

Core Idea
Wujub is not a positive attribute—it is a negative concept predicated of non-existence.

Sanusi anticipates an objection: what if the necessity that two necessary existents share is a negative concept? Then no composition follows. To refute this, he must prove that wujub is indeed negative. He provides two proofs, the first based on the equivalence of impossibility and necessity of non-existence, and the second by showing that wujub cannot be a positive thing. This passage grounds that defense.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Notice that the objector claims composition only follows if wujub is a positive concept. If negative, no composition. Learn the two matters that need clarification.
The objector does not assert wujub is negative; he raises it as a possibility.
2
Follow the first proof
Trace how Sanusi argues that wujub is predicated of non-existence, so it cannot be an existent positive thing.
Key premise: everything impossible has necessary non-existence.
3
Analyze the second proof
Examine the division into three possibilities (essence, part, external) and why each is impossible. Understand the reasons for each impossibility.
This proof shows wujub cannot be a positive attribute at all.
4
See the conclusion
Realize that wujub must be a negative concept, and thus the objection fails. The original proof against two necessary existents stands.

Commentary
This passage is the presentation and refutation of the first objection to the earlier proof that two necessary existents are impossible. The objector argues that the entire proof rests on the assumption that the concept of necessity (wujūb) is a positive attribute (thubūtī). If instead it is a negative concept (ʿadamī), then when two things share a negative concept, no composition (tarkīb) follows; the distinction comes from the complete essence (tamām al-māhiyyah), not from a positive individuator. To answer this, the author must establish two points: (1) that wujūb is indeed a negative concept, and (2) that if so, composition does not occur. He then tackles the first point (al-maqām al-awwal) with two proofs. Therefore the concept of necessity cannot be an existent thing (amr mawjūd). Second proof: If necessity were a positive thing, it must be either the complete essence, a part of it, or external to it. Thus wujūb cannot be a positive attribute (ṣifa thubūtiyya).

Commentary
This is the fifth objection and its lengthy answer. The objector asks: Why can't one god's will occur and not the other's? Commentary
This passage presents the second proof for God's oneness based on kalam principles. It is a structured disproof by contradiction.

The proof begins with a conditional: If there were two omnipotent gods, then each would have power over exactly the same objects as the other. The impossibility is shown by considering three possibilities for any given effect (maqdūr): (1) it occurs by both together, (2) it occurs by neither, (3) it occurs by one alone. Each is shown to be impossible.

For (1): If the effect occurs by both together, then each agent independently necessitates it. The necessity of its occurrence by one prevents its attribution to the other, leading to the contradiction that it both occurs and does not occur by each. Hence a combination of contradictories.

For (2): If it occurs by neither, then there must be a preventer (māni‘) for each. The only possible preventer for one is its occurrence by the other or its opposite. But if it does not occur by either, there is no such preventer, and the effect would still require a cause. So non-occurrence by both forces occurrence by each, a contradiction.

For (3): If it occurs by one alone without the other, then since both are equal in power and effect, this would be a preponderance without a preponderant (tarjīḥ bilā murjīḥ), which is impossible.

All three logical possibilities are impossible, so the hypothesis of two omnipotent gods is impossible. This proof is more detailed than the first and examines the coordination of two agents on a single effect.

The second proof for establishing that the God of the world is one, based on the principles of the theologians (al-mutakallimīn): We say: If we suppose the existence of two gods, both powerful over all possibles, then it necessarily follows that each of them is powerful over exactly the same object of power (maqdūr) as the other. And this is impossible. So the assertion of the existence of two gods is impossible. As for the conditional, it is obvious. As for the consequent being impossible, its elucidation: Since each of them is powerful over all possibles, then everything that is within the power of this one is exactly the same object of power for that one. And if that object of power occurs, then either it occurs by both together, or it occurs by one of them, or it occurs by one without the second. The three divisions are all false, so the assertion of two gods is false. We say: It is impossible for it to occur by both together, because when this powerful agent is independent in bringing about (īqā‘), and that act with this agent becomes necessarily occurring by him, the necessity of its occurrence by him prevents it from being attributed to the second, and the necessity of its occurrence by the second prevents it from being attributed to the first. So if they both concur upon it, then the necessity of its occurrence by each of them would prevent its occurrence by the second, so it would follow that it occurs by both while simultaneously it is impossible for it to occur by each of them, and that necessitates the combination of two contradictories (jam‘ bayn al-naqīḍayn), which is impossible. And we say: It is impossible for it not to occur by either of them, because the preventer of its occurrence by this one is nothing other than its occurrence by that one and the opposite. And the effect (athar) only exists upon the existence of the cause (mu’aththir). So if it were impossible by both together, it would necessitate that it occur by each of them, so that its occurrence by this one prevents its occurrence by that one and the opposite. Then it would follow that it occurs by each of them. The conclusion: In the first division, its occurrence by each of them prevents its occurrence by both. In the second, its non-occurrence by both necessitates its occurrence by both, because the effect only exists upon the existence of the cause, and the cause of the impossibility of its occurrence by each of them is its occurrence by the other. And it is impossible for it to occur by one without the second, because since they are equal in effect (ta’thīr) and in power, its occurrence by one without the other would be a preponderance of one side of the possible over the other without a preponderant, and that is impossible.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof for God's oneness based on kalam principles. It is a structured disproof by contradiction.

The proof begins with a conditional: If there were two omnipotent gods, then each would have power over exactly the same objects as the other. The impossibility is shown by considering three possibilities for any given effect (maqdūr): (1) it occurs by both together, (2) it occurs by neither, (3) it occurs by one alone. Each is shown to be impossible.

For (1): If the effect occurs by both together, then each agent independently necessitates it. The necessity of its occurrence by one prevents its attribution to the other, leading to the contradiction that it both occurs and does not occur by each. Hence a combination of contradictories.

For (2): If it occurs by neither, then there must be a preventer (māni‘) for each. The only possible preventer for one is its occurrence by the other or its opposite. But if it does not occur by either, there is no such preventer, and the effect would still require a cause. So non-occurrence by both forces occurrence by each, a contradiction.

For (3): If it occurs by one alone without the other, then since both are equal in power and effect, this would be a preponderance without a preponderant (tarjīḥ bilā murjīḥ), which is impossible.

All three logical possibilities are impossible, so the hypothesis of two omnipotent gods is impossible. This proof is more detailed than the first and examines the coordination of two agents on a single effect.

Second Possibility: Neither Agent
Razi proves the impossibility of a single effect being produced by neither god.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why it is impossible for an effect to occur by neither of two omnipotent agents.

Core Idea
An effect cannot fail to occur by either agent because a preventer would be required but absent.

This lesson examines the second possibility: that the effect occurs by neither of the two gods. Razi argues that this leads to a paradox because the only conceivable preventer for each agent is the effect's occurrence by the other or its opposite, leading to a forced occurrence by both.

The preventer (māni‘) of occurrence by one is the occurrence by the other or its opposite (al-ḍidd).

Follow the reductio

This shows the impossibility of the second case.

Third Possibility and Conclusion
Razi refutes the third possibility and concludes the proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a single effect cannot be produced by only one of two equally powerful gods, completing the proof for God's oneness.

Core Idea
Choosing one equal agent over another without a preponderant is rationally impossible.

In this final lesson, we examine the third possibility: that the effect occurs by one god alone. Razi argues that since both gods are equal in power and influence, singling one out would be an arbitrary preponderance without a cause—an impossibility in rational theology. With all three possibilities refuted, the hypothesis of two gods is proven false.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the brief passage on the third possibility. It is a single argument.
Note the terms 'istiwa' and 'tarjīḥ bilā murjīḥ'.
2
Understand equality
Both gods are equal in influence (ta’thīr) and power (quwwa). There is no distinction.
3
Recognize the impossibility
Selecting one over the other would be a preponderance without a preponderant, which is rationally impossible.
This principle is a cornerstone of kalam.
4
Reflect on the overall proof
All three possibilities are impossible, so the conditional leads to a false consequent, disproving the existence of two gods.
This completes the second proof.

Refutation of three possible attributions of a single effect to two agents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the impossibility of two gods is proven by refuting all possible ways a single effect could be attributed to two agents.

Core Idea
A single effect cannot have two independent agents; each possibility leads to a contradiction.

This passage presents a powerful logical refutation of polytheism by examining every conceivable way two gods could produce a single effect. The author, a master of kalam, systematically shows that each scenario is rationally impossible, thereby proving that the existence of two gods is false. As you read, pay attention to how he builds his argument step by step.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the three possible ways two agents could produce a single effect, demonstrating that the existence of two gods is impossible. The author first enumerates three divisions: (1) the effect occurs by both together, (2) by one of them, or (3) by one without the other. Each is shown to be impossible. For the first, if each agent is independent and the act becomes necessary with each, then the necessity of occurrence by each prevents attribution to the other, leading to a contradiction when combined. For the second, if it does not occur by either, then the prevention from each is due to the other's occurrence, but the effect requires a cause, so it must occur by both, which contradicts non-occurrence. For the third, if the agents are equal in influence and power, occurrence by one without the other would be an arbitrary preponderance without a preponderator, which is impossible. The argument follows the principle that effects require causes and equal possibilities need a preponderator to determine one side. The conclusion is that all three divisions are false, so the existence of two gods is false.
