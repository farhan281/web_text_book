Definition of Tanzih and Proof against Composition
Defines tanzih and presents three proofs against composition in God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of tanzih (divine transcendence) and the rational proofs that God cannot be composed of parts.

Core Idea
God cannot be composite because composition implies need, and need implies contingency.

In this passage, the author defines tanzih as negating from God anything that does not befit His majesty. He then focuses on the first aspect: negating multiplicity by composition. He provides three logical proofs demonstrating why composition is impossible for the Necessary Being. These arguments form the foundation of Islamic monotheism (tawhid).

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the English translation to grasp the overall argument.
Notice the author's concise style and logical progression.
2
Identify the three proofs
The passage contains three distinct arguments against composition. Locate each one and note the reasoning used.
The first proof focuses on need, the second on the status of parts, the third on mutual need.
3
Understand the logical force
Reflect on why each proof leads to the conclusion that composition is impossible for God. Ask yourself what each argument assumes about the Necessary Being.
The proofs are independent but complementary.

قال : الفصل الثاني : في التنزيهات أي سلب ما لا يليق بالواجب عنه وفيه مباحث : الأول : في نفي الكثرة عنه بحسب الأجزاء ، بأن يتركب من جزأين فأكثر وبحسب الجزئيات بأن يكون الموجود واجبين أو أكثر، واستدل على نفي التركيب، بأن كل مركب محتاج إلى الجزء الذي هو غيره، وكل محتاج إلى الغير ممكن، لأن ذاته دون ملاحظة الغير لا يكون كافياً في وجوده. وإن لم يكن ذلك الغير فاعلاً له خارجاً عنه، وبأن كل جزء منه، فإما أن يكون واجباً فيتعدد الواجب وسنبطل تعدد الواجب، أو لا فيحتاج الواجب إلى الممكن فيكون أولى بالإمكان. وبأنه إما أن يحتاج أحد الجزأين إلى الآخر فيكون ممكناً، ويلزم إمكان الواجب، أو لا فلا يلتئم منهما حقيقة واحدة، كالحجر الموضوع بجنب الإنسان.
He said: The second chapter: on the transcendent acts (tanzihāt), i.e., negating what is not fitting from the Necessary Being, and it contains investigations. The first: on negating multiplicity from Him with respect to parts, by being composed of two or more parts, and with respect to individuals, by there being two or more existent necessary beings. And he gave proof for the negation of composition: that every composite is in need of the part which is other than it, and everything in need of another is contingent, because its essence without consideration of the other is not sufficient for its existence, even if that other is not an efficient cause external to it; and that every part of it is either necessary, then the necessary would be multiple and we will refute multiplicity of the necessary, or not, then the necessary would be in need of the contingent and would be more deserving of contingency; and that either one of the two parts is in need of the other, making it contingent, and the contingency of the necessary would follow, or not, and then no single reality would be formed from them, like a stone placed next to a human.

Commentary
This passage is a combination of a definition and a multi-part proof. The author first defines 'tanzih' (transcendence) as negating from God anything unbecoming. Then he introduces the first investigation: negating multiplicity both in terms of parts (composition) and individuals (multiple gods). He then focuses on composition and gives three proofs. First: every composite needs its parts; need implies contingency, so the necessary cannot be composite. Second: each part is either necessary (leading to multiple necessary beings, which is false) or contingent (then the necessary depends on the contingent). Third: the two parts either need each other (contingency) or do not (no real union, like a stone next to a person). The conclusion is that composition is impossible for the necessary being. The three arguments follow a logical order: from dependency, from the status of parts, and from mutual need.

First Proof Against Multiple Gods: Composition Argument
Presents the first argument against multiple gods and answers an objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first rational argument against the possibility of multiple necessary beings and how the author defends it against the objection that shared necessity might be accidental.

Core Idea
If two beings shared the same necessity, they would be composite, which is impossible for the Necessary Being.

Sanusi begins his proof against multiple gods with a logical argument based on composition. He anticipates an objection that the shared attribute might be accidental, and refutes it by showing that necessity (wujūb) must be identical to the essence. This lesson unpacks that argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice how the objection begins with 'لا يقال' and the answer with 'لأنا نقول'.
2
Identify the three parts of the argument
The passage has a proof, an objection, and an answer. Find each part and understand how they connect.
The proof is from 'واستدل' to 'وهو محال'. The objection is from 'لا يقال' to 'لا يوجب التركب'. The answer is from 'لأنا نقول' to the end.
3
Analyze the proof's logic
Break down the proof: if two necessary beings exist, they must be distinct; distinction involves a differentiating factor; that factor is not the same as the shared factor (necessity); thus each being is composed of two parts, which is impossible for the necessary.
The key move is that composition implies contingency, and the necessary must be simple.
4
Understand the objection and answer
The objection says composition only follows if necessity is constitutive (part of the essence). The answer proves necessity is identical to the essence, not accidental, by showing that if it were accidental, the essence would cause it, leading to circular priority.
The phrase 'علة متقدمة على المعلول' is crucial.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
واستدل على امتناع تعدد الواجب بوجوه : الأول : لو كان الواجب مشتركاً بين اثنين لكان بينهما تمايز لامتناع الاثنينية بدون التمايز وما به التمايز غير ما به الاشتراك ضرورة. فيلزم تركب كل من الواجبين مما به الاشتراك، وما به الامتياز وهو محال . لا يقال هذا، إنما يلزم لو كان الوجوب المشترك مقوماً، وهو ممنوع الجواز أن يكون عارضاً، والاشتراك في العارض مع الامتياز بخصوصه لا يوجب التركب. لأنا نقول وجوب الواجب نفس ماهيته. إذ لو كان عارضاً لها كان ممكناً معللاً بها، إذ لو علل بغيرها لم يكن ذاتياً ، وإذا علل بها يلزم تقدمها على نفسه . لأن العلة متقدمة على المعلول بالوجود، والوجوب . وإذا كان الوجوب نفس الماهية، كان الاشتراك فيها اشتراكاً في الماهية. والماهية مع الخصوصية مركبة قطعاً.
And he gave proof for the impossibility of multiple necessary beings by [several] aspects. The first: If the necessary was shared between two, there would necessarily be differentiation between them, because two-ness is impossible without differentiation. And that by which differentiation occurs is other than that by which sharing occurs, necessarily. So it follows that each of the two necessary beings is composed of that by which sharing occurs and that by which distinction occurs, and that is impossible. It cannot be said: this only follows if the shared necessity is constitutive (muqawwim), and it is not admitted that it could be an accident; and sharing in an accident with specific distinction does not necessitate composition. For we say: the necessity of the necessary is its very essence. For if it were an accident to it, it would be contingent, caused by the essence, because if it were caused by something else it would not be essential; and if it is caused by the essence, it would imply the essence's precedence over itself, because the cause is prior to the effect in existence. And if necessity is the very essence, then sharing in it is sharing in the essence. And the essence together with particularity is definitely composite.
Source pages p. 32

Commentary
This passage is a proof followed by an objection-and-answer. The author presents the first proof against multiple necessary beings: if two shared the same necessity, they would need to be differentiated; the differentiating factor is not the same as the shared factor, so each would be composed of a common essence and a differentiating accident, making them composite—impossible for the necessary. An objector argues that composition only follows if the shared necessity is constitutive (part of the essence), but it could be an accidental attribute. The author answers that necessity cannot be an accident, because if it were accidental, the essence would cause it, leading to circular priority. Therefore, necessity is identical to the essence, so sharing necessity means sharing essence, and essence with particularity yields composition.

Second Objection & Independent Proof against Multiplicity
Handles a second objection and offers an independent proof against multiple necessary beings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second objection to multiplicity of the necessary being and the independent proof that refutes it.

Core Idea
The particularity distinguishing two necessary beings cannot be accidental without causing dependence.

This passage presents a second objection against the possibility of multiple necessary beings. The objector suggests that the particularity (khāṣṣiyya) distinguishing two necessary beings could be an accident. The answer demonstrates that such an accident would inevitably imply dependence, which contradicts necessary existence. The author then reframes this as an independent proof: the determination (ta'ayyun) that distinguishes hypothetical multiple necessary beings must be identical to or derived from the essence, or it would entail need—both impossible.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the opening phrase 'If it is said...' to grasp the objector's claim that particularity could be accidental.
This is a common line of argument: try to explain away the distinction as non-essential.
2
Trace the answer's logic
Notice how the answer breaks down the possibilities: the accident is caused by the essence, its attributes, or an external cause. Each leads to contradiction.
Pay attention to the phrase 'leading to need' — this is the key flaw.
3
Recognize the independent proof
The passage then generalizes the reasoning: if multiple necessary beings existed, their determining factor (ta'ayyun) must be either identical to the essence or caused by it, or else it would require an external cause, implying need.
The proof is structured as a disjunction (either A or B or C) leading to impossibility.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Even though the text cuts off, the conclusion is that multiplicity is impossible. Consider why each option fails.
This proof reinforces tawhid through rational necessity.

Source Text

فإن قيل : لم لا يجوز أن تكون الخصوصية من العوارض . قلنا : لأنها تكون معللة بالماهية ، أو بما تقوم بها من الصفات وهو ينافي التعدد المفروض ، إذ الواجب لا يكون بدون تلك الخصوصية أو بأمر منفصل؛ فيلزم الاحتياج المنافي للوجوب الوجود. وهذا يصلح أن يجعل دليلاً مستقلاً. بأن يقال : لو تعدد الواجب فالتعين الذي به الامتياز إن كان نفس الماهية الواجبة أو معللاً بها أو بلازمها فلا تعدد وإن كان معللاً بأمر منفصل فلا [؟]

And this can serve as an independent proof. For it could be said: If the necessary being is multiple, then the determination (ta'ayyun) by which distinction occurs: if it is the necessary essence itself, or caused by it, or by its necessary concomitant, then there is no multiplicity; and if it is caused by a separate thing, then [no multiplicity?] …

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer followed by an independent proof. The objector suggests that the particularity (khāṣṣiyya) distinguishing two necessary beings could be an accident. The answer: such an accident would be caused either by the essence or its attributes, which contradicts the scenario of two independent necessary beings (since each would depend on the other's essence?), or by an external cause, leading to need—impossible for the necessary. The author then notes that this reasoning can stand as an independent proof: if two necessary beings existed, the determining factor (ta'ayyun) that distinguishes them must either be the essence itself (then no distinction), caused by it or its concomitant (still not separate), or caused externally (then need). Thus multiplicity is impossible. The text is cut off, but the conclusion is clear.

Why God Cannot Be Called a Body Even Metaphorically
Author states prohibition of applying 'body' to God even in a metaphorical sense.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the term 'body' cannot be applied to Allah even in a metaphorical sense, based on both revelation and reason.

Core Idea
Calling Allah a body is forbidden by revelation and misleading by reason.

Sanusi begins by establishing a fundamental rule: even if someone intends a harmless meaning (like 'existing'), it is not permissible to call Allah a body. The Lawgiver has not permitted it, and reason warns that it leads to anthropomorphism and Christian Trinitarian errors. This lesson clarifies this foundational prohibition.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then its translation. Notice the two types of prohibition: sam'an (by revelation) and 'aqlan (by reason).
The author mentions both categories to cover all sources of knowledge.
2
Identify the two reasons
Underline the two reasons given: (1) the Lawgiver did not permit it, (2) it suggests anthropomorphic views and Christian doctrines. Reflect on why each is important.
The second reason shows that even metaphorical usage can lead to serious misbelief.
3
Ask: why start here?
Consider why Sanusi opens the refutation with this prohibition. It sets the stage: before examining arguments, he establishes that the very term is inappropriate.
This prevents the opponent from claiming that they use 'body' in a different sense.

Source Text

أن يجزأ على ذلك، ولا على إطلاق الجسم عليه بمعنى الموجود . إما سمعاً فلعدم إذن الشارع، وإما عقلا فلإيهامه بما عليه المجسمة من كونه جسماً بالمعنى المشهور، ولما عليه النصارى من أنه جوهر واحد ثلاثة أقانيم على ما سيجيء . وأما القائلون بحقيقة الجسمية والحيز والجهة فقد بنوا مذهبهم على قضايا وهمية كاذبة تستلزمها، وعلى ظواهر آيات وأحاديث مشير بها. أما الأول : فكقولهم كل موجود فهو إما جسم أو حال في جسم والواجب يمتنع أن يكون حالاً في الجسم لامتناع احتياجه فيتعين أن يكون جسماً . وكقولهم : كل موجود إما متحيز أو حال في التحيز ويتعين كونه متحيزاً لما مر، وكقولهم : الواجب إما متصل بالعالم أو منفصل عنه ، وأياً ما كان يكون في جهة منه . وكقولهم : الواجب إما داخل في العالم فيكون متحيزاً أو خارجاً عنه فيكون في جهة منه ، ويدعون في صحة هذه المنفصلات، وتمام انحصارها الضرورة والجواب : المنع كيف وليس تركيبها عن الشيء ونقيضه، أو المساوى لنقيضه، وأطبق أكثر العقلاء على خلافها، وعلى أن الموجود إما جسم أو جسماني، أو ليس بجسم، ولا جسماني، وكذا باقي التقسيمات المذكورة، والجزم بالانحصار في القسمين، إنما هو من الأحكام الكاذبة للوهم، ودعوى الضرورة مبنية على العناد والمكابرة، أو على أن الوهميات كثيراً ما تشتبه بالأوليات .
It is not permissible to do that, nor to apply the term 'body' to Him in the sense of 'existing' — neither by revelation because the Lawgiver did not permit it, nor by reason because it suggests what the anthropomorphists (al-mujassima) hold, that He is a body in the well-known sense, and also what the Christians hold, that He is one substance and three hypostases, as will come. As for the first: such as their saying, 'Every existent is either a body or an accident in a body, and the Necessary Existent cannot be an accident in a body because of the impossibility of His needing it, so He must be a body.' And their saying, 'Every existent is either located in space (mutahayyiz) or an accident in what is located, and He must be located for the same reason.' And their saying, 'The Necessary Existent is either connected to the world or separate from it, and in either case He would be in a direction from it.' And their saying, 'The Necessary Existent is either inside the world, in which case He would be located, or outside it, in which case He would be in a direction from it.' And they claim necessity (al-darura) for the validity of these disjunctions and their complete exhaustiveness. The answer: denial — how so? For their composition is not from a thing and its opposite, or equivalent to its opposite. Most rational people have agreed contrary to them, and on the fact that the existent is either a body or a corporeal (jismani) or is neither a body nor corporeal. And likewise the remaining mentioned divisions. And certainty of exhaustiveness in the two divisions is only among the false judgments of the imagination (al-wahm). And the claim of necessity is based on obstinacy and contrariness, or on the fact that imaginary premises (al-wahmiyyat) often resemble primary ones (al-awwaliyat).

Commentary
This passage refutes the rational arguments of those who affirm that God is a body (jism) or has location (hayyiz) and direction (jiha). It begins by stating that it is not permissible to apply the term 'body' to God even in the sense of 'existing' (al-mawjud), because scripture does not allow it and rational consideration warns against leading to anthropomorphism. Then the author turns to the proponents of corporeality (al-qā'ilūn bi-ḥaqīqat al-jismiyya). They base their doctrine on false wahmiyya (imaginary) premises and on apparent meanings of texts. The author first presents their rational arguments (al-awwal): they claim that every existent is either a body or an accident (ḥāl) in a body; the Necessary Existent cannot be an accident, so He must be a body. Similarly, they argue that every existent is either located in space (mutahayyiz) or an accident in what is located, so God must be located. They also present dichotomies about connection to the world and interior/exterior. They claim these disjunctions are exhaustive and necessary. The author's answer (al-jawāb) is simple denial (al-manʿ). He points out that their disjunctions are not genuine contradictions (not a thing and its opposite). Moreover, most rational people (al-ʿuqalāʾ) reject them, agreeing instead that an existent can be a body, a corporeal (jismānī, i.e., something associated with a body), or neither. The claimed exhaustive division into two categories is a false judgment of the imagination (al-wahm). The claim of necessity (ḍarūra) is either obstinacy or confusion of wahmiyyāt with awwaliyyāt (self-evident truths).

The Anthropomorphists' Four Rational Arguments
Author presents the four rational arguments of those who affirm corporeality for God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the four rational arguments used by anthropomorphists to claim that God must be a body or located in space.

Core Idea
Anthropomorphists reduce existence to a false dichotomy: body or accident.

After establishing the prohibition, Sanusi turns to the arguments of those who affirm corporeality (al-qā'ilūn bi-ḥaqīqat al-jismiyya). The claim of necessity is central to their argument.

First Rational Proof: Impossibility of Two Independent Agents on One Effect

Learning Objective

Core Idea
One effect cannot have two independent agents—only Allah's power suffices.

Al-Taftazani begins with a rational argument: the servant's act is possible, and every possible is subject to Allah's power. The proof is structured as a modus tollens: if P (servant creates) then Q (knows details), but not Q, therefore not P.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and clarification of the Ash'ari position on moral good and evil (al-husn wa al-qubh) against the Mu'tazila. It begins by stating the core thesis: the intellect has no judgment of good and evil in the sense of deserving praise (madḥ) and blame (dhamm) from God. This is contrary to the Mu'tazila. The author then clarifies that other meanings of good and evil—such as attribute of perfection/imperfection (like knowledge vs. ignorance) or compatibility with purpose (like justice vs. injustice)—are not disputed; they are rationally knowable. The Ash'ari position is that good is defined by God's command (al-amr) and evil by His prohibition (al-nahy); they are identical, not that command follows an inherent goodness. The Mu'tazila hold that command follows the goodness of an act, and prohibition follows its evilness; if these were not perceived by reason, revelation would merely uncover (kāshif) what is already true, not declare (mubayyin) what becomes obligatory. The author then explains why this topic is included among discussions of God's acts, even though God's acts are not described as good/evil in this sense, because the opponent relates these moral properties to divine acts. The well-known summary is that for Ash'aris good and evil are revelational (shar'iyyān), for Mu'tazila rational ('aqliyyān). The author stresses that the dispute is narrowly about divine recompense: whether an act in itself, by its essential or accidental attributes, makes the doer deserve reward or punishment. The Ash'ari view is that only revelation determines this; the intellect cannot deem an act good or evil in God's judgment. An act becomes good simply by being commanded, and evil by being prohibited, even if God were to change the ruling. The Mu'tazila hold that the act has an intrinsic aspect (jiha muḥassina or muqabbiḥa) that grounds the divine ruling.

Thus, according to Ash'aris, reason does not judge an act to be good or bad in God's sight; rather, what is commanded is good and what is prohibited is bad, without any inherent aspect of goodness or badness in the act itself or in its attributes. Even if God were to command what He prohibited, it would become good, and vice versa. For the Mu'tazilites, acts have an aspect that makes them good or bad in God's judgment, which reason perceives either necessarily (e.g., beneficial truthfulness is good, harmful lying is bad) or reflectively (e.g., beneficial lying is good, harmful truthfulness is bad) or by revelation (e.g., fasting on Arafa is good, fasting on Eid is bad).

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection raised against the second Ashʿarī proof and how the Ashʿarīs defended their position.

Core Idea
Even if the will preponderates, the chain must end in an external cause, nullifying human independence.

The second proof faced an objection: the preponderator is the servant's will, which permits action without necessity. The Ashʿarī response argues that the will itself must be determined by something external, ultimately leading to compulsion and the impossibility of moral evaluation.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
The objector claims that the preponderator is the servant's will, which allows the act without compulsion. Note the exception of Abu al-Ḥusayn.
The will is said to 'specify' (takhṣīṣ) one alternative.
2
Understand the concession
Even if one concedes that the act becomes necessary through choice (wujūb bi al-ikhtiyār), the objector says this does not negate choice or imply compulsion.
This is a common Muʿtazilī argument.
3
Examine the Ashʿarī rejoinder
The reply asserts that the chain of preponderators must end in an external cause that necessitates the act, nullifying the servant's independence. Such an act cannot be good or evil.
This is the core of the proof: true independence is impossible.
4
Note the final dismissal
A secondary objection about the act of the Creator is dismissed by referring to an earlier answer.
The author assumes the reader has already encountered this.

Second: If good and evil were by reason, then none of the acts of servants would be good or evil rationally, and the consequent is false by your own admission. The aspect of the implication: The act of the servant is either compulsive (iḍṭirārī) or accidental (ittifāqī). Neither of them is good or evil rationally. As for the major premise, it is by agreement. As for the minor premise, the servant either is not able to refrain – then that; or if he is able, then if the act does not depend on a preponderator (murajjiḥ), but rather emanates from him sometimes and not at other times without a new occurrence, it is accidental, leading to preponderance without a preponderator; and in this is the blockage of the door to proving the Maker. And if it does depend, then that preponderator if it is from the servant, the discourse is transferred to it and it leads to infinite regress; if it is not, then with it, if the act is not necessary, but rather both emanation and non-emanation are possible, the equivocation returns and the detriment occurs; if it is necessary, the act is compulsive and the servant is coerced. And it was objected that the preponderator is the will (irāda) which is characterized by preponderating and specifying, and the emanation of the act with it according to us is on the way of permissibility (ṣiḥḥa) not obligation, except according to Abu al-Ḥusayn. And even if it is conceded, obligation by choice does not negate choice nor necessitate compulsion which contradicts goodness and the validity of obligation. And it was answered: It has been established by proof the necessity of ending at a preponderator that is not from the servant, and the act is necessary with it, and the independence of the servant is nullified, and something like this is neither good nor evil, nor is obligation valid with it according to them. As for the objection that it is independence in the face of necessity, and it is refuted by the act of the Creator – you have already known its answer.

Commentary
This lesson contains the second proof of the Ashʿarīs, which is a complex argument linking moral evaluation to determinism. The proof claims that if good/evil were determined by reason, then no human act would be rationally good or evil. This is shown by analyzing the nature of human acts: every act is either compulsive (iḍṭirārī) – the agent has no power to refrain – or accidental (ittifāqī) – it occurs without a sufficient reason. Neither type is subject to moral judgment. The argument further explores causation: if an act occurs without a preponderator (murajjiḥ), it is accidental; if it depends on a preponderator, that preponderator must ultimately come from outside the servant, making the act compulsive. An objection is raised that the preponderator is the servant's will, which does not compel but merely makes the act possible. The reply asserts that the chain of preponderators must end in a cause outside the servant, which necessitates the act and nullifies the servant's independence, thereby eliminating the basis for moral evaluation. This proof ties into the Ashʿarī doctrine of acquisition (kasb) and the denial of human causal efficacy.

Key Takeaway
The Ashʿarīs maintain that any chain of causation must end in an external necessity, making human acts determined and thus outside the scope of rational moral judgment.
