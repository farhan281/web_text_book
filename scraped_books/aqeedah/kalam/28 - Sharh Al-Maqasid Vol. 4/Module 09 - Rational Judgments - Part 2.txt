Third Ash'ari Proof: Lying for a Noble Cause
Al-Taftazani argues that lying can be good when saving a prophet, disproving intrinsic evil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Ash'aris use the example of saving a prophet to argue that moral values are not intrinsic but are determined by divine command.

Core Idea
Lying can be good when it saves a prophet – proving moral value depends on context.

This lesson presents the third Ash'ari proof against the idea that acts are inherently good or evil. Al-Taftazani argues that if lying were evil in its essence, it would never be good in any situation. But we find that lying to save a prophet from destruction is obligatory and therefore good, showing that moral value is not intrinsic.

Second Argument: Conditional and Division of Actions
Al-Taftazani presents the conditional and divides actions into compulsory and accidental.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure of the second argument against rational good and evil, specifically the conditional statement and the division of human actions into compulsory and accidental.

Core Idea
Human actions are either compulsory or accidental; neither can be judged by reason alone.

Al-Taftazani now presents a powerful argument: if goodness and badness were determined by reason, then human actions would be evaluated rationally. But he shows that all human actions fall into two categories — compulsory or accidental — and neither can be rationally good or bad. This passage lays the foundation for the detailed analysis to come.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the concise style of kalam argumentation.
2
Identify the conditional structure
The passage begins with 'If...then...'. Identify the antecedent and consequent. The antecedent is the assumption of rational good and evil; the consequent is that some human actions would be rationally good or bad.
3
Understand the division of actions
The author divides all human actions into two types: compulsory and accidental. Ask yourself whether these two categories indeed cover all possibilities.
Compulsory means the actor has no power to refrain; accidental means it happens without a specific cause or preponderant factor.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The author claims that neither compulsory nor accidental actions can be rationally good or bad. Why might that be? Think about the requirements for rational moral judgment.
This sets up the need for a preponderator, which is explored in the next lesson.

The second: If goodness and badness were by reason, then no human action would be good or bad rationally — and the consequent is false by your own admission. Commentary
This passage is a proof that goodness and badness cannot be known by reason alone, as the Mu'tazila claim. The argument is structured as a conditional syllogism: if rational good and evil existed, then some human actions would be rationally good or bad; but the consequent is false (by Mu'tazilite admission), so the antecedent is false. The author justifies the entailment by dividing human actions into two exhaustive categories: compulsory (idṭirārī) and accidental (ittifāqī). Neither can be rationally good or bad. He then unpacks the minor premise: if the servant cannot refrain from an action, it is compulsory. If he can refrain but the action occurs without a preponderant factor, it is accidental and leads to the problematic doctrine of preponderance without a preponderator, which undermines proofs for the Creator. If the action depends on a preponderant factor, and that factor is from the servant, an infinite regress follows; if from outside, and the action is not necessitated, the problem returns; if necessitated, the action is compulsory and the servant is coerced — thus no rational moral judgment applies. The author then gives an objection: the preponderator is the servant's will, which does not necessitate the action (except in Abu al-Husayn's view). The answer is that even if will is involved, it ultimately leads to an external necessitating factor, nullifying the servant's independence. Hence such actions are neither good nor bad rationally.

Fourth Argument: The liar paradox and intrinsic goodness
Al-Taftazani uses a version of the liar paradox to refute intrinsic good and evil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the liar paradox is used to demonstrate that good and evil cannot be intrinsic properties of actions.

Core Idea
Intrinsic moral qualities lead to logical contradictions like the liar paradox.

This passage presents the fourth argument against the Mu'tazilite doctrine of intrinsic moral qualities. Al-Taftazani uses a logical paradox—similar to the famous liar paradox—to show that if good and evil were objective and knowable by reason, then even a simple future statement would force a contradiction. The argument proceeds through several formulations, each exposing the impossibility of intrinsic moral attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Let the logical structure sink in without trying to analyze it deeply yet.
The passage is dense; do not worry if it seems confusing at first.
2
Understand the first formulation
Focus on the statement 'I will lie tomorrow.' Follow the two cases: if the statement is true, the speaker is truthful (good) but entails lying tomorrow (bad). If false, the speaker is lying (bad) but entails not lying tomorrow (good). In both cases, good and bad combine.
This is the core of the paradox.

Resolving the Liar Paradox: Distinction Between Property and Predicate
Al-Taftazani resolves the liar paradox with a distinction between property-of-judgment and predicate-judgment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Taftazani's resolution of the liar paradox through the distinction between truth as a property of a judgment (hal li al-hukm) and truth as a predicate (hukm) of a thing.

Core Idea
Truth and falsehood can coexist when one is a property of judgment and the other a predicate.

Having presented the liar paradox as an objection against inherent goodness, al-Taftazani now offers his own resolution. He distinguishes between truth as a property (hal) of the judgment itself and truth as a predicate (hukm) predicated of a thing. This distinction shows that in self-referential statements, truth and falsehood apply to different referents and therefore do not constitute a genuine contradiction. However, he humbly concludes that the best answer is to admit inability to fully solve the difficulty.

They are about different things, so no contradiction.

He presents a solution but acknowledges its limitations.

For if both the tomorrow statement and the yesterday statement are true, it entails their untruth, and vice versa. This is a fallacy that confuses the minds of the intelligent and the elites of the clever. For this reason I named it the fallacy of 'deaf root'. I have perused the sayings and found nothing that quenches the thirst, and I reflected much, but nothing appeared except the least of little, which is: that truth or falsehood, just as it can be a property (hal) of the judgment—i.e., of the affirmative or negative relation to the concomitant in all propositions—it can also be a judgment (hukm)—i.e., a predicated thing, predicated of a thing derivatively, as in our saying: 'This is truthful' and 'That is a liar.' And they are contradictory only if they are considered as two properties of one judgment, or two judgments on one subject, as opposed to the case when one is considered a property of the judgment and the other a judgment, because of the clear difference of referent—as in our saying: 'The sky is beneath us... truthful or false'—or subtle difference... as in the particular proposition which is the locus of the fallacy. So if we suppose it false, nothing follows except the truth of its contradictory, which is our saying: 'This statement is truthful.' So truth occurs as a judgment of the particular proposition, not as a property of its judgment; rather the property of its judgment is falsehood, as we supposed. And truth is a property of the affirmative relation which is the judgment of the contradictory, and a judgment of the particular proposition which is the original. So they have not combined as two properties of one judgment nor as two judgments on one subject. Likewise if we suppose it true. In that case, perhaps the respondent may deny the contradiction of the mutually entailing truth and falsehood, based on one of them referring to the judgment of the particular proposition and the other to its subject. But the correct view in my opinion in this matter is to refrain from answering and to admit inability to solve the difficulty.

Commentary
This passage is an objection against the theory that goodness and badness are inherent (dhatiyy) in actions, followed by a detailed response. The objector argues that if goodness and badness were inherent, then a self-referential statement like 'I will certainly lie tomorrow' would entail a contradiction: both goodness and badness would simultaneously apply to the same statement. He illustrates this with an example. However, he concludes that the best course is to refrain from a definitive answer and admit inability to resolve the difficulty, showing intellectual humility.

Core Idea
Not all impossibilities are equal; the ranks depend on the source of impossibility.

In this passage, Sanusi clarifies a key dispute in kalam: Is it permissible for God to obligate what is impossible? He first notes that some scholars even allow obligating the inherently impossible, while others consider any obligation of what God knows will not occur as 'unbearable'. Commentary
This passage classifies the three ranks of 'what cannot be borne' (ma la yutaq) in the debate on obligating the impossible. The author first notes the views of some scholars who permit obligating even the inherently impossible, and others who consider obligating what God knows will not occur as 'taklif ma la yutaq'. He then states that the ranks are three: (1) the lowest rank: what is impossible only due to God's knowledge, will, or information—there is consensus that such obligation actually occurs (e.g., the disbeliever is held accountable) and is permissible. (2) the highest rank: what is inherently impossible, like combining opposites—there is hesitation about its conceivability and thus its obligation. The author discusses whether the impossible can be conceived, referencing discussions in Ibn Sina's Shifa. (3) the middle rank: what is possible in itself but not within the servant's power, either inherently (creating a body) or customarily (ascending to heaven). This is the actual point of dispute: is it permissible to obligate such acts in the sense of demanding performance and punishing failure, as opposed to the challenge (tahaddi) which is meant to show incapacity? The author notes that such challenge obligations are unquestionably 'ma la yutaq'.

Arguments of the Permitters for Obligating the Seemingly Unbearable
Sanusi enumerates four arguments of the permiters for taklif ma la yutaq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four arguments (wujuh) presented by the permiters (al-mujawwizun) for the permissibility of obligating what appears unbearable, and Sanusi's critical observation regarding the second and third arguments.

Core Idea
The permiters argue from Quran, power, timing, and divine foreknowledge.

The permiters (al-mujawwizun) advance four distinct arguments to show that taklif ma la yutaq is not only possible but actual. Their first argument cites Quranic verses as cases of incapacitation; the second argues from divine power; the third from the timing of power; and the fourth from the obligation of the disbeliever. As you read, pay attention to how Sanusi notes that the second and third arguments, if taken literally, would lead to absurdity.

Read the passage and translation

The second argument states that acts are created by Allah's power, so they are not within the servant's power—yet the obligation is related to the servant's power. The third argument distinguishes between 'qudra' (soundness of causes) and 'istita'a' (ability with the act).

Sanusi observes that if the second and third arguments were taken at face value, they would imply that all obligations are 'taklif ma la yutaq', which is false. This shows a flaw in those arguments.
This critique is important—not all permitter arguments are equally valid.
5

The fourth argument cites the consensus case of a disbeliever known to die in disbelief being obliged to believe, even though belief is impossible from him due to God's foreknowledge.
This argument uses divine foreknowledge to show that obligation can exist despite apparent impossibility.

So they imagined that conveying bliss to one who did not do righteous deeds is of this kind. But there is no hiddenness that this is either on the assumption of obligation (taklif) or on the assumption of its absence and that humans are not obligated with any command or prohibition. So how can one imagine evil in bestowing eternal joy upon him without causing harm to others?

Commentary
This passage presents the final answer (jawab) to the Mu'tazilite position. The author first states that he does not accept the premise that reward and exaltation are not good without prior merit. He then argues on two levels: (1) If we adopt the Ash'ari view that nothing is evil for God (no rational good or evil), the point is obvious—God may reward without merit. (2) Even if we accept rational good and evil (as the Mu'tazilites do), conferring benefit without harm to the giver or anyone else is pure generosity (karm) and wisdom (hikma), not injustice. The Mu'tazilites' error stems from confusing two things: merit obtained through works (istihqāq) and the fitness (liyāqa) of the gift to the recipient. Giving something inappropriate (like exalting children or animals) is not generous, but giving eternal bliss to a human being—a form of existence capable of receiving it—is appropriate whether or not they performed good deeds. This holds true whether we assume the existence of obligation (taklif) or not. Therefore, there is no rational evil in God bestowing eternal joy without prior works. Thus the Mu'tazilite argument collapses.

Mu'tazila: Lutf, Tawfiq, and 'Isma
Mu'tazila define lutf, divide it into muhassil and muqarrib, and assign tawfiq and 'isma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila definition of divine grace (lutf), its two types (muhassil and muqarrib), and how tawfiq and 'isma relate to it.

Core Idea
Lutf is divine grace that aids free choice without compulsion.

The Mu'tazila developed a nuanced theory of divine grace to reconcile divine omnipotence with human free will. In this passage, they define lutf (grace) and its subtypes, showing how God assists believers toward obedience while preserving their moral responsibility. This concept also explains their interpretation of certain Quranic verses about divine will.
