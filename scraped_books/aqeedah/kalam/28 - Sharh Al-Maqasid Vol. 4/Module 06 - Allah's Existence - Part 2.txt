Sixth Argument: Agreement or Impediment
Al-Taftazani presents the sixth argument against multiple gods: agreement leads to coordination, disagreement leads to mutual impediment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why two gods cannot coexist either in agreement or disagreement.

Core Idea
Whether they agree or disagree, the existence of two gods leads to incoherence.

This brief but powerful argument presents a dilemma: if two gods agree on an action, you have two agents on one act (coordination, which is impossible); if they disagree, they hinder each other, showing imperfection.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument
Read the short Arabic and translation. Notice it presents two options.
2
Examine 'agreement'
Understand why two agents performing the same act is impossible (it would be a single act with two independent causes).
This is the 'توارد' problem.
3
Examine 'disagreement'
Mutual impediment means each god can hinder the other, implying imperfection.

The ways of the theologians in negating multiplicity and plurality.
Fourth: If there were two gods and the intended action occurred. Either it must be as it ought to be for inference, or by both of them, necessitating [an act] between two capable beings; or by one of them, necessitating preponderance without a preponderator, because the relationship of possible acts to them is equal. For capability is by essence and possibility is by [the thing's] possibility.
Fifth: If one of them willed a thing, then if the other is unable to will its opposite, he is incapable, since there is no obstacle except the attachment of the first's power. But if he is able, then from the assumption of its occurrence either the occurrence of the two opposites follows, which is impossible, or their non-occurrence, which is an incapacity for both, along with impossibility in such a case as the motion of a body and its rest; or the occurrence of only one of them, which is a preponderance without a preponderator, along with the incapacity of the one whose will did not occur.
Sixth: If they agree upon an action, coordination follows; otherwise, mutual impediment.
Seventh: What distinguishes them: if it is among the necessary concomitants of divinity, it is false; otherwise, it is possible for it to be removed, and then the duality is removed.
Eighth: There is no proof for the second [god], so its negation is necessary, otherwise impossibilities would follow.
Ninth: If there were multiple, they would not be finite, since there is no priority.
Tenth: The scriptural evidences from consensus and definitive texts, and in some of what preceded there is weakness not hidden.

Commentary
This passage enumerates ten arguments (طرق) used by theologians to negate the possibility of multiple gods. It begins with the heading 'The ways of the theologians in negating multiplicity and plurality.' Then each argument is presented as a numbered point:

1. Fourth argument: If two gods existed, for any intended action, either it is performed by both, which would entail a single act being between two independent agents (impossible), or by one, which would require a preponderator without any preponderating factor, since both are equal in capability and the act is equally possible for both.

2. Fifth argument: If one god wills something, the other either cannot will the opposite (then he is incapable) or can. If he can, then assuming both wills occur, either both opposites occur (impossible) or neither occurs (incapacity for both, impossible), or only one occurs (preponderance without preponderator).

3. Sixth argument: If they agree on an action, it would be a single act by two agents (coordinated); otherwise, mutual impediment.

4. Seventh argument: Whatever differentiates them must be either a necessary concomitant of divinity (which would be false, as divinity is one) or accidental, in which case it can be removed, and thus duality is removed.

5. Eighth argument: There is no proof for a second god, so it must be negated, otherwise impossibilities follow.

6. Ninth argument: If there were multiple, they would be infinite, as no priority exists.

7. Tenth argument: Scriptural evidences from consensus and definitive texts also refute polytheism, though some of the earlier rational arguments have weaknesses.

Seventh Argument: What Distinguishes Them
Al-Taftazani presents the seventh argument against multiple gods: the distinguishing feature leads to contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the distinguishing factor between multiple gods cannot be essential or accidental.

Core Idea
Whatever distinguishes two gods either undermines their divinity or their duality.

This argument looks at the basis of differentiation. If two gods are distinct, there must be a distinguishing feature. If that feature is essential to divinity, then each would be different, contradicting the concept of divinity. If accidental, it can be removed, and then they become one.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument
Read the Arabic and translation. It is a short conditional argument.
2
Understand the two possibilities
The distinguishing feature is either a necessary concomitant of divinity or not.
3
Analyze each possibility
If it is a necessary concomitant, then each god would have a different essence, but divinity is one. If it is accidental, it can be removed, and duality disappears.

Eighth Argument: No Proof for a Second
Al-Taftazani presents the eighth argument against multiple gods: absence of proof necessitates negation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument from lack of evidence for multiple gods.

Core Idea
If there were a second god, we would have evidence; absence of proof indicates impossibility.

This is an argument from the principle of sufficient reason. If a second god existed, there must be some indication of its existence. Since there is none, it cannot exist. Any attempt to assume otherwise leads to contradictions.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument
Read the Arabic and translation. It is a straightforward claim.
2
Understand the necessity of proof
For the existence of a second god, there must be a proof (دليل). Without proof, we must negate it.
3
Consider the consequence
If we do not negate it, impossibilities would follow, meaning the assumption itself is impossible.

Ninth Argument: Infinite Regress
Al-Taftazani presents the ninth argument against multiple gods: no priority leads to infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that multiple gods lead to infinite regress.

Core Idea
Multiple gods would have no ordering principle, leading to an infinite chain.

This argument states that if gods are multiple, there is no reason for any one to be first or prior. Hence they would be infinite, which is impossible for necessary beings.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument
Read the Arabic and translation. It is very brief.
2
Understand 'no priority'
If multiple gods exist, each is equally necessary; there is no reason for one to be prior to another. Therefore, the set would have no first element, implying infinity.
3
Why infinity is impossible
An actual infinite of necessary beings is absurd because necessary beings must be uncaused and non-composite; an infinite set would violate the principle of causality.

Tenth Argument: Scriptural Evidences
Al-Taftazani presents the tenth argument against multiple gods: scriptural evidences from consensus and definitive texts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the role of scriptural arguments and the acknowledgment of weaknesses in some rational arguments.

The Second Proof Against Indwelling: A Dilemma
Sanusi presents the second proof against indwelling as a dilemma and then records two objections to each horn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure of the second proof against indwelling (ḥulūl) and the initial objections raised against it.

Core Idea
Indwelling in a locus either makes God dependent or changes His independence—both impossible.

Sanusi presents the second proof against indwelling, structured as a dilemma: if God's indwelling in something is necessary, then He depends on that locus, implying contingency; if it is contingent, then He would be independent of it, but the nature of a dweller demands dependence, leading to a contradiction. Two objections challenge each horn of the dilemma, questioning whether dependence necessarily follows.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dilemma's two horns
Read the proof carefully: either indwelling is necessary (first horn) or contingent (second horn). Note the consequences drawn for each: dependence and contingency on the first, and change from independence to dependence on the second.
The proof aims to show both options lead to absurdities for the Necessary Being.
2
Examine the first objection
The first objection argues that on the necessary horn, dependence does not follow because God's essence could necessitate the locus without needing it, just as a cause necessitates its effects without needing them.
This objection targets the inference from 'necessitating' to 'needing'.
3
Examine the second objection
The second objection denies that a dweller is always dependent, using the analogy of a body dwelling in a space without being essentially dependent on that space.
This challenges the premise that 'the dweller must be dependent on the locus'.

The proof is a dilemma: if God's indwelling in something is necessary, then He depends on that locus, implying contingency (imkān) and that the locus is prior to Him, even necessary; if indwelling is contingent, then God would be independent of it, but the nature of a dweller (ḥāl) demands dependence, so He would change from independent to dependent. The Imām (likely al-Rāzī) formulated this. The first objection argues that on the first horn, necessity does not imply dependence because God's essence could necessitate the locus and the indwelling without needing them—just as a cause necessitates its effects without needing them. The second objection denies that a dweller is always dependent, giving the example of a body dwelling in a space without being essentially dependent on that space. The author then reiterates that if God were independent by essence (mustaghanī bi-l-dhāt), that would imply contingency and locus priority, which is absurd. Another objection says that lack of essential self-sufficiency does not entail essential need, so the conclusion does not follow. The answer asserts that mere lack of essential self-sufficiency entails contingency because the Necessary Being is essentially self-sufficient. Finally, the objection that absence of essential need does not preclude accidental need is dismissed as irrelevant, since accidental need would still conflict with necessary existence.

The Mu'tazila's claim of negating power from God Almighty. He said: And for them in negating power: That if He had power, it would not be connected to creating bodies, because the power of the witnessed [i.e., human] is not so except due to a common cause which is its being power. And because it either resembles the power of the witnessed or differs from it by the extent of its difference. We said: Perhaps the cause is more specific and the difference is greater. He said: And for them in negating power: The Mu'tazila adhere to the impossibility of the Creator Almighty being powerful by power, because if it were so, He would not be powerful to create bodies, and the consequent is false by consensus. Explanation of the entailment from two aspects: First, that the unsuitability of the slave's power for creating bodies is a common judgment; it must have a common cause, and it is nothing other than its being power. So if the Creator also had power, it would be the same. Second, that the power of the Creator, assuming its realization, either is similar to the power of servants, then it would follow that it is not suitable for creating bodies, because the judgment of similars is one; or it differs from it, and that difference is not greater than the difference among the powers of servants among themselves, yet none of them is suitable for creating bodies. So likewise that which differs by this amount of difference. The answer: We do not accept that a common judgment must have a common cause. Rather, it is permissible to explain it by different causes, since it is not impossible for different things to share a single necessary consequent. So here it is permissible to explain the unsuitability of the servants' powers for creating bodies by their particularities. Even if accepted, we do not accept that there is no commonality among them besides their being power; it is possible that it be something more specific than that, which includes the power of servants and does not include the power of the Creator. And we do not accept that the difference of the Creator's power from the servants' power is not greater than the difference among them [the servants' powers], because it is possible that it is characterized by a particularity not found in any of them, so it is suitable for creating bodies unlike them.

Commentary
This passage refutes the Mu'tazila argument that God cannot have power (qudra) because if He did, it would be incapable of creating bodies, similar to human power. The argument is presented in two proofs: (1) the unsuitability of human power for creating bodies is a common property derived from its being power, so God's power would also be unsuitable; (2) God's power would either be similar to human power (hence unsuitable) or differ, but the difference is no greater than differences among human powers, which still are all unsuitable. The author responds by denying that a common judgment requires a single common cause; different causes can produce the same effect. He also suggests that the common cause could be something more specific than 'being power' that excludes divine power. Additionally, God's power may have a unique particularity making it suitable for creating bodies, unlike any human power. The refutation thus preserves God's omnipotence by rejecting the analogy with human power.

Proof of God's Power (Qudra)
Sanusi enumerates three ways to complete the proof of God's power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God's power (qudra) is proven from the fact that He is a pre-eternal Maker who produces temporal effects, and the three ways to complete this proof.

Core Idea
God's power is proven because temporal acts cannot come from a necessitating cause.

In this passage, Sanusi lays the foundation for proving God's power (qudra). He argues that if God were a necessitating cause, the universe would have to be eternal along with Him, but since the universe is temporal, God must act by power and will. He then outlines three ways to establish this proof.

Commentary
This passage presents the key proof for God's power (qudra) based on His being a pre-eternal agent producing temporal acts. The author argues that if God were a necessitating cause (mūjib), the effect would have to be co-eternal with the cause, but since the world is temporal, God must be a powerful agent who acts by will. The author states that this proof requires establishing that some temporal events are directly from God without intermediary. This can be achieved by showing that God is pre-eternal and the world is temporal (as the theologians proved), or by showing that God cannot be a necessitating cause because He is a willing agent to whom events are attributed (which even the opponents accept), or by refuting the philosophers' claim of eternal motion whose temporal parts are conditions for further events. The author references earlier discussions on the impossibility of an infinite regress and the pre-eternity of motion. Then a quote from Imam al-Haramayn illustrates the impossibility of an infinite series of past events: if there were an infinite number of cycles before the present, it would be impossible to traverse one after another, like the example of cycles, parents, seeds, and hens. He contrasts this with an infinite future (like Paradise) which is not actualized all at once, but is successive. The example of conditional giving of a dirham and dinar shows that a condition of pre-existence leads to an impossible regress, while a condition of post-existence is possible because future events can succeed indefinitely.

Refutation of Infinite Past Events
Imam al-Haramayn refutes infinite past events and contrasts them with infinite future events using analogies.

The analogy of conditional giving illustrates the logical impossibility.

This latter type of regress (every event preceded by another without beginning) is a different kind of infinite regress whose impossibility must be proven separately. Thus, the objection's simplified proof is insufficient.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an objection against a standard proof of God's power (qudra). The author first makes a general statement: contingency (ḥudūth) contradicts the negation of priority but not the negation of posteriority. Then he presents an objection: one might argue that the proof can be formulated without needing either of the two mentioned requirements, citing a formulation from al-Mawāqif. That formulation states that if God were not powerful, four possibilities arise: either no contingent exists, or a contingent exists without an agent, or it ends with an eternal being but with regress, or it ends with an eternal being that necessitates a contingent without intermediary. The objection claims this is a simpler formulation. The author responds by saying that this is still the same well-known argument but with unnecessary added premises—namely the three conditionals (al-sharṭiyyāt al-thalāth). He explains: the discussion concerns the power of the eternal being to which everything ends, yet in the first two possibilities the consequent is identical to the antecedent, so the objector turned away from that. Then the author presents an alternative formulation: if the Creator were a necessitator by essence, the contingent would be eternal, because if it originated it would require a contingent condition leading to regress. But this formulation is incomplete unless one establishes certain things: that everything besides God is contingent, and the impossibility of successive contingents without end subsisting in His essence, or that each daily contingent is not dependent on another contingent preceded by another ad infinitum, preserved by a perpetual motion. The reason is that without these, the fourth conditional in the first formulation fails, and the absurdity in the second formulation arises because it is possible that contingents end with an eternal being that necessitates an eternal being by choice rather than necessitation, so no failure or regress occurs, and it is not proven that there is an eternal being necessitating a contingent without intermediary. Instead, every contingent could be preceded by another without beginning, as the philosophers claim for motions—this is a type of regress whose impossibility is not automatically accepted (unlike the regress of causes and effects). Therefore, one must also demonstrate the impossibility of this other type of regress (every contingent preceded by another without end) to complete the argument.

Two Arguments from Contingency and Necessitation
Taftazani presents the first two arguments for God's power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first two rational arguments Taftazani uses to prove that God is powerful: the argument from the termination of contingents and the argument against necessitation in creating the world.

Core Idea
God must be powerful because contingency ends in a Necessary Being and creation cannot be by blind necessitation.

Taftazani begins his enumeration of proofs for God's power with two closely related arguments. The first builds on the established conclusion that all contingent events must terminate in a Necessary Being. The second examines the nature of God's creative act, showing it cannot be a form of necessitation. Together, they lay the foundation for understanding God as a free agent.

The first argument says: since contingents end with the Necessary Being, He must be powerful. If not, then either He would necessitate a contingent without an intermediary (leading to delay) or not (leading to regress). Both are impossible.

Certainty from Design for the Truth Seeker

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the argument from design provides decisive certainty for the sincere seeker of truth, not mere probability.

Core Idea
The design in creation yields certainty, not probability, for the truthful seeker.

This passage from Sharh al-Maqasid addresses a common objection: that the argument from design only yields probability. The author insists that for a sincere seeker, the observed order of the heavens and earth leads to decisive certainty. We will explore the distinction between the philosopher's view and the seeker's attainment of certainty.

Commentary

Proof of God's Knowledge from the Order of Creation
The author presents a rational proof for God's knowledge using two premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof for God's knowledge (عالم) based on the perfect order and arrangement of the universe, as presented by the mutakallimun.

Core Idea
The perfect order of creation necessarily implies a knowing Creator.

This passage presents a foundational proof used by theologians to establish God's attribute of knowledge. It is based on a simple observation: whenever we see a perfectly arranged piece of work, we know with certainty that its maker possessed knowledge. The author applies this principle to the entire universe, demonstrating that its intricate order points directly to a knowledgeable Creator, and that relying solely on scripture for this is circular.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the structure: the author states the conclusion, then gives the proof with two premises.
The passage begins with 'المبحث الثالث في أنه عالم' and ends with 'وإلى ما يقول'.
2
Identify the two premises
The proof has a major premise (الكبرى) and a minor premise (الصغرى). Find each one and note how the author supports them.
The major premise is that a perfectly solid act implies a knowledgeable agent. The minor is that God created the universe with perfect order.
3
Understand the major premise
The author says the major premise is known by necessity (بالضرورة). Observe the example: beautiful lines or eloquent words indicate a knowing author. Think of examples in everyday life.
This is a rational intuition, not something learned from scripture.
4
Understand the minor premise
The minor premise is that God created the heavens, elements, minerals, plants, and animals with harmony and perfection. The author cites specific sciences (astronomy, anatomy) that reveal this order.
The sciences witness the intricate design that man cannot fully comprehend.
5
Reflect on the conclusion and the role of scripture
From the two premises, the conclusion follows necessarily: God is knowing. Note the author's remark that relying on scripture alone (التمسك بالسمعيات) is circular.
This proof is rational, not dependent on revelation, making it universally accessible.

Commentary
This passage presents the first proof for God's knowledge (عالم) from the order and perfection of creation. It begins by stating that according to us (the Ash'ari school), God is the Maker of the world with its order and solidity, and He is powerful and choosing. The proof is agreed upon by most rational people. The well-known argument of the mutakallimun has two premises (وجهان). The first premise (major) is that whoever produces a perfectly solid and accurate act is knowledgeable (عالم). This is known by necessity: when one sees beautiful lines or hears eloquent words that convey precise meanings, one knows with certainty that the agent is knowledgeable. The second premise (minor) is that God is the Creator of the celestial spheres, elements, minerals, plants, and animals—all in a harmonious, regular, accurate, and solid arrangement that bewilders the intellect. Even books cannot encompass their details, as evidenced by astronomy, anatomy, the study of celestial and terrestrial phenomena, and zoology. The author notes that man has been given little knowledge and cannot reach the essence of things. He then rhetorically asks how much more baffling it would be to ascend to the spiritual realms. From these two premises, the conclusion necessarily follows: God is knowing. The author also mentions that relying solely on scriptural evidence is circular and thus not the primary proof.
