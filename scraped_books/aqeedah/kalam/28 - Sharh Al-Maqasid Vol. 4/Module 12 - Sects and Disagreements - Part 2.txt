The Mu'tazilite View on the Purpose of Moral Obligation and Its Refutation
Outlines and refutes the Mu'tazilite claim that taklif is for exposing to reward.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite argument that the purpose of moral obligation (taklif) is exposure to reward, and the Ash'ari refutation based on divine grace and alternative purposes.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazilites insisted on deserved reward; the Ash'aris replied that reward can be pure divine grace.

In this passage, al-Sanusi presents a critical theological debate regarding why God imposes moral obligations on humans. The Mu'tazilites argue that the sole purpose is to expose humans to reward, which can only be good if deserved through hardship. The refutation challenges the necessity of such desert, offering other purposes and emphasizing that reward is ultimately a divine favor. This discussion highlights key differences between Mu'tazilite rationalism and Ash'ari theocentrism.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the translation to grasp the overall argument.
Notice the concise style of kalam arguments.
2
Identify the three Mu'tazilite proofs
The Mu'tazilites use a scriptural proof (Quranic verse), a consensus proof (no other purpose), and a moral proof (obligation without desert is injustice). Find each in the text.
The moral proof is the most philosophical.
3
Understand the main refutation points
The refutation argues that reward may be pure favor, rejects consensus, proposes alternative purposes, and questions the necessity of any purpose in divine acts.
Focus on how the refutation shifts from necessity to possibility.
4
Reflect on the concluding argument
The passage ends by acknowledging that difficult acts do have effect in establishing desert, citing Quranic verses, but this does not prove the Mu'tazilite claim.
Consider how the author balances both perspectives.

Commentary
This passage is a presentation and refutation of the Mu'tazilite position on the purpose of taklif (moral obligation). The author begins by stating the Mu'tazilite view: the purpose of taklif is to expose the servant to reward, and this exposure is only good when it is deserved (istihqaq) through the hardships of obligation. They offer three proofs: (1) a Quranic verse linking obedience to entering gardens; (2) a claimed consensus that there is no other purpose; (3) a moral argument that obligation without desert would be harmful and unjust, so the only justifying aspect is the benefit of reward. The refutation follows: first, the reward may be a pure favor (fadl) from God, not a necessary effect of the obligation; one cannot conceive how eternal bliss is deserved by a mere word and faith. Second, the claimed consensus is rejected; others propose alternative purposes such as trial, gratitude, preservation of order, or something unknowable. Third, even if consensus were accepted, it still requires proving that purpose is necessary in God's acts, which has not been established. The author also argues that exposure to reward is not good without desert, and that difficult acts do indeed have an effect in establishing desert, citing Quranic verses that show reward is tied to deeds. The passage ends with citations stressing that reward is from God as a result of obedience.

Definition of Rizq and First Refutation
The author defines rizq and rebuts the first Mu'tazila objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the linguistic and technical definitions of rizq and how the Ash'arite refutes the Mu'tazila claim that haram cannot be provision.

Core Idea
Rizq is defined by divine attribution, not by moral permissibility.

This passage begins by clarifying the definition of rizq (provision) through linguistic and technical lenses, addressing ambiguities and the concept of ownership. It then presents the Mu'tazila objection that forbidden things cannot be considered rizq due to their ugliness (qabih), and the Ash'arite refutation using a Qur'anic verse and a logical counterexample.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice how the author moves from definitions to argument.
2
Identify the definitions discussed
The passage discusses various interpretations of rizq: ownership, benefit, and the verbal noun form. Identify each and note why some are problematic.
The author prefers a definition that retains relation to the Provider.
3
Find the Mu'tazila objection and the refutation
Locate the Mu'tazila claim that haram cannot be rizq because of its ugliness, and then the author's proof from the Qur'an and the counterexample.
The counterexample of a person who eats only haram shows the absurdity of their position.
4
Understand the 'answer' to the potential rebuttal
The author anticipates a possible answer from the Mu'tazila and shows it is invalid by a case of death without any food.
The phrase 'your answer is our answer' is a rhetorical closure.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer argument concerning the definition of rizq (provision) and whether it includes haram (forbidden) food. The author begins by discussing the linguistic and technical definitions of rizq, noting that the Mu'tazila hold that haram cannot be rizq because it is qabih (ugly). He then refutes their position by stating that their principle is corrupt, and demonstrates a logical consequence: if a person eats only haram his whole life, then according to them Allah has not provided for him, but the Qur'an says every creature's provision is from Allah. Next, he presents an answer to a potential counter: Allah may have brought much halal to him, but he turned away—yet this is refuted by the case of one who dies without eating anything. Then the Mu'tazila raise a second objection: if haram were rizq, then it would not be permissible to prevent it from him, nor to blame or punish him for it. The author responds by denying the premise; rather, blame and punishment are for committing the prohibited act (the means of acquiring the rizq), not for the rizq itself. He explains that seeking rizq can be obligatory, recommended, permissible, or forbidden depending on the means, and if one uses forbidden means, he is blameworthy. Thus, the answer distinguishes between the rizq as a divine provision and the moral ruling on the act of acquisition.

Second Objection and Rulings on Seeking Rizq
The author responds to the second Mu'tazila objection and outlines the rulings on seeking rizq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'arite response to the Mu'tazila claim that if haram were rizq, then preventing it and punishing for it would be unjust, and how the rulings on seeking rizq depend on the means.

Core Idea
Blame and punishment are for the forbidden act of acquisition, not for the provision itself.

The Mu'tazila raise a second objection: if forbidden things are truly provision from Allah, then it would be wrong to prevent them or to punish the one who consumes them. The Ash'arite response distinguishes between the provision and the means by which it is acquired, clarifying that moral rulings apply to the act, not the thing. The passage then outlines the five rulings on seeking rizq based on intention and means.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Read the Mu'tazila objection and the Ash'ari response (denial). Notice the logic: They argue that if haram is rizq, then it cannot be prevented or punished. The response denies the connection.
The key is the distinction between the rizq itself and the act of acquisition.
2
Understand the distinction
The author explains that blame and punishment are only valid if the person is committing a prohibited act. The rizq as provision is not blameworthy; the means matter.
3
Examine the five rulings on seeking rizq
The passage lists rulings: obligatory (need), recommended (expanding), permissible (increase without prohibition), and prohibited (via forbidden means). Identify each case.
The prohibited case includes ghasp (usurpation), sariqa (theft), and riba (usury).

Definition of Si'r and the Ash'ari Position on Pricing
Definition and theological argument on pricing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of si'r, its causes, and the Ash'ari theological stance that pricing is ultimately from Allah alone, in contrast to the Mu'tazila.

Core Idea

Commentary

Voluntary Payment of Compensation Is Not Permissible
Fifth branch on voluntary compensation payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument against voluntarily paying the oppressed's compensation on behalf of the oppressor, based on the necessity of retribution.

Core Idea

The fifth branch examines whether it is permissible for someone (e.g., a third party) to voluntarily pay the compensation due to the oppressed on behalf of the oppressor. If voluntary payment were allowed, it would imply that leaving retribution (intiṣāf) from the oppressor is permissible, which is false.

قلنا: ذاك بعد تسلیم استلزام الأمر الإرادة إنما هو في حكيم محتاج إلى طاعة الأولياء أو رجوع الأعداء، ويتعزز بكثرة الأعوان والأنصار، ويعظم لديه الأقدار، ويكون للشيء بالنسبة إليه مقدار وقد يتمسك بأن عند وجود الداعي والقدرة، وانتفاء الصارف يجب الفعل . ورد بأن ذاك بعد التسليم وجوب عنه بمعنى اللزوم عند تمام العلة. والكلام في الوجوب عليه بمعنى استحقاق الذم على الترك، فأين هذا من ذاك؟ لنا بعد التنزل إلى القول لوجوب شيء على الله ، وأن ليس الصلاح والفساد بخلق الله تعالى وجوه : الأول : لو وجب عليه الأصلح لعباده لما خلق الكافر الفقير المعذب في الدنيا والآخرة، سيما المبتلى بالأسقام والآلام والمحن والآفات. الثاني : يلزم على ما ذكرتم من الأمثلة أنه يجب على كل أحد ما هو أصلح لعبيده ولنفسه فإن دفع بأن المكلف يتضرر بذلك، ويلحقه الكد والتعب. أجيب بأنه يلزم حينئذ أن لا يجب عليه شيء مما هو كذلك. : فإن قيل : يترتب عليه ثواب يربي عليه فيحسن لذلك. قلنا : فليكن الأصلح كذلك. الثالث : يلزم أن يكون الأصلح للكفار الخلود في النار. إذ لو كان الخروج أو عدم الدخول أصلح لفعل . فإن قيل : نعم يلزم أن الأصلح لهم الخلود لعلمه بأنهم لو ردوا لعادوا لما نهوا عنه .
We say: That [analogy] is after conceding that commanding necessitates will, and that [occurs] only in a wise person who needs the obedience of friends or the return of enemies, and is strengthened by many supporters and helpers, and [for whom] matters have significance for him, and things have value relative to him. And [the Mu'tazila] might also cling to [the claim] that when the motive and ability are present and the obstacle is absent, the act is necessitated. And it is replied that that, after conceding, is [a] necessity from it in the sense of necessary consequence when the cause is complete. But the discussion is about obligation upon Him in the sense of deserving blame for omission, so how is this that? For us, after descending to the position that something is obligatory upon God, and that benefit and harm are not by the creation of God Almighty, [we have] several arguments: First: If what is most beneficial for His servants were obligatory upon Him, He would not have created the disbelieving poor person tormented in this world and the next, especially the one afflicted with ailments, pains, trials, and afflictions. Second: It would follow from your examples that everyone must do what is most beneficial for his servants and for himself. But if [the opponent] objects that the accountable person would be harmed by that, and would incur fatigue and trouble, then the answer is that it would then follow that nothing of that sort is obligatory upon him. And if it is said: There results from it a reward that outweighs it, so it is good on that account. We say: Then let the most beneficial be likewise. Third: It would follow that what is most beneficial for the disbelievers is eternal abiding in the Fire, for if exiting or not entering were more beneficial, He would have done it. If it is said: Yes, it follows that what is most beneficial for them is eternal abiding because He knows that if they were sent back they would return to what they were forbidden from. [The answer is that this concedes the point.]

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite doctrine of al-aslah. The author (Taftazani) begins by responding to their analogy: the analogy holds only for a wise person who needs the obedience of friends or return of enemies, and who gains strength from supporters, and for whom things have value. God, however, is absolutely self-sufficient and not in need. Then he addresses their further argument that when motive and ability exist and obstacle is absent, the act is necessitated. He replies that this necessity is logical necessity when the cause is complete, not moral obligation deserving blame for omission. The discussion is about the latter, so the argument is irrelevant. Then he presents three arguments (وجوه) after conceding for the sake of argument that something is obligatory upon God and that benefit and harm are not created by God. First: If the aslah were obligatory, God would not have created the poor disbeliever tormented in both worlds, nor the afflicted person. Second: Their examples would imply that everyone must do the most beneficial for his servants and himself; they cannot escape by citing harm or fatigue, because then nothing of that sort would be obligatory. If they say the reward outweighs the harm, the reply is that the same could be said for God's actions not being aslah. Third: The aslah for disbelievers would then be eternal punishment in Hell, because if something else were better, God would have done it. If they object that God knows they would revert to disbelief, that actually concedes that eternal punishment is indeed the aslah for them, which undermines their doctrine's moral force.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The term 'وجوب' (obligation) in kalam always refers to moral obligation that deserves blame if omitted.
Correction: Taftazani shows that 'وجوب' can mean logical necessity (luzum) when a cause is complete, not necessarily moral blame. The Mu'tazilite argument confuses these two meanings.

Key Takeaway
Taftazani's refutation shows that the Mu'tazilite doctrine of al-aslah leads to absurd consequences, such as the existence of suffering and the eternal punishment of disbelievers being the most beneficial for them.
