The Linguistic Meaning and Derivation of 'Falak'
The author defines falak as the orbit of the stars and explains its etymology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the basic meaning of the word 'falak' as the orbit of the stars and its linguistic derivations from the concept of a spindle whorl.

Core Idea
The word 'falak' originally means 'orbit' and is linguistically related to a spindle whorl.

This lesson examines the linguistic definition of the Quranic term 'falak'. The author begins by citing a Quranic verse where 'falak' is used for ships, then explains that the primary meaning is the course of the stars. He traces its etymological root to the spindle whorl and shows how it applies to other curved objects. Understanding this derivation helps us appreciate the richness of the Quranic vocabulary.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the Quranic quotes and the flow from definition to derivations.
The Arabic has a possible typo 'وال' which likely means 'والفلك'.
2

Find where the author states 'والفلك مجرى الكواكب' (the orbit is the course of the stars). This is the basic meaning.
Contrast it with the immediate context of ships in the first Quranic verse.
3
Trace the derivations
Note the etymology from 'فلكة المغزل' (spindle whorl) and how it extends to a woman's breast and a camel's suckling device. All share a curved or circular shape.
This shows the semantic field of 'falak'.

Commentary
This passage provides a linguistic definition of the word 'falak' (فلك). The author begins with a Quranic citation (10:22): 'حتى إذا كنتم في الفلك' (Until when you are in the ships). The word 'falak' here is explained as the course (مجرى) of the stars, i.e., the orbit. It is called 'falak' because it resembles the 'falk' which is a spindle whorl (فلكة المغزل). The author then cites another Quranic verse (21:33): 'وكل في فلك يسبحون' (And each in an orbit are swimming). The derivation of the word is traced: from 'falkat al-mighzal' (whorl of the spindle), it is also used for the curvature of a woman's breast (فلك الثدي) and for a device placed in a camel's tongue to prevent suckling (فلكت الحديد). Finally, the author provides a textual note: a reference to the dictionary Mufradat Alfaz al-Quran and a variant reading in copy B where 'al-bia' (environment) is read instead of 'al-hay'a' (astronomy), indicating a possible scribal variation.

Linguistic Clarification on Ru'ya with 'Ila'
Clarifies a linguistic point about ru'ya in al-Mawaqif.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise linguistic issue concerning the use of ru'ya with the preposition 'ila' in the context of the verse, and how it is resolved by interpreting nazar as ru'ya.

Core Idea
Ru'ya cannot naturally pair with 'ila'; only nazar can.

In theological discussions, every word and its grammatical connection matters. This short passage from al-Mawaqif addresses a subtle linguistic point: how the word ru'ya (vision) relates to the preposition 'ila' in a verse. Without careful clarification, one might misunderstand the verse's implication. Read on to see how the scholars resolved this potential confusion.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic sentence once to get its flow. Notice the key terms: الرؤية, علم, نظر, إلى.
The passage is short and technical.
2
Understand the claim being critiqued
Identify the claim from al-Mawaqif: that ru'ya can be used for knowledge but connecting it with 'ila' is far-fetched.
The passage calls this claim 'very far' (بعيد جداً).
3
Grasp the resolution
See how the issue is resolved: by interpreting nazar (looking) to mean ru'ya, so that the preposition 'ila' attaches to nazar instead.
This preserves the verse's integrity.
4
Reflect on the theological significance
Ask why this linguistic precision matters for theology: it protects the concept of ru'ya from being misapplied to knowledge of Allah.

Commentary
This passage is a textual note commenting on a statement in al-Mawaqif. It addresses a linguistic issue concerning the word ru'ya (vision) and its usage with the preposition 'ila' (to). The author states that although ru'ya can be used in the sense of knowledge, it is far-fetched to connect it with 'ila' in this way. Instead, the word nazar (looking) is interpreted to mean ru'ya, so when nazar is connected to 'ila', it is as if ru'ya is connected. Otherwise, the verse does not contain 'ru'ya' connected with 'ila'. This is a precise grammatical and theological point to avoid misunderstanding.
