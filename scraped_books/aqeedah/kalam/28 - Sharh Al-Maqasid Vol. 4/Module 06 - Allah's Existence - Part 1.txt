First Argument: Impossibility of Self-Existence for Possibles
Sanusi presents the first argument (wajh) for the existence of the Necessary Being.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first argument for the existence of the Necessary Being: a reductio that shows possibles cannot exist from themselves.

Core Idea
If no Necessary Being exists, possibles must cause themselves—and that is absurd.

This lesson introduces the first of several arguments for God's existence from the Kalam tradition. It is a crisp reductio ad absurdum: if there were no necessary being, then every possible being would have to exist from its own essence. But a possible being, by definition, cannot do that. Hence, a necessary being must exist. Let's examine this reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic sentence and its translation. Notice the structure: 'If not X, then Y, and Y is false.'
2
Understand the definition of 'possible'
The argument hinges on the meaning of 'possible' (mumkin). A possible being is one that does not exist from itself. If it existed from itself, it would be necessary, not possible.
3
Reflect on the reductio logic
Ask yourself: What if there were no necessary being? Would all possibles just disappear? No, the argument assumes they still exist, but then they'd be self-causing, which is contradictory.

Commentary
This is the first argument (وجه) for the existence of the Necessary Being. It is a brief reductio ad absurdum. The premise is: if there were no necessary being in existence, then every possible being would have to exist from its own essence (i.e., by itself). But a possible being, by definition, does not have existence from itself; it requires a cause. Therefore, the conclusion that it exists from itself is inherently corrupt (فساده بين). Hence, there must be a necessary being.

Second Argument: The Totality of Possibles Needs an Independent Agent
Second argument: from the totality of contingent beings to an external cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the totality of all possible beings cannot be self-sufficient and must depend on an external Necessary Being.

Core Idea
The total set of possibles can't cause itself, so its cause must be outside.

Sanusi presents his second rational argument for the existence of God. He considers the entire collection of all possible things — nothing left out. Since each possible is contingent, the whole collection is contingent too. This contingency demands an agent that is independent, and Sanusi shows why that agent cannot be part of the collection itself.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text once through, then read the translation. Note the logical flow from premise to conclusion.
Sanusi writes in a dense style; each 'because' is a step.
2
Define the totality of possibles
Understand what 'مجموع الممكنات' means: the set of all possible beings, taken as a whole without exception. Why must this set itself be contingent?
3
Follow the elimination argument
The argument eliminates possibilities: the independent agent cannot be the totality itself, nor any part of it. Track each reason given.
4
Conclude the necessary external cause
After elimination, only one possibility remains: the agent is external, and that is the Necessary Being. Ask yourself why each elimination is valid.

The sum of possible beings points beyond itself to a self-sufficient cause.

The Fourth Proof: The Complete Cause Must Be Necessary or Contain the Necessary
The author presents a proof that the complete cause of an event must be necessary or contain the necessary, and answers two objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the complete cause of any event cannot be entirely contingent and must either be necessary or contain something necessary.

Core Idea
Every event requires a cause that is either necessary or contains the necessary; otherwise absurdities follow.

In this passage, the author presents a sophisticated argument rooted in the concept of a complete cause. He shows that if the complete cause of an event were itself entirely contingent, it would lead to either infinite regress, a violation of the principle of sufficient reason, or an impossible conversion from essential impossibility to possibility. The argument forces the conclusion that the ultimate cause of any event must be necessary or include a necessary element.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of complete cause
Note that the complete cause (العلة التامة) is simultaneous with its effect—the effect cannot precede or lag behind it. This property is essential to the argument.
The author assumes the principle that effect cannot be separated from its complete cause.
2
Follow the conditional reasoning
Assume the complete cause is entirely possible (ممكنة بتمامها). Then consider two possibilities: it has an external cause or it does not. Trace each branch.
This is a reductio ad absurdum: if the cause were possible, absurd consequences follow.
3
Examine the two branches
Branch 1: external cause → the cause would not be complete (contradiction). Branch 2: no external cause → then either its existence before the event is impossible (leading to an impossible conversion) or possible (leading to a preponderance without a preponderator).
Both branches lead to absurdity, so the original assumption is false.
4
Consider the objections and responses
The author anticipates two objections: (1) the argument is rebutted by a composite that includes the Necessary; (2) the opponent might choose that the cause's existence before the event is always impossible. The author dismisses both briefly.
The responses rely on earlier discussions; the key is that the argument stands.

Core Idea

Fourth Argument: Preponderance Without a Preponderator
Al-Taftazani presents the fourth argument against multiple gods: the problem of preponderance without a preponderator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the existence of two equal gods logically necessitates an impossible preponderance without a preponderator.

Core Idea
Two equal gods cannot both cause the same act without a reason for one to prevail.

This argument tackles the scenario where two gods both intend the same action. Since they are equal in power, any outcome would be arbitrary unless one has a preponderating factor. Al-Taftazani shows this leads to contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice the either/or structure.
2
Identify the impossibility
The argument says if both gods cause the act, it becomes a single act by two agents (impossible). If one causes it, there must be a reason why that one rather than the other.
This is the principle of preponderance without a preponderator
3
Connect to divine attributes
Reflect: if both are truly equal, why would one act rather than the other? This shows the need for a single, unique necessary being.
