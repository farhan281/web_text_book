Proof of Divine Self-Knowledge and the Objection of Concomitants
Al-Taftazani presents the second proof that God knows His essence and responds to an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the kalam argument that God's knowledge of His essence entails knowledge of all things, and how the objection regarding necessary concomitants is answered by the concept of complete knowledge.

Core Idea
God's complete knowledge of His essence includes knowledge of all its necessary concomitants.

In this passage, al-Taftazani presents a second proof for divine self-knowledge and extends it to God's knowledge of creation. An objection challenges whether knowledge of the essence automatically yields knowledge of all its concomitants. The resolution introduces a key distinction: complete knowledge (al-ilm al-tamm) encompasses all aspects without regress.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation once to get the overall flow. Note the three main parts: proof, objection, and answer.
Pay attention to technical terms like 'lazim' (concomitant) and 'ilm tamm' (complete knowledge).
2
Understand the proof of self-knowledge
The proof states that for an immaterial being, intellection is simply the presence of the essence to itself. This establishes that God knows His own essence.
This is based on the principle that an immaterial being cannot have an image of itself, so knowledge is direct presence.
3
See how knowledge extends to other things
The text explains that because God knows His essence, which is the principle (cause) of all possible things, He thereby knows all effects as concomitants of His essence.
The key link: knowledge of the cause necessitates knowledge of its necessary concomitants, including effects.
4
Analyze the objection
The objection argues that not all necessary concomitants are manifest (bayyin) such that knowing the essence automatically yields knowledge of them. The example is the triangle's angles summing to 180 degrees.
If any concomitant were known automatically, then one would have to traverse an infinite chain of concomitants, leading to regress.
5
Grasp the answer: complete knowledge
The answer is that divine knowledge is complete (ilm tamm)—knowledge of a thing as it is in itself, which includes all its aspects and concomitants. Thus no regress occurs.
Complete knowledge is exhaustive, so it does not require a step-by-step progression.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof that the Necessary Being knows His own essence, followed by an objection and its answer. The proof states that for an immaterial being (mujarrad), 'intellection' (ta'aqqul) of its essence simply means the presence of its essence to itself, without any image, since an image would involve two similar things. The Second Argument Against Divine Self-Knowledge and Its Partial Answer
Al-Taftazani presents a two-part argument against God's self-knowledge and answers the first part.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two-aspect argument against God's self-knowledge and the response that conceptual distinctness suffices for the relational aspect.

Core Idea
God's self-knowledge is attacked via duality and multiplicity, but defended by conceptual distinctness.

This passage presents a sophisticated challenge to the doctrine of God's knowledge of His own essence. The argument is divided into two main faces: the first targets the relational nature of knowledge, the second argues that knowledge is distinct from the essence, leading to an absurdity. Al-Taftazani then provides a response to the first face, relying on the notion of conceptual distinctness. Let's examine each part carefully.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two-fold structure
The argument has two main faces (wajhan). The first face itself contains two sub-arguments. The second face is a separate point. Notice how the text labels them: 'أحدهما' and 'وثانيهما'.
The first face is about the nature of knowledge; the second about its distinctness.
2
Examine the first face's sub-arguments
First sub-argument: knowledge as relation or relational attribute necessitates duality and distinction between knower and known, impossible in the truly One. Second sub-argument: knowledge as form equaling the known; different known objects require different forms, leading to multiplicity in the essence.
The second sub-argument uses the idea that knowledge of one thing can be separate from another.

Study the second face
The second face argues that knowledge is distinct from the essence (based on earlier proofs), so it must be a possible accident caused by the essence. But then the essence would be both receptive (qabil) and active (fa'il), which is impossible.
This is a strong a priori argument against divine self-knowledge.
4
Analyze the response to the first face
Al-Taftazani answers the first face by conceding that if knowledge is a relational attribute, it does require distinctness, but this distinctness can be conceptual (i'tibari) rather than real, as in human self-knowledge.
The response is partial; the text cuts off before completing.

The argument relies on the definition of knowledge as a form equal to the known (ṣūra musāwiya lil-maʿlūm).

Commentary
This passage presents the first proof (al-wajh al-awwal) of those who deny that God knows anything at all. The proof has two sub-parts: (i) knowledge inherently requires duality and differentiation between knower and known, inconceivable for the truly One; (ii) knowledge would multiply forms in the essence, since each known thing has a distinct form impressed in the knower. The answer (jawāb) begins by conceding the implication of differentiation but argues that conceptual diversity (taghayyur al-iʿtibār) suffices, as in human self-knowledge. It then refutes a potential objection that this conceptual diversity depends on the prior existence of knowledge, leading to circularity. The refutation clarifies that the dependence is not causal but concomitant: the diversity is inseparable from knowledge, like the inseparability of effect from cause. The second part of the answer denies that divine knowledge involves impression of forms; rather, it is a pure relation (taʿalluq) to the known. An elaborate analogy from the later scholars is given: when a human conceives an external object, the form emanates from the agent with participation from the object, yet there is no multiplication of forms. If this holds for humans, then even more so for God, whose intellection is purely self-sufficient without any external cause or in-dwelling. The passage concludes by noting that multiplicity of attributes in the essence is not impossible in our Ashʿarī school, but only according to the philosophers.

Refutation of the circularity objection regarding self-knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the objection that divine self-knowledge requires a distinction between knower and known, which would lead to circularity.

Core Idea
Divine self-knowledge does not require a real distinction—only inseparability like cause and effect.

The first objection against God's knowledge of Himself argues that knowledge requires a distinction between the knower and the known, leading to an infinite regress if God knows Himself. The author clarifies that the dependence is not one of temporal precedence but of logical inseparability, using the example of the soul's self-knowledge to illustrate that numerical unity does not preclude self-knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the first objection

2
Understand the nature of dependence
Notice that the author distinguishes between dependence of precedence and need versus inseparability. This is key.
3
Analyze the soul analogy
The author uses the example of the soul knowing itself: it is one in essence but can be knowing from one aspect and known from another. This refutes the need for numerical distinction.
Think about how this applies to God.

Knowledge: if acquiring knowledge depended on distinction, then circularity would follow. The defect is only objected concerning our knowledge of ourselves if the soul were one in every respect, like the Necessary, which is denied. So it is possible for it to be knowing in one respect and known in another. For we say: circularity only follows if the dependence of knowledge on distinction were a dependence of precedence and need, which is denied; rather, its utmost is that it is inseparable from knowledge, just as the effect is inseparable from its cause. What is meant by 'defect' is that the soul knows its very essence which is knowing, not that the knower is one thing and the known another. Secondly: His knowledge is nothing but a relation to the known without the impression of a form in the essence, so there is no multiplicity except in relations and attributes. Its verification, as mentioned by some later scholars, is that the obtaining of things for Him is the obtaining of the agent by necessity, and the obtaining of intelligible forms for us is the obtaining of the recipient by possibility. Yet that does not require forms distinct from them, for you conceive a thing by a form that you imagine and bring to mind; it emanates from you with some participation from another (the external thing). Yet you do not possess that form without another; rather, just as you conceive that thing by it, so you also conceive the form itself without the forms multiplying in you. If your state with what emanates from you through the participation of another is this, then what do you think of the state of one who conceives what emanates from His own essence without any interference from another? Moreover, being a substrate for those forms is not a condition for intellection, as evidenced by your intellection of your own essence without that. Rather, what matters is the presence of the form for every state, whether it is subsisting or not. The essential effects of the self-active intellect are present to it without inherence in it; thus it is intellective of them without their being states in it, given that multiplicity of attributes in the essence is not prevented according to us, but only according to the philosophers and their followers. The second [objection] was answered by denying the impossibility of a single thing being both receptive and agent.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of two objections concerning divine knowledge. The first objection claims that knowledge requires a distinction between knower and known, leading to circularity if God knows Himself. The author denies that the dependence is one of precedence; rather, knowledge and its object are inseparable like cause and effect. The soul can know itself without numerical distinction. The second objection argues that knowledge requires a form impressed in the essence, implying multiplicity. The author responds that God's knowledge is a pure relation without impression, and that in human intellection, forms are not inherent but present as modes. The author concludes by noting that multiplicity of attributes is accepted in Sunni theology, unlike the philosophers.

Refutation of the Objection Regarding Ignorance
The author answers an objection about divine knowledge of non-existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the objection that divine knowledge of non-existent future events would be ignorance.

Core Idea
Divine knowledge of future non-existence is not ignorance because it knows the temporal state as it is.

The objection claims that if God knows something will not happen, that would be ignorance when it does not occur. The author has a precise answer: knowledge attaches to the relation of non-existence at a specific time, not to existence. This lesson unpacks that response.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
The objection concerns 'assertive knowledge' (al-ʿilm al-taṣdīqī) and argues that God's knowledge of a future event that does not occur would be ignorance due to the non-existence of its object.
Note the phrase 'independent relation' (al-nisba al-istiqlāliyya).
2
Identify the author's denial
The author answers by 'denial' (al-manʿ): the attachment of knowledge in the state of non-existence is that the event will exist, not that it exists now.
This is the key move: the object of knowledge is the relation, not the actualized event.
3
Understand the distinction
The author distinguishes between attachment in the state of non-existence (it will exist) and attachment in the state of existence (it exists now). Ignorance would require the latter in the former state.
True ignorance is to attach existence-knowledge to a non-existent state.
4
Follow the example of the world
The example: God eternally knows the non-existence of the world in pre-eternity, its existence in the future, and its annihilation later—without any change.
This shows that multiple temporal attachments coexist in God's eternal knowledge without contradiction.
5
Appreciate the refutation of the Imam's objection
The Imam's objection (If God knows the world exists now, then either His prior knowledge remains causing ignorance or ceases causing change) is repelled: God's knowledge of the world's non-existence in pre-eternity does not remain in the present; rather, the present state is known differently.
The principle: what is proven eternal cannot cease, but the knowledge of temporal states is eternal in its scope, not its content.

Commentary
This passage presents and then refutes the position that God's knowledge of particulars changes over time. It begins by attributing this view to Hisham and Abu al-Husayn, who claimed God knows essences (māhiyāt) in eternity but only knows individuals and states after they occur. This position is also associated with Jahm ibn Safwan. The argument is that God's essence necessitates knowledge only on condition of existence, so such knowledge arises only when the object exists. They see no impossibility in God acquiring new relational knowledges (talluqāt) dependent on temporal conditions without implying change in the essence. The author then presents an objection (from Imam al-Razi?) that if an attribute (such as knowledge) occurs to the Necessary Being, either the essence alone suffices for its existence, in which case it would be eternal, or it depends on an external condition, and since the essence is never without that attribute when the condition is met, the essence becomes dependent on that condition, implying contingency (imkān) of the Necessary. The author refutes this objection as extremely weak, because 'never being free from something' does not imply dependence; for example, Zayd's existence is not dependent on Amr's existence or non-existence even though they coincide. Introduction to the fourth investigation on God's will, stating consensus and proof from free agency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the universal consensus that God is described as willing (مريد), the proof from His agency by choice, and the distinction between 'chooser' and 'willer' that sets up the debate on the nature of divine will.

Core Idea
All thinkers agree God is willing; the proof lies in His free agency, but the meaning of His will remains debated.

This lesson opens the fourth investigation on God's will. The author begins by stating a remarkable consensus: theologians, philosophers, and all sects affirm that God is willing. The proof is drawn from the fact that God is an agent by choice, which implies intention and will. However, as the passage notes, this agreement is only on the word; the real debate is on what God's will actually means.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note the consensus
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Observe that the author explicitly states that all groups—theologians and philosophers—agree that God is described as 'willing' (مريد). This consensus is found in the Qur'an and Sunnah.
The consensus is on the attribution, not the meaning.
2
Identify the proof from agency by choice
The passage states that the proof for God being willing is that He is an agent by choice (فاعل بالاختيار). Since choosing implies intention and will, God must have will.
This proof connects divine will to divine action.
3
Understand the distinction and the remaining debate
The author distinguishes between 'chooser' (مختار) and 'willer' (مريد): the chooser considers two sides and inclines to one, while the willer focuses on the preferred side. Despite consensus on the term, there is abundant disagreement on the meaning of God's will.
This sets up the next lessons which explore different theological and philosophical views.

The Many Views on God's Will
Enumerates different theological positions on God's will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the diversity of theological views on the nature of God's will and the Ash'ari position within that spectrum.

Core Idea
God's will is understood differently by every major theological school.

In classical Islamic theology, the nature of God's will (إرادة) was a matter of intense debate. Different schools proposed radically different definitions: some saw it as an eternal attribute, others as a created one, and still others as identical to knowledge or essence. This lesson presents the key positions, setting the stage for the Ash'ari proof that will follow.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get an overview. Then read the translation to understand each distinct view.
Notice that each school is identified by a key term or phrase.
2
Identify each school and its definition
Go through the list one by one. For each school, note how they define God's will. Compare the definitions to see how they differ.
There are eight distinct views listed.
3
Focus on the Ash'ari view
The Ash'ari view is presented first (عندنا). Understand that it considers will an eternal attribute additional to the essence. Compare this to the other views.
4
Reflect on the diversity
Ask yourself: why are there so many views? What does this tell us about the complexity of divine attributes? This diversity sets the stage for the need for rigorous theological reasoning.
The passage shows that this is a central issue in kalam.

Commentary
This passage lists the different theological views on the nature of God's will (إرادة). The author enumerates them as follows: (1) According to the Ash'aris (عندنا), it is an eternal attribute additional to the essence, subsisting in Him like other real attributes. (2) According to the Jubba'ites (the followers of Abu 'Ali al-Jubba'i and his son), it is an additional attribute but subsisting without a locus (لا محل). (3) According to the Karramites, it is a created attribute (صفة حادثة) subsisting in the essence. (4) According to Dirar ibn 'Amr, it is the essence itself (نفس الذات). (5) According to an-Najjar, it is a negative attribute: that the agent is not compelled and not evil. (6) According to the philosophers, it is knowledge of the most perfect system (النظام الأكمل). (7) According to al-Kalbi, God's will for His own act is knowledge of it, and for the act of another it is the command to it. (8) According to the verifiers among the Mu'tazilites, it is knowledge of the benefit in the act. This enumeration shows the wide range of opinions, setting the stage for the Ash'ari proof that will follow.

The Ash'ari Proof for Will as a Distinct Attribute
Ash'ari proof that will is a distinct attribute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari argument that God's will is a distinct eternal attribute, and how it is distinguished from power and knowledge.

Core Idea
Specification of opposites requires a specifier – that specifier is God's will, distinct from His essence.

The Ash'ari school argues that the fact that God creates some things and not others, at specific times, cannot be due to His essence alone, because the essence is equally related to all possibilities. A distinct attribute of will is required to explain this specification. This lesson presents the core proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the passage starts with the problem of specification.
2
Understand the problem of specification
If God's essence were the only factor, all opposites would be created equally. But they are not. So there must be a specifier.
3
Identify the attribute of will
The specifier cannot be external (God would be dependent). It must be an internal attribute called will.
Will is defined as the attribute that specifies one possibility over another.
4
Distinguish will from power and knowledge
Power relates equally to all possibilities; will tips the balance. Knowledge is universal; will is particular.
5
Confirm the conclusion
The passage affirms that will is distinct, clear to the intellect, and additional to the essence.

تمسك أصحابنا بأن تخصيص بعض الأضداد بالوقوع دون البعض وفي بعض الأوقات دون البعض مع استواء نسبة الذات إلى الكل، لا بد أن يكون لصفة شأنها التخصيص لامتناع التخصيص بلا مخصص، وامتناع احتياج الواجب في فاعليته إلى أمر منفصل وتلك الصفة هي المسماة بالإرادة، وهو معنى واضح عند العقل، مغاير للعلم والقدرة وسائر الصفات شأنه التخصيص والترجيح لأحد طرفي المقدور من الفعل والترك على الآخر، وينبه على مغايرتها للقدرة أن نسبة القدرة إلى الطرفين على السواء بخلافها وللعلم أن مطلق العلم نسبته إلى الكل على السواء، والعلم بما فيه من المصلحة. أو بأنه سيوجد في وقت كذا سابق على الإرادة والعلم بوقوعه تابع للوقوع المتأخر عنها وفيه نظر. إذ قد لا يسلم الخصم سبق العلم بأنه يوجده في وقت كذا على (۳) إرادته ذلك، ولا تأخر علمه بوقوعه حالاً عن إرادته الوقوع حالاً. وما يقال أن العلم تابع للوقوع ، فمعناه أنه يعلم الشيء كما يقع ، وأن المعلوم هو الأصل في التطابق لأنه مثال وصورة له، لا بمعنى تأخره عنه في الخارج البتة. والحق أن مغايرة الحالة التي نسميها بالإرادة للعلم والقدرة وسائر الصفات ضرورية، ثم لقد تبين قدمها، وزيادتها على الذات
Our companions adhere to the fact that the specification of some opposites to occur rather than others, and in some times rather than others, while the relation of the essence to all is equal, must be due to an attribute whose function is specification, because specification without a specifier is impossible, and the impossibility of the Necessary needing in His agency a separate matter. And that attribute is called 'will'. It is a meaning clear to the intellect, distinct from knowledge, power, and other attributes. Its function is specification and weighting of one of the two sides of the possible—act or omission—over the other. It indicates its distinctness from power: that the relation of power to both sides is equal, unlike it; and from knowledge: that absolute knowledge's relation to all is equal, and knowledge of the benefit in it. Or that it will exist at a certain time is prior to the will, and knowledge of its occurrence follows the occurrence that is later than the will. But there is a problem: the opponent may not concede the priority of knowledge that He will bring it into existence at a certain time over His willing that, nor the posteriority of His knowledge of its occurrence at present to His willing its occurrence at present. And what is said that knowledge follows occurrence means that He knows the thing as it occurs, and that the known is the origin in correspondence because it is an example and image of it, not meaning its posteriority in external existence at all. The truth is that the distinctness of the state we call 'will' from knowledge, power, and other attributes is necessary. Then its eternity and its being additional to the essence are established.
Source pages p. 128

Commentary
This passage presents the Ash'ari proof that God's will is a distinct eternal attribute. The argument proceeds as follows: (1) The specification of some opposites (e.g., creating the world at one time rather than another, or creating one thing rather than its opposite) cannot be due to the essence alone, because the essence's relation to both opposites is equal. A specifier is needed. (2) That specifier cannot be an external cause, because the Necessary Being cannot depend on anything external in His agency. Therefore, it must be an attribute inherent in Him, which is called 'will'. (3) This attribute is distinct from power because power is equally related to both opposites, while will specifies one. It is distinct from knowledge because knowledge is equally related to all things, while will involves a particular inclination. The author then addresses an objection: some might claim that will is nothing but knowledge of the benefit or knowledge of the time of occurrence. The author argues that knowledge of the benefit is not the same as will, because will involves a specific inclination. Regarding the timing, if someone says God knows He will bring something into existence at time t, and that knowledge is prior to will, the opponent may not concede that. The proper understanding of 'knowledge follows occurrence' is that knowledge corresponds to the known, not that it is temporally posterior. The conclusion is that the distinctness of will from other attributes is self-evident, and its eternity and addition to the essence are proven.
