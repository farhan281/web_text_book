The First Proof for God's Knowledge: The Argument from Perfect Design
Sanusi states the first proof for God's knowledge with two premises and Quranic support.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first kalam proof for God's knowledge, which argues that the precise order of creation necessarily points to a knowing Creator.

Core Idea
Perfect order in creation demands a knowing Maker.

Sanusi presents the first of two classical theological arguments for God's attribute of knowledge. The argument is straightforward: the universe displays remarkable precision and order, which can only come from a knowledgeable agent. This lesson unpacks the two premises of this proof, showing how everyday examples and scriptural evidence support it.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how Sanusi structures the proof: a claim, then two premises.
The passage ends with a Quranic verse; the objection comes in the next lesson.
2
Identify the two premises
Find the minor premise (that God's creation is precise) and the major premise (that precision implies knowledge). See how the major is called 'necessary'.
3
Examine the supporting examples
Look at the examples of fine writing and eloquent speech, and the scientific fields cited to illustrate creation's intricacy.
4
Reflect on the Quranic verse
The verse (2:164) is presented as evidence for the minor premise. Consider how it highlights signs for those who reason.

The majority of rational people agree on it, and the well-known method of the theologians (mutakallimūn) is two ways: First: He is an Agent doing an accurate, precise act, and everyone who does that is knowing. As for the major premise, it is by necessity, and he drew attention to it: whoever sees beautiful lines or hears eloquent words that indicate precise meanings and sound purposes knows with certainty that their agent is knowing. As for the minor premise, it is because it is established that He is the Creator of the spheres and elements including what is in them of accidents and substances, the types of minerals, plants, and kinds of animals with consistency, order, perfection, and precision that confuse minds and understandings, and notebooks and pens do not suffice for their details, as attested by astronomy (ʿilm al-hayʾa), anatomy (ʿilm al-tashrīḥ), the science of celestial and terrestrial effects, and the science of animals and plants, while man has not been given of knowledge except a little, and has not found a way to the essence. How then when one ascends to the world of spiritualities from the earthly and heavenly realms, and to what the philosophers (ḥukamāʾ) say about immaterial beings. {Indeed, in the creation of the heavens and the earth, and the alternation of the night and the day, and the ships which sail through the sea with that which benefits people, and what Allah has sent down from the heavens of rain, giving life thereby to the earth after its lifelessness and dispersing therein every [kind of] moving creature, and [His] directing of the winds and the clouds controlled between the heaven and the earth are signs for a people who use reason.} If it is said: If what is meant by order and perfection in every respect, meaning that these effects are arranged with no defect at all and are suitable for the benefits and interests desired from them such that nothing more fitting or better can be conceived, then apparently they are not so, rather the world is full of evils and afflictions; and if what is meant is in general and from some aspects, then most effects of non-rational agents are likewise, and also many rational philosophers have attributed the wonders of animal creation and the formation of limbs to an insentient power they call 'the formative power', so how can the claim that the major premise is necessary be valid? We say: What is meant is that the effects and acts include subtleties of craftsmanship, brilliant arrangement, good suitability for benefits, and conformity to interests in a perfect manner, even if they involve some kind of defect, and it is possible that there is something more perfect above it. And the knowledge that such cannot be produced except by a knower is necessary, especially when repeated and multiplied, and the obscurity of the necessary for some rational people is permissible. And what is said: 'Why is conjecture not sufficient?' is refuted by repetition and multiplication, and by the fact that mere conception suffices for establishing our purpose.

Commentary
This passage presents the first of two well-known kalam proofs for God’s knowledge. It begins by stating the consensus of rational people and then outlines the first proof: the argument from order and precision. The proof has two premises: (1) the minor premise that God produces acts that are accurate and precise, such as the creation of the heavens, earth, and all beings with amazing order; (2) the major premise that anyone who produces such acts must be knowing, and this is known by necessity. The author illustrates the major premise with examples: fine writing or eloquent speech necessarily indicate a knowing agent. For the minor premise, he points to the intricate design of the universe, referencing astronomy, anatomy, and other sciences, and even quotes the Quranic verse (2:164) as evidence. Then an objection is raised: if the order is claimed to be perfect from every aspect, the existence of evil and defects disproves it; if only in general, then many non-rational agents produce similar effects, and some philosophers attribute animal formation to an insentient nature. The answer clarifies that the intended order is the presence of subtle craftsmanship, brilliant arrangement, and suitability for benefits, even if some defects exist. It asserts that knowledge of such works requiring a knower is necessary, especially given the abundance and repetition of these works, and that necessity may be hidden from some. Conjecture is insufficient, but the repeated evidence makes it certain. The purpose is established by mere conception of this argument.

Objection and Answer: Defects in the World and the Need for a Knowing Creator
Sanusi refutes an objection regarding evil and disorder and defends the necessity of the proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi responds to the objection that the world's imperfections and defects undermine the argument from design for God's knowledge.

Core Idea
Imperfections in the world do not disprove the necessity of a knowledgeable Creator.

The argument from design seems vulnerable to a serious objection: the world contains evil, suffering, and apparent disorder. If the creation is meant to be perfectly ordered, then its defects would suggest an imperfect or ignorant maker. Sanusi addresses this objection head-on, clarifying what is meant by 'order' and why even imperfect order points to a knowing agent.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objector claims that if order is absolute, the world's evils disprove it; if only general, then many non-rational agents also produce order.
The objection also mentions philosophers attributing design to a natural 'formative power'.
2
Examine the answer
Sanusi redefines the order in terms of subtle craftsmanship and suitability, even if some defects exist.
3
Note the reinforcement
He adds that knowledge of the agent is necessary, especially when the pattern is repeated and multiplied, and that necessity may be hidden from some but is still valid.
4
Refute the alternative explanation
The objection's reference to a 'formative power' (المصورة) is countered by the necessity of the major premise and the insufficiency of conjecture.

The Design Argument Defended Against Objections
Refutes objections against the design argument for God's knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the design argument responds to objections about imperfections and non-conscious formative powers, and why the argument's major premise is considered necessary.

Core Idea
Flaws in creation don’t disprove design—they highlight its subtle perfection.

The argument from design (dalil al-itqan wa al-ihkam) is a cornerstone of Islamic natural theology. Critics often point to evil, suffering, and the claim that animal anatomy can be explained by a blind formative power. This passage tackles both objections head-on, defending the necessary rational judgment that such intricate order can only come from a knowing Creator. We will read the objection, then the response, and see how repetition and multiplicity elevate the argument to certainty.

Guided Path
1
Read the full passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the verse citation at the beginning sets the stage.
2
Identify the two objections
The objector raises two challenges: first, if design requires absolute perfection, the world is full of flaws; second, if only general order is meant, non-rational forces like the 'formative power' could explain it. Find these in the text.
The objector also cites philosophers attributing anatomy to a non-sentient force.
3
Analyze the answer's clarification of 'design'
The answer clarifies that design means subtle craftsmanship (lata'if al-sun') and magnificent order, not absolute perfection. It allows for some flaws. Identify the key phrases describing what is meant.
Note the phrase 'on a perfect manner' despite possible flaws.

Understand why the judgment is necessary
The answer argues that knowledge of such order coming only from a knower is necessary (daruri), especially when repeated and multiplied. Notice how this overcomes the objection that probability would suffice.
Reflect on why repetition elevates the argument to certainty.

Indeed, in the creation of the heavens and the earth, and the alternation of night and day, and the ships which sail in the sea with what benefits people, and what Allah sends down from the sky of water, reviving the earth after its death, and dispersing in it every creature, and the directing of the winds and the clouds subjected between the sky and the earth, are signs for a people who reason. And if what is meant is generally and in some respects, then most of the effects of non-rational agents—rather all of them—are like that. Also, many of the rational philosophers have attributed the wonders of animal creation and the detailed formation of organs to a non-sentient force they call the 'Musawwira' (formative power). We say: The intended meaning is that the effects and actions contain subtleties of craftsmanship, magnificent order, good suitability for benefits, and conformity to interests in a perfect manner, even if they include from the purpose some kind of flaw. And it is possible that there is above it something more perfect. The knowledge that such things do not issue except from a knower is necessary (daruri), especially when they are repeated and multiplied. And the concealment of the necessary from some rational beings is permissible. And what is said: 'Why is probability (zann) not sufficient?' is refuted by repetition and multiplicity, and by the fact that conceptualization suffices for establishing our purpose.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer regarding the argument from design for God's knowledge. The objector first cites the Quranic verse 2:164 as evidence of divine order, then raises two challenges: (1) If the argument requires absolute perfection and optimal benefit in every detail, the world clearly contains evils and flaws, so the premise is false. (2) If only general order is meant, then non-rational natural causes produce similar effects, and some philosophers attribute animal anatomy to a non-conscious 'formative power'. Thus, the major premise—that such order necessarily implies a knowing agent—is not necessary. The answer clarifies that the design argument does not demand absolute perfection; it suffices that the effects exhibit subtle craftsmanship, impressive order, and suitability for benefits, even if some flaws exist. The conviction that such things only come from a knower is a necessary (daruri) judgment, especially when one observes repeated and multiplied instances. That some rational beings fail to see this necessity does not negate its evident nature. The objection that probability (zann) would be enough is dismissed because the repeated occurrence elevates the judgment to certainty, and the conceptualization (tasawwur) of the design itself is sufficient to prove the point.

The Ash'ari school argues that the verse 'لَا تُدْرِكُهُ الْأَبْصَارُ' does not deny the possibility of seeing God; rather, the very fact that God praises Himself for not being seen indicates that vision could be possible, otherwise the praise would be empty. Refuting the Limitation of Conceptualizing God
Taftazani rebuts the claim that knowledge of God can only be gained by intuition, definition, or description.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the objection that God cannot be conceptualized through intuition, definition, or description is refuted by alternative epistemic avenues in kalam.

Core Idea
Knowing God is not limited to human categories; revelation and direct witnessing open the door.

Taftazani now addresses a second objection that targets our very ability to conceptualize God. The objector argues that all conceptualization falls into three categories, none of which apply to the Necessary Being. Taftazani’s response is twofold: first, he challenges the exhaustive claim, introducing alternative means like inspiration and direct witnessing; second, he argues that even description can lead to knowledge. This passage thus defends the possibility of knowing God against a skeptical challenge.

Commentary
This passage begins the third investigation regarding the reality of al-ajal (the term of life). It first gives the linguistic definition: ajal means time, and can refer to the entire duration of something or its endpoint. As a technical term, it has come to mean the specific time at which God knows that the life of a living being will end; that time is unique and fixed. Death is an act of God, though it may follow a human act like killing. The slain person still dies at his appointed term – had he not been slain, we cannot be certain either that he would die or live at that moment. The passage then explains that allowing for the possibility of death being delayed by prayer or charity does not change God's knowledge; rather it affirms that had the killing not occurred, God would have known that too. Finally, the killer is punished for his own act of transgression, not for causing death as such, since death is from God. The passage concludes with an interpretation of Quran 6:2 regarding two terms: some interpret them as the term of death and the term of resurrection, others as the period from creation to death.

Commentary
This passage presents two opposing views on the term of the killed person. Abu al-Hudhayl claims that if not killed, the person would definitely die at the same time; many Mu'tazila claim he would definitely live until his natural term. The author then provides Sunni proofs. First, Quran 7:34 states that when the term comes, it cannot be delayed or advanced, indicating the term is fixed and unalterable. Second, if the term is unknown, we cannot know death or life. Third, the verse 'Nor is anything decreased from its life' (35:11) is interpreted to mean that decrease refers to another person's lifespan, not the same individual. Hadith about increasing lifespan through righteousness are from solitary reports (āḥād) and imply increase in good and blessing, not actual extension of lifespan. Allowing the hypothetical delay of death if not killed does not change God's knowledge but confirms it, since such a scenario is only possible based on God's knowledge. Finally, the killer's requital is for his sinful act and violation of prohibition, not for causing death, since death is from Allah.

Tenth Objection: Al-Aslah Eliminates Divine Favor and Choice
The tenth objection refutes al-aslah by showing it negates God's voluntary favor and selection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the obligation of al-aslah (most beneficial) would negate God's voluntary favor and choice, and how Quranic verses affirm God's absolute freedom.

Core Idea
Al-aslah would make God a dispenser, not a generous Lord.

The tenth and final objection strikes at the heart of the Mu'tazilite principle: if God must do what is most beneficial for creation, He loses the freedom to bestow favor voluntarily. The author cites four Quranic verses that explicitly affirm God's choice and mercy, showing that divine generosity cannot be compelled. Let's examine this powerful rebuttal.

Commentary
This is the tenth and final objection (العاشر) in this sequence. The author argues that if the principle of al-aslah (most beneficial) were obligatory upon God, it would negate the concept of divine favor (tafaddul) and choice (khīrah). God would be compelled to act in a certain way, leaving no room for voluntary generosity (in'ām wa ifḍāl). This contradicts numerous Quranic verses that affirm God's absolute freedom to create, choose, and bestow mercy and wisdom as He wills. The author cites four verses: Surah al-Qasas 28:68 ('Your Lord creates what He wills and chooses'), Surah al-Baqarah 2:105 ('He singles out for His mercy whom He wills'), Surah al-Baqarah 2:269 ('He gives wisdom to whom He wills'), and Surah Al 'Imran 3:33 ('Indeed, God chose Adam, Noah...'). The author then adds a concluding remark: by his life (lā 'amrī), the corruptions (mafāsid) of this Mu'tazilite principle are too obvious and numerous to count. He ends with a sharp statement: if the doctrine of al-aslah were true, the Mu'tazila would not have strayed from the path of guidance—meaning their deviation itself disproves their principle.
