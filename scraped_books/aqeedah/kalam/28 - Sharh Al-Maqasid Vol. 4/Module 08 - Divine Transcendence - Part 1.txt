The Four Topics of Divine Transcendence
The author lists the four topics of Chapter Two on divine transcendence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four central topics that structure the discussion of tawhid and divine transcendence in Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Divine transcendence is defended through four fundamental negations.

This passage serves as a chapter index, outlining the key investigations of Chapter Two. It establishes the core agenda for understanding tawhid not just as affirming God's oneness but also as actively negating any resemblance to creation. These four topics—negating corporeality, substance, accident, union, and temporal events—form the backbone of Islamic theological discourse on God's incomparability.

Guided Path
1
Read the chapter index
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that it is a list of four distinct investigations (mabāḥith).
The word 'mabāḥith' indicates these are topics to be studied in detail.
2
Identify the first topic
The first topic is 'on tawhid and transcendences'. Understand that tawhid here encompasses both affirming oneness and negating imperfections.
Tanzīh (transcendence) is a key concept alongside tawhid.
3
Identify the negation of corporeality
The second topic negates that God is a body, substance, or accident. This refutes anthropomorphism.
These three terms cover all created entities in classical physics.
4
Identify the negation of union and temporality
The third topic negates uniting with another, and the fourth negates being described by temporal events. These defend God's unique nature and timelessness.
Together, the four topics comprehensively safeguard divine transcendence.

Refuting Dualism: The Oneness of Good and Evil's Source
Al-Taftazani refutes dualist and Magian arguments for two principles of good and evil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Taftazani refutes the dualist argument that good and evil require two separate divine principles.

Core Idea
A single source of good and evil does not make God both good and evil.

This passage addresses a classic objection from Dualists and Magians: if one God created both good and evil, He would be both good and evil, which is impossible. Al-Taftazani shows how this entailment fails by carefully defining what 'good' and 'evil' mean when applied to God. The argument also considers the objection that an all-good God who allows evil must be powerless or evil Himself—a challenge answered by pointing to hidden wisdom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dualist sects and their beliefs
Read the description of the Dualists (light/darkness) and Magians (Yazdan/Ahriman). Notice their shared assumption: good and evil need separate origins.
2
Analyze their central doubt (shubha)
The doubt is that a single source of both good and evil would be both good and evil, which is impossible. Understand why they think this is a contradiction.
This is the key premise al-Taftazani will dismantle.
3
Examine al-Taftazani's answer (al-jawab)
He prevents the entailment: 'good' and 'evil' can be used in a qualified sense (predominance) or a general sense (creator). The term 'evil' is not properly applied to God.
The answer distinguishes between moral evil and the creation of evil.
4
Consider the counter-objection
If God is all-good and all-powerful, why doesn't He stop evil? The passage acknowledges this objection and hints at an answer involving hidden wisdom.
The text cuts off, but the response likely references divine wisdom.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of dualist and Magian polytheists. It begins by describing the Dualists (thanawiyya) who believe in two gods: light (source of good) and darkness (source of evil). The Magians (majus) believe in Yazdan as source of good and Ahriman as source of evil, with disagreement on whether Ahriman is eternal or originated from Yazdan. Their proof (shubha) is that if good and evil came from a single source, that source would have to be both good and evil, which is impossible. Al-Taftazani gives the answer (al-jawab): the entailment is denied if by 'good' one means that which predominantly brings good, and by 'evil' that which predominantly brings evil (or perhaps 'evil whose harm is impossible'? text unclear). The entailment is also blocked if one means simply the creator of good and creator of evil in general. The term 'evil' (sharrir) is not properly applied to God because it implies predominance of evil. Then a counter-objection (wu'rida) is raised: if the good (God) cannot repel evil, He is powerless; if He can but does not, He is evil. The answer would be (though the text cuts off) that the preservation of evil may be for hidden wisdom, as the Mu'tazila argue regarding the creation of Iblis. The passage ends abruptly with 'perhaps the same' (fal-'ala nafs), suggesting an unfinished argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic response to claims that prophets are sons of God, and the metaphorical interpretation of familial terms in scripture.

Core Idea
Words like 'father' and 'son' in scripture are metaphors, not biology.

This passage addresses two major claims from Jews and Christians about prophets being sons of God. The author provides a reasoned refutation based on scriptural interpretation and logical principles, showing that such language is metaphorical, not literal.

Understand the answer

Grasp the conclusion
Realize that the negation of partnership in divinity (shirk) is established by both reason and revelation. Worship is due to God alone.
The passage ends with a Quranic quotation emphasizing God's oneness.

Philosophical Arguments for Divine Transcendence
Philosophers' proofs that God is not body, substance, or accident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophical arguments that God cannot be a body, substance, or accident because each entails dependence or composition.

Core Idea
Physical categories imply need; the Necessary Being needs nothing.

This lesson introduces the central thesis that God transcends all physical categories. The philosophers (al-Ḥukamāʾ) present concise arguments showing that describing God as a body, substance, or accident is impossible, as each category involves dependence on something else. Understanding these arguments is foundational for grasping divine transcendence in Islamic theology.

3
Reflect on the underlying principle

This connects to the concept of Necessary Being (wājib al-wujūd).

Theological Proofs: God is Not in Place and Not a Body
Theologians' proofs that God is not in place and not a body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the theologians' arguments that God does not occupy space and is not composed of parts.

Core Idea
Spatial occupancy leads to absurdities; composition implies a need for a composer.

The theologians (al-Mutakallimūn) offer a more elaborate argument focusing on the implications of spatial occupancy and composition. They begin by defining substance and accident, then argue that the body is originated and requires a particularizer. They then show that if God were to occupy space, it would entail impossible consequences such as eternal originated things and the need for a particularizer. Finally, they argue that every body is composed of parts, so it cannot be the simple Necessary Being.

Guided Path
1
Read the theologians' arguments
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the structure: definitions, properties of body, reductio ad absurdum, and composition argument.
2
Analyze the reductio about space
The theologians imagine if God occupied space. Trace the steps: it would make space eternal, make God contingent, lead to particularization issues.
This is a powerful logical argument showing that spatiality is impossible for God.
3
Understand the composition argument
The passage concludes with a separate argument that the Necessary is not a body because every body is composed of parts (intellectual and existential).
This argument is common in kalām and falsafah.
4
Synthesize the lessons
Combine the philosophical and theological arguments to form a comprehensive understanding of divine transcendence.
Both traditions converge on God's incorporeality.

The Third Proof Against Corporeality: A Trilemma and Its Defense
Third disjunctive proof with objection and answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the trilemma argument against God being a body and how the objection regarding divine attributes is answered.

Core Idea
God cannot be a body: three impossible options all lead to contradiction.

Sanusi presents a third, more refined proof against corporeality. It is a disjunctive argument that considers three possibilities if God were a body, each leading to an impossibility. He then anticipates an objection that might undermine the proof and provides a clear distinction between divine attributes and bodily opposites.

Guided Path
1
Understand the trilemma structure
Recognize that the argument considers three and only three possibilities if God were a body: having all bodily attributes, none, or some.
This is a classic disjunctive syllogism.
2
Examine the first horn (all attributes)
If God had all bodily attributes, opposites like motion and rest would coexist, which is impossible.
3
Examine the second horn (no attributes)
If God had no bodily attributes, some necessary concomitants would be negated, but a body cannot lack both opposites simultaneously.
E.g., a body must be either moving or at rest.
4
Examine the third horn (some attributes)
If God had some but not all bodily properties, a specifier would be needed; without it, preponderance without cause arises.
Both options are impossible for the Necessary Being.
5
Review the objection and answer
The objection claims the same reasoning could be used to deny God's actual attributes. The answer distinguishes essential perfections from contingent opposites.
Divine attributes are inherent perfections; their opposites are deficiencies God is free from.

Thus, the assumption that God is spatially located leads to absurdity, proving His transcendence above place and direction.

Proofs Against Union and Indwelling in God
Al-Maturidi presents two proofs against union and indwelling in God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational arguments proving that the Necessary Being cannot unite with another or indwell in anything.

Core Idea
Union and indwelling imply dependence, contradicting divine necessity.

Al-Maturidi now turns to two key implications of divine transcendence: God does not unite with another being nor does He indwell in anything. These ideas are central to preserving God's absolute independence. The following passage presents two distinct proofs—one against union, two against indwelling—each grounded in the impossibility of dependence in the Necessary Being.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic once without over-analysing. Let the structure emerge: first union, then indwelling with two proofs and an objection-answer.
Notice the compact style; each proof relies on the concept of need (iftiqār).
2
Understand the argument against union
The union argument is brief: union of two things would make the Necessary become possible and vice versa, which is absurd. Reflect on why this is impossible by necessity.
This builds on earlier discussions about impossibility of union.
3
Analyze the first proof against indwelling
The first proof: whatever indwells depends on its locus. That dependence negates necessity. Then an objection about mixture (imtizāj) is answered: mixture is a bodily property involving division, so it reduces to body-in-place indwelling.
Note the universal scope: body, accident, form, attribute—all cases fall under the same dependency.
4
Analyze the second proof against indwelling
The second proof is a disjunction: if indwelling occurred, either it would be necessary or possible. If necessary, God would depend on the locus, implying His possibility and the locus's priority. If possible, He is independent—so indwelling is unnecessary. Either way, indwelling is impossible.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.

Commentary
This passage is a proof that the Necessary Being (al-wājib) neither unites with another nor indwells in anything. Then it gives two proofs against indwelling (ḥulūl). Either way, indwelling is impossible for the Necessary Being.

Correct Doctrine: Annihilation in Divine Unity (Fana')
Al-Taftazani distinguishes true spiritual annihilation from incarnation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the correct doctrine of spiritual annihilation (fana') in tawhid and how it differs from false doctrines of incarnation and union.

Core Idea
Spiritual annihilation is losing the illusion of otherness, not becoming God.

In this passage, al-Taftazani addresses two doctrines that might be mistaken for incarnation (hulul) or union (ittihad) but are actually orthodox. The first is the state of fana'—annihilation in divine unity. The seeker becomes so absorbed in contemplation of God that he loses awareness of his own existence and sees only God. This is a transformative spiritual experience, not a literal metaphysical change. Let's examine the text to understand this subtle but crucial distinction.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the two schools
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Note that the author mentions two schools that give the impression of incarnation and union. Focus on how he emphatically separates them from those false doctrines.
The passage only explains the first school; the second is mentioned but not detailed.
2
Understand the description of the first school
The first school describes the seeker's essence vanishing in God's essence and his attributes in God's attributes. The seeker becomes absent from everything else and sees only God. This is called 'annihilation in tawhid'.
The phrase 'vanishes in' (تضمحل) is key—it indicates a subjective state, not an actual ontological change.
3
Reflect on why this is not hulul or ittihad
The author explicitly says these schools 'give the impression of incarnation and union, but they are not part of it at all.' Why? Because fana' is about the seeker's perception, not a literal merging of essences.
The cutting off of the hadith suggests further scriptural support, but the point is clear: this is an orthodox spiritual state.

Commentary
This passage clarifies two correct doctrines that superficially resemble hulul and ittihad but are fundamentally different. The author names them as 'two other schools'. He then explains the first: it is the state of annihilation (fana') in tawhid. In this state, the seeker's self-awareness and awareness of created things vanish. He is so absorbed in contemplation of God's unity that he sees nothing but God. However, this is a subjective experience, not an actual ontological union. The author cites a divine hadith (hadith qudsi) to support this, though the quote is cut off. This doctrine is orthodox and does not imply incarnation.

Refutation of Divine Incarnation and Indwelling
Author rejects claims that God appears in human form or indwells mystics.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the claims of God appearing in human form or dwelling in a mystic are rationally and theologically unacceptable.

Core Idea
God's transcendence precludes any form of physical indwelling or union.

The author addresses two extreme claims: that God may appear in human form (analogous to angelic apparitions), and that a mystic may become a locus for divine indwelling, even uttering 'He is I.' These views violate divine transcendence, and the author dismisses them as self-evidently false. Let's examine the arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the author uses a very dismissive tone at the end.
2
Identify the two erroneous views
The first view claims God can appear in human form, analogizing from angels. The second view claims God can dwell in or unite with a mystic. Find these in the text.
The first view is linked to Ali and his descendants; the second to certain Sufis.
3
Understand the author's dismissal
The author says 'the corruption of both views is clear without need for explanation.' Ask yourself why he considers them so obviously false.
Think about Islamic teachings on God's transcendence (tanzih).
4
Reflect on theological implications
Consider what this passage teaches about the nature of God in Islam. How does it protect divine transcendence?
This connects to the broader theme of tawhid.

Understanding Fanā' and the Hadith of Divine Closeness
Author discusses Sufi fanā' and refutes literalist interpretations of a hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the proper interpretation of the hadith 'I become his hearing...' and why it does not imply pantheism or indwelling.

Core Idea
Divine closeness is experiential, not literal indwelling or union.

The hadith of divine closeness is often misunderstood. In this passage, the author explains that the language of such experiences is limited, and that fanā' is a path of direct vision, not rational proof. He also refutes the pantheistic notion that all existence is one absolute being. This lesson helps you navigate these subtle distinctions.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith and author's caution
Start by reading the quoted part of the hadith and the author's note that expressions of indwelling or union arise from inadequate language, not reality.
Notice the author immediately distances himself from literal interpretations.
2
Understand fanā' as experiential
The author states that the path of fanā' is through direct vision (ʿiyān), not demonstration (burhān). Grasp the distinction between experiential knowledge and rational proof.
3
Refutation of absolute existence
The author presents and rejects the view that the Necessary Being is absolute existence without multiplicity. Understand why he considers this outside reason and revelation.
This part references an earlier discussion on existence.

Commentary
This passage is an argument and commentary by the author on the famous hadith regarding divine closeness. The author begins by quoting part of the hadith: 'I love him, and when I love him, I become his hearing...' He then notes that such expressions may lead to statements implying indwelling (ḥulūl) or union (ittibād) due to the incapacity of language to fully describe the spiritual state. The author distances himself from such interpretations, emphasizing that the path of fanā' is experiential, not demonstrative. He then presents a second view: that the Necessary Being is absolute existence, without multiplicity, and that apparent multiplicity is illusory. The author rejects this as well, stating it is outside reason and revelation. He concludes the first part by referencing his earlier refutation. He then provides the full citation and text of the hadith from Bukhari. The overall purpose is to caution against literalist or pantheistic readings of the hadith.
