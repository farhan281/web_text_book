Commentary
This passage presents the theological (mutakallimun) method for negating substance and accident from God. The author explains that early mutakallimun base their definitions on language, not on philosophical usage. In language, 'jawhar' refers to the essence or constituent of a thing, and 'arad' to what cannot endure. While in observable reality, substances are self-subsistent and accidents subsist through others, the names are applied based on the linguistic meanings, not the philosophical categories. Examples from Arabic usage illustrate: 'noble jawhar' means noble origin, 'jawhari cloth' means well-woven, and 'arad' denotes fleeting occurrences like a cloud's shadow. Hence, God's eternal attributes, which subsist by His essence, are not called accidents even though they inhere in Him, because the linguistic understanding of 'arad' implies impermanence, which does not apply to divine attributes.

Core Idea
In kalam, you defeat an objection by conceding premises step by step, not by outright denial.

The author presents a structured method for defending prophetic infallibility against rational objections. Using the example of Moses asking to see Allah, he shows how a defender can systematically dismantle the objection by making conditional concessions (taslim). Understand the objection

Scriptural Conflict Resolved by Reason
The author notes scriptural conflicts and justifies recourse to two rational proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ash'ari theologians resolve apparent contradictions in scriptural evidences by prioritizing two specific rational proofs: the proof of the determining motive and the proof of eternal knowledge.

Core Idea
When scriptures conflict, reason becomes the final arbiter.

This passage reveals a crucial methodological principle in Ash'ari theology: when scriptural evidences seem to contradict each other, the theologian must turn to rational proofs for resolution. The author notes that Imam al-Razi compiled many scriptural types but ultimately grounds his position on two rational arguments that, as a Mu'tazili admitted, are the ultimate refutation of their school.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological note about how the Ash'ari theologian (specifically Imam al-Razi) handles the apparent conflict between scriptural evidences. The author says that listing all the types of verses individually would be lengthy; al-Razi has already done so in his work al-Matalib al-'Aliya. He also cites many hadiths that align with the types of verses. But the key point is that scriptural evidences (al-adilla al-sam'iyya) are contradictory on this issue — some verses seem to support free will, others predestination. Therefore, the final resort is to rational proofs (al-'aqliyyat). Al-Razi's main arguments in this context are two: the proof of the determining motive (al-da'i al-mujib) and the proof of eternal knowledge (al-'ilm al-azali). The former argues that God's knowledge determines the action, the latter that the motive necessitates the act. The author adds a clever remark from a Mu'tazili: these two proofs are the 'two enemies' of Mu'tazilism, meaning they destroy their position; otherwise, if you cannot refute them, you have lost your case (lost the 'platform').
