The Second Zahiri Argument and Its Refutation
Second Zahiri argument and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second Zahiri argument based on perfect knowledge and its refutation, including the obligation according to capacity and the role of Shari'ah.

Core Idea
Our obligation is according to capacity, not absolute knowledge.

The Zahiris also argued that if additional attributes existed, the perfect knowers would know them. This lesson unpacks that argument and shows how the author refutes it by limiting obligation and pointing to Shari'ah as a source of knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation. Note that this argument has two parts: the perfect knowers' knowledge and the limited ways to know attributes.
The refutation is detailed: denying the obligation, questioning the knowledge of perfect knowers, and rejecting the limited means.
2
Analyze the Zahiri reasoning
The Zahiris claim we must know all attributes, yet we (perfect knowers) do not know additional ones, so they don't exist. Also, attributes can only be known through actions and transcendence, which don't indicate more.
3
Examine the refutation points
The author refutes by denying the obligation (we are only obligated according to capacity), questioning the assumption that perfect knowers didn't know another attribute, and rejecting the claim that Shari'ah is not a way to know attributes.
The final rhetorical question emphasizes Shari'ah as a valid source.

Al-Baqa': The Ash'ari Proof and the Majority's Objections
Ash'ari asserts baqa' as an attribute and the majority counter with two objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari argument for baqa' as an additional attribute and the two main objections from the majority that baqa' is not additional to existence.

Core Idea
The existence of a thing does not guarantee its permanence; thus the Necessary Being must have baqa' as a distinct attribute.

The attribute of al-baqa' (permanence) is one of the disputed divine attributes. Ash'aris affirm it as a meaning subsisting in the divine essence, distinct from existence itself. In this passage, al-Ash'ari presents his proof and then the majority raise two objections, arguing that baqa' is merely existence continued in time. We will unpack each part.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation to grasp the overall flow. Notice the shift from Ash'ari's proof to the majority's objections.
2
Identify the proof and the objections
Ash'ari's proof: because the Necessary is permanent, there must be a meaning of permanence. Then two objections: first, baqa' is just existence related to a second time; second, if baqa' were additional, it would compromise self-sufficiency.
3
Reflect on the dialectic
Notice how each side uses the concept of necessity. The majority's second objection is especially sharp: an added attribute would make the Necessary dependent.
This connects to the broader debate on divine simplicity.

Among the disputed attributes is al-baqa' (permanence). Shaykh al-Ash'ari and his followers among the Ahl al-Sunnah affirm it, because the Necessary Being is necessarily baqin (permanent), so there must subsist in Him a meaning which is baqa', just as in the knowing and the powerful. For al-baqa' is not among negations or relations, and that is apparent; nor is it an expression for existence, but rather additional to it, because a thing may exist and not remain (yabqa), like accidents, especially the transitory ones. The majority went to the view that it is not an attribute additional to existence, for reasons: First: that what is understood from it is the continuation of existence (istimrar al-wujud), and there is no meaning to that other than existence in terms of its relation to the second time after the first time. Second: that if the Necessary were baqin by a baqa' that is not identical to His essence, He would not be necessary of existence in essence, because that which exists by essence, by itself, is baqin by essence necessarily, for that which is by essence never ceases. And if baqa' is interpreted as existence in the second time, the implication of absurdity is more evident, because it leads to the Necessary being existent in the second time due to something other than His essence. And Sahib al-Saha'if objected that the necessary consequence is only the dependence of an attribute on another attribute arising from the essence, and there is no impossibility in that, like will depending on knowledge and knowledge on life. But it is nothing, because existence is not from the essence. And even if granted, its dependence on something other than the essence contradicts necessity by essence.

Commentary
This passage begins the discussion of the attribute al-baqa' (permanence). The author states that Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari and his followers affirm baqa' as an attribute because the Necessary Being is necessarily baqin (permanent), so there must subsist in Him a meaning (ma'na) that is baqa', just as in the case of knowledge and power. This is because baqa' is not a negation (salb) or relation (idafa), nor is it identical to existence, since things may exist but not continue, like accidents, especially transitory ones. The majority, however, argue that baqa' is not additional to existence. They give two reasons. First: baqa' rationally means continuation of existence, which is just existence itself considered in relation to a second time. Second: if the Necessary were baqin by a baqa' not identical to His essence, then He would not be necessary in essence, because that which exists by essence is baqin by essence. If baqa' is interpreted as existence in a second time, then the Necessary would exist in the second time due to something other than His essence, which contradicts necessity. The author then reports an objection from Sahib al-Saha'if (al-Samarqandi): the only consequence is that an attribute depends on another attribute arising from the essence, which is not impossible, like will depending on knowledge and knowledge on life. The author disagrees: existence is not from the essence. Even if granted, dependence on something other than the essence contradicts necessity by essence.

Responding to Sahib al-Saha'if's Objection on Baqa'
Sahib al-Saha'if objects, and the author offers a two-part rebuttal.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Dependence on another attribute arising from the essence is not the same as dependence on something outside the essence.

Third Argument: The Trilemma Against Baqa'
Sanusi presents the third argument as a trilemma against baqa' as an additional attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the trilemma (dawr, khulf, ta'addud al-wajib) presented as the third argument against baqa' as an additional attribute, and why each horn is problematic.

Core Idea
The third argument traps the doctrine of baqa' in a trilemma: circularity, contradiction, or multiple necessary beings.

The majority theologians argue that if baqa' were an additional attribute, it would lead to one of three absurdities. This trilemma is a powerful logical tool to show that the divine essence must be eternal by itself. In this lesson, we will trace the reasoning behind each horn and see why the Ash'ari position fails.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trilemma structure
Recognize that the argument presents three possibilities for dependence between the essence and baqa'.
Each horn is spelled out clearly in the passage.
2
First horn: circularity (dawr)
If baqa' depends on the essence, then each needs the other for continuance in the second time, leading to circularity.
Circularity is invalid because it prevents independent subsistence.
3
Second horn: contradiction (khulf)
If the essence depends on baqa', then baqa' would be the necessary being, not the essence—a contradiction.
This contradicts the primacy of the divine essence.
4
Third horn: multiplicity of the necessary (ta'addud al-wajib)
If neither depends on the other, each would be independent of everything, implying multiple necessary existents.
This is impossible because only one necessary being exists.
5
Conclusion of the argument
All three horns are absurd, so the assumption that baqa' is an additional attribute must be false.

The third argument is a trilemma: if the essence were baqin by a baqa' not by itself, then either (a) the attribute baqa' depends on the essence – leading to circularity (dawr) because each depends on the other for continuance in a second time; (b) the essence depends on baqa' despite being self-sufficient – then baqa' would be the necessary, not the essence (khulf); (c) neither depends on the other, but both occur together as al-Mawaqif suggests – then both would be independent of everything else, leading to multiple necessary existents (ta'addud al-wajib). Moreover, baqa' cannot be independent of the essence because attributes necessarily depend on the essence. The fourth argument: if baqa' were an eternal attribute additional to the essence, then baqa' itself would be baqin necessarily, requiring either another baqa' ad infinitum (tasalsul) – invalid, and also implying subsistence of meaning in meaning – which is invalid; or baqa' would not be baqin – then it would be like a knower without knowledge, which is false. An objector says: baqa' is baqin by itself without additional baqa'. The author replies: then by analogy, God could be baqin by a baqa' that is His essence and knowing by knowledge that is His essence, thus no additional attributes are established.

The Objection to Attribute Endurance and a Rejected Solution
Objection and first rejected solution to attribute endurance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that eternal attributes cannot endure without an additional attribute, and why the proposal to simply avoid attributing endurance to them fails.

Core Idea
Eternal attributes must endure, but a 'solution' that denies their endurance is self-defeating.

Sanusi presents a strong objection to the earlier claim that the attributes are identical to the essence. If they are distinct, how can they be eternal without an infinite chain of attributes? The first proposed solution—to say attributes are not enduring—is quickly shown to be impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
The objection argues that eternal attributes like knowledge and power are necessarily enduring, yet they cannot have an additional baqa' (endurance) because that would lead to a meaning subsisting in a meaning and a multiplicity of eternal entities. Understand the logical dilemma.
The objection builds on the earlier proof that attributes are not identical to the essence.
2
Identify the first solution
Some early scholars proposed that we only say the essence endures with its attributes, but we do not say the attributes themselves are enduring. This attempts to sidestep the problem.
This solution is presented and then immediately refuted.
3
Understand the refutation
Sanusi states that the falsity of this solution is clear because an eternal attribute's endurance is necessary (ضروري). Denying it would contradict the nature of eternal attributes.
This refutation shows that the problem cannot be dismissed so lightly.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection to the previous answer and then gives three solutions (wujuh) to the problem of how eternal attributes (like knowledge, power) can be enduring without requiring an additional attribute of baqa'. Second Ash'ari solution to attribute endurance with analogy and defense.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Each attribute is self-enduring, just as a body's spatial mode is itself spatial by itself.

The second solution, attributed to al-Ash'ari, avoids infinite regress by claiming that each attribute's endurance is identical to the attribute itself. Sanusi elaborates with an analogy of a body and its spatial mode, and then answers an objection concerning overlap of functions.

Guided Path

Read the second solution

The analogy clarifies how something can be both an attribute and its own endurance.

Address the objection

This preserves the distinct roles while avoiding contradiction.

Core Idea

By the end, the defender turns the tables, placing the burden of proof on those who posit additional attributes.

Ash'ari's Third Proof on the Eternity of Attributes
Presentation and refutation of Ash'ari's third proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Ash'ari's third proof that attributes are eternal by the essence's eternity due to lack of distinction, and the objections and refutation that follow.

Core Idea
Attributes are eternal by the essence's eternity because there is no distinction, unlike substance and accidents.

Al-Ash'ari presents his third proof for the eternity of divine attributes: they are eternal by the very eternity of the essence since there is no distinction (maghayara) between essence and attributes. This is contrasted with the case of a substance and its accidents. However, the proof faces objections: if attributes are not distinct, they are not identical either, and an attribute subsisting in an essence cannot be attributed to what is not that essence. A final objection about knowledge being powerful is dismissed. This lesson examines this dense argument.

Guided Path
1

Al-Ash'ari argues: because there is no distinction between essence and attributes, the attributes are eternal by the same eternity as the essence. This is unlike a substance and its accidents, which are distinct.
The proof relies on the key concept of 'lack of distinction' (adam al-mughayara).
2
Understand the first objection
The objector says: attributes are neither identical to the essence (they are not 'ayn) nor distinct (ghayr). Therefore, an attribute that subsists in an essence cannot be attributed to something that is not that essence.
This challenges the logic of attributing the essence's eternity to the attributes.
3

If attributes are eternal by the essence's eternity, then they would be knowing by His knowledge, powerful by His power, etc. The response says this is invalid because knowledge's eternity is established, but being powerful is not analogous.
The objection tries to push the argument into absurdity, but the response differentiates between attributes.

الفصل الرابع في أحواله : ذهب أهل الحق إلى أنه تعالى مع تنزهه عن الجهة والمقابلة يصح أن يرى ويراه المؤمنون في الجنة، خلافاً لسائر الفرق، ولا نزاع لهم في إمكان الانكشاف التام العلمي ولا لنا في امتناع ارتسام الصورة أو اتصال الشعاع، أو حالة مستلزمة لذلك، بل المتنازع أنا إذا نظرنا إلى البدر قلنا حالة إدراكية نسميها الرؤية مغايرة، وكما إذا غمضنا العين، وإن كان ذلك انكشافاً جلياً، فهل يمكن أن يحصل للعباد بالنسبة إلى الله تعالى، تلك الحالة، وإن لم يكن هناك مقابلة؟ لنا على الإمكان وجهان : أحدهما قوله تعالى حكاية عن موسى عليه السلام (ورب أرني أنظر إليك) الآية. وذلك أن موسى طلب الرؤية، ولم يكن عابثاً ولا جاهلا والله تعالى علقها على استقرار الجبل، وهو ممكن في نفسه. واعترض على الأول بأنه : إنما طلب العلم الضروري أو رؤية آية، ولو سلم فالجهل بمسألة الرؤية لا يخل بالمعرفة، ورد بأن (لن تراني) نفي للرؤية لا للعلم، أو رؤية الآية كيف والعلم حاصل...؟ والآيات كثيرة، والحاصل منها حينئذ إنما هو على تقدير الاندكاك دون الاستقرار، والرؤية المقرونة بالنظر الموصول (بإلى) نص في معناها والقوم إنما يصدقون للنبي فيكفيهم إخباره بامتناع الرؤية أو لا، فلا يفيد حكايته عن الله تعالى ولا يليق بالنبي تأخر رد الباطل كما في طلب جعل الإله ولا طلب الدليل بهذا الطريق، ولا الجهل في الإلهيات بما يعرفه آحاد المعتزلة.
The fourth chapter on His states: The people of truth held that He, exalted, despite His transcendence over direction and facing, it is correct that He be seen, and the believers will see Him in Paradise, contrary to the other sects. There is no dispute among them about the possibility of complete scientific disclosure, nor among us about the impossibility of the impression of an image or the connection of a ray, or a state entailing that. Rather, the disputed point is: when we look at the full moon, we say there is a perceptual state we call vision, different from when we close the eye, even if that is a clear disclosure. So is it possible that this state occurs for servants with respect to God, even if there is no facing? We have two proofs for possibility: First, His saying, telling of Moses, peace be upon him: 'My Lord, show me [Yourself] that I may look upon You.' That is because Moses requested vision, and he was not frivolous nor ignorant, and God Almighty suspended it upon the mountain's stability, which is possible in itself. An objection was raised against the first: that he only requested necessary knowledge or the vision of a sign; even if granted, ignorance of the matter of vision does not impair knowledge. It was replied that 'You will not see Me' is a negation of vision, not of knowledge; or [if it was] vision of a sign, how, when knowledge was already attained? And the verses are many, and what is obtained from them then is only upon the assumption of crumbling, not stability; and vision coupled with looking connected by 'to' is explicit in its meaning. And the people only believe the prophet, so his informing them of the impossibility of vision suffices or not, so his narrative from God does not benefit, and it is not fitting for the prophet to delay refuting falsehood, as in the request to make a god, nor the seeking of proof in this way, nor ignorance in divine matters of what the individual Mu'tazilites know.

Commentary
This passage is a combined statement of doctrine and proof. It begins by declaring the position of Ahl al-Haqq (the people of truth, i.e., Sunni Muslims) that it is permissible for Allah to be seen, and that believers will see Him in Paradise, unlike other sects. It clarifies that there is no disagreement about the possibility of complete intellectual disclosure (scientific knowledge) or about the impossibility of physical processes like image impression or light rays. The real dispute is whether a special perceptual state, which we call 'vision' (ru'ya) when looking at the moon, can occur for humans with respect to God even without physical facing. The author then presents the first proof: the story of Moses (peace be upon him) who asked to see God (Quran 7:143). Moses was not frivolous or ignorant, so his request shows that vision is possible. God suspended the fulfillment on the mountain's stability, which is a possible event. An objection is raised: perhaps Moses sought necessary knowledge or a sign, not literal vision. The reply is that the response 'You will not see Me' specifically negates vision, not knowledge; and if it were a sign, knowledge was already present. The passage then addresses further objections about the prophet's role and the appropriateness of such a request, concluding that the Mu'tazilites' objections are invalid.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think that because Moses was denied actual vision, the proof shows impossibility.
Correction: The proof is about possibility, not actual occurrence. The condition shows it is possible in itself, even if not actualized.

Checkpoint
What does the condition of the mountain's stability indicate about the vision?
A) That vision is impossible because the mountain did not stay.
B) That vision is possible because the condition is possible.
C) That Moses was mistaken.
D) That the vision is metaphorical.

Key Takeaway
Moses' request for vision, made by a prophet without frivolity, and the conditional response from Allah, proves that seeing Allah is possible.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Moses, a prophet, yearned to see Allah, yet you have access to knowledge of His existence without such a request. Do you feel the same longing to truly 'see' your Lord with the eyes of your heart, or have you become complacent with mere intellectual assent? What will you do today to stir that desire?
?
If you were in Moses' place, would you have dared to ask to see Allah? What does your answer reveal about the strength of your certainty (yaqīn) and your relationship with the Divine?
?
The proof shows that asking for the impossible is unbecoming of a prophet. Reflect: are there any 'impossible' excuses you use to avoid drawing closer to Allah (e.g., 'I can never be pious' or 'I am too sinful to repent')? Are you treating these as if they are obstacles in Allah's power? How can Moses' example shatter that illusion?
