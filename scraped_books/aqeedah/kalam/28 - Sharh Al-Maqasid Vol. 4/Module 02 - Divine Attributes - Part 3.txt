Three Additional Proofs: Consensus, Perfection, and Order
Sanusi presents three additional arguments for divine power and knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand three supplementary arguments for God's power and knowledge: consensus and scripture, the nature of perfection, and the order of the cosmos.

Core Idea
Three quick proofs: scripture says so, perfection is good, order implies a wise maker.

After the sixth proof, Sanusi offers three more arguments that are often used to establish that the Creator is powerful and knowing. These are drawn from consensus and revelatory texts, the inherent perfections of attributes like power and life, and the observed order and craftsmanship in the universe. Each argument has its own strength and weaknesses, which the author will later critique.

Guided Path
1
Read the three proofs
Read the Arabic passage and translation. Identify each proof: the proof from consensus and scripture (al-ijma' wa al-nusus), the proof from perfection (al-kamal), and the proof from cosmic order (al-nizam).
2
Understand the first proof: consensus and scripture
The first proof appeals to the consensus of Muslims and the definitive texts of Quran and Sunnah, which affirm God's power and knowledge. Consider the role of revelation in theological reasoning.
3
Understand the second proof: perfections and their opposites
The second proof argues that power, knowledge, and life are perfections, and their opposites (ignorance, inability, death) are deficiencies. Since God must be free from deficiency, He must possess these perfections.
4
Understand the third proof: order implies a wise maker
The third proof is a classic design argument: the universe displays subtle craftsmanship, perfect order, and solidity, so its maker must be knowing and powerful by necessity.
5
Reflect on the cumulative force
Consider how these three proofs together reinforce the conclusion, but note that each relies on certain premises that may be challenged, as the author will later do.

This passage is a continuation of arguments for God's power and knowledge, presenting the sixth proof and three additional proofs, followed by a critique. The ninth proof (causality) assumes that observed phenomena are directly caused by God without intermediate causes, a claim made by philosophers but not universally accepted. The passage ends on a hopeful note: the sincere seeker of truth may still attain certainty from these arguments.

Critique of the Proofs: A Scholarly Assessment
Sanusi critiques the six proofs and three additional proofs, noting their weaknesses but affirming their potential.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the potential weaknesses of the proofs for God's power and knowledge as critiqued by the author, and why they may still yield certainty for the sincere seeker.

Core Idea
The proofs are not flawless, but they can still convince the truth-seeker.

After presenting several proofs, Sanusi does not present them as unassailable. He offers a critique, pointing out that each proof has grounds for discussion. The first six proofs (presumably those from earlier sections) are only fully understood by those versed in philosophy. The scriptural proof may involve circularity, the perfection argument rests on disputed premises, and the cosmic argument assumes a particular view of causality. Yet he ends on a hopeful note, suggesting a sincere seeker may still attain certainty.

Guided Path
1
Read the critique passage
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice how the author systematically addresses each type of proof: the first six, the seventh (scripture), the eighth (perfection), and the ninth (cosmic order).
2
Understand the critique of the first six proofs
The author says these proofs are 'hardly hidden from the one who reflects and is versed in philosophy.' This implies they are not easily accessible to everyone and may rely on philosophical concepts.
3
Understand the critique of the seventh proof (scripture)
The scriptural proof depends on the authority of revelation, but accepting that authority itself requires prior belief in God's power and knowledge, creating a potential circularity.
4
Understand the critique of the eighth proof (perfection)
This proof relies on premises such as what constitutes a perfection in God and that God must possess all perfections. These premises can be debated.
5
Understand the critique of the ninth proof (cosmic order)
This proof assumes that the observed phenomena are directly caused by God without intermediate causes, a claim made by philosophers but not universally accepted.

First Objection: Need for a Preponderating Factor and Its Refutation
Objection against divine power and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first objection against divine power regarding the need for a preponderating factor, its implication of infinite regress, and the refutation that denies both implications by appealing to the will itself and distinguishing between types of preponderating.

Core Idea
God's will is inherently preponderating — no external factor needed.

Opponents of divine power often raise a dilemma: either God's power needs a preponderating factor to choose an action, leading to infinite regress, or it does not, making it impossible to prove God's existence through His actions. Sanusi records this objection and then offers a concise refutation that denies both horns. Let's examine how he defends divine power.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and its dilemma
First, identify the opponent's argument: if the attachment of power requires a preponderating factor (مرجح), then infinite regress follows; if not, then we cannot prove the Maker. Understand the two horns.
The opponent tries to force a contradiction in the concept of divine power.
2
Study the refutation's two points
The refutation denies both implications. It first says the preponderating factor can be the will itself (الإرادة) by its essence, stopping the regress. Second, it distinguishes between a capable agent's choice and a possible thing's existence: the former does not need an external cause, the latter does.
The key distinction: agent's self-determination vs. thing's coming into existence.
3
Reflect on the significance
Consider why this refutation is important: it preserves God's free will as self-sufficient, without requiring an external cause for His choices, and avoids the absurdity of infinite regress.
This is a classic kalam argument for divine freedom.

Commentary
This passage records the first of several objections typically raised by opponents (likely philosophers or theologians who deny that God acts with free choice) regarding the attribute of power (القدرة). The objection poses a dilemma: If God's power requires a preponderating factor (مرجح) to select one action over another, this leads to an infinite regress (تسلسل) because that factor itself would require another, and so on. If instead no factor is needed, then the door to proving the existence of the Maker (إثبات الصانع) is closed, because we would have no way to distinguish God's act from mere chance. The refutation (ورد) denies both implications (منع الملازمتين) by offering two points. First, the preponderating factor could simply be the attachment of will (الإرادة) by its very essence (لذاتها)—that is, God's will is inherently decisive and does not need an external cause. Second, the opponent's argument confuses two senses of 'preponderating without a factor'. When a capable agent (القادر) chooses between two possible actions, his choice without an external inclination (داعية) is simply self-determination. This is different from a possible thing (الممكن) existing without an effective cause (مؤثر), which would be absurd. So the dilemma fails.

The Attachment of Divine Power and Will: Eternal or Temporal?
Second objection and its refutation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection that God's power and will must be either eternal or temporal is refuted by distinguishing the direction of attachment from the creation event.

Core Idea
God's eternal will can be directed to a future creation without the world being eternal.

This passage tackles a subtle objection about the relationship between God's eternal attributes and temporal creation. Critics argue that if God's power and will are attached to creation, that attachment must itself be either eternal or temporal — both posing problems. The refutation shows two ways the attachment can avoid these pitfalls, preserving both divine transcendence and creatio ex nihilo.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the English translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before interpreting.
Notice the concise style: the objection is stated in a few lines, then refuted.
2
Identify the two horns of the objection
The objection presents a dichotomy: the attachment is either eternal (قديم) or temporal (حادث). List the consequences of each: eternal → world eternal; temporal → infinite regress.
3
Understand the refutation's two responses
The refutation denies the forced choice (المنع) and offers two alternatives. First: the attachment is eternal but directed to creation at a future time (في ما لا يزال). Second: the attachment itself is temporal by its own essence (لذاتها). Trace how each avoids the unwanted consequences.
This shows that the attachment's mode need not imply the world's eternity.
4
Reflect on the theological significance
Consider how this defense maintains Allah's transcendent eternity while affirming that the world began in time. The eternal attribute attaches to a temporal act without contradiction.
This is a key move in kalam to reconcile divine attributes with creation ex nihilo.

الثاني : أن تعلق القدرة والإرادة إما قديم فيلزم قدم العالم، وإما حادث فتتسلسل الحوادث ورد بالمنع الجواز أن يتعلقا في الأزل بإيجاده في ما لا يزال، أو يكون حدوث تعلقهما لذاتها.

Commentary
This passage presents the second objection: the attachment (تعلق) of God's power and will to creation must be either eternal (قديم) or temporal (حادث). If eternal, then the world must be eternal (since the attachment would have always been effective), which contradicts Islamic creation ex nihilo. If temporal, then the attachment itself would be a temporal event requiring another attachment, leading to an infinite regress of temporal events (تسلسل الحوادث). The refutation (ورد) denies the necessity of this dichotomy (المنع) and offers two possibilities: either the attachment is eternal in the sense that it is directed in pre-eternity (الأزل) to bringing the world into existence at the future time (في ما لا يزال) — that is, God's eternal will decided to create at a specific later time — or the attachment itself becomes temporal by its own essence (لذاتها), meaning it is inherently a temporal event without needing a prior attachment. Thus, the world need not be eternal, and the regress is avoided.

The Fifth Objection: Need or Futility?

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An act's appropriateness can be intrinsic or for others, avoiding both need and futility.

This lesson presents the fifth objection to divine freedom of action: if an act is more appropriate than refraining, it implies God seeks perfection; if not, it's futile. The refutation offers a nuanced answer: the act can be more appropriate in itself or for the benefit of creation. Let's examine this argument closely.

The refutation says that it suffices to negate futility if the act is more appropriate either in itself (في نفسه) or in relation to another (بالنسبة إلى الغير). This dissolves the dilemma.

Core Idea
An effect can be possible in itself yet not actual in eternity because it depends on a free choice.

The sixth objection presents a dilemma: if an eternal act of a free agent were impossible from eternity, then later becoming possible would imply a change. If it were possible, then an eternal effect could depend on a free agent. The refutation cuts through by distinguishing between intrinsic possibility and impossibility due to being an effect of a free cause. Let's examine the passage.

The First Objection on Divine Power and Its Refutation
First objection on divine power and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first objection against the Ash'ari view of divine power and how it is refuted by denying both implications and distinguishing between specification and causation.

Core Idea
The refutation distinguishes an agent's choice from a cause's preponderance.

In this passage, Sanusi presents an objection that challenges the Ash'ari doctrine of divine power and will. The objection claims that if God's power needs a preponderating factor, it leads to infinite regress; if not, the proof for God's existence collapses. The refutation denies both implications and clarifies the role of the divine will.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Start by reading the Arabic objection and its translation. Notice that the objection presents a dilemma with two horns: either divine power requires a preponderating factor (leading to infinite regress) or it does not (closing the door to proving God).
The objection is structured as a conditional argument.
2
Understand the two horns of the dilemma
The first horn: if the attachment of power depends on a preponderating factor, that factor itself would need another, causing an infinite chain (tasalsul). The second horn: if it does not, then the principle that every possible requires a preponderating factor (used to prove God) is violated.
This dilemma targets the consistency of the Ash'ari position.
3
Examine the refutation
The refutation (radd) denies both implications (man' al-mulazamatayn). It offers two reasons: first, the preponderating factor can be God's will itself, which attaches by its essence; second, the powerful agent's specification of one of two possible acts without a motive is not the same as a possible thing existing without an effect.
Pay attention to the distinction between two senses of 'without preponderating factor'.
4
Synthesize the lesson
Reflect on how the refutation defends the Ash'ari view: God's will is a self-sufficient preponderator that does not require another factor, and choosing between possibilities is different from causing existence.
This lesson shows how kalam objections are refuted through precise conceptual distinctions.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
الأول : تعلق القدرة إن افتقر إلى مرجح تسلسل، وإن لم يفتقر انسد باب إثبات الصانع، ورد بمنع الملازمتين لجواز أن يكون المرجح تعلق الإرادة لذاتها ولأن ترجيح القادر أحد مقدوريه بلا مرجح بمعنى تخصيصه بلا داعية غير ترجيح الممكن بلا مرجح، يعني تحققه بلا مؤثر .

It is refuted by denying both implications because it is possible that the preponderating factor is the attachment of the will in itself, and because the preponderance of the powerful agent of one of his two possible acts without a preponderating factor in the sense of specifying it without a motive is not the same as the preponderance of the possible without a preponderating factor, meaning its existence without an effect.

Commentary
This passage is a classical kalam objection against the Ash'ari view that God acts by power and will. The objection states that if the attachment of divine power to a specific act requires a preponderating factor (marjiḥ), that factor itself would need another, leading to infinite regress (tasalsul). If it does not require a factor, then the principle that every possible requires a preponderating factor (which is used to prove God's existence) collapses, closing the door to establishing the Maker (insidad bab ithbat al-sani'). The refutation (radd) denies both implications: first, the preponderating factor can be God's will itself, which attaches to one of two equal possibilities by its essence (li-dhatiha), similar to a hungry person choosing one of two identical loaves. Second, the powerful agent's specification of one possible act without an external motive is not equivalent to a contingent thing's existence without a cause (mu'aththir); the former is about choice, the latter about creation, so the objection conflates two different senses of 'without preponderating factor'.

The Second Objection: Eternity vs. Origination of Divine Attachment
Presents the second objection on the eternity or temporality of the attachment of power and will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second objection against divine power and will concerning the attachment's eternity or temporality, and how it is refuted by distinguishing eternal will to a temporal effect.

Core Idea
Eternal will can target a temporal creation without making the world eternal.

This lesson presents a classic kalam objection: if God's power and will are attached to creation, that attachment must be either eternal or originated. Each option seems problematic. The Ash'ari response clarifies that an eternal attachment can be directed to a future creation, avoiding both the eternity of the world and an infinite regress.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its English translation. Note the key terms: تعلق القدرة, قديم, حادث, تسلسل الحوادث.
2
Identify the two horns of the dilemma
The objection presents a dichotomy: attachment is either eternal (qadim) or temporal (hadith). Understand why each leads to a problem.
Eternal attachment implies eternal effect; temporal attachment implies infinite regress.
3
Examine the refutation's two proposals
The refutation offers two ways out: (a) the attachment is eternal but to a future creation, or (b) the attachment's temporality is intrinsic. Focus on (a) as the main Ash'ari answer.
Proposal (b) is more subtle and often debated.
4
Ask: how does this defend Ash'ari theology?
Consider how this response preserves both God's eternal attributes and the world's temporality. This is a key move in the atomistic occasionalist framework.

Commentary
This second objection argues that the attachment of divine power and will to the creation of the world must be either eternal (qadim) or temporally originated (hadith). If eternal, then the effect (the world) must be eternal, which is false. If temporally originated, then that attachment itself would be a new event (hadith) requiring another attachment, leading to infinite regress of attachments (tasalsul al-hawadith). The response refutes this by stating two possible alternatives: either (a) the attachment is eternal but relates to the world's existence in the future (fi ma la yazal), meaning God eternally willed the world to exist at a specific later time, or (b) the temporality of the attachment (i.e., its being a new state) is due to its own essence (li-dhatiha), not requiring another cause. Both options avoid the regress.

Third Objection: Agent's Necessity and Free Choice
Presents the third objection on the compatibility of necessity and choice in the agent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third objection regarding the compatibility of necessity and free choice in the agent, and how the response distinguishes between necessity from a powerful agent and necessity from a mere necessitator.

Core Idea
Necessity from a powerful agent does not negate choice; it confirms it.

This lesson presents the third objection raised against the view that God acts by free choice. The objection claims that if an agent possesses all prerequisites, the act becomes necessary, eliminating freedom. The response offers a crucial distinction: necessity arising from the agent's own power and will is not compulsion but rather the realization of choice.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the structure: the objection is stated first, then the refutation.
2
Identify the objection
The objection argues that when an agent has all necessary conditions, the act becomes necessary because non-occurrence is impossible. Understand why this seems to negate free will.
3
Understand the response
The response distinguishes between two types of necessity: from a powerful agent (al-qadir) and from a necessitator (al-mujib). Note the key phrase: 'الوجوب من القادر لا ينافي الاختيار بل يحققه'.
In the first type, the agent could have refrained if He willed; in the second, He cannot.
4
Apply the distinction
The objection confuses voluntary necessity with involuntary compulsion. Reflect on why this distinction is important for divine attributes.

The Fourth Objection: Power's Relation to Existence and Non-Existence
The fourth objection and its refutation regarding power's equal relation to existence and non-existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth objection against the Ash'ari view of divine power and its refutation through the distinction between positive and privative power.

Core Idea
Power over non-existence means ability to refrain, not ability to create nothingness.

This lesson presents a subtle objection: if divine power relates equally to existence and non-existence, and non-existence is eternal and cannot be an object of power, then existence too cannot be an object of power. The response clarifies that 'power over non-existence' is simply the ability to abstain from creating, not the power to produce non-existence as a positive act. Understanding this distinction is key to the Ash'ari theory of divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the objection in Arabic and translation. Notice the parallel drawn between existence and non-existence.
2
Understand the flaw pointed out
The objection says non-existence is pure negation (nafy mahd) and eternal, so not a valid object of power, then concludes existence is likewise invalid.
This would undermine God's creative power.
3
Examine the response
The refutation reinterprets what 'power over non-existence' means. It is not the ability to create non-existence but the ability to refrain from creating existence.
The response uses a conditional: if He wills, He does not do; if He does not will, He does not do.
4
Reflect on the distinction
Contrast positive power (to bring about existence) with privative power (to abstain). This saves the doctrine.
This distinction is central to Ash'ari theology.

The Fifth Objection: Preference, Futility, and the Refutation
Objection and refutation on divine preference and futility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that divine free choice leads to either God's need for completion or futility, and how the Ash'ari response resolves it.

Core Idea
An act can be better for others without God needing anything.

In this lesson, we encounter a classic objection against the Ash'ari view that God acts by free choice (ikhtiyar). The objection presents a dilemma: if an act is more appropriate for God than refraining, it implies God needs that act for completion; if not, creation is futile. The refutation cleverly shifts the basis of appropriateness away from God's own perfection. By exploring this exchange, you will see how careful distinctions in kalam defuse apparent theological dilemmas.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Start by reading the Arabic passage and translation. Notice the two conditional statements that form the dilemma.
The objection assumes that 'more appropriate' must relate to the agent's own perfection.
2
Identify the two horns of the dilemma
The first horn: if the act is more appropriate for the agent, it implies completion by another (استكمال بالغير). The second: if not, the act is futile (عبث). Understand each implication.
3
Analyze the refutation
The response rejects the dilemma by stating that 'more appropriate' can be in itself (في نفسه) or in relation to another (بالنسبة إلى الغير). The latter suffices to negate futility without implying God's need.
The refutation does not deny that God's acts are appropriate; it denies that this appropriateness must be for God's own completion.
4
Consider the underlying principle
Reflect on how this response upholds God's absolute self-sufficiency while affirming the wisdom and purpose of creation. The act's benefit for creatures is sufficient reason.
This is a foundational move in defending divine free will and wisdom.

Source Text

The fifth: That if the act is more appropriate for the free agent than refraining, it entails completion by another; otherwise [if not more appropriate] it is futile. It is refuted by [saying] that it suffices for negating futility that it is more appropriate in itself or in relation to another.

Commentary
This objection targets the Ash'ari view that God acts by free choice (ikhtiyar). It states: if performing the act is more appropriate (awla) for the agent than refraining, then the agent needs that act to complement Himself, implying imperfection. If the act is not more appropriate, then creation is futile (abath), which is unworthy of God. The response counters that the act may be more appropriate in itself (fi nafsihi) or in relation to another (bil-nisba ila al-ghayr), such as benefiting creatures. This suffices to negate futility without implying that God is completed by something external. Thus, the objection wrongly assumes that preference for an act must be for the agent's own perfection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the sixth objection against the temporal creation of the world by a free agent and its refutation based on the distinction between possibility in itself and impossibility due to being an effect.

Core Idea
Something can be possible in itself yet impossible due to the absence of the agent's will.

This lesson examines the sixth objection concerning how the world could be created in time if it was possible in pre-eternity. The objector presents a dilemma: either the effect was impossible in pre-eternity, implying an alteration in God's attributes, or it was possible, leading to an eternal effect. The refutation offers a nuanced distinction that avoids both horns.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dilemma
Read the objection carefully. Notice the two horns: if the effect is impossible in pre-eternity, then alteration (inqilab) follows; if it is possible, then an eternal thing depends on a free agent.

2

The response distinguishes between the effect's intrinsic possibility (mumkin li-dhatihi) and its impossibility due to being the effect of a free agent (athar al-mukhtar). This means the effect is possible in itself but cannot exist until the agent wills it.
This avoids both alteration and an eternal effect.
3
Reflect on the implications
Consider how this preserves divine freedom and avoids logical problems. The world's non-existence in pre-eternity is not due to any intrinsic impossibility but because the agent has not yet chosen to create it.
This is a key argument for the Ash'ari view of temporal creation.

Presents the seventh objection on knowledge determining possibility and its refutation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Divine knowledge does not necessitate; it follows divine power.

This lesson examines a classical objection against Ash'ari theology: if God knows eternally that an act will occur, doesn't that make its existence necessary? The objection claims this negates divine power over the act. The brief yet profound refutation clarifies the relationship between knowledge and power in Ash'ari thought.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the objection carefully: it argues that God's eternal knowledge fixes the outcome, making the act either necessary or impossible, and therefore not an object of power.
Notice the logical structure: knowledge of existence → necessity; knowledge of non-existence → impossibility.
2
Analyze the refutation
The refutation states that He knows its existence 'by His power.' Understand how this shifts the relationship: knowledge does not independently determine; it is contingent on the free exercise of power.
This is a key Ash'ari principle: knowledge follows the known, not vice versa.
3
Grasp the theological implication
Consider why this response is important: it preserves divine freedom and the genuine possibility (maqdur) of actions, avoiding fatalism.
The objection would imply determinism; the refutation reaffirms voluntary agency.

The objection assumes knowledge as a separate determining factor, but in Ash'ari theology, knowledge follows the object known (al-'ilm tabi' li-l-ma'lum).

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof, called dalīl al-tawārud (proof of conflict). The argument goes: if an event could be brought about by the power of a creature (al-ghayr) while it is also within the power of God, and we suppose both wills are directed to it, then either it occurs by one of the two powers, which would require a preponderance without a cause (tarjīḥ bilā murajjiḥ), or it occurs by both, which would require two independent causes producing one effect, which is impossible because each is supposed to be independent, so the aggregate cannot be the cause. The author gives an exception: the motion of a body subjected to two forces (attractive and repulsive) is not analogous because there is no evidence that each force independently produces the motion; rather, the combined effect is from both. Then the author mentions an objection: it can be said that God's power is more complete (akmal), so the act occurs by it and the creature's power is overridden (tadmaḥil). The lesson ends with this objection; the answer is not given here but may be addressed in the next proof.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Immateriality implies intellection because an immaterial being is intrinsically intelligible.

Al-Taftazani presents the first of two philosophical proofs for God's knowledge. This argument draws on the concept of immateriality: if something is free from matter, it is automatically intelligible and thus must be actually intellected. The reasoning is subtle but foundational for understanding how theologians and philosophers conceive of divine omniscience.

Guided Path
1

The Second Proof: Knowledge from Self-Knowledge
Argument from self-knowledge, objection, and answer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' second argument for God's knowledge from His self-knowledge, the objection that not all concomitants are evident, and the answer that God's knowledge is complete.

Core Idea
Knowledge of the cause entails knowledge of the effect, but only if it is complete knowledge.

The philosophers argue that because God knows His own essence, He must also know all things that proceed from it. This raises a key question: does knowing something automatically mean knowing everything that follows from it? An objection about non-evident concomitants and the distinction between partial and complete knowledge provides a powerful answer.

Guided Path
1
Understand the self-knowledge argument
Read the passage carefully. The philosophers claim God knows His essence because His essence is present to itself. Then they extend this to knowledge of all possible things.
The argument relies on the principle that knowledge of the cause entails knowledge of the effect.
2
Identify the objection
The objection uses the example of a triangle: knowing the triangle's essence does not automatically give knowledge that its angles sum to two right angles. This challenges the claim that knowing the essence automatically yields knowledge of all concomitants.
Not all concomitants are evident (lazim bayyin).
3
Learn the answer: complete knowledge
The answer distinguishes between partial and complete knowledge. God's knowledge is complete (ilm tamm), meaning He knows each thing as it is in itself, including all its properties. Therefore, the objection does not apply.
Complete knowledge includes all logical consequences.

Commentary
This passage presents the second argument of the philosophers for God's knowledge, followed by an objection and its answer. The second argument (al-thani) states that God knows His essence. The explanation is that for an immaterial being, self-knowledge is nothing but the presence of its essence to itself, meaning it is not absent from itself, because forming a mental image of itself would involve two identical forms, which is impossible. This, based on their principles, proves that He is knowing in general (fi al-jumla). The philosophers then attempt to prove His knowledge of things other than Himself. They argue: He knows His essence, which is the principle (mabda') of all possible things. Knowledge of the cause (al-illa) entails knowledge of the effect (al-ma'lul), because knowledge of a thing necessitates knowledge of its necessary concomitants (lawazim). Causality ('illiyya) is not intelligible without the effect; the effect itself and all subsequent effects are among the concomitants of the essence. Thus, knowing the essence gives knowledge of everything that proceeds from it. An objection is then raised: a necessary concomitant (lazim) of the essence, even if it is co-existent without intermediary, is not necessarily an evident concomitant (lazim bayyin) that is known immediately upon intellection of the essence. For example, knowing the essence of a triangle does not automatically give knowledge that its angles equal two right angles; that requires additional reasoning. If every concomitant were immediately known, then knowledge of one thing would lead to knowledge of all its near and far concomitants, leading to an infinite regress. The answer is given: the discussion concerns complete knowledge (al-ilm al-tamm), which is knowledge of a thing as it is in itself (bi ma lahu fi nafsihi), including all its attributes and relations. There is no doubt that God's knowledge is complete in this sense, so the objection does not apply.
