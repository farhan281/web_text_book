Refutation of Objections to the Distinctness of Will
Refutation of objections that will is reducible to knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari response to objections that will might be reducible to knowledge, and why the distinctness of will is necessary.

Core Idea
Knowledge does not cause will; will is a distinct state, eternally additional to God's essence.

Some opponents argue that God's will is merely His knowledge of what is beneficial or His knowledge of when things will occur. This lesson examines and refutes these objections, concluding that will is a distinct, eternal attribute.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
The objection claims that knowledge of the benefit or knowledge of the time of occurrence is prior to will, implying will is not needed.
The phrase 'أو بأنه سيوجد في وقت كذا' introduces the alternative explanation.
2
Understand the response
The author rebuts by noting that the opponent may not concede the priority of such knowledge over will, and that knowledge of occurrence is not temporally prior.
3
Interpret 'knowledge follows occurrence'
This phrase means that knowledge corresponds to the known, not that it is temporally later. The known is the pattern for knowledge.
The author clarifies: 'ليس بمعنى تأخره عنه في الخارج البتة.'
4
Confirm the conclusion
The distinctness of will is necessary (daruriyya), and its eternity and addition to the essence are established.

Commentary
This passage presents the Ash'ari proof that God's will is a distinct eternal attribute. The argument proceeds as follows: (1) The specification of some opposites (e.g., creating the world at one time rather than another, or creating one thing rather than its opposite) cannot be due to the essence alone, because the essence's relation to both opposites is equal. A specifier is needed. (2) That specifier cannot be an external cause, because the Necessary Being cannot depend on anything external in His agency. Therefore, it must be an attribute inherent in Him, which is called 'will'. (3) This attribute is distinct from power because power is equally related to both opposites, while will specifies one. It is distinct from knowledge because knowledge is equally related to all things, while will involves a particular inclination. The author then addresses an objection: some might claim that will is nothing but knowledge of the benefit or knowledge of the time of occurrence. The author argues that knowledge of the benefit is not the same as will, because will involves a specific inclination. Regarding the timing, if someone says God knows He will bring something into existence at time t, and that knowledge is prior to will, the opponent may not concede that. The proper understanding of 'knowledge follows occurrence' is that knowledge corresponds to the known, not that it is temporally posterior. The conclusion is that the distinctness of will from other attributes is self-evident, and its eternity and addition to the essence are proven.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof section. It begins by listing various definitions of divine will (irada) from different theological and philosophical schools: some say it is a self-subsistent attribute, Dirar says it is the essence itself, al-Najjar says it is a negative attribute, the philosophers say it is knowledge of the most excellent order, al-Kulabi says it is knowledge of His own act and command for others' acts, and the Mu'tazila say it is knowledge of the benefit in the act. Without a specifier, specification is impossible, and the Necessary Being cannot depend on an external factor in His agency. That attribute is called irada (will). This meaning is clear to reason and is distinct from knowledge, power, and other attributes. Its function is specification and preponderance of one side of the possible (action or inaction) over the other. The author then emphasizes its distinction from power: power is equally related to both sides, but irada is not. Similarly, knowledge is equally related to all, while irada specifies. Then he discusses a potential confusion about the relationship between knowledge and will: if knowledge precedes will, then will is not truly specifying; but the author notes that the opponent may not accept the priority of knowledge. He concludes by stating that the distinctness of the state called irada from knowledge, power, and other attributes is necessary, and it has been shown to be pre-eternal and additional to the essence, just as was established for knowledge and power.

The Ash'ari Proof of Irada as a Distinct Attribute
Presents the Ash'ari argument from specification to prove irada as a distinct attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari argument that divine will is a distinct attribute necessary for explaining why God creates specific things at specific times.

Core Idea
Specification of opposites requires a specifier—irada.

The Ash'aris argue that the existence of particular things and times, rather than others, cannot be explained by power or knowledge alone; it requires an attribute whose function is specification. This lesson presents that argument, which establishes irada as a distinct attribute.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its translation, noting the logical flow.
2
Identify the premise
The premise is that the divine essence is equally related to all opposites, yet some occur and others do not.
Equal relation means no inherent preference.
3
Understand the need for a specifier
Since specification cannot happen without a specifier, and the specifier cannot be external, it must be an attribute of God.
This attribute is called irada.
4
Recognize its function
The function of irada is to preponderate one side over the other, making it distinct from power and knowledge.
Power relates equally, knowledge is universal.

Examine the Mu'tazila argument
They argue: will is an act of the willing subject; every agent has consciousness of his act; we only have consciousness of the pure motivation or the preponderant factor. Therefore will must be the motivation.

The example of the thirsty person at two equal waters or the fleeing person at two equal roads shows that inclination can occur without a preponderant motivation, refuting the need for a motivation.
This is a concrete counterexample to the Mu'tazila claim.

Commentary
This passage is a conclusion (khātima) concerning whether God perceives smells, tastes, temperature, texture, etc. The author states the Ash'ari doctrine: God indeed perceives these things (yudrik al-rawā'ih wa al-ṭu'ūm), but the Sacred Law does not apply the terms 'shāmm' (smeller), 'dhā'iq' (taster), or 'lāmis' (toucher) to God, and reason does not permit it. The example of smelling an apple without perceiving its smell illustrates that the verbal expression does not capture the essence of perception.

Core Idea
God possesses all perfect perceptions, but no physical acts.

He emphasizes that while God must be attributed with the effects of these perceptions, He is absolutely free from the physical actions and organs associated with them. Proofs That Allah is a Speaker
Al-Taftazani enumerates two proofs for divine speech.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two main proofs (tawatur and perfection argument) that establish Allah as Mutakallim (the Speaker).

Commentary
This passage is a combination of a proof and a classification of theological positions regarding the divine attribute of speech (kalam). Another proof is from the principle of perfection: silence (the opposite of speech) in a living being is a defect, so God must possess speech. The Mu'tazila hold that speech is an accident created in some body (e.g., the speech of the tree or the angel), and God's 'speaking' means He creates that speech in another locus. The author implicitly refutes all these views by upholding the Ash'ari position that speech is an eternal attribute subsisting in God's essence, not composed of temporal elements.
