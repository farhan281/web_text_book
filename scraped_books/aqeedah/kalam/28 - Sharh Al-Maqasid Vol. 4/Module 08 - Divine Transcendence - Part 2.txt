Defining the Dispute on Temporal Accidents
Al-Taftazani defines the scope of the dispute and refutes a preliminary argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise meaning of 'temporal accident' in this dispute and why relational attributes are not included.

Core Idea
Not all temporal associations are real accidents; only those entailing change are disputed.

Al-Taftazani opens by clarifying exactly what is under dispute. The Karramiyya claimed that God can be qualified by temporal accidents in the sense of things that come into existence after non-existence. But not all temporal relationships—like being a creator at a certain time—imply a change in God's essence. This lesson sets the boundaries of the argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage defining the dispute
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. Note how al-Taftazani distinguishes between real temporal accidents and relational or negative attributes.
The term 'al-ḥādith' here means 'temporal accident' as something that originates after non-existence.
2
Understand the Karramiyya position
The Karramiyya (a theological group) held that God can be described with temporal accidents. Al-Taftazani refutes this by limiting the scope.
3
Analyze the refutation of a false premise
Some argued that the corrector for qualification is the attribute itself, regardless of pre-eternity. Al-Taftazani shows this is invalid because pre-eternity might be a condition.
Notice the three possibilities he offers: the corrector is the pre-eternal attribute itself, or pre-eternity is a condition, or temporality is a preventer.

The Fourth Section: The Impossibility of the Eternal Being Being Qualified by Temporal Accidents. (On the impossibility of His being qualified by the temporal accident in the sense of that which exists after non-existence, contrary to the Karramiyya. As for His being qualified by that which has temporal relationships, or by what is renewed of negations, relations, and states, that is not part of the disputed matter, so it cannot be used as a proof for them. And the argument that the corrector for qualification is the attribute absolutely, since there is no consideration for pre-eternity because it is non-existent, is invalid because it is possible that the corrector is the reality of the pre-eternal attribute, or that pre-eternity is a condition, or that temporality is a preventer. Second: that being qualified by a temporal accident is change, and change is impossible for Him. Third: that if it were permissible, it would be permissible in pre-eternity because of the impossibility of transformation, and this necessitates the permissibility of the existence of a temporal accident in pre-eternity because being qualified by something without it is impossible. Fourth: that if it were permissible, it would necessitate that He is never free from a temporal accident, because before that temporal accident He would be qualified by its opposite, which is temporal, due to its passing away and His receptivity to the temporal accident, according to what has passed. And the first was weakened by saying that it is possible that temporal accidents are successive perfections, the beginning of all of them being conditioned by the end of the other, and there is consideration in this. And the second: that change in the sense of alteration in attributes without being affected by another is the very matter of dispute. And the third: that what is necessary is the pre-eternity of permissibility, but the impossibility is the impossibility of pre-eternity. And the fourth: denial of the necessary implication.)

Commentary
This passage presents the main topic and four proofs for the impossibility of the necessary existent (al-wājib) being qualified by temporal accidents (al-ḥawādith). The author begins by clarifying that the dispute is specifically about real temporal accidents (existing after non-existence) as claimed by the Karramiyya, not about relational attributes or negative attributes which are permissible. Then he refutes an opposing argument that the corrector for qualification is the attribute absolutely, regardless of pre-eternity. The author then gives four proofs: First, consensus (ijmāʿ) that if the necessary existent can have a perfection attribute, He must have it eternally; otherwise He does not have it at all. Second, being qualified by a temporal accident implies change (taghayyur), which is impossible for the eternal. Third, if such qualification were possible, it would have been possible in pre-eternity (due to the impossibility of transformation from non-qualification to qualification) which leads to the absurdity of a temporal accident existing in pre-eternity. Fourth, if it were possible, then the necessary existent would never be free from temporal accidents, because before a given accident He would have its opposite (also temporal) and must be receptive to it. After the proofs, the author addresses and refutes potential objections to each proof, showing that the first objection (that temporal accidents could be successive perfections) is debatable, the second (that change is just the matter of dispute) is invalid, the third (that pre-eternity of permissibility is impossible) is denied, and the fourth (denial of necessary implication) is countered by affirming the implication.

Proofs from Consensus and Impossibility of Change
Al-Taftazani presents the first two proofs: consensus and impossibility of change.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first two rational proofs for why the eternal being cannot be qualified by temporal accidents: consensus and the impossibility of change.

Core Idea
God cannot change, and consensus confirms He lacks temporal perfections.

After clarifying the scope, al-Taftazani presents his first two proofs. The first is based on consensus (ijmāʿ) that if something is compatible with God as a perfection, He must have it eternally; otherwise He does not have it at all. The second proof argues that being qualified by a temporal accident entails change, which is impossible for the necessary existent. These two proofs establish a foundational barrier against temporal accidents.

Guided Path
1
Read the first proof (consensus)
Read the Arabic for 'al-awwal: al-ijmāʿ...' and its translation. Understand the logical structure: if an attribute of perfection is possible for God, He must have it eternally; if not, He cannot have it at all.
This argument relies on the principle that God is perfect and lacks nothing potentially perfect.
2
Read the second proof (change)
The second proof: being qualified by a temporal accident is a change, and change is impossible for God. Consider why change is impossible for the necessary existent.
Change implies potentiality and dependence, which are incompatible with necessary existence.
3
Connect to the broader argument
These two proofs are the first in a series of four. They collectively establish why temporal accidents cannot inhere in God.

The Third Proof: Impossibility of Transformation
Al-Taftazani presents the third proof, arguing from the impossibility of transformation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third proof: if a temporal accident were possible for God, it would have been possible in pre-eternity, leading to the absurdity of a temporal accident in pre-eternity.

Core Idea
If God could acquire a temporal accident now, He could have acquired it always, which is impossible.

The third proof is more subtle. It argues that if it were permissible for the necessary existent to be qualified by a temporal accident at some time, then it would have been permissible in pre-eternity as well. This is because the transition from not being qualified to being qualified is a transformation (inqilāb), which is impossible for the eternal being. Therefore, the only consistent possibility is that it was always permissible, but that would require a temporal accident to exist in pre-eternity, which is a contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Read the third proof in Arabic
Read the Arabic for 'al-thālith...' and the translation. Notice the logical steps: permissibility now implies permissibility in pre-eternity, which would entail existence of a temporal accident in pre-eternity.
Key concepts: 'jāza' (permissible), 'al-azal' (pre-eternity), 'inqilāb' (transformation).
2
Understand the impossibility of transformation
Why is transformation impossible? Because it would mean God changes from a state of not having an attribute to having it, which implies change.
The proof says 'li-stiḥālat al-inqilāb' (due to the impossibility of transformation).
3
Grasp the absurd conclusion
If permissible in pre-eternity, then a temporal accident would exist in pre-eternity, but temporal accidents cannot exist in pre-eternity by definition.
A temporal accident (al-ḥādith) is something that comes into existence after non-existence, so it cannot be eternal.

The Fourth Proof: Never Free from Temporal Accidents
Al-Taftazani presents the fourth proof, showing that permissibility leads to an infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth proof: if God could be qualified by a temporal accident, He would never be free from them, leading to an infinite regress or a contradiction.

Core Idea
If God could have one temporal accident, He would have to have an endless series of them, which is absurd.

The fourth proof builds on the same assumption. If it is permissible for God to be qualified by a temporal accident at one time, then before that accident He must have been qualified by its opposite (also temporal), because the opposite would have been present and then ceased. This would require an endless chain of temporal accidents, implying that God is never free from them. This contradicts the fact that temporal accidents are originated and dependent.

Guided Path
1
Read the fourth proof
Read the Arabic for 'al-rābiʿ...' and the translation. Focus on the reasoning: if a temporal accident occurs, before it there must be its opposite, which is also temporal, leading to an infinite series.
Key phrase: 'lā yakhlu ʿan al-ḥādith' (never free from a temporal accident).
2
Understand the infinite regress
The proof shows that if one accident is accepted, a prior one must exist, and so on, resulting in an infinite chain.
This is a version of the argument that whatever can accept temporal accidents must always be subject to them.
3
See the absurdity
An infinite regress of temporal accidents is impossible because they are all contingent and would require a beginning.
The proof does not elaborate further; al-Taftazani simply states the conclusion.

Commentary
This passage presents the main topic and four proofs for the impossibility of the necessary existent (al-wājib) being qualified by temporal accidents (al-ḥawādith). The author begins by clarifying that the dispute is specifically about real temporal accidents (existing after non-existence) as claimed by the Karramiyya, not about relational attributes or negative attributes which are permissible. Then he refutes an opposing argument that the corrector for qualification is the attribute absolutely, regardless of pre-eternity. The author then gives four proofs: First, consensus (ijmāʿ) that if the necessary existent can have a perfection attribute, He must have it eternally; otherwise He does not have it at all. Second, being qualified by a temporal accident implies change (taghayyur), which is impossible for the eternal. Third, if such qualification were possible, it would have been possible in pre-eternity (due to the impossibility of transformation from non-qualification to qualification) which leads to the absurdity of a temporal accident existing in pre-eternity. Fourth, if it were possible, then the necessary existent would never be free from temporal accidents, because before a given accident He would have its opposite (also temporal) and must be receptive to it. After the proofs, the author addresses and refutes potential objections to each proof, showing that the first objection (that temporal accidents could be successive perfections) is debatable, the second (that change is just the matter of dispute) is invalid, the third (that pre-eternity of permissibility is impossible) is denied, and the fourth (denial of necessary implication) is countered by affirming the implication.

Permissible Attributes and Refuting al-Razi's Objection
Al-Taftazani enumerates permissible divine attributes and refutes al-Razi's objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand which types of attributes are permissible for God according to the majority of Islamic theologians, and how this refutes al-Razi's objection that all sects are committed to temporal accidents in God's essence.

Core Idea
God's attributes can change in relation without changing His essence.

In this passage, al-Taftazani addresses a key theological tension: how can God be described with new attributes (like being the creator of a specific person) without implying change in His eternal essence? He clarifies that certain types of attributes—negations, relations, and real attributes with changing relations—are permissible because they do not affect God's essence. This understanding provides a direct response to Imam al-Razi's charge that all Islamic sects inevitably ascribe temporal accidents to God.

Guided Path
1
Understand the central prohibition
The passage begins by stating the majority view: God cannot be qualified by a temporal accident (something that begins to exist). The Karramiyya are the exception. This sets the boundary.
2
Identify the three permissible categories
Al-Taftazani explains three types of attributes that are permissible despite involving change on the creature's side: negations/relations, real attributes with changing relations, and states (aḥwāl) according to Abu al-Husayn al-Basri. Note that each involves a change in the correlate, not in God's essence.
3
See how this refutes al-Razi
With these categories established, al-Taftazani shows that the Ash'ari position does not entail God being a locus for temporal accidents. The new relations that arise when a creature is created are merely relational, not essential changes.

Second Proof: The Change Argument and Its Answer
Presents the second proof and its answer distinguishing possibility in pre-eternity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second proof against temporal attributes (the change argument) and the subtle distinction made in its answer between the eternity of the possibility of a temporal event and the possibility of the event itself being eternal.

Core Idea
The impossibility of change in God only rules out a temporal event being eternal, not the eternal possibility of such an event.

This lesson tackles the second and most central proof of those who deny that God can be described with temporal events (ḥawādith). The philosophers argue that such description would entail change, which is impossible for God. The answer, however, makes a subtle logical distinction that preserves both divine transcendence and the possibility of temporal attributes. Let us examine the proof and then carefully unpack the response.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof and answer
Read the Arabic passage once. Then read the translation. Notice the structure: first the proof (1 sentence), then the answer, which contains a distinction and an analogy.
Focus on the words 'الثاني : وهو العمدة عند الحكماء' to see its importance.
2
Unpack the key distinction
The answer distinguishes between 'azaliyyat jawāz al-ḥādith' (eternity of the possibility) and 'jawāz azaliyyat al-ḥādith' (possibility of the event's eternity). Understand why only the former follows from the premises.
The impossible is the temporal event existing in pre-eternity, not the eternal possibility of its occurrence.
3
Grasp the analogy and conclusion
The analogy of divine power to create the world illustrates the point. God has the power in pre-eternity to create, but He cannot create the world in pre-eternity (since the world is temporal). Apply this to the case of temporal attributes.
This affirms that attributes do not imply change.

This is like saying that the divine capacity to create the world is realized in pre-eternity, as opposed to His capacity to create the world in pre-eternity, i.e., He can create it in pre-eternity, but He cannot create it in pre-eternity. The basis of the discussion is that the temporal event is considered with the condition of temporality; otherwise, there is no doubt about the possibility of its existence in pre-eternity.

This passage presents the second proof against temporal attributes, which is central to the philosophers: describing God with a temporal event would entail change, which is impossible for God. The answer makes a subtle distinction: from the impossibility of change, we infer that description in pre-eternity is possible only if pre-eternity qualifies the possibility itself, not the description. This yields the eternity of the possibility of the temporal event, not the possibility of the event itself in pre-eternity. The impossible thing is the existence of a temporal event in pre-eternity, not the eternal possibility of its occurring. The analogy of divine power to create the world illustrates this: God has the power in pre-eternity to create the world, but that does not mean He can create the world in pre-eternity, because a temporal event by definition cannot exist in pre-eternity. Therefore, temporal attributes do not necessarily imply change in God.

Commentary
This passage contains the fourth argument (al-rābiʿ) against the view that God can be described with originated (ḥādith) attributes, followed by a refutation of its two supporting proofs. The argument states: if it were permissible for God to be described with an originated thing, then He would not be free of it (i.e., He would have to be characterized by it), and consequently He would be originated—because being characterized by something that changes or is temporary implies that the subject itself is subject to change and thus originated. The two proofs for this implication (mulāzama) are then presented. The first proof: the one described by an originated cannot be free from it or from its opposite; but the opposite of an originated is also originated (since it terminates in the originated); and nothing eternal has opposites that are originated; moreover, what is proven eternal cannot cease to exist. The second proof: the described cannot be free from the originated nor from its potentiality (qābiliyya), which is originated; the pre-eternity (azaliyya) of that potentiality would imply the permissibility of the pre-eternity of the potential recipient (maqbūl)—i.e., the originated thing would be pre-eternally possible, which is absurd. The author then refutes both proofs, calling them weak (ḍaʿīf). For the first: the conventional understanding of 'opposite' (ḍidd) is not accepted—not every attribute has an opposite, nor is it necessary that the subject be free from two opposites. If 'opposite' is defined more broadly as anything that contradicts it (whether existent or non-existent), then the opposite of an originated is not necessarily originated; rather, 'eternity' (qidam) and 'origination' (ḥudūth) are attributes of existents specifically. The non-existence of an originated thing before its existence is neither eternal nor originated (since non-existence has no temporal status). If they are applied to non-existents by virtue of being preceded by existence or not, then that non-existence would be eternal—but the impossibility of ceasing applies only to existent things, because pre-eternal non-existence can cease for every originated thing. For the second: potentiality is a mental consideration (iʿtibār ʿaqlī) meaning the possibility (imkān) of being described. Even if its pre-eternity were granted, it would only entail the pre-eternity of the permissibility (jawāz) of the potential recipient—i.e., its mere possibility—not the permissibility of its actual pre-eternity (which would entail the impossible). The author concludes that the distinction (between possibility and actual pre-eternity) is well known.

Core Idea

A common objection against divine omnipotence is that if God can do anything, He could also do evil. This would imply either foolishness or ignorance in God. Rejected Interpretation and Metaphorical Principle

Learning Objective

Metaphorical interpretation quickly negates any illusion of God resembling creation.

This passage addresses a specific far-fetched interpretation of 'eyes' in the Quran and then articulates the broader method used by scholars of eloquence: terms like 'hand', 'eye', and 'istiwa' are metaphors for divine attributes. The goal is to prevent any suggestion of corporeality. As you read, note how the author distinguishes the quick negation of anthropomorphism from the deeper recognition that these are merely representations for human understanding.

Commentary
This passage first dismisses an alternative interpretation that 'أعين' (eyes) refers to springs that burst from the earth, calling it 'far-fetched' (بعيد). The Beatific Vision: Sunni Doctrine and the Point of Dispute
Sanusi states the Sunni doctrine on seeing God and clarifies the actual point of dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the actual point of dispute regarding the vision of God in the afterlife between Ahl al-Sunnah and other sects, and how Sanusi clarifies it.

Core Idea
The real dispute is about a perceptual state like vision without direction, not about knowledge or physical mechanisms.

Sanusi begins by stating the Sunni doctrine that believers will see God in Paradise without physical constraints. He then identifies groups who disagree and clarifies what is not in dispute. Finally, he pinpoints the exact issue: whether a state analogous to vision, which in this world requires direction and facing, can apply to Allah, who is transcendent beyond direction and place.

Commentary
This passage defines the Sunni position on the beatific vision and clarifies the actual point of dispute. The author begins by stating the Sunni doctrine: Allah may be seen, and the believers will see Him in Paradise, but their vision is transcendent above facing, direction, and place. He notes that all other sects disagreed, particularly the Anthropomorphists (al-Mushabbiha) and the Karramiyyah, who affirm seeing in direction and place because they consider God a body—which is impossible for Him. The author then removes two issues from the dispute: first, the opponents do not deny the possibility of complete intellectual knowledge of God; second, the Sunnis do not affirm the physical mechanisms of vision like image imprinting or ray emission. The actual point of dispute is defined by an analogy: knowing something by definition is one type of knowledge; seeing it with eyes closed is a higher type; seeing with eyes open is a still higher type called vision, which in this world requires direction and place. The question is whether that same perceptual state can occur without direction and place, and whether it can relate to God's essence, which is transcendent. Thus, the dispute is about a specific perceptual state, not about all perception.

Two Proofs from Musa's Request for Vision
Author presents two aspects of the proof from the verse for the possibility of seeing God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two textual proofs that the Sunni tradition uses to argue for the possibility and actual occurrence of seeing God in the afterlife, derived from Mūsā's request in the Qur'an.

Core Idea
Musa's request for vision proves that seeing God is possible, not impossible.

In this passage, the author tackles a classic theological question: Can believers see God in the afterlife? By examining the story of Mūsā (Moses) in the Qur'an (7:143), he extracts two decisive proofs that establish the possibility of ru'yat Allāh. Both proofs rely on transmitted revelation, but they are so strong that they leave no room for doubt.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get a feel for the language, then read the translation. Note that the author acknowledges the proofs are based on transmitted text, not reason.
2
Understand the first proof
Focus on the first proof: if vision were impossible, Mūsā would not have asked. The author explains the implication: Mūsā was either knowledgeable or ignorant, both incompatible with prophethood.
This proof relies on the established fact that Mūsā asked for vision.
3
Understand the second proof
Consider the second proof: the conditional statement linking vision to the mountain's stability. Since the condition is possible, the conditioned is also possible. This establishes that vision is not inherently impossible.
The author uses the principle that what is conditioned on the possible is itself possible.

Commentary
This passage presents two proofs (wajhān) for the possibility of ru'yat Allāh (seeing God), derived from the story of Mūsā (Moses) in the Qur'ān (7:143). The author first notes that the historical fact that Mūsā asked to see God and that God conditioned vision on the mountain's stability are matters established solely by transmitted revelation (naql), not reason. Nevertheless, the transmitted proof is decisive (qaṭ'ī), leaving no room for dispute about the possibility or even actual occurrence of vision for believers. The first proof is based on Mūsā's request: if vision were impossible, a prophet of Mūsā's stature would not have requested it. The implication (luzūm) is explained: either Mūsā knew God's attributes and what is permissible for Him, in which case requesting the impossible would be futile and unbefitting a prophet; or he was ignorant, which would disqualify him from prophethood. Both alternatives are unacceptable. The second proof uses the conditional statement: God said, 'You will not see Me, but look at the mountain; if it remains in its place, then you will see Me.' The stability of the mountain is possible, and what is conditioned on something possible is itself possible. The impossible cannot be attached to any condition.

Mu'tazilite reinterpretations contradict the explicit wording and context of the Quranic verse.

This passage presents the first two objections raised by the Mu'tazila against the proof from Mūsā's request, along with the author's refutation. Moreover, the phrase 'look at you' (anẓuru ilayka) with the preposition 'ilā' is explicitly about vision, as stated in al-Irshād. The passage also clarifies a point from al-Mawāqif: that 'ru'ya' is sometimes used for knowledge, but that is far-fetched when connected with 'ilā'; the proper interpretation is that 'naẓar' here means vision, so the connection is of vision itself.

Commentary
This passage presents and refutes the third Mu'tazilite objection, attributed to al-Jāhiz (head of the Jāhiziyya sect) and his followers. Their claim is that Mūsā did not actually request vision for himself; rather, he asked on behalf of his people who had demanded to see God openly (Qur'ān 4:153, 2:55). Mūsā attributed the request to himself to teach them the impossibility of vision by a fortiori argument (al-ṭarīq al-ūlā). They cite Mūsā's later statement: 'Will You destroy us for what the foolish among us have done?' (7:155) as evidence. The author declares this interpretation invalid (fāsid) for three reasons: First, since deeming vision permissible is false (bāṭil) and even kufr according to most Mu'tazila, a prophet like Mūsā would not delay refuting error by posing such a request. He would have immediately corrected them, as he did when they asked for an idol (7:138). Second, Mūsā's response only informed of non-occurrence (al-ikhbār bi-'adam al-wuqū'), not impossibility. The people intended obstinacy (ta'annut), not a genuine request. Third, if the people already believed Mūsā, his mere statement that vision is impossible would suffice; there was no need to request the impossible or for them to witness terrifying events.

Commentary
This passage presents the rational proof used by early Sunni theologians for the possibility (imkan) of seeing God (ru'yat Allah). The proof begins by establishing that we do, in fact, see substances and accidents by necessity—such as bodies, lights, colors, and spatial locations—and this is agreed upon even by opponents. Some might object that certain perceived things like length are not pure accidents but extended substances; however, the proof counters that length can be perceived by composing several substances in alignment without any accident being mentally noted. Another argument is that we discriminate between types of bodies and colors visually without any of those qualities inhering in the eye. The core reasoning follows: since we see both substances and accidents (the two main categories of created things), and since the correctness of vision is something that obtains when the object exists and is negated when it is nonexistent, there must be a cause for this correctness to avoid an arbitrary preference. This cause must be common to both substance and accident, because a single effect (correct vision of both) cannot have two different causes unless they coincide—but here the effect is a single type of correctness. The only candidates are existence (al-wujud) or origination (al-huduth), and origination is invalid because it depends on nonexistence (being an accident) or on existence after nonexistence, both of which are either purely relational or secondary. Nonexistence itself has no causal role. Therefore, the common cause is existence. And existence is shared by the Necessary Being (God), as established earlier. Thus, seeing God is possible. This is a classic kalam proof relying on the principle of a common cause (jami') to infer the possibility of seeing an object that shares the relevant property (existence) with objects we already see.

This passage discusses the controversial term mā'iyyah (quiddity/ whatness) in relation to Allah. Theologians refer to this as the question of mā'iyyah. Core Idea
Knowledge is not merely impression of forms; Allah's knowledge transcends representation.

Al-Taftazani introduces the first philosophical argument against knowing Allah's essence, based on the definition of knowledge as impression of forms. He then systematically refutes it through a series of concessions and denials. This lesson unpacks both the argument and its refutation.

Core Idea

This lesson presents the fourth rational argument, which uses a reductio ad absurdum. It shows that if God were the creator of all human acts, then He would be described with those acts—including disbelief, injustice, and mundane actions. Since such descriptions are impossible for Allah, He cannot be the creator of our actions. The source passage is a concise statement of this argument.

The Objection of Evil Actions and Its Refutation
Objection and Ash'ari refutation regarding God's creation of evil actions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that God cannot create evil actions and the Ash'ari refutation based on possible good consequences and the distinction between creator and agent.

Core Idea
Creating evil may have a good end; the unjust is the one who does it, not God.

This passage addresses a key objection to the Ash'ari position that God creates all actions. The objector argues that a wise God cannot create evil. The refutation concedes rational good and evil but offers two arguments: that evil actions may have a commendable outcome, and that the agent of evil is the one who performs it, not the creator.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the first part of the passage which lists examples of ugly actions and the argument that a wise God would not create them.
Notice the examples: injustice, polytheism, etc.
2
Understand the concession
The refutation begins by conceding rational good and evil (al-ḥusn wa al-qubḥ al-'aqliyyān). This is important to note.
The Ash'ari position does not deny that actions have inherent moral qualities.
3

The first argument is that creating an ugly act may have a commendable ultimate consequence, so it is not ugly in that respect.
This distinguishes between the act itself and its outcome.
4
Grasp the second argument
The second argument distinguishes between the one who creates the act in another (God) and the one who performs it (the servant). The unjust person is the one characterized by injustice, not the creator.
This clarifies that God is not the agent of evil.

The Mu'tazila argue that if God creates evil deeds, then He would be the doer of evil and thus described as an evil-doer. This would lead to describing God with the worst possible attributes. The author presents this objection before refuting it.

The Mu'tazilite Principle of Hidden Wisdom and Its Refutation
Al-Taftazani presents a Mu'tazilite response to divine evil and refutes it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Mu'tazilites attempted to resolve the problem of evil using a principle of hidden benefits, and why Al-Taftazani argues that this principle actually undermines their own position.

Core Idea
A principle that seems to defend God may actually be used against those who propose it.

The problem of evil is a classic theological challenge. Some Mu'tazilites proposed that actions appearing evil have hidden benefits, using examples from scripture. In this passage, Al-Taftazani presents their argument and then refutes it by showing that the very principle they rely on invalidates their own objection.

Guided Path

The Mu'tazilites claim that an act may be deemed ugly at first glance, but when hidden wisdom becomes apparent, the judgment turns to approval. They illustrate with the piercing of the ship and killing the boy by al-Khidr, and with disciplining a child.
These examples show that initial judgment can be mistaken.

They extend the principle to all divine actions that appear without goodness, citing the angels' wonder at Adam's creation (Quran 2:30). They argue that creating harmful things like Iblis and his progeny is thus good.

The principle backfires, as it admits that judgments may be reversed.

Consider how this refutation uses the opponent's own logic. The phrase 'this principle is against you, not for you' is the key turning point.
This shows the power of internal critique in kalam.

Defining Injustice and Its Application to Divine Acts
The author defines injustice and applies it to divine actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite definition of injustice and why divine causing of pain is not considered unjust.

Core Idea
Injustice is undeserved harm without benefit; deserved or beneficial pain is not injustice.

The concept of injustice (ẓulm) is central to debates about divine actions. The Mu'tazila defined it precisely to defend God from the charge of injustice while allowing for pain in the world. This passage lays out that definition and applies it to examples like punishment, travel hardships, and even God's burning of an infant.

2

Each example falls under one of the criteria: deserved, beneficial, or customary.
3
Understand the conclusion
The passage concludes that if pain is deserved, beneficial, or customary, it is good (ḥasan) and permissible from God without compensation. This is key to Mu'tazilite theology.

Commentary
This passage defines injustice (ẓulm) as understood by the Mu'tazila and applies it to divine actions. The definition states that injustice is a harm that is not deserved (ghayr mustaḥaqq) and does not involve any benefit or prevention of harm that is known or presumed. Additionally, it is not itself a repulsion of harm nor a customary action. Then the author lists examples that are excluded from being injustice: punishment (al-ʿiqāb) is excluded because it is deserved; the hardship of travel and cupping are excluded because they involve benefit; repelling an attacker is excluded because it prevents harm; and God's burning of an infant thrown into fire is excluded because it is not done out of injustice but perhaps for some divine wisdom. The passage concludes that if causing pain (īlām) meets these criteria (deserved, beneficial, or customary), it is not injustice but good (ḥasan) and permissible for God without compensation. The Permissibility of Pain Under Divine Justice
Lists three modes of permissible infliction of pain.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three ways pain can be permissible by God's command, permission, or enabling with delayed justice, and the divine compensation involved.

Core Idea
Pain is not inherently unjust when it serves a greater purpose under God's justice.

The author begins by addressing a common concern: how can a just God allow pain? He explains that pain can occur by God's direct command, by His permission, or by His enabling while delaying justice to the afterlife, where compensation is given. This lesson unpacks these three scenarios and their theological basis.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the three categories: by command, by permission, and by enabling with delayed justice.
The passage uses the example of animals and oppressed persons to illustrate.

3

God's transcendence (تعاليه عن الظلم) is the ultimate guarantee.

Commentary

Abu Hashim disagrees, arguing that one may choose between them as between two benefits, since inflicting pain, now having the purpose of compensation and grace, is no longer futile or unjust.
