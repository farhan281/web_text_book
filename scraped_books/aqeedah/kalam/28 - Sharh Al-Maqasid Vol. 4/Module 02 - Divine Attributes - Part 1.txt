The Ash'ari Doctrine of Divine Will
States consensus on God's will and defines Ash'ari position.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the universal consensus that God is described as 'willing' and the specific Ash'ari teaching that everything that occurs is willed by God, including things He prohibits.

Core Idea
God's will encompasses all that happens, even prohibited acts.

This passage opens the fourth section on God's will (إرادة). The author first establishes that all rational theologians and philosophers agree that God is called 'willing' (مريد), based on revelation and the nature of His action. He then concludes with the core Ash'ari position: everything God wills occurs, and everything that occurs is willed by Him, whether good or evil, commanded or prohibited. This view aligns with the Salaf and contrasts with the Mu'tazila.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice the flow: first the consensus, then the doctrinal conclusion.
The passage is dense with technical terms; take your time.
2
Identify the basis for consensus
The author says the consensus is based on scriptural usage and the rational proof that God acts by choice. Consider why choice implies will.
3
Analyze the Ash'ari definition
Focus on the sentence beginning 'مذهب أهل الحق'. Break down what it means for everything willed to exist and everything existing to be willed.
Note the key phrase 'وإن لم يكن مرضياً ولا مأموراً به بل منهياً عنه' — this is the distinctive claim.
4
Contrast with the Mu'tazila
The passage ends by noting Mu'tazila opposition in 'the two principles'. Reflect on what two issues they likely denied.
The two principles are likely: (1) that God wills all events, (2) that even sinful acts are willed by God.

This is the Salaf's view, summarized as 'what God wills is, and what He does not will is not'. God is Speaking and the Eternal Speech
Proves God as speaker and gives two arguments for the pre-eternity of speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Islamic theology establishes that God is speaking and that His speech is pre-eternal, based on prophetic testimony and rational arguments.

Core Idea
God’s speech is eternal; He is the speaker in whom speech inheres, not a creator of sounds.

This lesson introduces the sixth section on God's attribute of speech. It first establishes that God is indeed 'speaking' through the testimony of prophets, whose truthfulness is independently verified by miracles. Then it presents two rational arguments for the pre-eternity of God's speech, distinguishing it from created sounds and mental forms.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once then the English translation. Notice the concise style and the logical flow from proving God is speaking to proving His speech is eternal.
The passage contains two main units: the proof of God as speaker, and two arguments for pre-eternity.
2
Understand the proof for God being speaking
The proof relies on recurrent reports from prophets (تواتر) and their miracles, avoiding circularity. Also a rational analogy to hearing and sight is mentioned.
This part assures that God is described as 'speaking' (متكلم) without needing a separate divine speech to confirm the prophets.
3
Learn the first argument for pre-eternity
The first argument defines the speaker as the one in whom speech inheres (قام به الكلام), not the one who produces speech elsewhere.
This distinguishes God's speech from created voices.
4
Learn the second argument for pre-eternity
The second argument explains that true speech consists of ordered audible letters, not mental or written forms.
God's speech is not the imagined or written form but the eternal attribute.
5
Reflect on the distinctions
Consider why these arguments are necessary: to affirm that God's speech is eternal and not a created accident.
This connects to the broader Islamic theology of attributes.

Refutation of the Four States View
Refutation of a mistaken theological view about divine essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the view that Allah's essence is distinguished only by four (or five) accidental states is fundamentally mistaken.

Core Idea
Confusing accidental attributes with the essence itself leads to theological error.

The author now addresses a specific theological position that attempts to explain how the Necessary Being is distinct from contingent beings. Some theologians proposed that the essence of the Necessary is essentially the same as others, and only differentiated by four states. This lesson unpacks why this view is categorically rejected as a confusion of accident with substance.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the two main parts: the statement of the wrong view and the author's dismissal.
Pay attention to the terms 'ahwal arba‘a' (four states).
2
Identify the wrong view and its variants
The passage mentions four states (necessary existence, life, knowledge, power) and optionally a fifth (divinity). These are said to distinguish the essence which is otherwise similar to others.
3
Understand the author's refutation

This refutation preserves the uniqueness of Allah's essence. If the essence were similar, then existence and attributes would be accidental, undermining tawhid.

The Mainstream Ruling on Divine Transcendence

Core Idea
God's essence does not change, but new relations and knowledge of new events are permissible.

This passage delineates the boundaries of divine transcendence.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Conceptual distinction fails because attribute terms are derived, not merely agreed upon.

This passage addresses a common objection: if we only make a conceptual distinction between attributes and essence, why can't we avoid the absurdities? If those were the Essence itself, the impossibilities would arise.

Learning Objective

In this passage, Taftazani wraps up his case for real attributes. He uses the logic of derived names and the impossibility of deficiency to show that attributes like knowledge and power are necessary and distinct from God's essence. He then critiques the Mu'tazilite position as absurd.
