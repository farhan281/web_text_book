Al-Ghazali's Distinction: Attributes as Informing, Names as Disposal
Al-Ghazali distinguishes naming from attributing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Ghazali's distinction between naming (tasmiya) and applying attributes (sifat), and why attributes are generally permissible while naming is restricted.

Core Idea
Attributes inform about God's reality; names dispose over God without permission.

Al-Ghazali draws a sharp line between using descriptive terms for God (attributes) and naming Him. While attributes are acts of truthful informing and thus permissible, naming is a disposal over God that requires explicit authorization. This lesson unpacks that distinction and its theological implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the distinction
Start by reading the Arabic and translation that present the response to the objection and al-Ghazali's view. Notice how he categorizes naming as a 'practical matter' and attributes as 'informing.'
The passage is dense; read slowly.
2
Identify the two categories
Identify the two categories: 'tasmiya' (naming) as an act of the tongue and disposal over the named, and 'ijra' al-sifat' (applying attributes) as informing about a true meaning. Note the different rules: attributes are permissible unless a preventing factor exists; naming is restricted to those with authority.
The authority for naming God comes only from revelation.
3
Understand the rationale for permissibility of attributes
Reflect on why attributes are permissible: because informing truthfully is not only allowed but recommended. The default assumption is permission unless a proof prevents it.
This contrasts with naming, where the default is prohibition.
4
Ask: what is the preventing factor?
Consider what might prevent applying an attribute. Though not elaborated here, later passages show that suggestive connotations (iham) can be a preventing factor.
This connects to the next lesson.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion by responding to an objection about attributes. The author first reports a response that naming is a practical matter (ʿamaliyya) of the tongue, and then presents al-Ghazālī's view. Al-Ghazālī distinguishes between applying attributes and naming: applying attributes is an act of informing (ikhbār) that a meaning is true of God, and since truthfulness is permissible and even recommended, it is allowed unless there is a preventing factor. Naming, however, is a disposal (tasarruf) over the named, and no one has authority over God except Himself as revealed. Then an objection is raised: why not use terms like ʿārif, ʿāqil, faṭin, dhakī? The answer explains that these terms carry suggestive meanings (īhām) due to their common usage in specific contexts. For example, 'maʿrifa' might imply prior ignorance, 'ʿaql' may imply a certain relation to the world, and 'fiṭna' and 'dhakāʾ' imply quickness of perception. Similarly, 'dirāya' suggests trickery. Any term with such suggestion is impermissible without explicit permission from revelation, by scholarly consensus. This is contrasted with terms like al-Ṣabūr, al-Shakūr, al-Ḥalīm, al-Raḥīm, which are permissible because they are revealed.

The Problem of Suggestive Connotations (Īhām) in Divine Names
Objection and response about terms like 'alim and 'aqil due to iham.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why terms like ʿārif, ʿāqil, etc., are not permissible for God due to their suggestive connotations, and the general rule that terms with iham require explicit permission.

Core Idea
Seemingly positive terms can be impermissible if they suggest unworthy meanings about God.

After establishing the general permissibility of attributes, an objection arises: why can't we use terms like ʿārif (knower) or ʿāqil (intelligent) for God? The answer lies in the principle of iham (suggestion) – these terms carry secondary meanings that imply imperfection, such as prior ignorance or a calculating mind. This lesson explores that reasoning and the consensus that such terms are prohibited without explicit permission.

These meanings are unworthy of God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why some terms found in the Quran and Sunnah are not used as names or attributes for Allah, based on the principle of contextual appropriateness (maqām and adab).

Core Idea
Not every divine description in revelation becomes a name.

The answer clarifies that mere occurrence is insufficient; the context and respectful usage determine appropriateness.

قلنا : يحتمل أن يكون خارجاً، ويكون زيادة شرف التسعة والتسعين وجلالتها بالإضافة إلى ما عداه ، وأن يكون داخلاً فيها لا يعرفه بعينه إلا نبي أو ولي. الثالث: أن الأسماء منحصرة في التسعة والتسعين والرواية المشتملة على تفصيلها غير مذكورة في الصحيح، ولا خالية عن الاضطراب والتغيير، وقد ذكر كثير من المحدثين أن في إسنادها ضعفاً، وعلى هذا يظهر معنى قوله عليه السلام «إن الله وتر يحب الوتر» أي جعل الأسماء التي سمى بها نفسه تسعة وتسعون ولم يكملها مائة لأنه وتر يحب الوتر، ويكون معنى إحصائها الاجتهاد في التقاطها من الكتاب والسنة، وجمعها وحفظها على ما قال بعض المحدثين. إنه صح عندي قريب من ثمانين يشتمل عليه الكتاب والصحاح من الأخبار، والباقي ينبغي أن يطلب من الأخبار بطريق الاجتهاد والمشهور أن معنى إحصائها عدها، والتلفظ بها حتى ذكر بعض الفقهاء أنه ينبغي أن تذكر بلا إعراب ليكون إحصاء، ويشكل بما هو مضاف كمالك الملك، وذو الجلال ، وقيل حفظها ، أو التأمل في معانيها.
We say: It is possible that it (i.e., the hundredth name) is excluded, and thus the honor and majesty of the ninety-nine are increased in addition to what is other than them; and it is possible that it is included among them, but no one knows it specifically except a prophet or a saint. Third: That the names are limited to ninety-nine, and the narration that includes their detailed enumeration is not mentioned in the Sahih, nor is it free from confusion and alteration, and many hadith scholars have stated that its chain has weakness. On this basis, the meaning of his statement, peace be upon him, 'Verily God is Witr (odd) and loves the witr' becomes clear, i.e., He made the names by which He named Himself ninety-nine and did not complete them to one hundred because He is Witr and loves the witr. And the meaning of their enumeration (ihsa'iha) would be striving to collect them from the Book and the Sunnah, and gathering them and memorizing them, as some hadith scholars said. Indeed, about eighty names have been established for me that are contained in the Book and the authentic reports, and the remainder should be sought from reports by way of ijtihad. The well-known view is that the meaning of their enumeration is counting them and uttering them, so much so that some jurists mentioned that they should be mentioned without inflection in order to constitute enumeration. But this is problematic for what is compounded, such as Malik al-Mulk and Dhu al-Jalal. And it was said: their memorization, or reflection upon their meanings.

Commentary
This passage discusses the interpretation of the hadith about the ninety-nine names of Allah. The author presents three possibilities regarding the hundredth name that is not mentioned. First, the hundredth name may be excluded from the ninety-nine, meaning the honor of the ninety-nine is increased by being separate from others. Second, it may be included within the ninety-nine, but no one knows it except a prophet or a saint. Third, the names are limited to exactly ninety-nine, and the hadith listing them is not authentically transmitted in Sahih collections and suffers from weaknesses in its chain. On this third view, the meaning of the Prophet's statement 'God is Witr and loves the witr' becomes clear: He set the names at an odd number, not completing them to one hundred. Consequently, the meaning of 'ihsa' (enumeration) is not mere counting but rather striving to collect the names from the Quran and authentic hadith, as stated by some hadith scholars. The author mentions that about eighty names are authentic from the Quran and sound hadiths, and the rest must be sought through ijtihad. The well-known view among scholars is that ihsa' means counting and uttering the names, so much so that some jurists say one should say them without grammatical inflection to mark them as enumerated. However, this view faces difficulty with compound names like 'Malik al-Mulk' and 'Dhu al-Jalal'. Other scholars interpret ihsa' as memorizing the names or reflecting upon their meanings.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Ihsa' of Allah's names only means to count them out loud without any deeper engagement.
Correction: Taftazani shows that this view has problems with compound names and that other scholars interpret ihsa' as memorization or reflection, which are more meaningful.
