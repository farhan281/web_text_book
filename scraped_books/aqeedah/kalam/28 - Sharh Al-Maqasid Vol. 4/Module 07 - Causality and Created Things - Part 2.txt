Answer to a Mu'tazilite Objection on Divine Knowledge and al-Aslah
The author answers a Mu'tazilite objection by pointing out their neglect of divine foreknowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the Mu'tazilite position on al-aslah by pointing out their inconsistency regarding divine foreknowledge.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila ignore divine knowledge when claiming God must compensate sinners.

The Mu'tazila hold that God must do what is most beneficial for His servants. But what about those He knows will reject faith? In this passage, the author exposes a contradiction in their reasoning: they demand compensation while ignoring that God's knowledge already determines the outcome.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once and then the literal translation. Notice the structure of the argument: it begins with 'It is answered:' indicating a response to an objection.
The passage is a single but dense sentence.
2
Understand the Mu'tazilite claim
The Mu'tazila claim that if God knows someone would sin if charged, He must compensate them. Identify this claim in the passage and understand its logic.
This is their doctrine of al-aslah (the most beneficial).
3
Identify the author's refutation
The author argues that the Mu'tazila fail to consider the aspect of 'the known' (al-ma'lum), i.e., God's foreknowledge. He points out the contradiction: if God knows the person will sin, He also knows they will not receive the compensation but fall into punishment.
4
Reflect on the alternative scenario
The author mentions that if God had caused death before full intellect, the person would be saved for reward, further undermining the Mu'tazilite position.
This shows that the Mu'tazilite logic leads to absurdity.

Commentary
This passage is an answer (jawāb) to a previous Mu'tazilite objection. The author points out a contradiction: the Mu'tazila claim that if God knows someone would sin if given responsibility, He must compensate them—but then they also claim God would not actually hold them accountable in that case. The author argues that if God had caused death before full intellect, the person would be saved for reward, so the Mu'tazilite position on compensation is inconsistent. The key term 'al-aslah' (the most beneficial) is central: the Mu'tazila hold that God must do what is best for His servants, but here the author shows their logic leads to absurdity regarding divine foreknowledge.

Key Takeaway

Objection and Answer: The Child's Death and Inconsistency of al-Aṣlaḥ
The author refutes the Mu‘tazilite claim with historical counterexamples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author of Sharḥ al-Maqāṣid refutes the Mu‘tazilite claim that causing a child's death to prevent future misguidance is consistent with the principle of al-aṣlaḥ (the most beneficial).

Core Idea
If God kills a child to prevent evil, why didn't He kill Pharaoh and other tyrants as children?

The Mu‘tazilites held that God always does what is most beneficial for His creatures. In this objection, they argue that God may cause a child to die early because He knows that child would later go astray and lead others astray, thus preventing greater harm. The author responds with a devastating counterexample: if that logic holds, then why were the greatest misleaders in history—Pharaoh, Haman, Mazdak, Zoroaster—allowed to live and cause immense harm? This short but powerful argument exposes the inconsistency of the Mu‘tazilite position.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Note that the objection is presented as a hypothetical ('If it is said...') and the answer begins with 'We say...'.
2
Analyze the logical force
Ask yourself: why is the list of tyrants so effective? The author does not need to prove that God actually kills children; he simply shows that the Mu‘tazilite principle cannot be applied consistently.
Consider what the author implies by asking 'how would it not be foolishness and injustice?'

Source Text

فإن قيل : علم من الطفل أنه إن عاش ضل وأضل غيره فأماته لمصلحة الغير. قلنا فكيف لم يمت فرعون وهامان ومزدك وزرادشت وغيرهم من الضالين المضلين أطفالاً ، وكيف لم يكن منع الأصلح عمن لا جناية له لأجل مصلحة الغير سفهاً وظلما.
If it is said: It was known of the child that if he lived he would go astray and lead others astray, so He caused him to die for the benefit of others. We say: So why were Pharaoh, Haman, Mazdak, Zoroaster, and others of the misguiding misled not died as children? And how would the prevention of the most beneficial from one who has no wrongdoing for the sake of the benefit of others not be foolishness and injustice?
Source pages p. 333

Commentary
This is another objection-answer pair. The Mu'tazilite objector says: God knows that a particular child, if allowed to live, would go astray (ḍalla) and lead others astray (aḍalla ghairah), so God causes that child to die early for the benefit (maṣlaḥah) of others—this is consistent with al-aslah because it prevents greater evil. The author responds by asking why then did God not cause Pharaoh, Haman, Mazdak, Zoroaster, and other notorious misleaders (ḍāllīn muḍillīn) to die as children? They caused immense harm, so if the principle holds, God should have killed them early to prevent that harm. Furthermore, the author argues that depriving an innocent child (lā janāyata lahu) of the most beneficial outcome (preventing his misguidance by killing him) for the sake of others is actually foolishness (safah) and injustice (ẓulm). This shows the Mu'tazilite position is inconsistent: they cannot consistently apply al-aslah across all cases.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The author is arguing that God should have killed Pharaoh and other tyrants as children.
Correction: The author is not prescribing what God should have done; he is using a hypothetical to show that the Mu‘tazilite principle, if applied consistently, would lead to absurd conclusions. The point is to expose the inconsistency in the Mu‘tazilite argument, not to advocate for killing.

Checkpoint

B) To prove that God kills all evil people as children
C) To ask why they were not killed as children if the principle of al-aslah is applied consistently
D) To argue that they deserved to live long

Key Takeaway
The author uses a reductio ad absurdum to show that the Mu‘tazilite principle of al-aṣlaḥ leads to inconsistency and injustice when applied to a child's early death.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?

?

?

How does this passage remind you that God's actions are never foolish or unjust, even when you cannot understand? What step will you take to trust Him more deeply in a current difficulty?
