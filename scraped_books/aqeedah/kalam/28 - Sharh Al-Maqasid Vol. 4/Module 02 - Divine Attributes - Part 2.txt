Rational Proof for Extra-Essential Attributes
Taftazani argues that divine attributes are extra-essential meanings, not mere relations or identical with essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Taftazani's rational proof that divine attributes are real meanings distinct from the essence, and how he refutes the view that they are mere relations or identical with essence.

Core Idea
God's attributes are real, distinct meanings, not just mental considerations or relations.

Taftazani presents a sophisticated argument to establish that attributes like knowledge and power are extra-essential meanings. He uses an analogy and logical reasoning to show that they cannot be mere relations or identical with essence. This lesson unpacks his proof step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the translation. Let the author's argument sink in before interpreting.
Notice the dense style and multiple sub-arguments.
2
Understand the analogy of the number one
Taftazani compares the Mu'tazilite position to saying the number one is itself half-ness, third-ness, etc. Grasp why he considers this absurd.
This illustrates that multiple relations can be predicated without multiplicity in the essence.
3
Distinguish mental considerations from real meanings
He argues that attributes like knowledge and power are real meanings, not mere mental considerations like temporality. See why this distinction is crucial.
If they were mere mental considerations, they would have no external reality.
4
Follow the logical conclusion
If attributes were the essence itself, absurdities follow; if they are beyond the essence, the point is proven. Trace this logical step.
The conclusion is that attributes are meanings additional to the essence.

Commentary
This passage is a rational proof (dalil 'aqli) for the doctrine that God's attributes (such as knowledge and power) are real, distinct meanings (ma'ani) additional to His essence, against the Mu'tazilite view that they are identical with the essence. The argument proceeds in several steps. First, the author likens the Mu'tazilite position to the absurdity of saying the number one is itself half-ness, third-ness, etc., while being a single existent. This illustrates that multiple relations can be predicated without multiplicity in the essence, but the attributes are not mere relations. Second, he states that if knowledge and power were the essence's relation to objects, then the essence itself would be the relation, which is false. Rather, the basis of derivation (makhadh al-ishtiqaq) — the actual knowledge and power — must be a meaning beyond the essence. Calling them 'relations' does not help because they are real entities, not mere mental considerations like temporality. Third, he argues that if they were the essence, the absurdity returns; if they are beyond, the point is proven. Fourth, he notes that descriptions like 'knowing' are only realized after the relation, so there must be a distinguishing particularity. Fifth, he concludes that derived names like 'knowing' must refer to real meanings, and since being devoid of them would be a deficiency, they must be established. Finally, he asserts that these meanings cannot be the essence because they would then subsist by themselves, which is impossible. He ends with a remark on the Mu'tazilites' stubbornness in denying the attributes.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof (al-wajh al-thalith) for the reality of God's attributes, namely scriptural evidence (nusus) from the Quran. Taftazani states that these texts indicate knowledge and power in a way that cannot be interpreted away (la yahtamil al-ta'wil). He cites four verses: (1) 'Anzalahu bi-'ilmihi' — He sent it down with His knowledge, showing that knowledge is an attribute distinct from the act of sending down. (2) 'Fa'lamu annama unzila bi-'ilmillah' — So know that what was sent down is with the knowledge of God, where 'bi-'ilmillah' means accompanied by knowledge as a quality, not merely coincidental, lest one be forced to say knowledge itself was sent down, which would require allegorical interpretation (ta'wil). (3) 'Inna al-quwwata lillah jami'an' — Power belongs entirely to God, affirming power as an attribute. (4) 'Inna Allaha huwa al-razzaqu dhu al-quwwati al-matin' — God is the Provider, the Possessor of Strength, the Firm, affirming power as a real attribute (dhu al-quwwa). These verses are presented as clear proof that knowledge and power are real attributes of God, not merely figurative or identical with essence.

Refuting the Claim that Attributes Make God Both Subject and Agent
First objection and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first objection against additional attributes and how the author refutes it by denying the premise of impossibility.

Core Idea
The opponent's first argument is refuted simply by rejecting its claim of absurdity.

The opponents argue that if attributes are distinct, God would be both the receiver and producer of His own attributes—a combination they consider impossible. In this lesson, we examine this objection and the author's very concise refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the passage presenting the first objection: that if all things depend on God, especially His attributes, then He would be both receptive (qabil) and active (fa'il).
Notice how the objection derives a necessary consequence from the assumption of distinct attributes.
2
Identify the refutation
The refutation is simply 'by denying its falsity'—meaning the opponent's claim that the consequence is absurd is not conceded.
This is a dialectical move: the burden of proof remains on the opponent.
3
Reflect on the logic
Think about why this refutation is considered sufficient. In kalam, if an opponent asserts impossibility without proof, one can simply reject it.
The author does not explain why it is not impossible; he only denies the opponent's assertion.

Commentary
This passage presents four objections raised by opponents (likely Mu'tazila and others) against the doctrine of additional attributes, along with their refutations. 

First objection: If attributes are distinct, then God would be both the subject (qabil) and the agent (fa'il) of His own attributes, which is absurd. Refutation: The opponent's claim of absurdity is denied; it is not necessarily impossible. 

Second objection: Attributes are perfections, so affirming them implies God is perfected by another, which is impermissible. Refutation: The attributes are not 'other' than God in the sense of being separate entities; even if one grants that, the very point in dispute is whether God can be described with such perfections. 

Third objection: God's omniscience is necessary, and a necessary thing cannot have a cause. Refutation: Even if we grant that omniscience is other than knowledge (i.e., an attribute), the term 'necessary' here means that the Essence cannot be devoid of it, not that it is uncaused. We do not accept that an attribute arising from the Essence cannot be a cause. 

Fourth objection: Saying there are multiple eternal entities is unbelief by consensus. Refutation: There is no real distinction (taghayur) here between essence and attribute, so no multiplicity. Even if there were distinction, it does not mean each is eternal (qadim) in the sense of being pre-eternal (azali); rather, attributes are self-subsistent but not separate. Even if granted, the consensus is about multiplicity in the sense of independent entities, not about attributes inhering in the essence. The Christians erred by positing three hypostases in one essence, but that is not analogous.

The passage is structured as a series of objections and concise refutations, demonstrating dialectical skill.

Third objection and its response concerning necessary attributes.

Learning Objective

Fourth Objection: Multiplicity of Eternals
Fourth objection and its layered response regarding multiplicity of eternals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth objection that believing in multiple eternal beings is kufr and the layered Ash'ari response rejecting any genuine multiplicity in God's attributes.

Core Idea
Attributes are not other beings, so no multiplicity of eternals.

The fourth objection warns that if attributes are eternal along with the essence, that would imply multiple eternal beings, which is kufr. The Ash'ari response dismantles this by showing that attributes are not other than the essence, and even if they were, they are not independent agents. The response has multiple conditional steps to block the charge.

1
Read the objection and response

The response builds a cascade of defenses.

Understand the final reference
The response ends by drawing an analogy to the Christian Trinity: the attributes are not separate essences. The Ash'ari view avoids polytheism.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An attribute's contingency does not imply imperfection when it is necessitated by the divine essence.

This passage tackles a fundamental challenge from the philosophers against the Ash'ari doctrine of additional attributes. The argument claims that if God had attributes distinct from His essence, they would be contingent and thus imperfect. The Dilemma of Perfection and Its Refutation
The author refutes the argument that an additional attribute is either not perfection or entails perfection by another.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second argument against additional divine attributes and the Ash'ari response based on the 'neither He nor other than He' distinction.

Core Idea
Attributes are neither identical to Allah nor entirely other—they arise from His essence.

The second argument attempts to force a contradiction: either an attribute is not a perfection (so it must be negated) or it is a perfection (so Allah would be perfected by another). The Ash'ari response rejects both horns with key insights about the nature of divine attributes. This lesson unpacks the logic and the reply.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice the structure: a dilemma presented, then an answer beginning with 'وأجيب'.
The passage is compact but dense in meaning.
2
Identify the two horns of the dilemma
Find the two possibilities: the attribute is not perfection (first horn) or it is perfection (second horn). Note the conclusion for each.
The first horn leads to negation, the second to deficiency in essence.
3

Does God Have Composition? Refuting the First Doubt
Sanusi presents and refutes the objection that attributes would make God composite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Ash'ari response denies that the attributes imply composition in the divine essence, preserving God's oneness.

Core Idea
Attributes do not make God composite; they are necessitated by His essence.

The first doubt raised against the existence of attributes in God is that it would imply composition, making Him contingent. But this objection misunderstands the relationship between essence and attributes. In this passage, al-Sanusi presents the objection and gives the decisive answer: the divine reality is the essence that necessitates the attributes, not a composite of parts.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Identify the logical steps: if God has attributes, then the divine reality would be composed of essence and attributes; every composite is contingent; therefore God would be contingent. This is impossible, so the existence of attributes is false.

2
Examine the answer: denial of implication
The answer (al-jawab) denies the implication (man' al-mulazama). It does not accept that attributes lead to composition in the usual sense.
This is a standard kalam move: breaking the logical chain.

Sanusi states that the divine reality (haqiqat al-ilahiyya) is the essence that necessitates the attributes (al-dhat al-mujiba lil-sifat). This means attributes are not added parts but necessary concomitants of the essence.

Commentary
This passage is a critical evaluation of additional arguments often used to prove God's power and knowledge. Taftazani lists three other types: (i) consensus and definitive scriptural texts, (ii) the argument from perfection (that attributes like power and knowledge are perfections, and their opposites are deficiencies), and (iii) the argument from the order of creation (an a fortiori argument). He then notes that these arguments are not free from points of criticism. Regarding the first six (the rational arguments previously given), they are clear to those familiar with philosophy. The seventh (scriptural) is problematic because acceptance of scripture and miracles presupposes belief in a powerful, knowing God. The eighth (perfection) depends on the premise that it is permissible for God to have such attributes and that they are perfections, which may be contested. The ninth argument (which is incomplete in the text) is also based on contested premises. Thus, the rational arguments (the six) are more robust.

The Sixth Proof: Animal Forms Require a Willful Creator
Sanusi presents the sixth proof for God's power and will from animal forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the diversity of animal forms points to a creator with power and will, not mere natural necessity.

Core Idea
Animal diversity cannot come from blind nature; it demands a choosing agent.

This lesson explores a powerful argument from the animal world: the intricate and diverse forms of animals and their limbs cannot be the result of a blind, necessary cause. If nature alone were at work, all animals would be simple spheres. The fact that they are not shows that their maker must be powerful and willful.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how the author sets up a conditional argument: if the cause were natural necessity, then the result would be spherical; since it is not, the cause must be a free agent.
The passage begins with a general principle about necessary causes and preponderance.
2
Understand the principle of necessary cause
The first sentence explains that if a cause is necessary (without choice), it would treat all parts equally, leading to a uniform result. This principle is key to the proof.
3
Identify the two scenarios
The author considers two possibilities: the sperm is simple or composite. In both cases, a necessary cause would produce a spherical shape. Observe how the argument covers both bases.
4
Draw the conclusion
Since animals are not spherical but have diverse forms, the cause cannot be a necessary nature. Therefore, the maker must be powerful and willful (al-qadir al-mukhtar).
This conclusion is the core of the sixth proof.
5
Reflect on the significance
Consider how this argument uses observable creation to infer an attribute of the Creator. It is a teleological argument based on design and purpose.

Commentary
This passage is a continuation of arguments for God's power and knowledge, presenting the sixth proof and three additional proofs, followed by a critique. The sixth proof focuses on the creation of animals: the maker of animals and their limbs must be powerful and willful. If the cause were merely natural necessity (e.g., the nature of the sperm), then the resulting animal would have a simple spherical shape (if the sperm is simple) or a composite of spheres (if the sperm is composite), but we observe diverse forms, so the cause must be an agent with will. Then the author presents three further proofs: (1) consensus and definitive texts from Quran and Sunnah; (2) the argument that attributes like power and knowledge are perfections, and their opposites are deficiencies from which God must be free; (3) the argument from the observed order and perfection of the world, which implies a knowing and powerful maker by necessity. The author then states that these arguments are not free from discussion, and briefly critiques each. The first six proofs (likely those previously mentioned) are not hidden from one versed in philosophy (i.e., they are understood by philosophers but may not be decisive). The seventh proof (scripture) depends on prior acceptance of God's power and knowledge, creating a potential circularity. The eighth proof (perfection) relies on premises about what constitutes perfection and its attribution to God, which can be disputed. The ninth proof (causality) assumes that observed phenomena are directly caused by God without intermediate causes, a claim made by philosophers but not universally accepted. The passage ends on a hopeful note: the sincere seeker of truth may still attain certainty from these arguments.
