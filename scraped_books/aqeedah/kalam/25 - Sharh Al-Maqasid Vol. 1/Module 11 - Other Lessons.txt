Origin of Greek Dialectic and the Sophist Anecdote
Origin of Greek dialectic and the sophist anecdote

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how dialectic originated as a respected science in Greece and how the anecdote of the sophist teacher and student illustrates its shift toward argumentative victory over truth.

Core Idea
Dialectic began as a revered science but became a tool for winning arguments over seeking truth.

This passage traces the early history of dialectic in Greece, where it was a respected discipline taught by sages. It then shares a striking anecdote that reveals the moral ambiguity of the craft: a student uses his training to avoid paying his teacher, and the teacher responds in kind. This sets the stage for understanding why Islamic theologians later developed a more truth-centered methodology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation to grasp the full narrative.
Notice the shift from describing dialectic's status to the anecdote.
2
Understand the Sophist vs Philosopher contrast
Identify why the Sophist was initially more prestigious and how Pythagoras redefined the term.
The key is humility: philosopher loves wisdom, sophist claims to possess it.
3
Analyze the anecdote's logic
Trace the student's argument for not paying and the teacher's counter-argument. What does this show about the goals of dialectic?
The teacher's response reveals that winning was prioritized over truth.
4
Reflect on the broader lesson
Consider why this anecdote is included in a text on Islamic theology. How does it critique a purely adversarial approach to argument?
This sets up the need for a sound method like kalam that seeks truth.

Dialectic in its first origin in the state of the Greeks was a science with rules and principles, greatly revered among the sages and their students and the multitude of those concerned with wisdom and knowledge. The name 'Sophist' was more prestigious than the name 'philosopher', because the sophist belongs to the goddess of wisdom 'Sophia', so he is the sage inspired by that goddess and freed from the burden of knowledge. When the sage Pythagoras appeared, he deemed this claim too great and humbly called himself a philosopher, i.e., a lover of wisdom. Some people set out to teach dialectic or rhetorical proofs, whom students would seek out to learn how to overcome their opponents in the field of dispute and debate, and fathers would place their sons in their care to train them in the craft of dialectic and influence in the way of persuasion by argument, whatever its share of truth. It is related about a dialectic teacher that he agreed with a student of his that he would train him to defend in court and public disputes for two years for an agreed fee. When the two years ended, the teacher asked for his fee. The student said: 'Rather, I will argue with you about this fee: do you deserve it by your work or do you claim it unjustly? If I convince you that you do not deserve it, then you have no right to it by your own admission, and your silence is evidence of this admission. And if I do not convince you, then you have no right to it, because you did not teach me how to establish proof for my claim.' The teacher's answer was an example for his student of the proof required in this craft. He said to him: 'I accept to argue with you, but on a different outcome than you concluded. I argue with you about my right: you give it to me once if it is proven against you, and you give it twice if I do not prove it before you, because I taught a student what enables him to defeat his teacher in the craft of proof.'

Commentary
This passage describes the early origins of dialectic in ancient Greece and its high status among sages and the public. It contrasts the Sophist, who claimed divine wisdom, with Pythagoras who humbly named himself a philosopher (lover of wisdom). Then it recounts an anecdote about a dialectic teacher and his student that illustrates the moral ambiguity of the craft: the student tries to avoid paying the fee by using a trick, and the teacher responds with a counter-trick, showing that the goal was winning arguments rather than seeking truth. The passage highlights how dialectic became a tool for manipulation, setting the stage for the need for a sounder methodology (ilm al-kalam) that prioritizes truth.

Describes severe famine and its impact on society.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Calamities are divine reminders to return to Allah's path.

This passage from the sharh describes a severe famine that struck many lands in the 8th century AH. It depicts extreme suffering, including the selling of children and eating of forbidden animals, and concludes with a Quranic verse about divine punishment. As you read, consider the moral lesson intended by the author.

Refutation of the Claim About the Intermediate
The author presents three objections against a claim about the intermediate state and partially defends it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three objections raised against the philosophical concept of the intermediate (al-wasita) and the author's partial defense.

Core Idea
The intermediate state is neither existing nor non-existing, but this view faces crucial contradictions.

The concept of an intermediate state between existence and non-existence is a key point of debate between theologians and philosophers. The author of al-Mawaqif proposed a basis for it, but our author here refutes it with three objections, while also offering a possible rebuttal to two of them. This lesson unpacks the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic source and its full English translation carefully. Get a sense of the author's argumentative flow before diving into analysis.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Understand the opponent's position
Identify the claim being refuted: that the intermediate is based on concepts that cannot receive existence, like secondary intelligibles. Note the contrast between the theologians' opposition of affirmation/negation and the philosophers' opposition of possession/privation.
The opponent's view is presented in the first sentence.
3
Analyze the three objections
The author gives three objections using 'أما أولاً', 'أما ثانياً', 'أما ثالثاً'. For each, note the specific flaw being pointed out. The first objection concerns the distinction between non-existence and the impossible; the second concerns the status of the intermediate as more distant from existence; the third concerns a contradiction with the definition of the intermediate.
These objections build on each other.
4
Consider the rebuttal and conclusion
The author offers a possible rebuttal to the last two objections by distinguishing between independent and dependent actualization. Reflect on how this saves the intermediate concept from the criticisms.
The rebuttal is partial; the first objection stands.

أقول ؛ ذكر صاحب المواقف على ما نطق به أصل النسخ : أنه يظن ظناً قريباً من اليقين، أن مبنى إثبات الواسطة على أنهم وجدوا من المفهومات ما يتصور عروض الوجود لها، فسموا تحققها وجوداً، وارتفاعها عدماً، ومنها ما لا يتصور عروض الوجود لها أصلاً، كالاعتبارات العقلية التي تسميها الحكماء معقولات ثانية، فجعلوها لا موجودة ولا معدومة بمعنى أنها ليست متحققة ، ولا من شأنها التحقق فعندنا تقابل الوجود والعدم تقابل إيجاب وسلب. وعندهم تقابل ملكة وعدم. والحق أن هذا الظن لا يغني من الحق شيئاً .

وأما ثالثاً : فلأنه ينافي ما ذكره في تفسير الواسطة، في أنه المعلوم الذي له تحقق لا باعتبار ذاته، بل تبعاً لغيره، أو الكائن في الأعيان ، لا بالاستقلال بل تبعاً لغيره، ويمكن دفع الأخيرين بأن المراد بالتحقق الذي يتصور عروضه للمعدوم دون الواسطة هو التحقق بالاستقلال، وأن الواسطة تكون أقرب إلى الوجود من حيث أن التحقق بالتبعة حاصل له بالفعل.
I say: The author of al-Mawaqif mentioned, according to what the original copy states, that he thinks, with a suspicion close to certainty, that the basis for affirming the intermediary (al-wasita) is that they found among concepts some to which occurrence of existence conceivably attaches, so they named their actualization existence, and their absence non-existence; and some to which occurrence of existence does not conceivably attach at all, like the mental considerations that the philosophers call secondary intelligibles, so they made them neither existing nor non-existing in the sense that they are not actualized and are not of the nature to be actualized. So with us, the opposition between existence and non-existence is an opposition of affirmation and negation. But with them, it is the opposition of possession and privation. The truth is that this suspicion does not avail anything of the truth. As for first, because it would only be valid if non-existence with them is distinct from the impossible in that it is not applied to it at all, as the author of al-Talkhīṣ mentioned: not more general, according to what the author of al-Mawāqif and others established, because it is obvious that occurrence of existence does not attach to it at all. As for second, because then the state (al-ḥāl) would be more distant from existence than non-existence, since it has neither actualization nor possibility of actualization. But it is not so, because they make it have surpassed the limit of non-existence in determination, actualization, and confirmation, and not have reached the limit of existence, and therefore they permitted it to be a part of an existent, like the blackness of blackness. As for third, because it contradicts what he mentioned in the interpretation of the intermediary, that it is the known thing which has actualization not by virtue of its own essence, but dependently on another, or that which exists in the external beings, not independently but dependently on another. It is possible to rebut the last two by saying that what is meant by actualization whose occurrence is conceivable for non-existence but not for the intermediary is independent actualization (al-taḥaqquq bi-l-istiqlāl), and that the intermediary is closer to existence in that dependent actualization (al-taḥaqquq bi-l-tabi'a) is actually attained for it.

Commentary
This passage presents a refutation of a claim about the intermediate state (al-wasita) between existence and non-existence. The author begins by stating the opponent's view: that the notion of the intermediate is based on the idea that some concepts can 'receive' existence (they call their actualization 'existence' and their absence 'non-existence'), while other concepts, like the secondary intelligibles of the philosophers, cannot receive existence at all and are thus considered neither existing nor non-existing. The opponent contrasts the Muslim theologians' view (existence and non-existence as affirmation and negation) with the philosophers' view (opposition of possession and privation). The author then declares this suspicion worthless. He provides three objections (أما أولاً, وأما ثانياً, وأما ثالثاً). The first objection: the claim is valid only if non-existence (al-ma'dum) in their view is distinct from the impossible (al-mumtani') and is never applied to it. The second objection: the intermediate (al-ḥāl) would then be more distant from existence than non-existence, since it has neither actualization nor possibility of it, yet the philosophers claim it is more determined than non-existence. The third objection: it contradicts the definition of the intermediate as something that has actualization not by itself but dependently on another. Finally, the author offers a possible rebuttal to the last two objections: that 'actualization' in the context of non-existence means independent actualization, while the intermediate has dependent actualization, making it closer to existence.
