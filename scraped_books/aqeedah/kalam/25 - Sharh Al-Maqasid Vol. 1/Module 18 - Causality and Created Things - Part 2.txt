Refuting the Objection about Persistence and Need
Al-Taftazani refutes the objection about persistence and clarifies the meaning of need.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that if need is due to imkan or huduth, then things would need a cause in persistence, and how al-Taftazani answers it.

Core Idea
Persistence is just existence in a second moment; need means impossibility without a cause.

After establishing the correct view, al-Taftazani addresses a powerful objection: if the need for a cause is due to imkan or huduth, which are continuous, then the possible/originated would still need a cause when persisting, leading to absurdities like 'obtaining the obtained' or implying independence. He provides a nuanced answer clarifying the meaning of need and persistence.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the objection: if the cause of need is imkan or huduth, then since these are constant, the thing would need a cause in persistence. That leads to two absurdities: either effect upon existence (tahsil al-hasil) or effect upon persistence (which is not the thing itself). Also, imkan would cause need in prior nonexistence.
Identify the two parts of the objection.
2
Understand the answer's key move
Al-Taftazani answers by redefining need: it means the thing's existence or non-existence depends on something such that it is impossible without it. Persistence (baqa') is just existence considered in relation to a second moment. So need in persistence is simply that the continued existence depends on the cause.
Notice the distinction between existence and persistence.
3
Apply the answer to the objection
Thus, the objection's 'tahsil al-hasil' is avoided because need in persistence is not for bringing into existence but for continuation. Also, the need in prior nonexistence is not a problem because need is only about the possibility of existence or non-existence, not about actual nonexistence.
Connect the answer to each part of the objection.
4
Reflect on the concept of baqa'
The passage ends by stating that baqa' is not a separate entity but existence taken with a temporal qualification. This is a key metaphysical point in kalam.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion about the cause of the need for a cause (al-ihiyaj ila al-mu'aththir). It begins by stating that it is already established that the possible (mumkin) needs a cause, but there is a difference of opinion: the philosophers (falasifa) and some mutakallimun say the need is due to its possibility (imkan), while the early mutakallimun say it is due to its origination (huduth). Others combine both. Then the philosophers present their proof: if the intellect considers a thing as not entailing existence or nonexistence by its essence, it judges that its existence or nonexistence must come from an external cause, regardless of whether it is preceded by nonexistence. They also argue against huduth as the cause: huduth is a description of existence and therefore posterior to it; if huduth were the cause of need, it would lead to infinite regress. The mutakallimun counter that the cause is huduth because the intellect, when considering a thing that comes into existence after nonexistence, judges it needs a cause to bring it out of nonexistence, even without considering its possibility. They reject imkan because it is a relation of existence to essence, which is posterior to existence, etc. The truth (al-haqq) is that both are intellectual considerations: the intellect observes either imkan or huduth and judges need accordingly, just as it is said that the cause of the product in kheir is determination in space (tahayyuz) — not in external reality. Thus the debate in negation is sophistry, but in affirmation the later scholars' (muta'akhkhirun) view is more apparent and more acceptable.

Then an objection (i'tirad) is raised: if the cause of need is imkan or huduth, which are inseparable from the possible/originated, then they would need a cause in the state of persistence (baqa'), because the effect persists with the persistence of the cause. But that is false because if the cause were to act upon existence, that would be obtaining the obtained (tahsil al-hasil), and if it acts upon persistence, that would be affecting something else, implying independence from the cause. Also, imkan as cause leads to the need of a possible thing for a cause in its prior nonexistence, which is a pure eternal negation. The answer (jawab): the meaning of need is that the attainment of existence, nonexistence, or their continuation depends on something in the sense that it is impossible without it. Continuation of existence (baqa') is just existence considered with respect to a second moment of time, and the fact that we say 'it existed, then it did not persist' only proves that baqa' is different from absolute existence, which is not disputed.

The Cause of Need for an Effect and Its Refutation
Al-Taftazani refutes an objection about the need for an effect to persist.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Taftazani refutes the objection that the need for a cause would persist eternally if the cause is possibility or origination.

Core Idea
Persistence of an effect is just continued existence depending on the same cause.

Al-Taftazani has been discussing the need of possible beings for an effect (a cause). He now addresses an objection: if the cause of need is possibility or origination, then the need would persist forever, leading to absurdities. His answer clarifies that persistence is not a new existence but a continuation of the same existence, which still depends on the cause. This lesson unpacks that subtle but crucial argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the objection begins with 'و اعترض بأنه' and the answer with 'الجواب أن'.
2
Identify the two views on the cause of need
The passage first mentions that the cause of need could be determination (taḥayyuz) or origination (ḥudūth). Note how al-Taftazani dismisses the negation arguments of both groups as fallacious.
He then says the later scholars' affirmative view is more evident.
3
Understand the objection
The objection argues that if possibility or origination is the cause of need, then the need would persist because they are permanent attributes. This would lead to either a redundant effect (if the cause acts on existence already present) or a new effect on something not persistent.
Also, possibility would imply need before existence, which is absurd.
4
Grasp the answer
Al-Taftazani responds that 'need' means dependence of both existence and continuation on the cause. Continuation is not a separate existence but the same existence related to a later time.
The fact that we say 'it existed and did not persist' only shows that continuance is conceptually distinct from existence, not that it lacks dependence.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
See how the answer resolves the objection without needing a new cause for each moment. The same cause that brought the thing into existence also sustains it.
This is a key point in the Ashʿarī doctrine of continuous creation.

First Objection Against Inherent Preponderance and Its Refutation
First objection that inherent preponderance leads to contradiction and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first objection raised by the majority against inherent preponderance and the author's refutation, including the logical dilemma and the distinction between preponderance and necessity.

Core Idea
Inherent preponderance does not imply necessity; it is a preponderance short of necessity.

The majority of theologians argued that inherent preponderance in a possible leads to contradictions. Their first objection presents a dilemma: either the preponderant side becomes necessary, or the possible side occurs without cause. In response, the author clarifies that the preponderance at issue is not absolute but a relative preference that does not reach the level of necessity. This passage sharpens the concept of inherent preponderance.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context of the objection
Recognize that the majority are arguing against the possibility of inherent preponderance in a possible entity. Why does this matter? If a possible has an inherent leaning, it might seem to imply necessity.
This objection is one of several arguments against inherent preponderance.
2
Follow the dilemma presented
The objection sets up two horns: either the other side becomes impossible (making the first necessary) or if still possible, its occurrence requires a cause, leading to a contradiction.
Notice the explicit 'إما...أو' structure in the Arabic.
3
Identify the refutation's key move
The answer denies that preponderance reaches the point of necessity. It is a preponderance short of necessity, so the horns do not apply.
The refutation hinges on the distinction between preponderance and absolute necessity.
4
Connect to broader doctrine
How does this refutation preserve the possibility of inherent preponderance without collapsing into necessitarianism?

Third Objection: The Dilemma of Essential Preponderance and Its Refutation
Third objection: essential preponderance leads to a dilemma, and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third objection to essential preponderance and how the author refutes it by distinguishing inherent preponderance from actual necessity.

Core Idea
Essential preponderance does not necessitate actual occurrence; external causes can override without removing it.

This lesson presents the third objection against essential preponderance (al-ula al-dhatiyya) and its refutation. The objection attempts to force a contradiction: if preponderance is essential, it must be either removable or not, each leading to a problem. The response clarifies that essential preponderance is not to the point of necessity and remains intact even when external factors determine actual occurrence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation once to grasp the overall argument. Notice the objection's two horns and the author's answer.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the structure
Identify the objection (with two conditional statements) and the answer introduced by 'Wa al-jawab'.
The objection uses 'fa-in amkana... wa in lam yumkin'.
3
Understand the two horns
First horn: if removable, preponderance depends on a cause and is not essential. Second horn: if not removable, the preponderant side becomes necessary, contradicting possibility.
Each horn is designed to show a contradiction.
4
Grasp the refutation
The answer denies that the impossibility of removal entails occurrence or necessity. It introduces the idea of essential entailment not reaching the point of wajub, and external causes determining actuality.
Key phrase: 'la yalzam min imtina' al-zawal... wuqu'ahu'.
5
Reflect on the core idea
Essential preponderance is a real but limited property; it does not force actual existence. External causes complete the determination.
This preserves both essential preponderance and contingency.

The Necessity of Preponderance Reaching Necessity for Actual Occurrence
Concluding argument that preponderance must reach necessity for a possible to occur.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why mere preponderance is insufficient for the occurrence of a possible, and how necessity through choice reconciles with free will.

Core Idea
Occurrence requires necessity, not just preference; necessity through choice preserves free will.

This passage presents the concluding argument that for any contingent thing to come into existence, mere preponderance (al-awlawiyyah) is not enough; it must reach the level of necessity (al-wujub). The author explains that if only preponderance existed, both sides could be possible, leading to a preponderance without a preponderant, which is impossible. He also clarifies that this necessity does not negate free choice because it is a necessity that arises through the agent's choice as part of the complete cause.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem of equality
Consider why if both occurrence and non-occurrence were equally possible, a preponderance without a preponderant would result, which is impossible.
This is the core logical problem that drives the need for necessity.
2
Grasp the conclusion: preponderance must become necessity
The author states that mere preponderance is not sufficient; it must reach the point of necessity, meaning the opposite becomes impossible by another.
Necessity here is conditioned necessity (wujub bil-ghayr).
3
Consider the intellect's role in priority
Note that the 'prior existence' mentioned is not temporal but intellectual; the intellect judges that nothing exists unless it becomes necessary.
4
Address the misconception about free will
Understand how necessity through choice does not negate free will—choice is part of the complete cause, making the act necessary but chosen.
This reconciles determinism with human responsibility.

Essential Priority and the Necessity of Existence
Objection and answer on the necessity of the possible's priority to existence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection against essential priority of possibles is answered and why mere priority must reach the limit of necessity for actual existence.

Core Idea
Essential priority alone cannot cause existence; it must reach the limit of necessity.

This lesson presents a key objection in Islamic theology: if a possible being has an essential priority towards existence, wouldn't that make it necessary? The answer clarifies that essential priority is not sufficient and that external causes bring it to necessity, preserving both possibility and free choice.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the objection: if the possible has an essential priority towards existence or non-existence, it would be either necessary or impossible, negating its possibility.
This objection challenges the concept of essential priority.
2
Grasp the answer
The answer distinguishes between essential priority and actual necessity. The possible's essence may necessitate something short of necessity; actual existence depends on external causes that bring it to the limit of necessity.
The essential priority remains unchanged but does not force occurrence.
3
Examine the supporting argument
The text argues that without necessity, occurrence would involve preponderance without a preponderator, which is impossible. So the possible must have a cause that necessitates its existence at the time of its actuality.
This is the classical principle of 'tarjih bila murajjih'.
4
Note compatibility with choice
This necessity does not contradict free choice because it is 'by choice' as part of the complete cause. The necessity is not coercive but stems from the free agent's choice.
5
Confirm the conclusion
Mere essential priority is insufficient; it must reach the stage where the opposite becomes impossible by another cause. This avoids the problem of preponderance without a preponderator.
The priority is like an inclination that needs external completion.
