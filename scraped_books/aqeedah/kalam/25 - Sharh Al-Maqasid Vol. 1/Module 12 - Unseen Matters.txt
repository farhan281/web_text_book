The Jewish Neighbor's Question about Resurrection
Narrative of a Jewish man debating resurrection with polytheists.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how this historical story illustrates the theological debate about resurrection that prompted the development of systematic kalam.

Core Idea
Pre-Islamic polytheists denied resurrection; a Jewish believer affirmed it.

Sanusi uses this story from Ibn Hisham to show the kind of challenge that faced early Muslims. Before any systematic argument, there were direct encounters between monotheists and polytheists. This narrative sets the stage for why kalam needed to provide clear proofs for the afterlife.

Guided Path
1
Read the story carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice who is speaking, to whom, and the core belief being debated.
The story is from the perspective of a young boy, Salamah ibn Salamah.
2
Identify the characters and their beliefs
The Jewish neighbor affirms resurrection, judgment, heaven, and hell. The polytheists reject resurrection after death. Mark their positions.
This clash of worldviews is the heart of the lesson.
3
Ask: why does Sanusi include this story?
Consider that this story shows the real-world need for rational proof. The polytheists' question 'do you really think this will happen?' demands an answer. Kalam provides that answer.
This connects to the purpose of Kalam as a discipline.

That is what Ibn Hisham relates in the Prophetic biography from Salamah ibn Salamah, one of the people of Badr, who said: We had a Jewish neighbor in Banu Abd al-Ashhal. One day he came out of his house and stood at Banu Abd al-Ashhal. Salamah said: I was at that time the youngest among them, wearing a cloak of mine, lying down in the courtyard of my family. So he mentioned the Resurrection, the Raising, the Reckoning, the Balance, Paradise, and Hellfire. He said that to a people who were polytheists, idolaters, who did not believe that there would be a resurrection after death. They said to him: 'Woe to you, O so-and-so, do you see this happening? Commentary
This passage is a narrative from Ibn Hisham's Sira, provided as an example of religious debate in pre-Islamic Arabia. A Jewish man speaks to polytheists about the resurrection, judgment, and afterlife. The polytheists, who deny any resurrection, question him in astonishment, and the Jewish man affirms his belief. The story illustrates the clash of worldviews and the kind of theological discussions that occurred, showing why a systematic kalam was necessary to address such issues rationally.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
The Jewish man affirmed the resurrection even though his neighbors mocked him. Do you believe in the afterlife with that same certainty when you are surrounded by people who dismiss it as wishful thinking? What would you say if someone asked you today: 'Do you really think people will be raised from the dead?'
?
The polytheists were astonished that anyone could believe in a future judgment. Have you ever treated the realities of the Day of Judgment as mere stories, living as if they are distant and unreal? If you truly believed that your deeds will be weighed on a scale, what would you change about your actions today?
?
The Jewish man stood alone in his faith among idolaters. Are you willing to stand alone for the truth of the unseen—even if it costs you reputation, comfort, or relationships? What is one step you can take today to live in light of the resurrection?
