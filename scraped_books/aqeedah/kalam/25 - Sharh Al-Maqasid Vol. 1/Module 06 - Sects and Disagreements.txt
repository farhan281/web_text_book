The Hadith of the 73 Sects: Motivation for Kalam
Author cites the hadith of sects as a foundational factor for kalam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the hadith of the 73 sects provides a religious motivation for the study of 'ilm al-kalam.

Core Idea
The hadith of the 73 sects makes defining true belief an essential act of preservation.

This lesson examines a key prophetic hadith that directly influenced the development of Islamic theology. The Prophet's foretelling of sectarian division among his community gave urgency to the task of defining and defending correct belief. As you read the passage, consider how this hadith transforms theology from an academic exercise into a religious obligation.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith and its translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the directness of the Prophet's warning.
Pay attention to the phrase 'all of them in the Fire except one'.
2
Identify the core elements of the hadith
Find three key elements: the parallel with previous nations, the number of sects, and the description of the saved sect.
3
Understand the implication for kalam
Reflect on why this hadith is considered a factor for engaging in kalam. The commentary explains how defining orthodoxy becomes a defense against deviation.
This connects to the motivation for studying theology.

Commentary
This passage presents the well-known hadith about the division of the Muslim community into 73 sects. The Prophet warns that his nation will follow the same pattern as the Children of Israel, even to grave sins, and will split into 73 sects, with only one saved sect. When asked which, he replied: 'What I and my Companions are upon.' This hadith directly motivates the study of kalam, because the science of theology aims to define and defend correct belief (aqidah) against deviant views. The hadith thus provides a religious imperative for engaging with kalam to identify and preserve the true path.

Parallels of Predestination Debates in Early Kalam
Enumeration of parallel schools on free will and predestination across Greek, Jewish, and Christian traditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Jahmiyya and Mu'tazila positions on free will and predestination parallel earlier Greek, Jewish, and Christian schools.

Core Idea
The debate over free will and predestination in early Islam echoed ancient Greek, Jewish, and Christian divides.

This lesson explores how the theological positions of the Jahmiyya (complete compulsion) and Mu'tazila (free will) were not entirely new. The author shows that similar debates existed among Greek philosophers, Jewish sects, and Christian groups, suggesting external influences on early Islamic thought.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on parallels
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice how the author lists each parallel.
2
Identify the two Islamic positions
The Jahmiyya hold that man is completely compelled, while the Mu'tazila hold that man creates his actions by his own power.
3
Find the corresponding Greek schools
The Epicureans are associated with free will (Mu'tazila), and the Stoics with fatalism (Jahmiyya).
4
Observe the Jewish and Christian equivalents
The Rabbanites deny predestination (like Mu'tazila) and Qara'ites affirm compulsion (like Jahmiyya). Similarly, Eastern Christians say free will, others say compulsion.

Add to that also the seepage of Jewish and Christian ideas into some Muslim thinkers through dialogue and contact. The Ilzam of Bishr al-Marisi on 'Ja'ala'
Debate on the meaning of 'ja'ala' and its theological consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Abd al-Aziz refuted Bishr al-Marisi's claim that 'ja'ala' always means 'khalaqa' by demonstrating its blasphemous implications in other Quranic verses.

Core Idea
A word cannot mean 'create' in every context without leading to blasphemy.

This passage records a famous debate before Caliph al-Ma'mun between the Mu'tazili theologian Bishr al-Marisi and the traditionalist Abd al-Aziz. Bishr argued that the verse 'We have made it an Arabic Quran' proves the Quran is created because 'ja'ala' means 'created.' Abd al-Aziz then forces Bishr to admit this applies to all instances of 'ja'ala' in the Quran, and then shows that this leads to absurd and blasphemous interpretations of other verses. This lesson traces the rhetorical technique known as ilzam – cornering an opponent with the logical consequences of their own position.

Guided Path
1
Read the debate passage
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the initial positions: Bishr claims 'ja'ala' in the Quranic verse means 'created', and Abd al-Aziz challenges him.
Pay attention to the different voices: Bishr, Abd al-Aziz, and Caliph al-Ma'mun.
2
Identify the key move
Abd al-Aziz asks Bishr if 'ja'ala' always means 'khalaqa' in every instance. Bishr confirms it does. This is the turning point; now Abd al-Aziz can use Bishr's own principle against him.
This narrowing of the claim is crucial for the reductio argument.
3
Examine the reductio verses
Abd al-Aziz cites three verses where interpreting 'ja'ala' as 'khalaqa' would be blasphemous: making Allah a guarantor, making Allah an obstacle, and assigning daughters to Allah. For each, the meaning would imply humans creating Allah or attributing partners to Him.
These verses show that Bishr's principle leads to manifest kufr.
4
Understand the conclusion
Al-Ma'mun declares that Abd al-Aziz's argument has defeated Bishr, who has effectively confessed to blasphemy by his own logic. The lesson ends with al-Ma'mun asking for further explanation of the different meanings of 'ja'ala'.
This victory illustrates the power of ilzam in theological debate.

Commentary
This passage presents a theological debate between Bishr al-Marisi and Abd al-Aziz in the presence of Caliph al-Ma'mun on the createdness of the Quran. The argument centers on the Arabic verb 'ja'ala' (to make). Bishr argues that in the verse 'إنا جعلناه قرآنا عربياً' (We have made it an Arabic Quran), 'ja'ala' simply means 'khalaqa' (created), thus proving the Quran is created. Abd al-Aziz refutes this by first appealing to the linguistic authority of the Arabs, noting that Bishr is a non-Arab misunderstanding Arabic. He then forces Bishr to admit that he believes 'ja'ala' always means 'khalaqa' in every context. Abd al-Aziz then cites several Quranic verses where such a meaning would be blasphemous: in 16:91 ('And have made Allah a guarantor over you'), if 'ja'ala' means 'created', it would imply humans create Allah; similarly in 2:224 ('Do not make Allah an obstacle'), it would mean 'do not create Allah'; and in 16:57 ('They assign daughters to Allah'), it would mean 'they create daughters for Allah'. Each of these leads to manifest disbelief (kufr) and renders the speaker's blood permissible. Al-Ma'mun accepts Abd al-Aziz's argument, declares Bishr's position defeated, and asks for further clarification. The passage illustrates the classical Sunni argument against the Mu'tazili/Jahmi position on the created Quran, using the method of reductio ad absurdum (ilzam) based on linguistic consistency.

Al-Taftazānī presents the Mu'tazilī denial of non-existence as something established.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Nonexistent essences are bare; all attributes arise only with existence.

This lesson presents a specific Mu'tazilite view on the nature of nonexistent essences. Abu Ishaq ibn 'Ayyash argues that in nonexistence, essences possess no attributes whatsoever. His reasoning hinges on the equality of all essences—if they are identical, there is no basis for any one to have an attribute over another. This position challenges the idea that nonexistent things can have any positive determinations.

Commentary
This passage presents the opposing view of Abu Ishaq ibn ‘Ayyash (a prominent Mu‘tazilite scholar) regarding the state of nonexistent essences. He argues that in nonexistence, these essences are completely devoid of all attributes. His reasoning, though cut off, begins: because since the essences are equal in essence (i.e., they share the same essential nature), the specification of some of them with an attribute (without a cause) would be arbitrary. The full argument would likely continue that such specification cannot occur without a specifying cause, and since there is no cause in nonexistence, no attribute can be attached. This view contrasts with the majority and with the very idea that nonexistent things can have any positive attributes.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That Abu Ishaq denies any reality to nonexistent things.
Correction: He only denies attributes in nonexistence, not the reality of the essence itself.

Key Takeaway
Abu Ishaq ibn 'Ayyash held that nonexistent essences are entirely devoid of attributes because all essences are equal in nonexistence, making any attribute arbitrary without a cause.
