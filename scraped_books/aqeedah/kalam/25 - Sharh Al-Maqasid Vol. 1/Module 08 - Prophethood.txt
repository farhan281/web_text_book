Learning Objective

Core Idea
Early Muslims believed through direct encounter, not theological proofs.

Ghazali has argued that kalam is not obligatory. He now provides historical evidence from the time of the Prophet. Through two anecdotes and a rhetorical question, he demonstrates that the earliest Muslims accepted Islam through simple observation and trust, not through complex arguments. This passage forms the empirical backbone of his critique of kalam.

Commentary
This passage provides the historical proof for Ghazali's earlier claim. It first states that Ghazali is not content with mere assertion but offers evidence from early Islam. He recounts two anecdotes: a Bedouin who recognized the Prophet's truthfulness by his face, and another who accepted Islam after the Prophet swore by Allah. Neither performed any kalam arguments; faith arose from direct perception and trust. Ghazali then rhetorically asks if anyone ever transmitted that the Prophet or Companions used the standard kalam proof for the world's origination (the argument from accidents). He answers that this never happened. He concludes that kalam was neither commanded by the Prophet nor practiced by the Companions. He supports this with a quote from Imam al-Shafi'i, who said that being afflicted with any sin except shirk is better than studying kalam. The passage powerfully argues that the method of kalam is an innovation not rooted in early Islamic practice.

Necessity of a Prophet for Salvation
Author distinguishes knowledge from salvation and proves prophetic guidance is essential.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that rational knowledge of God is insufficient for salvation; true salvation requires prophetic guidance and obedience to the Prophet's command.

Core Idea
Knowledge without prophecy does not save.

In this passage, the author moves from discussing the need for a teacher in acquiring knowledge to the more profound need for a teacher in attaining salvation. He clarifies that even if one arrives at a correct rational understanding of God's existence and oneness, that knowledge alone does not guarantee salvation. It must be accompanied by submission to a divinely sent prophet. This lesson explores why many who acknowledged the Creator were still disbelievers and why the Prophet Muhammad (صلى الله عليه وسلم) remains the ultimate guide for all humanity.

Mu'tazila Proof for Rational Obligation of Nazar and the Answer of Shared Implausibility
Mu'tazila proof for rational obligation of nazar via ifham al-anbiya and the answer of mushtarak al-ilzam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila argument that nazar must be rationally obligatory to avoid silencing the prophets, and the Ash'ari response of shared implausibility (mushtarak al-ilzam) that turns the argument back against them.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila's own argument boomerangs back on them.

The Mu'tazila argued that if nazar (speculative reasoning) were only legally obligatory, it would create a vicious circle that silences the prophets. Their proof seems compelling, but the Ash'ari response — mushtarak al-ilzam or 'shared implausibility' — turns the tables. In this lesson, we'll follow the logical steps and see how the same dilemma applies to the Mu'tazila's own position.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazila argument carefully
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the scenario they construct: the prophet demands nazar, the unbeliever refuses based on lack of obligation.
Pay attention to the vicious circle they describe.
2
Identify the logical structure
Break down the argument: (1) If obligation is only shar'i, then before Sharia there is no obligation. (2) But Sharia is established through nazar. (3) Therefore the prophet cannot compel nazar, leading to ifham.
This is the core of the Mu'tazila proof.
3
Consider the objection and clarification
The text raises an objection: the unbeliever's claim that he need not look unless it is obligatory is false, because looking does not depend on its obligation. The author agrees but notes that the prophet still cannot compel him — that's the essence of ifham.
The objection does not fully refute the Mu'tazila.
4
Understand the answer of shared implausibility
The answer (mushtarak al-ilzam) argues that the Mu'tazila's own reasoning implies the same problem for their claim of rational obligation — the obligation of nazar itself is a theoretical matter requiring nazar, causing a similar circle.
The answer is only briefly introduced here; it continues in later pages.

Commentary
This passage presents the Mu'tazila proof that the obligation of nazar (speculative reasoning) must be rational (aqli) rather than purely legal (shar'i). They argue that if it were only legally obligatory, it would lead to ifham al-anbiya (silencing the prophets), i.e., the prophets would be unable to compel the unbeliever to investigation, rendering prophethood useless. The logic: when a prophet demands that a person look at his miracle to verify his claim, the person can respond: 'I will not look unless it is obligatory upon me. Abandoning what is not obligatory is permissible. But the obligation cannot be established until the Sharia is established, and the Sharia cannot be established until I look (since its proof is theoretical, not self-evident).' Thus a vicious circle arises, preventing the prophet from obligating the look. A potential objection is raised: the person's claim 'I will not look unless it is obligatory' is false because looking does not depend on its obligation. The author concedes this but notes that the prophet still cannot compel him, because compulsion only applies to what is obligatory. This is the silencing. The answer provided first (أجيب أولاً) is that this argument is mushtarak al-ilzam: an argument that equally applies to both sides, i.e., it forces the opponent to acknowledge a contradiction in his own position. The explanation: the Mu'tazila's own reasoning implies that the same problem applies to their rational obligation, since the obligation of nazar itself is a theoretical matter. The text cuts off during the elaboration of this answer.
