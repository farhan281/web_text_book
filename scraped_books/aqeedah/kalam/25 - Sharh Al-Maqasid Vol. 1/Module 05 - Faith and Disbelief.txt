The Hadith on Inner Whispers and the Need for Kalam
Author cites a hadith as evidence for kalam's necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a specific hadith about inner whispers (waswasa) serves as a foundational argument for the necessity of 'ilm al-kalam.

Core Idea
Prophetic guidance on theological doubts confirms the need for a science like kalam.

This lesson presents a hadith reported by Imam al-Baghawi to illustrate how the Prophet's teachings addressed inner doubts. The Companions were troubled by disturbing thoughts, and the Prophet’s response not only reassured them but also laid the groundwork for a systematic approach to theological questions. Understanding this hadith helps us see why kalam emerged as a necessary discipline.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith and translation
Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation carefully. Notice the question the Companions asked and the Prophet's twofold response.
The hadith is from authentic collections; its chain goes through Abu Hurayra.
2
Understand the context
The Companions were ashamed of their thoughts. The Prophet called such questioning 'clear faith'—this reframes doubt as a sign of faith, not its absence.
This is the key insight that links the hadith to kalam.
3
Identify the practical guidance
The Prophet gives two instructions: when Satan whispers about creation, seek refuge and stop; when persistent questions arise about God's origin, affirm faith in God and His Messenger.
These responses show that theological inquiries are expected and have proper answers.
4
Connect to kalam's purpose
Reflect: Why does this hadith support the need for kalam? Because it shows that addressing such doubts is part of faith, and a disciplined science is needed to handle them properly.
This is the author's main argument in this passage.

Commentary
This passage presents a hadith reported by Imam al-Baghawi to illustrate how prophetic teachings contributed to the development of kalam. The Companions were troubled by disturbing thoughts, and the Prophet responded by calling that questioning 'clear faith' (صريح الإيمان). He then gave practical guidance: when Satan whispers about the creation of God, one should seek refuge and stop; when people persistently question about God's origin, one should affirm faith. This hadith shows that dealing with theological doubts is a religious necessity, thus supporting the existence of a science like kalam to address such issues.

Ghazali's Critique of Mutakallimun: Faith as Divine Light
Ghazali's critique of mutakallimun for takfir and definition of faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ghazali's critique of mutakallimun for declaring lay Muslims disbelievers and his alternative view that faith is a divine light from Allah, not dependent on kalam.

Core Idea
Faith is a divine light, not a monopoly of the theologians.

Al-Ghazali, though engaging with philosophy and theology, ultimately rejected the elitism of the mutakallimun. In this passage from Faysal al-Tafriqa, he defends ordinary Muslims by arguing that faith is not dependent on rational proofs but is a direct gift from God. Let's explore his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand Ghazali's position
Read the passage to grasp Ghazali's overall critique. He is not rejecting all theology but the extreme stance of some theologians who declare laypeople disbelievers.
Notice the strong language: 'most excessive and extravagant'.
2
Identify the two errors
Find the two specific errors Ghazali accuses the mutakallimun of: first, narrowing God's mercy; second, ignoring the historical reality of the Prophet and Companions' era.
The first error is about mercy, the second about historical evidence.
3
Grasp the alternative definition of faith
Ghazali concludes that faith is not kalam but a light from Allah. Understand this as a direct spiritual gift, not an intellectual acquisition.
This challenges the notion that knowledge of proofs is necessary for belief.

What concerns us is the opinion of al-Ghazali. That he is a writer who traveled in every field and attacked in every position, and debated the philosophers and exposed their falsehood, and attacked the Batinites and refuted their falsehood, and approached the theologians (mutakallimun), but he rejected their methodology. Al-Ghazali, may Allah have mercy on him, says in his book Faysal al-Tafriqa between Islam and Heresy: 'Some of the most excessive and extravagant people are a group of the mutakallimun who declared the common Muslims to be disbelievers, and claimed that whoever does not know kalam as they know it, and does not know the religious beliefs according to their formulated proofs, is a disbeliever. These people first narrowed the vast mercy of Allah over His servants, and made Paradise a monopoly for a small band of mutakallimun, and then they were ignorant of what is transmitted through the sunnah from them, secondly, when it appeared to them in the era of the Messenger of Allah and the era of the Companions, may Allah be pleased with them, their ruling regarding the Islam of groups of coarse Arabs who were occupied with worshipping idols and did not engage in the science of proof, and had they engaged in it they would not have understood it. And whoever thinks that the means of faith is [only] kalam and formulated proofs and ordered divisions has narrowed the boundary of faith. Rather, faith is a light that Allah casts into the hearts of His servants.'

Commentary
This passage presents Al-Ghazali's critical stance towards the mutakallimun (theologians). It begins by describing Ghazali's background: he was a prolific writer who engaged with philosophers and Batinites, and while he approached the mutakallimun, he ultimately rejected their method. Then Ghazali quotes his own book, Faysal al-Tafriqa, to attack the mutakallimun for two errors: first, they declare ordinary Muslims disbelievers for not knowing kalam, thus narrowing God's mercy; second, they ignore the historical reality that during the Prophet's time and the Companions' era, many coarse Arabs who worshipped idols embraced Islam without studying proofs. Ghazali emphasizes that faith is not dependent on kalam—it is a light from God directly cast into the heart. The passage defends the inclusivity of faith and criticizes the elitism of the mutakallimun.
