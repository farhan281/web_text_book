Learning Objective

Core Idea
Jadl is not just arguing—it is a disciplined craft of compelling opponents using accepted premises.

This passage begins by distinguishing jadl from kalam, then explores its linguistic roots and formal definitions from key authorities. Understanding jadl helps us grasp a major method of theological debate.

The science of jadl (dialectics): its origin and development. We said at the beginning of our discussion about the science of kalam: if this is a science by which the articles of faith are defended, and its defensive weapons are reason and its tools, then what is the other science called? The science used by the opponents of Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah from the Khawarij, the Batinites, the Jahmiyyah, and the rest of the misguidedsects that deviate from the school of the Salaf. And when? Yes. What is this science called? We see, and many scholars and thinkers share our opinion, that it is the science of jadl. If that is so, then what is its origin, and what stages did it pass through until it reached us? Ibn Sidah says: 'jadala al-shay'a yajudluhu jadlan: he twisted it tightly. And al-jadl means to throw down onto al-jadalah, which is the ground, so named for its hardness (1).' The author of Lisan al-'Arab says: 'al-jadl linguistically is obstinacy in disputation and ability for it. One says jadalahu mujadalah and jidal, and a man is jadil and mijdal: severe in jadl. It is said: jadaltu al-rajula fajadaltuhu jadlan, i.e., I overcame him. And a man is jadil if he is strongest in quarrel. And the noun is al-jadal (2), which is intensity of disputation.' And in the hadith from the Prophet (peace be upon him): 'I am the seal of the prophets in the Mother of the Book, and Adam was munjadil in his clay' meaning thrown on al-jadalah, which is the ground (1). Jadl conventionally: The reality of jadl is negotiation by way of contention and overpowering to compel the opponent. Al-Jurjani said: 'jadl is an expression for an intention related to manifesting doctrines and confirming them (2).' Abu al-Baqa' said in the book al-Kulliyyat: 'jadl is an expression for repelling one's opponent from the corruption of his statement by a proof or a doubt, and it is only through contention with someone else (3).' Ibn Sina says in his definition of jadl: 'Our purpose now in this art is to acquire a craft by which we can bring constraint upon every matter that is posed as a sought conclusion from accepted premises, and that when we answer it, something may be taken from us that contradicts our position (4).' It is good to place alongside this definition of Ibn Sina the definition of Aristotle to show the difference between them. Aristotle says in the introduction to the Topica: 'Our purpose in this book is to find a way by which we may construct a syllogism from accepted premises on every problem that is proposed and that when we give an answer we may not say anything contradictory (5).' Commentary
This passage defines and discusses the science of jadl (dialectics). It begins by distinguishing kalam – which defends Islamic beliefs using reason – from another science used by opponents of Ahl al-Sunnah, such as the Khawarij, Batinites, and Jahmiyyah. The author identifies this science as jadl. Then he investigates its origin and development. First, he gives the linguistic meaning: from Ibn Sidah, jadl means to twist tightly or to throw down onto the ground (al-jadalah). The Lisan al-'Arab defines it as obstinacy and strength in disputation, with the verb jadala meaning to overcome someone in argument. A hadith uses the derived form munjadil to mean 'thrown on the ground'. Next, the author turns to the technical (istilahi) definition. He cites al-Jurjani: jadl is an intention to manifest and confirm doctrines. Abu al-Baqa' defines it as repelling an opponent from a corrupt statement using a proof or doubt, which requires contention. Ibn Sina defines jadl as a craft that allows one to compel an opponent using accepted premises (mushayyadi'ah) and to avoid contradicting one's own position when answering. Aristotle's definition from the Topica is provided for comparison: it is a way to construct syllogisms from accepted premises for any problem and to avoid self-contradiction when answering. The passage ends mid-sentence stating that the definition consists of three elements (but the text cuts off at page end). The author is building up to a full conceptual understanding of jadl as a formal discipline of argumentation.

Jadal in the Quran is not always negative; it can be commanded when done kindly.

Definition of al-Millah and Its Distinction from Din
The author defines millah and explains its distinction from din.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the specific meaning of al-millah in Islamic theology and how it differs from the broader term al-din.

Core Idea
Al-millah is tied to a prophet; al-din can be attributed to God, individuals, and specific practices.

This passage clarifies a key distinction between two terms often used interchangeably: millah and din. While both refer to divine legislation, millah has a more restricted usage, being specifically linked to the prophet who brought it. Understanding this distinction helps in interpreting classical theological texts correctly.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice the definition of millah and its similarity to din.
2
Identify the difference in attribution
The passage states that millah is only attributed to the prophet it is traced to, e.g., 'millah of Ibrahim', not to God or individuals.
Contrast with din, which can be attributed to God, oneself, and others.
3
Note the scope of usage
Millah is used only for the whole set of laws, not for individual acts like prayer, whereas din can be used for both.
Example: one says 'din of God' for prayer but not 'millah of God'.
4

Review the given examples: 'millah of Ibrahim' is valid, but 'millah of God' and 'my millah' are not. This reinforces the restriction.

Commentary
This passage is a lexical and technical definition of the term 'al-millah'. The author states that millah is like din (religion) and is defined as what God legislated for His servants through the tongues of the messengers to reach nearness to God. Then he highlights the difference between millah and din: millah is only attributed to the prophet whose law it is, e.g., 'millah of Ibrahim', and is not attributed to God directly nor to individuals of the community. It is only used for the entire body of laws, not for individual acts like prayer. In contrast, din can be attributed to God, to a person, and to individual practices, e.g., 'din of God', 'my din', 'din of Zayd', and 'din of God' for prayer. The key distinction is that millah has a more restricted usage tied to the prophetic source.

In this passage, the author distinguishes between two types of propositions that form the basis of certain knowledge. Recurrent Reports: Definition and Hidden Syllogism
Author defines recurrent reports and their hidden syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what recurrent reports are and how the intellect uses them to arrive at certainty through an impossibility of collusion.

Core Idea
Recurrent reports yield certainty through the impossibility of collusion on falsehood.

Sanusi introduces recurrent reports as propositions that the intellect accepts based on overwhelming testimony from multiple reporters about an observable event. He explains the hidden syllogism that operates when a multitude reports something, making falsehood impossible. Let us read his definition and uncover its logical structure.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then its translation. Notice the key terms: متواترات (recurrent reports) and قياس خفي (hidden syllogism).
The passage is concise; each phrase carries weight.
2
Identify the conditions
Find the conditions that make a report mutawātir: multitude, observable basis, impossibility of collusion on falsehood.
The multitude must be such that collusion is impossible.
3
Understand the hidden syllogism
The hidden syllogism is: 'If this judgment were not true, this group would not have reported it.' Reflect on why this reasoning is implicit.
This syllogism joins sensory hearing to rational necessity.

Refuting the Necessity of a Middle Between Existence and Non-Existence
Refutation of the necessity of a middle between existence and non-existence with response to infinite regress objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how scholars refute the claim that there must be a middle state between existence and non-existence by analyzing the nature of attributedness and mental judgments.

Core Idea
Attributedness is a relation, not a thing that exists or does not exist.

This passage addresses a key objection in Islamic theology: the claim that positive and negative attributes must lead to an infinite regress or require a middle state between existence and non-existence. The author explains that attributedness is a relational concept that subsists in the mind, not externally, thus avoiding the regress. He also denies any conceivable middle between being and non-being.

Guided Path
1

Read the opening lines where the objector argues that a positive attribute like attributedness leads to a chain of judgments. Notice how they derive an infinite regress.
The objector claims that if the body is described by attributedness, then that attributedness itself must be described, leading to an infinite chain.
2
Understand the response about negation
The author answers that the form of negation (e.g., non-attributedness) is not necessarily non-existential; it is a relational concept.
The term 'form of negation' refers to concepts like 'non-attributedness,' which are not absolute non-existences but mental relations.

Grasp the nature of mental judgments
Learn why mental judgments do not require correspondence to external reality; they are relations that occur in the intellect when it considers external objects.
This shows that attributedness can be a mental relation without implying an external existential attribute.

The author concludes that no middle between existence and non-existence is conceivable, and that frequent proponents do not constitute evidence in intellectual matters.
The passage references al-Mawāqif to argue that sensory consensus is not valid for rational concepts.

Sanusi refutes the objection that the argument from variation is conclusive.

Conditions for Sound Nazar and Definition of Jiha al-Dalala
Sanusi states conditions for sound nazar and defines jiha al-dalala.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the additional conditions required for a consideration to be sound, including the need to focus on genuine evidence from its proper aspect of indication, and how the mind moves from evidence to conclusion.

Core Idea
Sound nazar must engage the evidence at its essential indicative aspect.

Having established the general conditions for any nazar, the author now turns to what makes nazar sound. This passage introduces two key restrictions: the object of nazar must be a genuine evidence, not a fallacy, and the consideration must be from the correct aspect of its indication. The author then defines the crucial concept of 'aspect of indication' (jiha al-dalala) with a classic example.

The word 'شبهة' refers to a specious argument that resembles evidence but is flawed.

Understand the definition of jiha al-dalala
The aspect of indication is the means by which the mind transfers from the evidence to the indicated. It is the specific attribute that connects them.

Study the cosmological example

This distinction prevents confusion between the different elements of an inference.

Wajh al-dalālah is the noticing of the conclusion in the premise, distinct from the conclusion itself.

Al-Taftazani opens by quoting al-Ghazali's definition of wajh al-dalālah and the confusion it caused. The key question is whether this aspect is the same as the indicated or something else. This lesson establishes the definition and the correct view that they are distinct.

There is no concealment of the distinction of the first two, and likewise of the distinction of the third from them, because it is knowledge of a relation between the proof and the indicated that is distinct from them. This statement might give the illusion of disagreement regarding the distinction of the knowledge of the indication of the proof from the knowledge of the indicated, since an explanation was needed and he made the knowledge that it is the indication an example by the possibility of the world, along with a face for the knowledge of the essence of the proof, suggesting the saying that the aspect of indication is the proof itself. In Naqd al-Muhassal, there is something that indicates disagreement concerning the necessity of its being distinct from the proof and the indicated, because he said: This issue only occurs among the theologians when they use the existence of other than God to prove His existence, and they say: It is not permissible that the aspect of the indication of the existence of other than God upon His existence be distinct from them both, because what is distinct from His existence is included in the existence of what is other than Him, and what is distinct from the existence of what is other than Him is only His existence. The answer is that the knowledge of the aspect of indication of the proof upon the indicated, which is distinct from them both, is a rational mental consideration (amr i‘tibārī ‘aqlī), not existing externally, as will come in the investigation of correlation (taḍāyuf). This is his statement. And you are aware that the relational mental consideration (al-amr al-i‘tibārī al-iḍāfī) is the indication of the proof upon the indicated, not the aspect of indication which is a quality in the proof, like possibility and temporal origination in the world. Then the apparent meaning of his statement is that the thing judged is a mental consideration, namely the knowledge of the aspect of indication, and its corruption is evident.

Commentary
This passage is an investigation into whether the aspect of indication (wajh al-dalāla) in a syllogism is identical to or distinct from the indicated (madlūl). Al-Taftazani begins by quoting al-Ghazali: The aspect of indication is the noticing of the conclusion potentially in the premise. This is obscure to some, leading to confusion. The truth is that the sought conclusion is distinct from this noticing. The main disagreement is about the distinction of the aspect of indication from the indicated, and consequently about whether the knowledge of each is distinct. Al-Taftazani sides with the majority that they are distinct. He then notes a discrepancy: al-Mawāqif mentions a different framing of the disagreement, but it is not found in famous books. He then explains al-Razi's position that there are three knowledges: knowledge of the essence of the proof, knowledge of the essence of the indicated, and knowledge of the proof's being a proof. The third is relational and distinct. However, al-Razi's wording may suggest that the aspect of indication is the proof itself. Then an objection is raised from Naqd al-Muhassal: when proving God's existence from contingent things, some argue that the aspect of indication cannot be distinct because what is distinct from God is part of contingent existence, etc. The answer is that the knowledge of the aspect of indication is a mental consideration, not an external existent. But al-Taftazani criticizes this answer, saying that the mental consideration is the indication itself, not the aspect of indication which is a quality in the proof. He concludes that the answer is corrupt.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think that the aspect of indication (wajh al-dalālah) is identical to the indicated (madlūl) because both relate to the conclusion.
Correction: The passage clarifies that the wajh is the noticing or recognition, which is different from the conclusion itself. They are distinct.
