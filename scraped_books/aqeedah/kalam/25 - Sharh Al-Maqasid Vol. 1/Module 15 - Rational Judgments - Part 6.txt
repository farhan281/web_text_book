Future Possibility and the Condition of Present Non-Existence
Discussion of future possibility and refutation of the condition of present non-existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of future possibility in kalam and why stipulating present non-existence as a condition leads to a logical contradiction that is resolved by distinguishing between possibility of existence and possibility of non-existence.

Core Idea
Future possibility is about the unknown future, not a requirement of present non-existence.

This passage from al-Maqasid tackles a subtle point in the logic of possibility. The author explains that possibility concerning the future is defined without reference to the present or past, because possibility is the opposite of necessity. Some scholars, however, insisted that for a thing to be possible in the future, it must not exist now. This leads to a puzzle: if we require present non-existence for possibility of existence, then for possibility of non-existence we would require present existence, leading to contradiction. The author resolves this by clarifying that the condition is only meant for the possibility of the occurrence of existence, not for the possibility of the occurrence of non-existence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of future possibility
Read the first part of the passage where the author defines future possibility as the permissibility of a thing's existence in the future without regard to the past and present, because possibility is opposite to necessity.
Notice that the author emphasizes the future as the realm of uncertainty.
2
Grasp the objection to the condition of present non-existence
Some stipulate present non-existence for future possibility. The author objects: if existence is necessary in the present, then non-existence is also necessary, leading to a requirement of both, which is impossible.
This objection shows the logical flaw in the condition.
3
See the resolution
The author resolves the objection by distinguishing: the stipulator intends the possibility of the occurrence of existence, not the possibility of the occurrence of non-existence. For the latter, present existence would be required, but that is not the intended meaning.
Thus, the condition does not lead to contradiction when properly understood.

He said: And it may be considered with respect to the future. Commentary
This passage discusses the notion of future possibility (al-imkan al-istiqbali). The author first presents a definition: considering possibility with respect to the future, and notes that some scholars stipulate present non-existence as a condition for future possibility. The author explains that this condition is based on the idea that possibility of existence in the future requires that the thing not exist now, but this does not lead to a contradiction because possibility concerns the future. The rationale is that possibility is the opposite of necessity; the future is unknown, so it is more deserving of the term 'possible'. Then the author presents an objection: if one stipulates present non-existence because existence is necessary (i.e., if it existed now, it would be necessary), then one must also stipulate present existence for the possibility of non-existence, because non-existence is also necessary. This would lead to requiring both, which is impossible. The author resolves this by noting that the one who stipulates present non-existence actually means the possibility of the occurrence of existence, not the possibility of the occurrence of non-existence. If one considers the possibility of the occurrence of non-existence, then present existence is required, but that is not the intended sense. So the objection is answered.

The Definition of Imkan and Its Distinction from Wujub and Imtina'
Al-Taftazani defines imkan as a rational judgment distinct from wujub bi al-ghayr and imtina' bi al-ghayr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how imkan is defined as a rational judgment about essence considered in itself, distinct from necessity-by-other and impossibility-by-other.

Core Idea
Imkan is a conceptual possibility, not a real third state between existence and non-existence.

Al-Taftazani clarifies a subtle but crucial point in kalam: possibility is not a real property that can exist independently. Instead, it is a rational consideration that arises when we think about an essence without regard to its existence or non-existence. This lesson unpacks that definition and shows how imkan relates to necessity and impossibility.

Guided Path
1
Understand the three rational judgments
Read the passage and identify how the same essence can be considered necessary, impossible, or possible depending on what is assumed.
Notice that the essence itself doesn't change; only our consideration changes.
2
Grasp the concept of 'by-other'
Necessity and impossibility here are not intrinsic but acquired from external factors (existence or its cause).
This distinguishes 'bi al-ghayr' from 'dhati' judgments.
3
Distinguish conceptual from actual
The passage emphasizes that possibility is separate only in thought, not in reality—every possible thing is either existent or non-existent.
This guards against the misconception of imkan as a real third state.

Commentary
This passage defines the concept of possibility (imkan) as a rational judgment distinct from necessity and impossibility, highlighting that it is considered relative to existence and non-existence. The author explains that when an essence is considered with its existence or the existence of its cause, it becomes necessary-by-other (wajib bi al-ghayr). When considered with its non-existence or the non-existence of its cause, it becomes impossible-by-other (mumtani' bi al-ghayr). The key point is that possibility is conceptually separable from necessity and impossibility, but in actuality every possible thing is either existent (thus necessary-by-other) or non-existent (thus impossible-by-other), except for those who posit an intermediate state.

Defining Al-Hal: The Intermediary Attribute
Al-Taftazani defines al-hal with logical and semantic clarifications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of al-hal (the state) as an attribute of an existent that is neither existent nor non-existent, and the logical relations that necessitate this category.

Core Idea
Al-hal is a third rational category between existence and non-existence for certain attributes.

Al-Taftazani begins by clarifying the logical relationship between two types of non-essential attributes (wujub bi al-ghayr and imtina' bi al-ghayr). He then introduces al-hal as an intermediary concept, defined as an attribute of an existent that is itself neither existent nor non-existent. This definition is carefully qualified to avoid confusion with existential or negative attributes.

Guided Path
1
Understand the logical preamble
Read the opening lines about the two non-essential attributes sharing the name of necessity when their objects are opposite, and how they relate to one another. This sets the stage for the concept of al-hal.
The 'two non-essential' refer to wajib bi al-ghayr and mumtani' bi al-ghayr.
2
Identify the definition of al-hal
Find the explicit definition: an attribute of an existent that is neither existent nor non-existent. Note the examples given: 'being-knowing' and 'being-powerful'.
3
Examine the three qualifications
The definition includes three cautionary phrases: 'of an existent' excludes attributes of non-existents, 'not existent' excludes existential attributes, and 'not non-existent' excludes negative attributes. Understand each.
These qualifications prevent common misunderstandings.

Commentary
This passage discusses the concept of al-hal (the state) as an intermediate category between existent and non-existent, particularly those attributes that are neither actually existent nor non-existent in themselves. The author begins by explaining the logical relationship between the two non-essential attributes (wujub bi al-ghayr and imtina' bi al-ghayr): they share the name of necessity when their objects are opposite, and they are mutually exclusive when the object is the same. Then he introduces the argument that the non-essential attribute is the state (al-hal), defined as an attribute of an existent that is neither existent nor non-existent, like being knowing or powerful. The author clarifies the definition, cautioning that the state is an attribute, not an essence, and that it qualifies an existent but is itself neither existent nor non-existent. He then presents al-Katibi's objection that this definition fails according to the Mu'tazila because they consider substantiality (jawhariyya) as a state even though it pertains to an essence in both existence and non-existence. The author responds by noting that the objection relies on a specific Mu'tazili view attributed to Abu Hashim, but not all Mu'tazila hold that view; some do not affirm states at all, and others affirm them differently. The passage then traces the origins of the concept of al-hal to Abu Hashim, explaining his distinction between accidents that are conditional on life and those that are not. It further contrasts the views of other scholars like al-Qadi and Imam al-Haramayn regarding the extent of states. Finally, the passage ends with a discussion of the Mu'tazila's stance on the non-existent (al-ma'dum), noting that most of them reject an intermediate between existence and non-existence, and they hold that everything is either existent or non-existent based on whether it has the existence of entities (kawn al-a'yan) or not.

Core Idea
Al-Katibi's objection fails because Mu'tazila disagree among themselves about the existence and definition of states.

He then traces the origin of the concept to Abu Hashim and presents various later formulations.

Sanusi presents a logical proof and its refutation concerning the categories of non-existent and negated.

Core Idea

The Distinction Argument for the Non-Existent and Its Refutation
Al-Taftazani presents and refutes the distinction-based argument for the external fixity of the possible non-existent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that the possible non-existent is externally fixed because it is distinct, and al-Taftazani's refutation of that argument.

Core Idea
Mental distinction does not imply external existence.

Al-Taftazani presents an opponent's argument: the possible non-existent must be externally fixed because it can be distinguished from other things. He then refutes it by showing that distinction is a mental operation and only requires mental fixity. This lesson unpacks the logic of the argument and its rebuttal.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation, to get the flow of the argument.

2
Identify the argument's structure
The opponent's argument has a minor premise (the non-existent is distinct) and a major premise (distinct things are fixed). Identify each part.
The minor premise is supported by examples: known, willed, powerful over.
3
Understand the pointing principle
The major premise relies on the idea that distinction requires mental pointing, and pointing requires the pointed-to to exist. Grasp this link.
The opponent claims pointing to pure non-existence is impossible.
4
Study the refutation
Al-Taftazani's answer distinguishes between external and mental distinction. If by 'distinct' you mean external, it's denied; if mental, it only proves mental fixity.
This refutation shows the argument equivocates on 'distinction'.
5
Reflect on the significance
Consider why this refutation is important: it blocks a key 'proof' for the external existence of non-existents, reinforcing that mental existence suffices.

Possibility as Mental Consideration
Al-Taftazani refutes the claim that possibility is an external attribute requiring external fixity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Taftazani argues that possibility is a mental consideration, not an external attribute, and how this refutes the claim that the possible non-existent must be externally fixed.

Core Idea
Possibility is a mental tool, not a real property.

This passage presents the second argument of a group claiming that the possible non-existent must be externally fixed because possibility is a positive attribute. Al-Taftazani refutes this by clarifying that possibility is merely a mental consideration. He then points out the absurd consequence that impossibilities would also have to be externally fixed if possibility were an external attribute.

Guided Path

2

If possibility required external fixity, the same logic would force impossibilities like the partner of God or the conjunction of contradictories to be externally fixed, which is absurd. This is a reductio ad absurdum.

Objection and Response on Non-Existent Blackness
Author responds to objections about blackness remaining blackness when non-existent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author defends the view that non-existent things do not retain their properties by distinguishing between negation and alteration and by clarifying the role of mental abstraction.

Core Idea
Non-existence does not strip an essence of its identity; it simply means the property is absent.

This passage presents a debate about whether a non-existent thing can still be called by its essence. The opponent argues that blackness must always be blackness, even when non-existent. The author responds with two key distinctions: between pure negation and alteration, and between disregarding something and negating its existence. By mastering these distinctions, you will see how logic protects theology from false conclusions.

Guided Path
1
Read the full passage
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. Notice the two exchanges: first an objection and answer, then a second objection and answer.
The passage uses technical terms 'salb' and 'ʿudūl'.
2
Identify the first objection and response
The opponent says if blackness is not blackness, that's impossible. The author splits into two meanings. Learn what 'salb' and 'ʿudūl' mean in this context.
Negation (salb) simply denies the property; alteration (ʿudūl) attributes the opposite.
3
Analyze the second objection and response
The opponent argues that every thing has an essence independent of other things. The author replies that disregarding something does not negate its existence.
Consider how 'qaṭʿ al-naẓar' (disregarding) is different from actual non-existence.
4
Connect to larger theological issues
Ask yourself why this matters for theology. How does the author's distinction preserve the principle that non-existent attributes do not cause impossibility?
This reasoning is used in arguments about divine attributes and creation.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer exchange regarding the status of non-existent blackness. The opponent first objects that if blackness loses its blackness, it would be impossible for it to be blackness. The author responds by clarifying two possible meanings of 'blackness is not blackness': either it is pure negation (salb), in which case no impossibility arises because the property is simply absent; or it is alteration (ʿudūl), meaning the essence of blackness is considered as having the attribute of non-blackness, which also does not lead to a logical impossibility. The opponent then raises a second objection: everything has an essence independent of other things, so blackness must be blackness even when non-existent. The author counters that disregarding (qaṭʿ al-naẓar) something does not negate its existence—i.e., mental abstraction does not eliminate external reality. The author thus defends the view that non-existent things do not retain their properties in external existence.

Argument from Distinction and Its Refutation
First argument from distinction and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the argument from distinction for the external stability of non-existent possible things is constructed and refuted.

Core Idea
Mental distinction does not entail external existence.

Proponents of the external stability of non-existent possible things argue that such things are distinct in the intellect, and everything distinct must have external stability. But this confuses two kinds of distinction. Let us examine their reasoning carefully.

Guided Path
1
Read the proponents' first argument
The argument is based on distinction: non-existent possibles are known, willed, etc., and distinction necessitates external stability via mental indication.
2
Analyse the minor premise
Notice that distinction is asserted from being known, willed, and capable. This is a mental distinction.
The minor premise: it is distinct.
3
Analyse the major premise
The major premise is that every distinct thing is fixed because indication points to a referent. This is the key claim.
Major premise: distinction requires external stability.
4
Understand the refutation
The author distinguishes: if the distinction is mental, it only proves mental stability; if external, it's denied. The argument therefore fails.
5
Apply the lesson
Reflect on how this distinction prevents conflating mental concepts with external realities.

Commentary
This passage presents a dialectical argument about the existence of 'al-hal' (the state), a concept in kalam that is an intermediate between existence and non-existence. The author first states that the proponents of al-hal argue for it based on two proofs.

The first proof: Existence (al-wujud) cannot be existent, otherwise there would be infinite regress, nor can it be non-existent, otherwise it would be qualified by its opposite (non-existence). Therefore, existence must be an intermediate state. The author responds (qulna) with a choice: either existence is existent and identical to its existence, or it is non-existent, and the objection about being qualified by its opposite only holds if existence were the same as non-existence.

The second proof: The universal (al-kulli) cannot be existent, otherwise it would be individuated (which universals are not), nor non-existent, otherwise it could not be part of existent things. Similarly for genus and species. If the universal existed, it would require accidents subsisting in accidents. The author responds that there is no real composition in the external world; these distinctions (e.g., human and Zayd's particularity) are mental, not external. Hence the universal and genus are only mental.

Then the text continues with an additional objection (apparently from a critic) that if the universal or existence were intermediates, they would have existence and cause infinite regress, and they cannot be non-existent because they would then be described by their opposite. The author clarifies that non-existence on the assumption of an intermediate is not the opposite of existence but more specific; the true opposite is 'non-existence' (al-la-wujud).

The author of al-Tajrid (Nasir al-Din al-Tusi) answers that existence is not subject to the division into existent and non-existent, so it is neither. But the author notes that this is a concession of the claim and admission of an intermediate.

Another objection is raised: the intermediate must be a part of what is established (thabit), but existence is not established just as it is not negated; it is establishment itself. Just as establishment and negation are neither established nor negated, so existence need not be an intermediate. The author responds that this excuse is worse because it affirms an intermediate between established and negated by removing both opposites.

The Imam (likely al-Ghazali or Fakhr al-Din al-Razi) then gives two possible answers: first, we choose that existence is existent, and its existence is identical to it, so no infinite regress; its distinction from other existents is by a negative condition (no quiddity). Second, we choose that it is non-existent, and the impossibility of being qualified by its opposite applies only in cases of agreement (e.g., 'the existent is non-existence') or derivation (e.g., 'existence is possessed of non-existence'), not here. So the impossibility is not conceded, as it is like the word 'laysa' meaning non-existence, unlike 'aysa' meaning existence.

Commentary
This passage presents a dialectical argument about the existence of 'al-hal' (the state), a concept in kalam that is an intermediate between existence and non-existence. The author first states that the proponents of al-hal argue for it based on two proofs.

The first proof: Existence (al-wujud) cannot be existent, otherwise there would be infinite regress, nor can it be non-existent, otherwise it would be qualified by its opposite (non-existence). Therefore, existence must be an intermediate state. The author responds (qulna) with a choice: either existence is existent and identical to its existence, or it is non-existent, and the objection about being qualified by its opposite only holds if existence were the same as non-existence.

The second proof: The universal (al-kulli) cannot be existent, otherwise it would be individuated (which universals are not), nor non-existent, otherwise it could not be part of existent things. Similarly for genus and species. If the universal existed, it would require accidents subsisting in accidents. The author responds that there is no real composition in the external world; these distinctions (e.g., human and Zayd's particularity) are mental, not external. Hence the universal and genus are only mental.

Then the text continues with an additional objection (apparently from a critic) that if the universal or existence were intermediates, they would have existence and cause infinite regress, and they cannot be non-existent because they would then be described by their opposite. The author clarifies that non-existence on the assumption of an intermediate is not the opposite of existence but more specific; the true opposite is 'non-existence' (al-la-wujud).

The author of al-Tajrid (Nasir al-Din al-Tusi) answers that existence is not subject to the division into existent and non-existent, so it is neither. But the author notes that this is a concession of the claim and admission of an intermediate.

Another objection is raised: the intermediate must be a part of what is established (thabit), but existence is not established just as it is not negated; it is establishment itself. Just as establishment and negation are neither established nor negated, so existence need not be an intermediate. The author responds that this excuse is worse because it affirms an intermediate between established and negated by removing both opposites.

The Imam (likely al-Ghazali or Fakhr al-Din al-Razi) then gives two possible answers: first, we choose that existence is existent, and its existence is identical to it, so no infinite regress; its distinction from other existents is by a negative condition (no quiddity). Second, we choose that it is non-existent, and the impossibility of being qualified by its opposite applies only in cases of agreement (e.g., 'the existent is non-existence') or derivation (e.g., 'existence is possessed of non-existence'), not here. So the impossibility is not conceded, as it is like the word 'laysa' meaning non-existence, unlike 'aysa' meaning existence.

The author then declares this proof weak (ḍaʿīf) because what depends on the absence of the cause is the actual occurrence, not the priority itself. So if it implied one of them by essence, the two contradictories would be combined: i.e., implication of equality and non-implication of it. The answer: We do not grant that the contingent implies equality of the two sides; rather, it does not imply the occurrence of either one, and that does not contradict the implication of one side not to the extent of necessity, which is what is meant by priority.
Source pages p. 494

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof (al-thānī). It argues that the contingent, by its nature, requires (yaqtaḍī) the equality of existence and non-existence because both come from an external cause. If it inherently implied one side, then it would both imply equality (by its nature) and not imply equality (by the supposed inherent priority), which is a contradiction. The answer (al-Jawāb) denies the premise: the contingent does not imply equality, but rather does not imply the occurrence of either side. This non-implication does not contradict an inherent inclination (iqtiḍā') for one side as long as that inclination does not reach the level of necessity. Thus the proof fails because it assumes a stronger sense of 'implication' than is intended.

Correction: The answer shows that contingents have no inherent determination, but they can have an inclination (priority) from an external cause without implying equality.

The second proof fails because it conflates the contingent's lack of inherent determination with an implication of equality; in reality, the contingent simply does not determine itself, leaving room for priority from an external cause.

Third Proof: The Dilemma of Inherent Priority
Al-Taftazani presents the third proof as a dilemma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third proof against inherent priority in contingents and why it poses a dilemma for its proponents.

Core Idea
Inherent priority cannot be both essential and removable.

This lesson introduces a key kalam argument that challenges the notion of inherent priority in contingent beings. Al-Taftazani presents a dilemma: if priority is essential, it cannot be removed; if it can be removed, it is not essential. This reasoning exposes the logical difficulty in maintaining that any side in a contingency has an inherent advantage. We will work through the proof step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully

Notice that the answer is cut off; focus only on the proof as presented.
2
Analyze the two branches
Identify the two branches: (1) priority can be removed by a cause → it is not essential; (2) priority cannot be removed → the side would be necessary, contradicting contingency. Consider why each branch is problematic.
Essential properties (bi al-dhat) are those that cannot be removed by an external cause.

The Second Objection: Possible Necessitates Equality — Refuted
Second objection and its refutation regarding possible beings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the objection that a possible being necessitates equality is flawed, and how the author defends preponderance without compromising the nature of the possible.

Core Idea
A possible does not necessitate equality; it simply does not necessitate either side, allowing preponderance.

This passage presents the second objection against the first position: if a possible being is inherently neutral, doesn't that force equality? The opponent argues yes, but the author refutes by clarifying what the possible does and does not necessitate. The key is that non-necessitation is not the same as necessitating equality.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic and translation of the objection. Notice the claim: the possible necessitates equality of existence and non-existence with respect to its essence.
The objection uses the concept of 'necessitating equality' (اقتضاء التساوي).
2
Identify the refutation's main point
The author denies the claim: 'We do not concede that the possible necessitates equality.' Instead, the possible does not necessitate the occurrence of either side. Understand this distinction.
3
Connect to the broader argument
The refutation allows preponderance (أولوية) from an external cause. The possible remains neutral in itself, so an external preponderance does not contradict its essence.
This preserves the rational principle that a possible requires a preponderant cause.
