Existence as Predicate: The Doubt and Its Answer
Raises a doubt about the disjunctive proposition when existence is taken as predicate and answers it

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical doubt about taking existence as a predicate in a disjunctive proposition and how the distinction between conceptual difference and real identity resolves it.

Core Idea
The same thing can be one in reality but different in thought.

This passage presents a theological-logical doubt: if we say a body is either existent or non-existent, what does that imply about the relationship between existence and essence? The objection exposes dilemmas on both horns of the dilemma, and the answer relies on a key distinction between conceptual difference (taghāyur) and real identity (ittiḥād). This lesson walks you through the doubt and its resolution.

Guided Path
1
Read the doubt
Read the first part of the passage which presents the disjunctive proposition and the two possible relationships between existence and quiddity.
The doubt is a standard kalam objection against using existence as a predicate.
2
Identify the consequences
Notice the problems that arise if existence is identical to quiddity (futile affirmative, contradictory negative) and if it is different (existence inhering in non-existent, infinite regress, etc.).
3
Read the answer
Read the answer starting from 'والجواب' which introduces the distinction between difference in concept and unity in reality.
The answer uses the example of 'body' and 'existent' being the same in reality.
4
Apply the distinction
Understand that subject and predicate can be different in concept but refer to the same reality, resolving the logical puzzles.
This is a key principle in kalam and philosophy.
5
Note the caveat
The passage ends by noting that this analysis applies only when existence is taken as a predicate, not as a copula (e.g., 'the body is black').
The copula usage avoids these problems.

Commentary
This passage presents five objections (اعتراضات) against the doctrine that the existence of the Necessary Being is additional to its essence, followed by a collective response. The objections are:

First Proof: Abstraction Leads to Regress or Multiplicity
Sanusi presents a proof by contradiction that existence is additional to essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the argument from abstraction proves that existence is additional to essence, and why the objection regarding mere absence fails.

Core Idea
Existence cannot be abstract from essence without contradiction.

This lesson presents the first proof from Sanusi's Umm al-Barahin that existence is not merely abstract from essence. The argument explores two possibilities—if abstraction is by itself or by another—and shows both lead to absurdity. A clever objection and its response deepen the nuance. By tracing this reasoning, you'll grasp a foundational argument in Kalam for the distinctness of divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Understand the premise
The proof assumes the opposite of what is to be proven: that the existence of the Necessary Being is abstract (مجرد) from conjunction with its essence. Grasp what 'abstract' means here—it means existence is not added to essence but is merely a concept without extra reality.
This is a hypothetical assumption for reductio ad absurdum.
2
Examine the two cases
The proof splits the assumption into two possibilities: either the abstraction is by itself (i.e., essential to existence) or by another (i.e., caused by something else). Follow each case to see why they fail.
3
Consider the objection and response
An objector might say abstraction is merely the absence of anything requiring conjunction. The proof counters that even that absence would be a requirement, leading to dependence. Understand why this objection does not salvage the premise.
4
Grasp the resolution
The final response shifts the ground: the Necessary Being's existence is a particular existence (وجود خاص) that differs in reality from other existents. So it is not a universal abstraction that could be predicated of everything. Realise how this resolves the argument.
This is the key insight: existence is specific, not universal.

The proof that existence is additional confuses absolute existence with particular existence.

Commentary
This passage is the fourth investigation (mabḥath) which asserts that existence is synonymous with affirmation (thubūt) and equivalent to thing-ness (shay'iyya). The Impossibility of Power Affecting Essence or Existence
Sanusi presents the dilemma of power's effect on essence or existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why divine power cannot affect the essence (which is eternal) nor existence (which is a state), and why the necessary consequent is false.

Core Idea
Divine power cannot act on the eternal essence nor on a state; thus the claim that it must is false.

Sanusi begins by stating that the necessary consequent is false, then explains the logical implication: the effect of power must be either on the essence itself or on existence. Sanusi clarifies the implication of negating power and defends his position.

Core Idea
Negation of power over existence implies eternal attribution, not eternal existence.

The author now addresses a potential misunderstanding: if power does not affect existence, then existence would be eternal. He clarifies that it is the attribution of existence to the essence that is eternal, not existence itself. He then answers objections about the possibility of power affecting the attribution, the problem of regress in affirmations, and the attribution of the non-existent, linking to Imam al-Razi's doctrine.

Non-existence as a Negative Attribute: Objection and Answer
The author presents an objection and answers it by distinguishing two meanings of 'attribute of negation.'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that non-existence is a negative attribute qualifying the non-existent, and how the author answers by distinguishing two meanings of 'attribute of negation.'

Core Idea
Having a negative attribute does not imply that something is non-existent.

In this passage, the author addresses a logical objection to the doctrine of thubut al-ma'dum. The objector claims that if non-existence is a negative attribute, then anything qualified by it must be non-existent. The answer shows that this only follows if we take 'attribute of negation' in a specific sense, and that the Necessary Being Himself has negative attributes while being fully existent.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify its structure
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that it consists of an objection (beginning with 'الثاني : أن المعدوم متصف بالعدم') followed by an answer (beginning with 'وأجيب بأنه').
The objection is the second one in a series, but we only have this one here.
2
Understand the objector's reasoning
The objector argues: Non-existence is a negative attribute; the non-existent is qualified by it; anything qualified by a negative attribute is negated (i.e., non-existent); therefore the non-existent is non-existent — but the real point is that being qualified by a negative attribute entails negation of the subject.
The objector uses the analogy: just as an affirmative attribute affirms the subject, a negative attribute negates it.
3
Analyze the answer's two distinctions
The answer distinguishes two senses of 'attribute of negation': (1) an attribute that is negation itself in essence, and (2) an attribute that is the negation of something else. For (1), not every non-existent has such an attribute; for (2), even existents have negative attributes, like Allah's non-spatiality.
This shows that having a negative attribute does not force non-existence.

Commentary
This passage presents the second objection and its answer concerning the doctrine of thubut al-ma'dum. The objector argues that the non-existent (al-ma'dūm) is qualified by non-existence (al-'adam), which is a negative attribute. Anything qualified by a negative attribute is itself negated (i.e., non-existent), just as something qualified by an affirmative attribute is affirmed. The answer distinguishes two meanings of 'attribute of negation' (ṣifat al-nafy). First, if it means an attribute that is negation itself, such that being qualified by it means the subject is negated, then not every non-existent is qualified by such an attribute. This would only follow if non-existence were identical to negation, but it is not. Non-existence is more general, being the contradictory of existence, which is more specific than affirmation. Second, if it means an attribute that is the negation of something else, like non-spatiality or non-origination, then the subject qualified by it is not necessarily negated. For example, the Necessary Being (al-wājib) is qualified by many negative attributes, yet He is existent. It is not impossible for an existent to have privative attributes, just as it is impossible for a non-existent to have existential attributes.

The Problem of Attributes and Non-Existence
The author presents an argument and counter-answer on attributes and non-existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that attributes cannot belong to non-existence and how the answer refines the concept of the essence.

Core Idea
The dispute over attributes stems from confusing the absolute essence with the specific essence.

This passage presents a sophisticated theological argument about whether divine attributes can be attached to non-existence. The author first constructs a chain of reasoning that leads to a problematic conclusion, then provides an answer that resolves the difficulty. As you read, notice how the argument hinges on a precise distinction between two meanings of 'essence'.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Try to follow the logical steps: why a specific attribute cannot belong to the essence or another attribute, and why it must belong to something distinct.
The argument uses technical terms like mūjib and mukhtār.

This leads to the conclusion that the attribute must be in a state of existence.

Understand the answer and the crucial distinction

This distinction is key to resolving the problem.

معينة لا تكون لذاته وهو ظاهر ولا لصفة أخرى وإلا تسلسل، بل لمباين ولا يجوز أن يكون موجباً لأن نسبته إلى الكل على السواء فيكون مختاراً. وفعل المختار حادث فيلزم كون المعدوم مورداً للصفات المتزايلة وهو باطل بالاتفاق ، فتعين أن يكون ذلك حالة الوجود . والجواب : أنه يجوز أن يكون لذاته المخصوصة، فظهر أن مبنى كلام الطرفين على عدم التفرقة بين العارض الذي هو الذات المطلقة، والمعروض الذات المخصوصة

And the act of the free chooser is originated (hādith), so it necessitates the non-existent being a locus for changing attributes, which is invalid by consensus. So it must be that that [attribute] is in a state of existence. And the answer: it is permissible that it be for its specific essence. So it became apparent that the basis of the speech of both sides is the failure to distinguish between the accidental which is the absolute essence and the substratum which is the specific essence.

Commentary
This passage is an argument-and-answer unit. It begins by arguing that a specific attribute cannot belong to the essence itself (since that is obvious) nor to another attribute (to avoid infinite regress), so it must belong to something distinct (mubāyin). Then it presents a further problem: if the agent were a necessitating cause (mūjib), its relation to all things would be equal, so it must be a free chooser (mukhtār). But the act of a free chooser is originated (ḥādith), which would entail that the non-existent (ma'dūm) becomes the locus of changing attributes, which is invalid by consensus. Therefore, the attribute must only occur in a state of existence (ḥālat al-wujūd). The answer (al-jawāb) is that it is permissible for the attribute to belong to the essence in its specificity (al-dhāt al-makhṣūṣa). The conclusion is that the whole dispute arises from not distinguishing between the accidental attribute (al-ʿāriḍ) which is the absolute essence, and the substratum (al-maʿrūḍ) which is the specific essence.

Distinction vs. Substantiality in Kalam
The author records the disagreement on whether distinction differs from substantiality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the different positions on whether distinction (tamayyuz) is distinct from substantiality (jawhariyya), and the terminology used by the majority.

Core Idea
The majority see distinction as a conditional attribute of existence; others equate it with substantiality itself.

This passage introduces a key debate in kalam about the relationship between two attributes of substances: substantiality (jawhariyya) and distinction (tamayyuz). The author presents the majority position that they are distinct, with distinction being a conditional attribute tied to existence, and an opposing view that identifies them. Understanding this disagreement helps clarify how theologians analyze the properties of created substances.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic and its literal translation to get the overall argument.
2
Identify the two positions
Find where the majority view ends and the opposing view begins. Note the key terms.
The majority view is introduced by 'فَالْجُمْهُورُ عَلَى أَنَّ' and the opposing by 'وَذَهَبَ الشَّحَّامُ'.
3
Understand the majority terminology
Focus on the terms 'kawn' and 'kā'iniyya' and how they relate to distinction.
Distinction is called 'al-kawn' and the substance's occurrence in space is 'al-kā'iniyya', caused by 'al-taḥayyuz'.
4
Understand the opposing argument
Consider why al-Shahhām and others say substantiality is itself spatialization.
They argue that there is no meaning of substance except that which is inherently spatialized.

And among them is their disagreement concerning whether distinction (al-tamayyuz) is different from what is substantiality (al-jawhariyya)? The majority (al-jumhūr) hold that substantiality is an attribute dependent on the substance in the states of existence and non-existence, and distinction, which is the substance's requiring a certain space, is a dependent attribute established for the substance when existing, i.e., emanating from the substance on condition of origination (hudūth), and they call it al-kawn, and the substance's occurrence in the specific space they call al-kā'iniyya, which is caused by spatialization (al-taḥayyuz) in the sense of al-kawn. Commentary
This passage presents a classification of opinions regarding the relationship between distinction (al-tamayyuz) and substantiality (al-jawhariyya). The majority (al-jumhūr) distinguish between them: substantiality is an attribute that accompanies the substance in both existence and non-existence, whereas distinction—defined as the substance's requiring a specific space—is an attribute that only exists when the substance is existent and is conditioned by origination (ḥudūth). They call this attribute 'al-kawn' (spatial occupation) and the substance's occurrence in a specific space 'al-kā'iniyya', which is caused by spatialization (al-taḥayyuz) understood as al-kawn. In contrast, al-Shahhām, al-Basrī, and Ibn 'Ayyāsh hold that substantiality is identical to spatialization; they argue that the meaning of 'substance' is simply that which is inherently spatialized (mutaḥayyiz bi al-dhāt).

He then argues that if power were to affect something, it would have to be either the essence itself (which is eternal) or existence (which is a state). Both possibilities are ruled out, leading to the conclusion that power cannot affect eternal attributes.

Answering the Objection of Multiple Qualifications
Taftazani answers the infinite regress objection via the thabit/mawjud distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that qualification leads to infinite regress and how Taftazani refutes it using the distinction between existents and affirmed things (thabitat).

Core Idea
Infinite regress is impossible for existents, not for affirmed things.

Taftazani now addresses two objections against his position that power could affect the qualification of essence by existence. The first objection is that if qualification were real, it would itself require another qualification, leading to an infinite regress. And states (ahwal) are not subject to power by agreement of those who affirm them, and because the non-dependence of the blackness-quality of blackness and the knowledge-quality of the one in whom knowledge inheres upon the effect of power is necessary. As for clinging to the claim that if it were like the knowledge-quality of the knower and the motion-quality of the mover by the agent, but without knowledge and motion, and it leads to invalidating the doctrine of accidents, its weakness is not hidden. Then the negation of being subject to power does not entail the affirmation of eternality, such that the eternality of existence would follow; rather, the eternality of the essence’s being qualified by existence, based on its being a relation between them, does not depend on anything else, because they allow affirmation without a cause or with a non-powerful cause. And it was answered by denying the exclusivity, because it is possible that the effect of power is in the essence’s being qualified by existence. It should not be said that the qualification is non-existent. As for the first: because if it were established, it would have a qualification by affirmation and an infinite regress. As for the second: because of what preceded: that there is no qualification between essence and existence in terms of the external, as there is between whiteness and body; rather, that is only in terms of the mind; otherwise, the qualification of the non-existent by existence would follow, because essence without existence is in terms of the external only non-existent, since essence considered as such is only in conception. Only—we answer the first: that if the increase of the qualification of the qualification by affirmation over the qualification itself is granted, we do not concede the negation of the impossibility of infinite regress in the affirmed things (thabitat), for the proof has only been established for existents. And as for the second: we do not concede the impossibility of the qualification of the non-existent that is affirmed (al-ma'dum al-thabit) by existence, and its becoming at the time of qualification existent by that existence, like a non-black body being qualified by blackness and becoming black by that blackness. That is only impossible for what is not affirmed in the external. And this is what the Imam mentioned: that the doctrine of the affirmation of the non-existent is branched upon the doctrine of the augmentation of existence, meaning that they claimed that the existence of blackness is additional to its essence, then they claimed that it is permissible for that essence to be devoid of the attribute of existence, and also because they believed that existence is an attribute that occurs to the essence and subsists in it, and they did not conceive of that in pure negation. This produced for them the notion that the essence is affirmed before existence, and the reverse is permissible, because if the essence is affirmed before existence, then existence is not the same as it; otherwise, its affirmation would be its affirmation and its removal would be its removal.

Commentary
This passage is a complex argument about the relation of divine power to eternal attributes and existence. The author (al-Taftazani) first presents a proof that power cannot affect eternal attributes, then responds to possible objections. The proof argues: if divine power were to affect something, it would either affect the essence itself (which is eternal, and the eternal cannot be subject to power) or affect existence (which is a state—either against those who affirm states as an implication or against those who deny them). States like blackness-quality and knowledge-quality are not subject to power by agreement. The author then dismisses a weak objection about accidents. He clarifies that negating subjection to power does not entail that existence is eternal, only that the essence's qualification by existence is eternal because it is a relation. The answer to the main objection is that power could affect the qualification of the essence by existence, not the essence itself. Then he addresses two sub-objections: first, if qualification were real, it would lead to an infinite regress of qualifications (answered by saying infinite regress is not impossible in affirmed things, only in existents); second, that there is no real qualification between essence and existence externally (answered by allowing that a non-existent but affirmed entity can be qualified by existence and become existent, like a non-black body becoming black). The author concludes by noting that the doctrine of the affirmation of the non-existent is based on the view that existence is additional to essence.

Third Objection: Do Affirmed Essences Become Necessary?
Taftazani presents the third objection and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third objection against the thubut of essences in non-existence and how Taftazani refutes it.

Core Idea
An essence affirmed without a cause does not necessarily become a necessary being.

This passage presents a subtle objection: if essences have a reality in non-existence without any external cause, wouldn't they be necessary? Taftazani unpacks the reasoning and shows why the conclusion does not follow, revealing a key distinction between being affirmed and being necessary.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the full Arabic passage and its translation. Note the objector's initial claim: if essences are affirmed in non-existence and their affirmation is not from another, then they would be necessary.
The term 'necessary' here means existence not from another.
2
Analyze the two branches
The objector presents an alternative: if essences are affirmed, their affirmation is either necessary or possible. Trace each branch's consequences.
Necessary leads to multiple necessary beings; possible leads to being originated and preceded by negation.
3
Understand the answer
Taftazani responds that being preceded by negation does not negate affirmation in non-existence; it only shows temporal precedence.
The objection's assumption that 'every possible is originated' does not disprove thubut.

The third: if essences (dhawat) were affirmed (thabita) in non-existence, and according to you their affirmation is not from another, they would be necessary (wajiba), for there is no meaning of 'necessary' other than this; so the necessity of possibles and the multiplicity of the Necessary would follow. Commentary
This passage presents the third objection (al-thalith) against the view that essences have a reality (thubut) in the state of non-existence. The objection argues that if essences are affirmed in non-existence and this affirmation is not from another (i.e., not caused), then they would be necessary beings (wajib), because the definition of a necessary being is one whose existence is not from another. This would lead to two absurdities: (1) all possible things would become necessary, and (2) there would be multiple necessary beings (contradicting the uniqueness of God). The objectors also present an alternative: if the affirmation of essences is possible (not necessary), then they would be originated (muhdath) and preceded by non-existence, which contradicts the claim that they are affirmed without existence. The author (al-Taftazani) gives the answer: the objection is based on the assumption that every possible of affirmation (mumkin al-thubut) is originated and preceded by negation. But even if we grant that, it does not negate the claim that essences are affirmed without existence. At most, it shows that their affirmation in non-existence is preceded by their negation—meaning they were not affirmed before. This does not contradict their being affirmed in non-existence after being negated. The point is subtle: the objector's reasoning does not refute the reality of essences in the state of non-existence; it only shows that their affirmation is temporally preceded by non-affirmation, which is consistent with their being affirmed without existence (since existence is not the same as affirmation).
