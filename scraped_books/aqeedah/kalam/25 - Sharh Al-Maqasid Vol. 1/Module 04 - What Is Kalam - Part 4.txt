Objection and Answer on the Abstracted Essence
Author answers an objection about the mental consideration of mujarrada and concludes on its existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between the abstracted essence as a mental concept and as an actual entity, and why the latter is impossible.

Core Idea
The abstracted essence exists only as a mental abstraction, not in reality.

This passage addresses a critical objection in the philosophy of essence. The objector argues that 'taken with the condition of nothing' is merely a mental construct. The author responds by clarifying that mental abstraction does not preclude external existence, but then he distinguishes two senses of 'mujarrad' to resolve the issue.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection and its answer, followed by a conclusion. The objector says: 'taken with the condition of nothing' (al-maʾkhūdh bi-sharṭ lā) has no meaning other than what the intellect considers as such. The author answers: If that is the case, then it is not impossible for such a thing to exist in the external world, because it can be conjoined with accidents in reality while the intellect considers it abstracted. The author then draws a distinction: If 'mujarrad' means something that is inherently not conjoined with any accidents at all (whether absolutely or external), then its existence is impossible both externally and mentally. But if it means what the intellect considers as such (i.e., as abstracted), then its existence is permissible in both realms. In other words, the abstracted essence as a mental consideration can exist as a concept, but not as a real entity devoid of all accidents.

Resolving the Objection and Clarifying Plato's Forms
Objection-answer with clarification of Plato's Forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author resolves an objection about impossibility in the mind and clarifies the true meaning of Plato's theory of Forms.

Core Idea
Plato's Forms are not mind-independent essences but either divine ideas or governing substances.

This lesson addresses a critical objection to the concept of mujarrad (abstracted essence). The author shows that the apparent claim of Plato's Forms is actually compatible with his own position. We'll see how the doubt of the absolute unknown is resolved and how Plato's Forms are reinterpreted.

This sets up the need to reinterpret Plato.

The author explains that Plato's Forms are either species in God's knowledge or abstract substances governing species, not independently existing abstracted essences.
3
Examine the two justifications
The passage gives two reasons why the author's interpretation holds: abstractness with capacity for opposites, and eternity/everlastingness. Reflect on these.
These justifications align Plato's Forms with the author's own doctrine.

The Third Division: Mahiyyah La bi-sharṭ shay'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third division of mahiyyah (essence) known as 'not conditioned by anything', and how it exists externally as the concrete individual itself.

Core Idea
The absolute essence exists as the concrete particular itself, not as a part.

After discussing the first two divisions of mahiyyah (conditioned by something and conditioned by absence), Sanusi now turns to the third and most fundamental division: the essence considered without any condition. This passage clarifies that this absolute essence is not a separate entity but is identical to the external individual itself.

Commentary
This passage defines the third division of mahiyyah (essence). The author states that this division does not involve either mixing (with accidents) or abstraction (of universals). It is taken 'not conditioned by anything' (la bi-sharṭ shay'), meaning it is considered in itself without any condition of being with or without something. This absolute essence is more general than the mixed essence (makhluṭah). Crucially, it exists externally as the concrete individual itself, not as a part of that individual. The reason given is that there is no distinction in the external world between the absolute essence and the individual; any distinction between them exists only in the mind.

Proof of Composition through Participation in an Essential
al-Sanusi presents a proof of composition from participation in an essential, with an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how participation in an essential attribute with difference in another essential or necessary accident demonstrates that quiddities are composite.

Core Idea
Shared essential with differing essential proves composition.

This passage introduces a classic proof in Islamic metaphysics: that quiddities (essences) are composed of parts. The author, al-Sanusi, uses the observation that two things can share an essential attribute while differing in another essential attribute (or a necessary accident) to argue that each must be composite. The proof is subtle, but it lies at the heart of kalam reasoning about the structure of reality.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the translation to grasp the overall argument.
Notice the technical terms: dhātī (essential) and lāzim (necessary accident).
2
Identify the key claim
The author says participation in a dhātī with difference in a dhātī or a lāzim indicates composition. Locate this statement.
3
Understand the reasoning
If two quiddities share an essential but differ in another essential, the shared part cannot be the whole quiddity; otherwise they would be identical. Therefore each must be composed of the shared part and the differing part.
The same logic applies if they differ in a necessary accident.
4
Consider the objection
An objection is raised: if by 'essential' we mean a part of the quiddity, the argument becomes circular. Reflect on why this objection might be weak.
The author implies the objection is invalid, but the answer is not given here.
5
Reflect on the significance
This proof is foundational for later arguments about the existence and attributes of God. Understanding it prepares you for deeper theological discussions.

And that which indicates composition is participation in an essential (dhātī) with difference in an essential or something from the necessary accidents (lawāzim) of the quiddity, not merely participation or difference in an essential. Commentary
This passage presents a proof (dalīl) for the composition of quiddity (tarkīb al-māhiyya) based on participation (ishtirāk) in an essential (dhātī) with difference (ikhtilāf) in another essential or in a necessary accident (lāzim). The author explains that if two quiddities share an essential attribute but differ in another essential attribute, then each quiddity must be composed of the shared part and the differentiating part. Similarly, if they share an essential but differ in an accident that is a necessary consequence (lāzim) of the quiddity, then the shared essential cannot be the entire quiddity, otherwise the difference in necessary accidents would be impossible. Therefore, it must be a part, proving composition. A potential objection is then raised: if by 'essential' (dhātī) one means a part of the quiddity, then the argument becomes circular or trivial (laghw). The author implies this objection is invalid, as the argument is valid when 'essential' is understood properly. The passage cuts off before the answer to the objection is fully given in the text.

The Disagreement on the Making of Quiddity and the First Proof
Sanusi states the disagreement on quiddity and gives the first proof of the mutakallimun.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the core disagreement about whether quiddity (essence) is made, and the first proof of the mutakallimun for the making of all quiddities, including simple ones.

Core Idea
Is the very essence of a thing created? The mutakallimun say yes, absolutely.

After establishing that the existence of a possible being is from an agent, scholars disagreed about its quiddity (essence). The mutakallimun argued that both simple and composite quiddities are made, while philosophers and Mu'tazila disagreed. This lesson presents that disagreement and the first proof, along with responses to objections.

Guided Path
1

Read the Arabic passage once with translation to get an overview of the three positions on quiddity being made.
Notice that the disagreement is over whether quiddity itself is made, not just existence.
2

Identify the mutakallimun's position (absolute making) and the philosophers/Mu'tazila position (no making at all), and the intermediate view (only composites).
The passage uses 'مطلقاً' to indicate 'absolutely'.
3
Understand the first proof and the objection
Follow the proof: every possible needs an agent because possibility is the cause of need. Then see the objection that possibility implies duality, contradicting simplicity.
The proof is stated concisely; the objection is in brackets.
4
Reflect on the answers given
Consider the response that possibility is a relation between quiddity and existence, not between parts, so it does not compromise simplicity. Also consider the additional argument about simples.
The second answer uses a reductio ad absurdum: if simples aren't made, composites can't be made either.

Commentary
This passage is a combination of a statement of a doctrinal disagreement and a theological proof. It begins by stating the fourth issue (الرابع) concerning what is unknown (المجهول) – whether it is quiddity, existence, attribution, or joining of parts – and notes that all are quiddity, but this is refuted. Then it moves to the core disagreement: After agreeing that the existence of a possible being is from an agent, scholars differed about whether the quiddity (essence) itself is made by that agent. The philosophers and Mu'tazila deny that the quiddity is made absolutely, meaning nothing at all is made; some hold that composites are made but not simples. The mutakallimun then present their first proof (الأول). The argument runs: both composite and simple quiddities are possible (mumkin), and every possible needs an agent because the cause of need is possibility (imkaan). An objection is raised: possibility is a relation that implies duality (ithnayniyya), which contradicts simplicity. The author gives a response: possibility is not a relation between parts of the quiddity, so it is not confined to composites; it is a relation between the quiddity and its existence, expressing the lack of necessity of existence or non-existence. Thus, when disregarding existence, possibility cannot be conceived as occurring to a simple or composite quiddity; rather, it is intrinsic in the sense that if the intellect relates the quiddity to existence, it perceives a relation of possibility, and this is sufficient to establish need for an agent. A further response (وقد يجاب) adds that if simples were not made, then composites would not be made either, because if all simples of a composite are externally established without a maker, the composite is necessarily established. An objection (لا يقال) is raised: perhaps each part has its own establishment and the composite's establishment depends on the whole, as with concepts. The answer (لأنا نقول) explains that the distinction between the totality of establishments and the establishment of the whole is not intelligible externally; it is only a mental distinction.

Commentary
This passage is a proof that ta'ayyun (determination) is i'tibari (a mental consideration), followed by an objection and its answer. The author begins by presenting the argument: if ta'ayyun were real (exists in the external world), then its occurrence for a particular individual of a species would depend on that individual's existence and its distinction from other individuals. But if the distinction comes from this same ta'ayyun, that is circular (dawr); if it comes from another ta'ayyun, infinite regress follows. This is the meaning of the common kalam statement: if ta'ayyun existed, its conjunction with the essence would require prior distinction of that essence. The author then rejects a potential objection that the essence is distinguished by itself and its differentiae, not by ta'ayyun. Next, an objector asks: why not consider the subject (ma'rud) to be the already-distinguished instance, avoiding circularity? Analogy: the subject of whiteness is the white body, not another whiteness. The author replies that this involves 'dawr ma'iyya' (circularity of simultaneity), because the essence when it exists is already specified and distinguished by the ta'ayyun that attach to it—just as individuals of a genus are distinguished by differentiae. But the specification does not depend on a prior distinction. The author then gives his own answer: the existence of the subject is necessarily prior to the accident, and so its distinction is prior because it accompanies the prior existence. This is different from differentiae and instances, where distinction is mental. He notes a subtlety: the priority of the subject over ta'ayyun is in essence, not time, so it does not require temporal priority. Finally, the objector presses: if the prior subject is this instance, then 'this-ness' (which is ta'ayyun) is prior. The author agrees, but clarifies that 'this-ness' here means being a subject of 'this-ness', not that ta'ayyun is a real property.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification. The third investigation: determination. Determination depends on the impossibility of sharing mentally, so it is not obtained by combining the universal with the universal, even if such that it prevents sharing externally. Rather, according to us, it is based on the will of the powerful chooser, or according to some on external existence, because it is definitely realized, and individuals multiply by the multiplicity of existences. It was objected that co-variation does not indicate causality, even if conceded, so the discussion is about particular determinations. According to the philosophers, it is based on the essence itself, so it is confined to one individual, or on matter individualized by accidents that attach to it according to successive dispositions, so it multiplies by the multiplicity of matters capable of multiplication by themselves. It was objected that the determination of accidents is only by the determination of matter, so their determination by it is circular. It was answered that their determination is by the accidents, not by their determinations. Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification. The author begins by defining 'determination' (al-ta'ayyun): it is the state of a concept such that it cannot be mentally shared among multiple individuals. This condition is called 'impossibility of sharing mentally' (imtina' al-shirkah dhihnan). The author then states that this determination is not achieved by combining a universal with another universal, even if the combination seems to prevent sharing externally. He then lists different views on what grounds determination: (1) According to 'us' (the Ash'ari school), determination is based on the will of the powerful chooser (i.e., God's will). (2) According to some (likely the Mu'tazila or others), it is based on external existence, because every existent is necessarily particular. (3) The philosophers hold two positions: some say determination is based on the essence itself, so each essence is confined to one individual; others say it is based on matter individualized by accidents, so individuals multiply as matter multiplies. An objection is raised against the philosophers: if accidents are determined by matter, then saying matter determines accidents is circular (dawr). The response is that accidents determine matter, not the other way around. The author then counters with a reductio: if that were so, then the determination of the essence would be by its own attributes, and individuals multiply accordingly—a position that seems to lead to an infinite regress or unacceptable implications. The author thus rejects the philosophical views and affirms the Ash'ari position that determination is by divine will.

This passage defines the concept of 'al-hal' (the state) in Islamic theology. Ontological vs. Linguistic Discussion of 'Thing'
Taftazani distinguishes ontological debate from linguistic usage of 'thing'.

Core Idea
Ontology and language are separate: thing-ness in reality vs. applying the word 'thing'.
