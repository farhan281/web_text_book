Refuting the Objections: Taftazani's Responses
Taftazani provides refutations to five objections against thubut al-ma'dum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Taftazani refutes each of the five objections against thubut al-ma'dum, including his key argument about the distinction between existence and thubut.

Core Idea
Taftazani refutes the objections by distinguishing thubut from existence, denying premises, and limiting the scope of non-existent thubut.

Having presented the objections, Taftazani now provides their refutations. He begins with responses to the first and second objections (not stated in this passage) and then addresses the third, fourth, and fifth. His refutations involve key distinctions such as that between thubut and existence, and the denial of certain logical leaps. This lesson carefully follows the order of his replies.

Guided Path
1
Read the refutations in Arabic and translation
First read the Arabic passage containing all the replies. Then read the English translation.
Note that the refutations are given in order: first, second, third, fourth, fifth.
2
Match each reply to its corresponding objection
Identify which reply addresses which objection. The third and fourth replies correspond to the third and fourth objections from the previous lesson.
The first and second objections are not given in the text but are implied.
3
Understand the key distinction in the third reply
Taftazani distinguishes between dependence in existence (wujud) and dependence in thubut. This is crucial to avoid multiplicity of the Necessary.
The Necessary Existent is independent only in existence, not in thubut.
4
Analyze the fourth reply's denial
The fourth reply denies that a finite part can prove the whole infinite set is finite. Understand why this is weak.
Taftazani calls the proof weak, not necessarily false.
5
Grasp the nuance in the fifth reply
The fifth reply shows that 'not being pure negation' does not mean 'pure affirmation'. Only some aspects of the non-existent are thabit.
This subtle distinction undermines the objection's conclusion.

Commentary
This passage presents three objections (the third, fourth, and fifth) against the view that non-existent things (al-ma'dum) have a kind of stable existence (thubut), and then gives the refutations (rudud) to these objections as well as to the first and second objections (which were presumably stated earlier). The argument is set within the kalām debate over whether non-existent quiddities are 'established' (thabit) prior to creation. The third objection argues that if quiddities were established independently (not from another), they would share the attribute of self-sufficiency with the Necessary Existent, leading to a multiplicity of necessary beings—a clear impossibility. The fourth objection notes that if non-existent quiddities are infinite (since there are infinitely many possible things), but only a finite number actually exist, then the whole set would be only finitely more than the existing ones, implying finitude—a contradiction. The fifth objection uses logical relationships: if the non-existent (al-ma'dum) is either equal to or more specific than the negated (al-manfi), it would not be established; if more general, it would not be pure negation, and thus would be established, which when applied to the negated would entail thubut of the negated—also impossible. The reply (rada) to the first objection (which dealt with the temporality of existence) allows that the attribute of the essence with existence may be temporally originated by divine power, and denies the impossibility of regression (tasalsul) in matters of stability (thubut) rather than existence. The reply to the second objection denies the premise if 'attribute' means negation or a negated quality. The reply to the third clarifies that the Necessary Existent is independent in its existence, not in its thubut, so the threat of multiplicity is avoided. The reply to the fourth denies that the finite comparison is valid, as proving finitude through a direct mapping is weak. The reply to the fifth explains that not being pure negation does not entail pure affirmation; only some aspect of the non-existent may be established, so the thubut of the negated does not follow. A final objection (fa-in qīla) is raised: if the non-existent were more general than the negated, it would be distinguished and thus established as an implication. The answer (qulnā) rejects this by stating that then many premises would be invalid.

Second Objection: Non-Existent Described by Non-Existence and Its Answer
Taftazani presents an objection and answer regarding the non-existent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that the non-existent is described by non-existence and the key distinction that resolves it.

Core Idea
Negative attributes do not imply non-existence; even God has negative attributes.

Taftazani presents a second objection against those who affirm reality for the non-existent. The objection argues that being described by non-existence (a negative attribute) entails being non-existent. However, the answer reveals a crucial distinction: non-existence is not identical to mere negation, and negative attributes can apply to existents like God. Let's examine the reasoning.

2
Identify the two meanings of 'attribute of negation'
In the answer, Taftazani distinguishes two possible meanings. Find them and note how each leads to a different conclusion.
The first meaning is 'negation in itself'; the second is 'negation of a specific thing'.
3
Apply the lesson to God's attributes
Think about how God is described by negative attributes like non-spatiality (lā taḥayyuz). Why doesn't that make God non-existent?

Commentary
This passage presents the second objection (al-thani) against the reality of the non-existent (ma'dum). The objection argues that the non-existent is described by non-existence ('adam), which is a negative attribute; and anything described by a negative attribute is itself negated (non-existent). Therefore, the non-existent is truly non-existent and has no reality. The author (al-Taftazani) then gives the answer by distinguishing two meanings of 'attribute of negation'. First, if it means a pure negation in itself (like 'not existing'), then the objector's claim is not conceded: non-existence is not identical to negation but is more general, being the contradictory of existence (which is more specific than affirmation). Second, if it means the negation of a specific thing (e.g., non-spatiality, non-origination), then being described by such a negative attribute does not require the subject to be non-existent. For example, God (al-wajib) is described by many negative attributes (like being non-spatial), yet He is existent. In fact, it is possible for an existent to have privative attributes, just as it is impossible for a non-existent to have existential attributes. Thus the objection fails.

Existence as an Accident and the Fixity of Essences
Explains how the claim that existence is an accident leads to the fixity of essences before existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the claim that existence is an accident leads to the conclusion that essences are fixed before existence, and why this implies existence is not identical to the essence.

Core Idea
If existence is an accident, then essences are fixed and pre-exist their existence.

This passage explores a key doctrine in kalam: the idea that existence is an accident (ziyadat al-wujud) added to the quiddity. The proponents of this view argue that the quiddity can be conceived without existence, leading to the conclusion that the quiddity is fixed before existence itself. Let's read the original argument and unpack its logical steps.

Commentary
This passage presents the argument of those who hold that existence is an accident (ziyadat al-wujud) added to the essence. They claim that blackness (used as an example) has an existence additional to its quiddity, and that the quiddity can be stripped of existence. Since they believe existence is an attribute that inheres in the quiddity, they cannot conceive of this for pure non-existence. The consequence they draw is that the quiddity is fixed (thabit) before existence, and the opposite order is also possible: if the quiddity is fixed before existence, then existence is not the same as the quiddity, because if they were identical, the fixity of the quiddity would be the fixity of existence, and its removal would be the removal of existence.

Non-Existents and Negative Attributes
Objection and answer regarding negative attributes of non-existents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection that non-existents are qualified by non-existence is answered by distinguishing pure negation from specific negative attributes.

Core Idea
Not all negative attributes imply non-existence; even the Necessary Existent has them.

This passage tackles a key objection against the fixity of essences in non-existence. The objector argues that since non-existents are described by non-existence (a negative attribute), they must be purely negated. The answer reveals a subtle distinction between two kinds of negative attributes, showing that some negative attributes apply even to existent beings like God. Let's work through the objection and its precise response.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the first part of the passage identifying the objection: the non-existent is qualified by non-existence, which is a negation, so it is negated.
Notice the parallel: as affirmation affirms, negation negates.
2
Understand the answer's distinction
The answer distinguishes two meanings of 'attribute of negation': one is pure negation itself, the other is the negation of a specific thing. Only the first would lead to the objection's conclusion.
The key example: God (al-Wajib) is described by 'non-spatial' but is not non-existent.
3
Apply the distinction
See how the second meaning allows existents to have negative attributes without being negated, which defeats the objection.
This preserves the possibility that non-existents have some fixity of essence.

The second [objection]: That the non-existent is qualified by non-existence, which is an attribute of negation, because it is the removal of existence which is an attribute of affirmation, and the one qualified by an attribute of negation is negated, just as the one qualified by an attribute of affirmation is affirmed. Commentary
This passage presents the second objection against the claim that essences are fixed in non-existence. The objector argues: The non-existent is described by non-existence, which is a negative attribute. Since non-existence is the removal of existence, which is a positive attribute, anything described by a negative attribute is itself negated (i.e., non-existent). Therefore, non-existents cannot be said to have any positive fixity. The answer distinguishes two meanings of 'attribute of negation'. If it means pure negation (the attribute is negation itself), then not every non-existent is described by that; non-existence is more general than negation, as it is the contradictory of existence, which is more specific than affirmation. If it means a specific negation like 'non-spatial' or 'non-originated', then being described by such a negative attribute does not entail that the subject is non-existent. For example, God (the Necessary Existent) is described by many negative attributes, yet He is existent. Thus, it is possible for an existent to have privative attributes, but impossible for a non-existent to have positive attributes. So the objection fails.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical argument about whether there can be a mediator (wāsita) between existence and non-existence. The discussion begins by noting that if such a mediator existed, it would have to be existent, leading to an infinite regress, or non-existent, but then it could not be the opposite of existence (since the true opposite is lāwujūd, not ‘adam). The Universal's Mode of Existence
The author argues that the universal (al-kulli) is neither existent nor non-existent, and refutes objections.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the universal (al-kulli) is considered neither existent nor non-existent in Islamic logic and how this reasoning applies to genera and accidents.

Core Idea
Universals are not concrete existents, but they are not pure nonexistents either.

In classical Islamic thought, the status of universals has been a subject of rigorous debate. Commentary
This passage argues that the universal (al-kulli), which has actual particulars like 'man', is neither existent nor non-existent. If it were existent, it would be individuated and thus not universal; if non-existent, it could not be a part of its existent particulars (e.g., Zayd), because an existent cannot be preceded by a non-existent. The same reasoning applies to the genus (jins) like 'animal': it is not existent due to its universality, nor non-existent because it is a part of the true quiddity (al-māhiyya al-haqīqiyya) like 'man'.
