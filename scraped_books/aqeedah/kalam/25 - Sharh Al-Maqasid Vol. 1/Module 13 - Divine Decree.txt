Divine Decree and Human Agency: The Khariji Debate
Imam Ali refutes a Khariji's misunderstanding of qada and qadr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Ali refuted the Khariji misconception that divine decree negates human choice and moral accountability.

Core Idea
Divine decree does not mean compulsion; it is Allah's command and judgment.

In this famous exchange from Nahj al-Balagha, a Khariji confronts Imam Ali with a seemingly logical trap: if every step is by Allah's decree, then no one deserves reward or blame. Ali's response dismantles this fatalism, showing that the true meaning of qada and qadr preserves human responsibility and the moral order of reward and punishment. This passage is a cornerstone of Sunni understanding of free will and divine decree.

Guided Path
1
Read the debate narrative
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to grasp the flow of the conversation between the Khariji and Imam Ali.
Notice how the Khariji's initial question leads to a conclusion that he thinks logically follows.
2
Identify the Khariji's mistake
The Khariji assumes divine decree is a 'binding compulsion' (qada' lazim, qadr hatm). Find where Imam Ali exposes this error.
The phrase 'ظننت قضاء لازماً، وقدراً حتماً' is key.
3
Understand the consequences of compulsion
Imam Ali lists what would be nullified if decree were compulsion: reward, punishment, commands, prohibitions, etc. Reflect on why these depend on free choice.
This shows the logical necessity of human agency for the moral order.
4
Learn the correct meaning of qada
Imam Ali defines qada as 'command' (amr) and 'judgment' (hukm), quoting the Quranic verse 'Wa qada rabbuka alla ta'budu illa iyyah' to demonstrate the true meaning.
This redefines decree as a moral directive, not a deterministic force.

Section E: The debate with the Kharijites. It is mentioned in Nahj al-Balagha and its commentary by Ibn Abi al-Hadid. A shaykh stood before Ali, may Allah be pleased with him, and said: Inform us about our march to Syria; was it by the decree (qada) and predestination (qadr) of Allah? He said: By Him who splits the seed and creates the soul, we have not trodden any step nor descended any valley except by the decree and predestination of Allah. The shaykh said: Then I count my toil as lost with Allah; I do not see any reward for me. He said: Stop, O shaykh! Allah has greatly rewarded you for your march while you are going, and for your return while you are returning, and you in none of your states were coerced or compelled. The shaykh said: How so, when decree and predestination have driven us? He said: Woe to you! Perhaps you thought it is a binding decree and an absolute predestination. If that were the case, then reward and punishment, promise and threat, command and prohibition would be nullified, and there would be no blame from Allah for a sinner, nor praise for a good-doer, and the good-doer would not be more deserving of praise than the evildoer, nor the evildoer more deserving of blame than the good-doer. That is the saying of the idol worshippers, the soldiers of Satan, the false witnesses, the people blind to the truth – they are the Qadariyya of this Ummah and its Magians. Allah the Exalted commands by choice, forbids as a warning, tasks with ease, and He is not disobeyed by being overpowered, nor obeyed reluctantly, and He did not send the messengers to His creation as a game, and He did not create the heavens and what is between them in vain; that is the assumption of those who disbelieve; so woe to those who disbelieve from the Fire. The shaykh said: Then what are the decree and predestination by which we only proceed? He said: It is the command (amr) from Allah and the judgment (hukm). Then he recited the words of the Exalted: {And your Lord has decreed (qada) that you worship none but Him}. The shaykh then rose joyfully saying: You are the Imam whom we hope for obedience on the Day of Resurrection, from the Most Merciful, satisfaction. You have clarified from our religion what was obscure; may your Lord reward you for it with goodness.

Commentary
This passage is a debate with a Khariji (Kharijite) about the nature of divine decree (qada) and predestination (qadr). The Khariji asks Imam Ali whether their military march to Syria (the Battle of Siffin) occurred by Allah's decree. Imam Ali affirms that every step is by decree, but when the Khariji concludes that therefore he deserves no reward (since it was compelled), Imam Ali corrects him. The Imam distinguishes between a decree that is necessary and absolute (qada' lazim, qadr hatm) and the actual decree which allows human choice. He argues that if everything were by compulsion, then the entire system of reward and punishment, promises and threats, commands and prohibitions would be meaningless. Such a belief is attributed to idol worshippers and the 'Qadariyya and Magians of this Ummah' (the Qadariyya here likely means those who deny predestination, interestingly). Imam Ali then clarifies that Allah's decree (qada) in the sense of 'command' (amr) and 'judgment' (hukm) is what governs. He quotes Quran 17:23 'وقضى ربك ألا تعبدوا إلا إياه' (And your Lord has decreed that you worship none but Him), where 'qada' means 'commanded'. The Khariji becomes satisfied. The passage illustrates the Sunni position that divine decree encompasses all events but does not negate human agency and moral accountability.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Divine decree means that humans are compelled to act, leaving no room for free will or moral accountability.
Correction: Imam Ali explains that divine decree is Allah's command and judgment, which does not compel; rather, it coexists with human choice, as evidenced by the existence of reward, punishment, commands, and prohibitions.

Key Takeaway
Divine decree (qada and qadr) is Allah's command and judgment, not a force that compels human actions; human responsibility and the moral order remain intact.

Imam Ali's Refutation of Fatalistic Qadar (Part 1: The Danger of Determinism)
Exchange where Imam Ali refutes deterministic interpretation of qada and qadar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why believing that qada and qadar compel actions destroys the basis of moral responsibility.

Core Idea
If qada compels, reward and punishment become meaningless.

In this exchange, a questioner assumes that if everything is by qada and qadar, humans have no free choice and thus deserve no reward. Imam Ali first affirms that all happens by decree, but then corrects the fatalistic interpretation. This lesson explores the first part of the dialogue, where Ali shows the disastrous consequences of deterministic thinking.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage that begins with the question and Ali's initial affirmation, and ends with Ali labeling the deterministic view as the statement of idolaters.
Notice how the questioner jumps from 'by qada' to 'no reward'.
2
Identify the two opposing beliefs
The questioner believes that qada and qadar are a binding force that compels action. Ali argues that if that were true, reward and punishment would be null. Identify where each belief is stated.
Ali's main argument is in the line: 'law kana dhalika kadhalika labatala al-thawab wal-iqab'.
3
Analyze the consequences of determinism
List the six things Ali says would be nullified: reward, punishment, promise, threat, command, prohibition. Understand why each depends on free will.
This shows the logical necessity of human choice.
4
Label the deterministic view
Ali calls those who hold this view 'Qadariyya' and 'Magians of this community'. Understand the historical context: Qadariyya typically denied predestination, but Ali uses it inversely for determinists.
The label emphasizes that determinism is akin to idolatry.

Commentary
This passage is a narrative from Nahj al-Balagha illustrating Imam Ali's refutation of deterministic predestination (qadar). A man (likely a Kharijite) asks whether their military campaign was by Allah's qada and qadar. Ali first affirms that everything occurs by qada and qadar, which the man interprets as fatalism—that their actions are predetermined without choice, so they deserve no reward. Ali corrects him emphatically: Allah does reward them precisely because they were not compelled (mukrah) or coerced (mudtar). The man persists: if qada and qadar drove them, how can they have free will? Ali explains that if qada were binding and qadar inevitable, then all moral responsibility would collapse: reward, punishment, commands, prohibitions, blame, and praise would be meaningless. That (deterministic) view is the saying of idolaters, devils, and false witnesses. Those who hold it are the Qadariyya (a sect that denied divine decree in favor of absolute free will, but here Ali uses it inversely for determinists) and the Magians of this community. Ali then clarifies the correct understanding: Allah commands by choice (takhyir), forbids by warning, charges by facilitation; He is not disobeyed by being overpowered nor obeyed reluctantly; He did not create in vain. Finally, the questioner asks for the true meaning of qada and qadar. Ali defines them as 'the command of Allah and His judgment' and quotes Quran 17:23: 'And your Lord has decreed that you worship none but Him.' The man is satisfied and praises Ali for clarifying the religion. The core lesson is that qada and qadar refer to Allah's wise command and judgment, which leave room for human choice and responsibility, not fatalistic compulsion.

The Correct Meaning of Qada and Qadar (Part 2: Allah's Command and Judgment)
Imam Ali explains the true meaning of qada and qadar as Allah's command and judgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam Ali's positive definition of qada and qadar as Allah's wise command and judgment, not as compulsion.

Core Idea
Qada is Allah's command; qadar is His judgment — both consistent with human choice.

After refuting determinism, Imam Ali provides the correct understanding. He explains that Allah commands by choice, forbids by warning, and is not forced. Then when the questioner asks for the true meaning, Ali defines qada and qadar as the command and judgment of Allah, citing the Quran. This lesson covers the second half of the narrative.

Guided Path
1
Read the second part of the passage
Read from 'Inna Allaha ta'ala amara takhyiran' to the end. Notice the shift from refutation to positive exposition.
Ali now describes Allah's attributes related to human responsibility.

Examine Ali's description of divine actions
Ali says Allah commanded by choice (takhyir), forbade by warning (tahdhir), charged by facilitation (taysir). He was not disobeyed by being overpowered, nor obeyed reluctantly. Understand the implications: Allah's commands leave room for human response.
This shows that divine decree does not negate human agency.
3
Analyze the questioner's final query
The man asks: 'What then are the qada and qadar by which we move?' Ali answers: 'They are the command of Allah and His judgment.' Then he recites the Quranic verse: 'Your Lord has decreed that you worship none but Him.'
The verse uses 'qada' to mean 'commanded', reinforcing the definition.
4
Contemplate the conclusion
The old man stands up joyfully and praises Ali for clarifying what was confused. Reflect on the importance of correct understanding of divine decree.
Correct theology brings contentment and clarity.

Ali explains that if qada were binding and qadar inevitable, then all moral responsibility would collapse: reward, punishment, commands, prohibitions, blame, and praise would be meaningless. That (deterministic) view is the saying of idolaters, devils, and false witnesses. Those who hold it are the Qadariyya (a sect that denied divine decree in favor of absolute free will, but here Ali uses it inversely for determinists) and the Magians of this community. Ali then clarifies the correct understanding: Allah commands by choice (takhyir), forbids by warning, charges by facilitation; He is not disobeyed by being overpowered nor obeyed reluctantly; He did not create in vain. Finally, the questioner asks for the true meaning of qada and qadar. Ali defines them as 'the command of Allah and His judgment' and quotes Quran 17:23: 'And your Lord has decreed that you worship none but Him.' The man is satisfied and praises Ali for clarifying the religion. The core lesson is that qada and qadar refer to Allah's wise command and judgment, which leave room for human choice and responsibility, not fatalistic compulsion.

The First Disagreement: Genus-Attributes of Nonexistent Essences
Presents the first point of disagreement and the majority view with an answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rationale behind the majority's view that nonexistent essences can be qualified by genus-attributes, and the key objection to this position.

Core Idea
Equality in essence does not mean identity—differences require attributes even in nonexistence.

In this passage, the author presents the first major disagreement among those who hold that the non-existent is a 'thing.' The question is whether non-existent essences (like substances or blackness) have genus-attributes (e.g., substantiality, blackness-ness) before they come into existence. The majority argues that they must, otherwise all essences would be one. But a key answer refutes this argument, preserving the possibility of real difference without pre-existent attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the central question
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Look for the examples given: substance with substantiality, blackness with blackness-ness.

The majority (الجمهور) gives three reasons for their affirmative answer. The second argument uses a disjunction: essences are either similar or different; both lead to attributes.

Finally, read the counter-answer: equality in essence does not prevent difference in reality, just as existing realities can differ despite sharing existence. Ask yourself: why does this answer undermine the majority's arguments? What does it preserve?
This counter shows that the mere fact of sharing essence does not force pre-existence attributes.
