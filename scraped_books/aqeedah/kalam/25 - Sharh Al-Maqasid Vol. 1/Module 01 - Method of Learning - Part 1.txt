Opening Praise and the Foundation of Tawhid
Author opens with praise and introduces the science of tawhid.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Tawhid is the unchanging core of all divine messages; laws may vary, but the creed of God's oneness is eternal.

This lesson presents the opening of Sharh al-Maqasid, where the author begins with praise and then introduces the science of tawhid. He emphasizes that while specific religious laws can change according to circumstances, the fundamental belief in God's oneness remains constant across all prophetic missions. Understanding this distinction is crucial for studying kalam.

Commentary
This passage is an opening doxology and introduction to the book. The author begins with the basmalah and hamdalah, praising God using names that emphasize His oneness and uniqueness (الواحد الأحد, الفرد الصمد). He then sends blessings upon the Prophet Muhammad, describing him as the chosen one for the best message and the receiver of the best book for the best nation. This is a traditional opening. Then 'Wa ba'd' signals the main introduction. The author states that 'ilm al-tawhid (the science of God's oneness) is a noble science of great standing, honored by its relation to God, and it is the foundation (أساس) of every beneficial and useful science. This establishes the importance of the subject. Next, he explains that the creed of tawhid (that God alone is deserving of lordship and worship) is a universal message that came with every prophet; it never changes or alters regardless of time or place. This guards against the misunderstanding that religious truth changes. In contrast, the specific laws (shara'i') built upon this creed can change according to the circumstances of each nation, as indicated by Quran 5:48. The author then notes that with the completion of human development, the final message (Islam) came, containing a tolerant (samha') law suitable for all times and places, encompassing general principles and chief virtues, bringing happiness in this world and the next, with ease (yusr) and absence of hardship (haraj). Thus, the passage sets the stage for the book by affirming the unchanging core of tawhid and the adaptable nature of its derived laws, with Islam as the final and comprehensive revelation.

The Dignity of Tawhid and its Unchanging Creed
The editor praises tawhid and explains the fixity of creed vs. change of laws.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the science of tawhid is considered noble and how its core creed remains unchanged while subsidiary laws may vary across nations.

Core Idea
The creed of tawhid is unchanging; laws change, but Islam is the final perfect law.

This passage from the editor's introduction establishes the foundational importance of the science of tawhid. It explains that while the essential belief in Allah's oneness never changes, the legal systems built upon it were tailored to different communities. The final revelation of Islam is presented as the complete and eternal law for all humanity.

This is the central claim of the passage.
3

Commentary
This passage is an introduction written by a modern editor of Sharh al-Maqasid. It begins with the basmalah and a traditional praise of Allah and the Prophet. The editor then declares that the science of tawhid (Islamic theology) is of great dignity because of its subject matter—Allah—and that it is the foundation of all beneficial knowledge. The creed of tawhid, which affirms Allah's oneness and His exclusive right to lordship and worship, is unchanging, though the legal systems based on it varied among earlier nations. The final revelation of Islam, being the last and most complete, is a tolerant law for all times and places. The editor then links this to the science of kalam (dialectical theology), which has the same subject matter and is used to establish and defend the creed. He mentions that scholars have authored many works on kalam, with varying levels of depth. Among the most profound and comprehensive books are: al-Matalib al-'Aliya by Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, al-'Aqa'id al-Nasafiyya by al-Nasafi, al-Mawaqif by al-Iji, Tawali' al-Anwar by al-Baydawi, and al-Maqasid by Sa'd al-Din al-Taftazani. He singles out al-Maqasid as particularly difficult but valuable because it covers rational investigations and fills gaps left by earlier works. The editor then reflects on the pedagogical value of studying difficult books: engaging with challenging texts develops strong intellectual faculties and a distinct scholarly personality, unlike studying only easy works. He notes that al-Maqasid was studied in al-Azhar during a time when scholars were devoted to deep study.

Commentary
This passage is a polemical introduction that argues for the necessity of the science of kalam (dialectical theology) in confronting modern heresies. The author begins by posing a rhetorical question: can we learn from the origin of kalam and its continuance? He then states that the negative answer (that Muslims no longer need kalam) is false, because we need it to refute the tyranny of the twentieth-century Jahmiyyah (anthropomorphists who also denied the uncreatedness of the Qur'an). He contrasts the old Jahmiyyah, who said the Qur'an is created, with the modern Jahmiyyah (Orientalism and missionary followers) who assert the human authorship of the Qur'an — that Muhammad composed it as a reflection of Arabian society. Next, he asks how to debate destructive modern schools: the Nominalists (who deny that the word 'Allah' refers to a reality), the Empiricists (exemplified by Feuerbach's progression from God to reason to man), and the Rationalists (who call this the age of enlightenment and seek to replace religion with reason, as per Wolff). He then compares the ancient Batiniyyah (esotericists) who denied God's existence by saying He is neither existent nor non-existent, to the modern Batiniyyah (nihilism of Nechaev, atheism of Marx, destructiveness of Freud, and Darwinian evolution) who declare religion a myth and prophets liars. He cites verses from the Qur'an condemning their words. He then asks what intellectual weapons the 'school of the Qur'an' has prepared to fight these new waves of atheism, noting that the arrogant atheism uses arguments youth are unfamiliar with — such as the indivisible atom, the individual substance, and Sartre's existential concepts — and that they have not been trained in these deceptive modern standards. He ends with a call to scholars of 'Aqeedah to respond.

Commentary
This passage defines the science of jadal (dialectic) and distinguishes it from kalam. The author begins by recalling that kalam defends Islamic creedal beliefs using rational arguments and tools. Jadl is either a universal method or a specialized art—this shapes its logical status.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and illustrative survey of the term 'jadl' (dialectic/disputation). It begins by contrasting Aristotle and Ibn Sina on the nature of jadl. Core Idea
The Nizamiyya schools were a landmark in Islamic educational history.

This passage from al-Taftazani's Sharh al-Maqasid describes a key moment in Islamic institutional history: the founding of the Nizamiyya schools by the vizier Nizam al-Mulk. These schools became the model for later madrasas and spread across the Islamic world. Let's read what the text says.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the concise style and the key points: who founded them, why they were named, and their spread.
2
Identify the founder and naming
Note that the schools were created by Nizam al-Mulk, a vizier under Alp Arslan and Malik Shah. They were named after him.
3
Consider their significance
Think about why al-Taftazani mentions their majesty and spread. These schools were centers of learning, especially for Shafi'i jurisprudence and Ash'ari theology.

ثم كانت المدارس وكان إنشاؤها على يد الوزير نظام الملك وزر لألب أرسلان والملك شاه و نسبت هذه المدارس إلى منشئها نظام الملك فعرفه باسم المدارس النظامية وكانت غاية في الجلال والعظمة . ثم كانت كثيرة العدد شملت الأمصار والبلدان .
Then the schools were established, and their creation was by the hand of the vizier Nizam al-Mulk, who served Alp Arslan and Malik Shah. These schools were attributed to their founder Nizam al-Mulk, so they became known as the Nizamiyya schools. They were of utmost majesty and grandeur. Then they became numerous in number, encompassing the cities and countries.
Source pages p. 87

Commentary
This passage describes the establishment of the Nizamiyya schools by the vizier Nizam al-Mulk during the reign of Alp Arslan and Malik Shah. It notes that these schools were named after their founder, and they were highly esteemed and widespread.
