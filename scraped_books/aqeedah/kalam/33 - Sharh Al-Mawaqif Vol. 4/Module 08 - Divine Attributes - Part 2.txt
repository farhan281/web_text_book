Defending the Proof of Infinite Motions
Objection-and-answer commentary on a proof about infinite motions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the commentator defends the proof of infinite motions against the objection that volitional motion undermines its validity.

Core Idea
The homogeneity of the celestial sphere saves the proof even for volitional motion.

This passage tackles a critical objection to a key proof about celestial motions. Al-Muhakkam argues that if the sphere's motion is volitional, the proof fails because will is indivisible. The commentator refutes this by appealing to the simple, homogeneous nature of the celestial sphere, showing that will is proportionally distributed in every part. Understanding this response deepens our grasp of how kalam arguments handle counterexamples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scope of the proof
Read the opening line to understand that the claim is general but the proof is specific. Not every detail of the claim is directly proven.
This sets up the defensive move.
2
Understand al-Tusi's apology
Al-Tusi defends the proof by noting that the purpose was to show impossibility of forms causing infinite motions. The proof suffices for that purpose.
3
Grasp al-Muhakkam's objection
Al-Muhakkam counters: the proof only works if motion is natural, not voluntary. For voluntary motion, will is not divided with the body.
This is the core challenge.
4
Follow the commentator's response
The commentator argues that because the sphere is simple and homogeneous, the imprinted form pervades all parts equally, giving each part power and proportional will.
This preserves the proof's validity.
5
Reflect on the significance of homogeneity
Consider why the sphere's simplicity is crucial. Without homogeneity, the objection would stand. This illustrates how physical assumptions underpin theological proofs.

The Simultaneity of Cause and Effect
Sanusi argues that the cause and effect are simultaneous.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sanusi insists that the cause's bringing into existence (ījād) and the effect's existence must be simultaneous, not temporally separate.

Core Idea
Cause and effect are not two distinct moments; they are one and the same reality.

One of the most important principles in Islamic theology is that a cause and its effect cannot be separated in time. Sanusi addresses a common misunderstanding—that the causing happens in one moment and the effect appears later. He explains why this is impossible by looking at what it means for something to be a cause. Let's follow his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the tight logical structure: definition, analogy, application, conclusion.
2
Understand the definition of causing
Sanusi defines causing (al-ījāb) as the effect's existence being dependent on the cause's existence. If the cause ceases, the effect ceases. This sets up the argument.
This dependence is what will rule out temporal separation.
3
Examine the breaking analogy
The author uses the example of breaking (al-kasr) to illustrate that the act of breaking and the occurrence of breakage are not two separate things. This is key to understanding the identity of ījād and existence.
Try to see how the analogy forces us to see the cause-effect relationship as one unitary event.
4
Follow the conclusion
Sanusi concludes that there is no bringing into existence from the cause in a state of non-existence. Any imagined temporal gap is false. He extends this to both necessitating and voluntary causes.
The final line emphasises that this holds for all types of causes—an important point for the divine attributes debate.

Definitions of Cause as Changing or Renewing the Ruling
The commentator presents and critiques definitions of cause based on change or renewal of the ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why defining cause as 'changing the ruling' or 'renewing the ruling' fails for eternal attributes and for attributes that arise at the first moment of existence.

Core Idea
Cause does not always require change; eternal causes are changeless.

The commentator examines two proposed definitions of cause: one as what changes the ruling of its locus, and another as that by which the ruling is renewed. Both seem intuitive, but they exclude important cases like eternal divine attributes and attributes that come into existence at the first moment of their subject. Let's see why these definitions are inadequate.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to get the meaning.
Notice the technical terms: علة, حكم, محل, تغير, تجدد.
2
Identify the two definitions
The passage presents two definitions: (1) cause is what changes the ruling of its locus, and (2) cause is that by which the ruling is renewed. Understand how they differ.
The second definition uses تجدد (renewal) instead of تغير (change).
3
Understand the critique of eternal attributes
Both definitions exclude eternal attributes like God's knowledge. Why? Because eternal attributes have no change or renewal, yet they are causes (e.g., God's knowledge causes His omniscience).
This shows that cause does not require temporal change.
4
Understand the critique of first-moment attributes
The first definition also excludes attributes that occur at the very first moment of their subject's existence, like a pitch pot's blackness. There is no prior ruling to change from, so the definition fails.
This highlights that a cause can bring about a new ruling without changing an existing one.
5
Reflect on the implications
Consider what these counterexamples teach us about the nature of causality in kalam. A definition of cause must cover eternal and instantaneous cases.
Think about how a better definition might avoid these pitfalls.

Two Ash'ari Views on the Cause of Vision
Al-Iji presents two opinions on the cause of vision and their implications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two positions within the Ash'ari school on whether the existence of a substance is the cause of vision, and the implications for the relationship between existence and essence.

Core Idea
Whether existence causes vision determines if it is distinct from essence or identical to it.

This passage from al-Iji's Mawaqif presents a key intra-Ash'ari debate about the cause of the vision (ru'ya) of God. The discussion centers on whether the existence (wujud) of a substance (jawhar) serves as a cause ('illa) for vision, and what that entails for the relationship between existence and essence (dhat). Two positions are outlined, each with different theological commitments and proponents.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to get an overview of the two positions. Notice the logical flow: the first position leads to a consequence, the second avoids a problem.
Pay attention to the terms 'cause', 'existence', 'essence', 'increase', and 'identical'.
2
Identify the first position and its consequence
The first position holds that the existence of a substance is the cause for vision. This leads to the necessity of saying that existence is an increase (ziyada) over the essence, because existence is shared between substance and accident.
This consequence is problematic because it implies that existence is a separate attribute.
3
Identify the second position and its advantage
The second position holds that existence is identical to the essence. Therefore, existence cannot be a cause of vision (since it is not separate), and there is no problem of increase.
This position avoids the theological difficulty of adding an attribute to the essence.
4
Note the proponents and the ilzam argument
The first position is attributed to al-Qadi (al-Baqillani) and the majority of Ash'aris; the second to al-Ash'ari himself. The commentator clarifies that those who deny existence as a cause do not deny vision itself; instead, they use the existence argument to refute the opposing view via ilzam (requiring).
This shows that the debate is about the metaphysical status, not the reality of vision.

Misconception: Some may think that a necessary judgment must also have a cause, assuming condition and cause are the same.
Correction: The passage shows that while the condition is agreed upon, whether a cause exists is disputed; they are distinct concepts.

Checkpoint
What is agreed upon concerning the necessary judgment of omniscience?
A) It has a cause.
B) It does not have a condition.
C) It does not exist without its condition.
D) It is unconditioned.

Key Takeaway
A necessary judgment always requires a condition, but whether it requires a cause is a point of scholarly disagreement.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Think of a core belief you hold about God that you consider absolutely necessary. What condition (like life for knowledge) must that belief rest on? Have you ever examined that condition, or do you just assume it? How does this awareness affect your certainty?
?
The lesson shows that even for necessary judgments, the cause can be disputed. Is there a point in your faith where you demand a clear cause for something, while neglecting the condition? How can you shift your focus to appreciating the conditions God has set?
?
When you explain God's attributes to others, do you emphasize conditions or causes? Reflect on a time you may have overemphasized a cause without acknowledging the condition. How might acknowledging the condition deepen your understanding and humility?
