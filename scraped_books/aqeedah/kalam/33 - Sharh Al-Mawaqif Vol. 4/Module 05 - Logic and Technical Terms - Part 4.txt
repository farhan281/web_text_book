Condition as Locus of Judgment and the Role of Cause
Explains condition as locus and distinguishes cause as attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the locus of a judgment (mahall al-hukm) is a condition for the judgment, not a cause; the cause is an attribute of the locus.

Core Idea
The locus is a condition, not a cause; the attribute is the true cause.

In causal analysis, we must distinguish between the thing that receives a judgment (the locus) and the attribute that brings about the judgment. This passage explains why the locus cannot be the cause and demonstrates how it serves as a condition.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on locus and cause
Read the Arabic text and translation focusing on the fourth point. Note the key statement: 'الشرط قد يكون محل الحكم، والعلة صفته'.
The author then explains why the locus is not a cause: it does not affect, the attribute affects.
2
Understand the commentary on conditionality
The commentary elaborates that the locus is a condition for the judgment insofar as the cause depends on the locus. If the cause is outside the locus, the conditionality may not hold.
The qualification 'without mediation' indicates the locus is directly a condition for the judgment.
3
Reflect on the deeper reason
The commentary ends with an argument: the locus cannot be the cause because a single thing cannot be both receptive (locus) and active (cause), and the attribute of being knowing is not caused by its locus.
This reinforces the distinction.

It is not impossible for the condition to be non-existent, like the absence of opposites of knowledge relative to its existence, for there is no meaning of condition except that upon which the conditioned depends in its existence, not that which affects the existence of the conditioned, so that it may be non-existent. Some held that the condition must be existential. Third: The condition may be multiple, meaning that for one conditioned there are conditions such that its non-existence follows from the non-existence of each one, like life and the absence of opposites relative to the existence of knowledge; or composite, meaning that several things are one condition for the conditioned. Fourth: The condition may be the locus of the judgment, and the cause is its attribute, meaning that the locus of the judgment may not be a cause for the judgment because it does not affect, rather what affects is the attribute of that locus which is the cause, as you have learned; but the locus of the judgment is a condition for the judgment insofar as its existence depends on it. [Commentary:] His saying: (the condition) i.e. without mediation, so its division into multiple and composite becomes apparent, and it is not valid that the parts of the composite are also conditions, making it multiple. His saying: (as you have learned that the cause is an attribute that necessitates a judgment for its locus). His saying: (it is a condition for the judgment etc.) i.e. insofar as the existence of the cause depends on it, and that is when the cause is inherent in the locus of the judgment, and whatever is a condition for the existence of the cause is a condition for the judgment, and it may not be a condition for the judgment based on the permissibility of the cause being external to the locus of the judgment; he restricted it with 'insofar as' because it is impossible for the judgment to depend on it apart from this respect, due to the impossibility of the effect depending on a condition. His saying: (or composite) the difference between it and the multiple—even though what depends on the composite depends on each of its parts, so that upon which it depends also becomes multiple here—is that the dependence here is essentially on the whole, and dependence on the parts is by mediation, and this is not the case in the multiple; also, the composite is a single essence, and this is not the case with the multiple mentioned. His saying: (because it does not affect) not because a single thing cannot be both receptive and active, but because it is necessary that the attribute of being knowing is not caused by other than knowledge, and it is not a locus for it.

Commentary
This passage defines the nature of a condition (shart) in the context of causal relationships. It begins by stating that a condition may be non-existent, such as the absence of opposites of knowledge being a condition for the existence of knowledge. The author clarifies that a condition is merely that upon which the conditioned depends, not that which produces an effect. Some scholars, however, require the condition to be existent. The passage then lists two types of conditions under a third point: a condition may be multiple, meaning several independent conditions each necessary for the conditioned, like life and the absence of opposites for knowledge; or composite, meaning several things together form a single condition. The fourth point discusses the condition as the locus of the judgment: the locus itself is not a cause but its attribute is the cause; however, the locus is a condition for the judgment because the judgment's existence depends on the locus. The commentary then explains that the condition here is without mediation, and distinguishes between multiple and composite conditions: for a composite, dependence is directly on the whole, while for parts it is indirect, whereas for multiple conditions each is directly necessary. The commentary also explains that when the cause is inherent in the locus, the condition for the cause's existence is also a condition for the judgment, but if the cause is external, it may not hold. Finally, the commentary addresses why the locus cannot be a cause: a single thing cannot be both receptive and active, and the attribute of being knowing is not caused by anything other than knowledge itself.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The locus of a judgment is the cause of that judgment.
Correction: The locus is only a condition; the cause is an attribute inherent in the locus.
