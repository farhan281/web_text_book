Why the Author Used the Dual Pronoun
Commentator explains the feminine dual pronoun reference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the commentator explains the use of the feminine dual pronoun 'ahaduhuma' as referring to the implied 'two matters' rather than the investigations.

Core Idea
The dual pronoun refers to the implied 'two matters', not the investigations.

In this passage, the commentator addresses a subtle point of grammar and logic in the author's phrasing. The author used the dual pronoun 'ahaduhuma' (one of them), and the commentator explains that it refers to the two matters (amrayn) implied by the context, not to the investigations (abhath) themselves. This clarification ensures consistency in the text.

Core Idea
Dispositional potentiality is not a real quality but a relational concept.

He argues that the supposed evidence from intensity and weakness is inconclusive, and that the concept reduces to essential potentiality with a relational twist. This lesson traces his line of reasoning and its implications for the nature of change and the subject of accidents.

Commentary
This passage presents the commentator's (al-Sharif al-Jurjani) critique of the argument that dispositional potentiality (al-imkan al-isti'dadi) is an existing quality. The commentator notes that the later scholars made disposition a fourth type of quality (kayfiyya), but he refutes this. He argues that the susceptibility of disposition to intensity and weakness is merely a necessary attribute derived from the closeness of emanation from the cause; it does not prove an additional quality. The truth, he says, is that 'dispositional potentiality' is the same as essential potentiality (al-imkan al-dhati) but measured relative to the closeness of one of its sides (existence or non-existence) according to conditions; the difference is only conceptual. If so, each event can have its own disposition without needing a subject. Even if we grant it is existent, it does not force the subject to be matter in the sense the philosophers intend; a separate substance (jawhar mujarrad) could be the subject for a temporal substance or accident, like intellects and souls. The philosophers cannot generalize matter to include separate substances without undermining their own conclusions (e.g., that intellects have perfections in act). The commentator then mentions the view of the ancients (al-qudama') that disposition is not an existing thing but merely a change from state to state, and such change cannot occur in non-existence, so the event must have a prior state. The passage ends abruptly with 'and it is refuted' (wa yuradd...), indicating a response to follow.

Defending the Necessity of the Judgment
Responds to the objection that the claim of necessity is disputed, citing consensus of rational people and interpreting Sufi statements metaphorically.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

In this passage, the author defends his claim by arguing that this issue is not actually disputed by any rational person of significance. The commentator begins by noting that the author's own definition of General Matters (from the beginning of the Second Station) is 'that which is not specific to any division of existents' (i.e., not specific to the Necessary, substance, or accident). If that definition is used, it is proper to include causality because causality is not specific to any one category. However, according to the Ahl al-Sunnah, this lack of specialty is not apparent in causality because their established principles deny any real causal relation between contingent things. This was already stated in the Fourth Goal of the Fifth Observatory on the Rulings of Theoretic Inquiry, and will be reiterated in the Tenth Goal: there is no relation or causality between contingent things; rather, one is created after another solely by the running of habit (ʿādah). Given that, one might think discussing causality in General Matters is a digression. But the commentator argues that since the majority of the observatory's investigations concern causality, treating it as a digression is far-fetched. Therefore, the commentator suggests that the proper basis for including causality is the interpretation of General Matters that he himself transmitted (the one not specific to divisions), not the author's own interpretation (which may be narrower). He also notes that it is plausible to read 'ūrida' (he presented) in the commentary in the passive voice to avoid conflict.

Commentary
This passage is a statement of the Ash'ari position on the attribution of multiple effects to one simple cause, followed by the philosophers' objection and commentary. The passage begins by affirming that it is permissible (يجوز) in the Ash'ari view that multiple effects be attributed to a single simple cause (مؤثر واحد بسيط). The author explains that all possible things, however numerous, are attributed directly (بلا واسطة) to God, despite His being free from composition (منزه عن التركيب). The philosophers (الحكماء) forbid this unless there is multiplicity of an instrument (آلة), condition (شرط), or receptacle (قابل). The commentator then explains each phrase: 'meaning the Ash'aris' specifies the group; 'simple' means no composition, and the controversy with philosophers is whether aspects must be multiple; 'without mediation' because indirect attribution is also accepted by philosophers; 'except with multiplicity' addresses a potential objection about exhaustive enumeration, noting that other types of multiplicity are not needed because these three suffice. The commentator also justifies why the author only mentions Ash'aris, even though Mu'tazilites also hold this view, because Mu'tazilites sometimes attribute effects to other creatures, and Maturidis are generally included with Ash'aris. The passage thus establishes the core disagreement.
