Defining Opposites: The Conditions of Time and Aspect
Defines opposites and explains the conditions of unity of time and aspect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of opposites (al-mutaqābilān) and why the conditions of unity of time and aspect are necessary to distinguish true opposites from correlates and other relations.

Core Idea
Opposites require both unity of time and unity of aspect to be truly opposite.

This passage from al-Mawaqif defines the philosophical concept of opposites. The discussion begins with the basic definition: two things that do not meet in one time. But this simple formula requires careful refinement. We must also consider cases of metaphorical meeting, and the special case of correlates like father and son. Let's see how the author unpacks these conditions.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition and conditions
Read the matn: 'Al-mutaqābilān amrān lā yajtamiʿān fī zamān wāḥid.' Notice the two conditions mentioned in the commentary: unity of time and unity of aspect.
The definition comes from the philosophers (al-ḥukamā').
2
Understand the need for explicit mention of time
The commentary explains that the word 'meeting' already implies simultaneous existence, but explicit mention of 'in one time' prevents metaphorical interpretations where meeting might be understood as occurrence at different times.
This also allows opposites to meet in different entities at the same time, which is permissible.
3
Grasp the addition of 'from one aspect'
This condition is added to include correlates (al-mutadāyifān) like father and son, which are not true opposites because they occur from different aspects. True opposites must be from the same aspect.
This also addresses a potential counterexample to the definition.

Commentary
This passage provides the definition of opposites (al-mutaqābilān) by the philosophers: they are two things that do not meet in one time. The commentary then explains why the condition 'in one time' is explicitly stated: to rule out the metaphorical understanding of 'meeting' as mere occurrence in a single entity over different times, and also to exclude cases where opposites meet in different entities at the same time (which is permissible). An additional condition 'from one aspect' is introduced to include correlates (al-mutadāyifān) like fatherhood and sonship, which occur to the same person from different aspects. The passage then discusses the classification of opposites based on whether one is the negation of the other. The commentator clarifies the meaning of 'meeting' as occurrence in a mode of companionship, which inherently requires unity of time but not necessarily unity of entity. He also addresses potential objections, such as the inclusion of similars in the definition, and explains that 'meeting' here is used in a broad sense (ittiṣāf) to cover affirmation/negation and privation/possession, and that the phrase 'in one entity' avoids problems with parts of a body having different attributes.

Definitions of Opposite: Philosophers vs. Theologians
Contrasts definitions of opposite between philosophers and theologians.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the philosophers' definition of 'opposite' is more specific than the theologians' and how relational opposites and similar things fit into these definitions.

Core Idea
Philosophers restrict 'opposite' to things with utmost difference; theologians include relational opposites and similar things.

This passage from Sharh al-Mawaqif compares the definitions of 'opposite' used by philosophers and theologians. It clarifies that relational opposites (like father and son) are included in the theologians' definition but excluded by philosophers who require utmost difference. The commentary also discusses whether similar things that cannot coexist are included.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and comparison. It states that the term 'opposite' (ḍidd) is more specific in the usage of philosophers than in the usage of theologians because relational opposites (al-mutaḍāyifān) are included in the theologians' definition (which does not specify a certain aspect) but not in the philosophers' definition (which requires utmost difference). Similarly, some similar things (al-mutamāthilān) that cannot coexist are also included in the theologians' definition. The passage then introduces the second type of opposition: one opposite being a negation of the other, which divides into two kinds depending on consideration of a subject. The commentary explains that the inclusion of relational opposites depends on the disputed existence of relational opposites; if they exist, they are included in the theologians' definition. It also clarifies that the earlier statement about similar things not being included means in all their individuals, but the imaginer tried to use it to force them as a division. Finally, it explains that the 'relation to a subject' refers to the opposition of privation and possession (or negation and affirmation) with respect to a capable subject, as discussed in other works.

The Second Type of Opposition: Negation and its Divisions
Explains the second type of opposition and its subdivision.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second type of opposition where one opposite is a negation of the other, and its division into two categories based on relation to a subject.

Core Idea
The opposition of negation divides into two kinds: with or without consideration of a potential subject.

After discussing the first type of opposition (the definition of opposite), the commentator moves to the second type: one opposite being a negation of the other. This type is further divided into two, depending on whether the relation to a subject is considered. The passage also references discussions from other works like Sharh Hikmat al-‘Ayn and al-Tajrid.

Classification of Opposites
Al-Jurjani classifies opposites into four types using a dichotomous schema.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourfold classification of opposites into correlatives, contraries, privation/disposition, and negation/affirmation, and the criteria for each.

Core Idea
Opposites fall into four categories based on existentiality and relationality.

After establishing the definition of opposites, al-Jurjani presents the well-known classification that divides opposites based on whether both are existential or one is privative, and further according to how they are conceived. This classification is a standard tool in logic and kalam for analyzing opposed concepts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first division
Notice that the classification first divides opposites into those where both are existential (positive) and those where one is existential and the other privative.
'Existential' here means the concept does not involve negation.
2
Understand correlatives vs. contraries
Within existential opposites, if each is conceived in relation to the other, they are correlatives (e.g., fatherhood and sonship). If not, they are contraries (e.g., whiteness and blackness).

The key is whether the privative term implies a potential for the existential attribute.
4
Review the gloss on 'existential'
The gloss clarifies that 'existential' means negation is not part of the concept of either term, which helps distinguish the two main branches.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection to the classification of opposites and the author's detailed response. The objection states that both opposites could be privations, as in the case of blindness and non-blindness. The answer begins by distinguishing absolute non-existence (al-‘adm al-mutlaq) from relative non-existence (al-‘adm al-mudaf). Absolute non-existence does not oppose itself because it is a single concept. Relative non-existence does not oppose another relative non-existence because they can coexist in any subject that is different from what each is relative to. For example, blindness and non-blindness: if non-blindness means the negation of the absence of sight, it is actually sight itself, so the opposition is between existence and privation. If it means the negation of capacity, then it is a negation of a capacity, so the opposition becomes affirmation and negation. The author then elaborates: for genuine opposition, the two must be referred to a single subject. If there is no intermediary (wasiṭa) between the dispositions (malkatayn) of the two relative privations—for instance, if both are universal concepts like 'thing' or 'possible'—then there is no opposition because they are not related to a single subject. If there is an intermediary, the two privations coexist in it, so the objection is refuted. The commentator's gloss from al-Tajrid is then examined: it suffices to say that if one privation is found in a concept, the other is found in it, but this assumes the existence of a concept that contains both, which is impossible for absolute privations. Finally, the example of squint and sight capacity on a wall is addressed: the opposition between them is not essential but derivative, so they fall outside the definition of opposites.
