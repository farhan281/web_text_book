Definition of Numbers as Sums of Units
The author defines numbers as composites of their units and rejects the need for an accidental form.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Numbers are exactly the sum of their units, nothing more.

This passage from al-Mawaqif clarifies the essential nature of numbers. The author argues that numbers like ten are simply the sum of their units, and any claim that a formal part is required is superficial. Let's read carefully.

Aristotle's Argument on the Composition of Ten
Aristotle's argument and the objection-answer dialectic about ten's composition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Aristotle's argument that ten is not composed of other numbers, including the supporting reductio and the responses to objections.

Core Idea
Ten can be conceived without its supposed parts, so those parts are not essential.

Aristotle's argument against the composition of numbers may seem arcane, but it illustrates a key logical principle: if a thing can be fully conceived without some alleged component, that component is not part of its essence. In this passage, the author presents Aristotle's reasoning along with objections and answers. This dialectical exchange sharpens our understanding of what it means for something to be 'composed' of other things.

Guided Path
1
Read the main argument
Read the opening claim: ten is not three and seven, etc., because one can conceive ten without those numbers. This is the core idea.
Notice the phrase 'بكنهها' (in its essence).
2
Examine the supporting reductio
Study the argument about equal possibility: why not two and eight instead of three and seven? This leads to preference without preponderator or composition from all pairs leading to independence.
The term 'ترجيح بلا مرجح' is a key logical principle.
3
Consider the first objection and answer
The objection says maybe each pair constitutes ten via a common element (the units). The answer shows that the common element is precisely the units, conceding the point.
The objector inadvertently admits the conclusion.
4
Analyze the second objection and answer
Another objection: the same equal possibility problem applies to units. The answer: units suffice for ten, so the particularities of numbers are irrelevant.
This answer returns to the first argument.

وقال أرسطو: إنها (أي العشرة) ليست ثلاثة وسبعة ولا أربعة وستة، وغير ذلك من الأعداد التي يتوهم تركبها منها؛ لإمكان تصور العشرة بكنهها مع الغفلة عن هذه الأعداد، فإنك إذا تصورت حقيقة كل واحدة من وحداتها من غير شعور بخصوصيات الأعداد المندرجة تحتها، فقد تصورت حقيقة العشرة بلا شبهة فلا يكون شيء من تلك الأعداد داخلاً في حقيقتها. بل عشرة مرة واحدة. وربما يستدل على ذلك بأن تركب العشرة من الاثنين والثمانية ليس أولى من تركبها من الثلاثة والسبعة أو الأربعة والستة، أو الخمسة والخمسة، فإن ترکبت من بعضها لزم الترجيح بلا مرجح، وإن ترکبت من الكل لزم استغناء الشيء عما هو ذاتي له؛ لأن كل واحد منها كاف في تقويمها فيستغني عما عداه. فإن قلت: جاز أن يكون كل واحد منها مقوماً لها باعتبار القدر المشترك بين جميعها، إذ لا مدخل في تقويمها لخصوصياتها. قلت: القدر المشترك بينها الذي يفي بحقيقة العشرة هو الوحدات فما ذكرته اعتراف بالمطلوب. نعم ربما ينقض الدليل بأن تركبها من الوحدات أيضاً ليس أولى من تركبها من تلك الأعداد، فيلزم الترجيح بلا مرجح؛ لأن اشتمال تلك الأعداد على الوحدات لا يفيد ترجيحاً. ويجاب بأنه لما كفت الوحدات في تحصيل العشرة لم يكن لخصوصيات الأعداد المندرجة فيها مدخل في تحصلها، وهذا بالحقيقة رجوع إلى الاستدلال الأول.
And Aristotle said: It (i.e., ten) is not three and seven, nor four and six, nor other numbers of which it is imagined to be composed; because it is possible to conceive ten in its essence while being heedless of these numbers. For if you conceive the reality of each of its units without awareness of the particularities of the numbers subsumed under it, then you have conceived the reality of ten without doubt, so none of those numbers is included in its reality. Rather it is ten once. And perhaps one may argue in support of that by [saying:] that the composition of ten from two and eight is not more preferable than its composition from three and seven, or four and six, or five and five. For if it were composed of some of them, then preference without a preference-giver would be necessary; and if it were composed of all of them, then the thing would be independent of that which is intrinsic to it, because each one of them is sufficient for constituting it, so it would be independent of what is other than it. If you say: It is possible that each one of them constitutes it with respect to the common element among them, since their particularities have no entry in constituting it. You say: The common element among them that suffices for the reality of ten is the units, so what you have mentioned is an admission of the desired conclusion. Yes, perhaps the argument may be refuted [by saying] that its composition from units also is not more preferable than its composition from those numbers, so preference without a preference-giver would be necessary, because the inclusion of those numbers in the units does not give preference. And it is answered: Since the units suffice in achieving ten, the particularities of the numbers subsumed under it have no entry in its actualization, and this is in reality a return to the first argument.
Source pages p. 41

Commentary
This passage is a proof by argument. It presents Aristotle's view that ten is not composed of other numbers (like three and seven) because one can conceive ten in its essence without thinking of those numbers. The argument is supported by a reductio: if ten were composed of some pair (e.g., two and eight) rather than another, there would be no reason for the preference (tarjīḥ bilā murjīḥ). If it were composed of all such pairs, each pair would be sufficient to constitute ten, making the other pairs superfluous. An objection is raised: perhaps each pair constitutes ten under a common element (the units). The answer is that the common element is exactly the units, so this concedes the point. A further objection is that composition from units also suffers from the same problem of equal possibility. The answer is that units suffice for actualizing ten, and the particularities of the numbers subsumed under the units are irrelevant. This returns to the first argument: units are sufficient, so numbers are not needed.

Conceptualizing by essence requires conceiving its essential attributes.

In this passage, the commentator clarifies a key condition for conceptualizing a thing by its essence (bi-l-kunh). He explains that such conceptualization is only possible when one has a complete conception of the thing's essential attributes (dhātiyyāt). This lays the foundation for the argument about numbers.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary that explains and defends the conceptualization argument. It clarifies that conceptualizing a thing by its essence (bi-l-kunh) requires conceiving its essential attributes (dhātiyyāt). When you conceive the units of a number without considering their specific arrangement into other numbers, you have a complete conception of that number.
