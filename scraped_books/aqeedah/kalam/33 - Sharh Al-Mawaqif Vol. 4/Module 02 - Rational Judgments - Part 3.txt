The Generality of the Proof of Tatbiq
The author explains that the proof of tatbiq is general and criticizes its restricted applications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the proof of tatbiq (application) works and why the author insists it applies universally to all existing things, not just those with coexistence and order.

Core Idea
The proof of tatbiq is universal; restricting it to special cases makes it invalid.

The proof of tatbiq is a central argument for the impossibility of infinite regress. Some scholars tried to limit its application by requiring two conditions: coexistence in time and one-to-one order. In this passage, the author explains why such restrictions are unfounded and, in fact, would destroy the proof itself. We will work through the argument step by step.

Examine the analogy of mountains and pebbles

One-to-one correspondence is automatic for continuous lengths but requires detailed mapping for discrete items.

Analyze the proposed restrictions
Some scholars concluded that the proof requires coexistence and order. Read the author's rejection of this restriction and understand his reasoning: limiting the proof to some cases means it fails for others, thus undermining the proof entirely.
The author argues that if the proof is not universal, it is invalid because it is open to counterexamples.

Commentary
This passage is a combined argument and commentary on the proof of application (burhān al-taṭbīq) for the impossibility of infinite regress. The author first describes the proof: if two infinite series are aligned, each term of the first should correspond to a term of the second. However, it is possible that many terms of one series correspond to one term of the other, unless the intellect precisely maps each term individually. But the intellect cannot grasp infinite details at once or in finite time, so the application fails and the proof is not straightforward. The author then illustrates with an analogy: if you have two mountain ranges of equal length, applying one end to the other suffices for every part of one to correspond to a part of the second. But with discrete entities like pebbles, you must consider each one individually. From this, some say the proof requires two conditions: that the two series coexist in time and have a one-to-one correspondence (with order). They claim that without these, the proof does not apply to successive events or unordered collections. The author (al-Iji) rejects this restriction, stating that the proof is general and applies to everything that has existence. Restricting it to only those with coexistence and order is an admission that the proof fails for other cases, which invalidates the proof because it is then subject to counterexamples. Thus, the author maintains the proof's universality.

This lesson presents the third argument against infinite regress. Commentary
This passage presents and explains the third argument against infinite regress, along with responses to objections. The argument states: what lies between a specific effect (the last in the chain) and any cause in the supposed infinite chain is finite because it is bounded by two limits—that effect and that cause. Since an infinite cannot be bounded, the entire chain must be finite. The commentary then addresses three possible objections. First, the two series must exist together in actuality; but even if they are purely hypothetical, the proof works. Second, human minds cannot grasp infinite details, but higher powers can. Third, if the two series are actual and distinct, the proof fails because it only shows a single infinite series is possible, but here we have two series that are not distinct (the part and the whole). The commentator then clarifies the phrasing 'what is between this specific effect and every cause' is imprecise because there is no gap between the last effect and its proximate cause. He reformulates: the whole chain does not exceed the finite segment between the effect and some cause except by one cause from the side of the causes. Since each segment is finite, adding one more makes the whole finite. He distinguishes this from a fallacious argument where the starting point shifts; here the starting point (the last effect) is fixed, so the reasoning is sound, analogous to saying if a certain distance does not exceed a league, adding a point keeps it finite. The commentary continues with detailed notes on the phrasing and the need to consider the increase from one side only.

Commentary
This passage presents and explains the third argument against infinite regress, along with responses to objections. The argument states: what lies between a specific effect (the last in the chain) and any cause in the supposed infinite chain is finite because it is bounded by two limits—that effect and that cause. Since an infinite cannot be bounded, the entire chain must be finite. The commentary then addresses three possible objections. First, the two series must exist together in actuality; but even if they are purely hypothetical, the proof works. Second, human minds cannot grasp infinite details, but higher powers can. Third, if the two series are actual and distinct, the proof fails because it only shows a single infinite series is possible, but here we have two series that are not distinct (the part and the whole). The commentator then clarifies the phrasing 'what is between this specific effect and every cause' is imprecise because there is no gap between the last effect and its proximate cause. He reformulates: the whole chain does not exceed the finite segment between the effect and some cause except by one cause from the side of the causes. Since each segment is finite, adding one more makes the whole finite. He distinguishes this from a fallacious argument where the starting point shifts; here the starting point (the last effect) is fixed, so the reasoning is sound, analogous to saying if a certain distance does not exceed a league, adding a point keeps it finite. The commentary continues with detailed notes on the phrasing and the need to consider the increase from one side only.

Misconception: The intuitive proof applies to all cases, including quantities without any condition.
Correction: The passage explicitly states that the intuitive proof does not apply to quantities unless the divisibility into number is assumed for their parts.

Checkpoint
Under what condition does the intuitive proof apply to quantities?
A) It never applies to quantities.
B) It applies only if the quantities are assumed to be divisible into countable units.
C) It applies without any condition.
D) It applies only if the quantities are continuous.

Key Takeaway
The intuitive proof for impossibility of infinite regress requires that the items be 'existing together' and, for quantities, that they be considered countable; the proof of application avoids this requirement through a superposition argument.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
The lesson shows that the intuitive proof has conditions; it does not apply to everything without qualification. Are there areas of your faith where you have relied on an argument without checking its assumptions? Will you today examine one theological claim you hold to see if it truly meets its own conditions?
?
The proof of application works even without assuming countable units. This means God's creation could be finite even if we cannot count its parts. Do you sometimes limit God's power by imagining it only in measurable ways? What is one aspect of His creation—perhaps in your own life—that you have failed to see because you were looking for 'countable' evidence instead of trusting in His infinite wisdom?
?
The contradiction in the proof shows that an infinite quantity leads to absurdity. In your spiritual life, are you caught in an 'infinite regress' of sin and repentance without true change? What 'proof of application' would break that cycle—what concrete superposition of God's mercy and your effort would force a resolution today?
