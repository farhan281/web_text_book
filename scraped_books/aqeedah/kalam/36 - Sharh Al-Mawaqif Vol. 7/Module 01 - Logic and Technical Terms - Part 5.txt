Third Proof: The Infinity of Bodies and Its Refutation
Third proof for the soul's pre-eternity and its refutation using the condition of ordering.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third argument about the infinity of souls and bodies, and the refutation based on the condition of ordering.

Core Idea
Infinite souls can coexist if they are not ordered.

The third proof argues that if souls are originated with bodies, and bodies are infinite, then souls would be infinite and coexist, which is impossible. The refutation shows that the impossibility of infinite coexisting things only applies when they are naturally or positionally ordered; souls are not ordered, so their infinity is permissible.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation of the third proof and its refutation.
This argument is more complex; read carefully.
2
Understand the opponent's argument
If souls are originated, they arise with bodies. Bodies are infinite due to celestial cycles, so souls are infinite. But bodies are sequential; souls coexist after separation, leading to an infinite number of coexisting things, which is impossible.
3
Understand the refutation
The impossibility of infinite coexisting things requires that they be ordered (natural or positional). Souls are not ordered, so infinite souls are possible.
This is a key principle in kalam about actual infinites.

Commentary
This passage presents three objections (wujūh) from an opponent who argues that the soul is pre-eternal (qadīma), along with their refutations. The first objection: every originated thing requires matter, so if the soul were originated, it would be material. The answer: the matter required by origination is broader—it can be matter in which the thing subsists or to which it is attached; a non-material substance can be attached to matter. The second objection: if the soul is not pre-eternal, it cannot be everlasting; but the soul is everlasting by consensus. The implication is that whatever is originated perishes. The answer: denial of the implication—an originated thing is inherently possible of non-existence, but it may be prevented from perishing by another (e.g., God's power). The third objection: if souls are originated, they are originated with bodies, which are infinite due to celestial cycles; thus souls would be infinite. But bodies are sequential, so their infinity is not problematic, whereas souls coexist without order, making an infinite actual collection impossible. The answer: the impossibility of infinite coexistence only applies when things are naturally or positionally ordered; souls are not ordered, so infinite souls are possible.

Aristotle's Principle on Emanation and Refutation of Metempsychosis
Aristotle's condition for emanation and its application against metempsychosis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Aristotle's principle that every originated thing requires a condition from the originated world leads to the refutation of metempsychosis.

Core Idea
Each body gets its own soul; two souls cannot inhabit one body.

In this remark (tanbīh) from al-Jurjānī's commentary, we encounter Aristotle's key principle governing emanation: every originated being must arise from the eternal principle through a condition that is itself originated. This principle not only explains how the soul comes into existence but also provides a decisive argument against metempsychosis—the belief that souls can transmigrate from one body to another. Let's unpack the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its literal English translation. Let the original wording sink in before diving into analysis.
Pay attention to the flow: principle → condition → application to soul → refutation.
2
Identify Aristotle's principle
Notice that Aristotle asserts every originated thing must be traced back to the pre-eternal principle through a condition that is also originated. This prevents circularity and infinite regress.
The phrase 'دفعاً للدور والتسلسل' explains why the condition is needed.
3
Apply the principle to the soul
The condition for the soul's emanation is the origination of the body, because the body is the receptive disposition (qābil mustaʿidd) for the soul's actions. When the body occurs, a soul must emanate due to universal emanation.
This shows the soul cannot exist without a body.
4
See how this refutes metempsychosis
If a body already has a transmigrating soul, the emanation of a new soul would also occur, resulting in two souls for one body—which everyone knows is false. Thus metempsychosis is impossible.
The argument relies on the self-evident fact that each person experiences only one soul.

Aristotle uses this to refute metempsychosis: if a body is already attached to a transmigrating soul, the emanation of a new soul would still occur, resulting in two souls for one body, which is self-evidently false since each person experiences a single soul.

Circularity in Aristotle's Argument and Alternative Refutations of Metempsychosis
Shows circularity in Aristotle's reasoning and presents two alternative ways to refute metempsychosis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Aristotle's argument for the soul's origination is circular and how alternative proofs attempt to refute metempsychosis, along with their limitations.

Core Idea
Aristotle's reasoning on the soul is trapped in a circle; breaking free requires independent proof.

This passage from al-Mawaqif critiques Aristotle's argument for the soul's origination, exposing a circular dependency. To escape the circle, the commentator introduces two alternative ways to disprove metempsychosis, though he ultimately finds them insufficient. Let's trace the logical structure and see why circular reasoning is a serious flaw in kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the circularity
Read the first part where Aristotle proves soul origination via metempsychosis and vice versa. Notice that neither premise is independently established.
Circularity (دور) is when A depends on B and B depends on A without independent proof.
2
Examine the first alternative proof (memory)
Consider the argument that if souls transmigrate, they would remember past lives. Why might this not hold? The commentator notes memory may depend on continued attachment to the body.
The example of someone not remembering being a camel is given.
3
Examine the second alternative proof (uniformity of souls and bodies)
Understand the argument that souls arise uniformly with bodies, so mass deaths would leave souls idle. What is its weakness? The number of animal bodies might equal the number of separated souls.
Historical example: 200,000 killed in one day in Greece.
4
Reflect on the reliability of these alternatives
The commentator concludes that neither way is fully reliable. Ask yourself: does this undermine the entire refutation of metempsychosis?

اعلم أن هذا الذي ذكره أرسطو في حدوث النفس وبطلان التناسخ دور صريح فإنه بين حدوث النفس بلزوم التناسخ على تقدير قدمها وإبطاله ثم بين بطلان التناسخ بحدوث النفس. وإنما يصح له ذلك لو بين أحدهما بطريق آخر مثل مايقال في إبطال التناسخ أنه يلزم تذكرها لأحوالها في البدن الآخر أو أن استعداد الأبدان للنفوس وتكونها (أي حدوث النفوس) على وتيرة واحدة فإنه كلما استعد بدن حدث نفس بخلاف مفارقة النفوس مع حدوث الأبدان إذ قد يتفق وباء (أي فساد هواء) أو جانحة (أي حادثة مستأصلة كالطوفان) أو قتل عام يهلك فيها من النفوس دفعة ما يعلم بالضرورة أنه لم يحدث في ذلك الزمان بخلاف العادة ذلك المبلغ من الأبدان كما نقل من أنه وقع حرب في أرض يونان فقتل في يوم واحد مائتا ألف من الجانبين. ومن المعلوم أنه لم يحدث في ذلك اليوم أبدان بهذا العدد في جوانب العالم لتتعلق بها تلك النفوس المفارقة عن أبدانها فلو كان تعلق النفوس على طريقة التناسخ لزم تعطل بعضها إلى أن يحدث بدن تتعلق به. وليس شيء منها والأظهر منهما (أي من هذين الطريقين الآخرين) يصلح للتعويل إذ لا نسلم لزوم التذكر لأحوالها في البدن السابق لجواز كونه مشروطا بالتعلق به على أنه قد نقل عن بعضهم أنه قال: إني لا تذكر كوني في صورة الجمل. ولا نسلم أن عدد أبدان الحيوانات الصغيرة والكبيرة في البحور والبراري لا يساوي عدد تلك النفوس المفارقة.
Know that what Aristotle mentioned regarding the origination of the soul and the falsity of metempsychosis is explicit circularity. For he proved the origination of the soul by the necessity of metempsychosis on the assumption of its pre-eternity and its falsity, then he proved the falsity of metempsychosis by the origination of the soul. This would be valid for him only if he proved one of them by another way, such as what is said in refuting metempsychosis: that it necessitates that it [the soul] remembers its states in the other body, or that the preparedness of bodies for souls and their becoming (i.e., the origination of souls) proceeds at a uniform rate. It is known that bodies of that number did not occur on that day in the regions of the world to which those souls separated from their bodies could attach. And we do not concede that the number of bodies of small and large animals in the seas and deserts does not equal the number of those separated souls.

Commentary
This passage critiques Aristotle's argument as circular (dawr ṣarīḥ): he proves the soul's origination by the necessity of metempsychosis if it were pre-eternal, then proves metempsychosis false by the soul's origination. To escape circularity, one must prove one of the two independently. The author presents two alternative ways (ṭarīqān) to refute metempsychosis: (1) the transmigrating soul would have to remember its previous states—but this is not conceded because memory might depend on continued attachment; (2) the uniformity of souls and bodies—each body's preparedness yields a new soul, but in events like plagues or wars, many souls leave bodies while few new bodies arise, leading to idle souls. However, neither way is fully reliable: memory is not guaranteed, and the number of animal bodies might equal the number of separated souls. The author thus leaves the criticism unresolved.

First Objection: Free Agent Acts Without Condition
First objection: denying the need for an originated condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first objection to Aristotle's proof: that a free agent can specify events without an originated condition.

Core Idea
Divine will supersedes deterministic conditions.

This lesson introduces a key objection from Islamic theology: that God, as a free agent, does not require a prior condition to create. This challenges Aristotle's assumption that every originated thing must have an originated condition.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection in Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic passage carefully, then the translation. Notice how the objection begins with 'For example, it may be said'.
The Arabic uses 'لا نسلم' (we do not concede) to signal the objection.
2
Understand the concept of a free agent
The objection contrasts the free agent (fāʿil mukhtār) with a necessitating cause (ʿilla mustalzima). A free agent can act without a motive or condition.
3
Connect to the broader argument
Reflect on why denying the need for an originated condition undermines Aristotle's proof for the soul's creation, which relies on such a condition.
This objection is the first of three that target different premises.

And on the basis of the proof by which he refuted metempsychosis, there are objections which you know if the principles we have laid down for you are in your memory, so we will not repeat them for fear of prolixity. For example, it may be said: We do not concede that every originated thing must have an originated condition. Commentary
This passage lists several objections (iʾtirāḍāt) to the argument that Aristotle used to refute metempsychosis. The author refers to earlier principles without repeating them in detail. The first objection: it is not necessary that every originated thing have an originated condition; a free agent (fāʿil mukhtār) can cause events at specific times without a prior condition or motive. The second objection: even if a condition is needed, it need not be the body—another condition could suffice. The third objection: even if the body is the condition, it does not automatically necessitate emanation of a new soul; emanation is only necessary if no transmigrating soul already occupies that body. Finally, it is suggested that Aristotle intended his proof of the soul's origination (not the metempsychosis argument) as the basis for refuting metempsychosis.

And if you mean by it rapid affectability and division into small parts and thinness of the people, then subtlety is applied to these meanings, so you choose that it is not subtle and its visibility like the sky does not follow. Except that its easy formation into any shape it wishes is problematic. Therefore he said: How? And the Omnipotent Chooser may bestow upon it, despite its subtlety and thinness, great strength. For strength is not attached to constitution (al-qiwam) in thinness and thickness, nor is it concerned with smallness and bigness. Do you not see that the constitution of a human is inferior to the constitution of iron and stone, yet you see some of them kill iron and break stone, and produce from themselves what cannot be attributed to thickness of constitution? And you see animals differing in strength not according to difference in constitution and body, as in the lion compared to the donkey. Some people said: They are the earthly souls (al-nufus al-ardiyyah). For if the soul is the manager of the celestial bodies, then it is the celestial soul (al-nafs al-falakiyyah); and if it is the manager of the elements, then it is the earthly soul, i.e., the lower soul. And they are diverse: among them are the earthly angels, and the Prophet, peace be upon him, alluded to them in his saying: 'The angel of mountains, the angel of rains, and the angel of seas came to me.' And in some copies, instead of 'earthly' it is 'karubiyyah' with lightening of the ra', i.e., the cherubim (the close angels). But it was objected that this is inappropriate because the cherubim among the angels are those who are overwhelmed and absorbed in the lights of the majesty of Allah, exalted and high, such that they do not attend to anything else with Him at all, neither to the management of bodies nor to effecting them. And among them are the jinn, and among them are the devils, and other than that. These are the hosts of your Lord; none knows them but He. And some people said: They are the rational souls (al-nufus al-natiqah) that separate. The good ones among those that separate from bodies attach to the good ones among those that are attached, with a kind of attachment, and assist them in good and rightness, and they are the jinn. The evil ones among them attach to the evil ones and assist them in evil and corruption, and they are the devils. And Allah knows best the realities of things.

Commentary
This passage from al-Iji's Sharh al-Mawaqif addresses two interconnected topics: the definition of subtlety (al-latafah) and the classification of souls (al-nufus).

The discussion begins by examining the concept of subtlety. The author notes that a transparent object (lacking color) can still have strength to perform difficult actions and is not quickly affected, which is why we do not see it. He distinguishes two possible meanings of subtlety:
1) If subtlety is equated with transparency, then such an object is considered subtle, but it does not follow that it lacks strength.
2) If subtlety is understood as rapid affectability, divisibility into small parts, and thinness, then the object is not subtle, and it does not follow that it should be visible like the sky. However, the ease with which such a subtle object can be shaped into any form is problematic.

The author resolves this by stating that the Omnipotent Chooser (al-Qadir al-Mukhtar) can bestow great strength upon subtle and thin objects, because strength is independent of the constitution's thinness or thickness, and is not concerned with size. He provides examples: human constitution is inferior to iron and stone, yet humans can kill iron and break stone; animals vary in strength not according to their constitution (e.g., lion vs. donkey).

Then the passage shifts to classifying souls. It presents two main opinions:
- Some say the entities in question are earthly souls (al-nufus al-ardiyyah). These are distinguished from celestial souls (al-nufus al-falakiyyah) which manage the celestial bodies. Earthly souls manage the elements. They are diverse: earthly angels (alluded to in hadith as angels of mountains, rains, seas), jinn, devils, etc. The author notes a variant reading where 'earthly' is replaced by 'karubiyyah' (cherubim), but he rejects this because cherubim are absorbed in contemplation of God and do not engage in management or influence over bodies.
- Others say they are rational souls (al-nufus al-natiqah) that separate from bodies. Good rational souls attach to good attached souls (i.e., humans) and assist them in good – these are the jinn. Evil rational souls attach to evil humans and assist in evil – these are the devils.

The passage ends with a disclaimer: 'And Allah knows best the realities of things.'
