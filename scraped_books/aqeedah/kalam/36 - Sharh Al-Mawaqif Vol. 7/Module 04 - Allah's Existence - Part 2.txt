The Origin of the Human Type and Eternal Genera
Commentator resolves the apparent conflict between the origination of the human type and the eternity of specific forms by genus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the human type can be originated while maintaining that specific forms are eternal by genus.

Core Idea
A new species can arise within an eternal genus without contradiction.

This passage addresses an apparent tension: if the human type is originated, does that contradict the philosophers' view that specific forms are eternal? The commentator offers two explanations: the origination refers to the sphere of fire, or the eternity is by genus, not by species. This clarifies how new types can emerge without negating the eternal nature of generic forms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent contradiction
Read the passage and note that the human type is said to be originated, yet philosophers held that specific forms are eternal. What seems to conflict?
2
Examine the first explanation
The commentator suggests that 'origination' might refer to the sphere of fire produced by celestial motions. How does this avoid the contradiction?
3
Examine the second explanation
Alternatively, the philosophers may mean that specific forms are eternal by genus, not by species. How does this allow for a new species like the human type?
4
Conclude
Both explanations show that origination of a new type does not negate the eternity of the broader genus. This harmonizes the positions.

Commentary
This passage contains two comments (qālahu) from the commentator on the main text. The first comment explains the phrase 'indeed, the individualizing forms in both' from the earlier discussion of celestial and elemental bodies. The commentator clarifies that an individualizing form (ṣūra mushakhkhiṣa) cannot be eternal by its type (nawʿ) or genus (jins), because that would require an infinite regress of further individualizing forms. The only conceivable way for such a form to be eternal is by a general accident (al-ʿaraḍ al-ʿāmm), but that is not meaningful. The second comment addresses the example of the human type being originated. The commentator notes that if the human type were originated, it might seem to contradict the philosophers' view that the human type is eternal. He explains that the philosophers may mean by 'origination' the origination of the sphere of fire (kurat al-nār) through celestial motions via generation and corruption, which is a different type from the fire particles in compounds. Alternatively, the philosophers' statement that 'their specific forms by their genus' indicates that elemental specific forms are eternal by genus, not by individual types, so a new type like human could originate within that genus without contradicting the principle of eternal generic forms. The commentator concludes by noting that the author and the commentator both assert that there is no impossibility in such origination.

Al-Razi's Argument from Composition and Possibility
Al-Razi presents his fourth proof that every body is possible because composite, leading to its origination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi uses the composition of bodies to prove that they are possible and therefore created, and how this relates to the nature of the Necessary Existent.

Core Idea
Composite things cannot be necessary, so bodies are possible and must be produced from non-existence.

Al-Razi's fourth proof for the temporality of bodies is a concise yet powerful argument. He begins with a simple observation: every body is composite. From this, he deduces that bodies cannot be necessary existents, which are necessarily one and non-composite. Instead, they are possible entities that require a producer, and production implies origination from non-existence. This lesson unpacks the logical steps of this proof and its implications for theology.

Al-Razi's Third Proof: Motion or Rest in Pre-Eternity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's argument that an eternal body cannot be either moving or at rest in pre-eternity, and why that refutes the existence of an eternal body.

Core Idea
Motion and rest are accidents that require a beginning, so no body can be eternal.

This lesson unpacks Imām al-Rāzī's third proof for the originatedness of bodies. Building on earlier arguments, al-Rāzī presents a concise disjunction: any eternal body would be either moving or at rest in pre-eternity. Both options are impossible, so an eternal body cannot exist. The author's brief treatment assumes familiarity with earlier elaborations, so we will clarify the reasoning step by step.

Al-Nazzam's Argument and the Problem of Accident Permanence
Al-Iji presents al-Nazzam's argument and the problem of circularity for accident permanence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Nazzam's argument against bodily permanence and why it leads to circularity for accidents.

Core Idea
Accidents cannot be permanent, but bodies can — the key is in the conditions of permanence.

Al-Nazzam argued that bodies cannot be permanent because accidents cannot be permanent. This lesson examines his argument, its basis in the composition of substances from accidents, and the circularity that arises if we try to make accidents permanent.

Guided Path
1

Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that al-Nazzam applies the accident proof to bodies via analogy.

The Proof from Parallelism Against Infinite Dimensions
Iji summarizes the parallel lines proof that dimensions cannot be infinite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Proof from Parallelism uses geometric reasoning to show that dimensions cannot be infinite.

Core Idea
If dimensions were infinite, a finite line moving from parallel to concurrent would create an angle with no first point of meeting — a logical impossibility.

This passage presents an argument known as the Proof from Parallelism, which aims to refute the possibility of infinite dimensions. By assuming a finite line parallel to an infinite one and then rotating it until it intersects, the author shows that a contradiction arises: either meeting is impossible or there must be a first meeting point, both of which are absurd. This lesson will guide you through the geometric reasoning and the logical summary.

Study how the motion creates an angle at the fixed endpoint. Note the reference to Euclid's theorem that every angle of straight lines can be bisected indefinitely.
This shows that the angle is potentially infinitely divisible.

Understand why there can be no first point of meeting: the angle's infinite divisibility implies that before any supposed first point, a smaller angle (and thus an earlier meeting) exists.
This refutes the notion of a first meeting point.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof known as the 'Proof of Parallelism' (برهان الموازاة) to demonstrate that dimensions cannot be infinite. It begins by defining the geometric situation: a finite line is assumed parallel to an infinite line, then rotated until it becomes concurrent (intersecting) with the infinite line. The key idea is that when the finite line moves from parallel to concurrent, it traces an angle that is always divisible, so there can be no first point of meeting. The author (al-Iji) summarizes the argument: if there were an infinite dimension, we could assume this configuration, but it leads to a dilemma—either meeting is impossible, or there is a first meeting point. Both are impossible: the first because meeting actually occurs (the motion causes it), the second because any supposed first point of meeting would be preceded by another due to the infinite divisibility of the angle. Thus, the assumption of infinite dimensions is false. The proof is attributed to Euclid's geometry.

Eliminating Natural Movement as the Cause of Celestial Motion
Iji proves celestial movements are not natural via a paradox.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why circular celestial motion cannot be natural, and how this leads to the need for voluntary movement.

Core Idea
Circular motion's paradox—seeking and abandoning the same position—undoes natural movement as an explanation for celestial motion.

This section from Iji's Aqaid al-Adudiyya argues that celestial movements are voluntary by first eliminating natural movement. The author uses a clever paradox: in circular motion, every position is both sought and abandoned, which is impossible for a natural motion. We'll explore the argument and its refinement.

Understand the classification of movement

This is a common kalam technique: exhaustive division and elimination.

The author argues that if circular motion were natural, then the same position would be both naturally sought and naturally abandoned—a contradiction. Reflect on why this is impossible.
This is the core of the argument against natural movement.
3
Consider the refinement and reply
The passage includes a response that the abandonment is not of the same position but its like, and then offers a better formulation: the alternating seeking and abandoning requires will and perception.
This strengthens the argument by connecting it to volition.

Proof that Celestial Motions Are Voluntary
Al-Jurjani argues that celestial motions are not natural and refutes an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why celestial motions cannot be natural and must be voluntary, and how al-Jurjani refines the argument against an objection.

Core Idea
Circular motion's simultaneous seeking and abandoning of a position proves it requires will, not nature.

Al-Jurjani begins the investigation of celestial souls by arguing that their motions are voluntary. He uses a clever argument from the nature of circular motion: if it were natural, the same position would be both desired and shunned, which is impossible. He also addresses an objection and strengthens the argument.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context
The passage is part of the third observation on immaterial souls. The first purpose is on celestial souls, which are immaterial because their motions are voluntary.
This sets up the need to prove the motions are voluntary.
2
Learn the classification of motions
Al-Jurjani states that all essential motions are either natural, forced, or voluntary. To show celestial motions are voluntary, he first eliminates natural and forced.
This is a standard division in kalam.
3
Grasp the argument against natural motion
In circular motion, every position the mover reaches is both sought (since it moves toward it) and abandoned (since it leaves it). If it were natural, the same position would be naturally desired and naturally abandoned, a contradiction.
This is the core proof.
4
See the objection and refinement
An objection says that leaving a position is not identical to seeking it. Al-Jurjani refines the argument: the mover seeks a specific position and then abandons it, which requires perception and will.
The refined argument is stronger.

Proof that Celestial Motions Are Not Forced
Al-Jurjani gives two reasons why celestial motions are not forced.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Al-Jurjani rejects forced motion for celestial spheres and the two arguments he uses.

Core Idea
Celestial motions cannot be forced because there is no nature to oppose and observations show diversity.

Al-Jurjani continues the investigation into celestial motions by examining the possibility that they are forced. He provides two compelling arguments: one based on the absence of natural inclination, and another based on the observed diversity of motions. This lesson unpacks these proofs.

Commentary
This passage continues the proof that celestial motions are voluntary. First, Al-Jurjani argues they are not forced: force requires a natural inclination to be contrary to, but celestial spheres have no natural inclination (as shown by the failure of the natural motion hypothesis). Moreover, if they were forced, all motions would be similar due to a single coercer, but observations show diversity in direction and speed. Thus forced motion is false, leaving voluntary motion. Second, he argues that voluntary motion of such constancy and perpetuity cannot arise from a corporeal imagination, which produces variable and intermittent motions. Instead, it must proceed from a universal intellection (ta'aqqul kullī) encompassing infinite particulars, and such intellection requires an immaterial subject (a soul). This prepares for the later proof concerning human souls.

Uniform perpetual motion cannot come from imagination but requires an immaterial soul with universal intellection.

Two Corollaries on Celestial Souls
Al-Jurjani derives two implications of celestial souls being immaterial and rational.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why celestial souls, despite being rational, need corporeal powers for particular motions and why they lack senses, desire, and anger.

Core Idea
Even perfect intelligences require particular powers for motion; they have no need for imperfect faculties.

Al-Jurjani has established that celestial spheres have immaterial, rational souls. Now he draws two important implications. First, since these souls engage in motion, they must have a power that can specify which particular motion to perform—something universal reason cannot do alone. Second, because celestial beings are perfect, they have no need for the lower faculties like sense, desire, or anger. Let's follow his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice the two corollaries introduced with 'al-awwal' and 'al-thani'.
The second corollary is cut off in the source, but the point is clear.
2
Identify the two corollaries
The first corollary states that celestial souls have corporeal powers (impressed souls) for particular motions. The second states they lack external senses, desire, and anger.
3
Understand why rational power alone is insufficient
Universal intellection relates equally to all particulars. To specify one motion over another, a particular perception and will are needed, which requires a corporeal power like imagination.
This is the key argument for the first corollary.
4
Understand why lower faculties are absent
Senses, desire, and anger are for seeking benefit and repelling harm. Celestial souls, being perfect, have no such needs, so they lack these faculties.
This completes the second corollary.

First Rational Proof: The Intellect as the First Creation
Argument eliminating candidates for first creation to prove it is the intellect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof that the first emanation from God must be the intellect, based on the principle that from the One only one simple thing can proceed.

Core Idea
From the truly One, only one simple thing can directly emanate; only the intellect fits.

This lesson presents the first rational proof (al-wajh al-awwal) from the classical kalam tradition, based on the nature of divine unity. The author systematically eliminates all candidates for the first creation—body, matter, form, accident, soul—until only the intellect remains. This argument is foundational for understanding the hierarchy of creation in Islamic philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Principle
Learn the key premise: from the truly One (God) only one simple thing can proceed directly. This is the foundation of the proof.
The 'truly One' means no multiplicity in any respect.
2
Examine Why a Body Cannot Be First
See why a body is eliminated due to its composition (parts precede the whole, implying multiplicity and priority of parts).
A body is composite; therefore it cannot be the first simple emanation.
3
Eliminate Dependent Entities
Consider why accident and soul are also impossible: an accident depends on a substance, a soul depends on a body for action. Both lack independence.
Independence in existence (for accident) and independence in effect (for soul) are required.
4
Conclude with the Intellect
Recognize that the intellect alone is both simple (non-composite) and independent in existence and effect, making it the only candidate for the first emanation.
The intellect is self-subsistent and does not need a body for its essential activity.
