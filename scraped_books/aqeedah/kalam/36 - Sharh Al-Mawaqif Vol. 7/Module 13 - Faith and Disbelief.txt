The Objection and the Nature of Knowledge
Objection and first part of response: knowledge as relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that division of knowledge implies division of the soul and the author's initial response redefining knowledge as a relation.

Core Idea
Knowledge is a relation, not a form; the soul need not be divisible.

This lesson presents the first argument against the simplicity of the soul and the author's immediate counter. The objection claims that since knowledge in one part differs from another, the soul must be divisible. The author denies the premise that knowledge is a form subsisting in the soul, asserting instead that it is a mere relation.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Examine how the argument links division of knowledge to division of the soul. Notice the phrase 'الحال في أحد جزئيه غير الحال في الجزء الآخر'.
The objection assumes knowledge is a state (حال) in the soul.
2
Understand the author's denial
The author rejects that intellection is acquisition of a form. He defines knowledge as a mere relation (تعلق) between knower and known.
This is a key Ash'ari position.
3
Analyze the first concession
Even if intellection were form-acquisition, the soul would hold the form of the simple, not the simple itself, and the form need not be simple.
Examples from earlier discussions support this.

The third observation - the second aim: concerning human souls. In it because the state in one of its two parts is not the state in the other part, and that is the division of the state which is knowledge contradicts simplicity in the known, because knowledge must conform to its known. It is answered that it is based on the soul being a locus for the intelligible, because intellection is the acquisition of the form in the rational faculty, and this is denied, for knowledge with us is a mere relation between the knower and the known by which the known is distinguished for the knower, and that relation is a mental consideration with which the knower is qualified, not an existent thing subsisting in it. And if it is conceded that knowledge is by the acquisition of the form of the known, then the locus, i.e., the soul then is a locus for the form of the simple whose relation [is to the simple], not for the essence of the simple. And conformity between the form and the possessor of the form is not necessitated in all respects, for the form of the simple may not be simple. Do you not see what they said: that it is permissible for the external simple to have two or more intellectual forms, as has passed in the investigations of the state. And if it is conceded that the form of the simple must be simple, we do not concede that every extended thing is divisible, for that is based on the denial of the indivisible part, and this is denied. In that case, it is permissible for the soul to be an atomic substance, as some have said. And if it is conceded that every extended thing is divisible, then do not concede that the state in the divisible is divisible, like the surface subsisting in your view in the body which is divisible in all directions while it is not divisible in depth, and like the line subsisting in the surface while it is not divisible in width, and like the point subsisting in the line while it is not divisible at all. In short, divisibility of the state is only necessitated if the inherence is penetrative, and this is not conceded in what we are discussing. And if it is conceded that it (i.e., the state in the divisible) is divisible, then it is divisible potentially like the body, not actually, and that does not contradict simplicity because it is possible that the aspect of its division is not the aspect of its simplicity. For the simple body in your view is potentially divisible infinitely while it is actually simple, since there are no actual joints in it, so there is no actual division, and there is no contradiction between division and non-division from the aspects of potentiality and actuality, because they are two distinct aspects.

Commentary
This passage presents the first argument against the simplicity of the soul and the author's detailed response. The passage begins by stating the objection: since the state (knowledge) in one part of the known differs from the state in another part, the division of knowledge contradicts the simplicity of the known (the soul). The author answers that this objection assumes the soul is a locus for intelligibles (i.e., that intellection is the acquisition of a form in the rational faculty). He denies this, asserting instead that knowledge is a mere relation between the knower and the known, not a subsisting entity. Then he considers a series of hypothetical concessions (إن سلم). First, if knowledge were by acquisition of a form, the soul would be a locus for the form of the simple, not for the simple essence itself, and the form need not be simple. Second, even if the form must be simple, we do not concede that every extended thing is divisible—this rests on denying the indivisible part, which is disputed. Third, even if extended things are divisible, the state in the divisible need not be divisible—examples: the surface is not divisible in depth, the line not in width, the point not at all; only penetrative inherence entails divisibility, which is not conceded. Fourth, even if the state is divisible, it is potentially divisible like the body, and potential division does not contradict actual simplicity. The author thus upholds the soul's simplicity.

Further Concessions: Extension, Divisibility, and Potentiality
Three conditional concessions defending simplicity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the remaining three conditional arguments that defend the soul's simplicity against objections from extension and divisibility.

Core Idea
No divisibility, no penetrative inherence, and potential division do not violate simplicity.

In this lesson, the author continues his defense of the soul's simplicity by granting further premises but showing they do not entail actual divisibility. He addresses the assumption that extension implies divisibility, the nature of inherence, and the distinction between potential and actual division.

Guided Path
1
Follow the second concession
Even if the form must be simple, the author denies that every extended thing is divisible, citing the possibility of atomic indivisible parts.
This challenges the opponent's ontology of extension.
2
Analyze the third concession
Even if extended things are divisible, the state in the divisible need not be divisible – examples of surface, line, point.
Inherence here is not penetrative (سرياني).
3
Understand the fourth concession
Even if the state is divisible, it is potential, not actual – like a simple body divisible potentially but actually simple.
Potential division does not negate actual simplicity.

Second Argument: Cognition of Existence
Presents the second argument for the soul's simplicity and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second argument for the soul's simplicity based on its cognition of existence, and the author's response through referring to previous denials.

Core Idea
The soul's cognition of simple existence does not prove its simplicity if the premises are denied.

This lesson presents the second argument from the five aspects: the soul cognizes existence, and existence is simple, so the soul must be simple. The author, however, dismisses this argument by pointing to denials already raised against its premises, particularly those from the first argument. Let's see how this unfolds.

Understand the response

This shows the author's method of refuting by common points.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider why this argument fails according to the author. The key is that the premises are not accepted.
Connects to broader discussion of soul's immateriality.

Third Argument: Cognition of Universals
Presents the third argument and its response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third argument for the soul's immateriality based on its cognition of universal concepts, and the key responses that show the argument's weaknesses.

Core Idea
Cognizing universals seems to require immateriality, but inherence and conformity can be non-penetrative.

This passage presents a nuanced argument: if the soul can think in universals, it cannot be material, because material things are particular. But the response shows that universals can inhere in material substrates without losing their universality, if we understand the nature of mental existence and conformity. Let's work through the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the argument's structure
Read the first part of the passage which presents the third argument: the soul cognizes universals, so it is immaterial. Identify the two premises: (1) the soul makes judgments about universals, (2) if the soul were material, the universal would inhere in a positioned, quantified locus and thus be particular, not universal.
The argument uses 'khalaf' (contradiction) to show the assumption leads to absurdity.
2
Examine the response's key moves
The response denies the second premise. Note two main points: (a) we do not concede that the cognizer of the universal is a locus for it, because that assumes mental existence; (b) even if it inheres, inherence need not be penetrative (sariyani) – attributes of the locus do not necessarily transfer to the inhered form.
The term 'al-hulūl al-sariyānī' is a key concept meaning penetrating inherence.
3
Analyze the example of the sheikh painting
Read the analogy: the form of a sheikh in a painting can differ in size from the actual sheikh yet still conform. The form is stripped of accidental individuation (like size) and thus remains universal in essence.
This illustrates that conformity (muṭābaqah) is about the essence, not accidental features.
4
Grasp the refined definition of conformity
The passage concludes that the meaning of conformity is that the form, when divested of accidental attributes acquired from its locus, is applicable to many. This shows that universality does not require a distinct immaterial location.
The example of the sky's image in common sense reinforces this point.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Mental forms of opposites are not real opposites; they are representations, not the things themselves.

This passage presents the fourth argument that the soul's ability to cognize opposites proves its immateriality. The author then offers a twofold response, first denying that mental forms are opposites, and second showing that even if they were, they could be in different parts of a material substrate. Fifth Argument: The Soul's Cognition of Its Own Locus
Presents the fifth argument and its response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure of the fifth argument against the soul being a body, and the response that denies the implication.

Core Idea
The soul cannot be corporeal because cognition of its locus is neither constant nor impossible.

This lesson presents the fifth argument in the series refuting the corporeality of the soul. The author argues that if the soul were a body inhering in the body, it would either always or never cognize its own locus, but experience shows neither is true. A detailed response follows, denying the strict dichotomy.
