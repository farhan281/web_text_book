The Perpetual Existence of Generic Forms and the Temporality of Specific Forms
Explains that while the genus of forms is perpetual, specific forms can be temporally originated.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the perpetual existence of the genus of forms is compatible with the temporal origination of specific forms.

Core Idea
The genus of forms is eternal, but specific forms may be created in time.

This passage from Sharh al-Mawaqif addresses a subtle point: while the generic aspect of forms is continuously existent through successive species, individual specific forms—including those of compound bodies—can originate temporally. The commentator clarifies this with the example of the human species and addresses a potential objection regarding eternal species.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, carefully read the Arabic passage and its literal translation to grasp the author's line of reasoning without interpretation.
Notice the dense argumentative style typical of kalam commentaries.
2
Identify the main claim about forms
The passage begins by asserting that bodies are never devoid of specific forms; one form must always be present. These forms share a genus but differ in specific quiddity, so the genus persists through species.
3
Understand the argument about individualizing forms
The commentator then argues that individualizing forms (الصور المشخصة) cannot be eternal in type or genus, as that would lead to infinite regress. They are temporally originated.
This is a key logical point.
4
Examine the example and objection
Using the human species as an example, the passage shows that there is no impossibility in a specific form being originated. It then addresses the objection that the human species was considered eternal by some, offering a resolution involving the sphere of fire.
The objection and response clarify the intended meaning.
5
Synthesize the lesson
Reflect on how this passage reconciles eternal genus with temporal species, and how it guards against the misconception that specific forms cannot be originated.
This connects to the learning objective.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the continuity of forms in bodies. It begins by stating that bodies are not free from all specific forms; instead, one form must be present. The objection that human species was eternal according to them is answered by suggesting that they meant the origination of the sphere of fire, which differs from elemental fire, or that specific forms are eternal in genus only, not in species.

The Possibility of Another World: Theologians vs Philosophers
Theologians permit another world; philosophers' first argument and answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the theologians affirm another world is possible and how they answer the philosophers' first argument about direction.

Core Idea
The boundary of this world does not define all possible directions.

In this lesson, we examine the eighth goal of the text: whether another world similar to this one can exist. The theologians argue yes, citing God's power and the principle of similarity. The philosophers object, claiming that any other world would be confined by this world's boundary. The author presents their first argument and offers a nuanced response that leaves the possibility open.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its literal translation to get an overall sense of the discussion. Notice the structure: theologians' position, philosophers' argument, and the author's answer.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the two positions
Identify the theologians' position: they permit another world based on similarity and the Quranic verse. Then identify the philosophers' position: they deny, giving three reasons. Only the first reason is elaborated here.
The Quranic verse is Surah Ya-Sin 81.
3
Understand the argument and answer
Focus on the philosophers' first argument: an external world would be in a direction already determined by this world's boundary, causing a contradiction. Then see the author's answer: the boundary only determines some directions (like up and down), not all. Other boundaries can define other directions.
This is the key insight: the boundary does not exhaust all possible directions.

Commentary
This passage begins the eighth goal (al-maqṣad al-thāmin) on the possibility of another world. The theologians (mutakallimūn) affirm its possibility based on the principle that similar things share judgments, and they cite the Quranic verse about God's power to create similar to the heavens and earth. The philosophers (ḥukamā’) deny it, giving three arguments. Only the first is fully presented here: if another world existed outside this one, it would be in a certain direction relative to the boundary of this world. But the direction would then be determined by the boundary before the existence of the new world, which is impossible because the boundary itself is supposed to determine directions. The author's answer is that the boundary only determines some directions (such as up and down) but not all directions. There could be other directions determined by other boundaries, and this world could be located in one of those directions. So the argument does not prove that another world is impossible.

Avoiding the Void Between Worlds
Second argument and answer on void between worlds.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second philosophical argument against multiple worlds (the void argument) and the author's response that the void can be avoided by embedding worlds in a larger sphere.

Core Idea
Two worlds need not create a void if they are embedded in a larger sphere.

This lesson presents the second philosophical argument against the existence of multiple worlds: that there would be an impossible void between them. The author offers a clever answer: the space can be filled by a larger sphere containing both worlds. By studying this passage, you will see how kalam scholars used physical analogies to defend their theology.

Guided Path
1

Notice that the author concedes the impossibility of void as a premise.
2

After conceding void is impossible, the author denies that a void must exist. See how he proposes a filler (mālī’) to occupy the space.

The author uses the example of Mars being three times larger than the sun's sphere to show that a larger sphere could contain many smaller spheres.
This is based on Ptolemaic astronomy, not modern science.
4
Reflect on the implications
The impossibility of void does not prevent multiple worlds, as long as they are embedded in a larger body.
This shows how kalam scholars harmonized philosophy with theology.

Objection and Response on Celestial Faculties and Internal Senses
Objection and response on celestial faculties and internal senses

Core Idea
The purpose of faculties is not limited to preservation; they may exist for perfection or other benefits.

The argument then extends to internal senses, where the objector similarly denies that their benefit is confined to sensory preservation. First Proof of Immateriality: Apprehending the Simple
First proof: the soul apprehends the simple, so it must be simple.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first argument for the soul's immateriality, which is that it apprehends the simple and must therefore be simple itself.

Core Idea
The soul apprehends simple realities; a composite locus cannot know the simple without dividing it.

This lesson presents the first of five proofs that the soul is immaterial. The argument has two parts: first, the soul apprehends the reality of realities, which is either simple or composed of simples; second, if the soul were corporeal, it would be divisible, and the knowledge in it would be divided, contradicting the simplicity of the known.

3
Study the second part about divisibility
If the soul were corporeal, it would be divisible, dividing knowledge.
This contradicts the simplicity of the known.
4
Notice the conclusion that the soul must be simple
Since knowledge of the simple cannot be divided, the soul cannot be corporeal.

Commentary
This passage is the conclusion of the discussion on intellects, now turning to jinn and devils. It begins by stating that they are also hidden substances beyond our senses. According to the religious people (al-milliyyun), they are bodies that can take any shape and can enter animals' interiors. There is disagreement about whether they differ in species from angels and humans, but all agree they are obligated beings. The philosophers object, arguing that if these bodies are subtle, they would be too weak; if not, they would be seen. The author responds that their subtlety means transparency (lack of color), so they are not too weak nor necessarily visible, thus the objection is invalid. The text cuts off but likely continues to elaborate.
