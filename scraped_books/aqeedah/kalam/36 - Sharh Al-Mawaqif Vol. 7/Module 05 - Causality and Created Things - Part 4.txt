Counterargument from the author of Lubab al-Arba'in
A later scholar's objection against the proof of finite dimensions is refuted.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the attempted reversal of the argument for finite dimensions fails, and how the impossibility of extending a line outside the universe refutes it.

Core Idea
Contact with non-existent points is inconceivable; the universe has no void or plenum.

The author of Lubab al-Arba'in attempts to turn the proof of finite dimensions against its proponents by showing it would imply infinite points. However, the commentator quickly dismisses this objection, pointing out that it relies on imagining contact with points that do not exist outside the universe. This short passage is a crisp example of rebuttal in kalam reasoning.

The key is that it assumes points outside the universe exist for the line to contact.

Refuting the Void Argument for a Single World
Second proof against another world involving void and its refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second philosophical proof against another world based on void and how it is refuted by the possibility of a filler or embedding in a larger sphere.

Core Idea
The void between worlds is avoidable through a filler or a larger containing sphere.

This passage presents the second argument against the possibility of another world: if two worlds existed, there would be a void between them, which the philosophers considered impossible. The answer, however, cleverly avoids this consequence by proposing that a filler could occupy the space, or that both worlds could be embedded within a larger sphere—just as celestial spheres contain many bodies. Let's explore this nuanced rebuttal.

Guided Path
1
Understand the proof's claim
Read the proof carefully: it argues that another world would create a void because contact between spheres is only at a point, and contact with a non-sphere leaves a gap. Since void is impossible, another world is impossible.
Note that the proof assumes the impossibility of void—this is a shared premise.
2
Examine the answer's strategy
The answer grants the impossibility of void but denies that void necessarily follows. It offers two ways to avoid void: a filler occupying the gap, or embedding both worlds in a larger sphere.
The answer does not reject the premise but shows it does not lead to the conclusion.
3
Reflect on the supporting analogy
The answer uses the example of celestial spheres: the sphere of Mars is said to be vast and could contain elements and compounds like ours. This shows that a larger containing sphere is conceivable, making the existence of another world possible.
This step shows the author's method of analogical reasoning.

First Branch: Celestial Souls Have Intellectual and Physical Powers
Arguments that celestial souls require physical powers for particular motions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why celestial souls require physical powers alongside their intellectual power to account for particular motions.

Core Idea
Universal intellection alone cannot cause particular motions; physical imagination is needed.

Having established that celestial spheres have immaterial souls, the author now explains that these souls must also possess physical powers. The reason is that the intellectual power, being universal, cannot specify which particular motion occurs at a given time. This lesson unpacks that argument and describes the nature of those physical powers.

1
Read the passage carefully

Identify the two types of powers
The passage distinguishes between intellectual power (قوة عقلية) and physical powers (قوى جسمانية). Understand the role of each.
Intellectual power is like our rational soul; physical powers are like imagination.

Understand the argument against universal intellection
Why can't universal intellection alone cause particular motions? Because it relates equally to all particulars and cannot specify one without a particular will and perception.
This is the key logical step in the argument.
4
Learn about the nature of these physical powers
The passage describes them as similar to human imagination, but simple and pervading the whole sphere. They are called 'imprinted souls' (نفوس منطبعة).

First Proof for the Intellect as the First Emanation
Al-Jurjani gives the first proof that the first emanation is the intellect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first philosophical proof that the first emanation from God must be the intellect, based on God's absolute unity and the independence required of the first created being.

Core Idea
The intellect is the only being that can be both simple and independent, making it the necessary first emanation.

Al-Jurjani begins by citing the philosophers' statement and a Prophetic hadith about the first creation. He then explains how different hadiths can be reconciled. The core of the lesson is the first proof: because God is absolutely one, only one thing can proceed directly from Him, and that thing cannot be composed or dependent. This leads to the conclusion that the first emanation must be the intellect.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice how the argument is structured: a hadith citation, reconciliation, then the proof.
The Arabic includes key technical terms like 'واحد حقيقي' (truly one) and 'الصادر الأول' (first emanation).
2
Understand the hadith reconciliation
The author explains that the same reality (the first effect) can be called 'intellect', 'Pen', and 'light' depending on different aspects. This reconciles three hadiths.
This shows the compatibility between revelation and philosophical reasoning.
3
Follow the elimination of candidates
The proof eliminates body, part, accident, and soul as candidates for the first emanation, each for a specific reason. Trace each reason.
Body is composed (implies multiplicity), part is incomplete, accident depends on substance, soul depends on body.
4
Grasp the conclusion and summary
Only the intellect remains, as it is simple, independent in existence and effect. Read the summary at the end.
The summary condenses the argument into three conditions: oneness, independence in existence, and independence in effect.
