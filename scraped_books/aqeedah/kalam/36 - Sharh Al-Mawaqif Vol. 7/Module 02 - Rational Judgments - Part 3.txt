The Ladder Proof Simplified
Presents the third proof using diverging lines with equal spread and addresses the cylinder objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logic of the Ladder Proof (al-burhan al-sullami) and how it refutes the possibility of infinite dimensions.

Core Idea
Infinite distance bounded by two divergent lines is impossible.

In this passage, al-Sanusi presents the third proof against infinite dimensions, a refined version of Ibn Sina's famous Ladder Proof. He introduces a crucial simplification that makes the argument more concise and powerful. We will walk through the proof step by step, understand the objection about an infinite cylinder, and see why the defense holds.

Guided Path
1
Set up the two diverging lines
Understand the initial configuration: from a point, two lines diverge like sides of an equilateral triangle, with the spread equal to the extension.
The simplification lies in assuming the spread equals the extension.
2
Follow the argument to infinity
See how if the lines extend infinitely, the distance between them is also infinite, but that distance is bounded by the lines themselves, leading to contradiction.
3
Address the cylinder objection
Consider the opponent's challenge about an infinite cylinder and see why the third proof still refutes infinite dimensions absolutely, with the first two proofs covering any gaps.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof, which is a simplified version of Ibn Sina's Ladder Proof (al-burhan al-sullami). The proof begins by taking a point and drawing two lines that diverge like the sides of an equilateral triangle, such that the distance between them increases at the same rate as their extension: after one unit of extension, the spread is one unit; after two units, two units, and so on. The author notes that if one simply says the spread equals the extension, the proof is more concise. If these lines extend infinitely, the distance between them is also infinite. However, this infinite distance is bounded between the two lines themselves, so it would be a bounded infinite, which is impossible. This is the core argument. The author identifies this as the Ladder Proof of Ibn Sina, but with a simplification that even the great scholars could not achieve; it was discovered by the author of al-Mutarahat. The simplification lies in assuming the spread equals the extension, removing many complexities. The proof refutes infinite dimensions from all directions—meaning it works regardless of the direction in which space is extended—unlike the first two proofs which only refute from one direction. An objection is considered: what about an infinite cylinder? The response is that this proof still works because the cylinder would have a bounded cross-section, but the third proof's logic does not apply to that case; however, the first two proofs already refute infinite dimensions absolutely, so the cylinder is also impossible.

The Universal Ladder Proof with Fixed Ratio
A proof by contradiction (khalaf) using a universal ladder analogy with a fixed ratio.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Ladder Proof demonstrates the impossibility of actual infinity using a fixed ratio between spread and extension.

Core Idea
A finite-to-infinite ratio is impossible, revealing the contradiction in assuming infinite extension.

This lesson presents one of the most elegant proofs in Islamic theology: the Universal Ladder Proof. The proof uses a simple geometric setup—two lines diverging from a point—to show that assuming an infinite extension leads to a contradiction involving a fixed ratio between spread and extension. The argument relies on the principle that straight lines diverge uniformly, preserving a constant ratio.

Guided Path
1
Understand the geometric setup
Imagine two straight lines starting from a single point, forming the sides of a triangle. The spread between them can be equal, greater, or less than the extension.
This is a universal triangle, not necessarily right-angled.
2
Grasp the preserved ratio
Because the lines are straight, they diverge uniformly. The ratio of the spread to the extension remains constant. For example, if at 10 cubits extension the spread is 1 cubit, at 20 cubits it is 2, etc.
This ratio is called nisba muhfaza (preserved ratio).
3
Follow the contradiction for infinite extension
Assume the lines go to infinity. Then you have a finite initial extension (e.g., the first 10 cubits) and an infinite total extension. According to the preserved ratio, the ratio of this finite extension to the infinite extension equals the ratio of two finite spreads, which is a specific fraction. But a finite-to-infinite ratio is not a specific fraction—it is undefined or zero. Contradiction.
4
Examine the objection and rebuttal
An objector says: 'Perhaps the spread at infinity is also infinite.' The rebuttal: If the spread is infinite, then that infinite spread is bounded by the two lines, which is impossible because an infinite cannot be enclosed between two boundaries.
This reinforces the proof's conclusion.
5
Reflect on the proof's significance
This proof shows that actual infinity cannot exist in physical quantities (like extension and spread), which has implications for arguments about the eternity of the world.
It is a key proof in kalam for the temporal finitude of the universe.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth proof, which is the universal form of the Ladder Proof (al-burhan al-sullami al-mutlaq). The author summarizes it clearly. The proof assumes two lines emerging from a single point, forming the sides of a triangle. The spread between them can be equal to the extension, greater, or less, but crucially, the ratio of the spread to the extension is constant (nisa muhfaza). This is because straight lines diverge uniformly. The author gives an example: if extension is ten cubits and spread one cubit, then at twenty cubits extension the spread is two cubits, at thirty three cubits, etc. Therefore, the ratio of the first extension (say ten) to the second extension (twenty) equals the ratio of the first spread (one) to the second spread (two). But the ratio of a finite to a finite is a specific fraction (e.g., 1/2, 1/3), whereas the ratio of a finite to an infinite is not a fraction (it is zero or undefined). This is a contradiction (khalaf). The author then addresses an objection: what if the spread at infinity is also infinite? If that were the case, then the infinite spread would be bounded by the two lines, causing an infinite to be bounded by two boundaries, which is also impossible. Thus the proof stands.

The Wheel Proof: Dividing Space into Six Sectors
Sanusi presents the Wheel Proof to demonstrate finitude of dimensions via geometric sector division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Wheel Proof uses division of a circle into six equal sectors to demonstrate the finitude of the world's dimensions.

Core Idea
The Wheel Proof shows that spatial dimensions cannot be infinite by visualising them as bounded sectors.

The fifth proof in this series, known as the Wheel Proof, offers a geometric argument for the finitude of the world's dimensions. By dividing a circle into six equal sections and extending its radii to infinity, we examine each sector's width. The conclusion is that infinite dimensions would lead to a contradiction. This lesson walks through the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Setup
Visualize a circle divided into six equal sectors by drawing radii from the center to six equally spaced points on the circumference. Each sector is bounded by two radii, which we then extend to infinity.
The circle is called a 'wheel' (tars) because of its shape.
2
Consider Each Sector's Width
For each sector, ask: is the width (the spread between the two radii) finite or infinite? If infinite, then an infinite width is contained between two boundaries (the radii), which is impossible. If finite, then the whole space, being six such finite sectors, must also be finite.
This is a dilemma: both options lead to finitude.
3
Connect to the Ladder Proof
Notice that each sector forms an equilateral triangle: the central angle is 60°, and the base angles are also 60° because the chords are equal. This shows that the spread equals the extension, clarifying the earlier Ladder Proof.
The Wheel Proof is a visual elaboration of the third proof (al-sullami).

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth proof, known as the Wheel Proof (al-burhan al-tarsi). The method: take a circle (or a wheel) and divide it into six equal sections by drawing lines from the center to the circumference at six evenly spaced points, and then connecting opposite points to form six sections, each bounded by two radii (called 'sides'). We then extend all these radii to infinity, thereby dividing all of space (the extent of the world) into six infinite triangular sectors. For each sector, we examine its width (the spread between its two bounding radii). If the width is infinite, then an infinite is bounded between two boundaries (the radii), which is impossible. If the width is finite, then since the whole is composed of six such finite sectors, the whole is also finite (because six times a finite is finite). Thus, in either case, infinite dimensions are impossible. The author then notes that this proof is like a clarification of the Ladder Proof (al-sullami), because each sector is an equilateral triangle. To see this, take two points on the two radii equidistant from the center; the line connecting them is equal to each radius because the angles are all equal (the central angle is 60 degrees, i.e., two-thirds of a right angle, and the base angles are also 60 degrees). Therefore, the triangle is equilateral, meaning the spread between the two radii equals their extension, just as in the third proof. Thus, the wheel proof provides a visual and explicit way to see how two lines can diverge at a rate equal to their extension, which might otherwise be hard to imagine. Sixth Proof: The Application Argument (al-Tatbiq)
Presents the sixth proof (application) for the finitude of dimensions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the application argument (al-tatbiq) for the finitude of dimensions and how it refutes the possibility of an infinite extension.

Core Idea
Infinite lines cannot be superposed without contradiction, proving dimensions must be finite.

The sixth proof uses a simple geometric thought experiment: two lines extending to infinity from different starting points are compared. The impossibility of the shorter equaling the longer or continuing indefinitely shows the contradiction. This proof was also referenced earlier in discussions on infinite regress and corporeal powers.

Guided Path
1
Understand the setup
Read the passage describing the sixth proof. Visualize two lines: one starting from a point and extending to infinity, and another starting from a point before it by a finite distance, also extending to infinity.
The lines are parallel in direction but offset in starting point.
2
Consider the two possibilities
When the lines are superposed, the shorter line (the one starting later) must either equal the longer line or stop before it. Both options lead to impossibility.
If equal, then the starting points would be the same, which contradicts the setup. If it stops, then both lines stop, making them finite.

This passage is a proof for the sixth attribute: that intellects (عقول) and all abstract beings (مجردات) cognize universals (كليات). It is a structured argument. The author first states the claim: intellects cognize universals, and every abstract being cognizes universals. The proof proceeds step by step: 1) Every abstract can be cognized (يمكن أن يعقل) because its essence is free from material impurities; its quiddity does not need to be worked on to become intelligible. 2) Everything that can be cognized can be cognized together with another (يمكن أن يعقل مع غيره) because we know necessarily that there is no contradiction (تضاد) among intellections; also, every cognized thing can be judged by universal attributes, and judgment between two things requires their relation together. This passage is a proof that a simple intellect (mujarrad), existing externally, can actually conceive all universals. The author begins by stating that a universal quiddity can be conceived by the simple intellect, meaning the quiddity's concomitance with the intellect in external existence. Since whatever is possible for the intellect is actually present in it, it is actually an intellect for all universals. The core argument is then summarized: a simple intellect is valid as an object of intellection, and anything valid as an object of intellection can be conceived together with any other concept. If it can be conceived with another, then their quiddities can be concomitant because conception is the occurrence of quiddity in the intellect. The key step is that the possibility of such concomitance does not depend on the simple intellect's occurrence in the intellect (i.e., being thought of) because that occurrence is itself the concomitance. If it did depend on it, it would be circular (possibility depending on existence). So the concomitance can occur while the simple intellect is external, and this entails that the other quiddity occurs in the simple intellect, which is intellection. Since intellection is possible, it is actual because change is only for material beings.

The occurrence of the two things (like the abstract and the essence of another) in a third (which is the intellect) is different from the occurrence of one of them, i.e., one of the two things like the abstract, in the other like the intellect. Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer argument about the condition for intellectual comprehension (تعقل) of abstract entities. The author begins by stating that the occurrence of two things (like the abstract and the essence of another) in a third (the intellect) is different from the occurrence of one of them in the other. Then he argues that it does not follow that comparability between the abstract and another essence is conditioned by the abstract being in the intellect, otherwise it would be a self-conditioning. Then he presents an objection: the opponent might say that conceiving them together implies comparability in the intellect. The author answers that the opponent does not claim that every comparability regarding the abstract requires it to be in the intellect, only the comparability between the abstract and other intelligibles. Then even if we grant that both comparabilities are similar, it does not entail that the abstract can conceive of things, because that requires the abstract to be a knower, which is denied. Then another objection: conception is itself this comparability. The author denies their identity, as conception may be something else conditioned by comparability, and possibility of the condition does not imply possibility of the conditioned elsewhere.
