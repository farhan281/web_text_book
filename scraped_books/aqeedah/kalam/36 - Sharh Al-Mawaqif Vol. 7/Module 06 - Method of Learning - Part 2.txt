The Halo: Formation, Visibility, and Weather Signs
Describes the formation of a halo, its visibility, and weather indications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the optical mechanism behind the lunar and solar halo and how its appearance predicts weather changes.

Core Idea
The halo is a reflection, not a physical ring.

This passage from the classical text explains the halo around the moon and sun. It describes why we see a ring of light around the moon, not a direct image, and how its behavior forecasts weather. The author also notes the rarity of the solar halo and a case of a rainbow-colored halo.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the descriptive style with examples from Ibn Sina.
2
Understand the invisibility of the cloud part facing the moon
The author explains that the part of the cloud directly between the moon and the observer is invisible because the moon's brightness overwhelms it. Ask yourself: why does the rest of the cloud appear as a ring?
This is due to reflection of light.
3
Learn the three weather indications
The passage gives three signs: tearing from all sides (fair weather), thickening until vanishing (rain), and inclining to one side (wind). Memorize these.
These are practical predictions based on empirical observation.
4
Note the special cases: double halos and solar halo rarity
The author mentions double halos when two cloud layers exist, and the rarity of the solar halo. Also note the colored halos observed by Ibn Sina.
The solar halo is rare because sunlight breaks up thin clouds.

Commentary
This passage explains the phenomenon of the lunar halo (الهالة). The author begins by noting that the part of the cloud directly in front of the moon is not seen because the moon's brightness overwhelms it, while the surrounding cloud reflects the light and appears as a ring. The halo's behavior predicts weather: tearing from all sides indicates fair weather, thickening until disappearance indicates rain, and inclination indicates wind direction. If two cloud layers exist, two concentric halos appear, with the lower one larger. The sun's halo (الطفاوة) is rarer because sunlight dissolves thin clouds; Ibn Sīnā is cited as having observed one with rainbow colors. A moon halo with rainbow colors can also occur when the cloud is thick, causing refraction similar to the rainbow.

Defending the Majority View on Halo and Rainbow
A scholar rejects the author's explanation; the author invokes majority opinion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the author transmits the majority explanation for the halo and rainbow despite a scholar's objection.

Core Idea
The author follows the majority, not his own certainty.

In this passage, the author reports that a respected scholar of optics rejects the explanation for the halo and rainbow that the author has presented. Rather than engaging in detailed refutation, the author simply states that his account is the opinion of the majority, and he is transmitting it in deference to them. This reveals the author's methodological humility and respect for scholarly consensus.

Read the passage

Reflect on the author's response

The Causation of Accidents: Correlation and Suspension
Al-Iji presents and evaluates two views on causation of accidents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the correlation argument (dawaran) is insufficient to prove causality, and why al-Iji suspends judgment on the cause of a substance's acceptance of accidents.

Core Idea
Correlation alone does not prove causation; both views are possible.

This short note (tanbih) addresses a key methodological question in kalam: Can we deduce causality from coextensiveness? Some theologians argue that a substance's ability to accept accidents is caused by its occupancy of space, because they always occur together. Others point out that the correlation is symmetrical, so it does not favor one direction over the other. Al-Iji examines both arguments and concludes that without decisive proof, the intellectually honest stance is to suspend judgment.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once through, then the literal translation. Notice the structure: a claim, a counter-claim, and a conclusion.
The passage is dense; read slowly.
2
Understand the correlation argument
Identify the first view: acceptance is caused by occupancy because they co-vary. Grasp the logical principle of correlation (dawaran) as a method for establishing causality.
The argument uses the presence/absence test: if A and B always coexist, A is the cause of B.
3
Understand the objection
The second view rejects this because the correlation is symmetrical. Both occupancy and acceptance always appear together, so neither can be singled out as the cause without additional evidence.
This is a classic critique of inferring causation from correlation.
4
Grasp the conclusion of suspension
Al-Iji chooses tawaqquf (suspension) because both positions are rationally possible and there is no decisive proof. Recognize that this is a principled epistemic stance, not agnosticism.
In kalam, tawaqquf is a legitimate position when evidence is balanced.

تنبيه : منهم أي من المتكلمين من قال قبول الأعراض الثابت للجواهر معلل بالتحيز للدوران فإنه إذا وجد التحيز وجد القبول، وإذا عدم عدم والمدار علة للدائر : وقيل : لا لدوران كل منهما مع الآخر فليس إسناد أحدهما إلى الآخر أولى من العكس والحق التوقف لأن كل واحد من المذهبين ممكن ولا قاطع في شيء منهما.
A note: Among them — that is, among the theologians — some said that the acceptance of accidents which is established for substances is caused by occupancy (al-tahayyuz), because of correlation (al-dawaran): for when occupancy exists, acceptance exists; and when it is absent, acceptance is absent. And the coextensive term is the cause of the coextensive term. And it was said: No, because of the correlation of each of them with the other, so assigning one to the other is not more warranted than the reverse. The truth is suspension, because each of the two views is possible and there is no decisive proof for either of them.
Source pages p. 243

Commentary
This note (تنبيه) addresses the question of why substances (جواهر) have the property of accepting accidents. Some theologians say that this ability is caused by the substance's occupancy of space (التحيز). They argue by correlation (الدوران): whenever there is occupancy, there is acceptance; whenever acceptance is absent, occupancy is absent. This coextensiveness suggests a causal relationship. Others disagree, arguing that the correlation is reciprocal, so it is not clear which is the cause. The author (المصنف) concludes that the correct position is to suspend judgment (التوقف), because both views are logically possible and there is no decisive evidence for either.

Misconception: Some might think that constant correlation (dawaran) is sufficient to prove causation in kalam.
Correction: This passage shows that symmetry of correlation prevents a definitive causal attribution. The author suspends judgment precisely because correlation alone is not decisive.

Checkpoint

Key Takeaway
When evidence is mutually balanced and inconclusive, the rational method is to suspend judgment rather than make an unfounded causal claim.

What would change if you consistently applied the lesson's call to withhold certainty until clear proof is given?

Are there areas in your own life where you claim certainty without enough evidence—perhaps about others' intentions or about theological details? What would it cost you to stop there and say 'I do not know' in obedience to the method of tawaqquf?

Introduction to the Third Observatory on Souls
Al-Jurjani announces the Third Observatory on souls and lists four maqasid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Jurjani structures his work by transitioning from the study of bodies to the study of immaterial souls, and be aware of the four main sections that follow.

Core Idea
The study of souls begins where the study of bodies ends.

This short passage marks a major turning point in the Mawaqif. After a lengthy treatment of bodies and their accidental properties, al-Jurjani now announces the Third Observatory, dedicated to immaterial souls. He also gives a preview: the discussion will be organized under four central themes, or maqasid. Let's read the passage and then see what these four objectives are.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening passage
Read the Arabic sentence and its literal translation. Notice how the author signals a shift in topic.
The word 'marsad' (observatory) is a metaphor for a major section.
2
Identify the three key elements
The passage contains: (1) a subject announcement (souls), (2) a transition note (after bodies), and (3) a structural outline (four maqasid).
3
Understand the methodological significance
Ask yourself: Why would the author explicitly mark this transition? This shows that the Mawaqif is carefully organized, and each section builds on the previous one.
4
Look ahead to the four maqasid
The next lessons will detail each of the four maqasid. This lesson sets the stage for that detailed discussion.
