Learning Objective

Core Idea
Democritus introduces a middle ground: the simple body is composed of potentially divisible small bodies.

The debate so far has considered four positions on whether the simple body's parts are all actual or all potential. Now Jurjani introduces a fifth school—Democritus—who argues that the simple body is made of tiny bodies that are not actually divided but only divisible in thought. This opens the door to a mixed scenario that the earlier positions did not cover.

The Argument from Units Against Infinite Parts
Argues from the nature of number to refute universal infinite parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why every number must contain indivisible units, and how this refutes al-Nazzam's claim that bodies consist of an infinite number of parts.

Core Idea
Any number, finite or infinite, must contain true units—so infinite parts would still include finite units.

This passage from Sharh al-Mawaqif presents a foundational kalam argument against infinite parts. Before engaging with the ratio argument, the author establishes that any collection, even an infinite one, must have indivisible units. By identifying these finite units within an assumed infinite set, the theologian shows that the claim 'every body has infinite parts' is self-contradictory. The commentary elaborates on the meaning of 'units', the refutation of al-Nazzam's position, and the ratio argument, emphasizing that the size of a composite is the sum of its parts' sizes.

Focus on the claim: the ratio of size to size equals the ratio of parts to parts. Then note the contradiction when one side is finite and the other infinite.
2
Identify the contradiction
If body A has finite size and finite parts, and body B has finite size but infinite parts, then the ratio of sizes is finite:finite but the ratio of parts is finite:infinite. Equating them leads to a contradiction (khulf).
3
Consider the escape of interpenetration
The only way to avoid the ratio equality is if parts can interpenetrate, but interpenetration (tadakhul) is invalid in kalam.
4
Distinguish actual vs potential parts
The author concludes that these arguments do not negate potential infinite divisibility. The body can be divided endlessly in potential, but it does not contain actually infinite parts.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of al-Nazzam's view that bodies are composed of an infinite number of parts. The author first argues that any number, finite or infinite, must contain indivisible units (ahad) because otherwise there would be no true unit and the collection would not be a number. Applying this to the body, even if its parts were infinite, there would be finite units within them; combining these units yields a body with finite parts, contradicting the claim that every body has infinite parts. The author then addresses an objection that this argument applies only to composite bodies, not to the original created bodies. He replies that the argument still holds because the finite units exist within those bodies. Next, he presents a ratio argument: the body has finite size, and parts are assumed infinite; since size increases with parts, the ratio of sizes should equal the ratio of parts, but one ratio is finite-to-finite and the other finite-to-infinite, leading to a contradiction. The only escape is to posit interpenetration of parts, which is invalid. Finally, he clarifies that these arguments do not negate potential infinite divisibility; they only reject actual infinite parts. The commentary elaborates on the meaning of 'units', the refutation of al-Nazzam's position, and the ratio argument, emphasizing that the size of a composite is the sum of its parts' sizes.

The Two Meanings of 'Ittisal' and the Objection
The author defines two meanings of 'ittisal' and states the objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the objection against the proof of hylê rests on a homonymy of the word 'ittisal', which has two distinct meanings: the substantial form and the accidental dimensions.

Core Idea
Conflating the essential and accidental meanings of 'ittisal' produces the fallacy in the objection.

This lesson presents the core objection: that the proof of hylê is a fallacy because 'ittisal' is ambiguous. The author first clarifies the two meanings of the word—one referring to the permanent substantial form, the other to changing accidental dimensions. Recognizing this homonymy is key to seeing why the objection fails.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage identifying the two meanings
Read the Arabic passage and notice how the author explicitly states that 'ittisal' is said of two things: the substantial form (الصورة الجوهرية) and the dimensions themselves (نفس الامتدادات).
2
Distinguish the two meanings
First meaning: the substantial form that makes the body receptive to three dimensions, never leaves the body. Second meaning: the accidental quantity (كم) that is not a part of the body and can change.
The first is essential and permanent; the second is accidental and changeable.
3
Understand the fallacy of the objection
The objection treats the two meanings as one, arguing that since accidental connection can change without destroying the body, essential connection must also be changeable. The author shows this is a fallacy of homonymy.

The author first presents the objection: the questioner claims the proof is a fallacy because 'ittisal' has two meanings—the substantial form that makes the body receptive to three dimensions and that never leaves the body, and the dimensions themselves, which are accidental quantities. The opponent argues that the body's ability to change shape while remaining the same body shows that there is no need for a separate hylê. The author then responds by explaining that the real meaning of the body being 'receptive to connection' is not that a specific individual identity remains under different connections, but that there is something that preserves the corporeal essence (al-mahiyya al-jismiyya) across changes. This preserver (mustahfiz) is the real subject of connection and separation, and it is distinct from the changing accidental identities. The author illustrates with the example of water: when water is divided into multiple vessels, the personal identity of the continuous water is lost, but something remains (the essence) that is receptive to both unity and multiplicity. The dispute with the Illuminationists reduces to whether accidental connection is a differentiator (mushakhkhas) of the extended substance. If it is, then a separate preserver (hylê) is needed; if not, the extended substance alone suffices. The author's final answer is that 'receptive to separation' means it can accidentally have separation, while 'receptive to connection' means it is essentially connected—these are different aspects of the same thing, so no contradiction arises.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an objection against the proof of hylê (matter) by showing that the objection is based on a homonymy of the word 'ittisal' (connection). The author first presents the objection: the questioner claims the proof is a fallacy because 'ittisal' has two meanings—the substantial form that makes the body receptive to three dimensions and that never leaves the body, and the dimensions themselves, which are accidental quantities. The opponent argues that the body's ability to change shape while remaining the same body shows that there is no need for a separate hylê. The author then responds by explaining that the real meaning of the body being 'receptive to connection' is not that a specific individual identity remains under different connections, but that there is something that preserves the corporeal essence (al-mahiyya al-jismiyya) across changes. This preserver (mustahfiz) is the real subject of connection and separation, and it is distinct from the changing accidental identities. The author illustrates with the example of water: when water is divided into multiple vessels, the personal identity of the continuous water is lost, but something remains (the essence) that is receptive to both unity and multiplicity. The dispute with the Illuminationists reduces to whether accidental connection is a differentiator (mushakhkhas) of the extended substance. If it is, then a separate preserver (hylê) is needed; if not, the extended substance alone suffices. The author's final answer is that 'receptive to separation' means it can accidentally have separation, while 'receptive to connection' means it is essentially connected—these are different aspects of the same thing, so no contradiction arises.

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration and explanation of five possible opinions regarding the origin of the world, followed by the beginning of the first of six proofs for the origination of bodies. The author starts by stating that there are four divisions of opinions about originated bodies, but then lists five, possibly counting the 'not a body' group as one division and subdividing it. He first mentions those who say the world is not a body, and among them the Dualists (Magians) who hold that light and darkness are eternal and the world arises from their mixture. Second, the Harranians who believe in five eternal principles (soul and matter); they say the soul loved matter, leading to the generation of various creatures. The love is made transitive with the preposition 'ba' to imply attachment or obsession. Third, a view that unity itself divided into points, then lines, surfaces, and finally bodies; the author notes these may be symbolic statements. Fourth, the opinion that things are originated in essence but eternal in attributes—dismissed as necessarily false and only considered as a logical possibility. Fifth, the suspension of judgment regarding all except the fourth, attributed to Galen, who reportedly said on his deathbed that he did not know whether the world is eternal or originated. The author then introduces six proofs for the origination of bodies in both essence and attributes. The first proof, called 'the famous one', is stated: bodies are never free from accidents, and whatever is never free from accidents is originated in essence and attributes; therefore, bodies are originated. The second premise is said to be apparent because the eternity of what is never free from accidents would entail the eternity of accidents. The first premise (that bodies are never free from accidents) is supported by two aspects. The first aspect relies on earlier discussions that bodies are never free from modes (akwan) and composition and their accompanying accidents. The author suggests it is better to reference what will come in the sixth goal, where it is shown that bodies do not exist without differentiation, and differentiation is through accidents (since atoms are similar), and accidents are originated because they do not endure for two moments. The second aspect is that the body is never free from motion or rest, both of which are originated. This is proven by the body necessarily occupying a place; if that occupancy is preceded by another occupancy in the same place, it is rest; otherwise it is motion. An objection is raised that the proof fails for the body at the first moment of its existence, since at that moment it is neither moving nor at rest. The author answers that the discussion concerns a body that persists (al-baqi), not a newly originated body; the questioner’s objection thus does not refute the proof. The author then provides three reasons why motion is originated: (1) The essence of motion is defined as being preceded by another state, whereas eternity is defined as non-precedence; they are essentially opposite. (2) The essence of motion exists only in particular instances, and none of those instances exist in eternity because they must succeed one another; therefore, the essence itself is originated. (3) Every particular motion is preceded by an eternal non-existence, so all non-existences are gathered (the text cuts off).

Without accidents, a body becomes an abstraction with no real existence.

And so on, up to the tenth intellect, which is in the rank of the ninth of the spheres, namely the sphere of the moon, and is called the Active Intellect, the agent in the hylic matter of the lower world, the effuser of forms, souls, and accidents upon the simple elements and upon the composites from them, due to the preparednesses caused by celestial motions, planetary conjunctions, and their accidental positions. It is said: "These considerations, if they are existential, must have multiple sources; otherwise your statement 'the One does not originate except one' would be false, and then your whole proof would be invalid. And if they are conceptual, it is impossible for them to become a part that is a source of existential things." It may be answered that they are not a part of the agent but rather a condition for the effect, and a condition may be conceptual. But such considerations of negations and relations are accidental to the First Principle, so it is possible that by virtue of them it becomes a source for multiple things, like the first effect. That, however, is contrary to their doctrine upon which they built their discourse on the arrangement of existents. And the talk of attributing noble things to noble things is rhetorical, not to be considered in scientific inquiries. And the attribution of the eighth sphere, with its numerous stars of varying magnitudes, too numerous to count, to a single source, namely the second intellect, as they claim, is very problematic. Likewise, the attribution of the forms and accidents, which, despite their abundance, defy enumeration, to the Active Intellect is also problematic. In summary, it is not hidden from the intelligent and fair-minded person the weakness of what they rely upon in this following matter. In al-Mulakhkhas, they were confused: sometimes they considered in the first intellect two aspects—its existence and made it the cause of the intellect, and its possibility and made it the cause of the sphere; and some instead considered its intellection of its existence and its possibility as the cause of the intellect and sphere; sometimes they considered multiplicity from three aspects as mentioned in the text of the book; and sometimes from four aspects, adding its knowledge of that other, and made its possibility the cause of the hylic matter of the sphere and its knowledge the cause of its form. Thus it became clear that the intellects are incapable of comprehending the system of existents as they are in themselves.

Commentary
This passage is an objection and answer section followed by a concluding critique. It begins by referencing the tenth intellect (the Active Intellect) in the emanationist scheme, then poses an objection: if the considerations (like plurality of forms) are real, they require multiple sources; if they are merely conceptual, they cannot be actual causes. The objection is answered by distinguishing between a part of the agent and a condition. However, the author notes that this answer contradicts the emanationists' own principle of 'the One does not originate except one.' Then the author dismisses rhetorical arguments and points out difficulties: attributing the many fixed stars to a single intellect is problematic, and attributing countless forms and accidents to the Active Intellect is also problematic. The passage concludes by summarizing the confusion in al-Mulakhkhas, where different formulations of aspects were given, showing inconsistency. The final statement asserts that the intellects cannot grasp the true system of existents.

Key Takeaway
The emanationist system is weakened by rhetorical arguments, failure to explain cosmic multiplicity, and internal inconsistency, leading to the conclusion that such speculative intellects cannot grasp the true order of existence.
