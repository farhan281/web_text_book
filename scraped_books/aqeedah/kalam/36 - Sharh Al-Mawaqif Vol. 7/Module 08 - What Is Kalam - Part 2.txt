The Generative and Formative Powers
Defines generative and formative powers with Aristotle and Canon.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between the generative power (المولدة) and the formative power (المصورة) and their roles in embryonic development according to Aristotle and the Canon.

Core Idea
Generative power produces seed; formative power gives form to parts.

This lesson introduces two fundamental powers in the generation of living beings: the generative power, which produces semen, and the formative power, which organizes the parts into specific forms. These concepts come from Aristotle and the Canon of Medicine.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to get an overview of the content.
2
Identify the two types of generative power
Notice that the passage divides the generative power into two types: one that generates semen in males and females, and another that separates the temperamental qualities in the semen.
The second type blends the parts according to each organ.
3
Understand the formative power
The formative power exists in the semen when in the womb and bestows forms, powers, shapes, and measurements upon the parts, actualizing them.
4
Reflect on Aristotle's view
Note that Aristotle said the power does not separate from the two, meaning the resulting semen is uniform in reality.
This sets the stage for the discussion of temperaments.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification. It first presents Aristotle's view that a certain power (likely the generative) does not separate from two others, resulting in semen similar in reality. Then it cites the Canon (Qānūn) to divide the generative power into two types: one that generates semen in males and females, and another that separates the powers in the semen (the temperamental qualities) because the semen's parts have different temperaments. This second type blends these parts according to each organ's needs, assigning specific temperaments to nerves, bones, arteries, etc., from semen that is either uniform in parts or in mixture. Next, it defines the formative power, which exists in the semen when in the womb, and gives forms, powers, shapes, and measurements to the parts, actualizing them. These two powers (generative and formative) are served by the nutritive and growth powers: the nutritive power (apparent) and the growth power enlarge organs and widen passages for generation, which is why semen forms only after organs are mature. These four powers are served by four others, designated as servants for all the previous four, because they serve the nutritive and growth while those two serve the remaining two. The commentary then explains specific phrases: the connection of 'the beautiful does not distinguish' to 'similar in mixture'; the reference to two schools in the Canon; the explanation of 'temperamental qualities'; a question about how parts of different temperaments can be similar in reality, answered by noting that each temperament type has a wide range such that differences remain within the purpose; the blending by the generative power; the grammatical structure of 'benefits'; the meaning of 'He made them' and 'because they'; the reference to 'as' pointing back to earlier text; and interpretations of phrases about movement, exiting, and soon.

The Nutritive, Growth, and Four Servient Powers
Describes servient powers serving generative and formative powers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the nutritive and growth powers serve the generative and formative powers, and how four additional powers serve all of them.

Core Idea
Four servient powers assist the nutritive and growth powers in generation.

This lesson explains the hierarchy of powers in the body: the generative and formative powers are served by the nutritive and growth powers, which in turn are served by four other powers: attracting, digesting, retaining, and expelling. These work together in the process of generation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on servient powers
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully.
2
Identify the first two servants
Notice that the nutritive and growth powers serve the generative and formative powers. The nutritive power is apparent, while the growth power enlarges organs.
3
Understand the role of growth
The growth power enlarges organs and widens passages until they are suitable for generation, which is why semen forms only after organs are large.
4
Identify the four additional servants
These four powers (attracting, digesting, retaining, expelling) serve the four previous powers (generative, formative, nutritive, growth).
They serve the nutritive and growth, which in turn serve the first two.

The Attractive Power in the Stomach (First Three Proofs)
First three proofs for the attractive power in the stomach

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first three proofs that establish the existence of an attractive power in the stomach, based on the movement of food, vomiting experiments, and animal behavior.

Core Idea
Food's upward journey proves a pulling force in the stomach.

The first of the five proofs for the attractive power focuses on the stomach. By examining how food moves from mouth to stomach despite being heavy, and how the stomach attracts sweet substances and even rises in some animals, we see clear evidence of an inherent attractive force.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice the structure: definition followed by three proofs.
The first proof uses logical deduction (not natural, not voluntary -> compulsory).
2
Identify the three observations
List the three observations: (1) food moves upward when swallowed upside-down, (2) sweet food comes out last in vomiting, (3) stomach rises in short-esophagus animals.
3
Understand the conclusion
Each observation leads to the conclusion that a pulling force must exist in the stomach. Follow the reasoning for each.
The common thread is that these phenomena cannot be explained by natural tendency or voluntary action.

The first: the attractive power, which is that which attracts the needed part of the food. And its existence is indicated by five proofs. The first: the movement of food from the mouth to the stomach is not natural, otherwise its movement upward would be prevented, but it would have to move downward alone because it is heavy. And the consequent is false, since indeed a person may swallow (i.e., gulp) food when inverted, completely. Then its movement is upward. And it is not voluntary, either on the part of the food, for it has no consciousness so no will can be imagined from it, or on the part of the eater, for indeed food may move from the mouth to the stomach when there is severe need for it, without the will of the eater; rather, the person may want to prevent it to chew it, but the food overwhelms him and is attracted inward. So it must be compulsory, so there must be a compeller, which is either a push from above by saying: the animal pushes it voluntarily, and its falsehood has become clear, or an attraction from below, which is that the stomach attracts it by an attractive power in it, which is what is sought. The second: when a person eats some food, then takes something sweet after it and induces vomiting, the last thing that comes out by vomiting is found to be sweet, and that is only due to the stomach's attraction of it (i.e., the sweet) to its bottom by means of its natural love for it. And when a person takes a bitter medicine, the esophagus and stomach attempt to expel and reject it and do not swallow it except with difficulty, and perhaps it is ejected by vomiting involuntarily. The third: the stomach may ascend to attract food in some animals with a short esophagus, like the crocodile, until it comes out at the time of feeding so that it meets its mouth because it is wide, and that is only due to its desire to attract food. So these three proofs indicate that there is an attractive power in the stomach. The fourth: the uterus, after the cessation of menstruation, when it is free from excess fluids, its desire for semen intensifies until it is felt to attract the urethra into its interior like the cupping glass attracts blood into its interior. And some have called the uterus an animal longing for semen. So by this proof, the existence of the attractive power in the uterus is established. The fifth: the blood that is in the liver mixed with the three waste products, namely phlegm, yellow bile, and black bile, then these mixed matters become differentiated, and each organ receives a type of moisture suitable for it. Were it not that in each organ there is an attractive power for that moisture suitable for it, that differentiation and flow of each moisture to its organ constantly or mostly would be prevented. And this is a clear proof of the existence of the attractive power in all organs.

Commentary
This passage presents five proofs for the existence of the attractive power (الجاذبة) in the body. The first proof observes that food moves upward from the mouth to the stomach, which is not natural for a heavy substance, nor voluntary (since the food has no will and sometimes the eater cannot prevent it). Thus, a compeller is needed, which can only be a pulling force from the stomach. The second proof uses the example of vomiting: sweet food put after a meal comes out last because the stomach attracts it to its bottom due to natural affinity; bitter medicine is rejected. The third proof notes that some animals (like the crocodile) bring their stomach up to meet the food, showing attraction. These three prove an attractive power in the stomach. The fourth proof shows the uterus attracting semen, seen in its strong desire post-menstruation. The fifth proof argues from the differentiation of blood in the liver: each organ attracts the moisture that suits it, proving an attractive power in all organs collectively. The passage thus establishes the attractive power as a necessary faculty.

Commentary
This passage presents Aristotle's argument for the origination of the soul and the refutation of reincarnation, followed by criticisms and alternative considerations. It begins by stating that the origination of the soul from the Active Intellect requires a condition: the origination of the body, which is the receptive potential for the soul's management. Further Objections to Aristotle's Original Proof
Listing objections to Aristotle's proof from other perspectives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be aware of three specific objections that can be raised against Aristotle's original argument for the soul's origination and refutation of reincarnation.

Core Idea
Aristotle's proof is challenged on the necessity of a condition, the identity of the condition, and the necessity of emanation.

These objections question key premises: whether every originated thing needs an originated condition, whether the body is the specific condition, and whether a body necessarily receives a new soul when it occurs.

Guided Path

First objection: Condition not necessarily originated
The objector denies that every originated thing requires an originated condition; the free agent (al-fāʿil al-mukhtār) can specify times without a motivator.

2
Second objection: Body may not be the condition
Even if a condition is needed, it might be something else, not the body.
3
Third objection: Body does not necessitate a new soul
If a transmigrating soul is already attached to the body, a new soul does not necessarily emanate.
This directly responds to the refutation of reincarnation.

Commentary
This passage presents Aristotle's argument for the origination of the soul and the refutation of reincarnation, followed by criticisms and alternative considerations. It begins by stating that the origination of the soul from the Active Intellect requires a condition: the origination of the body, which is the receptive potential for the soul's management. When the body occurs, a soul necessarily flows upon it due to the generality of emanation and the presence of the receptive potential. This refutes reincarnation because if a body already had a transmigrating soul, a new soul would also flow upon it, resulting in two souls for one body, which is necessarily false. The passage then notes that this argument is circular, as Aristotle proves the soul's origination by the necessity of reincarnation (if souls were pre-eternal) and then refutes reincarnation by the origination of souls. Two alternative ways to refute reincarnation are suggested: (1) the transmigrating soul would remember past states, (2) the constant preparedness of bodies for souls versus sudden mass deaths disproves transmigration. Both are criticized: remembrance is not necessary, and the number of animal bodies is sufficient. Finally, further objections against the original proof are listed: the need for an originated condition is denied, the condition could be other than the body, and an occurring body does not necessitate a soul if a transmigrating soul is already attached.

Commentary
This passage defines the nature of the soul's attachment to the body. The passage concludes that this entire process is from the Free Agent (God) and does not require proving intermediate powers.

( الخامس : إنها عاقلة لذواتها إذ التعقل حضور الماهية المجردة عن الغواشي الغريبة ( عند الشيء ) المجرد القائم بذاته ولا شك أن ماهيتها حاضرة لذواتها فإن حضور الماهية أعم من حضور الماهية المغايرة وغير المغايرة ) والتغاير الاعتباري كاف في تحقق الحضور . The Third Objective: On the Rulings of the Intellects. (On the rulings of the intellects, and they are seven: First: They are not originated, because it has preceded that origination requires matter. Second: They are neither generated nor corrupted, since that is the leaving of a form of matter and the donning of another form, so it can only be conceived in a composite that has two aspects of receptivity and act. As for the simple, there are no two aspects of receptivity and act in it; thus the intellects, due to their simplicity, are not corruptible but eternal. Third: The species of each intellect is confined to its individual, since its individuation is by its essence; otherwise it would be by matter and its accompaniments, as has preceded. Fourth: Their essence encompasses their perfections; i.e., whatever is possible for it is actually present, and whatever is not present for it is impossible, because you know that origination requires matter whose preparedness is renewed by perpetual circular motion, so it can only be conceived in a material thing that is under time, while the intellects are immaterial and not temporal. Fifth: They are intellective of themselves, since intellection is the presence of an essence stripped of foreign veils to a thing that is immaterial and subsistent by itself, and there is no doubt that their essence is present to themselves, for the presence of the essence is more general than the presence of the other and the non-other, and conceptual distinction is sufficient for the realization of presence. There is a consideration in this, because it is possible that the condition for intellection is the presence of an other essence, as in the senses; for sensation occurs only by obtaining an other form at the sense, not by obtaining a form absolutely, otherwise the senses would perceive their own external forms, which is false. Sixth: They know universals, and likewise every immaterial thing that subsists by itself knows universals, because every such immaterial thing can know, since its essence is free from attachments foreign to its quiddity and material impurities that prevent intellection; so its quiddity does not need an operation to make it intelligible. For if it does not know, it would be a faculty of the knower. So every immaterial thing in itself can know. And everything that can know can know together with another, because we know by necessity that there is no opposition in acts of intellection. So every intelligible can be known together with every other intelligible. Also, everything that is known is not free from the correctness of judging it by universal attributes like unity, possibility, etc., and judgment between two things requires their togetherness, so every intelligible can be known with another in general. Then it is possible for it, i.e., the immaterial thing, to be accompanied by the abstract essence, i.e., the essence of another in the mind, because intellection is the obtaining of the essence of the intelligible in the knower. So if the immaterial thing knows together with the essence of another, both are present in the mind, so each accompanies the other therein. So if it is possible for it to accompany the abstract essence of another in the mind, then it is also possible for it to accompany it absolutely, i.e., whether the immaterial thing is existent in the mind or in the external world, since its being, i.e., the obtaining of the essence of the immaterial thing in the mind, is not a condition for absolute accompaniment and its validity, because if it were a condition for absolute accompaniment and its validity, then its accompaniment to the mind, which is more specific than absolute accompaniment, would also be conditional upon its being in the mind, because the more specific must be conditional upon what the more general is conditional upon. Then a circularity would result, because the being of the essence of the immaterial thing in the mind is the very accompaniment to it which is conditional upon it. And if the being of the immaterial thing in the mind is not a condition for the accompaniment between it and the essence of another, then accompaniment between them is permissible when the immaterial thing is existent in the external world. Commentary
This passage is an enumeration of seven rulings (ḥukm) concerning the intellects (al-ʿuqūl). The author lists each ruling followed by a brief explanation. The first ruling states that the intellects are not originated (ḥāditha) because origination requires matter, which they lack. The second ruling states they are not subject to generation and corruption, as those processes involve composition of matter and form, but intellects are simple. The third ruling asserts that each intellect's species is confined to its individual; individuation is by essence, not matter. The fourth ruling explains that all potential perfections are actual in the intellects because they are immaterial and not subject to time. The fifth ruling argues that intellects are self-aware, but the author notes an objection: perhaps intellection requires an other essence, as in sensation. The sixth ruling begins by stating that intellects know universals. A long argument follows to show that all immaterial substances can know, and that they can know multiple things together without incompatibility. The argument considers conditions for accompaniment of essences in the mind. The passage is cut off mid-explanation of the sixth ruling.
