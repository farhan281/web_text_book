The Eighth Proof: Creation as Beneficence
Author presents the eighth proof that creation is an act of beneficence from an eternal will

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the eighth proof from creation as beneficence and how it demonstrates the necessity of eternal beneficence from God.

Core Idea
Creation is either beneficence, harm, or futility—only beneficence fits God.

This lesson presents the eighth proof in the series, which argues that because God is supremely merciful and wise, creation must be an act of beneficence. The author examines the alternatives and shows that any omission would imply an imperfection in God.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Begin by reading the Arabic text and its translation to get a sense of the argument's flow. Notice the logical structure: trilemma, elimination, then further reasoning.
The passage is concise; each sentence builds on the previous.
2
Analyze the trilemma
Identify the three possibilities for creation: beneficence, harm, or futility. Understand why harm and futility are eliminated based on God's attributes.
Harm conflicts with mercy; futility conflicts with wisdom.
3
Examine the omission argument
Consider why God cannot omit beneficence: it would be due to inability, ignorance, or miserliness—all impossible for God. Conclude that beneficence must be eternal.
This reinforces the necessity of creation as an act of divine goodness.
4
Understand the role of eternal motive
Note that the full argument requires showing that the motive for beneficence is eternal, conditions were present, and obstacles absent. This makes the act necessary from eternity.
This connects to broader kalam discussions on divine will and eternity.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If the world were impossible in eternity, it could never become possible.

This lesson presents a second rational proof for the existence of a necessary being. The argument focuses on the world's status in eternity: if it were possible, it must be obtained; if impossible, it could not transition to possibility. By eliminating impossibility, the proof establishes the necessity of a cause that makes the world necessarily obtained.

Identify the main premise

2

The author then considers the falsehood of the premise. If it were not possible, its contradictory would be impossibility in eternity. This would imply a transition from essential impossibility to essential possibility.
Essential impossibility means something cannot be by its own essence.

Understand the refutation
The author claims this transition is impossible, citing many proofs. Therefore, the world was not impossible in eternity.
4

Since the world was not impossible, it must be possible in eternity. And being possible in eternity, it requires a cause to bring it into existence necessarily.
The conclusion is implied: the world is necessarily obtained through a necessary cause.

This passage shows how they reason that an everlasting thing cannot have a beginning, because if it did, it would be capable of ceasing to exist—which contradicts its eternal nature. Commentary
This passage presents the tenth proof for the eternity of the world, followed by the philosophers' reasoning to conclude that the world is eternal (sempiternal). It is a combined proof-and-conclusion passage. The proof begins by stating a property of the heavens: they cannot move in a straight line. From this, it is inferred that heavenly bodies do not undergo division, tearing, generation, or corruption. The philosophers then claim that through these ten proofs (including this one) it is established that the world is eternal in existence (abadī al-wujūd). They then argue that if the world is eternal in existence, it must also be sempiternal (azalī), i.e., without beginning. Their reasoning is a conditional argument: if the world were not sempiternal, it would have been non-existent in pre-eternity (al-azal). In that case, its essence would have the property of being capable of non-existence (qābiliyya li-l-‘adam). That property of being possible to be non-existent would remain forever, because it is an essential characteristic. Therefore, even after the world exists, it would still be possible for it to become non-existent. But they claim that this possibility of ceasing to exist is impossible (muḥāl) for the world, presumably because it is now actual and necessary in some sense. Hence, the premise that the world was non-existent in pre-eternity is false; therefore the world must be sempiternal. The passage concludes the research on this topic with a standard pious phrase: 'And God knows best what is correct.'

The second proof, which we relied upon in the theological books, is that it is said: The eternal (al-qadīm) is either necessary in itself (wājib li-dhātih) or possible in itself (mumkin li-dhātih). If it is necessary in itself, then non-existence is impossible for it, because what is meant by 'necessary in itself' is that whose essence is not receptive to non-existence, and whatever is like that, non-existence is impossible for it. As for if it is possible in itself, we say: Everything that is possible in itself has an efficient cause (mu'aththir). The first is invalid, because the free agent is one who acts by means of intention (qaṣd), and intending to bring something into existence while it is enduring is impossible; rather, intending its coming into existence is only possible either at the time of its non-existence or at the time of its origination. On both suppositions, everything that occurs by a free agent is originated (ḥādith), and the eternal is not originated, so attributing the eternal to a free agent is impossible. The second division is that it is said: That eternal is caused by an eternal cause. From the necessity of the existence of these two things follows the necessity of perpetuity of that effect and the impossibility of its non-existence. Commentary
This passage presents the second proof for the impossibility of the eternal ceasing to exist. It is a proof that proceeds by logical division. The author first divides the eternal into two categories: necessary in itself (wājib li-dhātih) and possible in itself (mumkin li-dhātih). If it is necessary in itself, then non-existence is impossible by definition because a necessary being's essence does not admit non-existence. If it is possible in itself, then it requires an efficient cause (mu'aththir). That cause is either a free agent (fā'il mukhtār) or an agent by essence (mūjib bi-dhāt). The free agent acts by intention, and intention to bring something into existence can only occur when the thing is non-existent or when it is being originated; but the eternal is never non-existent nor originated, so a free agent cannot be the cause of an eternal. Therefore, the cause must be an agent by essence, i.e., a necessary cause. That cause itself is either possible or necessary. If possible, the same division applies, leading to an infinite regress. So it must be necessary in itself. Then the effect of this necessary cause is either unconditional or conditional upon a condition. If unconditional, then the impossibility of non-existence of the cause entails the impossibility of non-existence of the effect. If conditional, the condition itself must also be necessary, and both the cause and condition being necessary entail the perpetual existence of the effect. Thus, in all cases, the eternal cannot cease to exist. The author then applies this to the specific case of 'rest' (sukūn) to show that rest, being subject to passing away, cannot be eternal.

The Third Objection: God's Influence and the World's Eternity
Poses an objection that God's influence entails either eternal world or an eternal opposite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third objection against temporal origination, which argues that God's influence on the world leads to the eternity of the world or its opposite.

Core Idea
God's influence on the world forces a dilemma: either the world is eternal or its opposite is eternal.

This lesson examines a sophisticated objection raised against the doctrine that the world originated in time. The objector argues that God's status as an influence on the world's existence forces a dilemma: either the world is eternal or an opposite of the world is eternal. By following the logic, you will see how the objection challenges the very possibility of temporal creation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Dilemma
The passage opens by stating that God is an influence on the world's existence, and this influence must be either by essential origination or by way of possibility. Recognize this as the two horns of the dilemma.
The term 'influence' (muʾaththir) is central—it implies agency.
2
Analyze the First Horn
If the influence is by essential origination (ījād dhātī), then because God's essence is eternal, the world must also be eternal. This is a direct consequence.
3
Analyze the Second Horn
If the influence is by possibility (ṣiḥḥa), then God must be truly capable of both action and inaction. Otherwise He would be an essential necessitator (mūjib bi-dh-dhāt).
Capability requires the possibility of both action and its opposite.
4
Examine the Argument on Inaction
The objector argues that inaction (tark) cannot be mere absence of the act, because power cannot affect non-existence (pure negation) and the original non-existence already remains. So inaction must be an act of a contrary (ḍidd).
This step refutes the possibility of simple non-action.
5
See the Conclusion
Thus, in eternity, God either acts (creates the world) or leaves it (creates its opposite). Either way, something eternal exists, undermining the claim that the world originated in time.

Commentary

This refutes the claim of temporal origination.
