Commentary
This passage presents the first proof of the proponents of necessary emanation (القائلون بالموجب). It begins by noting that earlier arguments for the free agent have already been given, so they are not repeated. Then it states that the necessitarians, who claim a necessary cause can only produce one effect, have several proofs. The first proof is based on the concepts of being a source for two different effects (A and B). If the cause's quiddity includes both concepts, the cause would be composite, which is impossible (since the necessary cause must be simple). If both concepts are outside the quiddity, they become accidents dependent on the quiddity, and then the question of how the quiddity could be a cause for two different concepts arises, leading to an infinite regress. If one concept is inside and the other outside, then the quiddity is composite, and the internal concept is not an effect of the quiddity, so only the external concept can be an effect, meaning only one effect is possible. This argument uses the impossibility of composition and infinite regress to defend the necessitarian position.

First Argument: Conceptual Composition of the Cause
Presents the first argument that a cause cannot have two distinct concepts of causation without composition or regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first argument that a single cause cannot be the source of two different effects without leading to composition, regress, or a single effect.

Core Idea
A simple cause cannot contain two distinct aspects of causation.

The first argument against a single cause producing multiple effects is rooted in conceptual analysis. It examines the relationship between the cause's essence and the concepts of being a source of each effect, showing that any way of relating them leads to absurdity. This argument highlights the importance of simplicity in the necessary being.

Guided Path
1
Understand the setup
The argument uses two hypothetical effects, 'alif' and 'ba', to examine the conceptual relationship between the cause and its effects. The concept of being the source of 'alif' is distinct from that of being the source of 'ba'.
These symbols represent any two different effects.
2
Follow the three possibilities
Consider whether both concepts are internal to the essence, both external, or one internal and one external. Each leads to a problem: composition, infinite regress, or a single effect.
Composition is impossible for a necessary being.
3
Grasp the conclusion
The only consistent outcome is that only one effect is possible, because an internal aspect cannot be an effect (it is prior) and an external aspect leads to regress.

Commentary
This passage presents five arguments (hujaj) used by those who maintain that a cause (al-'illa) which necessitates (al-mujib bi al-dhat) cannot produce more than one effect (ma'lul). The author begins by noting that these arguments have been mentioned before alongside counter-arguments, so he will not repeat the refutations, but he lists the proofs for reference. The first argument is conceptual: if the cause is the source of two different effects (symbolized as 'alif' and 'ba'), then the concepts of being the source of each are distinct. If both concepts are internal to the essence of the cause, the cause becomes composite (since it contains two distinct aspects), which is impossible for a simple necessary being. If both are external, they are accidents dependent on the essence, leading to an infinite regress of concepts. If one is internal and the other external, the essence is composite and the effect is one (since the internal aspect is not an effect). Thus, in all cases, only one effect is possible. The second argument is a reductio: if two different effects issued from the same cause by the same consideration (i'tibar), then by that consideration one effect would both issue and not issue (since the other effect is different), a contradiction. The third argument uses analogy from natural phenomena: different effects (cold from water, heat from fire) indicate different natures (taba'i') of the causes. So if a single cause produced different effects, its nature would be different from itself, which is absurd. The fourth argument posits that there must be similarity (mushabaha) and suitability (munasaba) between cause and effect. For example, knowledge necessitates being knowledgeable, not being movable. Therefore, one cause cannot be similar to two different effects simultaneously, as that would require it to be both similar and dissimilar to itself. The fifth argument is that if a necessary cause could produce more than one effect, there would be no reason to limit the number, leading to an infinite number of effects, which is impossible. These arguments collectively aim to prove the necessitarian position that a single cause necessitates a single effect.

Second Argument: Contradiction in a Single Consideration
Presents the second argument using reductio to show that two effects cannot issue from one consideration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second argument that a single cause cannot produce two different effects by the same consideration, as that leads to a contradiction.

Core Idea
A single consideration cannot result in both issuing and not issuing the same effect.

The second argument is a reductio ad absurdum: if a single cause produces two different effects by the same consideration (i'tibar), then by that consideration one effect both issues and does not issue, which is a logical contradiction. This argument emphasizes the need for a unified causal relation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the reductio setup
Assume a single cause produces two distinct effects, A and B, by the same consideration. Then the consideration that produced A also produced B (which is not A).
2
Identify the contradiction
By that same consideration, since it produced B (not A), it did not produce A. So we say: by the one consideration, A issued and did not issue.
This is a violation of the principle of non-contradiction.
3
Conclude
Therefore, it is impossible for two different effects to proceed from a single cause by the same consideration.

Fifth Argument: Impossibility of Infinite Effects
Presents the fifth argument that multiple effects would lead to an infinite regress, which is impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fifth argument that if a single necessary cause could produce more than one effect, it would lead to an infinite number of effects, which is impossible.

Core Idea
If one cause can produce multiple effects, there is no reason to stop at a finite number.

The fifth argument is a 'slippery slope' reasoning: if a single necessitator can produce more than one effect, there is no logical reason why it should produce exactly two or three rather than any other number. Since the cause is necessary and undetermined, it would have to produce an infinite number of effects, which is impossible. Thus, it must produce exactly one.

Guided Path
1
Understand the principle of indifference
If a necessary cause does not have a limiting factor, it would have no reason to prefer one number of effects over another.
2

The Sixth Objection: The Cause's Essence and Infinite Regress
The author attacks the notion of a cause having a single effect by analyzing its essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the objection that a cause can only have one effect leads to the same problem as multiple effects, namely infinite regress.

Core Idea
Even a single-effect cause leads to infinite regress when its essence and necessitation are distinguished.

This lesson presents a powerful objection that turns the opponents' argument against them. The author analyzes the very concept of a cause having a single effect and shows that it suffers from the same difficulty as multiple effects. This forces the opponent to abandon their line of reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection statement
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the translation to understand the flow of the argument.
The objection is presented as a refutation of the claim that a cause necessitates only one effect.

Understand the distinction between essence and necessitation

The author concludes that the same problem exists for both single and multiple effects, so the opponent cannot use it as a proof against multiple effects.

This passage is a refutation of an opponent's argument that a thing cannot be both a receiver (قابل) and an agent (فاعل) simultaneously. Each example shows that a single thing can be both receiver and agent, refuting the opponent's claim. The author concludes with 'وهو المطلوب' (that is the desired).

Why the First Emanation Cannot Be a Soul: Objection and Refutation
Objection and response: Why the first emanation cannot be a soul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophical argument against the first emanation being a soul and the refutation based on the problem of infinite regress and self-priority.

Core Idea
The soul's use of a corporeal instrument does not disprove it being the first emanation.

In this passage, the author engages with an objection that the first emanation cannot be a soul because a soul requires a corporeal instrument to act. The response cleverly shows that this line of reasoning either leads to an infinite regress or is self-defeating, thereby preserving the possibility that the first emanation could be a soul.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the structure: a question, an opponent's claim, then a rebuttal starting with 'قلنا'.
The passage is a typical kalam objection-response format.
2
Understand the opponent's claim
The opponent argues that a soul cannot be the first emanation because it only acts through a corporeal instrument. Identify the claim in the text.
3
Analyze the two possibilities
The author considers two ways the soul might influence its instrument: through another instrument (leading to infinite regress) or directly (which does not negate its being a soul).
This is the core logical move.
4
Recognize the logical consequences
The first possibility leads to either an infinite regress (tasalsul) or self-priority (tqaddum al-shay' 'ala nafsihi), both impossible. The second possibility does not refute the soul's nature.
5
Conclude the argument
Since the opponent's reasoning fails in both cases, the objection is invalid. The same logic applies to other similar cases.
Reflect on how this argument strengthens the possibility of the first emanation being a soul.

السؤال الرابع: لم لا يجوز أن يكون الصادر الأول نفساً؟ قوله: ولأن النفس هو الذي لا يؤثر في الأثر إلا بآلة جسمانية، قلنا: هذا الكلام خطأ. لأن تأثيرها في تلك الآلة الجسمانية. إن كان بآلة أخرى جسمانية لزم التسلسل، أو يلزم تقدم الشيء على نفسه وهو محال. وإن كان تأثيرها في تلك الآلة لا بواسطة آلة أخرى، مع أن هذا لا يقدح في كونها نفساً. فلم لا يجوز أن يكون الحال في سائر الصور كذلك؟
The fourth question: Why is it not permissible that the first emanation be a soul? His statement: Because the soul is that which does not affect the effect except through a corporeal instrument. We said: This speech is mistaken. Because its influence on that corporeal instrument, if it were through another corporeal instrument, then an infinite regress would follow, or the priority of a thing over itself would follow, and that is impossible. And if its influence on that instrument is without mediation of another instrument, then this does not impair its being a soul. So why is it not permissible that the case be likewise in other instances?

Commentary
This passage is a kalam objection and response. The opponent argues that the first emanation cannot be a soul (nafs) because a soul only acts through a corporeal instrument (āla jismāniyya). The responder (author) rebuts this as mistaken. The argument proceeds: If the soul's influence on that corporeal instrument itself requires another corporeal instrument, an infinite regress (tasalsul) would occur, or the thing would be prior to itself – both impossible. If, instead, the soul influences the first instrument without a mediating instrument, then the fact that the soul uses an instrument does not negate its being a soul. The responder concludes that the same reasoning applies to all similar cases, so the objection fails.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The objection stands because the soul's use of an instrument proves it cannot be the first emanation.
Correction: The responder shows that the use of an instrument does not necessarily imply an infinite regress or negate the soul's nature, so the objection is not valid.

Key Takeaway
The objection that the soul requires a corporeal instrument does not preclude it from being the first emanation, as the instrument itself does not necessitate an infinite regress or self-priority.
