The Philosophers' View on Multiplicity from the First Emanation
Explains and critiques the philosophers' emanation theory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' argument for how multiplicity arises from the First Principle and why a direct emanation of many things from the One is impossible, as well as the proposed solution involving the possible essence.

Core Idea
Multiplicity enters the universe through the first effect's own possibility, not from the One directly.

This passage tackles a classic challenge in philosophical theology: if God is absolutely simple and one, how can a world of multiplicity emanate from Him? The philosophers' response is presented in its strongest form, then scrutinized for logical consistency. You will see how the argument leads to the conclusion that the first effect must combine its own possibility with existence from its cause, thereby generating multiplicity without compromising divine unity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it, then read the English translation. Notice the flow of the argument from problem to solution.
The passage is cut off at the end; focus on the logical structure presented.
2
Follow the logical dilemma
Identify the two scenarios: either the first emanation produces only one thing (leading to infinite causal chains) or it produces many things. The author argues the first is false, and the second leads to a dilemma about multiplicity being necessary or possible.
Pay attention to the refutation using the two grains of wheat.
3
Understand the solution based on possible essence
The solution is that the first effect has possibility from itself and existence from its cause; combining these yields multiplicity. This avoids the One directly producing many.
This distinction is key to preserving divine simplicity.

Commentary
This passage is a critique and refinement of the philosophers' doctrine regarding how multiplicity arises from the First Principle. It begins by stating the topic: explaining the philosophers' view on how the multiplicity found in possible things emanates from the first emanation. The author then presents the argument in the best possible form. The first scenario considered is that the first emanation itself only produces one effect, which would require an infinite chain of causes and effects, implying that any two existents are related as cause and effect. This is refuted by a counterexample: two grains of wheat are neither causes nor effects of each other. Therefore, it is necessary that the first effect emanates more than one. But if the first effect contains multiplicity, that multiplicity is either necessary or possible. If necessary, then there would be more than one necessary being. If possible, it must come from the Necessary Existent, meaning the One directly produced multiplicity, which is impossible. Thus, the only remaining solution is to consider the possible essence: it has possibility from itself and existence from its cause. When these two aspects—its own possibility and the existence it receives from its cause—are combined, a multiplicity arises that can serve as the source for many effects. The passage concludes by reiterating that the first effect has possibility in itself and existence from another, setting up the foundation for subsequent discussion.

The Philosophers' Problem of Multiplicity and Its Solution
Author explains the solution to the multiplicity problem using three aspects of the first effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the philosophers explained the emergence of multiplicity from a single first cause without compromising divine unity.

Core Idea
Multiplicity arises not directly from the One but from the aspects of the first effect.

The philosophers faced a logical puzzle: if only one proceeds from God (the First Cause), how can there be multiple creatures? The solution lies in analyzing the first effect's own aspects. This passage presents the core argument and the three-aspect solution that became foundational in Islamic philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the initial problem
Read the Arabic text from 'اعلم أنا نعبر' to 'وهو محال' and its translation. Notice how the author sets up the dilemma: if only one proceeds from each cause, then all existents would be causally linked, which is false.
Pay attention to the grain of wheat example – it illustrates a non-causal relationship between two similar things.
2
Identify the proposed solution
Read the next part starting from 'فلم يبق إلا أن يقال' to 'عللا لموجودات ثلاثة'. The solution involves recognizing three aspects in the first effect: possibility, existence, and necessity-by-another.
These three aspects become the principles for matter, form, and the second intellect.
3
Connect the three aspects to the cosmos
Notice how the author assigns each aspect as a cause for a different component of the outermost sphere: possibility -> matter, existence -> form, necessity-by-another -> the moving intellect.

Explanation of the doctrine of these philosophers concerning the sphere of the first sources. How does multiplicity that occurs in possibles proceed from it? Know that we express the words of the people in the best manner, and we say: The first effect. It may be said that only one proceeds from it as well, so the discussion about the second is like that about the first. Then it would follow that no two existents exist among the possibles unless one of them is a cause of the other, and it is known that this is false. For this grain of wheat is not a cause of that other grain, nor an effect of it. So if it is necessary to acknowledge that more than one must proceed from the first effect, and everything from which more than one proceeds has multiplicity, then it follows that multiplicity occurred in the essence of the first effect. If that multiplicity is necessary for its essence, then the Necessary Existent would be more than one; and if it is possible, then it proceeds from the Necessary Existent, so the One has produced more than one, which is impossible. So nothing remains except to say: The possible quiddity has possibility from itself and existence from its cause. So when what it has from itself is joined to what it has from others, a multiplicity occurs that can be made a principle for many effects. If you understand this, we say: The first effect acquired possibility from itself and existence from another. And a thing is not existent unless it is necessary [by another]. So three things have occurred here: possibility, existence, and necessity-by-another. So it is necessary to make these three considerations causes for three existents, and it is necessary to make the nobler a cause for the nobler, and the baser a cause for the baser. So possibility is a cause for the matter of the outermost sphere, and existence is a cause for its form, and necessity-by-another is a cause for the second act which is the intellect moving the outermost sphere. And in this way, from every intellect proceeds an intellect and a sphere, until it ends at the last sphere which is the active intellect. And know that the Chief Master (Ibn Sina) inserted another type of exposition in this discussion. He said: The first intellect intellects the quiddity of itself, and intellects the existence of itself, and intellects its necessity by the First, and intellects the First. So he made its intellection of its own quiddity a cause for the matter of the outermost sphere, and its intellection of its own existence a cause for the form of the outermost sphere, and its intellection of its necessity by the First a cause for the soul of the outermost sphere, and its intellection of the First a cause for the intellect of the outermost sphere. Since it is established that the nobler must be made a cause for the nobler. This is the detailed account of their statements in this chapter.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of the philosophers' doctrine of emanation, specifically how multiplicity arises from the First Cause. The author begins by explaining the problem: if only one proceeds from the First, then the same applies to the second, leading to an infinite regress where every two existents are cause and effect, which is absurd (e.g., two grains of wheat not causally related). So more than one must proceed from the first effect, but that introduces multiplicity in the first effect. If that multiplicity is necessary, then the Necessary would be multiple; if possible, then the One produced multiplicity directly, which is impossible. The solution: the possible quiddity has possibility from itself and existence from its cause. Combining these yields multiplicity that can serve as basis for many effects. Thus the first effect has three aspects: possibility (self), existence (from cause), and necessity-by-another (from cause). These three aspects become causes for three things: matter, form, and the second act (intellect) of the outermost sphere. Then the author presents Ibn Sina's version: the first intellect's intellections of its quiddity, existence, necessity, and the First produce respectively the matter, form, soul, and intellect of the outermost sphere. The passage concludes that this is their detailed account.

Ibn Sina's Four Intellection Scheme
Ibn Sina's detailed account of the first intellect's intellections generating the outermost sphere's components.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ibn Sina's version of emanation, where the first intellect's four intellections generate the matter, form, soul, and intellect of the outermost sphere.

Core Idea
The first intellect's self-awareness and knowledge of the First produce the components of the cosmos.

Ibn Sina refined the emanation scheme by focusing on the intellect's own cognitive acts. Instead of abstract aspects, he used the first intellect's intellections of its quiddity, existence, necessity, and the First as causes for the four elements of the outermost sphere. This passage presents his elegant formulation.

Guided Path
1
Read Ibn Sina's exposition
Read the Arabic from 'واعلم أن الشيخ الرئيس' to the end and its translation. Notice how he lists four intellections of the first intellect.
Each intellection corresponds to a different component: quiddity, existence, necessity, and the First.
2
Match intellections to cosmic components
Identify which intellection causes which: quiddity -> matter, existence -> form, necessity -> soul, knowledge of First -> intellect.
The principle 'nobler causes nobler' is applied here.
3
Reflect on the overall chain
Understand that this pattern repeats for each subsequent intellect until the Active Intellect.
This is the standard emanation scheme in Islamic philosophy.

Commentary
This passage is a complex objection (shubha) against the claim that a wise deity can begin creating the world after an infinite period of inaction. The argument is presented as a reductio ad absurdum, aiming to show that such an action would be incompatible with divine wisdom.

It begins by establishing two premises: (1) God is not a necessary cause but a free agent; (2) a free agent's action must be temporally originated (muhdath). From this it follows that if God created the world, He must have been refraining from action for an infinite time before creation. The objection then argues that it is impossible for a wise deity to begin acting after such eternal inaction.

The proof is given through two 'aspects' (wujuh) or sophisms (shubuhat).

The first sophism considers the specific time of creation: either that time had a special property that made it the appropriate moment for creation, or it did not. The first option is refuted by analyzing how that time could have such a property. The property cannot be essential to the time itself or a concomitant of the time, because then that time would necessitate all other events, undermining the argument from contingency for God's existence. Nor can the property be accidental, because the same problem of specification recurs for the property itself. Thus the first option fails, leaving only the second option.

The second sophism directly argues that if there is no reason for choosing that particular time, then beginning creation after infinite inaction is pure futility (mahd al-'abath). Such arbitrary action is unbecoming of a wise agent. The author draws an analogy: if we see a rational man suddenly start working after years of inactivity with no reason, we would deem him irrational. This is because the transition from eternal abandonment to action without a cause is only expected from foolish agents (like children or madmen), not from a wise deity. Therefore, the objection concludes, beginning intention to create is not befitting the wise agent; it would only befit an ignorant one. Hence, the objector argues, one must affirm that God did not begin creation after eternal inaction—implying either that the world is eternal or that God's action is not truly originated. The passage ends abruptly with "so it is necessary to affirm" (fawajaba ithbat), leaving the conclusion open, but the thrust is clear: this is a polemic against the orthodox view that God created the world in time.

Commentary
This passage is a complex objection (shubha) against the claim that a wise deity can begin creating the world after an infinite period of inaction. The argument is presented as a reductio ad absurdum, aiming to show that such an action would be incompatible with divine wisdom.

It begins by establishing two premises: (1) God is not a necessary cause but a free agent; (2) a free agent's action must be temporally originated (muhdath). From this it follows that if God created the world, He must have been refraining from action for an infinite time before creation. The objection then argues that it is impossible for a wise deity to begin acting after such eternal inaction.

The proof is given through two 'aspects' (wujuh) or sophisms (shubuhat).

The first sophism considers the specific time of creation: either that time had a special property that made it the appropriate moment for creation, or it did not. The first option is refuted by analyzing how that time could have such a property. The property cannot be essential to the time itself or a concomitant of the time, because then that time would necessitate all other events, undermining the argument from contingency for God's existence. Nor can the property be accidental, because the same problem of specification recurs for the property itself. Thus the first option fails, leaving only the second option.

The second sophism directly argues that if there is no reason for choosing that particular time, then beginning creation after infinite inaction is pure futility (mahd al-'abath). Such arbitrary action is unbecoming of a wise agent. The author draws an analogy: if we see a rational man suddenly start working after years of inactivity with no reason, we would deem him irrational. This is because the transition from eternal abandonment to action without a cause is only expected from foolish agents (like children or madmen), not from a wise deity. Therefore, the objection concludes, beginning intention to create is not befitting the wise agent; it would only befit an ignorant one. Hence, the objector argues, one must affirm that God did not begin creation after eternal inaction—implying either that the world is eternal or that God's action is not truly originated. The passage ends abruptly with "so it is necessary to affirm" (fawajaba ithbat), leaving the conclusion open, but the thrust is clear: this is a polemic against the orthodox view that God created the world in time.

The Fourth Section. Muhammad ibn Zakariya said: 'When it is established by proof that the world is originated, and it is established that the beginning of creating the world is not befitting the wisdom of God Almighty, then it must be that the initiator of bringing the world into existence is an ignorant agent, and that is the soul. Its exposition: The soul was heedless of matter from eternity to eternity, then it accidentally turned its attention to matter, and believed that if it mixed with matter, it could attain pleasures. So a natural, instinctive love for this matter occurred in the soul, and this love is beyond doubt. Do you not see that each soul's love for its own body is a complete and perfect love, no more perfect love can occur? So when that accidental turning of attention and that absent perception occurred to the soul, and from that turning of attention resulted this instinctive love, necessarily the soul attached itself to matter, and from its composition resulted the animal [soul]. The wise God knew that this attachment is a cause for opening the door of calamities, evils, and harms, and because matter does not accept a solid, perfect composition free from all aspects of harm. However, the Creator Almighty, just as He knew that, also knew that it is impossible to prevent the soul from that attachment, because love had reached such strength and perfection that it cannot be removed. And the wise God also knew a third thing: that the soul cannot make a good composition; rather, if one were to restrict to what the soul undertakes by itself, those compositions would be corrupt and quickly perishable. Thus severe and numerous pains would occur quickly from those compositions. So when the wise, merciful God knew these three meanings, He knew that the most correct and most beneficial is that the bodies which the soul attempted to attach to should be composed in the composition closest to well-being and farthest from accepting corruption, so that goods multiply and calamities diminish as much as possible. Then He Almighty pours the light of intellect upon the substance of the soul, so that it becomes manifest to it through the light of intellect that the calamities occurring in this composition are more than the goods occurring in it. Then the soul's nature becomes averse to mixing with matter, and it returns to its world empty, purified from attention to the world of matter.' He said: 'And along this path, those three proofs are not valid. As for the first proof, which is their saying: How is it befitting for an agent to move from abandonment to action without a reason? This is not valid here. Because this, although not befitting a wise agent, yet it is not unbecoming for an ignorant, frivolous agent. Then if the ignorant, frivolous agent undertakes an action that is not proper, and the wise agent knows that he cannot prevent him from it, the wise agent is excused in directing that action to the most correct and most beneficial aspect in it as much as possible.'

Commentary
This passage presents a philosophical proof for the soul as the initial cause of the world and then refutes the first of three objections against it. The proof begins by establishing two premises: (1) the world is originated (hadith), and (2) beginning the creation of the world is not befitting the wisdom of God (who is all-wise). Therefore, the initiator must be an ignorant agent, and that agent is the soul (al-nafs). The author then explains how the soul came to act: it was originally heedless of matter (al-hayula), but accidentally turned its attention to it and believed that mixing with matter would allow it to attain pleasures. This produced a natural, instinctive love (ʿishq) for matter. This love is so strong that it cannot be removed, as seen in the total love each soul has for its own body. Consequently, the soul attached itself to matter, resulting in the animal soul (al-hayawani, i.e., appetite and anger). The wise God (al-ilah al-hakim) then considered three things: First, this attachment causes calamities and evils because matter cannot receive a perfect composition. Second, it is impossible to prevent the soul from this attachment due to the strength of its love. Third, the soul itself cannot make a good composition; its attempts would be corrupt and quickly perishable, causing severe pains. Knowing these three, God in His wisdom and mercy decided to compose the bodies that the soul seeks in the best possible way—closest to well-being and farthest from corruption—to maximize goods and minimize calamities. Additionally, God pours the light of intellect (nur al-ʿaql) onto the soul, so it realizes that the calamities in this composition outweigh the goods, leading it to detach from matter and return to its pure world. The author then addresses the first objection: How can a wise agent (God) allow such a harmful action? The response is that the agent in question is not the wise God but the ignorant, frivolous soul. The wise God, being unable to prevent the soul, is excused in directing the action to the best possible outcome. This refutation preserves divine wisdom while explaining the existence of evil.

God's Wise Response to the Soul's Action
Razi describes God's threefold knowledge and His wise plan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi explains that God, knowing the soul's unavoidable attachment, directs the outcome to maximize good and minimize evil, and eventually frees the soul through intellect.

Core Idea
God's wisdom transforms unavoidable chaos into the best possible order.

In the previous lesson, we saw that the soul's accidental love forced it to attach to matter. How does God respond? This lesson shows that God knows three things about this situation and, in His wisdom and mercy, decides to compose the bodies in the best possible way. Understand God's decision

3

Refuting the First Objection: The Wise Agent's Excuse
Razi refutes the first objection by distinguishing agents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi refutes the objection that a wise agent cannot move from inaction to action without a reason, by distinguishing between the ignorant initiator (soul) and the wise manager (God).

Core Idea
A wise agent is excused when he redirects an unavoidable ignorant action to the best possible outcome.

The first objection against the soul-as-initiator theory is: how can a wise agent (God) begin creation without a reason? Razi's answer is clear: God is not the initiator; the soul is. God, as a wise agent, sees an ignorant agent (the soul) about to do harm and intervenes to minimize the damage. This lesson will unpack this defense of divine wisdom.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
The objection is: it is not befitting for a wise agent to move from not acting to acting without a reason. This seems to apply to God.
This is a standard theological problem.
2
Identify the two agents
Razi distinguishes between the wise agent (God) and the ignorant agent (the soul). The objection only applies to the wise agent, but in this scenario, the initiator is the ignorant soul.
3
Understand the excuse
When the ignorant agent acts improperly, the wise agent, unable to stop him, is excused in directing the action to the best possible outcome. This preserves God's wisdom.
This is a key theodicy move.

Then He Almighty pours the light of intellect upon the substance of the soul, so that it becomes manifest to it through the light of intellect that the calamities occurring in this composition are more than the goods occurring in it. Then the soul's nature becomes averse to mixing with matter, and it returns to its world empty, purified from attention to the world of matter.' Commentary
This passage presents a philosophical proof for the soul as the initial cause of the world and then refutes the first of three objections against it. The proof begins by establishing two premises: (1) the world is originated (hadith), and (2) beginning the creation of the world is not befitting the wisdom of God (who is all-wise). Therefore, the initiator must be an ignorant agent, and that agent is the soul (al-nafs). The author then explains how the soul came to act: it was originally heedless of matter (al-hayula), but accidentally turned its attention to it and believed that mixing with matter would allow it to attain pleasures. This produced a natural, instinctive love (ʿishq) for matter. This love is so strong that it cannot be removed, as seen in the total love each soul has for its own body. Consequently, the soul attached itself to matter, resulting in the animal soul (al-hayawani, i.e., appetite and anger). The wise God (al-ilah al-hakim) then considered three things: First, this attachment causes calamities and evils because matter cannot receive a perfect composition. Second, it is impossible to prevent the soul from this attachment due to the strength of its love. Third, the soul itself cannot make a good composition; its attempts would be corrupt and quickly perishable, causing severe pains. Knowing these three, God in His wisdom and mercy decided to compose the bodies that the soul seeks in the best possible way—closest to well-being and farthest from corruption—to maximize goods and minimize calamities. Additionally, God pours the light of intellect (nur al-ʿaql) onto the soul, so it realizes that the calamities in this composition outweigh the goods, leading it to detach from matter and return to its pure world. The author then addresses the first objection: How can a wise agent (God) allow such a harmful action? The response is that the agent in question is not the wise God but the ignorant, frivolous soul. The wise God, being unable to prevent the soul, is excused in directing the action to the best possible outcome. This refutation preserves divine wisdom while explaining the existence of evil.

First Objection: Does the Soul's Inclination Have a Cause?
Al-Razi rebuts the objection by turning it against the opponent.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The objection against al-Razi's view cuts both ways.

If it did, that undermines the proof of a Creator; if it did not, then why did the cause act at that specific moment? Defending the Timing of the First Act
Objection and two answers on timing of first act

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how two arguments – a tu quoque from Muhammad ibn Zakariyya and an eternal sequence model from the philosophers – jointly rebut the objection that the first act's timing is arbitrary.

Core Idea
The objection about timing is turned back on the opponent and dissolved by sequential creation.

This passage presents an objection to the idea that God's first act of creation occurred at a specific moment. The objector argues that the timing must have a cause, but either way the argument leads to a regress. The response comes in two parts: first from Muhammad ibn Zakariyya, who shows the objection is self-refuting, and then from the philosophers, who model creation as an eternal sequential process without a first moment, thus sidestepping the issue.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
The objection asks: why did the first act happen at that specific time? If due to a property of that time, why not attribute it to the agent; if due to another cause, why not apply the same reasoning elsewhere?
Note that the objector assumes the first act has a definite time.
2
Understand Muhammad ibn Zakariyya's response
He argues that the same question applies to the opponent, who also claims God created at a specific time after infinite inaction. Thus the objection is self-refuting. He adds that such absurd questioning fits only an ignorant fool.
This is a tu quoque (you too) argument.
3
Examine the philosophers' alternative model
The philosophers say celestial motions occur part by part without a first, each event preparing for the next. So there is no first time to question; the objection loses its force.
This model sidesteps the issue by denying a first moment entirely.
