The Mujbira: A Free Agent Indifferent to Human Welfare
Description of the Mujbira who affirm a free agent disregarding human interests.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the defining belief of the Mujbira sect and how it splits into two distinct groups regarding religious duties.

Core Idea
The Mujbira assert God's absolute freedom without regard for human interests.

In this section, the author introduces the fourth group among those who affirm a free agent for the world: the Mujbira. Their core claim is that God acts entirely as He wills, paying no attention to human benefit or harm. However, this group is not monolithic; it has two branches that differ sharply on whether they accept prophethood and religious obligations. Let's read the passage to see the precise distinction.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the passage carefully. Notice how the author first defines the Mujbira's core belief, then splits them into two groups.
2
Identify the two groups
Look for the phrase 'فريقان' and then the descriptions of each. The first denies obligations and prophethood; the second affirms them.
3
Reflect on the significance
Ask yourself: why does the author make this distinction? It shows that determinism can coexist with religion for some, while others take it to its logical extreme.
This connects to the broader debate about free will and divine justice.

Commentary
This passage introduces the fourth group, the Mujbira (الجبرية), who assert that God is a free agent who does as He wills without regard for human welfare. The author notes that this group divides into two subgroups. The first subgroup takes this principle to its extreme and denies religious obligations, prophethood, and eschatology. The second subgroup, those among the Mujbira who still adhere to religion, affirms obligations and prophethood. Thus, the Mujbira include both extreme determinists and those who maintain traditional religious teachings alongside determinism.

The Twelve Schools on Action and Agent
The author enumerates twelve major opinions on actions and the agent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to enumerate the twelve major opinions on actions and their agent as classified by the author, and understand how they derive from the fundamental division between necessity and free choice.

Core Idea
All theological opinions on action reduce to twelve positions, spanning from materialism to divine free will.

The author presents a comprehensive map of all major schools of thought regarding the relationship between actions and their agent. Starting from the earlier division on the justification of suffering, he extends the classification to twelve distinct positions. This lesson guides you through each opinion, showing how they emerge from basic commitments about necessity, free will, and divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Understand the preamble
Read the first part of the passage which divides groups into those justifying suffering by reincarnation, compensation (Mu'tazila), or absolute divine will (Jabriyya). This sets the foundation for the twelve opinions.
The Jabriyya are further split into those denying and those affirming prophetic missions.
2
Identify opinions 1–6
Work through the first six opinions: the Dahriyya (materialists), Democritus, occasionalists, necessitating cause with single emanation, necessitating cause with simultaneous production, and the dualist free agent with prime matter.
These range from pure atheism to dualistic theism.
3
Identify opinions 7–12
Continue with the remaining six: the soul-attachment theory, punishment-for-sins theory, compensation theory, the free agent who denies obligation, the Jabriyya who affirm obligation, and the skeptics.
The last two represent theistic and agnostic positions.
4
Reflect on the structure
Consider how the twelve opinions are systematically derived from the fundamental question: does the agent act by necessity or free choice? Note the author's intention to examine each for evidences and problems.
This classification is a tool for theological analysis, not a mere list.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of all major opinions regarding actions and their agent, culminating in twelve distinct positions. The author begins by recalling the previous division of groups concerning the justification of sufferings: those who believe in reincarnation (the reward for past sins), those who say sufferings are good due to compensations (the Mu'tazila, who split on whether compensation alone suffices or must be accompanied by consideration), and the fourth group—the Jabriyya—who affirm God's absolute free will and ignore human interests and corruptions. The Jabriyya are further divided into those who deny obligation and prophethood (extreme predestinarians) and those who affirm them (moderate Jabriyya). From this division, twelve article-types arise: (1) The Dahriyya (materialists) who say the heavens are necessary in their essences and attributes; (2) Democritus, who posits spherical solid particles moving by themselves as the origin; (3) Those who say events can occur without any cause; (4) Those who assert a cause that necessitates by essence, with the first emanation being one; (5) Those who say the cause necessitates but produces all possibilities at once in a possible order; (6) Those who say God is a free agent but affirms two things—good and evil—with prime matter; (7) Those who say the world arises from the soul's attachment to prime matter; (8) Those who say God creates pains as punishment for prior sins; (9) Those who say pains are good only because of compensations in the Hereafter; (10) Those who affirm a powerful free agent God but deny obligation and mission; (11) The Jabriyya who affirm obligation, reward, and punishment; (12) The skeptics who suspend judgment. The author then states that he intends to examine each section for its evidences and problems, seeking divine help.
