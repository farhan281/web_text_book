Refuting Temporal Precedence in Contingency
The author shows that temporal precedence leads to eternity of bodies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why interpreting 'preceded by non-existence' as temporal leads to an absurdity, and how the author refutes it by showing it entails the eternity of bodies.

Core Idea
Temporal precedence of non-existence leads to eternal bodies, so the definition fails.

The author now tackles a key misinterpretation. Some thinkers define the contingent as 'preceded by non-existence' without specifying the type of precedence. In this passage, al-Isfahani systematically tests each possible meaning—causal, essential, honorary, spatial, and temporal—and shows that only temporal is problematic, leading to an absurd conclusion. Let's follow his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the premise
The author asks: if 'preceded by non-existence' is meant, what type of precedence is intended? He examines each possibility.
This is a critical move in kalam: definitions must be precise.
2
Examine causal and essential precedence
Causal precedence is impossible because non-existence cannot cause existence. Essential precedence is conceded: the contingent's self is prior to its existence from another.
Honorary and spatial are dismissed as obviously not applicable.
3
Focus on temporal precedence
The key case: if non-existence precedes existence temporally, then time must exist. If the precedence has no beginning, time must be eternal.
This is where the argument gets interesting.
4
Follow the chain to absurdity
From natural science, time is a concomitant of motion, which is a concomitant of body. So eternal time implies eternal body. This contradicts the very idea of contingency.
Thus the definition fails if taken temporally.
5
Conclude
The author declares that the interpretation is false because it leads to the eternity of bodies.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the definition of 'al-hadith' as 'preceded by non-existence' if taken as temporal precedence. The author systematically examines each type of precedence. Causal precedence is impossible because non-existence cannot cause existence. Essential precedence is conceded: the contingent (mumkin) has non-deservingness from itself, so its self is essentially prior to its existence from another. Honorary and spatial are obviously not applicable. The critical case is temporal precedence. The author argues that temporal precedence requires the existence of time. If the non-existence of the world precedes its existence without beginning (i.e., infinite temporal priority), then time must be eternal (qadīm). From natural science, time is a concomitant of motion, which is a concomitant of body. Hence, if time is eternal, body must also be eternal. This leads to the absurd conclusion that bodies are eternal, contradicting the definition of contingency. Thus the definition is false when interpreted temporally.

Refuting 'Preceded by Another Existent'
The author examines the definition 'preceded by another existent' and shows it fails if temporal precedence is intended.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the definition 'preceded by another existent' fails if temporal precedence is intended, and how the other types of precedence are unproblematic.

Core Idea
Temporal precedence of cause over effect implies eternal time, which is absurd.

The author continues to test a second common definition of 'al-muhdath' (the originated): that it is preceded by the existence of another existent. He systematically examines five kinds of precedence to see which one could apply to the cause of the world. Only temporal precedence creates a problem, because it forces time to be eternal.

Guided Path

2

The author enumerates five types: causal, essential, honorary, spatial, and temporal. Write them down and note the author's judgment on each.
Causal, essential, and honorary are conceded; spatial is false; temporal leads to absurdity.
3
Understand why three types are conceded
The author concedes causal, essential, and honorary precedence because the cause (God) is indeed prior to the world in these ways. This is not controversial.
These precedences do not conflict with the world's possible nature.
4
Examine the spatial case
Spatial precedence is false by agreement, and even if assumed, it does not negate the possibility of eternal worlds. It is irrelevant to the definition.
Spatial priority does not prove the world is originated in time.
5
See why temporal precedence fails
If we take 'preceded' in time, then the cause's priority with no beginning implies time has no beginning, leading to the same absurdity of eternal time and bodies as before.
This repeats the earlier argument against the first definition.

Core Idea
Non-existence cannot cause existence; essential precedence is conceded.

In this passage, the author refines the discussion by distinguishing five types of precedence. He immediately eliminates causal precedence as impossible, because non-existence cannot be a cause of existence. He then concedes essential precedence, as the contingent world has non-existence inherent to its essence, while honorary and spatial precedence are obviously absurd for non-existence.

Why Temporal Precedence Leads to the Eternity of Bodies
Refutes temporal precedence by showing it entails eternal time and bodies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical consequence of interpreting 'non-existence precedes existence' as temporal precedence: it forces the eternity of time and therefore the eternity of bodies, contradicting the intended origination.

Core Idea
Temporal precedence of non-existence requires eternal time, implying eternal bodies.

After dismissing other types, the author now focuses on temporal precedence. He argues that if the non-existence of the world temporally precedes its existence, this precedence has no beginning, which requires time itself to be eternal. Since physics shows that time is a property of motion and motion of bodies, eternal time means eternal bodies. Thus, the very interpretation meant to prove origination actually proves eternity, making the interpretation invalid.

This passage is the fourth argument (al-hujja al-rabi'a) to establish a sixth type of priority for the priority of non-existence over existence. The argument begins with the obvious priority of yesterday over today. It shows that this priority is not causal, because parts of time are similar, so if one caused another, it would lead to circularity and self-causation. Therefore, priority among parts of time cannot be any of the five types; it is a sixth type. The argument concludes that if this is conceivable, then the priority of non-existence over the existence of an originated thing can be similarly conceived, thereby proving the desired claim.

The Dilemma of Modal Change

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A change in modality is impossible; it leads either to absurdity or to denying the Creator.

This second argument tackles the possibility of a temporal change in the world's modal status. The author presents a powerful dilemma: if the world's essence could change from impossible to possible, either that change occurs contingently or necessarily. The Ninth Argument: Refuting Necessary Concomitance
Proves that if a perfect cause necessarily entails its effect, the cause would be contingent, which is absurd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the claim that a perfect cause necessarily entails its effect is refuted through a reductio argument showing it would make the cause contingent.

Core Idea
If perfect causes necessitated effects, even God would be contingent.

This lesson presents a sophisticated reductio argument against the necessitarian view of causality. The author shows that if a perfect cause always produced its effect immediately, the cause would have to be contingent—an impossibility for the Necessary Being. We'll walk through the logical steps.

Guided Path
1
Understand the claim being refuted
The claim is that a perfect cause necessarily entails its effect immediately, with no possibility of separation. This is the view the argument aims to refute.
2
Observe the empirical premise
We observe that forms and accidents cease to exist. This is an undeniable fact that grounds the argument.
3
Trace the contrapositive reasoning
From the non-existence of effects, we infer the non-existence of their causes via the logical rule: if the consequent (effect) is absent, the antecedent (cause) must also be absent.
4
Examine the two cases for the cause
The cause must be either necessary in itself or possible in itself. The argument shows both lead to contradiction: necessary would become contingent; possible leads to infinite regress and absurdity.
5
Grasp the conclusion
Since both cases are impossible, the original claim is false: it is not necessary that the effect occurs with the perfect cause.

For this reason we said: it is necessary that every temporal event be preceded by another temporal event, without [first]. And the cause existing for all, the efficient cause of the occurrence of all, is that Eternal One. They said: and what establishes that this is the case: that motions are either natural, compulsive, or voluntary. And the meaning we mentioned obtains in all of these three divisions. As for natural motions, they are like the falling of a stone from above to below. And its explanation: that the cause of that falling is the weight residing in the substance of the stone, and that weight remains in all times unchanging, except that its effect in causing each part of those motions is conditioned upon the passing away of the preceding part. That is because if that body had not arrived by its preceding motion at that particular point of distance, then it would be impossible for the weight subsisting in it to cause its transfer from that particular point to another point. So the cause of each part of those motions is that weight. As for the condition of that weight being effective in that particular part of the motion, it is its arrival by the preceding part of the motion at that particular point of distance. And as for compulsive motions, the matter is also like that, because when a person throws a stone upward, he deposits in it a force that causes that stone to ascend upward compulsively. So the cause of all parts of that motion, except that the effect of the force in the particular part of that motion is conditioned upon its completion by the preceding part of the motion to that particular point. And as for voluntary motions, the matter is also like that, because when a person wants to go to a certain town to find his debtor and demand the debt.

Commentary
This passage presents the philosophers' response to the question of how an eternal cause can produce temporal effects. They explain that the eternal principle (al-mabda' al-fayyad) exists eternally, but the condition for each temporal event (hadith) to emanate from it is the prior passing away of the preceding event. Thus every temporal event is preceded by another, forming an infinite temporal series without a first temporal event. The eternal cause is the cause of all. They then illustrate this with motions: natural motion (e.g., stone falling), compulsive motion (e.g., thrown stone), and voluntary motion (e.g., person walking). In each case, the cause of each successive part of motion remains constant (weight, force, or will), but its effect on a particular spatial point is conditioned upon the arrival at that point via the preceding motion. The cause does not change; only the condition of the prior part's completion changes. This explains how an unchanging cause can produce successive effects without itself undergoing change.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some may think the philosophers deny a beginning to the world, but they assume an eternal world with an eternal cause, not a creation ex nihilo.
Correction: The philosophers argue for an eternal world with an eternal cause, but each temporal part depends on the passing of the previous part, avoiding simultaneity.

Checkpoint
According to the philosophers, what is the condition for the emanation of each temporal event from the eternal principle?
A) The eternal principle must change its nature.
B) The preceding temporal event must have passed away.
C) The event must be caused by another temporal event.
D) No condition is necessary.

Key Takeaway
The philosophers answer that the condition for each new temporal event is the passing away of the preceding event, ensuring an eternal cause's unchanging nature.
