The Classical Proof for the Temporal Origination of the Body
The author presents the mutakallimun proof that body is originated because it cannot be free from accidents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure and premises of the classical kalam proof that the body (and therefore the physical world) is temporally originated (hadith).

Core Idea
The body cannot be free from accidents, and whatever is not free from accidents must itself be originated.

This lesson presents the foundational argument used by the mutakallimun to prove that the physical world is not eternal. The proof is a simple syllogism, but its justification requires careful analysis of the nature of bodies and accidents. The author explains each premise, showing why bodies necessarily possess accidents and why those accidents must be originated, leading to the conclusion that bodies themselves have a beginning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the main syllogism
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice how the proof is presented as a conditional argument: if body is not free from accidents, then it is originated.
The syllogism is stated at the opening: 'The body is not free of accidents, and whatever is not free of accidents is originated, so the body is originated.'
2
Understand the first premise: body is not free from akwan
The author explains that 'akwan' (occupancy of space) are accidents additional to the body's essence, and the body always has some location. Therefore the body is never free from these accidents.
This establishes that the body is constantly associated with originated things.
3
Understand why akwan are originated
Each kaw in can cease to exist (e.g., by the body moving), so it is susceptible to non-existence, hence it cannot be eternal. Therefore every kaw in is originated.
4
Follow the reasoning for the second premise and conclusion
Since the body cannot be free from originated things, those originated things must have a beginning (infinite regress is rejected elsewhere). Therefore the body itself must have a beginning, i.e., it is originated.
The author references proofs against accidents without beginning, which is a separate discussion.

Commentary
This passage presents the classical mutakallimun proof for the temporal origination (huduth) of bodies, which is foundational for proving the existence of a Creator. The proof is a syllogism: (1) The body cannot be free from accidents (hawadith). (2) Whatever cannot be free from accidents is itself originated (hadith). Therefore, the body is originated. The author explains the first premise in detail. He states that 'the body cannot be free from akwan' – where kaw in (plural akwan) means the body's occupancy of a specific location. He shows that this occupancy is an attribute additional to the body's essence, and that the body always necessarily has some location. Thus the body is never free from akwan, and since akwan are originated (because they can cease to exist), the body is never free from originated things. Then the author explains the second premise: whatever is never free from originated things must itself be originated, because those originated things either have a beginning or not; the possibility of an infinite regress of accidents is refuted elsewhere (as in the proof against accidents without beginning). Therefore, the body must have a beginning. This proof is a standard kalam argument for the temporality of the physical world.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The proof is circular because it assumes that accidents are originated, which is what it tries to prove about bodies.
Correction: The proof does not assume accidents are originated without reason; it proves their originatedness separately by noting that each accident can cease (e.g., by movement), so it is temporal. The body's dependency on accidents then forces the body itself to be temporal.

Key Takeaway

The Second Proof: Bodies Are Receptive to Accidents
Author presents the second proof and its dilemma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second kalam proof for the creation of bodies, which relies on their universal receptivity to accidents, and the dilemma regarding the nature of that receptivity.

Core Idea
Bodies are never free from accidents, so they must be originated.

After establishing the first proof based on movement and rest, the author now presents a more general proof: bodies are receptive to all accidents. By examining whether this receptivity is identical to the body's essence or additional to it, we arrive at the same conclusion: bodies are created. This passage introduces the syllogism and begins the analysis.

Guided Path
1
Read the syllogism carefully
Identify the three premises and the conclusion: bodies are receptive to accidents; everything receptive to accidents is not free from them; everything not free from accidents is originated; therefore bodies are originated.
This is a standard kalam syllogism.
2
Compare with the first proof
The first proof focused only on movement and rest (al-akwan). This proof covers all accidents like light, darkness, heat, cold, and shapes. Recognize why this is more powerful.
3
Examine the dilemma
The author asks: is the receptivity (qabiliyya) of the body to accidents identical to its essence or additional to it? Note that this dilemma is crucial for the argument's validity.
If identical, proving receptivity is originated directly proves the body's originatedness. If additional, we need two separate proofs.
4
Analyze each branch
Understand what each option implies. For the 'additional' branch, the author states two requirements: prove receptivity is originated, and prove the body is never without it.
The passage begins demonstrating the first requirement but is cut off.
5
Reflect on the incomplete proof
The text ends mid-sentence. Consider how you would complete the proof: why must the receptivity itself be originated? Hint: it depends on the possibility of the accidents it receives.
This encourages independent reasoning.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof (al-hujja al-thaniyya) for the createdness of bodies, which is more general than the first proof that focused on movement and rest (al-akwan). The argument is structured as a syllogism: Bodies are receptive to accidents (qabilah lil-hawadith). Everything receptive to accidents is not free from accidents. Everything not free from accidents is originated. Therefore, bodies are originated. The author then explains the difference from the previous method: the first was specific to certain accidents (movement and rest), while this is general to all accidents like light, darkness, heat, cold, shapes, etc. Then the author examines the nature of this receptivity (qabiliyya). He poses a dilemma: either the receptivity of the body to these attributes is identical to the essence of the body (dhāt al-jism) or it is additional (zā'id) to it. If it is identical, then proving that the receptivity is originated proves directly that the body is originated. If it is additional, then one must prove that the receptivity is originated and also that the body is never free from it, so the body is never separated from accidental things and therefore is originated. The author then begins to demonstrate that this receptivity is originated by analyzing the possibility (imkan) of the essence being qualified by an attribute, but this part is cut off. The key point is that the receptivity itself must be originated because it depends on the possibility of the attribute which is itself contingent.

The Two Possibilities for Receptivity
Sanusi presents two possibilities for the body's receptivity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two possible relationships between a body's receptivity to accidents and its essence, and how this sets up the proof for the body's creation.

Core Idea
The body's receptivity is either its essence or additional — both roads lead to creation.

Sanusi begins his detailed proof with a simple observation: bodies are receptacles for accidents like light, heat, and motion. He then instantly sharpens the question: is this receptivity the very essence of the body, or is it something added to the essence? The entire argument hangs on this fork.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening observation
Notice that Sanusi takes the body's receptivity to accidents as a given. He does not prove it — it is self-evident.
The accidents listed are examples only.
2
Identify the two possibilities
He states two options: receptivity is either identical to the essence (ʿayn al-dhāt) or additional (zāʾid ʿalayhā). Understand the difference.
This dichotomy is exhaustive.
3
Follow the first path
If receptivity is the essence, then proving it is created directly proves the body is created.
No further steps needed in that case.
4
Follow the second path
If receptivity is additional, we need two separate proofs: that the receptivity is created, and that the body cannot exist without it.
This sets up the rest of the argument.

Proof that Receptivity is Created
Sanusi proves that the receptivity to created accidents is created.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the subtle argument that the possibility of being described by created attributes is itself created, based on the priority of self-existence.

Core Idea
A thing's possibility to describe another depends on its own possibility — and created things have no possibility in pre-eternity.

Sanusi now delivers the first of the two promised proofs: that the receptivity to created accidents cannot be eternal. His reasoning is delicate: the possibility of an essence acquiring an accident depends on the accident's own possibility of existence. Since an accident cannot exist in pre-eternity, its possibility to describe another also vanishes in pre-eternity. Therefore, the receptivity is a created thing.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the dependency claim
Sanusi states that the possibility of an essence being described by an attribute is subsidiary (farʿ) to that attribute's own possibility of existence.
This is a logical priority: self before relation.
2
Understand the priority argument
He argues that considering a thing in itself is prior to considering it in relation to another. So the attribute's own possibility is before its ability to describe something else.
This is intuitive: what can exist alone is a precondition.
3
Apply to pre-eternity
A created accident (like a specific heat) cannot exist in pre-eternity (al-azal). Therefore its possibility of existence is absent in pre-eternity, and consequently its possibility to describe another is also absent.
Thus the receptivity to such an accident must be created.
4
Conclusion
So the possibility of the essence being described by created attributes is itself a created matter and impossible in pre-eternity.
This satisfies the first required proof.

Receptivity as a Necessary Concomitant and Final Conclusion
Sanusi proves reception is necessary and concludes the body is created.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the receptivity must be a necessary concomitant of the body's essence and how this leads to the body's creation.

Core Idea
Receptivity cannot be accidental — that would cause infinite regress; it must be a necessary property, making the body inseparable from createdness.

Sanusi now provides the second required proof: that the receptivity is not a separable accident but a necessary concomitant (lāzim) of the body's essence. He argues by contradiction: if it were separable, an infinite regress would follow. Together with the earlier proof that receptivity is created, this forces the conclusion that the body itself is created — either directly or because it cannot escape what is created.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dilemma
Sanusi asks: is the receptivity a necessary part of the body's quiddity, or a separable accident? Two options.
Necessary = part of what the body is.
2
Follow the first option
If necessary, then the goal is reached: receptivity is a necessary concomitant.
No further steps needed.
3
Examine the second option: regress argument
If separable, then since the receptivity itself is possible, the essence would need another receptivity to receive it, leading to infinite regress (tasalsul), which is impossible.
Regress is a standard refutation in kalam.
4
Conclusion of the second proof
Thus receptivity is a necessary concomitant. Now combine with the first proof: receptivity is created. So the body is either identical to a created receptivity (first path) or inseparable from it (second path). In both cases, the body is created.
Sanusi invokes the principle: whatever is inseparable from accidents is itself created.
5
Note the two noble objectives
The proof yields two results: the creation of the body and perhaps another objective (cut off in text).

Commentary
This passage is the detailed explication (taqrīr) of the second proof. The author builds the argument in several steps. First, he notes that bodies are receptive to various accidents (light, darkness, heat, cold, shapes, motion, rest). Then he distinguishes two possibilities: either this receptivity (qābiliyya) is identical to the body's essence, or it is additional to it. If identical, then proving the receptivity is created directly proves the body is created. If additional, then one must prove both that the receptivity is created and that the body cannot exist without it. The proof that receptivity is created is subtle: the possibility (imkān) of the essence being described by an attribute depends on the possibility of that attribute existing in itself. Since a created accident is impossible in pre-eternity (al-azal), its possibility is also absent in pre-eternity. Therefore, the attribute's possibility of describing something else is also absent in pre-eternity, so the possibility of the essence being described by it is a created thing. Next, he proves that this receptivity is a necessary concomitant (lāzim) of the essence, using an argument against infinite regress (tasalsul). If receptivity were accidental, it would itself require an underlying receptivity, leading to regress. Therefore, it must be necessary. Finally, he concludes: if receptivity is the essence, the body is created directly; if not, the body is inseparable from it, and since it is created, the body must be created as well. The passage ends by noting that this proof yields two noble objectives (maṭlūbān sharīfān), though the details are cut off.

Finitude of size points to a free agent, not a necessary cause.

Al-Razi presents the fifth proof for the creation of bodies, building on the finitude of their magnitude. Why Two Necessary Existents Are Impossible
Razi demonstrates that two necessary existents would entail composition and circularity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the existence of two necessary existents is logically impossible according to Razi's first way.

Core Idea
Two necessary existents would each be composite, leading to an absurd dependency.

Razi presents a careful argument by contradiction. He assumes two necessary existents and shows that this leads to an impossible composition. By examining three possible relationships between their common necessity and individual distinctness, he demonstrates that only one Necessary Existent can exist. This lesson walks you through that reasoning.

Guided Path

Understand the assumption: two necessary existents

The second option is false because if temporality (ḥudūth) were an additional attribute, then that added attribute must either be eternal (making the attribute of a temporal thing eternal, a contradiction) or temporal (leading to an infinite regress of attributes). Both alternatives are impossible, so the original claim that a body is eternal is false. The author notes that a similar argument has appeared earlier in the book.

Shared essence plus differing attributes proves a free chooser.

Al-Razi begins by noting the Qur'anic method of inferring God from creation. Since creation implies planning, he sets out to explain the types of planning in heavenly bodies. Tenth Argument: Infinite Variations in Motion
Tenth argument from motion variations in a single sphere.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the infinite gradations of speed within a single rotating sphere point to a wise and precise determiner.

Core Idea
Infinite motion ranks in one sphere require an infinite intelligence to assign them.

The tenth and most detailed argument examines the variation in motions within a single sphere. The author shows that points on a rotating sphere have different speeds depending on their distance from the equator, and since points are infinite, the ranks of motion are infinite. Such precise and infinite specification cannot come from nature alone; it requires the determination of the All-Knowing, All-Powerful.
