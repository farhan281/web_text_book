Philosophers illustrate the answer with natural, compulsive, and voluntary motions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how natural, compulsive, and voluntary motions illustrate the philosophers' principle that each successive part of motion depends on the completion of the prior part.

Core Idea
All motions—natural, forced, or chosen—follow the pattern: each part depends on the prior part's completion.

To demonstrate that their answer applies universally, the philosophers examine three types of motion: natural, compulsive, and voluntary. In each case, the underlying cause remains constant, yet its effect is contingent on the preceding part of the motion. This lesson explores these examples and shows how they support the core principle.

Commentary
This passage presents the philosophers' response to the question of how an eternal cause can produce temporal effects. They explain that the eternal principle (al-mabda' al-fayyad) exists eternally, but the condition for each temporal event (hadith) to emanate from it is the prior passing away of the preceding event. Thus every temporal event is preceded by another, forming an infinite temporal series without a first temporal event. The eternal cause is the cause of all. They then illustrate this with motions: natural motion (e.g., stone falling), compulsive motion (e.g., thrown stone), and voluntary motion (e.g., person walking). In each case, the cause of each successive part of motion remains constant (weight, force, or will), but its effect on a particular spatial point is conditioned upon the arrival at that point via the preceding motion. The cause does not change; only the condition of the prior part's completion changes. This explains how an unchanging cause can produce successive effects without itself undergoing change.

Philosophers' Argument on Causal Conditioning
Philosophers argue for conditioned emanation and illustrate with three motion types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' argument that every temporal event is conditioned by the passing away of the previous event, and how they illustrate this with three types of motion.

Core Idea
An eternal cause can only produce a new effect after the previous effect ceases.

The philosophers present a sophisticated causal theory to explain how an unchanging eternal principle can give rise to a succession of temporal events. They argue that the key is a condition: each new event depends on the termination of its predecessor. To make this plausible, they provide examples from natural, coercive, and voluntary motions. Let's examine their reasoning.

2

The condition in each case is the completion of the preceding part.

Understand the conclusion

For this reason we said: it must be that every temporal event is preceded by another temporal event, without a first. And the cause existing for all, effective in achieving all, is that eternal (al-qadīm). They said: and what establishes that the matter is so is that motions are either natural or coercive or voluntary. And the meaning we mentioned is present in all of these three divisions.

As for natural motions, they are like the falling of a stone from above to below. Its demonstration is that the necessitator of that falling is the heaviness (al-thiqal) present in the substance of the stone, and that heaviness remains in all times unchanging, except that its effect in necessitating each part of the parts of that motion is conditioned by the passing away of the preceding part. That is because if that body had not ended by its preceding motion at that specific limit of the distance, then it would be impossible for the heaviness subsisting in it to be a necessitator of its transfer from that specific limit to another limit. So the necessitator of each part of the parts of that motion is that heaviness. As for the condition of that heaviness being effective in that specific part of the motion, it is its arrival by the preceding part of the motion at that specific part of the distance. As for coercive motions, the matter is also so, and that is because when a person throws a stone upward, he has placed in it a force (quwwa) that necessitates the ascent of that stone upward coercively. So the necessitator of all the parts of that motion is that force, except that the effect of the force in the specific part of that motion is conditioned by its ending by the preceding part of the motion at that specific limit. As for voluntary motions, the matter is also so. Commentary
This passage presents a detailed philosophical argument regarding causal conditioning and the theologians' response. It begins with the philosophers affirming an eternal principle (al-mabda' al-fayyāḍ) that is always existent, but they assert that each temporal event emanating from it is conditioned by the prior temporal event's passing away. This leads to an infinite regress of causes without a first event. They then illustrate this with three types of motion: natural (like a stone falling due to heaviness), coercive (like a stone thrown upward by force), and voluntary (like a person walking to another town due to a motive). In each case, the constant cause (heaviness, force, motive) remains unchanging, but its effect on each subsequent part of motion is conditioned by the completion of the preceding part. Thus, the eternal effecter (al-mu'aththir) is unchanging but its influence on each new event depends on the prior event's termination. The philosophers conclude that this demonstrates the impossibility of any gap in the chain of events and that this rational method is unique to their school, proving its superiority.

The theologians and investigators (al-mutakallimūn wa al-muḥaqqiqūn) respond by acknowledging the philosophers' precision but pointing out a remaining deep issue: the eternal cause's transition from not effecting a specific event (while the prior event existed) to effecting it (after the prior event's passing) is itself a new occurring state (ḥukm ḥādith). This new state requires a cause. If it has no cause, then something comes to be without a cause, which is absurd and would imply that contingent things can exist without an effecter—a contradiction. If it does have a cause, then that cause would be another temporal state, leading to an infinite regress. The text cuts off before fully discussing the second option, but the core objection is clear: the philosophers' account fails to explain the change in the causal relation itself.

Theologians' Objection: The New State Problem
Theologians object that the change in divine causing is a new state needing a cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the theologians' objection that the change in the eternal cause's relation to a new event is itself a new temporal state (ḥukm ḥādith) that requires a cause.

Core Idea
The shift from not-causing to causing is itself a temporal event needing explanation.

The philosophers presented a detailed causal theory, but the theologians point out a deep flaw: the transition from the eternal cause not effecting a specific event to effecting it is itself a new state. This state, being temporal, must have a cause. This leads to a dilemma that threatens the entire causal chain. Let's examine this objection.

Guided Path
1
Read the theologians' acknowledgment and objection
Begin by reading the opening where theologians acknowledge the philosophers' precision but then identify the remaining problem: the change in the causal relation is a new occurring state (ḥukm ḥādith).
2
Understand the new state and its implications
Focus on the definition of the new state: the eternal cause was not causing the specific event while the prior event existed, then it becomes causing after the prior event ends. This 'becoming' is a new state.
3
Examine the two options
The theologians present two possibilities: either this new state has a cause or it does not. Analyze why both lead to absurdity: without a cause, something occurs without an effecter (impossible); with a cause, that cause would be another temporal state, leading to regress.

Commentary
This passage presents a detailed philosophical argument regarding causal conditioning and the theologians' response. It begins with the philosophers affirming an eternal principle (al-mabda' al-fayyāḍ) that is always existent, but they assert that each temporal event emanating from it is conditioned by the prior temporal event's passing away. This leads to an infinite regress of causes without a first event. They then illustrate this with three types of motion: natural (like a stone falling due to heaviness), coercive (like a stone thrown upward by force), and voluntary (like a person walking to another town due to a motive). In each case, the constant cause (heaviness, force, motive) remains unchanging, but its effect on each subsequent part of motion is conditioned by the completion of the preceding part. Thus, the eternal effecter (al-mu'aththir) is unchanging but its influence on each new event depends on the prior event's termination. The philosophers conclude that this demonstrates the impossibility of any gap in the chain of events and that this rational method is unique to their school, proving its superiority.

The theologians and investigators (al-mutakallimūn wa al-muḥaqqiqūn) respond by acknowledging the philosophers' precision but pointing out a remaining deep issue: the eternal cause's transition from not effecting a specific event (while the prior event existed) to effecting it (after the prior event's passing) is itself a new occurring state (ḥukm ḥādith). This new state requires a cause. If it has no cause, then something comes to be without a cause, which is absurd and would imply that contingent things can exist without an effecter—a contradiction. If it does have a cause, then that cause would be another temporal state, leading to an infinite regress. The text cuts off before fully discussing the second option, but the core objection is clear: the philosophers' account fails to explain the change in the causal relation itself.

Commentary
This passage presents a powerful objection raised by the mutakallimūn and muḥaqqiqūn against the Ash'ari proof from the previous lesson. They argue that the very 'causativity' (mu'aththiriyyah) of the eternal agent with respect to any particular event is itself a temporal happening (ḥukm ḥādith) because at one moment the agent is not causing that event, and at the next moment it is. This temporal attribute must itself have a cause. The objection then proceeds to analyze all possibilities for this cause:
1. It may need no cause, which would imply that some things occur without a cause, undermining the principle of causality.
2. It may need a cause, which is then subdivided:
   a. The cause is non-existence (the prior event's non-existence). This is rejected because if non-existence can cause something, causality could be dispensed with entirely.
   b. The cause is an existent. That existent is either prior to the causativity or simultaneous.
      - If prior, then that prior cause is itself a temporal event requiring a cause, leading to an infinite regress of events (which the philosophers actually accept as possible). If such an infinite regress is allowed, it becomes impossible to prove a Necessary Existent.
      - If simultaneous, then that cause is either the same as the effect (circularity) or another existent (infinite regress of simultaneous causes and effects, which is impossible).
The passage concludes that the analysis forces one to accept one of five alternatives (though the five are not fully enumerated here). This objection critically examines the coherence of the Ash'ari model of continuous creation.

The Division of Causes: Non-Existence and Preceding Existence
Sanusi divides possible causes into non-existence and existence, then examines the first two sub-branches.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author divides the possible causes of an event into non-existence and existence, and why the first two options (non-existence cause and preceding cause) fail to establish a Necessary Existent.

Core Idea
A cause cannot be non-existence, and a preceding cause leads to infinite regress.

The author begins by stating that every event must have a cause. He then divides the possible cause into either non-existence or existence. In this lesson, we explore the first two branches: the absurdity of a non-existent cause and the implications of a preceding cause, which the philosophers themselves accept but which undermines their argument for a Creator.

Commentary
This passage is an argument by division (taqsīm) designed to force the opponent into an impossible choice. It opens with the premise that every originated event (ḥādith) must have a cause (ʿilla). The Simultaneous Cause and the Five Impossible Consequences

Learning Objective

A simultaneous cause leads either to circularity or infinite regress, both impossible; thus five unacceptable options remain.

Refuting the First Answer: The First Proof on Creation's Timing

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If the time was present in eternity, the effect must be eternal.

Razi now moves to the second station of his discussion, where he systematically refutes each answer given by his opponents. Here he tackles the first answer, which asserts that God's will is necessarily attached to a particular moment. The Benefit Does Not Necessitate the Act
Author argues that benefit does not necessitate timing of creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a greater benefit in a particular time does not compel God to create at that time, and how the argument preserves divine free agency.

Core Idea
Greater benefit does not bind the Creator.

In this passage, the author takes on a subtle objection: if a certain time offers more benefit, why didn't God create the world at that exact moment? The argument rigorously shows that if benefit compelled creation, God would be a necessitated being, not a free agent. On the other hand, if benefit does not compel, then the actual timing requires an additional reason—otherwise the choice would be arbitrary. This logical move defends divine freedom while refuting the notion that mere benefit can dictate the divine will.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully

The argument is structured as a dilemma.
2

The author asks: does the extra benefit necessitate creation at that time or not? Identify each branch and its consequence.
First branch: necessitarianism. Second branch: need for extra preponderator or arbitrariness.
3
Understand the first branch's implication
If benefit necessitates, then God would be 'wajib bi-dhat' (necessitated by essence) rather than a free agent. Ask yourself why that is problematic for Islamic theology.
This branch is rejected because it undermines divine freedom.
4
Trace the second branch's dilemma
If benefit does not necessitate, then why was the world created at that time? Either an additional preponderator is needed (so benefit alone is insufficient) or the choice is without any preponderator (impossible). See how both outcomes are problematic.
This shows that benefit cannot be the sole reason.

Learning Objective

Yet the occurrence of the form of the whole for that body which is the whole prevents the separation of some of those parts from others, because it is established that rupture in the spheres is impossible. For this reason, it is impossible for the supposed part in the depth of the sphere to occur on its surface, and vice versa.

Commentary
This lesson addresses the sixth objection (النقض السادس): the claim that a hypothetical part located in the thickness (ثخن) of the celestial sphere and a part on its surface share the same essence (ماهية), so they should be interchangeable. The author refutes this by referencing the doctrine of the philosophers (الحكماء). According to them, the celestial sphere is a single continuous body (جسم متصل واحد), not composed of discrete parts. It is one in itself and in sensory perception. However, under certain external causes (أسباب خارجية) such as illusion (الوهم) or varying attractions (اختلاف المجاذبات), parts may be conceptually distinguished (أجزاء بالفعل). But crucially, these parts are only supposed after the whole (بعد حصول الكل) already exists. The form of the whole (صورة الكل) prevents these hypothetical parts from separating—since rupture (الخرق) in the spheres is impossible. Therefore, the part in the depth cannot transfer to the surface, and vice versa. The impossibility of fission in celestial spheres blocks the interchangeability that the objection assumes.

One Nature, Different Conditions
Al-Razi answers the fifth objection about convex and concave surfaces.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Razi defends the unity of nature against the objection that convex and concave surfaces have different attributes.

Core Idea
One nature can produce different effects under different conditions.

Al-Razi faces an objection: if a sphere has a single nature, why do its convex and concave surfaces have many different attributes? He answers by introducing the concept of conditions (shara'it). This passage shows how a single cause can yield different results when conditions vary.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Start by reading the objection presented by the opponents: convex and concave surfaces differ in many attributes despite coming from the same spherical nature.
Notice that the objection assumes that a single nature should produce identical effects.
2
Understand Al-Razi's answer
Read Al-Razi's response: nature is one, but its causation differs according to the difference of conditions. He then gives the example of the sphere's exterior and interior.
The key insight is that conditions (shara'it) modify how a nature operates.
3
Reflect on the role of conditions
Consider how the exterior and interior of a sphere constitute different conditions. Ask yourself: what would happen if the conditions were the same?
This shows that attributing different effects to different conditions is rational and does not contradict the unity of nature.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. Al-Razi presents the fifth objection: the convex and concave surfaces of a sphere have many different attributes, yet they come from the same spherical nature. How can one nature produce two different surfaces? Al-Razi's answer is that the same nature can produce different effects when the conditions (shara'it) differ. The exterior of the sphere, due to its position and relation, necessarily gives rise to a convex surface that is wide, while the interior gives rise to a concave surface that is small. Since the conditions are different, it is not improbable that the effects differ. Thus the objection is answered by appealing to the variation of conditions.
