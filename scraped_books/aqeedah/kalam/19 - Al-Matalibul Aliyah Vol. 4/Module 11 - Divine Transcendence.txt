Argument that neither Quran nor Torah explicitly states creation of essences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the author argues that neither the Quran nor the Torah provides explicit scriptural evidence for the creation of essences from absolute non-existence.

Core Idea
The greatest scriptures lack an explicit statement on creation of essences, indicating the issue's difficulty.

In this passage, the author tackles a fundamental question: do the Quran and Torah explicitly state that God created essences from nothing? He argues that they do not, and that the Torah's account actually describes the arrangement of pre-existing matter. This challenges a common assumption and highlights the depth of the theological debate.

The author is not denying creation, but the lack of explicit scriptural proof for this specific point.

The Fourth Proof: Why Creation's Time Cannot Be for God's Benefit
The author begins a proof against temporal creation by analyzing specification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Muslim theologians argued that specifying a time for creation cannot be for any benefit returning to God, as that would imply God is subject to change or need.

Core Idea
God cannot specify creation's time for His own benefit because He is beyond need and harm.

The author begins the fourth proof against a temporal creation by examining the rationale behind a specific time. He argues that if the time was chosen for a benefit, that benefit cannot be for the Creator, as He is free from all need and harm. This lesson unpacks the first part of that argument.

The author uses 'إما أن يكون ... أو لا'.
3
Understand the first possibility
The first possibility is that the specification is for wisdom and benefit. The author says this is invalid. Ask yourself: why is it invalid?
The text begins to explain: the benefit cannot return to the Creator.

Grasp the impossibility of benefit for God
The author gives the first reason: if benefit returns to God, it would imply He is subject to benefit and harm, which is impossible for the Almighty.

5
Reflect on the incomplete argument

The Second Argument: Need Cannot Be Eternal or Temporal
Razi presents a dilemma against creation due to need.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's second argument that God's creation cannot be motivated by need, because any such need would either entail an eternal world or lead to an arbitrary temporal specification.

Core Idea
If God created out of need, that need must be eternal or temporal—both are impossible.

Razi presents a powerful dilemma against those who claim that God created the universe because He 'needed' something. In this argument, he shows that such a need cannot be eternal (lest creation be eternal) nor temporal (because then its timing is inexplicable). This argument reinforces God's transcendence and self-sufficiency.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument
Read the Arabic passage once and then the literal translation. Let the logical structure sink in before analyzing.
Notice that it's a conditional argument: 'if... then...'
2
Identify the two branches
The passage presents a dilemma: the need is either eternal or temporal. Identify the consequence for each branch.
The first branch leads to an eternal act; the second leads to an infinite regress.
3
Reflect on the impossibility
Understand why both possibilities are problematic: an eternal act contradicts the temporality of the world, and a temporal need requires an explanation that leads to infinite regress.
The argument concludes that the original assumption (creation due to need) is false.

If it is eternal, then the act of creation would also have to be eternal, which is false because the world is temporal. If it is temporal (i.e., the need arose in time), then we can ask: why did that need arise at that specific time and not earlier or later? This leads to an infinite regress or arbitrary specification, which is problematic. Thus, both possibilities are impossible, so the premise that God created out of need is false.

The third argument: God would be deficient
Razi presents the third argument: God would be deficient if needing completion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third argument against the view that God created for a need, which concludes that God would be deficient and need completion.

Core Idea
If God needed creation, He would be deficient—impossible for the Perfect.

Razi's third argument cuts to the heart of divine self-sufficiency. He shows that the supposition of God creating due to a need forces an unacceptable conclusion: God would be deficient and in need of something outside Himself. Since God is perfect and complete, this cannot be. Let's examine this concise proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the one-sentence argument carefully in Arabic and English. Notice how Razi presents it as a direct conditional: 'on this supposition, then God would be...'
The Arabic uses 'يكون' (would be) to indicate a logical consequence.
2
Identify the supposition and its consequence
The supposition is the view that God created the world due to a need. The consequence is that God would be deficient (naaqis) and in need of completion (istikmaal) by another (al-ghayr).
3
Understand the contradiction
Since God is perfect and self-sufficient, the consequence is impossible. Therefore, the original supposition must be false. This is a reductio ad absurdum.
This argument does not say God cannot use secondary causes, but that He cannot be perfected by anything external.

The Fourth Argument: The Greater Cannot Be Perfected by the Lesser
Razi presents the fourth argument: God is most perfect, so cannot be completed by inferior.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth argument for divine transcendence, which states that the more perfect (God) cannot be perfected by the inferior (contingent things).

Core Idea
The perfect cannot be improved by the imperfect.

This lesson presents the fourth argument from Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's al-Matalib al-Aliyah. Razi establishes God's supreme perfection and then draws a logical conclusion: the more perfect cannot be completed by the lower and inferior. This argument reinforces the transcendence of God above all creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice how Razi uses three descriptors for God: qadim, azali, wajib li-dhatihi.
2
Identify the premise
The passage begins by stating God's attributes: eternal, pre-eternal, necessary in Himself. From these, Razi concludes that God is akmal (more perfect) than all contingent things (muhdathat).
3
Understand the conclusion
Razi then declares that it is impossible (muhal) for the more perfect (al-akmal) to be perfected (mustakmilan) by the lower and inferior (al-akhass al-adwan). Ask yourself: why would this be contradictory?
The key insight: if the perfect needs the imperfect to be perfected, it is not truly perfect.

Razi refutes creation as benefit via dilemma of non-existent vs existent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's dilemma argument against the claim that creation benefits the creature.

Core Idea
Creation cannot be a favor to the creature: it would require benefiting a non-existent or an already existent.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi presents a sharp logical dilemma to refute the notion that God created the world for the benefit of others. The argument challenges whether creation (ijad) can be considered a favor (ihsan) to the creature. Razi dismantles both possibilities—benefiting the non-existent or the existent—and then addresses a potential objection about subsequent pleasures.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation to grasp the dilemma structure. Notice the two options and the objection-rebuttal.
2
Identify the two horns of the dilemma
Razi says if creation is a favor, it either benefits the creature when non-existent or when existent. Recognize why each is false.
First: non-existent cannot receive. Second: conveying existence to an existent is impossible.
3
Understand the objection and Razi's rebuttal
An objector says the favor is not creation itself but the subsequent pleasures. Razi replies that this concedes creation itself is not a favor, which is his point.
The discussion is about creation itself being a favor, not what follows.

First Objection: Essence vs Existence Dilemma
Al-Razi presents a dilemma against the essence-existence distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Razi's dilemma against the distinction between essence and existence, and why it poses a challenge to the principle 'from the one only one proceeds'.

Core Idea
A distinct essence leads to absurdity: either self-necessity or dual causation.

Al-Razi presents the first objection against those who claim that the first effect's possibility comes from its essence while its existence comes from its cause. He argues that this distinction forces a dilemma: the essence in itself must be either independent or needy, both of which lead to unacceptable consequences. This lesson walks through the objection step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic and translation to grasp the structure of the objection. Notice how Al-Razi sets up the dilemma.
2
Identify the two horns of the dilemma
Look for the conditional statements: the essence in itself is either independent (ghani) or needy (muhtaj).
This is the key logical structure.
3
Analyze the consequences of each horn
If independent, the essence becomes self-necessary (absurd). If needy, the First Principle becomes cause of both quiddity and existence, violating the principle of oneness.
The absurdity in the first horn and the violation in the second both undermine the opponent's position.
4
Understand why this nullifies the opponent's doctrine
The opponent claimed that from the one only one proceeds, but here two things would proceed from the First Principle: quiddity and existence. Thus the objection destroys the basis of their kalam.

So we say: that essence in itself is either independent of the agent or in need of the agent. If the first, then the essence in itself is necessarily realized by itself, which is absurd. And if it is in need of the agent, and that agent is the First Principle, then the First Principle is a cause of that quiddity and a cause of that existence, so two things have issued from it. This nullifies the basis of your doctrine.
Source pages p. 394

Commentary
This passage is an objection (السؤال الأول). Al-Razi argues that the opponent's distinction between the essence (dhat) of the first effect and its existence leads to a dilemma. The essence, considered in itself, must either be independent of the agent or in need of it. If independent, it would be self-necessary (واجبة التحقق لذاتها), which is absurd because only God is self-necessary. If needy, the First Principle (al-mabda' al-awwal) becomes a cause for both the quiddity (mahiyya) and the existence, resulting in two effects from one cause, violating the principle 'from the one only one proceeds'. Thus the opponent's view collapses.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might assume that essence and existence are identical, but the objection targets the claim that they are distinct.
Correction: The objection does not assume identity; it attacks the distinction by showing its consequences lead to absurdity.

Key Takeaway
Al-Razi's first objection forces the opponent into a dilemma that either implies a second self-necessary being or violates the principle 'from the one only one proceeds'—both of which nullify the basis of the opponent's doctrine.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Al-Razi's dilemma shows that trying to attribute independence to anything besides God leads to absurdity. Where in your life have you subtly assumed self-sufficiency in a matter that only God can sustain? What would it look like to honestly acknowledge your need for Him in that area today?
?
The opponent sought a middle ground: the essence's possibility from itself and existence from God. Are there areas in your faith where you try to combine reliance on God with reliance on yourself, thinking you can have both? How does this passage push you to re-examine that compromise?
?
The dilemma forces a clear choice: either the essence is independent (making it a second god) or completely needy. In your heart, do you live as if something or someone besides God is independent? What step can you take today to actively affirm that only God is self-sufficient?
