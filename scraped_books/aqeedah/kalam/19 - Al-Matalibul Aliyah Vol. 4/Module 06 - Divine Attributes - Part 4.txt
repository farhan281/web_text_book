Future Distinction Does Not Suffice for Eternal Knowledge

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Knowledge must conform to its object; the object's distinction must precede the knowledge of it.

In this passage, the author tackles a subtle objection: if God knows future events as distinct, couldn't that distinction arise in the future rather than from eternity? The refutation hinges on a fundamental principle of knowledge: it must match its object. Let's explore why this principle rules out the objector's proposal.

Refuting Circularity in God's Knowledge of Future Essences
Refutation of a counter-argument about God's knowledge of future distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical circularity that arises when one claims God's knowledge of future distinction is limited to the time of existence, and why prior conception is necessary for any judgment.

Core Idea
Knowledge always requires conception, and conception requires distinction—a circle if distinction only arises at existence.

This passage presents a sophisticated objection to the claim that God knows future essences without eternal distinction. The opponents try to escape by saying God knows them as they will be in the future. The author responds by exposing a logical circularity: to know that something will exist, you must already conceive it, but conception requires distinction, which the opponents say only happens at existence. Thus the objection collapses into an impossible circle.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Opponent's Claim
Read the first part where the opponents propose that God knows future distinction only when it occurs, not eternally. Note their assumption that this removes the problem.
Notice the phrasing: 'He does not know in eternity that blackness is distinct... but He knows that when it exists it becomes distinct.'
2
Identify the Author's Counter
The author immediately rejects this: 'This does not remove the problem.' Track his reasoning step by step.
3
Trace the Circularity Argument
Follow the chain: knowledge of future existence is a judgment (hukm) → judgments require prior conception (tasawwur) → conception of an essence requires its distinction from others → if distinction depends on existence, we get a loop.
Key terms: tasawwur (conception), tasdiq (assent), hukm (judgment), dawr (circularity).
4
Understand the Conclusion
The author concludes that such circularity is impossible (muhal), therefore the objection fails. Reflect on why this refutes the opponent's attempt.
This argument shows that the opponent's escape route is logically incoherent.

This passage is a further objection and answer. The opponents propose that God knows in eternity that blackness will be distinct from whiteness when it exists, not that it is distinct now. Conception of blackness requires that blackness be distinct from other essences. If that distinction only occurs upon existence, then we need distinction to conceive, but distinction depends on existence, leading to circularity (dawr). This refutes the objection.

Commentary
This passage is a multi-step proof (burhān) that the body's receptivity (qābiliyya) to temporal accidents is itself a temporal (ḥādith) attribute, not an eternal (qadīm) property of the body. The author begins by establishing a logical priority: the consideration of a thing in itself (e.g., its possibility of existence) is prior to its relation to something else (e.g., being described by an attribute). Therefore, the possibility of something else being described by an attribute depends on the possibility of that attribute existing in itself. Then he applies this to temporal events (ḥawādith): a temporal event is impossible in pre-eternity (al-azal), so its possibility of existing in itself is absent in pre-eternity; consequently, the possibility of the Divine Essence (al-dhāt) being described by such an attribute is also absent in pre-eternity. This proves that the possibility of the Essence acquiring temporal attributes is itself temporal and impossible in pre-eternity. Next, he turns to the body's receptivity (qābiliyya) to temporal accidents. He argues that this receptivity is either a necessary concomitant (lāzim) of the body's quiddity (māhiyya) or a separable accident (ʿāriḍ mufāriq). If it is a necessary concomitant, then the body is inherently temporal because it cannot exist without temporal accidents, which is the desired conclusion. If it is a separable accident, then an infinite regress (tasalsul) follows because the body would need to be receptive to that accident, and so on, which is impossible. Thus, the receptivity must be a necessary concomitant. Then, crucially, if the receptivity is identical to the body's essence, then the essence is temporal because the receptivity is temporal. If it is distinct, it is a necessary concomitant, implying the body is never free from temporal accidents, which is the definition of a temporal being (mā lā yanfakku ʿan al-ḥādith fa-huwa ḥādith). Therefore, the body is temporal. The passage is logically tight, employing the principle of impossibility of infinite regress and the argument from the inseparability of accidents.

The Receptivity Argument: Necessary Concomitant vs. Separable Accident
Sanusi proves that the body's receptivity to accidents is a necessary concomitant via infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi proves that the body's receptivity to temporal accidents is a necessary concomitant (lāzim) of its essence, using the impossibility of infinite regress.

Core Idea
If receptivity were accidental, an infinite regress would follow, proving it must be a necessary concomitant.

This lesson focuses on the second part of Sanusi's proof: establishing that the body's capacity to receive accidents (qābiliyya) is not a separable addition but a necessary concomitant of its essence. The argument uses a dilemma: either the receptivity is a necessary concomitant or a separable accident. If the latter, an infinite regress results, which is impossible. Therefore, it must be a necessary concomitant. This conclusion is crucial for linking the body's temporalness to its inherent receptivity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dilemma
Read the opening of this passage: the receptivity is either a necessary concomitant (lāzim) of the body's quiddity or a separable accident (ʿāriḍ mufāriq).
This is the classic 'division' (taqsīm) method.
2
Trace the first branch
If it is a necessary concomitant, the required conclusion is reached immediately: receptivity is intrinsic to the body.
3
Analyze the second branch
If it is a separable accident, then the body must be receptive to that accident, leading to another receptivity, and so on infinitely. This infinite regress (tasalsul) is impossible.
Each step repeats the same reasoning, creating a chain without end.
4
Conclude
Therefore, the receptivity cannot be a separable accident; it must be a necessary concomitant of the body's essence. This is then used to show that receptivity itself is temporal (as established earlier).
The passage ends with: 'So it is established that the body's receptivity to temporal attributes is a temporal matter.'

The Conclusion and the Two Noble Objectives
Sanusi concludes the argument and extracts two theological implications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi concludes that the body is temporal and derives two key theological principles: bodies cannot be eternal and God cannot receive temporal accidents.

Core Idea
The body's inseparable receptivity proves its temporalness, and the argument yields two profound truths.

This final lesson completes Sanusi's proof. Having established that the body's receptivity is a necessary concomitant and itself temporal, he now examines whether this receptivity is identical to the body's essence or distinct. In either case, the body's temporalness follows. He then articulates two noble objectives: bodies are not eternal, and God, being eternal, cannot be receptive to temporal accidents. This argument elegantly merges cosmology and theology.

Guided Path
1
Consider the identity of receptivity
Read the opening: if the receptivity is identical to the body's essence, then the essence is temporal because the receptivity is temporal. If it is distinct, it is a necessary concomitant, so the body always has it, meaning it never lacks temporal accidents.
This is a case analysis (taqsīm) similar to earlier.
2
Apply the principle that 'whatever is never free from the temporal is temporal'
Sanusi recalls a previously established principle: anything inseparable from temporal events is itself temporal. Since the body is never free from temporal accidents (due to the necessary concomitant), it must be temporal.
This principle is a standard kalām argument for the createdness of the world.
3
Derive the two noble objectives
First: bodies cannot be eternal because they are receptive to temporal attributes. Second: God, being eternal, cannot be receptive to temporal attributes. This protects divine transcendence.
The proof thus serves both cosmology (createdness of bodies) and theology (divine otherness).

Commentary
This passage is a multi-step proof (burhān) that the body's receptivity (qābiliyya) to temporal accidents is itself a temporal (ḥādith) attribute, not an eternal (qadīm) property of the body. The author begins by establishing a logical priority: the consideration of a thing in itself (e.g., its possibility of existence) is prior to its relation to something else (e.g., being described by an attribute). Therefore, the possibility of something else being described by an attribute depends on the possibility of that attribute existing in itself. Then he applies this to temporal events (ḥawādith): a temporal event is impossible in pre-eternity (al-azal), so its possibility of existing in itself is absent in pre-eternity; consequently, the possibility of the Divine Essence (al-dhāt) being described by such an attribute is also absent in pre-eternity. This proves that the possibility of the Essence acquiring temporal attributes is itself temporal and impossible in pre-eternity. Next, he turns to the body's receptivity (qābiliyya) to temporal accidents. He argues that this receptivity is either a necessary concomitant (lāzim) of the body's quiddity (māhiyya) or a separable accident (ʿāriḍ mufāriq). If it is a necessary concomitant, then the body is inherently temporal because it cannot exist without temporal accidents, which is the desired conclusion. If it is a separable accident, then an infinite regress (tasalsul) follows because the body would need to be receptive to that accident, and so on, which is impossible. Thus, the receptivity must be a necessary concomitant. Then, crucially, if the receptivity is identical to the body's essence, then the essence is temporal because the receptivity is temporal. If it is distinct, it is a necessary concomitant, implying the body is never free from temporal accidents, which is the definition of a temporal being (mā lā yanfakku ʿan al-ḥādith fa-huwa ḥādith). Therefore, the body is temporal. From this argument, two noble objectives (al-maṭlūbān al-sharīfān) are derived: (1) bodies cannot be eternal because they are receptive to temporal accidents; and (2) God, being eternal, cannot be receptive to temporal accidents. The passage is logically tight, employing the principle of impossibility of infinite regress and the argument from the inseparability of accidents.

Commentary
This passage presents the eleventh proof against the eternity of the world. It begins by stating that if a body were eternal (qadīm), it would share the attribute of eternity (qidam) with God. The author first defends the nature of eternity as an established attribute: it is the negation of prior non-existence, and the negation of that negation is affirmation, so the eternal is something real. If there is no difference, then the world would be equal to God, which is impossible. The proof is cut off but clearly concludes that the world cannot be eternal.

Composition Leads to Infinite Regress
Sanusi argues that sharing eternity forces composition and infinite regress

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why sharing the attribute of eternity between God and the world would entail composition and an impossible infinite regress, proving the world cannot be eternal.

Core Idea
If two beings share an attribute, they must differ in another, leading to endless composition.

Having established eternity as a real attribute, the author now builds the main proof. If the world were eternal, it would be equal to God in eternity. This equality must be accompanied by some difference, or they would be identical. That difference creates composition, and each component would itself need further composition, leading to an infinite chain of composites—an impossibility. This rigorous argument eliminates the possibility of an eternal world.

The passage is dense; read it slowly.

Understand why infinite regress is impossible
If both components are eternal, they themselves would require further composition ad infinitum, which is absurd.
The other two cases are also impossible because the temporal cannot be part of the eternal.
4
Note the incomplete conclusion
The passage ends mid-sentence, but the intended conclusion is clear: the eternity of the world is impossible.
The argument is complete in essence.

The Negative Nature of Qidam (Eternity)
Sanusi defines qidam as negative and proves via infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why qidam (eternity) is a negative attribute and how the argument from infinite regress establishes it.

Core Idea
Eternity is not a positive quality but the negation of being preceded by non-existence.

This passage presents a crisp logical argument from Imam al-Sanusi that qidam (eternity) is not a positive attribute but a negation. By analyzing what it means to 'not be preceded by non-existence,' the author shows that any attempt to turn this into a positive property leads to an infinite regress. Understanding this distinction is foundational to Islamic theology's treatment of divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the literal translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the argument uses a conditional statement.
2
Follow the regress argument
The author hypothesizes that if 'non-precedence' were a positive attribute, it would itself need to be preceded by non-existence, leading to an infinite chain. Identify this step and see why it is impossible.
Infinite regress (tasalsul) is considered a logical impossibility in kalam.
3
Understand the conclusion
Since the alternative leads to absurdity, the non-precedence must be a negative thing. The negation of non-existence is existence, so the concept of qidam is established as a real negative notion.
This shows that negative attributes can be real without being positive.
