The Four Logical Cases of Change and Refutation of the First
Author enumerates four possible states in change and argues the first is impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four rational divisions of change and why the possibility of both states being non-existent is impossible.

Core Idea
Non-existence has no reality — so change cannot occur between two non-existences.

When we observe change, like motion after rest, reason divides the possibilities into four categories. The first — both old and new states are non-existent — may seem possible, but careful analysis shows it is impossible. This lesson examines the four cases and the argument against the first.

Commentary
This passage classifies the possible logical cases when a change occurs, such as when a body moves after being at rest. The author identifies four possibilities: (1) both the accidental (new) state and the perishing (old) state are non-existent; (2) the new state is non-existence and the old is existence; (3) the opposite of (2); (4) both are existing and mutually opposed. He then refutes the first possibility absolutely. The reason is that non-existence has no reality (حقيقة), no individual identity (تشخص), and no persistence (ثبوت) whatsoever. Therefore, one non-existence cannot change into another, because there is nothing real to undergo change. This establishes that at least one of the states must be an existing attribute.

Proof that Impossibility and Possibility are Non-Existent Attributes
Sanusi proves that imtina' and imkan are not existent attributes but are non-existent (adamiyya).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why impossibility (imtina') and possibility (imkan) are non-existent (adami) attributes according to Sanusi, and the rational proof that supports this claim.

Core Idea
Impossibility and possibility are not real things; they are mental judgments.

Sanusi now addresses a subtle but critical point: Are the rational modalities (impossibility, possibility, necessity) real attributes that exist in the world? At first glance, it might seem that 'impossibility' is a quality of impossible things. But Sanusi argues that such an understanding leads to absurdities. In this passage, he proves that both impossibility and possibility are mere non-existent (adami) attributes—they have no positive existence. The argument relies on clear rational principles, including the law of non-contradiction and the rejection of infinite regress.

Guided Path

The author uses a reduction ad absurdum approach.
2
Identify the thesis

3
Follow the proof for impossibility
Walk through the conditional argument: if imtina' were existent, then its subject would have to be existent, leading to a contradiction.
4
Follow the proof for possibility
Examine the three-part division (necessary, impossible, possible) and see how each is eliminated.
Notice the infinite regress argument in the third case.
5
Conclude the lesson
Reflect on the conclusion: both modalities are non-existent attributes that can change.
This is a foundational principle in Sanusi's ontology.

Then it is established by proof: that impossibility (al-imtina') cannot rationally be an existent attribute, and likewise possibility (al-imkan) cannot rationally be an existent attribute. As for impossibility, the proof for it is: that if it were an existent attribute, then the subject qualified by that attribute would be more deserving of being an existent thing, due to the testimony of the clear intellect that an existent attribute cannot subsist in pure negation and sheer non-existence. Thus it would follow that it (the subject) is necessary of existence by itself (mumtani' al-wujud li-dhatihi, wajib al-wujud li-dhatihi) and that is impossible. As for possibility: because if it were existent, it would be either necessary by itself, or impossible by itself, or possible by itself. The first is false because possibility is an attribute of the possible, and the attribute of a thing is dependent on the subject, and that which is dependent on another is possible by itself. The second is false because everything that is existent cannot be impossible of existence. The third is false, otherwise the possibility of possibility (imkan al-imkan) would be different from it, and an infinite regress would follow, which is impossible. Thus it is established: that impossibility changes into possibility, while each of them is a non-existent attribute (sifa 'adamiyya).

Commentary
This passage presents a proof that impossibility (imtina') and possibility (imkan) are not real, existent attributes but rather non-existent (adami) concepts. The structure is a proof by reductio. The author first states the thesis: both are not rationally conceivable as existent attributes. Then he provides separate proofs for each. For impossibility: if it were existent, its subject would have to be existent (since an existent attribute cannot inhere in pure nothingness), leading to a contradiction (the subject would be both impossible and necessary). For possibility: if it were existent, it would have to be one of three categories: necessary, impossible, or possible by itself. The first two are ruled out by definition, and the third leads to an infinite regress (the possibility of possibility), which is impossible. The conclusion is that both are non-existent attributes that can change (e.g., impossibility becomes possibility). The argument is a standard kalam demonstration of the conceptual nature of modalities.

The Fourth Argument: Locus and Accident Distinction
Sanusi refutes the claim that a substance's being a locus for an accident is identical to the accident itself.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the inherence of an accident in a substance must be distinct from the accident itself, and how this leads to an infinite regress argument.

Core Idea
An accident's inherence is not the accident itself; otherwise infinite regress follows.

In this passage, Sanusi presents a fourth argument against the idea that a substance becoming a locus for an accident is just the accident itself. He shows that if we treat 'being a locus' as an additional accident, it leads to an infinite chain. This lesson unpacks the logic of this argument and its refutation of a potential objection.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the flow from claim to counter to refutation.
2
Identify the main claim and the counter
The passage begins with the claim that if being a locus is an additional accident, it leads to regress. Then anticipates a counter: that 'being a locus' is identical to the accident.
3
Analyze the refutation
The refutation has two parts: first, we can conceive the accident while doubting its inherence, proving they are distinct; second, the occurrence is a distinct relation (nisba) between accident and locus.
The key point is the logical distinction between the accident and its inherence.
4
Consider the concept of relation
The idea that the occurrence is a relation (nisba) distinct from both relata reinforces the distinction and shows how the objection returns.
The relation is a third entity, not reducible to the accident.

Fourth: This is that when motion (sukoon) comes to be in a locus (al-mahall), after it was not present in it, we say: This essence became a locus for this motion, after it was not a locus for it. Commentary
This passage presents a fourth argument and refutation, using the example of motion (sukoon) in a locus. The opponent argues that if a substance becomes a locus for an accident after not being so, then the 'being a locus' (mahalliyya) would be an additional accident, and then that accident would itself need a locus, leading to regress. The author anticipates a counter: that 'being a locus' is nothing other than the accident itself. The author refutes by stating that we can conceive of the accident independently of its occurrence in that locus, proving they are distinct. Furthermore, the occurrence is a specific relation (nisba) between the accident and the locus, which is distinct from both. Thus the original objection (that this leads to regress or additional accidental attributes) remains valid. The argument is a standard critique of the realist interpretation of inherence relations.

Four Arguments Distinguishing Types of Non-Existence
Razi enumerates four arguments showing that types of non-existence are distinct.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Fakhr al-Din al-Razi uses four distinct arguments to demonstrate that types of non-existence ('adam) are not all alike, but have different logical, modal, and temporal properties.

Core Idea
Non-existence is not a single uniform state — it varies by logical, modal, and temporal features.

Fakhr al-Din al-Razi presents four concise arguments to establish a subtle point: non-existence is not just 'nothingness' but takes on distinct forms depending on what is absent and the context. These arguments rely on logical relations (necessary concomitant vs. non-concomitant), opposites, modality (necessary vs. contingent), and temporality (pre-eternity vs. post-eternity). By the end of this lesson, you will see that 'adam is more nuanced than it first appears.

First: Non-existence of the necessary concomitant indicates non-existence of the thing necessitated, but non-existence of what is not a necessary concomitant is not like that. Second: Non-existence of a contrary in a subject renders possible the occurrence of the other contrary, but other non-existences are not like that. Third: Non-existence of that which is necessary in itself is impossible in itself, but non-existence of that which is contingent in itself is not impossible in itself. Thus one of the two non-existences is distinguished from the other. Fourth: Non-existence of the world is necessary in pre-eternity, but its non-existence is not necessary in post-eternity. Thus one of the two non-existences is distinguished from the other. And when the occurrence of this distinction and this difference is established, it is not rationally improbable that one of the two non-existences changes into the other.

Commentary
This passage presents four arguments to demonstrate that types of non-existence ('adam) are not all alike but have distinctions. The author first argues that the non-existence of a necessary concomitant implies the non-existence of the thing necessitated, whereas the non-existence of something that is not a necessary concomitant does not have that implication. Second, the non-existence of a contrary in a subject allows the opposite contrary to arise, whereas other non-existences do not have this property. Third, the non-existence of a necessary being (wajib li-dhatihi) is inherently impossible, whereas the non-existence of a contingent being (ja'iz li-dhatihi) is not inherently impossible—thus distinguishing the two types of non-existence. Fourth, the non-existence of the world is necessary in pre-eternity (azal) but not necessary in post-eternity (la yazal), showing a temporal distinction. Having established these distinctions, the conclusion drawn is that it is not rationally remote for one non-existence to change into another—for example, the non-existence that is necessary becoming non-existent later, or vice versa. This refutes the notion that all non-existences are identical, which would make change inexplicable.

Change Necessitates an Existent Attribute: Three Possibilities
Razi asserts self-evident necessity of existent attribute in change and lists three possibilities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that change requires at least one existent attribute and that three logical possibilities for change are all plausible without further proof.

Core Idea
Change always involves an existent attribute; three ways are equally possible.

Fakhr al-Din al-Razi here addresses questions about the nature of change. He asserts that we know by self-evident necessity that when change occurs, one of the two changing things must be an existent entity. He then presents three logically sound possibilities for how change can happen, warning that insisting on one without proof is ignorance. The passage concludes by rejecting the arbitrary claim that defining rest as non-existence of motion is no more valid than the reverse.

The Eternal Cannot Cease: A Proof from Causality
The author proves that whatever is eternal cannot cease to exist.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical proof that an eternal being cannot cease to exist, based on causal and conditional reasoning.

Core Idea
If something is eternal, its non-existence is impossible.

This passage presents a rigorous argument that whatever is truly eternal cannot later cease to exist. The author considers two scenarios: one where the eternal is a cause of another eternal, and another where its effect depends on a condition. By analyzing each case, the proof establishes that eternity entails necessary permanence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Let the author's own reasoning come through before analyzing.
Notice the two cases introduced with 'كان الأول' and 'وأما الثاني'.
2
Analyze the first case
The first case: if the cause is eternal and never ceases, its effect—also eternal—cannot cease. Non-existence of the effect would imply change in the cause.
This is a direct cause-effect scenario.
3
Analyze the second case
The second case considers when the cause's influence depends on a condition. Two sub-cases: if the condition is possible, the first case's reasoning applies; if it is necessary (wājib li-dhātihī), then both cause and condition are necessary, necessitating the effect's permanence.
The argument covers both possibilities thoroughly.

From both cases, the author concludes that whatever is eternal cannot cease. Then applies this to rest (sukūn), proving rest cannot be eternal because rest is subject to cessation.
This refutes the idea of eternal rest.

An eternal attribute that ceases is a contradiction in terms.

The author first restates the overall thesis: if determination is additional, then things are created. Then he focuses on proving that determination is indeed additional for things equal in essence. The first proof argues that since things equal in essence differ in their determinations, the aspect of participation (what makes them share the essence) must differ from the aspect of difference (what makes them distinct), so determinations are additional. The second proof analyzes the concepts of 'human' and 'this human': the latter includes the former but adds the feature of preventing commonality (shirka), proving that determination is an added element. The third proof invokes reason's intuitive judgment that 'this human' is a composite of 'this' and 'human', showing that 'this' (determination) is distinct from the essence. The fourth proof uses a reductio: if the concept of 'this human' were identical to the concept of 'human', then it would apply to another human, making them identical, which is absurd. Therefore, determination is additional.

The Fourth Proof: Reductio ad Absurdum
Fourth proof: reductio leading to identity of distinct individuals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth proof that determination is additional, through a reductio showing that otherwise distinct individuals would be identical.

Core Idea
If determination were not additional, two humans would be the same individual, which is absurd.

The fourth proof uses a reductio ad absurdum. The author argues: if the concept of 'this human' were identical to the concept of 'human', then it would apply to any human, making all humans identical. Since that is impossible, 'this human' must contain an additional element (determination) that distinguishes individuals.

Guided Path
1
Read the fourth proof and conclusion
Read the passage and translation. This proof is a bit longer and includes the final conclusion of all proofs.
2
Understand the reductio
The proof assumes the opposite: that determination is not additional, i.e., the concept of 'this human' equals 'human'. Then it shows a contradiction: because the concept of 'human' applies to another human, 'this human' would apply to that other human, making them identical.
This is a classic reductio.
3
Appreciate the conclusion
The impossibility shows that the assumption is false, so determination must be additional. Then the author concludes that all four proofs establish the thesis.

Two Proofs Against Deniers of Rational Good and Evil
Two proofs refuting denial of rational good and evil by showing it leads to arbitrary timing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two logical proofs that demonstrate how denying rational good and evil forces an absurd conclusion about God's specification of time.

Core Idea
Denying God's consideration of benefit leads to denying any reason for His timing of creation.

This passage presents two compelling arguments against those who deny that human reason can discern good and evil in God's actions. The author shows that such a denial forces one to accept that God specified the time of creation with absolutely no reason, which is incoherent. The first proof uses the concept of motive, the second uses a dilemma about the cause of time specification.

Commentary
This passage presents two proofs against those who deny rational good and evil (al-tahsin wa al-taqbih) in God's actions. The author begins by stating that such deniers must refrain from saying God specified the time of creation for the benefit of moral agents, because that would imply God considers benefit. Then two proofs are given. The first proof: every action depends on a motive. If God's actions are not based on benefit, then all harms and evils are by His decree, so He cannot be said to act for the sake of benefits. The second proof: the specification of a particular time for creation must have a cause. It cannot be due to the time's essence, because then every event would be attributed to that time, undermining the proof for God's existence. Nor can it be due to a concomitant of the time, because that leads to an infinite regress of specifications. Therefore, the deniers must say God specified the time with absolutely no specifying factor. The conclusion is that denial of rational good and evil forces one to accept that God's actions are arbitrary.
