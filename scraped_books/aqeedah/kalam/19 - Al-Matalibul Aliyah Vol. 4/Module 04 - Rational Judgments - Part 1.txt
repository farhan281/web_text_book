Sensible Qualities and the Five Senses
Al-Razi divides the first type of quality into sensible qualities and akwan, then enumerates sensible qualities by the five senses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Razi classifies sensible qualities according to the five senses and which specific accidents belong to each sense.

Core Idea
Sensible qualities are divided by the five senses: each sense perceives distinct accidental properties.

Al-Razi continues his classification of accidents by turning to those directly perceived by the senses. He first distinguishes sensible qualities from the states of being (akwān), then systematically lists what each of the five senses perceives. This passage provides a clear, traditional enumeration that will help you map the physical world to the categories of kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic passage carefully, then the literal translation. Notice the structure: a two-part division, then a five-part enumeration.
The Arabic uses the word 'أما' to mark shifts.
2
Identify the two main categories
The passage first divides the first type of quality into 'sensible qualities' and 'states of being (akwān).' Understand this split before moving on.
3
List the five senses and their objects
Work through each sense: sight (colors/lights), hearing (letters/sounds), taste (nine tastes), smell (odors), touch (heat, cold, moisture, dryness, and their combinations).
Touch has four primary qualities plus what results from their combination.
4
Reflect on the enumeration
Ask yourself: Why does Al-Razi consider 'letters' as objects of hearing? How does this classification align with everyday experience?
This shows how kalam systematizes empirical observation.
5
Connect to broader lesson
Understand that this classification is not exhaustive but a standard kalām framework for categorizing accidents.

Understanding 'Kawn': The States of Being and Their Types
Al-Razi defines 'kawn' and begins enumerating its four types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of 'kawn' as the occurrence of a substance in a place, and you will recognize the first three types: motion, rest, and conjunction.

Core Idea
The states of being are the fundamental ways a substance occupies space.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi defines one of the key concepts in the study of non-living qualities: the 'states of being' (akwān). He gives a precise definition of 'kawn' as the occurrence of a substance in a place, then begins listing its four types. Although the page cuts off after three, the three we have give us a clear picture of motion, rest, and conjunction.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of 'kawn'
Read the opening sentence where al-Razi defines 'kawn' as the occurrence of a substance in a place. Notice he says the explanation of 'ḥayyiz' (place) will come later.
This definition is the foundation for understanding the types.
2
Identify the three listed types
The passage lists motion (first occurrence in a place), rest (second occurrence in the same place), and conjunction (two substances in two places with no void between them). Read each definition carefully.
The fourth type is missing; in other sources it is often 'separation' (iftirāq).
3
Reflect on why these types matter
Ask yourself: how do these categories help us understand physical change and coexistence? They are essential for analyzing movement, stillness, and spatial relationships of substances.
This connects to larger discussions in Islamic physics and kalam.

Classification of Beliefs in Islamic Epistemology
The author classifies beliefs as part of attributes of perception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the detailed classification of beliefs (i'tiqadat) as presented by the author, including the distinctions between conceptualization and assent, certain and non-certain beliefs, and the various subtypes such as necessary, theoretical, traditional, compound ignorance, doubt, opinion, and illusion.

Core Idea
Beliefs are systematically classified by certainty and conformity to reality.

This passage presents a comprehensive classification of beliefs, which are a key component of perceptual attributes. The author begins by dividing beliefs into conceptualizations and assents, then carefully subdivides assents based on their certainty and conformity to reality. This structured approach helps clarify the epistemological categories used in Islamic theology and philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the hierarchical structure: from perception to beliefs, then to conceptualizations/assents, then further subdivisions.
2
Identify the main branches
Trace the first division: beliefs are either conceptualizations (tasawwurat) or assents (tasdiqat). Then assents are either certain or non-certain. List all subcategories you find.
3
Understand each subcategory
For each type (e.g., necessary, theoretical, traditional), note its definition and examples if provided. Pay special attention to 'primary' (awwaliyya) and 'compound ignorance' (jahl murakkab).
The author gives a concise definition for each term.
4
Reflect on the overall classification
Consider why such a classification is important in kalam. How does it help in analyzing claims about God and creation?
This connects to the broader field of epistemology in Islamic thought.

وأما الصفات التي تفيد الإدراك فقسمان : الأول : الإعتقادات وهي إما تصورات أو تصديقات . والتصديقات : إما أن تكون جازمة أو غير جازمة . أما الجازمة فإما مطابقة أو غير مطابقة ، والمطابقة إما أن تكون علوماً ضرورية أو نظرية أو تقليدية . أما الضروريات فإما أن تكون مستفادة من إحدى الحواس الخمس ، وإما أن تكون أولية بمعنى أن مجرد تصور موضوعها ومحمولها يوجب التصديق بإسناد محمولها إلى موضوعها أو بسلبه عنه . وأما النظريات فمعلومة ، وأما التقليديات فهو أن يعتقد الإنسان إعتقاداً جازماً في الشيء من غير دليل ولا شبهة . وأما الإعتقاد الجازم الذي لا يكون مطابقاً فهو الجهل المركب ، وأما الاعتقاد الذي لا يكون جازماً فإن كان التردد في الطرفين على السوية فهو الشك ، وإن كان أحدهما أقوى من الآخر فالراجح هو الظن والمرجوح هو الوهم
As for the attributes that convey perception, they are two kinds. First: beliefs (i'tiqadat), and they are either conceptualizations (tasawwurat) or assents (tasdiqat). Assents are either certain (jazima) or non-certain. As for the certain, they are either conforming or non-conforming. The conforming are either necessary knowledge (daruriyya), theoretical (nazariyya), or traditional (taqlidiyya). As for necessary knowledge, it is either derived from one of the five senses, or primary (awwaliyya) in the sense that mere conceptualization of subject and predicate necessitates assent to attributing the predicate to the subject or negating it from it. As for the theoretical, it is known. As for the traditional, it is that a person holds a certain belief about a thing without proof or doubt. As for the certain belief that is not conforming, it is compound ignorance (jahl murakkab). As for belief that is not certain, if there is equal hesitation between the two sides, it is doubt (shakk). If one side is stronger than the other, the stronger is opinion (zann) and the weaker is illusion (wahm).

Commentary
This passage is a detailed classification of beliefs (i'tiqadat) which are part of the attributes of perception. The author divides beliefs into two main categories: tasawwurat (conceptualizations) and tasdiqat (assents). Then he subdivides assents based on certainty and conformity. Certain conforming assents are further divided into necessary (daruri), theoretical (nazari), and traditional (taqlidi). Necessary knowledge is either sensory or primary (awwali) — where mere understanding of subject and predicate yields judgment. Theoretical knowledge is self-evident in the passage (m 'aluma), and traditional knowledge is belief without evidence. Non-conforming certain belief is compound ignorance. Non-certain belief is either doubt (equal hesitation) or opinion (stronger side) and illusion (weaker side).

The Priority of Time Parts: A Sixth Category
Mulla Sadra proves that priority among time parts is a sixth category, independent of time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Mulla Sadra's proof that temporal priority among time parts leads to an infinite regress and contradiction, thereby establishing a sixth category of priority distinct from the five familiar ones.

Core Idea
Time parts cannot be temporally prior to each other — that leads to absurd infinity.

Mulla Sadra tackles a subtle problem: if some parts of time are prior to others, what kind of priority is it? He systematically eliminates the familiar five categories (causality, essence, honor, place, time) and shows that temporal priority among time parts leads to an infinite regress and contradiction. This forces us to recognize a sixth, irreducible category of priority. This argument is crucial for understanding the nature of time and the relationship between existence and non-existence.

Why Priority Does Not Require Time
Razi proves that priority, simultaneity, and posteriority are not time-dependent.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Priority does not require time; God's transcendence demands it.

Razi begins with a rational axiom: any two things the intellect considers must be related as prior, simultaneous, or posterior. He applies this to God and the world, then shows that if these relations required time, God would depend on something else, making Him contingent—an impossibility. Guided Path
1
Understand the rational axiom
The passage starts with an axiom: for any two things the intellect points to, they must be in one of three relations: prior, simultaneous, or posterior. This is a basic rational judgment that cannot be denied.

Dilemma against temporal beginning of possibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the notion that possibility can arise at a specific time leads to absurd consequences and undermines the proof for God's existence.

Core Idea
Possibility cannot begin in time without contradiction.

In this passage, the author presents a powerful dilemma against the idea that the world's possibility had a starting point. By examining the two possible ways this change could occur, he shows that both lead to absurdity. The argument not only refutes the opponent's position but also reinforces the logical necessity of a Creator.

The Impossibility of Change in God's Power
A change in God's power from impossibility to possibility requires an invalid cause or leads to infinite regress.

A change in God's power cannot be explained without absurdity.

Refutation via Circularity (Dawr)
First refutation: circularity (dawr) in explaining will by knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the circularity (dawr) argument against explaining God's will by His knowledge.

Core Idea
Knowledge follows the known; explaining will by knowledge creates a vicious circle.

In this passage, the author presents the first of two refutations against the claim that divine will can be explained by God's knowledge. The argument hinges on a foundational principle: knowledge follows the known, not the other way around. If the specification of the world's creation is caused by will, and knowledge merely reflects that specification, then explaining will by knowledge leads to circularity. Guided Path

Understand the principle: knowledge follows the known

If the world's origination is specific to a time, God's knowledge relates to that specification. But the specification is due to God's will.

If we explain will by knowledge, then will (specification) depends on knowledge, but knowledge depends on the specification. This is a vicious circle (al-dawr).

Both arguments aim to show that the opponent's position is incoherent.

Refutation via Necessitarianism
Second refutation: necessitarianism (mūjib bi al-dhāt) and eternity of the world.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the necessitarianism argument: if will is explained by knowledge, God becomes a necessary agent, which is false and implies the eternity of the world.

Core Idea
If divine will is determined by knowledge, God is no longer a free agent but a necessary agent.

The author presents a second refutation based on a conditional dilemma: given specific knowledge of when the world should originate, either God's will could have chosen a different time or it could not. The first option negates knowledge as the cause, the second forces God into being a necessary agent (mujib bi al-dhat), which contradicts His free agency and leads to the eternity of the world. This lesson examines that argument.

Guided Path
1
Set up the conditional
Assume God has specific knowledge of the world's origination time. Then consider whether His will is capable of specifying a different time.
This is a disjunction: either the will is flexible or it is not.
2
First horn: will is flexible
If the will can specify other times, then the specific knowledge is not the cause of the actual specification.
3
Second horn: will is not flexible
If the will cannot specify other times, then God is a necessary agent (mūjib bi al-dhāt), not a free agent (fāʿil bi al-ikhtiyār).
4
Conclusion and consequence
The opponent rejects necessary agency, so the second horn is false. But if it were true, it would imply the world is eternal (qidam al-ʿālam).
The argument shows the opponent's position leads to unacceptable consequences.

Commentary
This passage presents two independent refutations of an unspecified answer that attempts to explain God's will by His knowledge. The first refutation (al-wajh al-thānī) argues that explaining the will by knowledge is false because knowledge follows the known—it does not precede or cause it. If the world's temporal origination is specific to a particular time, God's knowledge would correspond to that specification only if the origination were indeed specific. But the opponent claimed that the specification occurs because of God's will, not because of knowledge. If we then try to explain the will by knowledge, we would need the specification for knowledge to depend on, but the specification itself depends on will, thus a vicious circle (dawr) arises. The second refutation (al-wajh al-thālith) offers a conditional argument: given that God has specific knowledge of when the world should originate, either His will is also capable of specifying other times, or it is not. If the will is capable of other times, then the specific knowledge is not the cause of the actual specification. If the will is not capable of other times, then God is not a free agent but a necessary agent (mūjib bi al-dhāt), which is false, and if true would imply the world is eternal. Both arguments aim to show that the opponent's position is incoherent.

The Fifth Argument: Impossibility Cannot Become Possibility
Refuting the idea that something impossible in itself can become possible in itself.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Islamic theologians argue that something impossible in itself cannot become possible in itself, and how this preserves the rational basis for proving God's existence.

Core Idea
Essential impossibility is permanent; changing it would destroy all modal distinctions.

The objection that an impossible thing might later become possible challenges the foundation of arguments for God's existence. This lesson presents the refutation by examining the nature of possibility and impossibility, showing that such a change is rationally untenable.

Guided Path

Read the opening sentences: the objector proposes that something impossible in itself could turn possible. Note that the author declares this weak and then provides proofs.
2
Analyze the First Proof
The first proof concerns the world's possibility as a condition for God being its originator. If that possibility were not eternal, the conditions for God's action would be incomplete, which is false.
This proof is brief but important for connecting possibility to divine action.

The second proof has three sub-arguments. The first deals with whether possibility is a necessary concomitant or an accident of essence. The second considers the essence alone regarding tanzuh. The third shows the slippery slope of allowing modal conversion.

Commentary
This passage refutes the objection that something could be impossible in itself at one time and then become possible in itself. This argument guards against the idea that essential modalities can change over time.

Why Essential Impossibility Cannot Become Possibility
Provides three proofs that essential impossibility cannot become possible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three rational proofs demonstrating that essential impossibility cannot transform into possibility, and why this principle is foundational for arguments for God's existence.

Core Idea
Essential impossibility is immutable; it cannot become possibility.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi presents three rigorous proofs that what is impossible in essence (mumtani' li-dhatih) can never become possible. The first proof examines the logical relationship between possibility and essence, the second considers the essence in itself, and the third shows the disastrous consequences of denying this principle. Together, they establish a cornerstone of kalam cosmology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its English translation to get an overview of the three proofs.
Notice the logical structure: the thesis is stated first, then three proofs are listed.
2
Examine the first proof: essential relation
Focus on the first proof: either possibility is a necessary concomitant of the essence or an accident. Follow the two branches and the infinite regress argument.
This proof uses the concepts of lazam (necessary concomitant) and arad (accident).
3
Examine the second proof: essence considered alone
Consider the second proof: examine the essence in itself. Does it inherently repel existence or not? This leads to eternal impossibility or eternal possibility.
This proof emphasizes that the essence's property is not time-dependent.
4
Examine the third proof: consequences
Study the third proof: if impossibility could become possibility, then possibility could become necessity, which would undermine the argument from contingency for the existence of a necessary being.
This proof shows the broader theological implications.

The Second: That which is impossible in essence (mumtani' li-dhatih) cannot change into possible in essence (mumkin li-dhatih). And proofs indicate it: First: The being of the possible as possible is either a necessary concomitant (lazim) of that essence or an accident ('arad). If the first, then it follows that the possible is always possible. On this assumption, it is impossible to say that the impossible in essence changes into possible in essence. If the second, then possibility becomes an accident of that essence. That essence is then receptive to that possibility, if it is from among the concomitants. Then the goal is achieved. And if it is from among the accidents, the discussion returns as in the first, resulting in infinite regress. Second: This essence, considered purely in itself, apart from everything else, either possesses an imperviousness to receiving existents or does not possess this imperviousness. If the first, then it must be eternally impossible of existence. If the second, then it must be eternally possible of existence. As for the claim that it is possible in essence at some times and impossible in essence at others, this is contrary to clear reason. Third: If we allow that the impossible in essence changes into possible in essence, why should we not conceive that the possible in essence changes into necessary (wajib) in essence, and the impossible in essence changes into necessary in essence? Then it would not be improbable to say that the originated thing (muhdath) before its existence was impossible in essence, and upon entering into existence changed into necessary in essence. Then you could not use the origination of originated things and their possibilities to infer the Necessary of Existence in essence (wajib al-wujud li-dhatih).

Commentary
This passage presents three proofs that what is impossible in essence cannot become possible in essence. The argument is structured as an enumeration of three proofs (وجوه). The first proof examines whether possibility is a necessary concomitant (lazim) or an accident ('arad) of the essence. If it is a concomitant, the possible is always possible; if it is an accident, an infinite regress results. The second proof considers the essence purely in itself: either it inherently repels existence or not. If it repels, it is eternally impossible; if not, it is eternally possible. The third proof shows that if the impossible could become possible, then the possible could become necessary, undermining the standard argument from contingency for the existence of a necessary being. The author follows each proof to its logical conclusion, emphasizing that any temporal variation in essential impossibility is irrational.

Pre-eternity of the universe leads to an absurd change from impossible to possible.

This lesson examines a nuanced argument about the pre-eternity of the sphere of fixed stars. The Necessity of a Preponderator for Preference
The author refutes the claim that a capable agent can prefer without a preponderator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the claim that a capable agent can prefer one action over another without a preponderator is rationally unsound, based on analysis of capability and intuitive knowledge.

Core Idea
Capability alone cannot produce preference; intention and inclination are necessary.

This lesson examines a key objection to causal necessity: the idea that a capable agent can choose without any preponderating factor. The author dismantles this claim by showing that capability is equally related to both alternatives, and that our own self-awareness confirms the need for intention and inclination.

Guided Path

Read the passage

Understand the claim and its refutation

3
Analyze the two cases

4
Reflect on the intuitive argument
Consider the author's appeal to self-evident knowledge: we know from ourselves that we need intention and inclination to prefer one action over another.
5
Conclude the refutation
See how the answer collapses regardless of which case we take. In the first case it leads to absurdity; in the second, it requires a preponderator.

Analyzing 'Yurajjih' and the Two Cups Example
The author analyzes whether 'preponderates' adds a meaning to 'capable' and uses the two cups example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical analysis of the term 'yurajjih' and why the two-cup example actually supports the need for a preponderator.

Core Idea
'Yurajjih' must add meaning to capability; otherwise, acts occur by coincidence.

In this passage, the author delves into the precise meaning of 'yurajjih' (preponderates). He examines two possibilities and demonstrates that either leads to a contradiction or an absurdity. Then he brilliantly turns the opponents' own example against them, showing that genuine preference requires specification. Let's follow the argument step by step.

Guided Path

Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage once and then its translation. Try to grasp the overall flow before diving into analysis.
Notice the dense logical structure; take your time.

Identify the two possibilities for 'yurajjih'
The author sets up a dichotomy: either 'yurajjih' adds a meaning to 'capable' or it does not. Find where he states these.

Commentary
This passage is a deeper logical analysis of the key term 'yurajjih' (he preponderates). The author breaks the argument into two possibilities. First, if 'yurajjih' has an additional meaning beyond being capable (qadir), then saying the preponderance occurs 'without a preponderator' (la li-murajjih) contradicts that addition, combining affirmation and negation. Second, if 'yurajjih' has no additional meaning, then the agent's capability and will are equally related to both alternatives, yet the act occurs without any specification. This is repudiated by reason and leads to the absurd conclusion that the act happens by pure coincidence without any agent causality.
