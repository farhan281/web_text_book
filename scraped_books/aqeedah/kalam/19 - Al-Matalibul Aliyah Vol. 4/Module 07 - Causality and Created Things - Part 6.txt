The Continuous Need for a Cause
Argument that contingent being always needs a cause, with refutation of priority objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why contingent beings need a cause not only at their origination but also throughout their existence, and how the objection based on 'priority' is refuted.

Core Idea
Permanence does not remove contingency; the need for a cause is continuous.

This passage presents a key argument in Islamic theology about causality. The author argues that contingent beings require a cause not just when they first come into being, but for as long as they continue to exist. He then addresses an objection that might claim the thing gains a 'priority' for existence that makes it independent. Let us follow his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the nature of possibility
The passage begins with a discussion of whether possibility (imkan) is a necessary concomitant of the essence or an accidental attribute. This distinction is crucial for the argument.
Necessary concomitant means it is intrinsic and always present.
2
Grasp the core argument
Sanusi then states that it is self-evident that every possible being needs a cause, and since the contingent remains possible, it continues to need a cause.
3
Examine the objection about priority
The opponent claims that during continuance, the thing has a 'priority' (awlawiyya) for existence that obviates the need for a cause.
4
Follow the refutation
Sanusi shows that this priority either comes from the essence (which would have made it independent from the start) or from outside (which is just another name for the cause). The objection collapses into a verbal dispute.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
The conclusion is that the need for a cause is continuous, not just at origination. This has implications for the argument for God's existence as the sustaining cause.

Commentary
This passage presents the first argument for the claim that contingent beings (mumkin) need a cause (mu'aththir) not only at the moment of origination (huduth) but also during their continuance (baqa'). The argument begins with a discussion of possibility (imkan): either possibility is a necessary concomitant of the essence or it is an accidental attribute. If it is necessary, then possibility is always present; if accidental, it leads to an infinite regress. Therefore, possibility must be a necessary concomitant. Then it asserts that it is self-evident (badihi) that every possible needs a cause. From this, it concludes that every possible, while it continues to exist, is possible and therefore needs the cause. Thus, permanence (baqa') does not remove the need. Then it addresses an objection: perhaps during continuance, the thing has a priority (awlawiyya) for existence that makes it independent of the cause. The refutation considers two cases: if the priority comes from the essence, it should have been present at origination, making the thing independent from the start, which is false; if it comes from elsewhere, then it arose after origination, meaning the thing continues to exist because of this priority, which is itself a cause. Thus, the objector is admitting the need for a cause but merely renaming it 'priority'. The argument ends by stating that this is a mere verbal dispute, not a substantive one.

The Second Argument: Huduth as a Candidate Cause
Definition of huduth and initial claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of origination (huduth) and the initial claim that it cannot be the cause of the need for a cause.

Core Idea
Origination is not the cause of need; it is a property posterior to existence.

In this lesson, we examine the second proof from the author. He begins by defining what he means by 'origination' (huduth) and then asserts that it is impossible for origination to be the cause of the need for an effecter. We will explore the reasoning behind this claim, focusing on the nature of properties and their temporal priority.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note key terms
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Identify the key terms: al-hujja al-thaniya, huduth, illat al-haja, mu'aththir, mashuqiyya, sifa, muta'akhkhir bi al-rutba.
These terms are central to the argument.
2
Grasp the definition of huduth
Huduth is defined as 'existence preceded by non-existence'. This being-preceded (mashuqiyya) is a property (sifa) of existence.
Properties are ontologically posterior to their subjects.
3
Trace the regress of posteriority
The passage lists a chain: huduth is posterior to existence, which is posterior to the agent's effect, which is posterior to the need, etc. This sets up the argument that if huduth were the cause, it would be posterior to the effect, leading to absurdity.
Notice the cumulative list of things that huduth is posterior to.

الحجة الثانية : إنه يمتنع أن تكون علة الحاجة إلى المؤثر : هي الحدوث . وذلك لأن الحدوث عبارة عن كون وجوده مسبوقاً بالعدم . وهذه المسبوقية صفة لذلك الوجود ، وصفة الشيء متأخرة عنه بالرتبة ، فحدوثه متأخر عن وجوده ، المتأخر عن تأثير الفاعل فيه، المتأخر عن احتياجه إلى الفاعل ، المتأخر عن علة احتياجه إلى الفاعل، وعن جزء تلك العلة، وعن شرط تلك العلة . فهذا يدل على أن الحدوث ليس علة للحاجة ، ولا جزءاً من تلك العلة ، ولا شرطاً لتلك العلة . وإلا لزم تأخر الشيء عن نفسه بمراتب ، وذلك محال . وإذا ثبت أن الحدوث غير معتبر في حصول الحاجة أصلاً ، ثبت أنه سواء حصل الحدوث أو لم يحصل ، فإنه لا عبرة به في تحقق الحاجة إلى المؤثر .
The second proof: It is impossible for the cause of the need for the effecter to be origination. This is because origination is the fact that its existence is preceded by non-existence. This being-preceded is a property of that existence, and the property of a thing is posterior to it in rank. So its origination is posterior to its existence, which is posterior to the effect of the agent upon it, which is posterior to its need for the agent, which is posterior to the cause of its need for the agent, and to a part of that cause, and to a condition of that cause. This indicates that origination is not the cause of the need, nor a part of that cause, nor a condition of that cause. Otherwise, it would entail the thing being posterior to itself by multiple degrees, which is impossible. And if it is established that origination has no bearing at all on the occurrence of the need, it is established that whether origination occurs or not, it is irrelevant to the realization of the need for the effecter.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof (al-hujja al-thaniya) that the need for a cause is not due to origination (huduth). Huduth is defined as existence preceded by non-existence. This being-preceded (mashuqiyya) is a property (sifa) of existence. Since the property of something is ontologically posterior (muta'akhkhir bi al-rutba) to the thing itself, huduth is posterior to existence. Existence is posterior to the agent's effect (ta'thir al-fa'il), which in turn is posterior to the thing's need for the agent, and so on. Thus, if huduth were the cause of the need, it would be posterior to the need itself, leading to an absurdity where a thing is posterior to itself by multiple degrees. Therefore, huduth cannot be the cause, nor a part, nor a condition of the need. Consequently, huduth is irrelevant to the need for the cause; the need exists regardless of whether the thing is originated or not.

The Regress and Its Implication
Conclusion and absurdity of the regress

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the regress argument proves that origination cannot be the cause, a part, or a condition of the need for a cause.

Core Idea
If huduth were the cause, it would lead to a thing being posterior to itself.

In this continuation, we see the conclusion of the second proof. The author draws out the implications of the regress: that origination cannot be the cause, part, or condition of the need. Otherwise, an absurdity would follow. Finally, he concludes that origination is irrelevant to the need.

Guided Path
1
Read the conclusion passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. This passage is the conclusion of the second argument.
Notice the emphatic language: 'fa-hādhā yadullu ʿalā anna...'
2
Understand the absurdity of self-posteriority
The author argues that if huduth were the cause, it would entail the thing being posterior to itself (ta'akhkhur al-shay' ʿan nafsihi) by multiple degrees, which is impossible.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
3
Grasp the final implication
Since huduth has no bearing on the need, it is irrelevant whether the thing is originated or not; the need for a cause remains.
This reinforces the independence of the need from temporal origination.

The Need is for Existence as Possible
Analysis of three aspects to isolate the cause of need

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third proof that the need for an effecter arises from the possibility of existence, not from its non-existence or origination.

Core Idea
Need for a cause arises from possibility, not from origination.

In this passage, Sanusi presents a precise analysis of an originated thing to identify what truly requires a cause. By examining three aspects—prior non-existence, subsequent existence, and the fact that existence is preceded by non-existence—he eliminates two common misconceptions and establishes that the real needy entity is existence itself, but only insofar as it is possible. This argument shifts the focus from temporal origination to ontological contingency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three aspects
Read the first part of the passage and note the three matters of the originated thing: prior non-existence, subsequent existence, and being preceded by non-existence.
These are the three aspects to be analyzed.
2
Understand why non-existence is not needy
Non-existence is pure negation; it cannot be attributed to an effecter. Therefore, it cannot be the thing that needs a cause.
This eliminates the first candidate.
3
Understand why precedence is not the cause of need
The fact that existence is preceded by non-existence is a necessary concomitant of this existence; it is not a separate thing that could be in need. If the existence were necessary in itself, it would not need a cause.
This eliminates the second candidate.
4
See that existence itself is needy
Since the first two are invalid, existence itself must be the entity in need of the effecter.
But this needs further refinement.
5
Refine to existence as possible
Existence cannot need a cause simply for being existence, because that would require even the Necessary Being to have a cause. So the need must be due to existence being possible. This possibility is the true cause of need, and it persists whether the thing is originated or eternal.
This is the conclusion of the proof.

That which is in need of the effecter is not the prior non-existence, because non-existence is pure negation and cannot be attributed to the effecter; nor is it the fact that this existence is preceded by non-existence, because the being-preceded of this existence by non-existence is a necessary concomitant of this existence, for this existence cannot come to be and be established unless it is preceded by non-existence. But if it were necessary of existence in itself, its being in need of the effecter would be impossible. Commentary
This passage presents the third proof (al-hujja al-thalitha). It begins by analyzing the originated thing (al-hadith) into three aspects: (1) the prior non-existence (al-'adam al-sabiq), (2) the subsequent existence (al-wujud al-lahiq), and (3) the fact that this existence is preceded by non-existence (kawn hadha al-wujud masbuqan bi dhalika al-'adam). It then argues that the needy entity (al-muhtaj) is not the prior non-existence because non-existence is pure negation (nafy mahd) and cannot be attributed to the effecter. Nor is it the fact of precedence, because this precedence is a necessary concomitant (lazim) of this existence—it cannot be separated from it. However, if the existence were necessary in itself (wajib al-thubut li dhatihi), it would not be in need of a cause. Therefore, the needy entity must be existence itself. Then it considers whether existence needs the cause because it is existence simply (wujud faqat) or because it is possible existence (wujud mumkin). The first is false because it would entail that every existent needs a cause, including the Necessary Being, which is absurd. So the need is due to the existence being possible (mumkin). Thus, the cause of need is possibility (imkan), which is present whenever the being is possible, regardless of whether it is originated or existing eternally. Therefore, the need for the cause persists as long as the possibility remains.

Core Idea

This proof tackles a key objection to the eternity of the world. Some argue that if the world is eternal, it would be an 'eternal effect' – a concept some deem impossible.
