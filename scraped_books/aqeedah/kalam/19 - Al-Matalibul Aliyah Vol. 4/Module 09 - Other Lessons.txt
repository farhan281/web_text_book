The Shapes of the Elements and the Alternative View
Defines fire, air, water by triangle shapes; mentions alternative view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the geometric theory of elemental shapes attributed to some philosophers and the alternative view that qualities are superadded accidents distinct from shapes.

Core Idea
Elements are defined by triangular geometry, but the more apparent view is that qualities are separate accidents.

This passage presents a unique ancient theory that the four elements (fire, air, water) are distinguished by the number of triangular faces in their geometric form. It then contrasts this with the more widely accepted position that the elemental qualities (heat, cold, wetness, dryness) are accidents separate from these shapes. The author notes that this latter view is the more apparent one.

The First Opinion: Body Composed of Hyle and Form
Presents the first sub-opinion that body is composed of hyle and form.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first sub-opinion that body is composed of hyle and form, including its definition and the two groups who hold it.

Core Idea
The substrate (hyle) has no magnitude; bodiliness is an accident inhering in it.

In this passage, al-Matalib turns to the first of the opinions that claim primary matter is not body. It introduces the concept of hyle as a substrate without magnitude, in which bodiliness inheres to produce a spatial body. This view is then split into two camps: those who say hyle is a self-subsistent existent and those who say it is non-existent. Understanding these distinctions is foundational for later discussions on the nature of physical substances.

Guided Path
1

Identify the two groups

Group 1: al-Farabi, Ibn Sina, Plato, Aristotle. Group 2: those who say the non-existent is a thing.
3
Understand the key terms
Focus on the terms 'hyle', 'bodiliness', and 'magnitude'. The passage emphasizes that hyle itself has no magnitude or spatial occupation.
This corrects a common misunderstanding that hyle is a physical thing.

Presents the view that bodies originate from the four primary qualities.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Bodies are generated from heat, cold, moisture, and dryness, not from a substrate.

This lesson examines an alternative theory to the hyle-based account of body generation. Some philosophers and theologians argued that bodies arise directly from the four primary qualities rather than from a substrate. We'll read the passage that outlines this view and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully

Identify the key claim and its elements
The passage states that bodies are generated from qualities (heat, cold, moisture, dryness). It also explains the process: qualities are eternal, then corporeality occurs in them, producing simple bodies from individual qualities and composite bodies from mixtures.

The Pythagorean View: Units as the Primary Matter
Argument for units as primary matter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Pythagorean argument that unity is the foundation of all existence, making units the primary matter.

Core Idea
Everything depends on unity, but unity depends on nothing.

The Pythagoreans held that the ultimate substance of the world is not a physical stuff but a mathematical unit. In this passage, the author presents their reasoning: every existent requires unity, while unity itself is self-sufficient. This argument establishes the metaphysical primacy of the unit.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation to get the overall argument.
2
Identify the key premise
Notice the claim that everything existing has determination and therefore unity. Also, numbered things (ten, twenty) have a unity.
3
Understand the dependency relation
The passage argues that all essences depend on unity, while unity is independent of them. The independent precedes the dependent.
4
Grasp the conclusion
Therefore, the primary matter of all existents is the units (monads). Reflect on this metaphysical claim.

Source Text

القول الرابع للقائلين بالهيولى : إن هيولى هذا العالم ، هو الوحدات . وهذا القول منسوب إلى فيثاغورس ، وطائفته . وتقريره : أن شيئاً من الماهيات الموجودة في هذا العالم لا ينفك عن الوحدة، فإن كان كل ما كان موجوداً فله تعين ، ولسبب ذلك التعين وحدة. وأيضاً : المعدودات لها وحدة ما أيضاً ، لأن العشرة عشرة واحدة ، والعشرون عشروناً واحدة ، وكذلك القول في سائر الأشياء الواحدة والمعدودة . فثبت : أن كل موجود فإنه يمتنع دخوله في الوجود ، إلا إذا حصلت له الواحدة . وأما الواحدة فإنها غنية عن جميع الماهيات ، فإنه لا ماهية يشير العقل إليها ، إلا والوحده حاصلة متقررة مع عدمها . فثبت بهذا : أن ما سوى الوحدة ، فإنه مفتقر إلى الوحدة ، وثبت أن الوحدة غنية عما سواها ، والغني متقدم بالرتبة والوجود على المحتاج. فثبت : أن المادة الأولى لجميع الموجودات هي الوحدات

The fourth opinion of those who affirm hyle: The hyle of this world is the units. This opinion is attributed to Pythagoras and his sect. Its exposition: No essence among the essences existing in this world is free from unity. For if everything that exists has a determination, and because of that determination it has unity. Also, numbered things have some unity as well, because ten is one ten, twenty is one twenty, and similarly for all other singular and numbered things. So it is established that every existent cannot enter existence unless unity obtains for it. As for unity, it is independent of all essences, for there is no essence to which the intellect points except that unity is present and established along with its absence (?). So it is proved by this that everything other than unity is in need of unity, and it is proved that unity is independent of everything else, and the independent precedes in rank and existence that which is in need. So it is established that the primary matter of all existents is the units.

If two points combine, a line results; if two lines combine, a surface results; if two surfaces combine, a body results. Then if the body is surrounded by four triangular bases, it is fire; if surrounded by eight triangular bases, it is air; if surrounded by twenty triangular bases, it is water; if surrounded by six square bases, it is a cube, which is earth; if surrounded by twelve pentagonal bases, it is the celestial sphere. Then if the elements are mixed with each other, the three generated things originate. So it is established by what we have mentioned that the primary matter...

Commentary
This passage presents the Pythagorean view that the primary matter (hyle) of the world is units (monads). The argument proceeds as follows: (1) Everything that exists has a determination and therefore unity; even numbers (ten, twenty) have a unity. (2) Hence every existent requires unity to exist, while unity itself is independent of all essences. (3) Therefore unity precedes everything in rank and existence, establishing that the primary matter of all existents is the units. Then a geometric derivation: an abstract unity becomes a point when indicated; two points make a line; two lines a surface; two surfaces a body. Bodies with specific polygonal bases correspond to the four elements (fire, air, water, earth) and the celestial sphere. Mixing elements produces the three generated things (minerals, plants, animals). The passage concludes that the primary matter is units, though the text cuts off.

From Unity to the Cosmos: The Pythagorean Geometric Emanation
Geometric derivation from unity to elements and cosmos

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Pythagoreans derived points, lines, surfaces, bodies, elements, and the entire world from the unit.

Core Idea
Unity becomes point, point becomes line, line becomes surface, surface becomes body — and bodies become the elements and cosmos.

Having established that units are the primary matter, the Pythagorean system then shows how the physical world unfolds geometrically. An abstract unity, when indicated, becomes a point; combinations of points produce lines, surfaces, and bodies. These bodies, when given specific polyhedral shapes, become the four elements and the celestial sphere. This lesson traces that geometric cascade.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on geometric progression
Read the second part of the Arabic passage carefully. Note the sequence from unity to point to line to surface to body.
2
Identify the polyhedral shapes for elements
Notice that each element corresponds to a body with a specific number of polygonal bases: fire (4 triangles), air (8 triangles), water (20 triangles), earth (6 squares), celestial sphere (12 pentagons).
3
Understand the generation of the world
The elements mix to produce the three kingdoms: minerals, plants, and animals.
The text says 'the three generated things'.
4
Reflect on the overall scheme
This geometric emanation shows how the unity unfolds into multiplicity to produce the entire cosmos, each step adding complexity.

Commentary
This passage presents the Pythagorean view that the primary matter (hyle) of the world is units (monads). The argument proceeds as follows: (1) Everything that exists has a determination and therefore unity; even numbers (ten, twenty) have a unity. (2) Hence every existent requires unity to exist, while unity itself is independent of all essences. (3) Therefore unity precedes everything in rank and existence, establishing that the primary matter of all existents is the units. Then a geometric derivation: an abstract unity becomes a point when indicated; two points make a line; two lines a surface; two surfaces a body. Bodies with specific polygonal bases correspond to the four elements (fire, air, water, earth) and the celestial sphere. Mixing elements produces the three generated things (minerals, plants, animals). The passage concludes that the primary matter is units, though the text cuts off.

Completing the Classification: Galen’s Suspension Defended
The author completes the classification of views on the world's origin and comments on Galen's suspension.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the final three divisions of the classification of views on the world's origin: the completion of the third view, the dismissal of the fourth as self-evidently false, and the fifth view of suspension attributed to Galen, along with the author's defense of Galen's intellectual honesty.

Core Idea
Admitting uncertainty can be a sign of intellectual honesty, not ignorance.

In this passage, the author wraps up his discussion of those who hold the world eternal in matter but contingent in form. He then dismisses the notion that the world is eternal in attributes but contingent in essences as plainly false. Finally, he turns to the view of suspension—refusing to decide—attributed to Galen, and offers a striking defense: such uncertainty in the face of a profoundly difficult question is a mark of fairness, not failure.

Guided Path
1
Read the complete passage
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice the three distinct sections: conclusion of the third view, dismissal of the fourth, and discussion of the fifth.
2
Identify the three divisions
The passage explicitly mentions 'the third division', 'the fourth division', and 'the fifth division'. Mark them in the text. Note how each is treated: the third is completed, the fourth is dismissed outright, the fifth is discussed with a story.
The fourth view is called 'known to be false necessarily'—a self-evident claim.
3
Analyze the dismissal of the fourth view
The author says the fourth view is 'known to be false necessarily' without providing a proof. Ask yourself: why might he think this needs no argument? Consider the implications of eternal attributes with contingent essences.
This likely contradicts the necessary attributes of God being inseparable from His essence.
4
Reflect on Galen's suspension
Read the story of Galen's deathbed statement. Notice what he admits not knowing: whether the world is contingent or eternal, and whether the soul is the temperament or something else. Then see how the author defends Galen against critics who see this as ignorance.
The author calls this the strongest evidence that Galen was an impartial seeker of truth.
5
Consider the author's concluding remark
The author ends by noting the extreme difficulty of these questions, implying that suspension is reasonable. Ask yourself: what does this say about the role of certainty in theology?

The Fifth Question: Inform about the nature of this love? He answered, Muhammad ibn Zakariya, saying: 'This love is known to exist necessarily, for all animals are naturally disposed to love life and to love these bodily pleasures. If they attain these bodily goods, that is for them the ultimate happiness, and if they remain deprived of them, that is the ultimate misery. Then perhaps he becomes occupied with another type of these bodily pleasures, and that is like when a drunkard opens his eye for a moment and sees some things and hears some of them; then immediately he takes another cup and returns to his previous unconsciousness and his first heedlessness. This is the explanation of the soul's love for the body, and there is no doubt that whoever reflects on it knows that the matter is so.'

Learning Objective

Bodily pleasures intoxicate the soul like wine, causing it to forget reason and true happiness.

The Cycle of Soul Attachment Until the Great Resurrection
The author answers a question about the duration of the corrupted composition and the soul's transmigration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the process by which souls attached to bodies undergo a cycle of reincarnation until they are purified and the great resurrection occurs.

Core Idea
Souls that love the body are reborn in cycles until reason reveals the pain behind bodily pleasures.

The author addresses a critical question: if the soul dies, how long does its attachment to a corrupt body persist? His answer describes a cycle of reattachment and purification that culminates in the great resurrection. This lesson unpacks that process step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow, then read the literal translation. Notice the sequence of events from death to final resurrection.
2
Identify the stages of the soul's journey
Look for the stages: death with love remaining, reattachment to new bodies, strengthening of reason, realization of pains, aversion, and final separation.
There are six distinct stages in the answer.
3
Understand the role of the light of reason
The light of reason (nūr al-ʿaql) is key to purification. It allows the soul to see bodily attachments as pains, leading to aversion and eventual release from the cycle.
4
Recognize the final dissolution and resurrection
The process repeats for all souls until all are purified. Then the composition of bodies dissolves, and the great resurrection (al-qiyāma al-kubrā) occurs.
This is the ultimate end of the cycle.

The Sixth Question: If it dies, how long does this corrupt, false composition remain? He answered: These souls that are in love with this matter, if they die with this love remaining, they return to attachment to another body, and they keep repeating from body to body, until they attach to a particular body, and it happens for that [soul] in that body that the light of reason becomes strong, and it becomes clear to it that all bodily attachments are pains in the form of pleasures, and wounds in the guise of comforts. Then its nature turns away from them, and it has no inclination to them. Then this soul departs with aversion to these corporeal things and with desire for those spiritual things. Then that soul does not return after that to attachment to the body. Then this repetition continues, cycle after cycle, until this truth is revealed to all souls attached to bodies, so all souls become separated from all bodies, the composition dissolves, the great resurrection occurs, and the matter returns.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. The sixth question asks: If the soul dies, how long does this corrupt composition (the attachment to the body) persist? The answer describes the process: souls that love the body (al-nufūs al-ʿāshiqa) reattach to new bodies after death, continuing in a cycle. This cycle continues until a soul attaches to a body in which the light of reason (nūr al-ʿaql) strengthens and it realizes that all bodily attachments are actually pains disguised as pleasures. Then the soul becomes averse to the body and departs with a desire for spiritual things. That soul no longer returns to a body. The cycles repeat for all souls until this realization occurs for every soul attached to a body. Ultimately, all souls separate from all bodies, the composition dissolves, and the great resurrection (al-qiyāma al-kubrā) occurs. The answer thus portrays a gradual purification process leading to the final dissolution and resurrection.
