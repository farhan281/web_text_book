Refuting the Necessity of Divine Will for Creation
The author rejects attributing will to Allah and refutes the conditional argument for the world's temporality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the author argues that attributing will to Allah is rationally invalid and how he refutes the conditional argument that the world's origination requires intention.

Core Idea
Divine will cannot be rationally attributed; the world's origination does not require an act of intention.

This lesson presents a two-part argument. First, the author concludes from prior reasoning that the term 'will' carries no meaningful content that can be attributed to Allah. Second, he considers an objection: if the world is originated, it must be by intention. He refutes this by showing the impossibility of a preponderance without a preponderant, thereby defending the original position that the world is not originated.

1
Read the passage and translation

Understand the conclusion about will

Analyze the conditional argument
Identify the conditional: 'If the world is originated, then it must be by intention.' Then follow the explanation that shows this leads to an impossibility (preponderance without a preponderant). Understand how this refutes the conditional.
The key concept is 'preponderant' (al-murajjih) – what tips the scales.

Commentary
This passage presents two moves. First, the author concludes from previous reasoning that the concept of will (irada) cannot be meaningfully attributed to Allah, so any claim of such an attribute is false. Second, the author considers an objection: opponents might argue that since the world is originated, it must be due to an agent's intention and will. The author refutes this by showing the conditional's consequent is impossible. The explanation: the world was non-existent for eternity, and the agent existed, but in eternity no effect occurred. If the world came to be at a specific time without intention, that would be a preference for that time without a preponderant, which is rationally impossible. Thus the argument against the world's origination holds.

Proof that Intention Cannot Be Eternal or Originated
Sanusi presents a proof that intention cannot be eternal or originated.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why intention (qasd) cannot be eternal or originated, and why the existence of intention in the specific act of creation is rationally impossible.

Core Idea
Intention cannot exist because it would be either eternal or originated, both absurd.

We now examine a proof that intention cannot be the cause of creation at a specific time. The author argues that any such intention would have to be either eternal or temporal, but both entail absurdities. This lesson presents that argument and sets the stage for distinguishing eternal will from temporal intention.

Understand the two possibilities

3
See why both are false
Consider why an eternal intention is impossible (God's attributes are eternal, but intention is a relationship to a temporal act) and why an originated intention is impossible (God's will is eternal, so a temporal intention would imply change).
The passage does not elaborate, but the commentary clarifies.
4
Conclude with the author
The conclusion is that intention does not exist as a separate entity; rather, eternal will suffices.

Commentary
This passage begins by stating that it is impossible for the world's origination at that specific time to be due to intention and choice. Distinction Between Eternal Will and Temporal Intention
The author distinguishes eternal will from temporal intention using a human analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between God's eternal will and temporal intention, and why eternal will alone is not sufficient to produce an act at a specific time.

Core Idea
Eternal will is not the same as temporal intention; prior resolve requires knowledge to become intention.

Having shown that intention cannot be eternal or originated, the author now clarifies that God's eternal will is not an intention to act at that moment. Through a human analogy, he demonstrates that prior resolve and knowledge of time together generate a new intention. This lesson explores that distinction and its implications for understanding divine action.

Guided Path
1
Read the distinction passage

2
Understand the example

This illustrates that prior resolve is not intention.
3
Analyze the logical argument
If prior resolve were intention, then the man would intend even without knowledge. Since he does not, resolve and intention are distinct.
The author uses a reductio ad absurdum.
4
Grasp the correct view
The correct view: when resolve persists and knowledge of time arrives, a new intention is generated. Thus prior resolve alone is insufficient; a new intention must occur.

Distinct Essences of Resolve and Intention
Sanusi argues from contradictory consequences to distinct essences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why prior resolve (azm) and immediate intention (qasd) are considered different in essence based on their contradictory consequences.

Core Idea
Resolve and intention differ in essence because their consequences are mutually exclusive.

Sanusi now presents a second proof that prior resolve (azm) and immediate intention (qasd) are not merely different in degree but in essence. He argues by comparing their necessary implications in time. The passage shows that each has a distinct mahiyya (essence) by the fact that what they necessitate are incompatible.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation

The passage ends mid-sentence, but the core argument is complete.

The Fifth Proof: Pre-Eternal Motive Necessitates Pre-Eternal Creation
Fifth proof: a pre-eternal motive would necessitate the world's pre-eternity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fifth proof from motive (dā'iyah) that argues for the world's pre-eternity by showing that any originated motive leads to regress, and any pre-eternal motive necessitates creation from eternity.

Core Idea
A pre-eternal motive leaves no room for a temporal beginning.

This lesson presents the fifth proof from the Matalib al-Aliyah, which uses the concept of divine motive to argue that the world must be eternal. The proof systematically examines whether the Creator's motive for creation is eternal or originated, and shows that only an eternal motive without obstacles is coherent, leading to the necessity of creation from pre-eternity. Follow the logical branches carefully.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the main claim
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. The proof starts by assuming the world is originated and then deduces a contradiction. Notice the initial conditional: 'If the world were originated, then the Creator must have a motive.'
The proof is a reductio ad absurdum.
2
Trace the first branch: motive originated
Follow the case where the motive is originated. The argument says this leads to an infinite regress because the same question applies to that motive. Understand why this is problematic.
Infinite regress is rejected in kalam.
3
Trace the second branch: motive eternal and conditioned
If the motive is eternal but conditioned, examine whether the condition is eternal or originated. If the condition is originated, circularity (dawr) arises. If the condition is eternal, move to the obstacle analysis.
Circularity is impossible in causal chains.
4
Examine the obstacle argument
When the motive is eternal and unconditioned, consider whether an obstacle existed in pre-eternity. If an obstacle existed, it must be either necessary (impossible to remove) or possible (requiring an effecter, leading to an infinite regress that ends in a necessary necessitator, which also cannot be removed). Therefore, no obstacle could have existed.
This eliminates all possible hindrances.
5
Conclude: pre-eternal motive without obstacles necessitates creation from eternity
Since the motive was present from eternity and no obstacles existed, the act of creation was necessary from pre-eternity. This contradicts the assumption that the world is originated, so the world must be eternal.
This proof is a cornerstone of the argument for pre-eternity.

Commentary
This passage presents a kalam proof by contradiction against the view that the world is temporally originated. Hence, the eternal motive was present from pre-eternity with no obstacles, making the act (creation) necessary from eternity. This contradicts the premise that the world is temporally originated. Thus, the assumption of origination is false; the world must be eternal.

The second proof from the arguments based on knowledge: We say: Either the truth is that He, exalted is He, is not knowledgeable of particulars, or the truth is that He, exalted is He, is knowledgeable of particulars. On both suppositions, He must be a necessitating cause in essence. Then from His perpetuity follows the perpetuity of the known things. As for if the truth is the first division, i.e., that He is not knowledgeable of particulars, then on this supposition, it is impossible for Him to intend to bring the world into existence. For this intention can only occur if He knows that it is non-existent and then He wills to make it exist. So on the supposition that He is not knowledgeable of particulars, this knowledge is impossible, so the mentioned intention is impossible, so it is impossible for Him to intend its origination, creation, and formation. We only say that His being knowledgeable of particulars entails His being a necessitating cause in essence because since He is knowledgeable of particulars, He knows that a certain thing occurs at a certain time in a certain mode, and that a certain thing never occurs. And what God knows to occur is necessary of occurrence, because its non-occurrence would necessitate that God's knowledge turns into ignorance, which is impossible. And what necessitates the impossible is impossible. So its non-occurrence is impossible, thus its occurrence is necessary. And by this same proof also: what God knows not to occur is also impossible of occurrence. And a thing that is necessary of occurrence or impossible of occurrence cannot be that the powerful [agent] is able to do it or leave it. That impugns His being powerful, and necessitates His being a necessitating cause in essence. And if it is established that His being knowledgeable of particulars entails His being a necessitating cause in essence, and it is established that whenever He is a necessitating cause in essence, from the perpetuity of the cause follows the perpetuity of the effect. Commentary

It begins with the world's possibility in itself, then uses the fact that God knows its occurrence to infer its necessity. The structure: (1) God's knowledge of particulars implies necessary occurrence of what He knows; (2) impossible occurrence of what He knows not; (3) thus nothing is possible for Him, negating power; (4) thus He is an essential necessitator (mujib bi al-dhat), leading to the eternity of the world. The argument proceeds step by step: if God knows X will occur, X must occur, otherwise His knowledge would be ignorance (impossible). Therefore X is necessary. Similarly, what He knows not to occur is impossible. Hence nothing is contingent for God, so He cannot be truly powerful (qadir), but rather is a necessary cause. If He is an essential necessitator, then the world, as His effect, must be co-eternal with Him.

Objection to Knowledge Requiring Existence and the Milk-Sister Response
Objection and response about knowledge not requiring existence or distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that knowledge does not require the existence of the known, and the response using the example of an unknown milk-sister to demonstrate that knowledge can occur without the known being distinguished.

Core Idea
Knowledge does not depend on the existence or distinction of its object.

This lesson presents a classic kalam objection and response regarding the relationship between knowledge and its object. The objector argues that knowing impossible things like the partner of God shows that knowledge does not require existence. The responder counters with the example of knowing one has a milk-sister without knowing her identity, demonstrating that knowledge can exist without the known being distinguished.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the first part of the passage (beginning with 'فإن قيل') which presents the objector's argument. Notice the reasoning: knowledge of an impossible thing (partner of God) does not depend on that thing existing, because then the impossible would have to exist.
2
Understand the argument from impossibility
The objector uses the example of knowing that the partner of God is impossible. He argues that this judgment requires a conception of what a partner of God is. If that conception depended on the existence of the partner, then the partner would have to exist, which is contradictory. This is supposed to prove that knowledge does not require existence.
3
Read the response and the milk-sister example
Now read the response ('ثم نقول'). The responder concedes God's eternal knowledge but questions the requirement that the known be distinguished. He provides the example of knowing that one has a milk-sister in a city without knowing her specifically. This shows that knowledge can be present without the object being distinguished from other things.
4
Reflect on how the example refutes the objection
Consider how the milk-sister example addresses the objector's point. The objector assumed that knowledge requires existence or distinction. The responder shows that general knowledge (e.g., that there is a milk-sister) does not require the specific known to be distinguished. This undermines the objector's requirement.
5
Connect to the broader issue of God's knowledge
Think about how this discussion relates to God's eternal knowledge. The passage deals with whether God's knowledge of things in eternity requires those things to be existent or distinguished. The responder is arguing that it does not, using the milk-sister analogy to illustrate that knowledge can precede distinction.

If it is said: We do not accept that knowledge of something depends on that known thing being existent. The evidence for it is: when we know that the partner of God is impossible, this judgment cannot be obtained except after a conception of what the partner of God is. So if this conception were dependent on the existence of the conceived thing, then the partner of God would have to exist. On this assumption, judging the partner of God to be impossible would entail judging it to be necessary of existence. That is contradictory. Then we say: There is no dispute that God Almighty knew the realities of things in eternity. But why do you say that knowledge of something requires that the known be distinguished from others in the knowledge of that knower? Do you not see that when we know that we have a milk-sister in this city while we do not know her specifically, here knowledge of this known is obtained while this known is not distinguished from others in the knowledge of that knower. So it is established: knowledge of something does not require the known to be distinguished from others in the knowledge of that knower.

Commentary
This passage presents an initial objection and the first part of the response. The objector argues that knowledge does not depend on the existence of the known, using the example of knowing that the partner of God is impossible. The responder then argues that there is no dispute about God's eternal knowledge, but questions the requirement that the known be distinguished in the knower's knowledge, providing the example of knowing one has a milk-sister without knowing her identity, showing that knowledge can occur without distinction.

Distinction for Knowledge May Be Deferred to the Future
Response: distinction for knowledge may be future.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the distinction required for knowledge need not be simultaneous and how this argument defends God's eternal knowledge of future contingent things.

Core Idea
Future distinction suffices for present knowledge.

This passage presents a key response in the debate about God's eternal knowledge. The opponent argues that for God to know something, it must be distinguished from others at the moment of knowledge. Our responder grants the need for distinction but insists it need not be simultaneous; future distinction is enough. This opens a path to reconcile eternal knowledge with future contingents.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Let the argument's flow register. Then read the translation and note the concessive opening 'sallamnā' (we grant).
The passage is a single continuous argument.
2
Identify the central claim
The responder asks why distinction must be simultaneous with knowledge. Find the sentence that proposes an alternative: it can be future.
Look for 'wa-lam qultum inna dhālika al-imtiyāz yajibu husūluhu hāla husūli al-ʿilm'.
3
Analyze the example
The example of substances and accidents shows that God knows them in eternity even though they do not yet exist. Their future existence provides the needed distinction.
Notice how this solves the problem of God knowing future contingents.
4
Grasp the broader implication
This argument defends that God's eternal knowledge is compatible with changes in created things. It does not deny the need for distinction, only relaxes its timing.
Contrast with the opponent's insistence on simultaneity.

Then when they become existent and present, some become distinguished from others. So why do you say that this amount of distinction is not sufficient for eternal knowledge of these essences being differentiated and distinct?

Commentary
This passage is the second response by the responder, after granting that distinction is necessary. The responder argues that the distinction does not have to be present at the same time as knowledge; it could be future. He uses the example of God's knowledge of substances and accidents: God knows them in eternity, but they do not exist yet. Their distinction occurs when they become existent in the future. This future distinction suffices for eternal knowledge.

Established Does Not Entail Existent: The Case of Non-Existent as Established Thing
Argument that 'established' does not require existence, citing non-existents as established essences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that in Islamic theology, being 'established' (thabit) does not necessarily imply existence, as non-existent essences can be considered established in their own right.

Core Idea
Established does not mean existent — non-existent essences are still real in their nature.

This passage presents a key argument in the debate about divine attributes and the status of non-existents. The author grants that knowledge requires something to be established, but challenges the assumption that establishment entails existence. He cites a school of thought that non-existents can be established as essences apart from existence, raising the question of what evidence disproves this view.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
First, read the Arabic passage and its English translation. Pay attention to the flow: a concession followed by a challenge.
2
Identify the logical structure
Notice the two parts: granting that knowledge requires the object to be established, and then questioning why every established thing must be existent.
3
Understand the key claim
The group mentioned holds that a non-existent can be an established thing, meaning essences stripped of existence are still determined as essences.
This is not about weak existence but about the reality of quiddities.
4
Reflect on the challenge
The passage ends with a request for evidence against this view. Consider what implications this has for arguments about divine attributes and knowledge.
This challenge shifts the burden of proof to the opponent.

Eternal Knowledge Requires Eternal Distinction of Objects
The author rejects the sufficiency of temporal distinction for eternal knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the objects of God's eternal knowledge must be eternally distinct, based on the condition-conditioned priority principle.

Core Idea
Eternal knowledge cannot wait for future distinctions; the condition must be prior.

This passage answers a subtle objection: could God's eternal knowledge be about things that only become distinct later? The author calls this idea 'obviously corrupt' and explains why. As you read, pay attention to the logical relationship between condition (sharṭ) and conditioned (mashrūṭ), and how it applies to divine knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the English translation to grasp the flow of the argument.
Note the phrase 'ẓāhir al-fasād' – a strong dismissal.
2
Identify the objection and the response
The passage begins with an objection ('Why not...?'). Identify it, then see how the author refutes it with the correspondence principle.
The objection is about future distinction (lā yazāl).
3
Follow the logical chain
Trace the steps: knowledge must match its object → if distinction is known, distinction must exist → existence of distinction is a condition → condition precedes conditioned in rank.
The key move is the application of the condition-conditioned priority.
4
Reflect on the implications
Consider why this matters: God's knowledge is eternal, so all known distinctions must be eternal. This ties into discussions of divine attributes and the relationship between God and creation.
Think about how this argument supports the eternity of God's knowledge of particulars.

قوله: لم لا يكفي في حصول العلم الأزلي، حصول الامتياز في المعلومات في لا يزال؟ قلنا: هذا ظاهر الفساد. لأن العلم يجب أن يكون مطابقاً للمعلوم، وإلا لكان جهلاً. وعلى هذا التقدير، فيمتنع كون العالم عالماً بامتياز ذلك المعلوم عن غيره، إلا إذا كان ذلك المعلوم ممتازاً عن غيره. فلما كان كون المعلوم ممتازاً عن غيره شرطاً لتعلق العلم بامتيازه عن غيره، وثبت أن الشرط متقدم على المشروط في الرتبة، امتنع أن يكون حصول الامتياز في المعلومات متأخراً عن تعلق العلم بهذا الامتياز.
He said: 'Why is it not sufficient for the occurrence of eternal knowledge that distinction occurs in the known things in the future (lā yazāl)?' We say: This is obviously corrupt. Because knowledge must correspond to the known, otherwise it would be ignorance. On this basis, it is impossible for the knower to know the distinction of that known from others unless that known is distinct from others. Since the known being distinct from others is a condition for knowledge of its distinction from others, and it is established that the condition is prior in rank to the conditioned, it is impossible that the occurrence of distinction in the known things be posterior to the attachment of knowledge to that distinction.
Source pages p. 164

Commentary
This passage answers an objection: why can't eternal knowledge be about objects that become distinct only in the future? The author calls this 'obviously corrupt' (ẓāhir al-fasād). He reiterates the correspondence principle: knowledge must match its object. Thus, for God to know that X is distinct from Y, X must actually be distinct at the time of knowledge. Since God's knowledge is eternal, the distinction must be eternal as well. He uses the logical principle that the condition (sharṭ) is prior in rank (ruthba) to the conditioned (mashrūṭ). Therefore, distinction cannot occur after knowledge of it; it must precede or be simultaneous.
