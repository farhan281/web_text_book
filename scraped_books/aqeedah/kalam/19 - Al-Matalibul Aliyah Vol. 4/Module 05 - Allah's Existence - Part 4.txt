Ibrahim's Argument from Life and Death Refuting Human Agency
Ibrahim's Quranic argument refuting human agency in life and death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Prophet Ibrahim refuted the skeptic's claim that humans can give life and death through intermediaries, and why God is the ultimate cause.

Core Idea
True causation belongs to God; human actions are only secondary through intermediaries.

This passage presents a sophisticated objection from a skeptic who argues that if life and death occur through celestial and natural intermediaries, then humans can also cause them. Prophet Ibrahim's response demonstrates that humans lack control over the ultimate movers, thereby establishing God's exclusive authorship of life and death.

Guided Path

2
Identify the skeptic's challenge

This is a common objection against divine causation.

This establishes God's unique control over the intermediaries.

Ibrahim uses the example of the sun: God brings the sun from the east by moving it; humans cannot move the sun, so any effect via the sun is not truly theirs.
This clarifies the difference between God's agency and human agency.
5

The conclusion is that life and death caused through intermediaries are truly God's acts, not human acts. The skeptic's objection fails.
This passage also illustrates the Quranic method of argumentation.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer passage from al-Razi (likely) presenting and refuting a skeptic's challenge to Ibrahim's argument. Second, he gives the example of the sun: if God brings the sun from the east, He does so by moving the sun; humans cannot move the sun, so their actions via the sun are not truly theirs. The difference is that God has direct control over the celestial spheres, while humans do not. Therefore, the life and death caused through intermediaries are truly God's actions, not human actions. The author concludes by explaining that he has clarified this argument so that the true verification becomes apparent.

Ibrahim's Refutation of Nimrod: Divine Agency Behind Natural Causes

Core Idea
The mover behind the motion is God alone.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi unpacks the famous debate between Prophet Ibrahim and Nimrod. Nimrod claimed that since life and death occur through natural processes, there is no need for God. Ibrahim's response is a sophisticated causal argument: while God uses the spheres as instruments, He alone is their mover, and humans cannot claim agency over effects they do not control. This lesson walks through the logical steps of that refutation.

Infinite regress is impossible; motion ends in an Unmoved Mover.

Fakhr al-Din al-Razi begins by considering the movements of celestial bodies. If each movement required another sphere, we would have an infinite chain—which reason rejects. Thus, the movements must originate from a Creator who Himself does not move. This argument is grounded in Quran 16:12, which ends with 'for a people who reason.'

This is a classic argument in Islamic theology and philosophy.
3
See how Razi uses the Quranic verse
Razi quotes Quran 16:12 to show that the conclusion is not merely philosophical but aligns with divine revelation. The verse calls on rational people to reflect.

Commentary
This passage presents two complementary arguments (wajhān) for the existence of a free Creator, drawn from the nature of celestial movements and from observable variation in the natural world. The author, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, is refuting the view that natural phenomena are caused by necessary, blind forces (al-mūjib bi al-dhāt) and instead proving they originate from a powerful, free-choosing agent (al-fāʿil al-mukhtār).

Therefore, they must ultimately be caused by a creator who is Himself not subject to motion—that is, God. Razi connects this to Quran 16:12, which ends with "for a people who reason," implying that rational reflection leads to the conclusion that infinite regress is false and the chain of motion ends in an unmoved mover, who is the powerful, free-choosing God.

Second argument: Consider a single rose leaf: one side is intensely red, the other intensely black. The influence of the planets and natural dispositions is uniform across all parts of a single leaf. Yet we see variation. If the cause were a necessary agent (mūjib bi al-dhāt), its effect would be uniform, because a necessary cause acts without choice and produces the same result every time. Since variation occurs, the cause must be a free agent who chooses to produce different effects. Razi quotes Quran 16:13, which ends with "for a people who remember," urging the reader to recall the established rational principle that a necessary nature does not vary, and then recognize that the observed variation points to a free agent.

Commentary
This passage lists two distinct points of research that arise from the previous discussion of the philosophers' position. 

**First point:** The question is whether the First Principle (al-mabda' al-awwal) directly produces all contingent things or only one thing, which then produces multiplicity. The author notes that he has not seen any philosopher who holds the direct production view. Instead, all philosophers agree on the principle 'from the One only one proceeds' (al-wahid la yasdiru 'anhu illa al-wahid). Thus, God (exalted) is the cause of the First Intellect alone. That First Intellect then causes three things: the Second Intellect, the Soul, and the (outermost) Sphere. The details of this emanation scheme are promised later. 

**Second point:** The issue here is why one cannot combine essential necessitation and choice. That is, the Necessary Existent could cause the existence of bodies by necessity (essential necessitation) but then arrange or compose them with different attributes (qualities, forms) by choice. Under this view, God would be a necessitator (mujib) regarding essences (the very existence of bodies) but a chooser (mukhtar) regarding their attributes. This is a potential compromise between the necessitarian philosophers and the theologians who affirm free will in God. The author does not yet resolve it; it is presented as a topic for investigation.

The First Emanation: One Thing and a Substance
Razi establishes the first emanation is one thing and a substance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the principle 'from the One only one proceeds' is used to establish that the first emanation is one thing, and how logical disjunction forces it to be a substance rather than an accident.

Core Idea
The first creation must be both one and a substance, because accidents depend on substances.

The argument begins with a foundational principle of Islamic philosophy: from the absolutely simple One (God) only one thing can directly emanate. Razi then employs a simple classification: every possible existent is either a substance (jawhar) or an accident (ʿaraḍ). Through a careful elimination of accident, he concludes that the first emanation must be a substance. This lesson walks through that initial reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the principle of 'one from one'
Read the opening statement: it is established that God is free from multiplicity, so from Him only one can proceed. This is the basis for the whole argument.
This principle is a major point of discussion in Islamic philosophy.
2
Learn the classification of existents
Every possible existent is either a substance (self-subsistent) or an accident (dependent on substance). The first emanation must be one of these.
3
See why accident cannot be first
An accident depends on a substance and is posterior. If the first emanation were an accident, it would be a cause of substances, making accident prior to substance – a circularity (dawr). This is impossible.
Circularity is a standard logical impossibility in kalam.
4
Conclude substance
Since accident is refuted, the first emanation must be a substance. This sets the stage for further determination.

Commentary
This passage is a proof by logical disjunction (taqsīm) aimed at identifying the first creation or emanation (al-ṣādir al-awwal) from God. The argument proceeds in stages. First, it invokes the principle that from the One (al-wāḥid) only one can proceed, establishing that the first emanation is a single thing. Then, since every possible existent is either a substance (jawhar) or an accident (ʿaraḍ), the first emanation must be one of these. Accidents are dependent on substances and are posterior, so an accident cannot be the cause of substances (circularity). Therefore it is a substance. Next, this substance is either a body, a part of a body (matter or form), or neither. Bodies are composed, so they require prior parts; thus the first emanation cannot be a body. If it were matter, matter would be both receptive and effective, which is impossible; also matter is homogenous, so any matter would be a cause, which is false. If it were form, form would cause matter without matter's participation, making form independent of matter, which would deny its nature as form (form is inherently dependent on matter). So the first emanation is neither body nor part of body; therefore it is a simple substance (jawhar mujarrad). Simple substances are either dependent on a corporeal instrument (soul) or independent (intellect). The first emanation causes all bodies, so it cannot depend on a body (otherwise circularity). Hence it is an intellect. The argument concludes by identifying this intellect with the Supreme Spirit (al-rūḥ al-aʿẓam) mentioned in the Qur'an (78:38) and with the first creation mentioned in the hadith: 'The first thing God created is the intellect.' Thus the philosophers' metaphysical proof is aligned with revealed texts.

Commentary
This passage is a proof by logical disjunction (taqsīm) aimed at identifying the first creation or emanation (al-ṣādir al-awwal) from God. The argument proceeds in stages. First, it invokes the principle that from the One (al-wāḥid) only one can proceed, establishing that the first emanation is a single thing. Then, since every possible existent is either a substance (jawhar) or an accident (ʿaraḍ), the first emanation must be one of these. Accidents are dependent on substances and are posterior, so an accident cannot be the cause of substances (circularity). Therefore it is a substance. Next, this substance is either a body, a part of a body (matter or form), or neither. Bodies are composed, so they require prior parts; thus the first emanation cannot be a body. If it were matter, matter would be both receptive and effective, which is impossible; also matter is homogenous, so any matter would be a cause, which is false. If it were form, form would cause matter without matter's participation, making form independent of matter, which would deny its nature as form (form is inherently dependent on matter). So the first emanation is neither body nor part of body; therefore it is a simple substance (jawhar mujarrad). Simple substances are either dependent on a corporeal instrument (soul) or independent (intellect). The first emanation causes all bodies, so it cannot depend on a body (otherwise circularity). Hence it is an intellect. The argument concludes by identifying this intellect with the Supreme Spirit (al-rūḥ al-aʿẓam) mentioned in the Qur'an (78:38) and with the first creation mentioned in the hadith: 'The first thing God created is the intellect.' Thus the philosophers' metaphysical proof is aligned with revealed texts.

Core Idea
The soul's eternity is proven by the need for an ignorant agent to precede God's first act.

This lesson presents a unique proof for the soul's existence and eternity, drawing on the distinction between a free agent and a necessary cause. The author outlines two stations: first, that the soul is not the body, and second, that it is eternal. The key argument for eternity revolves around the impossibility of an eternal act from a free agent, requiring a prior ignorant agent—the soul.

The First Doubt: Impossibility of a Specific Time for Creation
Razi presents the first sophism against the possibility of a specific time for creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first sophism against the possibility of God beginning creation at a specific time, and the dilemma it poses regarding the property of the chosen time.

Core Idea
God's choice of a specific creation time seems arbitrary unless the time has a unique property.

Razi presents an objection from opponents of the Kalam position. They argue that if God is a free agent who began creating at a specific moment after infinite inaction, that choice appears arbitrary. To press this point, they introduce a sophism focusing on whether the chosen time has a special property. This lesson unpacks that sophism and its implications, showing the dilemma that the Kalam theologian must address.

Guided Path

Understand the premises
Read the first part of the passage that establishes two premises: God is a free agent, and a free agent’s action is originated. This leads to the conclusion that God was inactive from eternity until the time of creation.
These premises are foundational to the Kalam cosmological argument.
2
Identify the dilemma
The sophism presents a dichotomy: either the specific time of creation has a special property that makes it suitable, or it does not. The opponent argues that both options lead to problems.
3

If the time does have a property, the opponent then asks: does that property belong to the time by its essence, by a necessary concomitant, or by neither? The first two are rejected because they would imply that all times have that property, leading to creation at every time.
This is a crucial step—understand why essence and concomitant are rejected.
4

The passage cuts off before fully explaining the third option (property not due to essence or concomitant). Reflect on why that option might also be problematic (e.g., leads to infinite regress or arbitrary choice).
This sets up the need for the Kalam theologian to provide an alternative explanation.

Commentary
This passage presents an argument from opponents of the Kalam position, structured as a syllogism followed by a doubt. It begins by stating two established premises: (1) God is not a necessary cause by essence but a free agent, and (2) a free agent necessarily produces originated actions. The passage thus sets up a dilemma that the Kalam theologian must address.
