Second Argument: The Eternity of Duration and Time
A philosophical argument for the eternity of duration and time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument that duration and time must be eternal, including the trilemma and the reductio ad absurdum used to refute the idea of a beginning and end.

Core Idea
Duration and time cannot have a beginning or end without contradiction.

This lesson presents a sophisticated philosophical argument for the eternity of duration and time. The argument proceeds by considering all logical possibilities and showing that only one is coherent. As you read, notice how the author uses the concepts of priority and posteriority to derive the necessary eternal nature of time.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the trilemma
Read the Arabic passage and translation. The argument begins by listing three possibilities for duration and time: having no beginning and no end, having both beginning and end, or having one without the other. Identify each option.
The first option is the desired conclusion; the other two are to be eliminated.
2
Follow the refutation of the second option
The author assumes duration has a beginning and an end. Notice how this leads to two 'non-existences': one preceding duration (without beginning) and one following it (without end). Understand how these non-existences share the property of being non-existence but differ in priority and posteriority.
Key insight: priority and posteriority are distinct from non-existence itself.
3
Understand how priority and posteriority imply eternity
The argument claims that priority and posteriority are the essence of duration and time. Since the pre-duration non-existence has priority without beginning and the post-duration non-existence has posteriority without end, duration and time must themselves be without beginning and end. Recognize the contradiction: assuming a beginning leads to no beginning, so the assumption is impossible.
This is a reductio ad absurdum: assuming the opposite leads to a logical contradiction.

Commentary
This passage is a complex argument (hujja) for the eternity of duration and time. The author presents a trilemma: either duration/time has no beginning and no end (the desired conclusion), or it has both a beginning and an end, or it has one without the other. He then refutes the second option (having a beginning and end). He argues that if duration had a beginning, it would be preceded by a non-existence that itself has no beginning (since before the beginning of duration, there is an endless non-existence). Similarly, if it had an end, it would be followed by an endless non-existence. These two non-existences share the property of being non-existence but differ in priority and posteriority relative to the existence of duration. The priority of the first non-existence and the posteriority of the second are attributes distinct from their non-existence. These attributes (priority and posteriority) are the very meaning of duration and time. Therefore, duration and time must themselves exist as the measure of these relations. But these relations (priority of the pre-duration non-existence and posteriority of the post-duration non-existence) are themselves endless (the pre-duration non-existence has no beginning, the post-duration non-existence has no end). Hence, duration and time must be without beginning and without end. Thus, positing a beginning and end for duration leads to a contradiction (establishing beginning and end negates them), so it is impossible. Therefore, duration must be eternal. This is a typical philosopher's argument for the eternity of time. The author may be presenting this argument either as an opponent's argument or as a stepping stone to refute it. Given the context, it appears to be part of a series of arguments the author is responding to or analyzing. Without further context, we treat it as a self-contained argument.

The Eternity of Time from the Distinction of Non-Existences
Argument for the pre-eternity of time from the distinction of two non-existences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the distinction between two non-existences implies that time has no beginning and no end.

Core Idea
Time must be eternal because it distinguishes non-existences that lack beginning and end.

In this lesson, we explore a profound argument for the eternity of time. The author considers two non-existences: the non-existence before a thing exists and after it perishes. These share the property of being non-existence but differ in priority and posteriority. This difference cannot come from non-existence itself, so it must come from time, which therefore must be beginningless and endless.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two non-existences
Grasp that before something exists there is a state of non-existence, and after it ceases there is another non-existence.
These are sometimes called the two non-existences (al-ʿadamān).
2
Identify common and distinguishing factors
Both non-existences share the attribute of being non-existence, but they differ in that one is before and one is after.
This difference is priority (taqaddum) and posteriority (taʾakhkhur).
3
Recognize the necessity of time
The distinction of before/after must come from something beyond non-existence, which is time (al-muddah wa al-zamān).
Time is the measure of priority and posteriority.
4
See the implications for eternity
Since the first non-existence has no beginning and the second no end, time must also have no beginning and no end.
This is because time is what distinguishes them.
5
Conclude impossibility of time having beginning or end
If time had a beginning and end, that would contradict the nature of the non-existences it distinguishes.
Thus asserting a first and last for time is impossible.

The Third Argument: Time Must Be Pre-Eternal
Third proof that time is pre-eternal based on distinction of times of existence and non-existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the concept of origination (ḥudūth) logically requires the pre-eternity of time.

Core Idea
Origination cannot be conceived without a temporal distinction.

Sanusi presents his third argument for the pre-eternity of time, a dialectical proof that forces a crucial choice. The argument shows that if the world were originated, then either the time of non-existence is distinct from the time of existence, or it is not. Only the first option is coherent, and it entails that time exists before the world—hence, time is pre-eternal.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the conditional structure: 'if the world were originated, then...'
2
Identify the two divisions
The argument presents a dichotomy: either the time of non-existence is distinct from the time of existence, or it is not. Find where each division is stated and note the reasoning attached to each.
3
Understand why the second division is false
Ask yourself why the author rejects the idea that there is no distinction. The passage says reason cannot judge something as non-existent at one time and existent at another without temporal specification. Work through that reasoning slowly.
This step connects directly to the learning objective.

Without a temporal distinction, we could not conceive of the transition from non-existence to existence. Therefore, the only coherent possibility is that time is pre-eternal.

The First Branch: Specified Creation Leads to Infinite Regress
First division of the fourth argument: specified creation leads to infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why specifying a time for the creation of time leads to an infinite regress, and how that regress ultimately forces the conclusion that time is eternal.

Core Idea
If time is created at a specific time, that time itself requires creation — leading to an infinite chain.

Sanusi presents the first branch of his fourth argument. He assumes that if time is originated, God must have intended it. If He intended it at a particular moment, that moment itself becomes a problem. Let's follow the reasoning and see why it collapses into either impossibility or the desired conclusion.

If God specifies a time for creation, that time would itself need creation, leading to an infinite regress. Analyze the two possibilities for this regress.
The regress is either all-at-once or sequential.
4

The regress all-at-once is impossible. The sequential regress with no beginning is exactly the claim that time is eternal.
Thus the first case leads to either impossibility or the desired conclusion.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth argument (al-ḥujjah al-rābiʿah). It considers the hypothesis that time (al-muddah wa al-zamān) is itself originated. If so, it must be brought into existence by an agent (God). The author divides the divine intention into two cases: (1) God intends to create time but specifies a particular time for that creation. This leads to an infinite regress because that specified time would itself require a preceding time, and so on. If the regress occurs all at once (i.e., an infinite series of times created simultaneously), that is impossible. Thus, in both cases, the hypothesis that time is originated leads to the conclusion that time is eternal, which is a contradiction, so time cannot be originated; it must be pre-eternal.

This passage presents a sophistical argument (shubha) for the eternity of bodies, likely to be refuted later. This passage presents a philosophical argument (shubha) for the pre-eternity of bodies, which is a doubt that the author likely intends to refute later. Hence, the argument concludes that bodies are infinite and without beginning or end—this is the doubt concerning the pre-eternity of bodies.

Commentary
This passage refutes the first division of those who claim that God can annihilate the world directly by His power. This passage refutes the first division of those who claim that God can annihilate the world directly by His power. The author begins by stating that all three divisions are false, then focuses on the first: that the free agent (al-fāʿil al-mukhtār) directly causes the world's non-existence. He provides three proofs (wujūh).

The author then addresses a possible objection: that the same reasoning applies to the effect of power in creation. He responds that this objection only holds for those who define power as equally capable of opposites; but for those who hold that power together with a motive necessitates an effect, the objection does not apply.

Commentary
This passage presents the first of several proofs that it is not impossible for an eternal effect to be attributed to an eternal cause. The proof proceeds as follows: The contingent (mumkin) while persisting (hal baqa'ihi) remains contingent. Every contingent requires a cause. Therefore, the contingent while persisting requires a cause, showing that persistence does not remove the need for a cause. The author then demonstrates both premises. First, that the contingent remains contingent: He argues that possibility (imkan) is either an essential property (lazim li al-mahiyyah) or an accident ('arid). If it is essential, it must always be present. If it is accidental, then the possibility of its occurrence precedes its occurrence, leading to a regress of possibilities or ending with an essential possibility — which is what is sought. Second, that every contingent needs a cause is self-evident (badihi). Thus, the conclusion follows. The author then addresses an objection: someone might claim that the thing, while persisting, has a suitability (awlawiyyah) that makes it independent of a cause. The author responds by analyzing the source of this suitability: if it is from the essence, it should have been present at origination, eliminating the need for a cause at that time, which is absurd. If it is from something else, then the suitability arises at persistence, meaning the thing persists because of this suitability, implying it still needs a cause — just that the cause is called 'suitability'. Thus, the objection concedes the point and only argues about terminology.

The Self-Evident Need and the Objection of Suitability
The author presents the second premise, the conclusion of the proof, and responds to an objection about suitability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the need of the contingent for a cause is self-evident and how the author refutes the objection that a thing might be self-sufficient due to suitability (awlawiyyah).

Core Idea
Need for a cause is self-evident, and 'suitability' merely renames the cause.

After establishing that the contingent remains contingent, the author asserts that every contingent needs a cause as a self-evident truth. Then he addresses a potential objection: perhaps the contingent, while persisting, gains a suitability (awlawiyyah) that makes it independent of an external cause. This lesson unpacks the objection and the author's incisive response.

Understand the conclusion

This is a powerful refutation that shows the objection is verbal.

Why Occurrence Cannot Be the Cause of Need
The author argues that temporal origination cannot be the cause of need for a cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why temporal origination (huduth) cannot be the cause of a thing's need for a cause, based on the logical priority of existence and its properties.

Core Idea
Occurrence is a property of existence, and properties are too far downstream to cause the need for a cause.

This lesson presents the second proof from Al-Matalib al-Aliyah that challenges a common assumption. If a thing's temporal origination were the reason it needs a cause, then every event would require a cause. But the author argues that occurrence is a property of existence that comes after existence in logical rank, making it impossible to be the cause of need.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze. Then read the translation to get the flow of the argument.
2
Understand the definition of occurrence
The author defines occurrence as existence preceded by non-existence. This definition is key.
3
Trace the chain of posteriority
Note the ordering: occurrence is posterior to existence, existence posterior to agent's influence, influence posterior to need, need posterior to cause. Therefore occurrence cannot be the cause of need.
The argument is incomplete in the text, but the logical chain is clear.
