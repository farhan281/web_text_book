The First Objection: Composition of the First Intellect
First objection: composition implies multiplicity from God

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the argument from composition (jins and fasl) challenges the claim that only one effect emanates from God.

Core Idea
Composition implies multiplicity in the effect, contradicting 'from the One only one proceeds'.

This lesson examines the first of two objections raised against the view that the first emanation is a single intellect. The objection targets the metaphysical composition of the intellect itself: if it is a composite of genus and differentia, then its emanation from God would involve more than one thing, violating the principle of unity.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how the objection builds from the nature of substance as a genus to the composition of the intellect.
The phrase 'فعلى هذا' (so on this basis) is key to the logical flow.
2
Understand the genus-species framework
The objection assumes substance is a genus and intellect is a species. Grasp that any species is a composite of genus and differentia.
Differentia marks the specific distinction (e.g., rationality for humans).
3
Connect composition to multiplicity
If the intellect is composite, its effect from God involves its components, meaning more than one thing has emanated.
This contradicts the principle that from the one only one can proceed.

The Second Objection: Limited Divine Power
Second objection: limited divine power reduced to absurdity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that limiting God to one effect would make His power inferior to that of lesser causes.

Core Idea
A God who can only produce one effect is less powerful than any finite cause.

This lesson covers the second objection against the single-intellect theory. It argues that if God were capable of producing only one effect, His power would be less than that of ordinary causes that can produce many effects — an absurd conclusion for the Necessary Existence.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Note that it is the second objection starting with 'والثاني'.
This objection uses a reductio ad absurdum.
2
Understand the reductio
The argument assumes that if God only produces one effect, then His power is limited to that single act. Compare this to lowly causes that produce many effects.
The conclusion is that this would make God's rank lower than those causes, which is false.
3
Recognize the absurdity
The objection concludes that such a limitation is absurd and cannot be true, thus the premise that only one effect emanates must be false.
The argument is indirect: if P then Q, Q is false, so P is false.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth objection and a detailed answer. The objector questions why God, knowing that attachment to matter causes afflictions, did not prevent it. The answer, attributed to Muhammad ibn Zakariyya, has two parts. First, the love (عشق) of the soul for matter is innate and instinctive, so removing it would be impossible (ممتنع), and God only has power over what is possible. Second, God in His wisdom allowed the attachment but also provided the light of intellect (نور العقل) and worldly experiences to teach the soul the harm of its attachment, leading to its eventual self-correction and return to its spiritual origin. The author then gives a parable: a wise father lets his son travel to a bad country despite knowing the harm, because he sends a guide to show the son the truth, so the son returns willingly. This illustrates that God's permission of evil is not a flaw but part of a pedagogical process for the soul's ultimate happiness.

The First Proof: God Acts by His Will, Not for Benefit
The author presents an introduction and the first proof that God does not act for the benefit of creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first proof that God does not act for the benefit of creation, based on the dependency of action on the divine motive (al-da'i).

Core Idea
God’s will is the sole motive for action; no created benefit can condition it.

The author begins by contrasting the view of those who deny rational virtues and vices. They cannot claim that God creates at the best time for creatures because that would imply benefit as a motive. Instead, the first proof establishes that every action depends on a motive (al-da'i), and since God's decree encompasses all harms and evils, His acts cannot be contingent on created interests.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the parties
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice who is being described: those who deny beautification and vilification (tahsin wa taqbih) and affirm God’s absolute will.
The opening phrase 'في حكاية القول من أثبت العالم' introduces their view.
2
Understand the implication of denying tahsin wa taqbih
The passage explains that because they deny rational good and evil, they cannot claim that God created the world at a specific time for the benefit of humans.
This sets up the rejection of benefit as a motive.
3
Analyze the first proof: action depends on motive
The proof states: 'إن الفعل موقوف على الداعي' (action depends on the motive). Since all harms and evils are by God’s decree, His action cannot be contingent on considering interests.
Focus on the logical connection: motive → action, and motive is God’s will alone.
4
Grasp the conclusion
The conclusion is that it is impossible for God's action to be based on considerations of interests or benefits for creation.
This affirms God's absolute independence.

Commentary
This passage begins a new topic: the view of those who affirm that God does whatever He wills without regard to the interests or harms of creation. The author explains that because these people deny the Mu'tazilite principle of rational beautification and vilification (tahsin wa taqbih), they cannot claim that God specified the time of creation for the benefit of creatures, as that would imply He acts for their advantage. Then the author presents two proofs supporting this stance, but here we cover only the first proof. The first proof: Every action depends on a motive (al-da'i). Given that, all harms and evils are by God's decree (qada') and preordainment (qadr). Consequently, God's action cannot be contingent upon considering the interests or benefits of creatures. In other words, if God's will is the sole determinant, external benefits cannot be a condition for His acts.
