The Self-Evident Rational Answer
Al-Razi presents a series of self-evident rational judgments that settle the debate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi uses a series of self-evident rational judgments (badīha) to settle the objections and establish the world's temporal origination.

Core Idea
Self-evident truths of reason form the foundation that dispels all speculative objections.

Al-Razi now delivers his answer by invoking the foundational truths that every rational person must acknowledge. These self-evident judgments (badīha) about existence, necessity, possibility, and causation provide the definitive response to the earlier objections. In this passage, we see the core of his theological epistemology: the appeal to necessary truths of reason.

Guided Path
1
Read the answer passage
Read the Arabic passage from 'والجواب' to the end, then the translation. Notice the repeated use of 'البديهة' (intuition).
This section is dense with claims; read slowly.
2
Identify the series of self-evident judgments
Al-Razi lists several judgments: every existent is either necessary or possible; the necessary does not admit non-existence; the necessary is either free or necessitating; the possible must terminate with the necessary; the effect of the necessary remains as long as the cause; if the cause is not necessitating, it is free; every act of a free agent is originated.
Write each judgment down.
3
Understand how each judgment refutes the objections
For example, the judgment that the necessary cannot cease to exist directly answers the third objection about the eternal ceasing. The judgment that the effect of the necessary remains with the cause refutes the fifth objection's privative condition argument.
4
Recognize the dismissal strategy
Al-Razi concludes by saying the objections are like impugning necessary truths and should be ignored. This shows his confidence in the self-evident foundation.

Commentary
This passage presents five objections (the second through fifth) to a proof (likely the proof of the world's temporal origination from the principle of specification), followed by a comprehensive answer. The objections raise difficulties: the second question argues that if possible beings need a cause, then possible non-being also needs a cause, so the non-existence of the world before creation would need a cause. The third question addresses the problem of specification: if the world originated at a specific time, either the agent was necessitated (making the world eternal) or had a choice; if choice, the preference either requires a preponderator (leading to infinite regress) or is a preponderance without a preponderator, which the objector says would allow the eternal to cease without cause. The fourth question concedes that cessation requires a cause but asks why that cause cannot be a capable agent (qādir), and counters the claim that every act of a free agent is originated by noting that the connection between cause and effect is not conditional upon origination. The fifth question concedes a necessitating cause but argues that its effect may depend on a privative condition that can cease, allowing the effect to perish. The answer then invokes the self-evident intuitions (badīha) of reason: every existent is either necessary in itself (wājib li-dhātihi) or possible in itself (mumkin li-dhātihi); the necessary cannot admit non-existence; the necessary is either free or necessitating; the possible must terminate in the necessary; the effect of a necessary cause must endure as long as the cause; if the cause is not necessitating, it is free; every act of a free agent is originated (muḥdath). Thus, whatever admits non-existence is originated, and the eternal does not admit non-existence. The objections are dismissed as attempts to impugn necessary truths and are to be ignored.

Fifth Objection: Privation Conditions and Persistence of Effects
Presents the fifth objection using the example of gravity and a privative condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fifth objection against the proof for a free creator, which argues that a necessitating agent's effect may depend on a privative condition and thus need not be perpetual.

Core Idea
Causality may be conditioned on the absence of hindrances.

The objector concedes that the agent is a necessitator but argues that its effect may not be perpetual if a privative condition—like the absence of a chain—is removed. This challenges the proof that a perpetual cause must have a perpetual effect. The passage presents this objection with a clear example.

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth objection against the proof for a free creator and its answer using rational intuition (badīha). The objector concedes that the agent is a necessitator (muʾaththir mūjib) but argues that its perpetuity does not necessitate the perpetuity of its effect, because the effect may depend on a privative condition. For example, gravity causes a heavy object to fall only if there is no restraining chain. Similarly, the creation of the world might have been conditioned on an eternal privative condition (e.g., the non-existence of a hindrance), and that condition could cease, so the effect (the world) would cease. The answer rejects this by appealing to self-evident judgments of reason. First, reason directly knows that existents are either necessary in themselves (wājib li-dhātihi) or possible in themselves (mumkin li-dhātihi). Second, the necessary in itself cannot admit non-existence. Third, the necessary in itself is either free (mukhtār) or necessitating (mūjib). Fourth, every possible being must terminate at the necessary being, directly or indirectly. Fifth, reason attests that the effect of the necessary being must endure as long as its cause endures. Since the world is not eternal (as shown by other arguments), the cause cannot be a mere necessitator, because then the effect would be perpetual. Therefore, the cause must be a free agent. Sixth, any act of a free agent is originated (muḥdath). Hence, whatever can admit non-existence is originated, and the eternal (the necessary being) cannot admit non-existence. The objector's arguments are thus attacks on self-evident principles and should be ignored.

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth objection against the proof for a free creator and its answer using rational intuition (badīha). The objector concedes that the agent is a necessitator (muʾaththir mūjib) but argues that its perpetuity does not necessitate the perpetuity of its effect, because the effect may depend on a privative condition. For example, gravity causes a heavy object to fall only if there is no restraining chain. Similarly, the creation of the world might have been conditioned on an eternal privative condition (e.g., the non-existence of a hindrance), and that condition could cease, so the effect (the world) would cease. The answer rejects this by appealing to self-evident judgments of reason. First, reason directly knows that existents are either necessary in themselves (wājib li-dhātihi) or possible in themselves (mumkin li-dhātihi). Second, the necessary in itself cannot admit non-existence. Third, the necessary in itself is either free (mukhtār) or necessitating (mūjib). Fourth, every possible being must terminate at the necessary being, directly or indirectly. Fifth, reason attests that the effect of the necessary being must endure as long as its cause endures. Since the world is not eternal (as shown by other arguments), the cause cannot be a mere necessitator, because then the effect would be perpetual. Therefore, the cause must be a free agent. Sixth, any act of a free agent is originated (muḥdath). Hence, whatever can admit non-existence is originated, and the eternal (the necessary being) cannot admit non-existence. The objector's arguments are thus attacks on self-evident principles and should be ignored.

Commentary
This passage presents the view of the second division: those who affirm an efficient cause but hold it to be necessary by essence (al-mu'aththir al-mujib bi al-dhat). The argument proceeds as follows: 

1. The world is a body. 
2. Every body is contingent (mumkin) in itself, in its essence, and in all parts of its quiddity (mahiyya). That means its existence is not necessary from itself. 
3. Every contingent requires an efficient cause (mu'aththir) to bring it into existence. 
4. The efficient cause of all bodies cannot itself be a body. This is because if it were a body, it would also be contingent and require a further cause, leading to an infinite regress. 
5. Moreover, they claim that an efficient cause that acts by choice (ikhtiyar) is inconceivable, based on doubts previously mentioned (presumably arguments that choice implies change or imperfection in the agent). 
6. Therefore, the efficient cause must be necessary by essence (mujib bi al-dhat)—meaning it necessitates its effect by its very nature, without choice or deliberation. 
7. Consequently, the world is contingent in itself (possible of existence) but necessary through its cause (wajib al-wujud bi wujub sababihi wa 'illatihi). That is, given the necessary cause, the world necessarily exists as its effect. 

This is identified as the doctrine of the majority of philosophers, especially the followers of Aristotle, who supported this view.
