Truth of Conditionals and Divine Will
Logical analysis of conditional truth applied to divine will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that a conditional proposition can be true even if its parts are false, and how this logical fact refutes the claim that God's power requires actual alternation of will.

Core Idea
A conditional can be true even if both its parts are false.

This passage uses logic to defend God's transcendence. It examines the conditional 'If God wills the action, He does it' and shows that its truth does not depend on God actually willing at different times. Understanding the logical possibilities of conditionals is essential for correctly interpreting statements about divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Notice the logical terms used: 'conditional', 'antecedent', 'consequent'.
The passage is dense with logical examples; take your time.
2
Identify the four truth combinations
The author lists four possibilities for the truth values of the parts of a true conditional. Find each one: both true, both false, false antecedent/true consequent, and the impossible true antecedent/false consequent.
The fourth combination is impossible because truth cannot entail falsehood.
3
Examine the examples given
For each possible combination, the author provides an example. Understand why each example fits: e.g., 'If man is a donkey, he is an animal' has a false antecedent and true consequent.
These examples illustrate logical form, not theological content.
4
Apply to the divine will conditional
Now see how the author applies this logic to 'If He wills the action, He does it'. The truth of this conditional does not require that He actually willed the action or its leaving.
This refutes the idea that a powerful being must will different things at different times.
5
Grasp the conclusion
The conclusion: even if the definition of 'powerful' implies ability to will, it does not follow that God actually wills one thing and then another. The conditional remains true regardless of actual occurrence.
This protects the doctrine of God's transcendence and immutability.

Commentary
This passage is a logical refutation of a claim that God's power necessarily implies that He actually wills and acts in temporal alternation. The author, likely responding to an Ash'ari or Mu'tazili position, begins by analyzing the conditional statement 'If He wills the action, He does it' as a conditional proposition (qadiyya shartiyya). He clarifies the logical conditions for the truth of a conditional: a true conditional does not require both its antecedent and consequent to be true. He gives four possibilities: both true, both false, false antecedent with true consequent, and the impossible case of true antecedent with false consequent (since truth cannot entail falsehood). Applying this, he argues that the conditional about God's will and action can be true even if the antecedent (He wills) is not actually realized—that is, even if God never actually willed the action or its leaving. Therefore, the truth of the conditional does not prove that God's power necessarily translates into actual willing at different times. The argument preserves God's transcendence by severing a necessary link between power and actual will.

Refuting Hidden Benefit as Cause for Creation's Timing
Author refutes claim that God's will specified creation's time due to a hidden benefit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the claim that God specified the time of creation due to a hidden benefit leads to an impossible infinite regress (tasalsul) and is therefore false.

Core Idea
If God chose a time for creation because of a hidden benefit, that benefit would require another time, leading to an endless chain—impossible.

In this passage, the author addresses a common argument: that God must have created the world at a specific time because that time contained some hidden benefit. He demonstrates that this reasoning leads to a logical dead end—infinite regress. Moreover, he argues that all times are equal in essence, so no time can have a special benefit over another. This lesson breaks down the proof step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem of specification
The author starts by noting that creation occurs by God's action. The question then becomes: why did God assign each event a particular time of being earlier or later?
This sets up the problem.
2
Trace the infinite regress argument
If the specification of time is due to another time, then that other time itself would need a reason, leading to an endless chain. The author declares this impossible.
Infinite regress (tasalsul) is a classic impossibility in kalam.
3
Apply the refutation to hidden benefit
The author specifically targets the claim of a hidden benefit. If the benefit is in that time, then why that time? It collapses into regress. Additionally, all times are equal in essence, so no time is special.
This shows the claim is baseless.
4
Confirm the conclusion
The passage concludes that the statement about hidden benefit is false. The proof is solid.
Allah knows best.

The First Proof: Eternal Creation as a Necessary Favor
Presents the first proof that God must be eternally creating as a favor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical structure of the first proof that God must be eternally creating, based on the concepts of favor and wisdom.

Core Idea
God's eternal creation is a necessary favor, not an optional act.

This lesson introduces the first proof from Sanusi's text, which argues that God's creation of the world is an act of favor (iḥsān). Since a favor is better than its omission, and God always chooses the best, He must have been creating from eternity. The proof draws on fundamental rational judgments about good and evil, wisdom and futility.

Guided Path
1
Read and understand the premise
Read the Arabic passage once. Focus on the opening phrase: the proof is derived from concepts of good, evil, wisdom, and futility. Then identify the key premise: bringing the world into existence is a favor.
The term 'favor' (iḥsān) is central.
2
Grasp the moral principle
The passage states that a favor is better than abandoning it. This is a rational judgment accepted by the mutakallimūn. Understand why this principle is considered self-evident.
3
Trace the conditional argument
Notice the conditional: 'If God were not to bring the world into existence for an infinite duration, He would be abandoning the better...' This leads to an impossibility for God, so the antecedent must be false.
This shows God must be eternally creating.
4
Conclude with the necessary attribute
The conclusion is that God is an agent from eternity to eternity. Reflect on how this proof establishes God's eternal activity as a necessary consequence of His wisdom.

Commentary
This passage presents the first proof that God must be eternally active. It begins by noting that the argument draws from concepts of good (ḥusn), evil (qubḥ), wisdom (ḥikma), and futility (ʿabath). The proof proceeds: bringing the world into existence is an act of favor (iḥsān). Doing a favor is better than refraining from it. If God (al-Bārī) were not to create the world for an infinite duration, He would be omitting the better act for that whole period, which implies a preference for what is less good—impossible for God. Therefore, God must be an agent (fāʿil) from pre-eternity (al-azal) to everlasting (al-abad). The argument assumes that the better act must be performed by God, a premise often debated among kalām schools.

The Fifth Argument: Existence and Dependence
Presents the fifth argument that existence leads to dependence, which is inferior.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fifth rational argument against the claim that creation is an act of beneficence, which contrasts the states of existence and non-existence through the concepts of dependence and independence.

Core Idea
Existence brings dependence; non-existence brings independence—a divine attribute.

This lesson unpacks the fifth argument from the text, which directly challenges the idea that bringing creatures into existence is a kind of beneficence. The argument uses a comparison between existence and non-existence, focusing on the nature of need and independence. By examining this reasoning, we will see how the author defends divine transcendence from a rational perspective.

1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage once, then read the English translation. Pay attention to the key terms 'استغناء عن الشيء' and 'استغناء بالشيء'.
The argument is structured in a logical chain; notice the connective 'فإذا' and 'وإذا ثبت هذا'.
2
Identify the logical premises
Break down the argument step by step. Identify the initial premise (existence creates need), the comparison of states, the proof using divine attributes, and the final conclusion.
The argument uses a comparative judgment: independence is better than dependence.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
Consider why the author concludes that existence cannot be considered beneficence. What implications does this have for understanding God's actions?
This argument is part of a larger debate about divine goodness and creation.

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth argument against the claim that creation (existence) is an act of beneficence from God. The argument proceeds by comparing two states: existence and non-existence. When a person exists, he needs food, drink, etc. When God provides them, the person becomes dependent on these things (al-istighna bi-shay). If the person did not exist, he would have no needs and thus be independent of all things (al-istighna 'an al-shay). The argument asserts that independence from things is superior to dependence on things, because independence is a divine attribute, while dependence is a human attribute. Since God's attributes are more perfect, the state of independence is better. Therefore, bringing a person into existence results in losing that superior state (independence) and acquiring an inferior state (dependence). Hence, existence cannot genuinely be an act of beneficence, as it causes the creature to exchange a greater good for a lesser one. The author notes this proof leads to the conclusion that existence is not beneficence.

Necessity of Prior Mental Conception
Argument that distinction of essences cannot depend on external existence to avoid circularity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the distinction of essences must be known mentally prior to external existence to avoid logical circularity.

Core Idea
Logical circularity is avoided only if essences are distinguished in the mind before they exist externally.

This passage presents a foundational logical argument for the necessity of mental concepts. It shows that every judgment about something requires prior knowledge of its essence, and that this knowledge cannot depend on external existence without causing circularity. The argument is a classic proof in Islamic philosophy for the reality of mental existence.

The structure is: principle → example → conditional → conclusion.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
The conclusion is that circularity is impossible, so mental conception must be possible prior to existence. Ask yourself: why does this matter for proving mental existence?
This connects to broader discussions in Islamic philosophy about the reality of mental beings.

Commentary
This passage is a proof demonstrating the necessity of mental existence prior to external existence to avoid logical circularity. It begins by stating a foundational principle of logic: every judgment (tasdiq) is preceded by a conception (tasawwur). Then it applies this to a specific example: judging that blackness will exist requires first conceiving of blackness's essence, and this conception requires knowing that blackness is distinct from other essences. The argument is that if this distinction (imtiyāz) could only be obtained once blackness exists externally, then the conception would depend on external existence, but external existence itself requires conception (to know it as distinct), leading to circularity (dawr). The conclusion is that this circularity is impossible (muḥāl), so mental existence (conception) must be possible and prior.

Refutation of Distinction in Pure Negation
Refutation of an objection with two arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why pure negation cannot serve as a basis for distinction and the two rational arguments that refute this claim.

Core Idea
Pure negation has no essence, so it cannot be distinct from anything.

The opponent argues that distinction could exist even in pure negation. In this passage, the author refutes this by providing two arguments. The first shows that distinction requires a real essence, which pure negation lacks. The second points out that the known realities of the world are clearly not pure negations, making the objection irrelevant. Let's examine these arguments.

2
Understand the first argument
The first argument states that distinction requires a real essence different from others. Pure negation is not an essence at all, so it cannot be distinct.
The author defines distinction as being not like another essence.

Both arguments together show the impossibility of distinction in pure negation. The first is conceptual, the second is empirical/intuitive.
The author concludes that the objection is removed (فزال هذا السؤال).

Refutation of Non-Existent Essences as Things
Refutation with two arguments against non-existent essences as things.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the claim that non-existent essences are things is refuted through two arguments: the first distinguishes composite from simple quiddities, and the second proves that fixity in itself is nothing other than existence.

Core Idea
Non-existence cannot be a thing; fixity in itself is existence.

This passage tackles a key objection: why can't non-existent realities still be considered 'things'? The author offers two decisive arguments. The first distinguishes between composite and simple quiddities, showing the claim only applies to simples. The second proves that even if quiddities are fixed in themselves, that fixity is nothing other than existence.

1
Read the passage

This is the core of the refutation.

The Trilemma for the World's Origination
Trilemma for the origination of the world.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three categories that every originated thing must fall into and why only possibility is coherent.

Core Idea
Before origination, a thing must be either necessary, impossible, or possible.

The author begins the first proof for the world's contingency by positing a conditional: if the world is originated, then before its existence it must be one of three rational categories. This logical division sets the stage for proving that the world is possible in itself, not necessary or impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the passage begins with a conditional statement.
2
Identify the three categories
The passage presents three possible states for the world before its origination: necessary, impossible, or possible in itself. Find each term in the Arabic.
These correspond to wājib, mumtaniʿ, and mumkin.
3
Understand why the first two are rejected
The necessary option contradicts the world being originated, because the necessary cannot change. The impossible option would require an absurd transition. Understand the reasoning.
4
See how the third leads to further inquiry
The third option, possible, is left standing. This sets up the next part of the proof regarding the nature of possibility.

These are matters derived from the material cause concerning existence, namely that the world is possible in itself. The first proof: if the world were originated, then before its origination it must be either necessary in itself, or impossible in itself, or possible in itself. The first contradicts its being originated; the second implies that it transitioned from intrinsic impossibility to intrinsic possibility, which is impossible. The third implies that before its origination it was possible in itself. We say: this possibility is either a quality belonging to its specific essence, or it is said that this possibility means nothing other than that the Powerful (al-Qādir) is powerful to bring it into existence and create it. The second is false. Because the Powerful can bring into existence possible things, and cannot bring into existence impossible things. If there were no distinction between the possible and impossible by something (an attribute) belonging to it, then the Powerful's being powerful to bring the possible into existence would not be more appropriate than His being powerful to bring the impossible into existence. Thus it is established that the possible's being possible is an expression of a quality belonging to it.

Commentary
This passage presents the first proof for the world's contingency. The author states that the world is possible in itself (ممكن الوجود لذاته). The proof proceeds as a conditional: if the world were originated (حادث), then before its origination it must fall into one of three categories: necessary in itself (واجب لذاته), impossible in itself (ممتنع لذاته), or possible in itself (ممكن لذاته). The author then examines each: the first (necessary) would contradict its being originated, since the necessary cannot change. The second (impossible) would imply an absurd transition from intrinsic impossibility to possibility. The third (possible) is the only remaining option, and it implies that the world was possible in itself before its origination. Next, the author investigates the nature of this possibility (إمكان). Two interpretations are considered: either it is a real attribute (صفة) belonging to the world's specific essence, or it is merely a relation to an agent's power (meaning nothing but that the Powerful is capable of creating it). The second interpretation is rejected. The reason given is that if possibility were merely the agent's power, then there would be no distinction between the possible and the impossible. Since the agent cannot create the impossible, the distinction must reside in the thing itself. Therefore, possibility is a real attribute inhering in the possible essence.

Is Possibility a Real Attribute or a Relation?
Argument for possibility as an intrinsic attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why possibility must be a real attribute inherent in the possible thing, not merely the agent's power.

Core Idea
Possibility is a real quality that distinguishes the possible from the impossible.

After establishing that the world is possible in itself before creation, the author examines the nature of this possibility. Is it simply that God can create it, or is it a real attribute? The argument shows that without an intrinsic distinction, there would be no reason God could create the possible but not the impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Note the two interpretations of possibility being presented.
2
Recognise the two views on possibility
The author presents two possibilities: either possibility is a quality belonging to the thing itself, or it is merely the agent's power to create it.
3
Understand the refutation of the second view
The author argues that if possibility were just the agent's power, there would be no distinction between possible and impossible things, and the agent's power would be equally applicable to both.
This leads to an absurd conclusion.
4
Grasp the conclusion
The argument concludes that possibility must be a real attribute inhering in the possible thing itself, not merely a relation to an agent.

First Objection: Infinite Regress of Possibilities
First objection: infinite regress from possibility as attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first objection against possibility as an existing attribute: if possibility existed, it would require another possibility, leading to an infinite regress that is logically absurd.

Core Idea
If possibility were an attribute, it would need its own possibility, ad infinitum.

The first objection targets the core claim that possibility is a real, existing attribute. It argues that if possibility existed as an attribute of a possible thing, that attribute would itself be dependent on the possible, and thus would need its own possibility—setting off an endless chain. This lesson walks through that logical cascade.

Guided Path
1
Read the first objection passage
Read the Arabic text and its translation carefully. Notice the conditional structure: 'if possibility existed, it would be an attribute...'.
2
Trace the dependency chain
Identify how the attribute's dependence on its subject is used to derive a new possibility for the attribute itself.
The key move: what is dependent on the possible has its own possibility.
3
Understand the infinite regress
See how this leads to an endless sequence of possibilities, each requiring another. Recognize why this is deemed absurd (muhal) in kalam.
Infinite regress is a standard reductio ad absurdum argument.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
واعلم أن الكلام على هذه الحجة : أن يقال : لا نسلم أن الإمكان صفة موجودة . ويدل عليه وجوه : الأول : لو كان الإمكان موجوداً ، لكان صفة للمكن ، وصفة الشيء مفتقرة إليه ، والمفتقر إلى الممكن . فيلزم : أن يكون للإمكان ، إمكان آخر ، لا إلى نهاية . وهو محال . الثاني : وهو أن المحدث مسبوق بإمكان الوجود . فذلك الإمكان لو كان صفة موجودة ، لكان إما أن يكون قائماً به أو بغيره ، والأول محال . لأنه يلزم قيام الموجود بالمعدوم. والثاني محال لأن إمكان الشيء صفة قائمة به ، وصفة الشيء يجب أن تكون حاصلة فيه ، ويمتنع حصوله في غيره . والثالث : إن كون الممكن ممكناً ، سابق على كونه موجوداً ، لأنه ممكن لذاته ، وموجود بغيره . وما بالذات قبل ما بالغير. فاتصاف الماهية بالإمكان سابق على اتصافها بالوجود . فلو كان الإمكان صفة موجودة ، لزم أن يكون اتصافه بوجود غيره ، سابقاً على كونه في نفسه موجوداً . الرابع : إنه لا معنى للإمكان ، إلا قابليته للوجود . ولو كانت قابلية الوجود ، صفة موجودة ، لكانت قابلية تلك القابلية زائدة عليها . ولزم التسلسل . الخامس : إن تلك الهيولى ممكنة لذاتها، فيلزم افتقار ذاتها إلى هيولى أخرى . لا إلى نهاية. وهو محال . أجابوا عنه : بأن الافتقار إلى الهيونى إنما يحصل للمحدث ، وذلك لأنه لما كان محدثاً ، امتنع قيام إمكانه به . فلا بد من شيء آخر ، ليكون محلا لإمكانه . أما القديم فإن وجوده يكفي في أن يكون محلا لإمكانه ، فلا حاجة به إلى الهيولى
Know that the discussion of this argument is: It may be said: We do not accept that possibility is an existing attribute. Evidence for this is from several aspects: First: If possibility existed, it would be an attribute of the possible, and the attribute of a thing is dependent on it, and what is dependent on the possible (in itself) would then have its own possibility, leading to an infinite regress. That is absurd. Second: The originated thing is preceded by the possibility of existence. If that possibility were an existing attribute, it would either inhere in the originated thing or in something else. The first is absurd because it would entail an existent inhering in a non-existent. The second is absurd because the possibility of a thing is an attribute inhering in it, and the attribute of a thing must reside in it, and it cannot reside in something else. Third: The fact that the possible is possible is prior to its existence, because it is possible in itself and exists by another. What is by itself is prior to what is by another. So the description of the essence (mahiyya) with possibility is prior to its description with existence. If possibility were an existing attribute, it would entail that its description with the existence of another is prior to its own existence in itself. Fourth: Possibility means nothing other than its receptivity (qabiliyya) to existence. If receptivity to existence were an existing attribute, then there would be a receptivity of that receptivity additional to it, leading to infinite regress. Fifth: That hyle is possible in itself, so it would require another hyle for itself, ad infinitum, which is absurd. They answered: The need for hyle occurs only for the originated thing, because since it is originated, it is impossible for its possibility to inhere in it. So there must be something else to be a locus for its possibility. As for the eternal, its own existence suffices to be the locus for its possibility, so it has no need for hyle.
Source pages p. 172

Commentary
This passage is a series of five objections against the view that possibility (imkan) is an existing attribute, followed by a single answer from the proponents. The objections are: (1) If possibility existed, it would itself need a possibility, causing infinite regress. (2) The originated thing's possibility cannot inhere in itself (since it is non-existent) nor in another (since an attribute must inhere in its subject). (3) Possibility is prior to existence; if it were an existing attribute, the possible would have an existence (the attribute) before its own existence. (4) Possibility is mere receptivity; if receptivity were an existing attribute, it would need another receptivity, again infinite regress. (5) The hyle itself is possible, so it would need its own hyle, ad infinitum. The proponents answer that only originated things require an external locus for their possibility because they cannot sustain an attribute in non-existence. Eternal things, however, are self-subsistent and their own existence serves as the locus for their possibility, so no separate hyle is needed. This restricts the need for matter to contingent originated beings.

Second Objection: Inherence Impossible
Second objection: inherence dilemma for originated being's possibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second objection: the possibility of an originated being cannot inhere in itself (since it is non-existent) nor in another (since an attribute must inhere in its own subject).

Core Idea
Possibility cannot inhere in a non-existent originated thing, nor can it be transferred to another subject.

The second objection focuses on the originated being (al-muhdath) which comes into existence after not existing. Its possibility precedes its existence. The question is: where does that possibility inhere? If it were an attribute, it must have a subject. This lesson examines the two impossible alternatives.

Guided Path
1
Read the second objection passage
Read the Arabic and translation. Note the two alternatives: inherence in the originated thing itself or in something else.
2
Analyze the first alternative
Why can't possibility inhere in the originated thing itself? Because at the time of possibility, the originated thing is non-existent, and an existent attribute cannot inhere in a non-existent.
Inherence requires the subject to exist.
3
Analyze the second alternative
Why can't it inhere in something else? Because an attribute of a thing must be located in that thing, not another.
Subject-specificity of attributes.
4
Conclusion
Both alternatives are impossible, so possibility cannot be an existing attribute.

First Proof: Infinite Regress of Possibilities
First proof: infinite regress if possibility were an attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first proof that if possibility were an existing attribute, it would require a further possibility, leading to an impossible infinite regress.

Core Idea
An existing attribute of possibility would need its own possibility, ad infinitum.

The first proof attacks the notion of possibility as an existing attribute by showing it would necessitate an endless chain of further possibilities. This foundational argument establishes that possibility cannot be a real quality inhering in contingent things, but rather a relational concept.

Guided Path
1

2

The proof argues that if possibility were an attribute, it would itself need a subject, and that subject would require its own possibility, leading to an infinite chain.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.

In Islamic theology, an actual infinite chain of dependencies is considered impossible (muhal). This is a key principle in kalam.
This proof relies on the rejection of tasalsul (infinite regress).

Second Proof: Subsistence in Self or Another
Second proof: dilemma of subsistence for possibility attribute.

Learning Objective

The second proof examines where the supposed attribute of possibility could exist. It presents a dilemma: either it subsists in the thing itself or in another. Both options lead to contradictions, as the thing is not yet existent and the attribute must belong to its own subject.

Third Proof: Priority of Essence Over Existence
Third proof: priority of contingency by essence over existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third proof which uses the priority of essential contingency over existence to disprove possibility as an attribute.

Core Idea
Contingency is by essence and precedes existence; if an attribute, it would make existence-by-another prior to itself.

The third proof relies on the metaphysical priority of essence over existence. A contingent thing is possible by its own essence and existent by another cause. Since essence precedes existence, the qualification of possibility comes before the qualification of existence. If possibility were an existing attribute, then being qualified by existence (from another) would be prior to existing in itself, which is contradictory.

Understand the priority argument
Because the quiddity's being contingent is essential, it is prior to its receiving existence from an external cause. So possibility temporally (or ontologically) precedes existence.
This is a key principle in Islamic philosophy.

If possibility were an existing attribute, then the quiddity's being qualified by existence (from another) would be prior to its own existence in itself, which is impossible because a thing cannot be qualified by existence before it exists.
