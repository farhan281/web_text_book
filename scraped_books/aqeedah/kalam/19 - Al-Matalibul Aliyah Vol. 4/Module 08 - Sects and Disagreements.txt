In this lesson, we explore the first two of five positions on the world's origin. One view holds that the world is entirely created—matter and form alike—while the other claims it is entirely eternal. The latter is defended by Aristotle and his followers, who present a detailed account of celestial and elemental bodies and a philosophical argument linking contingency to an eternal cause.

The Pythagorean Proof: Units as First Matter
Proof that units are the first matter due to unity's independence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Pythagorean argument that the units are the first matter because unity is independent and prior to all essences.

Core Idea
What all things depend on is the first matter — and everything depends on unity.

Pythagoras and his followers held that the hyle of the world is not a body but the units — abstract units of oneness. In this passage, al-Razi presents their proof: every existent has a unity, but unity itself is independent of any essence. Therefore unity is prior and must be the first matter.

3
Understand the criterion for priority
The author uses the principle that the independent is prior to the dependent.
This is a standard kalam argument.
4

The conclusion is that the units are the first matter for all existents.

The fourth opinion of those who say hyle: that the hyle of this world is the units. And this opinion is attributed to Pythagoras and his sect. Its exposition: that no essence among the essences existing in this world is free from unity, for if everything that exists has a determination, and because of that determination it has a unity. Also: counted things have a unity as well, because ten is a single ten, and twenty is a single twenty, and similarly for all single and counted things. Commentary
This passage presents the fourth opinion on hyle, attributed to Pythagoras. It first gives a proof that the first matter is the units. The proof proceeds: every existent essence has a unique determination that gives it unity. Even collections like ten have a unity. So no existent can enter existence without the one. But the one itself is independent of all essences, as we can conceive the one without any essence. Therefore the one is prior and self-sufficient, and what is other than it is dependent. Thus the units are the first matter. Then the passage derives the physical world from unity: unity when pointed to becomes a point; two points make a line; two lines make a surface; two surfaces make a body. Then bodies with different numbers of faces become the four elements (fire, air, water, earth) and the celestial sphere. Mixing of elements produces the three kingdoms. This concludes the discussion of those who hold the world is eternal in matter and temporal in form.

Commentary
This passage is a classification (taqsim) of the possible positions regarding the cause or influence (mu'aththir) behind the corporeal world. The author begins by noting observable change in the world due to the sun's movement, then uses reason to consider unseen matters. He argues that there are exactly four possibilities: 1) no influence, 2) an influence that necessitates by essence (like a necessary cause), 3) an influence that acts by choice and cares for human interests, 4) an influence that acts by choice but does not care for interests. These four are exhaustive because the world either has an influence or not; if it has one, it is either necessitating or choosing; if choosing, it either considers or ignores creatures' welfare. The author then focuses on the first division (denial of any influence), stating that it is held by only a few corrupt groups. He subdivides it into three: (i) The Dahriyya (materialists) who hold that celestial bodies are necessary in themselves and generate elements by their necessary motion. (ii) The atomists like Democritus who believe spherical atoms combine by chance collisions to form the world. (iii) Those who allow absolute chance, claiming that events can occur without any cause, even celestial motions. These three all deny a genuine external influence. The passage ends with the beginning of the second division (affirming a necessitating influence). The author's purpose is to map out the landscape of false and true views before proceeding to the correct one.

Chapter One: The classification of schools according to the cause. Know: we observe with our eyes this elemental world, and we also observe that the states of this world change according to the changes of the sun in proximity to the zenith and distance from it. For by the movement of the sun the four seasons occur, and by their occurrence the states of this world change. This much is sensed. Then, upon this, the intellect ascends and considers states absent from sense, and that consideration is that it is said: This corporeal world either has no influence (mu'aththir), or it is said: it has an influence that is necessitating by essence (mujib bi al-dhat), or it is said: it has an influence that is an agent by choice (fa'il bi al-ikhtiyar) and does only that which agrees with the interests of people, or it is said: it has an influence that is a free agent (fa'il mukhtar) and does not care about the interests of people. These are four divisions, no more. Because this world either has no influence—that is the first division—or it has an influence, and that influence is either necessitating—that is the second division—or choosing, and that choosing either takes into account the interests of creation—that is the third division—or does not pay attention to them—that is the fourth division. This is the view of the people of knowledge (ahl al-ilm) regarding knowledge of the action and the agent, and there is no more beyond these four. As for the first division, which is the statement of those who deny any influence absolutely, I know of no group in the world that holds this view except a few. And these, despite the corruption of their statement and their small number, are three groups: The first group: those who said that these celestial spheres and planets are necessary of existence in themselves [and in their attributes], and that their specific natures necessitate their being circular in motion, and then their circular motion necessitated the generation of the elements, and also necessitated the mixing of some of those elements with others, until the three births (minerals, plants, animals) occurred. These are the pure materialists (dahriyya). The second group: those who said that these celestial spheres and elements are composed of spherical, solid parts that do not accept separation in existence, for they are moving by themselves from eternity to eternity, and it happened that in their different motions they collided in a specific manner, so this celestial sphere originated from those collisions, then from the motions of the sphere these different elements originated, then due to the motions of the sphere these four elements mixed, so the three births occurred. This is the statement of Democritus, among the ancients. The third group: those who allowed the occurrence of an event without any cause at all. They said: On this supposition, it is not unlikely that the celestial bodies are originated, but they originated without any influence, then they moved in a specific manner without any influence, then their motions became causes for the occurrence of events in this elemental world. And it is possible that the events occurring in this elemental world also occurred without any influence. So these three statements are derived from the statement of one who denies any influence absolutely. As for the second division: they are those who affirm the influence that necessitating by essence.

Commentary
This passage is a classification (taqsim) of the possible positions regarding the cause or influence (mu'aththir) behind the corporeal world. The author begins by noting observable change in the world due to the sun's movement, then uses reason to consider unseen matters. He argues that there are exactly four possibilities: 1) no influence, 2) an influence that necessitates by essence (like a necessary cause), 3) an influence that acts by choice and cares for human interests, 4) an influence that acts by choice but does not care for interests. These four are exhaustive because the world either has an influence or not; if it has one, it is either necessitating or choosing; if choosing, it either considers or ignores creatures' welfare. The author then focuses on the first division (denial of any influence), stating that it is held by only a few corrupt groups. He subdivides it into three: (i) The Dahriyya (materialists) who hold that celestial bodies are necessary in themselves and generate elements by their necessary motion. (ii) The atomists like Democritus who believe spherical atoms combine by chance collisions to form the world. (iii) Those who allow absolute chance, claiming that events can occur without any cause, even celestial motions. These three all deny a genuine external influence. The passage ends with the beginning of the second division (affirming a necessitating influence). The author's purpose is to map out the landscape of false and true views before proceeding to the correct one.

First Division Denied: The Atomists (Democritus)
Description of the second subgroup: the atomists led by Democritus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second subgroup of those who deny any cause: the atomists led by Democritus, who explain the world through the chance collision of eternal spherical atoms.

Core Idea
Democritus' atomism: the world arises from eternal, indestructible atoms colliding by chance.

The second subgroup within those who deny an external cause is the atomic theory of Democritus. According to this view, the universe is composed of tiny, solid, spherical parts (atoms) that are eternal and indivisible. They move by themselves from eternity, and by chance collisions in specific ways, they form celestial spheres, elements, and eventually all living beings.

Guided Path
1
Read the description of the second group
Read the Arabic text describing Democritus' view. Note the key elements: spherical atoms, their eternal motion, and the chance collisions that produce the cosmos.
This is an early form of atomism and mechanical philosophy.
2
Compare with the first group
How is this different from the pure materialists? Both deny an external cause, but the atomists invoke chance collisions rather than inherent necessity.
The Dahriyya believed in deterministic natures; Democritus introduces randomness.
3
Recognize the label
Al-Razi identifies this as the statement of Democritus (Dimaqratis) among the ancients. Understand that this is a historical reference.
This view was known in the Islamic world through translated Greek works.

First Division Denied: The Occasionalists (Pure Chance)
Description of the third subgroup: those who allow uncaused events.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third subgroup of those who deny any cause: the radical occasionalists who claim that events can occur without any cause at all, including the origin and motion of celestial bodies.

Core Idea
Radical occasionalism: events happen without any cause whatsoever.

The third and most extreme subgroup of those who deny an external influence goes beyond materialism and atomism. They assert that it is possible for events to occur without any cause at all—not even a natural one. According to this view, the celestial bodies could have originated without a cause, their motion could be uncaused, and all events in the elemental world could be absolutely spontaneous.

Guided Path
1
Read the description of the third group
Read the Arabic text. Note that they 'allow' (jawwazu) the occurrence of an event without any cause. They apply this to the origin of celestial bodies, their motion, and earthly events.
This is a radical denial of causality.
2
Compare with the previous two groups
The previous groups at least gave a mechanism (nature or chance collisions). This group gives no mechanism at all—sheer spontaneity.
They are even more extreme in denying any order or reason.
3
Understand al-Razi's summary
Al-Razi says these three statements are 'derived from' (mutasha''iba) the denial of any influence. They all share the rejection of a transcendent cause, but each differs in its internal explanation.
This sets the stage for refutation.

Commentary
This passage is a classification (taqsim) of the possible positions regarding the cause or influence (mu'aththir) behind the corporeal world. The author begins by noting observable change in the world due to the sun's movement, then uses reason to consider unseen matters. He argues that there are exactly four possibilities: 1) no influence, 2) an influence that necessitates by essence (like a necessary cause), 3) an influence that acts by choice and cares for human interests, 4) an influence that acts by choice but does not care for interests. These four are exhaustive because the world either has an influence or not; if it has one, it is either necessitating or choosing; if choosing, it either considers or ignores creatures' welfare. The author then focuses on the first division (denial of any influence), stating that it is held by only a few corrupt groups. He subdivides it into three: (i) The Dahriyya (materialists) who hold that celestial bodies are necessary in themselves and generate elements by their necessary motion. (ii) The atomists like Democritus who believe spherical atoms combine by chance collisions to form the world. (iii) Those who allow absolute chance, claiming that events can occur without any cause, even celestial motions. These three all deny a genuine external influence. The passage ends with the beginning of the second division (affirming a necessitating influence). The author's purpose is to map out the landscape of false and true views before proceeding to the correct one.

Three ancient sects all denied a real creator or cause, but for different reasons.

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration and description of three sects that deny any efficient cause for the universe. The author (Fakhr al-Din al-Razi) first states that only a few hold this erroneous view, and they are three groups. 

1. The first sect is the pure materialists (al-Dahriyya al-khalisa). This is an atomistic and mechanistic view.

They propose that the celestial bodies could have originated without an efficient cause, moved without a cause, and that their motions then produce events in the elemental world—also possibly without any efficient cause. This is the most extreme denial of causality. 

Thus, all three sects fundamentally deny that there is any truly efficient cause (mu'aththir) for the world, whether through necessity, chance, or absolute spontaneity.

Mu'tazilite View on Pains: Compensation and Benefit
The author explains the Mu'tazilite view that pains require compensation and a purpose to avoid futility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite argument that pains are justified by divine compensation and a beneficial purpose.

Core Idea
Pains are good when they come with compensation and purpose.

The Mu'tazila applied rational moral categories to God's actions. Observing pains in the world, they reasoned that without justification, these would be evil. This lesson explains their solution: God provides compensations and ensures a purpose to avoid both injustice and futility.

3

5

This is a summary of their position.

Mu'tazilite Argument for Compensation in Pain
Mu'tazilites argue compensation is necessary for pain.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite rational justification for why God must provide compensation (عوض) for all pains experienced by creatures.

Core Idea
Reason demands compensation for pain to preserve God's justice.

The Mu'tazilites, known for their rationalist approach, faced a theological problem: how can a merciful God allow pain? In this passage, they argue that since reason deems inflicting pain without cause as evil, God must have a reason, and the only acceptable reason is subsequent compensation.

Know that when the Mu'tazilites believed in the application of the judgment of the intellect's approval and disapproval in the actions of God and His rulings, they said: We observe the occurrence of these pains in the servants. The intellect has judged that causing them to the animal without cause is evil. This necessitates the impossibility of its coming from the merciful, wise deity. So their being good is either for a previous crime, as is the saying of the proponents of metempsychosis, and its falsehood has been established. So it must be that their being good is because the wise, merciful deity gives sufficient compensations that compensate for the aspects of their harms. Then the investigators among them said: Compensation is necessary to remove these harms from being injustice, and consideration is necessary to remove these harms from being frivolity. For if a man hired a man to drain the sea and pour it on the other side with a certain wage, and there was no benefit or interest in that work, that work would be removed from being injustice, but it would be frivolity, and its doer would be foolish. For this they said: These pains occur only through the combination of two things: compensation and consideration. If it is said: The animals that are extremely small, may be generated in the depths of the seas and the interiors of the earth, such that no one sees them, so how does consideration occur by causing pains to them? They answered: The angels, jinn, and devils witness them, and thereby attain various types of considerations in religion. Then the proponents of this view obligated upon God the resurrection of all fleas, bugs, lice, and worms, and the delivery of compensations to them. They claimed that if He failed to re-create any of them, He would become foolish and removed from divinity.

Commentary
This passage explains the Mu'tazilite position on the problem of pain and God's justice. The author begins by stating that the Mu'tazilites, believing in the rational judgment of good and evil in God's actions, observe the existence of pains in creatures. Reason deems inflicting pain without cause as evil, so it cannot come from a merciful God. They then consider possible justifications: either for previous sins (as in metempsychosis, which is false) or as a prelude to compensation. Thus, God must give adequate compensation (عوض) to make the harm just. The more thorough scholars among them add that compensation alone removes injustice, but consideration (اعتبار) is also needed to remove frivolity. They illustrate this with a parable: hiring someone to drain the sea pointlessly is not unjust if paid, but it is frivolous. Therefore, both compensation and consideration must be present. An objection arises: tiny animals in hidden places are not seen by humans, so how can consideration occur? They reply that angels, jinn, and devils witness them and gain religious insights. Finally, the Mu'tazilites go so far as to obligate God to resurrect all tiny creatures (fleas, etc.) and give them compensation, claiming that failure to do so would make God foolish and unworthy of divinity.

ثم إن القائلين بهذا القول ، أوجبوا على الله تعالى : حشر جميع البق والبراغيث والقمل والديدان ، وإيصال الأعواض إليها. وزعموا أنه لو أخل بإعادة شيء منها لصار سفيهاً ومعزولاً عن الإلهية
Know that when the Mu'tazilites believed in the application of the judgment of the intellect's approval and disapproval in the actions of God and His rulings, they said: We observe the occurrence of these pains in the servants. The intellect has judged that causing them to the animal without cause is evil. This necessitates the impossibility of its coming from the merciful, wise deity. So their being good is either for a previous crime, as is the saying of the proponents of metempsychosis, and its falsehood has been established. So it must be that their being good is because the wise, merciful deity gives sufficient compensations that compensate for the aspects of their harms. Then the investigators among them said: Compensation is necessary to remove these harms from being injustice, and consideration is necessary to remove these harms from being frivolity. For if a man hired a man to drain the sea and pour it on the other side with a certain wage, and there was no benefit or interest in that work, that work would be removed from being injustice, but it would be frivolity, and its doer would be foolish. For this they said: These pains occur only through the combination of two things: compensation and consideration. If it is said: The animals that are extremely small, may be generated in the depths of the seas and the interiors of the earth, such that no one sees them, so how does consideration occur by causing pains to them? They answered: The angels, jinn, and devils witness them, and thereby attain various types of considerations in religion. Then the proponents of this view obligated upon God the resurrection of all fleas, bugs, lice, and worms, and the delivery of compensations to them. They claimed that if He failed to re-create any of them, He would become foolish and removed from divinity.
Source pages p. 424

Commentary
This passage explains the Mu'tazilite position on the problem of pain and God's justice. The author begins by stating that the Mu'tazilites, believing in the rational judgment of good and evil in God's actions, observe the existence of pains in creatures. Reason deems inflicting pain without cause as evil, so it cannot come from a merciful God. They then consider possible justifications: either for previous sins (as in metempsychosis, which is false) or as a prelude to compensation. Thus, God must give adequate compensation (عوض) to make the harm just. The more thorough scholars among them add that compensation alone removes injustice, but consideration (اعتبار) is also needed to remove frivolity. They illustrate this with a parable: hiring someone to drain the sea pointlessly is not unjust if paid, but it is frivolous. Therefore, both compensation and consideration must be present. An objection arises: tiny animals in hidden places are not seen by humans, so how can consideration occur? They reply that angels, jinn, and devils witness them and gain religious insights. Finally, the Mu'tazilites go so far as to obligate God to resurrect all tiny creatures (fleas, etc.) and give them compensation, claiming that failure to do so would make God foolish and unworthy of divinity.

Refutation of Mu'tazilite View and Alternative Explanation
The author refutes the Mu'tazilite reasoning and cites a scholar's alternative view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the author's refutation of the Mu'tazilite position on animal compensation and the alternative explanation that compensation becomes like a new blessing after the animal forgets the harm.

Core Idea
When harm is forgotten, compensation becomes a fresh gift, not a delayed payment.

In this passage, the author directly refutes a Mu'tazilite argument that relies on rational ethics (tahseen al-'aql wa taqbeehuh). He then presents an alternative view from some investigators: if an animal suffers and later forgets after a long period, the compensation is like an initial blessing, disconnected from the past. This elegantly avoids the need for 'consideration' ('iwad) in the Mu'tazilite sense. Let's read the passage carefully to understand the critique and the proposed solution.

Commentary
This passage provides the author's refutation and an alternative explanation. The author first states that this Mu'tazilite position is a branch of their rational ethics (تحسين العقل وتقبيحه), and he asserts that the weakness and invalidity of that foundation is already known. Then he cites a more reasonable view from some investigators: if an animal suffers harm and is later resurrected after a long period (e.g., ten thousand years) having forgotten the pain entirely, then the compensation granted is like a new blessing, unconnected to the past. This avoids the need for consideration and the problematic obligation of resurrecting all tiny creatures. The author concludes that the Mu'tazilite discussion is lengthy, weak, and flimsy, so he chose to be brief.
