Third Proof: Motion Possible via Simple and Compound Analysis
Author proves motion is possible for all bodies via analysis of simple and compound.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the analysis of simple and compound bodies demonstrates that motion is possible for all bodies.

Core Idea
Identical sides of simples imply motion for contact change.

This passage presents a sophisticated proof from the mutakallimun that motion is possible for all bodies, even simple ones. By analyzing the structure of bodies into simple parts with identical sides, the author shows that change in contact requires motion, establishing motion's possibility. Let's follow the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Classify bodies as simple or compound
Read the first part of the passage where the author divides bodies into simple (بسيط) and compound (مركب). Understand that a compound body ultimately consists of simples.
This classification is foundational to the argument.
2
Focus on simple bodies and their sides
The author argues that every simple body must have two sides (right and left) that are identical in essence. If they differed, the body would be compound, contradicting its simplicity.
This step uses the principle of contradiction.
3
Apply the principle of equality
Since the sides are identical in essence, whatever is valid for one side must be valid for the other. In particular, if the right side can be in contact, the left side can also be in contact.
This step relies on the principle that identical things have identical properties.
4
Conclude motion is possible
Change in contact (e.g., from right side touch to left side touch) is possible, as we observe in everyday life. Such change requires motion of the parts. Therefore, motion is possible for all bodies, including simples.
This is the desired conclusion (المطلوب).

The third proof for the correctness of motion in all bodies: We say: The body is either simple or compound. If it is compound, every compound must have its composition analyzed down to simple things, so that compound must be composed of things each of which in itself is simple. Since they are identical, what is true of one side is true of the other. Contact with the right side implies contact with the left side is also possible. Change in contact requires motion. Since change in contact is possible (we observe it), motion of the parts must also be possible. Therefore, motion is possible for all bodies. The proof uses the principle that identical things have identical properties.

Fourth Proof: All Bodies Are Capable of Motion

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Both celestial and elemental bodies prove that all bodies are receptive to motion.

The author presents a fourth proof, described as 'ilzami' (compelling). It forces the opponent to accept the conclusion from premises they already hold. The argument divides bodies into two categories—celestial and elemental—and shows that each category entails motion capability. This passage demonstrates how a simple classification can yield a powerful theological argument.

Guided Path
1
Understand the argument structure
Read the passage and notice how it first divides bodies into celestial and elemental. Then examine how each type supports the conclusion that all bodies are movable.
This is a classic 'division and proof' structure.
2
Analyze the celestial case
The author says celestial bodies must be moving according to the philosophers. Reflect on why this premise is used: it relies on the opponent's own belief.
This makes the argument 'ilzami' (compelling).
3
Analyze the elemental case
For elemental bodies, the author points out that each part of an element can be moving. This is based on empirical observation.
Even if not always moving, the possibility is enough.
4
Synthesize the conclusion
Combine both premises: since both types are either necessarily or possibly moving, all bodies are receptive to motion. This conclusion challenges those who deny motion to bodies.

It explains that the soul receives influence from the divine realm and can affect matter. This classification is a foundational division in philosophical theology.

The Intoxicated Person Analogy: Soul's Love for the Body
Analogy comparing soul's love to intoxication cycle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the analogy of the intoxicated person and how it illustrates the soul's attachment to bodily pleasures despite moments of rational clarity.

Core Idea
The soul's love for the body is like a drunkard who momentarily sees truth but returns to intoxication.

In this passage, the author presents a vivid analogy to explain a subtle point about the human soul. By comparing the soul's attachment to bodily pleasures to a drunkard's cycle of intoxication and brief clarity, he makes the concept tangible. This analogy helps us understand why the soul repeatedly falls back into worldly desires despite moments of rational insight.

The bodily pleasures proceed in the manner of one who takes a huge cup of intoxicating drink and becomes like a raging, foolish, heedless drunkard. Commentary
This passage is an analogy. The author compares the human soul's love for bodily pleasures to a drunkard who occasionally regains consciousness but quickly falls back into intoxication. The bodily pleasures are like the drink that intoxicates the soul, causing it to forget its true nature. The 'light of reason' momentarily shows the soul the ugliness of these pleasures, but the soul soon becomes occupied with another pleasure and returns to its heedless state. The author concludes that this analogy explains the soul's love for the body, and reflection confirms it.
