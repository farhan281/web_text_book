Reason Needs Revelation for Preponderance
The author argues that reason alone cannot judge preponderance without a separate transmitted proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why unaided reason cannot decide between equally possible alternatives and why transmitted proof is necessary for such judgments.

Core Idea
Reason sees equality; only revelation can tip the scale.

In this passage, the author confronts a key question: can the human intellect determine which of two equally possible outcomes must occur? He argues that when reason perceives both sides as equal, it cannot prefer one without external evidence. This introduces the crucial role of transmitted proof (al-dalīl al-sam‘ī) in theological reasoning.

Commentary
This passage presents a logical argument about the limitations of reason. It begins by stating that it is impossible for reason alone to judge that one of two equally possible alternatives must occur. The reason is that when the intellect sees both sides as equal, any judgment about one side having preponderance (rujḥān) would require a factor that tips the balance. Since reason perceives only equality, it cannot determine which side preponderates unless there is an external proof. That proof is identified as transmitted evidence (al-dalīl al-sam‘ī) from revelation. Consequently, knowledge of the preponderance must come from revelation. If that were true, then whoever is ignorant of revelation would be ignorant of the preponderance. However, the author points out that this conclusion does not hold, because both atheists and monotheists are certain about the impossibility of certain things (e.g., a thing occurring without prior matter). This suggests that some rational certainties do not require revelation. The final sentence is ambiguous but seems to assert that both parties agree on the impossibility of some occurrence except from a prior material cause, implying that reason can indeed judge in some cases.

The True Criterion for Self-Evident Truths
The author refutes the claim that mutakallimun's opinions determine what is self-evident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the correct criterion for distinguishing self-evident truths from theoretical matters, and why the opinions of mutakallimūn in the heat of debate are not authoritative.

Core Idea
Sound rational nature, not polemical habit, is the true judge of self-evident truths.

In this passage, the author tackles a methodological objection that appeals to the authority of mutakallimūn in identifying self-evident premises. He argues that their judgments are unreliable due to their practice of accepting absurdities in debate. Instead, he proposes a criterion based on the innate human disposition (fiṭra) that has not been corrupted by sophistic argumentation.

Commentary
This passage is the author's refutation of a seventh objection which apparently claims that the mutakallimūn's judgments about what is self-evident should be authoritative. The author rejects this outright. He argues that the mutakallimūn, due to their habit of accepting absurdities in the heat of debate, cannot be trusted to distinguish self-evident truths from theoretical ones. He gives the example of the Jabriyya-Mu'tazila debate where the Mu'tazila, to avoid an undesirable implication, abandoned a self-evident premise. The author then lays down the correct criterion: the judgment of rational people who remain on the original fiṭra (sound innate disposition) and have not been corrupted by the practices of debate. He provides two examples: (1) a human appearing suddenly as an old man without parents or childhood, and (2) rivers appearing in a desert without prior source. All sound rational people would immediately reject these as impossible, proving that these are self-evident truths. Even the mutakallimūn themselves, when not engaged in debate, would agree. So the objection fails.

The Second Proof: Needs of Originated vs Persistent
The author presents the second proof comparing the needs of the originated and the persistent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second proof for istishab, comparing the factors required for the originated and the persistent to show the latter's preponderance.

Core Idea
The originated needs three factors; the persistent needs two—so the persistent is stronger.

The author continues his arguments for istishab by comparing the originated and the persistent. He shows that the originated requires more conditions for its existence than the persistent, making the latter more likely to remain. However, he also anticipates an objection about infinite regress.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof for the principle of istishab. It compares the needs of the originated (al-hadith) and the persistent (al-baqi). The originated requires three factors for its coming-to-be: (1) the coming-to-be of its own essence, (2) the coming-to-be of the time, and (3) the coming-to-be of its occurrence in that time. The persistent, however, does not require the coming-to-be of its essence—it already exists—but only requires two factors: (1) the coming-to-be of the time, and (2) the coming-to-be of its occurrence in that time. Since the originated depends on three things, it is outweighed (marjuh) relative to that which is content with two. Therefore, the persistent is preponderant. The objection raises a potential infinite regress: if origination depends on three things, each of those things are originated and would themselves depend on three things, ad infinitum. The text ends with 'كلام' (speech/argument), possibly introducing a response, but it is not given here.

Three Proofs for the Principle of Permanence
Three proofs that the original state is continuance, applied to bodies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three independent proofs establishing that the default assumption for any matter is its continuance, and that change requires a separate proof, especially as applied to bodies.

Core Idea
The default is permanence; change needs a proof.

In this passage, the author presents three compelling arguments from everyday experience and reason to show that our natural assumption is that things continue as they are. These proofs establish a foundational principle in theology and philosophy—the principle of permanence—which will then be applied to the question of the original state of physical bodies.

The third discourse: If the presumption of change were an effect of the presumption of permanence, then no one would understand anything from anyone. Because when the probability of the continuity of those expressions indicating previous subjects is equal to the probability of the disappearance of that indication, understanding does not rush to the side of continuity, but remains hesitant, and then it follows that no understanding occurs. But when understanding does occur, we know that it is only because the belief that things continue as they were is more preponderant than the belief that they have changed from what they were. The fourth: That whoever leaves a town and had seen some of its quarters inhabited and the other quarter ruined, then after leaving that town and being absent from it for a long time, he continues to assume that the quarter that was inhabited remains inhabited, and that the quarter he saw ruined remains ruined, and he does not turn away from this judgment except with an external proof. That indicates that innate reason judges that the original state of every matter is its continuance as it was. The fifth: That if he leaves a town, he writes to his relatives and friends, and that is only because the presumption of permanence is more preponderant than the presumption of change. Thus by these proofs it is established that the original state of every matter is its continuance as it was, and it is established that change may not be affirmed except by an external proof. If this is established, we say: The original state of these bodies, described with these specific compositions and specific attributes, is that they were nonexistent and then became existent in this manner. If a compelling external proof arises that they were nonexistent and then became existent, we judge accordingly. Otherwise, it is necessary to judge that they were in this state and attribute forever without change. The conclusion is that the affirmation of perpetuity is supported and strengthened by the rule of the original state. There is no need for an external proof for it. Only the affirmation of change and origination needs an external proof.

Commentary
This passage presents three independent proofs (the third, fourth, and fifth) that the default assumption (al-asl) for any matter is that it remains as it is (baqa'). The third proof argues from the fact that understanding occurs despite equal probabilities: if understanding were dependent on the presumption of change, it would never occur; but it does occur, so the presumption of permanence must be stronger. The fourth proof uses the example of a traveler who left a town: he naturally assumes the inhabited part remains inhabited and the ruined part remains ruined, showing that innate reason (badiha) judges permanence as the default. The fifth proof notes that when someone leaves a town, he writes to his relatives—an action based on the presumption that his relatives still exist, which is stronger than the presumption that they have changed. From these, the author concludes that the original state is permanence and that change requires a separate proof. He then applies this principle to physical bodies: the default is that bodies continue in their state unless a proof shows they were originated (hadith). The final conclusion is that the claim of perpetuity (dawam) is supported by the principle of the original state and needs no proof, while the claim of change and origination requires external proof.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical argument in which the author (Razi) first asserts that a body must be either moving or at rest, then immediately entertains an objection that not all bodies are necessarily extended (mutahayyiz). The objector cites a theory distinguishing between hyle (matter) and form: the substrate (hyle) is neither a volume nor extended, while volumetric extension (hajmiyya) is an attribute. Thus a body could exist without being in a space. Razi responds by granting ('sallamna') that body is extended when existing, but then attacks the notion of a specific space (hayyiz mu'ayyan). He analyzes space using a dichotomy: either space is pure non-existence or it is existent. If non-existence, a body cannot be 'in' non-existence. If existent, it must be either perceptible by sense or not. The perceptible case is subdivided: if space is independently perceptible, it is itself a body (jism), so saying a body is in space reduces to a body in a body, leading to absurdities of interpenetration or infinite regress of contact. If space is dependently perceptible, it is an accident, and a body cannot be contained by an accident because that would entail mutual containment. If space is existent but not perceptible, it is distinct from the body and cannot serve as a container. Thus the concept of a specific space as a container for body is refuted. The passage ends with 'This is the discussion on space.' The argument shows that the opponent cannot rely on a notion of space without clarifying its nature, and any coherent understanding leads to contradiction.

The first proof: Past movements are capable of increase and decrease. And everything that is like that is finite. Thus past movements are finite. As for the explanation of the first premise—our statement 'Past movements are capable of increase and decrease'—its elaboration is from ten aspects. First: Whenever Saturn completes one orbit, the Sun completes thirty orbits, so it is necessarily concluded that the number of the Sun's orbits is greater than the number of Saturn's orbits by thirty times. Second: There is no doubt that one year consists of twelve months, and one month consists of thirty days. Thus the number of days is greater than the number of months, and it is greater than the number of years. So it is established that past states have entered into increase and decrease. Third: If we take the day with its night as one thing, there is no doubt that it divides into night and day, so the number of the total is half the number of the total of the month and nights, and that is what is sought. Fourth: Whenever a day occurs, the past total is increased by one day, so the total obtained before this day is less than the total obtained after the occurrence of this day. And these increases and decreases occur perpetually. Fifth: If we take the past events from this present day to eternity as one total, and take them from the first time of the flood to eternity as another total, there is no doubt that the first total exceeds the second total by the amount that occurred from the time of the flood to this day. Then if we align these two totals from the side facing us, either the first side extends forever without any difference occurring, or the difference must appear. The first would require that the excess be equal to the deficit, which is impossible. The second requires that one of the two totals be deficient with respect to the other. Sixth: If we suppose that in every one of the past cycles something occurred and remained such that it never perishes at all, then on this supposition, if the past cycles were infinite, the sum of those things gathered in our time would be an infinite number. An example of this: According to the philosophers, in every one of the past cycles numbers of rational souls occurred. Then they said: Rational souls do not admit non-existence. On this supposition, it necessarily follows that the sum of the rational souls existing in this day is an infinite number. Then there is no doubt that this sum is capable of increase and decrease, because it is known necessarily that the number that existed at the time of the flood is less than the number that exists now.

Commentary
This passage presents the first of ten proofs that a body cannot be moving from eternity. The proof argues that past movements are finite because they are capable of increase and decrease. The author first states the proof: past movements admit increase and decrease; anything that admits increase and decrease is finite; therefore past movements are finite. Then he elaborates the first premise—that past movements admit increase and decrease—through ten aspects, of which six are given here. The first aspect compares the orbits of Saturn and the Sun: Saturn's slower orbit means the Sun completes more orbits, showing a definite numerical relationship, so past orbits are countable and thus finite. The second aspect uses the subdivisions of time (years, months, days) to show that past time units are related by increase. The third aspect shows that a day-and-night period divides into two parts, so the total number of such periods is half the total number of parts, implying a finite partition. The fourth aspect notes that each new day adds to the past total, so the total before was less, proving increase and decrease. The fifth aspect compares two intervals: from now to eternity and from the flood to eternity; if both were infinite, aligning them would either imply no difference or a difference; the first leads to an impossible equality of excess and deficit, so the second must hold, meaning one total is less than the other, proving finitude. The sixth aspect uses a hypothetical accumulation of imperishable entities per cycle; if cycles were infinite, the accumulated number would be infinite; but since the number at the flood is less than now, the total is capable of increase and decrease, contradicting the possibility of an infinite actual number. Each aspect reinforces that past movements are not merely conceptually finite but demonstrably subject to increase and decrease, which entails finitude.

Aspect 4: Daily Increment
Fourth aspect: daily addition to past totals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how each new day adds to the past total, proving that past events increase over time.

Core Idea
Every new day makes the past total larger than it was before.

The fourth aspect is straightforward: each passing day increases the total sum of past events. This obvious fact shows that the past is not fixed but grows, meaning it admits increase and decrease.

Guided Path
1
Read the short passage
Read the Arabic and translation. The argument: whenever a day occurs, the past total increases by one day. So the total before is less than after.
2
Recognize the logic
This is a direct observation: each new event adds to the count of past events. Therefore, the count changes over time.
3
Apply to infinity
If the past total were infinite, adding one more day would not change it (infinity + 1 = infinity), but we empirically experience increase, so the past must be finite.
4
Summarize the proof
This aspect shows concretely that past movements (days) are capable of increase, fulfilling the first premise.

Aspect 5: Comparing Two Intervals to Eternity
Fifth aspect: aligning two intervals to show finitude.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how comparing two intervals (from now to eternity and from the flood to eternity) demonstrates finitude through the impossibility of equal excess and deficit.

Core Idea
If two infinite intervals are aligned, either they are identical or one is shorter; both options imply finitude.

The fifth aspect uses a thought experiment: consider the total past events from today back to eternity, and from the time of the flood back to eternity. Aligning them from the present side reveals a logical contradiction unless the past is finite.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation. The passage describes two totals: A (from today to eternity) and B (from the flood to eternity).
2
Understand the alignment
Align the two totals from the present (our side). Either they extend equally without difference (meaning A and B are the same length) or there is a difference (one is shorter).
3
Analyze the first option
If they extend equally, then the excess (the period from flood to today) must be equal to the deficit (the same period, but on the other side), which is impossible because the excess is positive and the deficit is zero.
The text says 'the first would require that the excess be equal to the deficit, which is impossible.'
4
Conclude from the second option
If there is a difference, then one total is less than the other, meaning they are not both infinite (since infinite quantities cannot differ by a finite amount). Hence, the past must be finite.

Correction: The flood is used as a convenient marker; any finite interval in the past would work.

Checkpoint
What is impossible according to the first option when aligning the two totals?
A) The excess being equal to the deficit.
B) One total being shorter than the other.
C) The totals overlapping completely.
D) The existence of a flood.

Key Takeaway
Comparing two intervals to eternity leads to a contradiction unless the past is finite.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
If every chain of past events must have a starting point, what does that mean for your life? Are you living as if your own existence is an accident or a purposeful creation?
?
The argument shows that an infinite past leads to contradiction. Think of one area in your life where you act as if things will continue indefinitely—your health, relationships, or time to repent. How does the finitude of all things challenge you to change something this week?
?
You have just read that aligning two intervals to eternity forces a finite past. Does your daily schedule reflect a belief that your time is limited? What would you do differently if you truly felt that every moment is a finite gift from a Creator?
