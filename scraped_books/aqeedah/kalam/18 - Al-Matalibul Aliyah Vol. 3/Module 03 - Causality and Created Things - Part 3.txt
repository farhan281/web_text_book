The Agent Must Be a Free Chooser
Concludes the second proof against infinite regress and establishes the agent's free choice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the world's agent cannot be a necessary cause and must be a free chooser, based on the argument from particularization.

Core Idea
A non-physical necessary cause would necessitate uniformity; diversity proves choice.

This passage completes the second proof against infinite regress by showing that the cause of the world's diverse attributes cannot be a mindless necessary cause. Since a non-physical cause has equal relation to all bodies, if it necessitated, all bodies would receive the same attributes. But we observe specific attributes in specific bodies, so the cause must be a free agent that chooses.

Guided Path
1
Recall the four divisions of infinite regress
The author refers back to earlier divisions. Understand that infinite regress all at once or sequentially without first has been rejected. Now the fourth division is examined.
2
Follow the argument against a necessary cause
Read the paragraph where the author argues that if the determinant is non-physical, it must be either necessary or free. Then see why necessary is eliminated: equal relation to all bodies would produce uniform attributes.
Focus on the key terms 'qawābil' (recipients) and 'muʾaththir' (efficient cause).
3
Conclude that the agent is a free chooser
When the necessary cause is shown impossible, the remaining possibility is a free agent. The author concludes that the world's agent is fāʿil mukhtār. Also note the remark about the pivot of the whole argument.
This conclusion is a cornerstone of Islamic theology.

Commentary
This passage completes the second proof against infinite regress and establishes that the world's agent must be a free chooser. The author first summarizes the rejection of infinite regress (tasalsul) whether it occurs all at once or sequentially without a first. Then he addresses the fourth division: if the determinant (al-muqtaḍā) of the attributes in bodies is a body or corporeal, the same problem of particularization recurs. If it is neither body nor corporeal, then it is either a necessary cause (mūjib) or a free agent (mukhtār). It cannot be a necessary cause because a non-physical entity has no special relation of nearness or distance to any body; its relation to all bodies is uniform. Since all bodies are equally receptive, if the cause were necessitating, every body would equally receive the same attributes. But we observe specificity (e.g., fire burns but water does not), so the cause must be a free agent that chooses which body receives which attribute. The author concludes that the world's agent is a free agent (fāʿil mukhtār), not a self-necessitating cause. Finally, he notes that this proof and the preceding one rest on the rejection of an infinite series of events without a beginning (an early argument for the necessity of a first cause).

The Third Proof: Against the Agent Being a Necessary Cause
Presents the third proof that the world's agent cannot be a necessary cause and must be a free agent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the world's agent cannot be a necessary cause and must be a free agent, based on the impossibility of both one and multiple effects from a simple cause.

Core Idea
A necessary cause cannot produce the world's diversity without contradiction; only a free agent can.

This lesson presents the third proof from Al-Matalib al-Aliyah, which argues that the world's efficient cause cannot be a self-necessitating cause (mūjib bi-dhātih). Instead, it must be an agent by choice (fāʿil bi-l-ikhtiyār). The argument works by dividing the possibilities and showing each leads to absurdity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once and its translation. Notice the logical structure: division into two cases, then an objection and answer (though incomplete).
The argument is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the two divisions
The proof begins by dividing the possibilities: if the agent were a necessary cause, its effect would be either one or more than one. Both are shown impossible.
3
Analyze the first case: effect is one
Read why one effect leads to an infinite chain of causation among particles, which is false by necessity. Understand the argument that the effect of a simple cause must be one, then that effect would cause another, and so on.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
4
Analyze the second case: effect is more than one
Read the argument that a simple cause cannot produce two distinct effects. The author gives three scenarios: the concepts of causing each effect could be constitutive (making the cause composite) or external (leading to regress), etc.
This part uses technical terms like māhiyya and tasalsul.
5
Examine the objection and answer
Read the objection that the effect might be one, and the beginning of the answer. Note that the answer is incomplete in this excerpt, but it seems to deny that a single effect would lead to the infinite chain because the effect itself has aspects that prevent it.
The answer is cut off; we rely on the commentary for completion.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof that the world's agent cannot be a necessary cause (mūjib) but must be a free agent (mukhtār). The proof proceeds by dividing the possibilities: if the agent were a necessary cause, its effect would be either one or more than one. Both options are shown to be impossible. First, if the effect is one, then since the effect of a simple cause must be one, that effect itself would cause another one, and so on, leading to an infinite chain where every particle is causally linked to another. But we know by necessity that not every particle is the cause of another (e.g., two separate atoms are not causally related). Thus the effect cannot be one. Second, if the effect is more than one, the author invokes a philosophical consensus that a simple cause cannot produce two distinct effects directly. He then provides a logical argument: the concept of causing the first effect is different from the concept of causing the second. If these concepts are constitutive of the cause, the cause would be composite (contradicting its simplicity). If they are external to its essence, they are contingent attributes needing a cause, leading to an infinite regress. If one concept is internal and the other external, then the essence is composite (having parts). Therefore, a simple cause cannot have multiple effects. Since both possibilities fail, the agent cannot be a necessary cause; it must be a free agent. The passage then addresses an objection: why cannot we say the effect is one? The objection argues that if it is one, its effect would also be one, leading to an infinite chain. But the objection's answer (cut off) seems to deny that the effect of a single effect must be one, explaining that the effect itself has three aspects: possibility, existence, and necessity from the cause, which may prevent the chain. However, the text is incomplete.

Proof That a Simple Cause Cannot Produce Two Effects
The author proves that a simple essence cannot cause two distinct effects, concluding God acts by choice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the proof that a simple essence cannot cause two distinct effects and why this leads to affirming that God acts by choice.

Core Idea
A simple cause can only have one effect; otherwise it becomes composite.

This passage presents a rational argument from the philosophers that a truly simple being cannot be the cause of two different effects. The author uses this to prove that God, being absolutely simple, cannot be a necessary cause (which would necessitate a single effect) but must be a free agent. Let's walk through the proof step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the initial premise
The author begins with an obvious fact: one atom is not the cause of another atom. This sets the stage for the argument about causality and simplicity.
2
Grasp the philosophical consensus
The author notes that philosophers agree a simple cause cannot have more than one effect. This is a key premise for the proof.
3
Follow the three-case analysis
The proof examines three possibilities if a simple cause had two effects: (1) the concepts of the effects are constitutive of the cause, (2) they are external, (3) one internal and one external. Each leads to contradiction or infinite regress.
Focus on how each case undermines simplicity.
4
Conclude the impossibility
Since all cases are impossible, a simple cause cannot have two effects. Therefore, if God were a necessary cause, His effect would be either one or many, but both are impossible, so He cannot be a necessary cause.
5
Affirm free agency
The only remaining possibility is that God acts by choice (فاعل بالاختيار), not by necessity.
This conclusion aligns with Islamic theology.

Commentary
This passage is a proof by contradiction demonstrating that God cannot be a necessary cause (موجب بالذات), but must be a free agent (فاعل بالاختيار). The argument begins with a known premise: no single atom is the cause of another atom. Then the author states the philosophical consensus that a cause cannot have more than one effect. He then argues: if a simple essence were the cause of two effects, the concepts of those effects would either be part of the essence or external to it. If they are constitutive, the essence becomes composite, contradicting its simplicity. If they are external, they would be attributes that are possible in themselves and necessary by the essence, leading to a regress when analyzing how the essence relates to them. If one concept is internal and the other external, the essence becomes composite. Thus, a simple essence cannot be the cause of two effects. Consequently, if God were a necessary cause, His effect would be either one or more; both are impossible, so He cannot be a necessary cause. Hence, He must act by free will.

First Objection: A Necessary Cause Can Produce a Free Agent
Philosophers propose a two-step causal model to undermine the first proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' first objection: a necessary first cause could produce a free agent who then creates the world.

Core Idea
A necessary first cause can still be compatible with a world created by a free agent.

The philosophers begin their critique by granting the basic premises of the first proof—that the world is created and that an infinite regress is impossible. However, they propose an alternative model: a necessary first cause that necessarily produces a free agent, who then freely creates the world. This, they argue, shows that the first proof does not force the First Cause itself to be a free agent.

The first cause remains necessary; only the intermediate agent is free.
3
Understand the conclusion
They conclude that the first proof does not prove that the Necessary Existence (واجب الوجود) is itself a free agent. The proof only shows that the ultimate cause of the world is necessary.
This undermines the intended conclusion of the first proof.

Second Objection: The Problem of Temporal Particularization
Philosophers challenge the temporal particularization of the free agent's act.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' second objection: the free agent's choice of time for creation faces a similar difficulty as a necessary cause's temporal delay.

Core Idea
The free agent's temporal choice is as problematic as a necessary cause's temporal delay.

The philosophers raise a further objection: if a necessary cause cannot produce its effect at some times and not others (as is generally accepted), then why is the same not true for a free agent? They argue that the Muslim view—that a free agent creates the world at a specific time without an external particularizer—faces an analogous difficulty. This objection challenges the coherence of free agency if it is supposed to explain temporal origination.

Guided Path
1
Read the second objection
Read the Arabic passage. Notice it begins with 'ثم نقول' (then we say) introducing a new point.
The phrase 'وهو محال' in parentheses is likely the author's interjection.
2
Understand the question
The philosophers ask: why can't a necessary cause produce its effect at some times and not others? They acknowledge this is impossible for a necessary cause.
This is a standard Kalam position.
3
Grasp the analogy
They point out that the free agent's act of creation at a specific time (from eternity) also lacks an external particularizer. If that is acceptable, then the necessary cause's temporal delay should also be acceptable.
This is a tu quoque (you too) argument.

Third Objection: The Eternal Possibility of the World
Philosophers argue that eternal possibility of the world makes its eternity conceivable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' third objection: if the world's possibility is eternal, then its eternity is not impossible, undermining the proof of temporal origination.

Core Idea
Eternal possibility of the world implies its eternity is not impossible.

In their third and final objection, the philosophers target the notion that the world must be temporally originated. They argue about the possibility (صحة وجود) of the world: it either has a beginning or does not. They show that a beginning for possibility leads to an absurd change, so possibility must be eternal. If possibility is eternal, then the world's actual existence in pre-eternity is not impossible, and thus the proof that the world must be temporal collapses.

Guided Path
1
Read the third objection
Read the Arabic passage. Note that it is introduced as 'السؤال الثالث' (the third question).
This objection deals with the concept of intrinsic possibility (صحة ذاتية).
2
Understand the dichotomy
The philosophers present a dichotomy: the possibility of the world either has a beginning or does not. They then argue against the first option.
Follow the logical steps carefully.
3
Trace the refutation of a beginning
If possibility had a beginning, then before that beginning the world would be impossible in itself, which would then change to possible—an absurdity.
This is a standard Kalam argument against change in essential attributes.
4
Derive the conclusion
Therefore, possibility has no beginning (it is eternal). Thus the world could be eternal, and the proof that the world must be temporal is invalid.
The philosophers claim this undercuts the entire first proof.

Fourth Argument: Nature and Perfect Action
Philosophers' argument using nature and craftsmanship analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' argument that perfect actions can arise from non-conscious nature, and how this is used to challenge the necessity of knowledge in causation.

Core Idea
Perfect action does not require knowledge; even philosophers admit nature's actions surpass human craft.

This lesson examines the fourth argument in a theological debate, where philosophers are cited as claiming that nature, despite lacking consciousness, produces perfect animal bodies. They support this with an analogy from craftsmanship: a skilled artisan's work is said to be 'natural' to him. This argument challenges the assumption that perfect action must come from a knowing agent.
