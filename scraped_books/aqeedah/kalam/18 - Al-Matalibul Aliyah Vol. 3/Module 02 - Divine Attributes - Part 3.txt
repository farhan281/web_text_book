The Mutakallimun's Proof that God Knows All Things
States the mutakallimun's argument that God knows all things, based on three premises to be established.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical structure of the mutakallimun's proof for God's comprehensive knowledge and the role of the three premises.

Core Idea
God's essence necessitates knowledge of all things, given the possibility.

The mutakallimun (theologians) advanced a concise argument to show that God knows everything. This lesson presents their reasoning in summary form, which rests on three key premises to be established. Understanding this argument sets the stage for the detailed proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow of the argument. Then read the literal translation. Notice the key terms: 'al-mutakallimun', 'al-ma'lumat', 'al-mujib', 'al-sihha', 'al-wujud'.
The argument is stated very concisely; don't worry if it seems brief.
2
Identify the three logical components
The passage contains three elements: (1) the validity (sihha) of God knowing each infinite thing, (2) the cause (mujib) being His specific essence, (3) the necessity (wajaba) that results when validity and cause combine. Note that the author says these are three premises to be established.
The three premises are not fully explained here; they will be expanded later.
3
Understand the argument's flow
Recognize the logical flow: If it is possible for God to know each thing, and the reason for His knowledge is His essence (which is constant), then He must know all things. This is a deductive argument that requires the premises to be proven.
Without the premises, the argument is incomplete. The next lessons will establish them.

Commentary
This passage opens the third issue (mas'ala) and presents the argument of the mutakallimun (theologians) for God's comprehensive knowledge. The argument is stated in summary form: It is possible (ṣaḥḥa) for God to know each of the infinite known things. The cause (mūjib) for His being knowing is His specific essence (dhātuhu al-mukhṣṣaṣa). Since the possibility holds for all things and the cause is the same essence, He necessarily (wajaba) knows all things. The author then notes that this argument requires the establishment of three premises (muqaddimāt thalāth), which will be elaborated in the following text. The passage thus sets out the conclusion and the logical structure, leaving the detailed premises for subsequent discussion.

First Objection: Are the Terms Synonymous or Different?
First objection: dilemma between synonymy and difference

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first objection against the claim that the three terms do not imply multiplicity, specifically the argument that they are either synonymous or different, leading to an invalidation of the original claim.

Core Idea
The first objection forces a choice: either the terms are redundant or they indicate multiplicity.

This lesson examines the first of three objections raised against the view that the terms ‘aql, ‘aqil, and ma‘qul carry a single meaning. The objector begins by asserting that the three terms must be either synonymous or different, and then shows that neither option supports the original claim. We will follow the logic step by step.

Analyze the two possibilities

3
Understand why negative concepts are ruled out
The objector claims consciousness is positive, so negative is invalid.
This depends on an earlier proof not included here.
4
Conclude the implication
The terms are positive attributes of the essence, thus distinct and imply multiplicity.
This refutes the original claim.

One (1) and a speaker says: The objection to it is from aspects: First: The terms 'aql, 'aqil, and ma'qul are either synonymous or different. If the first, then there is no meaningful benefit in mentioning them; if the second, then they are either negative or positive concepts. The first is invalid because we have shown that the meaning of consciousness and perception is not a negative concept, so the second remains, and there is no doubt that they are not different concepts (2) but rather attributes of this essence (3), and then his statement that these three terms do not imply multiplicity in meaning becomes invalid. The second aspect: We have shown that the meaning of consciousness and perception is a relative, relational meaning, so it is impossible for it to be identical to that specific essence. The third aspect: Intellect ('aql) is derived from the occurrence of intellection (ta'aqqul), and its being intelligible to itself is derived from its being intelligent of itself, so these three concepts are derived from one another, so it is impossible for them to be a single concept. And God knows best.

Commentary
This passage presents three objections (wujuh) against an earlier claim that the terms 'aql, 'aqil, and ma'qul do not imply multiplicity. The first objection argues that the three terms are either synonymous or different. If synonymous, mentioning them is redundant. If different, they must be either negative or positive concepts. Since consciousness and perception are positive (not negative), they are attributes of the same essence, contradicting the claim that they do not indicate multiplicity. The second objection states that consciousness is a relational meaning, so it cannot be identical to the essence itself. The third objection shows that these concepts are derived from each other in a sequence (intellect, intellection, being intelligible), so they cannot be one concept. The objections serve to defend the multiplicity of attributes inherent in the essence.

Second Objection: Consciousness as a Relative Meaning
Second objection: consciousness is relational, so not identical to essence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second objection, which argues that consciousness is a relational concept and therefore cannot be identical to the essence.

Core Idea
If consciousness is relative, it cannot be identical to the essence itself.

The second objection shifts focus to the relational nature of consciousness and perception. The objector asserts that these concepts are relative (nisbī iḍāfī), meaning they inherently refer to something else. This relational character makes it impossible for them to be identical to the essence, which is absolute.

Third objection: sequential derivation prevents unity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third objection, which shows that the three concepts are derived from each other in a sequence, making it impossible for them to be a single concept.

The objector argues that intellect (‘aql) is derived from the act of intellection (ta‘aqqul), and being intelligible (ma‘qūl) is derived from being intelligent (‘āqil). This chain of dependency shows that the concepts are distinct and sequential, disproving their unity.

The First Premise: God's Knowledge of All Things is Possible
Sanusi presents the overall argument of the mutakallimun and then details the first premise with its proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first premise of the mutakallimun's argument that God knows all things, and the intuitive proof that establishes its possibility.

Core Idea
God's knowledge of all things is possible because He is living and powerful.

The mutakallimun argue that God knows all things by a three-premise argument. In this lesson, we focus on the first premise: that it is valid (sahh) for God to know every individual thing. The proof is surprisingly simple, relying on intuitive knowledge about life and power.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify structure
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the three-premise structure and how the first premise is presented.
The overall argument is stated first, then three premises are announced, then the first premise is detailed.
2
Focus on the first premise
Identify the first premise: that it is valid for God to know every one of the infinite known things. Understand that this is about possibility (sihha), not yet necessity.
The proof for this premise uses a logical chain starting from God being knowing and powerful.
3
Trace the proof chain
Follow the reasoning: God is powerful → every powerful being is living → every living being can know all things → this is intuitive → therefore it is not impossible for God to know all.
Each step is based on established or intuitive knowledge.
4
Reflect on the intuitive nature
Notice that the proof ends with 'knowledge of this is intuitive' (بديهي). Reflect on how the mutakallimun rely on self-evident truths.
This makes the argument accessible and foundational.

Therefore, whatever is not originated must not depend on an effecter. And God's omniscience is eternal, so its dependence on an effecter is impossible.

Commentary
This passage presents a kalam argument about divine attributes. It begins by stating a proof that God's knowledge cannot be other than His essence. The proof is that knowledge must be either a self-subsistent entity or an attribute inhering in a subject. It cannot be self-subsistent because knowledge is an attribute of the knower. So it must be an attribute dependent on the essence. Any dependent attribute is contingent and requires an effecter, which can only be the essence itself. The author then refutes this proof with three objections. The first objection: if the essence were the effecter of knowledge, then from the one God there would proceed both the world and knowledge, which violates the principle that from the One only one thing can proceed. The second objection: the essence would be both the subject and the effecter of knowledge, making it simultaneously receptive and active, which is impossible. The third objection: dependence on an effecter is only possible at the moment of origination, but since God's knowledge is eternal and not originated, it cannot depend on an effecter. Thus the proof that knowledge must be other than the essence is refuted.

As for the first group, they said: Indeed, the knowledge that a thing will happen is the very knowledge of its happening when it happens, so the knowledge remains and the change occurs in the known object. They argued for the correctness of their statement with aspects: First: He, exalted, knows all known things with one knowledge. Since the multiplicity of those known things does not necessitate multiplicity of knowledge, it must be that the change of the known things also does not necessitate change of knowledge. Second: Knowledge is an attribute by which known things are disclosed as they are. We mention for this meaning an example, so we say: If we suppose a mirror set up on a wall, then every person who passes by that mirror, his image is disclosed in it. If you understand this, we say: Knowledge is an attribute prepared to perceive what is presented to it, so that attribute remains in its state, and change occurs in the known things. The third aspect: They said: If we suppose that a person believes that Zayd will enter the house tomorrow, and we suppose that belief until Zayd entered the town, then when that is determined, he becomes a believer of Zayd being inside the town at this hour. The fourth: Knowledge is a real attribute subsisting in the essence of the knower, and its relation to the known is a relation between knowledge and the known. So when the known changes, the relation of that knowledge to the known has changed, and the essence of knowledge has not changed. This is the sum of the aspects held by those who say the first knowledge remains as it was.

Commentary
This passage presents four arguments (wujuh) from the group who hold that the first knowledge (al-ilm al-awwal) of a future event remains unchanged when the event occurs. The author first states their position: the knowledge that something will happen is identical to the knowledge of its happening when it happens; change occurs only in the known object, not in the knowledge itself. The passage concludes by summarizing these as the arguments of the group that asserts the first knowledge remains as it was.

The Fourth Argument: Relational Change
Fourth argument distinguishing essential vs. relational change in knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth argument that distinguishes between change in the essence of knowledge and change in its relation to objects, using the example of left-right orientation.

Core Idea
Knowledge itself does not change; only its relation to the known object changes, like a person's left-right orientation.

The fourth argument explains that knowledge is a real attribute with a relation to the known. When the known object changes, only the relation changes, not the knowledge itself. The analogy of a person who becomes left or right relative to another moving person illustrates this. This lesson clarifies the concept of relational vs. essential change.

Guided Path
1
Read the fourth argument passage
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice the distinction between the essence of knowledge (ذات العلم) and its relation (نسبة).
The example of the sitting person shifting left/right is key.
2
Understand the analogy
A person sits in one place; another sits to his right, then moves to his left. The first person remains unchanged, but his relation to the second changes from right to left.
3
Apply to knowledge
Knowledge is like the stationary person: its essence does not change, only its relation to the changing object.
This argument is philosophically sophisticated.

First Proof: Knowledge Changes When the Object Changes
First proof that knowledge changes when its object changes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that knowledge of a future event is not identical to knowledge of its present occurrence, and how new knowledge is generated from prior knowledge.

Core Idea
Future knowledge and present knowledge are distinct; new knowledge is generated, not just a change.

This passage presents a key proof from those who argue that knowledge changes when its object changes. The author uses a vivid scenario to show that believing something will happen is not the same as knowing it has happened. A new piece of knowledge is needed, and from the combination, a new knowledge is generated. This challenges assumptions about divine knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the literal translation to understand the scenario.
Notice the detailed description of the dark room setting.
2
Identify the hypothesis and scenario
The argument begins with a hypothetical: a person believes Zayd will enter tomorrow. He sits in a dark room unaware of daybreak. Zayd enters, but the person doesn't know it's tomorrow. Identify what the person knows and doesn't know.
3
Understand the inference
The author argues: if the original belief were identical to knowledge of present existence, then the person would automatically know Zayd has entered. Since he does not, the two knowledges are distinct.
This uses a conditional proof (if...then...).
4
Explore the generation of new knowledge
The author adds that if the person later learns that tomorrow has come, then from the two knowledges (future belief + awareness of present time) a third knowledge is generated: that Zayd has entered now.
This shows that knowledge can be generated from prior knowledge, not simply changed or abrogated.
5
Reflect on the broader implication
Consider how this argument relates to divine knowledge: does God's knowledge of the future change when events occur? This proof challenges the notion that God's knowledge is static with respect to temporal events.
The source notes a common misunderstanding: this argument is about created beings, but it has implications for divine attributes.

The Third Proof: Attributes Must Be Necessary Concomitants
The third proof using a tripartite classification of attributes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third proof that God's attributes must be necessary concomitants of His essence, and why any dependence on an external cause would contradict His necessary existence.

Core Idea
If an attribute depends on an external cause, God becomes contingent—an absurdity.

This lesson presents a powerful syllogism from the author's third proof. It examines three possibilities regarding the relationship between God's essence and any attribute. By showing that the first two options preserve immutability but the third leads to a contradiction, the argument demonstrates that all divine attributes are necessary consequences of the essence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the three cases
Read the passage and identify the three cases: essence sufficient for attribute, essence sufficient for negation, or neither. The first two imply eternality, the third implies dependence on an external cause.
Notice that the first two cases also prevent change in God.
2
Follow the logical chain for case three
Trace the argument: if the essence is not sufficient, the attribute depends on an external. Since the essence cannot be without either having or not having the attribute, the essence's state depends on that external. Therefore the attribute is contingent, making the essence contingent—a contradiction.
The phrase 'dependent on that which is dependent on something' reinforces the chain.
3
Appreciate the conclusion
The argument proves that attributes cannot be contingent; they must be necessary concomitants of the essence. This upholds divine immutability and necessity.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof (al-hujja al-thālitha) which is a sophisticated syllogism about the relationship between God's essence and His attributes. The argument classifies any attribute into three cases regarding the sufficiency of God's essence (al-dhāt). Case one: the essence is sufficient to necessitate the attribute; then the attribute must be eternal and unchanging, co-existent with the essence. Case two: the essence is sufficient to negate the attribute; then the negation is eternal, also preventing change. Change in God is thus impossible in both cases. Case three: the essence is neither sufficient to affirm nor negate the attribute. Then the attribute's affirmation or negation depends on an external factor (ghayr). But God's essence must always be either with or without that attribute. Since that state depends on an external, the attribute's existence or non-existence is contingent on that external. The author adds a logical chain: if A depends on B, and B depends on C, then A depends on C. Thus the attribute depends on something external. Anything that depends on an external is possible (mumkin) in itself. Therefore, God's essence, which is necessary in itself (wājib li-dhātihi), would become possible in itself—a contradiction. Hence the third case is impossible. Therefore, all attributes of God must be necessary concomitants of His essence, not originated. The author concludes that this argument is well-established and sound.
