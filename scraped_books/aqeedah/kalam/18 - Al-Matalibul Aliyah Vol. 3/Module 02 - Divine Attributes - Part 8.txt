The Impossibility of a Name Indicating the Essence of Allah
The author classifies names and argues against a name for the essence of Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why no name can directly indicate the essence of Allah, and how the classification of names informs Islamic theology.

Core Idea
The divine essence is unknown, so no name can indicate it directly.

In this passage, the author begins a new division of Allah's names. He classifies names into three types and argues that the first type—a name indicating the essence—is impossible for the Necessary Being. This is because Allah's essence is unique and unknown to creation, making it impossible to assign a name to an unknown meaning.

Chapter Seventeen [Original]. Division of the Names of Allah the Exalted. Know that the names of Allah the Exalted can be divided in several ways. The first division: that the name applied to a thing either has as its named thing the essence, or a part of the parts of the essence, or something external to the essence. Commentary
This passage begins a new section on the division of Allah's names. The author first classifies names in general into three kinds: those whose named thing is the essence itself, those whose named thing is a part of the essence, and those whose named thing is something external to the essence. The first type—the name that indicates the essence—is discussed here. The author notes that such a name is conceptually possible in a general sense, but then raises the question of whether it can be affirmed for the Necessary Being (wajib al-wujud). An objector argues that Allah's essence is a specific essence, distinct from all others, known by definitive proofs, but that essence in itself is unknown to creation. Since it is unknown, it is impossible to assign a name to it. The reasoning is that the purpose of a name is to fix a meaning between speaker and listener; both must know that meaning for the name to function. Because the specific reality of the Necessary Being (i.e., His essence) is unknown to any created being, placing a name for that reality is impossible. Thus, no name can directly indicate the divine essence itself; names only indicate attributes or actions.

Naming the Essence of Allah: Objection and Response
Sanusi classifies names and rebuts the objection to naming the essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first division of Allah's names and why naming His essence is rationally defensible despite its unknowability.

Core Idea
The essence of Allah, though unknown, can be named by divine revelation and spiritual benefit.

In this lesson, we examine a key debate in Islamic theology: can Allah's essence be named? The author first classifies names, then addresses a powerful objection that since the essence is unknown, naming is impossible. He offers a nuanced response showing the logical possibility and potential benefits of such a name.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
Read the opening lines where Sanusi states the first division of names: those indicating the essence, a part of the essence, or something external. Note the focus on the first category.
This classification is the foundation for the rest of the discussion.
2
Understand the objection
The objection argues that since Allah's essence is unknown to creation, setting a name for it is impossible because naming requires mutual knowledge of the meaning. Trace this argument carefully.
The objection seems strong but Sanusi will dismantle it.
3
Follow the multi-layered response
Sanusi offers several counterpoints: it is not proven that knowledge of the essence is impossible; even if impossible for humans, angels may know it; or Allah may reveal a name with spiritual benefits. Note each point.
The response does not claim we know the essence, but that naming is not categorically impossible.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Sanusi concludes that all these aspects are probable. Consider why he uses the term 'probable' and what it means for theological discourse.
This shows the rational flexibility in kalam.

Know that the names of Allah may be divided from aspects: The first division: the name applied to a thing is either that its named entity is the essence, or a part of the parts of the essence, or something external to the essence. As for the first division — and it is the name indicating the essence — this is reasonable in general. And is it rationally conceivable to affirm it with respect to the Necessary Being or not? For one may say: The essence of Allah is a specific essence, distinguished by itself and by its specificity from other essences, as has been established by conclusive proofs. And that specific essence, inasmuch as it is itself, is unknown to creation. And when it is not known, setting a name for it is impossible, because the purpose of the name is that two persons agree upon placing a specific word to define a specific meaning, so that when each of them utters that word, the other knows that the intended meaning of the speaker from that word is that meaning. According to this, setting a name is only conceivable when the meaning is known to the speaker and hearer. And since the specific reality belonging to the Necessary Being is unknown to any of creation, setting a name for that specific reality is impossible. The ancient philosophers expressed this meaning, saying: It is a reality that the intellects of creation do not perceive, so it has no name, except that we explain the reality as being necessary of existence by itself. And an answerer may answer saying: What is established for us is that we do not know that specific reality. But that it is rationally impossible to know it — that has never been established by proof. Also: suppose that human intellects are prevented from this knowledge — it has not been established that the holy angelic spirits are prevented from it. Also: suppose that human intellects and angelic spirits are prevented from attaining this gnosis — it is not improbable that that specific reality has a specific name, and then He, exalted, honors some of His servants by defining that name. And it is not improbable that mentioning that name is a cause for the purified holy spirits to submit to its mention. Also it is not improbable that its rememberer attains a strength in spirit that is not attained except through that path. All these aspects are possible.

Commentary
This passage is a classificatory and argumentative discussion about the first division of the names of Allah. The author begins by stating that the names of Allah can be divided in various ways, and the first division is based on what the name signifies: either the essence itself, a part of the essence, or something external to the essence. He then focuses on the first type—names that indicate the essence. He says that in general it is reasonable, but then raises an objection: someone might argue that since Allah's essence is unknown to creation (being unique and indemonstrable), it is impossible to assign a name to it, because naming requires mutual knowledge of the meaning. The objection further notes that the ancient philosophers said that reality has no name because it is imperceptible. The author then provides a response: while we admit we do not know the specific reality, it has not been proven that it is intellectually impossible to know it. Even if impossible for humans, Allah might grant knowledge to some servants. Moreover, angelic spirits might know it. Even if all created beings cannot know it, Allah might still have a specific name for that reality and reveal it to some, and such a name could have spiritual benefits. The author concludes that all these possibilities are plausible. The passage thus defends the possibility of a name for Allah's essence against rational objections.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Names signifying parts of essence are impossible for Allah; names for external attributes fall into seven logical types.

Sanusi continues his classification of divine names by first addressing a potential confusion: names that seem to indicate a part of Allah's essence are impossible because He is one and free from composition. He then turns to names that signify attributes external to the essence, and provides a comprehensive taxonomy of seven types. This structure is foundational for understanding the logic behind Allah's names in the Maturidi tradition.

Commentary

Al-Kibriya: The Divine Pride Beyond Creation's Reach
Author explains al-Kibriya via sacred hadith and contrasts with al-Adhamah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the divine name al-Kibriya and how it signifies God's absolute pride beyond human comprehension.

Core Idea
God's pride is His exclusive cloak, beyond all creation.

This lesson focuses on the divine name al-Kibriya (Pride). The author cites a sacred hadith that links pride to God's cloak and grandeur to His robe, indicating that pride is even higher than grandeur. This teaches us about God's supreme transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage about al-Kibriya
Read the second part of the source passage, starting with 'وأما الكبرياء'. Pay attention to the hadith and the comparison.
2
Understand the symbolism of cloak and robe
The Prophet said: 'Pride is My cloak, and Grandeur is My robe.' Why do you think these specific garments were chosen?
A cloak is worn over other clothing, symbolizing externality and supremacy.
3
Grasp why kibriya is higher than adhamah
The author says that specifying kibriya as the cloak and adhamah as the robe indicates kibriya is higher in station and further from created understanding.
This shows the intensity of God's transcendence.

Source Text

وأما الأكبر فقيه وجهان : الأول : أنه أكبر من كل ما سواه من الموجودات ، ويحتمل أن يكون قول المصلي : الله أكبر هذا . كأنه يقول : الله أكبر من كل ما سواه. وإنما قدم هذا القول أمام أفعال الصلاة ، لأن المصلي إذا عرف هذا المعنى قبل الشروع في الصلاة لم يتعلق خاطره بشيء سوى الله ، ولم يتعلق قلبه بغير الله . وأما الكبرياء فقال - عليه السلام - حاكياً عن الله : ( الكبرياء ردائي ، والعظمة إزاري ، وفي تخصيص لفظ الكبرياء بالرداء ، والعظمة بالإزار ما يدل على أن الكبرياء أعلى شأناً من العظمة ، وأبعد عن أوهام الخلق وأفهامهم .
As for al-Akbar, it has two aspects: the first is that He is greater than every existent other than Him, and it is possible that the saying of the worshipper 'Allahu Akbar' is this. It is as if he says: 'God is greater than everything other than Him.' And He only placed this saying at the beginning of the actions of prayer because when the worshipper knows this meaning before beginning the prayer, his mind does not become attached to anything other than God, nor his heart to anything other than God. And as for al-Kibriya, the Prophet – peace be upon him – said, relating from God: 'Pride is My cloak, and Grandeur is My robe.' And in the specification of the word al-Kibriya as the cloak, and al-ʿAdhamah as the robe, there is an indication that al-Kibriya is higher in station than al-ʿAdhamah, and further from the imaginations and understandings of creatures.

Commentary
This passage examines two more divine names: al-Akbar and al-Kibriya. For al-Akbar, the author gives two aspects: first, that God is akbar (greater) than all other existents, and second, that the phrase 'Allahu Akbar' used in prayer is understood as a declaration that God is greater than everything. The placement of this phrase at the beginning of prayer is explained: it purifies the worshipper's intention so that nothing else occupies the heart. Then the author discusses al-Kibriya (Pride). He cites the sacred hadith: 'Pride is My cloak, and Grandeur is My robe.' He notes that the specification of kibriya as the cloak and adhamah as the robe indicates that kibriya is even higher in station than adhamah, and more beyond the understanding of creatures. Thus, both names affirm God's absolute supremacy and transcendence.

?

?
The Prophet ﷺ said that pride is God's cloak. Have you ever tried to wear that cloak by looking down on others? Write down one situation where you did, and resolve to apologize or make amends.

The Two Meanings of al-Kabir (The Great)
The author explains the two meanings of the divine name al-Kabir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two aspects of the divine name al-Kabir and how it emphasizes God's transcendence over any resemblance to creation.

Core Idea
God's greatness is not physical but in His perfections and transcendence.

This passage from al-Matalib al-Aliyah explores the divine name al-Kabir (the Great). The author distinguishes between physical greatness and the greatness of intellectual and moral perfections. By understanding these two interpretations, we see that al-Kabir is a name affirming God's incomparability and supreme perfection.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice the structure: the author first discusses the general meaning of 'great' and 'small', then applies it to God.
Pay attention to the two 'aspects' (وجهان) mentioned at the beginning and again later.
2
Distinguish physical vs. intellectual greatness
The author clarifies that 'great' can refer to physical size, but God is beyond that. The relevant meaning is in degrees of intellectual perfections and rank.
This distinction is crucial for understanding God's greatness correctly.
3
Examine the two aspects for God
The first aspect: God is the most perfect being, so He is great relative to all others. The second: He is too great to resemble creatures. Both are about transcendence.
The second aspect directly refutes anthropomorphism.
4
Recognize al-Kabir as a name of tanzih
Both interpretations lead to the conclusion that al-Kabir is a name of transcendence (tanzih), affirming that God is free from any similarity to creation.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
ونقول : أما الكبير ففيه وجهان: أحدهما : أنه الذي يقع في مقابلة الصغير، وقد يعتبر الكبير والصغير في المقادير . والحق مقدس عن المقدار والحجمية فلا يكون كبره بحسب الجثة والحجمية. وقد يعتبر الكبر والصغر في درجات الكمالات العقلية فيقال : فلان كبير القوم ، وإن كان أصغرهم في الجثة ، ويقال فلان كبير في الدين . أي أن درجته عالية إذا عرفت هذا فنقول : في تفسير الكبير بهذا المعنى في حق الله وجهان : الأول : أنه ثبت أن الحق - سبحانه - أكمل الموجودات وأشرفها وأجلها وأعلاها ، فيكون ـ سبحانه - كبيراً بالقياس إلى كل ما سواه ، وكل ما يغايره فهو صغير بالقياس إليه . والثاني : أنه كبير بمعنى أنه كبر عن مشابهة المخلوقات. وعلى هذين الوجهين فيكون الكبير من أسماء التنزيه .
And we say: As for al-Kabir (the Great), it has two aspects: One of them: that it is what falls in contrast to al-saghir (the small), and great and small may be considered in magnitudes. And al-Haqq (the Truth, God) is sanctified above magnitude and corporeality, so His greatness is not according to body and corporeality. And greatness and smallness may be considered in degrees of intellectual perfections; it is said: So-and-so is great (kabir) of the people, even if he is the smallest in body, and it is said: So-and-so is great in religion, meaning his rank is high. If you have understood this, we say: In interpreting al-Kabir in this sense regarding God, there are two aspects: The first: that it is established that al-Haqq (God) is the most perfect of existents, the most noble, the most majestic, and the highest, so He is great in relation to everything other than Him, and everything other than Him is small in relation to Him. The second: that He is great in the sense that He is too great to resemble creatures. And according to these two aspects, al-Kabir is among the names of tanzih (transcendence).
Source pages p. 256

Commentary
This passage defines the divine name al-Kabir and explains its two interpretations. The author first notes the general usage of 'great' and 'small' in physical magnitudes, but clarifies that God is transcendent above physical dimensions, so His greatness is not bodily. Then he offers two meanings of 'great' in intellectual and moral ranks. Applying this to God, he presents two aspects: first, God is the most perfect and highest in comparison to all else; second, God is far above resembling creatures. Both interpretations make al-Kabir a name of transcendence, affirming God's incomparability.

Key Terms

الصغير
التنزيه
الكمالات العقلية

Key Terms from Source Passage

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: God's greatness is like physical bigness or size.
Correction: The passage clarifies that God is sanctified above magnitude and corporeality (مقدس عن المقدار والحجمية), so His greatness is not bodily. Instead, it is in perfections and in being too great to resemble creatures.

Checkpoint

B) God is great in the sense of being too great to resemble creatures.
C) God is great because He has a large body.
D) God is great only in comparison to other gods.

Key Takeaway
Al-Kabir is a name of transcendence, affirming God's supreme perfection and incomparability with creation.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?

Which aspect is more alive in your heart during prayer? Challenge yourself to mean both every time you say it.
?

?
The passage says God is too great to resemble any creature. Do you ever fall into the trap of imagining God's love, mercy, or power as merely amplified human versions? How can this understanding of al-Kabir reshape your relationship with Him?

The Meaning of al-Akbar and the Wisdom of Takbir
Author explains al-Akbar and the placement of takbir in prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first aspect of the divine name al-Akbar and why the takbir is recited at the start of prayer.

Core Idea
Allahu Akbar means God is greater than all else, freeing the heart from attachment.

The author explains one of the key names of God: al-Akbar (the Greater). He then connects this name to the opening phrase of Muslim prayer, 'Allahu Akbar', showing how understanding its meaning prepares the worshiper to focus entirely on God.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic text and translation to get the flow.
Note the two aspects mentioned, though only the first is detailed.
2
Understand the first aspect
The first aspect is that God is greater than everything else. This is the core meaning of al-Akbar.
3
Grasp the wisdom of takbir
Understanding that God is greater than all prevents the heart from attaching to anything else during prayer. That is why takbir is the first act.
The author uses 'tacalluq' (attachment) to explain the psychological effect.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
وأما الأكبر ففيه وجهان : الأول : أنه أكبر من كل ما سواه من الموجودات ، ويحتمل أن يكون قول المصلي : الله أكبر هذا . كأنه يقول : الله أكبر من كل ما سواه. وإنما قدم هذا القول أمام أفعال الصلاة ، لأن المصلي إذا عرف هذا المعنى قبل الشروع في الصلاة لم يتعلق خاطره بشيء سوى الله ، ولم يتعلق قلبه بغير الله
And as for al-Akbar (the Greater), it has two aspects: The first: that He is greater than everything other than Him among existents, and it is probable that the saying of the worshiper 'Allahu Akbar' is this; as if he says: 'God is greater than everything other than Him.' And this saying is only placed at the beginning of the actions of prayer, because when the worshiper knows this meaning before embarking on prayer, his mind does not attach to anything besides God, and his heart does not cling to other than God.
Source pages p. 256

Commentary
This passage explains the name al-Akbar (the Greater). The first aspect is that God is greater than all other existents. The author then relates this to the takbir (saying 'Allahu Akbar') in prayer: the worshiper means that God is greater than everything else. This is why the takbir is placed at the start of the prayer actions: when the worshiper understands this meaning before beginning, his mind and heart become focused solely on God, detached from anything else.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: 'Allahu Akbar' means 'God is big' in a non-comparative sense.
Correction: The passage explains that it means 'God is greater than everything else.'

The Hierarchy of Divine Names: al-Kibriya' and al-Azama
The author explains the name al-Kibriya' based on a hadith qudsi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a prophetic hadith distinguishes between the divine names al-Kibriya' and al-Azama, showing that al-Kibriya' denotes a higher degree of transcendence.

Core Idea
The cloak of al-Kibriya' outranks the waist-wrapper of al-Azama.

This lesson delves into a beautiful hadith qudsi where Allah Himself describes His attributes of majesty and pride. The choice of clothing imagery — cloak and waist-wrapper — is not arbitrary; it teaches us a profound hierarchy among divine names. We will see how the author uses this to assert that al-Kibriya' is more remote from human comprehension than al-Azama.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith and translation
Focus on the exact wording and the imagery of the cloak and waist-wrapper.
Notice that the hadith is narrated by the Prophet (peace be upon him) from Allah.
2
Understand the symbolism
Why a cloak and a waist-wrapper? What do they signify about rank? The cloak is worn over the waist-wrapper, implying higher status.
This symbolism is key to the author's argument.
3
Grasp the conclusion
How does the specification of these garments indicate that al-Kibriya' is higher in rank and further from human understanding?
The author explicitly states that al-Kibriya' is أعلى شأناً (higher in rank) and أبعد عن أوهام الخلق (further from imaginations).

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
وأما الكبرياء فقال - عليه السلام - حاكياً عن الله : ( الكبرياء ردائي ، والعظمة إزاري ، وفي تخصيص لفظ الكبرياء بالرداء ، والعظمة بالإزار ما يدل على أن الكبرياء أعلى شأناً من العظمة ، وأبعد عن أوهام الخلق وأفهامهم .
And as for al-Kibriya' (pride, magnificence), he (peace be upon him) said, narrating from God: 'Al-Kibriya' is My cloak, and al-Azama (majesty) is My waist-wrapper.' And in the specification of the word al-Kibriya' with the cloak, and al-Azama with the waist-wrapper, there is an indication that al-Kibriya' is higher in rank than al-Azama, and further from the imaginations and understandings of creatures.
Source pages p. 256

Commentary
This passage discusses the divine name al-Kibriya' based on a hadith qudsi where God says: 'Al-Kibriya' is My cloak, and al-Azama is My waist-wrapper.' The author notes that the choice of clothing items indicates that al-Kibriya' is more exalted than al-Azama, as the cloak (rida') is worn over the waist-wrapper (izar), symbolizing a higher rank. Thus al-Kibriya' is more transcendent and further from human comprehension.

Correction: The hadith and its analysis show that al-Kibriya' is higher and more transcendent than al-Azama.

?
The hadith places the cloak over the waist-wrapper, symbolizing a hierarchy. Do you ever treat Allah's attributes as if they are all equal, or as if you fully grasp them? How would your worship differ if you constantly reminded yourself that al-Kibriya' is far beyond your imagination?
?
