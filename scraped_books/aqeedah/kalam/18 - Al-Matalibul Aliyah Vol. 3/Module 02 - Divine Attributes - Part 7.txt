The Fourth Proof: Attributes as Relations
Author presents fourth proof for distinction between attributes and essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the relational nature of attributes proves their distinction from the divine essence.

Core Idea
Attributes are relations, not the essence itself.

In this proof, the author argues that attributes like knowledge are relations (nisab) between the essence and the object. Since relations depend on the essence and are not self-subsistent, they must be distinct from the essence. This passage lays out this concise yet powerful argument.

Identify the key premise

3
Follow the conclusion
Because attributes are relational and dependent, while the essence is self-subsistent, they must be distinct. The proof ends with 'All of this necessitates distinction.'
This is the main point of the proof.

Commentary
This passage is a proof (ḥujjah) for the distinction between God's attributes and His essence. The author argues that when we say God is knowing, we refer to a relation (nisbah) called knowledge between the knower and the known. This relation is an attribute (ṣifah) of the essence (dhāt), and it depends on the essence. Relations and additions (iḍāfāt) can be predicated of other things, but self-subsistent essences (dhawāt qāʾimah bi-anfusihā) are not like that—they are not mere relations. Therefore, the attribute (the relation) must be distinct from the essence. The rhetorical question 'How can we not say that?' emphasizes the clarity of the conclusion.

The Fifth Proof: Conceptual Distinction Between Power and Knowledge
Author presents fifth proof distinguishing 'powerful' and 'knowing' conceptually.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fifth proof that the divine attributes 'power' and 'knowledge' are conceptually distinct from each other and from the essence, and why this implies their real distinction from the essence.

Core Idea
If two concepts have different definitions, they cannot both be identical to the same essence.

This lesson presents the fifth proof from the text, which argues that because the concepts of 'being powerful' and 'being knowing' are different in their quiddity and definition, they cannot be identical to the divine essence. The author uses a simple thought experiment to show that equating each attribute with the essence would lead to a contradiction. By the end, you will see how this proof establishes that the attributes are really distinct from the essence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that it begins by stating the conceptual difference between being powerful and being knowing.
The passage is concise; pay attention to the logical flow.

Understand the reasoning

3
Examine the contradiction
The author argues that if both concepts were identical to the essence, the single essence would have to be two distinct things simultaneously, which is impossible.
This contradiction reveals that the attributes cannot be identical to the essence.
4
Apply the conclusion
Recognize that this proof implies the attributes are really distinct from the essence, not just conceptually. This is a key point in kalām.
The proof is part of a larger discussion on divine attributes.

Commentary
This passage is the fifth proof (al-ḥujjah al-khāmisah) for the distinction between attributes and essence. It relies on the conceptual difference between being powerful (qādir) and being knowing (ʿālim). The author notes that we can conceive of a powerful being that is not knowing, and a knowing being that is not powerful; their quiddities (māhiyyāt) and definitions (ḥudūd) are different. If both 'powerful' and 'knowing' were identical to the essence (ʿayn al-dhāt), then the one essence would have to be two distinct things (shayʾayn mutaghāyirayn) simultaneously, which is impossible. Thus, the concepts must be distinct, and since they are real attributes of God, they must be distinct from the essence as well.

The Sixth Proof: Distinction Between Knowledge and Power
Author presents sixth proof by conditional argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the sixth proof for the distinction between divine knowledge and power, based on the objects each attribute relates to.

Core Idea
Knowing something does not mean having power over it.

We now examine the sixth proof (al-ḥujjah al-sādisah), which uses a conditional argument to show that knowledge and power cannot be identical. The author first states the conditional premise, then draws an implication, and finally shows that implication is false by appealing to rational consensus about the Necessary and Impossible. This forces the conclusion that the two concepts—and hence the two attributes—are distinct.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Pay attention to the conditional structure: 'if... then...'. Notice the key terms: wājib li-dhātihi, mumtaniʿ li-dhātihi.
The footnote marker (٢) is part of the original text—ignore it for now.
2
Identify the conditional premise and its implication
The author says: if knowledge and power were identical, then everything known would also be an object of power. What would that lead to?
Think about what is known: the Necessary in essence and the Impossible in essence are known to Allah.
3
Understand the counterexample
The author then states that rational people agree that the Necessary and Impossible are known but not objects of power. This contradicts the implication, so the premise must be false.
This shows that the concepts of 'known' and 'an object of power' are different.
4
Conclude the distinction
Since the concepts differ, the attributes themselves—knowledge and power—must be distinct from each other and from the essence.

الحجة السادسة : لو كان كونه عالماً ، عين كونه قادراً ، لكان كل ما صح كونه عالماً به وجب أن يصح كونه قادراً عليه ، فكان يلزم في الواجب لذاته ، وفي الممتنع لذاته أن يكون مقدوراً ، كما أنه معلوم له وحيث أطبق أهل العقل أن الواجب لذاته والممتنع لذاته معلوم ، ( وليس بمقدور علمنا أن المفهوم من كون الشيء معلوماً ، مغاير للمفهوم من كونه مقدوراً ) (٢) وذلك يقتضي حصول المغايرة بين العلم وبين القدرة .
The sixth proof: If His being knowing were identical to His being powerful, then everything that is correct for Him to be knowing of would necessarily be correct for Him to be powerful over. Thus it would be necessary that the Necessary in essence (wājib li-dhātihi) and the Impossible in essence (mumtaniʿ li-dhātihi) be objects of power (maqdūr), just as they are known to Him. But all rational people agree that the Necessary in essence and the Impossible in essence are known, and are not objects of power. So we know that the conception of a thing being known is different from the conception of it being an object of power. That necessitates the occurrence of distinction (mughāyara) between knowledge and power.

Therefore, the concepts of 'known' (maʿlūm) and 'power over' (maqdūr) are distinct, and consequently knowledge and power themselves are distinct attributes.

The Seventh Proof: Power Ceases, Knowledge Remains
Seventh proof: example of created object ending power but not knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the seventh proof for the distinction between divine knowledge and power, based on the impossibility of re-creating what already exists.

Core Idea
Power ends with creation; knowledge remains. They must be distinct.

This lesson examines the seventh proof from the author's series distinguishing divine attributes. The argument uses a simple observation: once a powerful agent creates something, that specific act can no longer be repeated. Yet the created thing remains known. This difference in persistence shows that knowledge and power are not identical.

Commentary
This passage is the seventh proof (al-ḥujjah al-sābiʿah) for the distinction between knowledge and power. The author uses a clear example: a powerful agent (qādir) who creates something loses the ability to create that same thing again, because creating what already exists (ījād al-mawjūd) is impossible. The impossible is not an object of power (lā qudrah ʿalayhi). However, that created thing remains known (maʿlūm) to the agent after its existence. So power ceases with respect to that particular act, but knowledge persists. This difference in persistence—power ceasing while knowledge continues—shows that knowledge and power are distinct attributes.

The Eighth Proof: Distinction Through Definitions and Properties
Author presents eighth proof based on definitions, properties, and conceptual distinctness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the eighth proof that divine attributes are distinct from each other and from the essence based on differences in definitions, rulings, and properties.

Core Idea
Differences in definitions and properties prove real distinction among attributes.

Sanusi presents the eighth proof, which uses the diversity of quiddities (māhiyyāt) to argue that knowledge and power are distinct attributes. If they were identical, their terms would be synonymous, but definitions, rulings, and properties show otherwise. This lesson walks through the logical steps of the proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof structure
Notice that the proof begins by presenting two alternatives: either the quiddities of knowledge and power are identical or different. The argument then eliminates the first alternative.
The proof uses a disjunctive syllogism.
2
Examine the consequence of identity
If the quiddities were identical, the words 'knowledge' and 'power' would be synonymous. Reflect on why this would be unacceptable.
Synonymy would mean no real difference in meaning.
3
Identify the three grounds of difference
The author lists definitions (ḥudūd), rulings (aḥkām), and properties (khaṣāʾiṣ) as evidence that knowledge and power are not identical. See how each differs.
These are objective criteria for conceptual distinction.
4
Understand the conclusion
From the above, it follows that the intelligible content of 'knowing' differs from 'powerful', and both differ from 'existent'. This proves the desired distinction.
The proof thus establishes three distinct concepts.

الحجة الثامنة : إن العلم ماهية مخصوصة ، والقدرة أيضاً ماهية مخصوصة ، فإما أن تكون إحدى الماهيتين عين الأخرى أو غيرها ؟ فإن كان الأول كان لفظ العلم ولفظ القدرة لفظان مترادفان ، يفيد كل واحد منهما ما يفيده الآخر . ومعلوم أن ذلك باطل ، لأن حد العلم غير حد القدرة ، وأحكام العلم غير أحكام القدرة ، وخواص كل واحد منها مغايرة لخواص الآخر . وإذا كان الأمر كذلك وجب أن يكون المعقول من كونه عالماً ، مغايراً للمعقول من كونه قادراً ، وأن يكون المعقول من كل واحد منهما ، مغايراً للمعقول من كونه موجوداً . وذلك يفيد المطلوب
The eighth proof: Knowledge is a specific quiddity (māhiyyah makhṣūṣah), and power is also a specific quiddity. So either one of the two quiddities is identical to the other or different from it. If the former, then the word 'knowledge' and the word 'power' would be synonymous, each conveying what the other conveys. But it is known that this is false, because the definition (ḥadd) of knowledge is different from the definition of power, the rulings (aḥkām) of knowledge are different from the rulings of power, and the properties (khaṣāʾiṣ) of each are different from the properties of the other. Since this is the case, it is necessary that the intelligible (al-maʿqūl) from His being knowing is different from the intelligible from His being powerful, and that the intelligible from each of them is different from the intelligible from His being an existent essence. That yields what is sought.

Commentary
This passage is the eighth proof (al-ḥujjah al-thāminah) for the distinction between attributes and essence. The author argues from the diversity of quiddities (māhiyyāt). Knowledge and power each have their own specific quiddity. If they were identical, the words 'knowledge' and 'power' would be synonymous, which they are not: their definitions (ḥudūd), rulings (aḥkām), and properties (khaṣāʾiṣ) differ. Similarly, the intelligible content (al-maʿqūl) of being knowing, being powerful, and being existent are all distinct. Therefore, these attributes are distinct both from each other and from the essence. This is a clear statement of the proof from conceptual diversity (ikhtilāf al-mafhūm).

Philosophers argue that attributes cannot be necessary or possible, so they are not real.

Core Idea
Attributes cannot be necessary or possible; therefore they are impossible.

The philosophers present a powerful objection: if attributes exist, they must be either necessary or possible. But both options lead to contradictions. This lesson unpacks their reasoning step by step.

The Mu'tazila present a dilemma against multiple eternal entities showing both outcomes absurd.

Core Idea
Multiple eternal entities necessarily collapse into either infinite regress or polytheism.

The Mu'tazila used rational arguments to challenge the Ash'ari view that divine attributes are eternal and distinct from God's essence. This second general argument presents a dilemma: if there are multiple eternal entities, then either they differ in some constitutive part or they do not. Both alternatives, they argue, lead to absurdities. In this lesson, we follow the logical steps of this argument and examine its implications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the divine attributes of power and knowledge, being possible, must have their cause in the divine essence itself, and why this cause cannot be something other than the essence.

Core Idea
Divine attributes are possible realities necessitated directly by the essence.

Sanusi begins by clarifying what we mean when we say God is 'powerful' (qādir) and 'knowing' (ʿālim). These are not self-standing entities but specific relations or properties that depend on a cause. The following passage explains why that cause cannot be anything other than the divine essence itself.

Sanusi discusses whether divine attributes are necessitated directly by the essence or through an intermediary, and argues for direct causation.

Core Idea
When the intermediary is unknown, it is more proper to drop it.

Having established that the essence is the cause of the attributes, Sanusi now addresses whether this causation is direct or through some intermediary. He argues that since any intermediary is unknown, it is preferable to affirm direct causation. He draws a parallel with the argument for God's existence from the world's temporal origination.

Sanusi divides concomitants into those with and without an intermediary. Both are possible in this context.
2
Consider the possibility of an intermediary

3
Apply the rule of dropping the unknown intermediary
Since the intermediary is unknown and we must acknowledge the essence as the cause, it is better to drop the intermediary and affirm direct causation.
This is based on the principle of 'first and more proper'.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed theological argument (proof and counter-proof) concerning the nature of God's attribute of bringing into existence (al-takwīn). The author, al-Sanusi, presents the issue as a debate over whether God's act of creating a thing is identical to the created thing itself (al-mukawwan) or distinct from it. He begins by framing the question: when God brings something into existence, there are two aspects—the thing brought into existence and the act of bringing it into existence. He draws an analogy with observable examples: is the act of moving identical to the motion? Is the act of blackening identical to blackness? Those who hold identity analogize accordingly; those who hold distinction say the act is other than the effect. 

Sanusi then presents the argument for distinction (al-takwīn ghayr al-mukawwan). The proof is based on causality: if something exists, we ask why it exists, and the answer is because the powerful agent (al-qādir) brought it into existence. If the act of bringing into existence were identical to the existence of the thing, then saying 'it exists because God brought it into existence' would be like saying 'it exists for itself,' which would negate any causal role for God—a conclusion that is false. Therefore, the act of bringing into existence must be distinct from the existence of the thing itself. He further argues that this act (ījād or takwīn) is not identical to power (al-qudra) itself, because God is powerful over things He does not create (e.g., He could create many suns but has not). Thus, takwīn is an attribute distinct both from power and from the created thing. 

Next, Sanusi presents the counter-argument for identity (al-takwīn nafs al-mukawwan). Their proof is a dilemma: if takwīn were distinct from the created thing, it would have to be either eternal (qadīm) or originated (hādith). If eternal, then its eternal nature would necessitate the eternality of the created thing, because takwīn only applies when the thing comes to be—so the world would be pre-eternal, which is false. If originated, then that originated takwīn would itself require another takwīn to bring it into existence, leading to an infinite regress (tasalsul), which is impossible. Therefore, takwīn cannot be distinct. 

Sanusi concludes by noting that this is a summary of the rational discussions on this topic, and he promises to return to it in a work on origination and pre-eternity. The passage demonstrates careful dialectical reasoning, presenting both sides before implicitly favoring the view of distinction (as his own school, Ash'ari, generally holds that divine attributes are distinct from the essence and from created things).

Chapter Four: On whether the act of bringing a thing into existence is identical to it, or distinct from it? Know that when the Exalted brings a thing into existence, there are two matters: one is that thing brought into existence; the second is that the Exalted brought that thing into existence and brought it out of non-existence into existence. So the act of bringing into existence by God the Exalted—is it identical to that effect or distinct from it? The parallel of this inquiry in the perceptible world is: Is moving identical to motion? And blackening: is it identical to blackness? So he who says that the act of bringing into existence is identical to the thing brought into existence says that moving is identical to motion and blackening is identical to blackness. And he who says that the act of bringing into existence is distinct from the thing brought into existence says in this case that they are distinct. This inquiry is subtle and has great depth. We say: For those who say that the act of bringing into existence is distinct from the thing brought into existence, they may argue that if it is asked: Why did this thing occur? And why did it exist after it was non-existent? He says: Because the powerful agent produced it as an event and brought it into existence. Thus he explains its existence by the powerful agent's causing it to happen and bringing it into existence. If the powerful agent's causing it to happen were identical to the existence of that effect, then our saying: 'It only existed because the powerful agent brought it into existence' would become like saying: 'It only existed for itself.' But if it existed for itself and by itself, it would necessarily follow that the powerful agent has no effect in it, and that entails the negation of causation and the negation of the causal agent, which is false. Thus it is established by this that the powerful agent's bringing it into existence is distinct from its existence in itself. And that act of bringing into existence and originating is not identical to power itself, because the powerful agent may be powerful over things that he does not bring into existence. Do you not see that the Exalted is powerful over creating many suns and many moons, while He has not created them? Thus He is powerful over them but not their creator. So it is established by this that the act of bringing into existence is an attribute distinct from power and distinct from the thing brought into existence, and that is what is intended. As for those who say that the act of bringing into existence cannot be distinct from the thing brought into existence, they argued against it: that if the act of bringing into existence were distinct from the thing brought into existence, then that act of bringing into existence would either be eternal or originated. If it is eternal, then from its eternality would follow the eternality of the thing brought into existence, because the act of bringing into existence is only true when the thing brought into existence occurs. Do you not see that as long as the action remains in original non-existence, it is said of that powerful agent that he did not affect it, did not bring it into existence, and did not act upon it. Thus it is established that the act of bringing into existence only occurs when the thing brought into existence occurs. If the act of bringing into existence were eternal, it would necessarily follow that the thing brought into existence is eternal, and that entails the pre-eternity of the world. And if the act of bringing into existence is originated, then its own bringing into existence would require another act of bringing into existence, and that leads to infinite regress, which is impossible. So this is the summary of what is in this place regarding rational investigations, and we will return to this inquiry in the book that deals with origination and pre-eternity. Commentary
This passage is a detailed theological argument (proof and counter-proof) concerning the nature of God's attribute of bringing into existence (al-takwīn). The author, al-Sanusi, presents the issue as a debate over whether God's act of creating a thing is identical to the created thing itself (al-mukawwan) or distinct from it. He begins by framing the question: when God brings something into existence, there are two aspects—the thing brought into existence and the act of bringing it into existence. He draws an analogy with observable examples: is the act of moving identical to the motion? Is the act of blackening identical to blackness? Those who hold identity analogize accordingly; those who hold distinction say the act is other than the effect. 

Sanusi then presents the argument for distinction (al-takwīn ghayr al-mukawwan). The proof is based on causality: if something exists, we ask why it exists, and the answer is because the powerful agent (al-qādir) brought it into existence. If the act of bringing into existence were identical to the existence of the thing, then saying 'it exists because God brought it into existence' would be like saying 'it exists for itself,' which would negate any causal role for God—a conclusion that is false. Therefore, the act of bringing into existence must be distinct from the existence of the thing itself. He further argues that this act (ījād or takwīn) is not identical to power (al-qudra) itself, because God is powerful over things He does not create (e.g., He could create many suns but has not). Thus, takwīn is an attribute distinct both from power and from the created thing. 

Next, Sanusi presents the counter-argument for identity (al-takwīn nafs al-mukawwan). Their proof is a dilemma: if takwīn were distinct from the created thing, it would have to be either eternal (qadīm) or originated (hādith). If eternal, then its eternal nature would necessitate the eternality of the created thing, because takwīn only applies when the thing comes to be—so the world would be pre-eternal, which is false. If originated, then that originated takwīn would itself require another takwīn to bring it into existence, leading to an infinite regress (tasalsul), which is impossible. Therefore, takwīn cannot be distinct. 

Sanusi concludes by noting that this is a summary of the rational discussions on this topic, and he promises to return to it in a work on origination and pre-eternity. The passage demonstrates careful dialectical reasoning, presenting both sides before implicitly favoring the view of distinction (as his own school, Ash'ari, generally holds that divine attributes are distinct from the essence and from created things).
