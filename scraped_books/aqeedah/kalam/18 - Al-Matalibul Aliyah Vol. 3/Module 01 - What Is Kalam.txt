The Motive of Wisdom: Definition and Empirical Proof
Defining dā'iyat al-ḥikma and proving only need-based motives are observed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of dā'iyat al-ḥikma and the empirical argument that only the motive of need is observed in human action.

Core Idea
True altruism is not empirically observed; all apparent good acts reduce to self-interest.

This lesson introduces the concept of dā'iyat al-ḥikma—the motivation to bring good to others. Surprisingly, the author argues that human experience never shows purely altruistic action. By analyzing even seemingly selfless acts, we uncover selfish motives. This sets the stage for a theological discussion about divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition carefully
Start by reading the Arabic definition of 'motive of wisdom' and its translation. Notice that it is defined as 'striving to bring good to others.'
The term 'wisdom' here refers to purposeful goodness.
2
Understand the empirical claim
The author claims that experience shows we can seek our own benefit without seeking others' benefit. Reflect on how this sets up the proof that only need-based motives are observed.
3
Analyze the three selfish motives
In the example of the materialist helping a blind person, three selfish motives are listed. Identify each: self-praise, relief of compassion, and illusion of praise. Notice how these reduce altruism to egoism.
The materialist denies reward and punishment, so his motives are purely worldly.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The author concludes that only the motive of need is established in the witnessed world. Think about why the motive of beneficence is rejected. This has implications for understanding divine action.
This argument is important for later discussions on God's attributes.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and an empirical proof. The author first defines 'the motive of wisdom' (dā'iyat al-ḥikma) as striving to bring good to others. If these are removed, the action is not certain. Therefore, only the motive of need is observed in human actions, not pure beneficence.

The Two Types of Effective Agent: al-Qadir and al-Mujib
Defines al-qadir and al-mujib according to ahl al-milal wa al-nihal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between al-qadir (the capable agent) and al-mujib (the necessitating agent) as defined by the people of religious sects, and why this distinction is foundational for Islamic theology.

Core Idea
An effective agent either can act or refrain (qadir) or acts necessarily (mujib).

This passage introduces the key terms that frame the Islamic theological debate on divine action. Before engaging with the philosophers' objections, we must first grasp the classification of effective agents according to mainstream theologians. Here, the distinction between qadir and mujib is laid out clearly. Let us read the original text and understand its precise definitions.

Pay attention to the terms 'qadir' and 'mujib'.

The condition is whether the agent can possibly not effect.

The capable and the necessitating agent among philosophers, theologians, and people of religions. The people of religious sects said: The effective agent is either such that it occurs with the possibility that it does not effect—and that is the capable (al-qadir), or it effects without the possibility of not effecting—and that is the necessitating agent (al-mujib). So this division indicates that every effective agent is either capable or necessitating. Then after this they said: The capable is the one from whom it is valid to effect sometimes and not to effect at other times, according to motives. This is a summary of the discourse on the distinction between the capable and the necessitating agent. The philosophers said: The statement asserting an effective agent whose effecting is on the basis of possibility (sihha), not on the basis of necessity (wujub), is a problematic statement. Its clarification is from several aspects: The first proof: Every thing that we suppose to be effective in an effect: either everything necessary for it to be effective in that effect is present, or the totality of those things is not present. If everything necessary is present, then the occurrence of the effect becomes necessary, because if it were not necessary, then the absence of the effect would be possible, and everything that is possible, its occurrence is not necessarily impossible. So let us suppose that totality sometimes with the occurrence of the effect and sometimes without its occurrence. (1) The capable and the necessitating agent between philosophers and theologians and people of religions [addition]. (2) This is the problem of the necessitating agent by essence. That is that God and the world are co-eternal, like fire when it exists, burning exists with it, i.e., burning is inseparable from fire. That is, the world and God together. And this doctrine is false. And the agent by choice is the true doctrine.

Commentary
This passage begins by defining the distinction between two types of effective agents: al-qadir (the capable agent) and al-mujib (the necessitating agent), according to the people of religious sects (ahl al-milal wa al-nihal). They classify all effective agents (mu'aththirat) into two categories: those that can either effect or not effect (al-qadir), and those that effect necessarily, without the possibility of not effecting (al-mujib). Then they specifically define al-qadir as one from whom it is valid (yashihhu) to act sometimes and refrain at other times, depending on motives (al-dawa'i). This is presented as a summary of the distinction. The passage then transitions to the philosophers' objection: they find the concept of an agent that acts on the basis of possibility (sihha) rather than necessity (wujub) to be problematic. They present a proof: for any supposed cause-effect pair, either all necessary conditions for the effect are present, or they are not. If all necessary conditions are present, then the effect must occur; otherwise, it would be possible for the effect to be absent despite the presence of all conditions, which would imply that the effect is merely possible. But if it is merely possible, then it is not contradictory to suppose it occurs or not, which leads to an infinite regress. The proof is left incomplete. The footnotes add that the philosophers' position implies a necessitating agent by essence (al-mujib bi al-dhat), leading to the co-eternity of God and the world—a doctrine rejected as false by the footnote author, who asserts that the true doctrine is that God acts by choice (al-fa'il bi al-ikhtiyar).

The Philosophers' First Objection: The Problem of Contingent Agency
Presents the philosophers' first proof against contingent agency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' objection to the concept of an agent that acts on the basis of possibility (sihha) rather than necessity (wujub), and the first proof they present against it.

Core Idea
If all necessary conditions are present, the effect must occur necessarily.

Having established the distinction between al-qadir and al-mujib, the philosophers now challenge the very idea of a contingent agent. They argue that if an agent truly has all necessary conditions, the effect cannot fail to occur. This leads to a necessitarian view of divine action. Let us examine their first proof and see how it unfolds.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and proof passage
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Note the structure: the objection statement followed by the first proof.
The proof is a conditional argument.
2
Understand the proof's conditional
The proof says: For any cause, either all conditions for the effect are present or not. If all conditions are present, the effect must occur; otherwise, it could be absent. Then it asks us to consider a scenario where the effect occurs sometimes and not other times, even though conditions are the same.
This is the crux of the argument.
3
Reflect on the implication for qadir
If the philosophers' proof holds, then an agent that can sometimes effect and sometimes not (like al-qadir) would be impossible because the presence of all conditions would necessitate the effect. Ask yourself how a theologian might respond.
The proof is left incomplete, but the direction is clear.

They present a proof: for any supposed cause-effect pair, either all necessary conditions for the effect are present, or they are not. If all necessary conditions are present, then the effect must occur; otherwise, it would be possible for the effect to be absent despite the presence of all conditions, which would imply that the effect is merely possible. But if it is merely possible, then it is not contradictory to suppose it occurs or not, which leads to an infinite regress. The proof is left incomplete. The footnotes add that the philosophers' position implies a necessitating agent by essence (al-mujib bi al-dhat), leading to the co-eternity of God and the world—a doctrine rejected as false by the footnote author, who asserts that the true doctrine is that God acts by choice (al-fa'il bi al-ikhtiyar).

The Fourth Proof: Knowledge as Conformable Form
Al-Razi's fourth proof: knowledge as conforming form

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's fourth proof that knowledge of future events is distinct from knowledge of present events, based on the principle that knowledge is a form conforming to the known.

Core Idea
Knowledge conforms to its object; different objects require different forms.

Al-Razi now presents a fourth proof that relies on the very nature of knowledge itself. He defines knowledge as a form that corresponds to the known object. By comparing statements about the future and the present, he shows that their essences are different, and therefore the knowledges must be distinct. This is a concise yet powerful argument that highlights the importance of the correspondence between knower and known.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of knowledge
Al-Razi begins by stating that knowledge is a form (ṣūra) that conforms (muṭābiqa) to the known object. This is the foundational premise of the argument.
The term 'form' here means a mental representation or concept.
2
Contrast future and present statements
He then claims it is self-evident that the essence of the statement 'it will occur' is different from 'it is now occurring'. These are two distinct meanings.
Think about the difference in meaning between planning for something and experiencing it.
3
Apply the conformity principle
If the two essences are different, then the forms that conform to them must also be different, because what conforms to different things must itself be different.
This is a logical principle: if A corresponds to X and B corresponds to Y, and X ≠ Y, then A ≠ B.
4
Draw the conclusion
Therefore, the knowledge that something will exist cannot be identical to the knowledge that it exists when it occurs. They are two distinct knowledges.
This refutes any claim that future knowledge is simply the same as present knowledge in a different mode.
