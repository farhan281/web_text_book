The Second Scriptural Argument: Messengers Remove All Excuses
The second scriptural argument: messengers remove excuses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the purpose of sending messengers demonstrates that divine foreknowledge is not a preventer of faith.

Core Idea
If foreknowledge prevented faith, it would be the strongest excuse, but messengers remove all excuses.

This lesson presents the second scriptural argument: that God sent messengers to give people no excuse. If divine foreknowledge of disbelief were a preventer, it would be the greatest excuse, but God does not accept it as such. Therefore, it cannot be a preventer.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage for the second argument. It is brief but contains a logical deduction.
2
Identify the premises
The argument has two premises: (1) God sends messengers to remove excuses. (2) If foreknowledge were a preventer, it would be an excuse.
The conclusion follows logically.
3
Understand the conclusion
Since God does not treat foreknowledge as an excuse, it must not be a preventer.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the objection that God's foreknowledge and announcement that a person will not believe makes faith impossible. The author identifies this as the first of two stations in the chapter. The objectors claim that both scriptural and rational proofs indicate that divine foreknowledge does not render faith impossible; here the author presents the scriptural proofs. The first argument is that the Qur'an is filled with verses that condemn disbelief and question why people do not believe, implying that there is no effective prevention. If God had truly prevented them, such questioning would be morally repugnant, like a jailer asking his prisoner why he does not perform tasks. The author cites multiple Qur'anic verses where God interrogates about lack of belief or action, including verses addressed to Iblis, to Musa about his brother, and general rhetorical questions. He then quotes a statement from al-Sahib bin Abbad elaborating on the contradiction: if God prevents faith, how can He command it and blame for its absence? Each question in the Qur'an would be incongruous if divine prevention existed. The conclusion is that these verses explicitly affirm the absence of prevention, and if God's knowledge of disbelief were a preventer, all these verses would be false—which is impossible. The second argument appeals to the purpose of sending messengers: to remove all excuses. If divine foreknowledge were a preventer, it would be the strongest excuse for disbelief, but God has not allowed such an excuse; therefore it cannot be a preventer. The third argument (incomplete in this window) presents a verse where disbelievers claim their hearts are covered and ears deaf, and God condemns them for this claim. If divine foreknowledge prevented belief, their claim would be truthful, but God blames them, showing that their claim is false and that they are responsible for their unbelief. The passage clearly defends human responsibility while affirming God's foreknowledge.

The First Rational Proof: Knowledge Does Not Prevent
Rational reductio: if knowledge prevented, God would have no power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first rational proof that God's knowledge does not prevent His power from acting.

Core Idea
Knowledge does not prevent; otherwise God would have no power.

Sanusi opens his rational proofs with a powerful reductio ad absurdum. He shows that if knowledge of a thing's non-existence were a barrier to its coming into being, then God would be powerless over everything—an absurd conclusion. This lesson walks through the proof step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the assumption
The proof begins with a hypothesis: that God's knowledge of a thing's non-existence prevents it from existing. Identify this assumption clearly.
This is the premise being tested.
2
Follow the consequences
The assumption leads to two categories: what God knows will occur becomes necessary, and what He does not know becomes impossible. See how both categories remove any role for divine power.
Necessary occurs regardless; impossible cannot be done.
3
Recognize the absurd result
If nothing can be done by power, then God would have no power over anything—an impossibility.
This contradicts the fact that discussion about wisdom implies God is powerful.
4
Conclude the proof
Since the assumption leads to a false and self-defeating conclusion, the assumption itself must be false. Therefore, knowledge does not prevent.
The proof is valid and sound.

The first proof: If knowledge of the non-existence of a thing were to prevent its coming into being, then it would follow that God is not able to do anything at all, because what God knows will occur is necessary of occurrence, and what He does not know is impossible of occurrence. The necessary cannot be brought about by power, because if it is necessary of occurrence, it will occur whether the capable one does it or not. And it is known that the discussion about wisdom and foolishness is a branch of establishing that He is powerful. So if this speech detracts from His being powerful, it must be false.

Commentary
This passage presents a rational (aqli) proof. The author argues by reductio ad absurdum. Assume that God's knowledge of a thing's non-existence prevents it from existing. Then whatever God knows will occur becomes necessary (wajib), and whatever He does not know becomes impossible (mumtani'). For the necessary, no power is needed to bring it about—it occurs regardless. For the impossible, no power can bring it about. Thus, God would have no power over anything. But the discussion of wisdom (hikma) and foolishness (safah) is predicated on God being powerful. Therefore, this assumption must be false, and knowledge does not prevent.

Second Rational Proof: Knowledge Does Not Change Modality
Rational proof that knowledge conforms to the modality of its object.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof that divine knowledge does not alter the modality (necessity or possibility) of its object, and why this refutes the claim that foreknowledge necessitates impossibility.

Core Idea
Knowledge reflects reality; it does not change it.

This lesson presents the second rational proof from the text, establishing a key principle in Islamic theology: divine knowledge conforms to the nature of things. The author argues that since faith and disbelief are inherently possible, God's knowledge of them does not make them necessary. Understanding this proof safeguards against the misconception that God's foreknowledge compels human actions.

Second Argument: Knowledge Does Not Change Possibility
Al-Matalib al-Aliyah presents the second argument against the claim that divine knowledge prevents action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why divine knowledge does not alter the nature of possible things, based on the second proof from Al-Matalib al-Aliyah.

Core Idea
Knowledge relates to things as they are, not changing their possibility.

This lesson presents the second argument against the claim that Allah's knowledge makes acts necessary. The argument hinges on a simple logical principle: knowledge conforms to reality; it does not create reality. By understanding this, we see that faith and disbelief remain possible even though Allah knows them.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the translation. Let the author's logical flow come through.
Notice how the argument is structured as a conditional reasoning.
2
Understand the premise
The argument begins with a principle: knowledge relates to a thing exactly as that thing is in itself. If the thing is possible, God knows it as possible; if necessary, He knows it as necessary.
This principle is key to understanding why knowledge does not change reality.
3
Apply to faith and disbelief
Faith and disbelief, in their essence, are possible—they can exist or not exist. So they are known by God as possible.
This step applies the general premise to the specific case.
4
Consider the implication
If God's knowledge and report made faith and disbelief necessary (instead of remaining possible), that would mean knowledge changed their nature—turning possible into necessary. That is impossible because knowledge does not alter what is known.
This is the crucial logical step: an impossible consequence is derived from the opposing view.
5
Draw the conclusion
Thus, divine knowledge and report do not necessitate the impossibility of faith or disbelief. They remain possible, and human choice is meaningful.
The conclusion directly refutes the objection that foreknowledge makes action impossible.

This passage presents the second argument against the claim that divine knowledge prevents action. It is a logical proof based on the nature of knowledge. Therefore, knowledge and report do not impose impossibility on the object; the object remains possible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why commanding a disbeliever to believe does not contradict God's knowledge.

Core Idea
God's knowledge does not make the commanded act impossible.

This lesson presents the fourth argument from Al-Matalib al-Aliyah against the claim that divine knowledge prevents action. The argument uses a logical reductio to show that if knowledge prevented action, then God's commands would be commands to nullify His own knowledge, which is impossible. Therefore, divine commands are valid.

Learning Objective

Commentary

Allah's actions transcend created benefits — the proof uses a conditional argument.

This passage presents a logical argument that challenges the assumption that God acts for benefit. By examining the preservation of prophets and the survival of Iblis, the author constructs a conditional proof that reveals the flaw in benefit-based accounts of divine action.
