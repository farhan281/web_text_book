Second Proof: Moral Judgments Needed for Trust in Divine Promises
Argues that without rational moral judgments, God's promises and threats become unreliable, leading to absurdities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the second proof uses reductio ad absurdum to argue that rational moral judgments are necessary for trust in God's promises and threats.

Core Idea
Without rational morality, trust in God's promises collapses into absurdity.

This passage presents the second proof from kalam theologians. It builds on the first proof by showing that without rational judgments of good and evil, even divine promises become meaningless. The argument forces the opponent to choose between accepting absurdities or conceding that reason judges morality.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the hypothesis
Start by reading the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice that the proof begins with a conditional statement: 'If good and evil were not established except by the revealed law...'
This hypothesis is the starting point of the reductio.
2
List the absurd consequences
The proof lists several absurd scenarios that would be permissible if morality were only from revelation. Write down each one as you encounter it in the passage.
These consequences are deliberately extreme to show the flaw in the hypothesis.
3
Understand the reductio structure
Recognize that the argument is a reductio ad absurdum: it assumes the opposite of the desired conclusion and derives impossible consequences, thereby refuting the assumption.
The conclusion is that rational moral judgments exist independently of revelation.
4
Identify the conclusion
Find the final two lines where the argument presents the dilemma: either accept all absurdities (and abandon reason) or acknowledge that reason judges good and evil.
This forces the opponent to concede the necessity of rational morality.
5
Reflect on the significance
Consider why this argument is important for Islamic theology: it secures the basis for trusting divine promises and threats, which is essential for religious motivation.
Without this, the entire structure of reward and punishment becomes unreliable.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof (al-hujja al-thaniya). The argument is a reductio ad absurdum. It begins with the hypothesis that good and evil are only known through revelation. If that were the case, then nothing could be considered ugly from God, because revelation is from God and could not judge His actions. Consequently, God's promises and threats would become unreliable, because He could arbitrarily change them. The proof then lists a series of absurd consequences: God could reward wrongdoers and punish the righteous, support false prophets with miracles, command people to commit immoral acts, even command absurdities like jumping to the Throne. The conclusion is that if one accepts that all these are permissible (since God could do anything), then one has abandoned reason. But if one recognizes that these things are ugly, then one has admitted that reason judges good and evil independently of revelation. Thus, the proof forces the opponent to concede that moral judgments are rational.

Moral Concepts Must Precede Revelation
Presents third proof: good and evil precede revelation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the rational judgment of good and evil must be established prior to revelation for revelation to be intelligible.

Core Idea
Revelation cannot teach good and evil if the terms are meaningless.

This lesson examines a key argument from Islamic theology that reason must already know good and evil before revelation can guide us. The proof is simple: if revelation uses terms like 'good' and 'evil' without prior meaning, it would be speaking nonsense. Therefore, these concepts must be innate in the intellect.

The Circularity of Deriving Morality from Revelation
A proof that moral judgments must precede revelation to avoid circularity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why moral judgments cannot depend on revelation without leading to a vicious circle involving miracles and truthfulness.

Core Idea
Moral judgments are necessary to accept revelation, so they cannot depend on it.

This lesson presents the fourth proof, which demonstrates that if moral judgments depended on revelation, we would need moral judgments to even establish revelation. The proof uses the concept of a vicious circle (dawr) and the role of miracles in confirming prophethood. By following the logical steps, you will see why good and evil must be known prior to revelation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic text and its translation. Notice the structure: it begins with a premise, then presents a conditional argument, and concludes with the impossibility of circularity.
The proof is concise; every phrase matters.
2
Identify the key premise
The proof starts by stating that there is no dispute about the existence of good and evil in actions. This is the foundation for the argument.
3
Trace the circularity
Follow the logical chain: if good and evil depend on revelation, then revelation's truth depends on miracles indicating a prophet's truthfulness, which depends on the moral judgment that God does not support liars. If that moral judgment itself depends on revelation, a circle arises.
This is the core of the proof.
4
Draw the conclusion
Since the circle is impossible, the moral judgment about God not supporting liars must be known before revelation. Therefore, good and evil are established prior to revelation.
This conclusion refutes the idea that morality depends on revelation.

Rational Good and Evil: The Circularity Argument
Mu'tazilah present a proof that good/evil must be rationally known to avoid circularity in establishing prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Mu'tazilah used the impossibility of circularity to argue that good and evil are known rationally, prior to revelation.

Core Idea
If morality depended on revelation, then proving revelation would be circular — so morality must be rational.

The Mu'tazilah's fourth proof for rational good and evil is a sophisticated argument from circularity. It begins with a premise everyone accepts — that some actions are good and some evil — and then asks whether this moral judgment depends on revelation. The proof shows that if it did, we would be caught in a vicious circle when trying to establish the truth of revelation itself. This argument forces a clear choice: either accept rational morality or abandon the possibility of proving prophethood. The passage lays out this reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core premise
Notice that the argument starts with an uncontroversial statement: everyone agrees that some acts are good and some are evil. This is the starting point.
The Mu'tazilah are building on common ground.
2
Trace the conditional
The passage presents a dilemma: either good/evil depends on revelation or it does not. The proof then refutes the first option.
Pay attention to the word 'either...or'.
3
Unpack the circularity
The refutation shows a vicious circle: proving revelation needs miracles, miracles need the judgment that God wouldn't support a liar, and that judgment would itself depend on revelation. This is impossible.
Circularity (al-dawr) is the key concept.
4
Draw the conclusion
Since the first option fails, the second must be true: good and evil are known by reason prior to revelation. This is the point of the proof.
The conclusion is stated explicitly at the end.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the claim that knowledge of the goodness of beneficence and the badness of injustice is self-evident and necessary. The author, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, presents a response in two aspects, then deepens the analysis by establishing the foundational nature of pleasure and pain as the ultimate objects of desire and aversion.

He begins by stating the chapter: 'Chapter Eleven: The response to these arguments upon which they relied.' The first argument is the claim that we know by necessity that beneficence is good and injustice is bad. Razi responds with two aspects.

**First aspect:** He points out that every assent (tasdiq) must be preceded by a conceptualization (tasawwur) of its subject and predicate. Then he examines the intended meaning of 'good' and 'bad'. If by 'good' they mean what is naturally loved because it brings benefits, then he concedes that the knowledge of its goodness under this interpretation is necessary. Similarly, if by 'bad' they mean what is naturally hated because it causes pains and sorrows, then that knowledge is also necessary. However, this interpretation defines good and bad in terms of benefit and harm, interest and corruption. Such notions are impossible to apply to God, because God is not subject to benefit or harm. Therefore, the supposed necessary knowledge of good and bad cannot be used to establish moral obligations with respect to God. Thus, their argument fails if they mean natural inclination.

If, on the other hand, they intend something other than benefit and harm, then that meaning is inconceivable (ghayr mutasawwar). If it is inconceivable, then it cannot be assented to, let alone be self-evidently known. So either interpretation defeats their claim.

**Deeper analysis:** Razi then says: 'This is the true station which the intelligent verifier must contemplate.' He proceeds to uncover the reality of good and bad. He begins with a psychological observation: we know by necessity that we have things we desire and seek, and things we dislike and avoid. He then argues that this chain of means and ends cannot go on infinitely or circularly; there must be ultimate ends that are sought for their own sake (mahlub bi al-dhat) and ultimate aversions that are hated for their own sake (makruh bi al-dhat). After reflection, he finds that the ultimate objects of desire are pleasure and delight (ladhdha wa surur) or the removal of pain and sorrow (daf' al-alam wa al-ghamm). The ultimate objects of aversion are pain and sorrow or the removal of pleasure and delight. These premises are known by necessity.

From these foundations, Razi derives further consequences: anything known or thought to be a means to these ultimate ends is desirable or undesirable as a means (not for itself). He also notes that something inherently disliked may be a means to a greater desired end, and the intellect then weighs the degrees, tolerating a small disliked thing for a great desired thing. This completes his analysis of the nature of good and evil as grounded in pleasure and pain.

The passage ends in the middle of a sentence ('wa la shakka an'), indicating that the argument continues beyond the available pages.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the claim that knowledge of the goodness of beneficence and the badness of injustice is self-evident and necessary. The author, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, presents a response in two aspects, then deepens the analysis by establishing the foundational nature of pleasure and pain as the ultimate objects of desire and aversion.

He begins by stating the chapter: 'Chapter Eleven: The response to these arguments upon which they relied.' The first argument is the claim that we know by necessity that beneficence is good and injustice is bad. Razi responds with two aspects.

**First aspect:** He points out that every assent (tasdiq) must be preceded by a conceptualization (tasawwur) of its subject and predicate. Then he examines the intended meaning of 'good' and 'bad'. If by 'good' they mean what is naturally loved because it brings benefits, then he concedes that the knowledge of its goodness under this interpretation is necessary. Similarly, if by 'bad' they mean what is naturally hated because it causes pains and sorrows, then that knowledge is also necessary. However, this interpretation defines good and bad in terms of benefit and harm, interest and corruption. Such notions are impossible to apply to God, because God is not subject to benefit or harm. Therefore, the supposed necessary knowledge of good and bad cannot be used to establish moral obligations with respect to God. Thus, their argument fails if they mean natural inclination.

If, on the other hand, they intend something other than benefit and harm, then that meaning is inconceivable (ghayr mutasawwar). If it is inconceivable, then it cannot be assented to, let alone be self-evidently known. So either interpretation defeats their claim.

**Deeper analysis:** Razi then says: 'This is the true station which the intelligent verifier must contemplate.' He proceeds to uncover the reality of good and bad. He begins with a psychological observation: we know by necessity that we have things we desire and seek, and things we dislike and avoid. He then argues that this chain of means and ends cannot go on infinitely or circularly; there must be ultimate ends that are sought for their own sake (mahlub bi al-dhat) and ultimate aversions that are hated for their own sake (makruh bi al-dhat). After reflection, he finds that the ultimate objects of desire are pleasure and delight (ladhdha wa surur) or the removal of pain and sorrow (daf' al-alam wa al-ghamm). The ultimate objects of aversion are pain and sorrow or the removal of pleasure and delight. These premises are known by necessity.

From these foundations, Razi derives further consequences: anything known or thought to be a means to these ultimate ends is desirable or undesirable as a means (not for itself). He also notes that something inherently disliked may be a means to a greater desired end, and the intellect then weighs the degrees, tolerating a small disliked thing for a great desired thing. This completes his analysis of the nature of good and evil as grounded in pleasure and pain.

The passage ends in the middle of a sentence ('wa la shakka an'), indicating that the argument continues beyond the available pages.

Means-End Reasoning and the Trade-off Principle
The author derives secondary value from means-end relationships and presents a principle for weighing lesser evils against greater goods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how secondary goods and evils are derived from intrinsic ends, and how the intellect weighs a lesser evil to achieve a greater good.

Core Idea
Everything we seek as a means to pleasure or to avoid pain is indirectly good, and we may endure a small evil for a great good.

With intrinsic values established, the author now explains how other things acquire value as means. He also introduces a crucial principle for moral decision-making: if a disliked thing is a means to a greater desired end, the intellect tolerates it. This allows for a rational calculus of pleasure and pain.

Guided Path
1
Read the means-end derivation
Read from 'ثم كل ما علم أو ظن' to 'لأنه مفضياً إلى ذلك الذي هو مطلوب بالذات'. Understand that anything known or thought to be a means to an intrinsic end is desired or disliked secondarily.
This extends value to all things that lead to pleasure or pain.
2
Note the branching premise
Read the next sentence: 'ويتفرع على هذه المقدمة ...' This says that a thing disliked in itself may be a cause for obtaining something desired in itself.
This is the basis for the trade-off.
3
Understand the trade-off principle
Read the final part: 'ثم العقل مهنا يعتبر درجات الخير والمقابلة ...' The intellect compares degrees: if the disliked thing is trivial relative to the desired end, one bears the minor evil for the major good.
This is a rational principle for moral choice.
4
Apply the concept
Consider a real-life example: enduring a small pain (e.g., a vaccine) for a greater good (health). This illustrates the trade-off principle from the passage.

That beneficence is good and injustice is evil is a matter other than consideration of benefit and harm; this is inconceivable, let alone that the belief in it is intuitive. This is the correct position that the rational investigator must contemplate to uncover the truth of this issue. Commentary
This passage presents a rational proof for the existence of intrinsic good and evil and identifies them with pleasure and pain. It begins by asserting that the goodness of beneficence and the evil of injustice are not merely matters of benefit and harm; otherwise such judgments would be unimaginable as self-evident. The author then sets out a proper inquiry: we know intuitively that we desire certain things and dislike others. He argues that it is impossible for every desired thing to be desired for the sake of something else, and every disliked thing to be disliked for the sake of something else, because that would lead to either circular reasoning (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul), both false. Therefore, there must be things desired for themselves and things disliked for themselves. Upon investigation, he finds that what is desired for itself is nothing other than pleasure and delight, or the removal of pain and grief; what is disliked for itself is pain and grief, or the removal of pleasure and delight. These premises are known by necessity. From this, he derives a subsidiary principle: anything known or thought to be a means to these intrinsic ends is desired or disliked secondarily. Moreover, a thing disliked in itself may sometimes be a means to obtaining something desired in itself. The intellect then weighs degrees: if the disliked thing is trivial compared to the desired end, one endures it to achieve the greater good. This establishes a framework for moral evaluation based on pleasure and pain as ultimate intrinsic values.

Commentary
This passage applies the theory of intrinsic value to specific actions: injustice, lying, and frivolity. The author argues that injustice leads directly and strongly to pain and grief, therefore aversion to it is greatest. Lying also leads to pain and grief when the lie is discovered, but its connection is weaker because the harm is contingent on belief built on falsehood. Frivolity leads to pain and grief indirectly by wasting time that could be used for beneficial pursuits, thus its ugliness is least. The author then presents an objection: why is injustice and lying considered ugly if the doer benefits? The answer uses a universalization argument: the unjust person knows that if he condoned injustice generally, he himself could be harmed by it, so he judges it ugly to protect himself. The passage concludes with a brief statement about the goodness of beneficence, which leads to pleasure and delight, though the sentence is incomplete. The author consistently ties judgments of good and evil to their consequences in terms of pleasure and pain, but the universalization argument shows that even self-interested benefit does not override the rational judgment of evil because of the potential harm if the action were universalized.

Razi's Argument: Moral Judgments Do Not Extend to God
Razi argues that moral judgments are not transferable to God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Razi argues that accepting moral judgments for human actions does not automatically apply to God.

Core Idea
Correlation does not prove causation in moral properties.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi takes a concessive approach: he grants that human actions can be deemed good or evil. But he then challenges the opponent's leap from human to divine actions. Using a clever analogy of simultaneous relations, he shows that knowing one thing upon knowing another does not imply a causal connection.

Refuting Circularity Between Moral Judgment and Prophethood
Razi refutes the second proof by disjunction and showing circularity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi refutes the claim that denying rational good and evil undermines prophethood by exposing a logical circularity or independence.

Core Idea
Denying rational good and evil does not affect prophethood unless they are foundational—and if they are, the argument becomes circular.

Razi now tackles a second proof from those who argue that rejecting rational good and evil (ḥusn and qubḥ) would destroy the basis for prophethood and divine promises. This argument seems compelling, but Razi shows it is logically flawed. He presents a disjunction and draws out the consequences, then deals with a possible counterargument.

Understand the opponent's objection

This logical move forces the opponent into a dilemma.

Razi also anticipates and rebuts a counter-argument about implication.
