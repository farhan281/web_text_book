The Opponents' First Proof and Their Objection
Presentation of the opponents' first proof and their counter-objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the opponents' first proof that preponderance without a preponderator is impossible, and their objection that the author claims necessary knowledge in the very matter of dispute.

Core Idea
The opponents argue that preponderance without a preponderator is self-evidently impossible.

The author begins by presenting the first argument of those who hold that a capable agent cannot act without a determining factor. They argue that if the agent's relation to action and abandonment is equal, then giving preponderance to one without a reason would be impossible. The opponents then object that the author is claiming necessary knowledge about the very point under dispute.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the structure: first the opponents' proof, then their objection.
The proof uses the concept of 'equal relation' (السوية).
2
Identify the two components
The passage has two clear parts: the opponents' first proof (from 'إن القادر' to 'بالضرورة') and their objection (from 'فإن قالوا' to 'في محل النزاع'). Understand each separately.
3
Reflect on the objection
Ask yourself: Why do the opponents think the author is claiming necessary knowledge? What does that imply about the nature of the dispute?
This sets up the author's response in the next lesson.

Chapter Seven: On presenting the claim of those who say that an action does not issue from the capable agent except when a determining factor (dā‘) is present. Know that these people have argued with several proofs: The first proof: The capable agent, since his relation to action and to abandonment is equal, then if he were to give preponderance to one side over the other without a preponderator (murajjiḥ), then one of the two sides of an equally possible thing would have been given preponderance over the other without a preponderator, and that is known to be invalid necessarily. If they say: The dispute is about whether the capable agent can give preponderance to one of his two potential acts (maqdūrayn) over the other without a preponderator. And since you claim that this is known to be impossible necessarily, you have claimed necessary knowledge in the very matter of dispute. We say: Your statement 'The capable agent gives preponderance to one of his two potential acts over the other without a preponderator' is speech containing a subtle fallacy. Its exposition is that we say: Does your word 'gives preponderance' in your statement 'the capable agent gives preponderance to one of his two potential acts over the other without a preponderator' have an additional meaning beyond his being capable (qādir) or not? If it has an additional meaning, then that potential act is given preponderance over the other potential act only because this additional meaning has been joined to his being capable, so the two together become effective in its occurrence. On this assumption we say: This side is given preponderance over the other side only because this side is distinguished by that additional meaning. So your statement after this that 'the capable agent gave preponderance to one of his two potential acts over the other without a preponderator' entails the combination of two contradictories (naqīḍayn). That is because his saying 'gave preponderance' indicates something additional beyond his being capable, by which this side is given preponderance over the other side, and his saying 'without a preponderator' entails the negation of this additional thing. Thus it is established that this speech necessitates combining the additional preponderator with its negation. This is if we say that his saying 'gives preponderance' indicates an additional meaning beyond the meaning of his being capable. But if we say that it does not indicate any additional meaning at all, then there is no benefit in mentioning this word, so it must be omitted and one should suffice with his being capable. Then the substance of the speech reduces to his being capable of action and abandonment, and the relation of his power (qudra) to the two sides is equal, and that he did not distinguish the two sides by performance and preponderance, nor by any type of specification. Then, if one of the two sides occurs, it negates it. And if we state this speech in this manner, then the intuition of everyone's reason judges that it is false and invalid speech.

Commentary
This passage presents and refutes the first argument of those who claim that an action cannot issue from a capable agent except when a determining factor is present. The author begins by stating that these opponents have several proofs, and then examines the first proof. The opponents argue that if a capable agent could give preponderance to one of his two potential acts without a preponderator, then one of two equally possible sides would be preponderant without a preponderator, which is impossible. The author then notes that the opponents accuse him of claiming necessary knowledge in the very matter of dispute. The author responds by exposing a subtle fallacy in their phrasing. He explains that their statement 'the capable agent gives preponderance to one of his two potential acts without a preponderator' can be analyzed by asking whether the word 'gives preponderance' adds any meaning beyond mere capability. If it does, then that additional meaning itself acts as a preponderator, so the statement contradicts itself by both asserting and denying a preponderator. If it does not add any meaning, then the word is superfluous and should be omitted, leaving only the claim that the agent is capable with equal relation to both sides, and then the occurrence of one side negates the claim. In either case, the statement is logically contradictory or meaningless, thus the opponents' argument fails.

Analyzing 'Gives Preponderance' (Part 1): The Contradiction
First part of the author's refutation: exposing the contradiction if 'gives preponderance' adds meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the author's subtle logical analysis showing that the phrase 'gives preponderance' implies an additional meaning, which itself acts as a preponderator, leading to a contradiction.

Core Idea
'Gives preponderance' adds meaning; that meaning is itself a preponderator, creating a contradiction.

In response to the opponents, the author exposes a fallacy hidden in their wording. He asks whether the term 'gives preponderance' adds anything to mere capability. If it does, then that additional factor becomes the preponderator, contradicting the claim 'without a preponderator.' This is the first horn of the dilemma.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic from 'قلنا' through 'وبين نفيه'. Follow the logical steps.
The author uses a dialectical method: he asks a question and follows its implications.
2
Understand the question
The author asks: Does 'gives preponderance' have an additional meaning beyond being capable? If yes, then the additional meaning is the preponderator.
3
Trace the contradiction
If 'gives preponderance' implies an additional meaning, then the claim 'without a preponderator' is false. The statement as a whole asserts and denies a preponderator, which is a contradiction.
This shows the opponents' phrasing is self-defeating.

Analyzing 'Gives Preponderance' (Part 2): The Absurdity
Second part of the author's refutation: the absurdity if 'gives preponderance' adds no meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second horn of the author's dilemma: if 'gives preponderance' adds no meaning, the phrase is superfluous and the claim reduces to an obvious absurdity.

Core Idea
If 'gives preponderance' adds nothing, the claim is pointless and intuitively false.

The author completes his refutation by considering the alternative: if the word 'gives preponderance' does not indicate any additional meaning, then it should be omitted. The remaining claim is that the agent is capable with equal relation to both sides, yet one side occurs, which is clearly absurd.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read from 'وأما إذا قلنا' to the end. Note the alternative scenario.
The author presents the second horn of the dilemma.
2
Understand the reduction
If 'gives preponderance' adds no meaning, we omit it. The claim becomes: the agent is capable with equal relation, yet one side occurs, which negates the equality.
This is presented as obviously false.
3
Synthesize the refutation
Reflect on the overall argument: either the phrase implies a contradiction or it is meaningless. In both cases, the opponents' argument fails.
This completes the author's response to the first proof.

Guided Path
1
Understand the premise
The proof begins by establishing that when ability relates equally to two opposites, preference for one is impossible. Read the first few lines to grasp this starting point.
This premise is not proven here; it is taken from earlier discussions.
2
Follow the two cases
The argument considers two scenarios: equality (no preference) and when a preponderant motive arises. Notice that in equality, neither cause nor preventative exists; when preference occurs, the efficient cause for the opposite is absent and a preventative is present.
The preventative is the preference itself for the first side.
3
Compare impossibility
Since impossibility occurs in equality (where only absence of cause exists), it must also occur when both absence of cause and presence of a preventative are present. This forces the second side to be impossible and the first to be necessary.
The logic is a fortiori (awlā): if impossible without cause, then even more so with a preventative.
4
Appreciate the conclusion
The proof ends by stating that the second side becomes impossible and the first side necessary, with no middle ground. This is termed a decisive proof (burhān qāṭiʿ).

Commentary
This passage is a proof (ḥujja) in kalām demonstrating that when a capable agent (qādir) has equal ability towards an action and its omission, a preference (tarjīḥ) for one side cannot occur without a preponderant factor (murajjiḥ). The author, likely al-Āmidī or Ibn al-Ḥājib, begins by stating the premise: when ability is equally related to action and omission, preference of one side is impossible. Then he explains that in a state of equality, neither the efficient cause for preference nor the preventative exists. When a preponderant motive arises for one side, that very preference becomes a preventative against preference for the opposite side. This is because the two sides are contradictories (naqīḍ): the existence of one necessitates the non-existence of the other. Next, he compares the state of equality (where neither efficient cause nor preventative exists) and the state after preference (where the efficient cause for the second side is absent and a preventative is present). Since the first state makes preference impossible, the second state, which adds a preventative, makes the second side even more impossible. Consequently, the first side becomes necessary (wājib) of occurrence, as there is no middle ground between occurrence and non-occurrence. The proof is termed 'decisive' (qāṭiʿ) and is foundational for the doctrine that the capable agent does not act without a preponderant motive.

Core Idea

The philosophers and theologians who deny rational good and evil present their case in two stages. Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an argument posed by an opponent (likely the Mu'tazila) who claimed that an act of beneficence to a blind stranger in an empty desert proves that actions can be intrinsically good or bad, independent of any self-interest. Al-Razi defines two motives and argues only self-interest is witnessed.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Every observed motive of beneficence reduces to self-interest.

Al-Razi challenges the notion that humans can act purely for the benefit of others. Through a vivid thought experiment, he argues that even the most apparently selfless acts are driven by the motive of need—a desire to achieve personal benefit or avoid personal harm. This passage is central to his critique of ethical objectivism.

The mutakallimun debated whether a capable agent can prefer one action over another without any reason. Commentary
This passage presents two opinions among the mutakallimun (theologians) regarding the issue of preponderance (tarjīḥ) of one action over another by a capable agent (qādir). The first group concedes that preponderance without a preponderating factor (murajjiḥ) is impossible, but they claim that when a preponderating factor is present, the action becomes more likely (awlā) to occur, yet not to the point of necessity (wujūb). The second group holds that preponderance without a preponderating factor is not impossible for the capable agent. They give examples: a person choosing between two cups of drink, two loaves of bread, or a fleeing person at a fork in the road. In each case, the agent chooses one option without a discernible preponderating factor. They argue that if we insist on a preponderating factor, then the action would become necessary once it is present, eliminating the distinction between a necessitating cause (mūjib) and a capable agent. Since this distinction is known necessarily, we must admit that the capable can give preponderance to one possible act over another without a preponderating factor.

They give examples: a person choosing between two cups of drink, two loaves of bread, or a fleeing person at a fork in the road. In each case, the agent chooses one option without a discernible preponderating factor. They argue that if we insist on a preponderating factor, then the action would become necessary once it is present, eliminating the distinction between a necessitating cause (mūjib) and a capable agent. Since this distinction is known necessarily, we must admit that the capable can give preponderance to one possible act over another without a preponderating factor.

Two Refutations of Mu'jib bi al-Dhat
Objection and two refutations of mu'jib bi al-dhat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the mutakallimun refute the idea that God is a necessary cause (mu'jib bi al-dhat) through two distinct arguments based on the impossibility of an eternal world and the absurdity of infinite regress.

Core Idea
God acts by choice, not by necessity — an eternal world is impossible.

In this passage, an objector tries to salvage the notion of God as a necessary cause (mu'jib bi al-dhat) by appealing to the possibility of the world's pre-eternal possibility. The mutakallimun answer with two powerful refutations: the first combines the impossibility of an effect lagging behind its cause with the impossibility of an eternal world; the second shows that if God were a necessary cause, we would face an absurd infinite regress of creations. Read carefully to see how they dismantle the objection.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the opening hypothetical (fa'in qīla) carefully. The objector presents a dilemma: either the world was possible in pre-eternity (al-azal) or not. If possible, the world would be eternal; if impossible, the cause could still exist without effect because the recipient's state matters.
Notice the objector tries to use the recipient's possibility to allow a necessary cause without an eternal effect.
2
Analyze the first answer
The first refutation argues that both the effect's delay and the world's eternity are impossible. Since both cannot be true, the necessary cause cannot have existed in pre-eternity. Therefore, the eternal agent must be an agent by choice, not by necessity.
This answer establishes that the world's eternity is impossible, so any cause that would necessitate it cannot be present.
3
Analyze the second answer
The second refutation grants for argument's sake that pre-eternity might be an obstacle, but then shows that the obstacle is removed at the moment of creation. This leads to an infinite regress: for any time before creation, the world must have already existed, which is absurd.
This reductio demonstrates that mu'jib bi al-dhat leads to an infinite chain of prior creations.
4
Synthesize the conclusion
Both answers converge on the same point: God cannot be a necessary cause; He must be a free agent by choice (fa'il bi al-ikhtiyar). The objection fails because it cannot avoid the absurd consequences.

Commentary
This passage is an objection and refutation regarding the possibility of God being mu'jib bi al-dhat. The objector argues: The world was either possible in pre-eternity (al-azal) or not. Al-Razi's Third Proof: Conditionality Proves Distinction Between Foreknowledge and Present Knowledge
Al-Razi presents a proof based on conditional relations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Razi's argument that foreknowledge and present knowledge are distinct because of their different conditional relations to the object's existence.

Core Idea
What is conditioned cannot be identical to what is unconditioned.

Al-Razi presents his third proof for the distinction between foreknowledge and present knowledge. He builds on a simple rational principle: if one thing depends on a condition and another does not, they cannot be the same. Applying this to knowledge, he shows that foreknowledge is not conditioned by the present existence of the object, while knowledge of present existence is. Therefore they are different. He even strengthens the argument by showing they are opposites.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the rational principle
Al-Razi starts with an evident principle: a thing conditioned by a condition is different from a thing not conditioned by that condition. Also, a thing existing now is different from a thing that will exist later.
This is the foundation of the entire proof.
2
Apply the principle to foreknowledge and present knowledge
Foreknowledge (knowledge that something will exist) is not conditioned by the object's present existence; in fact, it is incompatible with it. Present knowledge (knowledge that it exists now) is conditioned by the object's present existence. Therefore, they are different.
Notice the contrast in conditioning.
3
See the incompatibility and absurdity of identity
Foreknowledge exists now and vanishes when the object exists; present knowledge does not exist now and arises when the object exists. They behave like opposites. Claiming they are identical is like saying one opposite is the other, which is absurd.
This strengthens the proof to a level of impossibility.

Al-Razi's Refutation of the First Objection on the Multiplicity of Knowledge
Al-Razi refutes the objection that knowledge does not multiply with multiple knowns.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Razi's two-pronged refutation of the objection that knowledge does not change when knowns change.

Core Idea
Knowledge either conforms to its object or relates to it—both entail multiplicity and change.

This lesson examines Al-Razi's response to a key objection against the multiplicity of knowledge. The objection claimed that since knowledge does not multiply with multiple knowns, it also does not change when knowns change. Al-Razi provides two compelling arguments: first, he denies the premise by showing that knowledge indeed multiplies; second, even conceding the premise, he attacks the logic of the objection. This passage sharpens our understanding of the nature of knowledge as either a form or a relation.

The Fourth Proof: Knowledge of Future vs Present Existence
Author presents a deductive proof using correspondence theory of knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth proof that knowledge of future existence is necessarily different from knowledge of present existence, based on the correspondence between knowledge and its object.

Core Idea
Different objects of knowledge demand different forms of knowledge.

Sanusi now presents a crisp logical argument: if knowledge is a mental form that matches its object, then two objects with different meanings must be known by two different forms. Since 'will happen' and 'is happening now' are necessarily different in meaning, the knowledge of a future event cannot be the same as the knowledge of that event when it becomes present.

If the objects differ, their corresponding forms must differ. Therefore, the knowledge of future existence cannot be the same as knowledge of present existence.
