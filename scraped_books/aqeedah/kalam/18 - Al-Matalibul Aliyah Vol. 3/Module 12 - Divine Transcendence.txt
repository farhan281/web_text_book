Second Objection: Purpose Implies Imperfection
Philosophers present an argument and rebuttal against divine purpose.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the philosophers argue that God acting for a purpose would imply imperfection, and how they rebut the counterargument that God acts to benefit others.

Core Idea
Acting for a purpose implies God is deficient and perfects Himself through the act.

This lesson examines the second objection from the philosophers against the view that God acts for a purpose. The objection claims that any agent acting for a purpose is deficient in himself and perfects himself by achieving that purpose, which is impossible for God. It then anticipates the response that God acts to benefit others, showing that this too implies a deficiency through a logical dilemma. By the end, you will grasp the core philosophical challenge.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once for overall sense, then read the translation. Note the logical flow: argument, anticipated objection, rebuttal.
Pay attention to the word 'غرض' (purpose) and 'كمال' (perfection).
2
Identify the structure
The passage has two main parts: (1) The initial argument that acting for a purpose implies deficiency. (2) The anticipated counterargument and the rebuttal using a dilemma about equality.
Notice how the rebuttal is structured as 'if they say... we say...'
3
Understand the core claim
The core claim: If an agent does X for a purpose, then X is needed for some perfection. Without X, that perfection is lacking; with X, it is attained. Thus the agent is deficient without X and perfects himself by X.
This applies to God only if we assume He acts for a purpose.
4
Analyze the dilemma
The rebuttal argues that even if God acts to benefit others, from His perspective the benefit and its absence are either equal or not. If equal, no reason to prefer; if not equal, the preferred state is better for Him, bringing back the original charge.
This forces the opponent to either abandon the idea or accept imperfection.

Commentary
This passage is the second objection from the philosophers. It argues that if God acts for a purpose, He would be lacking something that is attained by the act, thus implying imperfection. The structure: Any agent acting for a purpose must suffer a deficiency that the act remedies; thus God would be dependent on creation for His perfection, which is impossible. The objection then anticipates a counterargument that God acts to benefit others, not Himself. The philosophers respond with a dilemma: From God's perspective, the benefit to others and its absence are either equal or not. If equal, there is no reason to prefer one, so the act is not genuinely motivated. If not equal, then God has a preference, which implies that the desired state (with benefit) is better for Him, bringing back the original charge of imperfection. Thus, the concept of God acting for beneficence is unsustainable.

The Third Objection: Infinite Regress in Specifying Time and Place
A dilemma argument against divine purpose for creation's time and place.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' dilemma against the Ash'ari view of divine purpose: why specifying a time and place for creation leads to either infinite regress or an unmotivated act.

Core Idea
Specifying the time of creation traps purpose in an infinite regress.

The philosophers challenge the idea that God created the world for a purpose by focusing on the 'when' and 'where' of creation. If the universe began at a particular moment in infinite time, that moment must have been chosen for some reason. But all moments are alike, so any reason would need another reason, leading to an infinite regress. The alternative—no reason at all—contradicts the very notion of purposeful action. This is the third objection in a series against the Ash'ari position.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dilemma
The passage presents a choice: either the specific time and place were chosen for a wisdom (reason) or not. Read carefully and identify both horns.
The dilemma is the backbone of the argument.
2
Explore the first horn: infinite regress
If there was a wisdom for that specific time, then because all times are similar, that wisdom itself requires a cause, leading to an endless chain. Consider why this is impossible in kalam.
Infinite regress (تسلسل) is rejected as impossible.
3
Explore the second horn: no motive
If no reason, then God's act was without a preponderating motive (داعية مرجحة). This would contradict the principle that any contingent act requires a motive.
This horn is even worse as it undermines the whole notion of purposeful action.
4
Connect to the broader debate
The passage ends by promising a fuller treatment later. Think about why this objection is a serious challenge to the Ash'ari view.
This is part of a larger set of objections.

And if the root motive is negated, then the negation of the qualified motive – that is, the motive of spaces – is more necessary. So these are brief points in refuting this statement. And later will come a thorough investigation in mentioning the proofs that the explanation of God's acts and rulings by regard for benefits and purposes is a false statement. And God knows best.

Commentary
This passage is the third objection from the philosophers against the Ash'ari claim that God acts for a purpose. It focuses on the problem of specification: God created the world at a specific time and place, but since time before creation is infinite and space is infinite, all moments and places are alike. The argument proceeds as a dilemma: Either the choice of a specific time and place is due to a particular wisdom inherent in them, or it is not. If it is due to wisdom, then because all times and places are similar, that wisdom itself must be caused by something else, leading to infinite regress (which is impossible). If it is not due to any reason, then the act of creation would be without a motive, contradicting the very principle the Ash'aris used. Hence the philosophers argue that the notion of God acting for a purpose is untenable. The passage concludes by noting that these are brief points and that a fuller refutation will come later, emphasizing that explaining God's acts by considerations of benefits and purposes is invalid.

The Fourth Proof: God Acts by Choice, Not Necessity
A proof that if God were necessitated, the absence of temporal events would entail His non-existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth proof that God cannot be a necessitated cause, and that He must act by choice.

Core Idea
If God were necessitated, then removing the universe would remove God.

This lesson presents a powerful argument: if God were compelled to create the universe, then the non-existence of the universe would imply God's non-existence. Since this is absurd, God must create by choice. The passage walks through the chain of causes and addresses a subtle objection.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Note the conditional statement at the beginning and the conclusion at the end. The middle contains the explanation and an objection-response.
The argument is structured: conditional, entailment, objection, reply.
2
Trace the causal chain
Follow the reasoning from temporal events up to the First Cause. Understand why the removal of an effect implies the removal of its cause if the effect is a necessary concomitant.
This is the core logical move.
3
Understand the objection
Read the opponent's claim: non-existence of the effect does not necessitate the cause's non-existence, but merely reveals prior cessation. See how they distinguish 'necessitate' from 'reveal'.
The objection tries to break the entailment.
4
Analyze the reply
Notice how the author turns the objection: if cessation of the effect reveals prior cessation of the cause, the same reasoning applied upward forces the same impossible conclusion.
The reply strengthens the original proof.

The Fourth Proof: If the Exalted were necessitated by essence, then from the cessation and non-existence of these temporal events would follow the cessation and non-existence of the essence of Allah. This is impossible, so that is impossible. Explanation of the entailment: These temporal events necessarily need a cause, so their cessation would be due to the cessation of their cause, and the cessation of that cause would necessarily be due to the cessation of the causes of those causes, and these considerations continue ascending until they reach the First Cause. So if the effect is a necessary concomitant of the cause, and it is necessarily known that the cessation of the concomitant indicates the cessation of the concomitant entity, then from the cessation of these temporal events follows the cessation of their causes, and from the cessation of their causes follows the cessation of the causes of their causes, and so on until reaching the First Cause, so it would be necessarily concluded that the cessation of these temporal events reveals the cessation of the essence of the First Cause. And it is known that this is impossible and false, so it must be said that the influence of the First Cause on these effects is not in the mode of necessity and concomitance, but in the mode of validity and choice. And this indicates that the God of the world is an agent by choice, not necessitated by essence. If they say: We do not say that the non-existence of the effect necessitates the non-existence of the cause, rather we say that it reveals it, meaning we say: Were it not that the cause first ceased, so that from its non-existence the non-existence of this effect necessarily follows, otherwise this effect would not cease. We say: What you have mentioned is correct, but it does not harm our purpose. For we say: If the non-existence of the effect indicates that the cause first ceased and preceded, so that from its non-existence the non-existence of these effects necessarily follows, then the non-existence of these effects indicates that that First Cause first ceased, and then the mentioned impossibility follows. But if we say that He, the Exalted, influences the existence of the universe in the mode of validity and choice, then the existence of these effects is not a necessary concomitant of His essence, so the non-existence and cessation of these effects would not entail the non-existence of that specific essence.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth proof against the view that God is a necessitated cause (mujib bi al-dhat). It begins by stating the conditional: if God were necessitated, then the removal of temporal events would lead to the removal of God Himself, which is impossible. The proof explains the entailment: temporal events require a chain of causes ending in the First Cause. If the effect is a necessary concomitant (lazim) of its cause, then the removal of the effect indicates the removal of the cause. This would extend up the chain, so the removal of temporal events would imply the removal of the First Cause itself. Since that is impossible, the initial assumption that God acts by necessity must be false; rather He acts by choice (ikhtiyar). The passage then includes an objection from an opponent: they argue that the non-existence of the effect does not necessitate the non-existence of the cause, but merely reveals that the cause must have previously ceased. The author responds by turning the argument: if non-existence of the effect reveals prior cessation of the cause, then the same reasoning applies upward to the First Cause, leading to the same impossibility. Only by asserting that God acts by choice (not by necessity) does the removal of effects no longer imply removal of the cause, since the effects are not necessary concomitants of His essence.

Correction: The author shows that even if the opponent's distinction is accepted, the same upward reasoning still leads to the impossible conclusion. The proof does not rely on a simplistic notion of entailment.

The Fifth Proof: Necessitarianism Leads to the Absurdity of Producing the Eternal
A proof that if God were necessitated, the eternal cause would necessitate an eternal effect, leading to the absurdity of producing what already exists.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the necessitarian view (that God is a necessitated cause) leads to the absurd conclusion that an eternal effect would need to be produced, which is impossible.

Core Idea
If the cause is eternal and necessitated, the effect must also be eternal — but then 'bringing it into existence' is meaningless.

This lesson presents the fifth proof from Sanusi's text, which attacks necessitarianism (the view that God acts without will, like a natural cause). The proof shows that if God were a mere necessitated cause, the universe would be eternal and dependent on Him, but that dependence would be absurd because you cannot bring into existence something that already exists eternally.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the logical progression starting with 'if' (لو) and leading to 'impossible' (محال).
The passage is concise; each sentence builds on the previous.
2
Understand the initial assumption
The proof assumes the opposite of the correct view: that God is a necessitated cause (موجب بالذات) rather than a voluntary agent. Grasp what this means.
A necessitated cause acts by its essence without will or choice.
3
Trace the logical consequence
From the eternity of the cause, the effect must also be eternal. Then the argument shows that this eternal effect would still be dependent on the cause, which is absurd.
The absurdity is called 'takwin al-ka'in' and 'tahsil al-hasil'.
4
Identify the absurdity
Ask yourself: why is it impossible to 'produce the eternal'? The passage explains that an eternal being has no state except continuance, so needing a cause for its existence at any time implies producing what already exists.
This refutes the idea that God acts by necessity.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
The proof concludes that the initial assumption is false, so God must be a voluntary agent who brings the universe into existence from non-existence.
This is a key argument for divine will and creatio ex nihilo.

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth proof. It argues that if God were a necessitated cause (mujib bi al-dhat) rather than a voluntary agent, then because the cause is eternal (qadim), its effect would also be eternal. This would mean that the effect is eternal but still dependent on the cause. The proof states that this is impossible because an eternal being has no state other than continuance (baqa'). If we consider it at any moment as needing the cause, this would imply that something already existing (the eternal effect) still requires a cause for its existence, which is absurd. The absurdity is described as 'producing what already exists' (takwin al-ka'in) and 'acquiring what is already acquired' (tahsil al-hasil). In other words, if the effect is eternal, it never began to exist, so the act of bringing it into existence is nonsensical. Therefore, the initial assumption that God is a necessitated cause is false; He must be a voluntary agent who brings the universe into existence from non-existence.

The Reductio: Essence Cannot Be a Genus
Second proof: reductio against equality of mental form and divine essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why classical theology rejects the idea that God's essence could be a genus with individuals, based on a reductio argument.

Core Idea
If our knowledge of God fully captures His essence, then His essence becomes a genus—impossible.

This lesson presents the second proof of the seventh objection against the claim that we can fully know God's essence. The argument uses a reductio ad absurdum: if the mental form in our minds were identical in quiddity to God's essence, then God's essence would have to be a genus with multiple individuals under it. That contradicts the unanimous consensus that God is absolutely unique and indivisible.

This is a logical necessity if the mental form exactly matches the quiddity.

The consequence is declared false 'by consensus' (باطل بالاتفاق). In classical theology, God's essence is absolutely simple and unique; it cannot be instantiated in multiple individuals.

The Fourth Face: Reductio on God's Need of a Subject
Sanusi presents a reductio ad absurdum against the opponent's analogy between mental form and divine essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the fourth face of the objection uses a reductio ad absurdum to refute the opponent's claim about mental forms and the divine essence.

Core Idea
Properties of mental representations do not transfer to the divine essence.

This passage presents the fourth aspect of the seventh objection, where the author turns the opponent's argument against itself using logical symmetry. The opponent claims that since a mental form of God can coexist with other forms, God's essence can also. The author retorts that if this reasoning were consistent, it would also imply God needs a subject—an absurd conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation

Since the absurd consequence does not follow, the opponent's original claim fails. The analogy is invalid.
The reductio shows that similarity in one respect does not guarantee similarity in all respects.

The Rational Proof: Why God Cannot Be Defined by Mahiyyah
Provides the rational argument that God cannot be defined by essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three possible ways to define something and why only definition by external attributes applies to God, proving that God has no mahiyyah.

Core Idea
Definition by itself is circular, by parts is for composites, so only attributes work for God.

After the Quranic debate, the author provides a rational explication: definition can be by itself, by its parts, or by external matters. The first is impossible, the second applies only to composite beings, so God must be defined by external attributes. This lesson unpacks that logic.

Guided Path

Read the rational explication
Read from Moses' final answer through the end of the passage. Focus on the three types of definition.
2
Understand definition by itself
The passage explains that defining a thing by itself is circular and impossible because it requires prior knowledge.
3
Understand definition by parts
Defining by parts only works for composite entities; God is simple and not composed of parts.
4
Apply to God
Therefore, the only remaining possibility is definition by external matters, i.e., attributes. This supports Moses' answers.

In this passage, the author tackles a common objection against divine wisdom: that avoiding a small evil by omitting a much larger good is itself a great evil. By carefully analyzing the meaning of 'evil', he shows the argument is logically flawed. You will see how the opponent's claim either becomes self-referential or fails to cause harm.

The author deconstructs the meaning of 'evil' (sharr) into two possibilities: (1) leaving good, or (2) the occurrence of pain and harm. He then examines each interpretation. Thus, on both interpretations, the opponent's argument fails. The author concludes the investigation of this line of reasoning.

And when conception is lacking, affirmation of it is impossible. So the opponents must establish for praise and blame an effect beyond what we mentioned, so that we may consider whether it can be established in respect of God Almighty. And know that establishing something else beyond what we mentioned is almost hopeless, for we do not desire praise except because it brings joy, delight, and pleasure. And we do not dislike blame except because it brings grief, sadness, and pain in the heart. If we remove these effects from consideration, we find no effect at all for praise and blame. Complete reflection and meditation reveals the reality of what we mentioned in a way that no intelligent person doubts. And since the outcome of praise and blame reduces to these meanings, and it is established that these meanings cannot occur in respect of God Almighty, it becomes clear that the essence of good and evil (husn wa qubh) cannot be realized in respect of God Almighty. The second aspect of the answer to this question: We establish evidence for the impossibility of the essence of blame occurring in respect of God Almighty. We say: Divinity (al-ilahiyya) is incompatible with everything that entails deficiency and blame. Since His being a god is an attribute necessary for His essence, and it is established that divinity is incompatible by its very essence and identity with everything that entails deficiency, it is established that the realization of the meaning of blame in respect of God Almighty is impossible, by virtue of His very essence and identity. And every judgment that is established as necessarily attaching to a thing by its essence and identity is impossible for it to be caused by a separate cause. Commentary
This passage is the twelfth proof, which argues that God cannot deserve blame. It begins by defining obligation (wujub) as the state of an action whose abandoner deserves blame. Blame (dhamm) is then defined as a statement, action, or omission that causes heartache, disgust, and harm to interests. Since God is beyond such afflictions, He cannot deserve blame. The text then notes that this entails that the very meaning of obligation cannot apply to God. An objection follows: could God be said to deserve blame in a different sense? The answer has two parts. Part one argues that blame and praise are only understood by their psychological effects (grief and joy). No other meaning is conceivable, so the opponent must first prove such a meaning exists. Moreover, people only value praise for its pleasurable effects and avoid blame for its painful effects; if these are removed, praise and blame have no effect. Thus blame cannot apply to God. Part two argues from the nature of divinity (ilāhiyya) itself: divinity is inherently incompatible with any deficiency or blame. Since God’s being a god is necessary, and divinity by its essence excludes deficiency, blame is impossible for God by His very identity. Any necessary attribute cannot be contingent on an external cause. The passage ends abruptly, but the conclusion is clear.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Razi is denying God's justice or that God can hold people accountable.
Correction: Razi is not denying that God judges; he is arguing that the human concept of blame, which involves psychological deficiency, cannot apply to God. God's justice is on a different plane.

Key Takeaway
God's perfection precludes any form of blame, whether by psychological effect or by essence; thus, the very concept of obligation (as commonly understood) cannot apply to Him.
