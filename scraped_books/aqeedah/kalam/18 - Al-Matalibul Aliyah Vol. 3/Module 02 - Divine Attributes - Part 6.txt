Fifth Proof: Eternal Command Leads to Contradiction
Argument that eternal command leads to contradictory commands.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the impossibility of an eternal divine command is demonstrated through the argument that it would apply to all things, resulting in a contradiction.

Core Idea
An eternal command would force everything to be both commanded and forbidden.

This fifth proof focuses on the nature of eternal attributes. The author argues that if God's command were eternal, it would have to relate to all possible objects necessarily. Because the opponents deny rational good and evil, anything could be commanded or prohibited, leading to a logical contradiction—the same act being both ordered and forbidden. Such a contradiction is impossible, so the command cannot be eternal.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage carefully, noting the key terms: al-ṣifa al-qadīma, al-taʿalluq, al-ḥusn wa al-qubḥ al-ʿaqliyyān, ijtimāʿ al-ḍiddayn.
The passage is a single argument; try to trace its logical flow.
2
Understand the premise about eternal attributes
Notice the opening: an eternal attribute necessarily relates to all its possible objects. This is a key principle for the proof.
The author does not argue this principle; he assumes it.
3
See how the opponents' denial of rational good and evil is used
The proof leverages the opponents' rejection of objective moral values. If there is no intrinsic good or evil, then anything can be commanded or prohibited.
This is crucial: the proof does not assume objective morality; it uses the opponents' own view.
4
Follow the conclusion of contradiction
Since command and prohibition would both apply to everything, every act would be simultaneously commanded and forbidden—a direct contradiction, which is impossible.
The impossibility of the consequence shows the impossibility of an eternal command.

الخامس: إن الصفة القديمة الأزلية تكون تعلقاتها بمتعلقاتها أمراً ذاتياً لازماً واجباً. فلو كان أمر الله قديماً لوجب تعلقه بكل ما يصح تعلقه به، لكن الحسن والقبح العقليين باطل عند القائلين بهذا القول، فلا شيء إلا ويصح الأمر به، ولا شيء إلا ويصح عنه، فيلزم تعلق الأمر بكل الأشياء، وتعلق النهي بكل الأشياء، فيلزم كون الأشياء بأسرها مأموراً بها، منهياً عنها، وذلك يوجب اجتماع الضدين، وهو محال.
Fifth: An eternal, beginningless attribute (al-ṣifa al-qadīma al-azaliyya) has its relations to its objects as an essential, necessary, obligatory matter. So if God's command were eternal, it would be necessary that it relate to everything to which it can possibly relate. But intellectual good and evil (al-ḥusn wa al-qubḥ al-ʿaqliyyān) are invalid according to those who hold this view, so nothing exists except that it is possible to command it, and nothing exists except that it is possible to prohibit it. Thus, it would necessitate that command relates to all things, and prohibition relates to all things, and this would necessitate that all things are commanded and prohibited, which entails the combination of two opposites, which is impossible.

Commentary
The fifth proof is based on the nature of eternal attributes. An eternal attribute (al-ṣifa al-qadīma) necessarily has relations to all its possible objects in a necessary way. So if divine command were eternal, it would have to relate to everything that can possibly be commanded. But the opponents reject rational good and evil (al-ḥusn wa al-qubḥ al-ʿaqliyyān), meaning they do not recognize intrinsic moral values; thus anything could potentially be commanded or prohibited. Consequently, the eternal command would apply to all things simultaneously as both command and prohibition—the same act would be both commanded and forbidden. This is a contradiction (ijtimāʿ al-ḍiddayn), which is impossible. Therefore, the command cannot be eternal.

Sixth Proof: Reports Change with the Reported
The author presents an analogy-based proof against the eternity of divine reports.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the sixth proof that divine reports cannot be eternal because they change with the reported, analogous to the change of knowledge.

Core Idea
What changes cannot be eternal—just like knowledge, reports are tied to their objects.

This final proof completes the series of arguments against the eternity of divine reports. The author draws a parallel between knowledge and report, applying the same reasoning used earlier: if knowledge changes with its object, then reports must also change, making them temporal. The passage is concise but dense, building on previous discussions.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the literal translation. Notice the analogy structure.
The last word in Arabic is unclear; it likely means 'impossible'.
2
Identify the analogy
The author compares report (khabar) to knowledge (ilm). In what way do they change? The objects of knowledge (ma'lumat) change, so knowledge changes. Similarly, the object reported about (mukhbar 'anhu) changes, so the report changes.
3
Apply the rule
Anything subject to change cannot be eternal (qadim). The last part states that eternity implies impossibility of non-existence. Connect this to the argument.

السادس: إنا كما بينا: أن العلم بالشيء، يجب أن يتغير عند تغير المعلومات، فكذلك الخبر عن الشيء يتغير عند تغير المخبر عنه، وكل ما يتطرق التغير إليه وجب أن لا يكون قديماً، لأنه ثبت أن ما كان قديماً كان العدم عليه مالاً
Sixth: Just as we have explained that knowledge (al-ʿilm) of a thing must change when the objects of knowledge change, likewise the report (al-khabar) about a thing changes when the thing reported about changes. And everything to which change applies must not be eternal, because it is established that what is eternal, non-existence upon it is impossible [؟].

Commentary
This sixth and final proof draws an analogy between divine knowledge (al-ʿilm) and divine report (al-khabar). Already the author has mentioned (in the second proof's objection) that according to Abū al-Ḥusayn al-Baṣrī, knowledge changes as the known object changes. Here, the author applies the same reasoning to reports: a report about a temporal event must itself be temporal, because it changes when the event changes. For example, the report 'Zaid prayed' is true after the prayer, but before the prayer, that report is not yet applicable. Thus, the report is subject to change (taghayyur). Anything that is subject to change cannot be eternal (qadīm), because that which is eternal is necessarily unchanging and cannot cease to exist. The last part of the text is somewhat unclear but likely asserts that non-existence (al-ʿadam) is impossible for what is eternal.

The Third Argument Against Eternity of Divine Command
Sanusi presents a dilemma argument against the eternity of Allah's command.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical dilemma used to argue that Allah's command cannot be eternal, and how it relies on the principle that the eternal cannot cease to exist.

Core Idea
An eternal command would make discharge impossible or require cessation—both impossible.

This lesson examines the third argument advanced by theologians against the claim that Allah's command (al-amr) is eternal. The argument takes the form of a dilemma: if the command were eternal, then after obedience it would either persist (preventing discharge) or cease (but the eternal cannot cease). We will work through the logic step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Try to grasp the overall argument before diving into details.
The passage is short but dense; read slowly.
2
Identify the two horns of the dilemma
Find the two possibilities presented: either the obligation remains after the action, or it ceases. Notice how each leads to an impossibility.
The argument assumes the command is eternal and tests the consequences.
3
Understand the first impossibility
If the obligation remains, then Zayd can never discharge it, even after performing the prayer countless times. This contradicts the nature of obligation.
4
Understand the second impossibility
If the obligation ceases, then it would have to come to an end. But the eternal (qadim) cannot cease to exist according to the theologians.
This is a key principle in kalam.
5
Draw the conclusion
Since both options are impossible, the command cannot be eternal. This refutes the view that Allah's command is eternal.

And if that obligation does not remain, then it has ceased to exist, and the theologians insist that the eternal cannot cease to exist. So, since this obligation and this command are known, it follows necessarily according to their statement that it cannot be eternal.

This passage presents the third argument against the eternity of Allah's command (al-amr). The argument is structured as a dilemma: if Allah's command to Zayd to pray the dawn prayer is eternal, then either it remains after Zayd performs the prayer, or it does not. If it remains, then Zayd can never be free of the obligation — performing it countless times would not discharge it, which is absurd and contradicts the nature of obligation. If it does not remain, then it ceases to exist. But the theologians (al-mutakallimun) hold that the eternal (al-qadim) cannot cease to exist. Therefore, the command cannot be eternal. The argument uses a concrete example to show the impossibility of an eternal command given the realities of obligation and cessation.

Fourth Argument: Abrogation Implies Temporal Origin
Sanusi presents a brief deductive argument linking abrogation to temporality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the possibility of abrogation serves as a rational proof that Allah's command is temporal and not eternal.

Core Idea
Abrogation proves commands are temporal because what can cease cannot be eternal.

This lesson presents the fourth rational argument from classical kalam: the concept of abrogation (naskh) demonstrates the temporal origin of divine commands. By analyzing its definitions and implications, we see that a ruling which can be removed or ended cannot be eternal, thus refuting the view that Allah's commands are uncreated and pre-eternal.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
First, read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the concise, deductive structure: a premise (abrogation is permissible), definitions, and a logical conclusion.
2
Identify the two definitions of abrogation
The passage defines abrogation in two ways: removal of a ruling after its establishment, or expiry of its time period. Both definitions share the idea that the ruling ceases to exist after having existed.
Recognize that regardless of which definition is used, the key point is cessation.
3
Trace the logical implication
The conclusion follows: if a ruling can cease to exist, it cannot be eternal (qadim). Therefore, the divine command subject to abrogation must be temporal (hadith). This is a classic kalam argument from contingency.
The argument relies on the consensus that abrogation is permissible for divine commands.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth rational argument: the permissibility of abrogation (al-naskh) proves that Allah's command is temporal. Abrogation is defined as either the lifting of a ruling after it has been established, or the end of the time period of that ruling. In either case, the ruling ceases to exist after it had existed. But the eternal (al-qadim) cannot cease to exist. Therefore, since the ruling can be abrogated, it cannot be eternal. The argument relies on the theological consensus that abrogation is valid for divine commands, which implies change and temporality.

The Fifth Argument: Eternal Command Necessitates Contradiction
Shows that an eternal command would have to apply to all things, causing contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Ash'ari argument against an eternal divine command uses the necessity of an eternal attribute's relation to conclude that it would lead to impossible contradiction.

Core Idea
An eternal command would have to command and forbid everything, which is impossible.

This passage presents a key argument against the eternity of God's command. It uses the principle that an eternal attribute necessarily relates to all possible objects. If God's command were eternal, it would have to apply to everything, leading to a contradiction. Let's examine the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Begin by reading the Arabic text and its translation. Notice the logical structure: a premise about eternal attributes, a conditional statement, and a conclusion of impossibility.
2
Identify the key premise
The argument starts with the claim that an eternal attribute (al-sifa al-qadima) necessarily relates to all its possible objects. This is a foundational Kalam principle.
If the command is eternal, its relation to everything it could possibly command becomes necessary.
3
Understand the consequence
Given the denial of rational good and evil (al-husn wa al-qubh al-'aqliyyan), anything can be commanded and anything can be prohibited. So the eternal command would relate to all things, and the eternal prohibition would also relate to all things.
This yields a contradiction: everything is both commanded and forbidden.
4
Grasp the impossibility
The conjunction of two opposites (ijtima' al-diddayn) is impossible. Therefore, the command cannot be eternal. This refutes the claim that God's command is an eternal attribute.
The argument is a reductio ad absurdum.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
الخامس : إن الصفة القديمة الأزلية تكون تعلقاتها بمتعلقاتها أمراً ذاتياً لازماً واجباً . فلو كان أمر الله قديماً لوجب تعلقه بكل ما يصح تعلقه به ، لكن الحسن والقبح العقليين باطل عند القائلين بهذا القول ، فلا شيء إلا ويصح الأمر به ، ولا شيء إلا ويصح عنه ، فيلزم تعلق الأمر بكل الأشياء ، وتعلق النهي بكل الأشياء [؟]، فيلزم كون الأشياء بأسرها مأموراً بها ، منهياً عنها ، وذلك يوجب اجتماع الضدين ، وهو محال .

So if Allah's command were eternal, its relation to everything to which it could be related would be necessary. Thus, the command would relate to all things, and the prohibition would relate to all things, resulting in all things being both commanded and forbidden, which necessitates the conjunction of two opposites, and that is impossible.

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth argument: an eternal attribute (al-sifa al-qadima al-azaliyya) necessarily relates to all its possible objects in a necessary and essential way. If Allah's command (amr) were eternal, it would have to relate to every possible object of command. But according to those who deny rational good and evil (al-husn wa al-qubh al-'aqliyyan), anything can be commanded and anything can be prohibited. Consequently, the command would apply to all things, and the prohibition would also apply to all things, making everything both commanded and prohibited — a conjunction of opposites (ijtima' al-diddayn), which is impossible. Therefore, the command cannot be eternal.

The Sixth Argument: Change in Speech Proves Its Temporality
Sixth rational argument comparing knowledge and speech to prove speech is temporal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the sixth rational argument uses the analogy of changing knowledge to demonstrate that divine speech is temporal and cannot be eternal.

Core Idea
Change in speech proves it is not eternal.

This passage presents the final argument from the first set of rational proofs. It draws a comparison between knowledge and speech: just as knowledge changes with its objects, speech changes with what it reports. Anything subject to change cannot be eternal, leading to the conclusion that divine speech is created in time, not coeternal with Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the translation. Notice the analogy and the conclusion.
The passage has a clear logical flow.
2
Identify the analogy
Find where the author compares knowledge and speech. Understand the parallel: both change when their objects change.
The analogy assumes that knowledge changes (a premise the opponent may accept).
3
Understand the principle of change
The author states that anything subject to change cannot be eternal. Reflect on why this is so: the eternal cannot cease to exist.
The Arabic word 'مالاً' is unclear; it likely means 'impossible' or 'non-existence'.
4
Note the conclusion
The conclusion explicitly states that both talab (demand) and khabar (report) cannot be eternal, regardless of whether talab is identified with will.
This wraps up the six arguments against the eternity of speech.
5
Avoid the common misunderstanding
Remember that the argument does not deny that Allah's knowledge is eternal; it only uses knowledge as an analogy to prove speech is temporal.
The focus is on speech, not knowledge.

Second Proof: Disjunctive Syllogism on the Locus of Speech
Second rational proof using disjunctive syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second rational argument for the pre-eternity of divine speech, which uses a disjunctive syllogism to eliminate all possible ways speech could be originated, thus proving its pre-eternity.

Core Idea
If speech were created, it would have to subsist somewhere; all options are impossible, so speech must be pre-eternal.

The second argument builds on the consensus that God is speaking. If He speaks by an attribute, that attribute cannot be part of His essence, so it must be speech (kalam). The argument then examines the three possible loci for originated speech: in God's essence, in another, or not in any locus. Each is shown to be impossible, forcing the conclusion that speech is pre-eternal.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument's structure
Read the Arabic and translation for the second argument. Notice it presents a disjunction: either God speaks by essence or by speech. The first is rejected by consensus, so we focus on speech.
2
Understand the trichotomy for originated speech
If speech were originated (hadith), it would have to inhere in God's essence, in another being, or in no locus at all. These are the only possibilities.
This is a standard classification in kalam.
3
Examine each impossibility
Each option is ruled out: in God's essence would mean accidents in God (impossible); in another would allow attributing other deficits like ignorance to God; not in a locus is impossible for an attribute.
The argument uses analogical reasoning for the second case.
4
Conclude pre-eternity
Since all three are impossible, the speech cannot be originated; it must be pre-eternal.

Commentary
This passage presents four rational arguments for the pre-eternity (qidam) of divine speech (kalam). The first argument is based on the principle that for any attribute that can be predicated, the subject must be described either by that attribute or its opposite. Since God is living, speaking is possible; its opposite (muteness/silence) is a deficiency, impossible for God, so He must be speaking in pre-eternity. Third Proof: Speech as Attribute of Perfection
Third rational proof using perfection-deficiency classification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third rational argument for the pre-eternity of divine speech, which classifies speech as either a perfection or a deficiency and concludes that since God has it, it must be a perfection and therefore possessed pre-eternally.

Core Idea
If speech were a deficiency, God would not have it; since He does, it is a perfection and must be eternal.

The third argument is based on the duality of attributes: every attribute is either a perfection (kamāl) or a deficiency (naqṣ). If speech is a perfection, God must always have it, because being without a perfection is a deficiency. If speech were a deficiency, God could not have it at all. Since we agree God has speech, it must be a perfection that He possesses in pre-eternity.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument's classification
Read the Arabic and translation for the third argument. Note the initial division: speech is either an attribute of perfection or deficiency.
2
Consider the first branch
If speech is perfection, then God must be described by it always (eternally). The absence of a perfection in God would be a deficiency.
This is a necessary implication.
3
Consider the second branch
If speech is deficiency, it cannot be attributed to God at all, because deficiency is impossible for Him.
4
Reach the conclusion
Since we agree God is described with speech (based on revelation and consensus), it cannot be a deficiency; it must be a perfection, and therefore God has it pre-eternally.
The argument relies on a previously established agreement that God speaks.

Fourth Proof: Comparative Excellence
Fourth rational proof using comparative excellence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth rational argument for the pre-eternity of divine speech, which uses the principle that a speaker is more perfect than a non-speaker, and if God were not speaking in pre-eternity, a human speaker would be more perfect than God—an impossibility.

Core Idea
A speaker is greater than a non-speaker; if God were not speaking eternally, a human would surpass Him.

The fourth argument is based on self-evident knowledge (al-ʿilm al-ḍarūrī) that a speaker is more perfect and better than a non-speaker. Since every human is a speaker, if God were not a speaker in pre-eternity, the human would have a perfection that God lacks, making the human more perfect than the pre-eternal God—an obvious absurdity. Therefore, God must be described with speech in pre-eternity.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument's premise
Read the Arabic and translation for the fourth argument. The first premise is that we have necessary knowledge (ḍarūrī) that a speaker is more perfect than a non-speaker.
This is considered intuitive.
2
Apply the premise to humans
We are humans and we are speakers. So we possess the perfection of speech when we exist.
3
Apply to God in pre-eternity
If God were not a speaker in pre-eternity (i.e., before creation), then at that time He would lack speech while humans (who later exist) would have it. This would make the human more perfect than God at the moment of the human's existence.
4
Conclude the impossibility
That comparison is impossible (muḥāl). Therefore, God must have been a speaker in pre-eternity, meaning His speech is pre-eternal.

If God were not speaking in pre-eternity, then the state of each one of us at the time of his existence would be better and more perfect than God, exalted, when He was in pre-eternity, and that is impossible. So it is established that He, exalted, is described with speech in pre-eternity.

Commentary
This passage presents four rational arguments for the pre-eternity (qidam) of divine speech (kalam). The first argument is based on the principle that for any attribute that can be predicated, the subject must be described either by that attribute or its opposite. Since God is living, speaking is possible; its opposite (muteness/silence) is a deficiency, impossible for God, so He must be speaking in pre-eternity. The second argument uses a disjunctive syllogism: we agree God is speaking; either by essence (invalid by consensus) or by speech. That speech, if originated, must subsist in His essence, in another, or in no locus. All three are impossible: in His essence because it would entail accidents in God; in another because that would allow other deficiencies like ignorance to be attributed to God through an external substrate; without a locus by consensus. Also, an attribute must inhere in its subject. Therefore speech is pre-eternal. The third argument classifies speech as either perfection or deficiency. If perfection, God must always have it (absence of perfection is deficiency). If deficiency, He must never have it. Since He does have it, it is perfection, so He has it pre-eternally. The fourth argument uses comparative excellence: a speaker is more perfect than a non-speaker. We are speakers; if God were not a speaker in pre-eternity, we would be more perfect than the pre-eternal God, which is impossible.

The second objection raises a regress: if baqa' is an attribute, then that attribute itself is permanent, requiring another attribute of permanence for it, ad infinitum. The refutation examines two possibilities: (1) the permanence of the attribute is by itself – then the attribute would be self-necessary and more deserving of being an independent essence than God, which is absurd; (2) its permanence is by something else – that something must be either the essence (circular) or something else (regress). The Relational Character of Attributes
Sanusi presents the fourth proof that attributes as relations are distinct from the essence.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Attributes as relations depend on the essence and are not the essence itself.

This relation is an attribute that depends on the essence and is relative to something else. By considering these characteristics, we see that the attribute must be additional to the essence, not identical to it.

Fifth Proof: Distinct Concepts of Knowing and Powerful
Fifth proof uses distinct concepts to show attributes not identical to essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the distinctness of the concepts of knowing and powerful shows that the attributes are not identical to the divine essence.

Core Idea
Different concepts cannot be the same thing as the essence.

The fifth proof from al-Matalib al-Aliyah builds on a simple observation: we can think of a powerful being that is not knowing, and a knowing being that is not powerful. This shows that the concepts of 'powerful' and 'knowing' are different. If each were identical to God's essence, then the essence would have to be two different things at once – an impossibility. Let's see how this argument unfolds.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its literal English translation. Pay attention to the key terms: مفهوم (concept), ماهية (quiddity), حد (definition).
The translation is literal; the commentary will clarify the reasoning.
2
Identify the two premises
The passage gives two main premises: (1) The concept of powerful is different from the concept of knowing (because one can exist without the other). (2) Therefore their quiddities and definitions are different.
A 'quiddity' is the essence or nature of something.
3
Understand the logical conclusion
If each attribute (knowing, powerful) were identical to the essence, then the one essence would simultaneously be both – making it two different things, which is impossible. Therefore, the attributes are distinct from the essence.
The argument does not deny God is both knowing and powerful; it only denies that the concepts are identical to the essence.

Sixth Proof: Known Objects Are Not All Possible
Sixth proof uses the fact that some known objects are not possible to show knowledge and power differ.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the scope of God's knowledge and power differ, and why the impossibility of the necessary and impossible being objects of power but not knowledge demonstrates that knowledge and power are distinct attributes.

Core Idea
God knows even the impossible, but His power extends only to the possible.

This proof uses a simple logical argument to distinguish between two divine attributes. By examining what God knows versus what He has power over, we see that the necessary and impossible are known but not possible, which shows that knowledge and power are not the same. This lesson walks through the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the hypothesis
Start by grasping the conditional hypothesis: what would follow if knowledge and power were identical?
2
Examine the implication
If the hypothesis were true, then everything known would be within God's power.
3
Observe the counterexample
Notice that the necessary-of-existence and impossible-of-existence are known to God but are not within His power.
The necessary necessarily exists, the impossible cannot exist.
4
Draw the conclusion
Since the counterexample contradicts the implication, the hypothesis is false, and knowledge and power are distinct.

Seventh Proof: Knowledge Persists After Power Ceases
Seventh proof shows that after a possible object exists, power ceases but knowledge persists.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the seventh proof demonstrates that knowledge and power are distinct divine attributes by showing that after a possible object exists, power ceases while knowledge remains.

Core Idea
After existence, an object is known forever, but can no longer be powered.

The seventh proof is a short but powerful argument. It focuses on what happens to a possible object once it comes into existence. The author shows that the object remains known but is no longer within God's power (since bringing it into existence again is impossible). This simple observation requires that knowledge and power be different attributes of God. Read the proof carefully to follow the logic.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the key terms like 'tahsil al-hasil' (attaining what is already attained).
2
Identify the key premise
The proof relies on the principle that bringing into existence something already existing is impossible (tahsil al-hasil). This makes the object no longer an object of power.
3
Understand the conclusion
Since the object remains known after ceasing to be possible, knowledge and power must be distinct attributes.
This conclusion is stated directly: 'the truth of this negation and affirmation necessitates the difference between knowledge and power.'
