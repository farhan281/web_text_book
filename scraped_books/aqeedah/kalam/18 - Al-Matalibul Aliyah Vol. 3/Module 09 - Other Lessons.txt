Ten Arguments for Infinite Degrees of Benefits and Harms
The author lists ten arguments showing that the ranks of benefits and harms are disordered and infinite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the ten proofs that establish the infinite and disordered nature of the ranks of benefits and harms.

Core Idea
Benefits and harms have no fixed ranks; they vary by intrinsicness, intermediaries, mixtures, and perspectives.

The author presents ten aspects (wujuh) to prove that the ranks of benefits (masalih) and harms (mafasid) are not fixed but disordered and infinite. These arguments build on distinctions between intrinsic and incidental desirability, the role of intermediaries, experiential variability, and contextual dependence. By working through these proofs, you will see why moral evaluation cannot be reduced to simple calculations.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's enumeration come through before you interpret.
Notice that the author begins with the third aspect, implying earlier ones are not included here.
2
Identify the key distinction
The first aspect (third) establishes the distinction between what is desired intrinsically (pleasure) and incidentally. Understand how this creates initial disparity.
This is foundational for the subsequent arguments.
3
Track the layers added by each aspect
Each subsequent aspect introduces a new factor: strength of incidental good (fourth), number of intermediaries (fifth), multiple faces of one thing (sixth), mixtures (seventh), distance vs proximity (eighth), persistence effects (ninth), and personal variation (tenth).
Observe how the arguments progress from simple to more complex considerations.
4
Notice the conclusion
The final sentence states that by these ten aspects it is established that the ranks of benefits and harms are disordered and infinite. Reflect on how each aspect contributes to this conclusion.
5
Reflect on the significance
Consider why proving the infinitude of these ranks matters for theology and ethics. It shows that divine wisdom and human moral reasoning must account for this complexity.
Think about how this impacts our understanding of good and evil.

Commentary
This passage is an enumerated argument consisting of ten aspects (wujuh) demonstrating that the degrees (maratib) of benefits (masalih) and harms (mafasid) are not fixed but are disordered and infinite. The author begins with the third aspect (the first two are not included here). He first restates a foundational principle: what is desired intrinsically is pleasure and joy, and what is disliked intrinsically is pain and grief; all else is desired or disliked only indirectly. Since desire for the intrinsic good outweighs desire for the incidental good, the ranks of benefit and harm vary accordingly. The fourth aspect points out that an intrinsic good can sometimes be outweighed by an incidental good if the incidental good is much stronger, as in the example of finding valuable coins versus eating a single morsel. The fifth aspect considers the number of intermediaries (wasait) leading to an intrinsic good. Desire is stronger when intermediaries are few, but quality can counterbalance quantity of intermediaries. The sixth aspect appeals to experience: one thing can be beneficial in one respect and harmful in another; the more beneficial aspects it has, the more desirable it is, and these aspects are not limited. The seventh aspect notes that events in the world are not simple but mix, generating infinite degrees of mixtures, hence infinite degrees of benefit and harm. The eighth aspect briefly mentions that distance may be better than proximity, but high proximity may be better than small distance. The ninth aspect observes that persistence in an action can produce love in some and boredom in others, showing variability. The tenth aspect notes that what is delightful to one person may be disliked by another, and the same person may find something delightful via one means and disliked via another. The conclusion: these ten aspects establish that the ranks of benefits and harms are disordered and infinite.

Motives and Deterrents as Belief, Not Reality
The author distinguishes motives from actual benefits/harms and argues for their infinite variety.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that motives and deterrents are based on belief in prevailing benefits or harms rather than on objective benefits or harms themselves, and that this leads to an infinite variety of possible motives.

Core Idea
Motives are based on belief, not objective reality — and that opens the door to infinite possibilities.

In this passage, the author adds a crucial refinement to the earlier discussion of the ten aspects. He emphasizes that what drives human action is not the actual benefit or harm, but the belief that such benefit or harm will occur. This shift from objective to subjective has profound consequences: since thoughts and the essences of rational souls are infinitely varied, the motives and deterrents that arise are likewise beyond any finite list.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central claim
Read the first part of the passage where the author distinguishes motives from actual benefits and harms. Note the key phrase: 'motives and deterrents are the belief in the attainment of prevailing benefits or prevailing harms.'
This claim reframes the entire discussion from objective to subjective.
2
Understand the first reason for infinity
The author states that the transitions of thoughts and imaginations are not limited. Consider how this implies that beliefs about benefit/harm can vary endlessly.
Think of how many different scenarios you can imagine.
3
Understand the second reason for infinity
The author asserts that rational souls differ in essence, and their states vary accordingly. This means that even the same objective situation can be perceived differently by different souls.
This emphasizes inherent individual differences.
4
Synthesize the conclusion
Combine the two reasons: when you consider the infinite possible beliefs and the diverse soul essences, the resulting motives and deterrents become infinite. Reflect on why this matters for understanding human action.
The passage ends mid-sentence, noting that bodily states are subordinate to these infinite factors.

Vision vs. Impression: Razi's Three Proofs
Razi presents three proofs that vision is not mere impression.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Fakhr al-Din al-Razi argues that vision is not merely a physical impression in the eye but a distinct cognitive act of disclosure.

Core Idea
Vision is disclosure, not impression.

Razi begins by acknowledging that the eye is indeed affected by colors and images, but he then challenges the view that vision is nothing more than that physical impression. Through three powerful proofs—including the impossibility of the hemisphere of the world fitting in the eye—he demonstrates that vision must be a separate, cognitive state. Let's walk through his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
First, read the Arabic passage and its full English translation. Notice the structure: Razi starts with examples of eye affection, then presents two opposing views, and finally gives three proofs.
Pay attention to the technical terms like انطباع (impression) and انكشاف (disclosure).
2
Identify the two views
In the passage, Razi mentions two groups: one says the difference between seeing and not seeing is due to affection; the other says affection is present but vision is a different state. Understand this debate before moving to the proofs.
The second group is Razi's own position.
3
Analyze the three proofs
Focus on each proof: (1) the hemisphere of the world cannot be impressed but is seen; (2) impression is reception, while vision is disclosure; (3) looking at the sun strengthens impression but weakens vision. Reflect on how each proof shows vision is not mere impression.
The third proof is empirical and contrasts intensity of impression with clarity of vision.
4
Synthesize the conclusion
After studying the proofs, summarize Razi's conclusion: vision (ibṣār) is a state of disclosure and manifestation, distinct from the physical process of impression. Consider why this matters for understanding perception.
This distinction preserves the cognitive nature of vision.

Commentary
This passage is a philosophical investigation into the nature of vision (ibṣār). It begins with empirical examples showing that the eye is affected by colors and images, then presents a debate: some attribute the distinction between seeing and not seeing to this affection, while others argue that the affection is present but visual perception is a different state. It then provides three proofs that vision (ru'ya) is distinct from mere impression (inṭibā'). The first proof uses the example of seeing the hemisphere of the world, which cannot be physically impressed in the eye, so vision must be something else. The second proof distinguishes between impression (which is a form of reception and representation) and vision (which is disclosure and manifestation). The third proof uses the example of looking at the sun: the eye impression strengthens but vision weakens, showing they are different. The Nature of Hearing: Physical Impact vs. Distinct Perception
Razi begins an investigation into hearing, presenting two views on its nature.

Learning Objective
