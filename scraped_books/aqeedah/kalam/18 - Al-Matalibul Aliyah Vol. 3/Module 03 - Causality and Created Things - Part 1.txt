Definition of al-Qadir and Motives
Defines al-qadir and explains the concept of motives (dawāʿī) in human action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of al-Qadir and how motives (dawāʿī) explain human action and inaction.

Core Idea
Capability is not absolute; it depends on motives.

This passage from al-Matālib al-ʿĀliya lays the groundwork for understanding divine and human agency. Before we can discuss God's power, we must first understand what it means to be a capable agent (al-qādir) in general. The author provides a precise definition and then unpacks the concept of motives (dawāʿī) that drive action or refraining.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice the author's precise style.
The passage starts with 'المقدمات الأولي'.
2
Identify the definition of al-qadir
Find the definition given by the author. Notice that it links capability to possibility of acting or not acting.
The definition uses 'يصح منه أن يفعل تارة، وأن لا يفعل أخرى'.
3
Understand the three cognitive states
The author mentions three states: knowledge (علم), conjecture (ظن), and belief (اعتقاد). These give rise to motives.
These are the sources of decisive inclination or aversion.
4
Trace the path from motive to action
Follow the causal chain: cognitive state → decisive inclination/aversion → action/inaction (if limbs are sound).
The condition of sound limbs is crucial.
5
Grasp the meaning of al-dāʿī
The passage concludes that this whole cognitive process is what is meant by motive.
Motive is not just a feeling but a cognitive judgment.

First introductions: On the definition of al-Qadir. The best thing said about it is: that it is he from whom it is possible to do at one time and not to do at another, according to different motives (dawāʿī). Commentary
This passage defines the term 'al-qadir' (the capable/powerful agent) and then explains the concept of 'al-dawāʿī' (motives) that underlie human action. The passage begins by stating the best definition of al-qadir: it is the one from whom it is possible to perform an action at one time and to refrain from it at another, depending on different motives. This definition emphasizes that capability is not absolute but relative to motives. Then it explains what motives are: when a person has knowledge, strong conjecture (ẓann), or belief that a certain action contains a predominant benefit, one of these three cognitive states gives rise to a decisive inclination (mayl jāzim) in the heart towards the action. If the limbs are sound, the action follows from that inclination. Conversely, if the person knows, thinks, or believes that the action contains a predominant harm, this cognitive state causes a decisive aversion (nufra jāzima) from the action, leading to leaving it (tark) if the limbs are sound. The passage concludes that this cognitive state—whether it leads to inclination or aversion—is what is meant by 'al-dāʿī' (the motive). The argument follows the order: definition of qadir, then definition of motive, then explanation of how motive produces action or inaction.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Power and motive together cause action; each alone is insufficient.

This passage presents the second investigation of the discussion on causality. It outlines four logical possibilities for how power (qudra) and motive (dāʿī) interact to produce an action. After enumerating them, the author argues for the first view—that the combination of both is the effective cause—and defends it against an objection that might arise from a definitional misunderstanding.

Commentary
This passage is the second investigation from the earlier discussion. It begins by stating a key principle: when the motive calls to an action, the action necessarily occurs. Then it presents four possible positions (iḥtimālāt) regarding the causal role of power (qudra) and motive (dāʿī) in producing the action. The four are: (1) The effect is the sum (majmūʿ) of power and motive. (2) The effect is power conditioned (bi-sharṭ) by the motive. (3) The motive is a complete cause that makes the power of the agent a complete cause for the action; thus the final cause (motiv) is a complete cause for the efficient cause (the agent's power). (4) Power and motive together cause complete preparedness (istiʿdād tāmm) for the action, but the actual occurrence is from higher causes (al-asbāb al-ʿulwiyya, possibly celestial or divine causes). After listing these four, the author states that the closest (al-aqrab) view is the first: the effect is the sum of power and motive. The evidence is that power alone is insufficient for effect (as previously established by showing that without motive action is impossible), and motive alone is also insufficient. But when they combine, the effect occurs. Hence the effect is the sum. Then an objection is raised: if power is defined as having the capacity to effect, then saying it is alone not suitable for effect is contradictory because it implies power is not effective. The author answers: we define power not as inherent effectiveness but as the state of the limbs such that when the motive is added, the action occurs. So defined, the objection dissolves. The passage follows the order: principle, four possibilities, author's preferred view with evidence, objection, and answer.

The Four Possibilities for What Produces an Action
Enumeration of four possibilities for the cause of action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four distinct views on what combination of factors brings about an action.

Core Idea
An action requires both power and motive, but how they combine is a matter of debate.

The author begins this investigation by listing four possible ways to understand the relationship between power (al-qudra) and motive (al-da'i) in causing an action. These theories range from viewing the combination of both as the cause to seeing the actual coming-into-being as from higher causes. This lesson will help you grasp each of these four positions.

Guided Path
1
Read the enumeration of the four possibilities
Read the Arabic passage listing the four possibilities once without analyzing. Then read the translation to get a general sense.
The possibilities are numbered one through four.
2
Identify the key difference between possibility 1 and 2
Notice that possibility 1 says the effect is the combination of power and motive, while possibility 2 says the effect is power conditioned by motive. Understand the subtle distinction: in possibility 2, power is still the primary effect but with an added condition.
3
Understand the third possibility's reference to final cause
Possibility 3 introduces the idea of the motive as a complete cause (علة تامة) that makes power a complete cause. The author notes that this is why the final cause is said to be complete cause for the cause of the efficient cause.
This is a deeper philosophical concept from Aristotelian causation.
4
Note the fourth possibility's distinction between preparedness and actual occurrence
Possibility 4 separates the preparation (complete preparedness) caused by power and motive from the actual coming-into-being, which is attributed to higher causes.

Commentary
This passage presents a detailed investigation into the cause of action. The author asserts that when a motive (da'i) is present, the action inevitably occurs. He answers that he does not distinguish between parts of a cause and conditions; all existential requisites are equally necessary. He concludes that this discussion does not yet reveal which of the four is actually true.

The Second and Third Proofs: Knowledge, Not Purpose, Drives Action
Two proofs against purpose as cause, establishing knowledge as cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two arguments demonstrating that an agent's knowledge of an act's benefit, not the benefit itself, is what causes the act.

Core Idea
What comes after cannot cause what comes before.

This passage presents two logical proofs that the benefit or goodness of an action is not the real cause of the agent performing it. The author argues that since the benefit occurs after the action, it cannot be the cause; rather, the agent's knowledge of the benefit is what motivates the action. Let's examine the reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is a proof against the necessity of motive for action. The author presents the fifth argument: the phenomenon of slow motion. He analyzes slow motion into two possible explanations: either it consists of discrete states of rest (based on the theory of indivisible atoms) or it is a quality subsisting in the motion (based on the denial of atoms). In the first case, the agent causing the motion is simultaneously causing states of rest without being aware of them, so the act occurs without motive. In the second case, the agent causes both the motion and its slowness without awareness of producing two different types of act, so again the act occurs without motive. The conclusion is that action can occur without the agent being aware of all aspects, thus motive is not necessary.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might assume that slow motion is a single, unified act that the agent is fully aware of.
Correction: The argument shows that slow motion, under both metaphysical interpretations, involves multiple components (states of rest or a quality of slowness) that the agent produces without being aware of them. Thus, not all aspects of an action require conscious motive.

Key Takeaway
The fifth proof uses the example of slow motion to argue that an agent can act without being aware of all components of the action, thereby disproving the necessity of motive.
