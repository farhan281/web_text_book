Why the Ash'ari View Undermines the Argument from Design
Objection that Ash'ari causality undermines the argument from design for God's knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Ash'ari doctrine of habit (al-‘ādah) undercuts the teleological argument from design (al-ihkām wa al-itqān) for God's knowledge.

Core Idea
If causality is mere habit, design proves nothing about the designer's knowledge.

The argument from design observes purposeful structures in creation and infers a knowledgeable Creator. But what if the connection between structures and their functions is not necessary but merely habitual, as the Ash'ari school holds? This passage shows how such a view collapses the argument, making it impossible to claim that organs were created for specific purposes.

First proof: knowledge of essence does not entail knowledge of all
The author presents the first of three proofs against the premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first proof against the premise that knowledge of one essence requires knowledge of all its necessary properties, and how the author uses the threefold classification of relations between essences to build a reductio argument.

Core Idea
If knowing one essence implies knowing all its relations, we would know every essence—which is false.

The author now launches the first of three proofs against the idea that knowing an essence gives us knowledge of its necessary properties. He begins by classifying the possible relations any essence can have with other essences into three categories: necessitating, incompatible, or neutral. Then he shows that if knowledge of an essence entailed knowledge of these relations, we would be forced to know all other essences, which is absurd. This short but powerful argument is a classic reductio ad absurdum.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without interruption, then read the English translation to get the overall argument.
Notice the author's concise style.
2
Identify the three types of relations
The author classifies relations between any essence and others into three categories. Find them in the text and understand each one.
These are: necessitating (mustalzima), incompatible (munafiya), and neutral (la mustalzima wa la munafiya).
3
Follow the conditional reasoning
The author sets up a conditional: if knowledge of an essence implies knowledge of its necessary properties, then from knowing one essence we would know all its relations to other essences.
This is the core of the reductio.
4
Understand the absurd conclusion
Since knowing all relations would mean knowing all other essences (which are infinite), and we do not have such knowledge, the original premise must be false.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Knowledge requires an established known; non-existents cannot be known without circularity.

The third proof in Al-Matalib al-Aliyah targets the claim that God knows non-existent things. It argues that knowledge is a relation needing both terms established, but a non-existent cannot be established without prior knowledge, leading to an impossible circularity.

والوجه الثاني: أن نقول: خالق أفعال العباد إما أن يكون هو الله تعالى، أو العبد. فإن كان هو الله تعالى فحينئذ يكون الخير والشر كله من الله تعالى، وحينئذ يلزمهم ما التزموه علينا. وإن كان هو العبد فحينئذ تكون قدرة العبد صالحة للإيجاد، وإذا ثبت هذا فحينئذ يحتمل في جميع المعجزات أن يكون خالقها غير الله، وعلى هذا التقدير فلا تكون المعجزة دليلا على أن الله خصه بالتصديق. وتمام هذا الكلام، إنما يتم ببيان أن دلائل المعتزلة في أن غير الله لا يقدر على خلق الجسم والحياة ضعيفة، وسيأتي بيان ذلك في باب الجبر والقدر، فيثبت أن الإلزام الذي وردوه علينا، هو بعينه لازم عليهم.
The second aspect: We say: The creator of the actions of servants is either God or the servant. If it is God, then all good and evil are from God, and then what they imposed on us becomes incumbent upon them. If it is the servant, then the servant's power is capable of bringing into existence. And if this is established, then it is possible for all miracles that their creator is other than God. On this supposition, the miracle does not serve as evidence that God singled him out for verification. The complete discussion on this is only completed by showing that the proofs of the Mu'tazila that other than God is not capable of creating bodies and life are weak. This will come later in the chapter on determinism and predestination (al-jabr wa al-qadar). Thus it is established that the objection they directed at us is exactly the same as what is incumbent upon them.

Commentary
This passage presents the second objection, again a dilemma about who creates actions. If God creates actions, then the opponent's accusation about evil being from God applies to them. If the servant creates, then the servant's power is truly productive, which would mean that miracles could be created by someone other than God—undermining their evidential value for prophethood. Al-Razi notes that a full refutation requires showing the weakness of Mu'tazilite arguments that only God can create bodies and life, which he promises to address later in the chapter on predestination. Thus, the objection supposedly directed at him is equally binding on the opponent.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That if God creates all actions, then only the other side (the Ash'arites) has the problem of attributing evil to God.
Correction: Al-Razi shows that if God creates all actions, the same problem applies to the Mu'tazila as well, because they also believe God is the creator of all things — the dilemma is reciprocal.

Checkpoint
According to the second objection, what is a consequence if the servant is the creator of his own actions?
A) The servant becomes a prophet.
B) Miracles could be created by someone other than God.
C) Evil cannot exist in the world.
D) God must be unjust.

Key Takeaway
The creator-of-actions dilemma forces the Mu'tazila into the same difficulty they accuse Muslims of: either God creates evil or miracles lose their evidential force.
