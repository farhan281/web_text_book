The Eighth Argument: Reductio ad Absurdum
Al-Matalib al-Aliyah presents the eighth argument: a reductio ad absurdum against commanding the impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the impossibility of commanding what cannot be done is demonstrated through the absurdity of sending prophets to inanimate objects.

Core Idea
Mockery is impossible for God, so commanding the impossible is absurd.

This lesson presents the eighth and final argument from Al-Matalib al-Aliyah against the claim that divine knowledge prevents action. The argument uses a reductio ad absurdum: if commanding the impossible were allowed, then sending prophets to inanimate objects would also be allowed—but that would be mockery, which is beneath God. By highlighting this absurd consequence, the argument shows that the original premise is false.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to get the gist.
Notice the structure: 'if... then... and it is known that...'
2
Identify the absurd consequence
The passage says that if commanding the impossible were permitted, it would be permissible to send prophets to inanimate objects. List the three specific things that would be permissible.
They are: sending prophets, sending down books, and sending angels.
3
Understand why this is mockery
Inanimate objects cannot understand or obey. Such actions would be mockery and derision of religion. Ask yourself: why is that necessarily absurd?
This reveals the implicit premise: God does not engage in mockery.
4
Grasp the reductio structure
The argument shows that a certain premise (commanding the impossible is permissible) leads to an absurd conclusion (mockery). Since the conclusion is false, the premise must be false.
This is a classic logical proof by contradiction.
5
Connect to the broader debate
This argument refutes the claim that divine knowledge prevents action. If knowledge prevented action, then God could command the impossible. But that leads to mockery, which is impossible for God. Therefore, knowledge does not prevent action.
Human responsibility (taklīf) remains valid.

Commentary
This passage presents the eighth and final argument against the claim that divine knowledge prevents action. The argument is a reductio ad absurdum that highlights the inconsistency of the opponent's position. It states that if it were permissible for God to command what is impossible (as the opponent's view implies), then it would also be permissible for God to send prophets to inanimate objects, send down scriptures to them, and dispatch angels to convey religious obligations to them repeatedly. The absurdity is evident: inanimate objects cannot understand or obey. Such actions would be mockery (sukhriya) and derision (istihza') of religion. Since God is exalted above such behavior, the premise that divine knowledge prevents action (leading to impossible commands) is false. Therefore, knowledge and report do not prevent action, and human responsibility remains valid.

Refuting the Mu'tazilite Proof from Prophethood
Al-Razi answers the second proof that prophethood requires rational good and evil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Mu'tazilite attempt to prove rational good and evil through prophethood is logically invalid, specifically due to circular reasoning or lack of dependence.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazilite proof from prophethood is self-defeating: it either depends on rational good and evil or not—either way it fails.

The Mu'tazilites argued that prophethood and the divine promise of reward and threat of punishment presuppose that acts are inherently good or evil in reason. Therefore, they claimed, denying rational good and evil would undermine prophethood itself. Al-Razi responds with a crisp logical dilemma that exposes the flaw in this reasoning. Let's examine his refutation and see how it dismantles the opponent's case without rejecting the truth of prophethood.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation once to get the overall argument. Notice the conditional structure 'either...or...'.
The author uses a common refutation technique: reducing the opponent's claim to a dilemma.
2
Identify the two branches of the dilemma
The passage presents two possibilities: either prophethood depends on rational good and evil, or it does not. Mark these two branches and see what conclusion follows from each.
The first branch leads to circularity (دور), the second makes the proof irrelevant.
3
Understand the logical force of the conclusion
Ask yourself: why is circularity impossible? And if there is no dependence, why does attacking rational good and evil not harm prophethood? This shows the proof fails either way.
The final sentence states that the Mu'tazilite argument cannot establish what it claims.

وأما الحجة الثانية: فالجواب عنها: أن نقول: القول بصحة النبوات وصحة الوعد والوعيد، إما أن يكون مبنياً على القول بصحة الحسن والقبح في العقل، وإما أن لا يكون مبنياً عليه، فإن كان الأول فحينئذ يكون القول بصحة الحسن والقبح في العقل أصلاً للعلم بصحة الوعد والوعيد والنبوات. فلو أثبتنا العلم بصحة الحسن والقبح بالبناء على إثبات الوعد والوعيد والنبوات لزم إثبات الأصل بالفرع، وذلك يوجب الدور، وهو محال. وإما إن لم يكن القول بصحة الوعد والوعيد وبصحة النبوات محتاجاً إلى العلم بصحة الحسن والقبح فحينئذ لا يلزم من القدح في الحسن والقبح العقليين القدح في الوعد والوعيد، وفي النبوات. فيثبت بهذا البرهان: أن الذي ذكروه لا يصح الاستدلال به على إثبات هذا المطلوب
As for the second proof: the answer to it is that we say: The statement affirming the validity of prophethoods and the validity of promise and threat, either it is based on the statement affirming the validity of good and evil in reason, or it is not based on it. If it is the first, then the statement affirming the validity of good and evil in reason is a foundation for knowledge of the validity of promise and threat and prophethoods. If we were to establish knowledge of the validity of good and evil by building upon the establishment of promise and threat and prophethoods, that would entail establishing the foundation by the branch, and that necessitates circularity, which is impossible. And if the statement affirming the validity of promise and threat and the validity of prophethoods is not in need of knowledge of the validity of good and evil, then from impugning rational good and evil, it does not follow that promise and threat and prophethoods are impugned. So by this proof it is established: that what they mentioned cannot validly be used as evidence to establish this desired conclusion.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazilite argument that prophethood and the promise of reward and threat of punishment presuppose the rational good and evil of acts. The author presents a dilemma. Either (1) the validity of prophethood and the promise and threat depend on rational good and evil, in which case using prophethood to prove rational good and evil would be circular (establishing the foundation by its branch), or (2) they do not depend on it, in which case refuting rational good and evil does not harm the validity of prophethood. In either case, the Mu'tazilite proof fails. The conclusion is that this argument cannot serve as evidence for the existence of rational moral values.

Turning the Tables: Al-Razi's Twofold Ilzam Against the Mu'tazila
Al-Razi responds to the Mu'tazilite charge of circularity with a twofold ilzam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Razi refutes the Mu'tazilite charge of circularity by applying the same ilzam (necessary consequence) back to them through two logical dilemmas.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazilite position leads to either determinism or unreliable miracles.

The Mu'tazila claimed that rejecting rational good and evil would destroy the basis for prophethood. Al-Razi accepts they may use this as an ilzam, but then shows that their own position leads to even worse consequences. In this lesson, we explore two aspects of his counter-ilzam: one concerning the role of motives in action, the other concerning who creates human acts.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the two ilzams
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that it begins with the Mu'tazilite objection, then Al-Razi's response, and then two distinct arguments (الوجه الأول and الوجه الثاني).
The word 'إلزام' means a necessary consequence that forces the opponent into a contradiction.
2
Analyze the first dilemma: dependence on motives
The first aspect presents a dilemma: either an act depends on a motive or not. Follow each branch and see how both lead to conclusions the Mu'tazila reject—either divine coercion (jabr) or miracles losing their evidence value.
This shows that the Mu'tazilite position on human agency is self-defeating.
3
Analyze the second dilemma: creator of acts
The second aspect also presents a dichotomy: either God creates human acts, making Him the source of evil, or the servant creates them, granting the servant power to create miracles. Both options undermine Mu'tazilite claims.
Al-Razi notes that a full discussion of the Mu'tazilite proofs that only God creates will come later.
4
Reflect on the overall strategy
Consider why Al-Razi uses ilzam (a necessary consequence) rather than a direct proof. Notice how he accepts the opponent's method but shows it applies more forcefully to them.
This is a common method of refutation in kalam called 'mu'āraḍa' or counter-argument.

فإن قالوا : هب أن هذا لا يصح جعله دليلاً لإثبات هذا المطلوب ، إلا أنه يصح ذكره على سبيل الإلزام ، من حيث إنه لما كان القول بصحة النبوات فرع على القول بالحسن والقبح كان القدح في هذا الأصل يوجب القدح في ذلك الفرع . فنقول : إذا اقتنعتم بالإلزام ، واعترفتم بأنه ليس ببرهان . فنقول : هذا الإلزام وارد عليكم أيضاً من وجهين : الأول : وهو أن الفعل إما أن يتوقف على الداعي ، أو لا يتوقف عليه ، فإن توقف عليه ، لزم أن يتوقف صدور الفعل عن العبد على حصول الداعي ويلزم منه القول بالجبر ، وأن يكون فعل العبد فعلاً لله وعلى هذا التقدير فيكون الخير والشر من الله وعلى هذا التقدير فيلزمكم ما الزمتموه علينا ، وإما أن يتوقف الفعل على الدواعي ، فحينئذ يصح أن يخلق الله المعجزة لا لشيء من الدواعي أصلاً . وحينئذ لا تدل المعجزة على الصدق ، وحينئذ يلزم القدح في نبوة جميع الأنبياء والرسل . وهذا الإلزام قوي . والوجه الثاني : أن نقول : خالق أفعال العباد إما أن يكون هو الله تعالى ، أو العبد . فإن كان هو الله تعالى فحينئذ يكون الخير والشر كله من الله تعالى ، وحينئذ يلزمهم ما التزموه علينا . وإن كان هو العبد فحينئذ تكون قدرة العبد صالحة للإيجاد ، وإذا ثبت هذا فحينئذ يحتمل في جميع المعجزات أن يكون خالقها غير الله ، وعلى هذا التقدير فلا تكون المعجزة دليلاً على أن الله خصه بالتصديق . وتمام هذا الكلام ، إنما يتم ببيان أن دلائل المعتزلة في أن غير الله لا يقدر على خلق الجسم والحياة ضعيفة ، وسيأتي بيان ذلك في باب الجير والقدر ، فيثبت أن الإلزام الذي وردوه علينا ، هو بعينه لازم عليهم
If they say: 'Granted that this cannot be made a proof to establish this desired conclusion, but it can be mentioned by way of entailing necessary consequence (ilzam), in that since the statement affirming the validity of prophethoods is a branch of the statement affirming good and evil, then impugning this foundation necessitates impugning that branch.' We say: If you are content with ilzam and you admit that it is not a proof, then we say: This ilzam is also incumbent upon you from two aspects. The first: The act either depends on the motive (da'i) or does not depend on it. If it depends on it, then the occurrence of the act from the servant must depend on the presence of the motive, and that leads to the statement of coercion (jabr), and that the servant's act is an act of Allah, and on this assessment good and evil are from Allah, and on this assessment that which you made incumbent upon us is enforceable against you. And if the act does not depend on motives, then it is possible that Allah creates a miracle without any motive at all, and then the miracle does not indicate truthfulness, and then it necessitates impugning the prophethood of all prophets and messengers. And this ilzam is strong. The second aspect: We say: The creator of the acts of servants either is Allah Almighty or the servant. If it is Allah Almighty, then good and evil all come from Allah Almighty, and then that which they made incumbent upon us is enforceable against them. And if it is the servant, then the servant's power is capable of origination, and when this is established, then it is possible that the creator of all miracles is other than Allah, and on this assessment the miracle is not evidence that Allah distinguished him with confirmation. And the completion of this discussion is only achieved by explaining that the proofs of the Mu'tazila that other than Allah cannot create bodies and life are weak, and its explanation will come in the chapter on jabr and qadar. So it is established that the ilzam which they brought against us is necessarily incumbent upon them.
Source pages p. 357

Commentary
This passage is an objection-response section. The Mu'tazila object that even if their argument is not a proof, it can be used as an ilzam (a necessary consequence) to force the opponent into contradiction: if prophethood depends on rational good and evil, then rejecting the latter destroys the former. The author responds by accepting the ilzam form but then shows that the same ilzam is applicable to the Mu'tazila from two aspects. The first aspect: If actions depend on motives (dawa'i), then human agency is indistinguishable from divine coercion (jabr), making God the real agent and thus the source of both good and evil—undermining their own claim. If actions do not depend on motives, then God could create miracles without any reason, rendering them unreliable as signs of prophethood, thus destroying prophethood altogether. The second aspect: If God creates human acts, again good and evil come from God, which they deny. If man creates his own acts, then man's power is sufficient to create, and miracles might be created by other than God, so they cannot be evidence for prophethood. Thus the Mu'tazilite position is self-refuting. The author ends by noting that a full discussion of the Mu'tazilite proofs that only God creates will come later.
