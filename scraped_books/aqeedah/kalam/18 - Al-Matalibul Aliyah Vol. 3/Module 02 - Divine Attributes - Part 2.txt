Why Ash'ari Atomism Undermines Design
The objector argues that Ash'ari's atomism renders design (ihkam wa itqan) meaningless.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Ash'ari doctrine of occasionalist atomism appears to undermine the teleological argument from design, and how the objector uses a trilemma to argue that each atom could independently possess life.

Core Idea
If atoms can have life without bodies, then design—which depends on structure—is meaningless.

This passage presents a powerful internal critique of the Ash'ari position. The objector does not deny God's existence but argues that the Ash'ari denial of necessary causal connections between structure and function makes it impossible to speak of 'design' in the usual sense. The argument proceeds by first explaining Ash'ari's view that individual atoms can possess life and attributes, then using a trilemma to support that view, and finally concluding that if structure has no real effect, then the design argument collapses.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection's main claim
Begin by reading the opening lines where the objector states that 'ihkam wa itqan' is unintelligible on Ash'ari's school. Notice the bold assertion.
This sets the stage for the entire argument.
2
Understand Ash'ari's position on atoms and life
The passage explains that according to Ash'ari, bodily structure is not a condition for life. An individual atom can possess life, knowledge, power, hearing, and sight. This is a radical claim.
This is the key premise that leads to the problem.
3
Analyze the trilemma proof
The objector presents Ash'ari's proof: if a structure is composed of parts, either each part is receptive to life only if others are (circular), or some are receptive and others not (requires a distinguisher), or each is independent. The first two are invalid, so the third holds: each atom is independently receptive.
This is a formal logical argument.
4
Consider the reply about habit
When confronted with the fact that destruction of the body ends life, Ash'ari replies that this is merely a habitual occurrence (ʿada), not a necessary connection. This is the occasionalist position.
This is crucial: it denies any real causal link between structure and function.
5
Draw the conclusion for the design argument
If structure has no effect, then we cannot say that the eye is created for sight or the stomach for digestion. The very concept of design (ihkam) is lost. Therefore, the Ash'ari cannot use design as evidence for God's knowledge.
This is the final devastating implication.

Commentary
This passage is a fifth objection against the Ash'ari school's ability to use the argument from design. The objector begins by stating that the concept of 'skill and perfection' (al-ihkam wa al-itqan) is unintelligible on Ash'ari's principles. He explains that Ash'ari denies that bodily structure (al-binya) is a necessary condition for life and its attendant attributes (knowledge, power, hearing, sight). Instead, Ash'ari holds that a single indivisible atom (al-jawhar al-fard) can possess these attributes, making it the most perfect creature. Ash'ari's proof is based on a trilemma: if a structure is composed of parts, then either (1) each part is receptive to life only if the others are, leading to circularity; or (2) some parts are receptive and others not, requiring a distinguishing factor between similar things; or (3) each part is independent in receptivity. Since (1) and (2) are invalid, (3) is established, implying each atom can have life independently. To the obvious objection that destruction of the body ends life, Ash'ari replies that this is merely a habitual occurrence (ʿada) ordained by God, not a necessary connection. The objector then draws the devastating conclusion: if structure and organs have no real effect on functions, then terms like 'for the sake of' (li-ajl) lose meaning—the eye is not created for seeing, the stomach not for digestion. This negates the very concept of design (ihkam), which depends on the fitting of means to ends. Hence, the Ash'ari cannot use design as evidence for God's knowledge. This is a powerful internal critique of occasionalist atomism.

Commentary

Since the conceptions are present, their concomitants are known. The same applies to oppositions and neutral relations. Thus, once conceptions are established, all judgmental knowledge about them—concomitance, opposition, or their negation—follows. The author concludes that this method is more precise and less susceptible to objections.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Sanusi presents the fourth argument against the view that God can be mentally pictured. The opponent argues that if a mental image of God can coexist with other images, then God's essence itself should be able to coexist with other essences. Sanusi turns the tables: he applies the same logic to dependence, forcing the opponent to accept an absurd conclusion—that God would be dependent on a locus and become an accident.

Tenth Objection: Infinite Regress in God's Knowledge of Self
Objection that knowledge of objects does not necessitate self-knowledge, with two arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational objection against the necessary knowledge of God's essence and the two supporting arguments focused on perception duality and infinite regress.

Core Idea
Knowledge of something does not automatically entail knowledge of self without absurdity.

This passage presents the tenth objection in a series of challenges to the claim that God must know His essence. The objector argues that even if God knows other things, there is no necessary connection to self-knowledge. Two powerful arguments are presented: one based on the impossibility of a single faculty perceiving both particulars and universals, and the other on the impossibility of an infinite regress of knowledge levels. Understanding this objection is key to grasping the subtleties of divine knowledge in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection statement
Read the passage fully. Note that the objector concedes God knows other things but questions why He must know Himself. The objection cites the principle that knowing something implies knowing that one knows, which is said to necessitate self-knowledge.
2
Analyze the first argument
Focus on the first counterargument (الوجه الأول). It examines a simple judgment 'This person is human' and shows that the subject is particular and the predicate is universal. According to the Master, perceiving particulars requires a corporeal faculty, and universals an immaterial one—so a single faculty cannot do both, challenging the claim.
This argument targets the idea that knowledge of self follows from knowledge of objects.
3
Understand the second argument
The second counterargument (الوجه الثاني) points out that if knowing something requires knowing that one knows, then an infinite chain of levels of knowledge emerges. Since all possible ranks must be actual for the Necessary Being, this leads to infinite simultaneous knowledges and causal chains, which is impossible.
This is the infinite regress argument, a classic reductio ad absurdum.
4
Connect to the Necessary Being
Consider why the infinite regress is specifically impossible for God: because in the Necessary Being, every possible state must be actual, so the regress would be actualized, leading to absurdity.
This shows the objection is tailored to the nature of wājib al-wujūd.
5
Reflect on the overall objection
Step back and see how both arguments together undermine the claim that knowledge of objects implies self-knowledge. The objector does not deny God's knowledge but questions the necessity of self-knowledge based on these rational difficulties.
This prepares the ground for the answer (not included here).

Third Proof: Soul's Potential and Actual Knowledge
Avicenna's third argument for God's knowledge via soul and fire analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Avicenna's argument from the soul's potential and actual knowledge leads to affirming God's actual knowledge through self-subsistent forms.

Core Idea
If a form makes the soul knowing when indwelling, a self-subsistent form must be actually knowing.

This passage presents a philosophical proof for God's knowledge, drawn from Avicenna's work al-Mabda' wal-Ma'ad. It uses an analogy with the soul's acquisition of knowledge and the heating of fire to argue that the forms of knowledge, if self-subsistent, must be actually knowing. By following this reasoning, we see how the First Principle's knowledge is necessary and actual.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the full translation to grasp the argument's flow.
Notice the parallel structure: soul:form :: fire:heat.
2
Identify the core analogy
The argument draws an analogy between the soul becoming actual intellect through an abstract form and fire becoming hot through heat. Identify both sides.
The form (صورة) and heat (حرارة) play analogous roles.
3
Understand the conditional reasoning
If the form in the soul makes it potentially knowing, then a self-subsistent form would be more deserving of being actually knowing. The same applies to heat: if self-subsistent, it would be more deserving of being hot.
The conclusion points to God's knowledge as self-subsistent and actual.

The Infinite Regress Objection to Reflexive Knowledge
Second objection that knowledge requires infinite levels of meta-knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the argument of infinite regress refutes the claim that knowledge requires knowing that one knows.

Core Idea
Knowledge cannot require knowing that you know—it leads to an impossible infinite chain.

This passage presents a powerful reductio ad absurdum against the idea that knowledge entails reflexive awareness. If knowing something means you must also know that you know it, then an infinite series of higher-order knowledges would be required. For God, who must possess every possible perfection as actual, this would entail an actual infinity of distinct knowledges and causal relations—which is deemed impossible. Thus, the initial assumption must be false.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its English translation. Note the logical flow: a conditional premise, a regress, an application to God, and a conclusion of impossibility.
The argument uses a hypothetical 'if... then' structure.
2
Identify the key premise
Find the premise that triggers the regress: 'if someone who knows something must know that he knows it'. This is the assumption being refuted.
This premise is about necessary reflection or self-awareness in knowledge.
3
Trace the infinite regress
The argument says that if knowledge requires knowledge of knowledge, then there is a third level (knowledge of knowing that one knows), and so on ad infinitum. Understand why this regress is problematic.
The regress is not just infinite but 'infinite times'—endlessly repeated for each object of knowledge.
4
Apply the Necessary Existence axiom
The argument then invokes a key principle: everything possible for the Necessary Existence (God) must be actual. Since all those levels of knowledge are possible, they would have to be real in God.
This principle is based on God's perfection—no potentiality in Him.
5
Grasp the impossibility conclusion
Accepting the regress leads to an actual infinity of knowledges and causes/effects, which is considered impossible (muḥāl). Therefore, the original premise is false.
This is a standard reductio ad absurdum argument.

Soul and Form Argument for God's Knowledge
The Shaykh's proof from the soul's potential to actual intellect through forms.

Core Idea
God's knowledge is proven through the soul's journey from potential to actual intellect, but only by weak analogy.

This lesson explores the third method from the Shaykh for proving that God is knowing. The argument uses the soul's transformation from potential to actual intellect through abstract forms as a model, then applies the principle of priority to posit God as the most deserving of actual intellect. However, as we will see, this proof is critiqued for yielding only weak conjecture.

However, the author (the commentator) critiques that this proof only yields weak conjecture (ẓann ḍaʿīf) because it is based on analogy and the principle of priority, not certainty.

Circularity in the Distinction of Being Knower and Known
Sanusi refutes the second attempted solution by exposing circular priority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why using a conceptual distinction between 'knower' and 'known' to explain God's self-knowledge leads to circularity and is therefore invalid.

Core Idea
A conceptual distinction alone cannot ground Divine self-knowledge without circularity.

In this passage, the author refutes the second attempt to answer the original objection about God knowing Himself. The author shows that this leads to a vicious circle, because the distinction itself depends on knowledge. Let's see how.

1
Understand the premises

Since the second attempt leads to an impossibility, it fails. The objection remains unanswered.

B) Because awareness is a relational concept
C) Because awareness is a composite
D) Because awareness is eternal

Key Takeaway
The second and third objections highlight that relational and derivational aspects of the concepts prevent them from being identical to the simple essence or to each other.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you think about God's attribute of 'All-Knowing,' do you instinctively reduce it to a mere concept in your mind, or do you recognize it as a real, relational attribute that you can never fully grasp? How does this lesson expose your tendency to intellectualize God rather than stand in awe?
?
If logical priority shows that intellect depends on intellection, and you claim to know God through your intellect, are you willing to admit that your knowing is derived and limited? Will you let this humble your theological certainty?
?
The passage concludes 'And God knows best.' Do you truly live as though God's knowledge is greater than your own? In what area of your life do you need to surrender your intellectual pride and say 'Allahu a'lam'?
