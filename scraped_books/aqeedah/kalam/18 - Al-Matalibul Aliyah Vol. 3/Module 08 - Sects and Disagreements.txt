The Mu'tazila on Taklif and Compulsion
The author reports and begins to refute the Mu'tazila claim that taklif is invalid when the agent is compelled.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Compulsion does not automatically negate obligation; the classification of motives is more complex.

The author now turns to specific branches of the earlier principle. The first branch addresses a key Mu'tazila claim: that religious obligation (taklif) becomes invalid when a person has a pure motive toward action or abandonment. The author immediately declares this view weak, hinting at a more nuanced classification of human states. In this lesson, we will examine the Mu'tazila argument and the author's opening refutation.

The Incoherence of Hesitant Motives in Taklif
Refutes Mu'tazila claim that taklif requires hesitant motives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Mu'tazila condition of hesitant motives for valid taklif is self-refuting according to the author's argument.

Core Idea
Hesitation is just sequential compulsion; taklif needs no such condition.

The Mu'tazila argued that moral obligation (taklif) is only valid when a person has hesitant motives, to avoid compulsion. In this passage, the author dismantles that claim by showing that what we call hesitation is merely a rapid succession of decisive motives, each of which compels the agent. Thus, the supposed state of hesitation never actually exists, and the Mu'tazila condition is both unnecessary and impossible.

Identify the Mu'tazila claim and the author's refutation
The Mu'tazila claim is that taklif is only valid when motives are hesitant. The author dismisses it as weak and then gives a deeper argument.
The refutation has two parts: a reference to six divisions (not detailed here) and a logical analysis of hesitation.
3
Understand the critique of hesitation
The author argues that hesitant motives are just alternating decisive motives: at each moment, the agent has a single decisive motive and is compelled. Thus, the condition of hesitation is never actually present.
This shows that the Mu'tazila condition is meaningless because it can never be satisfied.

Change in Knowledge: The Second Group's View
Second group's doctrine on change in knowledge with causal reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second group's argument for change in knowledge and who among early theologians held this view.

Core Idea
Knowledge changes as its object changes: new object, new knowledge.

This lesson presents the view of a group of theologians who argued that knowledge necessarily changes when its object changes. They build a careful causal argument linking the knower's essence to knowledge conditioned by the existence of the known. We'll examine their reasoning and see who held this position.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation once. Let the structure of the argument become familiar before analyzing it.
2
Identify the causal framework
Notice the three key components: the specific essence (dhāt makhṣūṣa), the condition (sharṭ al-iqtiḍāʾ), and the necessity of knowledge.
The essence is the knower's essence; it necessitates knowledge only conditionally.
3
Trace the argument steps
Follow the logic: when the known exists, condition is fulfilled → knowledge arises. When the known ceases, condition is lost → knowledge ceases and new knowledge arises.
This is a continuous causal chain, not a static state.
4
Note the proponents
The passage attributes this view to Abu al-Husayn al-Basri (Mu'tazili), Jahm ibn Safwan, and Hisham ibn al-Hakam. Recognize these names as key early theologians.

Commentary
This passage introduces the doctrine of the second group of theologians (al-farīq al-thānī) who maintain that knowledge changes. They argue that when the object of knowledge (al-maʿlūm) disappears, the knowledge of it disappears, and a new knowledge of the new object arises. Their detailed reasoning: a specific essence (dhāt makhṣūṣa) – which is the knower's essence – necessitates knowledge of the known under the condition that the known exists. When the known exists in that specific manner, the condition for necessitation (sharṭ al-iqtiḍāʾ) is fulfilled, and knowledge necessarily arises from that essence. When the known ceases, that condition is lost, and a condition for another knowledge is realized, resulting in the disappearance of the first knowledge and the emergence of another. This view is attributed to Abu al-Husayn al-Basri (a Mu'tazili scholar), as well as Jahm ibn Safwan and Hisham ibn al-Hakam from the early theologians.

Author presents and refutes the Ash'arite argument

Core Idea
The Ash'arite argument fails by wrongly equating divine life with human life.

The author presents the first weak way of the mutakallimun, the Ash'arite argument, and then refutes it with two objections. The Ash'arites argue that God, being living, must have hearing and sight to avoid deficiency. The author questions the meaning of 'living' and the analogy with human life. This lesson examines the argument and its critique.

Commentary
This passage presents a famous theological proof against the Mu'tazili doctrine of divine justice. The proof takes the form of a hypothetical story about a woman with three sons: one dies as an adult believer, one dies as an adult disbeliever, and one dies as a child. Finally, the disbelieving brother objects: if God knew the child would disbelieve and spared him, why did God not spare the disbeliever as well, when God knew he would disbelieve? This objection exposes an inconsistency in the Mu'tazili position: if God spares the child based on foreknowledge of disbelief, He should spare the adult disbeliever too. Commentary
This passage presents an attempted rebuttal to the previous story by Abu al-Husayn al-Basri, a later Mu'tazili scholar. He records the debate in his book 'al-Qadar' and offers two distinct answers, reflecting two schools within Mu'tazilism regarding the principle of al-aslah (the most beneficial). The first answer is from those who do not hold that God is obligated to do the most beneficial in this world. According to this view, God's obligation (taklif) is a favor that He bestows on some but not others. God obligates the believer and the disbeliever, giving them the chance to earn reward. The child who dies before puberty simply does not receive that favor, and it is not incumbent upon God to give it to him. As for the disbeliever, he has no right to demand to be spared because God's favor is not obligatory. The objection regarding the child being spared due to foreknowledge is dismissed because God is not required to spare anyone based on foreknowledge; He does what He wills. The second answer is from those who affirm that God must do the most beneficial (wujub al-aslah). They argue that God obligated the believer because He knew that would be beneficial and that it would not cause harm to others. However, if the child had been obligated and reached puberty, it would have caused harm (mafsada) to some obligated ones—likely meaning that the child would have disbelieved and caused harm to others or that his obligation would entail problems. Thus, God's not obligating the child is actually the most beneficial. This answer differentiates between the believer (whose obligation brings no harm) and the child (whose obligation would bring harm). Abu al-Husayn concludes that with these answers, the original argument against the Mu'tazila does not stand.

Both answers attempt to show that God's action of preserving Iblis is compatible with acting for the best interests of humanity.

Mu'tazilite Arguments for Rational Good and Evil
Describes the Mu'tazilite two types of arguments for rational good and evil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite claim that rational good and evil are known by necessity in the witnessed world and then transferred to the unseen, as well as their second argument about intrinsic aspects.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazilites anchor moral knowledge in immediate rational intuition from common human experience.

The Mu'tazilites argued that moral values are rationally known, not just revealed. They put forward two main arguments: one based on necessity (ḍarūra) in everyday judgments, and another based on inherent aspects. In this passage, you will see how they defend the first argument with concrete examples, setting up a key debate in Islamic theology.

The Tenth Chapter: On the place of the Mu'tazilites' words regarding the assertion of rational approval and disapproval. Know that the people (the Mu'tazila) have two types of speech in this position: The first: the claim of necessity in the establishment of good and evil in the witnessed (world). Commentary
This passage presents the Mu'tazilite position on rational good and evil (taḥsīn al-‘aql wa taqbīḥ). It begins by stating that the Mu'tazila have two types of arguments at this point. The first argument claims necessity (ḍarūra) in the knowledge of good and evil in the witnessed world (al-shāhid), and then transfers this judgment to the unseen (al-ghā’ib) based on the principle that what is established in the witnessed must also apply to the unseen. The second argument asserts that goodness and evil are intrinsic to actions due to aspects inherent in them, and this is proven by rational evidence. The passage then elaborates on the first argument, which is the one most relied upon. It argues that anyone with a sound intellect automatically knows that a benefactor deserves thanks and praise, and a wrongdoer deserves blame and censure. Two examples are given: a person rescued from a tyrant naturally feels gratitude toward the rescuer, and a person whose face is dirtied by another distinguishes between the thrower and the dirt, instinctively finding blame for the thrower appropriate. This argument relies on immediate rational intuition (badīha) as the foundation for moral judgments.
