Definition and Division of Quantity (al-Kamm) in Philosophy
An editor defines al-kamm (quantity) and divides it into continuous and discrete.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how philosophers define quantity (al-kamm) and classify it into continuous and discrete.

Core Idea
Quantity is a fundamental accident that accepts division and measurement, divided into continuous (with a common limit) and discrete (without).

This footnote from an editor introduces the philosophical concept of quantity, which is essential for understanding categories of being. It provides multiple definitions and a basic division that underpins discussions in mathematics, logic, and metaphysics. Let's examine the precise formulations and their implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the translation. Notice that the passage collects multiple definitions and a division.
The passage is from an editorial footnote, so it is concise and definitional.
2
Identify the three definitions of quantity
The passage offers three distinct definitions: (1) magnitude that accepts measurement, (2) that in which a unit can be found, (3) an accident that accepts division, equality, etc. Note their differences.
The third definition is the most precise philosophical one.
3
Understand the division into continuous and discrete
Quantity is divided into continuous (muttaṣil) and discrete (munfaṣil). The continuous has parts sharing a common limit in potentiality; the discrete does not. Consider examples: line (continuous) vs. number (discrete).
The passage mentions a common limit like a point for a line.
4
Note the broader context in logic and metaphysics
The passage ends by placing quantity in logic (as extension of number) and metaphysics (as a category opposed to quality). This shows its foundational role.
Quantity is one of Aristotle's categories.

(۱) الكم في الرياضيات هو المقدار، وهو ما يقبل القياس، وقيل إنه الذي يمكن أن يوجد فيه شيء يكون واحداً عادا له سواء كان موجوداً بالفعل أو بالقوة وقيل : إنه عرض يقبل لذاته القسمة والمساواة واللامساواة والزيادة والنقصان . والكم: إما متصل وإما منفصل فالمتصل هو الذي يوجد لأجزائه بالقوة حد مشترك تتلاقى عنده وتتحد به كالنقطة للخط. امتداداً . فان كانت جميع أجزائه قارة ومجتمعة في الوجود سمي وان كانت غير مجتمعة زماناً. والمنفصل هو الذي لا يوجد لأجزائه بالقوة ولا بالفعل حد مشترك كالعدد. وكمية العــدد في المنطق ما صدقه والكم في علم ما بعد الطبيعة مقابل للكيف، وهو من مقولات العقل الأساسية، والكمي هو المنسوب الى الكم تقول : مذهب اللذات الكمي.
(1) Al-kamm in mathematics is al-miqdār (magnitude), which is what accepts measurement. It is said that it is that in which something can exist that is a unit (ʿādd) for it, whether existing in act or in potentiality. And it is said: it is an accident that accepts in itself division, equality, inequality, increase and decrease. And al-kamm: either continuous (muttaṣil) or discrete (munfaṣil). The continuous is that which has for its parts in potentiality a common limit (ḥadd mushtarak) at which they meet and by which they unite, like the point for the line. Extending (imtidād). If all its parts are stationary (qārra) and gathered (mujtamiʿa) in existence, it is called... and if they are not gathered in time. And the discrete is that which has for its parts neither in potentiality nor in act a common limit, like number. And the quantity (kammiyya) of number in logic is its extension (mā ṣadaqa), and al-kamm in metaphysics is opposed to al-kayf (quality), and it is among the fundamental categories (maqūlāt) of the intellect, and al-kammī is that which is attributed to al-kamm; you say: the doctrine of quantitative pleasures.

Commentary
This editorial note defines quantity (الكَم) in mathematics and philosophy. It gives three definitions: as magnitude that accepts measurement, as that in which a unit can be found, and as an accident that accepts division, equality, etc. Then it divides quantity into continuous and discrete, explaining the continuous as having a common limit in potentiality (like the point for the line) and the discrete as having no common limit (like number). It then mentions the classification of quantity in logic and metaphysics.

Outline of Avicenna's Canon of Medicine and Its Commentaries
Description of the Canon's books and its commentators.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure of Avicenna's Canon of Medicine and the contributions of two key commentators.

Core Idea
The Canon's five-book structure guided Islamic medicine for centuries.

This passage provides a concise overview of Avicenna's monumental medical encyclopedia, the Canon of Medicine. You will learn how it is divided into five books covering general principles, drugs, and diseases. The passage also introduces two important commentaries by Ibn al-Nafis and Fakhr al-Din al-Razi.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the five books
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Pay attention to the enumeration of the five books of the Canon.
The books are listed in order: general principles, simple drugs, regional diseases, non-specific diseases, and compound drugs.
2
Understand the logical structure
Notice how the Canon moves from general to specific, from simple to compound. The first two books cover general principles and simple drugs; the next two cover diseases; the last covers compound drugs.
This structure was influential in medical education.
3
Learn about Ibn al-Nafis's commentary
Ibn al-Nafis wrote a commentary and abridged it as al-Mujaz. He rearranged the anatomy section into one book, unlike Avicenna's original order.
This shows how commentators adapted the source text.
4
Recognize al-Razi's commentary and the reference
Fakhr al-Din al-Razi also commented on the Canon. The passage ends with a citation to Kashf al-Zunun, a bibliographic work.
This reference indicates the passage may be from a secondary source.
5
Reflect on the significance
Consider why a theologian's work might include a medical overview. This could be a digression or an indication of interdisciplinary scholarship.
The passage is likely from a discussion of authorities or textual history.

القانون الكلي للمعالجة وقسمه إلى خمسة كتب الاول في الامور الكلية من علم الطب الثاني: الأدوية المفردة، الثالث في الامراض الجزئية من الرأس إلى القدم الرابع : في الامراض الجزئية في التي لم تختص بعضو الخامس في تركيب الأدوية . ومن شروح كلياته شرح ابن النفيس علاء الدين على بن أبي الحزم القرشي الشافعي المتوفي سنة ٦٧٨ واختصره وسماه الموجز وأول الشرح بعد حمد الله رب العالمين الح ذكر فيه أنه رتبه على ترتيب القانون إلا في فن التشريح فإنه رأى أن يجمع الكلام في التشريح في كتاب واحد بعد الكلام في مباحث بقية الكتاب الاول، وشرحها الإمام فخر الدين محمد بن عمر الرازي المتوفي سنة ٦٠٦ هـ راجع كشف الظنون ۲ : ۱۳۱۱ - ۱۳۱۲
The Grand Canon of medicine and its division into five books: the first on general matters of medicine, the second on simple drugs, the third on particular diseases from head to foot, the fourth on particular diseases not specific to an organ, the fifth on composition of drugs. And among the commentaries on its 'General Principles' is the commentary of Ibn al-Nafis, 'Ala al-Din 'Ali ibn Abi al-Hazm al-Qurashi al-Shafi'i, died in 678 AH, and he abridged it and called it al-Mujaz. The beginning of the commentary after praising God, Lord of the Worlds, he mentioned that he arranged it according to the order of the Canon except in the art of anatomy, for he thought to gather the discussion on anatomy in one book after the discussion of the investigations of the rest of the first book. And also the commentary of Imam Fakhr al-Din Muhammad ibn 'Umar al-Razi, died in 606 AH. See Kashf al-Zunun 2:1311-1312.

Commentary
This passage is a brief, possibly editorial, description of Avicenna's Canon of Medicine (al-Qanun). It lists the five books of the Canon: general principles, simple drugs, regional diseases, non-organ-specific diseases, and compound drugs. It then mentions two famous commentaries: Ibn al-Nafis's commentary (which he also abridged as al-Mujaz) and Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's commentary. It notes that Ibn al-Nafis rearranged the anatomy section into one book. The passage ends with a reference to Hajji Khalifa's Kashf al-Zunun. This content seems out of place in a kalam work and may be a digression or a marginal note.
