Proof of One Soul per Body and One Body per Soul
Proves necessary one-to-one relation between soul and body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof for the strict one-to-one relationship between soul and body, and why violating it leads to absurdities.

Core Idea
Every soul is the sole director of one body, and no body has more than one soul.

This passage from Sanusi's al-Maqasid presents a concise logical argument establishing the necessary one-to-one correspondence between soul and body. It demonstrates why reincarnation (one soul in multiple bodies) and multiple souls in one body are impossible. Let's walk through the proof step by step.

Guided Path
1

Notice the compact style of the author.
2
Identify the key claim: equality
The passage states that soul and body are in a state of equality (تعادل). This means one body cannot have two souls, and one soul cannot have two bodies, neither at the same time nor alternately.
3
Examine the three absurd consequences
If the equality were violated, the author lists three impossible results. List each one and understand why it is absurd.
4
Reflect on the refutation of reincarnation
Think how this proof directly rules out doctrines like reincarnation (one soul in many bodies) and the idea of two souls in one body.

قال (ثم النفس ناطقة) بأن ليس معها في هذا البدن تدبر آخر، ولا لها تدبير في بدن آخر. فهما على التعادل ليس لبدن نفسان، ولا لنفس بدنانِ لا معاً ولا على البدل وإلا لزم أن تتذكر شيئاً من أحوال البدن الأول أن ينطبق عدد الكائنات على الفاسدات، وأن يجامعها نفس أخرى حادثة بتمام الاستعداد، وعموم الفيض.
He said: 'Then the soul is rational' — that there is not with it in this body another director, nor does it have direction in another body. So they are in a state of equality: one body does not have two souls, nor does one soul have two bodies, neither simultaneously nor alternately; otherwise it would necessarily follow that it would remember something of the states of the first body, that the number of beings would correspond to the number of perishing things, and that another soul, newly originated with complete preparedness and universal emanation, would join it.

Commentary
This passage presents a rational proof for the one-to-one correspondence between soul and body. It begins by citing the matn 'then the soul is rational' and then explains that every soul knows necessarily that it is the sole director of its current body and does not direct any other body. Therefore, the relationship is one of equality: no body has two souls, and no soul has two bodies, whether at the same time or in succession. If this were violated, three absurdities would follow: (1) the soul would remember experiences from a previous body (which we do not observe), (2) the number of living beings would equal the number of perishing bodies (which is false, e.g., in a flood many perish but few are born), and (3) a newly originated soul with complete readiness and universal emanation would have to coexist with the old soul in the same body (which is impossible). This proof directly refutes both reincarnation (one soul in multiple bodies) and the idea of multiple souls in one body.

Clarifying Metamorphosis and Resurrection Are Not Transmigration
Distinguishes transmigration from metamorphosis and bodily resurrection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why metamorphosis and bodily resurrection are not instances of transmigration (tanāsukh) as debated by theologians.

Core Idea
Transmigration is about this-worldly re-embodiment; metamorphosis and resurrection are not.

This passage addresses a common confusion: some might think that the Qur'anic metamorphosis of certain people into animals, or the bodily resurrection at the end of time, support the theory of transmigration. The author clarifies that the transmigration under debate is specifically about souls re-entering bodies in this world (al-dunyā), whereas metamorphosis is a one-time miracle/punishment, and resurrection is an afterlife event. Thus, they are not evidence for the theory.

Read the passage carefully

The passage is relatively short; try to identify the main claim and the answer.

The author acknowledges this appearance but then provides the distinction.
3
Understand the author's answer
The key distinction: the disputed transmigration is about souls attaching to bodies in this world (al-dunyā). Metamorphosis is a specific event in this world but not a cycle; resurrection is in the afterlife.

Metamorphosis happened in the past as a punishment. Resurrection is future. Transmigration would be a continuous this-worldly cycle.
This helps see why they are not the same.

The Philosophical Claim About Metempsychosis
Describes philosophical classification of souls after death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three-tiered philosophical claim about the fate of souls after death: perfect souls connecting to intellects, intermediate souls to celestial bodies or forms, and deficient souls to animal bodies.

Core Idea
Philosophers divide souls into three types, each destined for a different realm after death.

Many philosophers have speculated about what happens to souls after death. A specific theory, known as metempsychosis or reincarnation, claims that souls migrate to other bodies. In this passage, the author reports a detailed classification of souls according to their perfection, each connected to a different world or body. Understanding this view is essential before we see its refutation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of souls
Read the passage carefully and note the three types: perfect souls, intermediate souls, and deficient souls. Each has a different destination.
The author uses the phrase 'النفوس الكاملة' (perfect souls), 'المتوسطة' (intermediate), and 'الناقصة' (deficient).
2
Notice the basis for the classification
The classification is based on the habits and dispositions the soul acquired in its life. Deficient souls go to animal bodies that match their bad habits.
3
Reflect on the purpose of the cycle
The process is said to continue until the soul is purified through suffering. This is the philosophical concept of purification through reincarnation.
The phrase 'تتخلص من بما لقيت من أنواع العذاب' indicates purification through punishment.

Commentary

As for the view of necessary causation (al-ījāb), it is possible that there is in them some dense element by which vision occurs for some eyes but not others, and in some states but not others, or that they sometimes appear in dense bodies as a veil and cloak for them, so they are seen; or that their souls, temperaments, or specific forms are such that they necessitate preservation of their composition from dissolution and change of their forms according to differences of positions and states; or that they possess intelligence and cleverness by which they know the directions of winds and all causes of dissolution of composition, so they guard against them and seek shelter in places where no harm reaches them. As for the answer that it is possible that their subtlety (luṭāfah) is in the sense of transparency (shafāfiyyah) without delicacy of texture (riqqat al-qawām), that is inconsistent with what is reported about them of entering narrow openings and appearing at one time in different forms in smallness and largeness and the like.

Commentary
This passage provides an answer to an objection. The objection likely claimed that if jinn and angels are subtle bodies, they cannot be seen by human eyes. The author begins by stating that the answer is to deny the entailments (manʿ al-mulāzamāt) of the objection—i.e., the necessary connections that the objector assumes. He then explains how this denial works under two different metaphysical frameworks. First, on the view that contingent beings depend on a free chooser (al-qādir al-mukhtār), it is obvious that God can create vision of them in some eyes and not others, and can preserve their composition and change their forms by His power and will. Second, on the view of necessary emanation (al-ījāb), several possibilities are listed: (1) they may have a dense component in some of them that makes them visible; (2) they may sometimes appear in dense garments; (3) their souls or forms may preserve their composition; (4) they may have intelligence to avoid dissolution. The author then refutes a potential alternative answer that their subtlety is transparency rather than delicacy of texture, because that would contradict reports of them entering narrow openings and changing sizes rapidly. Thus, the author defends the possibility of seeing such beings despite their subtle nature.
