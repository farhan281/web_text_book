True Wisdom as the Sharia
Explains that true wisdom is the Sharia and divides wisdom into theoretical and practical, each with three subdivisions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why true wisdom is identified with the Sharia (Islamic law) and how it encompasses both knowledge and action, and learn about the division of wisdom into three parts each.

Core Idea
True wisdom is following the prophets and living the Sharia, not just knowing facts.

After defining wisdom, the author explains that due to widespread disagreement and error, it became necessary to follow those proven by miracles – the prophets – who guided humanity. True wisdom is therefore the Sharia, but not merely its legal rulings; it is the soul's knowledge of its duties and acting upon them. This understanding of wisdom as 'fiqh' (deep understanding) includes both knowledge and action. The passage concludes by noting that both theoretical and practical wisdom are each further divided into three parts, setting the stage for detailed classifications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem that led to following prophets
Read how the author flags the increase in disagreement and falsehood about perfection and truth.
This sets up the need for divine guidance.
2
Understand true wisdom as the Sharia
The author states that true wisdom is the Sharia, but not just practical rulings; it is knowledge of what is good for the soul and acting on it.
This is based on the Quranic verse and the view of the people of verification.
3
Learn the primary division of wisdom
The author recaps the division into theoretical and practical based on whether the knowledge relates to our choice or not, and then states each is divided into three.
This anticipates a further classification in the following lessons.

The passage then states that both branches are each further divided into three parts, setting up the subsequent detailed classification.

Degrees of Independence: Intellect vs Soul
Objection and answer on degrees of the soul's independence from matter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the soul and intellect are distinguished by their degree of independence from matter in creative and perceptual acts.

Core Idea
Independence from matter is not binary; it varies between creative and perceptual acts.

This passage presents an objection that would reduce the soul to intellect if it were fully independent. The answer clarifies that independence can be partial—a crucial point in distinguishing soul from intellect. It sets the stage for understanding the hierarchy of separate substances in Islamic philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice how the objection assumes full independence, while the answer introduces nuance.
The objection uses 'فإن قيل' and the answer 'قلنا'.
2
Identify the two types of acts
The answer mentions creative act (إيجاد) and perceptual act (إدراك). Understand each: creation brings existence, perception brings knowledge.
Both are kinds of acts of the substance.
3
Map the three cases
List the three possibilities: (1) independent in both acts → intellect, (2) independent only in creation → soul, (3) dependent in both → neither intellect nor soul.
This shows that the soul is a middle category.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Ask: why does this matter? It prevents conflating the first emanation with intellect, preserving the soul's distinct nature.
The answer shows that the objection's desired conclusion is not forced.

Commentary
This is an objection-and-answer pair. The objector says: if the first emanation is independent of matter in essence and act, then it would be what we mean by intellect. The author responds: the claim is to establish a separate substance in its essence and act, both creatively and perceptually. However, it is possible that the first emanation is independent in its creative act but not in its perceptual act. If the soul needs matter only for perception, then it is a soul; if it needs matter for both creative and perceptual acts, then it is something other than intellect and soul, and the desired conclusion (that the first emanation is intellect) fails.

Philosophical Definitions of Angels, Jinn, and Devils
The author enumerates philosophical definitions of angels, jinn, and devils.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophical definitions of angels, jinn, and devils as abstract intellects, spirits, and imaginative powers.

Core Idea
Philosophers define angels, jinn, and devils as abstract entities, not physical beings.

This passage presents the views of philosophers on the nature of angels, jinn, and devils. While these definitions differ from scriptural accounts, they reflect attempts to explain these beings within a rational framework. Let's explore the key philosophical concepts.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow. Then read the translation to understand the definitions.
Pay attention to the terms 'abstract intellects' and 'celestial souls'.
2
Identify the three definitions
The passage defines angels, jinn, and devils. Find each definition and note how they are described.
Angels are abstract intellects and celestial souls; jinn are abstract spirits; devils are imaginative powers.
3
Consider the alternative view
Some philosophers hold that human souls after death become jinn or devils based on their moral state. This is a secondary view.
This view is not the main one but shows variation among philosophers.
4
Understand the cherubim distinction
The passage mentions 'cherubim' as a special class of angels with no relation to bodies.
This distinction highlights the hierarchy among abstract intellects.

The author concludes that the existence of these beings is established by consensus, revelation, and mystical reports.

Belief in these beings rests on revelation and consensus, not rational arguments.
