The Reliability of Estimative Judgments in Geometry
Al-Taftazani defends estimative judgments in geometry against an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Taftazani considers estimative judgments in geometry reliable and how they differ from false pure estimative judgments.

Core Idea
Estimative judgments in geometry are as reliable as sense perception.

In this passage, al-Taftazani responds to an objection that the impossibility of a first point in an infinite line is not conceded because the argument involves a false estimative judgment. He clarifies that not all estimative judgments are false; those concerning geometry are correct and almost like sensory data because they are guided by reason. However, pure estimative judgments that impose sensible categories on intelligibles are indeed false.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and response
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Note the objection: the impossibility of a first point is not conceded because the division of motion and angle without end is a judgment of wahm and false.
2
Identify the two types of estimative judgments
Al-Taftazani distinguishes between geometrical estimative judgments and pure estimative judgments. Find the characteristics of each in the text.
Geometrical ones are correct and nearly sensory; pure ones are false when they apply sensible features to intelligibles.
3
Understand why geometrical judgments are reliable
Notice the reason given: they are under the command of reason and no one denies them except through obstinacy. Reflect on why this makes them akin to sense perceptions.
4
Apply the distinction to the objection
How does this distinction answer the objection? The objector claimed the argument relies on a false estimative judgment, but al-Taftazani shows that the relevant judgment belongs to geometry, which is sound.
Thus, the impossibility of a first point in an infinite line remains established.

Direction: Definition, Existence, and Indivisibility
Definition of direction and proof of its existence and indivisibility in motion/indication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the kalam concept of direction (jiha) as the edge of extension, its existence as a positioned entity, and why it does not accept division in the locus of motion and indication.

Core Idea
Direction is the indivisible endpoint of extension that motion seeks but cannot enter.

This passage introduces the concept of direction (jiha) in Islamic theology, where it is not a mere direction of the compass but a precise philosophical term. Sanusi defines it as the edge of extension, the goal of motion, and the endpoint of indication. He then establishes its existence as a positioned entity and proves that it cannot be divided, at least in terms of motion and indication. Understanding direction is essential for comprehending how bodies relate to space and the divine.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition carefully
Read the Arabic passage from 'طرف الامتداد' to 'فتكون الجهة منتهاها'. Notice the three aspects: edge of extension, endpoint of indication, goal of mover.
2
Understand the existence of direction
Sanusi argues direction is existent and has position because it is the goal of motion and sensory indication. Contrast with motion in quality to see why direction must be existent.
3
Grasp the meaning of 'obtaining in a direction'
The text clarifies that obtaining in a direction means arrival and proximity, not literal containment. This is crucial for the subsequent proof.
4
Follow the proof of indivisibility
The author provides a hypothetical division of direction into two parts and shows contradiction. Work through the argument step by step.
5
Note the clarification about absolute indivisibility
Sanusi ends by stating that direction may be a line or surface; it is only indivisible with respect to motion and indication, not absolutely.

Commentary
This passage is a conclusion (khatima) on the topic of direction (jiha). It begins by defining direction: it is the edge of extension, the endpoint of indication, and the goal of a mover in obtaining it. Therefore, direction is existent and has position, and it does not accept division; otherwise motion into it would occur, and direction would be its end. The author explains why the discussion of direction is placed as a conclusion to the discussion of the finitude of dimensions: because direction is essentially the end of extension. Then he clarifies that direction is existent and has a position because it is the goal of the mover and the endpoint of sensory indication; the non-existent or abstract cannot be obtained or indicated. This is contrasted with motion in quality, like a body changing from white to black—the blackness, as a goal, cannot be existent because it would be acquiring what is already acquired. Next, the meaning of 'obtaining in a direction' is explained: it is obtaining at the direction by arrival and proximity, and 'moving in a direction' means moving in a direction leading to it. Since both the mover and motion are divisible, they can only occur in something divisible. However, direction does not accept division in the locus of motion and indication. The author provides a proof: if direction were divided into two parts, then when the mover reaches the first part, either he does not go beyond it (so direction is just that part) or he goes beyond it, leading to motion either away from or towards the part, each case reducing to one part being the direction. Thus, direction is indivisible in this respect. This suffices to show that body or motion cannot occur in it, but it does not mean direction is indivisible absolutely—it may be a line or a surface. The passage ends with an incomplete statement about the edges of extensions being directions.

Proof that Body Cannot Occupy a Direction
Al-Taftazani proves that a body cannot be located in a direction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Taftazani argues that a body cannot be located in a direction by analyzing direction as the goal of motion and its indivisibility in that context.

Core Idea
Direction is the goal of motion, not a container for bodies.

Al-Taftazani presents a logical proof (burhan) that a body cannot occupy a direction. The proof hinges on understanding direction as the goal (maqsad) of a moving object, not a physical container. Since motion and the moving object are divisible, they require a divisible medium, but direction, in its relevant sense, does not accept division.

Commentary
This passage is a proof (burhan) that a body cannot be located in a direction (jiha), because direction is the goal of a moving object, and the moving object and its motion are divisible, whereas direction is indivisible in the relevant sense. The author begins by stating that direction is the goal of a moving object in terms of 'attainment' (tahsil), meaning arrival and proximity, not physical containment. Since the moving object and motion are divisible, they can only occur in something divisible. But direction does not accept division in the domain of motion and indication (ma'khadh al-haraka wa al-ishara). He then gives a hypothetical: if direction were divided into two parts, the moving object either stops at the first part (making that the direction) or passes beyond it, in which case the motion is either away from the first part or toward the second, but in either case the direction is only one part, not both. The objection that the moving object is 'in a direction' is refuted by noting that motion in a divisible thing is always either from or to a direction. The conclusion is that body and motion cannot occur in the direction itself. However, the author clarifies that this does not prove direction is absolutely indivisible like a point; it may be a line or surface—the indivisibility is only in the context of being a goal of motion.

Why Are There Only Six Directions?
Al-Taftazani explains the conceptual infinity of directions and the two reasons for the standard enumeration of six.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why directions are conceptually unlimited but conventionally counted as six, based on geometric and human-centered reasoning.

Core Idea
Directions are infinite in principle, but three dimensions give six practical directions.

In kalam cosmology, directions (jihāt) are not fixed categories; they are relative. Al-Taftazani begins by stating that directions are unlimited in number, yet the well-known view counts six. He then provides two distinct reasons—one rooted in geometry, the other in human anatomy. This lesson unpacks those reasons and shows how they shape Islamic theological discussions about God and the world.

Core Idea
What is true of each part is not always true of the whole.

Theologians have offered various proofs for the impossibility of an infinite chain of past events. One such proof relies on a logical move from the property of each individual event to the entire series. However, as the refutation shows, this move is not always valid. The proof states: since every event is preceded by non-existence, the totality of all events must also be preceded by non-existence. This is like saying: if every black person is black, then the group of all black people is black.

The fourth section: On the imaginary circles. They imagined for every place on earth a circle on the celestial sphere, separating between its visible and hidden parts, calling it the horizon circle, and its poles are the zenith and nadir. If they (the poles of the horizon) are the poles of the world, the horizon coincides with the equator; otherwise it cuts it, either at right angles, and it is called the level (horizon), or at non-right angles, and it is called the oblique horizon. Among them is the separating horizon circle, and it is [؟] the celestial sphere and its hidden part. Commentary
This passage is an astronomical classification of imaginary circles used in determining directions and celestial measurements. The author begins by introducing the fourth section on these circles. He explains that for each location on earth, a circle is imagined on the celestial sphere that separates the visible from the invisible part; this is the horizon circle (al-ufuq), with its poles being the zenith (samt al-ra's) and nadir (samt al-qadam). The final part returns to the condition when the horizon's poles are the world's poles, leading to equinoctial rotation; otherwise the horizon cuts the equator at two points (east and west equinoxes). Level Horizon vs Oblique Horizon
Explains the relationship between horizon and equator, defining level and oblique horizons.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the conditions under which the horizon is level (equinoctial) or oblique, and how it relates to the celestial equator and equinox points.

Core Idea
When the horizon's poles align with the celestial poles, the horizon coincides with the equator; otherwise it cuts it at an angle.

The final part of the passage examines the geometric relationship between the horizon and the celestial equator. Depending on the observer's latitude, the horizon either coincides with the equator or intersects it at right angles (level) or at oblique angles (oblique). This determines the type of rotation and the points of equinox.

Guided Path
1
Read the condition for alignment
The text states: if the poles of the horizon are the poles of the world, then the horizon coincides with the equator.
This occurs when one of the world's poles is at the zenith.
2
Identify the intersection points
When the horizon is not aligned, it cuts the equator at two points: the east and west equinox points.
These are also called the midpoints of east and west.
3
Distinguish level from oblique
If the intersection is at right angles, it's called the level horizon (ufuq al-istiwa'); otherwise oblique (ufuq ma'il).
There is only one level horizon but many oblique horizons.

Commentary
This passage presents the classical cosmological arguments for the earth being spherical and situated at the center of the universe. The author begins by stating the thesis: the earth is at the center (al-wasaṭ) like the center of a sphere relative to its circumference, and it is motionless—neither moving away from the center, nor towards it, nor around it. Then he categorizes the proofs into two types: mathematical (taʿlīmī) arguments, found in astronomy books, which establish the existence (al-āniyya) of the phenomenon; and natural (ṭabīʿī) arguments, which explain the cause (al-limiyya). The natural arguments are drawn from the science of the heavens and the world: (1) Elements and celestial bodies are simple; the natural shape of a simple substance is a sphere, because a single nature does not produce different effects. (2) The earth is absolutely heavy, so it naturally rests beneath everything else, i.e., at the lowest point, which is the center. (3) Since the earth is in its natural place, it does not move away or towards it. (4) The earth has a principle of rectilinear motion (as seen in its parts falling straight down), so it cannot have a principle of circular motion, because two opposite inclinations cannot coexist. Therefore, the earth cannot rotate on its axis, contrary to some who claim that daily rising and setting are due to the earth's rotation from west to east. The author dismisses this as weak, because natural tendencies can be overridden by external force—so the earth might not be spherical or central, and could move circularly by compulsion like the celestial spheres. The author then turns to the mathematical proofs, listing several: (1) The sphericity of water: if water were not spherical, a mountain on the coast would appear entirely at once to a distant traveler; but instead the peak appears first, then lower parts, which is consistent with a curved surface. (2) The earth's central position: if it were not central, stars would appear smaller in some regions and larger in others, contrary to observation. (3) The sphericity of the earth is shown by longitudinal extent: if the earth were flat, stars would rise and set simultaneously everywhere; if concave, the order would be reversed. But observations of lunar eclipses show that midpoints coincide at one moment but local times differ proportionally with distance, indicating curvature. Similarly for latitudinal extent: if flat, polar elevations would not change with travel; if concave, they would change inversely. In reality, polar elevations increase or decrease consistently with north-south travel, again indicating curvature. From the curvature of the visible portion, one infers the whole is spherical. The passage ends with an objection: it is possible that these phenomena could occur as described without the earth being spherical, but the answer presumably follows on the next page.

This passage defines and explains the concept of similarity of temperamental quality (tashabuh al-kayfiyya al-mizajiyya) in the context of mixture (imtizaj). The author begins with an example: the form of fire breaks the moisture of water by its dryness, not by its heat. Then he states the fourth point: the meaning of similarity is that every part of the mixture, whether composite or simple, has a quality that is equal in specific reality to the quality of any other part, differing only by the locus (i.e., the specific part it inheres in). For example, a fiery part in a mixture will have the same intermediate temperature and moisture as a watery part, because all parts share the same intermediate quality. If the qualities were actually different but appeared similar only due to intense mixing, there would be no real action and passion between the parts, and no genuine existential quality that prepares the mixture to receive a form—whether mineral, plant, animal, or human soul. Thus, true mixture requires a uniform intermediate quality throughout; otherwise it is mere composition (tarkib) or juxtaposition. The distinction is important: mixture (imtizaj) is specifically the production of a uniform intermediate quality, whereas composition is more general. Some make 'ikhtilat' synonymous with mixture, as in Ibn Sina's al-Shifa'. The author then critiques an alternative definition found in Sharh al-Qanun, which defines similarity by relativity (hot relative to cold, cold relative to hot). This alternative was intended to refute an objection by the Imam (likely Fakhr al-Din al-Razi) that such a definition would include the concomitants of temperament. But the author rejects it as contradictory to reason and transmitted knowledge. His own definition is the correct understanding from the wording and the consensus of the scholars.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think that similarity in sensation due to intense mixing constitutes true mixture.
Correction: The author clarifies that true mixture requires actual homogeneous quality throughout, not just sensory appearance.

Key Takeaway
True mixture is characterized by a real homogeneous intermediate quality throughout all parts, not merely an appearance of similarity due to intense mixing.
