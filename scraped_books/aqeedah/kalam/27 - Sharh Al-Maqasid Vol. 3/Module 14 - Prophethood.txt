The Claim that Celestial Bodies Have Smell
Reports the claim that celestial bodies have smell and lists its proponents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that some ancient philosophers, including Plato and Pythagoras, claimed that celestial bodies possess the sense of smell and contain fragrances.

Core Idea
Some philosophers believed the heavens have smell and fragrances.

This lesson introduces the philosophical claim that celestial bodies have the sense of smell. The passage reports that Plato, Pythagoras, Hermes, and others held this view. This sets up the debate that will be examined in subsequent lessons.

Guided Path
1
Read the claim
Read the Arabic passage that states the claim and its proponents. Notice the specific names mentioned.
The condition about air reaching the nose is mentioned as only applying to the elemental world.
2
Identify the proponents
Note that Plato, Pythagoras, and Hermes are named as holding this view. These are key figures in ancient philosophy.
3
Understand the significance
Think about why this claim would be controversial. The next lesson will show the refutation.

He said: 'Among the philosophers are those who claim that the celestial bodies have smell, and there are fragrances in them, and the condition of air reaching the nose is only in the world of elements.' It is reported from Plato, Pythagoras, Hermes, and others that the spheres and planets have smell, and there are fragrances in them. The Peripatetics refuted them by saying that there is no air there to be conditioned, and no vapor to decompose. It is only in elemental things. And from the sayings of some later ones: 'When we are connected to the celestial bodies in sleep or wakefulness, we smell from them fragrances better than musk and ambergris; indeed, what we have bears no proportion to what is there.' And for this reason, the masters of spiritual sciences agreed that for every planet there is a specific incense, and for every spiritual being a known fragrance that they inhale and enjoy, and with the smells of foods prepared for them, and they pour upon whoever arranges that what he is ready for.

Commentary
This passage discusses a philosophical claim about celestial bodies having the sense of smell. The text begins by quoting the original author (al-Taftazani's matn?) stating that some philosophers claim celestial bodies have smell and fragrances, but the condition of air reaching the nose only applies to the elemental world. It then reports that Plato, Pythagoras, and Hermes held that the spheres and planets have smell and fragrances. The Peripatetic philosophers (المشاؤن) refuted this by arguing that there is no air in the celestial realm to be conditioned (to carry smell) and no vapor to decompose. Smell only occurs in the sublunar elemental world. The passage then presents a later opinion from some spiritualists: when in mystical connection with celestial bodies, they experience fragrances superior to musk and ambergris. Consequently, the practitioners of spiritual sciences (أرباب العلوم الروحانية) agree that each planet has a specific incense and each spiritual entity has a known fragrance that they enjoy, and they bestow benefits on those who properly arrange such offerings. This section thus records two conflicting views: a philosophical refutation based on physical principles and a mystical affirmation based on experiential reports.

The Peripatetic Refutation of Celestial Smell
Presents the Peripatetic refutation of celestial smell using physical arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Peripatetic argument that celestial bodies cannot have smell because there is no air or vapor in the celestial realm.

Core Idea
No air, no smell — Peripatetics refute celestial fragrance.

This lesson presents the refutation of the claim by the Peripatetic philosophers (followers of Aristotle). They argue that smell requires air to be conditioned and vapor to decompose, both of which are absent in the celestial realm. Therefore, smell is only possible in the elemental world.

Guided Path
1
Read the refutation
Read the Arabic passage that states the Peripatetic argument. Notice the two conditions they mention: air and vapor.
2
Analyze the argument
The Peripatetics argue that smell requires air to be conditioned (to carry scent particles) and vapor to decompose (to release odor). Since the celestial realm lacks these, smell cannot exist there.
This is based on Aristotelian physics.
3
Contrast with the claim
Compare this refutation with the earlier claim. The refutation is based on physical principles, while the claim is based on authority (Plato etc.) and spiritual experiences.

Persistence of Perception of Particulars After Death
Taftazani warns that contrary to philosophers, perception of particulars persists after death in Islam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why perception of particulars persists after death in Islam and how this supports practices like visiting graves and seeking help from righteous souls.

Core Idea
In Islam, the soul perceives particulars after death, connecting with the living.

Taftazani presents a crucial warning (tanbih) regarding the soul's perception after death. He contrasts the philosophical view—that perception requires bodily instruments and ceases at death—with the Islamic view that the soul retains and even renews particular perceptions. This passage explains the basis for practices like visiting graves and seeking aid from the deceased righteous.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two opposing views
Read the first part of the passage to see the contrast: philosophers deny persistence of perception of particulars after death, while Islam affirms it. Note that for philosophers, perception depends on bodily instruments; for Islam, it does not.
The passage starts with 'According to them...' and 'but according to us it remains'.
2
Examine the Islamic rationale
The passage gives two possible reasons why instruments are not necessary for perception of particulars. Read these carefully: either perception does not involve a form in soul or sense, or the impression of a particular form in the soul is possible.
The Arabic says 'either because... or because...'.
3
Connect to practice
The passage then uses this doctrine to justify visiting graves and seeking help from the souls of the righteous. Understand that the soul retains an attachment to the body and soil, enabling interaction between the living and the dead.
This is a direct application of the doctrine.

[قال (تنبيه) : فعندهم لا يبقى إدراك الجزئيات عند فقد الآلات وعندنا يبقى بل الظاهر من قانون الإسلام، الإدراكات المتجددة أيضاً، ولهذا ينتفع بزيارة القبور والاستعانة من نفوس الأخيار] . لما كان إدراك الجزئيات مشروطاً عند الفلاسفة بحصول الصورة في الآلات، فعند مفارقة النفس وبطلان الالات لا تبقى مدركة للجزئيات ضرورة انتفاء المشروط بانتفاء الشرط، وعندنا لما لم تكن الالات شرطاً في إدراك الجزئيات. إما لأنه ليس بحصول الصورة، لا في النفس، ولا في الحس. وإما لأنه لا يمتنع ارتسام صورة الجزئي في النفس، بل الظاهر من قواعد الإسلام أنه يكون للنفس بعد المفارقة إدراكات متجددة جزئية، واطلاع على بعض جزئيات أحوال الأحياء، سيما الذين كان بينهم وبين الميت تعارف في الدنيا، ولهذا ينتفع بزيارة القبور والاستعانة بنفوس الأخيار من الأموات في استنزال الخيرات، واستدفاع الملمات، فإن للنفس بعد المفارقة تعلقاً ما بالبدن وبالتربة التي دفنت فيها ، فإذا زار الحي تلك التربة، وتوجهت نفسه تلقاء نفس الميت حصل بين النفسين ملاقاة وإفاضات.
He said (Warning): According to them (the philosophers), perception of particulars does not remain when the instruments are lost, but according to us it remains. Rather, the apparent (view) from the law of Islam (is that) perceptions are renewed also, and for this reason one benefits from visiting graves and seeking help from the souls of the righteous. Since perception of particulars is conditioned by philosophers on the occurrence of the form in the instruments, so upon separation of the soul and invalidation of the instruments it does not remain perceiving particulars necessarily by the negation of the conditioned with the negation of the condition. And according to us, since the instruments are not a condition for perception of particulars—either because it is not by the occurrence of the form, neither in the soul nor in the sense, or because the impression of the image of the particular in the soul is not impossible—rather the apparent from the principles of Islam is that for the soul after separation there are renewed particular perceptions and awareness of some particular states of the living, especially those between whom and the deceased there was acquaintance in the world, and for this reason one benefits from visiting graves and seeking help from the souls of the righteous from the dead in seeking good things and repelling calamities. For the soul after separation has a certain attachment to the body and the soil in which it was buried. So when the living person visits that soil and his soul turns towards the soul of the deceased, a meeting and emanations occur between the two souls.

Commentary
This passage is a warning (tanbih) regarding the persistence of perception of particulars after death. It contrasts the philosophical view with the Islamic view. Philosophers hold that perception of particulars requires bodily instruments, so at death it ceases. In Islam, instruments are not a condition; either perception is not through any form (in soul or sense), or the impression of the particular form in the soul is not impossible. Hence the soul has renewed particular perceptions after death, especially awareness of the living. This justifies the practice of visiting graves and seeking help from the souls of the righteous, as the soul retains a connection to its body and grave, enabling interaction between the living and the dead.

The Two Wisdoms: Theoretical and Practical
Definitions of theoretical and practical wisdom and their composite definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definitions of theoretical and practical wisdom as the perfections of the soul's two faculties, and how they combine into a comprehensive concept of wisdom.

Core Idea
Wisdom is the soul's perfection in both knowing truth and acting rightly.

This passage from Sharh al-Maqasid lays the groundwork by defining two fundamental forms of wisdom: one concerned with knowledge of reality, the other with right action. Understanding these definitions is essential before we can appreciate why prophetic guidance becomes necessary in the next part.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic definitions
Read the first part of the passage carefully, focusing on the definitions of theoretical and practical wisdom. Note the repeated phrase 'to the extent of human capacity.'
The author is careful to ground these perfections within human limits.
2
Identify the two faculties
Identify that theoretical wisdom perfects the theoretical faculty (knowledge of existents as they are), and practical wisdom perfects the practical faculty (doing actions as they ought).
3
Understand the combined definition
The passage then gives a broader definition of wisdom that includes both: the soul's transition from potentiality to actuality in both knowledge and action.
This sets up the later identification of true wisdom with Sharia.
4
Reflect on the purpose
Consider why these definitions are necessary for the overall argument. They establish what true human perfection is, before the author addresses how to achieve it reliably.
This will connect to the next lesson on prophetic necessity.

أن كمال القوة النظرية معرفة أعيان الموجودات وأحوالها وأحكامها كما هي، أي على الوجه الذي هي عليه، وفي نفس الأمر بقدر الطاقة البشرية، وتسمى حكمة نظرية، وكمال القوة العملية القيام بالأمور على ما ينبغي. أي على الوجه الذي يرتضيه العقل الصحيح، بقدر الطاقة البشرية، وتسمى حكمة عملية، وفسروا الحكمة على ما يشمل القسمين، بأنها خروج النفس من القوة إلى الفعل، في كمالها الممكن علماً ،وعملاً، إلا أنه لمـا كثـر الخلاف، وفشا الباطل والضلال في شأن الكمال، وفي كون الأشياء كما هي، والأمور على ما ينبغي لزم الاقتداء في ذلك بمن ثبت بالمعجزات الباهرة، أنهم على هدى من تعالى، وكانت الحكمة الحقيقية هي الشريعة، لكن لا بمعنى مجرد الأحكام العملية، بل بمعنى معرفة النفس ما لها وما عليها والعمل بها على ما ذهب إليه أهل التحقيق من أن الحكمة المشار إليها في قوله تعالى: { ومن يؤت الحكمة فقد أوتى خيراً كثيراً } هو الفقه، وأنه اسم للعلم والعمل جميعاً.
