On the Annihilation of Bodies: The Modes of Fana'
Taftazani enumerates modes of body annihilation and refutes objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the various theological explanations for how bodies can be annihilated after their creation and the refutations of opposing views.

Core Idea
Annihilation of bodies is possible by God withholding necessary accidents or creating an accident of annihilation.

Taftazani tackles the question of how bodies, which are enduring substances, can be annihilated. He presents different positions from kalām scholars on the mechanism of annihilation, showing that it does not require a contradiction. This passage also clarifies a misunderstanding about al-Nazzam's view and notes the philosophers' position on the annihilation of composite bodies versus prime matter.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation to get the overall argument.
The passage is dense with references; let the author's flow guide you.
2
Identify the three main methods of annihilation
Taftazani presents three ways annihilation can occur: by God not creating necessary accidents, by not creating the accident of continuance, or by creating an accident of annihilation. Find each in the text.
These are attributed to specific scholars – note their names.
3
Understand the correction about al-Nazzam
Taftazani refutes the claim that al-Nazzam considered body a collection of accidents. Notice his clarification that al-Nazzam held certain accidents to be self-subsisting bodies.
This corrects a common misunderstanding.
4
Grasp the philosophers' distinction
The philosophers agree on annihilation of composite bodies through loss of forms, but differ on total annihilation of prime matter. Understand why this relates to their belief in pre-eternity.
Taftazani promises to refute their arguments later.

Commentary
This passage discusses the annihilation of bodies (fana' al-ajsam) and refutes various arguments. Taftazani begins by referring to a previous discussion on the impossibility of the continuance of accidents to refute the necessary connection between the necessity of the consequent and the necessity of the antecedent. Then he presents the possibility of annihilation by God not creating the accidents that bodies need for continuance (as al-Qadi and Imam al-Haramayn held), or by not creating the accident of continuance (as al-Ka'bi held), or by creating an accident of annihilation—either multiple for each substance (Abu Ali) or a single one for all bodies (others). He also refutes the claim of al-Nazzam that body is a collection of accidents, clarifying that al-Nazzam actually considered certain accidents as self-subsisting bodies. Finally, he states that philosophers agree on annihilation through loss of specific forms but differ on total annihilation of prime matter and corporeal form, based on their belief in pre-eternity implying perpetuity. He promises to refute their arguments later.

Basic Components of the Celestial Spheres
Enumerates the partial spheres within the universal spheres.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the basic components of the Ptolemaic celestial spheres, including the deferent, inclined, eccentric, director, carrier, completers, and epicycle.

Core Idea
The cosmos is built from nested spheres offset from the earth's center.

This lesson introduces the technical vocabulary of Ptolemaic astronomy as used by Muslim theologians. The passage describes how the moon's deferent contains an inclined sphere, while other planets have eccentrics. We will learn the names and functions of these spheres.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
This passage is very technical; take your time.
2
Identify each sphere type
As you read, list the different spheres mentioned: the moon's deferent, the inclined, the eccentric for other planets (called director for Mercury, carrier for others), the completers, and the epicycle.
Notice how each sphere has a specific role.
3
Understand the relationships
Trace how each sphere fits inside another. For example, the inclined is inside the moon's deferent, the eccentric is inside the deferent of other planets, and the epicycle is inside the carrier.
This nested structure is key to the Ptolemaic system.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed description of the partial celestial spheres (al-afāk al-juz'iyya) that make up the universal spheres (al-afāk al-kulliyya) according to the Ptolemaic model, as presented in a kalam context. The passage begins by quoting a statement from the original text (matn) and then the commentator (shārih) explains it. The original text describes the structure: every hollow of the moon's deferent (mumaththil al-qamar) contains another sphere called the inclined (al-mā'il) centered on earth. For other planets, the deferents are eccentrics (khārij al-markaz) with specific names: for Mercury it is called a director (mudīr), for the rest a carrier (hāmil). These spheres touch the inclined or deferent at points called apogee (al-awj) and perigee (al-ḥaḍīḍ). Between the deferent and the eccentric, there remain two bodies called the completers (al-mutammimān) that taper from thick at the center to thin at the contact points. In the thickness of the director for Mercury is another eccentric called the carrier, creating two apogees, two perigees, and four completers. In each carrier's thickness is an epicycle (falk al-tadwīr), and the planet is placed touching its surface. The commentator then explains that this detailed knowledge comes from empirical observation (raṣd) of planetary positions and motions. He lists some partial spheres: the moon's deferent called al-jawzaharī (possibly a variant of jawzahr, the lunar nodes) and the moon's inclined sphere (ḥā'il al-qamar) which is an eccentric. The commentator describes the general eccentric sphere: a sphere encompassing the earth with its center offset, existing within a concentric sphere. The gap between them is filled by two round bodies (the completers) that are thick in the middle and thin at the points of contact, with the thick and thin parts alternating depending on whether the body is the encompassing or encompassed. He notes that in Mercury, an eccentric can be within another eccentric. Epicycles are spheres within the thickness of an eccentric, touching at a point called the apex (al-dhurwa) and perigee. The planet is fixed in the epicycle and considered solid. The commentator then clarifies terminology: 'then in the thickness of the inclined' indicates that the concentric containing the eccentric is the inclined for the moon, and for other planets it is their deferents. The 'carrier' is an eccentric that contains an epicycle, the 'director' is an eccentric that guides the carrier. For Mercury, this results in two apogees, two perigees, and four completers. Finally, the commentator summarizes the structure for each planet: the sun has a deferent with an eccentric containing the sun; the moon has a deferent, then an inclined, then an eccentric carrier with an epicycle containing the moon; Mercury has a deferent, then a director eccentric, then another eccentric carrier with an epicycle containing Mercury; the remaining four planets each have a deferent with an eccentric carrier containing an epicycle with the planet.

قال وكل جوف ممثل القمر فلك آخر مركزه الأرض يسمى المائل، ثم في ثخن المائل، وكل من الممثلات الغير القمر فلك شامل للأرض، خارج مركزه عن مركزها يسمى في عطارد مديراً، وفي باقي المتحيرة حاملاً يماس محدبه محدب المائل، أو الممثل بنقطة تسمى الأوج، ومقعره بنقطة تسمى الحضيض، ويبقى الفضل جسمين مستديرين على مركز العالم يسميان بالمتممين، يتدرج كل منهما من غلظ بقدر ما بين المركزين إلى رقة ينتهي عند نقطتي التماس على التبادل، بمعنى أن رقة الحاوي منهما عند الأوج، وغلظه عند الحضيض، والمحوى بالعكس، وفي ثخن المدير فلك آخر خارج المركز يسمى الحامل، ينفصل عن المدير، كالمدير عن الممثل فيكون لعطارد أوجان وحضيضان وأربع متممات والمائل في المتحيرة اسم للحامل، ثم في كل ثخن كل حامل كرة تسمى فلك التدوير، أحد طرفي قطرها يماس محدب الحامل، والآخر مقعره، والكوكب مفرق يماس سطحه سطحه والشمس في الخارج المركز كالتدوير في الحامل .
يريد الإشارة إلى تفصيل الأفلاك الجزئية التي يشتمل عليها الأفلاك الكلية وقد أرشدهم إلى ذلك ما أدركوا بالرصد للسيارات، من اختلاف الأوضاع والحركات، فمن الأفلاك الجزئية ممثل القمر ويسمى الجوزهرى لكونه المحرك للجوهريين ومشعر فيها ومنها حائل القمر وهو فلك في جوف ممثل القمر مركزه مركز العالم ، يسمى بذلك لكون منطقته مائلة عن منطقة البروج ميلاً ثابتاً لا يتغير فالجوزهري يماس بمحدبه مقعر ممثل عطارد، وبمقعره محدب المائل المماس بمقعره محدب كرة النار ومنها الأفلاك الخارجة المراكز والخارج المركز فلك محيط بالأرض خارج مركزه عن مركزها، ويكون في ثخن فلك،
يوافق المركز يماس بمحدبه محدب الموافق على نقطة واحدة هي أبعد نقطة عليه من مركز الأرض، ويسمى الأوج، وبمقعره مقعر الموافق على نقطة مقابلة أقرب نقطة عليه منه ، ويسمى الحضيض، فبالضرورة يبقى الفضل للأولى، هي من موافق المركز بعد انفصال الخارج المركز عنه جسمين مستديرين على مركز العالم، غليظي الوسط بقدر ما بين مركزي الموافق المركز، والخارج المركز يستدق ذلك الغلظ إلى أن ينعدم عند نقطتي المتماس المقابلتين لغايتي الغلظ. وهذان الجسمان يسميان بالمتممين، ويكون ذلك التدرج من الغلظ إلى الرقة فيهما على التبادل، بمعنى أن غاية رقة الحاوي منهما يكون عند الأوج، وغاية غلظه عند الحضيض، وغاية رقة المحوى عند الحضيض، وغلظه عند الأوج، وقد يكون الخارج المركز في ثخن خارج مركز آخر كما في عطارد ومنها التداوير، والتدوير كرة في ثخن الخارج المركز ، يماس سطحها محدب الخارج بنقطة يسمى الذروة ومقعره بنقطة مقابلة لها تسمى الحضيض، والكوكب مركوز فيهما بحيث يتماس سطحاهما على نقطة ولم يعتبر لها ولا للكوكب تقعير بل اعتبرا مصمتين فقوله، ثم في ثخن المائل إشارة إلى أن الموافق المركز المشتمل على الخارج المركز هو في القمر المائل، وفي البواقي ممثلاتها، وأن الحامل اسم للخارج المركز إذا كان في ثخنه التدوير لحمله مركز التدوير، وإن خارج المركز مديراً لإدارته مركز الحامل، وهو الخارج المركز الذي ينفصل عن المدير انفصال المدير عن الممثل، ويكون فيه التدوير لعطارد خارجاً مركز وأوجان، وحضيضان وأربع متممات والمائل في المتحيرة اسم للخارج المركز الحامل للتدوير إذا تقرر هذا فنقول للشمس ممثل في ثخنه خارج مركز في ثخنه الشمس، وللقمر ممثل في جوفه مائل في ثخنه خارج مركز هو الحامل للتدوير فيه القمر ولعطارد وممثل في ثخنه خارج مركز يسمى مديراً في ثخنه خارج مركز هو الحامل للتدوير فيه عطارد، ولكل من الأربع الباقية ممثل في ثخنه خارج مركز هو الحامل للتدوير فيه الكوكب.
Detailed explanation of the partial spheres contained in the universal spheres. He said: And every hollow of the moon's deferent is another sphere whose center is the earth, called the inclined, then in the thickness of the inclined. And each of the non-moon deferents is a sphere encompassing the earth, with its center outside the earth's center; it is called in Mercury a director, and in the remaining wanderers a carrier; its convexity touches the convexity of the inclined, or the deferent at a point called the apogee, and its concavity at a point called the perigee. And there remains the excess as two round bodies upon the center of the universe, called the completers; each of them progresses from thickness equal to the distance between the two centers to thinness ending at the two points of contact alternately, meaning that the thinness of the encompassing one of them is at the apogee, and its thickness at the perigee, and the encompassed is the opposite. And in the thickness of the director is another eccentric sphere called the carrier, separating from the director, like the director from the deferent; so for Mercury there are two apogees and two perigees and four completers. And the inclined in the wanderers is a name for the carrier. Then in every thickness of every carrier is a sphere called the epicycle; one end of its diameter touches the convexity of the carrier, and the other its concavity. And the planet is [??] touching its surface to its surface. And the sun is in the eccentric like the epicycle in the carrier. He intends to point to the detailed explanation of the partial spheres contained in the universal spheres. And what guided them to that is what they perceived by observation of the planets, of the variation of positions and motions. So among the partial spheres is the deferent of the moon, called al-jawzahari because it is the mover of the two jawhariyy and is a pointer in them. And among them is the barrier of the moon, which is a sphere in the hollow of the moon's deferent, its center the center of the universe, so called because its zone is inclined to the zone of the zodiac by an unchanging inclination. So al-jawzahari touches with its convexity the concavity of Mercury's deferent, and with its concavity the convexity of the inclined which touches with its concavity the convexity of the sphere of fire. And among them are the eccentric spheres. And the eccentric is a sphere encompassing the earth, its center outside the earth's center, and it is in the thickness of a sphere agreeing with the center; it touches with its convexity the convexity of the concentric at one point which is the farthest point on it from the earth's center, called the apogee; and with its concavity the concavity of the concentric at an opposite point, the nearest point on it from it, called the perigee. So necessarily there remains the excess for the first—that is, from the concentric after the separation of the eccentric from it—two round bodies on the center of the universe, thick in the middle to the extent of the distance between the centers of the concentric and the eccentric; that thickness tapers until it ceases at the two opposite points of contact at the extremes of thickness. And these two bodies are called the completers. And that progression from thickness to thinness in them is alternate, meaning that the extreme thinness of the encompassing one of them is at the apogee, and its extreme thickness at the perigee; and the extreme thinness of the encompassed is at the perigee, and its thickness at the apogee. And it may be that the eccentric is in the thickness of another eccentric, as in Mercury. And among them are the epicycles. And the epicycle is a sphere in the thickness of the eccentric; its surface touches the convexity of the eccentric at a point called the apex (al-dhurwa), and its concavity at an opposite point called the perigee. And the planet is fixed in them such that their surfaces touch at a point; and concavity is not considered for it nor for the planet, but they are considered solid. So his saying 'then in the thickness of the inclined' is an indication that the concentric containing the eccentric is for the moon the inclined, and for the rest their deferents; and that the 'carrier' is a name for the eccentric when in its thickness is the epicycle, for it carries the center of the epicycle; and that 'director' is a name for the eccentric that directs the center of the carrier—that is, the eccentric that separates from the director just as the director separates from the deferent. And in it, for Mercury, there is an eccentric, two apogees, two perigees, and four completers. And the 'inclined' for the wanderers is a name for the eccentric carrying the epicycle. If this is established, then we say: For the sun, a deferent in whose thickness is an eccentric in which is the sun. For the moon, a deferent in its hollow an inclined in whose thickness is an eccentric that is the carrier for the epicycle in which is the moon. And for Mercury, a deferent in its thickness is an eccentric called a director in whose thickness is an eccentric that is the carrier for the epicycle in which is Mercury. And for each of the remaining four, a deferent in whose thickness is an eccentric that is the carrier for the epicycle in which is the planet.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed description of the partial celestial spheres (al-afāk al-juz'iyya) that make up the universal spheres (al-afāk al-kulliyya) according to the Ptolemaic model, as presented in a kalam context. The passage begins by quoting a statement from the original text (matn) and then the commentator (shārih) explains it. The original text describes the structure: every hollow of the moon's deferent (mumaththil al-qamar) contains another sphere called the inclined (al-mā'il) centered on earth. For other planets, the deferents are eccentrics (khārij al-markaz) with specific names: for Mercury it is called a director (mudīr), for the rest a carrier (hāmil). These spheres touch the inclined or deferent at points called apogee (al-awj) and perigee (al-ḥaḍīḍ). Between the deferent and the eccentric, there remain two bodies called the completers (al-mutammimān) that taper from thick at the center to thin at the contact points. In the thickness of the director for Mercury is another eccentric called the carrier, creating two apogees, two perigees, and four completers. In each carrier's thickness is an epicycle (falk al-tadwīr), and the planet is placed touching its surface. The commentator then explains that this detailed knowledge comes from empirical observation (raṣd) of planetary positions and motions. He lists some partial spheres: the moon's deferent called al-jawzaharī (possibly a variant of jawzahr, the lunar nodes) and the moon's inclined sphere (ḥā'il al-qamar) which is an eccentric. The commentator describes the general eccentric sphere: a sphere encompassing the earth with its center offset, existing within a concentric sphere. The gap between them is filled by two round bodies (the completers) that are thick in the middle and thin at the points of contact, with the thick and thin parts alternating depending on whether the body is the encompassing or encompassed. He notes that in Mercury, an eccentric can be within another eccentric. Epicycles are spheres within the thickness of an eccentric, touching at a point called the apex (al-dhurwa) and perigee. The planet is fixed in the epicycle and considered solid. The commentator then clarifies terminology: 'then in the thickness of the inclined' indicates that the concentric containing the eccentric is the inclined for the moon, and for other planets it is their deferents. The 'carrier' is an eccentric that contains an epicycle, the 'director' is an eccentric that guides the carrier. For Mercury, this results in two apogees, two perigees, and four completers. Finally, the commentator summarizes the structure for each planet: the sun has a deferent with an eccentric containing the sun; the moon has a deferent, then an inclined, then an eccentric carrier with an epicycle containing the moon; Mercury has a deferent, then a director eccentric, then another eccentric carrier with an epicycle containing Mercury; the remaining four planets each have a deferent with an eccentric carrier containing an epicycle with the planet.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the specific arrangements of deferents, eccentrics, and epicycles for the sun, moon, Mercury, and the other planets.

Each planet has a unique nested sphere structure to explain its motion.

The commentator concludes by summarizing the sphere arrangement for each planet. How the Epicycle Causes Retrograde and Stationary Motion
Explanation of epicycle causing direct, slow, stationary, retrograde motion

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The epicycle's motion can add to or subtract from the carrier's motion, causing apparent direction changes.

In this final part of the passage, Taftazani explains the epicycle mechanism. The planet moves on a small circle (epicycle) whose center moves on a larger carrier sphere. When the planet is on the half of the epicycle that moves in the same direction as the carrier, it appears fast; on the opposite half, it appears slow or even retrograde. The conditions for station and retrogression are described quantitatively.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two halves of the epicycle
The epicycle is divided into an upper half (for superior planets) and a lower half (for the Moon) that agree with the carrier's motion, and opposite halves that disagree.

2
Fast motion case
When the planet is on the half that agrees with the carrier, both motions add, so the planet appears fast.
3

If epicyclic motion equals carrier's motion, the planet appears stationary (only for superior planets). If epicyclic motion exceeds carrier's, it appears retrograde.
The Moon never appears stationary or retrograde because its epicyclic motion is always less than the carrier's.

The soul's multiplicity proves it is created, not pre-eternal.

The author presents the most important proof for the soul's temporality. He starts from an observed fact: after attachment, souls are many. واعترض بوجهين : أحدهما : أنا لا نسلم بطلان كون كل فرد من أفراد النفوس نوعاً منحصراً في الشخص، إذ لم تقم حجة على أنه يجب أن توجد نفسان متحدتان في الماهية . وثانيهما : أنا لا نسلم امتناع أن يوجد جسم قديم تتعلق به النفس في الأزل، ثم تنتقل منه إلى آخر ، وآخر على سبيل التناسخ . كيف وعمدتهم الوثقى في إبطال التناسخ مبنية على حدوث النفس كما سيجيء . فلو بني إثبات الحدوث على بطلان التناسخ كان دوراً. فإن قيل: نحن نبين امتناع تعين النفس بالعوارض البدنية بوجه لا يتوقف على بطلان التناسخ . بأن نقول : لو كان تعين هذه النفس بالعوارض المتعلقة بهذا البدن، لما كانت متعينة قبله، فلم تكن موجودة، سواء كان التناسخ حقاً أو باطلاً. قلنا : الملازمة ممنوعة لجواز أن تكون قبل هذا البدن متعينة ببدن آخر معين وقبله بآخر، وآخر لا إلى بداية ، فتكون موجودة بتعينات متعاقبة ، فلا بد من إبطال ذلك . وقد يجاب عن الاعتراضين : بأن الكلام إلى إلزامي على من سلم تماثل النفوس وبطلان التناسح .
And it is objected in two ways: First: We do not grant the falsehood of each individual soul being a species confined to the person, for no proof has been established that two souls must exist sharing the same essence. Second: We do not grant the impossibility of an eternal body to which the soul is attached in pre-eternity, then it transfers from it to another, and another by way of reincarnation. How so, while their main reliance in refuting reincarnation is based on the temporal origination of the soul, as will come. So if establishing origination is based on refuting reincarnation, it would be circular. If it is said: We demonstrate the impossibility of the soul's individuation by bodily accidents in a way that does not depend on refuting reincarnation, by saying: If the individuation of this soul were through accidents related to this body, it would not be individuated before it, so it would not have existed, whether reincarnation is true or false. We say: The implication is not granted, because it is possible that before this body it was individuated by another specific body, and before that by another, and so on without beginning, so it would exist with successive individuations; therefore it is necessary to refute that. And it may be answered against the two objections: that the discussion is ad hominem against one who has accepted the similarity of souls and the falsehood of reincarnation.

Commentary
This passage contains two objections and their responses. The first objection denies that all souls must share the same essence, so they can be distinct without bodies. The second objection argues that a pre-eternal body could exist for the soul, thus reincarnation is possible, and the standard proof against reincarnation (based on the soul's origination) would be circular if it relies on that refutation. A further objection is then raised: even if reincarnation is false, the soul's individuation by bodily accidents would imply it did not exist before this body, which is impossible. The answer counters that the soul could have been individuated by a series of previous bodies in an infinite regress. Finally, a general response is given: the entire line of argument is ad hominem, directed at those who already accept the similarity of souls and the falsehood of reincarnation, so it does not need to prove these from scratch.
