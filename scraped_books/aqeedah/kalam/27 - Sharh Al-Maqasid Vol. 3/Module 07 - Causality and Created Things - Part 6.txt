Learning Objective

Core Idea
Souls cannot come from God directly because they are always tied to bodies.

Al-Taftazani presents a concise but powerful argument about the origin of souls. He insists that the source of all souls cannot be the Necessary Being Himself, nor can it be bodies. Instead, souls must proceed from the intellects—the intermediate immaterial beings. This lesson unpacks that reasoning.

Guided Path

Read the passage and translation

Bodies and their parts act only through participation—meaning they don't act by themselves. Souls act with bodies, not as bodies. So souls can't be from bodies.
This reinforces that souls are non-material and require an intermediate source.

Objection 5: Non-Existent Aspects in the Necessary Being
Fifth objection: negations and relations in God allow multiplicity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fifth objection: if non-existent aspects (negations and relations) are sufficient to produce multiplicity, then the Necessary Being's own negations and relations would allow multiplicity directly from Him.

Core Idea
God's negations and relations could produce many, too.

The philosophers use non-existent aspects (like possibility) in the lower intellects to explain multiplicity. Sanusi argues that if such non-existents are enough, then the Necessary Being Himself has many negations and relations (e.g., being not composed, being the First, etc.). These would then allow multiplicity to arise directly from God, contradicting the principle and making the emanation chain unnecessary.

Read the Fifth Objection

3
Recognize the Implications
This objection shows that the philosophers' reliance on non-existents is ad hoc. The Necessary Being has many negations and relations, so if non-existents count, He should produce multiplicity directly.

Third: The statement that the nobler is attributed to the nobler is rhetorical, not befitting demonstrative sciences. Fourth: Attributing the sphere of the fixed stars, despite their multiplicity, to the second intellect by virtue of its possibility establishes the emanation of many from the One; likewise attributing elemental forms and accidents to the last intellect. Fifth: If non-existent aspects and relational aspects were sufficient for the emanation of many from the One, then it would be permissible to attribute it to the Necessary Being by virtue of His negations and relations. Sixth: If the intellects differ in species such that the last is one at which the chain of intellects and spheres terminates—in that no sphere, intellect, or soul emanates from it—then it is possible at the beginning that from the first intellect only a second intellect emanates, and from the second only a third, and so on, until the emanation of the greatest sphere occurs after the emanation of many intellects. Commentary
This passage presents six objections against the philosophers' theory of emanation, specifically against the principle 'from the One only one proceeds'. The objections are enumerated (second through sixth, with the first being implicit in the opening sentence). Each objection highlights a different logical flaw in the emanationist framework: the impossibility of multiplicity arising from a simple One, the infinite regress of intellects, the rhetorical nature of their assumptions, the inconsistency in attributing multiplicity to lower intellects while denying it to the First, the sufficiency of negations and relations in the Necessary Being, and the arbitrary assumption that the chain ends at ten intellects. The passage concludes by noting that the entire discourse rests on weak premises and that mere probability is insufficient for certain knowledge.

The Elemental Mixture of Angels, Jinn, and Devils
Explains elemental composition and predominance for three types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how angels, jinn, and devils are composed from the four elements, with a predominance of fire or air that shapes their nature and abilities.

Core Idea
Elemental predominance determines the subtlety and power of spiritual beings.

This passage delves into the physical composition of angels, jinn, and devils according to the theory of the four elements. It clarifies that while all three are made from earth, water, air, and fire, the predominance of air or fire gives them their subtle and transcendent properties.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on elemental mixture
Read the Arabic passage once to get the overall idea. Then read the translation to understand the details of how the three types are composed.
Pay attention to the role of predominance (ghalaba) in determining elemental inclination.
2

Note that devils are dominated by fire, while angels and jinn are dominated by air. Understand that this dominance affects their substance and abilities.

3
Understand the variability of predominance
The passage explains that the mixture does not need to be exactly balanced; rather, one element can predominate to varying degrees. This variability accounts for different types of beings.
There is no fixed limit (لا حد معين) for this predominance.
