Interpretations of the Intellect Stages Among Scholars
Taftazani introduces different verbal interpretations of the intellect stages.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the different ways scholars define the terms for the intellect stages and the conceptual distinctions involved.

Core Idea
The intellect stages are not fixed terms; scholars vary in what they name: dispositions, soul, or faculties.

In this passage, Taftazani first compares the intellect to sunlight, then notes that scholars disagree on how to name the stages. He presents multiple interpretations for the material intellect and the intellect by disposition. This lesson clarifies these different verbal traditions and their conceptual bases.

Guided Path

The simile is part of the broader discussion.
2
Identify the three interpretative approaches

3

The same pattern applies to the other stages.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification of the intellect states. The author begins by stating that the relation of the intellect to us is like the relation of the sun to our eyes, indicating that the intellect is a light by which we see. Then he notes that scholars differ in terminology: whether the terms 'material intellect', 'intellect by disposition', etc., are names for the dispositions and perfections themselves, or for the soul qualified by them, or for the faculties that are their sources. The Acquired Intellect: Constant Presence or Mere Possession?
Taftazani presents two interpretations of the acquired intellect.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The acquired intellect is either angelic constancy or simple possession of truths.

Taftazani presents a key disagreement about the acquired intellect. Some scholars argue it requires perpetual awareness of all theoretical truths, reaching an angelic state; others say it is mere presence of truths. This passage explores both views and hints at the preferred position.

Taftazani presents al-Razi's classification of the soul's four states corresponding to the four intellects.

And the Imam mentioned in the explanation of the ranks: that if the soul is devoid of sciences while being capable of them, it is called in that state a material intellect (hayulani). Otherwise, if only the necessary sciences are acquired, it is then called an intellect by disposition (bi-l-malaka). Learning Objective

The practical intellect guides actions by deriving particular opinions from universal truths.

Taftazani begins by distinguishing the practical intellect from the theoretical. He defines it as the power that governs our actions, crafts, and moral discernment, and explains how it depends on the theoretical intellect for its particular judgments. This passage lays the foundation for understanding how human beings navigate both worldly and religious affairs through reasoned action.

The phrase 'يستعين بالنظري' highlights its dependence.

He said (As for the practical intellect): It is the power of acting upon subject matters, deriving crafts, and distinguishing good from corrupt for the ordering of livelihood and afterlife.
