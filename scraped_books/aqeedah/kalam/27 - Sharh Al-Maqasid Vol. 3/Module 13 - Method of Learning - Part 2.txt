Objection and Resolution: The Role of Aspect
Objection and answer about overlapping subject matter, and subdivision of practical wisdom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the concept of aspect (haysiyya) resolves the objection that divine and mathematical sciences overlap in subject matter, and how practical wisdom is divided.

Core Idea
The same object can be studied under different aspects in different sciences.

The classification of sciences faces an important objection: divine science seems to include topics like unity and causality that relate to matter, and mathematics includes abstract objects like number. The author uses the restriction of aspect to show how these are studied under different aspects in different sciences. The lesson also covers the final subdivision of practical wisdom into ethics, household management, and politics.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and the response
First read the objection from al-Mutarahat, then the author's resolution using 'the restriction of aspect' (qayd al-haysiyya). Notice how the same example (number) is used to illustrate the point.
2
Understand the concept of aspect
An aspect is a particular way of considering an object. A number can be considered in itself (abstract) or as applied to material things (e.g., counting apples).
This concept prevents overlap between sciences.
3
Follow the resolution for number
The author explains: number considered independently of matter belongs to divine science; considered in material things (addition, subtraction) belongs to mathematics.
Al-Shifa (Avicenna) is cited in support.
4
Examine the subdivision of practical wisdom
Practical wisdom is divided based on the scope of human association: individual (ethics), family (household management), city/state (politics).
This completes the classification of sciences.

وعلم تدبير المنزل، وعلم سياسة المدينة. لأن النظرية إن كانت علماً بأحوال الموجودات من حيث يتعلق بالمادة تصوراً وقواماً، فهي العلم الطبيعي، وإن كانت من حيث يتعلق بها قواماً لا تصوراً. فالرياضي . كالبحث عن الخطوط، والسطوح، وغيرهما، مما يفتقر إلى المادة في الوجود، لا في التصور، وإن كانت من حيث لا يتعلق بها لا قواماً ولا تصوراً فالإلهي، ويسمى العلم الأعلى، وعلم ما بعد الطبيعة، كالبحث عن الواجب والمجردات وما يتعلق بذلك . واعترض صاحب المطارحات بأن في الإلهي ما يتعلق بالمادة في الجملة، كالوحدة والكثرة ،والعلية والمعلولية، وكثير من الأمور العامة، وفي الرياضي ما قد يستغنى عنها ، كالعدد وهو مدفوع بقيد الحيثية . فإن العدد إذا اعتبر من حيث كان مستغنياً عن المادة، ويبحث عنه في الإلهي . وإذا اعتبر من حيث هو في الأوهام، أو في الموجودات المادية متفرقة و مجتمعة، فيبحث عن الجمع والتفريق والضرب والقسمة، فهو علم العدد المعدود من أقسام الرياضي، وإلى هذا أشار في الشفاء. إلا أنه قد يناقس في اختصاص حيثية الجمع والتفريق، والضرب والقسمة . وبالجملة المباحث الحسابية لغير المجردات، والحكمة العملية، إن تعلقت بآراء ينتظم بها حال الشخص، وزكاء نفسه. فالحكمة الخلقية، وإلا فإن تعلقت بانتظام المشاركة الإنسانية الخاصة، فالحكمة المنزلية والعامة، فالحكمة المدنية والسياسية .
And the science of household management, and the science of city politics. Because theoretical [science] if it is knowledge of the states of existents insofar as they are related to matter in conception and subsistence, then it is natural science; and if [it is] insofar as they are related to it in subsistence not conception, then mathematical, like investigation of lines, surfaces, and other things that require matter in existence, not in conception; and if it is insofar as not related to it neither in subsistence nor in conception, then divine, and it is called the highest science and metaphysics, like investigation of the Necessary and the immaterial and what pertains to that. And the author of al-Mutarahat objected that in the divine there is what is related to matter in general, like unity and multiplicity, causality and effect, and many universal matters, and in the mathematical there is what may dispense with it, like number. And it is repelled by the restriction of aspect (haysiyya). For if number is considered insofar as it is independent of matter, it is investigated in the divine; and if it is considered insofar as it is in the imaginations or in material existents, separated and combined, then investigation of addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division is carried out, so it is the science of number counted among the divisions of mathematics. And to this al-Shifa referred. Except that there may be discussion about the specification of the aspect of addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. In summary, the mathematical investigations are for non-immaterial things. And practical wisdom: if it is concerned with opinions by which the state of the person and the purity of his soul are regulated, then it is ethical wisdom; otherwise, if it is concerned with the regulation of special human partnership, then it is household wisdom; and [if with] general [partnership], then it is civil and political wisdom.

Commentary
This passage classifies the theoretical and practical sciences. It begins by completing the list of practical sciences: household management and city politics. Then it explains the division of theoretical science based on its relation to matter: if the science considers existents in terms of both conception and subsistence requiring matter, it is natural science; if it requires matter only for subsistence but not for conception (like mathematical objects which can be conceived without matter but exist in matter), it is mathematical; if it requires matter neither for conception nor subsistence, it is divine science, also called metaphysics, studying the Necessary Being and immaterial beings. An objection from the author of al-Mutarahat is then presented: that divine science also deals with matters related to matter, such as unity, multiplicity, causality, etc., and on the other hand, mathematics has objects like number that can be independent of matter. The objection is refuted using the restriction of aspect (haysiyya): number considered as independent of matter belongs to divine science; considered as applied to material things (addition, subtraction, etc.) belongs to mathematics. The passage notes that al-Shifa (Avicenna's work) alludes to this, though there may be discussion about the specification of these aspects. Finally, practical wisdom is subdivided into ethics (regulating the individual's soul), household management (regulating the family), and politics (regulating the city or state).

Weaknesses of the Emanation Scheme's Premises
Notes the philosophers' own admission of possibility and critiques weak premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the philosophers' emanation scheme is based on weak premises and why probability is insufficient for theological certainty.

Core Idea
Probability cannot substitute for certainty in scientific matters.

This passage presents a strong critique of the philosophers' emanation scheme by highlighting their own admissions and methodological flaws. The author argues that relying on probability and preference is insufficient for establishing scientific truths, a key principle in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once and then the translation. Notice the flow of the critique.
2
Find the philosophers' admission
Identify where the philosophers themselves admit the possibility of more spheres, which undermines their fixed scheme.
3
Understand the methodological critique
The author argues that probability and preference (al-ihtimal wa al-awlawiyya) are not sufficient for scientific inquiries. Why is this important?
This is a key methodological point in kalam.

First, how? And the particular spheres are many, each requiring a principle. — — And they admitted that it is possible that between the supreme sphere and the sphere of the fixed stars there are many spheres, and that each of the fixed stars is upon a sphere of this [kind]. And it is not hidden that their speech in this place, despite its being based on the principle that from the one only one issues, includes other weak premises, and that probability and preference do not avail much benefit in scientific inquiries.
Source pages p. 365

Commentary
This passage concludes the discussion by pointing out further weaknesses in the philosophers' emanation scheme. The author notes that the philosophers themselves admit possibilities that undermine their fixed scheme: for example, there could be many spheres between the supreme sphere and the sphere of the fixed stars, and each fixed star could have its own sphere. This flexibility contradicts their assertion of a fixed number of spheres and intellects. The author then delivers a final critique: their entire argument, which is built on the principle 'from one only one' (al-wahid la yasdur 'anhu illa al-wahid), includes other weak premises. Moreover, probability and preponderance (al-ihtimal wa al-awlawiyya) are not sufficient to yield certain knowledge in scientific inquiries. This is a strong methodological critique, emphasizing that kalam requires demonstrative certainty, not mere plausibility.
