Argument from the Sphere
Argument from sphere-plane tangency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the contact between a sphere and a plane demonstrates that surfaces are composed of indivisible atoms.

Core Idea
A sphere contacts a plane at a single indivisible point, showing matter is atomic.

The second argument uses a thought experiment with a perfect sphere on a flat plane. The initial contact is at a point, and as the sphere rotates, the line of contact sweeps across the plane, revealing that the entire surface is made of indivisible points. This supports the idea that matter is composed of atoms.

Guided Path
1
Visualize the scenario
Imagine a perfect sphere placed on a flat surface. The point of contact is where the sphere touches the plane.
2
Understand the rotation
When the sphere rotates, the contact point moves along a line. This line must be composed of the indivisible contact points.
3
Draw the conclusion
Since the contact point is indivisible, the line and the entire surface must be made of such indivisible units.

Argument from the minimal angle.

The Four Types of Division in Kalam Atomism
Al-Maqasid explains the meaning of 'absolutely indivisible' and distinguishes types of division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what the mutakallimun mean by 'absolutely indivisible' and how they distinguish four types of division: cutting, breaking, imagination, and supposition.

Core Idea
The atom is indivisible in four distinct senses, but the intellect can still conceive of it.

The mutakallimun argued that bodies are composed of indivisible atoms. To clarify what 'indivisible' means, they specify four types of division: physical and mental. This passage distinguishes them and explains the limitations of the estimative faculty compared to the intellect.

The intellect's divisions are universal, not actual.

Third Argument: Three Parts in Contact
Taftazani gives the third argument for divisibility of the atom from three contiguous parts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third argument for the divisibility of the atom based on three parts in contact, and its two logical consequences.

Core Idea
The middle part must either have distinct faces or be superfluous—both prove divisibility.

Taftazani presents a clever conditional argument: if three atoms are lined up, the middle one either prevents the outer ones from touching or it doesn't. Both possibilities lead to the conclusion that atoms must be divisible. Let's examine the logic carefully.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Start by reading the original Arabic passage and its literal translation. Get a feel for the logical flow.
2
Understand the setup
Imagine three indivisible parts in a line: left, middle, right. The middle is between the other two, touching each. This is the scenario.
Note that this is a thought experiment.
3
Follow the first branch
If the middle part prevents the outer two from touching, then its face toward the left must be different from its face toward the right. That means the middle part has at least two distinct faces, implying it is divisible.
This branch leads directly to division.
4
Follow the second branch
If the middle part does not prevent them, then the outer two would meet and combine, creating a volume from just the two outer parts. But that would make the middle part unnecessary, and repeating this would prevent the formation of any body. This contradicts the existence of bodies, so this branch is impossible.

Seventh Argument: Diagonal of a Square
Taftazani's seventh geometric argument refuting atomism using the diagonal of a square.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the diagonal of a square composed of indivisible parts leads to contradictions, refuting atomism.

Core Idea
The diagonal of a square cannot be a whole number of parts without contradiction.

The seventh argument considers a square made of four lines, each four parts long. The diagonal must be either composed of parts without gaps, in which case it would equal the side, or with a gap, making it equal the sum of two sides, both absurd. This demonstrates that indivisible parts cannot form continuous lines.

Circle and sphere geometry exposes contradictions in atomism.

He first defines the circle and sphere in terms of rotation, then shows that their properties cannot be explained if bodies are composed of indivisible atoms. Reflect on the completeness of the argument
Note that the passage ends abruptly at 'واللزوم' (the implication). The intended conclusion is that the necessary consequences are impossible, thus the premise of atomism must be false.
The reasoning is a classic reductio ad absurdum.

Sanusi presents and refutes the third argument from shadow ratios.

Learning Objective

The shadow argument only applies to bodies that have a half, not to minimal indivisible parts.

The third argument uses the fact that at a certain time a body's shadow doubles, implying that half the shadow corresponds to half the body. The passage then gives an example of dichotomous division (al-qisma al-thunā'iyya), the ideal according to Plato: starting with 'politics is a science', then dividing science into theoretical and practical, placing politics under theoretical, then dividing theoretical science into that which commands and that which determines, and so on until the meaning of politics is delimited.

The Peripatetic Proof for Hyle

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Hyle is the unchanging substrate that remains when bodies connect or separate.

The Peripatetics, heirs to Aristotle, developed a subtle argument to prove that all material bodies require a primordial matter, or hyle, that persists through change. Their reasoning begins by examining what it means for a body to be connectable or separable. This lesson walks through that argument step by step.

The Various Meanings of 'Ittisal' and Its Logical Role

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the different meanings of 'ittisal' (continuity) and its role as a differentia distinguishing magnitude from number in the category of quantity.

Core Idea
'Ittisal' is a key term with multiple meanings, primarily distinguishing continuous quantity from discrete number.

After defining the primary sense of 'ittisal' as the property of a substance that can receive three dimensions, al-Sanusi notes that the term has other accidental meanings. He classifies them into relational and non-relational, and then explains that in its primary sense, 'ittisal' serves as a differentia for quantity, dividing it into continuous (magnitude) and discrete (number).
