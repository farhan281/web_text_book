Core Idea
Continuity is a substance, not an accident—essential for understanding the body.

This passage addresses the first difficulty raised against the Ash'ari notion of continuity as a substance. The opponent argues that continuity is merely an accident opposite to discontinuity, so it cannot be a substance or part of the body. The author clarifies his intended meaning—a substance whose property is continuity—and then defends his position by countering an objection about the continuous identity being the body itself, distinguishing between perishing of a body and splitting of identity.

Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi raises a powerful objection against the concept of hyle (prime matter). He structures his argument as a dilemma: if hyle exists, it must be either spatially extended or not. The author's refutation of the objection.

Learning Objective

Lack of independent extension does not make hyle an attribute; body's inherence may condition hyle's extension.

The author now responds to Imam al-Razi's powerful objection. He does not reject the logical form but instead challenges the assumption that non-independent extension implies attribute status. In this lesson, you will see the subtle reply that preserves the possibility of hyle.

The atom's shape debate hinges on what 'finite' means.

Commentary
This passage is a continuous argument about natural shape and place. It begins by stating that every finite body has a shape (shakl), as shape is simply the form of the boundary enclosing the body. The need for a place (ḥayyiz) in the sense of a void the body occupies is considered a necessary judgment. These are usually mentioned only when requiring clarification or when there is disagreement. The author then presents the Ash'ari position (our school) that the specificities of shapes and places are attributed to the Powerful Chooser (God), while the philosophers (falāsifa) hold that each body has a natural shape and place determined by its nature, independent of external causes. The author then addresses an objection: one might argue that a body could have any shape or place, with the specific one being from an external cause like God's will; but this is consistent with the philosophers' view because if the natural shape is not specified, then the body would lack a definite nature. The philosophers deny that the essence alone necessitates a specific shape; rather, it is due to a necessary concomitant of the essence (lāzim al-māhiyya). The author clarifies that what is necessitated by a concomitant of the essence is natural, not forced. Then he defines ḥayyiz: it is not merely the inner surface of a container, nor the void (the philosophers, especially Ibn Sīnā, hold that every body has a ḥayyiz—either a place (makān), a position (waḍ‘), or an arrangement (tartīb)). Then there is an objection: if natural place is not caused by external causes nor by the body's nature, then it must be caused by something else, leading to infinite regress. The author replies that this difficulty was resolved earlier in the discussion of specific forms (ṣuwar naw‘iyya). Then he proves that natural place must be unique: because the requirement of the one (body) is one; if there were multiple natural places, the body would occupy impossible contradictions when free from constraints. Finally, al-Ījī (author of al-Mawāqif) is cited, who considers the proof for natural place to depend on the theory of hyle (matter), since atomists view all bodies as similar, differing only by accidents.

Commentary
This passage is a continuous argument about natural shape and place. It begins by stating that every finite body has a shape (shakl), as shape is simply the form of the boundary enclosing the body. The need for a place (ḥayyiz) in the sense of a void the body occupies is considered a necessary judgment. These are usually mentioned only when requiring clarification or when there is disagreement. The author then presents the Ash'ari position (our school) that the specificities of shapes and places are attributed to the Powerful Chooser (God), while the philosophers (falāsifa) hold that each body has a natural shape and place determined by its nature, independent of external causes. The author then addresses an objection: one might argue that a body could have any shape or place, with the specific one being from an external cause like God's will; but this is consistent with the philosophers' view because if the natural shape is not specified, then the body would lack a definite nature. The philosophers deny that the essence alone necessitates a specific shape; rather, it is due to a necessary concomitant of the essence (lāzim al-māhiyya). The author clarifies that what is necessitated by a concomitant of the essence is natural, not forced. Then he defines ḥayyiz: it is not merely the inner surface of a container, nor the void (the philosophers, especially Ibn Sīnā, hold that every body has a ḥayyiz—either a place (makān), a position (waḍ‘), or an arrangement (tartīb)). Then there is an objection: if natural place is not caused by external causes nor by the body's nature, then it must be caused by something else, leading to infinite regress. The author replies that this difficulty was resolved earlier in the discussion of specific forms (ṣuwar naw‘iyya). Then he proves that natural place must be unique: because the requirement of the one (body) is one; if there were multiple natural places, the body would occupy impossible contradictions when free from constraints. Finally, al-Ījī (author of al-Mawāqif) is cited, who considers the proof for natural place to depend on the theory of hyle (matter), since atomists view all bodies as similar, differing only by accidents.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of views on the necessity of accidents in bodies. The author first states that the first view (mentioned earlier) reduces to total affirmation, the second to total negation, and the most plausible is partial affirmation, meaning that every body must have some accident. He then details two subgroups: the Baghdad Mu'tazila specify colors, while the Basran Mu'tazila specify spatial states (motion/rest, conjunction/separation). Next, he presents the proof of the objectors (those who deny partial necessity) that a body in time must have motion/rest etc., but shows that this only proves partial necessity, not total. He then presents another proof based on individuation (tashakhkhus), showing that bodies need accidents for individuation, but again this only proves partial necessity (some accident suffices). He notes that this proof can be extended to all times and all accidents by analogy, but then concludes that the universal answer to both proofs is the denial of the two premises (man' al-muqaddimatayn). The passage is technical, engaging with Mu'tazilite positions.

Third Proof: Colorlessness of Air
Uses air to argue bodies can be colorless, replies to objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the colorlessness of air serves as a proof in kalam, and how the author refutes objections about perception and obstacles.

Core Idea
Absence of sensation with sound senses proves non-existence, else all knowledge collapses.

This lesson examines a concrete empirical argument used by theologians to show that bodies can be free from accidental properties like color. By analyzing the example of air, which appears colorless, the author demonstrates that if something is naturally perceptible but not sensed, it likely does not exist. He then addresses a skeptical objection that could undermine all empirical knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage carefully

2
Identify the main argument and the objection
The proof is: air is colorless, so bodies can be colorless. The objector says we only lack sensation, not that color is absent. The author responds with a principle about sensation.
3

The objector then says there might be an unseen obstacle. The author responds that this leads to radical skepticism (mountains and sounds unnoticed).
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
4

A different response says transparency is an opposite of color, so air has an accident (transparency) instead of being colorless.
This is a secondary point, not the author's main refutation.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof using the empirical example of air. The argument is that air is perceived to be colorless, so if bodies could not be free of all colors, air would have to have some color, but it does not. An objector says that we do not know air is free of color; we only know we do not sense it. The author replies that if something is naturally sensible, and the sense is sound and conditions are met, then lack of sensation indicates non-existence. The objector then raises the possibility of an unknown obstacle (māniʿ). The author responds that this leads to skepticism: we could claim there are mountains and sounds we don't perceive due to obstacles, which is absurd. Finally, an alternative answer is mentioned by others: transparency (ash-shafīf) is an opposite of color, not a mere absence, so air actually has an accident (transparency) rather than being free. This proof and its refutations illustrate the kalām method of empirical argument and refutation by reductio ad absurdum.
