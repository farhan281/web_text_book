The Argument for the Eternity of the World from Sufficient Conditions
Presents the argument for the world's eternity from the sufficiency of conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first argument for the world's eternity based on the sufficiency of conditions in pre-eternity, and how it is structured from two angles.

Core Idea
If all conditions for the world existed in pre-eternity, it must exist then—or so the argument claims.

This lesson presents the first of several arguments for the pre-eternity of the world. The argument is based on a dichotomy: what is indispensable for the world's existence either existed in pre-eternity or not. Since the second option leads to an infinite regress, the first must hold, implying the world is eternal. The argument is then detailed from two angles.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the translation to understand the overall argument.
Notice that the argument begins with a dichotomy.
2
Identify the dichotomy and conclusion
Find the two possibilities: either all necessary conditions existed in pre-eternity or they did not. The argument concludes the first must be true.
3
Examine the two ways the premise is supported
The argument provides two proofs: first, if all conditions existed, the world must exist because an effect cannot lag behind its complete cause; second, if not all conditions existed, an infinite regress would follow.
These two ways are enumerated explicitly.
4
Reflect on the appeal to complete cause
The first proof relies on the principle that an effect cannot be delayed when its complete cause is present. This principle will be challenged later.
This is a key premise in the debate.

يقدم العالم بوجوه. جميع الأول: أن ما لا بد منه لتأثير الصانع في العالم وإيجاده إياه، إما أن يكون حاصلا في الأزل أو لا. والثاني: باطل فتعين الأول وهو يستلزم المطلوب. وتقريره من وجهين: أحدهما: أنه لما وجد في الأزل جميع ما لا بد منه بوجود العالم لزم وجوده في الأزل، والمقدم حق فكذا التالي، أما اللزوم فلامتناع تخلف المعلول عن تمام علته لما مرّ، وأما حقية المقدم، ولأنه لو لم يكن جميع ما لا بد منه لوجوده، إما أن يكون حاصلاً في الأزل أو لا ويتسلسل. والجواب: النقض إجمالاً وتفصيلاً. أما إجمالاً فهو أنه لو صح الدليل لزم أن لا يكون ما يوجد اليوم من الحوادث حادثاً لجريانه فيه لا يقال الحادث اليومي يتوقف على استعدادات في المادة مستندة الى الحركات والأوضاع الفلكية والاتصالات الكوكبية، ووجود كل منها مشروط بانقضاء الآخر لا إلى بداية على سبيل التجدد والانقضاء دون الترتيب في الوجود، على ما هو شأن العلل والمعلولات ليلزم التسلسل المحال. فإن البرهان إنما قام على استحالة التسلسل في المبادىء المترتبة دون المعدات المتصرمة لأنا نقول بعض البراهين كالتطبيق والتضائف يتناول ما يضبطها الوجود مترتبة سواء كانت مجتمعة أو متصرمة كما آنفاً. سبق ولو سلّم فالكلام في العالم الجسماني فيجوز أن يكون حادثاً مستنداً الى حوادث متعاقبة لا أول لها كتصورات أو إرادات من ذات مجردة مثل ما ذكرتم في الحادث اليومي. لا يقال تعاقب الحوادث، إنما يصح في الجسمانيات دون المجردات لما سبق من أن كل حادث مسبوق بمادة ومدة. لأنا نقول: قد سبق الكلام على ذلك هنالك. وأما تفصيلاً فهو أنا لا نسلّم أنه لو كان جميع ما لا بد منه في إيجاد العالم حاصلا في الأزل كان العالم أزلياً، وإنما يلزم لو لم يكن من جملة ما لا بد منه، الإرادة التي من شأنها الترجيح والتخصيص متى شاء الفاعل من غير افتقار الى مرجح ومخصص من خارج قولكم يلزم تخلف المعلول عن تمام علته وهو باطل لامتناع الترجيح بلا مرجح. قلنا: لا نسلم بطلان التخلف في العلة المشتملة على الإرادة والاختيار
He presents the world's pre-eternity in aspects. The first of all: that what is indispensable for the Maker's effect in the world and His bringing it into existence is either existent in pre-eternity or not. The second is false, so the first is established, and it entails the desired conclusion. Its explication is in two ways: One of them is that when all that is indispensable for the existence of the world existed in pre-eternity, the existence of the world in pre-eternity is necessary, and the premise is true, so likewise the conclusion. As for the necessity, it is because of the impossibility of the effect's lagging behind its complete cause, as has passed. As for the truth of the premise, and because if not all that is indispensable for its existence were existent in pre-eternity, it would either be existent in pre-eternity or not, and an infinite regress would result. And the answer: refutation in general and in detail. As for in general, it is that if the proof were valid, it would necessitate that what exists today of temporal events would not be temporal, because the same reasoning applies. It cannot be said that the daily temporal event depends on preparations in matter based on movements, astronomical positions, and planetary conjunctions, and the existence of each is conditioned by the expiration of the other without beginning, in the manner of renewal and expiration without order in existence, as is the norm with causes and effects, so that an impossible infinite regress would follow. For the proof is based only on the impossibility of infinite regress in ordered principles, not in transient instruments. For we say: some proofs, like the proof of correspondence and parallelism, address whatever delineates existence as ordered, whether they are simultaneous or transient, as previously stated. And even if conceded, the discussion concerns the physical world, so it is possible that it be temporal, depending on successive events with no beginning, like concepts or wills from an abstract entity, similar to what you mentioned regarding the daily temporal event. It cannot be said that the succession of events is only valid for physical things, not abstract entities, because of what has preceded that every temporal event is preceded by matter and time. For we say: the discussion on that has already occurred there. As for in detail, it is that we do not concede that if all that is indispensable for the existence of the world were existent in pre-eternity, the world would be eternal. Rather, it would follow only if among what is indispensable is not included the will, which is characterized by giving preponderance and specification whenever the agent wills, without being in need of a preponderant and specifier from outside. Your statement: 'the lagging of the effect behind its complete cause would follow, and that is false because of the impossibility of preponderance without a preponderant.' We say: we do not concede the falsity of lagging in the case of a cause that includes will and choice.

Commentary
This passage presents and refutes the first argument for the world's pre-eternity. The argument claims that whatever is indispensable for the world's existence must either exist in pre-eternity (al-azal) or not. Since not existing would lead to an infinite regress (tasalsul), it must have existed in pre-eternity, so the world is eternal. The refutation is given in two forms. In general (ijmalan): if this argument were sound, it would also apply to daily temporal events, making them not temporal—which is absurd. The specific reason is that the argument only works for ordered principles, not transient instruments (mu'addat mutasarrima). Then the author addresses a potential objection about the physical world and abstract entities, stating that the discussion has already occurred. In detail (tafsilan): we do not concede that if all conditions were present in pre-eternity the world would be eternal, because one of those conditions is divine will (al-irada), which can choose any time without needing an external determinant. Therefore, the effect's lagging behind the cause is permissible when the cause includes will and choice (ikhtiyar), contradicting the claim of impossibility.

General Refutation: The Argument Would Apply to Daily Events
Provides the general refutation of the eternity argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the general refutation that shows the argument would wrongly make all daily temporal events eternal, and the distinction between ordered and transient causes.

Core Idea
If the argument were sound, even today's temporal events would be eternal—which is absurd.

After presenting the argument, the author now refutes it. The general refutation points out that the same logic would apply to any daily event, making it eternal, which is impossible. The author then clarifies that the prohibition of infinite regress applies only to ordered principles, not to transient instruments. A potential objection about the physical world and abstract entities is also addressed.

Guided Path
1
Read the general refutation passage
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. Notice that the refutation begins with 'As for in general' (أما إجمالاً).
The refutation is divided into general and detailed; this is the first part.
2
Understand the application to daily events
The argument is turned against itself: if it were valid, then daily temporal events (like today's events) would also be eternal because the same reasoning applies.
3
Distinguish types of infinite regress
The author distinguishes between infinite regress in ordered principles (mabadi' mutarattiba) and transient instruments (mu'addat mutasarrima). Only the former is impossible.
This is a key nuance in the refutation.
4
Consider the objection and response
An objection is raised that the discussion concerns the physical world, and the response allows for the world being temporal with a succession of events from an abstract entity.
The author refers to prior discussion for full treatment.

Detailed Refutation: The Role of Divine Will
Presents the detailed refutation emphasizing divine will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the detailed refutation that identifies divine will as a missing condition, allowing the world's existence to be delayed despite all other conditions being present.

Core Idea
Divine will is a condition that can choose any time, so the world's existence can be delayed even if all other conditions are met.

The detailed refutation focuses on a crucial oversight in the original argument: it omitted divine will from the list of necessary conditions. Divine will (al-irada) has the power to specify and give preponderance without needing an external determinant. Therefore, even if all other conditions were present in pre-eternity, the world need not exist then because Allah's will could choose to create it later. The objection that this would imply an effect lagging behind its cause is rejected for causes involving will and choice.

Guided Path
1
Read the detailed refutation passage
Read the Arabic and translation, focusing on the part starting 'As for in detail'.
This is the second part of the refutation.
2
Identify the missing condition
The refutation states that divine will is among the indispensable conditions. Without it, the world's non-existence in pre-eternity is possible.
Will is characterized by giving preponderance at will.
3
Understand the role of will in causation
Divine will can specify the timing of the world's existence without needing an external preponderant. This breaks the necessity of immediate existence.
4
Examine the objection and response
The objection that effect cannot lag behind cause is rejected for causes that include will and choice.
This is a key point in the refutation.

Therefore, the effect's lagging behind the cause is permissible when the cause includes will and choice (ikhtiyar), contradicting the claim of impossibility.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the existence of a separate intellect through the motion of the celestial spheres. The author presents the third argument (قال الثالث). Thus the beloved must be an existent with infinite perfections, but not the Necessary because then motions would not differ. Therefore it is an effect, proving multiple intellects.

The Detailed Proof: Seeking a Likeness
Detailed expansion of the proof for intellect from celestial motion.

Core Idea

In this expanded version of the argument, the author walks through every logical possibility for what the sphere desires. He eliminates sensible goals, then intelligible goals of essence or attributes, leaving only the pursuit of likeness. But that likeness cannot be static; it must be renewed indefinitely, proving that the beloved is a created intellect with infinite perfections enabling such successive mirroring.

Since the beloved is not the Necessary (else motions uniform), it must be a created intellect, proving multiple intellects.

He said (the third): Indeed, the perpetuity of the motions of the spheres through will cannot be except for the attainment of a perpetual likeness, indivisible, of an intellect perfect in act, infinite in perfection; otherwise cessation or pursuit of the impossible would follow. And it is not the Necessary, otherwise the motions would not differ, so the intellect is determined. And it was refuted by denying most premises. That is, the third aspect of argument for the existence of the intellect: it has been established that the motions of the spheres are voluntary, so the desired object is either sensible or intelligible. The first is false. For the pursuit of the sensible is either for attraction or repulsion; attraction of the agreeable is appetite, and repulsion of the disagreeable is anger, and both are impossible for the sphere because it is simple, uniform in states, not changing from a disagreeable state to an agreeable one. So the second is determined, namely that the desired object is intelligible, and that desired object is beloved, because the perpetuity of motion is only due to excessive pursuit necessitated by excessive love, which is passionate love. So the lover is the seeker. Either he wants to attain its essence, or its attributes, or a likeness of one of them; otherwise there would be no connection with the beloved. The first two are false. For the essence or attribute, either it is attained at all, then cessation of motion would follow because pursuit of what has already been attained is impossible, and that is impossible because it is the cause of the existence of time; or it is not attained at all, then despair of obtaining something of this nature would be necessary, and cessation would follow, or perpetual pursuit of the impossible. Moreover, attainment of the attribute is impossible because it cannot be removed from its locus. So the third is determined, namely that the pursuit is for the attainment of a likeness of the beloved. And it cannot be a stable likeness, otherwise cessation would follow, or pursuit of what has already been attained. Rather, it is an unstable likeness, i.e., likeness after likeness, such that one likeness passes away and another occurs, and that must be preserved by succession of individuals, without end, otherwise cessation would follow. So it is established that the desired object is the acquisition of infinite likenesses that occur gradually in infinite times, lest cessation of motion follow. So the beloved is an existent qualified with infinite perfections. By its motion, the sphere extracts the possible positions from potentiality to actuality, and with each position it attains a likeness of the beloved who is already in actuality from all aspects. It continues, one position passing away and another occurring; similarly, one likeness passes away and another occurs. Each of them is preserved by the succession of individuals. And the sphere accepts from it (the beloved) the reception through those likenesses. And it is not possible that this is the Necessary, otherwise the motions would not differ. So it is determined that it is an effect, and thereby the multiplicity of intellects is established.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the existence of a separate intellect through the motion of the celestial spheres. The author presents the third argument (قال الثالث). He states that perpetual voluntary motion of the spheres must aim at attaining a perpetual, indivisible likeness of an intellect that is perfect in act and infinite in perfection. If not, either motion would stop or the impossible would be sought. This intellect cannot be the Necessary (God) because then motions would not differ. The argument is summarized as: the motions are voluntary, so the object desired is either sensible or intelligible. Sensible is impossible because the sphere is simple and uniform, not subject to appetite or anger. So the object is intelligible, and is a beloved. The lover (sphere) either seeks its essence, attributes, or a likeness. Seeking essence or attributes leads to absurdities: if attained, motion stops; if not attained, despair or perpetual pursuit of impossible follows. So it seeks a likeness, which must be unstable and successive, producing an infinite series of likenesses over infinite time. Thus the beloved must be an existent with infinite perfections, but not the Necessary because then motions would not differ. Therefore it is an effect, proving multiple intellects.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The beloved of the celestial spheres is God Himself.
Correction: The author explicitly argues that the beloved cannot be the Necessary because if it were, all motions would be identical, but they differ. Hence the beloved is a created intellect.
