Defining Body with Orthogonal Dimensions
Definition of body with orthogonal condition and refutation of surface inclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the kalam definition of body (jism) as requiring three dimensions intersecting at right angles, and why this excludes surface (sath) from being considered a substance.

Core Idea
Body is defined by three mutually perpendicular dimensions.

The passage establishes a precise definition of what constitutes a body (jism) in Islamic theological physics. It begins with the basic criterion—that a body must allow the supposition of three dimensions—and then refines it with the condition that these dimensions intersect at right angles. The author also addresses a potential confusion with the concept of surface (sath), clarifying why the definition does not accidentally include it.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the literal translation. Notice the author's step-by-step geometric explanation.
The passage uses geometric examples to clarify the concept.
2
Understand the core definition
Identify the two parts: the basic definition (that in which three dimensions can be supposed) and the added condition (orthogonal intersection).
3
Follow the geometric explanation
Trace the description of perpendicular lines and right angles. See how the third dimension is uniquely determined when intersecting the first two orthogonally.
This is a key conceptual step.
4
Grasp the refutation of surface inclusion
Understand why the definition might seem to include surface (based on atomist theory) and how the condition of orthogonal dimensions excludes it.
The author references al-Mawaqif for this point.
5
Distinguish mathematical body from natural body
Note the final clarification: the mathematical body (jism ta'limi) is not refuted; only the natural body is considered, which results from accumulation of surfaces.

That in which three dimensions can be supposed, and some added the condition of intersection at right angles. Its meaning is that when a line stands on another, if it is perpendicular to it, i.e., not inclined to either side, then the two resulting angles are equal and called right angles; but if it is inclined, inevitably one of the two angles is smaller and called acute, and the other greater and called obtuse. So if we suppose in a body a dimension arbitrarily, then another intersecting it in any direction we wish such that four right angles result, then a third intersecting both such that from it with respect to each of the first two four right angles result—this third is determined, multiplicity cannot be conceived in it. This is the meaning of intersection of dimensions at right angles. This condition is to realize that what is considered in the body is acceptance of dimensions in this manner, although it may be capable of many dimensions not in this manner. What is mentioned in al-Mawaqif that the substance (jawhar) capable of dimensions is only so, and that which accepts dimensions not in this manner is only the surface (sath), should be an indication of the correctness of intersection at right angles, not of intersection itself, and to repel the illusion of one who imagines that the definition of substance capable of dimensions includes the surface based on its composition from indivisible atoms (jawahir farda). This was the intent of whoever said it is a warning against the surface, i.e., on the supposition of its being a substance. The mathematical body (jism ta'limi) is not refuted because they do not imagine it, rather they make the result of the accumulation of surfaces the natural body (jism tabi'i) only.

Commentary
This passage defines the physical body (jism) in the context of kalam cosmology. The author first presents the core definition: a body is that in which three dimensions can be supposed. Some scholars add the condition that these dimensions intersect at right angles (zawaya qa'ima). The author explains this geometrically: if a line stands on another, if it is perpendicular, the two angles are equal and called right; if inclined, one is acute, the other obtuse. For a body, one supposes a dimension arbitrarily, then a second intersecting it such that four right angles are formed, then a third intersecting both such that with respect to each of the first two dimensions four right angles are formed. This third dimension is uniquely determined. This condition ensures that the body is considered to accept dimensions in this orthogonal manner, even though it may accept many dimensions otherwise. The author then references al-Mawaqif, which states that the substance (jawhar) capable of dimensions is only like this, and that which accepts dimensions not orthogonally is the surface (sath). The author clarifies that this reference is about the correctness of orthogonal intersection, not about intersection in general, and serves to repel the misconception that the definition of a substance capable of dimensions could include surface, based on the theory that surface is composed of indivisible atoms (jawahir farda). This was the intent of those who said the condition is a warning (ihtiraz) against including surface, on the supposition that surface is also a substance. The mathematical body (jism ta'limi) is not refuted by this definition because it is not imagined as a substance; rather, the accumulation of surfaces yields only the natural body (jism tabi'i).

Why Possibility Suffices Over Supposition in Defining Body
The author argues that 'possibility' replaces 'supposition' in the definition of body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the definition of body uses 'possibility' (imkān) rather than 'supposition' (farḍ) and what 'general possibility' means in this context.

Core Idea
Possibility of dimensions, not their actuality, defines a body.

In defining a body, scholars introduced the term 'supposition' to avoid limiting the definition to actual dimensions. But as this passage explains, even supposition is not strictly necessary — mere possibility suffices. Let's explore the reasoning behind this refinement.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem with actual dimensions
The author explains why bodiliness cannot depend on actual dimensions — they change while the body remains. Notice the examples: a melting candle, rarefaction and condensation.
2
See why supposition was initially used
Supposition helped conceive of dimensions even when not actual. But the author argues that even the act of supposing is unnecessary.
3
Grasp why possibility suffices
Mere possibility (imkān) of dimensions is enough for the definition, as immaterial entities cannot even be supposed to have dimensions.
4
Identify the scope of 'general possibility'
The passage concludes that 'general possibility' includes actual, changeable, and potential dimensions. Understand each category.

And it may be said that the meaning of the warning against the surface is that the 'capable of dimensions' should not remain including it, so that it becomes a special characteristic of the body, suitable for being a differentia, because it becomes more hidden than the substance absolutely, not in one respect. This is only complete if with this condition it does not remain including the mathematical body. Supposition (farḍ) was considered only because the bodiliness of the body is not due to its actual dimensions, because they may change while bodiliness remains, as in a candle, and may increase or decrease by rarefaction and condensation, and because the body may be conceptually separate from surface and line, as in the conception of an infinite body, and even from line in existence, as in a solid sphere and cylinder. Commentary
This passage continues the discussion of the definition of body, focusing on why the term 'supposition' (farḍ) is used and why it can be replaced by 'possibility' (imkān). The author begins by considering a potential explanation for the warning (ihtiraz) against surface: that it ensures the phrase 'capable of dimensions' does not include the surface, making it a true differentia of body. However, this only works if the condition also excludes the mathematical body (jism ta'limi). The author then explains why 'supposition' was introduced: bodiliness is not due to actual dimensions, because dimensions can change (as in a candle melting) while the body remains the same, and can increase or decrease by rarefaction and condensation. Moreover, a body can be conceptually separate from surface and line (e.g., an infinite body) and even exist without a line (e.g., a solid sphere or cylinder). Therefore, the mention of 'possibility' suffices because the act of supposition itself is not necessary; mere possibility is enough. For immaterial entities (mujarradāt), supposing dimensions is impossible. Thus, mentioning possibility or capacity (qabilīya) alone is adequate. Finally, the author clarifies that the intended possibility is 'general possibility' (imkān 'āmm), which includes bodies with actual dimensions (like celestial spheres), those with changeable dimensions (elemental bodies), and those with dimensions only in potentiality (like a solid sphere).

First Interpretation: Dimensions as Quantity
First interpretation: dimensions as quantity of mathematical body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first interpretation that dimensions are the extensive quantity of the mathematical body, which is an accident distinct from the natural body and unchanging.

Core Idea
Dimensions are the pervasive quantity of the mathematical body, not the natural substance.

This lesson introduces the first of three scholarly interpretations of 'dimensions' (ab'ād). Here, dimensions are identified with the quantitative extension of the mathematical body, an accident that pervades the natural substance. The passage uses the example of a square body to illustrate how the mathematical body with its three dimensions is an accident, not composed of actual lines, and remains unchanging.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the passage once to get the overall meaning. Note the key terms: mathematical body, natural body, accident.
Pay attention to the distinction made between the mathematical body and corporeal form.
2
Identify the core claim
The passage states that dimensions are the extensions (imtidadāt) that constitute the reality of the mathematical body. Identify that the mathematical body is a quantity (kammiya) subsisting in the natural body.
3
Understand the example
The example of the square body shows that between its six surfaces there is a substance (the natural body) and an accident (the mathematical body). The three dimensions are parts of this accident, not lines.
Lines would be actual, but here the dimensions are not actual lines.
4
Note the contrast with corporeal form
The passage explicitly contrasts this extension with the corporeal form (ṣūra jismiyya), which is actual in every body and subject to change, whereas this mathematical accident is unchanging.
This distinction is key to avoid confusion.

I mean the quantity (kammiya) subsisting in the body, pervading it, bounded by surfaces, such that between the six surfaces of a square body there is a substance (jawhar) which is the natural body (jism tabi'ī) and an accident ('arad) pervading it which is the mathematical body having three dimensions which are its parts, not in the sense of lines, for if they were actually in it they would be in every body actually (and this is not the extension which is the corporeal form (ṣūra jismiyya) occurring in every body actually) such that no change or alteration affects it at all. Commentary
This passage presents three interpretations of what the 'dimensions' (ab'ād) in the definition of body might refer to, based on different scholarly views. First interpretation: dimensions are the extensive quantities (kammiya) of the mathematical body (jism ta'limī) that pervade the natural body and are bounded by surfaces. For example, in a square body, the natural body is the substance (jawhar) and the mathematical body is a pervasive accident ('arad) with three dimensions as its parts, not to be confused with lines (if lines were actual in it, they would be actual in every body). This mathematical accident does not change. Second interpretation: dimensions are the lines that exist only potentially in a stationary sphere but actually in a moving sphere like the celestial sphere (e.g., the axis is an actual line). Third interpretation: dimensions are the surfaces and lines that are boundaries; these are denied for an infinite body. The author notes that it is clear that these interpretations do not refer to dimensions that intersect at right angles; otherwise the intended lines would be the imagined intersecting lines (length, breadth, depth), which are not actual in either the natural or mathematical body. The passage ends by clarifying that separation (infiṣāl) is also potential, not opposed to connection, so the body is not disconnected from actual connection.

Second Interpretation: Dimensions as Lines
Second interpretation: dimensions as lines (potential vs actual).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second interpretation that dimensions are lines, which exist potentially in stationary bodies and actually in moving bodies like celestial spheres.

Core Idea
Dimensions as lines: potential in rest, actual in motion.

This lesson covers the second scholarly interpretation of dimensions (ab'ād) as lines. The passage contrasts a stationary sphere, where these lines exist only in mere potentiality, with a moving celestial sphere, where the axis is considered an actual line. This introduces the important philosophical distinction between potential and actual existence.

Read the short passage

The passage is brief but packed with meaning.

Understand the contrast

3
Reflect on the example of the axis
The axis of a rotating sphere is an actual line because it is the center of rotation. This illustrates that in moving bodies, the dimension (line) becomes actual.
This ties dimensions to motion and function.

The Outermost Sphere as Determiner of Directions
Sanusi argues that the outermost sphere determines all directions.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Direction is defined by the ultimate circumference, not internal points.

The author begins by establishing that the determination of direction requires a single encompassing body. The lower direction is not defined by the earth itself but by the center of the outermost sphere. Thus, the sphere of spheres is the true determiner, not the earth or any combination.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of the astronomical model imagined by astrologers/astronomers (often referenced in kalam works for refutation). It describes how they conceived six intersecting circles on the poles of the zodiac to divide the celestial sphere into twelve equal parts (the signs of the zodiac). The passage first explains that they imagined six circles cutting the zodiac belt at equal distances, with one circle passing through the two intersection points of the celestial equator and the zodiac. They then supposed these circles cut the entire universe, dividing the greatest sphere into twelve sections called 'burj' (signs). Each sign was subdivided into 30 degrees, each degree into 60 minutes, etc. They named the equinoxes and solstices based on the sun's position. The vernal equinox is where the sun crosses to the north of the equator, the autumnal to the south; the summer solstice is the midpoint between equinoxes in the north, winter solstice in the south. They assigned the seasons to the sun's passage through specific signs: spring through Aries, Taurus, Gemini; summer through Cancer, Leo, Virgo; autumn through Libra, Scorpio, Sagittarius; winter through Capricorn, Aquarius, Pisces. The passage then elaborates on the geometry: since the zodiac belt and celestial equator intersect at two points, they imagined a circle through those intersection points and another through the four poles (the ecliptic's poles?), cutting at right angles, defining the total declination. These two circles divide the zodiac sphere into four equal quarters. On two adjacent quarters, they imagined two points, then drew four circles through those points and their counterparts, resulting in six circles that cut the world into twelve sections again. They further subdivided each sign into degrees, minutes, seconds, thirds, ad infinitum. The names of the signs were taken from imagined star formations that originally aligned with those sections, but even after the stars shifted due to the precession of the eighth sphere, they retained the names for convenience. The author (Taftazani) is likely presenting this model as a prelude to critiquing it from an Islamic theological perspective, but here he simply describes what the astronomers imagined.

The Director of Mercury and the Correction of al-Mawaqif
Enumeration of motions against the order and correction of a mistake in al-Mawaqif.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the concept of simple motions against the order and the specific parameters of Mercury's director motion, as well as the correction to a mistake in al-Mawaqif.

Core Idea
Mercury's director motion exemplifies motion against the order, and even scholars can err in astronomical details.

Al-Taftazani begins by enumerating the simple motions of the partial spheres that go against the order of signs. Among these, the motion of Mercury's director is highlighted with precise angular measurements. In this context, he corrects a significant mistake made by the author of al-Mawaqif, offering a valuable lesson in careful astronomical observation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation to grasp the technical terms and the flow of the enumeration.
Pay attention to the values given for each motion.
2
Identify Mercury's director motion
Note the parameters: 59 minutes, 8 seconds, 20 thirds per day, and that it moves against the order on a plane distinct from the equator and ecliptic.
3
Understand the correction
Compare al-Taftazani's statement with the error in al-Mawaqif: the apogee and epicycle center are in conjunction, not opposition; the director moves the apogee, not the epicycle center; Mercury's distance is greater in Aries than in Libra.
4
Reflect on the broader enumeration
Notice that other motions (representatives, eccentrics, epicycles) are listed with their values, all against the order or with the order as specified.
This shows the complexity of celestial motions.

Commentary
This passage enumerates the simple motions of the partial spheres (الحركات البسيطة للأفلاك الجزئية) that are against the order of signs (خلاف التوالي). It begins with the daily motion of Mercury's director (مدير عطارد), specifying its parameters: fifty-nine minutes, eight seconds, twenty thirds per day, on a plane different from the equator and ecliptic. Then it gives values for the representative (ممثل) and inclined (مائل) motions for various planets, noting that the representatives of planets other than the moon move with the order according to the eighth sphere. For the eccentrics (خارج المركز), it lists values for the sun, Saturn, Mars, Venus, Mercury, and the moon. It then discusses the epicycles (تداوير) of the superior planets and the moon, explaining that the upper half moves with the order and the lower half against the order. A key point is the correction of a mistake in al-Mawaqif: the author (al-Iji) wrongly placed the center of the epicycle and the apogee in Libra and opposition in Aries, whereas they are in conjunction; he misnamed the director as the mover of the epicycle center instead of the mover of the apogee; and he claimed Mercury's distance from the sun is greater in Libra than in Aries, but the opposite is true. Finally, it describes the moon's representative motion (حركة ممثل القمر) which causes the nodes (الجوزهر), and the moon's inclined motion (حركة مائل القمر) which causes the apogee motion, sometimes collectively called the motion of the apogee. All these motions are against the order.

The Moon's Motions: Representative, Inclined, and Epicycles
Description of the moon's simple motions: representative and inclined.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the moon's representative motion (jawzahar) and inclined motion (apogee), and how the epicyclic motion results in opposite directions for the upper and lower halves.

Core Idea
The moon's motions are split into representative and inclined, which together produce its complex movement and impact on nodes and apogee.

Al-Taftazani now focuses on the moon's unique simple motions. The moon's representative motion, also called the motion of the jawzahar (nodes), moves daily by three minutes and twelve seconds. Its inclined motion, which drives the apogee, is much faster at eleven degrees and nine minutes per day. These motions are crucial for understanding the moon's orbit and its nodes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage about the moon's motions
Focus on the two distinct motions: representative and inclined. Note their daily rates and effects.
2
Understand the term 'jawzahar'
The representative motion causes the nodes (head and tail) to move, so it is called the motion of the jawzahar.
3
Understand the term 'apogee motion'
The inclined motion moves the eccentric and its center, and is called the motion of the apogee because it appears there.
Sometimes the sum of both motions is called the motion of the apogee.
4
Relate to the epicycle discussion
Recall from lesson 1 that the moon's epicycle has the lower half moving against the order, while the upper half moves with the order.

Understand the direction
The final sentence states that the lower half of the wandering planets' epicycles and the upper half of the moon's epicycle move opposite to the order of the signs (i.e., retrograde direction).
This is a key detail for the overall motion.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the daily motions of the epicycles of the six planets (the five wandering planets plus the moon). It begins by stating that the motion of the center of the planet appears in the centers of the epicycles, and it is measured relative to the celestial equator, the ecliptic, and their poles. Then it describes two types of motion: the motion of the upper half of the epicycle for each wandering planet (relative to its deferent plane) and the motion of the lower half of the epicycle for the moon (relative to its deferent and inclined plane). The passage then specifies the daily rates: for the superior planets (Saturn, Jupiter, Mars), the epicycle's daily motion equals the excess of the sun's motion over the motion of their deferents; for Venus (37 minutes per day), for Mercury (3 degrees 6 minutes), and for the moon (13 degrees 4 minutes). Finally, it notes that the lower half of the epicycles of the wandering planets and the upper half of the moon's epicycle move opposite to the order of the zodiacal signs.
