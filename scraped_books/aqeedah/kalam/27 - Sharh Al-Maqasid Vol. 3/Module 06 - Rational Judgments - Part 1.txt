Hadd or Rasam? The Definition of Jawhar
Razi's refutation of the definition of jawhar as a hadd, and the counter-argument defending it as a rasam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the definition of jawhar as 'al-mawjud la fi mawdu' is considered a rasam rather than a hadd, and the key logical objections raised by the Imam.

Core Idea
The definition of substance fails as a true definition because its components are not real genera and differentiate.

This passage presents a crisp logical dispute about the definition of substance (jawhar). The Imam (al-Razi) argues that the famous definition cannot be a proper hadd because its supposed genus is a secondary intelligible and its differentia is not a real entity. A defense reinterprets the definition as a rasam, clarifying the roles of logical terms.

Guided Path
1
Understand the issue: hadd vs. rasam
Begin by grasping the kalam distinction between a hadd (definition proper) and a rasam (description). A hadd requires a real genus and real differentia; a rasam is merely an identifying description.
2
Examine Razi's argument against hadd
Read the Imam's three objections: (1) jawhar is not a real genus because it is a secondary intelligible and its differentia is privative, (2) if it were a genus, the differentiate would lead to regress or accident dependence, (3) qabiliyyah (receptivity) is not a real differentia as it is an intentional concept.
Each objection aims to show that the definition fails the conditions of a hadd.
3
Explore the defense and counter-arguments
The response reinterprets the definition as a rasam on one view. It argues that the attribution of genus to differentiate is accidental (not requiring a new act), and the real differentia is the receptive thing itself (qabil), not the abstract qabiliyyah. This preserves the logical structure.
4
Synthesize the lesson
Reflect on why the debate matters: it clarifies the logical tools used in kalam to define entities. Understanding this distinction helps in evaluating other kalam definitions.

He said: Their statement is uncertain whether this definition is a hadd or a rasam. And the Imam invalidated it being a hadd, because jawhar is not a jins for it, since it is interpreted as the existent not in a subject, and existence is additional, but rather it is among the secondary intelligibles, and 'not in a subject' is a privation. And because if it were a jins, then the differentiation of substances would be by fusul, which would either be substances leading to regress or accidents leading to the subsistence of substance by accident. And qabiliyyah and its like cannot be a fasl because they are among considerations that have no stability in external realities; otherwise they would inhere in a locus and regress would be necessary in things that have order and actual existence, and that is invalid by consensus. And it was answered: 'The existent not in a subject' is a rasam for jawhar according to one view, and the attribution of the jins to the fasl is accidental and does not require another act, and the fasl is not qabiliyyah, but rather al-qabil—meaning the thing whose property is acceptance—and its being in existence the very essence of jism does not harm, as in other fusul.

Commentary
This passage is an argument about whether the definition of jawhar (substance) as 'al-mawjud la fi mawdu' is a hadd (definition proper) or a rasam (description). The author states that the scholars are uncertain. The Imam (likely Fakhr al-Din al-Razi) argues it cannot be a hadd because jawhar is not a true genus: it is defined as 'existent not in a subject', but existence is a secondary intelligible, not a real genus, and 'not in a subject' is a privation. Also, if it were a genus, the differentiae would have to be either substances (causing regress) or accidents (making substance dependent on accident). Also, qabiliyyah (receptivity) cannot be a differentia because it is an intentional concept with no external reality. The response defends the definition as a rasam, not a hadd, and argues that jawhar indeed serves as a genus in the logical sense, and the real differentia is not qabiliyyah but the receptive thing itself, which is the essence of body.

Why Jawhar Cannot Be a Genus for Body
Presents arguments against jawhar as a genus for body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why jawhar (substance) cannot be a genus (jins) for body according to the Imam's refutation, involving arguments about secondary intelligibles, privation, and regress.

Core Idea
Jawhar is a secondary intelligible, not a real genus; 'not in a subject' is a privation, not an essential property.

This lesson examines the first part of al-Razi's refutation of the definition of body as 'jawhar qabil li al-ab'ad'. The Imam argues that jawhar fails as a genus for body due to its abstract nature and the logical problems that arise if it were a real genus. We will explore the specific arguments, including the issue of secondary intelligibles and the regress of differentiae.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Pay attention to the logical flow of the Imam's arguments.
The passage begins with Ibn Sina's uncertainty, then presents the Imam's rejection.
2
Identify the two main arguments
The first argument claims that jawhar is a secondary intelligible with no external reality, so it cannot be a real genus. The second argument shows that if jawhar were a genus, its differentiae would lead to regress or accidents.
3
Understand the secondary intelligibles argument
Jawhar is defined as 'existent not in a subject'. Existence is additional to quiddity, and 'not in a subject' is a privation. Privation cannot be essential, and secondary intelligibles are mental constructs.
The passage distinguishes logical universals from natural ones like 'body' or 'animal'.
4
Analyze the regress argument
If jawhar were a genus, its species differ by differentiae. Those must be either substances (leading to regress) or accidents (making the substance depend on accidents, which is absurd).
5
Reflect on the conclusion
The arguments show that jawhar cannot serve as a genus in a real definition (hadd) of body. The definition fails as a proper hadd.

It was transmitted from Ibn Sina that he was uncertain whether this is a hadd or a rasam. And the Imam invalidated it being a hadd, because jawhar is not suitable as a jins for jism, nor is qabiliyyat al-ab'ad suitable as a fasl. As for the first, there are several arguments: among them that jawhar is interpreted as the existent not in a subject. Existence is additional to quiddity and is not essential to it, but rather it is among the fixed intelligibles that have no realization except in the mind, so it cannot be a part of a real quiddity. And not needing a subject is a privation that cannot be an essential attribute of the existent. It may not be said that all genera, but all universals, are from secondary intelligibles, for we say: logical ones are from them, not natural ones like jism and animal and the like. Another argument: if it were a jins for substances, then their differentiation would necessarily be by fusul, as is the case for species under a jins. Those fusul would either be substances – then the discussion transfers to that by which they are differentiated and regress follows – or accidents, leading to the subsistence of jawhar by accident, which is invalid because it necessitates jawhar's need for a subject. Also, it necessitates the accident being predicated of jawhar and of itself in existence, as is the case for the fasl with the species. As for the second: because the meaning of qabiliyyah, possibility of supposition, validity, and similar expressions are things that have no realization in the external world. And the inherence in a locus that is receptive necessarily makes it among accidental meanings, not substantial. So we transfer the discussion to that qabiliyyah, and regress follows in existing ordered things – necessarily, because the realization of each qabiliyyah depends on another qabiliyyah.

Two main points: (1) jawhar cannot be a genus because it is a secondary intelligible and 'not in a subject' is privative; also if it were a genus, differentiae would either be substances (regress) or accidents (dependence on accident). (2) qabiliyyat al-ab'ad cannot be a differentia because it has no external reality and would cause regress of dependencies. The passage follows a logical structure of objections and proofs, concluding that both premises fail.

Why Qabiliyyah Cannot Be a Differentia for Body
Presents arguments against qabiliyyah as a differentia for body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why qabiliyyat al-ab'ad (receptivity of dimensions) cannot be a differentia (fasl) for body, due to its lack of external existence and the resulting regress of dependencies.

Core Idea
Qabiliyyah is a relational concept with no external reality, so it cannot be a real differentia.

This lesson presents the second part of al-Razi's refutation, targeting the notion of qabiliyyah (receptivity) as a differentia for body. The Imam argues that such capacities are mental constructs with no extra-mental existence, and that trying to make them the basis of a real distinction leads to an infinite regress.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice that this argument focuses on the nature of qabiliyyah as a mental concept.
The passage begins with 'As for the second' connecting to the previous lesson.
2
Understand the nature of qabiliyyah
Qabiliyyath, possibility of supposition, etc. are expressions that have no external reality. They are mental constructs.
3
Identify the regress argument
If qabiliyyah were a real differentia, it would need a receptive locus. That locus would be a subject, making it accidental. Then that qabiliyyah itself would need another qabiliyyah, leading to infinite regress.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Thus, qabiliyyah cannot be a fasl in a hadd. The definition fails completely.

Second Proof: Inequality of Magnitudes Requires Indivisible Parts
Second argument: without indivisible parts, a mountain and a mustard seed would be equal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second proof for the existence of indivisible atoms, based on the necessity of inequality of magnitudes, and how the objection regarding infinite parts is answered.

Core Idea
Inequality of magnitudes proves indivisible atoms; without them, a mountain and seed would be equal.

The second proof builds on the first by showing that without indivisible parts (al-juz' alladhi la yatajazza), all magnitudes would be equal in size—an obvious falsehood. This lesson walks through the argument, an objection, and the rebuttal, culminating in an absurd consequence that solidifies the necessity of atoms.

The absurd consequence (equality) is used to prove the necessity of indivisible parts.

Understand the objection and answer
The objection claims equality of infinite parts does not imply equality of magnitude. The answer counters that magnitude depends on the number of parts.
The text says 'differences in magnitudes are only due to differences in parts'.
4
Examine the refined argument
The author refines the argument without assuming actual infinite parts: even potential infinite divisibility leads to absurdity (mustard seed sheets covering the sky).
The answer clarifies that infinite divisibility does not mean actual infinite parts can be separated.

Proof that Motion and Distance Consist of Indivisible Parts
Al-Taftazani proves motion and distance are atomic via the present moment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Taftazani proves that motion and distance are composed of indivisible parts (atoms) using the concept of the present moment.

Core Idea
Motion's present moment is indivisible, and everything it touches shares that indivisibility.

Al-Taftazani begins with a simple observation: when something moves, only the present instant exists. But what is the nature of that present? He argues that it cannot be divided, because if it were, part of it would be past or future, contradicting its presence. This leads to the conclusion that motion itself is made of indivisible moments, and by correspondence, so is distance.

Commentary
This passage is a proof that motion, distance, and time are composed of indivisible parts (ajza' la tatajaza'), a foundational doctrine of kalam atomism. The author begins by observing that motion is intuitively known to occur in a distance, and we are certain that the past part of motion no longer exists, the future does not yet exist, and only the present (al-hadir) actually exists. The present moment of motion must be indivisible; if it were divisible, part of it would be before or after the rest, contradicting its being fully present. This would also require the present to be stationary and composite, which is absurd. Since motion is superimposed on distance (each part of motion corresponds to a part of distance), the distance must also be composed of indivisible parts. The author then provides an alternative formulation: each present part of motion is followed by another indivisible present part, so motion is a series of such parts; hence it is composed of indivisible atoms. The same reasoning applies to distance due to its correspondence with motion. The argument is strengthened by an appeal to time, wherein instability is even more evident. Since time is a continuous measure of motion, and motion is composed of indivisible parts, time must also be composed of indivisible instants (anawat). The author contrasts this with Aristotle's view that time is a continuous quantity and rejects it on the basis that the existence of indivisible atoms is necessary. The passage concludes that time corresponds to motion, which corresponds to distance, and all three are composed of indivisible parts.

Commentary
This passage is a proof that motion, distance, and time are composed of indivisible parts (ajza' la tatajaza'), a foundational doctrine of kalam atomism. The author begins by observing that motion is intuitively known to occur in a distance, and we are certain that the past part of motion no longer exists, the future does not yet exist, and only the present (al-hadir) actually exists. The present moment of motion must be indivisible; if it were divisible, part of it would be before or after the rest, contradicting its being fully present. This would also require the present to be stationary and composite, which is absurd. Since motion is superimposed on distance (each part of motion corresponds to a part of distance), the distance must also be composed of indivisible parts. The author then provides an alternative formulation: each present part of motion is followed by another indivisible present part, so motion is a series of such parts; hence it is composed of indivisible atoms. The same reasoning applies to distance due to its correspondence with motion. The argument is strengthened by an appeal to time, wherein instability is even more evident. Since time is a continuous measure of motion, and motion is composed of indivisible parts, time must also be composed of indivisible instants (anawat). The author contrasts this with Aristotle's view that time is a continuous quantity and rejects it on the basis that the existence of indivisible atoms is necessary. The passage concludes that time corresponds to motion, which corresponds to distance, and all three are composed of indivisible parts.

First Proof: The Point Necessitates an Indivisible Atom
Al-Taftazani's first proof for the indivisible atom based on the point.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the existence of a geometric point leads to the conclusion of an indivisible substance, the atom.

Core Idea
A point is either a substance or an accident, but in either case, an indivisible atom must exist.

This lesson examines the first of three proofs for atomism presented by al-Taftazani. The proof begins by establishing the existence of points as real entities with position, then argues that whatever underlies a point must be indivisible. We will follow the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation once to get the flow of the proof.
Notice how the proof is structured as a disjunctive syllogism.
2
Identify the sub-arguments
The proof has three main parts: establishing the existence of the point, classifying it as substance or accident, and deriving the indivisible substrate.
Pay attention to the key terms: نقطة, جوهر, عرض.
3
Understand the logical structure
The proof uses a case analysis: if the point is a substance, it is itself indivisible; if it is an accident, it requires a substrate that must be indivisible to avoid division of the point.
This is a classic kalam argument form: enumeration of possibilities.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Consider why the conclusion is 'an indivisible substance is established' regardless of which option holds.
The proof is valid whether one accepts the category of accident or not.

Commentary
This passage presents three proofs (wujuh) for the existence of an indivisible substance (jawhar la yaqbalu al-inqisam), i.e., the atom, as held by the mutakallimun. First, it sets up the context: the philosophers (al-hukama') deny the indivisible present moment in time and consider the existent motion to be a continuum between start and end, just like bodies. The third proof involves a perpendicular line: a line standing on another line touches at an indivisible point. Moving the line across reveals that the underlying line is composed of indivisible parts, because what the indivisible point falls upon must itself be indivisible. Together, these three proofs demonstrate the necessity of indivisible atoms in geometry and physics, thereby supporting the kalam doctrine of atomism.

Second Proof: Sphere and Surface Reveal Indivisible Parts
Al-Taftazani's second proof for the indivisible atom using a sphere on a surface.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how placing a real sphere on a real surface demonstrates the existence of indivisible parts.

Core Idea
A sphere touching a surface must do so at an indivisible part, otherwise the sphere loses its spherical nature.

This lesson presents the second proof for atomism, which uses physical geometry. By considering a perfect sphere on a perfect plane, al-Taftazani argues that their contact point must be an indivisible part. Rotating the sphere then shows the entire surface is composed of such parts.

3
Identify the conclusion
The contact part must be indivisible, and this part is either a substance (atom) or an accident requiring an indivisible substrate.
The proof mirrors the first proof's disjunction.
4
Consider the rotation argument
Rotating the sphere on the surface shows that the entire surface is composed of such indivisible parts, strengthening the atomist position.
This extends the conclusion from one point to the whole surface.

الثالث : أنه يستلزم امتناع لحوق السريع بالبطيء إذ ابتداء الحركة بعده لأنه إذا قطع جزءاً فالبطيء أيضاً. قطع جزءاً إذ لا أقل منه ضرورة، ولا تخلل للسكنات بشهادة الخس والبرهان، وإنما اعتبر البطيء دون الواقف مع أنه كذلك لأنه حينئذ يكون ذكر السرعة لغواً، ويصير هذا بعينه طريق امتناع قطع المتحرك مسافة ما، ووصوله إلى غاية ما، ولا يخفى أن هذا الوجه جار فيما إذا كانت الأجزاء متناهية، وأن الوجوه الثلاثة إنما تنتهض على من يقول بلا تناهي الأجزاء في كل امتداد يفرض في الجسم ، وفيما بين كل طرفين من أطرافه، وجهتين من جهاته، وأما على القول بلا تناهيها في مجموع الامتدادات وفيما بين جميع الأطراف والجهات فلا إلا بين تناهي عدد الامتدادات .
The proponents of the atom argued that the parts of the body are finite, rejecting the statement of al-Nazzam, through proofs: First: They are bounded between two boundaries, and everything that is so, whether number or magnitude, is necessarily finite. Second: That the infinity of parts entails the impossibility of the moving object reaching any limit in distance, because it is dependent on crossing its half, and half of its half, and so on ad infinitum, and that is inconceivable in a finite time. This argument is expressed as the impossibility of traversing a given distance in a finite time. It is established that the infinity of the parts of the distance entails the infinity of the parts of motion that correspond to it, and that entails the infinity of the parts of time that correspond to the motion. Third: That it entails the impossibility of the swift overtaking the slow, since [the swift] begins motion after it [the slow] because when it covers a part, the slow also covers a part, since there is no smaller part necessarily, and there is no intervening rest, attested by sense and proof. The slow is considered rather than the stationary, although the same applies, because then mentioning speed would be pointless. This becomes the very means of preventing the moving object from traversing a distance and reaching a limit. It is not hidden that this point applies when the parts are finite, and that the three arguments are effective only against one who claims the infinity of parts in every extension supposed in the body, and between every two of its extremities, and in two of its directions. Commentary
This passage presents three distinct proofs offered by the proponents of the atom (al-qāʾilūn bi al-juzʾ) to establish that the parts of a body are finite, thereby refuting the position of al-Nazzām who held that bodies are composed of infinite parts. The first proof (al-awwal) argues that the parts of a body are bounded by the two extremes of the body, and anything bounded between two boundaries—whether in number or magnitude—is necessarily finite. The second proof (al-thānī) argues that if parts are infinite, then a moving body could never reach its destination, because to traverse any distance it must first cross half of it, then half of the remaining half, ad infinitum; thus the motion could never be completed in a finite time. The proof extends to show that infinite parts of distance would require infinite parts of motion and infinite parts of time. The third proof (al-thālith) argues that if parts are infinite, a swift object could never overtake a slow one, because when the swift covers a part, the slow also covers a part (since there is no smallest part and no intervening rest). The slow is considered instead of the stationary object to avoid making speed irrelevant. The passage then clarifies that these three proofs are directed only against the claim that parts are infinite in every supposed extension and between every two extremities. If the opponent claims infinity only in the totality of extensions, then the proofs do not apply except by reductio on the finitude of the number of extensions.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the atomist doctrine (that bodies are composed of indivisible parts) with responses to objections. It begins by quoting the author's statement that the refutation targets the view that bodies consist of parts indivisible in actuality but only imaginarible. The commentator (al-Taftazani) then identifies the target as Democritus and some ancients who held that observable bodies like water are not absolutely simple but arise from minute, hard, similar simples that cannot be actually divided—only imaginarily—distinguishing this from the view of those who affirm the atom (al-juz' alladhī lā yatajazzā). The argument (taqrīr) proceeds: since these simples are admitted to be similar in nature, whatever is permissible for the aggregate is permissible for each part. Separative division (al-qisma al-infikākiyya) is not permissible for the aggregate (since the aggregate endures as one body), so it must be permissible for each part—otherwise the impossibility would apply to the aggregate as well. However, the possibility of separation by essence does not contradict its impossibility due to accidental factors like specific forms, extreme smallness, hardness, or lack of a cutting tool. This fends off the first objection by Imam al-Razi that bodily extensions are not persistent during separation and are abstract during connection, thus they are individuated and might prevent the common essence from causing division. The second objection denies the premise of equality of bodies in essence. The commentator responds that even if one denies equality, the argument still works for a single body where variation of nature is inconceivable; furthermore, the statement that division produces a duality equal in nature of each part to the whole should be understood as applying to the single body, not to composites. The passage then clarifies key terms: 'nature' (ṭabā‘i‘) is the source of the primary essential attribute (broader than nature/ṭabī‘a), and 'kinds of division' are three: 1) by tearing/cutting (leading to separation), 2) by imagination/supposition (no separation, merely conceptual), 3) by difference of two accidents (stable like color or non-stable like contact). The third was included as a distinct kind due to considering mere imagination. The passage ends by noting that when we say we are certain that the locus of whiteness differs from that of blackness, that is granted but only with respect to the accidents, not the essence of the body itself.

لكونها حادثة على ما سيأتي، ولقوله تعالى كل شيء هالك إلا وجهه وهلاك البسيط لا يتصور إلا بفنائه، ولا يخفي أن الحدوث إنما يقتضي إمكان العدم بالذات، وهو لا ينافي امتناعه بالغير وهو المتنازع . فإن استروح الى أن الحكم هو الإمكان حتى يثبت ما به الامتناع كان ذكر الحدوث مستدركا . أنا أي من أحكام الأجسام أنها باقية زمانين وأكثر بحكم الضرورة بمعنى نعلم بالضرورة أن كتبنا وثيابنا وبيوتنا ودوابنا هي بعينها التي كانت من غير تبدل في الذوات، بل إن كان ففي العوارض والهيئات، لا بمعنى أن الحس يشاهدها باقية ليرد الاعتراض بأنه يجوز أن يكون ذلك بتجدد الأمثال كما في الأعراض، وقد يفهم من البقاء الدوام وامتناع الفناء، وعليه يحمل ما قال في التجريد أن الضرورة قاضية ببقاء الأجسام وبين بأن غاية أمرها التفرق والانقسام، وهو لا يوجب الانعدام. وأنت خبير بأن دعوى الضرورة في ذلك في غاية الفساد. كيف وقد صرح بجوازه في بحث المعاد واستدل على جواز العدم تارة بالحدوث، فإن العدم السابق كالعدم اللاحق لعدم التمايز ، وقد جاز الأول فكذا الثاني، وتارة بالإمكان، فإن معناه جواز كل من الوجود والعدم نظراً إلى الذات. وأجيب بأن هذا لا ينافي الامتناع بالغير على ما هو المتنازع ، فإنه يجوز أن يكون الشيء في ذاته قابلاً للعدم السابق واللاحق جميعاً، ويمتنع أحدهما أو كلاهما لعلة، والحاصل أن الحدوث لا ينافي الأبدية كما في النفس الناطقة على رأي أرسطو، والإمكان لا ينافي الأبدية والأزلية كما في القدماء لزمانية دون الذاتية على رأي الفلاسفة ويمكن () أن يقال : إنه لما كان الحكم بجواز العدم نفي ذلك الحكم حتى يقوم دليل الامتناع . لا يقال على هذا لا حاجة إلى اعتبار الحدوث لأن الجسم ممكن جائز الوجود والعدم قديماً كان أو حديثاً لأنا نقول هو مراعاة لطرق المتكلمين حيث يقسمون الوجود إلى القديم والحادث لا الواجب والممكن وبيان لجواز حصول العدم بالفعل وذلك في الحدوث دون مجرد الإمكان.
Because they are temporally originated (ḥāditha) as will come, and from His saying the Exalted: 'Everything is perishing except His Face' (Quran 28:88), and the perishing of a simple being cannot be conceived except by its annihilation. And it is not hidden that temporal origination (ḥudūth) only implies possibility (imkān) of non-existence in essence, and it does not contradict its impossibility by another, which is the point of dispute. So if one takes comfort in the fact that the judgment is possibility until what establishes impossibility is proven, then the mention of temporal origination would be superfluous. I mean from the judgments of bodies is that they remain for two times or more by virtue of necessity, meaning that we know necessarily that our books, clothes, houses, and beasts are the very ones that were without any change in essences; rather, if there is change, it is in the accidents and states. Not in the sense that the sense perceives them as permanent, lest the objection be raised that [stated permanence] could be by renewal of similar instances as in accidents. And permanence (baqā') may be understood as perpetuity and the impossibility of annihilation (fanā'), and upon this is carried what was said in al-Tajrīd that necessity judges the permanence of bodies and it is shown that their ultimate fate is disintegration and division, which does not necessitate non-existence. And you are aware that the claim of necessity in that is utterly corrupt. How so? For it has been affirmed regarding its possibility in the discussion of the resurrection, and the possibility of non-existence has been argued sometimes by temporal origination, for prior non-existence is like posterior non-existence due to lack of differentiation, and since the first was possible, so is the second; and sometimes by possibility (imkān), for its meaning is the permissibility of both existence and non-existence with respect to the essence. And it was answered that this does not contradict impossibility by another, as is the point of dispute. For it is possible that a thing is in its essence receptive of both prior and posterior non-existence, and one or both are prevented by a cause. And the conclusion is that temporal origination does not contradict eternity (abadiyya) as in the rational soul according to Aristotle's opinion, and possibility does not contradict eternity and pre-eternity as in the temporal pre-eternals according to the philosophers' opinion, not essential pre-eternals. And it can be said: Since the judgment of the permissibility of non-existence is negated until proof of impossibility is established, it is not said that there is no need to consider temporal origination because the body is possible, permissible of existence and non-existence whether it is ancient or originated, for we say it is out of consideration for the ways of the mutakallimūn, since they divide existence into the ancient (qadīm) and the originated (ḥādith), not into the necessary (wājib) and the possible (mumkin), and it is a demonstration of the actual occurrence of non-existence, and that is in temporal origination, not merely possibility.

Commentary
This passage is a complex argument defending the possibility of annihilation (fanā') for bodies against those who claim necessity of permanence (baqā'). The author begins by referencing the Quranic verse 'Everything is perishing except His Face' and notes that simple beings can only perish by annihilation. He then clarifies a key principle: temporal origination (ḥudūth) only implies possibility (imkān) of non-existence in itself (bi al-dhāt), not impossibility of non-existence by another (bi al-ghayr). The dispute is about the latter. If one accepts that the default judgment is possibility until impossibility is proven, then mentioning temporal origination would be redundant. [The text then transitions] The author then discusses the claim that bodies are necessarily observed to remain for two times or more, but notes that this is not a rational necessity and could be due to renewal of similar instances. He further criticizes the claim of necessity. Then he presents arguments for the possibility of non-existence: one based on hadath (since prior non-existence was possible, posterior is similar) and one based on imkan (the essence allows both existence and non-existence). The response to the objection that this contradicts impossibility by another is that a thing may be receptive to non-existence in itself but prevented by an external cause. The author concludes that hadath does not contradict eternity (as in Aristotle's soul) and imkan does not contradict temporal eternity. Finally, he explains that the mutakallimūn consider hadath rather than imkan because they divide existence into qadīm and ḥādith, and they want to show actual occurrence of non-existence which is in hadath only.

Key Takeaway
Temporal origination only indicates the possibility of non-existence in itself; actual annihilation is prevented if there is an external cause necessitating persistence.
