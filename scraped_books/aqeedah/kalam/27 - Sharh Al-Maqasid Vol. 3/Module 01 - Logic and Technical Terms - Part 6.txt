Distinguishing Celestial and Animal Cognition
Answering objection on celestial cognition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the answer distinguishes the type of sensation and intellection in celestial bodies from that in animals, preserving the differentiators for animality and humanity.

Core Idea
Not all sensation and intellection are alike; celestial bodies have a different kind.

The objection claims that celestial bodies also have sensation and intellection, so these cannot differentiate animal/human souls. The commentator responds by citing Avicenna's al-Shifa to clarify the specific meanings of these terms. This lesson examines that critical distinction.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the objection carefully: the objector claims that celestial bodies also have sensation and intellection, so these cannot differentiate animal/human souls.
Notice the logic: if X and Y share an attribute, that attribute cannot distinguish them.
2
Identify the distinction in the answer
The answer, citing Avicenna's al-Shifa, specifies that sensation here means passive imprinting of forms, and intellection means potential intellect. These are not present in celestial bodies.
The key terms are infi'aal, irtisaam al-mithaal, al-'aql al-hayulani, and al-'aql bi al-malaka.
3
Reflect on the implication
Understanding this distinction preserves the role of sensation and intellection as differentiators for animal and human souls.
This is a typical kalam move: clarifying terms to resolve an objection.

For it might be said: They have mentioned that the celestial bodies have sensation, motion, and universal intellection; accordingly, these [attributes] are not suitable as differentiators for animality and humanity. We answer: It is mentioned in al-Shifa that by sensation here is meant that which is in the way of passion (infi'aal) and imprinting of the form (irtisaam al-mithaal), and by intellection [is meant] that which is the concern of the material intellect (al-'aql al-hayulani) and the intellect in habitu (al-'aql bi al-malaka); but the case of celestial bodies is not like that.

Commentary
This is an objection-and-answer passage. The objector says that philosophers have attributed sensation, motion, and universal intellection to the celestial bodies (al-samawiyyat). If so, then these attributes cannot serve as differentiators (mumayyiz) for animal and human souls, since celestial bodies share them. The answer, citing Avicenna's al-Shifa, distinguishes the meanings: by 'sensation' here is meant the kind that occurs through passion (infi'aal) and the imprinting of forms (irtisaam al-mithaal)—i.e., a passive reception. By 'intellection' is meant that which is the domain of the material intellect (al-'aql al-hayulani) and the intellect in habitu (al-'aql bi al-malaka)—i.e., an intellectual capacity that develops from potentiality to actuality. The celestial bodies do not have this kind of passive or potential cognition; their knowledge is different (e.g., intuitive or always actual). Therefore, the distinction stands.

Why Humans Have One Soul, Not Three
Explaining why philosophers reject multiple souls in one body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the philosophers reject the idea of three separate souls in a human body, and how a single rational soul encompasses the functions of vegetative and animal souls.

Core Idea
Higher souls include the powers of lower souls—no need for three souls in one body.

If every soul is defined by its effects, a human body seems to require three souls: vegetative, animal, and rational. But the philosophers clarify that each composite body has one soul that performs all the functions of its level, with higher souls incorporating the powers of lower ones. This passage explains that hierarchy and resolves the apparent implication.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice the initial statement of implication and the response that the matter is not so.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the apparent implication
The commentator explains that if each soul has specific effects and each body has a specific form, then a human would seem to have three souls. Find that argument in the text.
This is the 'mawjab' (implication) referenced.
3
Understand the resolution: hierarchy of forms
The resolution from Sharh al-Isharat describes four levels of composites: mineral, vegetative, animal, and rational. Each higher form includes the functions of the lower ones. Trace how a rational soul alone does everything.
Notice that the rational soul is 'abstract' and performs all acts.
4
Reflect on the philosophical point
Why do philosophers prefer a single soul over multiple? Consider simplicity and the unity of the human person. The passage shows that a single principle can produce diverse effects.
This is a key insight for understanding Islamic psychology.

Commentary
This passage explains and resolves an implication of the philosophers' principles. The commentator quotes the original text: if every soul is the principle of specific effects and every body has a specific substantial form, then in a human there would seem to be three souls—vegetative, animal, and rational. However, the philosophers state this is not the case; instead, the vegetative soul produces what the mineral power produces, the animal soul produces its own effects, and the human soul produces effects of all. The commentator then expands: the implication would be that in a human there is a soul for intellection, one for motion/sensation, and one for nutrition/growth. But in reality, according to Sharh al-Isharat, composite bodies are of four types: (1) those with a mineral form, which only preserves the collected opposing materials from disintegration; (2) those with a vegetative soul, which additionally gathers elements for nutrition, growth, and reproduction; (3) those with an animal soul, which adds sensation and voluntary motion; (4) those with an abstract (rational) soul, which adds reason and its concomitants. Thus, each composite has a single soul—the higher soul includes the powers of the lower ones. In a human, the rational soul itself performs all functions: it is the principle of nutrition (through its vegetative aspect), sensation (through its animal aspect), and reason. There are not three separate souls; rather, one soul at different levels of manifestation.

The Philosophers' Hierarchy of Forms
Taftazani explains the philosophers' view on the three souls and the hierarchy of forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' view on the hierarchy of forms and why they do not posit multiple souls in one body.

Core Idea
A single form can produce multiple effects, not multiple souls.

Taftazani presents the philosophers' account of how forms operate in composite bodies. While their rules might suggest multiple souls in one being, they actually argue that a single form at a higher level encompasses the functions of lower forms. This passage outlines a clear hierarchy from mineral to rational soul.

Early Opinions on the Soul and the View of Many Mutakallimun
Sanusi lists early materialistic opinions and the view of many mutakallimun on the soul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the range of historical opinions on the soul, from materialistic theories to the view that it consists of the original remaining parts of the body.

Core Idea
The soul was described as fire, air, or temperament before mutakallimun refined it.

Sanusi begins by cataloguing a series of ancient and medieval opinions on the nature of the soul. These range from fire and air to the four elements and humors, and even to the idea that each person's soul is their unique temperament. He then presents the view of many early mutakallimun that the soul is the original remaining parts (al-ajzāʾ al-aṣliyya al-bāqiya) from birth to death, interpreting earlier corporeal descriptions as referring to the sensible structure that perceives. This lesson guides you through these opinions to see the development of thought.

Guided Path
1
Identify the series of 'قيل' opinions
Read through the list of early opinions introduced by 'قيل' (it was said). Notice the progression from simple elements to more complex mixtures like humors and temperament.
These represent pre-Islamic and early philosophical views.
2
Note the indivisible part view
One of the opinions states the soul is an indivisible part (جزء لا يتجزأ) located in the heart. This is a precursor to atomistic views.
3
Understand the view of many mutakallimun
The passage says 'كثير من المتكلمين' hold that the soul is the original remaining parts that persist throughout life. This is a corporeal but enduring substance.
They interpreted the earlier 'sensible structure' as that which perceives, not the physical body itself.
4
Reflect on the doctrinal significance
Ask yourself how this view preserves the soul's identity across time while still being a body. This prepares for the more refined majority view in the next lesson.

Commentary
This passage catalogues various historical opinions on the nature of the soul. It begins with ancient materialistic views: fire, air, water, the four elements, love and dominance (desire and anger), the four humors, temperament, and an indivisible part in the heart. Then it presents the view of many mutakallimun that the soul is the original remaining parts (al-ajzāʾ al-aṣliyya al-bāqiya) from birth to death, interpreting the earlier phrase 'sensible structure' as that which perceives. The majority of mutakallimun, however, hold a more refined view: the soul is a body different in quiddity from the physical body, luminous, elevated, light, living by itself, penetrating the limbs like rosewater in a rose or fire in coal, unchanging, and its departure is death. A secondary view among some is that it consists of subtle bodies formed in the heart or brain and flowing through arteries or nerves. Finally, the author states the preferred view of the investigators (muḥaqqiqūn) among philosophers and Muslims: the soul is an immaterial substance (jawhar mujarrad) that manages and disposes the body, with its primary attachment being a cardiac spirit formed in the left ventricle from the vapor of nourishment, which pours forth power throughout the body.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof for the immateriality of the soul based on its capacity to conceive certain types of concepts, and then addresses objections. The author first argues that the soul must be immaterial because it conceives immaterial substances, universals, indivisible concepts, and incompatibles—all of which cannot inhere in matter. He then summarizes this into four forms: the soul conceives immaterial things, universals, simples (indivisibles), and incompatibles. He then presents an opponent's objection: that all this rests on premises the opponent does not accept. The author lists seven specific premises that are contested and implicitly answers them by questioning why the opponent rejects them. The structure is a concise dialectical exchange: the author gives his proof, the opponent challenges the premises, and the author counters by questioning the opponent's rejection. The commentary follows the author's order: first the original argument, then the fourfold summary, then the answer refuting each contested premise.

Refuting Objections to the Soul's Immateriality
Sanusi presents and refutes seven objections to the proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key objections raised against the immateriality proofs and how Sanusi responds by questioning the opponent's rejection of the premises.

Core Idea
The opponent's objections are based on arbitrary denials of obvious premises.

Having presented four proofs, Sanusi now anticipates the opponent's challenges. The opponent argues that each proof relies on unproven assumptions. Sanusi's response is not to prove the premises directly but to turn the tables: he questions why the opponent would deny these seemingly intuitive principles. This lesson explores the dialectical exchange.

Proof That the Soul Is Not a Pervading Substance
Al-Taftazani refutes the claim that the rational soul is a substance pervading the body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the rational soul cannot be a substance pervading the body, based on a reductio argument involving the impossibility of constant intellection.

Core Idea
The soul's intermittent self-awareness refutes its supposed material pervasion of the body.

This lesson dives into a powerful proof that the rational soul is not a material substance spread throughout the body. The argument uses a reductio ad absurdum: if the soul pervaded the body, it would always be aware of it, yet we know we are not always conscious of our bodies. The reasoning explores two possible explanations for intellection and shows both lead to contradictions. By the end, you'll see why the soul must be immaterial.

Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage silently, then the English translation. Notice the logical flow of the proof.
2
Identify the basic argument structure
The argument is a conditional with a false consequent. Find the initial conditional and the evidence that rejects its result.
The consequent is 'perpetual intellection or none at all'.
3
Follow the two sub-branches
The author considers two ways intellection could relate to bodily presence: either mere presence suffices, or an image is needed. Trace each branch to its contradiction.
4
Reflect on the underlying principle
The argument relies on the idea that perception requires a mental image (form) of the perceived. Notice how this principle is used to reject the material soul theory.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the view that the rational soul is a substance pervading the body (or an accident in it). The author presents a proof by contradiction. He argues: If the soul were a substance pervading the body, then its intellection of the body would be either constant (always perceiving the body) or never at all. But we observe by experience that we sometimes perceive our body and sometimes are heedless of it. Therefore the consequent is false. The connection is analyzed: either the body's own presence before the intellective faculty is sufficient for intellection, or it requires an image. If mere presence sufficed, intellection would be perpetual (since the body is always present to the soul that pervades it), just as the soul always knows itself and its own attributes. But if an image is needed, then the soul would acquire the image at the moment of intellection, implying two images of the same body in the same matter: the body's own continuous form and the acquired image. This is impossible because two distinct images cannot coexist in one matter without distinction of matter. The argument rests on the principle that perception requires the presence of an image (form) of the perceived, not merely a relation. Additionally, the two images are not similar: the acquired image is in the soul, the original is in the body. Even if considered similar, their conjunction would require removal of distinction, but here distinction remains because they inhere in different substrates. Thus the initial assumption—that the soul is a substance pervading the body—is false.

The Argument from Celestial Motion for Immaterial Souls and Corporeal Powers
Al-Taftazani argues from motion to celestial souls.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the kalam argument that celestial motion must be voluntary, implying the existence of both immaterial souls for intellection and corporeal powers for imagination in the celestial spheres.

Core Idea
Celestial motion cannot be natural or forced—it must be voluntary, requiring both intellect and imagination.

This passage from al-Taftazani presents a philosophical proof for the existence of celestial souls. The argument proceeds by analyzing the nature of circular motion: ruling out natural and forced motion leaves only voluntary motion, which demands both an immaterial intellect to grasp universals and a corporeal power to imagine particulars. However, the author also notes that many premises are open to objection, reflecting the dialectical nature of kalam.

Guided Path
1
Understand the background
Celestial spheres move in circular, perpetual motion. This motion is observed and needs explanation.
The argument assumes that motion requires a principle of motion.
2
Eliminate natural motion
Natural motion is from a disagreeing state to an agreeing state. In circular motion, each point is both an endpoint and a starting point, so it would have to be both desired and fled—a contradiction.
Thus celestial motion cannot be natural.
3
Eliminate forced motion
Forced motion is against nature and ceases when the coercing force is removed. Celestial motion is perpetual and uniform, so it cannot be forced.
Also, a forced motion would not be perpetual.
4
Conclude voluntary motion
The only remaining category is voluntary motion, which requires will. Will in turn requires intellect (to grasp universals) and imagination (to grasp particulars). Therefore, celestial spheres must have immaterial souls (for intellect) and corporeal powers (for imagination).
The author notes that many premises are open to denial, indicating a dialectical stance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two main opinions on the nature of souls—whether they are similar in essence or different—and how different philosophical schools argue for each position.

Core Idea
Souls may share a definition but differ in their specific realities.

This passage from Sharh al-Maqasid introduces a foundational debate in Islamic theology and philosophy: are all souls essentially the same, or are they fundamentally different? The author outlines two broad opinions, both of which he considers weak, before delving into the specific views of philosophers who considered souls as bodies or as incorporeal substances. This discussion is crucial for understanding later arguments about the afterlife, human distinction, and the soul-body relation.

The passage ends with the beginning of a refutation of the opponent's proofs (أحج الخصم بوجوه), which is cut off. The author's argumentative order is systematic: first the claim, then anticipated objections and answers, then independent proofs.

The Two Intellects: Theoretical and Practical
Al-Taftazani defines the two intellects based on the soul's power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between the theoretical and practical intellect according to al-Taftazani.

Core Idea
The soul's power has two aspects: being affected for knowledge and affecting the body for action.

Al-Taftazani explains that the soul possesses a power that can be viewed in two ways: as receptive to higher principles for theoretical completion, or as active upon the body for practical perfection. This lesson defines these two fundamental faculties.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage carefully, noting the contrast between the two definitions.
The term 'power' (quwwa) is used for both aspects.
2
Identify the two intellects
Find the phrases that define the theoretical intellect and the practical intellect.
3
Understand the relationship
Notice that the practical intellect is also a perfection of the soul, using the body as an instrument.

The fifth research: The power of the soul with respect to its being affected by the principle for completion as theoretical intellect, and with respect to its effect upon the body for perfecting as practical intellect. As for the theoretical, its ranks are four: It is called: either a weak preparedness which is the pure receptivity of the soul for intelligibles, and it is called hyulani intellect; or intermediate, which is the preparedness for theoretical matters by obtaining necessary truths, and it is called intellect by disposition; or strong, which is the ability to recall theoretical matters without acquisition because they are acquired and stored, and it is called intellect in act; or perfection for it in that, which is the presence of theoretical matters before it as witnessed, and it is called acquired intellect. It has already preceded that the term 'power', just as it is applied to the principle of change and action, so also to the principle of change and passion. So the power of the soul with respect to its being affected by what is above it from principles for completion by sciences and perceptions is called theoretical intellect; and with respect to its effect upon the body to perfect its substance (although that also returns to the perfection of the soul, in that the body is an instrument for it in acquiring science and action) is called practical intellect. And it is well known that the ranks of the theoretical are four: It is called because it is either perfection or preparedness toward perfection: strong, intermediate, or weak. The weak is the pure receptivity of the soul for perceptions, called hyulani intellect, by analogy to the primary matter which is in itself devoid of all forms receptive to it, like the capacity of a child for writing. The intermediate is its preparedness for acquiring theoretical matters after obtaining necessary truths, [called] intellect by disposition, because of the disposition it has acquired to transition to theoretical matters, like the illiterate person prepared to learn writing; and people's ranks in that differ greatly according to different degrees of preparedness. The strong is the ability to recall theoretical matters whenever it wishes without needing new acquisition, because they are acquired and stored, present by attention, like the one able to write when he is not writing, and he can write whenever he wishes, [called] intellect in act, because of its strong proximity to act. As for perfection, it is that theoretical matters are present as witnessed, like the writer when he writes, and it is called acquired intellect, i.e. from outside, and it is the active intellect which brings our souls from potentiality to act, with respect to the perfections it has.

Commentary
This passage defines the two faculties of the soul — theoretical and practical intellect — and then focuses on the theoretical intellect, enumerating its four ranks. The author begins by stating that the soul has power in two respects: as affected by higher principles for theoretical completion (theoretical intellect) and as affecting the body for practical perfection (practical intellect). For the theoretical intellect, there are four ranks. First, weak preparedness (al-isti'dad al-da'if), which is the pure receptivity of the soul for intelligibles, called hyulani intellect (al-'aql al-hayulani), by analogy with prime matter. Second, intermediate preparedness (al-mutawassit), which is the preparedness to acquire theoretical matters after necessary truths are obtained, called intellect by disposition (al-'aql bi-l-malaka), like an illiterate person ready to learn writing. Third, strong preparedness (al-qawi), which is the ability to recall theoretical matters without new acquisition, because they are already acquired and stored, called intellect in act (al-'aql bi-l-fi'l), like a skilled writer who can write at will. Fourth, perfection (al-kamal), which is the actual presence of theoretical matters before the soul as witnessed, called acquired intellect (al-'aql al-mustafad), like the writer actively writing. The author then notes that scholars differ on whether these four terms refer to states of the soul, to the soul itself in those states, or to the powers that are the principles of these states. There is also a discussion on whether the acquired intellect is merely the presence of known truths (making it existentially like the intellect in act but higher in honor) or a complete presence that never disappears (which is impossible or extremely rare in this life). The first view is deemed more appropriate. Finally, the passage reiterates the definition of power and provides analogies for each rank, emphasizing that people vary greatly in their preparedness. The active intellect (al-'aql al-fa''al) is identified as the external cause that brings the soul from potentiality to actuality.

The Four Ranks of the Theoretical Intellect
Al-Taftazani enumerates the four ranks of the theoretical intellect and describes the corresponding states of the soul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to name and distinguish the four ranks of the theoretical intellect: hyulani, bi-l-malaka, bi-l-fi'l, and mustafad.

Core Idea
The theoretical intellect progresses from pure potentiality to full actuality through four ranks.

After defining the theoretical intellect, al-Taftazani enumerates its four stages of development. These ranks describe the soul's preparedness or perfection regarding intelligibles, from the lowest potential to the highest actuality.

1
Read the enumeration

The ranks are: weak (hayulani), intermediate (bi-l-malaka), strong (bi-l-fi'l), and perfection (mustafad).
2
Match each rank to its description

3
Understand the soul's states
The final sentence summarizes the soul's states: devoid, adorned with necessities, adorned with theories without presence, or with presence.

Each rank of the intellect is like a stage in learning writing.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of the soul's perceptions during sleep and a classification of dreams. It begins by stating that the soul connects to the world of the unseen (ʿālam al-ghayb) when the senses are at rest, producing an individual perceptual image either directly or through the imaginative faculty (al-mutakhayyila). The author then classifies dreams into three types: (1) A true dream (ruʾyā ṣādiqa) occurs when the image remains unchanged as it reaches the common sense (al-ḥiss al-mushtarak). (2) A dream requiring interpretation (ruʾyā taʿbīr) occurs when the imaginative faculty alters the image, but it can be traced back to the original through analysis. (3) Confused dreams (aḍghāth aḥlām) are those that cannot be traced back, including images that arise from the imagination due to physical conditions (e.g., seeing yellow things when yellow bile predominates). The passage then elaborates on the second division, explaining that when awake, the soul is preoccupied with sensory input, so it rarely connects to spiritual substances (al-jawāhir al-rūḥāniyya). In sleep, the sensory spirit is confined, allowing the soul to connect and imprint images of things, especially those relevant to itself and its surroundings. These images may be particular or universal, and the imaginative faculty represents universals as particular images, which then reach the common sense and become visible. The text ends mid-sentence, continuing the explanation.

Objections to Perpetual Celestial Motion
A series of objections against the necessity of perpetual celestial motion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure and content of the objections raised against the necessity of perpetual celestial motion.

Core Idea
Objections do not deny motion; they challenge its necessity and proposed cause.

In this passage, the objector systematically challenges key premises of the proof for perpetual celestial motion. Each objection begins with 'We do not concede' (laa nusallim), aiming to show that the proof's conclusions do not necessarily follow. Understanding these objections is crucial for evaluating the strength of the original argument.

And the objection against it: We do not concede the necessity of the perpetual motion of the heaven and the impossibility of its cessation; nor do we concede that the desire for the sensible object is only for attraction or repulsion. Why might it not be for knowledge of it, or for assimilation to it, or something else? Nor do we concede the impossibility of appetite and anger in the celestial spheres. Nor does the similarity of their parts in essence necessitate similarity of their states. Nor do we concede that from the failure to attain the beloved's essence or state despair necessarily follows, nor that from attaining it pursuit ceases. Why might hope not continue, or the beloved or its state be something non-permanent, whose type is preserved through the succession of individuals, as you mentioned concerning similars? Even if some of these objections can be rebutted, the desired conclusion is not achieved unless all are rebutted.

Commentary
This passage is an objection to a previous proof that the heavens are perpetually in motion due to a love for an infinite beloved. The objector begins by stating that he does not concede the necessity of perpetual motion or the impossibility of its cessation. He then denies the premise that desire for a sensible object is only for attraction or repulsion, suggesting it could be for knowledge, imitation, or other reasons. Next, he does not concede that appetite and anger are impossible for celestial spheres, and he argues that similarity of parts does not imply similarity of states. He further challenges the connection between attaining the beloved and the cessation of pursuit: despair need not follow from non-attainment, nor cessation from attainment, as hope could continue or the beloved could be non-permanent, preserved by succession. Finally, he denies that the beloved with infinite perfections must be the intellect, unless those perfections are all simultaneously present. He concludes that even if some points are rebutted, the overall argument fails unless all are rebutted.
