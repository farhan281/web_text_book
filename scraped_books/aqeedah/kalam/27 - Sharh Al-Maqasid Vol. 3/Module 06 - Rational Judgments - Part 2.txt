The Proof of Contact for the Finitude of Dimensions
Taftazani presents a logical proof that dimensions must be finite using a sphere and parallel lines.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument known as the 'Proof of Contact' that kalām scholars use to demonstrate that all physical bodies have finite dimensions.

Core Idea
Infinite dimensions lead to logical impossibility, proving all bodies are finite.

Taftazani presents one of the most important arguments in Islamic theology for the finitude of the physical world. The argument uses a thought experiment involving a sphere moving towards an infinite line to show that the assumption of an infinite distance leads to a contradiction. This lesson unpacks the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Note the structure: a claim, a hypothetical scenario, logical consequences, refutations of denials, an objection, and historical context.
The passage is dense; take your time to follow the logic.
2
Understand the core argument
Identify the key steps: assume an infinite distance, a sphere moves toward it, its diameter tilts to contact the line, this requires a first point of contact, but on an infinite line any point has a previous point, leading to an infinite regress.
The 'true imagination' (الوهم الصادق) plays a role in judging divisibility.
3
Examine the refutations and objection
Notice how Taftazani refutes three denials (of entailment, of the sought, and of the non-existence of the consequent). Then consider the objection from Imam al-Razi and its answer based on pure imagination.
This shows the robustness of the proof against challenges.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed proof (برهان) that all bodies have finite dimensions, a key position in kalām against the possibility of actual infinities. It begins by stating that among the properties of bodies is that their dimensions (الأبعاد) are finite. The first proof (الأول) uses a hypothetical sphere moving toward an infinite line. If an infinite distance (بعد غير متناه) existed, a sphere could move toward it; its diameter, parallel to the line, would tilt to become coincident (مسامتة) with it. This would require the imagination (الوهم) to fix a first point of contact—impossible for an infinite line because before any point's contact, there is contact with a point farther up. The angle and motion involved are infinitely divisible in the 'true imagination' (الوهم الصادق), so the half-contact comes before the whole, leading to an infinite regress. The author then refutes three denials: (1) denial of the entailment (منع الملازمة) is refuted by pointing out the impossibility of an infinite consequent; (2) denial of the sought conclusion (منع ثبوت المطلوب) is refuted because the impossibility only follows from the assumption of infinite distance; (3) denial that the consequent fails (منع انتفاء اللازم) is refuted because the infinite divisibility of angle and motion is only imaginary, not actual. Next, an objection from Imam (likely Fakhr al-Dīn al-Rāzī) is presented: the longest possible line is the world's axis; contact with it must follow contact with an external point, leading to an infinite regress. The answer (فجوابه) is that this is purely imaginary (من الوهميات الصرفة). Then the author notes that this proof treats bodily dimensions as actual (متحقق) unlike void (خلاء). He reports that the doctrine of infinite dimensions was held by Indian sages, some early scholars, and Abu al-Barakat. The well-known proofs (المشهور من أدلة المانعين) against infinite dimensions are three; the first is the 'proof of contact' (برهان المسامتة), whose presentation is clear from the text. The sphere motion is chosen because the tilt from parallelism to contact is intuitively obvious, almost sensed. Parallelism (موازاة) means the lines never meet, even if extended infinitely.

The Proof of Tangency: Refuting Infinite Distances
Al-Taftazani presents the proof of tangency and its core logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the basic structure of the proof of tangency (برهان المسامتة) and why it shows infinite distances lead to a contradiction.

Core Idea
The first point of tangency cannot exist if distances are infinite.

Al-Taftazani introduces a key argument against infinite distances: the proof of tangency. This proof uses the simple geometry of a rotating sphere and a parallel line to show that assuming infinite distance forces an impossible regress. We will unpack the core reasoning and see how theologians deploy it.

Guided Path
1
Understand the setup
Visualize a sphere rotating around its axis. A line parallel to the diameter of the sphere will eventually become tangent to the sphere. This shift from being parallel to tangent requires a first point of tangency.
The proof relies on the transition from parallelism to tangency.
2
Trace the contradiction
If the distance from the sphere to the line is infinite, then before any point of tangency there must be another point, due to the infinite divisibility of the angle and motion. Thus, no first point exists, but tangency must begin somewhere – a contradiction.
3
Identify the entailment
The key logical step (ملازمة) is that the occurrence of tangency after not having been necessitates a first point. Denying this entailment is the main objection Al-Taftazani addresses.

Commentary
This passage presents and defends the proof of tangency (برهان المسامتة) used to refute the possibility of infinite distances (لا تناهي الأبعاد). The argument proceeds as follows: First, the proof is outlined: For a sphere rotating, a line parallel to its diameter will eventually become tangent. The first point of tangency must be determined, but if the line is infinite, such a point cannot be determined because between any two points, there is always another, and the angle and motion involved are divisible without end. This shows that assuming infinite distance leads to a contradiction. The author then addresses the denial of the entailment (منع الملازمة), the denial of the establishment of the desired conclusion (منع ثبوت المطلوب), and the denial of the non-existence of the consequent (منع انتفاء اللازم), each based on the imagination (وهم). The answer is that this is a mere imaginary (وهمية صرفة) not actual. The proof is then explicitly stated as the first of three proofs used by those who deny infinite distances. The motion of the sphere is considered because the transition from parallel to tangent is clearly sensed. The definition of parallel lines is given (they never meet). The point is considered only in imagination because actual points exist only if the line is cut. Finally, the author refines the proof against an objection that denies the necessity of a first point of tangency. The objection claims that because angle and motion are infinitely divisible, there is no first tangency. The author responds that this objection fails because the entailment (ملازمة) stands: if distance is infinite, then both affirming and denying a first point leads to contradiction, thus the original proof is sound.

Defending the Proof: Objections and Logical Refinements
Al-Taftazani addresses objections and refines the proof's logical structure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Taftazani defends the proof of tangency against key objections, including the claim that the first point is not necessary and the argument from the axis of the world.

Core Idea
Even if infinite division is imaginary, the impossibility stands because the antecedent is impossible.

Understand the objection from infinite divisibility

See Al-Taftazani's response

Examine the Imam's objection

The 'Now' Method for Proving a First Event
Taftazani presents the 'now' method as a proof against infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the 'now' method uses the comparison of two infinite series to demonstrate that an infinite regress of past events is impossible.

Core Idea
Mapping two infinite 'now' series forces either absurd equality or a finite end.

Taftazani begins his proof of a first event with a clever thought experiment. Imagine two infinite sequences of moments—one starting from the present and the other from an ancient point like the Flood. By aligning them, we either get a contradiction or a necessary stopping point. This lesson unpacks that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the setup
The method assumes two infinite series of 'nows': one from the present moment backward, and another from the day of the Flood backward, each without end. Take a moment to grasp this initial assumption.
This is a hypothetical used to test the opponent's claim of infinite past.
2
The mapping procedure
We map the first 'now' of one series to the first of the other, and so on. This is a one-to-one correspondence by rational assumption.
3
Two possibilities
Either the series coincide perfectly (every now in one has a match in the other) or they diverge. Consider what each outcome would mean.
4
First outcome: coincidence
If they coincide, then the whole set from now to infinity equals the part from the Flood to infinity—an absurdity because the part (Flood onward) is shorter than the whole (now onward).
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
5
Second outcome: divergence
If they diverge, the flood-series must end (since it maps onto a subset of the now-series), forcing the now-series to be finite as well. Hence an infinite past is impossible.
The conclusion: a first event must exist.

Taftazani shows that these objections are not decisive, and the debate requires careful logical analysis.
