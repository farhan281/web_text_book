Classification of Jinn and Shayatin in Islamic Theology
Classification of spirits into angels, believing jinn, and shayatin.

The Philosophers' Proof That Self-Knowledge Is the Soul's Essence
Philosophers argue that self-knowledge is the soul's essence, perpetual and intuitive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' argument that the soul's self-knowledge is identical to its essence and therefore perpetual and intuitive.

Core Idea
Self-knowledge is not an accident; it is the soul's very essence.

In this objection, the philosophers present a concise argument that the soul's knowledge of itself is not a separate image but identical to the soul itself. This leads to the conclusion that such knowledge is always present and cannot be acquired through demonstration. Let's trace their reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the full Arabic passage once, then its literal translation. Focus on the flow of the argument without analyzing yet.
Notice how the philosophers begin by stating their doctrine and then offer a division.
2
Identify the logical division
The philosophers divide self-knowledge into two possibilities: either an image other than the essence, or the essence itself. Identify this division clearly.
This is a classic dichotomous argument (division and rejection).
3
Understand the refutation of the first option
They argue the first option (image other than essence) leads to two absurdities: coexistence of similars and indeterminacy of state versus locus. Work through these reasons.
4
Trace the conclusion and its implications
Once the first option is rejected, they conclude identity. Then they infer perpetuity because the essence is always present, so knowledge is always present. Finally, they conclude such knowledge cannot be sought by demonstration.
This leads to the final point that soul knowledge is intuitive (badīhī).

The Second Objection: The doctrine of the philosophers (al-ḥukamā’) is that a thing's knowledge of itself is its very essence. In this regard they say: the intellect, the intelligent, and the intelligible are one. They argued for it: since it is established that a thing's intellection (ta‘aqqul) is an image (ṣūra) equivalent to the intelligible in the intellect of the intelligent. So we say: a thing's knowledge of itself is either an image other than its essence, subsisting in its essence, or it is its very essence. The first is invalid, otherwise the coexistence of two similars would be necessary, which is absurd. Also, on this supposition, neither would be more deserving of being a state (ḥāliyya) than the other being a locus (maḥaliyya). And when this division is invalidated, it is established that the soul's knowledge of its specific reality is its very essence. And since its essence is present in all times of the duration of that essence, it follows that its knowledge of its specific essence is perpetual with the perpetuity of its essence. And if this is the case, it is impossible for this knowledge to be sought through demonstration, because what is already obtained cannot be obtained a second time. Thus, by these strong investigations, it is established that knowledge of the substance of the soul must be primary intuitive knowledge, independent of definition, acquisition, and seeking. Yet you have made it among demonstrative investigations, so the difficulty is necessary.

Commentary
This passage presents the philosophers' objection (al-su’āl al-thānī) against the position that knowledge of the soul's essence is sought through demonstration. The philosophers hold that a thing's knowledge of itself is identical to its essence (dhāt). They support this with the principle that intellection (ta‘aqqul) is an image (ṣūra) equivalent to the intelligible in the intellect. For a thing's self-knowledge, either that image is other than the essence and subsists in it, or it is the essence itself. The first option is false, because it would require two similar things (the essence and the image) to coexist in the same locus, which is impossible. Moreover, there would be no reason to prefer one as a state and the other as a locus. Thus, the soul's knowledge of its specific reality is its very essence. Since the soul's essence is always present (present at all times of its duration), its self-knowledge is perpetual (dā’im) and therefore cannot be sought through demonstration—what is already present cannot be reacquired. The philosophers conclude that knowledge of the soul's substance is primary and intuitive, not subject to demonstration, thereby criticizing those who treat it as a demonstrative inquiry.

Consequently, each soul differs from another, so a body suitable for one soul is not automatically suitable for another. Thus the proof that a body can accept any soul fails.

The soul's essence retains knowledge even after the body dies.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer section. The objector raises two points against the earlier memory-based disproof of transmigration. First, he suggests that knowledge of a past body's conditions depends on the soul's connection to that body; when the body dies, the condition is removed, so memory is lost. Second, he argues that intense preoccupation with present life's affairs causes forgetfulness, just as someone in danger forgets family. The author (Razi) answers each separately. For the first objection, he presents a threefold division of the self (ana): either it is the body itself, or something needing the body, or a distinct independent substance. If the first two, transmigration is impossible anyway; if the third (the soul as independent substance), its essence is receptive to knowledge and the persistence of that knowledge through repetition makes it indelible. Death of the body does not erase such knowledge. For the second objection, he argues that we often have quiet moments of leisure and reflection, yet we never recall any past lives, which shows the claim of forgetfulness due to preoccupation is false. Talisman Owners' Doctrine of Universal Celestial Spirits
Report of talisman owners' doctrine with Quranic evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the talisman owners' belief in a universal spirit for each celestial sphere, its branching spirits, and their attempted Quranic proof.

Core Idea
For talisman owners, each sphere has one managing spirit with many branching spirits.

The talisman owners held a unique cosmology where each celestial sphere, especially the Throne, is governed by a single universal spirit from which countless lesser spirits branch, akin to the faculties of the human soul. They even claimed support from the Quran. This lesson presents their doctrine as reported by the author, noting that it is not endorsed.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the translation. Let the author's report come through cleanly.
The passage is a report, not an affirmation.
2
Identify the core claim
The talisman owners affirm a universal spirit (روح كلي) for each sphere that manages it, plus many branching spirits from that origin.
Note the use of 'أثبتوا' (they affirmed).
3
Study the example of the Throne
They say the Throne has a 'Greatest Spirit' (الروح الأعظم) called the 'universal soul' (النفس الكلية), from which many spirits branch to its parts.
This parallels human psychology.
4
Appreciate the analogy with the human body
Just as the body's managing soul branches into faculties like attraction and retention, so the celestial soul branches into many spirits.
The analogy shows coherence in their system.
5
Examine the Quranic evidence they cite
They interpret 'the Spirit' in Quran 78:38 as the celestial soul and 'the angels' in Quran 39:75 as the branching spirits.
The author does not endorse this interpretation.

Commentary
This passage is a report of the beliefs of the talisman owners (أصحاب الطلسمات) regarding celestial spirits. Note: The author is not endorsing this interpretation but reporting the view of the talisman owners.

Learning Objective
