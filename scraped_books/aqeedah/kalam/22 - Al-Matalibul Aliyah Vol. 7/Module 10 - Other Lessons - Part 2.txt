Learning Objective

Core Idea
All human morals stem from three fundamental psychic powers: reason, anger, and desire.

The sages of ancient wisdom classified the human soul's faculties into three primary powers, each associated with a bodily organ. This classification explains the origin of all moral qualities. In this passage, the author presents this foundational framework, which is essential for understanding human psychology and ethics in the Islamic tradition.

Commentary
This passage presents a classification from the sages (al-ḥukamā’) regarding the fundamental powers of the soul. They state that the original psychic powers (al-quwā al-aṣliyya al-nafsāniyya) are three. The highest and most noble (ashrafuhā) is the rational or logical power (al-quwwa al-nuṭqiyya), associated with the brain (dimāghiyya). This means that any moral quality or character trait can be traced back to one of these three powers: rationality, anger/spiritedness, or desire. This psychological framework is foundational for understanding human behavior and the causes of differences among individuals.

Al-Razi argues for mutual soul-body influence using choleric temper example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi uses the example of choleric temperament to demonstrate the mutual causal influence between the soul and the body.

Core Idea
The soul and body continuously affect each other—anger can be both a result of and a cause of bodily bile.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi presents a classic philosophical argument for the interaction between the soul and the body. He uses a simple, observable phenomenon: the relationship between the choleric temperament (dominance of yellow bile) and the emotion of anger. This example illustrates a two-way causal connection, challenging any view that sees the soul and body as entirely separate or that causation flows only one way.

Commentary
This passage presents an argument demonstrating the mutual influence between the soul and the body. Al-Razi begins with a general statement: the mixture of humors (mizaj al-akhlat) in the soul corresponds to the soul's emotional variation. He then explains by giving an example: if a person has a dominant choleric temperament (al-mizaj al-safrawi), he becomes angry frequently. The argument is based on observable correlation: the body's humoral state affects the soul's emotion (anger), and the soul's emotion (anger) in turn affects the body by producing more bile. This is a classic proof in philosophical psychology for the interaction between the two substances.

Galen's Second Objection: The Symmetry Argument

Learning Objective

If descending arteries don't become nerves, why would ascending ones?

Galen offers a second objection based on symmetry. The artery from the heart divides into ascending and descending branches. The argument is that there is no special reason for transformation only in the brain.

Proof that Rational Souls are Incorruptible with Objection and Answer
The author refutes the corruptibility of souls and answers an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the author's logical proof that rational souls cannot be corrupted and how he refutes the opposing argument based on possibility of non-existence.

Core Idea
The soul's lasting nature makes it impossible to be conditioned by what does not last.

This passage presents a compact but powerful argument for the incorruptibility of rational souls. The author first refutes a prior claim by analyzing whether the supposed conditions for the soul's existence are permanent or transient. He then anticipates an objection from those who argue that since the soul is originated, it must be receptive to non-existence, and provides a concise rebuttal. Mastering this argument helps clarify the distinction between possibility of origination and possibility of corruption.

Commentary
This passage is a proof of the incorruptibility of rational souls, followed by an objection and its answer. The author begins by refuting the claim that the soul is subject to non-existence and corruption. He argues that the conditions for the soul's existence either persist or do not. If they persist, the earlier division applies. If they do not persist, they cannot be conditions for the soul because the soul is lasting while they are not, and it is impossible for a lasting thing to be conditioned by what does not last. Thus it is proven that rational souls are not receptive to non-existence or corruption. Then the opponents argue that since the soul is originated, it must be receptive to non-existence, and this receptivity is either a necessary concomitant or accidental. If necessary, the soul must be receptive to corruption; if accidental, then that receptivity requires another receptivity ad infinitum, which is impossible. The author answers that this argument presupposes the soul's origination, which is a point of contention. Moreover, the difference between the possibility of origination and the possibility of corruption after existence has already been explained, so there is no need to repeat it.
