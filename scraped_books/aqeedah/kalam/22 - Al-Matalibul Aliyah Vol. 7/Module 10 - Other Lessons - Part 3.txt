Can One Soul Manage Two Bodies? A Rational Exploration
Argument for the possibility of one soul managing many bodies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational argument for the possibility of a single soul managing multiple bodies, including the analogical and teleological reasoning.

Core Idea
One soul can manage many bodies because its attachment is purely managerial, not intrinsic.

In this passage, the author tackles a fascinating speculative question: could a single soul simultaneously manage two bodies? He presents an argument based on the soul's role as a manager and ruler, and then strengthens it with a teleological consideration about the soul's desire for physical pleasures. Let's explore his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without breaking it down. Notice the double question and the author's remark that these investigations are obscure.
Pay attention to the phrase 'اعلم : أن هذه المباحث غامضة' – it sets the tone of cautious inquiry.
2
Identify the core argument
The author argues that one soul attaching to many bodies is not impossible. Find the reason: the soul's attachment to the body is only by way of management (تصرف) and administration (تدبير), just as one ruler can govern two kingdoms.
This is an analogy: ruler→kingdoms :: soul→bodies.
3
Examine the teleological reinforcement
The author adds that the soul loves the body because it uses bodily instruments to obtain physical pleasures. Using two bodies would serve this purpose more strongly, so attaching to many bodies might even be obligatory, or at least possible as a rarity.
This shifts from possibility to desirability, but the conclusion remains possibility.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The passage does not settle the matter definitively but argues that one soul for many bodies is not impossible and at least possible in a rare manner. Consider how this argument fits into broader theological debates about the soul.

Sanusi lays out the necessary premise for the soul's managerial relation to the body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the necessary premise for discussions about multiple souls in one body or one soul in multiple bodies: the soul is a simple substance that only manages the body as a tool for obtaining pleasures.

Core Idea
The soul uses the body like a tool for pleasure, not as an essential part of itself.

However, he immediately points out a crucial premise that such investigations rely on: the soul must be understood as a non-corporeal substance with only a managerial relationship to the body. He then elaborates on this premise, explaining that the soul's motivation for managing the body is to use its organs to attain physical pleasures and desires. Finally, he illustrates this relationship with a powerful analogy: the soul's attachment to the body is like a craftsman's attachment to a tool – valuable only for its utility, and discardable if a better tool appears.

First General Argument: Sense Specialization Refutes Soul's Perception of Particulars
First general argument against soul perceiving particulars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the innate specialization of the senses serves as a general argument against the soul directly perceiving particulars, and how the objection from instrumental use of organs is answered.

Core Idea
Innate sense specializations prove the soul does not directly perceive particulars.

This lesson examines the first of four general arguments that the soul does not perceive particulars. The argument appeals to our innate knowledge that each sense has a specific function. If the soul were the direct perceiver, these specializations would be meaningless. We will see how Avicenna's view is challenged and how an objection involving the soul's attention to organs is refuted.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the main claim
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice that the passage starts by stating the soul's capacity and then presents Avicenna's view that the soul only perceives universals.
Look for the phrase 'النفس الناطقة لا تقوى إلا على إدراك الكليات والمجردات'.
2
Understand the appeal to innate specialization
The first general argument uses 'بديهة' (innate knowledge) that vision is in the eye, hearing in the ear, etc. If the soul were the perceiver, these specializations would be invalid.
The argument is: we know directly that the tongue tastes and the eye sees. If the soul did it, why have different organs?
3
Analyze the objection and the answer
The objection: the soul uses organs as instruments by turning attention to them. The answer: if the soul turns to the tongue, does the tongue taste? If yes, then the soul is not the perceiver; if no, then the tongue is like any other organ, invalidating specialization.
This refutation shows that the objection cannot explain the observed specialization.

اعلم أن الغالب على الطباع العامية: الحكم بأن أقوى وأكمل السعادات: لذة المطعم والمنكح. ولذلك فإن جمهور الناس لا يعبدون الله تعالى إلا ليجدوا المطاعم اللذيذة في الآخرة، وإلا ليجدوا المناكح الشهية هناك.
وهذا القول مرذول عند المحققين من أهل الحكمة، وأرباب الرياضة.
ويدل عليه وجوه:
الحجة الأولى: لو كانت سعادة الإنسان متعلقة بقضاء الشهوة، وإمضاء الغضب. لكان الحيوان الذي يكون هو أقوى في هذا الباب من الإنسان: أشرف منه. لكن الجمل أكثر أكلاً من الناس، والذئب أقوى في الإيذاء من الإنسان. والعصفور أقوى على السفاد من الإنسان، فوجب كون هذه الأشياء أشرف من الإنسان. لكن التالي معلوم البطلان بالضرورة، فوجب الجزم بأن سعادة الإنسان غير متعلقة بهذه الأمور.
الحجة الثانية: كل شيء يكون سبباً لحصول الكمال والسعادة. فكل ما كان ذلك الشيء أكثر حصولاً، كانت السعادة والكمال أكثر حصولاً. فلو كان قضاء شهوة البطن والفرج سبباً لكمال حال الإنسان ولسعادته، لكان الإنسان كلما كان أكثر اشتغالاً بقضاء شهوة البطن والفرج، وأكثر استغراقاً فيه، كان أعلى درجة وأكمل فضيلة. لكن التالي باطل. لأن الإنسان الذي جعل عمره وقفاً على الأكل والشرب والسفاد، يعد من البهائم، ويقضي عليه بالدناءة والخساسة. وكل ذلك يدل على أن الاشتغال بقضاء هاتين الشهوتين، ليس من باب السعادات والكمالات، بل من باب دفع الحاجات والآفات.
الحجة الثالثة: إن الإنسان يشاركه في لذة الأكل والشرب، جميع الحيوانات الخسيسة. فإنه كما أن الإنسان يلتذ بأكل السكر، فكذلك يلتذ الجعل بأكل السرجين. فلو كانت هذه اللذات البدنية، هي السعادة الكبرى للإنسان، لوجب أن لا يكون للإنسان فضيلة على هذه الحيوانات الخسيسة، بل نزيد فنقول: لو كانت سعادة الإنسان متعلقة بهذه اللذات الحسية، لوجب أن يكون الإنسان أخس الحيوانات. والتالي باطل، فالمقدم مثله.
وبيان وجه الملازمة: أن الحيوانات الخسيسة مشاركة للإنسان في هذه اللذات الحسية البدنية، إلا أن الإنسان تنتقص عليه هذه المطالب بسبب العقل. فإن العقل سمي عقلا، لكونه عقالاً له، وحبساً له عن أكثر ما يشتهيه، ويميل طبعه إليه. فإذا كان التقدير أن كمال السعادة ليس إلا في هذه اللذات الحسية. ثم بينا: أن هذه اللذات الحسية حاصلة على سبيل التمام والكمال للبهائم والسباع من غير أن يكون الإنسان أخس الحيوانات. ولما كان هذا معلوم الفساد بالبديهة، ثبت: أن هذه اللذات الحسية، ليست موجبة للبهجة والسعادة.
Chapter Twenty-Two
On explaining that rational pleasures are nobler and more complete than sensory pleasures
Know that the prevailing disposition among common natures is the judgment that the strongest and most complete happinesses are the pleasure of eating and intercourse. Therefore, the majority of people worship God Almighty only to find delicious foods in the Hereafter, and to find desirable sexual partners there.
This saying is rejected by the verifiers among the people of wisdom and the practitioners of spiritual discipline.
Evidence indicates it through several points:
The first proof: If human happiness were dependent on fulfilling appetite and executing anger, then the animal that is stronger in this regard than man would be nobler than him. But the camel eats more than people, the wolf is stronger in harming than man, and the bird is stronger in copulation than man. So it would be necessary that these things be nobler than man. But the consequent is known to be false necessarily, so it is necessary to be certain that human happiness is not dependent on these matters.
The second proof: Everything that is a cause for the attainment of perfection and happiness—the more that thing is obtained, the more happiness and perfection are obtained. So if fulfilling the appetite of the stomach and genitals were a cause for the perfection of man's state and his happiness, then the more a man engages in fulfilling the appetite of the stomach and genitals and is absorbed in it, the higher his rank and more complete his virtue. But the consequent is false. Because a man who makes his life a consecrated devotion to eating, drinking, and copulation is counted among the beasts and is judged to be base and low. All that indicates that engaging in fulfilling these two appetites is not among happinesses and perfections, but rather among repelling needs and harms.
The third proof: All base animals share with man in the pleasure of eating and drinking. For just as man takes pleasure in eating sugar, so too does the dung beetle take pleasure in eating dung. So if these bodily pleasures were the greatest happiness for man, it would be necessary that man have no virtue over these base animals. Rather, we add and say: If human happiness were dependent on these sensory pleasures, it would be necessary that man be the basest of animals. The consequent is false, so the antecedent is likewise.
And the explanation of the entailment: base animals share with man in these sensory bodily pleasures, except that man is diminished in these pursuits because of intellect. For the intellect is called 'aql because it is an 'iqāl (restraint) for him, holding him back from most of what he desires and to which his nature inclines. So if the estimation is that the perfection of happiness is only in these sensory pleasures, and then we have shown that these sensory pleasures are obtained completely and perfectly by beasts and predators without man being the basest of animals—and since this is known to be false by intuition, it is established that these sensory pleasures do not cause delight and happiness.

Commentary
This passage is a philosophical argument presenting three proofs that sensory pleasures are not the highest form of human happiness. The author begins by stating the common misconception: most people believe the most powerful happiness lies in eating and intercourse, even worshipping God only for these rewards in the afterlife. This view is rejected by the verifiers among philosophers and spiritual practitioners. The author then provides three arguments.

First proof: If human happiness were tied to satisfying appetite and anger, then animals stronger in these areas would be nobler than humans. But the camel eats more, the wolf is more harmful, and the bird copulates more—yet these animals are not nobler. Therefore, happiness is not in these things.

Second proof: If fulfilling these appetites causes perfection and happiness, then the more one indulges, the more perfect one should become. But the glutton and lecher are considered base and beastly, not virtuous. Therefore, these activities are not sources of happiness or perfection but merely necessities.

Third proof: Base animals share in sensory pleasures, so if these were the greatest happiness, man would have no superiority over them. In fact, man would be the basest because his intellect restrains him from full indulgence. Since this is absurd, sensory pleasures are not the cause of true delight and happiness.

The Second Proof: Indulgence Does Not Bring Perfection
Second proof using the principle of proportionate causality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's second argument: if sensory pleasures caused perfection, then greater indulgence would mean greater virtue, but the opposite is true.

Core Idea
If sensory pleasures caused happiness, then gluttons would be the most perfect people.

Al-Razi's second argument uses a causal principle: if something is a cause of perfection, then more of it should produce more perfection. Applying this to sensory pleasures leads to an absurd conclusion: the person who spends his life eating, drinking, and fornicating would be the highest in virtue. But we know such a person is considered base, proving these pleasures are not sources of happiness.

Guided Path
1
Read the second proof.
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice the conditional: if A causes perfection, then more A means more perfection.
Al-Razi uses a general principle about causality.
2
Apply the principle to sensory pleasures.
If fulfilling appetite causes perfection, then the more one indulges, the more perfect one becomes. But in reality, the glutton is despised.
Contrast the logical implication with common sense.
3
Understand the conclusion.
Since the outcome is false, the premise is false: fulfilling these appetites is not a cause of happiness, but merely a way to repel harm.
This argument refutes the idea that indulgence leads to happiness.

The Third Proof: Shared Base Pleasures and the Role of Intellect
Third proof using shared pleasures and the restraining role of intellect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi's third argument uses the fact that base animals share sensory pleasures and that intellect restrains humans, to prove that sensory pleasures cannot be the ultimate happiness.

Core Idea
If sensory pleasures were the highest good, then man would be the basest animal because his intellect restrains him.

Al-Razi's third argument points out that base animals also enjoy sensory pleasures—even the dung beetle enjoys its food. If these pleasures constituted the highest happiness, then humans would have no advantage over these animals. Worse still, because the intellect restrains humans from indulging fully, humans would be inferior to animals. This absurdity shows that sensory pleasures are not the cause of true delight.

Guided Path
1
Read the third proof.
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice the comparison between humans and base animals like the dung beetle.
Al-Razi uses the example of a dung beetle enjoying dung to show relativity of pleasure.
2
Understand the entailment.
If sensory pleasures were ultimate happiness, humans would have no superiority over animals. In fact, because intellect restrains them, they would be worse off.
The word 'aql (intellect) is said to be an 'iqāl (restraint).
3
Recognize the conclusion.
Since it is absurd to think humans are the basest animals, the premise that sensory pleasures are the highest happiness must be false.
Thus, sensory pleasures do not cause true delight and happiness.

But the camel eats more than people, the wolf is stronger in harming than man, and the bird is stronger in copulation than man. So it would be necessary that these things be nobler than man. But the consequent is known to be false necessarily, so it is necessary to be certain that human happiness is not dependent on these matters.
The second proof: Everything that is a cause for the attainment of perfection and happiness—the more that thing is obtained, the more happiness and perfection are obtained. So if fulfilling the appetite of the stomach and genitals were a cause for the perfection of man's state and his happiness, then the more a man engages in fulfilling the appetite of the stomach and genitals and is absorbed in it, the higher his rank and more complete his virtue. But the consequent is false. Because a man who makes his life a consecrated devotion to eating, drinking, and copulation is counted among the beasts and is judged to be base and low. All that indicates that engaging in fulfilling these two appetites is not among happinesses and perfections, but rather among repelling needs and harms.
The third proof: All base animals share with man in the pleasure of eating and drinking. For just as man takes pleasure in eating sugar, so too does the dung beetle take pleasure in eating dung. So if these bodily pleasures were the greatest happiness for man, it would be necessary that man have no virtue over these base animals. Rather, we add and say: If human happiness were dependent on these sensory pleasures, it would be necessary that man be the basest of animals. The consequent is false, so the antecedent is likewise.
And the explanation of the entailment: base animals share with man in these sensory bodily pleasures, except that man is diminished in these pursuits because of intellect. For the intellect is called 'aql because it is an 'iqāl (restraint) for him, holding him back from most of what he desires and to which his nature inclines. So if the estimation is that the perfection of happiness is only in these sensory pleasures, and then we have shown that these sensory pleasures are obtained completely and perfectly by beasts and predators without man being the basest of animals—and since this is known to be false by intuition, it is established that these sensory pleasures do not cause delight and happiness.

Commentary
This passage is a philosophical argument presenting three proofs that sensory pleasures are not the highest form of human happiness. The author begins by stating the common misconception: most people believe the most powerful happiness lies in eating and intercourse, even worshipping God only for these rewards in the afterlife. This view is rejected by the verifiers among philosophers and spiritual practitioners. The author then provides three arguments.

First proof: If human happiness were tied to satisfying appetite and anger, then animals stronger in these areas would be nobler than humans. Second proof: If fulfilling these appetites causes perfection and happiness, then the more one indulges, the more perfect one should become. But the glutton and lecher are considered base and beastly, not virtuous. Therefore, these activities are not sources of happiness or perfection but merely necessities.

Third proof: Base animals share in sensory pleasures, so if these were the greatest happiness, man would have no superiority over them. In fact, man would be the basest because his intellect restrains him from full indulgence. Since this is absurd, sensory pleasures are not the cause of true delight and happiness.

When all its body is submerged and its head remains out of the water, all those animals climb up to its head, then it submerges its head little by little, so those animals transfer to the dead skin and gather in it. When the fox senses their transfer to that skin, it throws it into the water and comes out of the water safe and free from those harmful animals. Undoubtedly, this is an amazing trick to repel those harmful things.
Source pages p. 307

Commentary
This passage presents the seventh example, describing how a fox cleverly rids itself of fleas and mosquitoes by using a piece of dead skin as a decoy. The fox gradually submerges its body in water, causing the insects to move upward to avoid the water, eventually concentrating on the head. The fox then submerges its head, prompting the insects to transfer to the piece of skin it holds in its mouth. Finally, the fox throws the skin into the water, freeing itself. The author marvels at this ingenious behavior.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?

?

?
