The Eye as an Instrument: Answering the Distance Objection
The author answers the fifth objection that if the soul sees, vision would not vary with distance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the soul's role as the true perceiver can be reconciled with the fact that vision varies with distance, because the eye serves as an instrument whose condition affects sight.

Core Idea
The eye is an instrument; the soul is the true seer.

This lesson presents the author's response to an objection against the soul being the perceiver. The objection argues that if the soul truly sees, then vision should not change based on distance. The author clarifies that the eye, though not the seer itself, functions as an instrument for sight. Just as an instrument affects the outcome, the eye's condition can explain why vision differs with proximity and distance.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice how the author first states the objection and then gives the answer.
The passage begins with a fragment from a previous discussion; the main answer starts at 'والجواب عما ذكروه خامساً'.
2
Identify the key terms
Find the terms 'النفس' (soul), 'الرائي' (seer), 'الإبصار' (sight), and 'آلة' (instrument). Understand their roles in the argument.
3
Understand the role of an instrument
The author draws an analogy: the eye is like a tool. A tool does not perform the action itself but is used by the agent. Similarly, the soul uses the eyes to see, and the condition of the eyes affects the quality of vision.
This analogy refutes the objection that variation disproves the soul's role.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider how this argument strengthens the position that the soul is the true perceiver. The variability of sight does not undermine the soul's agency; it simply shows that instruments have limitations.
This answer preserves the unity of the perceiver while explaining empirical observations.

First Argument: Universal Winged Square
The author presents the first argument: imaginary universals imply corporeal perception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the ability to imagine a universal winged square leads to the absurdity that the body perceives universals, which would negate the soul.

Core Idea
Imagining universals shows that imagination cannot be purely corporeal.

The first argument of the author's reply tackles the objection head-on. If imagining a specific winged square proves corporeality, then imagining a universal winged square would also be corporeal, leading to the unacceptable conclusion that the body perceives universals.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation once without stopping. Notice how the author starts with a conditional statement.
The argument is a reductio ad absurdum.
2
Identify the logical structure
The author argues: if imagining a specific winged square implies imagination is corporeal, then the same would apply to a universal winged square. That would make the body the perceiver of universals, which contradicts the opponent's own belief in the soul.
3
Reflect on the consequence
Consider why the opponent cannot accept that the body perceives universals. This undermines their objection.
The opponent wants to prove the soul is corporeal, but this argument shows their method leads to denying the soul.

Second Argument: Brain Capacity and Imprinting
The author presents the second argument: brain size limitations disprove corporeal imprinting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second argument: the small size of the brain cannot fully imprint a large image like a mountain, so the opponent's theory of corporeal imagination fails.

Core Idea
A small brain cannot hold a large image, so imagination must be non-physical.

The second argument uses a physical limitation: the brain is small, yet we can imagine vast scenes like mountains. If imagination were corporeal, the image would need to be imprinted physically, which is impossible given the brain's size.

Guided Path
1
Read the second argument
Read the Arabic and translation for the second argument. Pay attention to the dilemma presented.
The author gives a dilemma with two options.
2
Understand the dilemma
If the brain is the location of imagination, and a mountain image is imprinted, only part fits. The opponent can say either: (a) the rest is not imprinted, which means no image is imprinted at all (absurd), or (b) all parts are imprinted in the same spot, which would mean multiple images coexist. The author then argues that coexistence does not hinder distinction, so allowing the winged squares to coexist is possible.
This undermines the objection that two squares cannot coexist in one imaginal space.
3
Reflect on the outcome
The opponent's objection relied on the impossibility of two squares coexisting, but this argument shows coexistence is possible if we can distinguish them.

Third Argument: Travel and Brain Capacity
The author presents the third argument: world travel proves the brain cannot accommodate all details.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third argument: the vast amount of images from a world tour cannot each have a specific brain part, so the opponent's demand for specific imprinting is unreasonable.

The third argument appeals to common experience: a person can travel the world and recall countless scenes. If imagination required a separate brain part for each detail, the brain would be insufficient. This shows that the opponent's assumption of one-to-one mapping is false.

The passage explains that when this power judges, it produces decisive will, which then activates the body's power to move. It ends with an open question about whether animals perceive universals (kulliyat), indicating the commonly held view is mentioned but the text cuts off.

Each Intellect as a Unique Species: The Rational Proof
The author presents and critiques the claim that each intellect is a unique species.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' argument that each immaterial intellect must be its own species, and how the author addresses the scriptural objection that angels share functions.

Core Idea
Immaterial beings cannot be individuated by matter, so each intellect is a unique species.

The philosophers argued that angels, being immaterial, cannot share a species because individuation requires matter. This lesson unpacks that logical argument and the author's response to a potential scriptural counterexample.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation

The passage is a self-contained argument with a claim, proof, objection, and reply.
2
Understand the logical argument
Identify the conditional reasoning: if two intellects were under one species, they would need matter to be distinct. Since they are immaterial, each must be a unique species.
This is a reductio ad absurdum argument.
3
Analyze the objection and response
The objection from scripture mentions angels with different functions. The author replies that sharing effects does not imply sharing quiddities. Consider why this response is consistent with the proof.
The author does not fully endorse the philosophers' claim; he presents it as part of their system.

Core Idea
The rational soul is overwhelmed by 19 bodily forces, making human intellect weak compared to celestial intelligences.

In this passage, the author continues his series of arguments by presenting a seventh proof based on the internal conflict within human beings. He enumerates the forces that oppose the rational faculty and then draws a stark contrast with the pure, unimpeded intellects of celestial beings. This lesson will walk you through the enumeration and the argument's conclusion.
