The Need for an Agent and the Trilemma
Al-Razi argues that contingent souls need an agent and divides possible agents into three categories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why contingent souls require an agent and the three categories of possible agents: body, accident, or neither.

Core Idea
Every contingent being needs an agent, which must be either material, an accident, or immaterial.

Having established that human souls are contingent, Al-Razi now argues that they must have an originator. He then sets up a trilemma: the agent must be a body, an accident in a body, or neither. This division frames the subsequent proofs.

Guided Path
1
Understand the necessity of an agent
Read how Al-Razi states that contingent beings inevitably require an influencer (mu'aththir) and a bringer into existence (mujid).
This is a foundational principle of kalam: whatever is possible needs a cause.
2
Identify the trilemma
Notice the threefold division: the agent can be a body, an accident in a body, or neither. This is a logical partition covering all possibilities.
Al-Razi will then argue against the first two, leaving only the third.
3
Set up for the proofs
Recognize that the next lesson will provide five proofs against the first possibility (body). This passage prepares the ground.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and refutation. The author, likely Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, first enumerates three possible positions regarding human rational souls (al-nufus al-natiqah): (1) they are necessary of existence in themselves (i.e., part of the Necessary Existent), (2) they are not necessary but are eternal (as the philosophers claim for celestial intellects and souls), or (3) they are contingent (mumkin) and temporally originated (muhdath zamanan). He then dismisses the first position by citing proofs that only the One is necessary of existence. The second position he says has already been discussed. He then takes up the third position: since the souls are contingent, they require an agent (mu'aththir) and originator (mujid). That agent must either be a body (mutaḥayyiz), an accident in a body, or neither a body nor an accident. He argues that it cannot be a body, providing five reasons: (1) Bodies are similar in quiddity; if one were a cause, all would be, which is false. (2) A single thing cannot be both receptive and agent; bodies are receptive, so they cannot be agents. (3) Bodies are weaker than immaterial substances; the weaker cannot cause the stronger. (4) Souls are nobler (knowing, powerful) than bodies; the nobler cannot be an effect of the baser. (5) Bodies only act via spatial position (wad'), influencing near things first; this requires position, but the agent of souls is not spatial. The fifth proof includes an argument that if bodies acted without position, they would affect near and far equally, which is not observed. The text cuts off mid-sentence, so the conclusion is incomplete, but the argument clearly denies that the agent is a body.

Five Proofs Against a Corporeal Agent
Al-Razi provides five proofs that the agent cannot be a body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the five arguments Al-Razi presents to refute the possibility that the agent of human souls is a material body.

Core Idea
A body cannot be the agent of souls because bodies are similar, receptive, weaker, baser, and spatially limited.

Al-Razi now presents five specific reasons why the agent—which must exist to bring human souls into being—cannot be a body. These proofs draw on principles of quiddity, receptivity, metaphysical strength, nobility, and spatial causation. The fifth proof cuts off, but the argument is clear up to that point.

Guided Path
1
Examine the first proof: similarity of bodies
Read how Al-Razi argues that all bodies are similar in quiddity, so if one caused souls, all would, which is false.
This relies on the principle that things identical in essence have identical causal powers.
2
Examine the second proof: mutual exclusivity of reception and agency
Note the philosophical principle that one thing cannot be both receptive and agent; bodies are receptive, so they cannot be agents.
This is a common argument in Islamic philosophy.
3
Examine the third proof: strength hierarchy
Understand that bodies are weaker than immaterial substances because they need space; the weaker cannot cause the stronger.
4
Examine the fourth proof: nobility asymmetry
Souls are nobler (knowledge, power) than bodies, so the nobler cannot be an effect of the baser.
5
Examine the fifth proof: spatial causation
Read why bodies act only through spatial proximity (wad'), which would not apply to the agent of souls. Note that the text cuts off mid-proof.
The proof is incomplete, but the point is clear: the agent must be free from spatial limitations.

The Active Intellect as Cause of Human Souls: Proof and Refutation
Al-Razi states the proof that the cause of human souls is the active intellect, then refutes its basis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's presentation of the proof that the active intellect is the cause of human souls and his critique of its foundational principle.

Core Idea
Al-Razi offers a proof based on 'from one only one', then declares it weak.

Al-Razi presents a philosophical proof that the cause of human souls is the active intellect, but he does not endorse it. Instead, he immediately points out the weakness of its foundation—the principle 'from one only one proceeds'. This lesson examines the proof and its refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its English translation to get the overall argument.
Pay attention to the phrase 'وقد عرفت ضعفه وفساده'.
2
Identify the proof and its premise
The proof concludes that the active intellect is the cause of human souls. Notice that it is based on the principle that from one only one proceeds.
Al-Razi calls this method a 'ṭarīqa' and says it is built on that principle.
3
Note al-Razi's critique
Al-Razi explicitly states that the foundation is weak and false, thus rejecting the proof. Then he shows a hypothetical consequence if it were accepted.
This shows al-Razi's dialectical style—he presents an argument even if he disagrees.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof for the identity of the cause of human souls, then immediately critiques its foundation. Al-Razi begins by stating the conclusion: after rejecting the possibility of more than one proceeding from one, it is established that the originator of these souls is only the last intellect (al-ʿaql al-ākhir), which governs the sublunar realm and is called the active intellect (al-ʿaql al-faʿʿāl). He then clarifies that this is the full articulation of this method (ṭarīqa). The method is built on the principle 'from one only one proceeds' (al-wāḥid lā yaṣduru ʿanhu illā al-wāḥid). But al-Razi immediately undermines it: 'You have known its weakness and corruption.' Thus, the proof is not sound. Nevertheless, he continues dialectically: if this principle were accepted, then it would be established that it is not impossible for the souls governing the celestial bodies and planetary bodies to be the effective agents in the existence of these rational souls. The argument is presented as a hypothetical consequence of a flawed premise.
