Second Objection: Specification by the Receptacle
Razi argues that the receptacle can specify the universal intention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi argues that the receptacle's nature can specify a universal intention into a particular effect, using the example of the celestial sphere's motion.

Core Idea
The receptacle, not just the agent, can determine the specific effect of a universal intention.

Razi's second objection concedes that the agent's intention may be universal and shared, but raises a crucial point: why can't the receptacle itself be the source of specification? This lesson unpacks his reasoning through a vivid astronomical analogy and shows how this aligns with the philosophers' own doctrine of emanation.

Walk through the example of the celestial sphere's motion: when it reaches a point, it cannot stop, go back, or move sideways; it must continue in its cycle. This shows that the sphere's nature only accepts one specific motion.

Connect to the philosophers' doctrine

This strengthens the objection by showing it aligns with the opponent's own views.

Consider the common misunderstanding that this objection denies universal causation. Actually, it affirms that universal intention exists but is specified by the receptacle.
This clarifies the precise point of the objection.

Third Objection: Circularity in Intention and Knowledge
Razi's third objection uncovers a logical circularity in intending particulars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi's third objection reveals a logical circularity that undermines the argument that divine intention directly causes particular motions.

Core Idea
Intention and knowledge cannot both be prior; that would create a vicious circle.

This lesson examines Razi's third objection to the claim that divine intention causes particular motions. He reveals a logical paradox: intending a specific motion requires knowing it, but knowing it as that motion requires the motion's existence. If intention causes the motion, a cycle results. We will see why this circularity is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the literal translation. Notice the concise logical structure.
Pay attention to the terms 'condition' (مشروط) and 'circularity' (دور).
2
Identify the two conditional claims
The passage states two conditions: (1) intention requires knowledge, (2) knowledge requires the actual existence of the thing. Find these in the text.
The second condition is about knowledge of a specific motion requiring that motion to be actual.
3
Trace the circularity
If intention causes the motion, then motion depends on intention, intention depends on knowledge, and knowledge depends on motion. This forms a circle. See how the passage describes this.
Ask yourself: why can't this circle be broken?
4
Understand why circularity is impossible
In logic, circular reasoning (دور) is invalid because it makes a thing depend on itself. The passage explicitly states 'وهو محال' (it is impossible).
This is a standard logical principle in kalam and philosophy.
5
Connect to the broader argument
The passage concludes that the three objections together weaken the first argument. Reflect on how this third objection fits with the earlier ones (not provided here).
Consider the cumulative force of the objections.

Refuting the Persistence of Universal Intention
Razi refutes the claim that universal intention remains and answers the objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a universal intention cannot cause a specific motion, and how Razi refutes the objection that universal intention remains.

Core Idea
A universal intention cannot specify which particular motion occurs.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi tackles a key objection to the second argument against causality. The objection claims that a universal intention remains constant and can cause successive motions. Razi argues that such a universal intention cannot explain why one specific motion occurs rather than another. He systematically dismantles the objection by showing that an absolute intention is equally related to all possibilities, so it cannot be the cause of any particular one.

1
Understand the objection

This sets up the entire discussion.
2
Examine Ibn Sina's suggestion
Notice the quote from the Sheikh (Ibn Sina) proposing successive universal volitions and conceptualizations as the principle of motion. Understand why this might seem plausible.
3
Follow Razi's dilemma
Razi presents two possibilities: either the agent wills a specific transition (particular volition) or an absolute transition (universal volition). Determine which one leads to the desired conclusion.
This is the core logical move.
4
Analyze the absolute intention case
Study the argument that an absolute intention is equally related to all motions, using the examples of transitions between zodiac signs and directions. See why it cannot cause any specific motion.
The example with Aries, Taurus, north, south illustrates the point.
5
Conclude the refutation
Razi concludes that the objection is refuted because the same three questions that defeated the first argument apply here. Recognize that the second argument is thereby weakened.

Commentary
This passage classifies the views on celestial motions. After discussing natural motions, the author turns to the second division (al-qism al-thānī): voluntary motions (ḥarakāt irādiyya). Those who hold this view split into two groups. The first group denies that the heavens have any determinate purpose (gharaḍ mu'ayyan) in choosing their motions. Their argument rests on the nature of celestial bodies: they are simple (basā'iṭ) and all points in them are similar in reality and equal in quiddity. Hence, any motion on any of the infinitely many possible orbits is equally possible. If some were possible and others not, then identical things would differ in their properties, which is absurd. Therefore, all possible motions are equally possible. Since the agent (the celestial soul) is a free chooser (fā'il mukhtār), it can select any one of these equally possible motions without any cause, just as a hungry person may choose between two identical loaves without a preponderant reason. This argument denies the need for an external determinant for celestial motion, relying on the homogeneity of the celestial substance.
