The Fourth Argument: Imagining a Sphere
Imagining a sphere requires a corporeal substrate because an image cannot exist in a non-spatial entity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that imagining a sphere proves the corporeality of internal perception because an image with spatial properties cannot exist in a non-spatial entity.

Core Idea
Imagining a sphere requires a spatial substrate because images have position and magnitude.

This lesson presents the fourth argument for the corporeality of internal perception. The author argues that when we imagine a sphere, its image must be impressed in the perceiver. However, an image with spatial properties cannot exist in an immaterial substance that lacks position and magnitude. Therefore, the imagination itself must be corporeal.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Notice the concise logical structure: premise, principle, impossibility, conclusion.
The Arabic uses 'الرابع' to indicate this is the fourth argument.
2
Identify the two premises
The first premise is that imagining a sphere requires an image of the sphere to be present in the perceiver. The second is the general principle that perception consists of an image equal to the perceived object.
The principle 'الإدراك صورة مساوية للمدرك' is key.
3
Understand the impossibility and conclusion
The image of a sphere has position and magnitude, so it cannot be impressed in something without these. Therefore, the imagining faculty must have position and magnitude, i.e., be corporeal.
This argument shows that the internal sense (imagination) is not purely spiritual.

Two Proofs that Imagination is Corporeal
Two arguments that imagination requires a corporeal substratum: distinction of similar images and variation in magnitude.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two rational proofs that imagination requires a corporeal substrate, based on the distinction of similar images and variation in imaginal magnitudes.

Core Idea
Imagination must be corporeal because we can distinguish identical images and images vary in size.

In this passage, the author presents two arguments that the faculty of imagination is not purely spiritual but requires a physical substrate. The first argument uses the mental distinction of two identical squares, showing they must occupy separate loci. The second argument notes that imaginary images vary in size, which must correspond to the size of the part of the imagination they occupy. Both point to a corporeal imaging faculty.

Guided Path
1
Read the first proof carefully
Read the Arabic text of the first proof and its translation. Notice how the argument unfolds from the distinction of two imaginary squares.
2
Analyze the first proof's logic
Identify the key steps: we distinguish identical squares, they must exist mentally, if they were in one locus they would be indistinguishable, so they must be in separate loci, implying the locus is corporeal.
This is a reductio ad absurdum argument.
3
Read the second proof
Read the second proof about size variation. Contrast the imagination of a sea and an atom.
4
Analyze the second proof
Understand why the size variation cannot be from the external object (since we can imagine non-existent things) and must be from the size of the part of imagination that receives the image.
This demonstrates that imagination has parts of different sizes.
5
Synthesize both proofs
Reflect on how both proofs converge on the same conclusion: imagination is corporeal. Consider why this matters for a theological understanding of the soul.
These arguments are foundational for discussing the nature of the human soul and its faculties.

This passage is a classification of the ranks of human souls with respect to knowledge and sciences. The Sixth Proof: Angels vs. Animals on True Happiness
Razi argues by analogy with angels who have superior happiness without sensory pleasures, proving true happiness is not sensory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's sixth proof that true happiness is not sensory pleasures, using the analogy of angels and animals and the objection about divine imitation.

Core Idea
If sensory pleasures were happiness, animals would be better than angels—absurd!

Razi presents a powerful argument by comparison. He observes that angels, despite lacking sensory pleasures, are superior to animals that enjoy them. This shows that true happiness must be something beyond mere physical sensations. The argument also addresses an objection about divine perfection being unattainable for creatures, using the concept of imitating God's attributes. Let's follow the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the comparison
Notice that Razi starts by stating a necessary truth: angels' happiness is more complete and noble than that of base animals like donkeys and insects. This is the foundation of the argument.
This knowledge is considered 'necessary' (بديهي) by Razi.
2
Identify the logical consequence
Angels lack sensory pleasures (eating, drinking, intercourse), while base animals have them. If ultimate happiness were only these pleasures, then animals would be higher than angels. Since that is false, the premise must be false.
This is a reductio ad absurdum argument.
3
Consider the stronger point
Razi adds that God's perfection is incomparably greater, yet He is free from these base pleasures. This reinforces that perfection can exist without sensory pleasures.
4
Examine the objection and reply
An objector might say God's perfection is due to divinity, which is impossible for creatures. Razi replies that the Prophet said 'Adopt the character traits of God' and philosophers spoke of imitation of the divine—meaning reducing needs and doing good, not indulging in pleasures.
This shows that creatures can attain a form of perfection without sensory pleasures.

The Fourth Proof: Geometric Proportion and Life
Geometrical proportion argument for celestial life.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the fourth proof uses geometric proportion and intuitive moral reasoning to argue that celestial bodies must possess life.

Core Idea
The largest part of creation should have the best attribute: life.

This lesson examines the fourth proof for the life of celestial bodies, which draws on a striking geometrical observation: the four elements are to the celestial spheres as a point is to a great circle—negligible in size. The author then appeals to reason's intuitive judgment that life is superior to inanimateness, concluding that the vast celestial realm, being the greatest part of creation, must be alive.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth proof (al-hujja al-rabi'a) for the life of celestial bodies. It begins with a geometrical observation: the four elements (earth, water, air, fire) are to the totality of the celestial spheres as the center is to the great circle—i.e., negligible in size. The intuition of reason (badihah al-‘aql) tells us that life is superior to inanimateness (al-jamadiyya). If the celestial bodies had no life, then most of God's creation would be deficient and lacking in the effects of His mercy and wisdom, which is improbable. Moreover, celestial bodies far surpass earthly bodies in permanence, purity, and clarity, while life and intellect far surpass inanimateness and death in honor and virtue. Hence, to attribute noble qualities to base bodies and deny them to noble bodies would contradict mercy and wisdom. Therefore, celestial bodies must be alive. The argument uses a combination of quantitative proportion (the heavens are the largest part) and qualitative comparison (the best attributes should belong to the best substances).
