Third Proof: Heart's Earned Recompense
Proof from three Quranic verses that recompense depends on heart's effort.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quran establishes that reward and punishment depend on what the heart earns, not merely outward actions.

Core Idea
The heart is the real seat of moral accountability.

In this proof, Imam al-Sanusi brings together three Quranic verses to show that Allah's recompense is tied to the heart's intention. The first verse distinguishes between careless oaths and those that involve the heart's earning. The second explains that it is piety from the heart, not physical offerings, that reaches Allah. The third identifies the heart as the locus of piety. Together, they ground the doctrine that spiritual responsibility is centered on inner conviction.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text slowly, noting the three verses cited. Notice that the author first states a thesis, then supports it with verses.
The thesis: 'that the deserving of recompense is only for what is in the heart of effort.'
2
Examine the first verse (al-Baqarah 2:225)
Consider the distinction between unintentional oaths and oaths that involve the heart's earning. What does 'earned by hearts' imply?
This verse directly links accountability to the heart's intent.
3
Examine the second verse (al-Hajj 22:37)
Reflect on how the verse contrasts physical offerings with piety (taqwa) from the heart. Why is this significant?
Piety is an inner quality that originates in the heart.
4
Connect to the third verse (al-Hujurat 49:3)
This verse explicitly states that Allah tests hearts for piety. How does this confirm the previous point?
It shows the heart is the locus of piety.
5
Synthesize the argument
Bring the three verses together. How do they collectively prove that reward depends on what the heart earns?
The heart is both the agent of earning and the place of piety.

The third proof: The verses indicating that the deserving of recompense is only for what is in the heart of effort. Allah most high said: “Allah will not hold you accountable for what is unintentional in your oaths, but He holds you accountable for what your hearts have earned.” And He said: “Neither their flesh nor their blood reaches Allah, but what reaches Him is the piety from you.” Then He explained in another verse what the locus of piety is, and said: “Those are the ones whose hearts Allah has tested for piety.”

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof that moral responsibility centers on the heart. It cites two Quranic verses: Surah al-Baqarah 2:225, which distinguishes between unintentional oaths and those earned by the heart, and Surah al-Hajj 22:37, which states that piety (taqwa) from the heart reaches Allah, not the physical meat or blood. It then refers to another verse (Surah al-Hujurat 49:3) where Allah describes those whose hearts He has tested for piety. The argument is that since reward and punishment are based on what the heart 'earns' and on piety, the heart is the true locus of moral accountability.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That outward rituals alone guarantee reward and that unintentional actions are equally accountable.
Correction: The verses show that accountability is only for what the heart intentionally earns, and that physical offerings do not reach Allah; only the piety from the heart does.

Key Takeaway
The Quran teaches that reward and punishment are based on what the heart intentionally earns and the piety it contains, not on outward actions alone.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?

Faith in the Heart: Quranic Proof
Proof from Quran that faith is attributed to the heart in numerous verses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the Quran consistently attributes faith (iman) to the heart, not to outward actions or speech alone, as a proof that true faith is a matter of the heart.

Core Idea
In the Quran, faith belongs to the heart, not the tongue or limbs.

This lesson examines the eighth proof from the Quran: whenever Allah mentions faith, He attaches it to the heart. Through three clear verses, the author shows that genuine iman is an inner reality, not a mere outward display. This argument cuts against those who reduce faith to verbal confession or ritual compliance.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the translation. Notice the concise structure: a claim followed by three Quranic citations.
The passage is short but packed with evidence.
2
Identify the three Quranic examples
List the three verses cited. For each, note which aspect of the heart's role in faith is highlighted.
The verses cover hypocrites, forced speech, and divine inscription.
3
Reflect on the significance
Consider why attributing faith to the heart is important. How does it counter the view that faith is merely verbal or external?
This proof emphasizes inner conviction over outer compliance.

الحجة الثامنة : إنه تعالى كلما ذكر الإيمان في القرآن ، أضافه إلى القلب كقوله تعالى : ( من الذين قالوا : آمنا بأفواههم ، ولم تؤمن قلوبهم ) وقال: ( إلا من أكره ، وقلبه مطمئن بالإيمان ) وقال: ( كتب في قلوبهم الإيمان )
The eighth proof: He, exalted be He, whenever He mentions faith in the Quran, He attributes it to the heart. Like His saying most high: “Among those who say, ‘We believe with their mouths,’ but their hearts do not believe.” And He said: “Except for one who is forced while his heart is at peace with faith.” And He said: “He has written faith in their hearts.”
Source pages p. 172

Commentary
This passage presents the eighth proof, arguing that in every Quranic mention of faith (iman), it is attributed to the heart. The author cites three examples: Surah al-Ma'idah 5:41, where hypocrites profess faith with their mouths but their hearts do not believe; Surah an-Nahl 16:106, where one forced to disbelieve is excused if his heart remains firm in faith; and Surah al-Mujadila 58:22 or similar, where Allah describes writing faith in the hearts of believers. These verses consistently place faith in the heart, not in outward actions alone, emphasizing the heart's centrality in true belief.

Key Takeaway

?
