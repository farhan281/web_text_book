The Third Argument: Dividing the Intellectual Form
Al-Razi states the third argument about dividing the form into generic and specific parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure of the third argument for the immateriality of the intellect, based on dividing the form into generic and specific parts.

Core Idea
The third argument claims that if the form were bodily, its division would lead to impossible consequences.

Al-Razi presents what he calls the 'third argument' for the immateriality of the intellect, attributed to the Shaykh (Avicenna). The argument considers a scenario where the intellectual form is composed of generic and specific parts that could be divided. Understanding this argument is crucial before we see how Al-Razi dismantles it.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument scenario
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation to grasp the scenario of dividing the form.
Notice the technical terms 'generic part' and 'specific part'.
2
Identify the generic and specific parts
The argument posits that the generic part and specific part are in different halves. Understand what these terms mean in the context of the intellect's form.
3
Notice the conclusion of impossibility
The argument concludes that if the form were bodily, dividing it would lead to an impossibility, implying the form must be immaterial.
This is the core of the argument.

Third: We posit that the generic part occurs in one half, and the specific part occurs in the second half. If we change the division, the generic part must transfer from its place to the second place resulting from the second purpose of the division [and also] if we place a division within a division like this, then it is necessary that the genus be divided, even if the differentia is not divided. That is impossible. So this is the full exposition of this argument as mentioned by the Shaykh. Know that this argument is extremely weak for several reasons: First: It is based on the view that knowledge of a thing is the acquisition of a form that is equal to the quiddity of the known in the essence of the knower. And we have already shown in the beginning of the Logic of this book with conclusive proofs the corruption of this doctrine. Second: We say: Suppose we grant you this principle. Then why is it not permissible that that form [consists of] two parts similar in quiddity? – his statement: 'On this assumption that form would be a non-abstract form' – we say: The discussion has two aspects: First: That this is also necessary for you. That is because this form, if it inheres in the substance of the rational soul, is a particular form in a particular soul, and it is accompanied by the other accidents that inhere in that particular soul. So if we consider that form together with all these accompaniments, it is not an abstract form, but is conjoined with many accompaniments. And that prevents it from being universal. Then if they say: What is meant by its being universal is that that form, when we remove those accompaniments and consider it in itself without those accompaniments, is universal. We say: If this is permissible, then why is it not permissible to say: This form, even if it inheres in a particular material body with a particular amount and a particular shape, yet if it is considered in itself, with the removal of those accretions, it is universal. So it is established that the objection raised by the Shaykh applies equally to himself. And the second aspect in explaining the weakness of this proof: This amount, if it is sound, suffices independently to achieve the desired end, without mentioning these divisions. For the intellectual form, whether it divides by the division of its locus or does not divide, and whether its division is into two parts similar in quiddity or different in them, it is correct to say: If it were in a body, it would not be abstract. But it is abstract, so it must not be bodily. So this statement suffices to achieve this desired end if it is sound. And then there is no need to mention the divisions that he mentioned. So it is established that this argument is extremely unsound. And God knows best.

Commentary
This passage presents and refutes what the author calls the 'third argument' for the immateriality of the intellect. The argument, attributed to 'the Shaykh' (likely Avicenna), posits that if the intellectual form were bodily, it would be subject to division; the author gives a specific scenario with generic and specific parts leading to an impossibility. The author then declares the argument extremely weak for two main reasons. First, the argument presupposes a particular definition of knowledge as the acquisition of a form that equals the quiddity of the known, which the author claims to have already refuted in the Logic section of his book. Second, even if that definition were accepted, the argument still fails. The author elaborates a reply: the opponent's objection that the form is not abstract because it is particular in the soul can be turned against them, since if we consider the form in itself, it is universal, regardless of its locus. The second aspect of the weakness is that even the core idea—that a form, if bodily, would not be abstract—suffices to prove the point without the complex divisions, rendering the argument superfluous. The passage ends by concluding that the argument is utterly unsound, with 'And God knows best'.

First Refutation: The Flawed Definition of Knowledge
Al-Razi gives his first reason: the argument's assumption about knowledge is false.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Razi's first objection to the third argument: that it relies on an unproven definition of knowledge.

Core Idea
The third argument fails because it assumes knowledge is a form that matches the quiddity of the known.

Having presented the third argument, Al-Razi now begins its refutation. He first attacks the very foundation of the argument: its definition of knowledge. He claims to have already shown this definition to be false in the Logic section of his book. This lesson examines that first reason.

Guided Path
1
Read the first reason
Read the Arabic and translation for the first reason. It states the argument's assumption about knowledge.
This is a direct critique of the epistemological basis.
2
Understand the assumed definition of knowledge
The argument defines knowledge as the acquisition of a form equal to the quiddity of the known in the knower's essence. Grasp this definition.
3
Recall Al-Razi's prior refutation
Al-Razi claims to have refuted this definition already. This is a rhetorical move to undermine the argument without repeating himself.
The refutation is not repeated here.

Second Refutation: Turning the Argument Against Itself
Al-Razi presents two aspects of his second refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Razi's second refutation, which shows that the third argument's objection applies equally to its own position and that the complex divisions are unnecessary.

Core Idea
The third argument's own reasoning can be turned against it, and its detailed divisions are superfluous.

Al-Razi's second reason for the weakness of the third argument is twofold. First, he shows that the objection about the form being non-abstract due to its particularity applies equally to the opponent's view. Second, he argues that even the core idea—that a bodily form would not be abstract—suffices to prove the point, making the complex divisions unnecessary.

Guided Path
1
Understand the first aspect: the objection boomerangs
Read the first aspect where Al-Razi argues that the same problem of particularity applies to the opponent's view if we consider the form in itself.
This is a classic tu quoque argument.
2
See the second aspect: the argument is superfluous
Al-Razi points out that the core idea—if bodily then not abstract—already proves the point without the division scenario.
The divisions are unnecessary.
3
Conclude the total defeat of the argument
Al-Razi ends by declaring the argument extremely unsound. Understand why both aspects together destroy the argument.

Since an abstract substance is not a body, it cannot be the locus of multiple distinct imaginary forms. Therefore, the imaginary form cannot inhere in an abstract substance at all. The Rational Proof that the Soul is Distinct from the Body
Author logically deduces that the soul is distinct from the body using two premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a simple two-premise argument from attribution of ownership logically proves that the soul is distinct from the body.

Core Idea
Ownership attribution implies otherness: the soul is not the body.

This lesson presents a concise rational proof for the distinction between soul and body. The author builds on the concept of ownership attribution (إضافة الملكية) found in the Quran, tradition, and rational intuition. By analyzing two premises, he demonstrates that the entity to which all body parts are attributed must be other than those parts themselves. This is a foundational argument in Islamic philosophical theology.

Guided Path
1
Contextualize the 'third station'
This passage is the third of several stations (maqamat) in the author's broader discussion. Understand that he is now providing a logical proof for the soul's distinctness.
The first two stations are not provided here, but this argument stands on its own.
2
Identify the first premise
The author states that attributing body parts to the self (as in 'my hand') is valid by Quran, tradition, and immediate rational intuition. Recognize that this premise establishes a factual relationship.
The term 'إضافة الملكية' means attribution of ownership.
3
Identify the second premise
The second premise is that attributing a thing to itself is false by immediate intuition. For example, you cannot say 'my hand is mine' in an ownership sense because that would be a tautology.
This premise is a rational axiom, not a linguistic claim.
4
Combine premises to reach the conclusion
From the two premises, it necessarily follows that the entity to which all body parts are owned (the 'I') must be distinct from those parts. Therefore, the soul (self) is distinct from the body and all its organs.
The conclusion is logically entailed, not merely plausible.

The First Proof: Stability of Perception Despite Changing Temperament

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first proof that the soul's essence is distinct from the body's temperament, based on the observation that perception and character remain constant despite radical changes in physical temperament.

Core Idea
Perception stays the same when temperament changes; therefore it depends on the soul, not the body.

Sanusi begins the proofs for the difference of soul substances with a powerful observation from everyday experience. A person can undergo dramatic shifts in physical temperament—from hot to cold, from wet to dry—yet their ability to perceive and their core character remain unchanged. If these were caused by the body's mixture, they would vary with it. Since they do not, the cause must be something deeper: the specific essence of each soul. Let's read this argument closely.
