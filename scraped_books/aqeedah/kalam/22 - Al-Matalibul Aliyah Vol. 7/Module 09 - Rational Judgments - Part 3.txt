Third Objection: Composition Does Not Imply Corporeality
The third objection refutes the tamathul argument by analogizing composition of genus and difference in colors to composition in souls.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the third objection refutes the argument for tamathul by showing that composition through genus and difference does not necessitate corporeality.

Core Idea
Composition of genus and difference does not make something a body.

The third objection to the tamathul argument acknowledges that the original reasoning leads to composition in the soul's essence. However, it challenges the assumption that all composition implies corporeality. By drawing an analogy with colors, the objection demonstrates that logical composition (genus + difference) can exist in non-physical entities. Let's examine this objection closely.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection passage
Read the Arabic text and its translation to grasp the flow of the argument.
2
Identify the concession and the counterpoint
Notice that the objection first concedes (for argument's sake) that composition follows, but then denies that this entails corporeality.
3
Analyze the analogy of blackness and whiteness
See how blackness and whiteness share the genus 'color' and differ by specific differences, yet they are not bodies. This supports the claim that composition does not equal bodyhood.
4
Apply the analogy to the soul
The objection concludes that, similarly, even if the soul's essence had composition (from genus and difference), it would not become a body.
5
Understand the conclusion
The passage ends by declaring the original argument weak and invalid. Make sure you see why.

Commentary
This passage is the third objection (السؤال الثالث) to the tamathul argument. The objection grants, for the sake of argument, that the pro-tamathul reasoning would lead to composition (tarkīb) in the soul's essence. But it then argues that such composition does not necessarily entail corporeality (jismiyya). Similarly, even if souls were composed of genus and difference, they would not be bodies. Therefore, the original argument's claim that composition is impossible for incorporeal substances is refuted, because composition of genus and difference does not imply composition that requires extension or corporeality. Thus the argument for tamathul is shown to be weak and invalid.

First Proof: Constancy of Perception Despite Temperament Change
First argument that soul essences differ, based on invariant perception despite temperamental change.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first argument that soul essences differ, based on the observation that perception and character remain constant despite changes in bodily temperament.

Core Idea
Constant perception despite changing temperament proves souls differ by essence.

This proof begins with a simple observation: a person's temperament can shift dramatically from hot to cold, wet to dry, yet their basic mental and moral faculties remain unchanged. The author then uses a conditional reasoning: if temperament were the cause, a change in temperament would cause a change in perception and character. Since no such change occurs, the cause must be the soul's own essence. This logical move establishes one of the key foundations for the doctrine of the immaterial soul.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation. Note the examples of temperament change and the constancy of perception and character.
The passage uses two concrete examples involving choleric and phlegmatic temperaments.
2
Identify the logical structure
Recognize the conditional (if...then) reasoning: if temperament causes differences in perception/character, then changing temperament should change them. The author observes that this consequent does not happen.
3
Examine the examples
Consider the person with choleric temperament who gets phlegmatic fever, and the phlegmatic person with hot delirium. In both cases, their original perception and character remain.
These examples show that even major temperamental shifts do not alter core faculties.
4
Understand the conclusion
Since the observed data contradicts the conditional's prediction, the antecedent (temperament as cause) is false. The author concludes that the difference must be due to the soul's own essence.
This is a classic modus tollens argument.
5
Reflect on implications
Think about what this means for the soul's independence from the body. If the soul's essence were tied to temperament, such changes would cause different souls, but they don't.

The Conditional Proof for the Soul's Survival
Al-Razi presents a conditional argument for the soul's survival based on universal human perception of pleasure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's modus tollens argument for the survival of the soul, based on universal human perception of pleasure and its implications.

Core Idea
Universal human consensus that minimizing pleasure is perfection proves the soul's survival.

Al-Razi constructs a powerful logical argument for the soul's survival that starts not from revelation but from a universal human observation. He notices that people universally respect those who abstain from worldly pleasures, indicating that minimizing such pleasures is seen as perfection. This observation becomes the foundation for a conditional argument that, if valid, forces the conclusion that the soul must survive death. In this lesson, we will unpack the steps of his argument and see how it works.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully

Identify the conditional structure

The Sixth Proof: Induction from Lifespan
Sanusi presents the sixth proof using induction from lifespans.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi uses induction (istiqra') from human and animal lifespans to argue for the soul's permanence.

Core Idea
Induction from lifespan shows that perfecting the soul preserves it.

This lesson presents the sixth proof from Sanusi's arguments for the soul's permanence. It is based on empirical observation: people who revere God and are compassionate tend to live long, while the violent die young. Sanusi extends this to animals and draws a conclusion about the soul itself. Let's examine the passage carefully.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the literal translation. Let the argument's structure emerge naturally.
Pay attention to the flow: observation, extension, definition, conclusion.
2
Identify the two types of people and animals
Notice the contrast between those who magnify God's command and show compassion, and those who kill and harm. Similarly for harmless vs. harmful animals.
This establishes the inductive pattern.
3
Understand the terms: theoretical and practical faculties
Sanusi defines magnifying God's command as perfecting the theoretical faculty, and compassion as perfecting the practical faculty. These are key to the argument.
The theoretical faculty deals with knowledge, the practical with action.
4
Follow the analogy: preservation of body implies preservation of soul
Since perfecting these faculties preserves the body, and the soul is the locus of these faculties, it is more fitting (awlā) that perfecting them preserves the soul itself.
This is an argument from analogy (qiyās awlā).
5
Reflect on the strength of induction as a proof
Consider the inductive generalization (dominant induction) and how it leads to a rational conclusion. What are its strengths and limitations?
Sanusi uses empirical observation as a starting point for rational inference.

The Seventh Proof: Three Ranks and Permanence of Spiritual Things
Sanusi classifies human states and argues for the soul's permanence via comparative longevity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's classification of human states into three ranks and how he uses the relative permanence of spiritual things to argue for the soul's permanence.

Core Idea
Spiritual things outlast physical things, so the soul must be permanent.

This passage presents the seventh proof for the soul's permanence. Sanusi begins by categorizing human states into three types, then argues that whatever is connected to the spiritual realm enjoys greater permanence. The soul, as a spiritual substance and the locus of divine knowledge, is most deserving of permanence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the three ranks
Identify the three categories of human states: purely physical, mixed, and purely psychic. See the examples given for each.
The lowest rank includes buildings for good deeds; the middle includes speech; the highest includes true knowledge.
2
Note the argument from permanence
Observe how Sanusi compares the longevity of physical things connected to spirituality (like mosques and scriptures) with ordinary physical things.
He uses the examples of the Sacred Mosque and the Aqsa Mosque, and the revealed books.
3
Apply the reasoning to the soul
See how the soul, being purely spiritual and the receptacle of the greatest elixir (knowledge of God), must be permanent.
The argument is a fortiori: if attached physical things are long-lasting, the soul itself is even more so.
4
Reflect on the examples
Consider the examples of the Sacred Mosque and the Aqsa Mosque, and the revealed books, to see the pattern of relative permanence.
These examples are not meant to be eternal but to show exceptional longevity compared to other physical things.

The Soul's Incorruptibility: A Regress Argument
The author proves the soul's incorruptibility via a regress argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument that proves the soul is incorruptible, specifically how composition leads to an infinite regress or circularity.

Core Idea
The soul's simplicity saves it from the infinite regress of matter.

This passage presents a rigorous philosophical proof demonstrating that the human soul cannot undergo corruption (fasad). The argument hinges on the nature of corruption itself: it requires something that undergoes change, and that something must have parts. By analyzing what it means to be corruptible, the author shows that if the soul were corruptible, it would entail an infinite regress or circularity—both impossible. Therefore, the soul must be a simple, incorruptible substance.

1
Read the passage and translation

The conclusion follows from the impossibility of regress; the soul must be a simple substance.

The author uses this as a test case: if the tongue can taste, then the supposed speciality of other organs is not unique; if it cannot, then the tongue is like other organs.
