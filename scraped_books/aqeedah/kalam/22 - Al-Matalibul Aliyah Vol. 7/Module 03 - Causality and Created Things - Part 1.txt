Definition and Division of Spirits Attached to Bodies
Author defines spirits attached to bodies and divides them into celestial and elemental directors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of spirits attached to bodies and their two main categories: celestial directors and elemental directors.

Core Idea
Spirits attached to bodies are immaterial directors of celestial and elemental realms.

In this lesson, we examine the second kind of non-spatial beings: spirits that are immaterial but manage bodies. The author clarifies their nature and then divides them into two fundamental types based on the bodies they direct.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Let the author's own definitions come through.
Notice the careful phrasing: 'مجردة في ذواتها عن الجسمية والحلول في الجسمية'.
2
Identify the definition
Look for the description of these spirits: they are substances that are immaterial in essence but attached to bodies for direction and management.
They are not bodies nor do they inhere in bodies, but they have a relation of management.
3
Understand the division
The author divides these spirits into two: those directing celestial bodies and those directing elemental bodies. Note the distinction.
Celestial bodies refer to the spheres and stars; elemental bodies refer to earthly matter.

Argument by Analogy for Souls Administering the Sublunar Sphere
Analogical argument for spirits managing sublunar realm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the analogy of innate heat in the heart is used to argue for the existence of souls governing the sublunar realm.

Core Idea
If tiny heart heat needs a soul, vast lunar heat must need souls too.

This passage presents a fascinating analogical argument from Islamic theology. It compares the innate heat in the human heart, which requires a soul to manage it, to the subtle heat pervading the region under the moon. The author argues that just as the heart's heat is not without a soul, the greater heat of the sublunar sphere must also be administered by spirits. We'll examine the logic and the additional support from observable phenomena.

Consider the additional arguments

This strengthens the plausibility of the claim.

Reflect on the theological significance

It shows that even physical processes may have spiritual agents.

An empirical observation is cited: mountains with snow produce worms that are like thin skins filled with cold water. Since spirits can attach to these worms, it is not far-fetched that they attach to those celestial spheres. Thus the author concludes that the existence of such spirits is plausible and the matter is detailed here.

Commentary
This passage is both a proof for the existence of celestial souls and a classification of separate entities. The author begins by stating that certain spiritual beings—angels of provisions and war—are affirmed by the Prophet and by talisman practitioners, so their existence must be investigated. He then presents a proof by analogy: just as the small innate heat in the heart cannot exist without a soul that manages it, the subtle heat under the lunar sphere cannot be devoid of souls that manage it. This analogy argues that celestial heat—the principle of motion in the sublunary region—requires directing souls, i.e., celestial souls. Next, he reports an additional claim: it is not impossible for many spirits to be attached to the celestial spheres, using the example of mountains producing worms from snow. This suggests that even inanimate or natural phenomena can have spiritual attachments. Finally, he summarizes the hierarchy of separate entities according to later philosophers like al-Farabi and Ibn Sina: the Necessary Existent, intellects, celestial souls, and human souls. He notes that these philosophers deny jinn and devils, whereas religious communities and talisman practitioners affirm them. The passage thus establishes a rational proof for celestial souls while distinguishing between philosophical and religious views on spiritual hierarchies.

Learning Objective

Void as an Abstract Substance
Author uses void to prove abstract substance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the concept of void (al-khala') is used to prove the existence of an abstract substance, and why void cannot be considered a body.

Core Idea
Void is not a body because place does not move.

This lesson examines the second proof from the author, which uses the idea of void—an abstract dimension—to argue for the existence of a non-material substance. The proof rests on the definition of body as something that moves between places, while void itself is a place and therefore immobile. Understanding this argument clarifies a key distinction in Islamic metaphysics between material bodies and abstract entities.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its English translation. Pay attention to the concise structure—the author presents a proof, defines a term, and draws a conclusion. Let the original wording shape your understanding.
Note that 'void' here is not empty space in the modern sense, but a metaphysical concept.
2
Understand the definition of body
The author defines a body as 'a substance that accepts movement from place to place.' This is crucial—body is defined by its capacity for locomotion, not by extension or solidity.
3
Apply the definition to void
Since void (al-khala') is itself a place (a dimension), it cannot move from one place to another—it is the very container of movement. Therefore, void does not fit the definition of body.
Think: if place moved, it would need another place to move into, leading to infinite regress.
4
Reflect on the infinite regress point
The author argues that if place could move, it would require another place ad infinitum—an impossibility. This strengthens the conclusion that void is not a body.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum argument in Islamic philosophy.
5
Conclude the argument
Since void is not a body and yet exists (as evidence shows), it must be an abstract substance (jawhar mujarrad). This is the second proof for the existence of non-material entities.
This conclusion supports the broader theological framework of the text.
