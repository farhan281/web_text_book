Refutation of the Philosophers' Claim about the Thinking Power
The author refutes the philosophers' claim about the thinking power with two proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the philosophers' notion of a separate thinking power is rationally untenable, and how the author identifies it with jinn.

Core Idea
The thinking power is not a human faculty but an independent spiritual entity—jinn—that acts against our will.

The philosophers object that the thinking power, which moves from one state to another, could be the agent of involuntary words. The author declares this false and offers two proofs. This passage demonstrates the rational method of refuting a philosophical objection through logical division and necessary knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and dismissal
Read the philosophers' objection and the author's immediate declaration that it is absolutely false. Notice the confident tone.
The author does not engage with the objection directly yet; he first asserts falsehood.
2
Study the first proof
The first proof argues that the human is a unified agent of all perceptions and actions, so thought cannot be a separate doer. Follow the reasoning that the human did not intend these utterances, so they cannot be from him.
This proof relies on the principle that a single body must have a single agent.
3
Study the second proof
The second proof uses a tripartite division: the thinking power is either the human, an attribute, or an independent agent. The first two are eliminated, leaving an independent agent.
Pay attention to the elimination of 'attribute' because an attribute cannot act against the human's will.
4
Follow the deduction about the independent agent
The independent agent must be knowing and powerful, and cannot be an accident (since accidents cannot perform difficult actions). Therefore it is a self-subsistent essence.
Necessary knowledge (العلم الضروري) is used to rule out accidents.
5
Understand the identification and conclusion
The author identifies this essence as jinn and devils, saying the disagreement is merely verbal: the philosophers call it thinking power, while we call it jinn.
This reconciles the terminology and ends the argument.

The philosophers said: 'Why is it not permissible that the doer of those words and those utterances is the thinking power (al-quwwah al-mufakkirah), which moves from attribute to attribute and from state to state?' And we say: This speech has been long in hearing by man, and for this reason it has become entrenched in their hearts. And it is absolutely false, and two aspects indicate it: The first: We have shown in previous articles that that which is described with all perceptions and which disposes with all kinds of dispositions in this body must be one thing, and it is the human being. And if it is like that, it is impossible to say that thought (al-fikr) does something other than the human being. For we have proven that the human being did not do these utterances because he does not intend them; rather, he dislikes them. So this speech is false. The second: This thing that you have called the thinking power: either it is the human being, or it is an attribute of his attributes and a state of his states, or it is an independent agent, having preceded the actions by his own choice. The first is false, for we have shown that the human being does not intend this state. The second is false, because an attribute subsisting in the essence of the human being cannot initiate an action contrary to the human being's will. And since these two divisions are false, it is established that the doer of those words is another doer, other than the human being and other than the totality of his attributes. So we say: That doer cannot bring about those words unless he is knowing and powerful. For if he is not knowing of the states of those words, intending to produce them is impossible because intending what is unknown is impossible. So that doer is knowing of the realities of things and powerful to produce those words. And it is something different from the essence of the human being and the totality of his attributes. Then that thing is either an accident inhering in a body or a self-subsistent substance. The first is false because necessary knowledge (al-ilm al-daruri) is obtained that an accident subsisting in another cannot be living, knowing, powerful, and doer of difficult actions. And since this is false, it is established that it is a self-subsistent essence, knowing, powerful to bring about actions contrary to the human being's will, and powerful to convey those words to the interior of the human being's heart. And we do not mean by 'jinn and devils' (al-jinn wa al-shayatin) except this. So we have named it with this name, and you have named it with the thinking power. So the disagreement is only in wording, but as for the meaning, it is agreed upon. This is the completion of the speech in this chapter.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the philosophers' objection that the thinking power (al-quwwah al-mufakkirah) could be the agent responsible for producing speech and words. The author first states the objection: why can't the thinking power be the doer? He then declares it absolutely false and provides two proofs. The first proof argues that the human being is the unified subject of all perceptions and actions in the body, so thought cannot be a separate agent. The second proof uses a tripartite division: the thinking power is either the human, an attribute, or an independent agent. The first two are eliminated because the human does not will such states, and an attribute cannot act against the human's will. This leaves an independent agent, which must be knowing and powerful. It cannot be an accident (since accidents cannot perform difficult actions), so it must be a self-subsistent essence. The author identifies this essence as jinn or devils, concluding that the dispute is merely verbal—the philosophers call it 'thinking power,' while the author calls it 'jinn.' The passage thus defends the reality of spiritual agents and harmonizes the differing terminology.

Waswasa and Ilham Only Affect the First Stage
Proof that whispering/inspiration affect only the first stage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why satanic whispering and angelic inspiration can only affect the initial stage of human action—the stage of knowledge or awareness—and not the later stages of inclination and power.

Core Idea
Satan can whisper, but he cannot force your will.

In this passage, the author narrows down where external influences like devilish whispers and angelic inspirations can truly have an effect. He systematically eliminates the two later stages of action—where inclination and power combine—leaving only the initial stage of knowledge and awareness. This shows that Satan cannot directly compel you to sin; at most, he can remind you of forgotten desires.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the three stages mentioned: last, intermediate, first.
2
Identify the three stages
The passage examines three possible loci for whispering and inspiration: the last stage (power+inclination), intermediate stage (inclination alone), and first stage (knowledge). Write them down.
3
Understand why the last stage is impossible
The author argues that the effect of power combined with inclination on the act is necessary and inherent. The Necessary Being (God) cannot have any effect from another in it. So whispering cannot affect this stage.
This uses a principle: necessary things cannot be altered by external factors.
4
Understand why the intermediate stage is impossible
The author says that belief in benefit or harm necessarily produces will or aversion. This effect is self-essential and necessary. Therefore, external influences like waswasa cannot affect it.
The key word is 'ḍarūrī' (necessary).
5
See the conclusion and examples
Since the first two stages are impossible, the only possible effect is on the first stage. The author gives examples: Satan reminds a person of forgotten pleasure in sin, and an angel reminds of forgotten joy in obedience.
Thus, waswasa and ilham work by reminding or causing forgetfulness, not by changing inclination or power.
