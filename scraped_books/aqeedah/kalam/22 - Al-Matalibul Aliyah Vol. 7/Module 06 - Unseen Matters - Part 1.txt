Two Reasons for Spirits' Non-Attachment to Bodies
Al-Razi enumerates two causes for spirits' non-attachment to bodies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two possible reasons for spirits not being attached to bodies for management, and how al-Razi grounds his discussion in logic and revelation.

Core Idea
Spirits may avoid bodies either from supreme perfection or from utter weakness.

Al-Razi presents a logical division: when spirits are not managing bodies, it can be for two opposite reasons. One is exalted perfection, the other is debilitating weakness. He then acknowledges the limits of human reason and points to a Quranic verse as an indication. This lesson unpacks his reasoning.

2
Identify the two possibilities

The first cause is perfection and exaltation; the second is weakness and inability.
3
Reflect on the Quranic reference
Al-Razi cites Quran 53:26. Consider how this verse relates to the division he just outlined.
The verse mentions angels whose intercession avails nothing, which may point to the weak category.

Actually the verse is about angels whose intercession is not accepted, which might indicate that some angels are weak? Al-Razi does not specify which possibility the verse refers to; he just says the verse alludes to this division. The commentary should follow the text: first the two possibilities, then the statement of human ignorance, then the Quranic reference.

Celestial Spirits and the Human Soul Analogy
Al-Razi explains the analogy between celestial spirits and human souls in managing bodies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Razi's analogy between the celestial spirits (angels) and human souls, which explains how a single universal soul can manage an entire sphere through particular souls.

Core Idea
Celestial spirits manage spheres like human souls manage bodies — through a hierarchy of forces.

Al-Razi presents a powerful analogy to help us conceive how the celestial spirits (angels) manage the vast celestial bodies. He compares the spheres to the human body, the planets to the heart, and the radiating rays to subtle bodily fluids. This analogy leads to the idea that just as the human soul emanates particular powers to each organ, the universal soul of the sphere emanates particular souls to each part.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Human souls are immaterial managers of the body, not its inhabitants.

Al-Razi presents the prevailing position on the nature of human rational souls. He affirms their immateriality while clarifying their unique relationship with the body—one of management rather than spatial indwelling. This distinction is crucial for theological understandings of the soul's independence from the body.

The Second Proof: Universal Human Practice
Al-Razi argues from universal human practice of blessing or cursing the dead.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Razi's proof from universal human practice for the soul's survival after death and how it relies on innate disposition.

Core Idea
The universal practice of blessing or cursing the dead testifies to the soul's survival.

Al-Razi presents his second proof, drawing on a fact observable across all times and cultures: humans universally bless the good dead and curse the evil dead. If the soul perished with the body, this practice would be meaningless. But the entire human race cannot unanimously engage in a vain practice; it must be rooted in an innate truth. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully

The passage is compact; each sentence carries logical weight.
2
Identify the observation and the conditional
Al-Razi begins by observing a universal practice: people invoke mercy for the good dead and curse for the evil dead. He then poses a conditional: if the person does not survive death, this practice would be vain.
The conditional is the key move: it sets up the absurdity.
3
Understand the argument from impossibility of universal falsehood
Al-Razi argues that it is impossible for all people across all times to be engaged in a vain and false practice. Since the practice is universal, it must reflect a genuine reality.
This is a consensus argument, but grounded in innate disposition (fitra).
4
Formulate the conclusion
The only way to avoid the absurdity is that the soul survives death and is distinct from the body. The practice presupposes a living subject after death.
The conclusion is that the human being (self) is other than the body.

He distinguishes animal souls from human rational souls: animal souls are physical powers (quwā jismāniyya) and not immaterial substances (jawāhir mujarrada), whereas human rational souls are immaterial substances in their very essences. However, Ibn Sīnā then claims that all human rational souls are equal in the entirety of essence and reality (tamām al-māhiyya wa al-ḥaqīqa). To account for observed differences among humans in intelligence, dullness, and morals, he attributes these variations solely to differences in bodily temperaments (amzija al-badaniyya). For example, a person with a choleric temperament (ṣafrāwī al-mizāj) tends to predominate in joy and good morals. Thus, the passage sets up the debate for the chapter by contrasting two positions: one that equates all souls (ancient philosophers) and another that distinguishes animal from human souls but equates all human souls (Ibn Sīnā). The author leaves the question open for further discussion.
