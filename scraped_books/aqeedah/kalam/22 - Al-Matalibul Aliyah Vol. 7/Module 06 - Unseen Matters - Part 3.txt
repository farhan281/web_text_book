The Soul Cannot Cease: Refuting the First Possibility via an Opposite
Three arguments refuting soul's annihilation by a new opposite

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the rational soul cannot be destroyed by the existence of an opposite, based on three logical arguments.

Core Idea
The soul is indestructible because no opposite can overpower it.

This lesson continues the proof of the soul's immortality by examining one specific way it might cease: through the emergence of an opposite. The author presents three decisive reasons showing this scenario is impossible, relying on principles of opposition, strength, and empirical observation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the literal translation. Notice the logical structure: an exhaustive division followed by a focused refutation.
The passage is dense; take time to absorb the flow.
2
Identify the exhaustive division
The author sets up two possibilities for the soul's cessation: either due to the existence of something it needed to lack, or the absence of something it needed to have. Recognize why the first possibility is selected for detailed refutation.
The second possibility is not elaborated here; it is refuted elsewhere.
3
Analyze each of the three reasons
The author gives three reasons why the first possibility is impossible: (1) Opposition is mutual, so repelling is easier; (2) Circularity arises if we attribute removal to a new opposite; (3) The supposed opposite can only be accident, body, or non-material substance—each is shown incapable.
Pay special attention to the third reason, which uses induction to show souls do not oppose each other.

Reflect on the use of induction
The author uses complete induction (استقراء تام) as evidence that souls are not mutually repelling. Consider why this empirical observation is considered convincing in this rational argument.
Induction here provides predominant conjecture (ظن غالب) not absolute certainty, but suffices to refute the possibility.

Commentary
This passage is a structured proof (الحجة الثانية) showing the rational soul's immortality. It begins by setting up an exhaustive disjunction: if the soul could cease, it would be either because something it needed to be absent came into existence, or because something it needed to be present ceased. Both are refuted. The author then focuses on the first division. If the soul were destroyed by something coming into existence, that thing must be an opposite (ضد) of the soul's substance, and it must have originated after the soul. This is impossible for three reasons: (1) Opposition is mutual (من الجانبين). If a new opposite appears, it is just as likely that the soul repels it as that it destroys the soul; indeed repelling is easier (الدفع أسهل من الرفع). (2) If we try to explain the removal of a previous opposite by the new one, circularity results. (3) The supposed opposite can only be an accident, a body, or a non-material substance. Accidents and bodies are weaker than the non-material soul, so cannot destroy it; moreover bodies and accidents coexist with souls without opposition. A non-material substance (another soul) cannot be an opposite either because all souls are either similar (so no difference to generate opposition) or different (but empirical observation—complete induction—shows that souls do not oppose each other). Therefore, the first division is impossible.

Core Idea

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why rational souls cannot be annihilated by any contrary—whether accident, body, or another immaterial substance.

Core Idea
No contrary can destroy a rational soul, whether physical or spiritual.

This passage systematically refutes the notion that rational souls can be destroyed by a contrary. It examines every possible type of contrary—accidents, bodies, and other immaterial substances—and demonstrates that none can cause annihilation. The argument is built on the nature of substances and observed coexistence.

Second Proof: The Soul Knows Particulars
Al-Razi argues that if the soul manages this particular body, it must know it as a particular.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Razi uses the soul's management of the body to prove that it knows particulars.

Core Idea
Managing this body requires knowing it as a particular.

Al-Razi presents a logical argument that moves from the soul's management of the body to its knowledge of particulars. He first establishes that the soul is a substance separate from the body, then uses a dichotomous reasoning to show that it must manage this specific body, and therefore must know it as a particular. This proof is a key example of rational argumentation in kalam.

Guided Path
1
Understand the premise
Al-Razi begins with the established premise: the soul is a substance not inhering in the body. This sets the foundation that the soul is a separate entity capable of acting upon the body.
2
Follow the first dichotomy
The soul either manages this body or it does not. If it does not, there is no connection, which contradicts our experience of the soul's influence on the body. So the soul must manage it.
3
Second dichotomy: which body?
Does the soul manage the absolute (any) body or this particular body? If it managed any body, its effect would be equally present in all bodies, which is false. Therefore, it must manage this particular body.
4
Conclude knowledge of particulars
Managing this particular body requires knowledge of it as this particular. Thus the soul knows particulars.
Note that the passage ends with an objection that is not answered here.

Commentary
This passage is a rational proof by Fakhr al-Din al-Razi that the human soul, after separating from the body at death, continues to know particular things and can act upon them, and that this knowledge and action do not depend on bodily organs. The argument proceeds by presenting a dichotomy: either the soul's perceptions and actions depend on bodily instruments or they do not. If they do not depend on them, then after death the soul becomes even stronger in perceiving particulars and performing actions, which is the desired conclusion. If they do depend on bodily instruments, Razi argues that this is impossible. He refutes the dependency thesis by analyzing how the soul perceives the instrument itself. The soul's perception of the instrument must be either universal or particular. If it is universal (i.e., the soul has a general concept of the instrument), then the soul's being specifically tied to this particular body and instrument rather than any other body or instrument would require explanation—why this specific instrument? This is problematic because a universal concept does not entail a particular attachment. If the perception is particular, then the soul's perception of that specific instrument cannot be via the use of that instrument, because using the instrument presupposes already perceiving it, leading to a vicious circle (dawr): perception requires use, and use requires perception. Thus the dependency thesis is impossible. Therefore, the correct position is that the soul's knowledge of particulars is not dependent on bodily instruments, and after death it continues and even strengthens.

The First Proof Against Jinn: Dense or Subtle Bodies?
Presents the first argument: if jinn exist, they must be one of two body types, both impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical structure of the first argument against the existence of jinn and why both possibilities are considered false.

Core Idea
Jinn cannot exist because they would have to be either dense or subtle bodies, and both are impossible.

This lesson examines the first rational argument presented against the existence of jinn and shayateen. The argument sets up a simple dichotomy: if jinn exist, they must be either dense or subtle bodies. By showing that neither category can account for the properties attributed to jinn, the author concludes that jinn do not exist. Let's walk through the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the English translation to grasp the overall argument.
The argument is concise; pay attention to the logical connectors: 'if...then', 'both are false'.
2
Identify the dichotomy
Notice that the author presents two and only two possibilities for the physical nature of jinn: dense or subtle. This is a complete division.
In logic, this is called a disjunctive syllogism.
3
Examine the refutation of dense bodies
The author argues that if jinn were dense, they would be visible. If we accept invisible dense objects, we could doubt all sensory experience, leading to sophistry.
Sophistry (safsata) here means radical skepticism.

For subtle bodies, two problems arise: they would be fragile (scattered by wind) and incapable of strenuous deeds attributed to jinn.
The argument appeals to physical plausibility.

Refuting Non-Existence and Mental Existence
Razi refutes the first two categories of the trilemma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi refutes the possibilities that inner experiences are pure non-existence or merely mental, thereby proving their external reality as jinn and devils.

Core Idea
If inner experiences were nothing or just mental, then all sensory experience would be equally doubtful — impossible.

In the previous lesson, we saw Razi classify inner experiences into three categories. Now he systematically eliminates the first two. First, he argues that if these specific words and images were pure non-existence, then by analogy all sensory objects could also be considered nothing, which is absurd. Second, he argues against mental existence with two proofs: it leads to skepticism (السفسطة) and it raises insurmountable difficulties regarding the locus of images. Thus, only external existence remains, confirming that jinn and devils are real entities that interact with ascetics.

Commentary

It begins by acknowledging that no rational proof definitively establishes or refutes their existence, so the matter remains in suspension (توقف).
