The Heart Between Two Fingers: Understanding the Metaphor
Sanusi explains the prophetic metaphor of the heart between two fingers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the metaphorical interpretation of the hadith about the heart being between two fingers and how it demonstrates Allah's absolute control over human motives.

Core Idea
Allah creates motives in the heart, directing human actions like turning an object between fingers.

This lesson unpacks a profound prophetic metaphor. The hadith 'the heart of the believer is between two fingers of the Merciful' is often misunderstood as literalism. Sanusi's commentary reveals it as an analogy for divine creation of motives, showing Allah's absolute control over human will. We'll explore this interpretation step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith and its explanation
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that the hadith is quoted first, then interpreted. Let the words settle before analysing.
2
Understand the metaphor: fingers = motives
The passage explains that the 'two fingers' symbolise two motives: motive for action (dāʿī al-fiʿl) and motive for abandonment (dāʿī al-tark). These are created by Allah in the heart.
This is a key point – the metaphor is not literal but about divine causality.
3
Reflect on the analogy of turning an object
Sanusi draws a comparison: as a person turns an object held between two fingers, so Allah turns the heart by creating motives. This illustrates His absolute control.
The analogy makes the concept of divine decree more accessible.
4
Grasp the theological implications for qadar
The passage concludes that the heart is the locus of motives. This affirms that Allah creates human intentions and actions, a core Ashʿarī position on qadar.
Consider how this relates to free will and divine predestination.

And he (peace be upon him) said: 'The heart of the believer is between two fingers of the fingers of the Merciful.' He meant by that: that the occurrence of the act at one time, and the abandonment at another from the servant, is dependent on the occurrence in the heart of a motive for the act or a motive for abandonment, and the occurrence of these motives is from Allah Almighty. So the Prophet (peace be upon him) expressed those two motives as 'two fingers.' Meaning: just as when a man takes something between two fingers, he turns it however he wishes, so too the heart is subjugated between these two motives which Allah Almighty creates in it. So He Almighty disposes of the hearts of servants by means of creating these motives in them. And this indicates that the locus of motives and incentives is the heart.

Commentary
This passage provides an explanation of a well-known prophetic hadith. It first quotes the hadith: 'The heart of the believer is between two fingers of the Merciful.' Then it interprets it. The explanation states that the servant's performance or abandonment of an action depends on whether a motive for action or a motive for abandonment occurs in the heart. These motives are created by Allah. The Prophet metaphorically called these two motives 'two fingers.' The analogy is that just as a person holding an object between two fingers can turn it as he wishes, so the heart is subjugated between these two motives created by Allah. Thus, Allah disposes of hearts by creating motives in them. The passage concludes that this indicates that the heart is the locus of motives and incentives. The explanation emphasizes God's absolute control over human actions through the creation of internal motives, which is a key point in the debate on divine decree and human agency.

Tenth Argument: Contentment with Decree as the Greatest Door
Presents the tenth argument of deniers: contentment as greatest door, du'a as unauthorized, silence as contentment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the tenth argument of those who deny the efficacy of du'a, based on the consensus of spiritual practitioners that contentment with decree is superior, and why they hold that du'a is an unauthorized interference.

Core Idea
Contentment with decree is the greatest door to God; du'a is unauthorized interference.

This lesson presents the final argument from those who deny that supplication (du'a) can change the divine decree. They appeal to the consensus of spiritual masters who consider contentment (rida) with God's decree as the highest spiritual state. According to them, engaging in du'a implies a lack of contentment and an attempt to interfere in divine disposal without permission. Let's examine their reasoning and the conclusion they draw.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that this is the tenth and final argument from the deniers.
2
Identify the three key claims
The passage presents three claims: (1) consensus that contentment is the greatest door, (2) du'a is unauthorized interference, (3) silence is contentment. Find each in the text.
The claims are connected logically.
3
Understand the conclusion
Based on the three claims, the argument concludes that silent contentment is the most complete method and best way. Ask yourself: does this follow from the premises?
This is the deniers' reasoning; later it will be refuted.
4
Consider the common misconception
One might think contentment means never supplicating. But the deniers present this as an extreme ideal. The affirmers will later argue that du'a is part of the decree.
Be careful not to adopt this as the final position.
5
Reflect on the implication for your practice
Think about how this argument challenges the common understanding of du'a. Does it resonate with any spiritual advice you have heard?
This prepares you for the affirmers' response.

Commentary
This passage presents the tenth and final argument from the deniers, followed by a concluding remark. It cites consensus among the practitioners of spiritual exercises (arbab al-riyadat) that contentment (rida) with the divine decree is the greatest door to God. In contrast, engaging in du'a is described as interfering in divine disposal without permission (khawd fi al-tasarruf min ghayr al-idhn). The proper state is silence of heart and tongue, which is true contentment. Therefore, this silent contentment is the most complete method and best way. The passage ends by stating that this is the exposition of the doubts of those who deny the benefit of du'a.

?

The Ninth Proof: Higher Causes and the Impossibility of Prayer
The ninth proof of deniers of prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the ninth proof used by deniers of prayer, which argues that the higher causes' prior non-response proves the impossibility of the request.

Core Idea
If higher causes are generous, their refusal before prayer shows prayer's impossibility.

This lesson presents one of the arguments put forward by those who deny that prayer has any effect. It focuses on the claim that the higher causes are inherently generous, so if they do not grant something before being asked, that thing must be impossible. We will examine the logic of this proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and English translation to get the full argument. Note the structure: the premise about higher causes, the observed refusal, and the conclusion.
The text includes a footnote marker (1) and (3) indicating variant readings; ignore for now.
2
Identify the two possibilities
The argument concludes that the request is impossible either by its own nature (essential impossibility) or because it conflicts with the world's welfare. Find these two alternatives in the text.
The Arabic uses 'imma...aw' to present these options.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
Consider what the deniers aim to prove: that prayer cannot be effective because the higher causes are already generous and would have granted the request if it were possible.
This is presented as a proof (hujja) to be refuted later.

Commentary

The tenth proof cites the consensus of spiritual practitioners that contentment with God's decree is the greatest door to God, while engaging in prayer is an unauthorized interference; thus silence and contentment are the superior path.
