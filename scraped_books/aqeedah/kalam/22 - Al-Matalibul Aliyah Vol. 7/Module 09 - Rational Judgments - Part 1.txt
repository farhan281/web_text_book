Author argues that only a specified cause can be efficient.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the only possible efficient cause of contingent beings must be a specific, determinate entity (the One).

Core Idea
Contingency requires a specific, self-existent cause; an unspecified cause cannot exist.

In this passage, the author presents a concise rational argument to establish that no efficient cause exists except the One (Allah). The argument hinges on the impossibility of an indeterminate cause existing in itself. Let's follow the logical steps carefully.

Objection and Answer on Knowledge of the Self
Razi raises an objection about self-knowledge and resolves it via a key distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi formulates an objection about self-knowledge being self-evident yet disputed, and how he resolves it by distinguishing existential from quidditative knowledge.

Core Idea
Knowledge of your own existence is obvious; knowledge of your true nature requires demonstration.

This passage presents a classic objection in Islamic philosophy: if knowledge of the self is the most obvious thing, why are there so many disagreements? Razi formulates the objection rigorously and then offers a precise answer by distinguishing between two kinds of knowledge. Understanding this dialectic is key to grasping how kalam deals with foundational epistemological puzzles.

If someone says: The most known of known things and the most apparent of apparent things to everyone is his knowledge of the thing referred to by his saying 'I' (anā), which is his specific self and his particular essence. Since this is the most known of known things and the most clear of conceived things, how then did these great disagreements and obscure doubts occur concerning it? And I saw in the treatise known as 'Al-Tuffāḥa' (The Apple), which is the treatise containing the discussions that took place before Aristotle near his death. It was said to him: How can it be understood that a person asks another about his own state? The philosopher answered: It is like the sick person asking the doctor about his ailment, and the blind person asking those around him about his color. And I say: Whoever recalls the formulation of this objection properly, then seeks the answer. As for the formulation of the objection, it is that we say: The most known of things to a person is his self, and the evidence for it is that his knowledge of others depends on his knowledge of his self. For when I say about something: 'I know it,' this is a judgment about my self that it is knowing that thing. And every affirmation (taṣdīq) is preceded by a conceptualization (taṣawwur) of its two sides. The result is: Whenever I judge about my self that I know something, then my knowledge of my self by my self is prior to my knowledge of that judgment. Thus it is established that my knowledge of my self is prior to my knowledge of everything that is other than me. When this is established, it becomes clear that my knowledge of my self is more evident than my knowledge of everything else. So if we were to seek knowledge of the self through knowledge of something else, this would be like defining what is more evident and clearer by what is deeper and more hidden, and that is impossible. Moreover, since knowledge of what is other than the self is conditioned by knowledge of the self, if knowledge of the self were derived from something else, there would be a vicious circle (dawr), which is impossible. The answer: Knowledge of the existence (wujūd) of the self, inasmuch as it is something, is one thing; and knowledge of what it is in detail and specifically is another thing. The clear and evident is the first part, and it is not the object sought by demonstration (burhān) at all. As for the second part, it is what is sought by demonstration; and knowledge of other things is not dependent on knowledge of the self according to this consideration. Thus the objection falls.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection and its answer concerning knowledge of the human self (nafs). The objector claims that the self is the most obvious thing to everyone, so how can there be disagreements about it? He references a story about Aristotle to illustrate that one can be ignorant of one's own state. The author then formulates the objection rigorously: Knowledge of the self is prior to and more evident than knowledge of anything else; therefore, it is impossible to derive knowledge of the self from something else, as that would involve a vicious circle (dawr). The answer distinguishes between two types of knowledge: (1) knowledge of the existence of the self as a bare fact, which is obvious and not in need of proof; and (2) knowledge of the quiddity (māhiyya) of the self in detail, which is what demonstration (burhān) seeks. Since knowledge of other things does not require knowledge of the self in the second sense, the alleged circularity is resolved. The entire exchange is a classic example of kalam dialectic, showing the importance of precise formulation and distinction in addressing philosophical puzzles.

Why the Essence Cannot Be Proven by Demonstration
Razi enumerates two arguments against proving essence through demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's twofold argument that the essence of a thing cannot be known through demonstrative proof because no middle term can connect a thing to itself, and any candidate mediator would be less known than the essence.

Core Idea
A thing's own essence is known directly, not through proof.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi tackles a foundational question in epistemology and metaphysics: can we prove the essence of something using logical demonstration? He argues that it is impossible, and his reasoning reveals deep principles about the structure of proof and what can be known immediately. As you read, pay attention to the two distinct arguments he offers and their shared conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text once to get a sense of its flow. Don't worry about understanding every word; just notice the rhythm and key terms like 'حد أوسط' and 'برهان'.
The passage is concise; each sentence carries weight.
2
Understand the first argument
Razi's first argument focuses on the impossibility of a middle term between a thing and its essence. A demonstration requires a middle term that links subject and predicate, but here the subject and predicate are identical (the thing and its essence). No third term can fit between them.
Think of a demonstration like a bridge: you need something to connect two banks, but if the banks are the same point, no bridge is needed or possible.

The author counters by noting that people manifestly differ about this subject and it is recognized as obscure, so the objection itself undermines a priori truths. The passage systematically defends the possibility and necessity of demonstrative argument in kalam for establishing negative attributes of the soul.

Knowledge of the essence was intuitive, and this requires that our knowledge of our own particular essence be intuitive knowledge. And if this is established, then it is necessary that our essence be this body. So this argument is built on three premises: The first premise: Our knowledge of the attributes of our souls is intuitive knowledge. What indicates it is that I know necessarily that I saw, heard, said, knew, thought, desired, became angry, entered the house, left, traveled to such-and-such town, and returned from it. Whoever disputes that these items of knowledge are intuitive knowledge has disputed the clearest and strongest intuitive knowledge. So it is established that our knowledge of the attributes of ourselves is intuitive knowledge. The second premise: Our saying: If knowledge of an attribute of the soul is intuitive, then knowledge of the essence of the soul must be intuitive. The proof for it is that my knowledge that I saw, heard, reasoned, and reflected is a judgment upon myself of the affirmation of these attributes for it. The one who judges one thing about another must have already had a conception of both sides. If my knowledge of my soul's existence were acquired (kasbī), then before that acquisition I would be doubting my soul's existence. Whoever doubts the soul's existence cannot be knowing the occurrence of attributes specific to it. Since we demonstrated that this knowledge is intuitive, it is necessary that knowledge of the soul's existence be intuitive. This is the well-known issue among the rational people that whenever knowledge of an attribute of a thing is intuitive, knowledge of the essence itself must be intuitive. The third premise: Since it is established that everyone's knowledge of his own particular soul is intuitive, then the soul must be this body or something inside it. The proof for it is that affirming something other than this body and other than parts of this body is a matter of inference (istidlālī), not necessary (ḍarūrī), and that is something about which there is no dispute among the rational people. At this point a syllogism of the second figure is formed. It is said: Every person's soul is known to him intuitively. A non-physical entity that is not a body nor a corporeal entity is not known intuitively. The conclusion: It is impossible for the soul to be something that is not a body nor corporeal. And that is the desired.

Commentary
This passage presents the first argument for the thesis that the human soul is the body. The author begins by stating the conclusion: our knowledge of our own essence is intuitive, implying that our essence must be this body. Then he lays out three premises that support this argument. The first premise is that our knowledge of the attributes of our souls is intuitive. He argues by appealing to self-evident examples: we know without reflection that we have seen, heard, thought, desired, etc. Anyone who denies this intuitiveness is disputing the clearest of intuitive truths. The second premise establishes a principle: if knowledge of an attribute is intuitive, then knowledge of the essence that bears that attribute must also be intuitive. The proof is that judging an attribute of something presupposes knowledge of the thing itself; if knowledge of the self were acquired, we would first have to doubt its existence, which is impossible since we do not doubt. Therefore, knowledge of the soul's existence is intuitive. The third premise argues that since intuitive knowledge of one's own soul is universal, the soul must be either the body or something within it. The reason is that any other non-physical entity would not be known intuitively but only by inference. Then he constructs a second-figure syllogism: the soul is known intuitively; a non-physical entity is not known intuitively; therefore, the soul cannot be a non-physical entity. The conclusion is that the soul is the body.

Learning Objective

Razi presents a clever reductio ad absurdum: if the soul were a non-physical manager of the body, it could in principle switch bodies. But then we could never be sure if the person we meet is the same as before. Since we are certain of identities, the assumption must be false.

Refuting the Objection: The Individuation Argument and Infinite Regress
Al-Razi refutes the objection that only individuation is perceived and shows the necessity of perceiving universals.

Learning Objective

If we only perceived individuation, we couldn't differentiate between qualities like black and white.

So it is established that the perceiver of this pain is a perceiver of pain, and pain is a universal quiddity. So it is established that the perceiver of particulars must perceive universals. We answer the first [objection] as follows: When we perceive this blackness and this whiteness, either the visible is the mere being this being this and that being that, without the visual faculty attaching to the quiddity of blackness and whiteness, or the visible is the composite of its being this and its being blackness, or the composite of its being that and its being whiteness. Second: Even if determination is assumed to be something fixed, then this determination and that determination each share in being determination, so this determination and that determination are two individuals falling under one species, with no difference in quiddity at all. Since that is false by intuition, we know that the object of sight is not merely being this and that, but that being blackness or whiteness is also sensed by sight. Third: Even if determination is assumed to be something fixed and the sensed is the determination, yet determination is also a universal quiddity, so the object of sight would also be a universal, unless it is said that the sensed is not determination but the determination of determination. Then the first argument would recur, and an infinite regress would result.

Eighth Proof: The Soul Must Be a Body Because It Requires Contact to Move Bodies
A proof and objection-answer for the soul's materiality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the eighth proof for the materiality of the soul, which argues that if the soul were non-bodily, it could move all bodies without contact, and why the objection that the soul might only need contact for other bodies is invalid.

Core Idea
If the soul were non-bodily, it could move any body without touch — but it can't, so it must be a body.

This passage presents a crucial argument in Islamic philosophical theology that the soul must be a body. The argument proceeds by assuming the opposite and showing it leads to an absurd conclusion. After presenting the proof, the author anticipates and refutes an objection. Understanding this argument clarifies the relationship between the soul and physical causation.

The proof is a reductio ad absurdum. Trace the 'if...then' statements: if soul non-bodily → no contact needed → can move any body without contact → but we know we can't → therefore soul is bodily.

The eighth proof for those who say that the soul is a body: They may say: If the soul were a substance abstracted from volume and occupation of space, then it would be impossible for its action to depend on contact with the locus of the act. Because what is not occupying space cannot become in contact with what occupies space. If that is the case, then its action would be by way of invention without need for contact and meeting between the agent and the locus of the act. And if that were the case, then it would be necessary that each one of us be able to move bodies without touching them, and without touching something that touches them. This is because the substance of the soul, since it is able to move the body without there being contact between it and that body, we know that the substance of the soul is able to move the body without the intermediary of contact. And other bodies are receptive to motion, and the relation of the substance of the soul to all bodies is equal. So since the soul is able to move some of them without contact, it would be necessary that it has the ability to move the rest without contact. And since that is false by intuition, we know that the soul is not able to move except on condition that it touches what touches it. And everything that touches something of bodies is occupying space. So it is necessary that the soul be a substance occupying space. That is what was sought.
If they say: Why is it not permissible to say that the soul's effect in moving its own body is not conditioned by contact, while its effect in moving other bodies depends on the occurrence of contact between its body and those bodies? For if you do not invalidate this possibility, your proof is not complete.
The answer: Since the body's acceptance of the soul's actions does not depend on the occurrence of contact between the soul and the body, it is necessary that the situation be the same in moving other bodies. Because all bodies are equal in accepting motion, and the relation of the soul to all of them is equal. Because if the soul is abstracted from volume and the attachments of volume, then its essence's relation to the whole is equal. And when the essence of the agent is equal in relation to the whole and the essences of all recipients are equal in relation to the essence of the agent, then the effect must be equal in relation to the whole. So if the agent is independent of contact with the locus of the act in the case of some, then it must be the same for the whole. And if it needs contact in the case of some, then it must need it for the whole.
The utmost that can be said in this matter is that the soul is like a lover of this body, but this intense love necessitates that its attachment to this body is greater, and its action in this body is more. But that the requirement of its essence should change with respect to these bodies—that is impossible. And this is a proof that yields certainty in this sought matter.

Chapter Three: On the narrative of the proof that is the familiar [argument] in establishing the immateriality of the soul. We say: There have come into existence existents that are not divisible, so it necessarily follows that knowledge of them is not divisible, so it necessarily follows that the one described by that knowledge is not divisible. And every locus (mutaḥayyiz) and every state in a locus is divisible. So it necessarily follows that the one described by those sciences is neither a locus nor a state in a locus. Know that this proof is based on four premises: The first premise: An explanation that there have come into existence existents that do not accept division. And it is indicated by several proofs: The first: The Necessary Existence (wājib al-wujūd) by itself is not divisible in any way—neither quantitative division, nor conceptual division arising from genus and species, nor conceptual division arising from matter and form—because everything divided needs its part, and its part is other than it. So everything divided is in need of something other than itself, and everything in need of something other than itself is possible by itself. So everything divided is possible by itself. It follows that everything that is possible by itself is free from composition and combination, thus it is pure individual. The second: The point (nuqṭa) is impossible to be divided. We have already mentioned in the question concerning the establishment of the indivisible substance (al-jawhar al-fard) many proofs for this claim. Among them: The point is the endpoint of a line, so if the point were divided, each of its halves would not be the endpoint of the line. This entails that everything that is an endpoint of a line is impossible to be divided. The third: Unity (al-waḥda) is the furthest thing from the natures of multiplicity. So if unity exists, that is what is sought; and if it does not exist, then multiplicity exists. But multiplicity is a collection of unities, so whenever multiplicity exists, unity exists. It is thus established that it is necessary to acknowledge the existence of unity in any case. The fourth: There is no doubt that in existence there are existing essences (māhiyāt). These essences are either simple (basīṭa) or composite (murakkaba). If they are simple and individual, that is what is sought; and if they are composite, every composite is composed from simples. So in all cases it is necessary to acknowledge the existence of a simple essence free from all aspects of composition. So it is established by these four proofs that there have come into existence existents that do not accept division.

Commentary
This passage presents the overall proof for the soul's immateriality and then details the first of its four premises. The author begins by stating the conclusion: there are indivisible existents, so knowledge of them is indivisible, so the knower is indivisible and neither a locus nor a state in a locus. He then announces that this proof rests on four premises and proceeds to the first premise, which aims to show that indivisible existents do exist. Four arguments are given:
1) The Necessary Existent (God) is indivisible in every sense—quantitatively, conceptually (by genus and species), and by matter-form composition—because division implies need for parts, and need implies contingency, but the Necessary Existent is not contingent. Hence it is a pure individual.
2) The geometric point cannot be divided because if it were, its halves would not be endpoints of a line; thus endpoints are indivisible.
3) Unity (oneness) is an indivisible concept; it either exists or not, but if it does not, multiplicity (which depends on unity) could not exist, so unity must exist.
4) Essences in reality are either simple or composite; composites reduce to simples, so simple (indivisible) essences must exist.
These four proofs together establish the existence of indivisible entities, which is the first premise.

Refutation of Division into Parts Differing in Essence
Refutes the claim that the universal can be divided into parts differing in essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why dividing a universal mental form into parts differing in essence leads to an infinite regress and is therefore impossible.

Core Idea
A universal cannot be divided into essentially different parts without infinite regress.

This passage tackles a specific objection: that the universal mental form splits into parts differing in essence. The author argues that even if we suppose the form corporeal, the divisions are infinite, leading to absurd consequences. Let's see the two reasons given.

Guided Path
1
Understand the claim being refuted
Read the passage and identify the claim: that the universal form divides into two parts differing in essence. This is the second division mentioned earlier.
The claim is presented as an objection to the main argument.
2
Follow the conditional argument
The author says: if the mental form were corporeal, it would have parts corresponding to possible divisions in a body. But those divisions are infinite, so the parts would be infinite.
This sets up the impossibility.
3
Examine the first reason
First reason: if each of these infinite parts exists in a different part of the body (locus), then each body part would have a distinct accident, resulting in a body composed of actually infinite parts—absurd.
This shows a contradiction with the nature of a body.
4
Examine the second reason
Second reason: any multiplicity requires a unifying element. If the form has infinite parts, then a single part has occurred, and the original division would recur, leading to infinite regress.
This shows the argument is self-defeating.
5
Conclude the impossibility
Both reasons show the claim leads to absurdity, so the assumption that the universal can be divided into essentially different parts is invalid.
The refutation is complete.

The Third Proof: Rational Soul's Capacity for Infinite Acts
A syllogistic proof for the immateriality of the rational soul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical proof that the rational soul is immaterial because it can perform infinite acts while bodily powers are limited to finite acts.

Core Idea
The rational soul's capacity for infinite acts proves it is not a bodily power.

This lesson presents a clear syllogistic argument from Islamic theologians for the immateriality of the rational soul. The proof hinges on a key difference: the rational faculty can engage in infinite perceptions and acts, whereas any bodily power is inherently limited by its divisibility. Let's examine the structure of this argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the syllogism structure
Read the passage and identify the two premises and the conclusion of the syllogism.
Note that the passage first states the syllogism, then explains each premise separately.
2
Examine the first premise
How does the rational faculty's capacity for infinite perceptions support the claim of infinite acts? Understand that perception is a type of act, so infinite perception implies infinite acts.
The proof relies on the idea that the rational form can have infinite perceptions, e.g., of truths or numbers.
3
Examine the second premise
Why does the divisibility of bodily powers lead to finitude? Because the capacity of a part is less than the whole, and the whole's capacity is a finite multiple of a finite part, hence finite.
The key principle is that a part cannot equal the whole, and anything less than something else is finite.
4
Understand the conclusion
The conclusion follows necessarily: the rational faculty is not a bodily power. Reflect on what this implies about the nature of the soul.
This is a foundational proof in Islamic theology for the immateriality of the soul.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth question, which challenges the claim that bodily powers cannot perform infinite actions. The opponent argues: every extended thing is divisible, and the state (accident) in a divisible thing is divisible. Therefore, the strength (power) of a part of the power is less than the strength of the whole. The lesser than another must be finite; so part-power is finite, so whole-power is finite. The author refutes by analyzing the premises. First premise (every extended thing is divisible) relies on the denial of the indivisible atom (atomism), which has been discussed before. Second premise (the state in the divisible is divisible) has already been refuted by eight counterexamples in the first argument. Third premise (the strength of part is less than the whole) is granted, but the author questions why the difference must be due to one ceasing before the other. Perhaps the difference is due to slowness, not finite duration. The opponent cannot prove that the difference necessitates finitude; they are required to establish that premise.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The divisibility argument proves that any bodily power must be finite because its parts are finite.
Correction: The argument assumes that difference in strength must be due to cessation, but the author shows that difference could be due to slowness, allowing both parts to be infinite in duration.
