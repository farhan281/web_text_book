The Second Proof: Independence of Physical and Psychological Differences
Second proof from lack of correlation between physical and psychological differences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second proof that soul substances are different, which uses the observation that two people with very different physical constitutions can have similar psychological traits, and vice versa, showing that physical and psychological domains are independent.

Core Idea
Physical similarity does not guarantee psychological similarity; therefore the soul is not a product of the body.

The second proof strengthens the case for the difference of soul substances by considering interpersonal comparisons. We often see two individuals who are very different in their bodily composition yet share similar levels of perception and character, while others with similar bodies have very different minds. This lack of correlation suggests that the psychological realm operates on its own principles, independent of physical temperament. Let's explore this argument in detail.

Guided Path
1
Read the second proof passage
Read the Arabic text for the second proof and its translation. Notice the comparative structure: two cases of similarity/difference.
2
Identify the two observations
The proof presents two scenarios: (1) two people differ in bodily composition but are similar in psychological traits, and (2) two people are similar in temperament but differ in psychological traits. These are the key observations.
This is a double argument showing lack of correlation.
3
Understand the logical conclusion
From these observations, the author infers that equality in temperament can exist without equality in psychological states, and difference in temperament can exist without difference in psychological states. Therefore, the two domains—physical and psychological—are separate principles with no causal connection.
The conclusion is that each domain is 'a principle in itself' (أصل بنفسه).

The Third Proof: Instruments Do Not Imply Faculty
Third proof from the instrument-faculty distinction (incomplete).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third proof for the difference of soul substances, which argues that bodily organs are mere instruments for the soul and their suitability does not guarantee the soul's faculties, as illustrated by the carpenter analogy.

Core Idea
Having a good instrument does not mean one has the skill; similarly, the body's fitness does not create the soul's abilities.

The third proof approaches the issue from a different angle: the relationship between instruments and the skills they serve. Just as a carpenter's sharp tools do not themselves carve wood—the carpenter's skill is separate—so too the body's organs are instruments for the soul's actions. The suitability of the organs does not produce the soul's faculties; rather, the soul possesses its own distinct essence that enables it to use those instruments. This argument further establishes the independence of the soul from the body. However, note that the passage is cut off at the end of the page, so the proof is incomplete.

Guided Path
1
Read the third proof passage
Read the Arabic text for the third proof (as far as it goes) and its translation. Notice the analogy with the carpenter.
2
Understand the instrument-faculty distinction
The proof distinguishes between an instrument (آلة) being suitable for a task and the agent (الفاعل) possessing the faculty (ملكة) to perform that task. The suitability of the instrument does not by itself give the agent the skill.
Example: a carpenter's adze may be extremely sharp, but that doesn't make him a good carver.
3
Apply to the soul-body relationship
Similarly, the bodily organs (like eyes, brain) may be perfectly suited for perceiving, but that does not necessitate that the soul has the faculty of perception. The soul's faculties are its own, not generated by the body's instrumentality.
The proof is incomplete; it likely concludes that the soul's essence is the source of faculties, not the body.

The Three Primary Faculties of the Soul
Al-Razi enumerates the three primary faculties of the soul: rational, irascible, and appetitive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three primary faculties of the soul as classified by al-Razi, their hierarchy, and how they give rise to all moral traits.

Core Idea
The soul's three faculties form a hierarchy of nobility, branching into all moral traits.

Al-Razi presents a foundational classification of the soul's powers that underlies Islamic ethical theory. This passage enumerates the three faculties from which all moral traits arise. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for grasping how human actions are analyzed in kalam and philosophy.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the three primary faculties of the soul according to the philosophers. Al-Razi states that the philosophers have explained in ethics books that the original psychic faculties (al-quwa al-asliyya al-nafsaniyya) are three. The middle is the irascible faculty (al-quwwa al-ghadabiyya), located in the heart (qalb). The lowest is the appetitive faculty (al-quwwa al-shahwaniyya), located in the liver (kabid). He concludes that all moral traits and attributes branch from these three. This classification is foundational for understanding human psychology and ethics in Islamic philosophy and kalam.

The cognitive faculty has two parts: intellect as root, acquired sciences as branch.

Classification and First Proof for the Origination of Rational Souls
Al-Razi classifies opinions and gives the first proof for the soul's creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the classification of opinions on the eternity of rational souls and the first proof that demonstrates their origination.

Core Idea
The soul cannot be eternal because in pre-eternity it cannot be either one or many.

Al-Razi presents a systematic classification of possible views on whether rational souls are eternal or created, then provides a powerful argument that eliminates both unity and multiplicity in pre-eternity. This first proof is foundational in Islamic philosophical theology, showing that the soul must be originated.

Guided Path
1
Understand the classification
Read the first part of the passage where Al-Razi divides the possible positions into four: eternal (either necessary or contingent) or originated (either pre-existent to bodies or not).
Notice that this classification covers all logical possibilities.
2
Follow the proof's initial division
The proof assumes souls are eternal and then argues they must be either one or many in pre-eternity. Both alternatives are examined.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum structure.
3
Examine the refutation of oneness
If souls were one, upon attachment to bodies they would either remain one (leading to all humans sharing one soul and identical knowledge) or become multiple (which either contradicts pre-eternal oneness or implies origination).
The absurdity of shared knowledge is a strong intuitive counterexample.
4
Examine the refutation of multiplicity
If souls were many in pre-eternity, they would need a distinguishing factor by essence, necessary concomitants, or accidents. Essence and concomitants are shared, and accidents require bodies (which don't exist in pre-eternity).
This shows that multiplicity is impossible without matter.
5
Grasp the conclusion
Since both unity and multiplicity in pre-eternity are impossible, souls cannot be eternal; they must be originated.
This proof is considered decisive by those who accept its premises.

Commentary
This passage presents a classification of opinions on the question of whether rational souls (al-nufus al-natiqa) are eternal (qadima) or originated (haditha) and then gives the first proof of those who hold origination. The author first divides the possible positions: souls are either eternal or originated. If eternal, they are either necessary in themselves or contingent but necessitated by a cause. If originated, they either existed before bodies (as some traditions suggest) or did not (the view of Aristotle and his followers). Then he presents the first proof: if souls were eternal, they would in pre-eternity (al-azal) be either one or many. Both options are impossible. If they were one, then upon attachment to bodies they would either remain one or become multiple. If they remain one, then all humans would share one soul, leading to identical knowledge and ignorance—absurd. If they become multiple after being one, then the newly multiple souls must either have existed in pre-eternity (contradicting the assumption of oneness) or come into existence after non-existence, which implies origination—the desired conclusion. Next, the author argues that multiplicity in pre-eternity is also impossible because distinction requires a distinguishing factor—either essence, necessary concomitants, or accidents. Essence cannot distinguish because souls are equal in essence. Concomitants are shared and cannot differentiate. Accidents require matter (bodies), which do not exist in pre-eternity. Therefore, souls cannot be multiple in pre-eternity. Since both unity and multiplicity are impossible, souls cannot be eternal; they must be originated.
