In this passage, the author presents a sharp reductio argument against the idea that the soul is a disembodied substance. By assuming the soul were separate, he shows that the human would then be identified with the body and its perceptions and movements. Pay attention to the logical move: the separate substance becomes something else, leaving the body as the true self.

The Ninth Proof: Human is a Corporeal Substance with Six Attributes
Razi argues from the definition of human that 'human' refers to the body with attributes, not an immaterial soul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that the human being is a corporeal substance (body) with six attributes, not an immaterial soul, based on the agreed definition of 'human' among philosophers.

Core Idea
The human is the body with attributes, not an immaterial manager.

This lesson presents Razi's ninth proof against the existence of an immaterial soul. Razi uses the philosophers' own definition of the human to show that 'human' refers to the body described by six attributes. He then addresses an objection that posits a separate manager, clarifying that discussion is about the human's essence, not its manager.

Commentary
This passage is a proof (al-ḥujja al-tāsiʿa) based on definition. The discussion is about the reality of the human (ḥaqīqat al-insān), not its manager; the manager of the human and all the world is God alone.

Tenth Proof: Common Sense Identifies Man as the Visible Body
Razi presents the tenth proof: common sense identifies man with the visible body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi uses universal common sense and ordinary discourse to prove that the human being is the visible body, not an immaterial soul.

Core Idea
Common sense and everyday language identify man with his body, not a hidden soul.

Razi presents the tenth proof, which he calls the one relied upon by theologians. It is not a philosophical argument but an appeal to what every rational person already knows. By examining how people actually talk and point, Razi argues that the human is the visible body — a claim so obvious that denying it would be absurd. Let's see how he builds this case.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage carefully without trying to analyze it. Then read the literal translation to grasp the flow of Razi's argument.
Notice how Razi uses the rhetorical tactic of appealing to universal consensus.
2
Identify the two parts of the argument
Razi gives two related tests: the 'pointing test' (asking what is man) and the 'discourse test' (who receives commands and punishments). Identify each part in the text.
Both tests lead to the same conclusion.
3

Razi repeatedly uses the term 'badāʼih al-ʻuqūl' (primacy/self-evidence of intellects). Reflect on what makes a belief self-evident and why Razi considers this a strong proof.
In kalam, self-evident knowledge does not require further proof.
4
Grasp the conclusion
Razi concludes that the identity of man and the visible body is 'self-evident knowledge' ('ilm badīhī). Ask yourself: What would it take to overturn such self-evident knowledge?
This sets the stage for debating dualism.
5
Reflect on the significance
Consider why this proof is important for the debate about the rational soul. If the human is the body, what happens to the idea of a separate immaterial soul?
Razi is presenting this as a refutation of those who affirm a distinct rational soul.

Commentary
This passage presents the tenth proof (al-ḥujja al-ʿāshira), which is said to be the one relied upon by the theologians (al-mutakallimūn). It is based on common sense and linguistic intuition. The proof argues that if you ask any rational person 'What is man?', they will point to the visible body (al-haykal al-makhṣūṣ, al-binya al-makhṣūṣa). If someone claims that man is something else, common sense (badāʾih al-ʿuqūl) declares them a liar. Additionally, the primary intuitions of the mind judge that commands, rewards, and punishments are addressed to this body. If someone claimed the addressee is different, all rational people would reject it. Therefore, the knowledge that man is this visible body is self-evident (badīhī awwalī). Criticizing it is invalid. The passage concludes that these are the total arguments of those who deny the rational soul (nafs nāṭiqa), affirming that they have refuted its existence as a separate substance.

Proof That Knowledge of an Indivisible Object Is Indivisible
The author proves that if the known is indivisible, knowledge of it must be indivisible.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Indivisible objects can only be known by indivisible knowledge – any division leads to contradiction.

This passage presents a rigorous logical proof in classical Islamic theology. The author systematically considers all possible ways that knowledge could relate to an indivisible object and shows that only one conclusion is consistent: knowledge of an indivisible object must itself be indivisible. Follow along as we unpack each step of the argument.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof that knowledge (al-ilm) of an indivisible object (al-ma'lum ghayr munqasim) must itself be indivisible. The argument proceeds by considering three possibilities for the relationship between the parts of knowledge and the indivisible object. The first two possibilities are rejected, and the third leads to the conclusion that knowledge of an indivisible object is itself indivisible. The author first states that if knowledge were composed of parts, then each part would either be knowledge of a part of the object (first division), or be knowledge of the whole object (second division), or be neither (third division). The first is impossible because the object is indivisible. The second is also impossible because if a part of knowledge knows the whole object, then the other parts would be redundant. The third division is examined in detail: if the parts together produce no additional reality, then the collection is not knowledge of the object, contradicting the assumption. If they produce an additional reality, that reality is either divisible or indivisible. If divisible, the same division applies; if indivisible, then it is the desired indivisible knowledge. Thus the conclusion is established: an indivisible object requires an indivisible knowledge.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The hylic intellect is pure potential, like a blank slate ready to receive knowledge.

Understand the meaning of 'hylic'
The term 'hylic' comes from Greek hyle, meaning matter or prime matter. In philosophy, prime matter is pure potentiality without form. Similarly, the child's soul is empty of knowledge but capable of receiving it.

Without receptivity, the soul could never learn.

This rank is the starting point of human intellectual development. It establishes that we are not born with knowledge, but with the capacity to acquire it. This is foundational for theories of learning and epistemology.

والثاني : أن يقال : إنها نفوس مجردة ، ليست أجساماً ، ولا حـالـة في الأجسام . وعلى هذا التقدير فههنا احتمالات . الأول : أن يقال : إنها عالمة قادرة سامعة مبصرة ، من غير أن يكون لها حاجة في حصول هذه الصفات إلى آلات جسمانية. وتلك النفوس بنوعياتها (٣) مخالفة للنفوس البشرية . والثاني : أن يقال : إنها تحتاج في كونها عالمة ، فاعلة إلى آلات جسمانية. إلا أن تلك الآلات جزء مخصوص من الهواء أو جزء (1) معين من كرة الأثير. وتبدد أجزاء تلك الأجسام وتحللها [ لا) ] يوجب موت تلك الأرواح . كما أن النفوس الإنسانية إنما تسمع وتبصر وتتفكر وتتخيل بواسطة أجسام لطيفة روحانية ، تتولد في القلب أو في الدماغ . ثم إن تلك الأجزاء تبدأ في التحلل والتفرق . ولم يلزم منه الطعن في كون الإنسان باقياً ، فكذا ههنا . الثالث : أن يقال : إن النفوس الناطقة التي فارقت أبدانها (٦) وبقيت دهراً داهراً ، وزماناً طويلاً في إشراق المعارف القدسية . لا شك أنها قويت [ وكملت . فإذا اتفق حدوث بدن قريب الشبه من أبدان تلك النفوس المفارقة (۱) ] وتعلقت نفس بهذا البدن . كاملة المشابية [ في جوهرها (٣) ) بتلك النفوس المفارقة ، لم يبعد أن يحصل لتلك النفوس نوع من أنواع التعلق بهذا البدن ، وبهذه النفس . وهذا أيضاً محتمل .
And the second: It is said: They are abstract souls, not bodies, nor inhering in bodies. On this assumption, there are possibilities: The first: It is said: They are knowing, powerful, hearing, seeing, without needing bodily instruments for acquiring these attributes. And those souls in their types are different from human souls. The second: It is said: They need bodily instruments for being knowing and acting, except that those instruments are a particular part of air or a specific part of the sphere of ether. And the dispersion of the parts of those bodies and their dissolution does not cause the death of those spirits. Just as human souls only hear, see, think, and imagine through subtle spiritual bodies generated in the heart or brain. Then those parts begin to dissolve and separate, yet this does not entail impugning that man remains. So likewise here. The third: It is said: The rational souls that have separated from their bodies and remained for a long age, a long time in the radiance of holy knowledge—undoubtedly they became strong and perfected. So if a body happens that is closely similar to the bodies of those separate souls, and a soul attaches to this body, complete in its similarity in essence to those separate souls, it is not far-fetched that those souls obtain a kind of attachment to this body and to this soul. And this also is possible.

Commentary
This passage presents the second way of affirming jinn: they are abstract souls (نفوس مجردة) that are neither bodies nor accidents. Three possibilities are then listed: First, they have knowledge, power, etc. without needing physical organs, and differ from human souls. Second, they do need physical instruments, but those instruments are special parts of air or ether that do not die when dispersed, analogous to human subtle bodies. Third, souls that have already separated and become perfected can reattach to a new similar body. Each possibility is proposed as plausible.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Abstract souls are completely independent of any material substratum.
Correction: The passage shows that possibilities 2 and 3 involve subtle instruments or reattachment to bodies, so abstract souls can still have limited material interaction.

Key Takeaway
The three possibilities offer a spectrum from total independence (no instruments) to partial dependence (subtle instruments or reattachment), all consistent with jinn being abstract souls.
