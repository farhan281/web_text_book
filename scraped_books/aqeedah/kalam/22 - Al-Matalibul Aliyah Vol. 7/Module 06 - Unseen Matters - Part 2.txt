Commentary
This passage presents Ibn Sina's first proof against the doctrine of metempsychosis (tanasukh) and three objections raised against it. The proof begins from two premises: that human souls are created (haditha) and that their cause is eternal (qadima). An eternal cause, according to Ibn Sina, can only produce a temporal effect if a temporal condition (shart hadith) is fulfilled—in this case, the occurrence of a body. Thus, when a body arises, a soul must necessarily emanate from the Necessary of Forms (Wajib al-Suwar). If a soul were to transmigrate into that body, then two souls would attach to one body: the transmigrating soul and the newly emanated soul. This is impossible, as we each experience ourselves as a single soul. The first objection attacks the premise that the soul is created, claiming circularity: the proof of the soul's creation depends on refuting metempsychosis, yet the refutation of metempsychosis uses the soul's creation. The second objection concedes the soul's creation but questions why the cause must be necessitating; if God acts as a free agent, He can create souls at will without the body's condition. This shifts the debate to the issue of determinism vs. free will. The third objection grants all premises but challenges the identification of the temporal condition with the body's temperament; it offers alternative possibilities such as intellectual progress, astronomical alignments, or astrological factors. The objector demands evidence that the temperament is the sole condition.

Three Objections to Ibn Sina's Proof Against Metempsychosis
Three objections are raised against Ibn Sina's proof, attacking circularity, necessitation, and the specific temporal condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three objections raised against Ibn Sina's proof and their implications for the soul and causation.

Core Idea
Objectors challenge Ibn Sina's premises, his necessitarian causation, and his identification of the body's temperament as the sole condition.

Ibn Sina's proof against metempsychosis was not without its critics. Three objections directly target his argument: the first claims circularity, the second questions why the cause must be necessitating rather than a free agent, and the third demands proof that the body's temperament is the only temporal condition. This lesson examines each objection in detail.

Guided Path
1
Read the objections
Read the Arabic passage containing all three objections. Then read the translation. Note that each objection begins with a conditional concession.
The objections are numbered (الاول, الثاني, الثالث).
2
Understand the circularity objection
The first objection points out that Ibn Sina's proof depends on the soul being created, but the proof of the soul's creation itself depends on refuting metempsychosis. This would create a vicious circle.
Circularity (al-dawr) is a logical fallacy.
3
Analyze the free agent objection
The second objection concedes the soul is created but denies that the cause must be necessitating. If God is a free agent, He can create souls without any temporal condition.
This shifts the debate to the issue of whether God acts by necessity or choice.
4
Examine the alternative conditions objection
The third objection grants all premises but argues that the temporal condition could be something other than the body's temperament, such as intellectual progress or astronomical events.
The burden of proof is on Ibn Sina to show why the temperament is the only condition.

Commentary
This passage is the sixth rational proof (al-hujjah al-sadisah) demonstrating that the soul does not die with the body. It consists of two parts: a causal argument and a subtle point about the hierarchy of existence. The author begins by recalling earlier proofs that the substance (jawhar) of the soul is like the substance of angels, that its cause (mu'aththir) must be a purely intellectual substance (jawhar 'aqli mujarrad), and that the source of its knowledge is also a purely intellectual substance. Since these causes persist, they necessitate the continued existence of the soul's substance and its attributes. To claim that the soul ceases with the cessation of the accidental relation would be to prefer the implication of the weak accidental matter over the implication of the essential, original matter, which is absurd according to the first principles of reason. Hence the soul must be immortal. The second part presents a subtle point (dabiqah) from the author's earlier work 'The High and Low Spirits.' If one claimed that the loss of the base relationship (body management) annihilates the soul's essence and knowledge, one would be treating the weaker side as nobler than the stronger side, which is impossible. Thus the soul's immortality is further confirmed.

The Precaution Argument for the Soul's Survival

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Precaution favors belief in the soul's survival because the stakes are eternal.

This lesson presents the second proof for the soul's survival: the argument from precaution. Unlike a direct demonstration, this argument weighs the consequences of two beliefs. By comparing the outcomes of believing in the soul's survival versus its annihilation, the author shows that reason demands we adopt the safer belief. The passage uses a pragmatic calculus that any rational person can appreciate.

Guided Path
1
Understand the premise: the body's time is negligible

The passage contrasts two beliefs: (A) the soul survives death and faces eternal reward or punishment, and (B) the soul perishes completely. Notice how each is described.

Belief A: best = eternal reward, worst = loss of temporary pleasures. Belief B: best = temporary pleasures, worst = eternal punishment.
4
Apply the principle of precaution
Now compare the two beliefs. Which one has a better worst-case scenario? Which offers the greater potential gain? The principle of precaution (ihtiyat) tells us to choose the safer path.

This lesson presents a crisp reductio ad absurdum from the classical tradition. Learning Objective

Refuting the Objection that the Soul's Attributes Depend on the Body
The author refutes the objection that the soul's attributes are conditional on the body using circularity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the soul's attributes (life, knowledge, power) must be intrinsic and cannot be conditional on the body, due to circularity.

Core Idea
The soul's attributes are intrinsic, not derived from the body – otherwise circularity.

The objection argues that perhaps the soul's attributes are given by the body. The author answers by showing that the soul's attachment to the body itself requires the soul to already possess those attributes. If attributes were conditional on attachment, then each would depend on the other, which is logically impossible.

These premises are the core of the argument.

Circularity makes the position impossible.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. The objector suggests that perhaps the soul's attributes of life, knowledge, and power are conditional on the body: the 'transient form' (الصورة الزائلة) that leaves the body at death could be a condition for the soul's substance to have those attributes. The author answers that the soul's attachment to the body itself is conditional on the soul already possessing these attributes in its essence. If the attributes were instead conditional on the soul's attachment to the body, then each would depend on the other: the soul would need the body to have its attributes, and the body would need the soul's attributes to be attached. This creates a circular dependency (دور), which is logically invalid. The conclusion is that the soul's attributes are intrinsic, not dependent on the body.
